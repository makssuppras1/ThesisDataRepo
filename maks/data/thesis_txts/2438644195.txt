Abstract
This thesis is a theoretical study of periodic/cyclic separation techniques for strip-
ping and distillation columns. Two of the Lewis cases have been re-derived, where
Lewis case II proved a double tray eﬃciency in respect to conventional operation.
Lewis case II has a tray conﬁguration, which is analogous to a periodic tray. This
analogy has also been derived. The classic deﬁnition of periodic or cyclic distil-
lation is in this project also referred to as simultaneous drainage operation. This
operating mode poses a periodic vapor interruption. The vapor interruption can
be avoided by employing the sequential drainage operation, which was published
by Tofteg˚ard et al. (2016) [1].
A novel model for the simultaneous drainage operation has been successfully im-
plemented together with established models for the simultaneous drainage and
conventional operation. Graphic representation methods by using average based
straight operating lines with belonging tray eﬃciencies have also been executed.
Performances of the three operating modes have been compared on two case stud-
ies. The ﬁrst case is a three staged stripping column for stripping ammonia from
water. The second case is a 9 staged (excluding condenser) distillation column for
separating a benzene-toluene mixture.
For the stripping operation, simultaneous drainage is superior by 123.00 % whereas
the sequential drainage operation is superior by 100.33 % in terms of average tray
eﬃciencies to the continuous conventional operating mode. For the distillation
case, the simultaneous drainage operation has an average tray eﬃciency enhance-
ment of 73.00 % whereas the sequential drainage operation is superior by 51.22 %
to the corresponding conventional operation. Thus, the separating ability can be
signiﬁcantly improved if a continuous conventional operating column is replaced
with a periodically operating column. Sequential operation is about 10 % less
eﬃcient in terms of tray eﬃciency compared to simultaneous operation. A ques-
tion arises if these 10% less eﬃciency outweigh a practical pressure buildup in the
corresponding simultaneous drainage operation.

1.
Introduction
Almost all commercial chemicals arise from a separation process that could be
improved. Of these processes, distillation is widely applied, not least accounting
for far most of the energy usage [2].
One of the best known purposes of distillation is reﬁning crude oil, which is a mix-
ture of hundreds of liquid hydrocarbons. Such mixtures are separated into their
main fractions; naphta, kerosine and gas oil. This constitutes the ﬁrst stage in con-
verting oil into bulk compounds that can be further manufactured into everything
from plastics to medicines. Dissolved in the crude oil are also some solids and
gases. The gases constitute lower members of the alkane family, predominantly
methane and ethane but often with some propane and butane. These are distilled
into liquid fractions with diﬀerent boiling point ranges which are then further pro-
cessed. Distillation also serves to manufacture and purify nitrogen, oxygen, rare
gasses, alcohols, and other bulk compounds [3]. Distillation is also employed in
many recovery steps of eg. alcohols from aqueous solutions in the bioindustry.
As stated, distillation processes require huge amounts of energy. In the US, sepa-
rations in chemical industry account for 10-15% of the nation’s total energy con-
sumption. By developing alternatives that do not use heat, 80% of thee separations
could become 10 times more energy eﬃcient [2]. Applying more energy eﬃcient
methods within the petroleum, chemical and paper manufacturing sectors, would
alone save 100 million tonnes of carbon dioxide emissions and US$ 4 billion in
annual energy costs [4]. In fact, distillation alone accounts for a quarter of the
energy consumption in the entire industry [2].
So, with the large energy costs and an increased focus on eﬃciency and sustain-
ability, implementation of optimized/intensiﬁed distillation represents major op-
portunities in chemical industry [5] and has therefore received mush attention in
the past decades. For this purpose, many kinds of integration and intensiﬁcation
mechanisms have been investigated such as: internal heat-integrated distillation
columns, dividing wall columns, reactive distillation, and even reactive dividing-
wall columns [6]. Another route is focusing on optimizing the distillation process
itself by looking into cyclic or periodic distillation, which presently receives re-
newed attention since it was ﬁrst introduced about 6 decades ago. The concept of
cyclic distillation will be described in the following section.

1.1
Cyclic Distillation
Cyclic distillation is an operational form for a distillation process, where control
signals are changed periodically. There is a vapor ﬂow period (VFP) and a liquid
ﬂow period (LFP). Together the VFP and LFP constitute a whole operating cycle,
which is then repeated. During the VFP, vapor ﬂows upwards through each tray
which has a stationary liquid holdup. This means that no downcomers on plates
are needed. The thrust of the rising vapor prevents liquid down ﬂow during the
VFP. Mass transfer occurs between liquid and vapor providing a tray wise liquid
concentration proﬁle against time. When the VFP is ﬁnalized, the vapor ﬂow is
interrupted and there is no vapor thrust. This is the beginning of the LFP where
the liquid holdups are mobilized to ﬂow down through the perforated plates by
gravity. During the LFP, reﬂux and feed is supplied, liquid holdups are dropped
from each tray to the tray below, and bottoms and distillate products are with-
drawn.
The above is the classical and most common description of cyclic distillation. As
will be described, this is not the only way to perform cyclic distillation. Common
to all is the periodic control and theoretically they may give a tray eﬃciency of
about twice that of a conventional column [7].
Pro’s and Con’s of Cyclic Distillation
There are several advantages of cyclic distillation compared to conventional distilla-
tion. The most signiﬁcant ones are increased tray and column eﬃciency. Increased
eﬃciency leads to a corresponding column height reduction, which also leads to
lower pressure drops. Alternatively, the reﬂux ratio may be reduced [8]. The above
statements serve to reduce operational and capital costs. Challenges have been to
obtain the theoretical performance in practice since mixing of liquids with diﬀer-
ent concentrations must be eliminated. A pressure buildup in the reboiler when
periodically interrupting the vapor ﬂow will also represent an expenditure since
huge control valves will be required for smooth operation [7].

2.
Background
In this chapter, a literature review is conducted where some of the most prominent
contributions to cyclic distillation will be chronologically reviewed. The literature
review will include theoretical approaches for solving a system of material balances
to describe the periodic operating form. Also, publications involving either experi-
mental setups or actual realizations that have been carried out through history will
be reviewed. Combining theory with practice in the historical aspect generates a
holistic view of the issues and ideas for further consideration. By this, the chapter
forms the basis for the work ahead in the present project.
2.1
Literature Review
Periodic separation in ﬁelds of distillation, liquid-liquid extraction, and particle
separation was ﬁrst introduced by Cannon in 1961 [9]. At the time, the operation
form was called ”Controlled Cycling” which, in terms of distillation, was the same
operating form as described in Section 1.1. Cannon argued for higher capacity,
simpler plate design and higher ﬂexibility due to the choice of diﬀerent period
times.
The works by Cannon, McWhirter and Gaska showed improvements in the perfor-
mance both by higher throughput and increased stage eﬃciency by experiments.
Controlled cycling on a 2-inch diameter column with diﬀerent types of plates with-
out downcomers was performed (see Figure 2.1).
They compared ”dual-ﬂow”
operation to cyclic operation on the same plates. It was concluded that cyclic dis-
tillation has a higher throughput than dual-ﬂow distillation [10]. However, using
the same trays for dual-ﬂow requires a lower vapor ﬂow to allow liquid to drain
through the trays, which naturally gives a lower throughput. Thus, the statement
of higher throughput is an erroneous statement made in many of the ﬁrst papers
as two operating forms require diﬀerent tray designs [7].
Cannon and workers
concluded that for high plate eﬃciency, the VFP should pose a big percent of one
operating cycle. For high capacity, the VFP should pose a small percent of one
operating cycle [11]. During the LFP, the holdups should only drop exactly one
plate at a time. It is not mentioned how this is ensured. It was also concluded
that by cyclic operation, a maximum of 160% overall plate eﬃciency (theoretical
stages per real tray) was achieved [11]. The work is purely empirical and does not
seem founded in theoretical considerations.

In 1963, McWirter and Lloyd were the ﬁrst to present a mathematical model for
periodic distillation, which showed that the column eﬃciency may be doubled for
cyclic operation. Experiments with a 5 stage, 6 inch diameter column were oper-
ated with and without periodicity. Results showed a more than doubled overall
stage eﬃciency for periodic operation; ”conventional” (dual-ﬂow) gave a 90% stage
eﬃciency whereas periodic gave 220% stage eﬃciency. They assumed plug ﬂow of
liquid. However, they were aware that some mixing is unavoidable, which will
destroy the concentration gradient across the column and lead to a decrease in the
separating ability. The paper also stated that ”control of the cycle time provides
an unusual degree of ﬂexibility, and gives a wide operating range.” They claimed,
like the previous papers, that that capacity could be increased by a factor 3 [12].
They were applying the same plates for dual ﬂow and continuous operation. For
a true comparison to continuous conventional operation, conventional plates with
weirs and downcomers must be used.
Schrodt (1965) carried out laboratory experiments in a 11/2 inch column. Five
times increase in throughput over ”conventional distillation” (dual-ﬂow) was re-
ported. This is not helpful as the same trays were applied for both operating
modes. A semi-plant scale column was designed, built, and operated, which re-
ported the same behaviour as in the lab. Solving simple material balances claimed
to reveal a driving force (closely related to eﬃciency) twice as large in comparison
to ”conventional operation”. An important mention in the paper is that the ideal
case for cyclic distillation is plug ﬂow and complete dumping of liquid from the
trays [13].
In 1966, Sommerfeld, Schrodt, Parisot, and Chien [14] set up a dynamic model for
6
==PAGE:13==
periodic distillation. Simulations were used to generate the stationary concentra-
tion proﬁle at the end of the VFP which was reportedly achieved for all practical
purposes after 150 cycles. They investigated theoretical eﬀects of various param-
eters on the column eﬃciency. Finally, they were the ﬁrst to demonstrate the
mathematical analogy between periodic distillation and Lewis case II for conven-
tional distillation. Lewis [15] proved that a case II conﬁguration gave a signiﬁcantly
greater plate to point eﬃciency ratio than the conventional case III conﬁguration.
In the succeeding paper, Chien, Sommerfeld, Schrodt, and Parisot found the sta-
tionary solution for a linear model and described how non-linear equations may be
solved iteratively by approximating linear equilibrium relationships on each tray.
They were also ﬁrst to describe a graphical method similar to the McCabe thiele
method [16, 7].
In 1967, Schrodt, Sommerfeld, Martin, Parisot, and Chien provided the ﬁrst re-
alized eﬀort for periodic distillation in a larger plant-scale 15 staged, 12 inch di-
ameter column. They did not manage to achieve even the maximum eﬃciency
as that obtainable with conventional operation. This is in spite their awareness
that, in theory, a tray eﬃciency twice as that for conventional operation should
be achievable. The encountered issue was explained by the non-uniform liquid
ﬂow behavior. In fact, the lower trays would drain completely before any liquid
would ﬂow from the upper trays. This was a result from tray design and pressure
diﬀerence. Plug ﬂow was necessary if the higher eﬃciencies as promised by cyclic
operation were to be achieved. Additionally, like the previous papers, the erro-
neous (due to applying the same trays) comparison of an ”impressive” increase
in throughput by periodic distillation over the ”conventional” dual-ﬂow was con-
cluded [17].
In the same year, Robinson and Engel (1967) emphasized the analogy between
Lewis case II and periodic distillation by replacing the distance axis as applied in
conventional distillation with the time axis. The degree of axial mixing in terms of
the fraction, φ, of liquid holdup that ﬂows down during the LFP was investigated.
If the fraction, φ, is greater than 1, more than one holdup ﬂows down during LFP.
If φ is smaller than 1, not all the liquid holdup on a tray is transferred to the tray
below during LFP. It was concluded that φ = 1 is the ideal case providing the best
performance, where all holdup is transferred only to the tray below, and thereby
no axial mixing between trays is encountered [18].
In 1968, Horn and May investigated the inﬂuence of liquid mixing on stage eﬃ-
ciency during LFP. Instead of plug ﬂow, a new model was proposed: liquid going
through m well stirred mixers in series when drained from tray to tray. They
concluded that if the liquid ﬂows simultaneously in every stage, mixing could not
be avoided. Thus, the plug ﬂow assumption was never actually realized. Follow-
ing this consideration, an interesting idea was proposed for transportation of the
7
==PAGE:14==
liquid: ”At the end of the VFP, the bottom stage is emptied ﬁrst, then the liquid
in the stage above is transferred to the stage below and so on, until the top stage is
emptied.” This eliminates one of the major issues of periodic distillation; mixing
between stages. At the time, this procedure was considered diﬃcult to do in prac-
tice and the case was relaxed. Horn and May introduced the transport number,
η, which is the ratio of the quantity of liquid transported per cycle to the liquid
holdup of a stage. This number η had the same deﬁnition as φ from Robinson
and Engel (1967). The simulations by Horn and May showed that for plug ﬂow
(m →∞) the maximum stage eﬃciency was obtained for η = 1 [19]. This conclu-
sion agrees with the conclusion made by Robinson and Engel (1967).
In 1969, Wade, Jones, Rooney and Evans did simulations, which showed that
the liquid would drain from the lowermost trays before it would begin from the
uppermost trays. These simulations supported the issues that Schrodt et al. en-
countered in practice in 1967. Wade et al. proposed a pressure equalizing manifold
which is open during LFP and closed during VFP. The idea was implemented and
simulations resulted in a signiﬁcant improvement on the performance [20].
Gerster and Scull (1970) developed a one-parameter mixing model.
The mix-
ing coeﬃcient was determined experimentally by stripping ammonia from ammo-
nia rich water by air. Experimental eﬃciencies agreed with the proposed model
and eﬃciency improvements for the cycled column over conventional column was
demonstrated. Oscillations caused dumping of liquid. A fraction of the liquid was
assumed to be left on the tray when the liquid from the tray above reached the
tray. This model predicted maximum stage eﬃciency for η = 1 if the liquid ﬂows
by plug ﬂow [21].
Furzer and Duﬀy 1976 developed a model (applying linear equilibrium relation-
ship) which treats liquid being transferred from one plate to the two successive
plates below. A fraction of the liquid is retained and mixed on each of these two
plates. This model would serve as a closer approach between theory and experi-
ment. The model allows for a prediction of the holdup distribution on the plates.
The liquid bypassing of a plate considerably reduces the separation ability [22].
In 1977, Rivas provided a design method involving analytic equations which are
solutions to a set of diﬀerential equations from mass balances over each tray in a
periodic distillation column. The solutions are used to determine the ideal number
of trays with the assumption of linear equilibrium relationship. These equations
for are analogous to the Kremser-Souder-Brown equations used in conventional
distillation [23].
In 1978 Duﬀy and Furzer presented an analytical model which is an extension
to one introduced earlier by Furzer in 1973. The analytical solution involves use
8
==PAGE:15==
of matrices and involves no iterations. The equilibrium relationship must be lin-
ear, point eﬃciencies may be diﬀerent from one, holdups are of equal magnitude,
and non-ideal liquid draining is included. The model provides tray eﬃciency as a
function of the number of plates, the fraction of liquid dropped, and other param-
eters [24]. In the same year, Duﬀy and Furzer modelled periodic distillation on a
single sieve plate by assuming plug ﬂow and zero weeping rate during the LFP.
Zero weeping rates could only be obtained by introducing baﬄes to regulate ﬂuid
oscillations on the plate. Experimental measurements of the same parameters as
modelled in their previous paper ([24]) were executed and provided conﬁrmation
of the improvements in separating ability, predicted by their own theory [25].

In 1980, Hentrich and Vogelpohl published results from a special tray design (see
Figure 2.2). A cylindrical storage tube is placed in the center of the column be-
tween bubble-cap trays. During the VFP, the vapor ﬂow causes the top of the
storage tubes to be closed (the bottom of them are open) and the system oper-
ates like ordinary cyclic distillation. During the LFP, the vapor ﬂow is interrupted
causing the top of the storage tubes to open and the bottom of the storage tubes to
close which leads the liquid into the tubes. Once all the liquid is in the tubes, the
VFP can commence where all liquid is dumped to the tray below. In this way liq-
uid mixing is eﬃciently avoided. Challenges are for the liquid to drain fast into the
storage tubes and also, later on, the method was considered too expensive [26, 7, 1].
In 1980 and 1981, Baron, Wajc, and Lavie brought emphasis back to the im-
portance of liquid plug ﬂow down the column to avoid back-mixing. The issue
with non-uniform liquid ﬂow had prevented large scale implementation of periodic
distillation. They suggested a possible alternative called ”step-wise periodic dis-
tillation” by directly manipulating the liquid ﬂow. During the VFP, vapor ﬂows
through the column exchanging mass with the stage wise liquid holdups. Mean-
while the condensate is sequentially collected in reservoirs 1 to N. When the N’th
9

reservoir is full all plates are simultaneously emptied through outlet valves, which
are mounted on every stage, into the boiler. This causes the holdups to completely
mix in the reboiler, which is not preferable. The LFP is in the paper referred to
as the replacement step. Baron’s setup can be seen in Figure 2.3. The setup was
compared to ordinary periodic distillation (Cannon). For a large number of stages,
both processes are equivalent. Baron et al. claimed their setup to be slightly supe-
rior for a ﬁnite number of steps [27]. The group published a second paper, where
they setup a model for their realization. The model claimed tray eﬃciencies of
200%, which is comparable to those of controlled cycling. The superiority of con-
trolled cycling remained anyhow to be demonstrated in practice [28].

In 1983, Furzer patented a new tray design called ”Time Delay Plates”. In the
LFP, liquid would be collected by gutter channels into a vessel. Before the vessel
is ﬁlled, the tray below is completely drained to avoid liquid mixing. This internal

reduce liquid mixing. The experiments showed that the new internals could not
completely prevent liquid from falling more than one plate down the column. It
was concluded that the theoretical advantages of cyclic distillation are yet to be
fully achieved in large scale columns [30].

In the years from 1989 to 2011 there was remarkably quiet in the academic ﬁeld
of cyclic distillation. There were no new ideas on how to realize the method in
practice with results closer to those promised by theory, and the case was put on
the pitch.
In 2011 a paper on periodic distillation was published on process intensiﬁcation.
Authors were V. Maleta, Kiss, Taran and B. Maleta. The paper states that the
throughput of a cyclic distillation column is two or more times higher than what
is reachable for conventional distillation at equivalent separation performance. In
the study, a theoretical stage model with perfect displacement of the ﬂows is
presented. The theoretical developments were implemented in industrial scale and
experiments gave a stage eﬃciency of 2-3 times that of conventional distillation.
With references to the Lewis case II analogy they state why periodic operation
has the improved separating ability. The authors obtained a series of patents for
realizations that together avoid axial liquid mixing during the LFP in industrial
scale. The realization involved sluice chambers, which were mounted beneath each
tray. Interrupting the vapor ﬂow allows liquid holdups to enter the sluice chambers
subsequently until all liquid holdups now exist in the sluice chambers, which are
closed at the bottom. Resuming the vapor supply subsequently opens the sluice
chambers and closes the trays obliging the liquid to ﬂow down to the empty tray
below. The principle resembles the realization of Hentrich and Vogelpohl from
1980. An illustration of Maleta’s trays is shown in Figure 2.6. This tray design
brings no complications in terms of liquid ﬂow with increased number of trays and
pressure drop. The paper introduces the ”perfect displacement mode operating
line” which is included in the authors’ version of the periodic counterpart to the
McCabe-Thiele method for evaluating the theoretical number of stages [6]. The
authors are founders of the company MaletaCD: Maleta cyclic distillation, which is
located in Estonia. According to their website, MaletaCD© has existed since 2005.
The copyright to industrial realization of the process is protected by patents in
the USA, Europe, and Eurasia [34]. Two industrial cyclic distillation columns are
now operated as part of the Lipinsky Alcohol Plant in Ukraine, one for increasing
the alcohol concentration and the other for concentrating impurities [6]. Further
expansions in the chemical process industry remains.
In 2015, B. Maleta, Shevchenko, Bedryk, and Kiss further developed the ”perfect
displacement operating line” to what they now call the operating lines of cyclic
distillation. Experiments on a pilot scale cyclic distillation column were compared
to those on an existing industrial beer column (used to concentrate ethanol) to
carry out an ethanol-water separation. With Maleta’s specially designed trays,
practical operation conﬁrmed 2.6 times fewer trays and energy savings of about
30% are possible in comparison to conventional distillation. A higher throughput is
possible if, consequently, the residence time of liquid during the VFP is decreased.
By replacing the internals and changing the operating mode, it is concluded that

2.1.1
Literature Review Discussions and Conclusions
A common statement in the earliest papers were ﬁrst and foremost the increased
throughput for cyclic operation. This was based on experimental and theoretical
comparisons between dual-ﬂow and cyclic operation using the same trays. This is
an irrelevant conclusion as the two operating forms require diﬀerent tray designs.
It was learned that ﬂuid oscillations and dumping should be prevented by intro-
ducing baﬄes or packed material on the plates [25, 1]. From operating the ﬁrst
plant scale column (Schrodt et al., 1967), major issues with non-uniform liquid
ﬂow were encountered.
Lower trays would drain completely before any liquid
would ﬂow from the upper tray. This was reasoned by tray design and pressure
diﬀerence.
After this diﬀerent eﬀorts were made for theory to approach reality and vice versa.
Simulations supporting the liquid ﬂow issues were created. Models, where a frac-
tion of the liquid is assumed to be left on the tray when liquid from above reaches
the tray, were presented. Mixing models rather than plug ﬂow models for the
holdups were also proposed. In terms of practical realizations, major attempts
were the Baron-Wajc ”stepwise periodic operation”, Furzer’s tray design to delay
the liquid down ﬂow, the Heintrich-Vogelpohl set-up, and Matsubara’s set-up by
using storage tanks between trays for the liquid. Pressure equalizing manifolds
were also mounted in hopes of obtaining the ideal case of plug ﬂow and complete
dumping of the trays. The focus was clearly on eliminating liquid mixing between
trays. After several attempts to realize the theory, satisfactory performance was
not obtained, and the case was at rest in the 90’s and 00’s.
In 2011 the Maleta trays were introduced. These use the same principle as the
Heintrich-Vogelpohl scheme where the vapor ﬂow controls opening and closing
of chambers for the liquid. The advantage with Maleta’s trays is a faster liquid
drainage into the chambers. Then Tofteg˚ard and Jørgensen presented the sequen-
tial drainage alternative. This scheme has a central rod in the column, which is
controlled independently of the vapor ﬂow. This means that the vapor ﬂow does
not need to be continuously interrupted, which saves potentially large pressure
build-ups in the reboiler. It is however unknown if the sequential drainage process
is suﬃciently eﬃcient in comparison to Maleta’s trays?
Concentration proﬁles against time have been used to quantify the performance of
a distillation column in cyclic operation. The analogy between cyclic distillation
and Lewis case II (which is also eﬃciency based) were ﬁrst carried out by Som-
merfeld et al. (1966), and Robinson and Engel (1967). The performance of the
two operating modes has also been quantiﬁed by the terms column and especially
tray eﬃciencies. Their eﬀects on various parameters have been diligently used
throughout. Analogies to McCabe-Thiele Method for graphic design method of a
column have been presented: one representation form by P˘atrut, et al. (2014), and

another by Nielsen et al. (2017). The increase in tray eﬃciencies can be directly
visualized by applying the graphic representation method by Nielsen et al. (2017).

3.
Problem Statement
Vision
Distillation is the most energy consuming process of all chemical separation pro-
cesses. Distillation is also by far the most widely applied separation process for
mixtures that can be vaporized [39]. Therefore, implementation of intensiﬁed dis-
tillation by a cyclic operating column is prone to major savings worldwide. Energy
savings in the ﬁeld will play a substantial part in protecting the environment. Fur-
thermore, if only half of the trays are needed to perform the same separation, both
capital and operating costs can be reduced, or products of higher purity can be
produced.
Issue statement
Issues regarding cyclic distillation have been addressed where liquid back mixing
between trays has been a main operational issue. Back mixing greatly reduces the
separation ability and also makes the operation unpredictable. Besides, cyclic dis-
tillation includes a periodic vapor interruption in the reboiler. To realize this, large
pressure vessels are required, not only to handle the large pressure buildup, but
also to ensure the same and uniform vapor ﬂow period after period. An alternative
periodic operating mode was introduced by Tofteg˚ard and Jørgensen (1989). The
solution is promising as it avoids back mixing and pressure buildups. It keeps
a continuous un-interrupted vapor ﬂow from the reboiler while trays are drained
sequentially. A three staged sequential drainage pilot plant stripping column has
been installed in the Pilot Plant Research Center of DTU Chemical Engineering.
In order to advance on the realization of sequential drainage operation, theoret-
ical foundation should be established.
The scope of this project is to validate
the eﬃciency increase promised by periodic operations. Then to set up a novel
model for the sequential drainage operating mode. Results from the model and
the applicability of the model is evaluated.
Solution method
Firstly, the argued superiority of cyclic operating tray eﬃciency will be validated
by re-deriving tray eﬃciencies for two diﬀerent tray conﬁgurations from the origi-
nal paper by Lewis (1936). Proceeding from this, the analogy between Lewis’ case
II with double tray eﬃciency and cyclic operation will be presented.

A novel model for the sequential drainage operating mode is set up, simulated
and investigated using MatLab version 2017b or newer. Due to its novelty, the
model is ﬁrst set up for the existing simple pilot scale stripping column employing
a ternary ammonia-water-air system where ammonia is stripped from water by air.
The model will be expanded for distillation, since implementation of the operating
mode for distillation purposes represents the high-potential goal.
The applicability of the sequential drainage operation model to graphic representa-
tions that are analogous to a McCabe-Thiele diagram will be examined. Moreover,
comparisons of theoretical results to the classic cyclic operating mode and to the
conventional continuous operating mode will be performed and evaluated.

Lewis Cases
In this section, the Lewis cases I, II, and III will be introduced. The three cases
are diﬀerent tray conﬁgurations for conventional distillation. Lewis (1936) proved
that the overall tray eﬃciency can be higher than the point eﬃciency on a tray
for the diﬀerent tray conﬁgurations. Lewis case II proved a double tray eﬃciency,
and authors through history have drawn analogies between the Lewis case II and
cyclic distillation to prove its superiority. Lewis’ publication will be looked into by
re-deriving cases I and II. The analogy between Lewis case II and cyclic distillation
will then be presented.

4.1.1
Analogy between Lewis Case II and Cyclic Operation
The Lewis case II conﬁguration has proven to be most eﬃcient in theory. To real-
ize the conﬁguration in practice, tubes or the like would be needed as downcomers
to transfer continuous ﬂowing liquid from a tray down to the same horizontal ini-
tial point on the tray below. Thus, for every tray spacing, a tube would surround
half the column circumference, giving downcomers a relatively long travel distance.
The extra installation would increase capital costs. Moreover, the period for down-
coming (LFP) would be too long in comparison to the period where the liquid ﬂows
across a tray inside the column experiencing mass transfer by vapor ﬂow (VFP).
This will lead to a ﬁxed LFP to VFP ratio. By contrast, for cyclic operation, the
LFP-VFP ratio can be altered by changing vapor and liquid ﬂow period durations
independently of one another.

5.
Model Setup
The purpose of this chapter is to examine how the sequential drainage operating
form presented by Tofteg˚ard and Jørgensen (2016) [1] performs in comparison to
the ordinary cyclic operating form and to continuous conventional operation. Both
the ordinary cyclic operating form and the sequential drainage operating form are
periodic operations. Therefore, to avoid confusion, the ordinary cyclic operating
form will also be referred to as the simultaneous drainage operating form. Thus, the
existing large scale periodic distillation columns employing Maleta’s [6] trays also
operate by simultaneous drainage. When referring to the simultaneous drainage
operation, the liquid ﬂow period may be referred to as the drain period instead,
and the vapor ﬂow is still interrupted during the drain period. By contrast, for
sequential drainage operation, the vapor ﬂow is never interrupted.
In this chapter, models will be set up for continuous conventional operation, si-
multaneous drainage operation, and sequential drainage operation.
The three
diﬀerent models are set up for the same case study, which is based on experiments
by Tofteg˚ard and Jørgensen (2016). The system is a simple three-staged strip-
ping column, which strips ammonia from an ammonia-water mixture by using air.
The ammonia-water liquid feed is introduced at the top of the column and ﬂows
down through the column during which the upward counter current ﬂowing gas
(air) strips the more volatile ammonia. The liquid product is withdrawn from the
bottom of the column. The air is introduced at the bottom of the column and
exits at the top with a certain gain in ammonia-content. To set up the models,
each stage is assumed to reach equilibrium and inlet speciﬁcations are required.
The solution method for the simulations is inspired by simulations of cyclic tray
wise liquid mole fractions against time by P˘atrut, et al. (2014) [36]. The aim is
to provide tray wise liquid mole fractions against time for the described stripping
column operating in conventional, simultaneous, and sequential drainage operat-
ing modes that are comparable.

In this section, a degrees of freedom analysis is conducted. This is done to get an

5.1.1
Assumptions
Assumptions for the stripping operation are [41]:
1. Steady state operation (steady state for conventional operation and pseudo
steady state for periodic operations)
2. Equilibrium on each stage
3. Constant pressure
4. Constant temperature
5. No heat losses
6. No heat of mixing
7. Equal, and constant, molar heats of vaporization
The assumption of constant pressure (assumption 3) is common and widely ac-
cepted also for distillation purposes. The presented column is operated under at-
mospheric pressure. The stripping column is also assumed to run under constant
temperature (assumption 4). This leads to a constant equilibrium constant (KNH3)
for water/ammonia, which usually is a function of temperature. The latter means
that equations for mechanical equilibrium, P G

referred to as constant molar overﬂow. Constant molar overﬂow results in straight

7.
Application to Distillation
This chapter aims to expand the novel model for sequential drainage operation
to distillation. With the main goal to implement this expanded model, a binary
mixture which behaves ideally in both liquid and vapor phase is selected for distil-
lation. A benzene-toluene mixture is chosen since this mixture satisﬁes the needs
for simplicity.
First, a periodic distillation column operating by simultaneous
drainage for separating the benzene-toluene mixture is modelled. Results from
the simultaneous drainage distillation are compared to results from simulations of
the continuous conventional operating mode and the sequential drainage operating
mode, that employ corresponding column designs. The results will be comparable
by applying the same average mass throughput against time.
7.1
Procedure
In this section, two methods that are used to solve a model for periodic distillation
by simultaneous drainage will be introduced. The ﬁrst is a design procedure and
the second is an iterative procedure for simulating a column in periodic operation
by simultaneous drainage. This section aims to clarify the diﬀerence between the
two procedures, and why they are used.
7.1.1
Design Procedure - Tofteg˚ard and Jørgensen (1987)
The goal is to calculate comparable product compositions achievable for a col-
umn operating by the diﬀerent modes.
Before doing so, the column needs to
be designed. The design procedure will be conducted for cyclic distillation with
simultaneous drainage by using a non-iterative design method, which was intro-
duced by Tofteg˚ard and Jørgensen (1987) [32]. The design algorithm has been
implemented in MatLab by Nielsen et al. [37], which is directly used. From a
predeﬁned mixture, ﬂows, and separation speciﬁcations, the existing program will
provide the number of stages, feed stage location, and internal compositions for
a cyclic column with simultaneous drainage. Further explanation on the design
algorithm can be seen in Section 7.2.3.
75
==PAGE:82==
7.1.2
Iterative Procedure - P˘atrut, et al. (2014)
P˘atrut, et al. (2014) [36] has published an iterative procedure for a classic cyclic
column. From a predeﬁned mixture, ﬂows, column speciﬁcations (number of stages
and feed stage location), and an initial guess for internal compositions, the prod-
uct purities and internal compositions are returned by iterating through the model
equations until pseudo steady state in the column is achieved.
7.1.3
Task Deﬁnition
The design algorithm by Tofteg˚ard and Jørgensen (1987) [32] will on-wards be
referred to as the design method. The method by P˘atrut, et al. (2014) [36] will
on-wards be referred to as the iterative method. Both methods employ the same
balance equations. The compact diﬀerence between the two methods is stated in

Starting from a speciﬁed bottom product composition, which is the liquid compo-
sition in the reboiler at the end of the VFP, the reboiler VFP balance is integrated
backwards in time from t = tvap to the initial point, t = 0. In this way, the liquid
composition at the beginning of the VFP in the reboiler (stage 1) is calculated.
Knowing this composition, the holdup composition on stage 2 at the end of the
VFP can be calculated. The stage 2 liquid composition is then integrated back-
wards in time from t = tvap to t = 0. The initial point for stage 2 equals the end
composition on stage 3. This procedure is done for the following stages until the
feed concentration is reached at which point the feed is introduced. Then, the
calculations are continued until the speciﬁed top product/distillate composition is
reached [32].
The resulting column has NT = 10 stages. The stages will be counted from the
bottom, the reboiler being stage 1 and the condenser being stage 10. The stages
1 (reboiler) to 9 are equilibrium stages. The feed ﬂow is introduced on stage 6.
The feed entry is deﬁned according to R.F. Nielsen et al. [37]. R.F. Nielsen et
al. enters the feed ﬂow during the VFP. This is in contrast to P˘atrut, et al. [36]
who introduced the feed during the LFP. For cyclic operation with simultaneous
drainage, the advantage of introducing the feed during the VFP is that the feed
can be a mixture of liquid and vapor, which is not possible if introduced during

7.4
Sequential Drainage Distillation
In this section, a model for distillation operating by sequential drainage will be
set up. The model uses the same column design as was used for treating simul-
taneous drainage. The sequential drainage model is solved to obtain internal and
product compositions by using an iterative method, which corresponds to the iter-
ative method used in the previous section for simultaneous drainage. The resulting
compositions can then be compared to the simultaneous operation in Section 7.6.
First, the operating mode is described.
For sequential drainage distillation, it takes NT stage drainings to return to the
same initial state as opposed to simultaneous draining, where only one drain pe-
riod is required. The stages open and close independently of the vapor ﬂow, which
is continuous here. This is opposed to simultaneous draining where the vapor ﬂow
is interrupted while all stages are being drained simultaneously.
An illustration of the liquid composition proﬁles on each stage during the course
of one period is seen in Figure 7.4. The period is divided into period sections
that have NT drain periods of length tdrain and one period section where all stages
undergo VFP. The period section (de) where all stages undergo VFP has length
tcvap. It is assumed that the composition for holdups which are drained from a
stage or ﬁlled into a stage do not change during the drain/ﬁll period duration.
This does however not apply for the reboiler and condenser. The reboiler and
condenser always undergo VFP where the composition changes continuously, and
the holdups are assumed added and removed instantaneously.
As an example, the reboiler drain period takes place in period section ef from
t = 3 · tdrain + tcvap to t = 4 · tdrain + tcvap (see Figure 7.4). The bottoms prod-

Discussion
The periodic operations, especially by simultaneous drainage, have proven im-
mense theoretical superiority to the conventional operations. In terms of separat-
ing ability, operations by simultaneous drainage can exceed 100% improvements.
The Maleta trays already exist to enable practical realization with the ability
to eliminate liquid back mixing. Despite decades of research, the simultaneous
drainage operating mode has not entered the industry remarkably and only a cou-
ple of commercial scale plants are operating periodically in the world. This may
be due to questions regarding practical convenience, capital costs, and operating
costs.
The periodic trays for both simultaneous drainage and sequential drainage oper-
ation are more advanced compared to the simple conventional plates. By this is
meant that they include more mechanical parts to operate and to be controlled.
The sequential drainage operating form may provide a higher risk of control issues
with a row of trays that need to be drained punctually, continuously, and indepen-
dently of the vapor ﬂow throughout the period. However, with the availability of
advanced technology today, we assume that controlling of the operations will not
be an issue. Furthermore, the periodic trays are costly because the tray design
is still new and more advanced. Yet the shorter column has been evaluated to
compensate for the extra cost in trays.
In terms of the simultaneous drainage operation, pressure regulating valves are
needed to control periodic pressure buildups in the reboiler due to vapor ﬂow in-
terruption. This will pose operational costs of a certain magnitude. By contrast,
the sequential drainage operating mode does not require these pressure regulat-
ing valves since the vapor ﬂow is not interrupted. Moreover, sequential drainage
operation is still far superior in terms of separating eﬃciency to the conventional
operation, however, less eﬃcient than the simultaneous drainage operation by
about 10% in separating eﬃciency as found in both case studies. A question to be
asked is how do these 10% less eﬃciency in sequential operation measure against
the pressure buildup in simultaneous operation?

Conclusion
An innovative model employing the state-of-the-art periodic sequential drainage
operating mode has been successfully implemented. Furthermore comparisons be-
tween conventional, subsequent drainage, and sequential drainage operating modes
for two case studies on stripping and distillation respectively have been conducted.
The ﬁrst case study was on a three staged stripping column where ammonia rich
water was entered at the top has been conducted. Air was introduced from the
bottom to ﬂow upwards counter-currently with the liquid, stripping ammonia from
the liquid. The three diﬀerent operating modes were implemented to employ the
same three staged column design with the same throughputs per time unit. The
theoretical product purity was improved by 61.76 % when replacing the conven-
tional operating column by a simultaneous drainage operating column. Replacing
the conventional column with a sequential drainage column improved the product
purity with 55.88 %.
It was discovered that the novel model for periodic sequential drainage could in
fact be expanded for distillation. A 9 staged column obtained from the periodic
simultaneous drainage design method by Tofteg˚ard and Jørgensen (1987) [32] was
employed to separate a benzene-toluene mixture. By using the iterative procedure
with periodicity conditions by P˘atrut, et al. (2014) [36], it was possible to produce
results for the column operating by three diﬀerent operating modes with identi-
cal throughputs. The conventional column required almost double the number
of stages to achieve the same separation as the periodic columns. This conﬁrms
ﬁndings from previous works on simultaneous drainage operation.
Analogies to the McCabe Thiele graphic representation method have been con-
ducted on the simultaneous and sequential drainage operating modes. The average
compositions during the period have been used to generate straight operating lines
and to calculate tray eﬃciencies. On average the simultaneous drainage operation
had a tray eﬃciency enhancement of 73.00 % whereas the sequential drainage
operation was superior by 51.22 % to the corresponding conventional operation.
In comparing the conventional stripping operation to the other operating modes,
simultaneous drainage was superior by 123.00 % whereas the sequential drainage
operation was superior by 100.33 % in terms of average tray eﬃciencies.
Thus, the separating ability can be signiﬁcantly improved if a continuous con-
118
==PAGE:125==
ventional operating column is replaced with a periodically operating column. In
comparing the two periodic operations, sequential drainage operation proved to
be less eﬃcient than the simultaneous drainage operation by about 10% in tray
eﬃciency. Based on the studied distillation case, it is unknown if these 10% less
eﬃciency outweigh a practical pressure buildup in the corresponding simultaneous
drainage operation.
The sequential drainage operating mode is presently a novel and a very interesting
direction.
To proceed on evaluating the operating form in respect to diﬀerent
inﬂuences, further studies are needed. Suggestions for future work in the ﬁeld of
sequential drainage are made in the following and ﬁnal chapter.

Future Work
In this chapter, suggestions for future work on the sequential drainage operating
mode are presented. These are divided in to two parts; Practice and Theory.
10.1
Practice
The three staged stripping column, on which the ﬁrst case study was made, has
been installed in the Pilot Plant Research Center of DTU Chemical Engineering.
The equipment has been retroﬁtted with a new control system and measuring
instruments. Initial tests should ﬁrst be conducted to ensure that the recently
implemented control system works appropriately and that all sensors measure
correctly as a part of commissioning the equipment. The realization could then
be further investigated experimentally by stripping ammonia from water, where
measurements are compared to model outputs. Tofteg˚ard et al. (2016) [1] have
previously conducted eﬃciency measurements on the same pilot plant, but there
is a demand for less noisy measurements. Images of one of the trays in open and
closed state are seen in Figure 10.1.
The trays are controlled by a pneumatic
cylinder in the center of the column. Clearly, other actuation principles or tray
designs could also be feasible [1]. Evolving on a smarter tray design could e.g.
serve the purpose of reducing the space between trays. Weeping and entrainment
limits would also be looked into.

10.2
Theory
It is advisory to examine the magnitude and extent of expenditures connected
to pressure regulating actions needed for a simultaneous drainage operation and
compare this to the separation eﬃciency decrease, a corresponding sequential op-
eration would provide. Furthermore, a comparison of cost evaluations from the
three diﬀerent operating modes could be done.
A new model describing the sequential drainage operation has been implemented
in this project. An investigation of the relationship between DP-VFP-total period
durations and eﬃciency can be investigated. It would also be obvious to imple-
ment the model for a larger number of trays. The sequential drainage will reach
a point where two trays are drained during the same DP, meaning there is no
period section where all trays are in VFP. The draining trays will be separated by
a number of trays determined by the tdrain
tperiod ratio [1].
A design method for sequential drainage operation can also be carried out. It is
straight forward to set up a design method, which is analogous to the existing
design method for a periodic column by simultaneous drainage, and examine if it
works.
Other actions could be to investigate the applicability of the presented model to
non-ideal mixtures and multi-component mixtures. An intensiﬁcation investigation
by e.g. reactive distillation employing sequential drainage operating columns is
also a possibility, but these investigations are considered too soon at this point in
time.
