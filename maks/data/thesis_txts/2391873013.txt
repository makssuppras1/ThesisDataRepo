Abstract
In a future energy system, based primarily on renewable energy sources, Solid Oxide Cells are well
positioned to play a central role in storage and conversion of renewable energy. SOC are electrochemical
cells that can operate both as fuel cell and as electrolyser. This allows the same stack to be used for
storing surplus energy, in electrolysis mode, when electricity supply exceeds demand and for generating
electric power, in fuel cell mode, when electricity demand exceeds supply.
In the ﬁrst part of the work modelling of system components such as Wind Turbine, PV module, Flat
Plate Solar Collector and Solid Oxide Fuel and Electrolyser cell is assessed. Each of those mathematical
models is then validated against the available literature or technical sheets.
The second part aims at the design of two dual-mode Solid Oxide Cell systems, one for each European
scenario, which can operate eﬃciently in both ways and with the ambition to use as few components
(heat exchangers, pumps, oﬀgas burners, blowers) as possible in order to reduce system cost and to
increase its compactness.
In the last part are shown and discussed the main results of the simulations. They seems encouraging as
per the potential for a reversible SOC to become a useful device for future power production and storage.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
A threat to mankind
Based on clear evidences our planet is constantly warming up mostly due to an increasing concentration
of carbon dioxide in earths atmosphere. The eﬀect of this threat, however, will be more clearly seen in
the long period, i.e. in the upcoming centuries. Even more alarming is the quick depletion of Fossil Fuels,
oil natural gas (NG) above all, since it mines the foundation of our society primarily based on those fuels.
In particular, based on Reserves-to-Production ratio of 2015 British Petroleum estimated in 2016 that
Coal, Oil and NG resources will be depleted in 114, 50.7 and 52.8 years, respectively [British Petroleum
(2016)].
1.2
The role of Renewables
It is a commonly agreed idea is that in order to solve this problem the exploitation of RES should be
increased. The goal is to made them the only source of power by the time Fossil Fuels will be deﬁnitely
depleted. Consequently, in recently past years the share of renewables in power generation has been
increased considerably. Also their capacity, the rate at which renewable-derived energy is generated, has
shown a notable rise in global energy market. According to REN21 Global Status Report on renewables
(2016) the year 2015 has seen the largest addition of power capacity in global renewable energy ﬁeld.
Regarding the power sector, Wind and PV share the most part of this expansion, accounting for about
77% of the new installations. Surprisingly enough, at the end of 2015 was estimated that the global
electricity production from renewable energy sources (hereafter RES) has been 23.7% of the total energy
production for that same year1. Furthermore, it is estimated that in future years this remarkable growth
will continue in a steady manner. In 2016 IEA stated that renewables are expected to cover more than
60% of global power capacity growth over the next 5 years and the share of renewables in electricity
production will rise to 28% by 2021. In 2015 they surpassed coal as to the largest source of electricity
capacity in the world. The markets that are expected to carry for the most part the momentum of this
expansion are the Chinese one (37%). The energy markets of USA (13%), EU (12%) and India (9%) are
far behind2. As all this data demonstrates the RES driven power plant are reaching a critical point above
which is needed a careful planning about the question of utility-scale storage.
1.3
Power Storage
A characteristic feature of power generated from RES is the impossibility to produce electricity according
to its demand. Instead the daily power production varies accordingly to the availability of the source

itself. For example a wind farm or a PV power plant produce electricity only when wind blows or sun
shines, respectively. This problem, combined with the fact that generated electricity can be either be
consumed or get lost as heat is regarded as the Achilles Heel of RES. Within the above described scenario,
a possible solution to solve this problem is to provide a reliable mean to store the electricity in a more
manageable energy form. Several attempts have been made in the past, and while some of them had
proven to be successful some others had not seen diﬀusion in the market due to problems of various
nature. Currently the most adopted electricity storage concepts belong to the following families3:
• Batteries, which store electrical energy into chemical energy of some compounds already present
inside it. This group includes the reversible closed electrochemical cells, such as the lithium-ion
batteries, as well as the redox-ﬂow batteries.
• Flywheels, able to convert electrical energy into rotational kinetic energy of one or more rotating
wheels, usually made out of a heavy material. The good feature of a Flywheel Energy Storage
(FES) system is the smoothness of operation but the downside is the restriction in either its rated
power or its storage duration.
• Pumped Hydroelectric Energy Storage (PHES) allows to store electric energy into potential energy
of water pumped from a lower to a higher reservoir, usually separated by a dam.
• Compressed Air Energy Storage (CAES) is designed to use electricity to run an air compressor
thus storing it as pressure energy. The compressed air is usually put inside an subterranean cavern
and will be used, when needed, to run a gas turbine. The unique feature of this system is its
demand for additional fuel which is burnt in a combustion chamber placed just before the turbine.
Consequently, the stored electricity is always less than the actual power output.
• Thermal Storage is a concept that allows to store electricity by using it to produce a hot or a
cold source which is in turn much easier to be stored. It includes Pumped Heat Electrical Storage,
Liquid Air Energy storage, ice or cooled water production, molten salt storage.
All these facilities share the possibility to reverse the process, since they are designed to store and give
back the electricity, providing a certain round-trip eﬃciency always lower than one. In table 1.1 are
summarised clearly the available storage facilities and their characteristic data. Furthermore, are also
available less diﬀused storage concept that can either reduce some of the negative features of the existing
facilities or improve the overall eﬃciency. One example is the Superconducting Magnetic Energy Storage
(SMES), a system which stores electric energy in the Magnetic ﬁeld with a roundtrip eﬃciency as high
as 95% (Cheung et al. (2003)). The main problem for this storage are the high cost due to its need for a
cryogenic operating temperature and for an expensive superconducting material.

1.4
Power-To-Gas (P2G) concept
The storage paradigm of power to gas comes into play only in recent years. This idea is based on a device
able to use power to produce a gaseous compound, thus storing the surplus electrical energy into chemical
3https://energystorage.org
3
==PAGE:7==
energy. The produced gas is usually a fuel and that has a high energetic as well as economic value and
can either be sold or used inside the production plant for various purposes4. The good feature of P2G is,
in fact, the ﬂexibility of employment of its ﬁnal product combined with the relatively low energy losses
experienced during its transport. It has been estimated, in fact, that the transportation losses inside a
gas pipeline network is one order of magnitude lower than the one inside a power grid network.
1.4.1
Solid Oxide Electrolyser Cell
One device that can perform this process is the electrolyser. Among the various type of electrochemical
cells, Solid Oxide Electrolyser Cell (hereafter SOEC) is the most promising technology since it has the
highest performance in terms of eﬃciency with respect to all the other devices.
A SOEC is able to
perform the simple electrolysis of water, i.e. splitting H2O molecule into Hydrogen and Oxygen, when
power is provided to the cell. It may also use water and carbon dioxide to produce syngas, a gas composed
of Hydrogen and carbon monoxide, in the so called co-electrolysis process. Both Hydrogen and Syngas
can be either stored or injected to a pipeline network. The advantages of this technology are multiple.
First and foremost its high eﬃciency, due to the intrinsic nature of the catalysed electrochemical process.
SOECs share one characteristic feature common to all electrochemical cells, the absence of mechanical
wear since their design does not include any moving part.
The main problems related with SOECs are their relatively high operating temperature range (700-850
oC) which leads to issues related to chemical stability and thermo-mechanical compatibility of adopted
materials. All this is accompanied by the need for the minimisation of the number of thermal cycles,
which bring thermal stress to the cell. Consequently, the ﬁxed cost for a SOEC system are currently
within the range of 8000-10000 Euro per kW of installed capacity. However, this cost is expected to
decrease in future years as this technology will beneﬁt from its diﬀusion and commercialisation.
1.4.2
An eﬀective tool for grid management
This work however is focused not only on the P2G storage concept but goes a step beyond that. Would be
a good opportunity to have the possibility to operate with a device that can successfully both carry out
P2G process and operate in the reversed way, i.e. produce electrical power. In this way, the mentioned
system will be transformed into a powerful and eﬀective tool for power grid management. In fact, this
system can potentially be the pivot of a smart grid network since it can store the renewable energy in
surplus when the production exceeds the demand and satisfy the peak load requirement when needed.
Indeed, a device able to achieve this goal already exist and is called Reversible Solid Oxide Cell (RSOC).
It is based on the intrinsic principle of reversibility of any chemical reaction. However, even if in theory
this is possible does not automatically imply that a practical application of this reversibility is feasible
as well. In fact one SOC device is usually designed built and optimised to work only in either SOFC or
SOEC mode. Thus a dual mode may not be eﬃcient or even it may damage the cells.
One recent study, [Graves et al. (2015)], illustrates that such a system may be not only feasible but also
beneﬁcial as the lifetime of the device is concerned. In particular, it came to the conclusion that by
operating the cell in simple electrolysis mode its microstructure tends to deteriorate in the proximity
of the interface between the oxygen electrode and electrolyte layer. Surprisingly enough if the cell is
operated in reversible mode no microstructural damage is observed. In other words, the fuel cell mode
either prevents or reverse the degradation mechanisms happening in electrolysis mode; the reason for this
observation is still unclear and further research on this topic should be performed.

energy. This compound is produced with two subsequent thermally integrated processes.
The ﬁrst one is the high temperature electrolysis of water to produce Hydrogen and Oxigen for which is
used a SOEC.
The second one is the methanation an exothermal process consisting in the production of methane
starting from carbon dioxide and hydrogen. The key to achieve a high round-trip eﬃciency is to use the
heat released by the methanation for the electrolysis, which is carried out in endothermicity in order
to minimise its input power.
The thermal integration is realised by introducing cooling water at 30
bar in the two methanation reactors, so that pressurised steam is produced. The steam is fed directly
to the SOEC which works at about 800oC and at a current density > 10000A/m2. The adoption of
a co-electrolysis, i.e. feeding simultaneously the electrolyser with H2O and CO2 will be also investigated 7.

Chapter 2
Component Modelling
In this chapter is illustrated the procedure followed in this thesis to ultimately produce the models needed
to design a complex system. A brief introduction on the softwares used in this project is also provided.
A system needs various components to work properly; basic components, such as a heat exchanger or
a compressor, which are already been implemented in the software DNA by other authors will not be
discussed further in this study. Instead it happened that most of the remaining component either are not
present in the database or already existing but with a model which is not appropriate to the purpose of
this study. In this case a model was created based on a theoretical background addressed in this chapter
before each description of the models.
2.1
Softwares
In the present work multiple softwares have been used. In the following subsections are mentioned the
main ones. In addition to those MS Excel for data management and the LaTeX code editor TeXstudio
for text writing were used.
2.1.1
Dynamic Network Analysis
This Thesis is mostly concerning in the use of the software called Dynamic Network Analysis (DNA).
Developed in DTU (1989) by the Thermal Energy Section of the Mechanical Engineering Department,
DNA is an open-source tool that allows the user to both build thermodynamic-based models of system
components and to use them in a network. As per the former mode the adopted programming language is
the well-known FORTRAN 77 to produce ﬁles with the extension .for, while for the latter should be used
a native language to give birth to .dna ﬁles. Any component is characterised by nodes representing mass
and energy ﬂows; by matching those nodes together the user is able to create the network. Furthermore
additional and start conditions have to be added in order to set up the input environment. In fact, the
solver DNA adopts to simulate the network is a Newton-Rhapson based one, thus the start conditions can
be decisive for the simulation to be convergent. One of the major strength of DNA is its intrinsic coherence
with the conservation laws, i.e. that of mass and energy, that are always satisﬁed and included in all
models by default. However, on the other hand, in some particular occasions, e.g. electrochemical devices
or components with no mass ﬂow nodes, this characteristic can lead to diﬃculties for the programmer
who cannot chose but use the conservation equations as they are written by someone else. When this
happens is up to the programmer himself to ﬁnd a solution to address the speciﬁc problem, as this may
change case by case.

2.3
Photovoltaic Cell Theory
A photovoltaic (PV) cell system is able to convert sunlight directly into electricity, and, unlike a wind
turbine, it is a static installation. The theory behind this conversion is related to the photovoltaic eﬀect.
This eﬀect was observed for the ﬁrst time by the French physicist Becquerel in 1839 and it consist of the
creation of an electric potential or current across a junction made up by two diﬀerent materials when it
is illuminated by (sun)light.
The most commonly used material in this ﬁeld is Silicon which is a semiconductor made up by a lattice
of tetravalent atoms. By forcing trivalent atoms, like Boron, or pentavalent atoms, such as Phosphorous,
inside its lattice is possible to alter its charge neutrality making the semiconductor positively (P) or
negatively (N) charged , respectively. This practice is named ’doping’.
In a Silicon PV cell, the so called P-N junction is usually formed between a thick layer of P-doped layer
and a thin N-doped layer. Due to the electronic conﬁguration of the doping impurities the former has
an excess of holes while the latter carries extra electrons within its lattice.
When the charge equilibrium is established between the two layers a potential gradient is created
due to the local diﬀusion and subsequent recombination of charge carriers at the interface, hence its
name ’depletion region’.
When light shines, penetrates the thin N-layer and reaches this region, a
photo-generated current is created.
This phenomenon occurs due to the absorption of the energy of incident photons by the electrons in the
valence band. If this energy is higher than the band gap of that material the electron ’jumps’ into the
conduction band leaving a hole (a positive charge) in the valence band.
An electron-hole pair is formed and, due to the potential gradient generated by the charge diﬀusion
across the p-n junction, it does not recombine immediately so that a current can ﬂow in an external
circuit; ﬁnally the electron will recombine with the hole thus closing the circuit.
The electrical contacts are positioned one at the back of the cell and the other at the front, in form of
”busbars” and ”ﬁngers”, which are the result of a trade-oﬀbetween minimisation of the covered area
and that of the electrical resistance (see ﬁg. 2.4).
The energy of the photon in surplus with respect to the band gap is lost as a degraded form of heat. For

2.3.1
PV Modules
A PV cell is able to provide around 7-9 A at 0.4-0.6 V to an external load. Such voltage values are too
low for most of power consuming devices. To overcome this problem PV cells are not sold singularly
to the user but they are always arranged in modules. A PV module consist of multiple (60-120) solar
cells connected in series, so that the nominal voltage of the module is the sum of the potential diﬀerence
realised in each cell.
Furthermore, PV modules are usually connected in series to obtain the desired voltage output. A PV
array is ﬁnally formed by connecting in parallel those strings of modules to increase the generated current.
In order to be able to understand the main speciﬁcations of a solar cell it is useful to explain the
current-voltage characteristic (or I-V curve) of a PV module.
Figure 2.6: Illustration of a typical current- voltage characteristic curve of a PV cell. [Folsom Labs]
This device works as generator in sunlight operations, I quadrant depicted in ﬁg. 2.6, and as a load in
dark operations, II and IV quadrants. At zero voltage, i.e. when the terminals of the device are short
circuited, the measured current value is called short circuit current, Isc. When the voltage increases
the device behave like an ideal current source. By doing so Power is increased up to a knee point, the
maximum power point (mpp) Pmpp = Impp · Vmpp, in which the current starts to decrease.
In the last part of the generator curve the measured current falls abruptly and the voltage is almost
constant, thus the device is comparable to a constant voltage source. The voltage corresponding to a
Current value of zero is called open circuit voltage, Voc.
Performance of a PV module is strongly inﬂuenced by numerous factors that are listed hereafter: the sun
irradiance G, which is the ﬂux of radiant energy per unit area, strongly inﬂuences the photo-generated
current and has slight eﬀect also on operating voltage and module eﬃciency; the sunlight incidence angle
θ can reduce the photo-generated in a non linear way; the maximum power point tracking, that can be
of various types, e.g. ’Incremental Conductance’ or ’Perturb and Observe’, is the key to operate always
at the maximum power point;
the operating cell temperature whose increase decreases signiﬁcantly the output power and voltage and
increases slightly the current; the presence of shading, always detrimental for any solar powered device;
module lifetime, since, as PV cells become older, their performances reduces noticeably.

Voc are always measured in a laboratory at Standard Test Conditions (STC), meaning the following
assumptions are fulﬁlled:
• Irradiance of GST C = 1000 W/m2 ;
• Cell temperature of Tc,ST C = 25 oC ;
• Air Mass of AM = 1.5 .

2.4
Solar Collectors Theory
A Solar Collector (SC) is a solar powered device built and optimised to capture solar radiation and
transfer it in form of sensible heat to a heat transfer ﬂuid (HTF). Consequently it acts basically as a heat
exchanger between a radiant source and a ﬂuid running inside it.
There are multiple design of Solar collectors, which can be divided in two big families: the concentrating
collectors, characterized by the adoption of mirrors in order to focus sunlight, and the non-concentrating
ones. Among the latter group can be clearly distinguished by substantial design diﬀerences the Flat Plate
Solar Collectors from (FPSC) the Evacuated Tube Collectors (ETC).
Another way to group SCs is by looking at the solar tracking mechanism. Most of the concentrating
collectors have at least a mono-axial tracker while almost all the non-concentrating ones are not equipped
with this expensive mechanism.

2.5
Solid Oxide Cell Theory
A solid oxide cell (SOC) system is an electrochemical device whose cell made up by ceramic materials
that works properly only at high (650-1000 oC) temperatures.
The direct consequence is the long
start-up time and the high thermo-mechanical stress on the cell layered structure, so that thermal cycles
must be avoided as much as possible. Ideally a device of this kind should be shut down only in case of
maintenance.
There are also advantages related to the high operating temperature. Unlike Proton Exchange Membrane
Cells, SOCs do not need expensive high quality catalysts, since the overall electrochemical process is
favoured by the high operating temperature.
Furthermore, solid oxide cell has an exceptional fuel ﬂexibility both in electrolysis (EL) mode, e.g.
co-electrolysis, and in fuel cell (FC) mode, e.g. syngas fuel. However it may be damaged permanently
by sulphur compounds present in the fuel even at part per million levels, and the phenomenon of carbon
deposition must be avoided by adopting some precautions.
As any electrochemical cell a SOC is made by three fundamental layers, the anode the electrolyte and
the cathode, plus one, the interconnect, always needed in case is built a stack of series connected cells
[Larminie et al. (2003)], [Lu (2013)]:
• The Fuel electrode, which is the Anode and the Cathode in FC and EL mode, respectively.
By deﬁnition, in fact, the anode (cathode) is the electrode in which a oxidation (reduction)
semi-reaction is realised. It is typically made, at the state of the art, by a cermet composed of
a Yttria-stabilised Zirconia, YSZ (Zirconia ZrO2 doped with 8-10%mol of Yttria Y2O3) matrix in
which are dispersed Nickel micro or nano-particles.
The YSZ has a high ionic conductivity (0.02 S cm−1 at 800oC) and it is porous to allow the diﬀusive
transport of fuel and products towards and from the electrolyte. The latter serves both as catalyst
for the oxidation and as electronic conductor.
• The Electrolyte is usually made of a dense layer of YSZ in which sometimes is added a small amount
of alumina to improve its mechanical properties. Its main goal is to conduct anions O2−, and, at the
same time, not to allow any fuel molecule to pass trough to avoid the occurrence of a combustion
reaction. In addition to this it is needed an electronic conductivity as low as possible, not to permit
the ﬂow of internal currents, which are detrimental to the cell performance.
• The Air electrode, which is the Cathode and the Anode in FC and EL mode, respectively. Also
this electrode needs to be porous in order to allow the reactant and product diﬀusion to and from
the triple phase boundary.
It is commonly made of Strontium-doped Lanthanum Manganite, LSM (La0.84Sr0.16MnO3), chosen
due to its thermal expansion compatibility with YSZ and for its good electronic conductivity.
However, it has poor ionic conductivity, so that other Mixed Electronic-Ionic Conductor (MIEC)
materials, such as the perovskite structured ones, are eﬀectively used as well.
• The interconnect, which is a metallic (e.g. Inconel, Crofer) or a ceramic (e.g. Lanthanum Chromite)
material characterised by high electronic conductivity. Its goal is to allow the electrons to ﬂow,

System Design
The main idea behind the methodology is to develop diﬀerent systems, each optimised for a particular
scenario. In particular, have been imagined two totally diﬀerent environments, explained in details in the
following sections. For each of those scenarios the components of the system are dimensioned according
to the particular need and resources of the corresponding geographical area. Also it must me stated that
the economical assessment is not taken into account when dealing with the design and the analysis of the
aforementioned systems.
3.1
Northern European Scenario
Figure 3.1: Distribution of the irradiation levels in the European continent [Solargis].
The Countries laying on the north of the European continent, such as UK, Germany, northern France,
Denmark, Sweden and Norway share some typical characteristics as climate and energy policy are
concerned. Due to their geographical position solar energy is not the best option to invest in among
the pool of RES. The average yearly global horizontal irradiation is in fact lower than 1100 kWh/m2 (ﬁg.
3.1) and the direct solar irradiation, useful for PV modules, is quite low as compared to the total one.

Southern European Scenario
Countries like Spain, Italy, southern France and Greece share the high availability of solar energy.

Chapter 5
Conclusions
In the ﬁrst part of this work modelling of a Wind Turbine, a PV module, a Flat Plate Solar Collector and
a Reversible Solid oxide cell were carried out. EES software was used to build and perfect the mentioned
models.
For the former three a validation procedure has proven their eﬀectiveness in predicting the
output values starting from the input parameters. As per the lumped parameter models of SOFC and
SOEC the validation process was more diﬃcult to develop due to the absence of experimental data.
However the output values of those models seem to be coherent with the corresponding ones found in
the scientiﬁc literature.
All the models built in EES were then implemented in DNA in order to make possible the integration
with other components in a power/storage plant.
The second part of this Thesis, in fact, has seen the design process of four systems, two for each imagined
futuristic Scenario: one placed in South Europe and the other in the Northern Part. The design took
care of the main resources and needs of these two geographical areas.
A DNA ﬁle was created for each designed system. The components included in the systems are both the
one modelled in the ﬁrst part of this work and the ones already present in DNA.
In the last part the main result for each system were pointed out. The SOFC modes are able to provide
an amount of power equal to 571 and 88.5 MW while the SOEC modes store the surplus power as energy
content of the oﬀuel, which is 701 and 194 MW for the Northern and Southern European Scenarios,
respectively.
The system eﬃciency of the power production mode ranged from 0.372 to 0.421, while that of the P2G
storage mode fell between 0.741 and 0.782.
The Reversible Solid Oxide cell Technology, although nowadays seems not to be ready for being used in
the type of power-storage plant shown in this work, especially for such a high power scale, has proven to
have the potential to be an eﬀective mean for the management of the future energy grid.
Some ﬁnal considerations are listed hereafter.
• DNA is a useful tool to build thermodynamic models, e.g. the ﬂat plate solar collector, but it
can give some problems when dealing with electrochemical processes or electronic devices. During
the modelling part of this work the implementation on DNA of the models was by far the most
challenging task.
• Modelling of electrochemical devices is a quite complex ﬁeld. This is true especially for SOEC for
which, since it is a relative new technology, are available in literature very few models.
Although the approach of a lumped parameter model, followed in this work, seems to be quite
appealing from the point of view of the user, it needs to acquire a good accuracy through a long
Validation process: the model-derived results should be tested against experimental results and if
they are not coherent is needed to proceed backwards and modify some parameters or even change
the whole model.

5.1
Recommendations for a future work
In this last section some suggestion for possible improvements are speciﬁed.
• DNA software needs to be improved in order to make the work of the programmer much easier
when dealing with electrochemical reactions. As of now although DNA can be considered one of
the best open source tool of its kind, there are some commercial software, such as Aspen Plus, that
are much more user friendly and they can provide the user with all the info he needs in a manual.
• The activation overvoltage of SOC models has shown not a good coherence with respect to the
reference. Although its inﬂuence on the cell Voltage is almost negligible, is needed a revision of the
applied ηact model. Also the assumption of using the same model for the overpotentials both for the
SOFC and the SOEC can be inaccurate, leading to diﬀerent result with respect to he experimental
ones.
• The inﬂuence of the operating cell pressure on the system behaviour should be investigated. In
this case he compression work will increase and this should have an impact on the overall system
eﬃciency.
• Instead of using a FPSC loop to preheat the incoming fuel a concentrating solar collector should
be adopted in order to improve the design of the SOEC system.
