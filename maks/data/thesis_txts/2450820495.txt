Abstract
In barely a decade, smartphones have transformed our way of living, with many of the
modern technological advances combined into a single device. With this brief history,
its usage has not been researched extensively compared to its significance in our daily
lives. In this study, we conducted a large scale data analysis on application usage
provided by Sony’s Sensor & Behavior Data Platform, consisting of over 3 million
users from the middle of 2016 to the end of 2018. This study explores how people
use their phones and how different demographics take advantage of the smartphone.
The findings are tied to prior researches as well as expanded on with new theories.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
With every new major technology the speed of adoption has gotten faster, the most
recent being the smartphone revolution, which has in barely a decade transformed
our way of living. A device that has unified the numerous technological advances in
the past into a single mobile computing device. It only took five years for smartphone
usage to match the desktop/laptop computers in usage and since then it has passed
them in both usage time and internet usage [MW18].
With such a short history,
smartphone usage has not been researched extensively compared to its importance in
our daily lives. Most studies done on smartphones have been questionnaires to fairly
small groups of people that are often biased by nationality and place [AP17; Van+15;
Lee+14]. Then we have companies that now and then release statistics but most
often do not study their data to the fullest [MW18; Mee14]. Other related researches
can be found for the many technologies found in the smartphone, such as the internet
and social media which for the most part correspond to their usage in a smartphone
[Rug00; Leu13; WW13; Sha96; Mül+16; WN07]. What is lacking are the large scale
data studies based on tracked data instead of questionnaires that can be unreliable.
That is where this study comes in as we were lucky enough to have access to a large
database of tracked data. It tries to support previous researches as well as bringing
statistics never seen before and make a conclusion on the results based on what is
known.
1.1
Scope of the thesis and goals
This study analyzed data collected by Sony’s Sensor & Behavior Data Platform that
has tracked diverse activities on smartphones and smart bands for millions of users for
the past four years. Our research focuses on the tracked application data of the users
in the last two and a half years, from the middle of 2016 until the end of 2018, where
the data is most stable. With smartphone usage increasing immensely in the last
years, we want to study how different demographic groups have been affected [MW18].
Previous researches have shown demographics having an impact when it comes to
technology usage. For instance, receptiveness towards new technology is greater for
younger people and that often leads to more usage [CB92; Pea+10]. The behaviour of
the genders in their usage has demonstrated evidence that females are more socially
oriented while males are more drawn to entertainment [Van+15; Lee+14]. We will
study the characteristics of the demographics in app usage to find out how the groups

CHAPTER 2
Literature review
In this chapter we will cover the prior research theories and findings on the subject
of this study. With smartphones having been around for a rather short time, few
recognized or well-established researches have been done in this field. Most previous
researches focus on single parts of technologies or behaviours that can be related to
smartphone usage. We will therefore both explore studies that can be related to the
smartphone and more recent studies that have been done on smartphones. First, we
will look into smartphone usage patterns for different demographics. This is followed
by the behavioural science behind addiction and habits and how it can be connected
to the smartphone.
Finally, we will look at one of the more popular subjects of
application usage, how their usage throughout the day can be predicted.

2.1
Smartphone usage studies
2.1.1
General facts concerning usage
In the past decade people have become very dependent on their smartphone, as the
average usage per day has increased from 20 minutes in 2008 to over three hours in
2017. Smartphones have become the ultimate device, capable of all previous technol-
ogy advances and mobile on top of that [MW18; NF10; Oul+12]. Therefore there is
no mystery on how its usage grew so fast, other technologies became obsolete and
their usage was transferred to the smartphone device, although some still have some
advantages over mobile, like size and speed. In a recent study done with a question-
naire it was found that 46% of people think they could not live without a smartphone
[Ans+16]. Furthermore, in an online survey of 1.000 people between the ages of 18
to 34, it was found that 87% of young people never let their smartphone leave their
side and for 80% of the people the first thing they do in the morning is check their
phone [Mee14]. The smartphone market has recently saturated at a point of 1,5 bil-
lion phones sold per year, but its usage is still increasing. Time spent on social media
per day increased by 50% between 2012 to 2017, from 90 to 135 minutes per day. In
China the smartphone usage overall (including new users) has increased by 60% from
March 2016 till March 2018 with the social media accounting for 47% of the usage in
2018. Video apps have grown in usage in China, making up 22% of the total usage,
followed by games with 13% and news with 11% in 2018 [MW18].
2.1.2
Demographics smartphone usage
Studies on the characteristics of problematic internet use have underlined that the
demographics may have some influence [BV12].
The difference between the genders in app usage has been found mostly to be
related to social fulfillment, where females are more socially needy and feel more
social pressure than males. Males are more drawn to process-oriented apps such as
gaming or gambling, often categorized as entertainment apps. It was found contrary
to previous research that females are more vulnerable to develop addictive usage
patterns, in consequence of their social pressure [Van+15; Lee+14]. One study showed
that based on only installed apps, they could predict with 70% accuracy the gender
of the user, underlining that there is a difference in how the genders choose their apps
[Sen+15].
The receptiveness towards new technology is greater for younger people, while
older people are slower to adapt and accept new technology. It is the difference in
ideology, where the more youthful generations view technology as a functional ne-
cessity while older generations think of it as a convenience but not essential [CB92;
Pea+10]. Similarly to other new technologies, smartphone usage decreases with age.
What was found in a recent study is that younger people feel more social pressure
and are worse at self-regulation than older people. Addictive behaviors are there-

2.2
Behavioural insights to smartphone usage
2.2.1
Uses and gratification theory for social media
Uses and gratifications theory (UGT) is a technique that tries to comprehend the
reasons why people are drawn to particular media for a short satisfaction. With the
emergence of interconnected mobile devices the use of the gratification theory has
never been more relevant [Rug00]. With social media inflating in the past decade,
people pursue it for recognition and respect of what they have been doing. When
this behavior is considered normal, the public is pressured to follow this narcissistic
behavior looking for acceptance from external relationships on their social media
instead of real relationships [Leu13].
A study was carried out with an interview
questionnaire, that was intended to get responses regarding uses and gratifications
people get from social apps. This study was conducted to find out what makes them
so appealing and rewarding. It was found to be numerous reasons. Most people use
it for social interaction to keep in touch with people that give them the feeling of
having a social life and be connected. Secondly, people use it to seek information,
information like birthdays, events, and news. Then people use it to pass the time, for
entertainment and relaxation when they are bored or want to escape reality, looking
through posts and videos [WW13].
2.2.2
Habitual behavior in smartphones
Habit is defined as a behaviour that occurs instinctively, happening regularly and is
provoked by circumstances such as surroundings and company [Oul+12]. Habits can
have consequences in both directions, being good or bad. They can make a complex
action more simple by allowing multitasking as well as giving behavior control in
new situations.
They can characterize an individual making him predictable for
others who look for his traits [WN07]. Habits that cause people to do actions that
do not benefit them and interfere with other tasks are considered bad, for instance,

the craving to check your phone. Smartphones have a good potential to create bad
habits as they are convenient devices with many benefits over laptops. They are used
considerably shorter each time but more evenly over the day totaling more time than
laptops. Bad smartphone habits can lead to extreme and impulsive smartphone usage
like unlocking to check for notifications or scroll through an endless feed of content.
It was found that 35% of sessions on the phone were to check the front screen for
either notifications or the clock [Oul+12]. To the extent, this behavior can become
bothersome when in a group of friends people look to their phone as a habit instead
of participating in the gathering [Sha96]. Social media apps can be partly blamed for
this behavior, they were maybe not designed to be addictive, but the platform can
lead to habitual behaviour. With the content contentiously changing and the feeling
of missing out on things, it creates a pressure to be constantly connected [Gri18].
Boredom can also lead to creating habits that follow you into your daily life.
In
a study that tracked phones of 54 participants for two weeks with self-reporting of
boredom, it was found that the best predicting factors were the frequency of using
communication applications followed by the time of day. Furthermore, they found out
that people are more likely to interact with recommended content on their smartphone
when they are feeling bored [Pie+15].
2.2.3
Addictive behavior in smartphones
Addiction is defined as a condition that makes you vulnerable to seductive things that
make gratifying feelings [Nes13]. Addiction can be substance based or behavioral,
the more known and common being intoxicants like alcohol, cigarettes, and other
drugs. Behavioral addiction is when an action is done to produce a good feeling,
relieving stress, and sorrow regardless of the consequences [Sha96].
Gambling is
considered a behaviour addiction and now quite recently since the internet has started
to be everyday necessity things such as social media and gaming have been shown
to produce symptoms of addiction.
Teenagers and young adults are using social
media more than before, resulting in what is often called an internet addiction that
shows signs of psychosocial distress.
Internet and other digital related addictions
are commonly caused by habitual behavior that eases bad feelings and offers an
escape from reality [Mül+16; Lee+14]. The internet generally and its use on the
smartphone have the same fundamentals, making the research of internet addiction
also apply to smartphones [Kwo+13].
When the user becomes too dependant on
smartphones, creating addictive behaviors, this can lead to negative effects on the
physical, psychological, and social aspects of life [You99]. What makes smartphones
so appealing is the mobility factor, making it easy to access whenever and wherever
you are and something you seek when bored [NF10; Oul+12].

Psychological impact of smartphones
The effect of smartphones on humans well being both socially and mentally has been
studied to some extent. The recent advancement in smartphones and an enormous
increase in smartphone ownership has changed the social landscape with new oppor-
tunities to be connected and socialize with others. With young people looking for
acceptance and a place where they belong, colleges and high schools have become
an environment where social media dictates people’s value. In a study done on col-

and phone usage. Social media usage could have healthy results and help them to
participate in activities and have a better overview of what was happening around
them. Social media is used to fulfill their psychological desire that encourages the

communication and on the contrary of making peoples lives easier, it has blurred the

less emotional and cognitive relations and is therefore preferred [Tur17].
2.3
Predicting application usage
Multiple studies have been done regarding predicting what app the user is going to
open at a given moment. The average amount of installed apps is around 100, with
10 of them used on average each day. To ease the struggle of finding apps, prediction
algorithms have been optimized for a faster and more time-saving experience, the
recommended apps are then displayed with a bar on the home screen [Xu+18]. Using
Naive Bayes classifier (NBC) in a machine learning model, the most useful feature
of predicting app usage was found to be the last app used, location, and time of
day/week in the order of importance [SHD12]. Similar findings were reported using
other machine learning methods, one demonstrating techniques to predict five apps
with 83% accuracy only using location data [Yu+18]. Another findings concluded that
the location and time of the day are the key contextual features when predicting apps
[Ver09]. Another study goes into how parallel tree augmented Naive Bayesian network
(PTAN/TAN) can handle the user cold start problem when there is no app history
and the app cold start problem when an app has not been used before [Bae+15].
In the past, machine learning approaches have been the way to go, often utilizing
methods like NBC and the hidden Markov model. But in recent years, new methods
using recurrent neural network (RNN) have been emerging, promising better results.

4.2.2
Application
The application dataset is one of the largest dataset that Lifelog measures. Consisting
of over 50 billion lines of data over the used span of four years, making it too large to
be loaded in memory or to be handled at once. Therefore a few single months from
every year were used to make decisions on how all of the months should be processed.
For the application data the powerful clusters utilizing Amazon EMR combined with
Pyspark got to shine. It took so much power running the processing methods through
all the months of the application data that the cluster power required for it was
calculated to have a cost over 2.000 $. Prior to working with the application data,
it was thought to be more reliable than it actually was, making the cleaning more
demanding than expected and requiring a good understanding of what data had
been collected. This section will go into the many obstacles and the time-consuming
process of removing unwanted data and how the application data was cleaned.
4.2.2.1
Exploring the data
Previous students or other available staff at Sony had barely touched on the applica-
tion data, so to my knowledge, the data is almost entirely unexplored. To realize how
the application data should be cleaned, we picked single months from every year to
find out how reliable the data was. By selecting those months and trying out plotting
various things, it was evident that something had to be done. When a histogram of
the average usage per day for the users was created, some users had up to 70 hours
of usage on average each day. From the top apps of these users, it became quite
clear what was happening, Lifelog was measuring background apps activities while
recording other apps on top of it. These background activities made it possible for
multiple apps to be registered at the same time frame and the usage to go way beyond
the 24 hours mark of a single day. Moreover, some lines were very long in time usage
for regular apps like the popular game Candy Crush with a single line of usage of 80
hours. To filter these out as best as possible the dataset went through several steps,
with inspection of the results between, discussed in the next sections.
4.2.2.2
Spotting background usage
Finding if an app was running in the background was multiplex and went through a
few stages. This process resulted in filtered data that is thought to be much more
reliable than before, while still having few odd occurrences. The data went through
filters of:
• Removing all single lines with app duration of over 4 hours or were zero in
length
• Removing all apps that have average app usage over 6 hours
• Apps in the category ”Other” had to be in a created white list of apps
==PAGE:42==
26
4 Methodology
With the assumption that a single line of over 4 hours is in most if not every case
an error, we filtered out all of the lines with very high usage, some consisting of up to
80 hours. It was noted that it could be possible that a person had a nonstop 4 hours
app session without taking a break, but it was considered that unlikely that it would
not skew the results. The lines with zero seconds in usage duration were also removed.
By calculating the average usage per day of each app, the topmost apps almost
all were some backgrounds apps, named lock screen, battery saver, anti-virus, and
so on. Removing all apps with over 6 hours on average per day seemed to fix this
problem. To note is that some of the apps were real apps with a small user base that
resulted in a high average time. But it was such an insignificant amount that it was
not considered to have any effect.
Lastly and the most substantial part of the cleaning is the so-called white list.
With Lifelog categorizing apps into categories based on Play Store genres, apps that
were classified as ”Other” often seemed to be background apps. By selecting the top
250 apps categorized as ”Other” and going manually through them, a white list of
apps was created. So only apps categorized as ”Other” that were on the white list
were used. A list of the top ”Other” apps that were removed can be seen in appendix
B.

the tree up until all nodes are pure and can not expand further. In figure 6.1 (b)

splitting deeper.

6.1.2.2
Logistic regression
Logistic regression relates much to classification in spite of what the name implies.
The process is equivalent to multiple linear regressions, with the anomaly that the re-
sult variable is binomial. The method tries to classify whether the person in our case,
is in group A or B based on the accessible features, very similar to the random forest
classification [Spe14]. One of the methods building upon this is called cross-validation
and works by dividing the data into a training and test set, the most common type
being the k-fold cross-validation.
k-fold cross-validation
The k-fold cross-validation splits the data into k many equal-sized subsets, where one
of these subsets are used as a testing set while the others are used as training sets.
This process is then looped k many times (every loop is called a fold) using all the
subsets once as a testing set. To make the final score, all the folds are then usually
averaged, this gives the result less randomness, as the picked training and test sets
can differ a lot [Fus11]. A 10 fold cross-validation was carried out on our data mea-
suring the coefficient and it features importance.
Feature importance
The logistic regression feature importance is very informative as it shows in what way
the feature is affecting the results. In table 6.3 each feature is showing a positive or
negative number, showing in which direction the feature is pointing, the direction of
0 sorting the user into the bottom 10%, while in the direction of 1 sorts the user into
the top 10%. The higher the value for a specific feature is the closer the user is to the
feature importance value. To take an example for the app Facebook, Messenger has
a score of 1,422, meaning that the higher the proportion of total usage in Messenger,
the more likely the user is to be in group 1.
6.1.2.3
XGBoost
XGBoost (eXtreme Gradient Boosting) is a gradient boost method that is often used
for classification and regression tasks. For the predictions, it uses an ensemble learning
method that combines different algorithms to create more robust results, in many
cases outperforming regular learning algorithms [CG16]. By parameter tuning the
learning model, it resulted in about a 1% increase from the random forest classification.
The feature importance for XGBoost was not looked into.

Conclusion
With the appearance of smartphones and their upsurge in usage for the past years
they have become an essential part of numerous lives, especially for the younger
generation. The smartphone includes many of the previous technology advances like
radio, TV, GPS, social media and the internet, that people can take advantage of
wherever they are [MW18; Pea+10]. Previous researches have mostly investigated
those singular parts of technologies instead of the effect of them on a smartphone.
This large scale data study confirms those previous findings and ties them to the
smartphone as well as expanding on them with new theories. This study investigated
how people use their phones and how different demographics take advantage of the
smartphone when it comes to their app usage, from the middle of 2016 til the end of
2018.
With a statistical analysis on the available information of each user, it was found
out that age was the most contributing factor when considering the total usage with
a roughly linear change of 3,5 hours average per day for 21 years old to an hour for
72 years old. Age was also crucial in what apps people spend their time on, with
younger people having much more tendency to use social apps, with the more recent
social apps having even more advantage. Older people had more tendency to use older
forms of technologies on the phone like calling, maps and internet browsers. This is
in accordance with prior researches with older people being more devoted to older
technologies [Pea+10; Edw19]. It was discovered that people over 30 had increased
their usage noticeably more than people under 30 and it was traced down to the de-
cline in Facebook usage for the last two years with similar findings in another research
[Edw19]. The genders had some diversity in their choice of apps with the social apps
being more appealing to females, while males are more drawn to entertainment, and
this was also observed in previous researches [Van+15; Lee+14]. Females were found
to have 5-7% more usage than males. Apart from Europe having 20% less usage
than other continents the others had a similar amount of usage. It was found that
apps could be highly continent based, with some apps barely used in some continents.
Asia was the most unusual with hardly any usage in the most popular social media
platforms, but instead using Line and browsers much more. It was found that BMI
did not have any significant influence on app usage, obese people only had slightly
more usage.
Machine learning methods were used for classification and regression. The former
was to decide if a person could be classified as an extreme user of a particular app
==PAGE:106==
90
8 Conclusion
based on their demographic. This was investigated in order to find how individual
features could affect a particular app usage. The results showed great promises for
some of the larger apps, with scores up to 85%. The results were in accordance with
previous findings with age having the most impact and continents being an excellent
deciding factor for some apps as they are often continent based. What stood out
compared to earlier findings was that gender did not have as much impact on the
model. For the models, the proportion of usage of popular apps was added and that
increased the model’s accuracy, where the more similarity to the app being predicted
the more the app’s feature mattered.
The users were also divided into five groups based on their change in usage over
time, from a high decrease to a high increase. It showed no significant differences
between the demographics but younger people were slightly more likely to have high
changes and the most vulnerable app categories for these changes were books, games,
and videos.
With a random forest regression model, we attempted to predict how much average
usage people had based on their demographics and proportion of usage in each app
category. The model had a 𝑅2 score of 0,29 with a mean absolute error of about an
hour. These results indicated that the attributes investigated in this study were not
enough to make a good prediction on the usage. With the usage distribution ranging
from 1 up to 6 hours for 75% of the users, the mean absolute error of an hour was
proportionally large. To investigate the characteristics of the extreme users of over 6
hours we divided the users into five groups based on their usage from low to high and
we observed that the differences in their demographics correlated much to previous
findings. Top users were more likely to be younger, females and not from Europe.
What could also be noticed was that the likelihood of using particular categories
differed for the groups, with categories such as books, games and videos having more
high usage users.
From the group analysis it was found that age and proportion
of usage in app categories were useful parameters for predicting app usage, but the
regression model simply needed more good features for a more accurate prediction.
8.1
Future work
The data provided by Sony has so much potential that we have barely scratched the
surface of it. This study just touched lightly on many of the multiple parameters
that we had available and now that the application data has been examined it can
help future studies work on it and to find interesting points to explore further. The
most interesting part would be to utilize location data more, as previous researches
have shown that location is good predicting factor for apps [SHD12; Yu+18]. With
the location data, the app usage can be exploited in relation to where people are, for
example, how the usage differs from staying at home, being on the move or at work.
Moreover, with the location data and app usage, a model to predict the user features
such as age and BMI could be fascinating. A follow up on a few loose ends that
were not explored in this study could also lead to captivating findings. The difference

apps they pursue compared to the others. Sessions showed some great promise of

the users could be extended, adding things like gross national income or the weather
