Abstract 
To synthesise enantiomeric pure polyfunctional polylysine dendrons, using a new method of 
convergent, liquid phase synthesis. The dendrons are created using readily available L-lysine, with a 
lysine with 2 Boc protective groups as a starting point. The coupling reagent used is COMU, and it 
was tested both as stand alone, together with Oxyma Pure, and with NHS, with the NHS route proving 
the optimal choice for liquid phase. COMU is reported as having high stereoselectivityi in creating 
the active estersii, which in turn reacts with the primary amines of L-lysine. A small foray into looking 
into combining this with solid phase synthesis was also made. While COMU has been shown to 
function as a coupling agent, using it with L-lysine proved a tough nut to crack, due to the solubility 
of lysine (or lack thereof). While a reproducible method was eventually reached, it was not reached 
in time to conduct steric tests of the dendrimers produced. The results of these tests will, hopefully, 
be ready at the defence of this report. Should the tests prove favourable, it would open up the 
possibility for convergent production of polylysine dendrons, as well as justify further research into 
analogue methods.

1.1 Project Aim 
To develop a method that allows for convergent synthesis of enantiomeric pure polylysine dendrons, 
allowing for rapid growth using commercially available L-lysine.

2. Theory 
Previous attempts at convergent liquid phase synthesis of 
lysine dendrons have proven unfeasible, due to the stereo 
chemistry not being preservediii. However, a new coupling 
agent, COMU, promises much better stereo selectivity, and 
also looks to be more safei to work with than conventional 
coupling agents, some of which are now banned due to risk 
of explosions. While COMU appears to be a very effective 
solid phase coupling agent, not much documentation on its behaviour in liquid phase was available.  
2.1 Active Esters 
COMU is a coupling agent based on an uronium salt, see figure 3, and on its own it will form an 
active Oxyma based ester with the carboxylic acid of an amino acid. This is why Oxyma is often used 
as a support to COMUiv to ensure complete activation.  
While the active ester formation is thought to be analogue to conventional methods, it is speculated 
that the preservation of the sterics is due to the morpholine group of COMU holding the amino acid 
in place while the active ester forms. 
The active Oxyma ester was found to be quite sensitive to hydrolysis, 
especially in the presence of H2O, and to combat this, N-
Hydroxysuccinimide (NHS), figure 4, replaced Oxyma as a support for 
COMU, resulting a much more stable active ester.  
2.2 L-lysine 
The greatest advantage of using readily available L-lysine for dendrons, the two primary amine 
groups, are also the source of the biggest obstacle for working with lysine: it almost exclusively 
dissolves in H2O. Moreover, since the reaction relies on a water sensitive active ester, this provided 
many initial complications. 
Attempts were made to mitigate this by slowly dripping the H2O containing lysine phase into the 
active ester, or the other way around. Even with the active ester being the more resilient NHS, the 
reaction time of dendrons proved far too slow to properly mitigate the hydrolysis of the active ester. 
Seeking an alternative solvent, lysine proved soluble in Formamide, due to its water-like behaviour.

2.3 Active ester coupling 
The reaction of the active ester with a free amine. The NHS is a good leaving group, but not as good 
as Oxyma. However Oxymas readiness to leave can cause issues with hydrolysis, especially in slow 
reactions. 
 
2.4 Racemization 
The racemization that we are trying to prevent is caused by an intermediate state called an azlactone 
ring, in which a carbanion is formed on the alpha-carbon. COMU is said to counter thisi through its 
morpholine ring. The use of NMM as a base, which also contains a morpholine ring could potentially 
strengthen this.

3. Results and Discussion 
 
Following months of experiments, a reproducible method for liquid phase synthesis of PLL dendrons 
was finally achieved. The initial strategy of either running the reaction in a water based environment, 
or mitigating waters effect by slowly dripping dissolved lysine into the active ester showed little to 
no results. The Oxyma ester proved very sensitive to hydrolysis, and thus the choice was made to use 
NHS as the active ester. Its increased stability was offset by its longer reaction time, and in the end 
still no success was had in water based enviroments. It was then suggested that formamide be used 
as 
an 
alternative 
solvent 
for 
L-lysine, 
and 
this 
finally 
showed 
promise. 
Then followed test to vary the different ratios of reactants, as well as re-examining the potential of 
the 
Oxyma 
ester, 
though 
NHS 
once 
again 
came 
out 
as 
preferable.  
It was noted that the active ester preferred to bind to L-lysine, rather than a lysine that already had a 
dendron attached to one of its amines. It was briefly explored if this was an avenue where it was 
possible to make asymmetrical dendrons, however it was deemed too difficult to control the ratio on 
liquid phase using only stoichiometrics. It did result in the realisation of yet another difficulty in this 
sort of reaction: Use an excess of lysine, and the product yield would be low, and a biproduct that 
gets harder and harder to remove per generation would form, see figure 6. Use an excess of the 
previous generation dendron, and you would be 
left with a mixture of 2 generations of dendrons, 
hard, if not impossible, to separate from each 
other. The dendron to lysine ratio has to be 2:1, 
and hydrolysis of the active ester has to be kept to 
a minimum. This was achieved when truly dry 
solvents where obtained. An incident with the 
drying temperature of mol sieves resulted in the 
supposed dry solvents in the lab containing 
more 
water, 
rather 
than 
less.  
 
The small foray into solid phase synthesis was a proof of concept that the compounds produced via 
liquid phase could be used on solid phase, with the same coupling reagent, to potentially produce 
more complex structures. This idea of combining convergent and divergent synthesis is not new, but

4. Conclusion and Future Experiments 
While the project was off to a rough start, eventually a method for using commercially available L-
lysine for convergent growth of Boc-PLL dendrons. While this technically fulfils the aim of the 
project, having reached this point earlier would have been preferable, not only to have had ample 
time to conduct the steric tests, but also to maybe reach bigger dendrons with more steric centres to 
test, but also to work on dendrons with orthogonal protection groups. Working further to get better 
yields is also something that has yet to be explored in-depth. All in all, this is barely scratching the 
surface of what is possible using COMU in liquid phase, and if the results of the steric tests come 
back positive: rapid bulk production of monomer PLL dendrons. Looking even more forward, the 
potential for asymmetric synthesis is there, and my guide in this project has already made some 
promising tests using a Cu(II) complex as a temporary protection group, with the added benefit of 
having distinct colors at different stages of the reaction.

5. Experimental 
5.1 General methods 
Chemicals were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich, Fisher Scientific and IRIS Biotech. Solvents were 
used as received or dried over molecular sieves (4 Å) when necessary. HPLC-MS analysis was 
performed on a Shimadzu HPLC 2010 and a Bruker micrOTOF-Q III, using a Phenomenex Kinetex 
C8 column (2.6 m, 100 Å, 50x2.1 mm) and a 1 mL/min linear gradient from 0 to 20% buffer B over 
2.7 min, then linear from 20% to 100% buffer B between 2.7 min and 4.5 min, 100% buffer B till 6 
min, then from 100% to 0% from 6 till 7 min, and end run at 10.0 min (buffer A, 0.025% TFA in 10% 
aq MeCN buffer B, 0.025% TFA in 90% aq MeCN) with a quadrupole mass spectrometer in ESI+ 
mode. HRMS (ESI+) was performed on a Dionex UltiMate 3000 system with a Bruker MicrOTOF-
QII-System with ESI-source nebulizer 1.2 bar, 8.0 dry gas L/min, 200oC, HRMS samples were 
calibrated by an automated pre-run internal mass scale calibration of the individual samples by 
injecting a sodium formate calibration solution prior to the measurement. NMR (1H, 13C, APT, H,H-
COSY, HSQC, HMBC) were performed on a 300 MHz Bruker Avance 300 equipped with a BBO 
probe and autosampler and a 500 MHz Bruker Ultrashield 500 plus. IR was performed on a Shimadzu 
IRAffinity-1 instrument using attenuated reflection technique (ATR) performed either on the 
crystalline compounds or on evaporated droplets from DCM solutions.

Boc-Lys(Boc)-OH 
L-lysine (15 g) is placed in a round flask (1 litre) and dissolved in 100 mL MilliQ water, and 300 mL 
1M NaOH is added under stirring. This solution is cooled on ice bath, and 48 g of tert-
Butylpyrocarbonate in 300 mL THF is added slowly over 5 min. The solution is stirred on ice bath 
for an additional 15 min, then stirred at room temperature for 2 hours. The solution is evaporated to 
½ volume in vacuo (max 30 C temp) and 300 mL of KHSO4 is added, followed by fast extraction 
with ethyl acetate. The organic phase is washed with brine (2x100 mL), dried with Na2SO4 and 
evaporated to a light yellow oil. This oil is then dispersed in H2O and freezedryed to yield the solid 
off-white product (34g, 96%). 
 
Boc-Lys(Boc)-OSu 
In a round flask, Boc-Lys(Boc)-OH (35.5 g, 103 mmol) and N-Hydroxysuccinimide (11.6 g, 103 
mmol) wgere dussikved ub dry CH2Cl2 (120 mL). The resulting solution was cooled on an ice bath, 
and N,N –diisopropylcarbidiimide (16.1 mL, 103 mmol) was added. The mixture was stirred 
overnight gradually heating up to room temperature. The solution was filtrated and the organic layer 
was washed with 1M NaHCO3 (4x100 mL) and brine (4x100 mL), dried with Na2SO4 anh. and 
evaporated to dryness in vacuo. The with solid was grinded by stirring in petrolether (50 mL); finally, 
the solvent was eliminated by decantation, thereby obtaining the product as a white solid (32.0 g, 
70%) 
 
Boc-G1-PLL dendron (conventional method) 
In a round flask, Boc-Lys(Boc)-OSu (15.2 g, 34.3 mmol) was dissolved in 40 mL of EtOH. Lysine 
(2.63 g, 18.0 mmol), dissolved in 20 mL of H2O, and NMM (7.6 mL, 68.6 mmol) were added. The 
mixture was stirred overnight at room temperature. The yellow solution was transferred to a 
separation funnel, where it was added 1M KHSO4 (80 mL). The product was extracted with EtOAc 
(60 mL), and the organic layer was washed with brine (3x30 mL), dried with Na2SO4 anh. and 
evaporated to dryness in vacuo. The residue was stirred with petroleum ether, and the product is 
obtained as a while solid (8.5 g, 59%).
