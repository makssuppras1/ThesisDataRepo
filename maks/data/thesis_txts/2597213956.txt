Abstract
Natural products (NPs) are naturally occurring compounds with structures that have
been naturally selected, and are therefore a great source of therapeutics. Compound
collections inspired by NPs have been of great interest due to prevalidated biological
activities of the guiding NPs.
Estranes are a class of steroids that are used to regulate a wide range of biological
responses, so a collection of compounds inspired by the structure of the A- and B-
rings of estrogen has been generated in the discovery of new bioactive compounds.
The key precursor (15) was used to generate a range of heterocyclic compounds by
fusion to the B-ring of 15 (Scheme 0.1). The key precursor (15) was generated from
the commercially available protected ketone (14) by demethylation with hydrogen bro-
mide. The intermediate precursor, α-bromoketone (18), was generated by copper(II)
bromide to access other scaﬀold reactions. From the key precursor (15), two hetero-
cyclic reactions were attempted, yielding three quinolines (17a-c) and two azaindoles
(32a + 33b).
From the α-bromoketone (18), three heterocyclic reactions were attempted, yielding
three thiazoles (21a-c), two imidazo[2,1-b][1,3,4]thiadiazoles (25a-b), and two quinox-
alines (30a-b).

Introduction
1.1
Natural products & their derivatives – a source of thera-
peutics
Natural products (NPs) represent a large pool of diverse compound classes that all
are naturally occurring primary or secondary metabolites produced by living systems.
NPs can originate from all sorts of plants, micro–, and marine organisms. NPs are
produced with the purpose to deliver speciﬁc functions; primary metabolites have
necessary functions needed to obtain homeostasis of the organisms that produce them,
whereas secondary metabolites can have various purposes, and these compounds are
often more complex in structure and highly functionalized.[1][2] Natural products not
only vary in size and function, but they often have unique structural elements, like
fused, bridged, or spirocyclic rings. These structures can be diﬃcult to synthesize by
a purely synthetic route, but they can sometimes be easily be produced by biological
systems.[1][3]
NPs can be classiﬁed according to their biological functions, structural similarities, or
source of origin. Examples of primary metabolites are easily recognized structures like
amino acids, carbohydrates, fatty acids, and DNA/RNA (Figure 1.1A) These are all
structures that are considered the building blocks of our cells or help to generate the
needed energy for many biochemical pathways.[4] Secondary metabolites are a large
group of other natural products produced by living organisms, and compound classes
like alkaloids, terpenes, and phenolic compounds show the large structural diversity in
this group of compounds (Figure 1.1B).
1
==PAGE:13==
Figure 1.1: Examples of natural products. A) Primary metabolites. B) Secondary
metabolites.
In medicinal chemistry, NPs can be ideal leads for basic research for potential bioac-
tive compounds or components for drug discovery. NPs have through millions of years
been naturally selected, and these structures have prevalidated bioactivities and are
often highly speciﬁc. NPs have through millennia been recognized and exploited for
medicinal purposes. Throughout history, humans have by trial–and–error discovered
certain plants and herbs to be advantageous towards certain problems or diseases.[1][2]
Even without knowing the cause behind these eﬀects, people have relied on them as
the medicine of the day. Later scientists began to methodically extract and isolate
natural products and use them as the active ingredient in therapeutics. Even some
therapeutics, which we still rely heavily on today, are based on NPs that for centuries
have been recognized for their therapeutic eﬀect. A few examples of these are shown
in Figure 1.2.
Acetylsalicyclic acid is a well-known inﬂammatory agent traded under the name of
Aspirin (Figure 1.2B). It is derived from salicin found in the bark of a willow tree.
It was ﬁrst isolated back in 1853, but it has been known for over 2,400 years for its
medicinal beneﬁts.[5] The salicin is hydrolyzed, oxidized, and then acetylated to gen-
erate the acetylsalicyclic acid used today.
Opium is another example that has been recognized for thousands of years for its anal-
gesic eﬀect. Opium is found in the unripe seeds of opium poppy, Papaver somniferum,
and is a source for at least 20 alkaloids, including known painkillers like morphine
2
==PAGE:14==
and codeine. Morphine was ﬁrst isolated back at the beginning of the 1800s (Figure
1.2B).[6][7]
Figure 1.2: Examples of NPs as a source of drugs. A) Acetylsalicyclic acid (Aspirin)
is derived from salicin. B) Morphine and codeine are naturally derived alkaloids.
During the 20th century, the concepts of drug discovery and development really took
shape, where a large amount of NPs was methodically extracted and isolated from the
original source for their medicinal beneﬁts. Traditionally, NPs were the main type of
compounds approved and sold as novel drugs. The increase in novel drug discoveries
really began in the 1940s with the discovery of Penicillin G and peaked during the
1980s.[8] During this period, the method for the discovery of novel drugs was mostly
by chance. Many antibacterial and antifungal agents were discovered during this pe-
riod.
Semisynthetic derivatives of many NPs were later developed by either chemical or
biological methods. NPs have evolved to act in speciﬁc environments, so they work
optimally in a speciﬁc environment in the organism that produces them. Thus, many
3
==PAGE:15==
NPs originally had issues with formulation and administration. Structural modiﬁca-
tions were needed to improve these factors, along with factors like potency, speciﬁcity,
and safety.[2] Therefore, many NP-based drugs have been iteratively modiﬁed from
the original NP to improve certain properties.
An example of this development is rapamycin, discovered as an antifungal agent back
in 1972 (Figure 1.3).[7][8] Rapamycin is a macrolide produced by the bacterium Strep-
tomyces hydroscopicus. It was later discovered to be a good mTOR inhibiting agent
used for the prevention of organ transplant rejection. Rapamycin had issues with the
formulation and oral administration, so a few semisynthetic derivatives developed to
address these issues.[7] Semisynthetic derivatives of rapamycin included temsirolimus
and deforolimus, where a hydroxy group was selectively modiﬁed. Temsirolimus was
approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 2007 as a prodrug of
rapamycin. The hydroxy group was exchanged with an ester di-hydroxy moiety. The
ester will be cleaved by rapid metabolism when consumed to generate rapamycin.[7][8]

In the last few decades, the original method of discovery by chance will rarely lead
to new lead identiﬁcation, so the rate of discovery has slowed down substantially.[4]
Thus, the pharmaceutical industry has shifted to a more targeted screening method
called High Throughput Screening (HTS) of synthetic chemical libraries for lead iden-
tiﬁcation and optimization due to proﬁt and time demands.[1] By screening large
compound libraries, HTS has an increased possibility of lead identiﬁcation within a
much shorter time frame. This strategy is most advantageous for synthetic small-
molecule drug candidates.[5] However, a fragment-based discovery has the potential
to promising leads for NP-inspired drugs. However, the amount of NPs, NP-derived,
and NP-inspired therapeutics on the market still occupy a considerable proportion of
newly developed drugs. The continuous study, done by Newman et al. of the origin of
drugs approved from 1981 to 2014, showed that NPs, their derivatives, and NP-derived
drugs still account for 25% of approved drugs in this period.[9] Many of these drugs
are of antibacterial and anticancer properties.
1.1.1
Natural products in drug development
NPs and their derivatives are often large, highly functionalized molecules with several
stereogenic centers. They often occupy chemical space with unique structural elements
and functional groups that are often not present in purely synthetic compounds.[1][2]
Functional groups like polyhydroxy groups, esters, epoxides, and α,β-unsaturated sys-
tems are more frequently occurring moieties within bioactive NPs compared to syn-
thetic small-molecule drugs.[7] NPs tend to be large complex molecules that contain
more oxygen and less nitrogen, sulfur, and halogen groups, more hydrogen bond donors
(HBD) and hydrogen bond acceptors (HBA), and fewer rotatable bonds.[3] Natural
products have many favorable structural and functional features since they are the
result of millions of years of evolutionary selection, and they are therefore biologically
prevalidated structures. Many NPs often have several highly speciﬁc targets, and they
are therefore good starting points for drug discovery.[2][10]
However, working with NPs can be challenging based on their large size, and com-
plex and highly functionalized structures. Through evolution, many NPs have been
selected based both on their speciﬁc targets, but often also to a very particular route
of delivery.[7] So, issues with oral bioavailability, stability, and too rapid clearance
in vivo often occur with these compounds.[4][7][11] Many NP-derived compounds are
5
==PAGE:17==
optimized NPs by a semi-synthetic route, done to improve certain properties, like se-
lectivity, potency, safety, ADME, pharmacokinetics and –dynamics in vivo, but still
retain the desired biological activity.[2][8]
The development of structure-activity relationship (SAR) properties have allowed the
optimization of lead compounds – both semisynthetic or fragment exchange of NPs and
synthetic small molecules.[3] These investigations have led to discoveries of bioactive
structure, which have given inspiration for NP-inspired compounds.

Mevinolin is a natural product ﬁrst isolated from Penicillium citrinum in the 1970s
and has been used for the treatment of cardiovascular disease. By further investigation,
it was recognized that the lactone ring of mevinolin (Figure 1.4, blue) is the pharma-
cophore, the essential part of the compound, that is involved in binding to the target
protein. Mevinolin is a chemical complex molecule containing 8 stereogenic centers.
Second-generation agents of mevinolin included HR-780 was developed to simplify the
structure of mevinolin while retaining the bioactive substructure.[3][12]

1.2
Strategies for synthesis of NP-inspired compound collec-
tions
In chemical biology research, a variety of small molecules have been identiﬁed to modify
the activity of a wide range of proteins, so the design of synthetic compound libraries
for HTS has been shifted toward generating selective small molecules with molecu-
lar scaﬀolds that might display unexpected biological activities.[13] These types of
compound libraries draw inspiration from compound classes that are biologically rel-
evant by deﬁnition, such as natural products.
NPs are a major source of inspira-
tion for NP-derived and NP-inspired compound libraries due to their prevalidation by
evolution.[14] NP-inspired compounds also help to expand the chemical space of small
molecules. Many NPs share common structural elements, but with diverse substituent
patterns, enable them to have diﬀerent bioactivity proﬁles. NP-inspired libraries are
often used for HTS against new targets for the development of novel therapeutics due
to their increased hit rates and variety of bioactivities compared to typical combina-
torial chemistry libraries.[13]
In generating NP-inspired libraries, diﬀerent strategies can be employed depending on
the purpose of the compound library.[11] The general concept behind these strategies is
by designing compounds that will mimic the structures and properties of the NPs, they
are more likely to be biologically relevant based on the guiding principle that similar
structures are likely to have similar activity.[15] NP-inspired libraries are generally
generated around a scaﬀold that is identical to the leading NP.[16] By simplifying the
scaﬀolds, it often makes the compounds less complex, more hydrophilic, reduces the
molecular weight, and reduce the number of rings and rotatable bonds.[11]
The discussed strategies are the approaches behind Diversity-Orientated Synthesis
(DOS), Biology-Orientated Synthesis (BIOS), and pseudo-NP compound libraries.
1.2.1
Diversity-Orientated Synthesis (DOS)
The DOS strategy mainly focuses on increasing structural diversity, and less on biolog-
ical speciﬁcity. DOS libraries do therefore not have to be directly based on NP struc-
tures, but a number of guiding NPs can be used as inspiration to generate more chemi-
cally diverse compound libraries that might have NP characteristics. The DOS strategy
helps to generate compounds that are stereochemically and skeletally diverse.[14]
DOS compound generation follows a build/couple/pair workﬂow (Figure 1.5). This
7
==PAGE:19==
three-steps workﬂow relies on planning the targeting compounds by incorporating func-
tional groups that can selectively be linked in one of the three steps as described.[17]
Build: Asymmetric synthesis of chiral building blocks that contain sets of function-
ality suitable for subsequent coupling and pairing steps.
Couple: Intermolecular coupling to join the building blocks in sets of two. In Figure
1.5, the black dots represent functional groups intended for the intermolecular cou-
plings. This results in compounds with a dense array of functional groups that can
undergo intramolecular reactions in distinct pairwise combinations.
Pair: Intramolecular coupling reactions are performed to join pairwise combinations of
functional groups incorporated in the ’build’ phase. In Figure 1.5, the red, green, and
blue dots represent pairing functional groups intended for intramolecular reactions.
Figure 1.5: The build/couple/pair workﬂow behind DOS.
8
==PAGE:20==
The building blocks can either be based on a core scaﬀold, speciﬁc structural elements
or just mimic structural features of speciﬁc NPs in a general sense. Prevalidated struc-
tural elements and systems commonly found in several NPs can often be incorporated
in the building blocks to give NP-like characteristics. This will increase the possi-
bility of bioactivity.[2][15] Chiral building blocks can either be prepared by enantio-
and diastereoselective reactions or compounds can be chosen from a chiral pool. The
stereochemical diversity arises in the ’build’ and/or ’couple’ steps, whereas the skeletal
diversity arises in the ’pair’ step.[17]
1.2.2
Biology-Orientated Synthesis (BIOS)
The concept of BIOS originated from the structural analysis of proteins and small
molecules, where many NPs were discovered to share certain structural elements.[13]
These structural elements have been validated to be of biological relevance, and it has
shown to have diverse biological activities in diﬀerent compounds or compound classes.
In BIOS, compounds are often based on these biological prevalidated structures. NP
scaﬀolds are mainly selected based on their biological relevance. By generating libraries
based on NP scaﬀolds instead of whole NP structures, it will help reduce the structural
complexity of NPs, yet retain – or even improve – biological activity compared to the
guiding NPs. These scaﬀolds are used as precursors to generate NP-inspired analogs
(Figure 1.6A).[14]
The BIOS strategy involves a hierarchical classiﬁcation of biologically relevant com-
pounds based on structural relationships and type of bioactivity. The ﬁrst Structural
Classiﬁcation of Natural Products (SCONP) was a concept of scaﬀold trees introduced
and applied to the Dictionary of Natural Products (DNP) by Waldmann et al.[15] This
NP tree charted the chemical space of NPs in the DNP by classifying and hierarchi-
cally arranging the scaﬀolds of NPs to reduce the high diversity of NP structures to a
manageable level. The SCONP provides guidelines for selecting scaﬀolds of bioactive
compound classes as starting points for the development of BIOS libraries.
This approach has the highest biological speciﬁcity of the three strategies, but BIOS
arrives at compound classes that often retain the same kind of bioactivity as the
guiding NP, hereby restricting the exploration of biological space.[14]

1.2.3
Pseudo-natural product libraries
The limitations of biological space in BIOS can possibly be overcome by extending the
chemical space more into that of synthetic small molecules. This should be possible
by the fragment-based compound design. NP fragments that represent NP structures
and properties are combined de novo to access novel scaﬀolds that may retain or im-
prove the chemical and biological characteristics of the guiding NPs (Figure 1.6B).
This approach expands the chemical space beyond what is explored in NPs. This
fragment-based strategy gives rise to pseudo-NPs that can include structural charac-
teristics across several NP classes, so they will not be accessible in existing biosynthetic
pathways. The idea is that pseudo-NPs may retain the biological activity of the guid-
ing NPs, yet the possibility to give rise to new, unexpected bioactivity, and hereby

Steroids are a large group of polycyclic NPs with prevalent scaﬀolds that are found in
various compounds in nature and therapeutics. Many steroids are hormones that play
essential roles in various biological processes, including energy metabolism, water and
salt balance, reproductive functions, and behavioral and cognitive functions.[22]
All steroids consist of a core structure of four fused rings: three cyclohexanes and one
cyclopentane. The rings are conventionally numbered and denoted by the letters A, B,
C, and D, which is shown for the sterol core in Figure 1.9.[23] Sterol is a subgroup of
steroids that share a hydroxy group at the C3–position of the A-ring. Sterols include
cholesterols and estrogens.

Estrogens as therapeutics
Estrogens are involved in several signaling pathways to induce diﬀerent biological func-
tions. They mediate their action by binding to their designated nuclear receptors,
estrogen receptors (ERs). The hormone-bound ERs form an active dimer that acts as
a transcription factor for speciﬁc gene transcriptions that will lead to certain biologi-
cal responses. Estrogens are also known to be involved in some extranuclear actions
through other receptors.[23][26] In the treatment of several diseases, the estrogenic
signaling pathway can be a good target due to the wide span of signaling pathways
estrogens are involved in. Compounds that might target the estrogenic signaling path-
ways, both as an agonist or an antagonist, can be good leads in drug development.
Synthetic estrogen is used today as an agonist for hormone replacement therapy for
postmenopausal syndrome and in contraceptives.[23]
Breast cancer is one of the most frequent types of cancer among women, and a third of
these breast tumors are hormone-dependent and express ERs on the cell surface.[27] A

Aims & objectives
Estranes are a class of steroidal natural products that possess a wide range of biological
activity. This project aims to generate a library of estrogen-inspired compounds to be
used for the discovery of new bioactive compounds. The A- and B-rings of estrogen

imidazothiadiazoles (25).

3.1
Deprotection of precursor (14)
6-hydroxy-1-tetralone (15) is the key precursor for all the generated derivatives. Its
structure is based on the A- and B-rings of the core structure of estrogen. The pro-
tected tetralone, 6-methoxy-1-tetralone (14), is a commercially available compound,
on which all the synthesized compounds are based on.
When constructing the synthetic pathway for the planned scaﬀolds, there are two op-
tions as to when to demethylate the compounds; either as the ﬁrst or last step of the
synthesis. It is preferable to demethylate 14 in the ﬁrst step, because it will signiﬁ-
cantly reduce the number of steps, instead of needing to demethylate all the derivatives
individually.
Using the demethylated ketone (15) as the key precursor can give rise to deprotonation
of the phenolic alcohol in reactions carried out in strong basic environments. Phenolic
alcohols are stronger acids (pKa of ∼10) compared to the corresponding cyclohexanol
(pKa of ∼16) due to delocalization into the aromatic ring, which leads to a more sta-
ble conjugate base.[30] Thus, phenolic alcohols are more prone to deprotonation under
basic conditions.

3.7
Synthesis of azaindoles derivatives
Azaindoles are a group of substituted indoles; a bicyclic structure consisting of a six-
membered pyridine ring edge-fused to a ﬁve-membered pyrrole ring. The nitrogen can
be placed in four diﬀerent positions in the pyridine ring, however, the 7-azaindoles are
the most investigated isomer.[45] Azaindoles are rarely found in nature, with only a
small number of NPs that have been discovered to contain the azaindole moiety.[45][46]
Yet, azaindoles have gained interest in medicinal chemistry research as a bicyclic sys-
tem comprising an electron-rich ring fused to an electron-poor ring. The 7-azaindole
is an interesting skeleton containing an HBD and an HBA in a rigid three-atom ar-
rangement. This gives the possibility for interaction with the peptide backbone of a
potential target.[45] Azaindoles have been reported to have a wide range of potential
medicinal applications, including anti-tumor, antiparasitic, cytotoxic agents, selective
B-Raf inhibitors, and selective ATP-competitive inhibitors of mammalian target of
rapamycin.[46]

4
Conclusion
The commercially available ketone (14) was used to generate the key precursor (15)
which resembling the A- and B-rings of estrogen. 15 was generated by demethylation
of 14 with a yield of 86%. The key precursor (15) was used to generate a number of
estrogen-inspired compounds by the fusion of heterocyclic systems to the B-ring of 15
through the ketone group. An intermediate precursor, the α-bromoketone (18), was
generated to access other scaﬀold reactions. The α-bromination of 15 was generated
by cobber (II) bromide, yielding 18 with a yield of 44%.
From the key precursor (15), two heterocyclic reactions were attempted, yielding three
quinolines (17a-c) and two azaindoles (32a + 33b) that were puriﬁed and prepared
for biological testing.
From the α-bromoketone (18), three heterocyclic reactions were attempted, yielding
three thiazoles (21a-c), two imidazo[2,1-b][1,3,4]thiadiazoles (25a-b), and two quinox-
alines (30a-b) that were puriﬁed and prepared for biological testing.
An overview of the reactions and synthesized compounds carried out during the project,
along with possible suggestions for future work to expand the compound collection (in
blue) is shown in Scheme 4.1.
All the scaﬀold reactions overall gave relatively low yields. There was a general problem
with poor solubility in organic solvents for the reaction mixtures and ﬁnal compounds.
This gave issues in the puriﬁcation steps. The reaction mixtures were mainly puri-
ﬁed by either ﬂash chromatography or preparative HPLC. Reactions puriﬁed by ﬂash
chromatography were all dry-loaded on celite due to poor solubility. In most cases,
this method of puriﬁcation resulted in poor separation and mixtures of the product
with other impurities. Puriﬁcation by preparative HPLC, large amounts of solvent was
needed to dissolve most reaction mixtures, leading to loss of more product, compared
to reaction mixtures dissolved in a small amount of solvent.
The scaﬀold reactions were only optimized to yield the desired compounds with high
purity (> 90%). With the time limits of the project and the unexpected restrictions
caused by the COVID-19 lockdown and re-opening, the main focus was to generate
diﬀerent scaﬀolds and analogs with high purity, so the reaction procedures were not
optimized to improve the yields.
42
==PAGE:54==
Some of the scaﬀold reactions partially or fully generated the oxidized products. This
was observed for some of the imidazo[2,1-b][1,3,4]thiadiazole, quinoxaline, and azain-
dole reactions. The oxidization occurred as an aromatization of the B-ring, generating
completely aromatic systems, giving very rigid and ﬂat structures. The oxidized prod-
ucts are not desired, because these ﬂat and rigid structures can give rise to issues with
solubility and non-speciﬁc binding to hydrophobic patches of proteins.
4.1
Future work
Within the time frame of the project, it was not possible to get all the novel structures
characterized by HRMS (ESI), but the samples were prepared, ready to be submitted
for the analysis.
In further pursuit of new scaﬀold reactions, it would be desirable to target less aromatic
scaﬀolds, due to the general issues with poor solubility. Examples of possible scaﬀold
reactions are illustrated in Scheme 4.1, marked in blue. A possibility will be spirocyclic
compounds (36), which will generate more three-dimensional structures. Furthermore,
the reactions to generate the hydroxypyrimidines (35) through the β-ketoester (34)
were not achieved during the project, but they are also an option.
The synthesized compounds also need to be sent for the biological screenings. If a
compound gives a biological response in the screenings, it would be possible to have
a more targeted approach to generate a number of speciﬁc derivatives of that scaﬀold
in a SAR study.
If a scaﬀold turns out to have derivatives that are bioactive, it might be preferable to
attempt to optimize the puriﬁcation method, possibly by recrystallization, to try and
improve the yield, or at least to reduce the number of puriﬁcation steps, if possible.

Experimental methods
5.1
General directions
Commercially available reagents were used without further puriﬁcation and all solvents
were of HPLC quality. Reactions were run under normal atmosphere unless otherwise
mentioned.
All reactions were monitored by thin-layer chromatography (TLC) and/or reversed-
phase ultra-performance liquid chromatography mass spectrometry (RP-UPLC-MS).
Analytical TLC was conducted on Merck aluminum sheets covered with silica (C60).
The plates were visualized under ultraviolet (UV) light.
Flash column chromatography was performed using Merck Geduran® Si 60 (40-63 µm)
silica gel, with pressure applied through a hand pump. Celite was used to prepare most
samples for dry-loading.
Microwave reactions were carried out in a Biotage® robot Eight with Initiator+ system.
All compounds were characterized by 1H NMR, 13C NMR, and MS (ESI) (byproducts
were not fully characterized). All new compounds were prepared for high-resolution
mass spectrometry HRMS (ESI). For the recording of 1H NMR and 13C NMR, a Bruker
Ascend with a Prodigy cryoprobe (operating at 400 MHz for proton and 100 MHz for
carbon) was used. Chemical shifts (δ) are reported in parts per million (ppm) and
coupling constants (J) in Hz. For recorded spectra in deuterated dimethyl sulfoxide
(DMSO-d6), signal positions were measured relative to the signal for DMSO-d6 (δ 2.50
ppm for 1H NMR and δ 39.43 ppm for 13C NMR). For recorded spectra in CD3OD,
signal positions were measured relative to the signals for CH3OH (δ 3.31 and 4.87 ppm
for 1H NMR and δ 49.0 ppm for 13C NMR). For recorded spectra in CDCl3, signal
positions were measured relative to the signals for CHCl3 (δ 7.26 ppm for 1H NMR
and δ 77.0 ppm for 13C NMR).
Analytical RP-UPLC-MS (ESI) analysis was performed on an S2 Waters AQUITY
RP-UPLC system equipped with a diode array detector using a Thermo Accucore
C18 column (d 2.6 µm, 2.1 x 50 mm; column temperature: 50◦C; ﬂow: 1.0 mL/min).
Eluents A (0.1% HCO2H in H2O) and B (0.1% HCO2H in MeCN) were used in a linear
gradient (5% B to 100% B) in 2.4 min and then held for 0.1 min at 100% B (total run
time: 2.6 min). The LC system was coupled to an SQD mass spectrometer.
Preparative RP-HPLC was carried out on a Waters Alliance reversed-phase HPLC
