Abstract
R&D projects documentation and data are a core for taking decisions about the portfolio of
Hempel’s products and allocation of resources to develop new solutions. Documentation and
data management systems used for speciﬁcation processes in Hempel do not provide enough
support for eﬃcient R&D project and portfolio management and KPI reporting.
The objective of this project is to study the problems with the current speciﬁcation processes,
especially d IT support systems used for R&D projects at Hempel and propose a suitable solu-
tion for those. This is done using the 5-step model for development of speciﬁcation processes.
Based on the employee interviews, a list of desired future state criteria is proposed and three
future scenarios are built, according to a 5-stage framework proposed by the Global Business
Network. The scenarios look into beneﬁts, drawbacks, resources, implementation plans and fu-
ture implications of two main systems: (1) a Product Lifecycle Management system for R&D
project documentation in Hempel, and (2) Upgrade of currently used system (Project Server)
including integration with PowerApps and PowerBI.
Each of the three scenarios is later evaluated against the criteria for desired future state with
accordance to the following multi-criteria decision making methods: weighted factor comparison,
analytic hierarchy process, best-worst method and technique for order of preference by similarity
to ideal solution. A cost-beneﬁt analysis is carried out to select the solution bringing the highest
Return on Investment and shortest payback period. Finally, a gap analysis is performed for each
scenario and for the current situation, to evaluate the potential improvement brought by each
scenario.
A recommendation to implement a scenario that assumes an upgrade of currently used system
(Project Server) including integration with PowerApps and PowerBI is made based on the
results of the performance evaluation and feedback received from Hempel. This solution oﬀers
a 146% Return on Investment and a payback period of 1 year. Additionally it reduces the gap
between the current performance and target performance for all evaluated factors.

1. PROJECT DESCRIPTION
1 | Project Description
This thesis project carried out at the Management Engineering Department at DTU in coopera-
tion with Product and Portfolio Management Department at Hempel A/S focuses on analyzing
speciﬁcation processes and associated data and documentation used for running and reporting
on Research and Development projects at Hempel and investigating the ways this data and doc-
umentation are stored. The documentation currently used is studied with a purpose of ﬁnding
out what data is used and why. The project also analyzes the data source and recipients of
various documents and reports generated for purposes of portfolio management with a goal of
analysing and reducing time spent on retrieving data. Another angle for the analysis is the
documentation storage possibilities. At the end, based on the list of needs and requirements of
various actors, a solution for data and documentation storage and sharing, that maximizes the
gain for the stakeholders involved is proposed.
1.1 | Background
Hempel is a world-leading coatings supplier oﬀering products for six diﬀerent segments: Dec-
orative, Protective, Marine, Container, Yacht and Industrial, as well as oﬀering services, such
as for example coating application speciﬁcation and advice. Hempel operates in more than 80
countries around the world providing work to more than 5,500 employees. The locations in
which Hempel operates are called ‘Hempel sites’. This covers 26 factories and 10 R&D facilities
in 22 countries, and their headquarters in Copenhagen, Denmark. [4]
Hempel’s strategy for years 2016-2020 is built around Journey to Excellence, presented in Figure
1. It aims at building a foundation for company’s long-term success by strengthening and im-
proving the way valuable products and services are delivered to the customers (Key Enablers); as
well as focusing on developing oﬀering to customers, especially in selected segments (Must-Win
Battles), but in general in the three chosen business areas (Our Business) within the coatings
industry. [1]
Key strategy initiatives of 2017 included introduction of a two-dimensional organizational struc-
ture, divided between functions and regions. The two dimensions are collaborating in order to
generate a greater alignment on a Group level. Another initiative was connected with increasing
the level of compliance between the products oﬀered and the customer needs. This was done by
extending the pipeline of innovative solutions oﬀered, among others to ensure meeting customer
needs in the areas where there are no products meeting these needs today. Other initiatives
included for example, increasing performance and decreasing costs of the Supply Chain orga-
nization, or developing new talent management program and enforcing personal development
tools for employees. According to the 2017 Annual report the plan for 2018 is to increase focus
on customer-centricity and internal excellence. [1]

Company’s strategy is reﬂected in it’s business model, where the customer is a central entity
around which all the operations are revolving. As presented on Figure 2 Hempel is not only
focusing on production excellency and communicating the beneﬁts of the products to the cus-
tomers in order to support sales, but also advises and supports their customers in terms of
ensuring quality during application of products, or optimization of their processes. Company
also creates their new solutions according to market needs, by partnering with customers to
deliver exactly the products that will beneﬁt the customers and contribute to their success.
Considering that Hempel operates in coatings industry, the products that the company provides
are very often applied on huge surfaces (for example on large container ships, or on the blades
of wind turbines), where reliability is a key feature. This is why the ﬁfth component of the
company’s business model is a focus on developing trustworthy solutions and improving them in
order to adjust them to improve their performance and tailor them to the conditions in which
they will be applied (for example climate conditions). [1]
A very important role in the customer value creation is played by Research and Development
function. It is a global organization, that in collaboration with both internal and external part-
ners and the customers, develops new solutions, optimizes existing ones and provides technical
support. Hence it can be said that the R&D organization operates in four diﬀerent areas: [1]
1. New Solution Development focuses on delivering trusted solutions to protect cus-
tomers’ assets;
2. Product Optimization works to continually optimize existing products to improve per-
formance;
3. Explorative Innovation and New Technologies explores new technologies that go
beyond coatings in order to deliver superior solutions to customers;
4. R&D Technical Advisory delivers technical support to the customers.

In order to deliver New Solutions, Customizations and Innovative Products, as dictated by both
strategy and business model of Hempel, R&D is collaborating closely with Product and Port-
folio Management (PPM) in terms of working on product development projects together. The
role of PPM, among others, is to evaluate if a given product development project is feasible
for the market and should it be given a ”GO” for development. It also includes evaluating if
some products should be introduced just in certain regions, or worldwide. Product and Portfolio
Management ensures a competitive pipeline of oﬀered products, while avoiding producing du-
plicated outputs. They are also involved in monitoring the progress of projects, once they have
been initiated, under diﬀerent angles and against various requirements. Product development
projects at Hempel are not only about the Research and Development part. Each project has
both a Technical and a Commercial Project Manager. Each of them has a diﬀerent range of
responsibilities and diﬀerent goals that need to be achieved by working together, in order to
ensure success of a project. Another role of PPM is monitoring the Key Performance Indicators
(KPIs) related to projects progress.

1. PROJECT DESCRIPTION
In order to ensure correct prioritization, progress and monitoring of projects certain documen-
tation is required. Moreover, as mentioned above, the project pipeline is constantly analyzed
against Key Performance Indicators, that are reported to diﬀerent stakeholders on a monthly ba-
sis. These operations require access to large amounts of data concerning the projects. Currently
at Hempel there are a lot of diﬀerent documents for each R&D project and almost every Project
Manager stores the documentation in a diﬀerent location of their choosing. Another issue is that
the project leaders ﬁll out documentation in a diﬀerent way, even though in theory they are fol-
lowing an enforced template. This leads to lack of standardization for the documents presented
for example at Project Board meetings and makes the process of retrieving information needed
for Key Performance Indicators reporting very time consuming and strenuous. Moreover It is
required that project managers input the same data in diﬀerent documents, for example gate
presentations and Project Server (solution used currently for monitoring projects and retrieving
project data), which is not optimal. It is also a known fact that not all of the project managers
follow up on updating Project Server in a timely manner, or with due frequency. The current
set up creates a lot of confusion and often leads to incomplete, or erroneous KPI reports, which
are a basis for decision making of, among others, Executive Management of Hempel.
1.2 | Research questions
This project focuses on the problems related to speciﬁcation processes at Hempel. Precisely,
accessing data needed to carry out Key Performance Indicators reporting, inconsistencies with
documentation prepared by diﬀerent actors, and the volume of diﬀerent documents and reports
that need to be prepared, presented and analyzed for each project. This can be summarized in
the following research questions:
1. How is the document and data handling for current speciﬁcation processes
supported today?
2. What are the performance and major problems in current speciﬁcation pro-
cesses?
3. What could be the future scenarios for supporting document and data handling
for the speciﬁcation processes?
4. How can the performance of scenarios for speciﬁcation processes be assessed?
5. What is the suggested future speciﬁcation process for Hempel?
6. What is the expected impact from the suggested future speciﬁcation process?

1.3 | Research goals
The goals of this thesis project aim at ﬁnding a feasible answer to the research questions by
analyzing the current state of aﬀairs in Hempel, and ﬁnding a solution that would result in
achieving a desired future state. They can be summarized in the following points:
• Mapping current new product development process;
• Mapping project documentation used at diﬀerent project phases;
• Identifying problems and issues with the current document storage solution;
• Generating a list of future criteria in terms of a data collection system;
• Evaluation of Product Lifecycle Management system to achieve the desired future state;
• Evaluation of upgrading the currently used system (Project Server) in order to achieve
the desired future state;
• Evaluation of performance of the solutions against the established criteria;
• Evaluation of the solutions against comments and suggestions from Hempel;
• Recommendation of suitable solution for Hempel.
1.4 | Data sources
Data needed to carry out the project will be obtained via interviews with employees from the
Product and Portfolio Management department in Hempel. The inputs from them will mostly
include information about the data needed to perform the tasks of the department, such as
prioritization of projects, creating and analyzing KPI reports. They will also provide inputs on
problems they are currently facing with access to data, as well as needs and requirements in

2.1 | Development of speciﬁcation processes
Speciﬁcation processes denotes business processes which are related to the analysis of needs of
a customer and deﬁnition of speciﬁcations of a product that can satisfy these needs. [5] The
framework around which this report is built follows a 5-step methodology of developing a spec-
iﬁcation process for Hempel, as outlined below.
1. Identifying and characterizing the most important speciﬁcation processes.
In step 1, the most important speciﬁcation processes in the company are identiﬁed. This is
done by means of identifying the most important speciﬁcations in the company, in relation
to a particular process, or function. Next a survey is made to ﬁnd the processes that lead
to producing these speciﬁcations. During the survey, the customers of the speciﬁcation
process are identiﬁed along with the characteristics of it and a description of important
problems in the current way of working.[5]
2. Requirements for the speciﬁcation process.
In this step the requirements for the future speciﬁcation process are analysed, as seen
from diﬀerent perspectives (for example customers, internal functions of the company,
etc.).
These requirements are the goals for the speciﬁcation process and they should
be expressed in terms of clear numerical values that explain how the process performs
in relation to the requirements from the surroundings.
In this step, a gap analysis is
performed for each speciﬁcation process, to compare the target and actual performance.[5]
3. Design of new speciﬁcation process.
Step 3 involves designing the future speciﬁcation processes on the basis of requirements
deﬁned in step 2. For each of the speciﬁcation processes chosen for evaluation, one or more
possible future scenarios are drawn and described.[5]
4. Evaluation and choice of solution.
In step 4, scenarios formulated in relation to the requirements for the speciﬁcation process
in step 3 are evaluated. This evaluation includes expected costs associated with develop-
ing and implementing a solution described in the scenario, a gap analysis outlining the
performance of scenario versus the established target [5], as well as compliance with the
future state requirements. Finally, one scenario is selected to be implemented.
5. Plan of action and organisation of further work.
In the last step, a plan of action, milestones and phases required to implement the selected
scenario are outlined. The time plan, budget and required resources are indicated and
recommendations regarding implementation of future speciﬁcation process are made. [5]

2.2 | Qualitative and quantitative research
This project utilizes mostly qualitative and to some extend also quantitative research methods.
According to Denzin and Lincoln (1994) qualitative research is a multi-method involving an
interpretive approach to subject matter. This means that qualitative researchers study things in
their natural settings, attempting to make sense, or interpret phenomena in terms of meanings
people bring to them. Qualitative research involves the usage and collection of a variety of em-
pirical materials, for example case studies, life stories, interviews, or observational interaction. [6]
Quantitative research on the other hand is referred to as investigation of phenomena using
statistical, mathematical, or computational techniques. A very important part of quantitative
research is measurement, as it provides a fundamental connection between empirical observa-
tion and mathematical expression of quantitative relationships. Quantitative data is always in
numerical form and can be analyzed by the researcher with the help of statistics. The goal is to
obtain an unbiased result that can be generalised to a larger population. [7]
The research carried out for purposes of this thesis project can be classiﬁed into the mixed meth-
ods research class of Qualitatively driven approaches in which the base of the research study is
qualitative and quantitative data, or methods, aimed at supplementing and improving the study
by providing deeper and more complex answers to the research questions. [8]
On of the main qualitative methods used for this project is interview, which is a method where
questions are asked to elicit information. This method has many advantages, for example it
allows to gather in-depth knowledge from the interviewee. Participants can help to paint a full
picture of event, including their perspectives. Observation of social cues, such as for example in-
tonation, or body language can make the picture of the situation even clearer for the researcher.
The information obtained via this method can however prove to be somewhat biased, and not
always objective. That is why it is very important to choose an appropriate group of intervie-
wees, who will be able to provide a broad overview of situation. [9]
In order to choose a representative group of interviewees, and decide what data to collect from
them, in such a way that the obtained information can be generalized to the whole population
(in this case to the whole of Hempel), sampling is performed. For the purposes of this project,
non-probability sampling will be performed. [10] Using this method does not enable to conclude
the probability of any particular member of the population being sampled. There are several
ways of performing the non-probability sampling: [11]
• Convenience sampling selects a sample on the basis of how easy it is to access. It is easy
to organize such a sample, but it might not be representative.
• Quota sampling divides the population into categories and selects a sample of a chosen
size from each category.

2.3 | Scenario planning and building
Another technique used for the purposes of this master thesis is scenario planning and build-
ing. This paper focuses on assessing the current situations, developing criteria for future state
and presenting and evaluating scenarios that could enable attaining this future state. Scenarios
are described as narrative stories that follow particular paths into the future based on research,
trends, and the key concerns of the managers who will use them.
[12] Scenario building is
based on the fact that in the business environment there are both uncertainties and predictable
occurrences. Scenarios are constructed by combining the two into various combinations in a
process known as scenario planning. [13] As there can be diﬀerent points of views about the
future, that depend on the environment, situation, or the scenario builder, a structured way to
develop scenarios is proposed by the Global Business Network1, so that diﬀerent scenarios can
be compared to each other in a quantiﬁable way. The GBN framework for scenario building
consists of ﬁve stages that can be summarized as follows [15]:
• Stage 1 aims at identifying focal issues of activities that need to be performed in scope of
scenario planning. In this stage a series of interviews with key staﬀis performed, in order
to ﬁnd out more about the challenges an organization is facing. Stage one is concluded
by gaining a certain level of clarity around the issue (or questions) that will be the focal
point of the scenario planning process. [15]
• Stage 2 is about exploring, the so called, drivers of change, which are external forces
that may have impact on the focal point determined in stage 1. In this stage, forces that
may have an unexpected impact on the organization in the future are determined. These
can either be ”predetermined elements”, impact of which is well understood and they are
possible to be predicted, or ”critical uncertainties”, having an impact diﬃcult to estimate.
[15]
1Global Business Network (GBN) was a leading consulting ﬁrm that specialized in helping organizations to
adapt and grow in an increasingly uncertain and volatile world. Using tools and expertise in scenario planning,
experiential learning, together with networks of experts and visionaries, GBN advised businesses, NGOs, and gov-
ernments in addressing their most critical challenges, helping them to gain the insight, conﬁdence and capabilities
they needed to shape their future. GBN was acquired by Deloitte in 2013. [14]

• Stage 3 is about synthesis and integration of information and using data gathered in
previous stages to create scenarios. The drivers of change and focal issues are prioritized
in terms of the degree of importance to the problem and degree of uncertainty. Scenarios
should be developed mainly based on the drivers that have a higher degree of uncertainty
around their potential future impact. In other words, the value of scenarios comes from
exploring the unknown to determine its impact on the known. [15]
• Stage 4 looks into implications that a certain scenario can have on the company, if it is
put into motion. In this stage, a set of questions are considered, such as: What if this
scenario is the future? What actions would I take today to prepare? Are there actions I
could take to create a desirable future, or to move away from a negative one? This is a

degree of learning an organization can gain from analysing the scenario. [15]
• Stage 5 deals with continual monitoring of external drivers and trends in order to facilitate

2.4.1 | Product Lifecycle Management system
Product Lifecycle Management (PLM) is a process of governing the entire lifecycle of a product
(or service) from the pre-production phase (idea, design), through manufacturing (i.e, pur-
chasing, production process, testing, storage), to post-production (sales, services, disposal), as
presented on Figure 3.
It is used to integrate people, processes, systems and data in order
to provide a wholesome product information baseline not only for the company, but possibly
also for its suppliers and collaborators. [16] security. Generally PLM can also be considered
a tool for collaboration in the entire supply chain network and for eﬃcient management of
creating new products and lifecycle processes in the highly networked business environment.
Product Lifecycle Management systems help to bring new products to the market faster and
more eﬃciently.
PLM does not refer to any individual computer software or method.
It is
a wide functional totality, understood as a set of systematic methods attempting to control
information about products. The actual task of PLM is to provide conditions for connecting
separate information data systems, processes and automation islets. Implementing a Product
Lifecycle Management System should result in obtaining an integrated totality to which vari-
ous information systems contribute. This adds value for the company by seamlessly integrating
information from organization-wide processes using diﬀerent information processing systems. [2]

Product Lifecycle Management can be deﬁned as a collection of practices, methods and tools
that help companies cope with challenges arising from internationalization of the contemporary

parties involved in the complex new product development projects. Information access of this

the products throughout their entire lifecycle. These properties allow the Product Lifecycle
Management system to become an enabler of innovation. [18] The core of the system is cre-

re-utilize, data needed for daily operations. This means that work that has already been done

Product Lifecycle Management systems emerged from Product Data Management system (PDM),

that uses a wide range of diﬀerent concepts, technologies, and tools, which extend to groups

lifecycle of a product. [2] Further, it became clear that the value proposition of PLM systems
includes also the possibility of data interchange between diﬀerent formats and systems used by

entire lifecycle, including analysis and test results, test speciﬁcations, environmental component,
engineering requirements, change orders, manufacturing procedures, product performance infor-

all of the aforementioned entities are documents, which according to Merriam-Webster Dictio-

PLM systems include also capabilities such as: workﬂow, or program management, important
for standardizing, automating, improving and speeding-up product management operations. [2]

Implementation of a Product Lifecycle Management system can result in impressive cost sav-

considering the high implementation costs (between 100.000 USD for a small company, up to
14.000.000 USD for large enterprises [24]) and considerable licence costs (from 50 to 150 USD
per user per month [25] plus a yearly maintenance fee, worth around 20% of licence price [2]), is

high brand image quickly to market, while being able to make informed lifecycle decisions over
the complete product portfolio during the lifecycle of each individual product. Excellent lifecycle
control over products also gives new opportunities to control product margins more carefully
and remove poorly performing products from the markets. This set of beneﬁts, driving top
line revenue growth and bottom line proﬁtability, makes ROI extremely compelling, with some
industry analysts characterizing PLM as a competitive necessity for manufacturing businesses.
There are two main areas of savings accomplished by implementing a PLM system: operations
and productivity of labor. [2]

2.5 | Cost-beneﬁt analysis
Cost-beneﬁt analysis (CBA) is a method used for this thesis project to assess the degree
to which the costs and returns (beneﬁts) of a given scenario are balanced against each other.
In other words, CBA is a systematic approach meant to facilitate estimation of strengths and
weaknesses of alternatives in order to determine options providing the greatest beneﬁts, while
preserving savings. [33] Cost-beneﬁt analysis has two main applications: either to determine if
an investment decision is sound in terms of beneﬁts outweighing the cost; or to provide means
for comparison of investment decisions, by contrasting total expected cost and total expected
beneﬁt. [34] CBA helps predict whether the beneﬁts of a policy outweigh its costs (and by how
much), relative to other alternatives. This allows the ranking of alternative policies in terms of
a cost–beneﬁt ratio. An accurate cost–beneﬁt analysis identiﬁes choices which increase welfare
from a utilitarian perspective and improve eﬃciency. [35] A cost-beneﬁt analysis will be per-
formed for each of the alternatives considered for the purpose of this project in the following
manner: ﬁrstly all cost for a given alternative will be calculated (implementation cost and yearly
fees). Second step will be to calculate the cost-beneﬁt that has a potential to occur on a yearly
basis. This will be assessed by means of Return On Investment (ROI) indicator, calculated
based on saved man-hours. [36] Saved man-hours will be calculated in two categories, such as:
document retrieval time and reporting time. Payback period for each solution, based on the
costs and saved man-hours will also be presented.
2.6 | Multi-criteria decision making
For achieving the expected outcomes of this thesis project, various techniques of multi-criteria
decision making (MCDM) will be used. This methodology will help to evaluate scenarios
built according to the GBN framework (as explained in Section 2.3). Multi-criteria decision mak-
ing is a sub-discipline of operations research that simultaneously evaluates multiple criteria that
have impact on a decision that needs to be made. When evaluating options, the criteria having
inﬂuence on them are usually conﬂicting (for example cost or price is usually one of the main
criteria, and some measure of quality is typically another criterion, easily in conﬂict with the
cost). [37] The purpose of the multi-criteria decision making is to support the decision-makers
facing complex problems and enable making more informed and educated decisions. Usually the
outcome of the MCDM study is that, there does not exist a unique optimal solution and it is
20
==PAGE:30==
2. LITERATURE
necessary to use decision-maker’s preferences to diﬀerentiate between solutions. [38] Solving of
the multi-criteria problem can however be deﬁned in a diﬀerent way than just choosing the most
preferred alternative of a decision-maker. An answer to multiple-criteria problem can also be
provided as a set (or sets) of ”good” alternatives that could be considered as equally beneﬁcial.
[39] There is a variety of techniques and methods in scope of the multi-criteria decision making.
Some of them require using a specialized software, while others can be performed without it.
Some of the methods have been designed especially to ﬁt a speciﬁc discipline (such as poli-
tics, business, or environment). [40] This paper will discuss four of MCDM methods: Analytic
Hierarchy Process (AHP), Technique for Order of Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution
(TOPSIS), Weighted Factor Comparison (WFC) and Best-Worst Method (BWM).
• Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) is a structured technique for organizing and an-
alyzing complex decisions, based on mathematics and psychology. It was developed by
Thomas L. Saaty in the 1970s and has been extensively studied and reﬁned since then.
[41] AHP can be applied and used in various situations and for a multitude of ﬁelds. AHP
helps to ﬁnd the alternative that suits the goal of the analysis in the best way by providing
a framework for structuring the problem, quantifying its elements and evaluating possible
solutions. [38] In practice, performing the AHP analysis consists of decomposing the de-
cision problem into a hierarchy of less complex sub-problems and analysing each of them
independently. After the hierarchy is built, its elements are evaluated systematically by
means of comparing them to each other two at a time, considering their priority (position)
in the hierarchy. The essence of AHP is that human judgements and not only underlying
data can be used to perform evaluations, as when making comparisons, the decision makers
very often rely on their own experience. [42] The advantage of AHP lies in the fact that
the evaluations made by the decision-maker can be converted into a numerical value which
can be processed and compared over the entire range of a problem. The analysis allows to
build a hierarchy and compare in a consistent way measures, which may be very diverse
and otherwise incommensurable. In the ﬁnal step of AHP process, numerical priorities are
calculated for each of the decision alternatives. These numbers represent the alternatives’
relative ability to achieve the decision goal. [41]
• Technique for Order of Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution (TOPSIS) is
another multi-criteria decision making technique, based on a concept that the chosen alter-
native must have the shortest geometric distance from positive ideal solution (PIS, which
maximizes the beneﬁt criteria and minimizes the cost criteria) and longest from negative
ideal solution (NIS, which maximizes the cost criteria and minimizes the beneﬁt criteria).
[43] TOPSIS belongs to the group of compensatory aggregation methods. This means that
it works by means of comparing a set of alternatives by assigning weights for each crite-
rion, normalizing the score (normalization is usually required as the parameters or criteria
are often of incongruous dimensions in multi-criteria problems) and calculating geometric
distance between each alternative and the ideal alternative (which is the best score in each
criterion). [39] Moreover, compensatory methods allow trade-oﬀs between criteria, which
provides a more realistic form of modelling the results, than non-compensatory methods,

• Weighted Factor Comparison (WFC) is a method of performance evaluation that
aims at assessing how much each of the proposed alternatives is aﬀected by each of the de-
ﬁned criteria. Weighted Factor Comparison provides an explicit method for evaluation of
options. The analysis is performed by assigning numerical values (weights) proportionally
to each of the factors (criteria) based on its degree of importance. Afterwards a numerical
score is assigned to each of the alternatives (scenarios), based on how it performs against
each factor. The scores are multiplied by weights and the products are summed to obtain
a total weighted score. [45] The alternative that obtains the highest total (sum) score is
perceived to be the most beneﬁcial solution to a stated problem. When using the weighted
factor comparison it is important to remember that it is not always easy to quantify sub-
jective factors, hence the scale needs to be ﬁtted on a case-by-case basis in a way that
ensures the best performance. It is possible to assign both positive and negative scores
to alternatives, based on their positive, or negative performance against a given criterion.
[39] It is important to treat each score assigning exercise as a separate case. It means to
ignore all other criteria, besides the one being evaluated at that moment. The WFC tool
is constructed in such a way, which ensures that all of the ﬁnal scores will be consistent
with one another. [45]
• Best-Worst Method (BWM) Best-Worst Method (BWM) is a multi-criteria decision-
making (MCDM) method developed by Dr. Jafar Rezaei (Delft University of Technology)
in 2015. This method can be applied to various decision making ﬁelds (such as: business,
engineering, agriculture, healthcare, etc.)
[46] The method can be used for any case
involving ranking and selecting an alternative for a set of possibilities.
According to
BWM, the most desirable (best) and the (worst) criteria are identiﬁed by the decision-
maker. The best criterion is the one which has the most important role in making the
decision, while the worst criterion has the opposite role. Next pairwise comparisons are
conducted between an arbitrary criterion and the best and worst criteria. [47] Next step
is formulation of an optimization minimax problem (a decision rule for minimizing the
possible loss for a worst case (maximum loss) scenario. [48]) to determine the weights of
criteria and alternatives. The ﬁnal scores of the alternatives are derived by aggregating the
weights from diﬀerent sets of criteria and alternatives, based on which the best alternative
is selected. [46]

5.1.1 | Overview of the proposed solution
Solution proposed in scope of Scenario 1 is introduction of a PLM software to store and manage

would only be implemented for this purpose and would not be extended and integrated into
other branches of the company (such as for example production, procurement, supply chain,
or sales). An integration of PLM with other systems could be considered as a further step,
or a future extension of the proposed solution.
This scenario will not evaluate which PLM
software would be the most suitable to implement in Hempel. Instead it will focus on evaluating
the features available in every PLM software and their impact on Hempel’s way of working.
This scenario will provide and overview of beneﬁts that could be gained by R&D and Product
and Portfolio Management organisations, if a PLM system would be implemented. It will also

5.1.2 | Beneﬁts
Scenario 1 brings numerous beneﬁts for Hempel in terms of project documentation storage and
management. The main advantages include:
• Possibility to utilize information in various contexts. Usually data, or information
is created in connection with some process, or to serve a given purpose. However a need
to utilize the same data in diﬀerent context may appear. Information processing systems
embedded in most of the PLM software can facilitate utilization of information in context
that was not initially intended by the data originator. [2] For example: sales data in the
NPV template are initially intended as a source of information for the project boards,
regarding a given product’s projected performance on the market. However, a lot of data
in the template can be (and is) used for another purposes, such as portfolio prioritization,
segment and regional portfolio strength calculations, overall predicted proﬁt from newly
launched products, etc. PLM could facilitate retrieval of the data for the speciﬁc purpose,
while ensuring the consistency of information during its transfer between diﬀerent units,
departments, or even companies.
• One point of storage for all documentation. One of the practical problems faced by
Hempel in terms of documentation storage is multiple points of documentation storage.
Because of that, there sometimes appears a problem of clarifying the location of the latest
version of a certain document, or investigating, if this version has been uploaded to all of
the required locations. Shortcomings in the processes, standards, and tools for information
storage can cause some erosion of the operations model in practice. People begin to update
the same information on their own storage, for example on their own workstations, and
they share information with their project team, or colleagues directly from there. Nobody
knows for sure whether the latest version is located in the agreed place (or places), or if
44
==PAGE:54==
5. DESIGN OF NEW SPECIFICATION PROCESS
one of the people who have access to the shared document have made any changes in it.
For example: currently it is required that the Project Manager creates and collects a very
wide variety of documents, some of them need to be uploaded to the Board meeting folders
for approval. Only after the documents have been approved, they need to uploaded to the
Project Site of Project Server. Additionally most of the documentation needs to be shared
with the project team, various parts need to be shared with marketing, supply chain, health
and safety, etc. At the end, the project manager keeps all of the documentation locally, at
his, or hers PC and shares relevant bits with relevant actors. However, if there is a change
to one of the documents shared, he, or she needs to remember to update the version in
all of the locations and for all of the colleagues who have access. Implementation of PLM
completely eliminates this problem, by providing a one storage point for all documentation,
allowing approvals within the system (so the approved documentation does not need to be
re-uploaded into another location), and pre-deﬁned sharing settings for diﬀerent document
and data types.
• Shorter time to market for the products. 80 up to 90% of time needed to bring a new
product to market is used in the planning an development phases. If the company wants to
bring the products to market faster, but not compromise their quality, most of the eﬀorts
should be concentrated on the planning phase, where the most signiﬁcant time savings can
be obtained. [51] The PLM system is an information processing system, which integrates
the core processes of a manufacturing company and connects, integrates and controls all
of the business processes via the products. In this way the workload associated with the
planning phase can be signiﬁcantly reduced. [2] For example: the information used in the
planning phase of a similar product, can be reused, or adapted, thus signiﬁcantly reducing
the time that would normally be needed to generate it from the beginning, approach the
customers, or research the competitors oﬀerings. Availability of information provided by
the PLM system ensures that these processes can be made signiﬁcantly more eﬃcient and
the overall time to market can be reduced.
• Access to documentation can be controlled on diﬀerent levels. All of the docu-
ments in a PLM system are stored in a common database, however it is possible to restrict
access for given documents, document types, or groups. The access rights can further
be regulated by permissions for speciﬁc users. Some of them would be able to create or
add documents, some of them only to contribute to existing ones and some only to view
them. For example: team members for a given project would have a possibility to con-
tribute to that project’s documentation, but not to the documents concerning projects of
their colleagues, even though they work in the same department. Board members would
have access to view all of the project documentation for all projects subject to ”their”
Board approval and project managers would be able to create and change the relevant
documentation.
• Consistent document naming and searching. PLM uses metadata to identify what
information is stored and where. The metadata is created automatically based on the
inputs and can include a unique identiﬁer(s) of a given project, such as for example project
name, or number. Further, metadata can be used to ”code” the ﬁle names, so that all

of the documents regarding the same project would be stored under the same project
name (and/or number). For example: all of the documentation regarding a project called:
Introduction of a new product, with a project number A4387 could be named in a following

– A4387 Introduction of a new product Gate 5 Presentation.pptx 03 April 2019 ver.1;

• Easy tracking of changes in the document and access to previous versions. The
system stores all versions of the documents and keeps track of their status. Drafts and all
versions of each document can be always accessed. Moreover, the system keeps track of
all actions that were performed on the document, such as viewing, editing, downloading,
printing, etc. For example: a project manager created a Gate 3 presentation template
and asked a member (A) of the team to update some data. On the next day the project
manager can see that changes in that document were made both by team members A
and B. In this case the project manager has a possibility to check what has been changed
and/or added into the document by each of them and decide if that data is correct. Later
the project manager submits the document to the board. They however do not agree with
some changes that were made to ﬁnancial data. The project manager can easily retrieve
an old version of the document, that has the correct calculations in it, and does not need
to recreate them from scratch.
• Workﬂow management. Diﬀerent people may need access to various types of tasks or
documents. Workﬂow management helps to increase the cooperation, as well as facilitate
access and distribution of documents within the stakeholders. A notiﬁcation can be auto-
matically issued to appropriate employees, deﬁned by the workﬂow, in diﬀerent scenarios.
For example: a project manager submits gate documentation, the board members are
notiﬁed and have time to familiarize themselves with the documents before the meeting.
After the gate meeting, if all the documents are accepted, the board chairman approves
them within the PLM system and a notiﬁcation is sent to Portfolio Management, saying
that the documents are ready to be processed and used for reporting. This clears the need
to store the documentation in multiple locations dependant on its status and ensures that
the same documents are always available to all interested parties.

The solution proposed in Scenario 2 looks in-depth towards implementation of various built-in

customers for the developed product, which is not speciﬁc to Gate 3. In the proposed solution,
information on this page would include only the data needed for gate 3 reporting, namely:
• Gate 3 approval date;
• Baseline NPV;

5.2.3 | Drawbacks and limitations
Besides its numerous advantages, Scenario 2 brings with it also some drawbacks and limitations,
such as:
• No change to the way documentation is stored. In this scenario, the documentation
storage and sharing problem is not tackled. New design of the Project Site and more
responsibilities and tools located on it could be a better encouragement for the project
managers and project teams to use it for documentation sharing, but it is not necessarily
a given that the libraries located there will be used to store the relevant documentation.
• Inﬂexibility to changes. One of the common answers in the interviews regarding Project
Server was that the tool is quite inﬂexible in terms of changes. When there is a change to
the process, or a requirement, it is very hard to implement it in a way that will not aﬀect
the information already stored in the database. This is a serious limitation of the system
and can cause various problems and lead to mistakes and misinterpretations of data.
• No easy way to review previous versions of a document, or work simultaneously
on a document. The documentation storage capabilities of Project Server do not cater
for document version management, or simultaneous work of more than one person on the
same ﬁle. In fact, if changes to a document need to be made, it has to be downloaded from
the Server, changed and saved in the local storage of a computer and then re-uploaded to
the Server. This is a big drawback of the system, especially nowadays, when the range of
collaborative tools that can be used for large companies is so wide and people are used to
working together on documentation, even if they are located in diﬀerent parts of the world.
It is also important to be able to track versions and changes, especially for documentation
that has been shared with a lot of colleagues. Accuracy of the documents is crucial, as
they are used for resource allocation and decision making, where a mistake can cost tens,
or hundreds of thousands of euros.
• Document naming consistency not enforced. Project Server upgrade solves a prob-
lem of documentation formats and contents, but this would also not necessarily mean that
the document naming will be consistent and ﬁnding relevant ﬁles will be as fast and ef-
ﬁcient as possible. The document created from the template via PowerApps could get a
predeﬁned name, but this name could be later changed by a user, so the naming structure
for at least some of the ﬁles would not be kept.

6.3 | Best-worst Method
Best-Worst Method is another mean used to evaluate the scenarios. In this method, all of the
criteria are compared to the ones which are selected to be the most important one (best), and
the least important one (worst). Numeric values from 1 to 9 are assigned for each criterion
when comparing it to the worst and the best one in order to establish its priority against that
criterion and all of the remaining ones. When comparing to the best alternative, a score of
1 means: as important as the best criterion; and 9: extremely less important than the best
criterion. When comparing to the worst alternative a score of 1 means: as insigniﬁcant (bad)
as the worst criterion; and a score of 9: far more important than the worst criterion. In case of
criteria evaluated in this project, the best one was selected to be: Data accessibility at all times;
and the worst: Changes tracking; based on criteria prioritization presented in Table 3. The aim
of the best worst method algorithm is to determine the optimal weights for all criteria, such that
the maximum absolute diﬀerences |wB/wj −aBj| and |wW /wj −aWj| for all j are minimized
(where wB and wW are weights of the best and the worst criterion respectively, wj is the weight
of each of the remaining criteria, aBj and aWj are preferences of all of the criteria over the best
and the worst criterion respectively). These maximum absolute diﬀerences will be denoted as
ξ. Calculation of the optimal weights is done using a solver with the following model fed to it:

6.8 | Feedback from Hempel
The results of the current state analysis, criteria for future state, proposed scenarios and their
evaluation have been presented to Hempel. The session was directed towards the Product and
Portfolio Management team (as they are the owners of the currently used tool and they are
ﬁnancing it). After the session, there was a number of questions and comments raised, which
are summarized in this section.
Current state analysis:
The results of the current state analysis were generally received well. The participants agreed
with the points raised in that section and the presented graphs. The only comments regarded
the fact that Product and Project Management processes at Hempel are not yet at the highest
level of maturity. This means that some of the pain-points or complaints raised by some of
the colleagues during interviews are only Hempel speciﬁc and should not be generalized, if one
ever wanted to use them to describe shortcomings of Project Server in general. For example,
Hempel is still developing a culture of project managers regarding it as their duty and not an
inconvenience to report on the project they are working on. This means that some of the issues

Future state scenarios:
There was a big interest with regard to all three scenarios. The one preferred by all participants

majority of problems faced by the stakeholders nowadays, while oﬀering the lowest price and

high expense associated with implementation would be more justiﬁable. In the future PLM and
Project Server could work in parallel, but with current needs and budget this is not likely to be

7. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
7 | Conclusions and recommendations
7.1 | Conclusions
The documentation and data supporting current speciﬁcation processes in R&D projects has
been mapped and studied in detail. Main issues were found in relation with the IT support
system used to collect and share that information. The performance of the current speciﬁcation
process is below the established target. There are also issues related to company’s culture with
relation to documentation and data management and the lack of related proper support and
guidance. There is no clear focus on how the documentation should be prepared and stored and
how the project data should be reported. This results in lack of information needed for decision
making, resource allocation and KPI reporting.
Based on the employee interviews, a list of desired future state criteria is proposed and three fu-
ture scenarios for supporting the document and data handling for the speciﬁcation process were
built. The scenarios take into account beneﬁts, drawbacks, resources, implementation plans and
future implications of two main systems: (1) a Product Lifecycle Management system for R&D
project documentation in Hempel, and (2) Upgrade of currently used system (Project Server)
including integration with PowerApps and PowerBI. Each of the three scenarios presents a dif-
ferent version of a possible future for document and data management in Hempel.
Each of the three scenarios was evaluated against the criteria for desired future state with accor-
dance to the following multi-criteria decision making methods: Weighted Factor Comparison,
Analytic Hierarchy Process, Best-Worst method and Technique for Order of Preference by Simi-
larity to Ideal Solution. This allowed to assess the performance of each of the document and data
management scenarios and rank them. Additionally, a cost-beneﬁt analysis and gap analysis
were carried out to select the solution bringing the highest positive impact on the future. The
ﬁnal recommendations are based on all of the aforementioned assessment methods and feedback
received from Hempel.
7.2 | Chosen scenario
The scenario chosen as the most suitable for the current needs in terms of supporting document
and data handling for speciﬁcation process is Scenario 2 - Upgrade of Project Server in-
cluding automatic generation of documents. This scenario is selected due to its very good
performance in cost beneﬁt analysis (payback period of 1 year and 145% Return on Investment)
and the preference towards this solution expressed by employees at Hempel. Scenario 2 brings
signiﬁcant advantages in many areas covered by the criteria for desired future state, has the
shortest implementation period and requires the lowest amount of training for the employees, as
76
==PAGE:86==

it is mainly an upgrade of the currently used solution. Additionally, it reduces the gap between
the current performance and target performance for all evaluated factors.
The remaining two scenarios are also an interesting alternative for Hempel, but it is not recom-
mended to implement them right now and in a capacity presented in this report for the following
reasons:
• Company is undergoing major organisational changes at the moment (new CEO, new
COO, Product and Portfolio management moved to a diﬀerent functional entity). These
changes may aﬀect they ways of working and documenting products and projects and a
big investment in a new system, which may need to be changed soon is not recommended.
• The implementation of PLM proposed in this thesis project report concerns only applying it
to documentation related to new product development projects. Considering the high costs
related to purchasing and implementing the system it would be more sensible to consider
a company-wide implementation which would take into account also other divisions of
Hempel. In this case the cost savings would be higher and the return on investment and
payback period for PLM would make for a better business case for the company to consider.
7.3 | Recommendations
A short term recommendation for Hempel to improve the data and documentation management
quality would consist of implementing simple Project Server related solutions. These could be
for example changing the design of Project Detail Pages as indicated in Appendix C. Another
solution for a short term is testing the possibilities of implementing the changes to Project Site,
as outlined in Scenario 2 description. A test of the new design could be scheduled for a few
of new projects entering the pipeline and feedback from users could be utilized to improve the
design. These improvements could be implemented by using Hempel’s internal resources, hence
they can be put in motion quite quickly.
If Hempel wants to achieve the full potential outlined by Scenario 2, they should start looking
for a consultancy ﬁrm to carry out the rest of the tasks needed for Scenario 2 as soon as possible.
Negotiation process and aligning details can take a signiﬁcant amount of time, so the sooner they
are started, the sooner the solution could be rolled-out and used. Knowledge of capabilities of
Project Server such as workﬂows, or demand management, to the extent presented in this thesis
can speed up the process, as the issues, or requirements for the new system can be presented to
the external company from the beginning.
The possibilities outlined in Scenario 2 can be rolled-out in steps, in order to speed up the
implementation process. As mentioned before, Project Detail Pages and Project Site improve-
ments can be implemented ﬁrst, using internal resources. Next, the demand management and
workﬂows can be rolled out with use of the external consultancy. While users are being trained

and get used to the new system, the implementation of PowerApps formulars can take place and
ﬁnally the integration with PowerBI. Introduction of the project prioritization can be the last
step and until it is included into Project Server, the current solutions can be used to facilitate
for continuity of work.
7.4 | Further research
There are some additional areas and questions that are not cover by this thesis, but could be
interesting to investigate in scope of documentation and data management for R&D projects at
Hempel. If Hempel would like to consider the implementation of a PLM system in the future,
the possibilities for integrating it with currently used systems and cost saving possibilities in
this area could be interesting to evaluate.
Further, it is possible that instead of investing in upgrading Project Server, it could be beneﬁcial
to buy an entirely new system to take care of Portfolio Management. Maybe one that would
cater for more ﬂexibility and have a more user friendly interface. It could also be interesting to
evaluate having a custom made system tailored directly per Hempel’s needs.
Further research could also include ﬁnding a system which would improve the quality of data
even more, for example by linking the project data with product road maps while enabling
reliable estimation of resource requirements and usage to develop new products.
