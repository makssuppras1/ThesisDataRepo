Abstract 
This thesis focuses on the experimental characterization of a novel Indium Phosphide 
(InP)-based Photonic Integrated Circuit (PIC) designed for Frequency-Modulated 
Continuous-Wave (FMCW) LiDAR systems. This PIC integrates a tuneable ring resonator 
laser, Semiconductor Optical Amplifiers (SOAs), tuneable couplers, and a frequency 
discriminator, achieving monolithic linear chirp generation. DC and RF characterizations 
confirmed stable single-mode operation, broad wavelength tuning, and FMCW modulation. 
Self-heterodyne measurements using fiber delays up to 4000 m yielded a chirp range of 
1.53 GHz, chirp rate of 0.0306 GHz/μs, and a distance resolution of 9.78 cm, with 
estimation errors below 5% in distance measurements. Predistortion algorithm was 
implemented to compensate the nonlinearities and to enhance the linearity at higher 
modulation frequencies. Initial integration with an external Optical Phased Array (OPA) 
demonstrated the beam steering dependence with the wavelength, obtaining 0.07°/nm of 
tuning efficiency and ±1.215° steering range, validating the transmitter’s potential 
functionality and integration. Future work will target on the full system integration with real-
time OPA control and dynamic FMCW beam scanning.

1. 
Introduction 
LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) systems are increasingly vital components of many 
competing technologies such autonomous vehicles, robotics, environmental surveying, 
and surveillance. These systems are able to generate high-resolution 3D environmental 
maps in almost real-time, and this has unlocked huge leaps and bounds in the area of 
perception and navigation. Despite the widespread and authoritative public understanding 
that LiDAR systems perform all the same tasks in essentially the same way and to the 
same extent, there are many different ways that LiDAR systems work, and each design 
has its own strengths and weaknesses. 
 
The most ubiquitous and conventional kind of LiDAR system is called Time-of-Flight (ToF) 
LiDAR or ranging LiDAR. A ToF LiDAR emits pulses of laser over short distances, records 
their travel time to a given target, and reflects a beam back to the source. The distance 
from the source to the target is measured by sort of doubling the round trip time. ToF LiDAR 
is a conceptually simple device with potentially good accuracy in distance measurements. 
Like all LiDAR systems, ToF has certain limitations: the system's range resolution is 
fundamentally linked to the temporal resolution of the system and the sensitivity of the 
detector, the performance of the instrument may degrade in bright light or when other laser 
lights interfere [1]. 
 
Figure 1: The basic working principle of the dToF [1]. 
 
Another typical method of implementation involves beam steering through MEMS mirrors—
small microelectromechanical (MEMS) systems that typically enable laser beams to be 
scanned across a scene. Overall, MEMS-based LiDAR is a strong combination of 
resolution, field-of-view, and compactness, enabling bimodal application development 
toward smaller platforms. For example, autonomous vehicles and drones, etc. Several 
MEMS issues remain, including mechanical stability, angular range, environment durability, 
and lifespan, especially for harsh environments [2].

Figure 2: (a) A flash LiDAR with diffused light; (b) The principle of an optical phased array 
(OPA) scanner; (c) A LiDAR motorized spinning scanner; (d) A microelectronechanical 
systems (MEMS) laser scanner [2]. 
Solving those issues and building on the well proven and established engineering 
fundamental, solid-state LiDAR solutions emerged, predominantly with FMCW approaches. 
FMCW LiDAR works by transmitting a laser, which is then frequency modulated linearly 
over time. When the transmitted light reflects off a target and returns to the receiver, it is 
coherently mixed with a portion of the original signal. The frequency difference (beat 
frequency) relates to the time delay, and thus distance measure to that object. Importantly, 
FMCW can also provide Doppler-based velocity measures, something not possible with 
traditional ToF without more extensive circuitry variances [3]. 
 
Some of the key advantages of FMCW LiDAR are: 
• 
Improves sensitivity through coherent detection and performs well in a low-SNR 
environment 
• 
Simultaneous range and velocity detection using the same optical signal 
• 
Better eye safety (lower peak powers) than pulsed systems 
• 
Better performance in light-based environments (robustness to ambient light and 
interference can be a benefit for outdoor deployments) 
• 
Potential for complete photonic integration (possible to deliver compact, low-cost, 
potential scalable solutions) 
• 
 
This thesis characterizes the transmitter section of a new InP-based integrated FMCW 
LiDAR PIC; a system that could take fully advantage of each of these strengths. The PIC 
consists of a ring-resonator-based tuneable laser, electro-optic modulators, semiconductor 
optical amplifiers, and frequency discriminator circuitry, all monolithically integrated 
together to produce a cohesive system that is both high-performance and compact. The 
system can also accommodate OPA, allowing for solid state beam steering without 
mechanical movement and increasing potential for future integrated systems. 
 
The characterization and optimization of FMCW modulation capabilities of this chip are 
expected to validate its performance based on chirp linearity, tuning sensitivity and distance 
accuracy; ultimately providing the evidence needed to demonstrate a fully integrated solid-
state LiDAR system suitable for use in real time dynamic environments.

1.1. 
Initial Project Objectives 
Before the starting of this thesis, the project was defined with a broad and ambitious scope, 
aiming to explore and validate a full solid-state LiDAR system based on integrated 
photonics. The intention was to experimentally characterize both the transmitter and 
receiver part together of the FMCW laser sections of a novel InP-based PIC and OPA to 
improve the FMCW laser results, integrating all components required for a compact, high-
resolution, and fast-scanning LiDAR platform. 
 
As detailed in the original project description, the main objectives initially included: 
• 
Characterization and precise control of the FMCW laser 
Realization of a feedback and modulation system for linear frequency chirp generation, as 
well as investigating parameters such as wavelength tuning range, chirp linearity, and 
optical output power. The coupling between the SOAs and EOPMs was also to be 
optimized so that the tuneability and stability of the laser could be improved. 
 
• 
System-level integration and testing 
Validation of the fully integrated FMCW LiDAR system through extensive performance 
evaluation with regards to key performance fields such as distance measurement accuracy, 
scanning performance and system robustness in different environmental conditions. 
 
• 
Integration and characterization of the Optical Phased Array  
Examination and analysis of the angular resolution, beam divergence, and overall steering 
range of the OPA while ensuring that the optical power was consistently and uniformly 
distributed across the elements of the array to avoid distortion. Another key performance 
focus was to develop and implement to the degree possible dynamic phase modulation to 
facilitate real-time solid-state beam steering without any reliance on mechanical systems. 
 
• 
Development of a robust electronic control system 
This involved coordinating the performance of the FMCW laser and the OPA in real-time 
while facilitating high bandwidth phase modulation to ensure effective and reliable optical 
scanning. 
 
Nevertheless, as the project proceeded, it quickly became apparent that some objectives—
namely, those focused on the OPA and full system integration—were not feasible due to 
limited time and equipment. As a result, the thesis primarily focused on experimentally 
testing and validating the transmitter section, especially the characterization of the FMCW 
laser, performance of the SOA, frequency discrimination using the AMZI, and the electronic 
interface through a custom-made PCB. 
 
These changes are further detailed in Section 1.2 (Deviations), which outlines the changes 
made to the original plan and the rationale behind them.

1.2. 
Project Deviations and Final Objectives 
Even though the original proposal for this thesis was a far-reaching, ambitious, and 
comprehensive experimental program, over the course of the project several relevant 
divergences resulted in defining the boundaries and aims of the project relative to the 
original proposal. The majority of these divergences emerged from the constraints of time, 
technical limitations, and practical challenges of operating with a new and untested PIC.  
 
First and foremost, a significant loss of time existed in the beginning of the project. Even 
though the thesis formally began on February 1, a loss of more than one and a half months 
was attributed to limited access to the laboratory as well as significant preparatory actions 
to interface with the chip reliably and safely. As described in section 3.1 and summarized 
in the project Work Plan, the preparatory steps to begin electrical and optical tests included 
the entire design and manufacture of a bespoke PCB, preparation for wire bonding, and 
alignment of the equipment. 
 
At the same time, the original goals were somewhat over-ambitious, really comprising two 
independent master's thesis, one for full characterization of the FMCW transmitter and 
another for the OPA characterization. To fully develop and assess of both in the same 
period was unrealistic, particularly because the PIC design was entirely new and thus 
invalidated. In fact, characterizing a stand-alone new PIC with numerous tuneable and 
active devices would be a thesis topic on its own, especially with the real possibility of 
unforeseen complications in fabrication, bonding, alignment or measurement. 
 
Another large difference arose in no access to a dual input/output IO port test setup in the 
lab. Originally, the transmitter and receiver sections of the PIC were meant to be tested 
together, requiring dual-port access simultaneously. Unfortunately, equipment constraints 
and time allowed no opportunity to build the required infrastructure to create the necessary 
dual port optical alignment and coupling. Therefore, it was decided that only transmitter 
characterization would be attempted, using a single IO port for all tests. 
 
As exhibited by the timestamp and the increasingly concerning technical challenges above, 
it was agreed upon that the best option would be to halt work on a custom OPA and instead 
work with an OPA that was being developed already at DTU. This allowed the optimal use 
of time and effort on the laser characterization and generation of FMCW signals, which 
were more achievable within the existing laboratory set up. For the combination of the 
above issues, there was no two-port optical testing setup, meaning it was impossible to 
also test the receiver section of the PIC with its own on-chip laser transmitter. Therefore, 
systematic integration of the two systems, together with a practical assessment of the laser 
characterisation (i.e., performance of the laser in a free-space use case with varying 
environmental conditions), could not be demonstrated within the extent of the thesis work. 
 
The overall goal of this thesis was updated to more simply contain the experimental 
characterization of the transmitter section of a new InP PIC designed for FMCW LiDAR 
applications. The experimental characterization of the chip-level light sources including the

2. 
State of the art  
This chapter presents a detailed overview of the technological foundation underlying the 
chip characterized in this thesis. We begin with a review of the background concepts and 
recent advances in FMCW LiDAR systems and OPA, focusing on key integrated 
technologies such as tuneable ring-resonator-based lasers with Vernier effect, frequency 
discriminators based on asymmetric Mach–Zehnder interferometers (AMZI), and mode 
selection through electro-optic phase control, which together define the transmitter 
functionality of the PIC developed by the research group. In parallel, OPAs rely on arrays 
of optical emitters with tuneable phase shifters, enabling high-speed, non-mechanical 
beam steering through constructive interference in free space. These technologies 
together aim to support fully integrated and reconfigurable LiDAR systems with compact 
and precise optical control. 
In the second part of this chapter, we describe the architecture and functional components 
of the specific PIC used in this work, which was designed for integrated FMCW signal 
generation and stabilization. Particular emphasis is placed on the transmitter section, which 
was the main portion experimentally tested and characterized in this thesis. 
2.1. 
Technology Background 
In the context of photonic integration for advanced optical sensing systems, many laser 
frameworks and frequency stabilization solutions have been developed for coherent 
detection and high-resolution ranging applications such as FMCW LiDAR. In particular, 
integration platforms that use InP have significant relevance because they are able to 
monolithically integrate active and passive components like tuneable lasers, modulators, 
photodetectors, and amplifiers onto a single chip. This high degree of integration is 
advantageous for FMCW systems where compactness, fast modulation bandwidth, and 
low phase noise are a requirement for each of these components. The importance of the 
OPA and its technology background is mentioned below in section 2.1.5. 
 
The PIC analyzed in this thesis was designed for FMCW LiDAR operation and was 
fabricated utilizing an InP based platform. Its laser section has not been developed based 
upon SG-DBR (Sampled Grating Distributed Bragg Reflector) technology as is done with 
many of the conventional tuneable sources. Instead, the PIC uses a ring-resonator based 
laser with wavelength tuning being accomplished through a dual micro-ring Vernier 
approach. This approach enables precise control of the lasing wavelength and allows for 
reliable single-mode operation over a broad spectral region while maintaining an ultra-
compact on-chip footprint. The operational principles and design of this architecture are 
explained in [4]. 
 
To support FMCW operation, the PIC integrates an EOPM inside the laser cavity, enabling 
high-speed frequency chirp modulation. The PIC also has an AMZI that performs frequency 
discrimination, where frequency deviation causes amplitude changes that can be 
harnessed for feedback to narrow linewidth, to monitor chirp in real time, or to monitor 
linearity. 
 
==PAGE:17==

To summarize, the transmitter architecture of the PIC architecture draws together several 
enabling technologies, InP as the integration platform, ring-based Vernier tuning, EOPM-
based chirp modulation, and AMZI-based frequency discrimination, to deliver a compact 
and tuneable source for integrated FMCW LiDAR applications that we are hopefully 
augmenting with OPA benefits. With the next sections we will describe their respective 
technologies in further detail, citing some of the most applicable research work that have 
contributed to their development and use. 
2.1.1. Material Platform: Indium Phosphide (InP) 
The PIC evaluated in this dissertation is based on the InP platform, which is a highly utilized 
III–V semiconductor material system that allows the monolithic integration of active and 
passive photonic components. Compared to silicon photonics, which typically requires 
external integration of active devices such as a laser or optical amplifiers, InP allows the 
on-chip integration of laser gain sections, modulators, photodetectors, and SOAs directly. 
This feature is especially beneficial for coherent systems such as FMCW LiDAR. 
InP can operate natively in the telecommunications C-band (approximately 1550 nm) and 
has very high electro-optic efficiency making it desirable for integrated tuneable lasers and 
high-speed modulation. Furthermore, due to its direct bandgap feature, it can generate and 
amplify light in an efficient manner, which is beneficial to reducing overall system 
complexity and improving the optical signal quality in a compact integrated design. 
Furthermore, InP will allow for electrically integrating electro-optical mirrors with 
bandwidths suited to GHz-range modulation, as required for FMCW applications. InP is 
also compatible with tuneable cavity designs like dual-ring Vernier filters which can operate 
in a narrowband, single-mode fashion while providing maximum wavelength tuneability. 
This maximizes the channels of spectral agility, while maintaining the integration density 
and stability of InP devices. Thus, InP stands out as a material system for performance in 
an advanced, compact multichannel integrated optical transmitter that is radiated with low 
phase noise. 
 
Recent demonstrators showed that monolithically integrated InP lasers could achieve an 
improved wavelength control through electro-optic tuning and frequency stabilization 
techniques [5]. These capabilities also bolster InP's credentials as a platform to develop 
integrated coherent transmitters for sensing systems like the one outlined in this thesis. 
2.1.2. Ring Resonator Laser 
The compatible laser source in the photonic integrated circuit analyzed in this thesis, is 
ultimately based on a ring-resonator laser cavity design. This is a small-form-factor, 
tuneable design that has gained the most attention as the result of significant 
advancements over the last decade, and it is compatible with photonic integrated circuit 
platforms, such as InP. Ring resonators offer an alternative approach to wavelength 
selection as compared to more traditional designs of lasers utilizing distributed gratings 
(i.e., SG-DBR and DFB lasers), while also utilizing micro-ring resonators to provide 
wavelength-selective feedback to the gain medium. 
 
In the designed architecture, a cavity containing two micro-ring reflectors with slightly 
different radii is arranged in a Vernier configuration. This configuration allows for 
==PAGE:18==
             
 
 
 16
wavelength selection based on the Vernier effect, explained in more detail in section 2.1.3. 
In addition to offering single mode operation across a larger spectral range, the Vernier 
effect achieves a small footprint and fabrication simplicity. 
 
A significant advantage of this ring-based design is that it is amenable to monolithic 
integration, enabling active parts (gain section, modulators) and passive parts (rings, 
couplers) to be combined together on one InP die. Also, ring resonator lasers generally 
have narrow linewidth and also enable fast modulation, making them ideal for coherent 
applications such as FMCW LiDAR. 
 
In this design, the cavity includes a Semiconductor Optical Amplifier as the medium for gain 
and an Electro-Optic Phase Modulator for the high-speed frequency chirp modulation. The 
dual-ring Vernier reflector system provides feedback and wavelength selection as noted in 
[4]. 
2.1.3. Vernier Effect  
The Vernier effect is an established and widely used optical filtering principle that enables 
very fine-wavelength selection and broad tuneability by coupling two optical resonators that 
have slightly different free spectral ranges (FSRs). This method has been widely adopted 
in integrated photonics to achieve single-mode operation and wavelength precision, and it 
has been used in compact laser configurations that are particularly relevant to ring-
resonator-based lasers. 
 
In the proposed PIC of this thesis, the Vernier effect is achieved using two micro-ring 
resonators with slightly different radii, which are incorporated into the laser cavity. Each 
ring independently supports its own set of wavelengths for resonance based on its optical 
path length. When the resonance conditions of the two rings overlap at a specific 
wavelength, that wavelength is selected for lasing due to constructive interference. By 
changing the refractive index of each ring (using thermal or electro-optic phase modulation 
methods), it is possible to shift the overlap point, thus giving both coarse and fine control 
over the laser wavelength. 
 
The Vernier effect's primary benefit is the capacity to achieve broad tuning ranges with 
narrow mode spacing and keep single-mode operation. For example, in FMCW LiDAR 
systems, the laser frequency must be tuned continuously across several nanometers while 
maintaining high spectral purity. Although the dual-ring Vernier allows the integration 
footprint of the tuning components to be minimized, it also contributes to side-mode 
suppression by forcing only one mode of resonance to be dominant. 
 
Compared to sampled-grating based designs, choosing to design with a Vernier-based ring 
resonator allows for faster tuning, better thermal management, and simplicity of fabrication; 
this allows for seamless integration with monolithic InP elements. Vernier formation is a 
crucial component of this PIC's laser design, and it helps with both wavelength agility and 
stability within the laser's on-chip cavity [6].

2.1.4. Frequency Discriminator 
The Asymmetric Mach-Zehnder Interferometer is a common structure in integrated 
photonics for filtering, sensing, and frequency discrimination. For frequency discrimination 
in FMCW LiDAR, or coherent laser stabilization, the AMZI can be thought of as a frequency 
discriminator, which is a passive optical component that takes frequency changes in the 
input light (typically caused by laser frequency modulation) and converts them into 
amplitude changes that are detectable. 
 
The structure of the AMZI consists of two optical paths, of unequal length, that interfere at 
the output. The difference in length creates a wavelength-dependent phase shift between 
the two arms, yielding an output transmission spectrum that is periodic with a FSR. The 
variable pattern at the output depends on the input frequency change, which gives a 
sinusoidal relationship for input frequency variation within the FSR. Hence, the AMZI allows 
frequency changes to be converted to amplitude changes. 
This is useful for examining chirp linearity in FMCW systems. The laser can be operated 
around the quadrature point of the AMZI's response (the laser frequency position where 
the amplitude-to-frequency slope is maximized) so that small frequency deviations can be 
converted into a change of amplitude in a proportional amount. Common practices are to 
use balanced photodetector configurations (two detectors at the output of the AMZI that 
indicate which output has the greater intensity), use the subtraction of the signals from 
each of the two detectors to suppress common mode noise, and to enhance sensitivity of 
frequency modulation. 
 
The potential of AMZI-based frequency discriminators for photonic integration has been 
widely demonstrated in recent literature [7, 8]. For example, in [7], the AMZI is used to 
monitor laser chirp behaviour and support amplitude and phase error correction in MZI-
based interferometric systems. 
2.1.5. OPA Technology 
Optical Phased Arrays are a critical enabling technology for compact, high-speed, and non-
mechanical beam steering in integrated photonics applications. Similar to RF technologies, 
OPAs rely on phase to control the steerable angle by adding emission from sources at the 
far field in a constructive interference. OPA's are critical for next-generation solid state 
LiDAR systems due to their speed, size, and reliability advantages over mechanical 
approach. 
 
The OPA system used in this case study was first developed through the Photonics 
Engineering Department of the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), then externally 
integrated with the FMCW transmitter that was characterized throughout this thesis. This 
OPA used an array of linear thermo-optic phase shifters that modulate the relative phase 
between output waveguides, enabling precise beam steering in the vertical plane. 
Horizontal steering is supported implicitly through wavelength tuning, due to the dispersive 
property of the slab grating structure integrated to the array. 
The DTU OPA is based on a grating-assisted slab coupler design, following a waveguide 
tapering and emitter spacing optimization strategy to minimize grating lobes and achieve a 
==PAGE:20==
             
 
 
 18
high-quality beam. A Gaussian Amplitude Distribution (GAD) is applied across the emitters, 
reducing side lobe levels to below –19 dB. This means that both lateral and vertical beam 
steering can be accomplished using phase modulation (for the lateral direction) and a 
wavelength change (for the vertical tilt), enabling full spatial coverage in principle. 
 
This OPA design fits very well with the modern integrated FMCW LiDAR system's 
requirements, as it allows for high-resolution beam steering with no moving parts. In the 
wider system studied in this thesis, the OPA is crucial in providing a fully integrated, solid-
state transmitter for high-speed and high-precision dynamic beam control. While the thesis 
did not approach full electro-optic beam steering directly because of experimental 
limitations (dynamic phase control unavailable, and the low frame rate of the camera), static 
beam steering was demonstrated via laser wavelength tuning. 
2.2. 
FMCW LIDAR PIC 
The Photonic Integrated Circuit characterized in this work was developed as part of the 
ACTPHAST framework, using an Indium Phosphide platform provided by SMART 
Photonics. It is designed by Hao Hu and Mu-Chieh Lo as a prototype for a fully integrated 
Frequency-Modulated Continuous-Wave LiDAR transceiver, combining both transmitter 
and receiver functionalities on a single monolithic chip, whose measures are 4.6 mm × 
2.075 mm [10]. 
 
Figure 5: Microscope phot of the PIC 2A. 
 
The main purpose of the chip is to serve as a versatile testbed for evaluating integrated 
FMCW transmission technologies, particularly those enabling high linearity chirp 
generation, narrow linewidth operation, and compact optical routing. The PIC includes all 
essential components for a coherent LiDAR transmitter: a ring-resonator-based tuneable 
laser cavity using a Vernier configuration, electro-optic phase modulation, an AMZI-based 
frequency discriminator, SOAs for optical gain, and a complex network of MMI-based 
couplers and optical I/O ports. 
The chip layout was carefully designed to allow isolated testing of subsystems, using both 
on-chip tap couplers and multiple input/output ports. In total, the chip includes 8 optical IO 
ports, a variety of MMIs with different splitting ratios (50:50, 85:15), and several 
==PAGE:21==

configurable electrical bias pads for tuning the laser, modulators, SOAs, and other photonic 
elements. 
Due to limitations in the testing environment and scope of the thesis, only the transmitter 
section has been characterized. Therefore, attenuators, balanced photodetectors (BPD), 
and the receiver architecture are not discussed in detail here and were not part of the 
experimental evaluation. 
In total, six design variants of the chip were fabricated, each incorporating specific 
modifications to key components in order to explore performance trade-offs. Three of these 
variants focus on the transmitter only. The first design employs an 85:15 MMI splitter 
between the laser output and the frequency discriminator and uses micro-ring reflectors 
with slightly mismatched radii of 200 µm and 201 µm to implement Vernier filtering, the 
characterized design studied. The second variant changes the splitter ratio to 50:50 while 
keeping the same ring radii, and the third uses a 50:50 splitter combined with ring radii of 
200 µm and 200.5 µm. These variations were introduced to assess the impact of splitting 
power between outputs and monitoring paths, as well as to fine-tune the spectral overlap 
conditions of the Vernier effect. 
 
The remaining three variants mirror these same configurations but include an additional 
semiconductor optical amplifier in the receiver section to boost detected signals. Since the 
focus of this thesis is exclusively on the transmitter characterization, these extra SOAs, as 
well as the entire receiver chain, were not studied in this work.

2.2.1. Transmitter Functional Blocks 
At the core of the transmitter is a tuneable ring-resonator-based laser cavity, monolithically 
integrated with a single gain section, a cavity phase section, and two micro-ring reflectors 
with slightly different radii. This asymmetry enables the Vernier effect, allowing the laser to 
operate in single mode over a broad tuning range by overlapping the resonance conditions 
of both rings. The reflectors are independently tuneable via an electrical bias, providing the 
coarse wavelength control, while the cavity phase section is being reverse-biased and 
provides high-speed electro-optic modulation to produce frequency chirps. The gain 
section is being forward-biased to produce the optical amplification necessary for continued 
==PAGE:22==
             
 
 
 20
lasing. The tight integration described above allows for a combination of precision spectral 
tuneability and rapid frequency modulation - both critical for the laser’s FMCW operation. 
 
Signal routing in the PIC is performed using a system of Multi-Mode Interference (MMI) 
couplers (1×2, 2×2) using a number of MMI’s with different splitting ratios (50:50 symmetric 
ratio and 85:15 for asymmetrical power-tap), Internal monitoring of the signal in the unit 
gives engineer the ability to run without significant losses on the main output. They are 
strategically located to take some of the laser output for chirp tracking, to recombine signals 
for heterodyne or reflection tests, or to direct optical power to either output ports or the 
frequency discriminator. 
 
The chip has eight optical input/output ports, labelled IO1 through IO8, located at top and 
bottom edges of the chip. These serve as the external interfaces necessary for fiber 
coupling and offer direct access to key nodes in the transmitter path (the laser output, AMZI 
input or internal taps). Some IO ports also allow for alternate uses, such as injecting an 
external laser into the frequency discriminator, or self-heterodyne measurements. With the 
reconfigurable MMI structure, it is feasible to conduct parallel tests of different subsystems 
without invasive re-routing. 
 
To boost the output power when needed, the chip integrates two semiconductor optical 
amplifiers. SOA_2 is located downstream of the laser and can be used to amplify the 
FMCW signal before it reaches the output. The activation of SOA_2 depends on the 
required output level and is controlled via an independent electrical pad. During some 
experiments. On the other hand, SOA_1 is positioned to boost the internal signal to 
recombine it with the received signal in the receiver; however, it was not tested in this work 
and remains outside the scope of the present thesis. 
 
The frequency discriminator employed in the chip is a monolithic Asymmetric Mach-
Zehnder Interferometer. The Asymmetric Mach-Zehnder Interferometer's diffractive design 
introduces a path length difference of ~2.72 mm, resulting in a free spectral range of ~30 
GHz. When the laser frequency changes, the AMZI converts these changes into an 
intensity modulation on the two outputs. The outputs are connected to two on-chip PIN 
photodiodes (PIN1 and PIN2), and the differential photodiode current acts as an electrical 
signal that is proportional to the instantaneous optical frequency. This produces tracking in 
real-time changes in frequency, assessing the linearity in each laser transition, and with an 
external controller, the ability to use as closed-loop feedback stabilization may be possible. 
The AMZI can be driven by either the internal laser or an external optical source injected 
via IO port 1 giving an incredible number of testing configurations. 
Although the chip includes additional components such as variable optical attenuators, 
balanced photodiodes beyond the AMZI, and a full receiver section with phase modulators 
and filters, none of these elements were characterized in this thesis. These subsystems 
were electrically isolated during the experiments and are reserved for future work.

2.2.2. Electrical Pad Interface  
Every active and tuneable component of the PIC has dedicated electrical pads located on 
the outer edges of the chip. These pads give the flexibility to control the operation 
parameters and are necessary for accurate characterization. The ring-resonator based 
laser has separate pads for the gain section, the cavity phase modulator, and two micro-
ring reflectors. The gain section is forward-biased using a DC current source for optical 
amplification, while the ring reflectors and cavity phase are usually reverse-biased with 
tuneable voltage sources to tune the resonance, and phase delay in the cavity. This allows 
both spectral tuning through the Vernier effect, and high-speed modulation to produce 
FMCW chirps. 
 
For example, SOA_2 can be turned on/off through its own bias pad; that is, this allows for 
SOA_2 to have less effect on the overall system performance, depending on the 
configuration desired. The PIN photodiodes at the AMZI outputs are also connected to 
electrical pads that allow for reverse-biased and also take out the photocurrents generated 
by the PD outputs; often the photocurrents will go to a differential amplifier or an 
oscilloscope. To measure the frequency discriminator’s output by subtracting PIN1 and 
PIN2 externally, a clean voltage representation of the optical chirp is obtained. 
 
The chip layout includes proper electrical isolation and contact labelling to facilitate probing. 
The characterization bench connects using RF probes or bonded wire leads to pads, 
allowing low-noise, and stable operation during testing.

3.1. 
Wire Bonding and PCB Design 
Prior to performing any electrical or optical characterization of the InP-based photonic 
integrated circuit, a reliable access method to the electrical connections on the chip was 
needed. The PIC bond pads were very small (100×100 µm) and spaced so closely that 
conventional laboratory probing would fail to connect effectively to the pads. Consequently, 
I decided to design a custom Printed Circuit Board (PCB) to act as an intermediate access 
platform between the PIC and laboratory instrumentation for the purpose of 
characterization. The PCB would allow access to all functional blocks of the chip and would 
provide stable electrical connections that were repeatable for the testing and 
characterization. 
 
The PCB design was developed using KiCad, an open-source PCB CAD software program 
that allowed me the flexibility to design the PCB layout for my application geometry based 
on the bonding pads geometry and electrical requirements. Each connection to the chip, 
whether it be for DC bias, signal input or photodetector readout, was routed to contact 
locations on the PCB that I could safely and systematically operate during experimentation. 
 
At this point, the board delivers stable, independent biasing and signal readout for the all 
functional blocks of the PIC, including ring-resonator-based laser (gain section, cavity 
phase, micro-ring reflectors), SOA_1, SOA_2, electro-optic phase modulators coexistent 
within the tuneable couplers and AMZI, and incoming outputs of the integrated PIN 
photodiodes. For each of these connections, dedicated bonding pads were designed on 
the PCB matching the PIC pad layout from Section 2.2.2, with a bonding pad size of 
150 × 400 µm, and with bonding pads spaced 170 µm apart. 
 
To connect the board to the external instruments, two connector types were used. For the 
low-noise signals lines such as those associated with the laser gain, SOAs, coupler EOPMs, 
PIN1, and PIN2, I selected RS PRO 50 Ω coaxial BNC connectors [11] which ensure good 
connections against vibrations while maintaining signal integrity. For the control signals 
such as cavity phase, micro-ring reflectors, and AMZI modulators, I used a row of 2.54 mm 
pitch straight pin headers, covering multiple horizontal menu selections for compact access 
to the bias and GND lines.

PIN Testing 
After verifying the basic function of the gain section and the SOAs, the next step shifted to 
evaluating the emissions of light and coupling of light from the integrated laser into the on-

apparent, where one photodiode had a larger response (constructive interference), while 
the other had a minimum response (destructive interference). This complementary 
interaction also confirms that the AMZI interferometer structure functioned correctly, along 
with the optical delivery path from the laser to the frequency discriminator.

for estimating the effective optical power available at the output ports of the chip.

4. 
RF Characterization of FMCW Laser  
This chapter introduces the dynamic characterization of the PIC transmitter under high-
frequency modulation. After verifying that everything achieved the appropriate DC 
performance, we modulated the laser cavity phase using triangular waveforms to create 
the frequency chirps needed to operate the FMCW LiDAR. We then assessed the linearity 
of the generated chirp and carried out full FMCW measurements to extract important 
system metrics, tuning sensitivity, chirp range, chirp rate, and range resolution. Moreover, 
predistortion techniques were applied to improve chirp linearity by digitally correcting the 
driving waveform. 
4.1. 
Linearity Characterization without Predistortion 
In this section, we characterize the linearity of the frequency chirp generated by the on-
chip laser when modulated via the cavity phase section. Using the integrated frequency 
discriminator and its two PIN photodiodes, we monitor the electrical response to various 
triangular modulation waveforms. This provides a direct and efficient method to evaluate 
the laser’s chirp behaviour and optimize the driving conditions before performing full optical 
FMCW beat measurements. The setup used and the results obtained are described in the 
following subsections. 
4.1.1. Linearity Set Up 
To evaluate the linearity of the laser’s frequency chirp in response to the triangular 
modulation, a setup configuration was implemented, shown in Figure 23. This setup 
focuses on measuring the electrical output of the integrated frequency discriminator, by 
observing the complementary behaviour of the PIN1 and PIN2 photodiodes, and in 
particular the differential signal (PIN1 – PIN2), which provides a direct voltage 
representation of the laser’s instantaneous frequency.

all controlled through MATLAB (to synthesize and export the binary waveform uploaded to

4.1.2. Linearity Results 
To assess the degree of linearity of the frequency chirp, we filled the cavity phase section 
of the laser with a triangular modulation signal. Then, we looked at the differential output 
signal from the integrated frequency discriminator (PIN1 - PIN2) and placed that onto a 
two-channel oscilloscope to directly show the electrical representation of the chirp taking 
place in the differential output signal. Essentially, we were expecting the output to 
maintaina triangular shape similar to the triangular modulation signal, only to a lower 
amplitude and once deformed by other problems associated with modulating the response. 
By observing the differential signal in the various modulation cases, we could compare the 
fidelity of the frequency chirp and develop bounds for the best condition of high linearity. 
 
To conduct these tests under optimal conditions, we examined several of the lasers 
operating in various positions across the spectral range by varying the reverse bias to 
Ring 1 and Ring 2, which control the two resonance positions of the two-ring Vernier laser. 
This was used to find the stable point of single-mode lasing while avoiding mode-hopping 
during modulation.After scanning through multiple combinations, one of the optimal 
configuration was found at –0.24 V on Ring 1 and –5.10 V on Ring 2, which locked the laser 
in single-mode operation at 1558.00 nm, with an observed SMSR greater than 40 dB, as 
confirmed on the optical spectrum analyzer. 
 
Simultaneously, I needed to make sure that the frequency discriminator's bias point was 
appropriately matched so that there was a linear electrical response to the chirp 
modulation. To do this, I manually swept the voltages on EOPM1 and EOPM2 across their 
operating range while I observed PIN1 and PIN2's output on the oscilloscope. I wanted to 
find the condition of when PIN1 - PIN2 = 0 V when the laser is not modulating, and biasing 
the AMZI at its quadrature point. The final values that satisfied this condition were -2.23 V 
for EOPM1 and -0.10 V for EOPM2, and these would be fixed for all subsequent FMCW 
experiments.

4.2. 
FMCW Modulation without Predistortion 
The objective in this section is to assess the intrinsic linearity and stability of the frequency 
chirp generated by directly modulating the cavity phase section with clean triangular signals. 
Using both the frequency discriminator output and the self-heterodyne beat frequency 
method, key parameters such as chirp range, tuning sensitivity, chirp rate, and distance 
estimation accuracy were extracted under various modulation frequencies and amplitudes. 
4.2.1. Beating Set Up 
In order to evaluate the frequency modulation capabilities of the PIC and perform beat 
frequency measurements using the self-heterodyne method, an extended experimental 
setup was implemented, similar to [17], as illustrated in Figure 26. This configuration builds 
upon the setup previously used for linearity characterization, incorporating additional 
optical elements to enable full beat signal detection in the RF domain. These additions 
allow for the observation of the frequency difference between a directly modulated signal 
and a delayed replica, providing accurate insight into the chirp characteristics of the laser. 
 
Figure 28: FMCW LIDAR set up diagram of blocks. (Blue paths: electrical connections, 
black paths: optical connections). 
 
==PAGE:47==
             
 
 
 45
The core signal modulation is generated by the AWG (Keysight N8241A), which delivers a 
triangular waveform to the cavity phase section of the laser. The waveform parameters 
(Vpp, frequency, offset) are configured via MATLAB. Reverse and forward biases to the 
micro ring reflectors, SOAs, and EOPMs are applied using programmable power supplies 
and current sources, identical to the previous setup. 
 
The laser output is extracted via a designated IO port aligned at 23°, as per coupling 
optimization guidelines (see Section 3.5), and then coupled into an in-line Erbium-Doped 
Fiber Amplifier (EDFA). This fiber-based optical amplifier increases signal power to offset 
coupling losses, enabling there to be enough optical power for future analysis. Directly 
following the EDFA, the signal encounters a tuneable optical filter, adjustable from 1530 nm 
to 1580 nm. The filter serves to separate the lasing wavelength and reduce ASE (Amplified 
Spontaneous Emission) noise. 
 
The filtered signal is then split by a 10:90 optical coupler. 
• 
10% of the power is sent to an OSA for real time monitoring of both the lasing 
wavelength and SMSR, which can help confirm single-mode operation during the 
chirp testing. 
• 
90% of the optical signal goes to a 3 dB coupler where the optical signal splits into 
two paths. 
o One path includes a polarization controller that deals with polarization drift and 
maintains the visibility of interference. 
o The other path passes through a fiber delay line of length L (e.g., 125 m or 
250 m), which creates the temporal offset needed for the self-heterodyne beat 
measurement. 
The two arms are recombined in a second 3 dB coupler, and the interference signal is 
directed into a BPD. This component converts the optical beat into an electrical RF signal, 
which is then analysed with a RF spectrum analyser to extract information on the beat 
frequency, chirp rate, and tuning range. 
 
A key improvement in this setup is the addition of a calibrated power meter at the BPD 
input stage to ensure that the optical powers in both interferometer arms are balanced. This 
condition is important for attaining maximum beat visibility and the best signal-to-noise ratio 
at the RF output. 
 
At the same time, the electrical outputs of PIN1 and PIN2 from the frequency discriminator 
are still being recorded with a two-channel oscilloscope, allowing for an upto-date view of 
the chirp development. The differential signal (PIN1 - PIN2) provides an additional 
reference for the modulation quality and can be used to cross-check the same RF-domain 
measurements. 
 
To sum up, this improved setup allows for complete optical-electrical characterization of 
the frequency chirp and is the experimental basis for the analysis found in the next section.

4.2.2. FMCW LIDAR Characterization 
The self-heterodyne setup described in the previous section enables the extraction of key 
parameters from the modulated laser by analysing the beat signal resulting from two time-
shifted replicas of the same frequency-modulated optical wave. When a triangular 
waveform is applied to the cavity phase of the laser, it produces a linearly chirped optical 
signal—that is, the frequency of the laser increases (or decreases) linearly over time during 
each half-period of the triangular waveform. 
 
This signal is split into two optical paths. One follows a direct route to the detector 
(undelayed), and the other is delayed by passing through a fiber of length L, introducing a 
fixed time delay 𝜏. When these two signals are recombined, the frequency offset between 
them results in an RF beat signal 𝑓𝑏𝑒𝑎𝑡, observable in the spectrum analyzer as a single, 
sharp peak. 
 
The physical origin of this beat frequency lies in the interference between two optical 
signals with slightly different instantaneous frequencies. Because the triangular waveform 
applied to the laser cavity induces a time-varying frequency shift, the undelayed signal and 
its delayed counterpart are out of phase in time. When they interfere, their frequency 
difference translates into a beat note. This beat frequency is directly proportional to the 
slope of the chirp (i.e., the chirp rate) and the time delay between the two signals. Visually, 
this can be interpreted as the recombination of two triangular waveforms displaced in time 
(see Figure 27), with the vertical offset between their frequencies representing the beat 
frequency 𝑓𝑏𝑒𝑎𝑡 [18].

4.3. 
Linearity and FMCW Modulation with Predistortion 
Even though the triangular waveform used for cavity phase modulation gave a mostly linear 
frequency chirp, the nonlinearity of the laser tuning response also created distortions in the 
chirp from practical limitations (electro-optic nonlinearity and thermal). It was noted that the 
linearity of the PIN1 - PIN2 output in the frequency discriminator could be better. 
Furthermore, while a frequency peak shows distinctly in the RF spectrum analyser during 
beat signal measurement, the peak was not sharply defined and could still be further 
optimised to get a clearer, more isolated beat frequency peak. As a result, predistortion 
was implemented to attempt to negate the internal nonlinearities in the system to improve 
chirp linearity. 
 
==PAGE:54==
             
 
 
 52
The purpose of predistortion is to pre-shape the electrical driving signal, so that after its 
nonlinear response has passed through the system, the resultant optical frequency chirp 
will be passed through a linear response.This involves replacing the ideal triangular 
waveform with a custom waveform that “overcorrects” areas of known distortion [22]. 
The procedure was as follows: 
• 
First, the uncorrected PIN1 – PIN2 output was measured using a clean triangular input. 
• 
The output was analyzed and compared with the ideal output expected. A MATLAB- 
script was developed to reshape the waveform accordingly. In regions where the 
system exhibited weaker frequency response (e.g., flatter slopes or compressed 
regions), the algorithm increased the slope or curvature of the waveform to compensate. 
In contrast, regions that showed excessive sensitivity were compressed. 
• 
The predistorted waveform was exported in binary form and uploaded into the AWG to 
replace the previous triangular signal. The new waveform on the cavity phase electrode 
was measured for the PIN1 - PIN2 output. 
• 
Multiple iterations took place and at every iteration the linearity of the output waveform 
was analyzed. The measure of convergence was the root mean square (RMS) 
difference between the measured output and the ideal triangular reference. The 
waveform with the lowest RMS error was chosen as the optimized signal. The number 
of iterations could have continued but after a certain point there was a diminishing 
return and in certain cases the performance even got worse [23]. 
The MATLAB algorithm used for this iterative correction process of predistortion is included 
in the Appendix 2. 
 
In order to assess the effectiveness of predistortion, two modulation frequencies were 
chosen as a comparison case: 10 kHz (the most linear native modulation condition, shown 
in Section 4.1), and 50 kHz (where chirp had more evident non-linearities). After engaging 
the iterative correction procedure previously described, driving waveforms were optimized 
for each frequency that resulted in the most linear PIN1 - PIN2 output encountered in our 
investigation.

5. 
OPA Integration and Evaluation 
In the last phase of this thesis, the FMCW laser transmitter was experimentally tested with 
an external optical phased array. The assessment was made of the beam steering 
capability of the laser initiated by wavelength tuning, and further assessment of the 
integrated wavelength tuning and transmitter output with the OPA. The experiments 
included detailed optical alignment, far-field measurements, angular calibrations, and 
measurement of beam displacement, divergency and steering efficiency vs. laser 
wavelength. The experiments are intended to provide a first demonstration of the potential 
for complete integration into a compact solid-state FMCW LiDAR system. 
5.1. 
OPA Set Up 
For the integration and preliminary evaluation of the OPA, a setup based on the previously 
described DC testing configuration was employed. The output signal for the OPA 
characterization was extracted from IO Port 2 of the PIC transmitter. The output fiber was 
aligned at 23° to the chip facet, based on the optimized coupling angle determined from 
the previous characterization stage. 
 
The optical signal was sent through an Erbium-Doped Fiber Amplifier (EDFA) to 
compensate for both coupling and insertion losses and was filtered through a tuneable 
optical filter to select only the lasing wavelength and reduce ASE noise. The filtered optical 
signal was then coupled to the input facet of the external OPA device. 
 
To facilitate precise optical alignment, the OPA chip was mounted on a probe station with 
high precision stages capable of sub-micron alignment accuracy, to optimize the coupling 
efficiency between the PIC output and the OPA input waveguide. The OPA used in this 
experiment was a multi-element photonic phased array developed at DTU, controlled via 
an eight-channel Digital-to-Analog Converter (DAC) that independently adjusts the phase 
of each antenna element to steer the output beam. 
 
For beam capture and analysis, the emitted far-field pattern was collected using a Thorlabs

5.2. 
OPA Evaluation  
In this final experimental phase, the aim was to evaluate the beam steering behaviour of 
the integrated OPA when driven by different laser wavelengths provided by the ring-
resonator-based FMCW laser previously characterized. Due to the wavelength sensitivity 
of phased array systems, even small variations in the laser wavelength can induce 
noticeable shifts in the far-field beam direction, a principle well-known in wavelength beam 
steering techniques. 
 
The underlying physical principle is that, for a given optical phased array with a fixed phase 
delay distribution across its emitters, changing the laser wavelength modifies the phase 
progression along the array, resulting in a different constructive interference direction [26]. 
Specifically, longer wavelengths generally cause the beam to steer toward larger angles, 
while shorter wavelengths produce smaller steering angles. This dependency can be 
exploited by scanning the beam laterally without modifying the driving electronics of the 
OPA. 
 
In this case, by tuning the laser cavity through the adjustment of the micro-ring resonator 
voltages (as described in Section 3.10), we were able to selectively lock the laser at 
different wavelengths while maintaining stable single-mode operation. However, the usable 
wavelength tuning range was limited due to camera constraints: the specific OPA 
configuration used in this setup was only available with laser wavelengths between 1520 
and 1540 nm. Because of that, after careful tuning, only two stable laser wavelengths were 
selected for this study: 1531.38 nm and 1535.90 nm, both exhibiting high SMSR and stable 
output power.

5.3. 
OPA results 
From the calibrated far-field measurements, we observe that varying the laser wavelength 
induces clear and measurable steering of the output beam along the horizontal (θx) 
direction, fully consistent with the expected wavelength beam steering effect inherent to 
the OPA architecture. Specifically, switching between the two tested wavelengths of 
1531.38 nm and 1535.90 nm resulted in beam center shifts of approximately Δθx ≈ 0.316°, 
as shown in Figure 30 and Figure 31. 
 
Given the wavelength difference of Δλ = 1535.90 nm – 1531.38 nm = 4.52 nm, we can 
calculate the effective wavelength-to-angle tuning efficiency as:

This value indicates that, for each nanometer increase in laser wavelength, the far-field 
beam shifts horizontally by approximately 0.07°. This confirms the strong wavelength 
sensitivity of the OPA steering mechanism, even without any phase control applied to the 
OPA electrodes.

Along with the primary beam steering behaviour, the divergence of the beams was also 
assessed through the FWHM analysis. Looking at the horizontal axis, Δθx, the beam was 
0.093° wide at 1531.38 nm and 0.074° at 1535.90 nm. The vertical axis, Δθy, resulted in 
1.187° and 1.25° respectively. This shows that the beam is still relatively collimated 
horizontally, while the vertical divergence is getting larger as expected based on the OPA's 
physical geometry, and emitter array design. 
 
The successful demonstration of the passive beam steering effect supports the idea that 
the transmitter section of the PIC is likely compatible with external OPAs and also indicates 
potential for wavelength-steered FMCW LiDAR designs. With a relatively narrow FWHM in 
the θx direction, it is probable that high angular resolution could be achieved with the 
addition of active phase control. The measured tuning slope of around 0.07°/nm gives a 
quantitative reference for building future architectures that combine wavelength and phase 
steering. 
 
It should be emphasized that in this initial test of integration, the OPA was yet driven 
dynamically with full electronic phase modulation under FMCW operation. Additionally, we 
could not observe dynamic FMCW modulation ourselves in real-time chirping, given the 
slow frame speed of the CMOS camera (249 FPS). Nonetheless, we have clearly satisfied 
our initial goals for this integration, which confirmed that each wavelength tuning alone, 
provides predictable and stable beam steering, represents an important first step in 
anticipation of the full integration of the FMCW transmitter system upcoming with real-time 
OPA beam control.  
Moreover, in a theoretical case for future evaluations we need a camera that allows us to 
see twice the effective horizontal angular coverage of the overall system (±1.215°), as it 
only allows us to see 20 nm wide, and in our PIC we confirmed at least 40 nm tuneability, 
that is, we would require a camera with a minimum coverage of double (±2.43°), to capture 
the full width of the system.

Environment Impact  
As the development and realization of this thesis project has minimal direct environmental 
impact, as the work was experimental in nature, performed in a laboratory setting, and the 
PIC being investigated is produced with InP technology to test its performance to generate 
and modulate FMCW laser signals for the LiDAR applications I was investigating, the level 
of laboratory equipment required for the experiments, such as power supplies, optical 
spectrum analysers, and RF instrumentation, were all within normal usage power 
consumptions. 
 
From an indirect perspective, the work positively contributes to the outlook on the 
environment by providing support for solid-state LiDAR technologies to develop and 
possibly replace mechanical scanning systems that have higher energy usage, are less 
reliable, and are larger in footprint as integrated systems such as the one analysed in this 
project can provide greater efficiency in power usage, miniaturized system installation, and 
long-term stability which is a significant consideration in a sustainable future that 
encompasses fully autonomous vehicles, smart cities, and environmental monitoring. 
In addition, the monolithic integration of various laser sources, modulators, and 
photodetectors into one single chip reduces the waste of materials and wasteful use of 
discrete optical components. Being monolithic in design further reduces manufacturing 
costs and packaging needs, contributing towards a more sustainable production chains in 
photonic technologies. 
 
There was no direct use of hazardous materials neither chemical processing in this thesis 
work, and energy consumption was limited to the operation of the test equipment. 
Therefore, the environmental cost of the project is low, while the environmental benefits of 
the technology being considered here may result in energy-efficient and environmentally 
friendly photonic systems over the longer term.

7. 
Conclusions and Future Development 
This thesis has provided the complete experimental characterization of the transmitter of a 
new InP-based PIC developed for FMCW LiDAR systems. The work has been developed 
to fully validate the design, function, and performance of the transmitter section, including 
detailed DC testing, RF chirp characterization, predistortion optimization and first 
integration of the OPA. 
All active components within the PIC were systematically validated through initial DC 
characterization. The ring-resonator based laser showcased proper single-mode lasing 
behaviour, significantly wide tuneability exceeding 40 nm, exceeding SMSR of 40 dB, and 
achieved threshold currents that were anticipated. The integrated SOAs, PIN 
photodetectors, tuneable couplers and frequency discriminator were also successfully 
validated for the same reasons indicating they were also functionally operational and could 
be implemented with high-speed FMCW modulation systems. 
The RF characterization work confirmed that the cavity phase modulation could create 
linear frequency chirps that were suitable for FMCW operation. The laser tuning sensitivity 
was characterized at several modulation frequencies and amplitudes with maximum 
sensitivities reaching 1.51 GHz/V for 10 kHz triangular modulation. Using self-heterodyne 
setup LiDAR specific metrics were extracted including tuning sensitivity of 1.51 GHz/V, 
maximum chirp range of 1.51 GHz, chirp rate of 0.0302 GHz/μs, and range resolution of 
9.93 cm. 
Adding the predistortion algorithm, which corrects the nonlinearity of the frequency 
discriminator output, helped further improve both the linearity and sensitivity of the 
transmitter, especially with the greater modulation frequencies. It was evident that the 
iterative predistortion decreased the RMS deviations and offered substantiating evidence 
that this could be a major asset in defining future efforts in real-time electronic control 
applications. Overall, we improved our results to: 
• 
Tuning Sensitivity: 1.53 GHz/V 
• 
Maximum Chirp Range: 1.53 GHz 
• 
Chirp Rate: 0.0306 GHz/μs 
• 
Range Resolution: ~9.78 cm 
After this improvement, distance measurements were made by measuring the beat 
frequency with known fiber lengths. The distances calculated, were quite close to their real 
fiber lengths, with relative distance estimation errors below 5% in nearly all cases, and as 
small as 2.13% in the best conditions (10 kHz, 1 Vpp) with predistortion. These results were 
helpful in validating that the transmitter could conduct FMCW distance measurements in a 
fiber control format. 
 
The last component of the work was integrating the transmitter PIC with an external OPA 
to conduct preliminary beam steering experiments with an output beam. From those 
experiments, clear wavelength-dependent steering was demonstrated, with the tuning 
efficiency of 0.07°/nm, a horizontal steering range of ± 1.215°. The beam divergence was 
also measured, showing highly collimated emission in the horizontal direction with FWHM 
values as low as 0.074°; confirming that the potential of this combination could apply for 
the use cases of solid-state beam steering LiDAR applications. 
 
==PAGE:65==
             
 
 
 63
In summary, the designed PIC proved to be reliable and flexible as a transmitter platform 
for FMCW LiDAR, demonstrating tunable and linear optical chirps, and the first successful 
step towards integrating the OPA provides strong evidence for the potential capability of 
fully integrated FMCW LiDAR transmitters with the capability to beam steer and employ 
other optical structures.  
Through this work, it became clear that with adequate time and resources, the project could 
have advanced even further towards full system-level integration. The work presented in 
this thesis offers a strong first step, but there are a few important research directions to 
investigate further:  
• 
Full Transmitter - Receiver Characterization: Develop some coupling adapters 
or packaging to allow for the simultaneous alignment of multiple IO ports, which 
would enable end-to-end testing of both the transmitter and receiver chains directly 
on the same PIC.  
• 
Thermal Stability: Implement active temperature control during operation of the 
laser to minimize drift in wavelength while maintaining improved chirp stability. 
• 
Characterization of Other PIC Variants: Characterization other chip variants 
fabricated with different MMI splitting ratios and ring dimensions, to understand the 
performance trade-offs. 
• 
Free-Space Validation: Move beyond current fiber-based experiments to outdoor 
testing, validating distance measurement and angular steering performance under 
uncontrolled real-world environmental conditions. 
• 
Fully Integrated FMCW + OPA Testing: In an upgraded system configuration with 
enough CMOS frame rate, and phase shifter control, conduct full FMCW 
experiments with full integration to the OPA for observing real-time beam scanning 
simultaneously during chirp modulation. 
• 
Velocity Measurements: Modify the FMCW signal processing to calculate Doppler 
shifts for target velocity estimation, an essential feature for automotive and 
sophisticated LiDAR platforms. 
• 
Fully Integrated LiDAR System: In the end, put all elements (transmitter, receiver, 
OPA beam steering, and electronic control) together to create a fully monolithic 
compact FMCW LiDAR system capable of real-time 3D sensing and long-range 
detection. 
 
All of these future developments would allow for a complete and fully integrated FMCW 
LiDAR system showing both coherent detection and solid-state beam steering all on one 
monolithic platform. Thus, the results presented in this thesis form a significant step 
towards compact, scalable, and low-cost LiDAR technology for next generation sensing 
applications.
