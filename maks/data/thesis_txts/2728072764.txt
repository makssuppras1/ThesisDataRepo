Abstract
This Master’s thesis aims to develop a reliable evaluation method to assess the perfor-
mance of wind turbines in power generation. The study uses power curves from various
wind farms with similar conditions and turbine models to derive an average power curve
that accurately represents the collective performance of multiple turbines or an entire wind
farm. The methodology combines uncertainties in bin-wise power and incorporates a cor-
relation study to estimate the degree of correlation for each source of uncertainty in each
pair of turbines, to capture the overall power generation behavior across multiple wind
turbines. Moreover, the thesis focuses on identifying and detecting outliers in the power
curve to optimize operational strategies.
By considering correlations among sources of uncertainty, incorporating appropriate weight-
ing, and detecting possible outliers in bin-wise power curves, the methodology developed
provides a different approach to power performance for wind farm projects, as it allows
for the assessment of the consistency of the individual power curves and provides the
means to identify any underperformance in power in specific wind speed ranges.

Introduction
This chapter provides an overview of the structure of this M.Sc. thesis, including its mo-
tivation and objectives. Additionally, it introduces the key research questions that will be
addressed throughout the study.
1.1
Structure
This M.Sc. thesis follows a structured approach, starting with an introduction that high-
lights the research motivation. The following sections provide background information on
current issues and challenges in the field and explore the theory of uncertainty.
The thesis then discusses existing practices in calculating power curve uncertainties, ex-
amining relevant standards to identify gaps and limitations. The methodology section
presents a proposed approach that incorporates propagation techniques, correlation stud-
ies, weighting, and outlier detection to accurately capture the overall power generation
behavior of wind turbines.
The results of the research are then presented, including the application of the method-
ology to real-world data, addressing uncertainties in power output, wind speed, tempera-
ture, pressure, and relative humidity. The Discussion section explores the two methods
by contrasting them with the obtained results. The conclusion section summarizes the
key findings and contributions of the research, emphasizing the aim of providing a com-
prehensive evaluation method that improves current practices.
1.2
Motivation
Transitioning from fossil fuels to sustainable energy sources is one of the most signifi-
cant challenges of our era. In this transition, wind energy plays a key role, and its rapid
growth requires important financial investments. Therefore, a precise estimation of the
energy production of wind farms becomes even more crucial to ensure the success of
wind projects.
Meteorological models and wind measurements are used to assess the wind resource
at potential sites to ensure precise estimates of energy production. However, estimating
energy production only on the basis of the wind resource is not enough. It is crucial
to also consider the power performance of the turbines, which shows how effectively a
turbine can generate energy under different wind conditions. Therefore, accurate wind
measurements are necessary and very important for conducting a power performance
test.
Currently, new measurement methods allow remote wind measurements from a signifi-
cant distance, such as Lidars. However, power performance testing has historically been
heavily based on measurements from instruments mounted on meteorological masts.
To address this, the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) developed the IEC
61400-12-1 standard [1]. This standard provides a consistent and reliable approach to
testing individual Wind Turbine (WT) power performance. It ensures accuracy and repro-
ducibility in measuring and analyzing power performance, benefiting stakeholders such
as turbine manufacturers, purchasers, operators, planners, and regulators, who can now
effectively assess and meet power performance requirements.

To achieve an accurate performance evaluation of a wind farm, one possible approach
could involve analyzing the power curves of each individual wind turbine within the farm.
However, this method could be economically impractical. Instead, in this M.Sc. thesis,

Uncertainty Theory
To comprehend the methodology employed in this M.Sc. thesis, it is crucial to grasp
the concept of measurement uncertainties, their different types, and the techniques used
to combine them. Uncertainties refer to the lack of perfect knowledge or precision in
measurements and calculations. They can arise from various sources, such as instru-
mentation limitations, environmental conditions, or inherent variability in the system. This
chapter introduces the basic concepts related to this subject. The main document used
to write this chapter is the Evaluation of measurement data - Guide to the expression of
uncertainty in measurement (GUM) [4].
Measurement uncertainty and measurement errors are two distinct concepts that are of-
ten confused. Measurement uncertainty represents the bounds within which a value is
expected to lie, while measurement error refers to the difference between an indicated
value and the true value. Measurement errors can be evaluated and corrected, but this
process may not be exact, leading to uncertainty in the measurement [5].
Mistakes or blunders, on the other hand, are different from errors and cannot be quantified
or considered part of the measurement uncertainty. They are purely human errors that
cannot be calculated mathematically [5].
Furthermore, the term ”tolerance” is commonly misused. Tolerance typically refers to the
allowed range of values for a defined quantity, such as the acceptable minimum and max-
imum dimensions of a manufactured component. To ensure accurate measurements,
measurement uncertainty must be smaller than the tolerance, with a minimum recom-
mended ratio of 1 to 4. The term tolerance is also applied to measuring instruments,
indicating the acceptable range of values indicated by the instrument when tested by the
manufacturer. When dealing with tolerances in measurements, it is crucial to understand
their intended use and interpret them appropriately [5].
3.1
General Principles
The main objective of any measurement is to determine the value of the measurand, the
specific quantity subject to measurement. This process involves specifying the measur-
and, establishing a functional model for measurement, and following a related measure-
ment procedure. However, no measurement is perfect, and imperfections in the process
lead to errors in the final result. Thus, a measured value is only an approximation to the
true value of the measurand, and its accuracy is complete only when accompanied by a
statement of the uncertainty in that approximation.
Errors in measurement results arise from two main sources: random and systematic com-
ponents. Random errors are due to variations in repeated measurements and result in a
scatter of values around a mean value. These errors cannot be eliminated, but can be
reduced by increasing the number of observations. On the other hand, systematic errors
arise from effects not accounted for in the original measurand specification and introduce
a bias or offset between the presumed true value and the experimentally determined mean
value. Systematic errors can be corrected, but there may still be uncertainty in the applied
correction.
To evaluate measurement uncertainty, GUM classifies the uncertainty components into
two categories: ”Type A” and ”Type B”, which are presented later in section 3.4 [4]. This

3.4
Type A and Type B Uncertainties
When uncertainties are evaluated, they are commonly classified as Type A or Type B.
This classification relates primarily to the method employed to evaluate uncertainties.

On the one hand, Type A uncertainty evaluation involves a statistical calculation from a
series of repeated observations. The statistically estimated standard deviation of these
observations is referred to as a Type A standard uncertainty. This analysis involves ex-
amining the statistical variations, including scatter, within the collected data. The Guide
to the Expression of Uncertainty in Measurement (GUM) designates Type A uncertain-
ties using the symbol s [4]. Statistical methods, such as the calculation of the standard
deviation or standard error, are used to quantify the variability and provide a measure of
uncertainty.
On the other hand, Type B uncertainties encompass all other types of uncertainty. These
uncertainties may arise from various sources, such as calibration certificates, expert judg-
ment, or external information. Unlike Type A uncertainties, which can be statistically eval-
uated, Type B uncertainties typically involve subjective evaluations or rely on external
sources of information. Type B components are further distinguished by their estimated
standard deviations, which, after being weighted by influence coefficients, produce the
corresponding Type B standard uncertainties [5]. The Guide to the Expression of Uncer-
tainty in Measurement (GUM) represents Type B uncertainties using the symbol u.
By distinguishing between Type A and Type B uncertainties, it is possible to appropri-
ately address and account for different sources of uncertainty in the measurement and
analysis processes. This classification allows for a comprehensive evaluation of uncer-
tainties, considering both the statistical nature of repeated observations (Type A) and the
uncertainties that arise from various external factors (Type B).
The identified standard uncertainty components, whether evaluated through Type A or
Type B methods, come together to form the overall uncertainty value associated with the
measurement result. This overall uncertainty is called the combined standard uncertainty
and reflects the comprehensive estimate of uncertainty for the measurement.

Type B
Type B uncertainties cover all other types of uncertainties that have not been evaluated
through statistical analysis or repeated observations. Assessing these uncertainties often
requires relying on our engineering judgment and estimation skills.
It is crucial to recognize that uncertainty estimation is often more approximate than pre-
cise. Therefore, it is advisable not to excessively report the result with multiple decimal
places. Instead, it is important to identify, quantify, and eliminate uncertainty components
that are insignificant or have a minimal impact on the overall uncertainty analysis.
By identifying the most significant uncertainties, it is easy to focus the efforts on address-
ing and mitigating those aspects that have the greatest influence on the final results. This
process of identifying and prioritizing uncertainties is often the most valuable and infor-
mative part of the analysis.
Category B uncertainties, according to the IEC 61400-12-1 standard, relate to factors that
can disrupt the measurements or introduce variations in each input parameter [1]. This in-
cludes aspects such as instrument calibration, data processing methods, and challenging
environmental conditions.
To calculate category B uncertainties, it is necessary to understand the impact of each
input parameter, identifying the factors responsible for errors or variations. This requires
investigating how instruments are calibrated, analyzing how data is processed, and un-
derstanding how environmental conditions can affect the results.
Once the factors have been identified, the guide GUM provides guidelines to estimate
their impact on the overall type B uncertainty [4]. For instance, if instrument calibration
is a factor in an input parameter, it is possible to rely on the manufacturer’s specifica-

contributions are explained in more detail in section A.1 following the IEC standard [1].

been estimated, the methodology seen in section 3.6 can be applied. The final Category B
uncertainty outcome provides a reliable estimation of the accuracy of the measurements

3.6
Combination of Uncertainties
Uncertainties usually come from different sources, not just one. It is common to encounter
several factors that contribute to the overall uncertainty of a measurement. This can be
attributed to various factors, such as the presence of multiple parameters within the mea-
suring system or the different ways in which a single parameter can introduce uncertainty.
To illustrate this concept, let us examine the measurement of the length of a piece of string
as a simple scenario. In this case, there are several potential sources of uncertainty to
consider. First, the ruler used for measurement may have inherent limitations, including
absolute error, sensitivity to environmental factors, such as temperature and humidity, or
calibration restrictions. Furthermore, there is the possibility of uncertainty in accurately
reading the measurement from the ruler. Additionally, the string itself may introduce un-
certainty due to curling or bending, as well as potential irregularities or fraying at its ends.
This example illustrates how uncertainties can arise from different aspects of the mea-
surement process, which encompass the instrument, the observer, and the measured
object. Quantifying and comprehending these combined uncertainties requires a system-
atic approach that encompasses all pertinent sources and their potential contributions to
the overall uncertainty budget. When these combined uncertainties are addressed and
taken into account, a more comprehensive and accurate understanding of the measure-
ment results can be obtained.

uncertainties typically arise from various sources, such as literature values, manufacturer
specifications, or expert judgment, where the uncertainty range is estimated based on
available information rather than repeated measurements [7, 4].
When dealing with Type B uncertainties, there is no direct statistical relationship
that allows the calculation of correlations between individual uncertainties, as there
is no possible way to obtain the distribution, only information from the range can be ob-
tained. In the absence of specific statistical data, assuming correlations between Type
B uncertainties can be challenging and often subjective. However, there are scenarios
where expert judgment can suggest the presence of correlations between Type B un-
certainties, as this M.Sc. thesis suggests [7, 4]. Experience-based insight and general
knowledge is especially required when estimating the degree of correlation between input
quantities arising from the effects of common influences, such as ambient temperature,
barometric pressure, and humidity [4].
It is essential to recognize that when working with Type B uncertainties, especially when
using quadratic distributions, the assumption of independence between uncertainties is
often more straightforward and more commonly applied. This simplification avoids the
need for complex statistical modeling, especially when the correlations are difficult to es-
timate or lack direct statistical evidence [7, 4].
It is important to note that the correlation being considered in this M.Sc. thesis is the
correlation type B uncertainties, denoted as r(uxi, uxj). This terminology might cause

Taking into account the nature and magnitude of the correlations between the uncertain-

3.7
Summary of the Steps on Determining Uncertainties and
Method of Reporting Results
Estimation of uncertainty involves a series of essential steps [5]:
1. Identify influential factors: Enumerate all factors that could affect the measured val-
ues during testing.
2. Initial estimation: Make an initial guess for the uncertainty values associated with
each factor and establish upper limits for insignificant contributions.
3. Quantify uncertainty: Assess and estimate the values of significant uncertainty fac-
tors, expressing them consistently, such as in the same units or as percentages, at
the one standard deviation level.
4. Standard uncertainties: Convert the estimated values to standard uncertainties,
considering sensitivity coefficients if relevant.
5. Explore interdependence: Investigate whether any uncertainty components are in-
terconnected and identify any dominant influences.
6. Combine component uncertainties: Mathematically combine interdependent uncer-
tainty components, considering their combined effect (whether additive or oppos-
ing), to obtain a net standard uncertainty.
7. Calculate the overall uncertainty: Combine the standard uncertainties of the inde-
pendent components and any derived net components by taking the square root of
the sum of their squares, resulting in the overall combined standard uncertainty.
8. Consider the confidence level: To achieve a desired confidence level, apply a cov-
erage factor (k) to the combined standard uncertainty. Typically, a value of k = 2
is used, leading to a confidence level around 95%. Although in current practices to
measure power curves, k = 1 is used.
When stating test results, it is needed to determine the expanded uncertainty’s significant
figures, usually up to two, and round the expanded uncertainty to the next value of the
least significant figure. Then, it is needed to round the test result to the nearest least
significant digit. Good practice involves recording additional information, such as calcula-
tion methods, uncertainty components and evaluation documentation, to finally report the
measurement result with the expanded uncertainty chosen for the required confidence
level [9].

DS/EN IEC 61400-12-2 Annex F and G
4.2.1
AEP Overview
AEP stands for the total amount of energy that a wind turbine is expected to produce over a
year. It is a crucial metric that is used to evaluate and compare wind turbine performance.
The AEP is derived from the power curve of a wind turbine, which, as previously seen, is
a function of (Vi, Pi).
To calculate the AEP, the power curve is combined with the probability distribution of wind
speeds at a specific site in which the wind turbine is installed. To model the wind speed,
the Rayleigh distribution is commonly used as it provides a reasonable approximation of
the actual wind speed occurrences. The AEP is then estimated by integrating the power
output of the turbine over a range of wind speeds for a year, taking into account the
probability of each wind speed occurring.
It is important to note that AEP is a theoretical estimate and not an actual measurement.
It is calculated based on the assumption that the power curve, obtained from a short test
duration with specific wind and atmospheric conditions, can be extrapolated to represent
a theoretical energy estimation for a full year. The AEP provides an estimate of the to-
tal energy output that can be expected from the turbine over typical annual conditions,
then it is used as a measure to compare the performance of different wind turbines. By
comparing the AEP of various turbine models, developers can make informed decisions
about which turbines are suitable for a specific location and optimize the overall energy
production of the wind farm. Refer to section A.1 of this M.Sc. thesis for more information
about how to calculate AEP.
4.2.2
Annex F and G
Annex F addresses the uncertainty challenges in analyzing test results from multiple wind
turbines to determine the mean annual energy production (AEP) and its uncertainty. When
testing multiple turbines, the quantities of interest are the mean AEP of the sampled tur-
bines and the uncertainty in that mean [2].
Two approaches are commonly used to estimate the mean AEP. The first approach cal-
culates the simple average of the AEP values for each turbine, but determining the un-
certainty in the average AEP is not as straightforward [2]. The second, a more rigorous
Multiple WT Power Curves Measurement Uncertainty and Performance Outlier Characterization
19
==PAGE:32==
approach, combines data from all turbines to create a park power curve that represents
the collective power production. The park power curve is then evaluated using the ap-
propriate wind distribution to estimate the mean AEP, but this second approach is not
covered in DS/EN IEC 61400-12-2 [2].
The choice between these approaches depends on the desired level of accuracy and the
specific requirements to estimate the AEP of the wind farm. Considerations should be
made about the advantages and limitations of each method.
Annex G applies the method developed in Annex F for the determination of uncertainty in
a measurement derived from nacelle power curve measurements on multiple test turbines
and shows an example of an uncertainty calculation from power curve test results on three
individual turbines.
To facilitate the combination of uncertainties between turbines, Annex G applies the ap-
proach developed in Annex F. It involves summing uncertainties first across bins, then
across turbines, and finally across components. This method considers the degree of
correlation between the uncertainty components for each pair of turbines. Although full
correlation or full independence assumptions can be used in certain cases, a more de-
tailed approach is recommended for larger samples of units where correlation varies. The
annex suggests a range of acceptable correlation coefficient values, since explicit deter-
mination of correlations is often impractical.
The annexes indicate that full independence among the individual test turbine results
cannot always be assumed. Full independence means that there is no correlation in the
individual test results from one unit to the next, as usually most individual test turbines
in a facility will utilize identical measurement systems, and calibrations will usually be
performed in the same facility.
However, it is important to acknowledge that these annexes have certain limitations and
rely on assumptions that must be carefully considered in real-world applications. One
such assumption is that there exists a relationship between the measurement errors of
different turbines, which can be challenging to quantify and validate in practice.
In summary, annexes F and G provide valuable guidance for estimating energy production
in wind farms with multiple turbines. However, it is essential to remain mindful of the
limitations and assumptions inherent in their application. Assuming a direct relationship
between measurement errors or assuming a specific pattern for uncertainties is not always
straightforward. It is also important to note that Annex G serves as a numerical example
illustrating the concepts presented in Annex F. Refer to section A.2 for a summary of these
Annexes F and G of the standard [2].

Weighting Study
As previously mentioned, by assigning higher weights to bins with higher uncertainty, the
final resulting combined uncertainty would be higher than in the case where equal weights
are applied. Conversely, weighted averaging with lower weights assigned to bins with
higher uncertainty would result in a combined uncertainty lower than the case in which
equal weights are applied.
The approach chosen for this M.Sc. thesis is equal weighting. This proposal is beneficial
because it does not apply any assumption that can affect the total uncertainty in each bin
rather than simply considering that each power curve is equally important. When consid-
ering this approach, the methodology provides an assessment of uncertainty without any
external alteration.

5.6
Outlier Detection
The second methodology proposed in this report is a bin-wise outlier detection. This
approach pretends to detect possible outliers, based on the quantity of the power curves
N studied and on the combined power curve obtained from the average approach (yi).
After reviewing different methods, such as the Modified IQR method, Peirce’s method,
Chauvenet’s criterion, Grubbs’ tests, Dixon’s tests, and the method based on the distribu-
tion of extremes, the simple IQR method is chosen, as it considers the data close to the
median when accounting for the quartiles Q1 and Q3 of the data, and it is considered a
robust method [13].
5.6.1
IQR Method
The interquartile Range (IQR) is a statistical measure used to assess the spread or vari-
ability of a data set. It provides information on the central 50% of the data by measuring
the range between the lower quartile (Q1) and the upper quartile (Q3). The IQR is not
affected by extreme values or outliers and is therefore considered a robust measure of
dispersion. The methodology chosen is based on [13] and [14].
To compute the IQR, the dataset needs to be ordered. If the data set has an odd number
of observations, the median is the middle value. If the data set has an even number of
observations, the median is the average of the two middle values. The lower quartile (Q1)
corresponds to the 25th percentile, and the upper quartile (Q3) corresponds to the 75th
percentile.
A box plot is commonly used to visualize the IQR. The box represents the range between
Q1 and Q3, with a line (whisker) on each side. The lower whisker limit (lower inner fence)
is calculated as Q1 - 1.5 * IQR, and the upper whisker limit (upper inner fence) is calcu-
lated as Q3 + 1.5 * IQR. Any value beyond these limits is considered an outlier.
Outliers can also be classified as mild if they fall beyond the inner fences only or extreme
if they fall beyond the outer fences, defined as Q1 - 3 * IQR and Q3 + 3 * IQR, respec-
tively. The IQR method is less sensitive to outliers compared to other methods and offers
simplicity and robustness by focusing on the central portion of the data distribution.
Figure 5.11: Box-plot showing the IQR (covering the central 50% of data), median,
whiskers (delimiting the interval of accepted values) and three (mild) outliers [13].
The choice of 1.5 as the multiplier in the criterion for identifying potential outliers based
on the IQR is a commonly used convention. Using 1.5 as the multiplier effectively sets
a range within which most of the data points are expected to fall. Any values below
38
Multiple WT Power Curves Measurement Uncertainty and Performance Outlier Characterization
==PAGE:51==
Q1 - 1.5 * IQR or above Q3 + 1.5 * IQR are considered potential outliers. Nevertheless,
this range is somewhat arbitrary and can vary depending on the specific application or field
of study. In this study case, a scaling factor of 1.0 has been found to be more accurate
for the purpose of this study.
Advantages and Disadvantages
The IQR may discard legitimate values in large data sets. This risk of discarding legitimate
values may increase as the data set may contain a wider range of values and variability,
as the IQR range can be less sensible to those changes. Despite its simplicity, the IQR
method provides a useful measure of dispersion, particularly when little information about
the data distribution is available. The sample size is not taken into account in this method,
even though the data are important, as the quartiles will be affected by these data. As
mentioned before, these quartiles will be more sensible to short data sets than to large
data sets.
The IQR method offers several advantages. It is simple to use and easy to explain, making
it very accessible. Additionally, its robustness to outliers allows it to focus on the central
part of the data distribution, reducing the sensitivity to extreme values. Another benefit is
that it can be applied even when detailed information about the data distribution is limited.
However, there are some downsides to using the IQR method. In particular, in large data
sets, it might incorrectly identify valid values as outliers because it depends on quartiles,
which are affected by the input data.
Differences with Other Methods
The main difference between the IQR method and the other methods proposed in [13]
(Modified IQR method, Peirce’s method, Chauvenet’s criterion, Grubbs’ tests, Dixon’s
tests, and the method based on the distribution of extremes) lies in their treatment of
sample size. The IQR method does not consider the sample size (even though the input
data influences the method), resulting in constant acceptance limits regardless of the size
of the data set. On the contrary, the other methods take the sample size into account and
adjust the acceptance limits accordingly.
Peirce’s method is more complex and requires numerical tables for determining accep-
tance limits, while Chauvenet’s criterion, though considering the risk of error, may exclude
legitimate values due to inherent flaws.
Grubbs’ tests use the t-distribution or normal distribution for critical values and adjust ac-
ceptance limits accordingly. Dixon’s tests are limited to smaller data sets and lack an
explicit connection between sample size and acceptance interval length. On the other
hand, the method based on the distribution of extremes considers extreme values, pro-
viding a conservative approach for smaller sample sizes.
The IQR is based on the range between Q1 and Q3, which divides the data into four equal
parts. Since the IQR only depends on the quartiles and not individual data points, adding
more data points does not have a strong impact, in a first approximation, on Q1 and Q3.
Although the median (Q2) may change slightly with more data, it does not significantly
affect the Interquartile Range (IQR) calculation.
Then increasing the sample size would help to obtain a more robust interquartile range
(data found between Q1 and Q3) to delimit the range of accepted values. Increasing the
size of the data set enables the possibility of applying expert judgement in inspecting the
data, considering the context and requirements of the project, and making an informed
decision about whether to adjust the scaling factor to be more or less sensitive to outliers.

7.1
Theoretical Ways of Minimizing Uncertainty
In accordance with the methodology outlined in chapter 5, the approach proposed in this
M.Sc. project involves an arithmetic average of the power curves. This approach allows
for the generation of an average power curve of the N = 9 curves along with its corre-
sponding power uncertainty. It has been demonstrated in chapter 5 that by estimating a
degree of correlation for each uncertainty source of each pair of turbines, the uncertainty
in power of this average power curve can be reduced compared to a direct arithmetic
average among the N individual uncertainty tests.
This behavior is clearly represented in Figure 7.1, where errors e1 and e2 are being com-
bined. When errors (or uncertainties) are added and considered uncorrelated (r = 0), the
resulting combination is its hypotenuse, as seen in the sky blue color of Figure 7.1. This
hypotenuse is shorter than the direct sum of the errors e1 + e2, but it is also greater than
the errors e1 and e2 alone.
Figure 7.1 also shows the case for the full correlation behavior (r = 1). In this case. e1 and
e2 are added one after the other, as seen in the black color of Figure 7.1. Intermediate
values of r are also shown in green, and these represent values higher than the null
correlation but at the same time lower than the full correlation cases.

outlined in the Annex F and G, several notable differences emerge:
Change in current industry practices: The implementation of the annex methodology
would require a change in respect to current industry practices, which typically involve
calibrating instruments at the same facility. However, both the proposed methodology in
the Annexes F and G and the methodology proposed in this M.Sc. thesis would necessi-

tate sending instruments to different facilities or installing two instruments instead of one.
This would inevitably result in increased study costs [2].
Validity of the methodology: Demonstrating the benefits and validity of the methodology
proposed in the Annexes F and G is crucial to gain support from industry stakeholders.
It is essential to provide tangible results and a clear explanation of the applicability of the
methodology.
Additionally, a detailed mathematical explanation is necessary to understand the under-
lying functions used in the Annexes F and G methodology and why it leads to a reduction
in the uncertainty of AEP. The Annex methodology also suggests using a straightforward
average of the overall uncertainty in AEP obtained from studying a specific number of
power curves. However, there is a need for further clarification on how this approach can
be practically implemented and made feasible in practice [2].
The proposed methodology in this M.Sc. thesis offers comprehensive mathematical ex-
planations to illustrate how a reduced uncertainty in power can be achieved.
Bin-wise Uncertainty Combination: The proposed methodology involves an arithmetic
average of N bin-wise power curves which results in a combination of their bin-wise un-
certainties in power by considering the correlation among the instruments for each pair
of turbines. This approach takes into account the variability and uncertainty in each bin
individually, offering a more detailed representation of the uncertainty profile across the
entire wind speed range.
Annex F and G convert uncertainties in power to uncertainty in AEP by considering the
occurrence of wind in each bin, assuming a Rayleigh distribution. These bin contributions
in uncertainties are then summed up to obtain the uncertainties in the AEP. However, this
assumption overlooks correlations at the bin level and instead considers correlations after
summing across the bins.
The average power curve from this M.Sc. thesis takes a different approach. It combines
uncertainties in power curves and provides the option to calculate AEP afterwards (even
though this AEP is not calculated in this M.Sc. project). This average power curve shows
a representation of the uncertainty values themselves, offering a more detailed under-
standing of the uncertainty profile.
This means that the proposed methodology offers the advantage of effectively captur-
ing the behavior of uncertainty, enabling a more precise assessment of the relevance of
each uncertainty component. The choice between these approaches is significant, and it
depends on the specific case of study.
Weighting of Power Curves: In contrast to the annex methodology, which assigns equal
weights to the N power curves when combining uncertainties in AEP, the bin-wise ap-
proach introduces the possibility of assigning different weights to each power curve. It
is worth nothing that these varying weights are not utilized for the final results obtained.
However, the inclusion of such flexibility enables the exploration of different approxima-
tions for the contributions of each power curve to the overall uncertainty.

Conclusion
In this project, the main focus is on determining a weighted average power curve, taking
into account an estimation of correlation among type B sources of uncertainty from N = 9
wind turbines. However, no compelling reason is found to assign distinct weightings to
individual power curves, which means that all weightings are uniformly set to 1/N = 1/9,
which makes the approach an arithmetic average power curve.
In addition, an outlier detection methodology is applied to identify potential bin-wise out-
liers in the power curves. Furthermore, a preliminary approach is proposed to incorporate
the uncertainty from the average power curve into the outlier detection process.
The findings of this M.Sc. thesis can be summarized by answering the Research Ques-
tions introduced in chapter 1:
Research Question 1: Can a representative power curve be derived from a group of N
wind turbines?
A weighted average power curve, representing the collective behavior of a group of N
wind turbines, can be definitively obtained. This weighted average also serves as an
arithmetic average power curve, with all weights set to 1/N.
Research Question 2: Can the combined uncertainty in power of the representative
power curve (from this group of N wind turbines) be reduced with respect to its direct
arithmetic average?
By estimating correlations among the sources of type B uncertainty for each pair of wind
turbines within the total group of N, the combined uncertainty in power can be reduced
compared to simply taking the direct arithmetic average of the total group of N individual
wind turbine total uncertainties.
A correlation study has been performed to estimate the correlations among type B sources
of uncertainty. In this correlation a typical correlation coefficient around r = 0.7 is cho-
sen among all sources. This scenario leads to a reduction of approximately 15% in the
combined uncertainty uc,i in power, compared to the assumption of full correlation (r = 1)
for all sources of uncertainty. Furthermore, considering the null correlation (r = 0) for
all sources results in an even greater reduction, following the 1/

noted that the exact square root of 1/

involved in the methodology are equal.
Wind speed uncertainty significantly influences the overall power uncertainty due to wind
speed estimation, as even minor fluctuations in wind speed can cause substantial varia-
tions in power output. The sensitivity factor to wind speed highlights its critical role, as it
exhibits a substantial range and can contribute significantly to overall power uncertainty.
On the other hand, uncertainties related to temperature, pressure, and relative humidity
have minimal impact on the bin-wise power uncertainty compared to the dominant influ-
ence of wind speed. Therefore, precise measurement and characterization of wind speed
are crucial to improve the accuracy of uncertainty estimation.
Increasing the number of power curves in the analysis also contributes to the reduction
in the combined Category A uncertainty, as the individual contributions are assumed to
be uncorrelated. This finding highlights the importance of collecting a sufficient number

of power curves to minimize random errors and improve the reliability of uncertainty anal-
ysis. This behavior is similar to the behavior seen above of the combined Category B
uncertainty in power, as it is also reduced by increasing the number of power curves N.
Research Question 3: Is it feasible to accurately quantify the degree of correlation among
the components/sources of uncertainty of the wind turbines?
Accurately quantifying the degree of correlation among type B sources of uncertainty is
not possible due to the lack of knowledge about their individual distributions. There is no
direct statistical relationship that enables the calculation of type B uncertainty correlations.
Therefore, estimations must be made based on engineering or expert judgment.
Research Question 4: How does the introduction of weights potentially reduce or in-
crease the combined uncertainty in power?
The introduction of weights can lead to both an increase or a decrease in uncertainty.
This is due to the fact that assigning higher weights to higher uncertainties will result in
a higher combined uncertainty, whereas assigning higher weights to lower uncertainties
will lead to a lower combined uncertainty.
An interesting finding is that the weighting is influenced by the correlation term r. Specif-
ically, when r = 1 is estimated among the sources of uncertainty, a linear relationship of
the combined uncertainty occurs. However, when r < 1, this linear relationship disap-
pears.
Nevertheless, no justification has been found for applying weightings in this M.Sc. thesis.
Therefore, weightings should only be considered if a genuine and compelling justification
is identified.
Research Question 5: In what ways can the representative average power curve derived
from a group of N wind turbines be used within the wind industry?
The power curve was initially intended to provide a more representative performance of
a specific wind turbine model and to support developers in contractual negotiations, de-
fending a reduction in uncertainty estimations for that particular model. However, it should
be noted that the average power curve does not fully capture the unique circumstances
of each individual wind turbine at a particular site.
The final outcome for the wind industry is achieved by combining the average power
curve with the outlier methodology demonstrated in this M.Sc. thesis. This combina-
tion becomes significant as contracts often specify a percentage for guaranteed energy
production in AEP, which may lack a comprehensive understanding of the turbine’s per-
formance, making it difficult to assess if that performance can be improved. As a result,
the two methodologies presented in this M.Sc. thesis offer an approach for identifying
potential underperformance of individual wind turbines by the use of the average power
curve with its combined uncertainty in power together with the outlier detection method,
enabling for the assessment of turbine underperformance in power in specific wind speed
ranges.
Nevertheless, one potential challenge associated with the proposed average power curve
methodology is the use of different sources of uncertainty to measure the same measur-
and. For example, when the same instruments are used, a high correlation among their
uncertainties could be estimated, and errors associated with these instruments would
tend to be similar, enabling easier comparisons between measurements. However, when

different instruments were used, differences in their uncertainties may arise, posing a
greater challenge for engineers in interpreting and reconciling the discrepancies.
To mitigate these challenges, a potential solution involves using two different sources of
type B uncertainty in the measurements at every site. For example, for N = 2 sites, use
two cup anemometers calibrated in the same place and two calibrated in different places.
By adopting this approach, it would be possible to effectively address possible differences
among the errors in their measurements and have the possibility of applying the average
power curve in order to obtain a reduced uncertainty in power.
Research Question 6: What are the key differences between the proposed methodology
and the methodology outlined in Annexes F and G of DS/EN IEC 61400-12-2 [2]?
The Annex F and G methodology outlined in the DS/EN IEC 61400-12-2 standard faces
challenges such as the need for further clarification of the mathematical approach used
and the lack of a comprehensive in-depth behavior of turbine performance, as the method-
ology offers to combine uncertainties in AEP, which does not give a comprehensive be-
havior of the power and uncertainty in power performance per bin [2].
The proposed bin-wise analysis methodology offers a different approach to the existing
current methodologies by providing average power curves with associated uncertainties
in power in a bin-wise format. This methodology captures uncertainty behavior more
effectively and enables a more detailed assessment of uncertainty components.
Research Question 7: Is there a method available to detect potential outliers in bin-wise
power? Additionally, is this outlier detection providing reliable results?
Outlier detection using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method has proven to be an effec-
tive approach for identifying outliers in the power curve data set. This method is robust as
increasing or decreasing the data set does not strongly influence the IQR=Q3-Q1. How-
ever, more informed decisions can be made by increasing the number of power curves,
as it gives more and more robustness to the values of Q3 and Q1, allowing for the possi-
bility of applying expert judgement in inspecting the data and making informed decisions
about whether to adjust the scaling factor to be more or less sensitive to outliers.
Another alternative method for outlier detection has been proposed employing the IQR
method. In this approach, the accepted values range is defined as the total combined
uncertainty identified in the average power curve. Outliers are detected when there is no
overlap between individual uncertainties and the accepted values range (and these values
are outside the accepted value range). This novel approach provides an additional means
to identify potential outliers in the power curve by giving a different vision on how to define
which values are detected as outliers. This approximation gives even more credibility to
the Research question 6, as it combines both methodologies found in this M.Sc. thesis.
In conclusion, uncertainty analysis plays a vital role in assessing the performance of wind
turbines in the wind energy industry. Future research and development should focus
on refining and validating these methodologies to enhance their practicality, accuracy,
and broader applicability. By continually improving uncertainty analysis practices, the
wind energy industry can make more informed decisions and maximize the reliability and
performance of wind turbine systems.

To simplify the analysis, the following assumptions are made regarding correlations be-
tween turbines:
a) All category A uncertainty components are assumed to be independent of each other,
regardless of whether they are from the same turbine or different turbines.
b) All category A uncertainty components are assumed to be independent of all category
B uncertainty components, regardless of whether they are from the same turbine or dif-
ferent turbines.
c) All category B uncertainty components of different types are assumed to be indepen-
dent, regardless of whether they are from the same turbine or different turbines.
d) Category B uncertainty components of the same type may exhibit some degree of cor-
relation between turbines, which can vary from 0 to 1.

tainty components of the same type between different turbines. The summation across
