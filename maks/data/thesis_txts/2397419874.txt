Abstract
Globally the number of individuals diagnosed with Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM)
increases and poses a still growing problem to the human health. A diagnosis of T2DM
can, with the right precautions be handled without additional organ damage, but when
the disease is not well controlled the ultimate consequence is treatment with insulin. This
is a sign of disease progression to a state where the body no longer produce insulin on its
own.
The focus has been to identify clinical markers potentially involved in the rate of dis-
ease progression to individuals needing insulin treatment, or found to be important when
building a model to predict this time frame.
The process involved data exploration to identify patterns and/or relations within the
variables and building a model to predict the progression, hence time to insulin treatment.
Throughout the process data was updated based on technical discussions and evaluation of
results. The performance was initially RMSE = 3.13 and PCC = 0.63 (mean of evaluated
models), after removing samples with TTI ≤1 and subgroup with GAD measure >
11U/ml the performance was RMSE = 3.18 and PCC = 0.57, indicating a very stable
model, but no prediction improvement.
The features found to correlate with the target value in this cohort was HbA1c mea-
sures at baseline, weight, BMI, and age at diagnosis. When exploring prediction modelling
using ANN, mainly time to mono- and dualtherapy were selected as most important fea-
tures, following weight, BMI, and age. Analysing these in a survival analysis showed that
mono- and dualtherapy do inﬂuence survival curves.

Introduction
The number of individuals diagnosed with diabetes is increasing all over the world, and is
becoming one of the worlds biggest health problems (WHO, 2016). The high prevalence
has resulted in increased awareness of the problem, and research focus on understand-
ing the disease and development of treatment. Diabetes comes in diﬀerent forms, with
the distinction of (i) insulin dependent diabetes mellitus (IDDM) and (ii) non-insulin
dependent diabetes mellitus (NIDDM), being the main two categories (Rowley et al.,
1992). However research has revealed multiple additional types of diabetes, including
autoimmune, lifestyle related, gestational (pregnancy related) and genetically associated
disease (Diabetes.co.uk, 2018a). The diﬀerent types are traditionally distinguished ﬁrst
of all by the initial disease development and by the rate of progression of disease. An
important parameter is, how long after diagnosis insulin or other medical treatment is
needed (Pozzilli and Mario, 2001; Diabetes.co.uk, 2018b). Another important indication
for distinguishing the diﬀerent types is the age of disease oﬀset . But because some of the
diabetes types show similar progression patterns, the process in distinguishing one type
from the other is more diﬃcult (Steenkamp et al., 2014).
The ﬁeld of computational biology and what we now describe as Bioinformatics is in
a still growing phase and the limits to what can be done is still to be identiﬁed. The
increase in computational power, software development and open source tools have made
it possible for a great number of scientists to explore the potential of the still increasing
amount of available health related data and information (Orozco et al., 2013).
The development of computational biology, bioinformatics and machine learning have
opened up the potential to improve the understanding of the biological mechanisms driv-
ing a disease oﬀset, treatment response, and development of potential side eﬀects and
toxicities (Sharma, 2015).
The computational and data driven analysis can result in
identifying features important in prediction, and potentially improve the understanding
of the individual disease progression and optimum treatment.
The approach for prediction modelling in this study will be applied on a Type 2 Diabetes
data set. The project involved building an Artiﬁcial Neural Network (ANN) prediction
model and performing a survival analysis, from data composed of clinical variables.
The report will describe the general framework of ANN, model optimisation and
how the model is evaluated. The model evaluation will include an inspection of which
biological features were most important in prediction. The concept of survival analysis
will be explained, and features found important in the prediction will be analysed for
impact on the survival function.
First an overview of the biological mechanisms involved in diabetes and how it inﬂuences
the overall metabolism will be presented.

number of individuals diagnosed with T2DM. In Denmark the total number of individuals

1.2
Metabolic Homeostasis
The key mechanism in maintaining homeostasis is the constant regulation by anabolic
and catabolic enzymes, mainly insulin or glucagon, respectively (Briant et al., 2016;
Unger and Cherrington, 2012). These opposite regulating hormones are important in
their action of activating speciﬁc pathways involved in the metabolism of nutrients.
Regulation of homeostasis in terms of energy and metabolic pathways can be described
from two main points (i) after ingestion of a meal (hyperglycemia) and (ii) in a fasting
situation (hypoglycemia) (Gropper et al., 2013). These will be the reference points when
describing the regulation of the major metabolic pathways of carbohydrate, protein, and
lipids. The organs and regulatory pathways responsible for metabolic regulation is very
closely linked, as illustrated in Figure 1. The regulation will be described with focus on
the three organs mainly involved in energy homeostasis, the liver, skeletal muscle, and
adipose tissue.

1.2.1
Metabolism in the Liver
After a carbohydrate rich meal the blood glucose levels are elevated and the body enters
the hyperglycemic state. In order to stabilise the blood glucose level the liver will initiate
(1) glycolysis to metabolise the glucose into potential energy sources or (2) glycogenesis
to store the glucose as glycogen in the liver. If glycogen storage are full the liver will
secrete glucose to the circulation, where it can be transported to adipose tissue and stored
as fat (Gropper et al., 2013).
When blood glucose levels are low the liver will, mediated by glucagon, initiate glycogenol-
ysis to utilise glucose stored as glycogen. In addition the liver will absorb fatty acids and
amino acids from the blood to initiate gluconeogenesis and lipolysis. The glucose will
then be secreted to the circulation (Gropper et al., 2013).
Glucose secretion and absorption in the liver, and most other organs, is not dependent
on insulin signalling. While for energy homeostasis, where skeletal muscle and adipose
tissue play a key role in regulating the glucose level, insulin signalling is necessary for
absorbing glucose (Saltiel and Kahn, 2001).
1.2.2
Metabolism in Skeletal Muscles
In hyperglymic state insulin is secreted from the pancreas and will target insulin receptors
on the skeletal muscle where it induces transport of glucose and amino acids into the cell
(Saltiel and Kahn, 2001). In the cell insulin induces (1) glycolysis to metabolise glucose,
(2) glycogenesis to store glucose as glycogen, if possible and (3) initiate protein synthesis.
The process will enable the cell to store energy and synthesise proteins and enzymes used
in cell growth (Saltiel and Kahn, 2001).
When glucose levels are low glucagon is secreted from the pancreas and stomach, and
acts to repress the actions activated by insulin. Glucagon activates (1) glycogenolysis to
utilise stored glycogen for energy production and (2) initiate protein degradation. The
amino acids are secreted to the circulatory system where they can be absorbed by the
liver for gluconeogenesis (Gropper et al., 2013).
1.2.3
Metabolism in Adipose Tissue
Adipose tissue represents the bodies main energy storage. When bound to insulin recep-
tors on adipose tissue, insulin initiates glucose absorption and will result in production
of glucose and fatty acids used in synthesis of triglycerides for storage (Gropper et al.,
2013; Turner et al., 2014).
In hypoglycemia glucagon represses this synthesis and instead induces lipolysis to break
down fat tissue and secrete fatty acids to the circulation (Gropper et al., 2013).
This constant regulation of absorption and storage or release and secretion is important
in regulating blood glucose levels. The process assure glucose is available for absorption
by other organs, and also aﬀect the blood amino acid and lipid levels (Gropper et al.,
2013).

1.3
Metabolic consequences of Diabetes
The main factor described in T2DM patients is the compromised or completely missing
regulation by insulin (WHO, 2011). When insulin is either not present or the sensitivity
is low, glucose and amino acids are not absorbed in skeletal muscle and the cell is not
provided with the needed energy or substrates for cell growth. Simultaneously, pathways
for protein degradation and glycogenolysis mediated by glucagon are not repressed (Basu
et al., 2005).
In adipose tissue, instead of storing the excess glucose, glucagon induced lipolysis, result
in elevated levels of triglycerides and fatty acids in the blood. The result, and what is
used to characterise T2DM patients, is an elevated blood glucose level and often also
elevated levels of triglycerides, LDL and cholesterol (Gropper et al., 2013).
One of the factors of progression from non-insulin dependent to insulin dependent T2DM
is the change of the pancreas morphology, speciﬁcally the mass of β-cells.
1.3.1
β-cell Degradation
The pancreas produces and secretes a number of enzymes and hormones involved in the
process of food digestion and uptake. Enzymes are produced by the exocrine cells, and
hormone secretion is carried out by the endocrine islet. The islet is partially made of
insulin producing β-cells and glucagon producing α-cells (Gropper et al., 2013; Rhodes,
2005). In an healthy individual the endocrine islets will be scattered over the surface and
cover ∼1 % of the surface (Figure 2a). Where ∼70 % of the islet cell is composed of
β-cells (Rhodes, 2005).
The β-cells are under normal circumstances well adaptive, and high demand of insulin
production will result in a higher quantity of islets on the pancreatic surface, and larger
islets due to a higher number of β-cells (Figure 2b). This adaptation could be a result of
long term exposure to high blood sugar levels, as a result to high level of carbohydrate
rich food intake. This is often found in obese individuals (Rhodes, 2005). At some point
the adaptation of β-cell mass fails and cell level decreases, which is deﬁned as T2DM. In
type 2 diabetes patients, the number of islet and β-cells is low and can be covered with
amyloid plaques (Figure 2c). This situation will result in low production of insulin and
in the worst case a situation where insulin must be injected (Rhodes, 2005).

1.3.2
Long term side eﬀects
Correct diagnosis of diabetes mellitus is very important as a long-term exposure to high
blood glucose, FFA levels, and increased protein degradation can have severe conse-
quences on health.
The high concentration of glucose can be detected as glycation on the haemoglobin
of the red blood cells (HbA1c). A state where the excess glucose are bound to surface
protein (non-enzymatic glycosylation). This also results in reducing the oxygen trans-
portation in the blood (Pu et al., 2012).
Incorrect management of diabetes increases the risk of cardiovascular disease and mi-
crovascular complication causing damage of kidney, eye, and nerves (nephropathy, retinopa-
thy and neuropathy respectively). Missing care can also result in an increased risk of limb
amputation (Blair, 2016; WHO, 2011).

T2DM Progression stages
Diagnosis of T2DM is primarily based on fasting plasma glucose level and measure of
glycation of the red blood cells. These levels reﬂect the immediate and long term expo-

sure to high blood glucose concentrations, respectively (WHO, 2011). The World Health
Organisation (WHO) recommends a fasting plasma level of ≥7.0 mmol/L and HbA1c
level at ≥6.5 % as the threshold for diabetes diagnosis (WHO, 2011, 2016). For compar-
ison, the HbA1c levels in an healthy individual should be < 6.0 %. Diagnosis should be
conﬁrmed with a repeated measure at least 8-12 weeks after the initial measure (WHO,

Upon diagnosis the patient is introduced to the expected progression course involving
multiple possible stages (ADA, 2017; IDF, 2012), a schematic overview of the progression
time line is given in Figure 3.
Figure 3: Upon diabetes diagnosis the main concern is to lower and stabilise blood glucose levels,
measured as HbA1c. The recommended procedure of care is to continuously monitor the patient and
gradually, if necessary evaluate the need for therapeutics (IDF, 2012).
Where in the progression line an individual enters, depends on the immediate pro-
gression status, mainly determined by the HbA1c measures and the individual treatment
course set by the doctor.
The target value is set at HbA1c <7.0% (IDF, 2012). The ﬁrst step after diagnosis is ini-
tiating a lifestyle change to increase exercise and implement an healthy diet, with the aim
to naturally lower blood glucose levels. If lifestyle change does not result in the desired
decrease in HbA1c levels, or the initial levels of HbA1c are >7.0% medical therapy, most
often Metformin, is initiated (IDF, 2012). If glucose levels are not stabilised or additional
factors are elevated a second drug is introduced (dualtherapy), often Sulfonylurea. If the
progression of diabetes is still not in control the next step is to initiate insulin treatment
(IDF, 2012).
1.4
Electronic Medical Records
Data provided for this study was initially recorded to identify all patients with diabetes
in the Tayside area in Scotland. The result was an electronic database with medical
records collected from both general practitioners, hospitals, medical prescriptions, and
biochemistry measures. The main aim of the electronic collection of data to one database
was to test the eﬀectiveness in identifying all diabetic patients, know as Diabetes Au-
dit and Research in Tayside Scotland (DARTS) (Morris et al., 1997). In addition to
clinical markers and health care records the study was expanded to also include genetic
information, know as GoDARTS (Genetics of Diabetes Audit and Research in Tayside
Scotland) (H´ebert et al., 2017), and have been used in scientiﬁc studies including Zhou
et al. (2014).
The GoDARTS data set represent four types of diabetes, and a short overview of the

1.5
Machine Learning and Prediction modelling
Machine Learning is a mathematical tool used in prediction modelling.
The original
thought and main aim of the process is to create a machine able to learn from past
experience to solve a problem in the future, with as little human interaction as possible
(Herlau et al., 2017). This process is mediated by analysing preferably large amounts of
data to discover potential complicated patterns or relationships. These algorithms are
present and increasing in numbers in our every day life. They are used to personalise
an individuals exposure to advertisements, recommendations of products and information
one might ﬁnd interesting based on previous choices. Present machine learning algorithms
and software show the potential for prediction modelling in various ﬁelds of biology.
Machine learning can be divided in three main parts, (i) data exploration/preparation
(ii) Model construction and (iii) Evaluation (Herlau et al., 2017).

1.5.1
Data Preparation
This initial data analysis is important in order to get a basic understanding and overview
of the data. By exploring distribution and possible correlation of variables as well as
visualisation of the data and correlation.
Principal Component Analysis
One of the challenges in visualising a large data set is the potential high number of dimen-
sions. PCA is a method to reduce the dimensions and create new principal components,
constructed to separate data points based on variance in data. The ﬁrst principal com-
ponent (PC1) is computed to capture the largest amount of variance. When plotting
data points in the reduced PC plane the aim is to separate the data points to potentially
observe data clustering.
Performing PCA is composed of two steps (1) subtract the mean from data and (2)
perform singular value decomposition (SVD). Mathematically SVD is deﬁned as:
X = U P V T
(1)
Where U and V represent the singular vektors (eigenvector matrix) while P contains the
singular values of the data matrix X (Varmuza and Filzmoser, 2009).
Subgroup Detection
When analysing and evaluating biological and health related data an important aspect
is that not all samples are likely to act similar or ﬁt the same patterns. Identiﬁcation of
samples that do not ﬁt the general pattern is of interest, as these individuals could be
said to behave diﬀerent from the other group of samples. These can then be used for
additional ﬁltering or forming subgroups of the data.
Subgroups can be identiﬁed based on variable interval. Another method could be to eval-
uate the similarity within samples in data. For this a distance matrix is computed using
the similarity coeﬃcient of the Gower’s distance. Then it is possible to ﬁlter samples of
low dissimilarity from samples of high dissimilarity.
Gower’s Distance
Gower’s distance is a measure of the similarity/dissimilarity within two samples for one
or multiple variables, described by Gower (1971). When computed the range of the spe-
ciﬁc variable is evaluated, the distance of two samples is then determined by where in
the variable range they are located. If two sample values are very close in relation to the
variable of interest, the dissimilarity measure is low.

Missing Data
Data exploration is also helpful in order to evaluate possible missing data and evaluate
if variables or individuals should be excluded or if the data should be imputed.
Data with missing values are not uncommon, and might not always be a problem. But
in a number of situations it can be a challenge to work with data composed of larger
amounts of missing data points.
After initial exploration, potential imputation, and ﬁltering are performed, the data
is ready for modelling.
1.5.2
Model Construction
The term Machine Learning cover multiple methods to perform prediction modelling and
can be performed as both supervised and unsupervised learning. In supervised learn-
ing it can be distinguished in two major groups of classiﬁcation and regression (Herlau
et al., 2017). For this project the machine learning framework know as Artiﬁcial Neural
Networks (ANN) is applied to solve a regression problem, which will be the focus of the
description.
Artiﬁcial Neural Networks
For this project a ”simple” Feed Forward network is used to train and test the prediction
model. In the computer based framework the most simple set-up is composed of a 3-layer
network (Figure 4), composed of: Input layer, Hidden layer and Output layer.

The process of training the network involve multiple iterations to capture potential
patterns in the data, this is also the process where the network weights are updated to
improve prediction.

The architecture of the network can be modiﬁed based on the prediction problem in
mind. The number of input units is determined by the number of input variables and

in the same sense and can variate a lot from model to model, depending on the data
and additional hyper parameters, including learning rate. In order to select the optimum
hyper parameters cross validation (CV) must be implemented (Krstajic et al., 2014).
Cross Validation
CV is applied in a model training process in order to select the optimum parameters and
evaluate the generalised performance of a prediction model. The basic feature of CV is
separating the data used for training from the data used for model testing. This is a
simple one-layer CV, favourable when evaluating model performance (Brockhoﬀ, 2017).
CV is also favourable in the process of selecting hyper parameters, like optimum learning
rate and number of hidden units. In order to make this optimisation, the data must be
split additionally, a two-layer (nested) CV. A schematic overview of the nested CV set-up
is given in Figure 5.

1.6
Survival Analysis
Survival analysis is a widely used method to identify variables impacting risk of event for
a given time. The method was ﬁrst used as a tool to deal with incomplete observations,
and dealing with data from clinical studies where all object might not be part of the entire
study time-frame. For this project the method is used to perform time-to-event analysis.
Where variables are evaluated for potential inﬂuence of survival time (time-to-event)
(Rich et al., 2010).
The inﬂuence of one or multiple covariates on time-to-event are evaluated using the
Kaplan-Meier plot. For this analysis two main variables are needed: (1) the survival time
(t, time from diagnosis to event or time from diagnosis to censoring), and (2) event status
(d), where d = 1 represent event, and d = 0 represent censoring. Additional variables
can then be included as covariates inﬂuencing the survival time (Cook, 2010).

1.7
Objectives
The aim of the project is to investigate, if it is possible to predict the time-to-insulin for
the T2DM patients provided in the GoDarts cohort. Furthermore, the aim is to identify
if it is possible to identify features (input variables) more important in the process of
prediction and progression. The identiﬁcation of these features can be used in formulating
hypothesis on how the disease progresses and to understand how to prevent development
of related diseases and organ damage. The project work included building an artiﬁcial
neural network model and evaluating the performance of diﬀerent parameter combinations
and optimisation algorithms. Additional to the prediction model a survival analysis will
be performed, with the main purpose to evaluate if features found important in the
prediction model and data analysis have an impact on the survival function.

2.1
Data Exploration
In order to understand the data and the potential inﬂuence of the various variables the
data set is analysed and visualised from diﬀerent perspectives to capture possible patterns,
outliers, and correlated variables.
2.1.1
Variable and Sample Filtering
Before analysing potential patterns in the data, it is important to assure that only the
desired samples and variables are present in the data set. This includes removing in-
dividuals not diagnosed with the phenotype of interest. For this project the data set
was ﬁltered to only consist of individuals characterised as ”Type 2 diabetes mellitus”
patients.
2.1.2
Variable Patterns
The data exploration involves a simple visualisation and inspection of the data, to detect
possible outliers and distribution of variables. For this the method R summary is applied
(R Core Team, 2016). This will result in a summary for each of the variables stating
minimum, median, mean, maximum, ﬁrst, and third quantile. In the summary table the
main interest is evaluating the proportion in minimum, maximum, and mean values to
identify possible outliers or ”skew” data.
Besides individual inspection of the variables, it can be of interest to identify potential
relation between attributes. With additional focus on the target attribute to get a better
understanding of the attribute and how other variables are related to it.
PCA plot is made using the R package FactoMineR (Lˆe et al., 2008) and factoex-
tra (Kassambara and Mundt, 2017). Because PCA can only handle countinuous data,
categorical variables are not represented in the analysis.
Variable Density
A method to visualise potential relations for the input variables is to compute and visu-
alise the densities. For this the R package ggplot2 (Wickham, 2009) is used. To assure
the diﬀerent variables are visible on the density plot the data is split in four group based
on the value range maximum of the variables. 1: < 25, 2: < 250, 3: < 3000, and 4
represent the categorical variables.

A method to identify patterns, is to evaluate variable correlations.

2.1.3
Identiﬁcation of Subgroups
Subgroup based on variable interval is made based on GAD measurements, where GAD
> 11 U/ml indicate autoimmune response and samples are removed.
Subgroup based on distance measure is performed using the R package ”StatMatch”
(D’Orazio, 2017), in this package distance = 0 represents high similarity.
When applied to all samples the result is a distance matrix and can be visually inspected
using a heatmap, constructed using the R package ”pheatmap” (Kolde, 2015). For this
matrix and heatmap the diagonal will be 0, as all samples have dissimilarity score 0 to
itself.
Filtering is then performed based on the dissimilarity score. To identify if the ﬁltering
has been successful in identifying a potential subgroup, the two groups are compared for
diﬀerences in variable distribution. Here a statistical test was used to evaluate if the
groups are signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from each other for each individual variable.
2.2
Imputation of Clinical Variables
Imputation is performed using the algorithm Multivariate Imputation by Chained Equa-
tions (MICE), using the R package ”MICE” (Buuren and Groothuis-Oudshoorn, 2011)
resulting in multiple data sets of imputed values. Initially the process can be controlled in
terms of what mathematical method should be used to impute the speciﬁc attributes and
which of the other attributes should be used in the imputation. In the speciﬁc case the
variable time-to-insulin is set to not be used in imputation of any of the other variables
while time to mono- and dualtherapy is set not to be imputed.
A cut-oﬀfor maximum missingness is set to 60% for the variables to be imputed, variables
with higher missingness is removed. The numerical variables are imputed using predic-
tive mean matching (pmm). Additional settings for the imputation is set to perform a
maximum of 100 iterations and compute 15 imputed data set. The main steps of the
process are illustrated in the ﬂow chart in Figure 7.
The imputation process
The full data is ﬁrst mean imputed for all NA values. The algorithm then selects the
initial attribute to impute (x1 in Figure 7). The goal is now to construct a set of re-
gression coeﬃcients to ﬁt the samples where x1 is present, based on the original values
and the mean imputed values. The coeﬃcients are then used to compute missing values.
The algorithm then moves to the next variable, original missing values are changed back
to ”NA” and samples with original data are used to compute regression coeﬃcients and
compute imputed values. This process is repeated ﬁrst for all attributes, and then re-
peated for the maximum number of iterations, or stopped if coeﬃcients do not change

subgroup of samples the list of individuals was ﬁltered for mean or median dissimilarity
> 0.4 and > 0.3. This measure was set in order to capture the samples with a recurring

Discussion
The work in this project was performed with the aim to identify clinical features impor-
tant in the process of predicting time-to-insulin for T2DM patients. The identiﬁcation
of clinical variables has included data exploration and neural network modelling. The
variables found to be associated with the target value and important in model predictions
were further analysed for inﬂuence on the estimated survival, illustrated by Kaplan-Meier
curves.
Target value - TTI
The data target value of progression to insulin treatment is deﬁned by two means, initi-
ation of insulin treatment or reaching a clinical requirement of insulin (2 Hba1cs > 8.5%
more than 3 months apart and on 2 drugs).
It reﬂecting that recommended guidelines are not always implemented in daily prac-
tise, when evaluating if insulin treatment should be initiated. This is an example of a
challenge when working with real life data. Similar is observed in the data composition
where not all individuals have the same number of measurements or frequency.
Another challenge in interpreting the data is also that the individuals enter the study at
diﬀerent stages of disease progression. Hence, some individuals are followed in the process
of pre-diabetes while others enter at diagnosis with initial demand of medical therapy.
This diﬀerence also reﬂects the variance in progression time and state of progression at
baseline.
For this project the target value have been deﬁned as the time to insulin, but during
the process it has been proposed for future work to potentially change the target to time-
to-failure-of-treatment. Then initially predict time to monotherapy (failure of lifestyle
intervention), followed by predicting time to dualtherapy (failure of monotherapy), and
lastly time to insulin (failure of dualtherapy). Essentially predicting when the current
treatment is no longer suﬃcient in keeping the HbA1c levels stable. These intermediate
points could beneﬁt in the understanding of disease progression.
This progression and the inﬂuence of speciﬁc clinical markers could then also be evaluated
by a survival analysis. How does the diﬀerent variables reﬂect on the diﬀerent treatment
types and progression stages.
Subgroup Identiﬁcation
One of the motivations for the subgroup identiﬁcation process was the perspective to
better understand the diﬀerent patient subgroups within the phenotype of interest. And
especially how this identiﬁcation could improve treatment to be more personalised.
The exploration process involved an identiﬁcation of potential subgroups evaluated using
a measure of dissimilarity for a selected number of variables. The process was successful
in separating two groups, while the only variable found to be diﬀerent within the groups
was time to insulin treatment (progression time). In the interest to identify other sub-
groups, the process of constructing the distance matrix and evaluating the measure of
separation could be evaluated using another distance measure. In addition, the distance
49

measures were here computed based on two variables (% HbA1c at baseline and TTI),
which in terms explain why a separation would be observed based on TTI. Repeating the
process additional variables could be included in the process of computing the distance
matrix.
Further a diﬀerent approach to grouping samples could be applied. In the modelling
process the subgroup of GAD+ samples were removed. Similar to that the samples could
be grouped according to intervals of age, BMI, type of mono- or dualtheraphy, DBP/SBP
etc. As these clinical markers could be of inﬂuence to the general progression pattern.
From the density plot (Figure 12) the DBP and SBP appears to form peaks. These peaks
would be of interest to investigate further and analyse if categorising samples based on
these intervals, could reveal subgroups or additional variable patterns. Or this obser-
vation could be used in the future for evaluations of the optimum method to scale and
group the data.
The subgroup with TTI < 1 was removed during the process. One of the arguments
for this separation was that this group represent a type of diabetes with very rapid
progression. Progression to insulin within the ﬁrst year might not be a relevant target
for a prediction model as the progression is so rapid.
Diabetes Diagnosis
The term Diabetes cover multiple phenotypes and data represents four types, with high-
est prevalence of T2DM. A disease usually thought to have a ”long” progression and not
necessarily result in insulin dependency (NIDDM). In the process a number of samples
were removed due to progression times less than one year, forming a subgroup of indi-
viduals with very rapid progression. Even though all samples were categorised as T2DM
patients, individuals where present with GAD antibody levels indicating an auto immune
response, a feature not characterised for T2DM. This pose a potential challenge, in the
doctors diagnosing patients not having the knowledge to distinguish between diﬀerent
types of diabetes, or do not know enough about the diﬀerent types and characteristics in
general.
In the eﬀort to get a better understanding of the disease a common set of guidelines
in diagnosis and treatment evaluation might be helpful in order to prevent misdiagnosis
and following treatment. To do this a better understanding is needed to characterise
the diﬀerent types of diabetes. Diabetes is a heterogeneous disease, both in terms of
characteristics, progression patterns, and how it is diagnosed.
In terms of the modelling process, this human factors could most likely inﬂuence the
performances of the models, as it would be diﬃcult to recognise a pattern when the data
set represents multiple types of disease.
Imputation
Before the initiation of this project the data was imputed using the method K-nearest
neighbours (KNN). When using KNN imputation, the missingness threshold was set at
50
==PAGE:57==
Carina Skaarup, s123496
February 12, 2018
maximum 25 %, which resulted in removal of additional variables. Because of that it was
desired to evaluate an alternative method for imputation.
The imputation process implemented for this project, and to be used for future work with
the data, involved a number of challenges and choices. The particular choice of method
(MICE) was selected because the multivariate imputation method would allow for the
imputation to evaluate the full data set. The strength of this method is particularly seen
when increasing number of iterations, and when computing multiple imputed data sets,
the distribution and density of imputed values is closer to the original data points. For
attributes with high missingness the construction of multiple data sets result in a more
robust imputation.
One of the challenges was how to implement the imputed values into the data. Because
multiple data sets had been computed, multiple imputed values were available. In this
work, for the continuous variables, a mean of the 15 computed values was selected. This
measure was selected as it would reﬂect the potential variance in the range of imputed
values. A diﬀerent approach could be to use the median, that would result in the actual
imputed value used in the ﬁnal data, and might reﬂect a potential trend in the imputed
values.
In terms of the categorical variables, the social deprivation score (HBSIMD5), a rounded
mean was chosen when implementing the imputation. The initial thought was to use the
mode, the number represented with the highest frequency. But this resulted in multiple
possibilities for one sample, as two scores might be represented equal number of times.
Then the challenge was changed to selecting the lower or higher mode value. By selecting
either, always to use the higher or lower score could potentially result in a bias in the
data, to avoid this the rounded mean was selected.
Variable Scaling
In this project continuous variables were scaled in the range 0-1 and categorical values
were sparse encoded (0/1). This approach was applied as a result of discussions with
other scientists and because this approach to scaling was used in other modelling work
done with the data.
The result of this scaling is that all variables are computed to be on the same scale and
in that sense it assures that the variables are weighted equally when introduced to the
model. But scaling to the ﬁxed range of 0-1 also pose the potential risk of loosing infor-
mation. One of the aims of the process was to identify what clinical variables inﬂuence
the progression. When scaling the data by this approach some of that information might
be lost.
Instead of scaling the input variables a diﬀerent approach could be to evaluate the in-
put variables as categories (assign sample to an interval group) rather than continuous
number, then use sparse encoding when introduced to the model. This approach could
then pose a diﬀerent challenge in creating a high number of columns and change the
input values to mainly be 0/1, that in a regression model might pose a challenge when
computing the target value.

Model Performance
The patterns illustrated in the performances evaluation plots, before removing therapy
information, reﬂects that most predicted values was in the range 2 to 10 years, while
the target values span from 1 to 20 years. The range of predicted values represent the
span from 1st quantile of 2.68 years to 3rd quantile of 8.47 years, given in the summary
table. The model prediction reﬂects this pattern of the most frequent observations. In
that sense the actual predictions might to a great extent reﬂect the overall trend in the
data, not the individual progression patterns. The samples identiﬁed based on dissim-
ilarity were characterised by longer progression times compared to the resulting group.
And could reﬂect this group of samples not well recognised by the model. Removing this
group might improve performances.
One of the challenges in improving the models from capturing the overall trend and
to really evaluate each sample to compute a prediction progression is the trade-oﬀof
performance and risk of over ﬁtting the data. Especially the cross-validation set-up is
used to avoid the model capturing sample speciﬁc patterns, that will result in over ﬁtting
and poor performances on test data.
Parameters Inﬂuencing Survival
When evaluating the relation and potential inﬂuences of clinical variables on survival time,
hence risk of progression to insulin within a certain time frame, the curves representing
BMI intervals showed that initially the groups of BMI < 25 and > 35 had overlapping
survival curves. The group with lower BMI represent a group where diabetes is not a
result of obesity, but potentially a result of genetic inheritance.
Similar patterns were observed when evaluating age. Younger individuals had higher risk
of progression to insulin than older individuals. When evaluating potential subgroups
this distinction in BMI and age could be interesting to analyse, and to evaluate if other
measures are diﬀerent for this group compared to other samples.
HbA1c and T2DM Diagnosis
The main feature for diagnosis and initiating drug therapy in T2DM patient is the %
HbA1c level. The use of HbA1c in diagnosis and disease surveillance is recommended by
both the World Health Organisation (WHO) and the International Diabetes Federation
(IDF) (WHO, 2011; IDF, 2012). The thought advantage of the HbA1c measures is that
it reﬂects the average blood glucose levels over the life cycle of the red blood cell (Cohen
et al., 2010).
But this exposure and level of glycation on haemoglobin might be a result of more than
just high blood glucose concentration. The event of glycation and the potential % HbA1c
compared to serum concentrations is thought to be similar for all individuals. But this
event could, as most other processes in the body, be inﬂuenced by individual markers
and characteristics (Cohen et al., 2010). It has also been proposed that ethnic and racial
background inﬂuence the level of HbA1c (Herman and Cohen, 2012; Cohen et al., 2010).
The main aim in diagnosing diabetes is to detect the possible state of hyperglycemia due
to insuﬃcient insulin action or decreased insulin sensitivity. This detection is important

might not be the best solution.
As the data also reﬂect, multiple measurements are
conducted for each patient. Evaluating all these data for potential patterns, represented
by the BMI, GAD measures, and other potential variable intervals, show it might be
possible to improve the understanding of the individuals progression rates.

Conclusion
Before initiating modelling the data was successfully imputed without creating large
changes and bias in the data.
The aim of this project was to identify and understand the importance of clinical markers
in the progression of T2DM patients. From the diﬀerent elements of the project work
including data exploration and modelling work the variables representing % HbA1c level,
BMI, weight, age, and time to mono- and dualtherapy were identiﬁed as the variables
important in understanding the target variable time to insulin, regarding correlation and
variance. In the prediction process time to mono- and dualtherapy were found to have
the greatest inﬂuence on predictions.
Based on the data exploration and the survival analysis, progression time for some sam-
ples might be reﬂected by clinical markers, or a combination of clinical markers, including
BMI and age. More work is needed in order to categorise these variables intervals and
further understand the progression patterns of the involved individuals.
Model performance for the full data set was RMSE = 3.13 and PCC = 0.63 (mean
of evaluated model) after removing TTI < 1 samples the performance improved. The
best performance was observed for the network with two hidden layers (RMSE = 3.017
±0.0016 and PCC = 0.6 ±0.0004). In general the networks with two hidden layers and
networks with backward selection implemented showed best performances.

Future perspectives
In future work for this framework of using a neural network to predict the time to insulin,
could include a more thorough evaluation of the individual clinical markers, and how they
are represented in the data and potentially represents smaller subgroups.
For future work, adding clinical measurements in addition to current baseline measures
could be interesting to introduce to the models, and evaluate if it could improve predic-
tion. In addition it would be relevant to change the set-up to potentially use baseline
values to predict time to monotherapy. Evaluation of measurements and potentially use
these progression patterns for prediction of time to dualtherapy and further prediction
on insulin treatment would be interesting.
With focus on the neural network architecture it would be interesting to explore the
possibilities in building a more complex structure and expand to deep learning or similar
frameworks. The complex structure might be able to recognise the heterogeneity features
of diabetes.
Another important factor in improving the potential of predicting T2DM progression
is to evaluate the parameters used for diagnosis. Based on observed GAD antibody lev-
els and rapid progressions, individuals categorised as T2DM patients might not be true
T2DM cases but rather LADA or MODY patients. A more distinct separation of these
cases could potentially improve understanding of progression.
The data is extensive, and hold potentially very important information for future mod-
elling work.
