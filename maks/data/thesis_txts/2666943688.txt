Abstract
The halophilic yeast Debaryomyces hansenii is a non-conventional yeast with a high biotechnolog-
ical potential due to its increased performance under harsh conditions such as high salt concentra-
tion and osmotic pressure. The absence of molecular tools for targeted gene editing in D. hansenii
has made it diﬃcult for researchers to fully understand the mechanisms in this yeast and limited its
applications in industrial bio-processes. In this study, D. hansenii sDIV115 strains were eﬀectively
transformed through electroporation and yellow ﬂuorescence protein was heterologously expressed
from an episomal vector. Furthermore, multiple single-guided CRISPR/Cas9 systems for targeted
genetic engineering were developed and proved high gene editing eﬃciencies (from 58% to 100%).
A constructed CRISPR/Cas9 system was applied for a gene knockout of KU70 through homolo-
gous recombination with a DNA repair template, proving the systems applicability for genetic loci
deletions. The functional CRISPR/Cas9 system, and the ability to introduce and express it eﬀec-
tively in the cells are major leaps in genetic and metabolic engineering of D. hansenii. This work
advances the molecular genetic tools enabling fundamental and applied research of D. hansenii,
hopefully in the near future unlocking the true biotechnological potential of this yeast.

Resumé
Den haloﬁle gær Debaryomyces hansenii er en ikke-konventionel gær med et højt bioteknologisk
potentiale på grund af dens øgede ydeevne under barske forhold med høj saltkoncentration og os-
motisk tryk. Manglen på molekylære værktøjer til målrettet genredigering i D. hansenii, har gjort
det vanskeligt for forskere at forstå mekanismerne i denne gær fuldt ud, og det har begrænset dens
anvendelser i industrielle bioprocesser. I denne undersøgelse, blev D. hansenii sDIV115-stammen
eﬀektivt transformeret gennem elektroporation og gult ﬂuorescensprotein blev heterologt udtrykt
fra en episomal vektor. Desuden blev ﬂere enkeltstyrede CRISPR/Cas9-systemer, til målrettet
genteknologi, udviklet og bevist høj genredigeringseﬀektivitet (fra 58% til 100%). Et konstrueret
CRISPR/Cas9-system blev anvendt til et gen-knockout af KU70 gennem homolog rekombina-
tion med en DNA-reparationsskabelon, hvilket beviser systemets anvendelighed til genetiske loci-
sletninger. Det funktionelle CRISPR/Cas9-system, samt evnen til at introducere og udtrykke det
eﬀektivt i cellerne, er store skridt i genetisk og metabolisk manipulation af D. hansenii. Dette arbe-
jde fremmer de molekylære genetiske værktøjer, der muliggør grundlæggende og anvendt forskning
i D. hansenii, hvilket forhåbentlig i den nærmeste fremtid åbner for det sande bioteknologiske
potentiale for denne gær.

Introduction
Non-conventional yeasts are great alternatives for various diﬀerent industrial bio-processes as they
have beneﬁcial phenotypic capabilities that can give fermentation advantages compared to Saccha-
romyces cerevisiae (S. cerevisiae), a common host within industrial biotechnology [28]. An example
of a non-conventional yeast with interesting capabilities is Debaryomyces hansenii (D. hansenii),
which for a long time has been recognized for its great biotechnological potential, mainly driven
by its ability to grow under very harsh conditions such as high salinity [3, 35]. Given this char-
acteristic, one of the advantages of applying D. hansenii in industrial bio-processes would be the
ability to use non-pure water sources such as sea water, and consequently reduce the production
costs and increase productivity [19, 27].
D. hansenii is currently used in biotechnology in production of e.g. xylitol and ﬂavenoids. However,
the production possibilities of this yeast remain unexploited and, despite the obvious potential of
D. hansenii, the ﬁeld of research in this organism has been sparse in recent years. The reason for
this is ultimately the absence of highly eﬃcient molecular tools for genetic manipulation, which
is the biggest unresolved limitation of D. hansenii [28, 35]. Most of the current knowledge on
gene functions and their corresponding proteins in D. hansenii is derived from studies in which
the genes were heterologously expressed in S. cerevisiae. Thus, it is crucial to ﬁnd eﬃcient genetic
and molecular tools and procedures to fully understand the physiology of D. hansenii and to be
able to engineer the yeast for improved biotechnological characteristics [35].
The evolution of CRISPR (clustered regularly inter-spaced short palindromic repeats) and CRISPR-
associated systems (Cas) has been a breakthrough within synthetic biology and has been of great
importance for genome editing in non-conventional yeasts. The CRISPR/Cas9 technology can be
one piece to the puzzle of solving the particular challenges in D. hansenii. It enables fast and
eﬃcient targeted gene editing by creating double stranded breaks inducing targeted alterations.
If simultaneously introducing a homologous repair template, it is simply cut and paste [21, 28].
However, this requires the ability to introduce a vector into D. hansenii, which is maintained in
the cells and expressed, which has proven diﬃcult over the years. Robust protocols and eﬃcient
processes are still needed for transformation of D. hansenii [35].
These challenges restraining fundamental research in D. hansenii will be attempted addressed in
this study.

Aim of study
The study aims to continue advancing molecular genetic tools in D. hansenii by establishing
functional standard protocols for transformation, heterologous expression, and targeted genetic
modiﬁcations with the CRISPR/Cas9 technology.
The study will attempt to achieve this aim through USER fusion cloning of a library of mod-
ular plasmids with diﬀerent variants of Cas9 and sgRNA modules targeting ADE2 and KU70
genes. After construction and veriﬁcation, the modular plasmids will be applied for transforma-
tion through electroporation of D. hansenii creating mutants of which genetic changes will be
analyzed. Furthermore, targeted gene knockouts with repair templates will be attempted.

Background theory
3.1
Debaryomyces hansenii
D. hansenii is a non-conventional hemiascomycetous yeast, which is known for its halophilic and
xerotolerant characteristics as it is able to tolerate conditions of high osmotic pressure, high salinity,
and low water activity [28].
D. hansenii has a good performance in a range of temperatures
(20–35°C) and pH conditions (3–10), and will grow in media containing up to 4M NaCl [28]. It is
a yeast commonly found on the surfaces of fermented food products such as various cheeses and
cured meat, but it was originally isolated from seawater [42, 12]. Apart from its application within
food products, D. hansenii is currently being applied in biotechnology to produce several products
such as xylitol, ﬂavenoids, and various mycocins eﬀective against Candida [35]. Furthermore, D.
hansenii is an oleaginous yeast capable of producing a variety of fatty acids and tends to accumulate
large amounts of glycerol when undergoing high osmotic stress [42]. The biotechnological potential
of D. hansenii has been highly acknowledged over the years due to its extremophilic characteristics
- mainly its ability to perform well under high salt concentrations and high osmotic pressure [3, 35].
3.1.1
Halotolerance
The halotolerant characteristic of D. hansenii has especially gained interest over the years and
several research groups have attempted to deﬁne the factors that give the yeast its halotolerance
[35]. Recently, Navarrete et. al (2020) showed that D. hansenii is halophilic rather than just
halotolerant as sodium salt rich media was necessary for optimal cell performance of this yeast
in bioreactors [27].
This enables cultivation in high salinity media such as seawater reducing
production costs by avoiding energy intensive sterilization processes and also increasing the overall
productivity. Additionally, with the high salt concentration the risk of contamination is limited
[19, 27].
The halotolerance has been correlated with cation ﬂuxes in D. hansenii. Genes coding for sodium
and potassium transporters involved in ﬂuxes of the cell have been identiﬁed in the genome [34].
Of these, two D. hansenii sodium eﬄux transporter genes, ENA1 and ENA2, were heterologously
expressed in S. cerevisiae increasing its ability to withstand high Na+ concentrations. However,
the increased tolerance was not equivalent to the halotolerant levels observed in D. hansenii [1].

In another study, two D. hansenii potassium inﬂux transporter genes HAK1 and TRK1 where

genetic and molecular tools and processes.
3.1.2
Molecular biology in D. hansenii
Some eﬀorts have been directed towards transformation and heterologous gene expression in D.
hansenii, yet the eﬃciency and reproducibility of the results published remain low and to this
day, eﬃcient and robust molecular tools are still lacking [28]. D. hansenii belongs to the fungal
CTG clade, which is a group of yeasts that mostly translate the CUG codon into serine, a polar
amino acid, instead of the expected leucine, a non-polar amino acid [31]. This particular codon
translation is common for the Candida genus, which D. hansenii is closely related to, and creates
a diversiﬁed protein capacity that enhances adaption to environmental conditions [26]. However,
it also entails codon optimization of a gene of interest if wanting to transfer it into D. hansenii
or another candidate of the CTG clade. The CTG codon optimization is done by replacing CTG
codons within a gene with another codon for leucine, which is necessary for the transformation
systems, resistance markers and reporter genes [32].
Gene expression is possible through expression on episomal vectors or through genome integration
methods performed by the DNA repair system in the host, which can be divided into two methods:
1) non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) and 2) homologous recombination (HR) [21, 28]. Both
methods are illustrated in Figure 1 where the DNA breakage is mediated by Cas9 and will be
described in more detail. With D. hansenii the challenge has been to introduce exogenous DNA
into D. hansenii and maintain the genes on a vector for expression. To maintain an episomal
expression vector in non-conventional yeasts, an autonomously replicating sequence (ARS), which
is a DNA replication starting point, must be built into the vector for the cells to recognize it as
genetic material and replicate it [4]. Usually a given ARS only functions in a few yeast species
as ARSs generally diﬀer in sequence to initiate function. Recently, an ARS that functioned in a
broad range of yeast species was identiﬁed giving it the name panARS [20].
Despite the genome of D. hansenii being sequenced in 2004 along with several other yeast genomes

been some of the factors hindering development of molecular biological tools for genetic approaches

3.2
CRISPR/Cas9 - an eﬃcient tool for targeted genetic engineering
Genetic and metabolic engineering is generally less trivial in non-conventional yeasts than in other
more classical production organisms such as S. cerevisiae and Aspergillus. Nevertheless, genetic
and metabolic engineering is crucial for the development and improvement of e.g. D. hansenii as
a production organism [28]. With successful metabolic engineering, yield, titer and production
rates can be increased, which requires an understanding of the host’s metabolism and available
molecular tools to perform genetic engineering [22].
Genome engineering has recently been revolutionized by CRISPR/Cas, with Cas9 being the most
commonly applied technique [14, 9]. The CRISPR/Cas9 system can be used in various organisms
with high eﬃcacy. For it to function properly it only requires an active Cas9 and a single guided
(sgRNA) [39]. The CRISPR/Cas9 systems enables fast and targeted genetic engineering as the
system generates double stranded breaks (DSB) in the organism in which the system is applied.
The DSB are repaired by the cells native repair system, but with random events that disrupt the
gene at target site [9]. The application of the CRISPR/Cas9 system is illustrated in Figure 1.

Figure 1:
CRISPR/Cas9 targeted gene editing - The CRISPR/Cas9 complex creates double
stranded breaks in the genome of the cell directed by the sgRNA, which is identical to a 20 bp pro-
tospacer immediately followed by the 5’-NGG-3’ protospacer adjacent motif sequence. The cell will repair
the DNA break with either non-homologous end joining that disrupts the gene or homologous recombi-
nation that can incorporate a repair template replacing the gene. Figure created with inspiration from
Cribbs et al. (2017) [7].
The sgRNA must be designed to match a 20 nucleotide protospacer sequence, which is identiﬁed
and localized immediately upstream of the protospacer adjustment motif (PAM) sequence. The
PAM sequence consist of three nucleotides NGG and this sequence is required for the Cas9 to cut
the DNA. Accordingly, any 20 nucleotide protospacer followed by the 5’-NGG-3’ PAM sequence
are possible targets for genomic engineering [9]. Once the Cas9 has been guided to the target locus
in the genome by the sgRNA and the PAM sequence is recognized, a DSB will occur in the DNA,
as seen in Figure 1. The gene editing event is occurring with assistance from the native repair
system using the NHEJ pathway or the homology directed repair using the HR pathway [21, 28].
Both methods are illustrated in Figure 1 where the DSB is mediated by Cas9. In the event of
NHEJ, the cell will try to rejoin the DNA strands despite little or no homologous regions, which
is prone to errors, thus with genetic changes to follow. In this way a gene may be inactivated

through small insertions or deletions of nucleotides. In the event of HR, the cell will incorporate
a donor DNA, i.e. a repair template with homologous regions and hence deleting the locus of a
gene or inserting a gene of interest [6, 9].
The preference for either the NHEJ pathway or HR pathway varies in yeasts. For S. cerevisiae
the HR pathway is very eﬃcient and only requires small homology regions of 38-50 bp ﬂanking a
gene of interest [23]. However, for non-conventional yeasts the NHEJ pathway is generally favored
over HR pathway, despite DNA repair templates being introduced and with increased length of
homologous regions. Thus, random integration has mostly been the strategy for non-conventional
yeasts. To combat this, various strategies to enhance HR or eliminate NHEJ have been tested and
e.g. the KU70 and/or KU80 disruption has improved targeted gene integration through HR in a
number of diﬀerent yeasts [9].
3.3
USER fusion cloning of multiple PCR products
The uracil-speciﬁc excision reagent (USER) fusion cloning technique is a rapid and eﬃcient method
for assembling multiple DNA fragments by using unique USER compatible complementary primer
overlaps [38]. The technique is a convenient alternative to other vector construction methods e.g.
through restriction digest and ligation-based cloning [11].
The USER fusion cloning technique is a ligase-free cloning method that relies on the USERTM
enzyme mix (New England BioLabs). This contains a uracil DNA glycosidase, which catalyses
uracil excision, and a DNA glycosylase-lyase endo VIII, which breaks the phosphodiester backbone
at the 3´ and 5´ of the site where uracil was excised creating the overhangs [2].
In combination with the above, a USER compatible plasmid vector backbone is required. The
compatibility relies on a USER cassette integrated in the plasmid vector. This is treated with
a restriction enzyme and a nicking enzyme preparing directed complementary overhangs to the
overhangs of the PCR products [11].
An overview of the USER fusion cloning technique and primer overlap sequences can be seen in
Figure 2. This ﬁgure illustrates cloning strategy applied throughout the CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid
construction of this study.

3.4
Transformation through electroporation
Transformation is an important technique within molecular and genetic biology. It is a technique
where a genetic alteration of a cell occurs as a result of the introduction of exogenous DNA. A
number of methods exist to transform intact cells, including several diﬀerent types of yeasts, and
have proven to be eﬃcient [15]. One method is transformation through electroporation where an
electric ﬁeld is applied facilitating the permeability of the cells to DNA. The obvious factors to
take into account during this process, are the electrical voltage level and the time of which the
pulse is applied. The electroporation causes a great stress to the cells, and ﬁnding an optimum of
time and voltage is critical to avoid cell death, but still obtain the desired eﬀect of cell permeability
[5]. The ability to transform D. hansenii through a promising protocol for electrotransformation
was published by Minhas et al. (2009 and 2019), and suggests applying a sodium phosphate buﬀer
containing dithiothreitol (DTT) to generate cell competency before applying the electrical ﬁeld of
2.3 kV and utilizing sucrose as the osmotic stabilizer [25, 24].

Experimental strategy and work
The experimental strategy and work conducted during this study can be divided into two main
parts being 1) expression vector construction and 2) transformation into D. hansenii. Both are

Materials and methods
5.1
Microorganisms and maintenance
DH5α competent E. coli cells were used for USER fusion cloning of various plasmids and plasmid
maintenance. The competent cells were produced by DTU Bioengineering and stored at -80°C
in 25% glycerol (v/v) and thawed on ice before use. After USER fusion cloning of various PCR
products, the cloned E. coli were grown on selective lysogeny broth (LB) plates (5 g/L Bacto Yeast
extract, 10 g/L Bacto Tryptone, 10 g/L NaCl, 20 g/L Bacto Agar, 100 mg/L ampicillin, Milli-Q
water) at 37°C over night (O/N). Afterwards cells were stored at 4°C.
D. hansenii CBS767 (sDIV115) was stored at -80°C in 25% glycerol (v/v) and provided by DTU
Bioengineering by streaking onto standard YPD plate (10 g/L Bacto yeast extract, 20 g/L Bacto
peptone, 20 g/L Bacto Agar, 2% D-(+)-Glucose (w/v), Milli-Q water) and incubated for 3-5 days
at 28°C and stored at 4°C.
5.2
DNA ampliﬁcation, puriﬁcation and veriﬁcation
5.2.1
PCR
Standard polymerase chain reaction (PCR) procedure was applied to amplify PCR fragments to
be combined and inserted into plasmids. All PCR products along with primers and templates used
are shown in Table 5 in Appendix C.
Every PCR product required that the following PCR mix was prepared in a PCR tube to a total
volume of 50 µl: 29 µl Milli-Q water, 10 µl 5X Phire Reaction Buﬀer (Thermo Scientiﬁc), 5 µl
dNTP at (2 mM) (DTU Bioengineering), 2 µl forward primer (10 µM), 2 µl reverse primer (10
µM), 1 µl DNA template, 1 µl PfuX7 DNA polymerase (DTU Bioengineering).
The PCR vial(s) were spun shortly before they were put in the PCR machine (Bio-Rad T100 Ther-
mal Cycler). The following program was run with sample volume set to 50 µl and lid temperature
set at 110°C: 2 min at 98°C, then 35 cycles of DNA separation at 98°C for 20 sec, annealing at 59°C
for 30 sec, and synthesizing at 72°C for 3 min (depending on length of longest band, 1 min/kb).
After the 35 cycles, samples were kept at 72°C for 5 min before stored at 12°C until collection.

Overlap extension PCR
A single overlap extension polymerase chain reaction (OE-PCR) was performed by adding 20 ng

5.2.3
Colony PCR
Colony PCR was applied both on D. hansenii transformants and E. coli clones. First, 6.5 µl sterile
Milli-Q water was added to a standard PCR tube. Then, with a sterile pipette tip, a small amount
of cell mass from a single colony was collected and dipped into the PCR tube containing the water.
For yeast colonies an initial lysis step was required, whereas for E. coli this was not required.
The lysis of yeast colonies was performed by placing the tubes in a PCR machine (Bio-Rad T100
Thermal Cycler) and running the following program: 5 min at 98°C, 30 sec at 4°C, and 10 min at
80°C before collection. Then, for both the D. hansenii and E. coli colonies, a PCR mix was added
to a ﬁnal volume of 15 µl: 7.5 µl Quick-Load Taq 2X Master Mix (New England BioLabs), 0.5
µl forward primer (10 µM), and 0.5 µl reverse primer (10 µM). The tubes were centrifuged with
a bench top centrifuge (Bio-Rad Mini centrifuge). Finally, the following PCR program was run
with sample volume set to 15 µl and lid temperature set at 110°C: 5 min at 94°C, then 35 cycles of
DNA separation at 94°C for 35 sec, annealing at 51°C for 45 sec, and synthesizing at 68°C for 3.5
min (depending on length of the longest band, 1 min/kb). After the 35 cycles, the samples were
kept at 68°C for 5 min before stored at 10°C until collection. After collection, the PCR fragments
were veriﬁed by gel electrophoresis and puriﬁed (Section 5.2.4 and 5.2.5).

Gel electrophoresis
The following procedure was applied for all gel electrophoresis: 1% agarose (w/v) in TAE buﬀer
was added into a mini tray or landscape tray placed in a casting stand followed by a comb casting
the wells (Embi Tec RunOne Electrophoresis System). Immediately after pouring agarose gel,

landscape tray respectively. The gel was placed into a RunOne system (Embi Tec) and additional
TAE buﬀer was added to the tank covering the gel. The wells were loaded with samples that had
been prepared in a 5:1 ratio with Bromphenol Blue, unless samples were ampliﬁed with Quick-Load
Taq 2X Master Mix (New England BioLabs). Furthermore, a standard 1kb ladder was applied
with fragment sizes of the following lengths for comparison (bp: 500, 1000, 1500, 2000, 3000,
4000, 5000, 6000, 8000 and 10000). Occasionally, a 100 bp ladder was used for shorter fragment
comparison. The gel electrophoresis program was set at 100V for 20 min after which the gel was
analyzed in a Gel DocTM XR+ imaging system (Bio Rad).

µg DNA) were added individually to a given cuvette and gently mixed. In selected cuvettes, 1

Transformations with ADE2 repair template
In Section 6.3 it was found that the CRISPR/Cas9 plasmids create DSB in the ADE2 gene of D.
hansenii and gene disruption events occur as a result of the NHEJ pathway. With the purpose of
promoting the HR pathway instead of the NHEJ to repair the DSB created, a repair template was
introduced during additional transformations.

along with their non-guided Cas9 plasmids pDIV489-490. The repair template was designed as a
deletion construct with a ﬂuorescing marker that would be integrated in the event of homology
directed repair. It was created with 65 bp homology regions immediately before and after the D.
hansenii ADE2 gene ﬂanking the mVenus CTG optimized gene coding for YFP. The results of
this transformation can be seen in Figure 10.

Discussion
In this study, D. hansenii was transformed through electroporation with a vector containing YFP,
which was heterologously expressed. The plasmid was maintained well in all transformants adding
another yeast species to the broad application of panARS in budding yeasts [20]. However, some
diﬀerences in colony size were found throughout the transformant populations. The issue was
predominantly observed in transformants with Cas9 expressing plasmids with sgRNA. Both white
and red colonies were occasionally much larger in size compared to the rest. Since the diﬀerences in
size disappeared on non-selective media, this indicates a plasmid copy number issue, which results
in a variable expression across cells likely caused by imperfect partitioning during cell division [22].
Contrary, when transformed with non-guided Cas9 expressing plasmids, the colonies were more
homogeneous in size, but overall colonies were smaller than the control transformants indicating
some Cas9 toxicity also reported by Spasskaya et. al (2021) in transformations of D. hansenii [40].
The transformation protocol was adapted from Minhas et al. (2009) with few modiﬁcations [25].
Overall the protocol functions well and promising plasmid uptake results were achieved. However,
there is room for improvement of the overall robustness. Various factors may be the reason for oc-
casional low eﬃciencies and failures in transformation with plasmid vectors previously transformed
with success. Using sorbitol instead of sucrose may be of interest as a signiﬁcant increased number
of transformants are reported [25] and was also applied in the recent publication with electrotrans-
formation of D. hansenii [40]. However, one could argue that the need is not to increase number
of transformants, but rather maintain a more steady outcome and achieving similar colony counts
per transformation. The cuvettes used in the electroporation event are possibly an issue as they
are worn down and the electrical ﬁeld may not be applied well in some cases. Also, the number of
transformations per experiment could be reduced to minimize time diﬀerences and avoid unwanted
mistakes. To minimize the eﬀect of uncertainties on the results, the number of replicates should
be increased and focused more on fewer experiments with selected CRISPR/Cas9 systems as the
overall proof-of-concept has been established.
The gene editing eﬀects when targeting ADE2 where not seen until one month of storage at 4°C,
and another one and a half month was required for a clear phenotypic result. This makes sense as
growth is slow at low temperatures. Growing the cells at 28°C could have been increased beyond
ﬁve days to avoid the time needed for the change in color to show. Otherwise, a growth medium
33
==PAGE:43==
Niklas Ladegaard Andersen - s153108
February 15, 2021
with sparse adenine availability could be applied for quicker results.
The multiple CRISPR/Cas9 systems with diﬀerent promoters were constructed as previous research
show the importance of the right choice of promoter [13, 36]. Generally, polymerase III promoters
are used for sgRNA expression and polymerase II promoters are used to drive Cas9 expression.
Schwartz et al. (2016) report variation in eﬃciencies depending on the chosen polymerase III
for sgRNA-tRNA expression in the oleaginous yeast Yarrowia lipolytica [39]. From the results
in this study, it seems that the choice of polymerase II promoter driving the Cas9 aﬀects more
the overall eﬃciencies, whereas the changes are minimal in the choice of promoter for sgRNA
expression. Surprisingly, low expression levels of Cas9 are favorable and will result in higher gene
editing eﬃciencies [36], which also was the case for Spasskaya et al. (2021) [40]. The expression
of CRISPR/Cas9 components under species-speciﬁc promoters have not shown improved gene
targeting eﬃciencies, which was reported necessary for C. lusitaniae [29], as the best performing
CRISPR/Cas9 systems in this study, all were expressed under the C. lusitaniae TDH3 promoter.
The ADE2 editing test in the recent published study shows an activity of 80 to 95% for the
CRISPR/Cas9 system with D. hansenii pTEF1-driven Cas9 expression [40], which is higher than
the species-speciﬁc systems created in this study.
HR is very eﬃcient in S. cerevisiae, whereas the NHEJ pathway is favored in many other yeasts
[22]. Previous studies have indicated that D. hansenii also prefers NHEJ over HR [37, 25]. This
tendency is also seen in this study, as most of the transformants applied NHEJ for DNA repair.
Out of 10 transformants with pDIV537 only one event of HR was found, as the ∆KU70 mutant was
successfully created with an integration of a repair template. The frequency is likely to be lower
as no event of HR was detected in 10 transformants of pDIV538, assuming that double stranded
break at target locus had occurred. Ideally, all colonies should have been tested for a quantitative
measure of frequency. A visible marker could be applied in the repair template to have an easier
measure of HR frequency in D. hansenii. Spasskaya et al. (2021) also concludes that NHEJ is more
frequent than HR in D. hansenii [40]. In the ADE2 repair template the YFP gene was ﬂanked by
two 65 bp homologous regions of the ADE2 locus which would make it possible to quantify the HR
frequency in D. hansenii transformants. A low HR frequency of up to 3% was detected, yet the
integration was not at target site, but instead randomly integrated. Thus, it is possible to create
mutants through random integration events, but it is less ideal than an integration at target site,
which was the case of the KU70 repair template. The diﬀerence in overhang sizes seems to have
34
==PAGE:44==
Niklas Ladegaard Andersen - s153108
February 15, 2021
an eﬀect, as the KU70 repair template had overhangs of ∼450 bp with no DNA insert, whereas the
ADE2 repair template had overhangs of 65 bp with the DNA insert being 775 bp. Spasskaya et al.
(2021) showed successful integration in ADE2 locus with a 117 bp homologous oligonucleotide with
4 mutations [40]. Optimizing the ADE2 repair template is necessary for a quantitative measure
of HR events. This should be done by ﬁnding an optimal ratio of homologous overhangs versus
length of DNA insert, as increasing the length of the overhangs improves targeting eﬃciency, while
increasing the length of the DNA insert reduces it [18].
To improve HR events a strategy of deleting the KU70 gene was applied in this study. In S.
cerevisiae, deletion of KU70 and KU80 does not increase the already frequent HR pathway, but
instead increases recombination defects [9]. However, the KU70-deﬁcient mutants of two oleaginous
yeasts showed higher HR frequency with defective NHEJ pathway [16, 17]. Furthermore, HR has
exponentially increased with independent deletion of KU70, KU80 and LIG4 [35]. The KU70/80
genes and the LIG4 gene are conserved in hemiascomycetes [35], thus the same approach may
improve HR frequency in D. hansenii enhancing gene knockouts, but has yet to be proven.
Recently, Navarette et al.
(2020b) reported that eﬃcient and robust molecular tools are still
lacking as the eﬃciency and reproducibility of the results published on D. hansenii transformation
remain low [28]. Because of the very promising results presented in this study and the research
published a month ago by Spasskaya et al. (2021) [40], which presents a similar approach of a
targeted CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing tool introduced and expressed in D. hansenii, the missing
eﬃcient molecular tools, enabling the possibility for metabolic engineering of D. hansenii, seem to
ﬁnally have been discovered.

8
Conclusion
The aim of this study was to continue advancing molecular genetic tools in D. hansenii by es-
tablishing functional standard protocols for transformation, heterologous expression, and targeted
genetic modiﬁcations with the CRISPR/Cas9 technology.
An electrotransformation protocol was successfully applied and D. hansenii heterologously ex-
pressed yellow ﬂuorescence protein on an episomal vector maintained in the cells. Furthermore,
19 out of 20 guided CRISPR/Cas9 systems with various promoters were applied and eﬃciently
(from 58% to 100%) disrupted the ADE2 gene and another CRISPR/Cas9 construct was applied
for homologous recombination with a repair template deleting KU70 in D. hansenii.
The results of this study are very promising, pushing boundaries of what has previously been
possible, which could lead to unlocking the true potential of D. hansenii.
9
Future perspectives
The ability to target multiple loci of D. hansenii is interesting for rapid gene knockout of multi-
ple genes in mutants. Several of the CRISPR/Cas9 systems, developed in this study, have been
prepared for multiplex gene editing with the addition of tRNAGly, but requires addition of other
sgRNA scaﬀolds. Also, the systems are yet to be tested to determine the multiplex gene editing
eﬃciencies. The sodium eﬄux transporter genes ENA1 and ENA2 and the potassium inﬂux trans-
porter HAK1, are obvious targets for gene knockouts as their eﬀects on the halophilic properties of
D. hansenii can be studied in the host rather than having them expressed heterologously in other
model organisms.
