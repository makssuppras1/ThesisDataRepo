Abstract
As the construction sector continues to place substantial pressure on the environment, contributing to
high levels of greenhouse gas emissions, resource consumption and waste generation, regenerative design
has emerged as a possible response to the need for systemic change. This thesis critically investigates how
regenerative design can be defined, operationalised and evaluated in the built environment, with a focus on
environmental performance.
The research is structured in three parts. First, a review of existing literature explores the foundations and key
principles of regenerative design, such as ecosystem restoration, systems thinking and place-based approaches.
While these values are widely shared, the review highlights diverse interpretations regarding scale, timeframes
and the role of human intervention.
Second, the thesis evaluates how certification frameworks, particularly the Living Building Challenge (LBC),
translate regenerative principles into practical criteria. Although the LBC aligns with many theoretical
foundations, it is limited by its focus on individual buildings and static compliance structure, which contrasts
with the dynamic and evolving nature of regenerative thinking.
Third, selected LBC criteria are applied to four Danish residential buildings, and these regenerative design
versions are compared to conventional references of the same buildings using Life Cycle Assessment (LCA).
The results show that regenerative strategies can significantly reduce environmental impacts such as global
warming, largely thanks to bio-based material choices. However, some impacts increase, including ozone
depletion and resource depletion, due to the additional technical components required. In addition, the
integration of decentralised solutions for energy and water does not consistently outperform centralised
systems, highlighting the importance of local context and design choices.
In conclusion, regenerative design offers meaningful theoretical principles, but remains conceptually broad
and challenging to fully operationalise. LCA can support design decisions, but must be complemented by
other tools to address the broader ecological, social and temporal aspects of regeneration. Rather than a
fixed goal, regenerative design should be seen as a direction to follow that requires contextual understanding,
iteration and integration across systems and scales.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Significance
Regenerative processes broadly refer to the restoration of places, systems or conditions to a better or more
resilient state. This concept is applied across various academic fields, including agriculture, economics,
education and business, and is often associated with systems thinking, long-term resilience and continuous
improvement (Buckton et al., 2023).
Within this broader understanding, regenerative thinking has been increasingly applied to the built environment.
In architecture and construction, regenerative design is commonly viewed as a progression beyond conventional
sustainability (Reed, 2007). Rather than aiming to reduce harm, it seeks to create systems that actively
restore ecosystems, enhance planetary health and community well-being, and contribute positively to natural
and social capital (Mang & Reed, 2012).
This shift responds to the urgent need for systemic change in the construction sector, which is a major
contributor to global environmental degradation.
Specifically, the sector is responsible for approximately 37% of global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions
(United Nations Environment Programme, 2024) and 42% of total energy consumption in the European Union
(European Environment Agency, 2024). Globally, buildings account for approximately 39% of energy-related
carbon emissions, with 28% resulting from operational energy use and 11% from the energy-related component
of embodied carbon in materials and construction processes (World Green Building Council, 2019). The
construction sector also consumes nearly 50% of annually extracted raw materials (World Green Building
Council, 2021), significantly contributing to biodiversity loss and ecosystem degradation.
In Denmark, the situation is comparable. The building sector is responsible for 17% of national GHG emissions
(excluding operational energy use), accounts for approximately 31% of national material consumption and
generates 40% of the country’s total waste (Rådet for Grøn Omstilling, 2024).
With the global building stock expected to double by 2050 due to urbanisation and population growth, the
environmental pressure of this sector is likely to intensify (World Green Building Council, 2019). Without a
fundamental transformation of how buildings are designed, constructed and operated, the sector risks pushing
the planet beyond critical climate and ecological thresholds. In addition, according to the Intergovernmental
Panel on Climate Change (2018), staying within the 1.5°C warming target set by the Paris Agreement requires
an 80–90% reduction in building-related emissions by 2050. This calls for a deep rethinking of architectural
practices, material sourcing and energy strategies, with an emphasis not only on minimising harm, but on
achieving positive environmental contributions.
National regulations are starting to address this issue. In Denmark, the revised Building Regulation (BR18)
mandates the calculation of a building’s climate impact over its entire life cycle (Bygningsreglementet DK,
2023). Currently, large buildings must remain below a threshold of 12 kg CO2-eq/m2/year, including both
embodied and operational emissions. From July 1st, 2025, more stringent thresholds will apply to most
building types, with an average new cap of 7.1 kg CO2-eq/m2/year, accompanied by a plan to gradually lower
this limit in the coming years (Social- og Boligministeriet, 2024).

Introduction
1.2
Research Objective
In this context, regenerative design has gained attention among architects, engineers and policymakers as
a promising alternative to conventional sustainability. However, despite its growing visibility, regenerative
design remains a loosely defined concept. The term is widely used in articles and design campaigns, often
accompanied by imagery of green roofs or plant-covered facades, but lacks a clear, widely agreed-upon
definition of what truly constitutes a regenerative project (Cole, 2012a). This ambiguity increases the risk of
misinterpretation, oversimplification and, ultimately, greenwashing.
In response, this thesis critically explores the proposed definitions of regenerative design and investigates how
this approach is operationalised and assessed in the context of the built environment. The study is structured
in three main phases:
• First, it explores the origins and conceptual development of regenerative design through a critical
literature review. This review identifies key definitional elements and conceptual dimensions, highlighting
areas of convergence and divergence across academic sources. Based on this analysis, the study investigates
whether a set of principles can be derived for evaluating regenerative performance from an environmental
perspective.
This leads to the first research question: How has the concept of regenerative design emerged and evolved
in scientific literature, and to what extent is there academic consensus regarding its definition and core
criteria?
• Second, it reviews certification systems that provide practical criteria for assessing whether a building
or project fulfils the definitions established in the first phase. The analysis examines how these systems
formalise and measure regenerative performance, and to what extent their criteria align with the
theoretical principles identified through the literature review.
This leads to the second research question: To what extent can existing certification systems support
the operationalisation of regenerative design principles, and what are their conceptual and practical
limitations?
• Third, it applies Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) to evaluate the environmental performance of regenerative
strategies in practice. This is done by comparing regenerative and conventional building scenarios across
a selection of Danish residential case studies. The goal is to determine whether these interventions
lead to tangible environmental improvements and how they align with current national climate targets.
The analysis considers trade-offs and identifies both the benefits and the limitations of regenerative
strategies in real-world applications.
This leads to the third research question: Can regenerative design lead to measurable environmental
benefits in the built environment, and what unintended consequences might it involve in addressing
environmental challenges, and to what extent can LCA capture these benefits, given its methodological
limitations and the broader ambitions of regenerative practice?

Methods
2
Methods
This section outlines the methodology followed in this thesis to answer the research questions and achieve the
objectives introduced in Chapter 1. The methodology is divided into three main phases, corresponding to the
three parts of the study, as shown in Figure 1:
1. Critical literature review: A review of academic sources was conducted to identify and compare key
definitions and conceptualisations of regenerative design.
2. Evaluation of certification frameworks: A comparative assessment of existing frameworks was
carried out, with a specific focus on the Living Building Challenge (LBC), which was selected as the most
comprehensive and operationalised certification system currently available for regenerative buildings.
3. Life Cycle Assessment of case studies: Four residential buildings, differing in typology and in the
use of construction materials, were modelled and assessed using LCA to quantify the environmental
impacts of regenerative strategies based on selected LBC Imperatives.
Figure 1: Overview of the methodology followed in the thesis, structured into three phases.
2.1
Critical Literature Review on Regenerative Design
This phase establishes the conceptual foundation for the thesis by examining how regenerative design is
defined and understood in academic literature.
2.1.1
Literature Search and Selection
The literature review was based on an iterative search carried out between February and March 2025 using
research platforms such as DTU Findit, Google Scholar and ResearchGate. The objective was to identify
sources that address regenerative design from a conceptual, definitional or methodological perspective,
particularly in relation to the built environment. The search included peer-reviewed articles, book chapters
and institutional reports written in English. A summary of the main search queries and keywords used in the
literature search is provided in Appendix A.1.
Among the sources consulted during the research phase, a final selection of 10 core texts was identified for
in-depth analysis. These texts were selected based on their recurring appearance in the literature, their
influence on other works and their direct relevance to the research focus. The selected publications are
presented in Table 1.

2.1.2
Analytical Process
To ensure consistency, the selected literature was analysed using a structured comparative framework based
on the following steps:
• Origin tracing – Identifying the disciplinary contexts and historical periods in which the term
“regenerative design” first emerged.
• Definition extraction – Collecting and comparing explicit and implicit definitions of regenerative
design across sources.
• Thematic mapping – Identifying conceptual similarities and differences based on recurring dimensions,
including:
– Human–nature relationship
– Key principles (e.g., circularity, co-evolution, net-positive impact)
– Core perspective (ecological, technological or social focus)
– Temporal framing (static goal vs. ongoing process)
– Spatial scale (building, site, region)
– Social and community dimension (e.g., presence of human well-being, social equity or community
engagement as part of regeneration)

2.1.3
Qualitative Reflections
To complement the academic analysis, informal conversations were conducted with PhD students at Henning
Larsen, an international architecture firm with a strong focus on sustainable design, and with professionals
from Living Future Europe, the European branch of the International Living Future Institute (ILFI). ILFI
is a non-profit organization that develops and manages certification programs such as the Living Building
Challenge (LBC), aimed at advancing regenerative design in the built environment. These dialogues offered
insight into how regenerative principles are interpreted and applied in practice, as well as the challenges
encountered when attempting to define or quantify regenerative performance. In particular, the professionals
from Living Future Europe shared project documentation and internal evaluation material, providing valuable
contextual input for the discussion. While these reflections and documents were not formally analysed, they
served as qualitative input to frame the practical dimensions explored throughout the thesis.
2.2
Evaluation of Certification Frameworks
This section describes the methodology used to evaluate whether existing certification systems reflect the
regenerative design principles identified in the literature.
2.2.1
Initial Screening of Frameworks
As a preliminary step, a brief review of existing frameworks and certifications claiming to support regenerative
approaches was conducted. The aim was to understand the broader landscape of regenerative-related tools and
to identify whether any of them offered clear, structured and verifiable criteria suitable for further analysis.
The sources consulted included general web searches, references found in the academic literature and informal
input from the same PhD students mentioned in Section 2.1.3.
Since the goal of this thesis is to model and evaluate the environmental impact of regenerative strategies through
LCA, only frameworks providing measurable and verifiable requirements were considered methodologically
appropriate. Any frameworks that failed to meet these requirements were omitted from further consideration.
An overview of all the screened frameworks and the rationale behind their exclusion is provided in Appendix A.2.
2.2.2
Focus on the Living Building Challenge
Following the initial screening, the LBC was selected as the only framework suitable for in-depth analysis due
to its explicit alignment with regenerative design principles and its provision of detailed criteria within a
performance-based structure. These features make it a suitable reference framework for the modelling and
evaluation carried out in this study.
The analysis was based on the LBC 4.1 Program Manual and official documentation published by the
International Living Future Institute (2024). The evaluation focused on all 20 Imperatives, which are grouped
under seven thematic categories known as “Petals”: Place, Water, Energy, Health & Happiness, Materials,
Equity and Beauty. Each Imperative was reviewed to identify its goal, specific compliance criteria and
implementation requirements.

2.2.3
Comparison with Literature-Based Principles
After identifying the LBC criteria, a comparative analysis was carried out to assess their alignment with
the regenerative design principles established in the literature review. A matrix was developed to map
each Imperative against key conceptual elements, allowing the identification of areas of alignment, partial
inclusion or omission. This comparative mapping served as a bridge between the theoretical understanding of
regenerative design and the operational structure of LBC. In addition to the mapping, a qualitative evaluation
was conducted on the broader characteristics of the certification system. It also included a critical reflection
on the strengths and limitations of LBC as a practical tool for implementing regenerative design.

2.3.5
Life Cycle Assessment Framework
The LCA methodology adopted in this thesis follows the principles and requirements defined in ISO 14040
(International Organization for Standardization, 2006).
The functional unit, defined to provide a consistent basis for comparing the environmental performance of
different building scenarios, is: the provision of housing by a residential building for one person in Denmark
over a 50-year reference period. Energy and water use per person were also modelled over the same timeframe
to ensure coherence and comparability across all case studies.
The following tools were used to support the modelling and analysis:
• LCAbyg 2023 (version 5.4.0.5), developed by BUILD (Aalborg University) (BUILD, 2023), was
used to model construction materials and perform building-level LCA calculations in line with the
Danish Building Regulation BR18. The life cycle inventories (LCIs) for all four case studies were
initially provided in LCAbyg file format and were modified as needed for the Regenerative scenario.
The modelling of materials relied on both product data from the LCAbyg database—based on GENDK
and ÖKOBAUDAT datasets—and manufacturer-specific Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs).
• SimaPro (version 10.2.0.0), based on the Ecoinvent 3.11 database, was used to model energy and water
systems not supported by LCAbyg.
LCA in this thesis follows a cradle-to-grave system boundary, covering the following life cycle stages in
accordance with European Standard EN 15978:2012 (European Committee for Standardization, 2012), which
specifically addresses LCA in the building sector:
• A1–A3: Product stage (material extraction, processing and manufacturing)
• A4–A5: Construction stage (transport and on-site assembly). As these stages are not mandatory under
BR18, they were not included in the original LCAbyg files downloaded from official sources and were
therefore manually integrated to ensure consistency with the cradle-to-grave boundary.
• B4, B6: Use stage (material replacement and operational energy use)
• C3–C4: End-of-life stage (waste treatment and disposal)

The following stages were excluded from the calculation, as they are not required under BR18: B1 (Use), B2
(Maintenance), B3 (Repair), B5 (Refurbishment) and C1–C2 (Demolition and transport to disposal site).
Lastly, Module D, which covers potential benefits and burdens beyond the system boundary (such as material
recycling or energy recovery), was calculated but reported solely as supplementary information and excluded
from the comparative assessment to ensure alignment with BR18 regulatory requirements.
It is important to note that LCAbyg models only building-related operational energy use (e.g., heating,
ventilation, lighting) under Module B6. In contrast, the LBC requires modelling total energy use, including
energy demands from residents’ activities. This additional energy demand was modelled in SimaPro and
integrated into the final analysis. The same applies to domestic water consumption, which is not included in
building-level LCAs and was therefore modelled separately in SimaPro.
Lastly, for the sole purpose of comparing Reference and Regenerative scenarios across all case studies with
respect to future climate targets defined by the Danish Building Regulation BR18, a separate assessment was
performed. In accordance with BR18, climate impacts are reported as annual emissions per gross floor area,
expressed in kg CO2-eq/m2/year. This additional evaluation is presented independently from the disaggregated
results by domain (e.g., energy, water, materials) and includes only the life cycle stages and the components
mandated by the regulation.
2.3.6
Environmental Indicators
The environmental performance of the buildings was assessed using the impact categories implemented in
LCAbyg, based on the characterisation method defined by the European standard EN 15804+A1 (European
Committee for Standardization, 2013). For systems modelled externally in SimaPro, the EPD 2018 (version
1.07) method was used to ensure methodological consistency with the LCAbyg framework.
The following indicators were included in the analysis:
• Global Warming Potential (GWP) (kg CO2-eq): Quantifies the contribution of greenhouse gas
emissions to climate change over a 100-year time horizon. In accordance with EN 15804+A1, biogenic
carbon flows are accounted for using the -1/+1 approach: carbon uptake during biomass growth is
considered a negative emission (–1) and its release at end-of-life is treated as a positive emission (+1).
Notably, EN 15804+A1 reports GWP as a single aggregated value, without distinguishing between
fossil and biogenic carbon contributions.
• Ozone Depletion Potential (ODP) (kg CFC-11-eq): Measures the potential of emissions to degrade
the stratospheric ozone layer.
• Acidification Potential (AP) (kg SO2-eq): Reflects the potential of airborne emissions to cause
acidification in soil and aquatic systems.
• Eutrophication Potential (EP) (kg PO43-eq): Assesses the potential contribution of nutrients to the
over-enrichment of aquatic ecosystems, leading to oxygen depletion and ecological imbalance.
• Photochemical Ozone Creation Potential (POCP) (kg C2H4-eq): Indicates the tendency of
volatile organic compounds and nitrogen oxides to form ground-level ozone under sunlight.
• Abiotic Depletion Potential for Elements (ADPE) (kg Sb-eq): Quantifies the depletion of
non-renewable mineral resources, expressed in antimony equivalents.
• Abiotic Depletion Potential for Fossil Fuels (ADPF) (MJ): Represents the cumulative use of
fossil energy carriers such as coal, oil and natural gas.

3.1
Conceptual Foundations of Regenerative Design
This section presents the results of the literature-based conceptual analysis outlined in Section 2.1 of the
methodology, focusing on the historical roots, definitions and key principles of regenerative design.
3.1.1
Origins of Regenerative Design
The concept of regenerative design was first introduced in the built environment field in the early 1990s. One
of the earliest structured definitions was proposed by John Tillman Lyle in his book Regenerative Design
for Sustainable Development (Lyle, 1994). In this work, Lyle defined regenerative design as the replacement
of linear systems with “cyclical flows at sources, consumption centres and sinks.” These systems allow for
“continuous replacement, through [their] own functional processes, of the energy and materials used in their
operation” (Lyle, 1994). Lyle’s thinking was influenced by developments in agriculture (Mang & Reed, 2012),
particularly the work of Robert Rodale, who coined the term “regenerative organic agriculture” in the late
1970s. This approach focused on improving soil health, increasing biodiversity and supporting long-term
ecosystem resilience (Rodale Institute, 2020).
Following Lyle, the notion of regenerative design evolved in parallel with emerging movements such as Cradle
to Cradle and Biomimicry, which share similar principles based on circularity, resource renewal and the
emulation of natural processes in human design (Mang & Reed, 2017). In 1995, the Regenesis Group expanded
Lyle’s ideas into a broader conceptual framework known as regenerative development (Mang & Reed, 2017).
This approach retained the ecological focus of earlier work but introduced a systemic view that emphasised
the co-evolution of human and natural systems. It proposed that built interventions should not only minimise
negative impacts but actively enhance the vitality and resilience of the social-ecological context in which they
are embedded.
This broader interpretation gained importance with the work of Bill Reed, who argued that the built environ-
ment should shift from mitigating harm to fostering regeneration (Reed, 2007). Reed clarified distinctions
among conventional, green, sustainable and regenerative approaches, positioning regeneration as an evolution
beyond sustainability.
In recent years, the idea of designing for a net-positive rather than a net-zero impact has gained growing
attention in both academic and professional contexts (Cole, 2015). This shift reflects a broader ambition: not
only to reduce harm, but to create built environments that actively restore and enhance the ecosystems and
communities they belong to (Cole, 2012a).
3.1.2

The literature review of selected scientific publications reveals that regenerative design remains a concept
without a commonly agreed-upon definition. Its meaning is interpreted in diverse ways, often shaped by the
disciplinary background, methodological approach or philosophical orientation of each author. In several cases,
the term is also used interchangeably with regenerative development, further complicating efforts to establish
a unified definition. Definitions from selected authors in the academic literature are presented in Table 7.

3.1.3
Common Ground and Divergences in the Literature
Despite the absence of a standardised definition, the literature reveals a set of recurring principles that help
to outline a shared understanding of what regenerative design entails. Most authors agree that regenerative
design:
• Closes resource cycles (energy, water, materials);
• Actively improves the local ecosystem (biodiversity, soil and air quality);
• Supports the community (social spaces, well-being, economic resilience);
• Adapts and co-evolves with the natural and social context.
These features represent the conceptual core around which most definitions converge. However, a deeper
analysis also uncovers important differences in how the concept is interpreted, particularly regarding its focus,
time scale and the role of human intervention. The following sections explore both the common ground and
the divergences identified across the selected sources.

Agreements
Overall Goal: Regenerative design is consistently described as an approach that aims to actively restore
and enhance both natural ecosystems and human well-being. As Mang and Reed (2020) summarise, “every
regenerative project seeks to achieve an enduring and mutually beneficial relationship between the human
and natural systems in a particular place.”
Systems Thinking: Regenerative design focuses not on isolated improvements but on supporting the health
and vitality of the system, understood as the interaction between human activities, the built environment
and the surrounding ecosystems. This requires an insight into the interconnections among these elements and
a design approach that reflects and reinforces these relationships. This idea is supported by Cole (2012a),
who notes that regenerative design, unlike conventional approaches, “seek[s] understanding of whole systems.”
Place-based Design: Regenerative design starts from the understanding that every place is different. Rather
than applying standard solutions, it focuses on creating responses that reflect the specific environmental, social
and cultural characteristics of a location. As Mang and Reed (2020) explain, regenerative design “is grown
from the uniqueness of a place.” Its goal is to restore and support local natural systems by aligning built and
natural flows not only within the site itself, but also in connection with larger areas such as neighbourhoods,
river basins or regions.
Beyond Sustainability: Regenerative design is commonly framed as a progression beyond conventional and
sustainable approaches. Cole (2012a) distinguishes between green, sustainable and regenerative design as
“doing less harm,” “doing no harm,” and “doing some good,” respectively. In this view, sustainability represents
a neutral baseline, necessary but not sufficient for regenerative action.
Process: Regenerative design is seen as a dynamic and ongoing process. Rather than viewing design as a
linear sequence that ends with construction, authors highlight the importance of continuous engagement,
learning and adaptation. Regenerative approaches involve setting in motion processes that evolve in response
to changing ecological and social conditions, requiring long-term responsibility and participation from both
designers and users.
Disagreements
Scope: The literature reveals that the concept of regenerative design has evolved substantially over time,
shifting from an emphasis on technical efficiency to one centred on ecosystem services. Early definitions, such
as Lyle (1994), focused on technical principles of self-sufficiency and the closure of energy and material cycles,
closely aligned with resource efficiency and performance-based approaches. From the mid-2000s onward,
the concept shifted towards a more ecosystem-oriented understanding that expands the scope to include
cultural, social and economic regeneration, viewing human and natural systems as interconnected and mutually
supportive.
Scale: While there is agreement that regenerative design should go beyond the individual building, the
scale at which it operates is interpreted differently across sources. Some authors frame it primarily at the
community level, while others advocate for a broader systems perspective. Du Plessis (2012) emphasises
that the regenerative process works across scales, starting from the macro-level (e.g., bioregion) and moving
toward the local.
Time: While the ongoing nature of regenerative design is acknowledged, there is no clear consensus on what
this means in practice. Zari and Jenkin (2009) suggests that regenerative processes may take place over “80
to several hundred years,” reflecting a deep temporal commitment, while most authors remain unspecific

regarding timeframes. Cole (2012a) and Mang and Reed (2020) argue that regenerative responsibilities extend
beyond construction, requiring systems that support the project’s capacity to remain regenerative over time,
although concrete strategies for ensuring this continuity vary or are left unspecified.
Human Role: Most of the literature considers humans as integral to regenerative design, viewing them
as part of the ecosystem and responsible for contributing to its renewal. Authors such as Zari (2012) and
Mang and Reed (2020) highlight that regeneration involves not only physical aspects but also psychological,
social, cultural, political and economic dimensions of human well-being. In contrast, earlier perspectives like
that of Lyle (1994) emphasise the autonomy of natural systems and suggest that regeneration should occur
independently of human intervention, envisioning ecosystems restored to their natural functioning prior to
human disturbance. These positions represent different emphases on the human role within regenerative
thinking: one that focuses on supporting natural resilience with minimal interference, and another that
recognises humans as active agents essential to sustaining and enhancing ecosystem renewal.
3.1.4
Criteria and Operationalisation of Regenerative Design
The absence of a clear and shared definition limits the application of regenerative design, as the term remains
open to subjective interpretation. The literature reviewed highlights a lack of consensus on how to translate
the definitions and conceptual foundations of regenerative design into concrete and operational criteria.
Cole (2012a) notes that although the core ideas of regenerative design are relatively well understood, their
implementation in real-world projects remains largely undefined. Similarly, Clegg (2012) points out that
academic discourse on regenerative development tends to focus heavily on theoretical aspects, with limited
references to practical applications.
Several sources (e.g. Mang and Reed, 2020; Reed, 2007) emphasise that regenerative design is inherently
context-specific and process-oriented. It is described as an approach that responds to the ecological, social and
cultural characteristics of each place, rather than applying universal performance benchmarks. In contrast
to conventional sustainability frameworks, often based on checklists and standardised criteria, regenerative
design resists standardisation (Cole, 2012a). Reed (2007) outlines a three-phase process that offers guidance
on how context-specific criteria may be developed in regenerative projects:
1. Understanding the unique “patterns of place” – identifying local ecological cycles, social dynamics and
cultural narratives;
2. Translating those patterns into design strategies – aligning interventions with the site’s natural systems
to support co-evolution;
3. Embedding continuous feedback and adaptation – ensuring that the design remains responsive and
regenerative over time.
This emphasis on local specificity and long-term engagement is the key reason why the development of
universal indicators remains particularly challenging. At the same time, the systemic nature of regenerative
design raises fundamental questions about the appropriateness of translating it into measurable metrics or
certification schemes. When a context-specific and evolving concept is converted into standardised tools,
there is a risk of reducing its transformative ambition. Rigid checklists may undermine the core essence of
regenerative design, transforming it into a compliance exercise rather than a dynamic process (Cole, 2012a),
as certification frameworks risk shifting the focus from meaningful change to the fulfilment of predefined
requirements. For these reasons, the lack of prescriptive guidelines does not represent a gap to be filled, but
rather reflects an intentional aspect of the paradigm itself. Its adaptive nature allows for diverse interpretations,

reinforcing the conceptual richness of the term.
Moreover, regenerative outcomes are not limited to tangible or easily measurable impacts. They also encompass
relational and qualitative dimensions, including community empowerment, cultural revitalisation and long-
term social resilience, as well as environmental aspects such as biodiversity restoration and ecosystem resilience.
These outcomes are difficult to isolate, challenging to quantify, and often not fully captured by conventional
assessment methods.
Despite these challenges, the need for structure remains essential. Without some form of shared guidance or
benchmarks, the concept of regeneration risks being applied inconsistently or losing credibility. This ambiguity
creates space for potential misuse or superficial adoption and raises significant obstacles for evaluation,
comparability and integration into design frameworks. A degree of standardisation is therefore necessary to
promote clarity, support comparability and safeguard the integrity of the concept.
For this reason, the term regenerative has been used throughout this thesis with critical awareness. Rather
than aiming to determine whether a building can be labelled as regenerative in absolute terms, the analysis has
focused on identifying strategies that reflect and support regenerative principles. This approach acknowledges
the conceptual ambiguity of the term while also recognising the importance of developing practical tools that
can inform design choices and evaluation efforts. In this context, regenerative design is understood not as a
static label, but as a direction of improvement, guided by the capacity to generate positive outcomes over
time.
3.2
Evaluation of certification: LBC
As detailed in Section 2.2, the LBC (International Living Future Institute, 2024) is the only established
certification system identified that explicitly seeks to translate regenerative principles into measurable
outcomes.
The comparative analysis between regenerative principles and the LBC Imperatives reveals that the LBC
aligns closely with many of the key concepts identified in the literature. It promotes net-positive environmental
impacts, supports place-based and contextual solutions, and embeds social, ethical and community values
throughout its framework. Several Imperatives also reflect systems thinking, biophilic design and ecosystem
regeneration. At the same time, the analysis identified aspects of the LBC that are less closely aligned
with regenerative design principles. One key limitation is its primary focus on the individual building scale,
which makes it less suitable for addressing outcomes that emerge at larger scales, such as neighbourhoods or
ecosystems. Moreover, since the certification is tied to a single point in time, there are no built-in mechanisms
to support ongoing learning or adaptation, which limits the framework’s ability to respond to changing
conditions over the long term.

4.1
Results in Relation to Existing Literature
4.1.1
Limits of Net-Positive Design and Alternative Pathways
The results of this study indicate that achieving positive environmental performance through regenerative
design remains a complex challenge. Although several Regenerative scenarios performed better than their
conventional ones, the improvements were not consistent across all impact categories. These outcomes suggest
that regenerative design, as currently implemented, does not automatically lead to environmental benefits,
either in absolute or in relative terms.
A similar conclusion is reached by Attia (2018), who uses LCA to evaluate whether buildings that claim to be
regenerative can achieve net-zero or net-positive performance. Nonetheless, it is important to first highlight
key differences in methodological approach. In particular, Attia accounts for the temporary storage of biogenic
carbon in timber and applies a time-based discounting of end-of-life emissions. This modelling choice allows
wooden components to retain a net-negative GWP over the full life cycle. In contrast, the methodology used
in this thesis follows the EN 15804+A1 standard, which applies a more immediate –1/+1 accounting approach
without a time delay. Furthermore, in line with current regulatory practices, this study does not account
for potential benefits from material reuse, recycling or energy recovery. As a result, the estimated climate
benefits from bio-based material substitution are more conservative compared to Attia’s findings. However,
even when favourable assumptions are applied, as in Attia’s work, the outcomes support the same conclusion:
regenerative design does not inherently guarantee net-positive environmental performance. Instead, the actual
benefits depend strongly on methodological choices and implementation strategies.
Given these limitations, it becomes essential to explore complementary or alternative strategies for achieving
regenerative goals. The LBC identifies renovation as a viable option for reducing environmental impacts, as
it enables the reuse of materials, avoids land consumption and minimises the need for new infrastructure.
This was highlighted during conversations with professionals from Living Future Europe, who shared internal
documentation on Cal Guerxo, a renovation and partial reconstruction project in Spain (International Living
Future Institute, 2023). The project, which is currently the only European building certified under the LBC,
achieved a 56% reduction in embodied carbon compared to a hypothetical new building of similar size and
function. This outcome was made possible through the preservation of structural components and the use
of low-impact materials. Although this thesis focuses on new construction, renovation clearly emerges as a
complementary and promising strategy to assess and mitigate the environmental impacts of the building
sector.
4.1.2
Timber-based Materials: Environmental Benefits and Trade-offs
Among the Regenerative scenarios analysed in this study, the most consistent environmental improvements
were observed in cases involving significant substitution of conventional construction materials with timber-
based alternatives. These results align with a growing body of literature identifying wood as a promising
material for reducing the environmental impact of buildings, particularly regarding climate change.
Andersen (2024), based on a comprehensive life cycle assessment of 45 wooden buildings, finds that wood
construction can substantially reduce GHG emissions compared to conventional typologies. Similarly, Cabeza
et al. (2021) demonstrate that timber generally has lower embodied energy and carbon values than materials
such as reinforced concrete and steel, confirming its potential as a low-impact alternative in climate-related
Page 26 of 77
==PAGE:34==
4
Discussion
impact categories. However, these benefits are not without limitations. Andersen (2024) also highlights that
timber-based systems can contribute more significantly to other impact categories, including ozone depletion,
due to the use of adhesives and chemical treatments in engineered wood products. Thus, the author stresses
the importance of assessing a broad set of indicators to avoid unintended environmental burden shifting.
Concerns have also been raised regarding land use and biodiversity. As timber construction scales up, the
demand for forestry resources increases, which can lead to habitat disruption and reduced ecological resilience.
Hansen et al. (2024), through a consequential LCA of expanded timber use in Denmark, shows that although
GHG emissions may decline, the overall land occupation and potential biodiversity loss rise substantially.
Similarly, Mishra et al. (2022) warns that large-scale transitions to wood-based construction may lead to the
simplification of ecosystems, if natural forests are replaced by managed plantations. These studies underline
that material choices must be considered not only for their immediate benefits, but also for their long-term
ecological consequences.
In summary, these findings support the results of this thesis while also reinforcing the need for careful
evaluation of material strategies within regenerative design. While timber offers considerable advantages in
selected categories, it cannot be viewed as universally beneficial. Its environmental value depends on design
choices, sourcing practices and how broader trade-offs are managed in each project.
4.1.3
Energy Systems and the Importance of Context
The results of this study show that the environmental benefits of decentralised energy systems depend strongly
on the local context. In a Danish setting, scenarios involving on-site PV generation did not consistently lead
to improved performance across impact categories. This is primarily due to the low carbon intensity of the
future projected Danish electricity grid, which includes a high share of renewables. As a result, the emissions
avoided by replacing grid electricity were not sufficient to offset the environmental burdens associated with
the production and installation of PV panels and battery systems.
These findings are consistent with recent literature. Martinopoulos (2020) demonstrates that in countries with
already decarbonised electricity systems, such as Sweden, Iceland and Norway, the installation of building-
integrated PV systems can lead to net negative environmental performance. This outcome is attributed to the
combination of low baseline emissions and limited solar irradiance, which results in lower electricity generation
and reduced environmental return. Kristjansdottir et al. (2016) also reports similar findings in Norway, where
electricity is primarily generated from hydropower.
In contrast, Zheng et al. (2021) show that in areas with fossil fuel-dominated grids, such as parts of the
United States, decentralised PV systems can deliver substantial carbon savings. However, their study also
highlights how these benefits decrease over time as national grids become progressively decarbonised. For
example, in projections for 2040, the carbon advantage of rooftop solar systems, especially when coupled with
battery storage, becomes marginal or even negative in regions where the grid is already largely supplied by
renewables. These findings underline that both spatial and temporal factors are critical when assessing the
environmental value of decentralised energy systems.
The technology used also plays a key role. In this thesis, only PV systems were considered. However, hybrid
configurations that combine multiple renewable sources, such as solar and wind, can reduce dependence on
batteries and improve environmental performance. Aberilla et al. (2020) find that such systems generate
lower impacts compared to PV-only setups, primarily because they reduce the size and frequency of battery
charging cycles. These insights reinforce the importance of designing context-specific solutions that take into
Page 27 of 77
==PAGE:35==
4
Discussion
account not only the grid mix and solar potential, but also the types of technology deployed and the overall
system design.
Taken together, these results suggest that while decentralised energy generation can contribute to regenerative
goals, its effectiveness is highly dependent on place-specific conditions. Regenerative design therefore requires
careful assessment of both current infrastructure and projected future developments to determine whether
decentralised systems are appropriate.
4.1.4
Water Systems and Trade-offs
The environmental performance of decentralised water systems is as well highly context-dependent. In the
single-family case studies examined, decentralised solutions such as RWH and greywater reuse sometimes led
to higher impacts in categories like GWP and fossil energy use. These outcomes are primarily attributed to
the material and energy required for the construction and operation of components like storage tanks, pipes
and treatment units.
Supporting these findings, Faragò et al. (2019) conducted an eco-efficiency evaluation of community-scale
rainwater and stormwater harvesting in Aarhus, Denmark. The study found that decentralised systems
increased impacts in categories such as ecotoxicity and climate change due to extensive material use and lower
treatment efficiency. Moreover, Yan et al. (2018) demonstrated that decentralised RWH systems, despite their
potential for potable water savings, may result in higher environmental burdens compared to centralised
systems due to the energy consumption and material inputs required for on-site treatment devices.
However, the effectiveness of decentralised water systems also depends on building density, topography and
climate condition. As previously discussed, in multi-family buildings, these solutions may not be beneficial.
In contrast, centralised systems may be less efficient in rural or complex terrains, where long pipelines and
pumping requirements increase environmental burdens. In such contexts, decentralised solutions can offer
both environmental and cost advantages. Risch et al. (2021) found that in low-density areas, centralised
wastewater infrastructure led to significantly higher resource depletion and energy use, supporting the case
for more localised approaches. Lastly, decentralised solution can be beneficial in drought regions where water
conservation is a critical issue. An existing LBC certified building, Desert Rain, demonstrates that it is possible
to meet self-sufficiency goals while being situated in an arid area (International Living Future Institute, n.d.).
This example shows that achieving self-sufficiency is possible by having large storage tanks to collect all the
water during the few heavy rain periods and using it throughout the rest of the time.
In conclusion, while decentralised water strategies can contribute to saving water and regenerative goals, their
net benefits must be critically assessed in light of local conditions, system design and resource inputs.
4.2
Contribution and Value of the Study
This thesis proposes a structured pathway to explore regenerative design in the built environment by connecting
conceptual analysis with environmental quantification. While many academic contributions tend to focus
either on theoretical framings or on technical applications, this study attempts to bridge these two aspects
by moving from a critical literature review to a comparative LCA of real residential buildings. The use of
LBC as a reference framework plays a key role in supporting this transition. Its structure offers a set of
practical criteria that helps translate broad regenerative intentions into building-scale interventions that can
be modelled and evaluated.
By documenting this process across different case studies, the study contributes to ongoing discussions on how

regenerative ambitions can be operationalised in practice. It helps clarify what regenerative design practically
involves and prevents misuse of the term as a decorative or loosely defined label. By linking design intentions
to measurable outcomes, the study offers a step toward reducing the risk of greenwashing. At the same time,
it highlights the complexity of regenerative design and the absence of any guarantee of improvement in terms
of environmental performance, revealing instead the presence of trade-offs across impact categories.
Finally, the study links the emerging design approach to key challenges currently faced by the building
sector such as emissions reduction, material efficiency and alignment with national carbon limits. Rather
than offering fixed solutions, the study encourages a critical perspective on how regenerative ambitions are
interpreted, helping frame them in relation to real-world constraints and policy demands.
4.3
Limitations and Directions for Future Research
This study has several limitations, reflecting both methodological choices and the complexity of evaluating
regenerative design. At the same time, these limitations suggest possible directions for future research.
The first limitation concerns the scope of the analysis. While regenerative design is understood as a multidi-
mensional approach, this study focused exclusively on environmental performance. Other relevant aspects
such as social inclusion, cultural regeneration or economic viability were not addressed. Future research
could integrate complementary methods, for example, social life cycle assessment or tools that account for
biodiversity and community well-being, to support a more holistic understanding of regenerative outcomes.
A second limitation relates to the context and scale of the analysis. The study was conducted on a small
number of residential buildings within Denmark. Although the case studies were selected to represent different
typologies and material systems, the findings are not intended to be generalised. Expanding the scope to
include buildings in other geographic, climatic and socio-economic contexts would help assess the broader
applicability of regenerative strategies. Moreover, since regeneration aims to restore relationships between
people and ecosystems, its potential is more likely to emerge at larger scales — such as neighbourhoods or
watersheds — where ecological and social dynamics intersect more directly. These spatial scales remain largely
underexplored in current assessment tools, despite being central to regenerative thinking.
A third limitation relates to the modelling approach. Several regenerative strategies, particularly those
involving water and energy systems, had to be represented using proxy datasets and generalised assumptions
for users consumption due to the limited availability of detailed data. As a result, the environmental profiles
presented may not fully reflect the complexity of real-world performance. Future studies could address
this issue through empirical data collection and post-occupancy monitoring, enabling more accurate and
site-sensitive modelling.
Finally, the study did not assess the technical feasibility or economic viability of the Regenerative scenarios.
Considerations such as cost, scalability and maintenance requirements, especially for decentralised solutions
related to water and energy systems, were not included in the analysis. However, these factors are crucial to
understanding whether regenerative strategies can move beyond conceptual proposals and be implemented
effectively in practice in a large scale. Addressing these aspects in future research would support a more
grounded and realistic assessment of regenerative design.
4.4
Implications for Stakeholders
The findings of this study offer useful insights for various stakeholders involved in advancing regenerative
practices in the built environment.

For architects and design professionals, the results highlight both the opportunities and challenges associated
with integrating regenerative principles. Identified trade-offs underscore the need for careful evaluation tailored
to each project. Regenerative design should not be understood as a fixed set of solutions, but rather as a
process that responds to place-based conditions and evolves through critical reflection. In this context, LCA
can serve as a valuable tool to support informed decision-making by testing assumptions and comparing
design alternatives based on environmental performance. Its role is particularly relevant during early-stage
design, where flexibility is greater and interventions can be adapted more effectively.
Policy-makers and regulatory authorities may find these results relevant to current debates on carbon reduction
and climate policy. While regenerative approaches can contribute to meeting environmental targets, the study
suggests that they do not inherently guarantee compliance with future carbon thresholds, especially when
evaluated through existing regulatory metrics. In the Danish context, where climate regulations are becoming
increasingly stringent, recognising approaches that aim not only to minimise harm but to deliver positive
outcomes could encourage more ambitious forms of innovation. This may also include expanding evaluation
criteria beyond global warming potential alone, in order to reflect a broader set of environmental priorities
and system-level interactions.
For certification bodies, the analysis offers critical insights into the potential and limitations of existing
frameworks. The LBC served as a useful reference to structure and guide the design modifications in this
study, but the results also indicate that its building-level focus may not fully capture the broader intentions
of regenerative practice. Evolving certification tools to allow for greater flexibility, responsiveness to context
and post-occupancy engagement could strengthen their role in supporting regenerative goals.
Finally, for researchers and educators, the study emphasises the value of combining conceptual frameworks
with applied methodologies. Regenerative design remains a developing field and further case-based exploration
is needed to better understand how its principles perform in practice. Expanding the methodological toolbox,
refining modelling approaches and fostering interdisciplinary collaboration can help build a more robust
foundation for research and teaching in this area.

5
Conclusion
This thesis investigated how regenerative design can be defined, applied and assessed within the context of
residential buildings, with a specific focus on environmental performance. The research combined a literature-
based conceptual analysis, an evaluation of certification frameworks and a comparative LCA of four Danish
case studies in which regenerative strategies were implemented.
The literature review highlighted a shared conceptual foundation across academic sources, including systems
thinking, circularity, place-based design and the ambition to generate net-positive outcomes. However,
significant divergence remains in how regeneration is interpreted and applied across spatial and temporal
scales. In the absence of a universally agreed definition or standardised method of assessment, translating
regenerative principles into concrete design criteria continues to pose a challenge.
Among the frameworks examined, the LBC emerged as the most comprehensive structure for operationalising
regenerative design at the building scale. It reflects many of the principles identified in the literature and
served as a practical reference in this thesis. Nevertheless, its fixed compliance model, focus on individual
buildings and lack of post-occupancy mechanisms limit its ability to fully capture the long-term and systemic
nature of regeneration.
The LCA of the four case studies showed that Regenerative scenarios generally outperformed conventional
ones in several environmental impact categories, particularly GWP, AP and EP. These benefits were largely
attributable to the use of bio-based materials. However, the analysis also revealed clear trade-offs. In all cases,
Regenerative scenarios resulted in higher impacts in ODP and ADPe, primarily due to the increased use of
material-intensive components such as insulation systems, photovoltaic panels and batteries. Furthermore,
in the Danish context, decentralised water and energy systems did not consistently offer environmental
advantages. This was mainly due to the additional materials required for on-site infrastructure and the already
high performance of the national water and energy grids.
Importantly, while most Regenerative scenarios led to lower GWP values, few were able to meet the more
ambitious targets set by future versions of the Danish Building Regulation BR18. This finding suggests that
regenerative design, as modelled in this study, is not sufficient to guarantee compliance with national climate
policy and broader systemic efforts and innovation remain essential.
The study also acknowledges its limitations, underlining the need for interdisciplinary frameworks that
combine environmental modelling with other forms of evaluation, including social, ecological and economic
aspects. As a result, the findings presented here reflect only a partial picture of what regenerative design
might entail in real-world conditions. Future research should focus on integrating quantitative and qualitative
evaluation tools, exploring regeneration at larger spatial scales and deepening the empirical understanding of
how regenerative design can support environmental and societal transformation in practice.
In conclusion, this thesis reinforces the idea that regenerative design is not a fixed outcome but a direction
and a dynamic and evolving approach that seeks to restore and enhance the relationship between buildings,
people and ecosystems. Its implementation requires attention to context, openness to change and continuous
evaluation. The strategies explored in this study represent first steps towards regenerative intent but also
reveal the practical, technical and conceptual challenges involved in achieving it.

A.2
Excluded Conceptual Frameworks Review
During the preliminary screening, several conceptual frameworks were identified that offer meaningful
contributions to regenerative development discourse. These approaches promote systems thinking, place-based
strategies and long-term transformation. However, they do not function as formal certification systems with
standardised criteria, measurable performance indicators or third-party validation. For this reason, they were
not included in the LCA certification-based comparative analysis presented in Section 2.2.
The frameworks listed below were considered during the theoretical review but excluded from the quantitative
comparison due to the absence of certifiable structures:
• Regenerative Development and Design (Regenesis Group)
Developed over more than two decades by the Regenesis Group (2024), this framework encourages a
process-oriented methodology rooted in living systems thinking. Its central focus is on identifying the
“place-sourced potential” of a site to guide context-specific, co-evolutionary interventions. Although
influential, it does not include prescriptive metrics, formal benchmarks or any mechanism for third-party
certification. Its primary function is educational, offering capacity-building programmes such as The
Regenerative Practitioner Series and The Regenesis School.
• REGEN – A Tool for Regenerative Thinking
Described by Svec et al. (2012), REGEN was conceptualised in collaboration with the U.S. Green
Building Council as a web-based tool to facilitate early-stage regenerative design conversations. It
aimed to help project teams explore system-level interconnections and map strategies to ecological and
social components. However, the tool was never operationalised and the authors explicitly clarified that
REGEN was not intended as a rating or certification system.
• Perkins&Will Regenerative Design Framework
This framework, presented by Cole et al. (2012), is part of Perkins&Will’s Living Design philosophy.
It integrates an issue-driven perspective with a procedural structure that uses reflective questions
to explore ecological and social dynamics. The approach intentionally avoids standardised scoring or
certification mechanisms, positioning itself as a driver for broader design thinking rather than a tool for
performance validation.
• LENSES (Living Environments in Natural, Social and Economic Systems)
Developed at Colorado State University’s Institute for the Built Environment and detailed by Plaut et al.
(2012), LENSES provides a structure for dialogue and visioning through three lenses: Foundational,
Flows and Vitality. It helps designers teams reflect systemically and define pathways from degenerative to
regenerative outcomes. Although it supports project guidance and learning, LENSES avoids quantitative
scoring and does not offer any certifiable or externally verified assessment.

A.4
Certification Frameworks - Relevance of LBC Imperatives in Case Studies
This appendix presents a qualitative assessment of the 20 Imperatives of the LBC. For each Imperative, a
brief description of its intent is provided, followed by a discussion of whether and how it was addressed in the
Regenerative scenarios developed for the selected case studies. As outlined in Section 2.3.2, each Imperative
was classified based on whether it could be: (i) assessed quantitatively through LCA modelling, (ii) assessed
qualitatively based on design assumptions or narrative evaluation or (iii) not assessed due to limitations in
data availability or incompatibility with the system boundaries. The descriptions of each Imperative and their
associated requirements are based on the official LBC documentation (International Living Future Institute,
2024), which provides comprehensive information for further consultation.
The evaluations presented in this appendix refer specifically to the two case studies based on real-world
buildings—MF_C and SF_W—as they provide adequate information on floor area, construction typology and
contextual elements that allow for a grounded interpretation of the place-based and technical requirements
associated with the LBC Imperatives. For the remaining two case studies, SF_C and MF_W, which were
developed as hypothetical models for the Wood in Construction project by Rambøll, such an evaluation was
not feasible due to the absence of site-specific and design-related data. It is assumed, however, that similar
considerations could be applicable to these buildings if sufficient project-specific information were available.
I01 – Ecology of Place
This Imperative requires that projects avoid development on sensitive ecological land (e.g., prime farmland,
wetlands, greenfields) and enhance the ecological integrity of the site. A baseline ecological assessment must be
conducted, followed by the development of an Adaptive Management Plan to restore and improve ecological
functions over time. In this study, Imperative I01 was not assessed for either case study. Compliance would
require access to detailed ecological baseline data, including habitat mapping and social equity analysis, none
of which were available from the original project datasets.
I02 – Urban Agriculture
This Imperative mandates that a defined portion of each project site be allocated to food production. The
specific requirement is determined by the LBC Transect classification system, which categorises project sites
on a scale from L1 (natural habitat) to L6 (dense urban core). Given that no information on food production
was provided for either case study, Imperative I02 was not represented in the LCA. However, a qualitative
assessment was performed based on the site area, building type and the designated Transect:
• Case Study MF_C – Multi-family Building: situated in Sønderborg and categorised as Transect L4,
this site would be required to dedicate 10% of its total area (~280 m2 out of 2,800 m2) to food
production. While implementation could be theoretically feasible—through rooftop planters or courtyard
gardens—the site’s urban density, limited unbuilt space constrained practical integration.
• Case Study SF_W – Single-family House: located in a low-density area aligned with Transect L2, the
site offers over 830 m2 of unbuilt land, allowing for the requirement of approximately 200 m2 to be
easily met.
Residential buildings must also include provisions for emergency food storage for two weeks per occupant.
However, emergency food resilience measures were not addressed in either case study.
I03 – Habitat Exchange
This Imperative requires that projects compensate for the environmental impact of land use by contributing
to the permanent protection of an equivalent or greater area elsewhere, typically through financial support to

land trusts or conservation organisations. Since the requirement pertains to financial contributions rather
than physical design or operational performance, it falls outside the scope of both qualitative and quantitative
evaluation within the system boundaries of this study. Consequently, this Imperative was excluded from
assessment.
I04 – Human-Scaled Living
This Imperative aims to reduce reliance on fossil fuel-based transportation by promoting walkability, access
to cycling infrastructure and human-powered mobility. Requirements vary by Transect and project type
and include limits on surface parking, provisions for secure bicycle storage and integration with pedestrian
networks. For multi-family projects, specific interventions to reduce single-occupancy vehicle use are also
required. Due to the primarily spatial and behavioural nature of this Imperative, it was not included in
the quantitative LCA. However, a qualitative review was conducted for the multi-family case study MF_C
based on available layout plans and descriptive project information. Located within Transect L4, the site is
subject to a 15% surface parking limit. The project provides 29 car parking spaces (around ~452 m2) on
a 2,800 m2 site, marginally exceeding the allowable 420 m2. A reduction of just two parking spaces would
suffice for compliance. The project performs well on cycling infrastructure, offering 30 covered bike spaces for
approximately 64 residents. Other requirements, such as pedestrian pathways, shared mobility services or
public transport proximity, were not documented and evaluated.
I05 & I06 – Responsible Water Use + Net Positive Water
Imperatives I05 and I06 were addressed quantitatively in the thesis to the extent allowed by available data
and modelling tools.
The LBC requires that 100% of a building’s water demand be supplied through on-site sources, including
captured rainwater, greywater reuse or other natural, closed-loop systems. In addition, all water must be
treated without the use of chemicals and potable water can’t be used for non-potable use, such as irrigation.
Reliance on municipal water supply and treatment is allowed only after all viable on-site alternatives have
been implemented. In such cases, the project must still demonstrate at least a 25% reduction in potable water
consumption compared to baseline standards. If municipal infrastructure is used for wastewater treatment
and does not meet LBC performance criteria (defined as chemical-free treatment, on-site or watershed-
scale ecological benefit, and no PVC use) the project is expected to compensate for its impact through a
“handprinting” mechanism. This entails supporting water efficiency improvements in another building within
the same watershed. This financial compensation strategy was not included within the scope of this thesis.
The Regenerative scenarios also addressed stormwater management, which, according to the LBC, must
be fully managed on site, without chemical treatment. The LBC further requires the implementation of a
resilience strategy capable of supplying potable water for all regular occupants for at least one week via
on-site storage.
Finally, a Water Conservation Plan must be developed, incorporating best-in-class water fixtures and leak
detection systems, but this element was not included in the scope of the thesis analysis.
I07 & I08 – Energy + Carbon Reduction + Net Positive Carbon
Imperatives I07 and I08 were addressed quantitatively in this thesis to the extent permitted by the available
data and modelling framework. These Imperatives require a comprehensive reduction of environmental impact
by targeting both energy consumption and embodied carbon emissions associated with building materials
and construction processes.
First, the LBC requires that buildings generate at least 105% of their total annual energy demand using

==PAGE:51==

on-site renewable energy systems. This requirement applies to both regulated energy uses (e.g., space heating,
mechanical ventilation) and unregulated loads (e.g., appliances, plug loads). Although buildings may remain
connected to the grid, they must maintain a net-positive energy balance over the course of one year, with
surplus electricity exported to the grid. In cases where full compliance through on-site generation is not
technically feasible—due to high energy intensity or spatial limitations—the project may apply the “Off-site
Renewable Exception,” which allows the procurement of certified renewable electricity from off-site sources. In
addition, LBC requires residential buildings to achieve a 50% reduction in total annual energy consumption
compared to a project-specific baseline. This baseline must be based on a representative building of similar
size, function, occupancy profile and climatic conditions.
For embodied carbon, the Imperatives require at least a 20% reduction in emissions associated with construction
materials. The assessment must cover life cycle stages A1 to A5, which include raw material extraction,
processing, transportation and construction. Where available, Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs)
have been used in this analysis to support the analysis and quantify emissions reductions. In the Regenerative
scenarios developed within this study, the strategy to reduce embodied carbon focused on the use of
alternative materials, particularly through the replacement of conventional high-carbon materials with
bio-based components, selected for their lower embodied emissions.
Finally, the Imperatives require full offsetting of all embodied carbon emissions associated with life cycle
stages A1–A5. This may be achieved either by incorporating carbon-sequestering materials into the building
design or by purchasing verified carbon offsets from an ILFI-approved provider. This final step was not
included within the scope of the thesis.
I09 & I10 – Healthy Interior Environment + Performance
These Imperatives address the quality of the indoor environment by promoting adequate daylight access,
non-toxic materials and indoor air quality, including requirements for air testing and cleaning protocols. These
aspects were not assessed in this study, as the analysis did not include operational monitoring, post-occupancy
evaluations or emissions data for interior finishes and products.
I11 – Access to Nature
This Imperative requires that building occupants have a continuous and meaningful connection to nature
through direct views, access to outdoor spaces and the integration of biophilic design elements. It also includes
requirements for post-occupancy evaluation of user experience. This Imperative was not assessed in this
analysis.
I12, I14 & I15 – Responsible Materials, Responsible Sourcing and Living Economy Sourcing
These three Imperatives address the environmental, health and social impacts associated with the extraction,
production, procurement and disposal of building materials. Their shared objective is to promote transparency,
reduce material toxicity, support ethical and local supply chains, while also contribute to a circular and
regenerative built environment.
In this thesis, several key requirements were assessed quantitatively based on available EPDs and material
specifications:
• FSC-certified wood: At least 80% of wood products must be certified by the Forest Stewardship Council
(FSC). Where EPDs included FSC-related data, an estimation was made of the proportion of certified
wood used.
• Traceable sourcing: Products with known and verifiable sourcing were prioritised. Manufacturer locations

sourcing thresholds.
• Local procurement thresholds: The LBC defines distance-based sourcing requirements and materials
must be sourced within 500 km, 1,000 km or 5,000 km, at a specific percentage. Due to the lack of cost

I13 – Red List
Imperative I13 requires that a minimum of 90% of new materials, calculated by cost, do not contain any
chemicals listed in the Living Building Challenge Red List. This list identifies substances known to pose
serious risks to human health and the environment and applies a strict concentration threshold of 100 ppm
(0.01%). In the context of this study, compliance with the Red List could not be verified for either case
study due to the lack of detailed chemical composition data for most construction products. Although some
of the materials used in the EPDs are declared compliant with the European “REACH Regulation” (2025)
(Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals), this cannot be considered equivalent
to Red List compliance. REACH applies a less stringent threshold of 0.1% and regulates a more limited set of
substances. In addition, the lack of disclosure could simply reflect a gap in reporting, rather than a definitive
indication of hazardous content. Furthermore, many of the chemical substances targeted by this Imperative,
particularly those commonly found in adhesives, sealants, coatings and surface treatments, are rarely included
in standard LCA datasets of materials. Given these limitations, this Imperative was not assessed in either
case study.
I16 – Net Positive Waste
This Imperative requires that projects minimise waste generation and support a circular economy by
implementing strategies for prevention, reuse and long-term material conservation. A key requirement is the
diversion of construction and demolition waste from landfill for each material stream. In the present study, no
Materials Conservation Management Plan or salvage actions were identified in the case study documentation
and the reuse of construction materials and the separation of post-occupancy waste fell outside the scope of
the analysis. As a result, this Imperative was excluded from assessment.

A.5.1
Construction Materials: Inventory, Substitution and Scenario Modelling
This section outlines the main modifications introduced in the domain of construction materials for each
case study, comparing the Reference and Regenerative scenarios as briefly described in Section 2.3.3. These
changes were implemented to reflect the intent of LBC Imperative I07, aimed at reducing embodied carbon,
as well as Imperatives I12, I14 and I15, which promote responsible sourcing, local procurement and material
conservation, as further detailed in Appendix A.4.
Inventory Modelling Approach
The strategy adopted in this thesis to reduce embodied carbon is based on the substitution of conventional
materials with lower-impact alternatives, primarily bio-based or responsibly sourced products.
The inventory modelling was carried out using LCAbyg 5.4, which requires inputs for each construction
element, including: Element category, Construction group, Product type and Quantities. Materials were
selected from either the Ökobau.dat 2020 II database or, where available, imported from manufacturer-specific
EPDs. These inputs capture the environmental burdens associated with the Product stage (A1–A3) and
End-of-life stages (C3–C4). Some products also report values for Module D (benefits and loads beyond the
system boundary), although this information is excluded from the comparative analysis. Each product’s service
life was selected manually based on manufacturer data or standard reference values. If the product lifespan
was shorter than the building’s assumed reference period (50 years), Module B4 (material replacement) was
automatically included by LCAbyg.
For Stage A4 (Transport), distances between production sites and construction sites were estimated manually
and transport was consistently modelled as road freight by truck >26 tonnes (EURO VI). For Stage A5
(Construction), information on material waste, electricity use during on-site construction and volume of soil
excavation was added manually, based on literature values and assumptions, as detailed later in this appendix.
The complete life cycle inventory (LCI), including all quantities, sources and modelling parameters, is provided
in the digital supplementary folder (Appendix C).
Rambøll Strategy for Bio-based Transition
This material substitution approach was inspired by the methodology developed in the Wood in Construction
project conducted by Rambøll (2024). This study evaluated the climate impact of replacing conventional
building materials with timber-based alternatives across various Danish building typologies. Each typology
was modelled in four variations—referred to as Case 1 through Case 4—which represent successive stages in
the transition from conventional to bio-based construction. The transition logic is illustrated in Figure 11.
All methodological details used in this study are publicly available in the original Rambøll report (in Danish),
cited here for reference.

used. For the Rambøll-based cases (SF_C and MF_W), where site locations are not publicly specified, the
same locations were assumed as those used for the corresponding case library typologies (single-family or

Additional LBC-Related Indicators
As described in Appendix A.4, additional requirements from the LBC were taken into consideration, particularly
those associated with Imperatives I12, I14 & I15. These indicators support a broader interpretation of
regenerative performance that includes material transparency, certification and regional sourcing criteria.
In this thesis, these indicators were assessed for the Regenerative scenarios of the SF_W and MF_C case
studies, as summarised below:
• FSC-certified wood: This criterion was fully met in MF_C, where approximately 95% of the wood-based
components used in the Regenerative scenario were explicitly declared as FSC-certified in their EPDs.
In contrast, in SF_W, only about 70% of construction wood was certified, not fulfilling the LBC
requirement.
• Traceable sourcing: Where possible, products with EPDs were selected in the Regenerative scenarios to
ensure transparent information about the material’s origin, transport mode and manufacturing method.
• Local procurement threshold: The LBC establishes that a significant portion of materials should be

regenerative cases: 86% of materials in SF_W and 89% in MF_C were sourced within 5000 km, based

A.5.2
Energy Systems: Demand, Supply and Scenario Modelling
This appendix presents the modelling approach adopted for the energy domain across the four case studies,
comparing the Reference and Regenerative scenarios. The interventions implemented in the Regenerative
scenarios were developed in alignment with the intent of Imperatives I07 and I08 of the LBC, which require a
reduction in energy demand and the supply of at least 105% of the building’s annual energy needs through
on-site renewable generation whenever feasible, as described in Appendix A.4.
Energy Demand Estimation
The first step in the analysis was to estimate the annual energy demand for each case study, both in the
Reference scenario and in the Regenerative variant, which applies a reduction target as required by the LBC.
Energy demand includes two components: domestic electricity use by the occupants and electricity used for
the building’s technical systems.
As explained in Section A.3, data for operational energy use in SF_W and MF_C were retrieved from the
official energy reports for each case, respectively Trykprøvning & Energimærkning Danmark ApS, 2019, 2021.
In contrast, for SF_C and MF_W, no such data were available in the original Rambøll documentation.
Therefore, the operational values for these buildings were estimated based on the DTU Master’s thesis by
Therkelsen and Johansen (2025), which modelled the buildings using standard assumptions. It is important
to note that the MF_C case study uses district heating to supply space heating, while all other buildings rely
on air-to-water heat pumps. As a result, heating energy is accounted for as electricity in SF_C, SF_W and
MF_W, while it appears separately for MF_C. In the Regenerative scenarios, the operational energy demand
for single-family houses was slightly increased to reflect the higher heating needs of timber-based construction.
This adjustment was also derived from the DTU thesis, which showed that buildings with wooden structures
can require additional energy due to thermal performance.
For domestic electricity use, only MF_C included resident consumption in its energy report. For the other
buildings, average electricity use per Danish household was obtained from national statistics Danish Energy
Agency (2024) and adjusted to reflect the number of occupants in each case. The benchmark value used
was 2777 kWh/year for a two-person household. In the Regenerative scenario, a 50% reduction in domestic
energy use was applied to comply with LBC requirements. Additionally, the electricity needed for on-site
water treatment and distribution was included in the total, as calculated in Section A.5.3.

On-Site Renewable Energy Production – Regenerative Scenario
In line with the LBC, buildings should prioritise the generation of renewable electricity on-site and are
required to produce at least 105% of their annual electricity demand, whenever feasible. Grid connection
remains allowed under the LBC, when electricity exported to the grid is greater than the amount imported
over the course of the year. When full on-site production is not achievable, it is permitted to purchase the
remaining electricity from verified off-site renewable sources.
In this thesis, PV systems were selected as the preferred strategy for on-site electricity generation. Battery
storage was included in all cases to support short-term autonomy, although the LBC does not specify the
required duration of storage. The PV technology used in the modelling was selected from market-available

period of this study is 50 years and the declared service life of the photovoltaic system is 25 years, the full
environmental impact of the installation, including all materials, was modelled twice in the LCA.

A.5.3
Water Systems: Demand, Supply and Scenario Modelling
This appendix describes the modelling approach adopted for the water domain across the four residential
case studies, comparing the Reference and Regenerative scenarios. The interventions implemented in the
Regenerative scenarios were developed in alignment with the intent of Imperatives I05 and I06 of the LBC,
which promotes fully on-site water supply and treatment where feasible. In situations where full on-site
implementation was not possible due to spatial constraints or urban context, connection to municipal systems
was maintained. In these cases, the Regenerative scenarios follow LBC requirements for a minimum 25%
reduction in water demand. Other LBC requirements are not included in the modelling, as discussed in
Appendix A.4.

the number of estimated residents in each building. The value adopted was 105 litres per person per day, as
reported by DANVA (2022). Table 25 summarises the resulting annual consumption for each case study.

Centralised Water System – Reference Scenario
The Reference scenario represents a conventional centralised water system, in which all water supply and
wastewater treatment processes are managed by municipal facilities. In this configuration, potable water is
assumed to be sourced entirely from groundwater, as is standard practice in Denmark, and delivered to end-
users after simple treatment processes such as filtration and disinfection (DANVA, 2022). Wastewater is then
collected and conveyed to centralised wastewater treatment plants (WWTP), in line with the conventional
municipal infrastructure in Danish urban settings. This scenario serves as a baseline for evaluating the
environmental benefits and trade-offs of decentralised and on-site solutions explored in the Regenerative
scenarios.
For the two case studies with known locations—SF_W (located in Grenaa) and MF_C (in Sønderborg)—the
actual service providers and treatment plants were identified through public data. In Grenaa, the water
supply is managed by Norddjurs Vand A/S and wastewater is treated at the Fornæs Wastewater Treatment
Plant (Norddjurs Kommune, n.d.), both operated by AquaDjurs (n.d.). In Sønderborg, services are managed
by Sønderborg Vandforsyning A/S (Sønderborg Forsyning, n.d.). For the remaining case studies, average
national values were adopted.
This scenario is referred to in the thesis as “Reference-Central” and was modelled in SimaPro using a
combination of specific and average datasets. The modelling included both infrastructure (e.g., network and
facilities) and operational processes (e.g., electricity for treatment and pumping).

Decentralised Water System – Regenerative Scenario
In line with the LBC, the Regenerative scenarios aim to prioritise on-site water supply and treatment through
decentralised systems, eliminating the use of potable water for toilet flushing, irrigation and laundry.
To support this approach, water demand was divided into potable and non-potable uses. Based on Rajski et al.
(2024), approximately 57% of total household demand corresponds to potable uses (e.g. drinking, cooking,
personal hygiene), while the remaining 43% is allocated to non-potable functions (e.g. toilet flushing, laundry,
irrigation). This breakdown was used for all case studies to evaluate the feasibility of rainwater harvesting
(RWH) as a decentralised supply strategy.
In this thesis, rainwater was chosen as the on-site water supply, including treatment to potable standards
using a non-chemical system that combines mechanical filtration and UV disinfection, in compliance with
LBC requirements. For each case study, the feasibility of decentralised supply was assessed through the
following steps:
1. Rainfall data: Annual average rainfall was obtained for Grenaa (SF_W) and Sønderborg (MF_C). The
same values were assumed for the other buildings of similar typology.
2. Catchment area: Roof areas were obtained from project drawings or estimated from building dimensions.
A 2% collection loss was applied, as recommended by Faragò et al. (2019).
3. Retention features: For multi-family buildings, additional retention through green roofs and walls was
considered, contributing to retaining up to 40% of total rainfall minimising urban runoff.
4. Water balance: The annual demand was compared with the annual volume of potentially collected
rainwater to assess whether RWH was sufficient as a water supply.
5. Tank sizing: Tanks were sized based on the ability to collect the 10-year return rainfall event of 52
mm/day (Faragò et al., 2019) and to compensate for monthly water deficits during dry periods.
The resulting tank capacities were: 6.7 m³ for both SF_C and SF_W, 25 m³ for MF_C and 28 m³ for
MF_W.
Single-Family Case Studies: For single-family buildings (SF_W and SF_C), the total water demand—both
potable and non-potable—could be met entirely on-site using rainwater. The tank structure, double piping to
account for dual distribution and filtration system materials were included in the LCA inventory, modelled
following the guidance of Faragò et al. (2019).
For water treatment, potable water after use was assumed to go to an on-site decentralised treatment system,
selected in this thesis as a constructed wetland. This wetland system enables partial reuse for non-potable
applications, significantly reduces the need for additional potable water and enhances the circularity of the
water system.
In accordance with Danish regulations for rural properties not connected to the public sewer system (Danish
Ministry of Environment and Energy, 1997), a urine-diverting dry toilet (UDDT) was implemented in the
model. Rajski et al. (2024) states that conventional toilets account for approximately 30% of total water
demand, showing that this dry system significantly reduces consumption. Instead of generating blackwater,
the system produces human organic waste that is managed entirely on-site. Urine is collected and stored in
sealed containers for potential reuse as a fertiliser, while faeces are composted passively over a long period

Multi-Family Case Studies: For multi-family buildings (MF_W and MF_C), full water self-sufficiency
was not achievable due to limited roof area and higher occupancy. According to the LBC framework, when a
project is unable to meet its full water demand solely through on-site resources, it is permitted to connect to
the municipal water supply for the remaining needs but a 25% reduction in overall water demand has to be
achieved.
Thus, in the Regenerative scenarios for MF_W and MF_C, a hybrid system was implemented. This system
integrates on-site RWH for non-potable uses with partial dependence on municipal supply for potable water.
The rainwater harvesting component covered approximately 20% of the total water demand, corresponding to
the full non-potable portion. Since rainwater was used only for non-potable applications, no disinfection or UV
treatment was included in the model. Electricity demand accounted only for the pumping of water from the
tank. Materials related to this system were included in the LCA, including tank components and an assumed
50% increase in piping to account for separate non-potable circulation. The remaining portion of the total
water demand was assumed to be supplied by the municipal network and managed through a conventional
centralised wastewater system, modelled identically to the Reference scenario. In addition, on-site treatment
of wastewater was not considered feasible due to the limited available space and high occupancy typically
associated with urban multi-family buildings.
These scenarios for MF_W and MF_C are referred to as "Regenerative-RWH" throughout the thesis and
illustrated in Figure 13.

Sub-scenarios for the Regenerative Scenario
To better understand the influence of key design choices, a set of sub-scenarios was developed to explore specific
modifications within the Regenerative variants. These alternatives allow for a more detailed comparison
between decentralised and centralised water supply and treatment strategies.
Two sub-scenarios were defined for the single-family case studies. The first variant, labelled "Regenerative-
CentralBW", maintains the same decentralised potable water supply and greywater treatment system used in
the base scenario. However, the dry toilet was replaced by a conventional flush toilet and all blackwater was
assumed to be sent to a centralised WWTP. Compared to the base case, this scenario required additional
piping for toilet connections (assumed to be +25%).
The second variant, labelled "Regenerative-NoReuse", aimed to evaluate the impact of not reusing treated
greywater. In this scenario, all greywater is treated on-site through the constructed wetland and then
discharged without reuse. As a result, the entire household demand, including non-potable uses, must be met
with new potable water. Since the available rainwater was not sufficient to cover this increased demand, part
of the potable water was assumed to be supplied from the centralised system. Compared to the base case,
this scenario required fewer internal pipes (assumed –25%) and a slightly smaller tank. Table 27 provides a

B.2
LCA Results
This section presents the quantitative results of the LCA conducted for the four residential case studies. The
analysis is structured in three parts: (1) an overall comparison between Reference and Regenerative scenarios
across all domains, (2) a detailed numerical evaluation of energy and water system sub-scenarios and (3) an
assessment of climate performance in relation to the Danish Building Regulations (BR18).
B.2.1
Reference vs Regenerative Scenarios
This section presents the LCA results for the four case studies, comparing the Reference and Regenerative
scenarios across impact categories with full numerical detail.
To facilitate comparison between case studies and scenarios, a visual representation of selected impacts
is provided, highlighting the contribution of each domain. GWP and ODP were chosen as representative
indicators of, respectively, climate performance and potential environmental trade-offs. Figures 14 and 15
display the results per resident for GWP and ODP, disaggregated by domain.
Figures 14 shows that multi-family buildings tend to have higher emissions per resident, with MF_C–Reference
displaying the highest impact due to the use of concrete and metal elements. As already analysed in Section 3.3,
the implementation of regenerative design strategies generally results in lower GWP, particularly in cases
with significant material substitutions. In MF_W, where few material modifications and large PV installation
were introduced, the GWP slightly increases. Lastly, in all cases, materials account for the largest share of
emissions.

presence of trade-offs discussed in the main text. MF_C–Regenerative exhibits an exceptionally high ODP,
which is attributed to the large amount of adhesives and insulation materials required to convert a heavy
concrete multi-family building into a timber one. The figure also shows that ODP impacts are mainly driven

B.2.3
LCA Results in Accordance with BR18
An additional analysis was conducted to evaluate the performance of the buildings in relation to the BR18
Regulation. Unlike the previous results, which are structured around the three specific domains, this assessment
follows the methodological requirements of BR18. The evaluation focuses on the GWP of both Reference
and Regenerative scenarios, expressed in terms of kilograms of CO2-equivalents per square metre per year
(kg CO2-eq/m2/year), as recommended by BR18. In accordance with the regulation, all permanent building-
integrated components were included in the LCA. Thus, this extended analysis accounts also for elements such
as water pipes and PV panels. Only the energy required for the operation of the building itself is considered
(regulated energy use), excluding individual user consumption.
Figure 16 illustrates the GWP contribution of each life cycle stage for the single-family and multi-family
concrete-based buildings (SF_C and MF_C, respectively), selected as representative cases for each typology
due to the high volume of replaced materials, which leads to more significant differences between scenarios.
Figure 16: Contribution to GWP (kgCO2-eq/m2/year) per life cycle stage for MF_C, for both the Reference
and Regenerative scenarios. The total GWP is calculated excluding stage D, which is shown to illustrate the
potential benefits of substitution and circular strategies.
The results are largely consistent across both case studies. In the Reference scenarios, stage A1–A3 (production
of materials) accounts for the highest GWP contribution, followed by stage B6 (operational energy use). When
comparing the Reference and Regenerative scenarios, a clear reduction in A1–A3 emissions is observed in the
Regenerative case. This reduction is primarily attributed to the biogenic carbon accounting of wood-based
materials, which temporarily store carbon and are credited as negative emissions during the production stage
(A1–A3). However, since the share of biogenic materials is not predominant, these negative emissions are not
sufficient to result in a net-negative GWP for this stage. The stored carbon is subsequently released during
end-of-life processing (stage C3), leading to a corresponding increase in emissions at that point. This dynamic
is more pronounced in the single-family case, where material substitutions are more extensive, amplifying
both the reductions in A1–A3 and the increases in C3.
Although stage D (beyond the system boundary) is not included in the official BR18 GWP calculation, it

To better visualize the contribution of each construction material group to the overall GWP reduction,
Figure 18 presents a comparison between the materials used in the Reference and Regenerative scenarios for
the multi-family case study (MF_C), chosen as representative for all case studies. As consistently discussed
throughout the report, the most significant reductions in GWP are primarily driven by the decreased use
of mineral building products (Category I), particularly concrete, and metals (Category II), which are also
the largest contributors to embodied carbon in the Reference scenario. In contrast, Coverings (Category VI),

carbon footprint.

Appendix – Supplementary Materials
This appendix refers to the digital supplementary materials submitted together with the thesis, structured
into two main folders: Methods and Results. The files contain detailed life cycle inventories (LCIs), modelling
assumptions and complete LCA outputs for each of the four residential case studies analysed.
All files are submitted within a folder named SUPPLEMENTARY_MATERIALS.
Methods
The Methods folder is organised by case study and modelling domain. For each of the four case studies, three
Excel files are provided, corresponding to:
• Construction materials
• Energy systems
• Water systems
This results in a total of 12 files. Each file contains all relevant input data used to model both the Reference
and Regenerative scenarios.
In addition, a shared Excel file includes the projected composition of the Danish electricity grid and district
heating network used for energy modelling.
Results
The Results folder contains the LCA results for all scenarios and sub-scenarios. One Excel file per case study
is included, with three sheets corresponding to the results for each domain, the analysis of GWP in relation
to the Danish BR18 climate regulation and a summary of the results extracted from LCAbyg.
