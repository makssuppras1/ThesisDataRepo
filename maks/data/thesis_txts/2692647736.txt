1. Abstract 
 
In the last decades, the discovery of proteins as therapeutic agents has greatly increased. Using proteins as pharmaceuticals 
still remains a big challenge due to the inherent difficulties associated with their complex structure, low half-life, and low 
stability among others. As an immune-modulating glycoprotein, interleukin-2 (IL-2) is of particular interest because it 
possesses strong antitumor activity by stimulating T cells and other immune cells, and numerous preclinical and clinical 
studies have show its efficacy against tumors. However, the short life and its related severe side effects, still remain a 
concern. Drug delivery systems have emerged as a potential solution to overcome all difficulties associated. Polymeric 
nanoparticles are one of the most suitable options for this purpose. Particularly, polylactic co-glycolic acid (PLGA), due 
to its biodegradability, easily modifyable composition, and versatility makes it a perfect candidate.  
 
Protein delivery systems generally follow a biphasic release profile. First, an initial burst release phase makes the drug 
released reach high concentration levels that may cause toxicity issues. This is followed then by a sustained release phase 
in which the drug releases in a slower and more controlled manner. Fine tunning the polymer composition of PLGA allow 
the modulation of this release profile. For example, by increasing the polylactic-to-glycolic acid ratio the hydrophobicity 
of the nanoparticle will increase, therefore making it more resistant to hydrolysis (water degradation). In this master thesis 
we have investigated the effect of these changes in the polymer composition, and have shown that release profiles of IL-
2 can be modified by using either 50:50 PLGA or 75:25 PLGA ratios. In addition, other variables have been found to 
play a big influence on the encapsulation and release process, such as IL-2 stability, polyethylene glycol (PEG) influence, 
synthesis method choosen, and storage conditions. Different strategies have been exploited to optimize these conditions, 
including the use of protein-protecting molecules (glucosamine), stabilizer molecules (sucrose and trehalose), and an 
opmitized protocol method for the nanoparticle synthesis regarding the sonication probe tip and the volume of reagents. 
Determination of the encapsulation efficiency (%EE) of the IL-2 within the polymeric nanoparticles has been evaluated 
using different methodologies, however accurate quantification of the amount of IL-2 entrapped in the polymer matrix 
remain a challenge due to the necessity of using methods to digest the PLGA nanoparticles and release the IL-2 to be 
measured without being degraded. Results indicate low values of encapsulation efficiency, however, these techniques 
need to be improved.  
 
Finally, the therapeutic potential of the IL-2 loaded polymeric nanoparticles was further expanded by inhibiting the PD-
1/PD-L1 immune checkpoint. Due to the facility to modify the surface composition of the nanoparticle, several strategies 
were investigated to decorate the nanoparticles with anti-PD-L1 antibodies. The first step consists of coupling the anti-
PD-L1 antibody to a linker molecule of our choice (DBCO-dPEG12-TFP Ester). in a 1:1 molecule ratio. The next step of 
the coupling strategy will be to bind these molecules to the polymeric nanoparticle surface to place both molecules at 
their convenient position and procure the correct functionalization of our nanocarrier.  
 
A proper anti-PD-L1 targeted PLGA nanoparticle that enables encapsulation of IL-2 cytokine and provides control over 
its release has been developed. This will serve as a combinational therapeutic platform to delivery highly immune-
stimulant protein and target the PD-1/PD-L1 immune checkpoint.

2.  Introduction 
 
2.1. Evolution of Nanoparticles and Development of Protein Encapsulation  
Nanomedicine is described by T.J Webster as the monitoring, repair, construction, and control of the human biological 
system at the molecular level, using engineered nanodevices and nanostructures [1]. Nanoparticles (NPs) are small 
particles that ranges between 1 to 100 nanometers in size that can be used as a drug delivery system [1][2][4][5]. These 
nanostructures can be useful tools for therapeutic, diagnosis, drug delivery, and molecular imaging applications [6]. In 
recent years, nanotechnology has been applied to human health with promising results, especially in tumor treatment 
because of its great potential to overcome the current limitations deriving by traditional treatments such as chemotherapy 
and radiotherapy. These include toxicity, drug resistance and heterogenicity issues [6][7]. 
Drug Delivery System (DDS) consists of any form of formulation or device that enhances therapeutic efficacy and safety 
of drugs by improving their pharmacokinetic properties according to their absorption, distribution, metabolism and 
excretion (ADME) profiles [8][9]. Their ultimate goal is to provide a platform where the drug is delivered in a controlled 
manner, improving drug release rates, tissue/cell-targeting and drug stability once it is administrated [9]. The release 
profile of macroscale conventional DDS in a major manner, and nanoscale as well, are affected by the local 
physicochemical environment conditions (pH, enzymes, clearance etc.). These circunstances hinder the drug possibilities 
of reaching and acting at their proper sites. However, nanosystems are able to overcome these difficulties offer certain 
advantages compared with the traditional treatments such as improved efficacy, specificity, reduced toxicity and 
improved patient compliance [4]. 
 
Initially, nanopharmaceuticals were developed to formulate small molecules to enhance their solubility, pharmacokinetic 
properties and reduce toxicity by releasing the drug at the correct site of action [10]. More recently, new classes of 
therapeutic biomolecules with great therapeutic potential have emerged: nucleic acids, proteins, whose therapeutic 
efficacy rely on efficient nanocarrier systems. Nucleic acids and proteins suffer from low stability, short half-life, or 
unexpected immuno-gene city and toxicity issues [11]. Nanoparticles can be used to overcome these challenges by 
protecting the active pharmaceutic ingredient (API) from environmental influences.  
 
In the last decades, increased the interest has raised in the discovery of proteins as new potential therapeutic agents. 
Proteins carry out crucial processes within our body, playing an important role in physiological and biological pathways 
as ligands for signaling, enzymes, receptors, antibodies, and many other vital functions [12]. However, the full 
exploitation of proteins as a pharmaceuticals is still challenging. The key to success is to have an efficient drug delivery 
system that allows the protein to reach its target site before degradation. The structural complexity of proteins makes 
them vulnerable towards disruptions of their chains that can lead to loss of activity or immunogenicity [13]. Proteins can 
be unstable, have a high molecular weight, complex structure, and less permeability across biological barriers [12]. For 
using proteins as an API, we must consider the route of administration, drug release profile, method of delivery, and 
fabrication process [12]. Even though all these challenges, they have rapidly emerged as potential pharmaceuticals due 
to their increased specificity, lower toxicity, and decreased risk of drug-drug interactions [14][17][18][15].  
 
Several drug delivery strategies have been investigated for this purpose. However, delivery of proteins by biodegradable 
polymer nanoparticles is the most suitable option to overcome the limitations mentioned before. Properties of these 
biodegradable polymers regarding biocompatibility, biodegradation kinetics, ease fabrication, and versatility make this 
delivery system a perfect candidate. 
 
2.2 PLGA Polymer Nanoparticle 
 
Polylactic co-glycolic acid (PLGA) is a linear copolymer composed of lactic acid (PLA) and glycolic acid (PGA) repeat 
units linked via hydrolyzable ester bonds. The degradation products of lactic acid and glycolic acid are introduced into 
the metabolic pathways without negative impact [16] (Figure 1).   
It is approved by the US-Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and European medical agency (EMA) for its application 
in clinical use as a part of drug delivery systems and medical devices [12][13][19]

PLGA is commonly used for entrapping several entities inside the polymeric nanocarrier, such as small chemicals, 
peptides, proteins, or nucleic acids. This polymer can be chemically modified to obtain an appropriate biofunctionality 
(e.g. tumor-targeting) and provide control on drug release rates. According to this last aspect, the degradation properties 
can be adjusted by changing the ratios of its monomer components. PLGA is a hydrophobic polymer itself, however, its 
hydrophobic nature can be changed by adjusting the PLA:PGA ratio. PLA is more hydrophobic than PGA because of its 
methyl-side group, thus the hydrophobicity of a PLA-rich polymer of PLGA will be higher thereby reducing the 
hydrolysis rate. If the PGA-ratio increases, the degradation rate will occur faster. In conclusion, adjustment of the PLGA 
ratio is a usefull property to adapt the polymer for optimal properties to serve the needs of the cargo or the desired release 
properties. Moreover, PLGA can be complemented and functionalized with other polymers to enhance its properties. The 
versatility and hydrophilic nature of polyethylene glycol (PEG) enhances PLGA properties and purposes mentioned at 
the beginning of the section [21][22][23]. Furthermore, PEG is used to create a ‘stealth’ nanoparticle allowing 
nanoparticle to become almost invisible to the reticulo-endothelial system (RES), which is in charge of the clearance of 
particles from the bloodstream, and also have a longer circulation time reducing interactions with different biomolecules 
[18][24]. This water-soluble and non-ionic polymer does not interfere with polymer synthesis methods due to facile end 
group modifications [22][29].  
 
The properties of PLGA nanoparticles such as size, size distribution, encapsulation efficiency, and release characteristics 
are strongly dependent on the synthesis method [18]. The synthesis methods can be divided into two main groups: 
conventional and new design methods. The conventional synthesis methods include single and double emulsion-solvent 
evaporation methods, nanoprecipitation, spray-drying, and emulsification/salting out. Traditional emulsification methods 
are based on the preparation of an oil-in-water (o/w) or water-in-oil-in-water (w/o/w) emulsion [26][18]. These emulsions 
consist of two different phases: oil/organic phase with PLGA, and an aqueous phase. The drug can be contained in both 
phases depending on its physicochemical properties. The single emulsion is suitable for encapsulating hydrophobic 
compounds; on the contrary double emulsion is used for encapsulating hydrophilic cargo [21][26]. The organic phase is 
added to the aqueous phase under sonication or homogenization followed by solvent evaporation. After these, 
nanoparticles can be collected and they can be freeze-dried to increase stability and get stored for long periods [18]. Other 
methods such as nanoprecipitation allows the synthesis of small-sized nanoparticles below 100 nm, and spray-drying 
technique is used for encapsulation of different types of molecules by spraying solid-in-oil (s/o) dispersions or w/o 
emulsions in a stream of heated air [21][28].  
Various issues associated with these conventional methods such as broad particle size distribution that could lead to 
problems with biodistribution, drug-loading, and encapsulation efficiency have prompted a deep development and 
investigation in the preparation methods for PLGA nanoparticles and novel procedures have emerged for this cause [18]. 
Microfluidics platforms, based on the manipulation of fluids at the nanoscale, can generate emulsions via a droplet-based 
microfluidic systems. This new procedure provides a new route to produce emulsion in a controlled way using different 
materials and reagents with a narrow size distribution and high reproducibility [21][29][19]. Membrane extrusion

2.3 Protein Release Profile and Ion Paring Effect 
 
Protein encapsulation is a major challenge to use these molecules as therapeutic agents. The main issues rely on the 
development of a proper drug delivery system that allows loading of large amounts of protein, and offer protection from 
degradation and control the release of the cargo. PLGA nanoparticles offer the required conditions for protein 
encapsulation and allowing the increase of circulating half-lives of proteins and stability among other advantages of these 
PLGA polymeric nanoparticles [14]. 
 
During the whole life-cycle of polymeric nanoparticles, there are two main stages in which protein stability is at risk of 
being harmed: synthesis and release. PLGA degrades via chain scissions of ester bonds by hydrolysis. During the initial 
stages of PLGA degradation, only polymer monomers and dimers are detected. As degradation continues, bigger 
polymeric oligomers and low core pH (1,5-3) appear [14].  
During the release, there are other issues to overcome regarding the initial diffusion of the protein from the polymeric 
nanocarrier, the consequent polymer degradation, and many other alterations such as aggregation, hydrolysis, or 
adsorption [31]. The acidic microclimate generated during the initial PLGA degradation will decrease the pH by the 
accumulations of these acidic monomers and oligomers that lead to protein destabilization and degradation. It can also 
occur by the use of organic solvents during the synthesis or by the protein mobility and adsorption to the polymer surface 
leading to a destabilization of their chemical and physical properties [14]. Other specific chemical reactions can alter the 
protein and PLGA structures such as deamination, amide bond hydrolysis, acylation, and oxidative reactions [14][31][32]. 
 
Protein release from PLGA nanoparticles is characterized by a rapid initial diffusion, surface, and bulk-erosion, called 
burst-release[32][33]. It is the most critical phase during drug release, since it could induce toxicity in case of high release 
of cargo while low release may be below the efficacy threshold. This initial phase lasts the first hours of release (0-16 h) 
[31]. The amount of protein released during the initial release can be related to the portion of protein molecules adsorbed 
on the nanoparticle’s surface and the diffusion of the proteins present near the surface. This process is closely related to 
protein-polymer interaction established during the manufacturing or storage processes, e.g. during lyophilization, proteins 
inside the polymer nanocarrier tend to move or approach the surface, facilitating the release of the protein in early stages 
[31][34][35]. 
 
After the initial burst phase, protein release continues in a constant phase according to the degradation process of the 
polymer, called the sustained release phase. This stage is characterized by a lower protein release rate, and it is influenced 
by processes such as hydrolysis, enzymatic degradation and other environmental conditions that degrade the nanocarrier. 
The two different phases occur due to inhomogeneous protein distribution within the nanoparticle; a significant fraction 
is localized near the surface with the remainder entrapped in the core [15][17][21][37]. 
 
Protecting the protein from aggressive conditions during the manufacturing and storage processes is crucial for protein 
integrity. For this purpose, several strategies have been established to prevent protein degradation by using various 
molecules of diverse nature at different stages of the whole procedure. Adding excipients such as surfactants into the 
aqueous phase in the course of the synthesis can protect and prevent protein denaturation and aggregation during, for 
example, the emulsification process [38]. 
Sugar molecules are perfect candidates acting as stabilization excipients. Trehalose, mannitol, and other glucose-derived 
molecules like glucosamine, present in the aqueous phase are well-known and can be used for this purpose. According to 
Weert et al, trehalose and other sugar molecules shield the protein from the organic solvent by preferential hydration.

Specifically, they can shield the hydrophobic amino acid residues on the protein surface,  and further reduce amino acid

One promising strategy to stabilize proteins is based on the ‘Ion-Pairing Effect’. This term is described by Yizhak Marcus

2.4 Targeted Delivery 
 
Polymeric nanocarrier formulations can overcome certain resistance and toxicity limitations, and many others related to 
the pharmacokinetic profiles of the drug or therapeutic agent. Due to their characteristic size, nanoparticles can penetrate 
and accumulate into solid tumor tissues via the leaky vessels and reduced lymphatic drainage by the enhanced 
permeability and retention (EPR) effect [40]. The EPR-effect is a unique particularity that offers new possibilities in 
cancer treatment. 
 
Aside from passive targeting caused by the EPR effect, all these nanosystems can be functionalized using specific ligands 
(antibodies, small molecules etc.) on their surface that can recognize and bind specific receptors that are present on target 
cells. Active targeting provides for drugs and nanoparticles the ability to enter into specific tissues faster and more 
effectively, and achieve higher drug concentration than with the traditional dosage forms [41][10][11]. According to 
P.Couvereur, based on pre-clinical and clinical data, active-targeted nanoparticle concentrations at tumor site are notably 
superior to conventional anticancer drugs [44]. D.Roselblum et al. state that passively targeted nanoparticles, which utilize 
the EPR effect, only reach a concentration less than 1% in high-EPR expressed tumors [45]. On the other hand, active 
targeting strategy reaches a concentration of around 0,5%-7% of the injected dose at tumor site per gram of tumor [44]. 
Despite the complications of reaching at tumor site and their  percentage numbers, active targeting of drugs should 
accumulate in target sites faster and effectively than the passively targeted nanoparticles. Moreover, the quality and 
quantity of the drug delivered should be higher than in the case of other delivery systems [41].  
 
Targeting specificity can be increased to the desired tissues or organs by using conjugation of certain ligands to the 
nanoparticles surface. This can be done by modifying the chemical composition of the polymer as mentioned before: 
different terminations of PLGA or PEG polymer. Tissue-specific ligands on the particle surface (antibodies, peptides, 
macromolecules, etc) will enhance therapeutic efficacy. Level and homogeneity of expression and selectivity are crucial 
factors to select our ligand for this active-targeting strategies [26][27]. In this work we decided to exploit the use of anti-
PD-L1 targeted antibodies. This strategy will allow us to reach a specific target, as explained in the coming section.  
 
2.4.1 Immunotherapy: evolution, solution, types, immune-checkpoints inhibitors, cytokines as 
therapy 
Immunotherapy has been established as a promising tool for fighting cancer in the last decades. It has rapidly placed as a 
consolidated pilar among the traditional cancer therapies. Cancer immunotherapies involve the use of different therapeutic 
alternatives that lead to manipulating the immune system or mediators by using immune agents such as cytokines, 
vaccines, cell therapies or transfection agents. All these therapeutic modalities stimulate the host’s anti-tumor response 
by increasing the effector functions of immune cells and production of mediators or by re-arming the immune system for 
fighting against the tumor or malignant cells [48]–[50]. Moreover, they can decrease the host’s suppressor mechanisms 
by inducing tumor killing and inflammatory environment and by modulating immune checkpoints. Modulation of immune 
checkpoints is one of the main pillars of immunotherapy against cancer. Immune checkpoint inhibitors are targeted 
molecules that serve as checks in the regulation of the immune responses [50][49]. Depending on their function, they are 
able to block inhibitory molecules or activate stimulatory molecules and enhance the immune response. Antibodies used

Particularly, PD-1 is a type of transmembrane protein receptor widely expressed in immune cells (T cells, B cells, 
macrophages, dendritic cells, etc.) and also in tumor cells. PD-L1 as its immunoregulatory ligand is also expressed in 
antigen-presenting cells (APC): macrophages and dendritic cells, lymphoid and non-lymphoid tissues, and all kinds of

reduce the proliferation of CD8+ T cells in lymph nodes by generating inhibitory signals [54]. As seen in Figure 2, under 
normal circumstances, the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway down-regulates the immune response during tumor development. 
However, PD-L1 is usually overexpressed in tumor cells causing a major inactivation of T cells and so immune response, 
allowing cancerous cells to escape from the immune system [54]. By inhibiting the interaction between both receptor and 
ligand, we can prevent cancer cells from evading the immune system. Immune therapy, using specifically targeted 
antibodies (anti-PD-1 or anti-PD-L1) against these proteins acting as an immune checkpoint inhibitor, can repress the 
signaling pathway between tumor cells and T cells and reactivate these immune cells [51][52][56].

PEG molecules in our PLGA polymeric nanoparticle. PEG end group modifications will allow to link the specific 
antibody to our nanoparticle through a specific coupling reaction involving Click-Chemistry. By blocking the interaction 
of these proteins using this particular antibody, we expect to increase T cell proliferation, in addition, to stimulate the 
immune response not only with this blockage but using other immunostimulatory proteins encapsulated in our PLGA 
polymeric nanoparticle. Moreover, by using the anti-PD-L1 targeting we will maximize the time where the PLGA 
nanoparticles are linked to the tumor cells and not being re-circulated into the bloodstream. Therefore increasing the 
chance to get the IL-2 specifically released at the tumor site and not elsewhere. 
2.4.2  Immunostimulatory molecules: general aspect of immunology, cytokine pathways 
(JAK/STAT), and IL2 
Interleukin-2 (IL-2) is a 15.5-16 kDa, four-a-helix cytokine. Several immune cells secrete IL-2, including T cells, natural 
killers (NK), dendritic cells (DC) and mast cells [51][52] . At resting conditions, CD4+ T cells (T-helper) are the main 
source of this cytokine, releasing constant and low levels. However, once the immune system is activated, IL-2 production 
rapidly rises. DCs become activated and start to produce larger amounts of IL-2 that will lead to T cells activation and

proliferation. At the same time, activated T cells (CD4+ and CD8+) start secreting IL-2 on their own to shelf-activate 
(autocrine signaling) and also to activate other nearby immune cells [51][52]. IL-2 has crucial functions during 
homeostasis via its effects on regulatory T cells (Treg) and the effector functions of effector lymphocytes. They are 
powerful growth and survival factors for T cells and promote their cytotoxic functions [57]. Thus, low doses of IL-2 have 
been used for immunosuppressive strategies by activating the Treg pathway against autoimmune pathologies, but in other 
cases, it can be used to stimulate anti-tumor immune responses at high doses via effector T-cells activation. This dose-
depending dual effect allows different strategies of using IL-2 as an immunostimulatory treatment molecule [51][52][59] 
IL-2 binds with low affinity to its receptor alpha-chain (IL-2R or CD25). As shown in Figure 3, this induces the 
recruitment of the other two subunits IL-2Rb (CD122) and IL-2Rg (CD132) chains, creating a high-affinity IL-2 trimeric 
complex receptor. Engagement of the molecule with its receptor leads to the internalization of the quaternary complex, 
on which the CD25 is recycled via endosomes to the cell surface and the other subunits, including the IL-2, are degraded 
in vesicles. This process triggers the signal transduction by three major pathways, particularly it activates JAK1 and JAK3 
with subsequent phosphorylation of STAT proteins (predominantly STAT5A and STAT5B but also STAT1 and STAT3) 
in the cytosol. Then, phosphorylated-STAT dimers translocate to the nucleus to activate or repress gene expression related 
to the effector functions of the immune cells. However, there is another pathway activated by this protein. IL-2 also 
provides signals for the development and suppressive functions of Foxp3+ for the regulatory T cells. Thus, IL-2 has this 
dual-potential for therapeutic applications mentioned before. For anti-cancer therapeutic strategies, efforts are now to 
redirect IL-2 signals on effector T-cells, preventing the activation of regulatory T cells that will lead to a non-active 
response (immune tolerance) against the tumor cells [58][60][61]

In this thesis IL-2 will be used due to its potential as an immunostimulatory molecule, and despite the beforehand

2.5 Motivation of the project: Hypothesis and aims. 
The project hypothesizes that changing the polymeric composition of the nanoparticle platform will  induce effective and 
beneficial alterations that will have an impact on the release and stability of the protein cargo and the polymeric 
nanocarrier. For this purpose, the strong immunomodulatory protein IL-2 will be encapsulated in PLGA-based 
nanoparticles to protect it from degradation. The overall goal is to develop optimal formulation conditions to load IL-2 
into PLGA nanoparticles to overcome and damp its initial burst release, and achieve sustained release of the protein cargo. 
Another specific objective of the project relies on achieving large amounts of protein encapsulation in the PLGA particles. 
Optimization of encapsulation efficiency will be investigated and will be evaluated comparing the different formulations.  
Finally, functionalization of the polymeric nanoparticle with anti-PD-L1 antibody as a targeted molecule, will be crucial 
to exploit the full immune-therapeutic potential of the system. Therefore the preparation and evaluation of targeting 
properties of the anti-PD-L1 nanoparticles will be validated.

3.1.2 Synthesis PLGA nanoparticles 
Particles were synthesized using w/o/w double emulsification method as previously described. Briefly aqueous solution 
was prepared by dissolving lypholized human IL-2 in a 0.05M PBS solution at a final concentration of 80µg/ml. Polymers 
were disolved in dichloromethane at a final concentration of 12.5g/L. For PEGylation PLGA-PEG 50:50 was mixed in at 
either 10%, 30% or 50% (w/w). Particles were synthesized using different procedures as describe herein after.  
 
Procedure (A): Briefly, 4mL of the PLGA solution (12.5 g/L in DCM) were added to 500mL PBS (0.05M) containing 
20µg of IL-2 in 15mL falcon tube. The solution was placed on ice and probe sonicated using a Q700 sonicator (QSonica, 
USA) with a 1/4" sonication probe tip (refered from now on as big tip) at 70% amplitude, 40 sec, 1/1 pulsed on/off (first 
emulsion). Second emulsion was prepared by adding the emulsion to 20mL PVA (1%v/w) in 30mL falcon tube. The 
mixture was sonicated with the big tip using the same settings on the instrument. The final emulsion was left overnight 
at room temperature under stirring conditions (1000rpm), with lid off to evaporate the DCM. The following day, the 
solution was centrifuged (10 minutes at 9000G) to pellets out large aggregates if any. The solution was washed 3 times

3.2 Dynamic Light Scattering for nanoparticle characterization 
 
Particle size, polidispersity (PDI) and zeta-potential were analyzed with dynamic light scattering (DLS) using a Malvern 
Zetaziser (Malvern Panalytical, UK). Freeze-dried nanoparticles were resuspended in 1mL PBS, vortex for 20-30 seconds 
and sonicated. Then, 50µL of the nanoparticle suspension was mixed with 800µL of miliQ water in a disponsable cuvette. 
Measurements were made in triplicates and in automatic mode. 
 
3.3 Release Study 
 
Aliquotes of the freeze-dried particles were rehydrated in PBS buffer containing 0.02% NaN3 and transfered into a 2mL 
low binding Eppendorf tube. The solution was centrifuged for 10 minutes at 6500g and 500µL of the supernatant was 
marked and labeled as 0h time point. Then 500µL of PBS 0.02% NaN3 was added and brought back to solution by 
vortexing or short sonication. Then the release study was initiated by placing the sample at 37ºC under shaking conditions, 
and the following aliquotes were collected at 3h, 6h, 24h (for the burst release) and then once a day (sustained release), 
and stored at the freezer (-20ºC) until concentrations of IL-2 were analyzed using an ELISA. 
 
3.4 Quantification of IL-2 using ELISA  
The release of IL-2 was measured using an Human IL-2 ELISA kit (R&D Systems Human IL-2 DuoSet ELISA, Catalog 
numb. DY202-05, Lot: P280796). The ELISA kit used for sandwich ELISA to measure human IL-2 contains all the 
specific reagents required: capture antibody, detection antibody, standard antobody, and Streptavidin-HRP. Each of them 
were reconstituted in their proper diluents as described in the company protocol. 
 
3.4.1 Plate Preparation 
The capture antibody was diluted to the working concentration in PBS without carrier protein. Immediately the 96-well 
microplate was coated with 100µL/well of the diluted capture antibody. The plate was sealed and incubated overnight at 
room temperature. Each well was aspirated and washed with Wash Buffer, process repeated two times for a total of three 
washes. Washes were done by filling each well with 400µL of Wash Buffer using a squirt bottle, manifold dispenser, or 
autowasher. The plate was blocked by adding 300μL of Block Buffer to each well. Then, the plate was incubated at room 
temperature for a minimum of 1 hour. Another wash was set (x3) and finally the plates were ready for sample addition. 
 
An standard curve was prepared by doing 2-fold serial dilutions of the stock solution aiming for a range from 1000pg/ml 
to 15.6pg/mL. Samples were diluted generally 1:50 to fit that range, however, digested samples were measured after a 
1:10000 dilution. 
 
3.4.2 Assay Procedure  
Sample or standards (100μL) were added in Reagent Diluent, or an appropriate diluent, per well and covered with an 
adhesive strip and incubated 2 hours at room temperature. After the washing step, a 100μL of the Detection Antibody 
was added, diluted in Reagent Diluent, to each well. Then covered with a new adhesive strip again and incubated 2 hours 
at room temperature. Washing steps were repeated again. After the washing, a 100μL of the working dilution of 
Streptavidin-HRP were added to each well. The plate was covered and incubated for 20 minutes at room temperature 
avoiding placing the plate in direct light. The aspiration/washing steps were repeated once again. Then, 100μL of 
Substrate Solution were added to each well and incubated for 20 minutes at room temperature protecting from light 
exposure. Finally, 50μL of Stop Solution were added to each well, and the optical density was determined of each well 
immediately using a microplate reader (TECAN, UK) set to 450 nm (+ 540 nm if correction is needed).

5. Discussion 
 
Glucosamine at 0,5 mg/mL protects IL-2 from the aggressive conditions during freeze-drying as well as increases its 
encapsulation efficiency. According to the results, this derivate-sugar molecule at 0.2mg/mL concentration has proven its 
potential in protecting our protein cargo. It has been demonstrated that by increasing the glucosamine concentration, we 
do not obtain better results regarding either the encapsulation efficiency (Table 1) or the release study (Figure 1). Both 
size and Z-Potential have not been affected by using this molecule. Thus, we decided to use a 0,5 mg/mL glucosamine 
solution into the aqueous phase in every single PLGA nanoparticle synthesis containing the IL-2 cytokine.  
 
As polymeric nanoparticles can be easily modified chemically for targeting purposes, different polymeric terminations 
were evaluated to determine their influence on polymer degradation and IL-2 release. PLGA-Ester-terminated and PLGA-
Carboxylic acid terminated were chosen as our candidates for this experiment. DLS measurements of both polymeric 
nanoparticles showed similar and acceptable results. However, the difference between them appears during the release 
study as we can observe in Figure 2. PLGA-Ester follows a classic biphasic profile with a burst release phase and a long 
sustained release with almost no release (plateau). On the other hand, in PLGA-COOH terminated sample the amount of 
IL-2 released in both phases were increased and the IL-2 release was higher. According to R.Lanao et. al., the fact that 
the carboxylic acid end groups are catalyzing the hydrolysis reaction is causing a faster degradation of the polymer 
nanocarrier [65]. Moreover, hydrophilicity of the polymer is increased by carboxylic end terminations, leading to a pH 
decrease inside the nanoparticle and an increase of water influx and therefore, higher hydrolysis rates [14]. Thus, the 
degradation degree of the polymeric nanoparticle with carboxylic acid terminations is notably higher compared to the 
ester termination that has not this chemical property. For this reason, we decide to work with PLGA ester terminated 
polymers for further experiments.  
We also evaluated the PEG influence as it is required for many issues mentioned before. Results obtained and showed in 
Table 4, showing larger sizes than expected and high PdI values led us to evaluate this aspect in the coming experiment.  
 
In line with the project main goal of developing a novel polymeric platform, our strategy was based on changing the 
polylactic acid and glycolic acid ratios of PLGA to increase the hydrophobicity of the nanoparticle, improve its resistance 
to degradation (hydrolysis), and modify the IL-2 release profile. According to M. Hirenkumar et al., polymer composition 
is the most important factor to determine the degradation rate of a polymer delivery nanocarrier. Results of their studies 
indicate that reducing the glycolic acid, thus increasing the lactic acid percentage, decelerates the weight loss of polymer. 
50:50 PLGA nanoparticles show a faster degradation than the 65:35 PLGA, and subsequently, 65:35 PLGA exhibits faster 
degradation than 75:25 PLGA. Thus, the amount of lactic acid is a critical parameter in tuning the hydrophobicity of the 
polymer and the degradation and drug release rate [66]. By modifying these ratios in our experiment from 50:50 to 75:25 
PLGA we demonstrated that it can be achieved. As we can see in Figures 3 and 4, the release profile has changed regarding 
both phases. Burst release slope has been reduced and damped in the first six hours and the long sustained release has 
increased, meaning that the amount of IL-2 released is increasing continuously during the experiment. PEG content did 
not influence in this aspect as the differences between the three samples using 10, 30, and 50 w% PEG are almost the 
same. According to that, we decide to continue using 30 w% PEG for future experiments. Because 75:25 PLGA DLS

Another issue arising from the synthesis was the different setup possibilities. Several sonication protocols were developed 
using different setups and sonication probe tip. Once the 75:25 PLGA 30 w% PEG nanoparticles were selected as our 
best nanocarrier candidate, we tried to synthesize them by using a different protocol with the small tip. The idea behind 
was to be more precise and reduce polydispersity as much as possible. Indeed, DLS measurements (Table 7) showed 
promising results. However, the release study revealed that the burst release was slightly increased and the sustained 
release was reduced. These results lead us to the hypothesis that small sonication tip may damage our nanoparticles during 
the synthesis. Protocols 2 and 3 only differ in the sonication probe tip, both are using the same setup: 50 Amplitude, 2 
second On/Off interval and 1-minute run. This means that the same amount of energy is been applied to the nanoparticles 
either with the small or big sonication probe tip. Using the small tip, the energy is more concentrated in a smaller area 
compared to the big tip that has a bigger area to project the same amount of energy causing less shear stress or damage 
for the nanoparticle and the protein. Moreover, using the small tip, the volume of reactants required for the synthesis is 
half of the big tip protocol. So, this energy is dispersed in a smaller volume and increasing damaging of the sample. For 
this reason, we decided to discard the small sonication probe tip from our nanoparticle synthesis and continue working 
with the big one.  
 
As we can observe during the various nanoparticle syntheses methods, the size and the polydispersity values of freeze-
dried samples show unpredictable results. Big size values, such as in Tables 6 and 8, indicate nanoparticle aggregation 
possibly caused by errors or malfunctions during the sonication process such as sonication probe tip placement, correct 
temperature, etc or aggressive conditions during the freeze-drying process. This process aims to increase the long stability 
of the nanoparticles. However, many parameters define its success: type of polymer, type and concentration of surfactant 
(PVA), type and concentration of cryoprotectants (sucrose and trehalose), and interaction between these molecules [67]. 
Different outcomes during different syntheses can be shown in our results tables due to these complications. Firstly, we 
try to evaluate the differences between a freeze-dried and a frozen (-80ºC) sample. Despite using PVA as surfactant and 
a 1% sucrose solution (cryoprotectant) during the synthesis, results shown in Table 8 indicate that is not enough. There 
must be something that is damaging the nanoparticles during the freeze-dried, because the same nanoparticles but in this 
case, frozen (Table 9) showed ideal DLS results. During the release, both freeze-dried and frozen samples have almost 
identical results as we can see in Figures 7 and 8.  
 
This variation leads us to the idea of using a second cryoprotectant, trehalose. Both disaccharides in a solution can obstruct 
the crystallization of ice that may generate mechanical stress on our nanoparticles leading to their destabilization, reducing 
the amount of freezable water [67][68]. Trehalose has many advantages compared with sucrose and other sugar 
molecules: less hygroscopicity, an absence of internal hydrogen bonds allowing more flexibility during its interaction 
with the nanoparticle via hydrogen bonds during the freeze-drying, and a higher glass transition temperature (Tg’) 
[63][65]. According to W.Abdelwahed et al., the type and concentration of cryoprotectant and stabilizing agent (PVA) 
must be optimized to ensure maximum stabilization and prevent the aggregation of the nanoparticles. Thus, we decide to 
try increasing the concentration of the cryoprotectant solution to 5%. After seeing Table 11, DLS measurements using 
5% trehalose, it confirms that trehalose seems to be the preferable cryoprotectant molecule.  
 
According to N.Kamaly et al., there are two main mechanisms involving PLGA for drug delivery: diffusion and 
degradation/erosion. In the early stages, water absorption leads to pore formation in the polymer matrix more rapidly than 
drug release. After this, the hydrolysis of ester bonds in PLGA polymers are produced by the water contact leading to 
glycolic and lactic acid production, which further catalyzes hydrolysis [70]. Water absorption in PLGA nanoparticles 
depends on several parameters, which include: polymer ratio, the molecular weight of the polymer, end-group capping 
(ester or carboxylic acid), and surrounding release medium among others [70]–[72]. In the experiment presented in Figure 
10, we plan to evaluate the diffusion of the IL-2 in nanoparticles with the same polymer composition and ratio, but 
different release volumes. Since all the previous release studies were performed in a 1 mL PBS medium, we want to 
evaluate the behavior of the nanoparticles and their diffusion/release in a bigger volume media (5 mL). Nanoparticles 
placed in 5 mL media show a vast IL-2 diffusion compared to the ones placed in 1 mL. The presence of more water 
molecules around the nanoparticles accelerates and increases the diffusion and degradation (hydrolysis) processes as well

The encapsulation efficiency of polymeric nanoparticles depends on several factors. Results can differ depending on the 
nanoparticle synthesis method, the drug entrapped inside, and the technique to measure it. In addition, PLGA protein 
formulations often suffer from low encapsulation efficiency and low protein loading [73]. For determinating the %EE we 
use the direct method, in which the nanoparticles are dissolved in an organic solvent (DCM or NaOH). However, by using 
this kind of method we have encountered dissolving and precipitation issues. Thus, the majority of studies for evaluating 
the %EE were invalid. After several tries, we measure the IL-2 entrapped the nanoparticle using this direct method and 
quantified by ELISA,  obtaining a 10% EE value twice. All the challenges encountered during the digestion of the 
nanoparticles make these results very unreliable. Thus, further research needs to be done to properly calculate the %EE 
of the IL-2 protein in PLGA nanoparticles.  
 
Coupling reaction between the anti-PD-L1 antibody and DBCO-dPEG12-TFP Ester is the first step for our targeting 
strategy. Results in Table 11 indicate that both molecules can bind in a 1:1 ratio at certain time-reaction points. Moving 
forward, coupling of the molecule complex to the PLGA nanoparticle via azide groups using a ‘Click Chemistry’ strategy 
needs to be performed and evaluated. In future work, the ability of these anti-PD-L1 coupled PLGA nanoparticles to bind 
the PD-L1 receptors on tumor cells will be investigated.   
 
6. Conclusion 
 
Despite the complexity of the IL-2 cytokine itself in terms of chemical structure, dosing, and mechanism of action, it has 
been proven that polymeric nanoparticles can help delivering it at a desired site of action. We have successfully 
synthesized a polymeric nanocarrier showing a stable and sustained release of IL-2, a potent immunoestimolatory 
molecule with great potential for further investigations. The 75:25 PLGA 30% PEG formulation has demonstrated to be 
the most optimal polymer nanocarrier for this purpose by reducing significantly the burst release and keeping constant 
the sustained-release phase for more than a week. This is of great relevance, as mantaining low doses of IL-2 in tumor 
over prolonged period of time is crucial to achieve a strong antitumor effect. However, there are several challenges 
concerning its synthesis, release, and encapsulation that need further investigation to confirm our observations. The 
protecting molecules, formulation strategies, synthesis, and storage methods, have been questioned in the development 
of this novel polymeric platform. Therefore, all these uncertainties indicate the difficulty and the arduousness of working 
in this promising therapeutic field. Overall, a polymeric nanoparticle encapsulating IL-2 has been developed with unique 
properties in despite of the difficulty of working with protein therapeutics. In addition, few exploratory steps were taken 
in order to functionalize the surface of the nanoparticles with antibodies to target the PD1/PD-L1 immune checkpoint 
axis, offering a dual therapeutic approach.
