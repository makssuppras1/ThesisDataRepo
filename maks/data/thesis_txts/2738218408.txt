Abstract
This thesis investigates the feasibility of using electrodialysis (ED) for phosphorus re­
covery from sewage sludge, addressing the critical need for sustainable phosphorus
management. Phosphorus, essential for agriculture, is becoming increasingly scarce,
needing innovative recovery methods. The study evaluates the impact of ED parame­
ters on sewage sludge from two wastewater treatment plants in Copenhagen.
ED treatments were conducted at 10mA for 1, 2, 3, 4 and 5 days for both Avedøre
and Lynetten sludges.
Additionally, four treatments were performed at 50mA for 2
and 4 days for both sludges. Experimental results indicate that longer ED treatments
enhance phosphorus recovery, with significant differences observed between sludge
types. For a 4­day treatment at 10mA, Avedøre sludge achieved a 40% extraction rate,
while Lynetten sludge reached 23%. Increasing the current to 50mA for the same dura­
tion substantially improved extraction rates to around 70% for both sludges. However,
higher current settings also introduced stability challenges, particularly with Avedøre
sludge.
The findings underscore the importance of optimizing ED process parameters to maxi­
mize phosphorus recovery efficiency while maintaining system stability. This research
provides valuable insights into enhancing the sustainability of wastewater treatment
plants through innovative phosphorus recovery techniques.

Introduction
In the quest for sustainability and resource efficiency, innovative approaches to waste
management and resource use have become essential. Phosphorus, a critical nutrient
for agricultural productivity, is increasingly recognized as a scarce resource with signif­
icant implications for global food security. The European Commission has highlighted
phosphorus as a critical raw material, emphasizing the need for secure and sustainable
recovery methods [1]. Phosphorus is essential for food production, as it plays a cru­
cial role in plant growth and development. Without adequate phosphorus, crop yields
and food quality would diminish, posing a serious threat to food security [2]. As society
transitions towards a circular economy, the recovery and recycling of phosphorus from
waste streams, such as sewage sludge, have become paramount [3].
Sewage sludge, a byproduct of wastewater treatment, represents a substantial un­
tapped resource [4]. Traditionally, sewage sludge is disposed of through land applica­
tion or incineration. However, these methods pose environmental challenges, including
the potential for pollutant contamination and logistical difficulties in sparsely populated
areas [5, 6]. Consequently, there is a pressing need to develop alternative, value­
added applications for sewage sludge, aligning with circular economy principles and
enhancing resource efficiency [7].
Phosphorus is a finite resource, primarily sourced from phosphate rock deposits, which
are geographically concentrated and subject to geopolitical tensions [8]. The depletion
of these reserves, coupled with increasing global demand, underscores the urgency
of developing sustainable phosphorus recovery methods [6, 8]. Current wastewater
treatment processes lead to the accumulation of phosphorus in sewage sludge, but
conventional disposal methods do not facilitate its recovery [9]. This research focuses
on phosphorus recovery from sewage sludge using electrodialytic treatment, address­
ing the critical need for innovative techniques to efficiently extract phosphorus, thereby
reducing environmental impact and enhancing resource resilience.
Electrodialysis (ED) emerges as a promising technology in this context, offering a method
to recover phosphorus in a controlled and sustainable manner. By applying an elec­
trical potential to separate ions, ED can effectively extract phosphorus from sewage
sludge, presenting a viable alternative to conventional disposal methods [10, 11].
This study explores the feasibility of using electrodialysis for phosphorus recovery from
sewage sludge, aiming to transform a waste byproduct into a valuable resource. By
investigating the application of ED, the research seeks to analyze different ED process
parameters to evaluate their significance and discuss more optimal experimental setups
for phosphorus recovery. This approach aligns with circular economy principles and
reduces reliance on finite phosphate rock reserves.
The research contributes to the Sludge2Bricks project, which investigates the possibility
of using sewage sludge as a secondary resource for phosphorus extraction and as
a partial replacement for clay in the production of bricks. As we progress towards a
circular economy, the management of resources and the innovative utilization of waste

The primary objective of this research is to assess the feasibility and effectiveness of

• Evaluating the phosphorus content and distribution in sewage sludge before and
after ED treatment.
• Analyzing the significance of various ED process parameters to determine their
impact on phosphorus recovery.
• Following the simultaneous removal of heavy metals alongside phosphorus re­
covery.
The scope of this thesis encompasses a literature review on wastewater treatment pro­

includes experimental setups, data analysis, and a discussion of the results within the
context of sustainable resource management.
By achieving these objectives, this research aims to advance our understanding of
phosphorus recovery through electrodialysis and offer practical insights for enhancing
the sustainability of wastewater treatment plants. The findings are expected to have
significant implications for policymakers, environmental engineers, and researchers fo­
cused on promoting circular economy practices in wastewater management.

Theory
This section provides a brief description of the roles and operations of wastewater treat­
ment plants, focusing on the use of digested sludge for phosphorus recovery. Addition­
ally, it delves into the electrodialytic process, a technique employed for the removal
of heavy metals and the recovery of phosphorus from municipal sludge. The section
also explores the phenomenon of water splitting at ion­exchange membranes, which is
critical to the functionality of the electrodialytic process.
2.1
Wastewater Treatment Plants (WWTPs)
Wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) play a vital role in managing and treating mu­
nicipal wastewater, ensuring that the effluent released into the environment is safe and
meets regulatory standards. These plants employ a series of physical, chemical, and
biological processes to remove contaminants from the water. This subsection will dis­
cuss the specific stages and mechanisms of WWTPs, with a particular focus on the
digestion process and its significance for phosphorus recovery.
The sludge used for the electrodialytic (ED) treatments in this thesis was sourced from
two different wastewater treatment plants in Copenhagen: Avedøre and Lynetten. Both
WWTPs are managed by Biofos, the largest wastewater utility in Denmark [15]. Biofos
provided the flow diagrams of both WWTPs, which are instrumental in understanding
the treatment processes and the characteristics of the sludge used in the experiments.
The WWTPs follow a structured process for treating municipal wastewater, with a focus
on removing contaminants and recovering valuable resources such as phosphorus.
The sludge used in this thesis was collected after the digester stage in both plants,
making it crucial to understand the processes leading up to this point and their impact
on phosphorus recovery.
Both WWTPs begin with preliminary treatment where influent wastewater is screened
to remove large debris and particles. This is followed by a grit and grease removal
stage. The wastewater then enters primary settling tanks where primary sludge settles
out from the liquid. This primary sludge is directed towards the concentration tank for
thickening before undergoing anaerobic digestion. This stage is consistent across both
WWTPs, aiming to reduce the volume of sludge and prepare it for further treatment.
During secondary treatment, both plants incorporate a bio­P tank where iron salt is
added to aid in the removal of phosphorus by forming insoluble compounds that pre­
cipitate out of the water. This is followed by aeration tanks where biological treatment
occurs, involving microorganisms that break down organic matter. The treated water
then moves to secondary clarifiers for further separation of the treated effluent from the
excess sludge.
The key similarities between Avedøre and Lynetten WWTPs in terms of phosphorus
recovery are the addition of iron salt during secondary treatment and the anaerobic

2.1.1
Digested sludge for phosphorus recovery
The choice of digested sludge as the medium for phosphorus recovery is supported
by several factors, particularly in the context of electrodialytic (ED) treatment. The use
of digested sludge, post­anaerobic digestion, offers significant advantages for effective
phosphorus extraction [11].
In many wastewater treatment plants, biological processes alone are insufficient to re­
move phosphorus to acceptable levels. Consequently, chemical precipitation is em­
ployed as a supplementary method. In Denmark, up to 95% of WWTPs use iron or alu­
minum salts to precipitate phosphorus as insoluble ortho­phosphate complexes [11].
The addition of these salts is also practiced in both Avedøre and Lynetten WWTPs,
ensuring efficient phosphorus removal from the aqueous phase and its accumulation
in the sludge.
However, the chemical precipitation of phosphorus using iron and aluminum salts has
been shown to decrease its availability to crops when the treated sludge or sewage
sludge ash (SSA) is used as a fertilizer. Krogstad et al. [16] noted that the formation of
insoluble iron and aluminum phosphates reduces the crop­availability of phosphorus,
limiting its agronomic value. This presents a challenge in balancing effective phospho­
rus removal with its reuse potential [17].
The rationale for focusing on digested sludge for phosphorus recovery is rooted in its
phosphorus­rich content, especially after anaerobic digestion processes.The most eco­
nomically feasible point for phosphorus recovery is from side streams or process waters
with phosphorus concentrations exceeding 50 mg/l [11].
Anaerobic digestion of sludge leads to the release of phosphorus into the liquid phase,
resulting in higher concentrations of phosphorus in the digested sludge. This makes it
an ideal candidate for recovery processes. Additionally, the anaerobic digestion pro­
cess stabilizes the sludge, reducing its volume and enhancing its suitability for subse­
quent treatment and resource recovery [18].
Electrodialytic (ED) treatment has emerged as a promising technology for the removal
of heavy metals and the recovery of phosphorus from wastewater and sludge [11][19].
The ED process involves the application of an electric field to drive the migration of ions
through selective ion­exchange membranes, facilitating the separation and recovery of
target ions such as phosphorus.
One of the significant advantages of ED treatment is its ability to recover phosphorus
in a form that is more readily available for crop uptake. By avoiding the use of iron or
aluminum precipitates during the treatment process, ED treatment can potentially yield
phosphorus in a more soluble and bioavailable form. This enhances the agronomic
value of the recovered phosphorus, aligning with sustainable agricultural practices [20].
Ebbers et al. [11] demonstrated that direct recovery of phosphorus using ED treat­
ment on reject water obtained after centrifugation of anaerobically digested sludge can
achieve high extraction efficiencies. In their study, up to 95% of phosphorus was ex­
tracted after 48 hours in a three­compartment (3C) ED experiment. This high recovery
rate underscores the effectiveness of ED treatment in reclaiming phosphorus from di­
gested sludge, making it a viable and advantageous option for wastewater treatment

The selection of digested sludge for phosphorus recovery in the context of this thesis is
well­supported by the inherent characteristics of the sludge and the advantages offered
by ED treatment. The high phosphorus content in digested sludge, coupled with the
potential for enhanced crop­availability of the recovered phosphorus, makes it an ideal
medium for sustainable phosphorus recovery processes. The practices at Avedøre
and Lynetten WWTPs, including the addition of iron salts and the implementation of
anaerobic digestion, align with the conditions identified by Ebbers et al. [11] for effective
phosphorus recovery, further justifying the choice of digested sludge for this study.
2.2
Electrodialytic Process
The electrodialytic (ED) process is a technology developed to efficiently separate and
recover valuable resources, such as phosphorus, from waste streams.
Originating
at the Technical University of Denmark in 1992 and patented in 1995, this innovative
method leverages the principles of electrochemistry and ion­exchange to achieve se­
lective separation of ions from complex mixtures like sewage sludge [10]. This process
is particularly important in the context of sustainable resource management and the
transition to a circular economy.
The ED process uses an electric field to drive the movement of ions through selective
barriers known as ion­exchange membranes. When an electric current is applied, posi­
tively charged ions (cations) move towards the negatively charged electrode (cathode),
and negatively charged ions (anions) move towards the positively charged electrode
(anode) [21]. This is known as electromigration [22]. The ion­exchange membranes
selectively allow specific ions to pass through, enabling the separation and concentra­
tion of the target ions.
At the core of the ED process are the electrochemical reactions occurring at the elec­
trodes. These reactions involve the splitting of water molecules into hydrogen ions (H+)
and hydroxide ions (OH –), which play crucial roles in maintaining the electrochemical
balance [10]:
At the anode:
2 H2O −−→2 H+ + 1
2 O2(g) + 2 e−
(2.1)
At the cathode:
2 H2O + 2 e−−−→2 OH−+ H2(g)
(2.2)
The overall process of water splitting can be summarized in terms of ion generation:
2 H2O −−→2 H+ + 2 OH−
(2.3)
This innovative technology employs ion­exchange membranes to facilitate the selective
separation of anions and cations, which is particularly useful in the recovery of valuable
resources such as phosphorus from waste streams like sewage sludge [23]. One of the
advantages of using ion­exchange membranes is that the products from the electrode
processes will not enter the solution [24].

As seen in Fig.2.3, the ion­exchange membranes used in the process are classified into
cation exchange membranes (CEMs) and anion exchange membranes (AEMs). CEMs
only allow the passage of cations, which migrate towards the cathode. For example,
during the ED process, H+ ions generated at the anode (Eq. 2.1) migrate through the
CEM towards the cathode but the hydroxide ions (OH –) produced at the surface of the
cathode will not enter the solution [24]. Conversely, AEMs allow only anions to pass,
moving them towards the anode. For instance, phosphate ions (PO4
3 –) move through
the AEM towards the anode [23].
In a 3C cell, the central compartment serves as an ion exchange medium, providing a
controlled environment for ion separation and minimizing contamination between the
anode and cathode compartments. This setup enhances the purity of the recovered
phosphorus and improves the overall efficiency of the process [10].
The application of electrodialysis in sewage sludge treatment has shown promising
results in terms of phosphorus recovery and the removal of heavy metals and organic
contaminants [11, 26]. For instance, studies have demonstrated that varying current
intensities can influence the efficiency of phosphorus recovery, with optimal conditions
achieving significant phosphorus extraction [11].
In summary, the electrodialytic process represents a sustainable and effective method
for resource recovery from sewage sludge. By leveraging the selective separation ca­
pabilities of ion­exchange membranes and optimizing process parameters, ED can con­
tribute to enhanced resource efficiency and support the transition towards a circular
economy [10][23].

2.2.1
Water splitting at ion­exchange membranes
Water splitting at ion­exchange membranes (AEMs and CEMs) in the electrodialytic
process significantly influences the pH dynamics within the middle chamber. Under­
standing these processes is crucial for optimizing conditions to achieve effective soil
remediation and resource recovery.
When water splitting takes place at the ion­exchange membranes, H+ and OH­ ions
are generated (Eq. 2.3) as seen in Fig.2.3. At the AEM, the hydroxide ions produced
will migrate towards the anode and the hydrogen ions will migrate towards the cathode,
acidifying the solution and enhancing the release of elements from the matrix.
On the other hand, if water splitting takes place at the CEM, the solution will become
more basic as the hydrogen ions will migrate towards the cathode and the concentra­
tion of hydroxide ions will increase. This could damage the remediation process as
hydroxide ions could precipitate with cations from the solution forming a hard layer next
to the membrane [24].
Ottosen et al. [24] suggest that this precipitation can lead to increased resistivity and
hinder ion transport efficiency through the CEM. Therefore, the presence of OH – ions
and subsequent alkalization of the middle chamber may pose challenges in achieving
optimal solution remediation and resource recovery outcomes. Due to this, the pH in
the cathode chamber has to be manually corrected to a value below pH 2 [11].
In order to determine the optimal conditions for 3C ED treatment, the concept of lim­
iting current density must be mentioned. The limiting current density represents the
maximum rate at which ions can be transported through the ion­exchange membranes
under specific operational parameters [24].
The optimal current for electrodialytic soil remediation occurs when the limiting current
of the AEM is exceeded, while the limiting current of the CEM is not exceeded. This
condition ensures that an acidic front moves through the soil from the AEM towards the
cathode, facilitating the mobilization of heavy metals in an acidic environment [24].
Exceeding the limiting current of the AEM promotes efficient H+ ion generation and min­
imizes OH – production, thereby maintaining the desired acidic conditions in the middle
chamber. In contrast, not exceeding the limiting current of the CEM helps prevent
excessive OH – production and associated alkalization, which could hinder the remedi­
ation process.
In summary, water splitting at ion­exchange membranes plays a critical role in influ­
encing the pH dynamics within the electrodialytic setup. Optimal conditions for 3C ED
treatment involve exceeding the limiting current of the AEM while not exceeding the
limiting current of the CEM to ensure the solution is acidified, which is beneficial for the
release of elements from the matrix. This understanding is particularly relevant for the
thesis as the aim is the recovery of phosphorus from sewage sludge.

Analyzing the anions in sewage sludge is crucial for understanding the presence and
mobility of dissolved ions and compounds within this material. This analysis is partic­

studies. By identifying the primary soluble anions, we can gain insights into the chemi­

• 50 ml plastic vials

• Shaking table

• 0.45 µm filters
• 20 ml plastic vials
• Distilled water
• Dried and crushed SS samples
The experiment began by weighing 10 grams of dried sewage sludge using a technical
balance. These weighed samples were then transferred into individual 50 ml plastic
bottles, ensuring proper labelling for identification throughout the experiment.

The carbonate content in sewage sludge is a critical parameter to assess, particularly
when considering the extraction of valuable nutrients such as phosphorus. Carbonates,
primarily present in the form of calcium carbonate, can significantly influence the chem­

In order to calculate the initial mass, to start with, the mean concentration of the ele­
ment in the solid phase (Csolid), mg/kg, was multiplied by the mass of the solid phase,
calculated as 400 grams multiplied by (1−water content %). This value was converted
to mg by multiplying by 10−3.
Mass in solid phase (mg) = Csolid × 400g × (1 −water content %) × 10−3
Similarly, the mean concentration of the element in the liquid phase (Cliquid), mg/l, was
multiplied by the volume of the liquid phase, calculated as 400 ml multiplied by water content %.
This value was also converted to mg by multiplying by 10−3.
Mass in liquid phase (mg) = Cliquid × 400ml × water content % × 10−3
The total initial mass of the element was determined by adding the masses from both
phases. As seen in the purple cells in Figure 3.15, the total initial mass of Al is 41.68
mg.
To calculate the total mass of elements at the end of the ED treatment it is essential
to include all components involved, not only the SS itself. This includes initial and final
samples of electrolytes, electrodes, membranes, and the stirrer too. After the treat­
ment, these components were soaked in nitric acid solutions to release the metals they
absorbed. The volumes of these solutions were recorded and the metal concentra­
tions were determined using ICP­OES analysis. The volumes measured for the S2B­1
treatment, are illustrated in Figure 3.16 , so the procedure can be followed easier.
Figure 3.16: Volumes measured for S2B­1 components.
To calculate the amount of the element inside each component, its concentration was
multiplied by its volume. For example, the amount of aluminium in the anode after the
ED treatment was calculated by multiplying 0.0723 mg/l (see Figure 3.15) by 66 ml
(see Figure 3.16) and 10−3 to obtain 0.006 mg (in Figure 3.15). The total mass of each
element was then calculated by adding the masses obtained from all components (blue
cells in Figure 3.15) being 39 mg for aluminium in this example.
Regarding the end sludge samples, the mean concentration (mg/kg) was multiplied by
the amount of end sludge after the ED treatment and 10−3 to obtain mg. It was decided
to calculate the ”dry sludge without loss” in order to quantify the amount of sludge lost
when moving the treated SS from the centrifuge containers to the petri dish. For some
experiments it was around 20% so it was decided to use the ”dry sludge without loss”
value for the mass balance calculations.

The relationship between conductivity and voltage can be explained using the principles
of Ohm’s Law and the properties of electrical conductivity.
First, Ohm’s Law states:
V = IR
where V is the voltage, I is the current and R is the resistance.

The resistance R of an electrolyte solution is related to its conductivity κ by the following
equation:
R = L
κA
where L is the distance between the electrodes, κ is the conductivity and A is the cross­
sectional area.
Combining these two equations, we obtain:
V = IL
κA
(4.1)
This shows that the voltage V is inversely proportional to the conductivity κ. As the
conductivity decreases due to the removal of ions during the ED process, the voltage
must increase to maintain the same current.
This relationship explains the observed behaviour in the experiments. As the ED pro­
cess progresses and ions are removed, the conductivity κ decreases, leading to an
increase in the voltage V required to maintain the constant current I.

5
Discussion
To start with, there is a clear difference in the samples due to their characteristics. As
stated in Table 4.1, the initial pH is similar for both Avedøre and Lynetten, but their con­
ductivity differs, with values around 4.65 mS/cm and 7.65 mS/cm, respectively. These
values must be taken into account as they will be crucial to understand the behaviour
of the sludges during the ED treatments.
As seen in Figure 4.3, a trend can be identified between the conductivity and the dura­
tion of the ED treatments run at 10mA. The longer an experiment is run, independently
of the sludge used, the conductivity of the sludge decreases. During the ED process,
ions are transported out of the middle chamber. This could be explained by water
splitting taking place in the AEM. As stated in section 2.2, H+ ions are generated thus
increasing their concentration in the suspension. These ions travel towards the cath­
ode, acidifying the solution. The acidification of the solution is beneficial for the release
of ions.
Regarding the distribution of P for the ED treatments run at 10mA for 3 different du­
rations seen at Figure 4.17, a clear trend is identified. The longer the duration of the
experiment, the more P is extracted from the solid phase of the SS, referred to as end
sludge. In the case of P, migratable compounds such as H2PO−
4 and HPO2−
4 , will move
towards the anode chamber which explains why the amount of P found in the anolyte
becomes more significant with the duration of the experiment [26].
Comparing the distributions of P for the same duration and different sludges, it can be
seen in Figure 4.18 that P is extracted faster from Avedøre than Lynetten. This specially
stands out comparing the 3­day treatment where the amount of P found in the anolyte
for A3 is double that of L3. The release and transport of ions play an important role.
As shown in Figure 4.18b, ions are released faster from Avedøre than from Lynetten.
This is evidenced by the comparison of phosphorus proportions in the end sludge, with
most of the released ions from Avedøre being transported to the anolyte (end +).
In Figure 4.18c the distributions obtained for the second batch of experiments (2 and
4 days) are also included. This shows a clear difference between the behaviour of
the sludges which could not be identified with Figure 4.18. While both Avedøre and
Lynetten sludges follow an overall trend of increasing P extraction over time, Avedøre
experiences a rapid extraction phase until day 4, after which the release rate slows
down. In contrast, Lynetten shows a steady and continuous increase in the extrac­
tion rate throughout the treatment duration as seen in Figure 4.25. This suggests that
Avedøre has a faster initial release of P, possibly reaching a near­saturation point by
day 4, whereas Lynetten’s P release rate improves progressively with extended treat­
ment duration. The maximum phosphorus extracted from both sludges after the 5­day
ED treatments run at 10mA is 40% and 36% for Avedøre and Lynetten, respectively.
To analyze the effect of current on the phosphorus extraction rate, additional experi­
ments were conducted at 50mA. This current value was chosen based on the findings
of Benjamin et al. [11], where direct phosphorus recovery from reject water achieved a
Closing the loop: Phosphorus Recovery from Sewage Sludge by Electrodialysis for Sustainable
Resource Management
50
==PAGE:59==
95% extraction rate after 48 hours at this current. While this method proved effective
for reject water, its efficacy for sewage sludge has not yet been established. Analyz­
ing Figure 4.22 suggests that increasing the current from 10mA to 50mA significantly
improves the phosphorus extraction rate for both Avedøre and Lynetten sludges. This
improvement is evident from the larger “end +” fractions at 50mA, indicating that higher
currents enhance the efficiency of phosphorus extraction. The enhanced extraction
rates at higher currents are further illustrated in Figures 4.26 and 4.27. The highest
phosphorus extraction rate achieved in this research was approximately 70% for both
sludges after a 4­day ED treatment at 50mA. This rate surpasses the 60% extraction
rate reported by [11] for digested sewage sludge, although it is important to note the
difference in treatment durations between the studies.
For practical applications, optimizing the current settings and treatment duration is cru­
cial for maximizing phosphorus extraction efficiency, considering the specific character­
istics of the sludge being treated. However, determining whether 50mA is the optimal
current requires a careful balance between extraction efficiency and system stability.
Although the extraction rate of P significantly increases at 50mA, the system must re­
main stable throughout the entire treatment, which is not the case for Avedøre sludge.
As stated in Table 4.7, the current for both A2 and A4 was not maintained constant
at 50mA. The difference in stability between the Avedøre and Lynetten sludge exper­
iments at 50mA, as observed through the significant voltage discrepancies in Figure
4.10, is intriguing. Despite both sludges showing similar drops in conductivity to near­
zero values after 24 hours, as seen in Figure 4.9, the Avedøre sludge exhibited much
higher voltage (up to 140V) compared to Lynetten (around 60V). However, although
the system was stable for Lynetten’s SS, the anion exchange membranes (AEMs) ap­
peared to be clogged upon dismantling the setup, as shown in Figure 4.15a. Despite
this, it did not seem to significantly affect the overall system stability. The fouling of the
AEM was also detected in previous researches [21, 28].
This raises an important question for future research: What factors could cause these
differences in stability and voltage between the two types of sludge? Understanding
these underlying causes could be crucial for optimizing the phosphorus extraction pro­
cess and ensuring system stability.
Simultaneous extraction of heavy metals and recovery of P took place. The order of
extraction for elements in the A5 experiment is Zn (89%)> Cu (66%)> Ni (54%)> Cd
(50%)> Cr (10%)> Pb (6%). For L5 experiment, similar trend was observed being the
order of extraction Zn (83%)> Ni (57%)> Cd (59%)> Cu (43%)> Cr (4%)> Pb (3%).
Ebbers et al. observed the same trend in the order of extraction of HMs but for ED
treatment run at 100mA for 24h [11].
Concluding, while increasing the current to 50mA improves phosphorus extraction rates
for both Avedøre and Lynetten sludges, the stability of the system at this current is a
significant concern, especially for Avedøre sludge. The substantial voltage differences
between the sludges highlight the importance of considering sludge­specific properties
when optimizing ED treatment parameters. Further research into the specific chemical
and physical characteristics of the sludges, as well as exploring different current densi­
ties, could help in finding the optimal balance between extraction efficiency and system
stability.

6
Conclusion
This research has demonstrated the significant potential of electrodialytic (ED) treat­
ment for phosphorus recovery from sewage sludge. The study compared the behaviour
of two different sludges, Avedøre and Lynetten, under varying current intensities and
treatment durations. The findings highlight several key insights regarding the impact of
sludge characteristics, phosphorus extraction efficiency, the effect of current intensity,
and simultaneous heavy metal extraction.
The initial conductivity and pH of the sludge samples were useful in determining their
behaviour during ED treatment. Avedøre and Lynetten exhibited distinct differences
in their phosphorus extraction rates and stability under the same treatment conditions.
Increasing the treatment duration at a lower current (10mA) resulted in progressive
phosphorus extraction from both sludges. Avedøre showed a faster initial release of
phosphorus, whereas Lynetten’s extraction rate increased steadily over time. The high­
est extraction rates achieved were 40% for Avedøre and 36% for Lynetten after a 5­day
treatment.
A significant improvement in phosphorus extraction was observed when the current
was increased to 50mA. Both sludges achieved extraction rates of approximately 70%
after a 4­day treatment. However, the stability of the system at higher currents var­
ied significantly between the sludges. Avedøre sludge experienced substantial voltage
increases, indicating stability issues, while Lynetten maintained a more stable system
despite membrane fouling. The ED process also facilitated the simultaneous extraction
of heavy metals, with varying efficiency depending on the metal and the sludge type.
This highlights the dual benefit of the ED treatment in recovering valuable resources
and reducing heavy metal content in sewage sludge.
In summary, while higher currents significantly enhance phosphorus extraction rates,
the stability of the ED system must be carefully managed. The findings underscore
the importance of considering the specific characteristics of the sludge being treated
to optimize current settings and treatment durations. Future research should focus on
understanding the underlying factors affecting system stability and exploring alternative
current densities to achieve an optimal balance between extraction efficiency and op­
erational stability. The successful application of ED treatment for phosphorus recovery
from sewage sludge can contribute to sustainable resource management and support
the transition towards a circular economy.
