Abstract
In this study the uncertainties related to a proper spacial delivery and distribution of a mZVI
product (Provect-IR) together with a bacterial culture (KB-1) in the establishment of a directly
injected treatment zone was assessed. The investigations were made in relation to a ﬁeld scale
technology development project initiated by the Capital Region of Denmark together with Cowi.
The distribution and delivery of amendment products were assessed by evaluating the chosen in-
jection technique together with ﬁeld- and laboratory results on soil investigations and groundwater
monitoring. The results showed that pressurized direct injections caused the amendment to dis-
tribute mostly in fracture formations complicating the overall monitoring of delivery. The fracture
trajectories were assumed to be governed by the inherent properties of two diﬀerent sandy sedi-
ment layers present in the subsurface injection depth, where a mixture of sandy till and melt-water
sand had created a low permeable transition zone between the two layers. Fracture distribution
patterns were investigated by extraction of treatment zone soil cores and amendment was found
to distributed poorly around the formed fractures. Presence of amendment concentrations were
evaluate based on a thorough investigation on the injected mZVI product content which lead to
a set of monitoring parameters directly related to product presence. Groundwater monitoring for
assessment of spatial amendment delivery showed that only few monitoring well screens were imme-
diately aﬀected by the introduced amendment. At later monitoring rounds, amendment induced
changes showed signs in additional wells leading to further enlightenment of the overall spacial
delivery of amendment in the subsurface. As a consequence of the minor seen amendment induced
alterations, the overall performance in degradation of chlorinated ethenes evaluated on degree of
dechlorination was found poor. Based on investigated pore water velocities the overall success
of the injected treatment zone is believed to improve as the resulting water ﬂow is expected to
elucidate amendment possibly delivered in the low permeable sediment zone in future monitoring
rounds.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Permeable reactive barriers (PRB) consisting of zero-valent iron (ZVI) has been recognized inter-
nationally as an eﬀective remedial strategy in removing chlorinated ethenes for more than 25 years
(Stroo and Ward, 2010), and in Denmark since 1998 (Kjeldsen, 2004). Due to the inherent proper-
ties of Fe0 to react with and degrade chlorinated ethenes, the remediation strategy is found highly
applicable at contaminated sites – especially at former industrial areas – where a lot of chlorinated
oil repellent and degreasing compounds as Perchloroethene (PCE) and Trichloroethene (TCE) has
been stored and used.
Since the ﬁrst constructions of traditional ZVI-based PRBs the strategy has evolved and since mil-
lennium the possibility of directly injected iron slurry treatment zones has gained interest (Stroo
and Ward, 2010; ITRC, 2011; Kjeldsen, 2004). The interest in iron slurry based treatment zones
can be attributed to limitations of the excavation based PRB where diﬃculties related to the ex-
cavation of the trench, limited depth of application, cost of implementation, space constraints, and
reactivity of the millimetric sized iron particles is governing (Luna et al., 2015; Velimirovic et al.,
2014).
Direct injections can be performed with Microscale Zero Valent Iron (mZVI) or Nanoscale Zero
Valent Iron (nZVI) which has been found to overcome most of the restrictions related to the use
of millimetric iron ﬁllings - especially in terms of possible applications and reactivity (Luna et al.,
2015). In addition, direct injections can be situated very locally in the subsurface target zone with
use of Direct Push Injection (DPI) technologies at much deeper levels - ensuring more eﬀective
and overall cost friendly remedial actions (Keane, 2009; Velimirovic et al., 2014).
Even though DPI technologies has gained interest in recent years there is still issues related to
the remedial action. This, especially related to achieving proper spatial distribution of the iron
amendments in the subsurface (Shi et al., 2015; Stroo and Ward, 2010; Luna et al., 2015; Chris-
tensen, 2019; ITRC, 2011; Velimirovic et al., 2014). Direct injections of mZVI amendment requires

Chlorinated Ethenes in Groundwater Aquifers
The characteristics, use and fate of chlorinated ethenes in groundwater aquifers will in the following
sections be assessed.
2.1
Use and Characteristics of Chlorinated Ethenes
Chlorinated ethenes as Perchloroethene (PCE) and Trichloroethene (TCE) are found at numerous
contaminated sites worldwide. These compounds has been used in a wide variety of settings in
the industrial sector, where they was found as great degreasing agents, chemical intermediates,
heat-transfer ﬂuids and dry cleaning ﬂuids (Kueper, B.H., Stroo, H.F., Vogel, C.M., Ward, C.H.,
2014; Stroo and Ward, 2010). In relation to the rapid industrialization emerging in the beginning
of the 20th century, the production and usage of chlorinated solvents increased rapidly. General
awareness of the problems related to chlorinated ethenes emerged in the 1970-80s - 50 years after
the onset of their industrial use. By this time large amounts of solvents had been used, and dis-
posed, with poor environmental awareness (Stroo and Ward, 2010; Rivett, 2010). This has resulted
in a vast amount of sites with chlorinated ethene contaminated groundwater.
Based on toxicology, metabolism, animal studies, and human studies, occupational exposure to
chlorinated ethenes has according to Ruder (2006) been associated with a number of adverse
health eﬀects including central nervous system, reproductive disorder, liver and kidney toxicity,
and carcinogenicity. Together with the inherent properties of being denser than water, relatively
volatile and immiscible in water (see Table 1), chlorinated ethenes also propose a risk to the
environment from reduction of soil, water and air quality.

2.2
Transport & Distribution
Spills of chlorinated ethenes are transported and distributed in the subsurface due to their inherent
physical-chemical properties. Chlorinated ethenes can be characterized as dense non-aqueous phase
liquids (DNAPLs) meaning that they withhold densities greater than water and therefore sinks
deeply into subsurface aquifers (Stroo and Ward, 2010; Siegrist, R. L., Crimi, M., Broholm, M.M.,
McCray, J.E., Illangasekare, T.H., Bjerg, P.L., 2012). A conceptual model of a general DNAPL
spill and the migration down through the vadose- and saturated zones can be seen in Figure 1.
Figure 1: Migration of a DNAPL spill down through the vadose- and saturated zone. Dissolved components forms
a groundwater directional ﬂowing plume from the residual components. Figure from WHO (2006)
DNAPLs migrate vertically through the aquifer from the force of gravity and soil capillarity. The
fraction of DNAPL retained by capillary forces in the porous media is categorized as the residual
fraction. When the DNAPL volume is great enough to exceed the fraction depleted by the residual
saturation in the vadose zone, DNAPLs continue its migration down through the saturated zone.
Vertical migration will continue until the volume is eventually exhausted by the residual satura-
tion process or until it is intercepted by a low permeable layer (Huling and Weaver, 1991). Lateral
migration begins as the DNAPL enters the saturated zone where the components not sorbed to
aquifer materials will dissolve and ﬂow with the groundwater ﬂow direction - causing a spreading
plume, as seen in Figure 1.
Exactly how and where the diﬀerent chlorinated ethenes are transported are very much determined
by the inherent properties of the individual chemicals and the subsurface characterization. The
physical-chemical properties of PCE, TCE, DCE (1,1, cis, trans) and VC, can be seen in Table 1.

3
Permeable Reactive Barriers with Zero-Valent Iron
Permeable reactive barrier technologies has progressed experimentally and conceptually to its cur-
rent status as a well known and widely used technology (Stroo and Ward, 2010). The PRB concept
7
==PAGE:24==
THEORETICAL BACKGROUND
involves emplacement of a barrier perpendicular to the contaminant trajectory where the barrier
content either passivates or degrades the contaminants passively ﬂowing through the permeable
boundary (Obiri-Nyarko et al., 2014). The type of reactive material is chosen based on the con-
taminant with reduction need. At former industrial areas where especially chlorinated ethenes is
contaminating the groundwater, zero-valent iron has been found very eﬃcient due to its inherent
properties to react with and degrade chlorinated compounds (Stroo and Ward, 2010).
3.1
Construction Methods
Two main construction methods exists for PRBs, with the ﬁrst being installations of a physical iron
barriers through either excavation or trench building and the second being subsurface installations
of a treatment zones through direct injections of ZVI slurries (Stroo and Ward, 2010; ITRC, 2011).
In general, excavation based construction is preferred at shallow, larger scale, open area reme-
diations, whereas directly injected slurry treatment zones are preferred at deeper contamination,
in urban areas and around buildings with minimal work space (ITRC, 2011). Due to its feasible
applicability, direct injections has gained increasingly more interest (Stroo and Ward, 2010; ITRC,
2011; Kjeldsen, 2004).
In this study, the focus will be on direct injections of zero-valent iron slurry for a treatment zone
establishment.
3.1.1
Amendment Products for Direct Injections
Many diﬀerent reactive medias can be used to establish a treatment zone and is chosen based on
the contaminant situation. Besides zero-valent iron as an amendment product, activated carbon,
zeolites, peat, saw dust, oxygen releasing compounds etc. have been used and evaluated. The
diﬃculties with these is though that they often are more expensive, diﬃcult to access or eﬀective
for only certain groups of contaminants (Obiri-Nyarko et al., 2014).
Various zero-valent iron products are available and used for directly injected treatment zones.
One of the main diﬀerence between the products lies in the size of the particles which ranges from
nanoscaled particles (nZVI), typically ranging between 50-300nm, to microscaled particles (mZVI),
typically ranging from 1-100µm (Provectus, 2014; Rosansky and Sirabian, 2013). Larger particle
ZVI compounds are available but is more rarely used in relation to direct injections (Rosansky
and Sirabian, 2013). Both nZVI and mZVI are highly reactive due to their large surface area.
This enables more rapid degradation of especially recalcitrant contaminants as chlorinated ethenes
(Comba et al., 2010). Zero-valent iron products for remedial purposes can be found as either bare
or modiﬁed particles. In most cases particles are modiﬁed as bare particles are found to agglom-

erate due to particle inherent properties (Vecchia et al., 2009; Comba et al., 2010; Phenrat et al.,
2009). Several polyelectrolytes, surfactants, and biopolymers has been proposed as modiﬁcation
to prevent agglomeration (Singh, R and Misra, V., 2015). Modiﬁcations could further be from
sulﬁdation of particles to mitigate passivation of particles in alkaline waters (Han and Yan, 2016;
Yawei et al., 2019).
In recent times, combinations of amendments are frequently injected together in order to eliminate
possible drawbacks of a single amendment product. This have resulted in enhanced long term
performance, sustained permeability, enhanced contaminant removal mechanisms and accelerated
removal rates (Obiri-Nyarko et al., 2014). Combinations can both be from additives in the product
itself where salts as oxygen scavengers or carbon substrates for hydrogen release are common. As
zero-valent iron degrades through abiotic dechlorination, a bacterial culture is often injected along
to create an abiotic-biotic treatment train (ITRC, 2011; Obiri-Nyarko et al., 2014).
3.1.2
Direct Injections and Distribution in the Subsurface
Directly injected treatment zones are made from a number of injection points arrayed in rows per-
pendicular to the ground water ﬂow direction. Iron slurry mixtures are injected with pressure in
each injection point and at diﬀerent intervals over the target depth, to obtain a "complete" spatial
coverage of the contaminant plume. The placement of injection points and overlapping injection
intervals thus becomes key in order to create as much of a continuous reactive zone as possible
(ITRC, 2011). Injected iron slurry is often mixed with a biopolymer carrier (shear-thinning ﬂuid)
to minimize clogging/sedimentation and particle agglomeration in the injection equipment, and to
enhance the distribution range of the iron particles in the subsurface media (Luna et al., 2015;
Vecchia et al., 2009). A conceptual illustration of a direct injection process can be seen in Figure 3.
In theory, the ideal direct injection would result in a complete spatial distribution of amendment
in the treatment zone, but in practice uniformity is found diﬃcult to achieve. The diﬃculties
related to achieving uniform distribution is mainly caused by low permeability zones and subsur-
face heterogeneity (Siegrist, R. L., Crimi, M., Broholm, M.M., McCray, J.E., Illangasekare, T.H.,
Bjerg, P.L., 2012). Site speciﬁc geological investigations is then key when monitoring amendment
delivery and distribution. Pressurized injections of slurry amendments typically leads to hydraulic
fracturing of the subsurface media as injections are made at pressures that exceeds the lithostatic
pressure and cohesive strength of the subsurface formation (Stroo and Ward, 2010; Luna et al.,
2015; Rosansky and Sirabian, 2013; ITRC, 2011). In theory, then achievement of homogeneous
distribution can be made by injecting amendment at lower pressures to prevent fracturing of the
subsurface media.

based on site speciﬁcs is needed in order to improve the knowledge on the technology applicability
and eﬃciency (Shi et al., 2015; Luna et al., 2015; Comba et al., 2010).

Monitoring Amendment Distribution in the Subsurface
To the authors knowledge, ﬁeld scale monitoring programs focusing solely on distribution of amend-
ment in the subsurface is scarce and only assessed in a few publications (Velimirovic et al., 2014;
Savoie et al., 2004). In other documented ﬁeld scale studies assessing distribution (Shi et al., 2015;
Savoie et al., 2004; Csóvári, M. and Csicsák, J. and Földing, G and Simoncsics, G., 2005; Luna
et al., 2015; Vecchia et al., 2009; Liang et al., 2000; Keane, 2009; Tosco et al., 2014; Hosseini and
Tosco, 2013), distribution is mainly evaluated based on long term performance monitoring of zero-
valent iron fate, reactivity and eﬃciency in target contaminant reduction. Multiple other processes
can though reveal amendment delivery and distribution in the subsurface and more comprehensive
investigations related to distribution of amendments during groundwater remediation is needed to
further elucidate the potential of this technology (Shi et al., 2015).
Monitoring distribution of amendment in the subsurface can be divided into two indication cate-
gories; direct and indirect indications. The two diﬀerent methods and the meaning of each will be
described in the following subsections.

4.1
Direct Monitoring
Direct indication methods are few and is usually divided into direct evidence of ZVI particles
and indications of amendment presence.
For direct evidence of ZVI, microscopic or magnetic
applications may be applied whereas indications of amendment presence is visualized on soil cores
in the ﬁeld (Shi et al., 2015). As microscopic and magnetic investigations detects the most reduced
state of the particle Fe0, proper sampling, sample handling and analysis is required to preserve even
small quantities. As soon as iron is oxidized to Fe2+ or Fe3+ it becomes diﬃcult to distinguish the
injected from the already aquifer present iron (Shi et al., 2015). It should be mentioned that some
naturally occurring iron oxides as magnetite also proposed magnetic proprieties as Fe0. Microscopic
evidence requires laboratory settings and is therefore rarely used in routine ﬁeld analysis. From
visual soil core inspections the charge of the iron particle becomes more irrelevant as the main focus
is related to colorimetric detection (Shi et al., 2015). From colorimetric inspection both evidence
of amendment and the amendment distribution pattern can be assessed.
4.2
Indirect Monitoring
From the limitations in direct methods, then naturally most methods used to assess the presence of
injected amendment is indirect. Soil samples can be collected for total iron analysis and other prod-
uct related constituents. This can reveal the exact amount delivered, but may lack in evaluation of
the distribution pattern as samples are taken over a range and mixed before analysis. Otherwise,
investigations are typically based on changes in water quality parameters in which the presence
or inherent properties of the injected amendment can be detected (Shi et al., 2015; Velimirovic
et al., 2014; Savoie et al., 2004). If amendment has been delivered very close to a monitoring
well, immediate evidence of amendment can be seen from groundwater chemistry alterations. If
amendment has been delivered upstream of a monitoring well groundwater chemistry alterations
can be expected to be seen over time as the amendment induced alterations will distribute with the
groundwater ﬂow. In the following sections some of the possible groundwater chemistry changes
and the diﬀerent processes leading to the speciﬁc change will be described.

et al., 2005).

50-100% of the VFAs is dissociated in a normal alkalinity titration. This then means that the
alkalinity measured is both a product of dissociated carbonate and the subsequently dissociated
VFAs.
To further evaluate the presence of VFAs in the normal titration, a Gran-titration can be used.
Here the titration is made to pH 3.5 which would then lead to that all VFAs is assumed dissociated
and the result is a measure of all carbonate and all VFAs.
All of the above mentioned processes will if assessed in a combination provide evidence for both
occurrence and reactive eﬀects of the injected amendment products (Shi et al., 2015; Stroo and
Ward, 2010).

Pre-Injection Test
An injection test was performed at site in 2019 by Cowi to try out diﬀerent amendment delivery
methods with two diﬀerent amendment products. The injection test was made in order to optimize
the outcome of the development project assessed in this study. The injection test resulted in the
optimal injection method being with use of Geoprobe hydraulic hammering (DPI), and as men-

target delivery zone. A detailed description of the injection test can be found in Cowi (2019a).

7.5
Observed Changes in Ground Water Parameters
The amendment induced changes in the aquifer and proof of amendment delivery in the subsur-
face were investigated based on water sampling from 15 monitoring wells located both upstream,
downstream and in between the injection points. In the following sections, the observed changes
in water quality parameters will be divided into a general baseline indication and then upstream-,
downstream-, and in between injection point changes. Not all wells were evaluated for immediate
amendment delivery as monitoring rounds were not performed consistently in all well screens during
the monitoring period. All monitoring data can be seen in Appendix EXCEL. All concentrations
used in the evaluation is total concentrations.
7.5.1
Baseline Conditions
Baseline estimations showed anaerobic and reducing conditions in all well screens besides MB12-2
with a positive reduction potential of 68.7mV. In the remaining well screens, baseline O2 varied
between 0.18mg/L - 0.54mg/L and baseline ORP between -14.2mV to -248.5mV. Speciﬁc con-
ductance and pH was found to be relatively uniform across all wells with a speciﬁc conductance
varying between 130mS/m to 170mS/m and near neutral pH around 7±0.5. Initial concentrations
of ammonium, nitrite and nitrate were in all wells close to zero. Chloride was found to vary be-
tween 79mg/L - 340mg/L, with most wells being around 250mg/L. Substantial amounts of sulphate
was discovered in all wells ranging between 35mg/L - 140mg/L, thus indicating sulphate reducing
conditions in the aquifer. Average concentrations of iron, calcium, potassium, and sodium were
detected at 5.8mg-Fe/L, 260mg-Ca/L, 4.4mg-K/L, 58,5mg-Na/L, respectively.

8.1
Provect-IR Analysis
The exact Provect-IR composition were investigated in order to know exactly what to expect when
evaluating amendment induced alterations in the subsurface. The product was found to contain
44% iron which were otherwise assumed 60%.
Product content were only analyzed in a pure
sample and the estimated product content was estimated under the assumption that the analysis
recovered 100% of the elements in the sample. This is though doubtful since iron recovery rates
were found to underestimate the content in the pre-known samples with an increasing degree of
uncertainty, with increasing slurry concentrations. With this in mind, the seen result from the
product content analysis could very well be underestimated with around 20% in itself (found for
higher slurry concentrations). If the product content is underestimated with 20%, the product
would contain ∼53% iron in contrast to the 44% and the given content of 60% could then very well
be true. Further investigations on diﬀerent slurry concentrations are recommended to investigate
the recovery of pure Provect-IR slurry samples. The total carbon content is known to be a mixture
of diﬀerent organic constituents and in order to see the exact contribution of which organic when
looking at the measured changes in water parameters, investigations on the organic part of the
product would need further investigations.

deviation in the sandy till layer were as well seen in the Cowi background samples where both a

8.3
Amendment Distribution Patterns in Soil
Amendment distribution evaluated on soil investigations is of great use to determine how the
amendment has distributed in the subsurface.
With the found geological transition, distribution was evaluated in both sandy till and melt-water
sand. Through soil core extractions and sediment analysis, distribution patterns of the injected
amendment in the subsurface were investigated. Results from the diﬀerent soil core investigations
revealed a rather singular distribution pattern independent on the sediment type. Mostly fractures
were detected in the soil core investigations and thus the distribution pattern looks a lot like other
ﬁeld scale studies evaluating distribution from direct push injections (Velimirovic et al., 2014; Luna
et al., 2015). The main reason for the fractured distribution pattern is expected to be due to a
pressure injection exceeding the critical pressure of the subsurface medias (Luna et al., 2015).
Two diﬀerent distribution patterns were detected in areas where substantial amounts of amendment
had been delivered - fractures and shades (more homogeneous distribution).
Two amendment
areas were sampled in the sandy till layer - both in fracture formation. From the mineral proﬁles
it was seen that there was a tendency between iron, TOC, sodium and sulphur - all increasing
simultaneously. For calcium, potassium and magnesium, the opposite tendency was observed. The
tendencies can be explained by the known product content and the background soil concentrations.
Both iron, TOC, sodium and sulphur was found at higher concentrations in the product than in
the soil. This indicates that where a lot of amendment was found, soil has been pushed away and
the measured concentrations resembles the pure product content. This was especially seen in the
ﬁrst sampled fracture (KB11) with the highest measured concentrations. Here, iron and calcium
increased/decreased to concentrations very similar to the estimated product content.
Calcium
in KB11 was seen to be at most places below the background level. This emphasizes the above
discussed uncertainty of sediment composition in the mixed zone between 13.00 - 14.5 mbgs as
the background for the melt-water sand would ﬁt the measured concentrations better. For all
fractured areas, no real indications of amendment distribution around the fracture was seen. This
could indicate that the pressure build-up causing the fracture has created a preferential ﬂow line
in which amendment has been delivered at a pressure higher than what makes it possible for the

8.4
Groundwater Monitoring
Where investigations in soil is mostly for evaluating how the amendment has distributed, the
groundwater monitoring can give a more precise indication of where the amendment has dis-
tributed based on changes that is suspected to be a result of amendment presence.
In the following sections, a discussion of the seen changes and their relation to amendment con-
stituents will be presented.
The discussion will ﬁrst evaluate the seen parameter changes and
its relation to other altered parameters. This will help to emphasize which trends in the aquifer
that can be directly related to the presence of amendment. The discussion will afterwards focus
on where the changes were seen in order to investigate the spacial distribution of amendment in
the aquifer. Here, the parameter evolution on the graphs seen over time will be used to evaluate
the amendment placement in relation to the monitoring wells. At last, the seen changes and the
spacial delivery in the diﬀerent monitoring well screens will be combined with the background soil
investigations and the degree of dechlorination to get a full overview of the injection success in
Hvedemarken.
8.4.1
Amendment Induced Changes in Water Quality Parameters
In general, little signs of amendment distribution was seen when taking all 15 monitored wells
into account. Of the 15 wells, 12 was monitored after 2 weeks but only 6 wells showed immediate
changes in groundwater quality (1 upstream, 2 downstream and the 3 wells in between injection
points). After 3 months, 3 more wells were found aﬀected at diﬀerent degrees. In the wells with
changing water parameters, signs of changes were all very similar and is therefore assumed to be
a consequence of amendment presence. An overview of the aﬀected wells can be seen in Figure 27
where the color intensity denotes the degree of changes in water parameters. Possible reasons for
the seen changes will be explained in the following paragraphs.
Anaerobic corrosion of zero-valent iron is a spontaneous reaction process and is therefore a good
indicator when evaluating where iron containing amendment has been delivered. The eﬀect of

acid production in the aquifer - assumed biotically driven. One well did though show increasing pH
values over the 3 months and this tendency can be kept in mind when discussing the biotic changes.
Chloride production from dechlorination was not found to be major in any wells. It is known that
the degree of dechlorination was found almost unaltered after 3 months and from that it makes
sense that only little chloride has been produced. In addition, chlorinated compounds is present in
the aquifer as µg/L concentrations and therefore a lot of contaminants should have been degraded
in order to see a real impact on the chloride mg/L concentration in the aquifer.
Biotic induced changes were monitored due to the introduction of organics with the amendment
products. Together with the estimated 20% TC in Provect-IR, KB-1 was injected and the presence
of either was monitored by looking at the organic changes in the aquifer. Four parameters was
assumed directly related to the presence of organics in the aquifer, NVOC, HCO3, HCO3* and
VFAs. NVOC is a measure of all non-volatile organics in the aquifer and was in all aﬀected wells
seen to increase immediately after injection of amendments. Since a lot of the organic additives
is expected to ferment to VFAs, it should be possible to see some similarities between all organic
parameters. This was also the case for most wells were the 4 diﬀerent parameters followed the
same evolution over time. As NVOC does not include the VFAs, the HCO3 concentration was
expected to at all times be higher than NVOC since both some non-volatile organics and VFAs
is dissociated in the alkalinity titration at up to 100% (Zhang et al., 2019). Again a dissimilarity
was seen in MB2-1, where NVOC increased rapidly after 2 weeks where non of the other organic
parameters increased at the same degree. This could emphasize the great amount of non-degraded
guar gum in the measurements which has kept a lot of the organics in suspension and delayed the

Gran titrations and VFAs were made/monitored from 2 months after injection to further inves-
tigate the presence of VFAs in the aquifer. The Gran titration was made to ensure dissociation
of all VFAs by titration down to a pH lower than the lowest pKa of the individual VFAs (see
Section 4.2.2). With the known relation between HCO3 and VFAs the expected result would be
an individual VFA measure below the normal alkalinity measure (HCO3 + some VFAs) below the
gran alkalinity (HCO3 + all VFAs). This was though not the case in all wells as both MB2-1
and MB13-1 showed normal alkalinity measures above both gran and VFA measures. The reason
behind the deviant results for the normal alkalinity and VFAs is unknown, but could be related
to high organic content in these two well screens. During gran-titrations at DTU it was observed
that pH varied a lot in high carbon content samples. The reason for this is expected to be from
evaporating CO2 formed under the dissociation of organics. Thereby it is possible that the noted
pH during these experiments is wrong and thereby leading to a false alkalinity measure.
The overall presence of VFAs in the aquifer is assumed related to the overall decreasing pH ten-
dencies. It is known from literature that bacterial fermentation in both primary and secondary
terms produces acids. And when a lot of organics is present in the subsurface, acid production is
believed to be greater than the H+ consumption and thereby decreasing the pH in the aquifer. In
the case with the one well screen showing increasing pH during the 3 month monitoring period,
the biotic content were also found low.
Methane production was monitored in relation to the biotic acetate production in the aquifer. In
no well screens a substantial increase in methane was detected which indicates that Monacolin K
added as an antimethanogenic additive in Provect-IR is functioning well.
Calcium was assessed together with the organics mainly due to the additive calcium dipropionate.
Calcium concentrations was seen to not follow the tendencies of the other organic parameters as
calcium concentrations both increased and decreased independent of the other. This could on one
hand emphasize the uncertainty of the constituents of the 20% TC in the product and on the other
hand be a result of calcite dissolution and precipitation due to the varying pH. In general, calcium
concentrations were found at higher levels after 3 months compared to the baseline estimation and
this indicates that calcite is dissolved as a consequence of the reduced pH after 3 months.
The eﬀect of the additive salt sodium sulﬁte was monitored in relation to changes in conductiv-
ity. It is known that dissolution of a salt in water will lead to cat- and anion production which

also seen to increase but in wells with minor concentrations, it was seen that sulphate decreased

known from literature that the primary fermentation of complex organic material from hydrolyses
produces N-holding amino acids which then could be the source of ammoniﬁcation.

All signs of amendment delivery so far in the development project besides the amendment delivery
in MB15-2 has been found to be in the most deep part of the aquifer - below the expected transition
zone. This emphasizes the great uncertainties related to amendment distribution with pressurized
injections in aquifers with low permeability layers. The minor presence of amendment in the most
shallow wells was somehow expected already after the visual inspections of soil cores, as no real
indication of amendment was seen in the depth between 14.5-15.5 mbgs. It is though known that
3 extracted cores will not disclose all amendment trajectories in the aquifer.
It is assumed based on the results that the seen amendment induced alterations in the deeper
melt-water sand layer is caused by injections in the same depth range. All daylighting incidents
were found to occur from 15.5 mbgs and up which could indicate that injections in the, or close
to the, low permeable layer leads the injected amendment trajectory upwards towards layers un-
der less lithostatic pressure and in some cases the surface. This does not mean that amendment
is not delivered in the low permeable zone where MB15 is a good example as most amendment
was found in the more shallow well screen. The reasons for this could though again very well be
related to the uncertainty of the transition zone. From Figure 30 it can be seen that the geo-
logical transition between the two layers vary a lot and it is possible that the transition zone is
situated deeper in this transect compared to the other transects upstream seen in Figure 28 and 29.
The exact transition between the layers can be further analyzed by looking at the contaminant
concentrations in the individual well screens. In transect A and transect B it is seen that the
majority of contaminants is measured in the most shallow wells. This could indicate that the
low permeability in the transition zone accumulates the contaminants and thereby increases the
total concentration. In transect C it is seen that the contaminant concentration varies a bit more
between the diﬀerent well screens. For example in MB11, where the majority of the contamination
is found in the most deep well which is additionally situated just below the assumed transition
between the two layers. With this, it is believed that the transition zone is situated deeper in the
southern part of the treatment zone.
With the known pore water velocities in the individual layers it is possible to determine more
precisely where the amendment has been delivered. For the three layers besides the transition zone
the water was estimated to have travelled 11.6-13.3 m/3months. In Figure 27 it is seen that the
length from the transect A to transect B is around 12m, which means that amendment presence in
a relevant depth, at a relevant amount, between the diﬀerent transects would have shown signs of
induced changes in the downstream monitoring wells in transect C. This indicates that only little
amendment is present at a relevant depth in the treatment zone as neither MB10, MB11, MB12
or MB14 in transect C have seen any signs of amendment induced changes.

Conclusion
In this study a ﬁeld scale injection and the related processes regarding subsurface delivery and
distribution of a microscaled zero-valent iron product (Provect-IR) together with a bacterial culture
(KB-1) were assessed. The investigation evolved around 4 aims which when combined helped to
enlighten some of the uncertainties related to in-situ placement of reactive treatment zones. A
thorough subsurface site investigation was coupled to the actual injection of amendment in order to
look for distribution patterns in soil and amendment induced changes in groundwater quality. Soil
cores were extracted from 3 diﬀerent places in between the injection points to evaluate the direct
evidence of amendment in the aquifer. Groundwater was sampled in 4 diﬀerent monitoring events
to evaluate where the amendment had travelled during the injection. At last, the seen distribution
was evaluated based on a general performance monitoring of the treatment zones ability to degrade
the target contaminants. Results from the diﬀerent investigations lead to the following learnings
and conclusions on a ﬁeld scale injection of an abiotic-biotic amendment mixture:
• Amendment was injected in a depth range where both sandy till and melt-water sand were
present. The discovery of a low permeable transition zone between the two sediment layers resulted
in a troublesome injection depth between 13.00-15.50 mbgs where 3 incidents of daylighting was
seen. The daylighting incidents is believed to have occurred as a result of a pressure injection
exceeding the strength of the low permeable subsurface media leading formed fractures towards
medias with higher permeability i.e. shallow layers.
• Few signs of amendment presence was seen on the extracted soil cores and most in fracture for-
mations. Discrete samples were taken in 1cm-intervals to evaluate the distribution in and around
amendment aﬀected areas. The composition of Provect-IR was analyzed in order to put the seen
results into perspective. Results showed that distribution in fracture formations lead to narrow
distribution of amendment. No amendment was detected around fractures and concentrations in
the fractures were similar to the estimated product content. In one area, amendment was found
distributed more homogeneously. This resulted in a more broad distribution pattern with mea-
sured concentrations equal to the background soil concentrations with an addition of the product
contents. The homogeneous distribution is believed to have occurred due to a lower injection pres-
sure in that area which corresponds well with literature on amendment distribution in soil from
direct injections.
• The discrete samples were compared to average iron samples and results showed that an average
investigation detects ∼70% of the iron detected in a discrete investigation. This makes the average
sampling methodology useful as an indicator on amendment presence. The lack of the average
sample methodology is though that the distribution pattern is unknown and in remediation projects
where the distribution pattern is important it is concluded that discrete samples can proof valuable.

CONCLUSION
• Water monitoring rounds showed only few sings of amendment delivery in the aquifer where
aﬀected well screens in most cases were situated below the expected depth of the transition zone.
Below the transition zone, one well screen was found aﬀected upstream the injection points and 2
wells were found aﬀected downstream the injection points. Well screens in between the injection
points showed in general little signs of amendment, which is believed to be a consequence of
equipment cleansing with guar gum between each injection interval. This theory ﬁts well with
the seen highest amendment concentrations furthest from the injection points. One shallow well
screen downstream was found aﬀected by amendment after the injection. With the known geology
it is believed that the reason for the seen changes in this shallow screen is due to a lower situated
transition from sandy till to melt-water sand in the most downstream transect. This emphasizes
the need for thorough geological investigations when injecting in two diﬀerent sediment types.
• Amendment presence was evaluated based on expected amendment induced alterations as a
consequence of the inherent properties of the injected products. Not all well screens were mon-
itored consistently and thereby only a selection of wells could be used for immediate detection
of amendments. In the immediately aﬀected wells, introduction of amendment was clearly seen
with increases in parameter relevant elements and alterations in pH and ORP from iron corrosion.
Despite literature, pH was seen to decrease during the monitoring period, which was concluded to
be a sign of biotic delivery in terms of acid production from fermentation. Some unaﬀected wells
showed increases in element parameters after 3 months similar to the immediately aﬀected wells
which indicates amendment delivery upstream the well screens.
• Pore water velocities from grain size distribution analysis made it possible to make a dimensional
assessment of the amendment delivery. Sediment samples investigated on each side of the transition
zone showed a similar pore water velocity with a groundwater movement of ∼12.5m/3months in
12-13 mbgs and 16.10 mbgs. With a length between the most outer placed transects of 12m it
can be concluded that unaﬀected wells in these depths will see no or only little sign of amendment
induced alterations in future monitoring rounds. The pore water velocity in the transition zone
was estimated to be lower than the other layers with a groundwater movement of ∼4.5m/3months
in 14.10 mbgs.
This indicates that if any amendment has been delivered in this depth range,
amendment induced changes is likely to be seen in future monitoring rounds.
• Most contamination was found in the depth of the expected transition zone. This is believed
to be a consequence of contaminant accumulation in the low permeable layer. Only little signs of
amendment distribution in this depth was though seen. This subsequently lead to a low degree of
dechlorination in the aquifer and so far the general performance of the injected treatment zone is
concluded to be poor. Due to the low groundwater ﬂow in the contaminant depth, it is though
expected that the degree of dechlorination, and hence the general performance, will increase as a
consequence of amendment reaching downstream well screens in the low permeable zone.
