Abstract  
10 
The brown shrimp (Crangon crangon) is an epibenthic species that plays a vital role in coastal food 
11 
webs, with a level of productivity sufficient to sustain one of the most valuable fisheries in the North 
12 
Sea. It has been proposed that brown shrimp migrate from the southern Wadden Sea to the northern 
13 
Wadden Sea, with the migration beginning in March-April, when adult shrimp move north. Since 
14 
2019, the Danish brown shrimp fishery has experienced declining yields potentially linked to con-
15 
struction of subsea power cables near the fishing grounds. Subsea power cables (SPCs) emit electro-
16 
magnetic fields (EMFs) that may disrupt marine life and habitats in the surrounding environment. 
17 
These EMF emissions can influence the distribution of marine species by attracting or repelling them, 
18 
potentially acting as barriers that hinder both local movements and long-distance migrations. This 
19 
study investigates whether the addition of two subsea power cables in 2018-2019 to the Danish brown 
20 
shrimp fishery impede northward migration during March-April. Using landings per unit effort 
21 
(LPUE) as an indicator for brown shrimp abundance, a generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) was 
22 
==PAGE:5==
2 
 
used to determine an effect of cable installation (before/after) in three different geographical areas 
23 
(south of the cables, between the cables and north of the cables), hypothesizing a larger decrease in 
24 
abundance in the northern areas. The GLMM revealed a negative effect of the cable installation on 
25 
brown shrimp abundance in all three areas. The decrease in abundance was smallest in southern most 
26 
area, which indicates changing migration patterns confirming the hypothesis. However, it remains 
27 
unclear whether this apparent change is caused by EMFs, as little is known about brown shrimp and 
28 
their ability to detect EMFs, as well as other factors that might influence the results, such as changes 
29 
in shelf currents, river runoff, predator-prey interactions, and fishermen’s behaviour. Hence, future 
30 
studies should aim to determine the magnetoreceptive ability of brown shrimp and assess whether 
31 
subsea power cables attract or repel them.

Introduction  
34 
There are several fisheries in the North Sea, among them the brown shrimp (Crangon crangon) fish-
35 
ery (Beier et al., 2023; Kerby et al., 2012; Madsen et al., 2013). The North Sea brown shrimp fishery 
36 
is a small-mesh beam trawl fishery with more than 500 vessels (DK, DE, NL) operating in shallow 
37 
coastal waters (Beier et al., 2023; Günther et al., 2021; Temming et al., 2017). The total annual effort 
38 
in the North Sea Crangon fishery has been decreasing since 2016, dropping from 13.6 million horse-
39 
power days at sea (hpDAS) in 2016 to 12.6 million in 2018, and reaching a minimum of 8.5 million 
40 
hpDAS in 2019 (ICES, 2025). A slight increase in effort occurred in 2023, reaching 9.6 million 
41 
hpDAS. Of this total, the Netherlands contributed 4.45 million hpDAS, Germany 3.41 million, the 
42 
United Kingdom 0.92 million, Denmark 0.47 million, Belgium 0.28 million, and France 0.05 million 
43 
hpDAS (ICES, 2025). The landings per unit effort (LPUE) have since 2018 been decreasing notably 
44 
for the Danish, Dutch and German fleet. The annual LPUE (kg/hpDAS) for 2023 was 1.5 for the 
45 
==PAGE:6==
3 
 
Netherlands, 1.8 for Germany, 2.9 for Denmark, 1.0 for Belgium, 2.0 for France, and 0.5 for the 
46 
United Kingdom (ICES, 2025). Despite the higher number of vessels, the fishery remains unmanaged, 
47 
and significant knowledge gaps still exist including the basic aspects of the brown shrimp’s life cycle, 
48 
which can ultimately impact the sustainability of the fishery (Temming et al., 2017).  
49 
Brown shrimp is an epibenthic species that plays a crucial role in coastal food webs with a produc-
50 
tivity high enough to support one of the most valuable European fisheries in the North Sea (Temming 
51 
et al., 2017). Brown shrimps are subject to predation throughout all of their life stages: juveniles are 
52 
preyed upon by various small predatory fish, crustaceans, and birds (Pihl, 1985; Temming et al., 
53 
2017; Temming and Hufnagl, 2015) while adults are primarily eaten by cod (Gadus morhua), whiting 
54 
(Merlangius merlangus) and turbot (Scophthalmus maximus) (Temming and Hufnagl, 2015). Brown 
55 
shrimp prey on several benthic invertebrates (Feller, 2006), as well as bivalves and flatfish larvae 
56 
(Beukema and Dekker, 2014; Van et al., 1991). Based on  studies by Boddeke (1976) and Respondek 
57 
et al., (2022) it is evident that adult brown shrimp migrate from the southern Wadden Sea to the 
58 
northern Wadden Sea in the spring (March-April) and return to the southern Wadden Sea in the au-
59 
tumn (October-December). 
60 
The decline in annual in landings for the Danish crangon fishery coincides with the installation of 
61 
two subsea power cables in the fishing grounds, namely The Horns Rev 3 cable and the COBRAcable. 
62 
The Horns Rev 3 wind farm, located adjacent to Horns Rev 1 and 2, 25-40 km off the Danish coast 
63 
of Jutland, became operational in 2018 and is connected to the mainland via an alternating current 
64 
(AC) cable (VATTENFALL, 2025). Not far from the wind farms is the COBRAcable (direct current, 
65 
DC), which began operating in September 2019. Subsea power cables (SPC), using DC or AC are an 
66 
essential component of modern infrastructure, and they facilitate the transmission of electricity from 
67 
offshore wind farms to onshore facilities and enable interconnection between regional or national 
68 
==PAGE:7==
4 
 
power grids (CMACS, 2003; Hermans et al., 2024). Various types of SPCs emit electromagnetic 
69 
fields (EMFs), and their presence in the marine environment may cause disturbances to both marine 
70 
life and habitats (Anderson et al., 2017; Kalmijn, 1978; Taormina et al., 2018). AC power cables 
71 
generate EMFs that fluctuate over time in response to changes in the energy transmission, while DC 
72 
cables produce static EMFs (Hutchison et al., 2020b; Taormina et al., 2018). EMFs are generated by 
73 
the flow of electric current through power cables and they can be divided into two components: elec-
74 
tric fields (E-fields, measured in V/m), and magnetic fields (B-fields, measured in microtesla, µT) 
75 
(Newton et al., 2019). The intensity of the EMF emissions from power cables depends on several 
76 
factors including the amount of transmitted power, the type of current (AC or DC), its frequency, the 
77 
amplitude of the AC current, the cable's construction (monopole, bipole, or tri-axial), and whether it 
78 
is lying on top of the seabed or buried (Cada et al., 2011; Kilfoyle et al., 2018). When the cable is 
79 
buried, the overlying sediment significantly reduces exposure to the highest EMFs intensities that are 
80 
in direct contact with the cable (Tricas, 2011). Regardless of cable type, their respective EMFs de-
81 
crease inversely with increasing distance to the cable (Hutchison et al., 2021). Submarine power ca-
82 
bles commonly operate with intensities of up to 1600 A, resulting in a magnetic field of approximately 
83 
3200 µT at the cable surface (Taormina et al., 2018). The magnitude of the field decreases to 320 µT 
84 
at 1 meter, 110 µT at 4 meters from the cable, and diminishing to levels comparable to the Earth's 
85 
geomagnetic field ( ̴ 50 µT) at distances beyond 6 meters from the cable (Bochert and Zettler, 2006; 
86 
Taormina et al., 2018).    
87 
All life on earth is exposed to electromagnetic fields of both natural and human-made origin (Gill et 
88 
al., 2014). Earth's magnetic field is affected by three components: the field generated by the Earth's 
89 
core (20-70 µT), magnetic fields from metallic materials in the Earth's crust (500-2000 nT), and var-
90 
iations caused by solar radiation (around 50 nT) (Lowes, 2016; Shirokov et al., 2021). Conductive 
91 
==PAGE:8==
5 
 
seawater moving through the Earth's geomagnetic field generates motionally induced electric fields, 
92 
making these, along with the geomagnetic field itself, the most significant natural EMFs in the marine 
93 
environment (Hutchison et al., 2020a).  Several marine species are believed to be magneto-sensitive, 
94 
with some organisms being electrosensitive, and some, particularly elasmobranchs (sharks, rays and 
95 
skates), displaying both sensitivities (Anderson et al., 2017; Meyer et al., 2005; Nordmann et al., 
96 
2017). EMF emissions can have the potential to alter the distribution of electro-sensitive species by 
97 
attracting or repelling them; act as a barrier that impedes local movements and long-distance migra-
98 
tions; affect feeding and reproduction; and ultimately lead to shifts in community composition and 
99 
changes in ecosystem functioning (Dhanak et al., 2016; Lohmann et al., 2001).  Elasmobranchs are 
100 
believed to be the species most sensitive to changes in the magnetic fields (Hermans et al., 2024). 
101 
Species such as the thornback ray (Raja clavate), sandbar shark (Carcharhinus plumbeus), and yellow 
102 
stingray (Urobatis jamaicensis) have all shown behavioural responses to changes in the magnetic 
103 
field in the laboratory (Albert et al., 2022; Anderson et al., 2017; Newton and Kajiura, 2020). Other 
104 
laboratory experiments have shown that also marine organisms, such as several fish species and crus-
105 
taceans, alter their swimming behaviour in response to changes in EMFs (Gill and Desender, 2020; 
106 
Lee et al., 2015; Newton et al., 2019; Scott et al., 2021, 2018a). In contrast, various other studies have 
107 
found limited or no behavioural effects of EMFs on fish and a range of other marine species (Dunham 
108 
et al., 2015; Fey et al., 2019; Taormina et al., 2020; Wyman et al., 2018) and the species-specific 
109 
responses to EMFs underscore the need for targeted research to assess the impact of subsea power 
110 
cables on different marine taxa (CMACS, 2003).     
111 
This study will investigate the potential behavioural changes in the northward migration of Crangon 
112 
following the recent installation of the power cables (Hornsrev 3 and COBRA) running through the 
113 
Crangon fishing grounds. These changes are analysed in relation to salinity, sea surface temperature, 
114 
==PAGE:9==
6 
 
wind speed, and wind direction to assess how these parameters may influence brown shrimp migra-
115 
tion patterns. It was hypothesized that if the cables act as a barrier for brown shrimp, shrimp abun-
116 
dance would be expected to decrease north of Horns Rev 3 compared to the more southern areas, as 
117 
the cables could hinder their movement and thereby impede migration. In addition, fishery dynamics 
118 
as well as the distribution of Crangon and bycatch species were analysed in relation to cable instal-
119 
lation. To address this, a spatial analysis was conducted using commercial fisheries data from the 
120 
Danish brown shrimp fishery while data on bycatch species were obtained through the brown shrimp 
121 
co-sampling program.

Materials and methods  
124 
The brown shrimp fishery 
125 
The fleets involved in the brown shrimp fishery are Germany, the Netherlands, Denmark, Belgium, 
126 
France, and the United Kingdom, with Germany having the largest fleet (ICES, 2025). Landings from 
127 
the brown shrimp fishery have in many years surpassed 30,000 tonnes, corresponding to economi-
128 
cally values reaching up to 100 million euros (ICES, 2023). However, total annual landings of brown 
129 
shrimp have been declining since 2019, with 14,967 tonnes landed in 2023, marking the second-
130 
lowest total recorded since 1990 (ICES, 2025). Currently, no management system exists for the brown 
131 
shrimp fishery, e.g. by quota restrictions (Respondek et al., 2014). To achieve a high long-term sus-
132 
tainable yield of brown shrimp and reduce by-catch, the first Marine Stewardship Council (MSC) 
133 
management plan was established in 2015 (ICES, 2025). Participation in the MSC certification, and 
134 
thereby adherence to the MSC management plan, is voluntary for fishermen. In 2015, the Cooperative 
135 
==PAGE:10==
7 
 
Fisheries Organisation (CVO) of the Netherlands, the MSC GbR of Germany, and the Danish Fish-
136 
ermen - Producers Organisation (DFPO) joined forces to enter the MSC program, sharing the costs 
137 
related to the management plan (MSC, 2015). As a result, the entire Danish brown shrimp fishery is 
138 
today regulated by the MSC guidelines. These guidelines state that vessels may not fish more than 
139 
200 days per year (4,800 hours), the mesh size must be greater than 24 mm, and the cod end must 
140 
have at least 125 rows (MSC, 2022). The maximum sustainable yield (MSY) of the brown shrimp 
141 
fishery is unknown, and to avoid recruitment overfishing, landings per unit of effort (LPUE) are used 
142 
as an indicator of stock status (ICES, 2023; MSC, 2022). A high LPUE indicates a high abundance 
143 
of brown shrimp, whereas a low LPUE suggests a decline in the stock. Based on this, the brown 
144 
shrimp fishery is regulated according to changes in the monthly average LPUE data collected from 
145 
all participating vessels. This means that fishing effort (hours at sea) is adjusted according to prede-
146 
termined reference values for the monthly average LPUE, so that a decrease in monthly LPUE leads 
147 
to a corresponding reduction in fishing effort (MSC, 2022). 
148 
 In 2015, the landing obligation was introduced and since January 2019, the discarding of all quota-
149 
regulated species (Total Allowable Catch, TAC) has been prohibited. However, some fisheries have 
150 
been granted exemptions, such as those where TAC-regulated bycatch is minimal (de minimis), where 
151 
the sorting and landing of such species would incur disproportionate costs, or where the species in 
152 
question demonstrate high survivability after release (Beier et al., 2023). In 2018, the brown shrimp 
153 
fishery was granted a de minimis exemption from the landing obligation. This exemption was subse-
154 
quently updated in 2019 and 2020, progressively reducing the allowed discard percentage from 7% 
155 
in 2019-2020 to 5% by 2023 (Beier et al., 2023). To secure the de minimis exemption, the EU fishery 
156 
on brown shrimp must show that the bycatch of TAC species is within these limits. The most common 
157 
TAC by-catch species in the brown shrimp fishery in 2019-2022 include plaice, whiting, herring, and 
158 
==PAGE:11==
8 
 
sprat. However, other species such as brill, cod, horse mackerel, lemon sole, sandeel, sole, and turbot 
159 
are also common by-catch in the brown shrimp fishery (Beier et al., 2023). The bycatch of juvenile 
160 
fish species in the brown shrimp fishery can potentially impact the recruitment and spawning stock 
161 
biomass of these species. Especially the discarding of 1- and 2 year old plaice in the brown shrimp 
162 
fishery is recognized as a major factor adversely affecting the North Sea plaice stock and its long-
163 
term fishery potential (Polet, 2002). In addition to targeted commercial species, the by-catch includes 
164 
a range of non-commercial fish and benthic fauna (crustaceans, echinoderms and molluscs), which 
165 
are discarded together with undersized fish and brown shrimp (Polet, 2002).

167 
Brown shrimp fishery data 
168 
To estimate the spatial distribution of brown shrimp landings, multiple data sources were integrated 
169 
in a stepwise process as described in (McLaverty et al., 2023) and (Eigaard et al., 2025). This was 
170 
done prior to the start of the project. Logbook and sales note data were linked to assigned landing 
171 
weights and values to individual fishing trips and hauls. Three types of positional (VMS, AIS, Black-
172 
box) data were merged, and vessel tracks were reconstructed using cubic Hermite spline interpolation 
173 
(based on the vessel course in the positional data) when the time interval between positions was less 
174 
than two hours or, alternatively, resampled to one position per minute. The merged positional data 
175 
were then linked to logbook data to assign gear types to vessel positions. Fishing positions were 
176 
identified based on vessel speed falling within defined intervals characteristic of specific gear types. 
177 
Consecutive fishing positions were connected into straight lines to construct trawl tracks. For bottom-
178 
contacting gears, the gear width was estimated based on vessel size and gear type following the ap-
179 
proach described by Eigaard et al. (2016), and the estimated width was assigned to the lines creating 
180 
==PAGE:12==
9 
 
polygons of swept area for each trip. Landings (weight and value) were distributed across hauls ac-
181 
cording to trip, date, or haul-period information from the logbooks, depending on data resolution. 
182 
Lastly, hauls (polygons and lines) were intersected with a 0.01° × 0.01° spatial grid, and landings 
183 
were allocated to grid cells based on the proportion of polygon area (bottom-contacting gears) or line 
184 
length (non-bottom-contacting gears) within each cell. 
185 
In the present study data from the Danish North Sea brown shrimp fishery for 2010-2023 was in-
186 
cluded as this was the data available. The data were provided daily and contained year, month, date, 
187 
longitude, latitude, swept area (m²), and landings (kg). LPUE (kg/m2 swept area) was calculated as 
188 
landings in kg per swept area. Upon initial investigation of the data, all zero LPUE values were re-
189 
moved, as these zeros could result from several issues, including zero catches, inconsistencies be-
190 
tween VMS and logbook data, and failed trawl hauls and were removed because it was impossible to 
191 
determine the exact cause of the zeros. To investigate if the cables could act as a barrier for the brown 
192 
shrimp migration during March-April the brown shrimp fishing ground was divided into three areas 
193 
namely, the area south of COBRA, the area between COBRA and Hornsrev 3 and the area north of 
194 
Hornsrev 3 (Fig. 1). The three areas were manually delineated in QGIS (version 3.38, Grenoble). A 
195 
1 km buffer was created around the cables to prevent overlap of datapoints between areas, after which 
196 
the final area boundaries were drawn. To reduce processing time the data for each of the three areas 
197 
were aggregated to 5x5 km grid cells using the mean of the swept area, landings and LPUE. All 
198 
analysis and investigations of the brown shrimp fishery data were conducted using R version 4.4.2 
199 
(R Development Core Team, 2024), and maps were created in QGIS (version 3.38, Grenoble) and 
200 
Python (version 3.13.5).

Environmental parameters  
203 
Salinity, temperature (bottom and surface) and wind (speed and direction) were related to the brown 
204 
shrimp fishery to investigate their impact on brown shrimp abundance. Salinity and temperature were 
205

206

207 
brown shrimp fishery data were already aggregated on a daily basis into 5x5 km grids. A buffer zone 
208 
of 3.5 km was created around each grid cell to account for horizontal variability in CMEMS data and 
209 
to ensure spatial overlap. For each date in the fishery data, the relevant CMEMS variables were fil-
210 
tered, converted to point geometries, and spatially intersected with the buffered grids. Mean values 
211 
of the sea surface temperature and salinity were then calculated per grid cell and integrated back into

the original brown shrimp fishery grid structure. Daily wind data were obtained from the ERA5 rea-
213 
nalysis dataset (ECMWF) via the Copernicus Climate Data Store. ERA5 provides global, high-reso-
214 
lution atmospheric data (~0.25° spatial resolution) using data assimilation and numerical models. For

Data analysis and statistics  
243 
In this study, LPUE data were used as a proxy for brown shrimp abundance, consistent with the MSC 
244 
management plan. To investigate whether the northward migration of C. Crangon was affected by 
245 
the installation of the cables, the mean LPUE for each year, divided into the three defined areas for 
246 
==PAGE:16==
13 
 
the migration period (March-April), was plotted as time series (Fig. 3). In addition, the mean LPUE 
247 
before (2011-2017) and after (2020-2023) cable installation for March-April was compared across 
248 
the three defined areas (Fig. 4). To assess the spatial changes in brown shrimp abundance during the 
249 
migration period a heat map of the mean LPUE before and after the installation of the cables was 
250 
created (Fig. 5).  
251 
To investigate whether the observed changes in brown shrimp abundance during migration following 
252 
the installation of the cables were significant, a generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) was con-
253 
structed to investigate the changes in mean LPUE. In the initial model, sea surface temperature, sa-
254 
linity, wind (speed, and direction), and the interaction between the three defined fishing areas and the 
255 
period (before or after cable installation) were included as fixed effects. These were included as fixed 
256 
effects as we were interested in how they directly influenced shrimp abundance. The positions (lon-
257 
gitude and latitude) of the 5x5 km grid cells were included as a random effect to account for spatial 
258 
autocorrelation. Year was also included as a random effect in the initial model to account for the 
259 
repeated measures on year. Based on the results from the initial model the final model was estab-
260 
lished. The final model included the log mean LPUE as the data only contained positive values, sea 
261 
surface temperature, salinity and wind direction were excluded from the model as they had no signif-
262 
icant effect on the log mean LPUE. The final model contained wind speed, the interaction between 
263 
the three defined areas and period. Furthermore, grid positions and years were included as random 
264 
effects in the final model. A prediction model was run for the performed statistical model to create a 
265 
prediction plot which show the development of the log mean LPUE based on the results from the 
266 
model (Fig. 6) divided into a period before the cables (2010-2017), an intermediate period (2018-
267 
2019) and an after period (2020-2023). Before performing the statistical test, Homogeneity of vari-
268 
ance was assessed by plotting residuals against fitted values. Normality of residuals was evaluated 
269 
==PAGE:17==
14 
 
using Q-Q plots, where theoretical quantiles were plotted against standardized residuals, following 
270 
the protocol described by Zuur et al., (2009). All statistical analyses were performed using R version 
271 
4.4.2 (R Development Core Team, 2024).   
272 
Cable Power data was used for a visual comparison between the residuals of the final statistical model 
273 
and the power going through the two cables (Fig. 7). The interest in this relationship stems from the 
274 
fact that an increase in power or more specifically, electrical current in a power cable results in a 
275 
stronger magnetic field around it. Therefore, this could help determine whether changes in power, 
276 
and thus directly in the magnetic field have an influence in the distribution of brown shrimp. Fishing 
277 
effort in the three defined areas during the migration period (March-April) was analysed to assess 
278 
whether changes in effort could explain the observed variations in mean LPUE (Fig. 8, 9 and 10). 
279 
However, no statistical tests were performed on fishing effort.

The six most common TAC by-catch species (brill, cod, horse mackerel, plaice, sprat, whiting) from 
281 
the brown shrimp fishery were examined using data from the co-sampling program. This was done 
282 
only graphically, as the amount of data was very limited and only available from after the installation 
283 
of the cables. No statistical analyses were conducted for the co-sampling data. A further description 
284 
of the co-sampling data can be found in Appendix A.     
285 
Initially, the overall patterns in total landings, fishing effort, and LPUE of the brown shrimp fishery 
286 
across the entire fishing grounds and throughout each month from 2010 to 2023 were investigated 
287

Discussion 
378 
The present study of LPUE in the commercial fishery in March and April found indications of a 
379 
disturbance of the migration patterns of brown shrimp after the implementation of two investigated 
380 
power cables. Salinity and sea surface temperature had no significant effect on the LPUE, and no 
381 
clear relationship was found between the power transmitted through the cables and the residuals of 
382 
the statistical model. The fishing effort during the brown shrimp migration period increased for the 
383 
two northernmost areas, which was surprising given the decrease in landings during the same period.       
384 
 
385 
Cables as a barrier for brown shrimp   
386 
It was hypothesized that if the cables were a barrier for brown shrimp migration a decrease in the 
387 
brown shrimp abundance would be expected north of Horns rev 3 as both cables would hinder the 
388 
==PAGE:27==
24 
 
northward migration during March and April. The present study found a significant effect of the two 
389 
cables (Horns rev 3 and COBRA) in terms of overall decrease in LPUE in all three areas after the 
390 
implementation of the cables (Fig. 4). However, the decrease was noticeably smaller in the southern 
391 
area (18.60%) compared to the areas further north, i.e., the area between the cables (42.33%) and the 
392 
area north of Horns rev 3 (49.05%). This decrease becomes even more evident when compared to the 
393 
yearly LPUE across all months (not only migration months) (Appendix B, Fig. B4), which shows 
394 
decreases of 14.17%, 38.44%, and 17.56% after cable installation in the areas south of COBRA, 
395 
between the cables, and north of Horns Rev, respectively, indicating that brown shrimp are more 
396 
affected during migration. These results suggest that the implementation of the cables could have an 
397 
influence on the brown shrimp distribution in the area during the migration period. It is also evident 
398 
from the changes in the spatial distribution of brown shrimp (Fig. 5) for March-April before (2010-
399 
2017) and after (2020-2023) the implementation of the cables. Specifically, following the implemen-
400 
tation of the cables, a larger proportion of the total area exhibited low brown shrimp abundance. South 
401 
of the COBRA cable, the low abundance is especially pronounced in one large area in the far southern 
402 
part of the region. In the area between the cables, brown shrimp abundance was pronounced mainly 
403 
in the outer parts, whereas north of Horns Rev almost the entire area showed low abundances after 
404 
cable installation. Overall, the largest decrease occurred between the cables and north of Horns Rev 
405 
3, supporting the hypothesis that the cables act as a barrier for brown shrimp migration.  
406 
The cables could act as a barrier for brown shrimp as they might change the geomagnetic field in the 
407 
area. Various studies have shown that several marine fish species have magneto-receptive abilities 
408 
and are subsequently affected by changes in magnetic field (Albert et al., 2022; Anderson et al., 2017; 
409 
Gill and Desender, 2020; Kalmijn, 1978; Lee et al., 2015; Newton et al., 2019; Newton and Kajiura, 
410 
2020). To the authors' knowledge, no studies have yet been published on the potential effects of 
411 
==PAGE:28==
25 
 
magnetic field changes on brown shrimp or shrimp in general. Yet, other studies have examined 
412 
magnetoreception in other crustations e.g., crab. Scott et al. (2018) assessed the effects of a simulated 
413 
electromagnetic field (peak at 40 mT) on the edible crab, Cancer pagurus. Over a period of 24 hours, 
414 
they measured physiological and behavioural responses of the crabs when exposed to the simulated 
415 
EMF. Changes in the EMF significantly disrupted circadian rhythms of L-Lactate and D-Glucose. 
416 
Furthermore, Crabs were strongly attracted to EMF-exposed shelters (69% vs. 9%) and spent 21% 
417 
less time roaming. In another study, Scott et al. (2021) investigated the effects of varying EMF 
418 
strengths, 250 µT, 500 µT, and 1000 µT, also on cancer crab. They found that the circadian rhythms 
419 
of L-lactate and D-glucose were disrupted, and the total haemocyte count was altered when exposed 
420 
to EMFs of 500 µT and 1000 µT. Furthermore, the crabs showed a strong preference for EMF-ex-
421 
posed shelters and significantly reduced their roaming time supporting the same findings obtained by 
422 
Scott et al. (2018). If the power cables in the North Sea act as a barrier to brown shrimp due to changes 
423 
in local EMFs, one would expect the shrimp to be repelled rather than attracted. In this context, the 
424 
findings of Scott et al. (2021, 2018) appear somewhat contradictory. Conversely, if shrimp are instead 
425 
attracted to areas with changing EMFs, then the cable would serve as a hotspot for brown shrimp. To 
426 
fully assess this, one possible approach would be to analyse the relationship between distance from 
427 
the cables and LPUE. If brown shrimp are attracted to the cables, this analysis could reveal a gradient 
428 
in LPUE around the cables indicating whether they are repelled or attracted to the cables. Another 
429 
approach would be to expose brown shrimp in the laboratory to similar conditions and EMF levels as 
430 
used by Scott et al. (2021, 2018), to determine whether the shrimp are attracted to EMF-exposed 
431 
shelters or areas and observe their reaction. Conversely, other studies have found no or limited be-
432 
havioural changes on crustaceans when exposed to increased EMF intensities (Albert et al., 2023; 
433 
==PAGE:29==
26 
 
Harsanyi et al., 2022). An example is the study by Albert et al. (2022) where they found no behav-
434 
ioural changes in the velvet crab, Necora puber following short-term (30-minute) exposure to in-
435 
creased EMF intensities ranging from 72 to 304 µT. These varying results raise questions about 
436 
whether, and to what extent, the tested crustaceans are truly affected by changing EMFs. Furthermore, 
437 
it highlights the current gap in our knowledge of the effects of changes in magnetic fields on crusta-
438 
ceans and more specifically on shrimps. Hence, more targeted research is needed to gain a better 
439 
understanding of this complex topic. In addition, the plots of the model residuals versus mean power 
440 
at Horns Rev 3 and COBRA (Fig. 7) did not show any clear trend or relationship between model 
441 
residuals and changes in power. This could indirectly suggest that there is no clear link between 
442 
changes in magnetic fields and brown shrimp distribution, as higher power levels are associated with 
443 
stronger EMF intensities. However, it is important to keep in mind that a 1 km buffer was created 
444 
around the cables when defining the three areas. This was done to prevent overlap of data points 
445 
between areas. EMFs decrease with increasing distance from the cable, which means that their effect 
446 
is strongest closest to the cable. As a result, possible trends or relationships between the amount of 
447 
power and therefore, changes in EMFs and the LPUE may not be visible in the plots, as the data 
448 
points closest to the cables were excluded in the analysis. Furthermore, to fully evaluate the connec-
449 
tion between power, EMFs and brown shrimp distribution, changes in EMFs with changing power 
450 
should be modelled and related to the LPUE and the distance to cable. In order to make such a model, 
451 
information on power cable construction and material as well as whether it is buried or not would be 
452 
required as it all affects the intensity of the magnetic field around the cable. Unfortunately, it was not 
453 
possible to get cable information within the timespan of the current study as this information was 
454 
confidential. Hermans et al. (2024) established a model and modelled the magnetic field (µT) for 
455 
twelve cables connected to the Netherlands, including the COBRAcable (see Appendix D for results 
456 
==PAGE:30==
27 
 
and a description of the study). They divided the outputs of the model into four scenarios of how 
457 
strong changes in magnetic field would be depending on how much power was going through the 
458 
cable. The four scenarios included: a minimum scenario (no wind/maintenance), in which only 1% 
459 
of the total capacity was used; an average summer scenario with 25% capacity usage; an average 
460 
winter scenario where 80% of the total capacity was used; and a maximum scenario where the cable 
461 
operated at full capacity. For the COBRAcable a magnetic field of 23 µT would according to their 
462 
model be produced in the maximum scenario. This change in magnetic field is much smaller than the 
463 
levels at which Scott et al. (2021, 2018b) observed behavioural changes in crabs. It remains unknown 
464 
whether shrimp and crabs have the same sensitivity to changes in EMFs. Despite this it could suggest 
465 
that the magnetic field changes to which brown shrimp might respond do not occur naturally and it 
466 
questions whether the observed changes in shrimp abundance following the implementation of the 
467 
cables are due to geomagnetic disturbances. Furthermore, earth’s natural EMF is around 50 µT, which 
468 
is higher than the expected change caused by the COBRAcable, so without the cable, the shrimp 
469 
would experience a stronger magnetic field naturally than that produced by the cable itself. 
470 
Hermans et al. (2024) did not examine changes in the magnetic field related to Horns rev 3, and the 
471 
maximum magnetic field changes associated with the cable remain unknown. But they did model the 
472 
magnetic field of five different cables all transporting AC power similar to the Horns rev 3 cable. For 
473 
all five AC cables the maximum magnetic field produced by the cable was 13.9 µT. As with the 
474 
COBRAcable this magnetic field is much below the field shown to have an effect on crabs (Scott et 
475 
al. 2021, 2018b). Again, this could indicate that the observed decrease in shrimp abundance after the 
476 
implementation of the cables were due to other factors than changes in the magnetic field around the 
477 
cable.

Environmental factors affecting the brown shrimp distribution         
481 
The Wadden Sea where the Danish brown shrimp fisheries take place is a highly productive ecosys-
482 
tem influenced by several abiotic factors including changing temperatures, salinity, ocean currents 
483 
and river runoffs (Maathuis et al., 2025; Temming and Hufnagl, 2015). In the final statistical model 
484 
both water temperature and salinity were excluded as they had no significant effect on the log mean 
485 
LPUE for March-April after the implementation of the cables. However, early studies (before 1950) 
486 
have indicated that both temperature and salinity are contributing to the initiation of the brown shrimp 
487 
migration (Boddeke, 1976). Broekema (1942) did laboratory experiments where brown shrimp were 
488 
subjected to changing temperatures and salinities. The conclusion of the study was that brown shrimp 
489 
prefer high salinities when the temperature is low, but low salinity when the temperature is high. In 
490 
another paper by Lloyd and Yonge (1947) they suggested that the seasonal migration of brown shrimp 
491 
was closely related to changes in salinity and temperature. Based on this it is surprising that neither 
492 
temperature nor salinity explained the changes in LPUE for March-April after the implementation of 
493 
the cables. Conversely, other studies have shown that adult and juvenile brown shrimp have a wide 
494 
temperature preference (Reiser et al., 2016, 2014) supporting that the brown shrimp distribution was 
495 
not affected by changes in temperature. The methods used in the present study and methods used in 
496 
the studies by Broekema (1942) and Lloyd and Yonge (1947) differ which could be why the results 
497 
contradict each other. Furthermore, the studies by Broekema (1942) and Lloyd and Yonge (1947) 
498 
were conducted a long time ago, and the Wadden Sea has undergone various changes since then 
499 
(Kirby et al., 2009). As a result, other abiotic factors, besides temperature and salinity, could have a 
500 
greater influence on brown shrimp distribution today.  
501 
==PAGE:32==
29 
 
In the final established statistical model windspeed was included as it had a significant effect on the 
502 
LPUE. More specifically, wind speed had a positive effect, indicating that higher wind speeds are 
503 
associated with greater shrimp abundance. Ocean currents are among other things driven by winds 
504 
and tidal forces (Lee and Ramster, 1968; NOAA, 2025) and it could be hypothesised that since the 
505 
brown shrimp distribution was affected by changes in windspeed that changes in ocean currents and 
506 
tides could also explain the obtained results. This is supported by a 30 year study conducted in Ger-
507 
man waters, where they found that the brown shrimp abundance was mainly influenced among others 
508 
by the Winter North Atlantic Oscillation Index (WNAOI) (Siegel et al., 2005). As a global physical 
509 
index, the NAO is thought to be one of the key factors which trigger the wind-driven North Sea 
510 
circulation (Siegel et al., 2005). Periods characterized by low NAO index values are also associated 
511 
with reduced winter sea surface temperatures in the North Sea (Loewe, 1996), which may, in turn, 
512 
contribute to delayed spawning or hindered growth of early life stages of brown shrimp. From this 
513 
perspective the yearly fluctuations in shrimp abundance (Appendix B and C, Fig. B3, B5 and C1) 
514 
could be a result of low NAO values during winter. However, given the strong correlation between 
515 
the NAO index and temperature, one would expect that if the NAO were the primary driver of the 
516 
observed patterns, sea surface temperature would have a significant effect on shrimp abundance dur-
517 
ing March-April and thus be included in the final statistical model. In the 30 year study conducted by 
518 
Siegel et al. (2005) they also found that brown shrimp abundance in autumn was correlated to autumn 
519 
river runoff. This finding was supported by Boddeke (1996) who investigated the brown shrimp abun-
520 
dance in 1980-1984 and 1991-1994 and found that the decrease in brown shrimp abundance was a 
521 
result of a reduction in the phosphate discharge to the study area by the river Rhine. Furthermore, 
522 
Boddeke (1996) concluded that the brown shrimp stocks had changed more along the Dutch west 
523 
coast than the north, likely due to higher nutrient levels and greater sensitivity to phosphate discharge 
524 
==PAGE:33==
30 
 
in the west. It is known that river runoff plays a key role for the planktonic production in coastal 
525 
waters (Siegel et al., 2005). Several Danish rivers flow into the Wadden Sea including Varde Å, Ribe 
526 
Å and Brede Å all contributing with freshwater and nutrients to the Wadden Sea. Hence, the obtained 
527 
results could be a result of changes in river runoff. But, since the NAO-index and river runoff were 
528 
not included in this analysis it cannot be concluded whether they could be the main contributors to 
529 
low shrimp abundance observed after the implementation of the cables. To improve the understanding 
530 
of the potential abiotic factors in the North Sea future studies should incorporate parameters such as 
531 
ocean currents, the NAO index, and river runoff into the analysis. When considered these parameters 
532 
alongside sea surface temperature and salinity, these factors may significantly influence the LPUE 
533 
following the implementation of the cables.  
534 
 
535 
Predation- and fishing pressure in relation to brown shrimp distribution 
536 
Other factors than changes in EMFs and environmental factors could have caused the observed de-
537 
crease in mean LPUE after the implementation of the cables. An increased predation pressure by 
538 
whiting on brown shrimp has been proposed as an explanation for the decrease in brown shrimp 
539 
abundance in 2016 (Campos et al., 2010; Respondek et al., 2022). Siegel et al. (2005) investigated 
540 
the effects of extreme gadoid predation on brown shrimp densities in autumn and found that high 
541 
densities of these predators significantly reduced the brown shrimp stock. In addition, Boddeke 
542 
(1996) explained the low densities of brown shrimp in September-October in 1981 as a result of a 
543 
strong year class of cod. From this perspective the observed yearly variations in the brown shrimp 
544 
distribution could be considered as a result of variations in predator abundance. However, both whit-
545 
ing and cod stocks have been declining since the 1990s, making changes in predation pressure an 
546 
unlikely explanation for the decrease in brown shrimp abundance (ICES, 2025b, 2024). Furthermore, 
547 
==PAGE:34==
31 
 
various other studies have reported that the natural mortality of brown shrimp from predation has 
548 
decreased during the last decades (Temming and Hufnagl, 2015; Tulp et al., 2012), resulting in the 
549 
fishery becoming the primary source of mortality. The brown shrimp fishery is currently not regulated 
550 
with Total Allowable Catches (TACs) or quotas and no assessments are conducted to estimate the 
551 
stock's potential yield (Siegel et al., 2005; Temming et al., 2017; Temming and Hufnagl, 2015). This 
552 
is primarily due to the lack of consistent effort data from the international fishery, the species’ short 
553 
life cycle (approximately two years), and the inability to determine age (Respondek et al., 2022).  
554 
Hence, significant efforts have been made to understand the biology and life cycle of brown shrimp. 
555 
Despite this substantial knowledge gaps remain, particularly regarding the contribution of different 
556 
seasonal egg production periods to the main catches and the mechanisms that determine juvenile 
557 
recruitment to the adult stock (Temming et al., 2017; Tulp et al., 2012). As a result, the chances of 
558 
overfishing especially on egg-bearing shrimp could be considered as very likely, which may explain 
559 
the decrease in shrimp abundance observed in recent years. Intense fishing of brown shrimp during 
560 
winter takes place off the Sylt islands (Germany). In 1990, significant debate arose over potential 
561 
negative impacts predicted as a result of increased fishing pressure, driven by the entry of larger 
562 
vessels transitioning from the Dutch flatfish fishery into the shrimp fishery (Respondek et al., 2022). 
563 
However, the discussion gradually diminished as average landings increased from around 20,000 
564 
tonnes before 1990 to approximately 30,000 tonnes in the following decade, contradicting the pre-
565 
dictions.   
566 
The total effort across all months has decreased after the implementation of the cables (Appendix B, 
567 
Fig. B1, B6 and B7) indicating that the brown shrimp fishery has decreased rather than intensified 
568 
after the implementation of the cables contradicting that overfishing is the reason for the decreasing 
569 
brown shrimp abundance. The brown shrimp fishery is regulated by monthly average LPUE data. 
570 
==PAGE:35==
32 
 
The MSC management plan stipulates that if e.g. the German brown shrimp fishery experiences low 
571 
monthly average LPUE values, the fishing effort must be reduced across the entire brown shrimp 
572 
fishery in all participating nations. This means that changes in fishing dynamics for the other nations 
573 
would affect the Danish brown shrimp fishery and therefore could be the reason for observed decrease 
574 
in the fishing effort. However, in the current study only fisheries data from the Danish brown shrimp 
575 
fishery were included, which limits the ability to assess the potential impact of the fishery on brown 
576 
shrimp to a relatively small part of the total fishery. Furthermore, to understand the impact of the 
577 
fishery on brown shrimp distribution a thorough investigation on the life cycle of brown shrimp 
578 
should be conducted. Predation pressure and fishery effects should be incorporated into a model, with 
579 
survey data to examine the relationship between brown shrimp abundance and factors such as the 
580 
timing of spawning, which would constitute a study in its own right.

General patterns of the brown shrimp distribution and fishery    
583 
When examining the spatial distribution of brown shrimp, across individual years and all months the 
584 
first large change in the brown shrimp distribution occurred in 2016 and 2017 where the shrimp 
585 
abundance was markedly lower over the entire fishing area compared to the previous years (Appendix 
586 
B, Fig. B5). The construction work related to the development of Hornsrev 3 began in May 2017 and 
587 
lasted until January 2018 (NS Energy, 2019; Vattenfall, 2017) and the construction work related to 
588 
the implementation of the COBRAcable started in 2016 and was completed in 2019 (windpower nl, 
589 
2019). Based on this the decrease in abundance across the entire fishing area could be a result of the 
590 
construction work related to the implementation of the two cables in 2016 and 2017 disturbing the 
591 
brown shrimp migration causing an increase in mortality. However, when considering the years after 
592 
2017, the distribution and abundance of brown shrimp generally fluctuates, with a high abundance 
593 
==PAGE:36==
33 
 
observed across the entire fishing area as early as 2019, followed by very low abundance in both 2021 
594 
and 2022 (Appendix B, Fig. B5). This could challenge that the observed low shrimp abundance in 
595 
2016-2017 were due to the construction work. Moreover, it questions whether the changes in EMFs 
596 
related to the cables really could influence brown shrimp migration as the abundance are also high in 
597 
some years after the implementation of the cables. Similar trends occur when looking at the LPUE 
598 
for the individual years for only March-April (Appendix C, Fig. C1) which again could indicate that 
599 
the obtained low shrimp abundance after the implantation of the cables were due to other factors than 
600 
changes in EMFs related to the cables.  
601 
The total effort (m2 swept area) decreased for the area south of COBRA and the area between the two 
602 
cables from 2018-2019 (Fig. 8) during the migration months. A decrease in the total landings across 
603 
all months occurred in the same period (Appendix B, Fig. B1). It could be postulated that the fisher-
604 
men reduced their fishing effort simply due to their own expectation regarding that the installations 
605 
of Horns rev 3 and COBRA would negatively affect the brown shrimp abundance. Hence, the lower 
606 
mean effort observed after the implementation of the cables could be due to changes in fisher behav-
607 
iour. However, when considering the mean effort before and after cable installation during the mi-
608 
gration period (Fig. 9) an increase in the mean effort occurred for the area between the cables (10.5%) 
609 
and north of Hornsrev (44.46%). This is quite surprising, as landings remained low after 2018 (Ap-
610 
pendix B, B1), indicating that fishing continued despite poor catch years. One possible explanation 
611 
is that, due to low shrimp abundance, they had to fish more, which increased the effort required to 
612 
catch anything. Another explanation is that, since no stock assessment exists for the brown shrimp 
613 
stock, the fishermen were not aware of the decline in brown shrimp abundance. As a result, they 
614 
continued fishing and moved further north in the hope of catching more there.  
615 
==PAGE:37==
34 
 
The spatial distribution of the brown shrimp fishery has decreased in terms of area after the imple-
616 
mentation of the cables both during the migration period and across all months, and it appears that 
617 
the fishery has shifted further toward the coastal areas (Fig. 10 and Appendix B, Fig. B8). It has been 
618 
hypothesized that the decrease in fishing effort was driven by factors such as storage bottlenecks in 
619 
the processing industry caused by strong catches the previous year (before 2019), the COVID-19 
620 
pandemic in 2020-2021, and high fuel prices resulting from the war in Ukraine in the beginning of 
621 
2022 (ICES, 2025). The shift toward coastal areas could be explained by high fuel prices, which 
622 
caused fishermen to stay closer to shore to save fuel, as they no longer had to sail long distances 
623 
before beginning to fish. However, in a study by Respondek et al., (2014) the factors influencing 
624 
fishing effort in the German brown shrimp fishery (2000-2009) were examined, including fuel prices, 
625 
and no significant effect of fuel prices on fishing effort was found. From this perspective, the observed 
626 
shift towards coastal areas after cable installation is most likely due to factors other than fuel prices.  
627 
Vessel dimensions, technical installations and skipper skills were not included and considered in the 
628 
current study. The overall decrease in fishing effort (Appendix B, Fig. B1 and B7) could be a result 
629 
of an increased fishing efficiency due to the introduction of new vessels or more trained skippers. 
630 
New vessels with advanced gear, hull, and propulsion system designs catch more per fishing hour 
631 
than traditional ones, even with the same engine power. Additionally, modern automatic boilers and 
632 
sorting devices reduce processing time, thereby increasing haul frequency (Respondek et al., 2022).  
633 
 
634 
The effect of cables on TAC species  
635 
An increase in whiting and plaice occurred during 2020-2022 (Fig. 11). As with brown shrimp, the 
636 
observed increase could be a result of changes in the magnetic field, either acting as a hotspot for the 
637 
==PAGE:38==
35 
 
species or as a barrier. In a study by Cresci et al. (2023), they found that cod larvae exposed to an 
638 
EMF of 22-156 µT exhibited a 32% reduction in mean swimming speed and a 30% reduction in 
639 
acceleration. Another study showed that juvenile Atlantic lumpfish Cyclopterus lumpus reduced their 
640 
swimming activity with 16% when exposed to an EMF of up to 230 µT  (Durif et al., 2023). Since 
641 
the co-sampling data were only available for three years, the data was very limited. To gain a better 
642 
understanding of the changes in TAC species one possible solution would be to include and analysis 
643 
discard data from the brown shrimp fishery instead of relying on co-sampling data, as this would 
644 
provide a longer time series.   
645 
 
646 
Limitations of the study  
647 
The hypothesis of the current study was that the Hornsrev 3 and COBRA cable would act as a barrier 
648 
for brown shrimp and hinder their northward migration. Only few studies describe this northward 
649 
migration (Boddeke, 1976; Respondek et al., 2022). Several other studies describe a coastal migration 
650 
of brown shrimp, in which they move from the open waters of the Wadden Sea to more brackish 
651 
coastal areas at the onset of warm weather in spring (Ehrenbaum, 1890; Havinga, 1930; Lloyd and 
652 
Yonge, 1947; van der Baan, 1975). To investigate a possible coastal migration water depth should be 
653 
included directly in the analysis as this would help understand the migration patterns of brown shrimp. 
654 
It is possible that both types of migration occur and both knowledge and reasons for the brown shrimp 
655 
migration are still poorly understood today. The current knowledge gap regarding among other things 
656 
the life cycle of brown shrimp has been a major limitation of this study, as it even questions the theory 
657 
which the hypothesis of the current study was based on. However, despite this knowledge gap, the 
658 
obtained results of the present study indicates that the described northward migration of brown shrimp 
659 
truly occurs. Another limitation of this study is the current knowledge gap of the effects of changes 
660 
==PAGE:39==
36 
 
in EMFs on shrimp making it difficult to predict the possible effects of the implementation of the 
661 
cables.  
662 
In the current study the fisheries data analysed did not contain any information on vessels due to 
663 
confidentiality. To better understand the distribution of the brown shrimp fishery, information on 
664 
vessels would have been beneficial, as it could reveal, for example, whether low effort was associated 
665 
with specific vessels. In addition, all zero LPUE values were initially removed, as these zeros could 
666 
result from several issues, including zero catches, inconsistencies between VMS and logbook data, 
667 
and failed trawl hauls and were removed because it was impossible to determine the exact cause of 
668 
the zeros. Nevertheless, zero LPUEs could also be traced back to specific vessels if information on 
669 
vessels were given. An investigation into whether these zero LPUEs were associated with certain 
670 
vessels could be conducted, potentially revealing the reason for the zeros and allowing them to be 
671 
included in the analysis. This would be advantageous, as it would enhance the understanding of brown 
672 
shrimp fishery dynamics. In this relation an interesting analysis would be to investigate whether more 
673 
zero LPUEs occurred after implementation of the cables or if they remained stable throughout the 
674 
entire time series.      
675 
 
676 
Conclusion 
677 
In conclusion, the present study found an indication of an effect of the two cables (Horns rev 3 and 
678 
COBRA) on shrimp abundance during migration after the implementation of the cables. The observed 
679 
decrease in mean LPUE was greatest in the area north of Horns rev 3 (49.05%) and smaller in the 
680 
area between the cables (42.33%) and south of COBRA (18.60%), supporting the hypothesis of a 
681 
==PAGE:40==
37 
 
disruption of the northward migration of brown shrimp in March-April by the cables. Plausible rea-
682 
sons for this were considered, in relation to changes in the magnetic field around the cables, several 
683 
environmental factors, including temperature, salinity, windspeed, ocean currents/NAO and nutrient 
684 
discharge from rivers as well as predation and fishing pressure. Currently, few studies have investi-
685 
gated the effects of changes in the magnetic field on crustaceans, and the results have been varying. 
686 
In addition, it can be questioned whether the intensity of the magnetic field where behavioural 
687 
changes for crustaceans have been shown would occur naturally. Hence, it could be doubted if the 
688 
obtained results are due to changes in magnetic field after the implementation of the cables. However, 
689 
there is currently a gap in our knowledge of the effects of changes in magnetic fields on shrimps. 
690 
Thus, it is not possible to exclude the implementation of the cables as the explanatory factor behind 
691 
the low mean LPUE values. Furthermore, it is recommended that additional investigations in terms 
692 
of for example laboratory experiments should be conducted to help understand the possible effects of 
693 
changes in magnetic fields on brown shrimp.

Appendix A 
888 
Co-sampling data 
889 
To obtain the de minimis exemption from the landing obligation and the requirement to discard all 
890 
TAC species in the brown shrimp fishery, the co-sampling programme was established to demon-
891 
strate that discards remain within the specified limits. Data from the co-sampling programme were 
892 
included in the present study to assess potential changes in TAC species following the installation of 
893 
the power cables. The co-sampling programme ran from 2019 to 2022 and covered approximately 
894 
95% of the total brown shrimp fishery in the North Sea. Sampling was conducted in Denmark, Ger-
895 
many, and the Netherlands. Belgium and France were not included due to their limited involvement 
896 
in the fishery. During designated co-sampling trips, a minimum of two unsorted catch samples were 
897 
collected per trip, ideally from hauls conducted both during the day and at night, with at least one 
898 
sample from one of the final hauls. Prior to sorting the catch, the total catch volume (height in bin, 
899
