Abstract 
Background: It has been estimated that cancer is the cause of over 8 million deaths annually and 
that the number of deaths per year will increase to 13 million before year 2030. Due to the 
toxicity often associated with chemotherapeutic agents new alternatives are sought. The novel 
platinum-containing BP-C1 is a promising anti-neoplastic agent which have shown high efficacy 
and low toxicity in preclinical and clinical trials. The polymeric backbone of BP-C1, called BP-Cx-
1, is comprised of lignin-derived polyphenolic compounds believed to exert their own 
immunomodulatory effects. It is these immunomodulatory effects and whether they can affect 
the tumor microenvironment that is the focus of this project. 
Methods: The anti-neoplastic and immunomodulatory effects of BP-Cx-1 and BP-C1 were 
investigated in three different systems incorporating flow cytometric cell analysis. An in vitro 
apoptotic assay was performed in order to examine the direct apoptotic capabilities of BP-Cx-1 
and BP-C1. BP-Cx-1 was tested further in cell culture systems to study the immunomodulatory 
properties on the differentiation of immune cells derived from the bone marrow. Lastly, the 
antineoplastic and immunomodulatory effects of BP-C1 on the tumor microenvironment were 
assessed in two separate murine cancer models utilizing the CT26-Luc and Ehrlich-Lettre ascites 
cancer cell-lines, respectively. 
Results: No apoptotic effect of either BP-Cx-1 or BP-C1 was observed at the concentrations used 
in this project. BP-Cx-1 did, however, seem to affect the frequency of several relevant immune 
cell types, including increases in DC, macrophage, and resident monocyte numbers and a 
decrease of overall arginase-1 expression, M2-polarized macrophages, inflammatory monocytes, 
and neutrophils. Unfortunately, neither of the two animal models displayed tumor growth of the 
magnitude needed for examining the effect of BP-C1 on the tumor microenvironment.  
Conclusion: Based on these experiments, intriguing indications of immunomodulatory 
mechanisms, which could be at the basis of the clinical antineoplastic effects of BP-C1, were seen 
in this project. Since these observations are based on in vitro culture systems the animal studies 
will have to be repeated in order to fully assess how BP-C1 can affect the immune cells of the 
tumor microenvironment in an in vivo setting.

Resumé 
Cancer er blandt de dødligste sygdomme i verden og er estimeret til at være årsag til mere end 8 
millioner døde årligt, et tal der forventes at stige til 13 millioner inden 2030. Grundet den høje 
toksicitet forbundet med konventionel kemobehandling  er der et behov for nye, mindre toksiske 
alternativer. BP-C1 er et nyt lægemiddel, der indeholder platin, og som har vist en lovende anti-
cancer effekt med høj effektivitet og lav toksicitet i prækliniske og kliniske forsøg. Den 
polymeriske grundstruktur af BP-C1, kaldt BP-Cx-1, udgøres af lignin-derivat-baserede 
polyphenoliske strukturer, som er tænkt at have immuno-modulatoriske effekter i sig selv. Det 
er netop disse immuno-modulatoriske effekter og hvorvidt de kan påvirke et tumor mikromiljø, 
som dette projekt forsøger at undersøge. I dette projekt bliver den anti-neoplastiske og immuno-
modulatoriske effekt af både BP-Cx-1 og BP-C1 undersøgt ved brug af tre forskellige opsætninger. 
I den første del undersøges hvorvidt BP-Cx-1 og BP-C1 har en direkte apoptotisk effekt på cancer 
såvel som normale cellelinjer. I den anden del testes BP-Cx-1 for eventuelle immuno-
modulatoriske egenskaber ved at undersøge dens indvirken på immunceller fra knoglemarven. I 
den sidste del bliver den antineoplastiske og immuno-modulatoriske effekt af BP-C1 på et tumor 
mikromiljø undersøgt in vivo med to forskellige musemodeller ved brug af CT26-Luc eller Ehrlich-
Lettre ascites cancer cellelinjerne. Dette projekt fandt ingen apoptotisk aktivitet af hverken BP-
Cx-1 eller BP-C1 ved de anvendte koncentrationer. Projektet identificerer dog en ændring i 
antallet af en række centrale immunceller ved behandling af celler fra knoglemarven med BP-Cx-
1; bl.a. virker lægemidlet til at øge antallet af DCer, makrofager og residente monocytter, samt 
at lede til et fald i antallet af M2-polariserede makrofager, inflammatoriske monocytter og 
neutrofiler, samt til et fald i den overordnede arginase 1 ekspression. Desværre var der i de to 
musestudier ikke tilstrækkelig tumorvækst og det var derfor ikke muligt at studere effekten af 
BP-C1 direkte på et tumor mikromiljø. 
Igennem dette projekt er flere mulige immuno-modulatoriske effekter af BP-C1 observeret, som 
kunne ligge til grund for den positive effekt, der er observeret ved klinisk brug af BP-C1. Dog er 
resultaterne i dette projekt baseret på in vitro systemer, og de museforsøg der ikke lykkedes i 
dette projekt, bør gentages for at klarlægge, hvordan BP-C1 påvirker immuncellerne i et tumor 
mikromiljø in vivo.

1. Literature Review  
1.1 Introduction to Carcinogenesis and Hallmarks of Cancer 
The growth and division of normal cells are tightly regulated in order for different cell types and 
tissues to be maintained at a functional state in the organism. This controlled regulation of cell 
growth and division is missing in cancerous cells and in spite of the large diversity, which exists 
between different types of cancer, they all share this fundamental defect. 
Simply put the life of a tumor begins when a single cell starts proliferating abnormally. Since each 
tumor cell divides into two new tumor cells there is a high potential for fast and uncontrolled 
growth of these abnormal cells. In this manner, single abnormal cells can develop into tumors 
comprising aggregates of abnormal cells, which have no purposeful function, and are 
characterized by their further uncontrolled growth and division.  These characteristics have, in 
some part, been explained by the presence of mutations, which result in abnormal cells 
undergoing one or more alterations that distinguishes them from normal cells1.  
Tumors are defined as any abnormal growth of cells and a critical aspect of cancer pathology is 
the distinction between benign and malignant tumors. A benign tumor is an aggregate of slow-
growing abnormal cells, which remains confined to its original location within the organism. The 
capability to metastasize, i.e. to invade surrounding tissue and spread to distant sites, is not 
present in benign tumors and these are therefore said to be non-cancerous. An in situ tumor is a 
pre-malignant state where the cells are still restricted from spreading from its original location 
but has the potential to become malignant if not removed. In contrast to benign, malignant 
tumors are cancerous masses of cells capable of metastasizing. These cells also often have a 
higher rate of proliferation than their benign counterparts2. 
There are two main theories of carcinogenesis: the somatic mutation theory (SMT) and the tissue 
organization field theory (TOFT). SMT is based upon three overall premises: (1) cancers are 
derived from single somatic cells, which have accumulated DNA mutations, (2) by default 
metazoan cells are in a quiescent proliferation state, and (3) cancer is a disease of cell 
proliferation caused by mutations in genes, which control proliferation and the cell cycle2. 
According to this theory cancer is primarily caused by mutations in dominant gain of function 
==PAGE:11==
2 
 
oncogenes and recessive loss of function tumor suppressor genes, which ultimately leads to the 
development of monoclonal cancers, i.e. the cancer is derived from a single mutated cell. While 
SMT is founded in reductionism, TOFT is based on organicism and depicts carcinogenesis as a 
deterioration of the tissue microenvironment due to extracellular causes3. The premises of TOFT 
are: (1) carcinogenesis takes place at tissue level of biological organization and are defects of 
tissue architecture and (2) cells are by default in a proliferative state. According to this theory 
chronic disruption of signaling between cells are the root of cancer development while mutations 
are the consequence, and not the cause of tissue-level event4. These two markedly different 
theories may be seen as incompatible given the fact that, SMT depicts cancer as irreversible and 
as a result of mutations in a single cell whereas TOFT argues that carcinogenesis is reversible and 
a problem of tissue organization due to disruption of cell interactions. Though this may be the 
case, arguments have been made that neither of the two theories are sufficient for describing 
carcinogenesis fully when taken alone and that they instead should be viewed as complementing 
instead of contradicting each other3. 
All though the forces driving carcinogenesis remains a subject of debate, the traits that are most 
commonly found in cancers have been described (Figure 1). These traits, or hallmarks, of cancer 
initially comprised six biological capabilities, which normal cell acquire during their 
transformation into malignant tumors: sustaining proliferative signaling, insensitivity to growth 
suppressors, resisting cell death, replicative immortality, inducing angiogenesis, and tissue 
invasion and metastasis1. Since these hallmarks were described, two additional emerging 
hallmarks have been added to the list: reprogramming of energy metabolism and evading 
immune destruction. Underlying these eight hallmarks of cancer are the two enabling 
characteristics genetic instability and tumor-promoting inflammation5. These enabling 
characteristics make the acquisition of the other biological capabilities possible. The most 
relevant of these hallmarks and characteristics that represent ways to breach the anticancer 
defense mechanisms will be described in more detail further below along with the normal 
anticancer defense mechanisms.  
 
==PAGE:12==
3 
 
There has been some disagreement regarding the inclusion and exclusion of specific hallmarks 
and perhaps more importantly about what defines a hallmark of cancer. It has been argued that 
a hallmark should be a characteristic, which distinguishes benign and malignant growths but 
within the initial six only the hallmark of tissue invasion and metastasis fulfill this criteria while 
the other hallmarks can be observed in both benign neoplasms and in malignant cancer6. Despite 
these discrepancies and in light of the emerging hallmarks and characteristics the hallmarks can 
be viewed as framework of cellular advantageous properties that promote transformation of 
phenotypically normal cells into malignant neoplasms. 
 
 
Figure 1. The eight described hallmarks and two enabling characteristics of cancer, which represent successful 
breaching of the anticancer defense mechanisms of cells and tissues and collectively dictate malignant growth5 
(Modified) 
 
A more detailed description of some of the hallmarks of cancer is necessary in order to fully 
understand and appreciate the objectives and scope of this project as several of them will in one 
way or another be relevant for the experiments performed.  
The ability of sustaining proliferative signaling and an altered stress response are two of the most 
fundamental capabilities of cancers and therefore act as a natural introduction to the most 
relevant hallmarks in this project. As described earlier cells are subject to a tightly regulated cell 
cycle and are dependent on growth signaling in order to proliferate. In cancer cells these signaling 
pathways can have several driving alterations, which give these cells a selective proliferative 
==PAGE:13==
4 
 
advantage. The alterations can affect both growth ligands, their receptors, and intracellular 
signaling pathways7. Alterations may cause aberrantly high production of growth-promoting 
factors, decrease in growth-inhibiting factors, overexpressed receptors, and mutations in 
proteins important in intracellular signaling, such as RAS, which can result in enhanced growth, 
suppression of apoptosis, and promotion of angiogenesis8. Furthermore, many cancers gain the 
ability to synthesize and excrete growth factors to which they themselves are responsive. This 
way positive feedback loops can be created in the form of autocrine growth-promoting signaling1. 
In addition to a self-sufficiency in growth signals cancers also show an insensitivity to anti-growth 
signals and an altered stress response that favors survival. Two of the main tumor-suppressor 
genes are the ones encoding the p53 and pRb proteins, which are important regulators of 
proliferation and apoptosis. pRb acts as a regulator of the cell cycle by controlling the transition 
from the G1 to the S while p53 exerts anti-proliferative and pro-apoptotic effects in response to 
various stressors e.g. DNA damage and hypoxia. Functional losses of these proteins are often 
observed in cancers making them insusceptible to these regulatory programs8.  
 
All tissues depend on adequate supplies of oxygen and nutrients and on the removal of waste 
products and due to the enhanced proliferation and growth of cancer cells they are greatly 
dependent on the development of new vasculature. Without it, tumors cannot grow beyond a 
critical size or metastasize to other tissues. The cancer-associated neo-vasculature generated 
through angiogenesis addresses these requirements. Angiogenesis is normally a self-limiting 
process which is regulated by pro- and anti-angiogenic factors and involves the division and 
sprouting of endothelial cells from pre-existing blood vessels8. Angiogenesis is normally activated 
during physiological processes such as wound healing, in cancer, however, angiogenesis is 
continuously activated leading to a normally quiescent vasculature to constantly grow new 
vessels that help sustain the cancer cells10. Various signals triggering angiogenesis have been 
discovered where some of the important factors are hypoxia-induced activation of hypoxia-
inducible factors (HIF), which controls VEGF levels. VEGF-A is an important angiogenic factor, 
which angiogenic effect is largely explained by its interaction with one of its receptors, VEGFR2, 
on endothelial cells leading to proliferation and differentiation of these cells11. With hypoxia 
being a common feature of most tumors it is not surprising that the activity of HIF and therefore 
==PAGE:14==
5 
 
expression of VEGF-A is higher in many cancers12. Another important factor is the angiopoietin 
Ang2, which exerts its effect by acting as an antagonist on Tie2, a receptor involved in stabilizing 
vessels, thus providing a destabilizing signal initiating angiogenic remodeling11. Tumor 
vasculature is characterized as being structurally and functionally abnormal compared to the 
vasculature of normal tissue. It is characterized by having high permeability and by being 
disorganized with excessive branching and variable blood flow leading to hypoxic and acidic 
environments10. These stressful conditions can lower therapeutic effectiveness, potentiate 
further angiogenesis, and allow for selection of cancer cells, which have lost their apoptotic 
response to hypoxia10.   
Anti-angiogenic agents, which can interfere with either angiogenic factors, their receptors, their 
downstream signaling, or target the vasculature directly are attractive therapeutics as 
angiogenesis is shared among most types of cancer.        
 
Another relevant hallmark is the acquired capability of tissue invasion. The ability to metastasize 
is one of the defining features of malignancy and involves the formation of secondary growth in 
surrounding and distant tissues. The process by which cancers invade and metastasize in tissue 
consist of multiple sequential steps and are in principle the same for all tumors (Figure 2).  
In order for established cancer cells to metastasize they must invade through the extracellular 
matrix (ECM), enter the circulation through intravasation, survive the transport through the 
circulation, leave the circulation and enter the parenchyma of distant organs through 
extravasation, proliferate, and survive within the metastatic organ13. 
 
==PAGE:15==
6 
 
 
Figure 2. Overview of the steps needed for cancer cells to metastasize. a) Formation of primary tumor.  
b) Angiogenesis and proliferation. c) Invasion of the ECM. d) Intravasation e) Extravasation into new organs.  
f) Proliferation and survival within metastatic organ13. 
 
The ability of carcinoma cells to invade, disseminate, and survive circulation have largely been 
explained by the epithelial-mesenchyme transition (EMT) in which the cells adopt a more 
mesenchymal phenotype, which leads to enhanced migratory capacity and increased resistance 
to apoptosis e.g. by avoiding anoikis. Important transcription factors involved in the EMT 
program include Snail/Slug, Zeb1/2, and Twist14. Biological traits evoked by these transcription 
factors include repression of E-cadherin gene expression, which is normally involved in 
suppression of motility and invasiveness15 and expression of matrix metalloproteinases which are 
important for degradation of the ECM16. Both of these traits are important for invasion and 
migration through the ECM in order to reach the circulation. The mechanism behind 
intravasation can partly be explained by the high permeability of tumor vasculature17. Once 
disseminated in the circulatory system the carcinoma cells must, among other things, survive 
shear forces and clearance by the immune system. This can be achieved by the expression of 
selectins, which interacts with blood platelets thus forming emboli that protects the carcinoma 
==PAGE:16==
7 
 
cells18. While the mechanism of intravasation can be explained by the leaky nature of tumor 
microvasculature, the mechanism behind extravasation cannot due to the low permeability of 
normal tissue. To overcome this problem some cancers are capable of secreting factors, which 
can induce vascular hyper-permeability. An example of this is seen in primary tumors capable of 
secreting various factors, including VEGF and Ang2, which can induce hyper-permeability prior to 
the arrival of circulating metastatic carcinoma cells and this way enable their extravasation 
through tissue of otherwise low permeability. Cancer cells can also upregulate the expression of 
chemokine receptors which drives their migration into specific organs in response to chemokines 
normally found within these tissues18,19. 
 
1.2 Anticancer Defense Mechanisms and the Tumor Microenvironment 
The involvement of the host immune system in cancer progression was postulated early20 and 
have since been well established. Great emphasis has been placed on the complex interactions 
between immune system and tumors, which either result in successful tumor eradication or 
evasion of the immune system by the tumor.  
 
Many of the myeloid innate immune cells, including dendritic cells (DCs), macrophages, and 
granulocytes, are involved in homeostatic programs, which include antigen degradation and 
presentation, tissue repair, and various other effector functions21. The anticancer defense 
properties of the myeloid compartment rely heavily on DCs and their ability to present cancer 
antigens and activate T cells in the lymph nodes (Figure 3). Immature DCs are normally activated 
when they encounter pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) through their pattern 
recognition receptors (PRRs), such as Toll-like receptors (TLRs), NOD-like receptors (NLRs), and C-
type lectin receptors (CLRs). When DCs encounter tumors, many of the same receptors recognize 
damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), which are expressed on the surface of damaged 
and dead cells22. When immature DCs encounter antigens from these neoplastic cells, or from 
cellular debris, along with inflammatory signals such as TNF-α, IL-1β and type I interferons (IFN-
α, IFN-β)23 they undergo a process of maturation. This maturation leads to profound changes in 
gene expression and is coupled with the upregulation of MHC II-antigen complexes, the 
costimulatory molecules CD40, CD80, and CD86 along with the activation of genes responsible 
==PAGE:17==
8 
 
for the production of the inflammatory cytokines IL-1β, IL-6, IL-12, and TNF-α22. These molecules 
make up signal 1, 2, and 3, respectively, and are all necessary for T cell activation and 
differentiation. Furthermore, a switch in chemokine receptor expression occurs during DC 
maturation in which chemokine receptors involved in retaining the DCs in the peripheral tissue 
are downregulated while CCR7, which is important for lymph node migration, is upregulated22. 
Classically activated macrophages (M1) are also relevant in anti-cancer immunity as producers of 
inflammatory cytokines, such as IL-6, IL-12, and TNF-α, which are needed for the activation of 
DCs and priming of effective anti-tumor type 1 immune responses. Furthermore, M1 
macrophages have also shown tumor-suppressive effect through the production of reactive 
oxygen species24.  
Besides DC and macrophages, neutrophils are also found infiltrating tumors and as the most 
abundant leukocytes they have the potential to have an immense impact on malignant tumor 
progression. Some of the antitumor functions of neutrophils involve the direct killing of tumor 
cells by either ROS production or antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity interactions25. 
 
Within the lymphoid compartment CD8+ T cell are considered the major anti-cancer effector cells 
but CD4+ T cells, natural killer (NK) cells, and B cells are all of importance in the context of cancer 
immunity. When the mature DCs reach the secondary lymphoid organs they can activate naive 
CD8+ and CD4+ T cells through presentation of tumor-antigens on MHC I and MHC II, respectively. 
The combination of antigen presentation (signal 1), co-stimulatory molecules (signal 2), and 
production of the inflammatory cytokines mentioned above (signal 3) enables the DCs to induce 
activation, differentiation, and clonal expansion of Th1 and cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTL). These 
clonally expanded CTLs are specific for the tumor-antigen presented by the DCs and can release 
high levels of cytotoxic mediators, such as granzyme A and B and perforin, through interactions 
of the TCR with MHC I-antigen complexes on tumor cells21. NK cells are innate lymphoid effector 
cells that share the cytotoxic pathway of perforin and granzyme exocytosis to kill transformed 
cells26. Other mechanisms utilized by CTLs and NK cells to kill neoplastic cells include expression 
of FasL and TRAIL, which are involved in death-receptor-mediated apoptosis26,27. Unlike CTLs and 
NK cells, Th1-polarized CD4+ T cells are not directly involved in the killing of cancer cells but are 
==PAGE:18==
9 
 
instead capable of producing inflammatory cytokines such as IL-2, TNF-α, and IFN-γ and promote 
macrophage cytotoxic activity. B cells also play a significant role in the modulation of the immune 
response to cancer. Besides being able to directly inhibit tumor progression through production 
of tumor antigen-specific antibodies they are also able to enhance killing of cancer cell by NK 
cells, neutrophils, and macrophages and help prime CD4+ and CD8+ T cells28.

bone marrow these cells can differentiate into immature DCs (2). When immature DCs encounter DAMPs from

where they can prime tumor antigen-specific CTL responses (5). Activated CTL may then migrate to the tumor site 
and exert their cytotoxic effects (red X)22. (Modified) 
 
Even though several immune cells of both the myeloid and lymphoid compartments display 
efficient anti-cancer properties, the fact is that cancers can progress even in immunologically 
competent hosts. The escape mechanisms of cancer cells are attributed to their ability of 
inhibiting a proper immune response to be generated and to directly inhibit the anti-cancer 
functions of the immune cells described above (Figure 4). There are three distinct stages of tumor 
immunology: immuno-surveillance, immuno-editing, and immune evasion29.  
==PAGE:19==
10 
 
Immuno-surveillance is mediated by the cells and effector functions described above and 
represents the ideal tumor clearance by the immune system. Immuno-editing describes the 
process by which the immune system shapes and controls the disease and encompasses the 
phases of elimination, equilibrium, and escape. Just as immune-surveillance, elimination refers 
to the successful clearance of cancer cells by the immune system. The elimination phase is 
followed by the equilibrium phase, wherein many of the original cancer cells may have been 
eliminated but due to genetic instability different cancer variants may have arisen carrying 
various new mutations. The continuous elimination of immunogenic cancer cells results in 
selection of cancer cells carrying mutations related to reduced immunogenicity29. Due to the fact 
that cancer cells can enter a quiescent state and remain latent for a long time the equilibrium 
phase can last for several years in humans30 with the end-result being new cancer variants with 
an increased resistance to immune pressure and capable of escaping immune-surveillance. 
During this phase of immune evasion cancer cells can utilize several escape mechanisms related 
to both alteration of the cancer cells but also to the interference with normal function of the 
immune system31. Cancer cells can express a range of non-mutated, as well as mutated antigens 
and as discussed earlier the ability of the immune system to recognize tumor-antigens is 
fundamental to its anti-cancer defense mechanisms. As an escape mechanism some cancers can, 
through the selective environment created by the immune pressure, lose their antigenicity either 
by mutation of the immunogenic antigens themselves or through deficiencies in antigen 
presentation either through loss of MHC expression or loss of antigen processing function within 
the cancer cell32. To support this the frequency of MHC I loss or downregulation has been 
observed in approximately 30 % - 50 % of breast, lung, and prostate cancers33. 
 
Alternatively, escape can be mediated through the establishment of a tumor microenvironment 
(TME) wherein cancers can selectively co-opt key players of the host immune system (Figure 4). 
As discussed above a myriad of immune cells infiltrate tumors and are involved in disease 
progression. Historically, these immune responses have been regarded as an attempt of the 
immune system to control and eradicate tumors, however, there is evidence that these tumor-
==PAGE:20==
11 
 
associated inflammatory responses, and especially innate immune responses, also have the 
contradictory effect of augmenting tumorigenesis5. 
Tumor-infiltrating immune cells operate in conflicting ways meaning that both cells with 
antitumor and tumor-promoting effects are present. As mentioned above CTLs and NK cells are 
the main tumor-antagonizing cells and while macrophages, neutrophils, and DCs are capable of 
exerting antitumor effects as well, subsets of these cells can also act as tumor-promoting 
inflammatory cells. The counter-intuitive presence of both tumor-promoting and tumor-
antagonizing cells can be rationalized by the chronic inflammation often associated with cancer34. 
Inflammation is normally referred to as either being acute where immune cells are recruited 
transiently, or chronic which is characterized by prolonged inflammation where the immune 
response is not terminated by the normal feedback mechanisms. This prolonged inflammation 
causes a shift from cell and tissue damaging to tissue-repairing and tumor-promoting processes 
to occur involving both pro- and anti-inflammatory molecules. These processes include increased 
expression of molecules including TGF-β, IL-10, IL-6, and TNF-α, and activation of transcription 
factors such as NF-κB, all of which can result in inflammation-dependent immunosuppression, 
tumor growth and progression34. Inflammatory responses may therefore be beneficial for the 
host in the early stages of tumor progression as it will activate and recruit immune cells capable 
of killing tumor cells but when the inflammation state becomes chronic the consequence of the 
inflammation changes and instead becomes a tumor-promoting feature.

Key players in the development of the immunosuppressive environment in the TME include 
tumor associated macrophages (TAMs), DCs, neutrophils, myeloid-derived suppressor cells 
(MDSCs), and regulatory T cells (Figure 5). Tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) constitute a 
large component of these cells. Whereas M1 macrophages are pro-inflammatory and can exhibit 
anti-cancer properties TAMs often also display M2-like properties linked to anti-inflammatory 
type 2 processes, which can promote tumor progression while inhibiting anti-cancer functions of 
the adaptive immune system through a myriad of effector functions. TAMs tend to infiltrate the 
hypoxic tissue of cancers where they can promote angiogenesis through the production of VEGF, 
PDGF, and Ang2 and can this way improve blood flow to areas deprived of oxygen35. Other pro-
tumorigenic functions of TAMs include secretion of PGE2 and TGF-β, which both contribute to 
immune suppression e.g. by enhancing regulatory T cell differentiation and function. 
==PAGE:22==
13 
 
Furthermore TAMs show high expression of IL-10 and CCL22 which promote Th2 cell 
differentiation and increase regulatory T cell activity and migration to the tumor site35. As 
discussed earlier DCs are essential for the initiation of an effective anticancer immune response. 
However, within the TME DCs are often dysregulated and display immature phenotypes, which 
promote T cell anergy and increased regulatory T cell activity. This deficiency of tumor-infiltrating 
DCs to initiate proper CTL responses have partly been explained by, the fact that, these DCs lack 
sufficient expression of co-stimulatory molecules due to the tolerogenic environment found 
within the TME characterized by high concentration of IL-10 and TGF-β35. Furthermore, these DCs 
produce lower levels of IL-12 which is an important signaling cue for the propagation of a type 1 
immune response35. In spite of the potential anti-tumoral functions of neutrophils there is a lot 
of evidence indicating that large numbers of tumor-infiltrating neutrophils are associated with a 
poor prognosis. This is partly explained by the ability of these cells to secrete metalloproteases 
and growth factors thus promoting angiogenesis and by releasing arginase, which can exert 
suppressive effects on CD8+ T cells25. Hence, a lot of the immune cells associated with tumor 
clearance can be both beneficial but also detrimental to the host. 
 In addition to TAMs, DCs, and neutrophils a major player in the suppressive TME is a 
heterogeneous group of immature myeloid cells termed myeloid-derived suppressor cells 
(MDSCs). Within the cancer environment MDSCs are dysregulated and deviate from their normal 
developmental pathways resulting in a group of highly immunomodulatory cells37. These cells 
utilize a range of different mechanisms to exert their immunomodulatory effects. One of these 
mechanisms revolve around the depletion of essentials nutrients, e.g. by the production of 
arginase that sequesters extracellular arginine required for lymphocyte function within the TME. 
The lack of nutrients can result in inhibition of T cell receptor expression and therefore also of 
antigen specific T cell responses against the cancer cells38. MDSCs can also act by production of 
reactive nitrogen species which is normally produced in order to clear pathogens but within the 
TME can affect T cell receptor and IL-2 receptor expression thus leading to inhibition of T cell 
activation and proliferation39. Yet another regulatory mechanism involves interference with CD4+ 
and CD8+ T cell and NK cell viability by inducing apoptosis of these cells35. Lastly, MDSCs are 
capable of promoting activation and expansion of regulatory T cells through the production of 
==PAGE:23==
14 
 
TGF-β and IL-1040. The involvement of MDSCs in cancer progression should therefore not be 
neglected as they play a tremendous role in the development of a tumor-promoting TME. The 
subgrouping of MDSC into polymorphonuclear (PMN)-MDSCs and monocytic (M)-MDSCs will be 
discussed in Section 1.3.

As indicated throughout this section many of the cells making up the TME exerts part of their 
immunomodulatory functions by promoting the activation and migration of regulatory T cells in 
the TME. Regulatory T cells are FOXP3-expressing CD4+CD25+ T cells, which are capable of 
suppressing other immune cells in order to abrogate immune responses against self-antigens. 
During cancer, however, regulatory T cells are also capable of suppressing the functions of tumor-
infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs)41. There are three main mechanisms of immune suppression 
utilized by regulatory T cells. The first mechanism is by scavenging of IL-2 through constitutive 
expression of high affinity IL-2 receptors which results in low levels of available IL-2 in the 
environment leading to decreased activation of CTLs. Secondly, regulatory T cells constitutively 
==PAGE:24==

express CTLA-4 which functions as an immune checkpoint by binding to and downregulating the 
co-stimulatory molecules CD80 and CD86 on antigen-presenting cells. This prevents other T cells 
from receiving co-stimulatory signals and can lead them entering a state of anergy. Last but not 
least, regulatory T cells can produce and secrete the same immune-suppressive cytokines as 
many of the other immune cells in the TME, TGF-β and IL-10 which can downregulate antigen 
presentation by DCs 41. 
 
Based on the literature presented in this section it is clear that cancer cells evolve a complex 
microenvironment and dynamic interactions with the tissue and immune cells residing within this 
environment. It has also been clarified how cancers rely on this microenvironment for survival 
and growth and why finding ways of targeting and manipulating cellular components of this 
dysregulated environment could result in significant improvements in cancer therapy 
 
 
1.3 Differentiation of Myeloid Effector Cells 
Myeloid cells comprise the most abundant group of nucleated hematopoietic cells in the body 
and are a collection of many distinct cell populations with various effector functions. Based on 
the description of the immune cells present in the TME it is clear that many of the myeloid cells 
play significant roles in the pathological progression of cancer as they can be turned into potent 
immunosuppressive cells35. Due to their significant role in cancer disease progression it is 
important to understand the origin of these cells, how they are differentiated, how they can be 
characterized, and how they differentiate aberrantly within the setting of the TME.   
All leukocytes, lymphoid and myeloid alike, are ultimately derived from the same progenitor cell, 
the hematopoietic stem cell (HSC) in the bone marrow. HSCs produce the two more specialized 
stem cells; common lymphoid progenitors, giving rise to the adaptive T and B and the innate NK 
lymphocytes, and the common myeloid progenitor cells (CMP), which is the precursor of 
granulocytes, monocytes, DCs, and macrophages42. Despite the lymphoid cells being crucial in 
cancer immunity this project has focused on monocytes, macrophages, DCs, and neutrophils as 
==PAGE:25==
16 
 
these all can have profound effect on the progression of cancer both beneficial and detrimental 
to the host as described above. 
The initial steps of myeloid differentiation are determined by cytokine signals within the bone 
marrow, which activate the myeloid-specifying transcriptional events and determine which cell 
type is going to be developed. The processes of terminal differentiation leads to the generation 
of mature myeloid cells and most often occur in the blood or in the peripheral tissue and depend 
on the cytokine environment or antigens which the cells are presented to43. For example, by 
sequentially losing differentiation potential, CMPs gradually become progenitors committed to 
specific linages such as monocyte and DC progenitors (MDPs) that can further differentiate into 
common monocyte progenitors (CMoP) or common DC progenitors (CDP)43.  
Monocytes were for a long time regarded as a developmental intermediates which had not yet 
differentiated into macrophages or DCs. However, it has now been demonstrated that while still 
able to differentiate into DCs and macrophages, monocytes also carry out effector functions 
during inflammation. More specifically monocytes can be divided into two main subtypes; 
classical inflammatory monocytes and non-classical resident monocytes (Figure 6). These 
subpopulations can be categorized based on their expression of chemokine receptor CCR2, Ly-6C 
and the integrin CD11c where inflammatory monocytes exhibit a phenotype of high Ly-6C and 
CCR2 expression while resident monocytes show low Ly-6C and CCR2 expression but higher 
CD11c expression44. Functionally, inflammatory monocytes are referred to as such given that 
they are rapidly recruited to inflamed tissue, can produce inflammatory mediators such as TNF-
α and nitric oxide, and are able to differentiate into inflammatory macrophages and DCs which 
are characterized by their high secretion of inflammatory cytokines. Inflammatory monocytes are 
also believed to differentiate into resident monocytes which, in contrast, patrol blood vessels 
and have been shown to be able to promote wound healing and can initiate intraluminal stress 
responses by recruiting neutrophils and clearing cellular debris42.

Besides monocyte-derived DCs there are two DC subpopulations which instead originate from 
CDPs; conventional DCs, which are specialized in cross-presentation of antigens and plasmacytoid 
DCs that are characterized as producers of type I IFNs in the peripheral tissue43. 
A less defined population of myeloid cell consist of the dysregulated MDSC population mentioned 
earlier. A two-signal model describing how MDSCs are accumulated in tumors have been 
proposed and suggests that the first signal is driven by tumor-derived growth factors and are 
responsible for the expansion of immature myeloid cells through inhibition of their terminal 
differentiation. The second signal is driven by pro-inflammatory factors produced by the tumor 
stroma and leads to the activation of these immature cells into MDSCs37.  This population of 
dysregulated myeloid precursor cells consist of two main groups; PMN-MDSCs which exhibit 
many of the immunosuppressive effects described earlier and the M-MDSCs37. M-MDSCs 
==PAGE:27==
18 
 
resemble monocytes and have been shown to differentiate into TAMs in tumors while PMN-
MDSC are phenotypically similar to neutrophils. PMN-MDSC makes up the majority of MDSCs and 
can, like neutrophils, be identified by their expression of Ly-6G and CD11b but also show some 
expression of Ly-6C. Generally, it has been difficult to differentiate between MDSCs and 
monocytes and neutrophils in a consistent manner due to overlapping surface markers but it has 
been demonstrated that neither monocytes nor neutrophils can be converted into potent 
immunosuppressive cells at the same level as MDSCs in vitro, which suggests that they are 
different cell populations. Features like higher arginase and inducible NO synthase (iNOS) 
expression can be used in order to differentiate MDSCs from the others. Nonetheless, 
discrepancies exist in the nomenclature especially regarding PMN-MDSCs and tumor-associated 
neutrophils (TAN) where immunosuppressive TANs are often denoted N2 but might actually be 
PMN-MDSC37. 
Due to the critical role of the myeloid cell department in the development and progression of 
cancer an increased knowledge of their origin and differentiation could help facilitate the design 
of treatment strategies aimed at modulating the tumor-promoting effects of these cells. 
 
1.4 The Current Anticancer Drug Market  
It has been estimated that cancer is the cause of over 8 million deaths annually and that the 
number of deaths per year will increase to 13 million before year 2030. Due to this being one of 
the leading causes of death, oncology has become the largest therapeutic area in terms of clinical 
trials and research and development spending45. In spite of the advances in molecular cancer 
biology and the numerous new potential drug targets discovered, failure to translate these 
discoveries into effective therapies have caused a late-stage attrition rate of cancer drugs as high 
as 70 % and a total failure rate approaching 90 %46. Regardless of the high failure rate of new 
anticancer drugs the global anticancer drug market is expected to grow tremendously in the 
coming years47. This is partly explained by the increased prevalence and incidence rate of cancer, 
higher awareness, and increased government funding. It is expected that low levels of side effects 
will be a significant driving force behind the growth of the market and that drugs exhibiting lower 
==PAGE:28==
19 
 
levels of side effects compared to drugs currently on the market will make up one of the 
segments showing the biggest growth47.A reason for the increased demand for anticancer drugs 
exhibiting less side effects can be explained by, that for a long time the search for anticancer 
drugs was driven by the fact that tumor cells exhibit an abnormally rapid growth rate. This led to 
the development of many drugs inhibiting cell division by causing non-selective DNA damage 
leading to cell death of tumor and healthy cells alike. With the many advances within molecular 
cancer biology and the increased knowledge of the hallmarks of cancer, which distinguishes 
malignant tumor cells from normal cells, the field of anticancer drugs has begun to move away 
from non-specific cytotoxic drugs and instead turn towards the development of more targeted 
and less destructive anticancer agents. There exists a lot of different types of cancer treatment 
forms and the choice of which to utilize often depend on the type of cancer and how advanced 
it is but in broad terms the global anticancer drug market can be segmented into chemotherapy, 
targeted therapy, and immunotherapy47. 
Traditional chemotherapeutic agents are cytotoxic and many act as DNA interactive agents or 
antimetabolites. Of the DNA interactive agents alkylating drugs are one of most commonly used 
in cancer treatment. These antineoplastic agents act by binding directly to DNA thus causing DNA 
damage and subsequent cell apoptosis while antimetabolites exert their effect by impeding DNA 
synthesis by interacting with essential biosynthesis pathways. Even though these cytotoxic 
compounds can be effective, there are also drawbacks such as severe side effects and resistance. 
Side effects are commonly observed given that many cytotoxic agents are non-selective and act 
on both tumor and healthy non-transformed cells48.  
As the knowledge concerning the association between specific genes and cancer grew and the 
hallmarks of cancer were identified the doors were opened for more targeted and selective 
cancer therapies. This group of therapeutic agents consists of both small molecule drugs 
targeting enzymes or receptors commonly overexpressed in cancers and of bigger 
biopharmaceuticals such as monoclonal antibodies, which can bind specific antigens on cancer 
cells. Examples of such targeted cancer therapies include the tyrosine kinase inhibitor erlotinib 
(Tarceva®) and the monoclonal antibody cetuximab (Erbitux®) which both act as inhibitors of 
EGFR, which is a growth factor receptor often excessively expressed on cancer cells 49. The last 
==PAGE:29==
20 
 
major form of cancer therapy is immunotherapy which refers to treatment strategies designed 
to elicit an effective immune response against the tumor cells in the patient. Active 
immunotherapy can be divided into two main categories: immune response modifiers and 
vaccines50. Immune response modifiers are molecules or compounds, either extrinsic or intrinsic 
to the host, which have immunomodulatory properties and can affect the propagation of an 
effective immune response. Common immune response modifiers include immunostimulatory 
cytokines such as IFN-α and IL-2, which can act by boosting the immune responses. The aim of 
cancer vaccines is to induce an effective immune response against specific tumor-associated 
antigens. This can be achieved by presenting the immune system with these tumor-associated 
antigens in combination with adjuvants or encoded by viral- or viral-like vectors50. Desirable 
characteristics of such vaccines include targeting of antigens specific for the tumor and activating 
both the cellular and humoral components of the immune system.    
Even though there is an immense focus on improving immunotherapy there is still a shortage of 
broad acting and highly effective drugs. Reasons for this include the immunosuppressive tumor 
microenvironment described above which co-opts immune cells central for effective anticancer 
immunity. An important next step for tumor treatment regimens is therefore to develop drugs 
that can disturb the tumor microenvironment that so efficiently inhibits effective anticancer 
immune responses. 
One of the most studied anti-cancer drugs is the DNA crosslinking platinum-based cisplatin, which 
is efficient in the therapy of a variety of cancers. Given that cisplatin acts by inducing intra- and 
inter-strand DNA crosslinks in cancer cells and healthy cells alike it is associated with several side 
effects such as hepato-, nephron- and cardiotoxicity51. This has resulted in combination therapies 
consisting of cisplatin and other cancer drugs being sought in order to combat the drug resistance 
and the considerable side effects observed with cisplatin monotherapy. One of the means to 
overcome these problems has been to develop metallo-organic complexes wherein polymeric 
compounds can be used as encapsulating and chelating agents.  
 
 
==PAGE:30==

Meabco’s BP-Cx-1 is an example of such a multicomponent composition, which comprises water-
soluble lignin-derived polyphenolic compounds. BP-Cx-1 is used as the platform for the novel 
platinum-containing antineoplastic pharmaceutical product BP-C1 which has been demonstrated 
to efficiently reduce cell viability in several human cancer cell lines by increasing pro-apoptotic 
and decreasing anti-apoptotic gene expression and thereby induce apoptosis through both 
intrinsic and extrinsic pathways52,53. BP-C1 has also displayed higher specificity, better 
pharmacokinetics, and comparable anti-tumor effects compared to cisplatin in vivo, even though 
it contains lower concentrations of platinum54. Moreover, clinical studies have shown 
intramuscularly administered BP-C1 to have significant inhibitory effect on tumor growth, exhibit 
low levels of toxic side-effects, and improve quality of life in stage IV breast cancer patients55–57. 
The low toxicity of BP-C1 have partly been explained by better release kinetics in the form of 
slower release of platinum and this together with the fact that the compound, unlike therapeutics 
such as cisplatin, is stable enough to be administered intramuscularly makes it a very cost-
effective drug candidate55. All though all of these properties have been observed both in vitro 
and in vivo BP-C1 has not yet been approved for clinical use, which might be explained by the 
fact that the exact mechanisms of action underlying the effects have not been fully elucidated. 
Besides the apoptotic effects and clinical effectiveness of BP-C1 it is also hypothesized that BP-
Cx-1 exerts its own biological effect but the extent of these effects have not yet been clarified as 
of yet58. 
In summary BP-C1 seems to have an antineoplastic effect comparable to other platinum-based 
compounds such as cisplatin while offering advantages in the form of higher stability, improved 
pharmacokinetics, and an improved safety profile.

1.5 Objectives  
BP-C1 has been shown to be a cost-effective anti-cancer drug with high efficacy, low toxicity, and 
a simple route of administration. Even in a competitive market with many key players these 
features make BP-C1 an attractive treatment form in first and third world countries alike. Based 
on the observed clinical efficacy of BP-C1 in the trials described, the objectives of this study is to 
further elucidate the mechanism of action underlying these effects. This will be achieved by 
answering the following questions: 
- Does the BP-Cx-1 platform itself display an immunoregulatory effect on bone marrow 
derived immune cells relevant for tumor immunology? 
- Does the platinum-containing compound BP-C1 affect the innate and adaptive immune 
cells in the tumor microenvironment? If so, how does it affect the activation state of these 
cells? 
- Does BP-Cx-1 and BP-C1 show significant inhibitory effects on cancer cell viability and 
tumor growth? 
 
The immunoregulatory effects of the BP-Cx-1 platform will be investigated by studying the effect 
of BP-Cx-1 on mouse bone marrow cells. The effect of the platinum-containing BP-C1 on the 
tumor microenvironment will be evaluated by utilizing different mouse models subjected to 
different cancer cell lines and by characterizing the immune cells present in the tumor 
microenvironment through flow cytometry. 
By answering these questions this project may provide some preclinical data and establish 
models, which can support and help facilitate regulatory approval of BP-C1. The approval and 
subsequent implementation of BP-C1 could have substantial clinical value as it would allow for a 
new treatment against cancer with an efficacy comparable to established drugs such as cisplatin 
while exhibiting lower levels of toxicity and fewer side effects.

2.1 Introduction to Methods 
2.1.1 Flow cytometry 
Flow cytometry is a method used to analyze multiple physical characteristics of single cells and 
allows multi-parametric analysis of complex samples. The properties, which can be measured, 
include the relative size, internal complexity, and fluorescence intensity of the cells. The 
fluorescent signal of a cell is dependent on which, and how many, fluorophore-conjugated 
antibodies that are bound to the target antigen in or on the cell. The cell analysis is performed 
by letting the sample flow in a fluid stream through an optic system comprised of a set-up of 
lasers and by recording how the cells scatter the light from the lasers and with which intensity 
they emit fluorescence. Flow cytometry is commonly used to characterize and quantify cells and 
was employed in this project to quantify certain populations of immune cells in the bone marrow 
and tumor tissue of mice. 
2.1.2 Quantification of tumor growth 
In order to quantify the growth of subcutaneous tumors caliper measurement has traditionally 
been the method of choice. This method is to some extent suitable for palpable subcutaneous 
tumor models but is not applicable when the growth of deeper tumors or metastases have to be 
assessed. Even for palpable subcutaneous tumors this method may fall short as it does not 
account for tissue surrounding the tumor and since the depth of the tumor cannot be measured, 
the calculated tumor volume is not always accurate. In this project the tumor volume was 
determined as: 𝑙𝑒𝑛𝑔𝑡ℎ x 𝑤𝑖𝑑𝑡ℎ2 x 0.5236.  
A method, which can replace or at least supplement physical caliper measurements, is non-
invasive bioluminescence imaging (BLI) that allows for real-time in vivo quantification of tumor 
size regardless of tumor location. In order to use BLI tumor cell-lines stably expressing luciferase 
have to be employed. When luciferin is injected into animals carrying these luciferase-expressing 
tumor cells an in vivo imaging system (IVIS) can be used to measure the light emitted from the 
==PAGE:33==
24 
 
cells and this way give a quantitative estimate of the tumor burden in a given region of interest59.  
This way tumor growth can effectively and repeatedly be monitored, thus BLI can be used to 
assess the efficacy on drug candidates on tumor growth and metastases.  
2.1.3 Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay 
Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) is a commonly used technique for detecting and 
quantifying analytes, e.g. cytokines, in a liquid sample. The principle behind ELISA relies on the 
immobilization of antigen and the subsequent complex-formation with a target-specific antibody 
linked to a reporter-enzyme. The detection of the analyte is accomplished by measuring the 
activity of the reporter after incubation with a substrate where a measureable product is 
produced. In the context of this project the sandwich ELISA format was used, wherein the analyte 
is bound between two primary antibodies and where the reaction between the reporter-enzyme 
and its substrate generates a luminescent signal, which can be read at 450 nm.

mm in diameter. Determination of tumor size, body weight, and general health were also 
examined three times a week. In this model instead of quantifying tumor size by measuring 
intrinsic luciferase in an IVIS facility, tumor size was determined by manually measuring the 
length and width of the tumor using a caliper. The aim was to euthanize the mice and remove 
the tumors when the Ehrlich carcinoma tumors reached a diameter of 16 mm. The tumors were 
then to be analyzed using flow cytometry in order to examine the effect of BP-C1 on the immune 
cell composition in the TME. A preliminary study was conducted on two tumor-bearing BALB/c 
mice in order to practice and optimize the staining protocol. 
2.4 Experimental procedures 
2.4.1. Cell Culture 
During this project, three cancer cell lines were used for in vivo experiments and three cell lines 
were used for in vitro assays. All cell lines were grown and maintained in a humidified incubator 
at 37 °C with 5 % CO2 before inoculation.   
CT26-Luc: CT26 is a colon carcinoma cell line derived from BALB/c mice. It is one of the most 
commonly used cell lines in drug development and shares features of aggressive human 
colorectal carcinoma cells and shows a high metastatic potential60. The cell line had been stably 
transfected with a luciferase reporter gene enabling tumor burden measurement using an IVIS 
facility. The cells were grown and maintained in RPMI 1640 supplemented with 10 % heat-
inactivated FBS, 1 % L-glutamine (2mM), 1 % Na-pyruvate (1mM), and 1 % Pen/Strep (henceforth 
called RPMI++++). 
Ehrlich Ascites Carcinoma: The ELA cell line is a spontaneous murine carcinoma exhibiting an 
undifferentiated phenotype. It can be adapted to ascites form and cultured in vivo by serial 
intraperitoneal passages in mice but can also be used as a model of solid tumors by subcutaneous 
injection. Characteristics of the ELA cell line include rapid proliferation and high malignancy61. 
The cells were acquired from American Type Culture Collection (ATCC) (CCL-77, lot no. F-15286) 
and grown and maintained in NCTC 135 medium supplemented with 10 % heat-inactivated FBS. 
==PAGE:37==
28 
 
4T1: 4T1 is a highly tumorigenic and invasive murine mammary carcinoma cell line, which 
originates from the BALB/c strain. The 4T1 cancer cell line exhibits several characteristics, which 
makes it a suitable model for human mammary cancer. These characteristics include easy 
engraftment in the mammary fat pad and spontaneous metastatic development from the 
primary tumor in pattern comparable to human mammary cancer62. The cells were grown and 
maintained in RPMI++++. 
Three additional cell lines were used to assess the efficacy of BP-Cx-1 and BP-C1 in inducing cell 
death. These cell lines were the human breast cancer cell line T47D, the human prostate cancer 
cell lines PC3, and the murine fibroblast cell lines NIH3T3. The human cancer cell lines were grown 
and maintained in RPMI++++ while NIH3T3 was grown and maintained in DMEM supplemented 
with 10 % heat-inactivated FBS, 1 % L-glutamine (2 mM), 1 % Na-pyruvate (1 mM), and 1 % 
Pen/Strep. 
2.4.2. Bioluminescence Imaging 
For in vivo imaging the mice were injected s.c. with 200 µL luciferin (Perkin Elmer), anaesthetized 
by isoflurane, and after 15 min the animals were placed onto a warmed platform within the 
camera box of the IVIS machine (Caliper) and received continuous exposure to isoflurane to 
maintain sedation throughout imaging. Every group of mice was imaged for approximately 10 
min. The signal emitted from the mice were detected by the IVIS camera system and regions of 
interest from the images was quantified using Living Image® software 4.0 (Perkin Elmer, Alameda, 
CA.).

2.4.3. Bone Marrow-derived Cell Cultivation 
Mice were euthanized by cervical dislocation and the abdominal cavity was opened with surgical 
scissors. Surface muscle and tissue was removed in order to expose the hip joint. The hind legs 
were then cut off above the hip joint in order to preserve femur integrity. Next, the muscle and 
tissue surrounding the femur and tibia was removed and the cleaned bones was immersed in 
ethanol for 20 seconds before putting them in HBSS. Both ends of the femur were cut off and a 
23G needle and a syringe was used to flush out the bone marrow with 10 mL ice-cold HBSS into 
a 70 µm cell strainer placed on a 50 mL tube. The same procedure was performed on the tibia 
using 5 mL HBSS. The flushed bone marrow was then mashed through the strainer using a 5 mL 
plunger and the strainer was washed with an additional 5 mL HBSS.  The cells were then spun 
down at 1500 rpm for 5 min at 4 °C and re-suspended in 1 mL red blood cell (RBC) lysis buffer and 
incubated for 5 min at room temperature. The lysis buffer was then neutralized by adding 5 mL 
RPMI 1640, 0.1 % v/v 50mM beta-Mercaptoethanol, and 10 % GM-CSF. The cells were counted 
and moved to a 96-well non tissue culture plate with 6x105 cells in 200 µL RPMI++++ and 10 % GM-
CSF in each well. Cells were treated with BP-Cx-1 (Meabco) in a spectrum of concentrations 
ranging from 0 to 40 μg/ml (0, 5, 10, 20, and 40 µg/mL). LPS was then added in order to obtain a 
concentration of 10 ng/mL LPS. Finally, the plates were stored in a 37 °C incubator (5 % CO2) for 
approximately 96 hours before staining. 
2.4.4. Enzyme-linked Immunosorbent Assay 
The ELISA assays were run using the supernatant obtained from 2.4.3 after incubation of bone 
marrow-derived cells in order to quantify the amount of TNF-α, which was produced by bone 
marrow-derived immune cells when treated with varying concentrations of BP-Cx-1 and LPS. For 
the quantification of TNF-α in the samples TNF-α ELISA MAXTM Standard Sets were purchased 
from BioLegend. The different reagents used can be found in Appendix A. 
In the first step of the assay set-up MaxiSorp flot bottom plates (Thermo Fisher) were coated 
overnight at 4 °C with 100 µL capture antibody solution per well. The next day the wells were 
washed four times with 300 µL wash buffer. 200 µl assay diluent were added to each well and 
incubated for one hour on a plate shaker at RT after which the wells were washed four times. 
The standards and samples were then added to the appropriate wells and incubated for two

captured TNF-α were incubated with 100 µl detection antibody solution containing a biotinylated

100 µl Avidin-HRP solution were added to each well and incubated at RT for 30 min with shaking.

2.4.6. Flow Cytometry

remaining tissue was dissociated further with a pipette and then passed through a 70 µm strainer

the strainer was washed with 10 mL HBSS. The cell suspension was centrifuged at 1200 rpm for 
8 min at 4 °C and washed twice in HBSS. The cells were counted and moved to a 96-well non 
tissue culture plate with 106 cells/well in 200 µL RPM before staining. 
For the staining procedures the cells were spun down at 2000 rpm for 3 min at 4 °C and re-

mix. After 30 min incubation at 4 °C, 100 µL wash medium was added, the plates were centrifuged

3.1 Effect of BP-Cx-1 on bone marrow-derived immune cells  
To investigate the immunoregulatory effect of BP-Cx-1, this project started out by evaluating the 
effect on bone marrow-derived immune cell differentiation by BP-Cx-1 by treating these cells 
with a spectrum of BP-Cx-1 concentrations. To mimic inflammatory conditions and to investigate 
the effect on cell differentiation and activation under such conditions cells were also stimulated 
with LPS.  
The effect of BP-Cx1 on TNF-α secretion from LPS-stimulated bone marrow-derived cells was 
initially tested in an ELISA assay. Here no significant difference in TNF-α signal could be detected 
between samples treated with either 1 µg/mL or 5 µg/mL LPS (Appendix F) indicating that these 
initial LPS doses were too high and that the LPS concentration had to be optimized in order to 
achieve a more moderate stimulation cue which more accurately reflect a natural activation 
pattern and not just maximum activation. An optimization study was thus carried out to 
determine the optimal concentrations of GM-CSF and LPS before the effect of BP-Cx1 was 
studied. Through measurements of cell viability, frequency of MHC II expressing cells (as a proxy 
for macrophage and DC numbers) and CD40 expression (as a marker of activation) 10 % GM-CSF 
and 10 ng/mL LPS were chosen for the stimulation in the final bone marrow study (Appendix F).  
In the final bone marrow study cells were treated with a spectrum of BP-Cx-1 concentrations 
ranging from 0 – 40 µg/mL, stimulated with 10 % GM-CSF and with or without 10 ng/mL LPS for 
96 hours. 
The effect of BP-Cx-1 on cell viability was first assessed and besides an initial drop of around 10 
% between treated and non-treated groups no significant decrease in cell viability was observed 
with increasing BP-Cx-1 concentrations (Figure 7).

3.2 Efficiency of BP-Cx-1 and BP-C1 in inducing cell death  
In spite of the broad effectiveness of cisplatin alternative antineoplastic compounds are sought 
due to its high toxicity. BP-C1 have shown clinical efficacy comparable to cisplatin and have 
exhibited lower levels of toxicity, thus making it an interesting alternative drug candidate. Here 
the multicomponent polyphenolic compounds BP-Cx-1 and BP-C1, and the established 
chemotherapeutic drug cisplatin and their capacity of inducing cell death in an in vitro assay was 
compared. In this study the human breast cancer cell line T47D was used to assess the effects of 
BP-Cx-1, BP-C1, and cisplatin on cell viability. The cells were treated with a spectrum of 
concentrations of each drug, incubated for 72 hours, and analyzed using flow cytometry after PI 
staining (Figure 15).  
 
Figure 15. Changes in viability of T47D human breast cancer cells as percentage of controls when treated with either 
BP-Cx-1, BP-C1, or cisplatin (CisPt). Each dot represents a measurement of cell viability at different concentrations of 
treatment. Cells treated with just media, i.e. the first dot on each plot, were used as controls.  
 
==PAGE:49==

Since BP-C1 consists of platinum molecules bound in BP-Cx-1 the treatment doses were 
determined in order to achieve equimolar amount of the BP-Cx-1 platform in both of these 
treatment groups explaining the difference in treatment concentrations. In the same way, the 
cisplatin treatment doses were determined as to achieve equimolar amounts of platinum in the 
BP-C1 and cisplatin groups. Based on this experiment it was observed that the viability of T47D 
cells did not seem to be affected much by BP-Cx-1 or BP-C1 at the concentrations used as neither 
treatment with BP-Cx-1, at a concentration up to 60 µg/mL, nor with BP-C1, at a concentration 
up to 72 µg/mL, caused a notable change in viability of the T47D cell lines with a viability of 
approximately 98 % at the highest treatment doses when compared to the controls. In contrast, 
viability of T47D was observed to decrease gradually with increasing doses of cisplatin with the 
highest treatment concentration of cisplatin at 12 µg/mL, which contained equal amounts of 
platinum as the highest BP-C1 treatment dose, causing a decrease in viability to approximately 
65 % when compared to the control.  In order to better assess the effect of BP-Cx-1 and BP-C1 
on the viability of cancer cells lines, the same experimental setup was designed and performed 
for the human prostate cancer cell line PC3. Furthermore, effect on viability was also supposed 
to be tested on the mouse fibroblast cell line NIH3T3 in order to assess the effect of BP-Cx-1 and 
BP-C1 on cell viability in non-cancerous cell lines compared to cisplatin. However, due to reasons 
discussed in Section 4.2 the data generated from these cell lines were not suited for further 
analysis.

growth and metastases and on the composition of immune cells within TME. This was supposed 
to be achieved by surgical removal of tumors from the two murine models presented above when 
tumors had grown large enough, followed by subsequent analysis of the removed tissue through 
flow cytometry, and by tracking metastases development in the mice post-surgery. However, the 
tumors in these two models showed clearance before the end of the study meaning that the 
original objective could not be investigated. Instead, tumors from untreated BALB/c mice 
inoculated with 4T1 cancer cells were acquired and used for setting up a protocol for the 
characterization of immune cells within the TME. Furthermore, based on the 4T1 – BALB/c study 
it was verified that the surgical removal of tumors from live mice was actually feasible and that 
the designed procedure was ready to be implemented.

4. Discussion  
The goal of this work was to further elucidate the mechanism of action underlying the clinical 
and non-clinical anticancer effects that have been observed of BP-Cx-1 and BP-C154–57. The 
mechanism of action was explored in three different ways. The efficacy of BP-Cx-1 and BP-C1 in 
inducing cell death directly was investigated using an in vitro apoptotic assay. Likewise, the effect 
of BP-Cx-1 on the differentiation of immune cells from the bone marrow was examined by an in 
vitro culture system to assess its potential immunoregulatory properties, and lastly the 
antineoplastic effect of BP-C1 was studied in vivo in two separate murine cancer models.     
4.1. The Effect of BP-Cx-1 and BP-C1 on Cell Viability  
In this study the three cell lines T47D, PC3, and NIH3T3 were chosen in order to assess the effect 
of BP-Cx-1 and BP-C1 on cell viability compared to cisplatin. Since most of the published clinical 
data of BP-C1 is based on breast cancer patients and because the strategy of Meabco is to 
approach the breast cancer segment of the cancer treatment drug market63 the human breast 
cancer cell-line T47D was chosen in order to most closely mimic the intended target. The human 
prostate cancer cell-line PC3 was chosen to assess the effect of BP-Cx-1 and BP-C1 on different 
types of cancer cells and to see if there was a stronger effect on one cancer type compared to 
the other. The mouse fibroblast cell-line NIH3T3 was chosen in order to assess whether BP-Cx-1 
and BP-C1 induced lower levels of cell death in normal fibroblasts compared to T47D and PC3 
which could indicate higher specificity of BP-Cx-1 and BP-C1 towards cancer cells compared to 
non-cancer cells.   
The PC3 and NIH3T3 cell-lines were deemed unsuited for further analysis due to a low viability of 
the cells regardless of treatment. This low viability was likely due to the cells reaching super-
confluency during the 72 hours incubation period because of a higher-than-expected growth 
rate. Super-confluency of the cell could explain the low viability in several ways including 
nutrient-starvation or a drop in pH as a result of excreted by-products of cellular metabolism. 
The results of this study indicated that treatment with BP-Cx-1 or BP-C1 did not have a marked 
effect on the viability of T47D human breast cancer cells when compared to the untreated 
samples, as shown in Section 3.2. Samples treated with cisplatin showed a substantial decline in 
==PAGE:61==
52 
 
viability with increased cisplatin concentrations which fits nicely with the well-documented 
toxicity of cisplatin. However, the decrease in viability to 65 % at 12 µg/mL observed in this study 
does not correspond with the degree of cell death induced by cisplatin in similar studies using 
comparable treatment doses. For example some studies have showed a decrease in viability to 
approximately 15 % when treated with cisplatin concentrations of 10 µg/mL for 96 h64. This 
difference could possibly be explained by the differences in incubation time but it might also be 
a sign indicating that the T47D cells were also affected by super-confluency during the 72 h 
incubation period. This overgrowth might not have led to nutrient-starvation in the same degree 
as the other cell-lines, which would explain the better viability of the T47D cells compared to PC3 
and NIH3T3. However, the super-confluent state could have caused the cells to enter a stationary 
plateau growth phase, which means the cells were not in an exponential growth phase when 
incubated with treatment. This could have affected the effect of cisplatin treatment, which acts 
on dividing cells, since cells remain in the slow growth lag phase for longer if they spend a long 
time in the plateau-phase prior to seeding65. This could explain the decreased effect of treatment 
on cell viability compared to prior studies and should be kept in mind when interpreting the 
results.    
In a study by Fares et al. 2014 T47D cells were treated with 100 - 1000 µg/mL BP-C1 for 48 hours 
and large decreases were found in cell viability of several cancer cell-lines through induction of 
apoptosis with an IC50 of 490 µg/mL52. The treatment doses used in that study are significantly 
higher than the concentrations used in this project where the treatment interval was 0 – 72 
µg/mL BP-C1. However, according to their results no noticeable reduction of viability was found 
at 100 µg/mL which corresponds with the findings in this project indicating that these lower 
concentrations of BP-C1 does not induce cell death of T47D. In another study by Fares et al. 2016 
the effect of BP-C1 on cancer cell viability was examined with treatment concentrations ranging 
10 – 250 µg/mL followed by incubation for 72 hours66. Here they also observed significant 
decreases in T47D viability with increasing treatment doses but they did not show noticeable 
apoptosis of cancer cells in what should correspond to the treatment doses used in this project, 
thus also indicating little-to-no tumor growth inhibition at these concentrations66. An issue with 
the two studies from Fares et al. include their inconsistent data presentation which makes it 
==PAGE:62==
53 
 
difficult to make out which concentrations of BP-C1 they used. On one hand they state to use 
concentrations ranging within 100 -1000 µg/mL and 10 – 250 µg/mL, respectively, while on the 
other they present viability development as a function of amount of BP-C1 per well. While they 
do indeed show that BP-C1 causes a decrease in cell viability at the high-end but not low-end 
treatment concentrations it still becomes a problem when they fail to state the volumes in the 
wells. This makes an accurate calculation of the concentrations used, and therefore an accurate 
dose-comparison, difficult.  
In the study conducted by Fares et al. 2016 they also investigated the effect of BP-C1 treatment 
on the NIH3T3 fibroblast cell-line. Here they showed that BP-C1 treatment only had a minor 
effect on the viability of normal fibroblast cells66. Since cisplatin induces death of cancer cells and 
normal cells alike and the results of Fares et al. 2016 indicate a higher apoptotic effect of BP-C1 
on cancer cells compared to non-cancerous cells this could suggest that BP-C1 works by a 
mechanism different from that of cisplatin. This alternative mechanism of action could be 
through potential immunomodulatory properties of BP-Cx1 discussed in Section 4.2.  
The prior studies conducted by Fares et al. indicate a high apoptotic effect of BP-C1 on cancer 
cell-lines which could explain part of the antineoplastic effect of BP-C1 observed in clinical 
studies. Nonetheless, the experiment performed in the current project does not imply the same 
direct effect of BP-C1 or BP-Cx-1 on cancer cell viability for the lower concentrations used. Due 
to the experimental errors discussed this experiment should be repeated and super-confluency 
of the cells should be avoided by either seeding at a lower density or by incubating the cells for 
a shorter duration before making any substantiated claims regarding the direct effect of BP-Cx-1 
and BP-C1 on cell viability at the concentrations used. Additionally, the experiment should be 
performed in replicates in order to achieve adequate statistical power.

4.2. The Potential Anti-inflammatory Effect of BP-Cx-1  
In this experiment immune cells were isolated from the bone marrow of BALB/c mice. The cells 
were treated with a spectrum of BP-Cx-1 concentration ranging from 0 to 40 µg/mL, and 
stimulated with GM-CSF and LPS to investigate whether the differentiation of the cells were 
affected by BP-Cx-1 under inflammatory conditions. A treatment interval similar to the one in the 
apoptosis assay was utilized and even though no noticeable apoptotic effect of BP-Cx-1 was 
observed at these concentration, as discussed in Section 4.1, the results from Section 3.1 indicate 
potential immunoregulatory effects of BP-Cx-1 on several key players in tumor immunology in 
this treatment range. 
The results presented in Section 3.1 indicate that BP-Cx-1 affect the frequencies of several cell 
types. These effects include increase in DC, macrophage, and resident monocytes numbers and 
decrease of overall arginase-1 expression, M2-polarized macrophages, inflammatory monocytes, 
and neutrophils (Table 1).  
When the overall expression of arginase-1 and iNOS was examined it was seen that even the 
lowest dose of BP-Cx-1 seemed to almost completely abrogate arginase-1 expression while no 
definite effect was observed on iNOS expression. To make sure the sudden drop of arginase-1 
expression was not due to BP-Cx-1 interfering with the fluorescence of the Arg1 antibody 
fluorophore it was investigated whether the addition of BP-Cx-1 had a similar effect on any of 
the other fluorophores in the panel. No fluorophores seemed to be affected by the addition of 
BP-Cx-1 and this was not deemed to be the cause of the sudden decrease. Both arginase-1 and 
iNOS are have been linked to carcinogenesis and it has been shown that arginase activity and NO 
quantities increase in parallel with cancer progression67. The poor prognosis associated with 
increased arginase-1 activity and quantity have been explained by depletion of nutrients essential 
for T cell function and by increased production of polyamines that are important for cell 
proliferation and may assist in the rapid proliferation of cancer cells67. It has also been shown 
that tumor growth can be inhibited in a dose-dependent manner by administration of arginase 
inhibitor and that this antitumor effect of arginase inhibition is due to increased T cell function38. 
If the decrease of arginase-1-expressing cells observed in the current in vitro study is also 
==PAGE:64==
55 
 
achieved in an in vivo tumor environment it might be an important aspect of the antineoplastic 
effect of BP-Cx-1. 
As initiators of T cell responses, DCs are at the foundation of tumor immunology and the 
increasing effect of BP-Cx-1 on DC frequency might therefore be a favorable antitumor property. 
However, while DC maturation does occur within tumors they may not operate at their peak 
capacity due to the suppressive environment often found within the TME which is partly 
maintained through the secretion of IL-10 by various suppressor cells22. BP-Cx-1 has been shown 
to inhibit the secretion of IL-10 in LPS-stimulated human inflammatory monocytes68. If both the 
DC promoting and the IL-10 inhibiting effects of BP-Cx-1 are present in an in vivo tumor setting it 
might be a significant antitumor mechanism as it could increase the maturation of DCs and thus 
potentially increase the initiation of tumor-specific  cytotoxic T cell responses. It was also 
observed that BP-Cx-1 seemed to decrease the frequency of M2-polarized macrophages in a 
dose-dependent manner. TAMs often show M2-polarized characteristics, and are linked to poor 
cancer prognosis. Besides arginase-1 production, characteristics of M2-polarized macrophages 
include high expression of IL-10 and CCL22 which facilitate type 2 responses and maintains 
regulatory T cell suppressive functions69. It has been shown that sustained IL-10-induced signaling 
is required for the M2-phenotype to be maintained and that removal of IL-10 is sufficient to 
reverse the phenotype in vitro70. The inhibition of IL-10 by BP-Cx-1 is then not only interesting 
with regard to DCs buts also regarding M2 numbers, which was decreased upon treatment and 
if this inhibitory effect persists in vivo it might help relieve the suppressive tumor environment 
and explain the suspected immunoregulatory effects of BP-Cx-1 and BP-C168.  
It has been shown that inflammatory monocytes can differentiate into M-MDSCs in tumor-
bearing mice and that these M-MDSCs can further differentiate into PMN-MDSCs instead of 
differentiating into DCs or macrophages as in healthy hosts. This way inflammatory monocytes 
can be seen as the main precursors of the important tumor-promoting M-MDSCs and PMN-
MDSCs cell populations71. The observed decreasing effect of BP-Cx-1 on inflammatory monocyte 
frequencies may therefore be an important anti-cancer property as it could lead to decreasing 
numbers of these cell populations and as it has been shown that suppression of MDSCs supports 
cancer eradication72. Furthermore, inflammatory monocytes have been shown to be recruited to 
==PAGE:65==
56 
 
tumors and differentiate into immunosuppressive M2-like TAMs73. This fits well with studies 
showing that the number of inflammatory monocytes are increased in pancreatic cancer patients 
and are associated with decreased survival73. Overall inflammatory monocytes act as a reservoir 
of precursors for the immunosuppressive M2-like TAM and MDSC cell populations which are 
associated with poor cancer prognosis. If BP-Cx-1 can decrease the numbers of inflammatory 
monocytes in an in vivo setting this might therefore be an important tumor-inhibiting 
mechanism. Moreover, not only does BP-Cx-1 seem to decrease the number of inflammatory 
monocytes, it has also been shown to be able to suppress the secretion of several inflammatory 
cytokines from these cells which might also be beneficial as it could lessen the chronic 
inflammatory environment found in cancer74. In normal tissue, the function of resident 
monocytes is to patrol and survey blood vessels and to aid in the removal of pathogens from 
these sites. It has been shown in metastasizing tumor mouse models that the number patrolling 
monocytes are augmented in the lungs and that they can reduce tumor metastasis through early 
interactions with metastasizing tumor cells. It has further been shown that resident monocytes 
might be able to promote NK cell recruitment and activation, which is highly beneficial in 
antitumor immunity75. The observation of a dose-dependent increase in resident monocyte 
frequencies in the current project might be an important aspect of the clinical efficacy displayed 
by BP-C1. Especially the potential anti-metastasizing effects of these cells is interesting as the 
efficacy of BP-C1 was displayed in patients with stage IV breast cancer which is characterized by 
metastases and because the lung metastases of several patients in the high-dose group were 
cleared during the trial55. However, resident monocytes have also been associated with a small 
subset of Tie2-expressing monocytes in the TME which show enhanced pro-angiogenic and lower 
pro-inflammatory activity compared to TAMs in the TME meaning that the anti- or pro-tumor 
roles of resident monocytes might not be that clear-cut76.  
Neutrophils are among the most abundant leukocytes in humans and are the first immune cells 
to be recruited to inflammatory sites. In the context of cancer neutrophils have been linked to 
several opposing functions; in some contexts neutrophils have shown to be able to clear cancer 
cells through various mechanism and in others neutrophils have been linked to a multitude of 
different tumor-promoting functions77. Based on the phenotype tumor-associated neutrophils 
==PAGE:66==
57 
 
display they are classified as either tumor-antagonizing N1 or tumor-promoting N2. Early N1 
recruited to the tumor environment are not immunosuppressive but aid in tumor clearance by 
directly killing cancer cells and by stimulation of T cell responses78. However, if co-opted in the 
TME, a process believed to in some part be driven by TGF-β, they can adapt an 
immunosuppressive N2-phenotype which share many features of PMN-MDSCs such as favoring 
ECM degradation, angiogenesis, and induction of immune suppression e.g. by the production of 
arginase77. Due to the overall poor prognosis of neutrophils in cancer patients the neutrophil-to-
lymphocyte ratio (NLR) has been introduced as a predictive biomarker for cancer progression and 
an elevated NLR has been strongly associated with poor survival in several cancers including 
breast cancer79. Due to this poor prognosis associated with neutrophils in cancer patients the 
large decreasing effect of BP-Cx-1 on neutrophil numbers observed in this project may help 
explain the efficacy of BP-C1 on tumor growth. Because of the large observed decreases in 
neutrophil frequencies it was investigated whether this could be due to neutrophilic death. As 
presented in Section 3.2 the quantity of dead neutrophils did indeed increase in a dose-
dependent manner with BP-Cx-1 treatment and this could suggest a possible cytotoxic effect of 
BP-Cx-1 on neutrophils especially since overall cell viability was not affected in the same manner. 
However, a decreasing effect on neutrophil numbers might not be enough to explain the clinical 
efficacy displayed of BP-C1 as neutrophil number did not decrease in stage IV breast cancer 
patients treated with BP-C155,56. This discrepancy between the indications of the effect on 
neutrophils numbers could potentially be explained by the fact that the patients enrolled in the 
clinical trials had already undergone several rounds of chemotherapy, of which a common side 
effect is neutropenia56. In the current project it was also shown that BP-Cx-1 caused a large drop 
in arginase-1 producing neutrophils already at the lowest dose which could suggest a mechanism 
different from the one causing cell death, and that BP-Cx-1 could theoretically be able to reduce 
the quantity of tumor-promoting N2-polarized (arginase-1 positive) neutrophils in vivo.    
It has previously been shown that BP-Cx-1 reduced the level of IL-10, this linked to the increase 
in DC numbers found in this study, may indicate that BP-Cx-1 can increase the activity of DCs and 
thus boost the activation of a tumor specific T cell response. In addition, a decrease in M2 
macrophages was found in this study, which are normally classified as a tumor-promoting cell 
==PAGE:67==
58 
 
type. Lastly, it was found that BP-Cx-1 reduced the frequency, and maybe also activity, of 
inflammatory monocytes and neutrophils which both gain tumor-promoting phenotypes when 
co-opted by the TME. 
Through in silico and in vitro screenings it has been demonstrated that BP-Cx-1 bind to, and may 
exert pharmacological effects against, several receptors involved in inflammation and immune 
responses. It has further been proposed that these effects may stem from a myriad of 
polyphenolic components likely contained in BP-Cx-1 of which many have shown anti-
inflammatory and anti-proliferative properties which could possible explain some of the 
observations made in the current project80. Since the effects presented in this section are based 
on in vitro assays it will be important to set up animal models to investigate whether these 
suspected anti-inflammatory and anti-suppressive effects of BP-Cx-1 are also present in an in vivo 
setting. Preclinical data on the immunoregulatory effects of BP-Cx-1 is therefore of utmost 
importance in order to elucidate the mechanism of action underlying the displayed clinical 
efficacy. 
4.3. Exploring the Effects of BP-C1 in Murine Models  
Two murine cancer models were utilized in this project in order to investigate the antineoplastic 
effect of BP-C1. In both of these studies the groups receiving BP-C1 treatment were administered 
125 mg/kg BW by s.c. injection three times a week. This treatment dose was chosen as it was 
showed by Solovyev et al. 2017 to yield the largest inhibitory effect on tumor growth54. BP-C1 
was given in 400 µL injections and the negative treatment control group received injects of 400 
µL PBS following the same schedule as the BP-C1 group. 
Preliminary studies using a 4T1 allograft model suggest that BP-C1 treatment does not display 
inhibitory effects on 4T1 mammary gland cancer cells in the BALB/c mouse strain (Tea Kirkegaard 
Nielsen – Unpublished data). In order to make sure that the lack of observable effect on tumor 
growth was not just due to the specific model the animal studies conducted in this project were 
chosen as a next step. In the first animal study of this project a slow-growing tumor was desired 
in order to investigate the immunoregulatory properties of BP-C1 and to better mimic the tumor 
growth rate found in humans. In order to achieve this a heterotopic CT26-Luc allograft model 
==PAGE:68==
59 
 
using the BALB/c mouse strain was chosen. Since the goal of this study was to investigate the 
effect of BP-C1 on both primary tumor growth and metastasis development the CT26 cancer cell-
line was chosen as it is a well-established immunogenic carcinoma with a high metastatic 
potential that is commonly used for testing cytotoxic and immunotherapeutic drugs60. On the 
other hand, 4T1 is considered poorly immunogenic which might explain the lack of observable 
effect of BP-C1 if it does indeed elicit part of its antineoplastic effects through the 
immunoregulatory effects discussed in Section 4.272. The CT26 cell-line has been shown to be 
compatible with the BALB/c strain when injected into the flank72. In this project it was decided to 
inject the cancer cells into the mammary tissue as this would possibly mimic the metastatic 
pattern of breast cancer more closely compared to injection into the flank. The rationale for 
injecting the colon-derived CT26 cancer cell-line in the mammary fat pad instead of the flank was 
based on the fact that a colon-derived cell-line was not expected to grow markedly different 
within these two tissues as they are both distinct from the colonic tissue of which the cells 
originate.  
As presented in Section 3.3.1 no palpable tumor growth was observed in the CT26-Luc allograft 
model. The lack of substantial growth could be explained by a lack of engraftment but this is, 
however, most likely not the case as the presence of tumor growth was measurable using 
bioluminescence imaging from day 22. If the lack of palpable growth was caused by a lack of 
engraftment then the measurable signal would be expected to have disappeared before day 22 
as the cells would be cleared before then. Another explanation could be that the injected tumor 
burden was low enough for the immune system of both the PBS and the BP-C1 treated groups to 
clear the cancer. Surprisingly, it was observed that total tumor clearance was actually significantly 
slower in the BP-C1 treatment group compared to the PBS group, something that might possible 
be explained by the potential anti-inflammatory properties of BP-C1 discussed in Section 4.2. 
Several of the immune cells discussed, e.g. neutrophils and inflammatory monocytes, are 
recruited to the site of a tumor and can in the early stages under acute inflammatory conditions 
help tumor-clearance by killing cancer cells. However, in chronic inflammatory settings these cells 
can be co-opted by the TME and shift to tumor-promoting phenotype. In this study, treatment 
was initiated a few days after tumor inoculation meaning that an established TME might not yet 
==PAGE:69==
60 
 
had been formed. The potential anti-inflammatory of BP-C1 in these early stages of inflammation 
might therefore have slowed tumor clearance by decreasing the numbers of otherwise tumor-
antagonizing neutrophils and inflammatory monocytes. As discussed previously these effects can 
be beneficial for the host under chronic inflammatory conditions which would be present if 
treatment is initiated after an established TME has been formed. However, in this case, where a 
tumor environment has yet to be formed, these effects seem to slow down the formation of an 
initial inflammatory, tumor-antagonizing immune response. As discussed in Section 4.1 an 
apoptotic effect of BP-C1 on cancer cells has previously been observed in prior studies. Since the 
in vivo studies in the current project did not show a faster decrease in tumor growth in the BP-
C1 treatment group compared to the PBS group it might indicate that this direct apoptotic effect 
on cancer cells, observed by others, is not the major factor driving the antineoplastic properties 
of BP-C1 but that it might instead be driven by immunoregulatory effects. If the current study is 
to be repeated then it should be considered whether the number of injected cancer cells should 
be increased, as there might simply have been too few for a palpable tumor to form in this 
heterotopic location before clearance by the immune system. 
The second animal study of this projects was designed to mimic a study conducted by Solovyev 
et al. where a 66 % inhibition of tumor growth was observed in an ELA allograft model using the 
Swiss-derived SHR mouse strain when treated with 125 mg/kg BP-C154. As we did not have access 
to the SHR mouse strain, the Swiss-derived CD1 strain was used instead in order to mimic the 
experimental setup of Solovyev et al. as closely as possible. Unexpectedly, no substantial tumor 
growth was observed as presented in Section 3.3.1 with a maximum tumor volume of 64 (± 28) 
mm3 at day 14 and 55 (± 18) mm3 at day 12 in the PBS and BP-C1 group, respectively, before total 
tumor clearance in both groups by day 21. In contrast, Solovyev et al. reported tumor volumes 
of approximately 400 mm3 and 200 mm3 by day 11 and of roughly 1400 mm3 and 600 mm3 by day 
23 in the PBS and BP-C1 group, respectively. This substantial difference in tumor volumes and 
the clearance of tumor, despite the same number of ELA cell being injected at the same 
anatomical location, could suggest that the ELA cancer cell-line and the CD1 mouse strain are not 
compatible leading to poor engraftment and rapid clearance by the immune system. However, it 
seems unlikely that the ELA cell-line would be incompatible with the Swiss-derived CD1 strain as 
==PAGE:70==
61 
 
the ELA cell-line has been shown to be compatible with a wide range of mouse strains including 
other Swiss and Swiss-derived strains when injected subcutaneously 54,61,81 and non-Swiss 
derived strains when injected either subcutaneously or in the peritoneal cavity82,83. Based on this 
CD1 was considered a suitable model as it should resemble strains which have been shown to be 
compatible with the ELA cancer cell-line. However, a quite important difference may be that 
several of the studies utilizing the ELA cell-line have maintained and passaged the ELA cells in 
vivo intraperitoneally in mice prior to subcutaneous injection of the ELA cells in other mice61,81,82 
and it has been suggested that the aggressiveness of ELA cells increases with the number of 
passages84. This might suggest that routine intraperitoneal inoculations might be necessary prior 
to subcutaneous inoculation in order for solid tumors to develop and this might explain the lack 
of ELA tumor growth observed in this project and should be considered if the study is to be 
repeated.  
In Section 3.3 it was shown that in neither of the two animal studies conducted during this project 
weight loss was observed in the groups treated with BP-C1 compared to the groups receiving 
PBS. Actually, a significant weight gain was observed in the BP-C1 group in Section 3.3.1. The 
group treated with cisplatin, on the other hand, displayed weight loss to a degree that required 
termination. This fits well with previous in vivo studies showing low toxicity of BP-C1 compared 
to cisplatin and carboplatin54. Clinical studies have also supported the safety profile as BP-C1 by 
showing that it does not induce biochemical or hematological toxicity56. The low non-specific 
toxicity of BP-C1 has partly been explained by better platinum release kinetics in the form of 
slower release and lower protein binding compared to other platinum-containing antitumor 
drugs such as cisplatin74. Studies on intracellular localization have also shown higher 
accumulation of BP-Cx-1 in tumor cells compared to normal fibroblasts and this could indicate 
higher specificity of BP-Cx-1 and BP-C1 towards cancer cells74. A higher specificity of BP-C1 
towards cancer cells could also help explain the reported higher apoptotic effect of BP-C1 on 
cancer cell-lines compared to normal fibroblast cell lines discussed in Section 4.1. 
There are a number of things which should be borne in mind when extrapolating data generated 
in mouse models into a human context. Firstly, when therapy that exerts its effect mostly on 
rapidly proliferating cells, such as cisplatin, is tested on cancer cell-lines with high growth rates it 
==PAGE:71==

gives a skewed image of the efficacy of the treatment in a clinical context as the tumors found in 
humans often grow at a slower pace. For testing BP-C1, which might partly exert its effect 
through immunoregulatory properties, a slow-growing tumor model would yield the most 
translatable data as this might resemble the human condition more closely and as it would allow 
time for the potential immunoregulatory effects of BP-C1 to affect the TME. Another problem 
can arise if treatment is initiated before the tumor is established. In the clinical trials displaying 
antineoplastic effects of BP-C1 treatment was given to patients with stage IV metastatic breast 
cancer meaning that treatment was commenced well after the tumor and the TME had been 
established. If treatment is started concurrently with tumor inoculation this might give false 
indications of the effects of the compound as discussed for BP-C1 above. This especially applies 
to compounds such as BP-C1 believed to exert some of their effect through immunomodulatory 
mechanisms. If the tumor is allowed to engraft and grow before initiating treatment the TME is 
going to mimic an actual established human tumor better. If using BLI to measure tumor growth 
then positive and negative allografts can be distinguished even earlier than if using physical 
measurement and the threshold for treatment initiation can therefore be set lower than if 
measured by palpation. This means it may not be necessary to inject as many cells, yielding 
experiments that can run for a longer time before the tumors grow excessively large. By letting 
the tumors grow to a predetermined size before initiating treatment it is also possible to reduce 
variability in the animal experiment as mice showing no tumor growth or growth not comparable 
to the others can be excluded from the study.

5. Conclusion  
This study set out to elucidate some of the mechanisms underlying the antineoplastic properties 
displayed by BP-C1 in both preclinical and clinical trials. This was sought to be achieved by 
investigating the effect of BP-C1 and BP-Cx-1 in three different systems.  
Firstly, an in vitro apoptotic assay was performed in order to examine the direct apoptotic 
capabilities of BP-Cx-1 and BP-C1. Secondly, cell culture systems were set up to study the effect 
of BP-Cx-1 on the differentiation of immune cells derived from the bone marrow. Lastly, in vivo 
cancer allograft mouse models were utilized with the aim of investigating the antineoplastic 
effects of BP-C1 and to examine whether BP-C1 could alter the immune composition of the tumor 
microenvironment.  
 
Neither BP-Cx-1 nor BP-C1 seemed to have direct apoptotic effects on cancer or non-cancer cell-
lines at the concentrations tested in this project. However, BP-Cx-1 did demonstrate a 
noteworthy effect on several immune cell types isolated from the bone marrow. These effects 
included significant reductions in the frequency of cells believed to exert tumor-promoting 
functions within the tumor microenvironment – e.g. neutrophils and inflammatory monocytes. 
What's more, BP-Cx-1 also showed to be able to increase the quantity of immune cell types which 
have been associated with anti-tumor properties. Together these results indicate a tumor-
antagonizing effect of BP-Cx-1 through anti-inflammatory and anti-suppressive mechanisms.  In 
order to investigate whether these effects were also observable in vivo, two animal studies were 
performed of which neither, unfortunately, showed tumor growth of the magnitude needed for 
examining the effect of BP-C1 on the tumor microenvironment. However, a protocol, which can 
be used for examining these anti-neoplastic and immunoregulatory effects, was established.  
 
Based on these experiments intriguing indications of mechanisms which could be at the basis of 
the clinical antineoplastic effects of BP-C1 were seen. Since these observations are based on in 
vitro culture systems the animal studies will have to be repeated in order to fully assess how BP-
C1 can affect the immune cells of the tumor microenvironment in an in vivo setting.

Appendix E: Gating Strategy for Bone Marrow Study 
 
Figure A-9: Gating strategy for investigation of bone marrow-derived immune cells.  
In the first step of the gating process the singlets are isolated through FSC-W vs. FSC-A and SSC-W vs. SSC-A single 
cell gates. These single cells were then plotted against a viability markers in order to isolate the live cells. The live 
single cells were then plotted in a SSC-A vs. FSC-A plot and from this a gate comprising monocytes and granulocytes 
were created based on cell granularity and size. 
This population of live single non-lymphocytes were investigated for their iNOS and Arg1 as a general indication of 
the expression of these markers in the whole population. Eosinophils were discarded from the non-lymphocyte 
population by gating out the Siglec-F positive cells. From the cleaned population neutrophils were isolated as Ly-6G+ 
CD11b+ F4/80- MHCII- cells and were further investigated for their expression of Arg1. Dead neutrophils were 
identified by the same strategy but based on the cell positive for the viability marker. Dendritic cells were isolated 
as the CD11c+ MHCII+ F4/80- cells. In order to isolate the monocytes cells with a low F4/80 expression were selected. 
From this population neutrophils were discarded based on their higher expression of Ly-6G and CD11b and the 
resulting population was divided into Ly-6C+ CD11c- inflammatory monocytes and Ly-6Clow resident monocytes.  
Macrophages was identified as F4/80+ CD11b+ Ly-6G- Ly-6C- cells and were sorted into the iNOS+ M1 population 
while the M2 population was identified based on Arg-1 expression.
