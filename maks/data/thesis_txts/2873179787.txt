Abstract
The EU bioeconomy strategy aims to address global challenges like climate change by promoting
the use of more biological feedstock for the production of bio-based products.
There is limited data of the value chains of bio-based materials but the BioMAT model and
database gives a good overview of the feedstock types used and the end products produced.
Few life cycle assessment studies have been conducted to assess the environmental impacts of
the bioeconomy and even fewer have used the novel method absolute environmental sustainability
assessment by relating the impacts to the planetary boundaries.
The goal of this study was to assess if biomass use as a carbon resource for bio-based production
in the EU can be considered absolutely sustainable relative to the planetary boundaries. The
functional unit is the annual use of biomass as a carbon resource to meet the EU demand for
bio-based products across key application sectors. The system boundaries are cradle-to-gate.
Different sharing principles was used (impact grandfathering, expenditure grandfathering, final
consumption expenditure only) and different scenarios were conducted (RER region, EU-27 region,
local crop use, feedstock type evenly distributed, handling multifunctional process by allocation at
point of substitution (APOS)). Two AESA methods was compared the LCIA-PB method and the
PB-LCIA method. In addition, the AESA of two products, polymers for plastics and man-made
fibres (bioproducts) was conducted as an example of the impacts of end products.
The results for the biomass use shows a transgression of the share of safe operating system
for 6 out of 15 impact categories for the PB-LCIA method. The transgression for the LCIA-PB
method are 10 out of 15 impact categories. The bioproducts results show a transgression of 8 out
of 15 impact categories and an addition 3 impact categories were transgressed depending on the
sharing principle. The highest relative change in % compared to the baseline are for the local crop
production, feedstock even share, and APOS scenarios.
Limitations are the use of BioMAT database that is still under development, the choosing of
sharing principle is subjective, the use of transformed ecoinvent processes, and only considered the
feedstock use and not the semi-final products that showed a higher impact.
It is recommended to update the official statistics to include bio-based chemical products and
develop the BioMAT model and database further. In addition, when choosing to use feedstock type
for the production of bio-based products it is recommended to consider using feedstocks with lower
impacts such as second, third, and fourth generation feedstocks.

Introduction
The EU (European Union)’s bioeconomy strategy aims to address global challenges like climate
change, ecosystem degradation, and resource scarcity by promoting sustainable production and
consumption practices. The EU bioeconomy encompasses all sectors and systems that rely on
biological resources, including agriculture, forestry, fisheries, aquaculture, and the production of
food, feed, bio-based products, energy, and services. In relation to circular economy, a sustainable
bioeconomy is the renewable segment exploring how to utilize biomass in an efficient way including
residues and waste (EC, 2018). Although there can also be environmental impacts and spillover
effects from activities along the bioeconomy supply chains. Thus, it is crucial to assess the supply
chain and the environmental impacts of these activities.
Most EU supply of biomass are produced within the EU but there is a high reliance of imports
of soybean and soybean meal. The main non-cereal crops are rape and sunflower and the main
cereal crops are oats, wheat, maize, and barley. In 2017, around 1 billion tonnes of dry matter
(tdm) biomass was produced in the EU where 50% was used for food and feed products, 28%
used as material in industrial processes and 22% used as bioenergy. Around 94% of the biomass
was domestically produced in the EU consisting of harvested crops, grazed biomass and collected
residues with a share of 68.8%, 12.9%, and 12.7% respectively. The uses of agricultural biomass
are animal feed and bedding, plant-based food and biofuels with a share of 57%, 13%, and 1.7%
respectively. Around 60% of harvested woody biomass are used directly or indirectly for energy.
Some knowledge gaps and lack of data remains for example 20% of all biomass use is unknown,
lost or discarded (Kowalczewska et al., 2023).
The availability of wood supply from stemwood and over bark of the EU-27 forests is 27.2 billion
m3 in 2020. This is around 89% of the forest areas of EU-27 where the harvest is unavailable for the
remaining percentage because of environmental, social, and economic restrictions (Kowalczewska
et al., 2023).
The supply of biomass will have a shortage between 40-70% compared to the demand for
bio-based materials and bioenergy projected from now to 2050. Furthermore, forest waste and
residue will only account for 10-15% of the increase in supply. The projections are conservative
and there will be major environmental consequences to meet the demand. Currently the amount
of forestry or cropland needed is unrealistic and requires the agricultural and forest landscape of
Europe to be completely remodelled (Kowalczewska et al., 2023).
There is also a need to evaluate and monitor the value chain of bio-based materials in which
1 of 34
==PAGE:8==
there are limited data on. Leeuwen et al. (2022) constructed a model to estimate the supply and
demand of the EU bio-based markets called BioMAT. It estimates the amount of feedstock types
needed to produce so called bio-based semi-final chemical products. This information can be used
to perform an environmental assessment of the impacts of biomass use by for example the LCA (life
cycle assessment) method.
The Bioeconomy Footprint is a method developed by Sinkko et al. (2023) using the LCA
methodology to evaluate and monitor the environmental impacts of EU bioeconomy sectors over
time. The Bioeconomy Footprint is comprised of a list of reference products covered by the Basket
of representative Products (BoP) where relevant sectors were defined. The sectors included were
agriculture (food/non-food), forestry, fishing and aquaculture, manufacture of food, wood and bio-
based plastics and biofuels. They found that food consumption has the highest share of total impacts
(83-85%) followed by bioenergy (9-10%) but the coverage of some sectors could be improved such
as non-food agriculture and bio-based chemicals (Sinkko et al., 2023).
Some bioeconomy LCA studies can have limitations with the way they are performed as found
by Talwar and Holden (2022). They reviewed 83 bioeconomy LCA studies from 2006 to 2021
and found that 85% of the studies did not present the goal and function of the system. The system
boundary was not defined by around 13% of the studies and the full life cycle i.e. the stages raw
material extraction, production, use and EoL (end-of-life) was only considered by 17% of the studies.
In addition, the majority of the studies were not in compliance with the ISO standards. Lastly, the
studies did not consider the bioeconomy technologies as transformers of the system but rather as a
displacement for feedstocks and processes.
Another way of estimating the impacts of an activity is to relate it to the PBs (planetary
boundaries) which are nine environmental limits that should not be exceeded for the earth system to
be in stable condition (Steffen et al., 2015). Currently, six of the nine PBs are already transgressed
and a seventh one, ocean acidification is almost transgressed (Richardson et al., 2023). This has led
to the development of implementing the PBs with LCA. Some methods matched the PBs metrics with
existing LCA impact categories which can be problematic as there is a misalignment between the
scope and impact pathways of the control variables of the PB-framework and LCA impact category
indicators. This has led to the development of the PB-LCIA method which has the impact categories
in the same metrics as the PBs. This impact assessment is called AESA (absolute environmental
sustainability assessment) to relate if an activity is sustainable in an absolute sense compared to
LCA’s which is a relative assessment (Ryberg, Owsianiak, Clavreul et al., 2018).
The overall objective of this study was to conduct an AESA of EU biomass use as a carbon
resource to evaluate whether it is considered absolutely sustainable relative to the PBs.

2
Methods
Bjørn et al. (2025) completed a guideline to perform an AESA. To fulfill the objective of the
study the following methods were used. Firstly, a literature search was conducted to find relevant
studies and data regarding biomass use as a carbon resource within the EU. Secondly, collection
of LCI (life cycle inventory) data before the assessment was done for the feedstock type and the
bioproducts considered (polymers for plastics and man-made fibres). Sharing principles were chosen
and methods to perform the AESA were chosen. Finally, scenario analyses was performed to test
the sensitivity of the methods.
2.1
Goal and scope definition
This study is a master’s thesis as the final assignment of the author’s programme. The study aims
to assess if biomass use as a carbon resource in the EU is absolute environmentally sustainable. It
was motivated by the EU’s bioeconomy strategy which aims at transitioning to a more circular and
sutainable bioeconomy by producing and consuming biological resources for materials and services
(EC, 2018). The decision context for this study was defined as accounting the environmental impacts
of biomass use which is situation C1 and in LCA it is recommended to model the LCI (life cycle
inventory) with the attributional approach, handling multifunctional processes with system expansion
when subdivision is not possible, and modelling background system with average processes (Wolf et
al., 2010). However, system expansion is not suited for AESA as the assessment becomes dependent
on the avoided emissions which makes the assessment relative instead of absolute (Ryberg et al.,
2021).
2.2
Functional unit
The functional unit is the annual use of biomass as a carbon resource to meet the EU demand for
bio-based products across key application sectors.
2.3
Literature review
2.3.1
BioMAT
A literature search was performed through the following databases: Web of Science, Scopus, Science
Direct and Google Scholar to review published articles relating to the EU bioeconomy with the
search string "biomass" AND "bio" AND "based" AND "EU".
3 of 34
==PAGE:10==
This search led to the EU H2020 BioMonitor project that have constructed the BioMAT (Bio-
based MATerials) model. This model quantifies the potential developments and value chains in the
EU bio-based markets by (i) tracking the historical market developments for bio-based chemicals and
feedstocks demand (ii) possibility to make future projection. BioMAT is comprised of two elements
(i) the BioMAT database and (ii) the BioMAT model which is a multi-regional partial equilibrium
model. The data for developing BioMAT was found through various sources such as official statistics,
literature reviews and expert knowledge. The main statistical source is the PRODCOM (production
of manufactured goods) but the information on the bio-based production, trade and use cannot
be directly extracted. Thus, the bio-based share was found from nova-Institute, interviews with
industries, market monitoring, and literature reviews. The calculation for the apparent use is the
production plus imports minus exports.
The main inspiration of BioMAT is the AGMEMOD (AGriculture MEmber state MODelling)
a database and modelling of the agri-food value chains in the EU. AGMEMOD begins with land
distribution for crops production and animal husbandry to the production and distribution of agricul-
tural products to different types of use (trade, stocks, seeds, food, feed, material and energy use).
BioMAT is designed similarly representing bio-based value chains beginning with the raw materials
of biological feedstocks to the first processing step of bioenergy and bio-based materials production,
and moving downstream the bio-based value chains towards intermediate steps to specific product
groups, primarily bio-based chemicals. For example polymers for plastics are processed from sugar
and starch via the ‘fermentation’ process: i.e. sugar and starch => bioethanol => bioethylene =>
polymers. There is an overlap of biological feedstocks covered in BioMAT (starch from corn,
potatoes and wheat, sugar and plant oils) with AGMEMOD product quantities available for energy
and material use (corn, potatoes, wheat, sugar beets and oilseeds) thus it is possible to link the
models such that BioMAT acts as an extension of AGMEMOD.

Impact categories and environmental indicators
The methods used to perform the AESA is both the LCIA-PB method and PB-LCIA version 2.03
developed by Ryberg, Owsianiak, Richardson and Hauschild (2018). The LCIA-PB method matches
LCA impact categories with the PBs. The impact categories from the EF (Environmental Footprint)

Lund, Schjerbeck and Bjørn (2025) for most of the impact categories except for Eutrophication -
marine where the carrying capacity was found from Serrano et al. (2025). The reason is because
the carrying capacity for Eutrophication - marine from Lund, Schjerbeck and Bjørn (2025) was
significantly low which gives a high impact value. Table 4 shows the carrying capacities for the 15

2.6.1
Biomass feedstocks
Representative processes from the ecoinvent 3.10 database was found for the feedstock types listed
in Table 1 by using SimaPro 10.1.0.4. The system model "Allocation, cut-off by classification" was
chosen as the boundaries between the processes in consequential LCI modeling and allocation by
system expansion will not match the sharing principles (Lund, Schjerbeck & Bjørn, 2025).
For the feedstocks that are produced locally the ecoinvent unit processes with a region code
of either {RER}, {Europe without Switzerland}, or {CH} was transformed to be in line with the
BioMAT dataset which includes data in the year 2018 from EU-28 countries that include the United
Kingdom as the study started before Brexit. It is mainly the electricity, heat, and tap water inputs
that were changed.
For the imported feedstock types the ecoinvent processes for specific countries outside of EU
were used including the {RoW} where the share of the import countries from specific feedstock
type was found from WITS (2025). For example in ecoinvent sugar, from sugarcane includes the
regions Brazil, Colombia, and RoW. From WITS (2025) the share of sugarcane import to the EU
from Brazil is 14% and Colombia 2%. These shares are distributed to a copied GLO sugar, from
sugarcane ecoinvent process including Brazil (14%), Colombia (2%), and RoW (84%). The LCI for
the other feedstock types can be seen in Supplemntary Material 1 (S1).
The ecoinvent processes for sawlogs and pulpwood are in the unit m3 thus these are transformed
into kg by finding the density of the softwoods (pine and spruce) and hardwoods (beech, oak,
and birch) used in the {Europe without Switzerland} processes. The input values with a detailed
description of the calculation steps can be seen in S1.
A literature search was performed through the following databases: Web of Science, Scopus,
Science Direct and Google Scholar to find the LCI for the missing feedstock types. For example
tapioca is a starch made from cassava thus the search string used are "life cycle assessment" AND
("cassava" OR "tapioca").
The LCI from the types not included in the ecoinvent database was found through these individual
studies: wheat starch from Suckling et al. (2025), tapioca from Usubharatana and Phungrassami
(2015), castor oil from Amouri et al. (2017), sunflower oil from Schmidt (2015), algae from Berger
et al. (2023).
The transport distances was taken from the "market for" processes if there is any for the individual
feedstock types. The transport distances for the imported feedstock types was copied from the {GLO}
market for process. For some local feedstock type in Europe the transport distances was found
from Eurostat (2025a, 2025b, 2025c) with the goods classification ’GT01 - Products of agriculture,

in Thailand was done in Google Maps and an estimated transport distance to Bangkok port was

2.6.2
Polymers for plastics and man-made fibres
For ’Polymers for plastics’ from Table 3 only ’PLA’ and ’Starch used for plastics’ will be modelled
as there is no EU bio-based production of ’PE’ and ’PET’. For man-made fibres only ’Rayon’ and
’Cellulose acetate’ will be modelled as there is no EU bio-based production of ’PTT’.
The ecoinvent process ’Polylactic acid, granulate {GLO}| polylactic acid production, granulate

2.8
Sharing principles for safe operating space
A part of performing an AESA is to allocate the SoSOS (share of the safe operating space) to
the assessed activity by using a sharing principle. An activity that does not exceed the assigned
SoSOS can be considered ’absolutely sustainable’ (Ryberg, Owsianiak, Clavreul et al., 2018).
Three different sharing principles was chosen to show the sensitivity of the PB-LCIA method using
different sharing principles. For the LCIA-PB method only the expenditure grandfathering and FCE
sharing principle will be used as the impact grandfathering relates the units to the PB-LCIA method.
The first principle is impact grandfathering where the allocated SoSOS of an activity is "inherited"
from its environmental impacts in a past reference year (Hjalsted et al., 2021). Firstly, the safe
operating space of EU (SOSEU) is found by multiplying the SOS with the ratio of EU´s population
(POPEU) to the global population (POPworld). The population of the EU for the year 2018 was
found from Eurostat (2018) and world population for the year 2018 was found from World Bank
(2025). The next step is to find the safe operating space of the aggregated biomass use (SOSbiomass)
by multiplying the SOSEU with the ratio of the total biomass impact (Impactbiomass) to the total
direct impact of EU (ImpactEU). Exiobase version 3.8.2 in the year 2018 in pxp (product by product)
tables was used for ImpactEU.
I-O (Input-output) tables can be in either ixi (industry by industry) or pxp. Industries are chosen
as defining unities in ixi tables where the advantage is the tables rely on real survey data. Although,
specific industries also produce goods or services which would have different input structures from
other industries that should be attributed to. This is done by constructing homogenous product
branches with the pxp tables (Nathani & Hellmüller, 2019). As this study looks at specific feedstock
types and thus products the pxp tables will be used.
To find ImpactEU the sum of "Carbon dioxide (CO2) Fuel combustion and cement", "Water
Consumption Blue - Total", "Phosphorous", and "Nitrogen" across all EU sectors in the F matrix
(total direct impact of industries in EU) and the F_Y matrix (total direct impact of final consumers
in EU) was found from Exiobase. The other impact categories were excluded because no similar
impact category was found in Exiobase or the units are not the same as the PB-LCIA method.
Finally, the AESR (absolute environmental sustainability ratio) is found by dividing the total bio-
mass impact (Impactbiomass) with the safe operating space of the aggregated biomass use (SOSbiomass).
The same equations are used for the bioproducts (biopolymers and fibres) AESA where instead
of ’biomass’ it would be ’bioproducts’. The equations of the steps are shown in Equation 2.1-

2.9
Scenario analysis
Scenario analyses shown in Table 6 are performed to evaluate the sensitivity of the PB-LCIA method.
A scenario analysis is a form of sensitivity analysis for changes of multiple inputs and can be used for
future developments or different possibilities of inputs (Hauschild, Rosenbaum & Olsen, 2018). The
first scenario is just the baseline without any changes. The second alternative scenario is changing
the electricity, heat, and tap water unit processes to the RER region. The third alternative scenario
is changing the values for AESA to an EU-27 context meaning excluding the United Kingdom
(UK). This is done for the EU population, total direct impacts of EU, and FCE of the EU. The
fourth alternative scenario is changing the share of feedstock types that are produced locally to
100% assuming there will be no import of that crop outside of the EU. This means that feedstock
use for sugar is assummed to fully originate from sugar beet while maize starch, soybean oil, and
sunflower oil are assummed to originate 100% from the EU. The fifth alternative scenario is to
17 of 34
==PAGE:24==
distribute the share of the feedstock types evenly from Table 1 e.g. maize, potatoes, wheat, and
tapioca have an even share of 25% of the starch impacts. The last alternative scenario is switching
the ecoinvent library from allocation, cut-off by classification - unit to APOS (allocation at point of
substitution) - unit by using the library switch function in SimaPro. The difference between these
system models is that the cut-off model considers by-products as burden-free and incentivizes the
use of recycled products, while APOS shares the burdens of the wastes to both the producers and the
users (Ecoinvent, 2024).

4
Discussion
4.1
BioMAT data
This study relied on the data from the BioMonitor project to do the AESA which gives a good
overview of the historical development of the EU’s bio-based chemical industry. But there can also
be some limitations of the data as the authors from that project have stated that the BioMAT model
is at the very early stages of development and it relied on expert knowledge to fill in some gaps in
the current statistical data used. The bio-based shares in the trade flows were assumed to be the
same as the bio-based share in production but in reality these can be different. These gaps can be
minimized by for example extending the official statistics to cover more bio-based products (Sturm
et al., 2023).
In addition, the feedstock categories in BioMAT includes a lot of varied feedstock types and
the description of what they include is vague for example for ’Lignocellulosic from agriculture’
they state that it includes straw and other by-products, flax and short rotation crops. Therefore, the
representative unit processes found in this study might not align with the feedstock categories in
BioMAT.
4.2
System boundaries
This study only considered the type of feedstock use thus the system boundary could only be a
cradle-to-gate but it could also be interesting to look at the full life cycle i.e. to do the AESA by
considering the production, use, and EoL of the final chemical products. Because of time constraint
and only the labor of one author it was not possible to consider all the final products to perform
such an assessment. Therefore, a focus and assessment on only two product groups, polymers for
plastics and man-made fibres, was performed to give a quick example of the impacts on product
level. The full life cycle would also consider the use and EoL stages. This will give the study an
added complexity as the use and EoL of these products is very different which again would be more
time consuming. Thus, the system boundaries for these bioproducts is also cradle-to-gate.
4.3
LCIA method and impact categories
Choosing either the LCIA-PB method or the PB-LCIA gives different context and interpretations as
shown in Figure 6 and Figure 7. The PB-LCIA method makes it easier for experts to communicate
the results that have worked with similar impact categories as the PBs while the LCIA-PB method
has the same impact categories as the EF 3.1 LCIA method thus making it easier for experts working
24 of 34
==PAGE:31==
with that method to communicate these results. Although, as already mentioned the traditional LCIA
method such as the EF 3.1 has different scope and impact pathways compared to the PBs. Choosing
either of these two methods depends on the goal of the study. The reason for choosing both of the
methods for this study is to compare them and see how different the results can be communicated.
4.4
Handling of multifunctional processes
This study handled multifunctional processes by using the cut-off system model from ecoinvent
which align with the guideline of performing an AESA. But the APOS system model also align
with an AESA. Therefore, one of the alternative scenario was to change the system model with
SimaPro’s library switch function. It can be seen from Table 8 that this switch shows that 11 of the
15 impact categories was significantly sensitive to this switch with higher impacts on all except for
Freshwater use - Global which has a negative impact. This is due to the fact that the cut-off system
model considers handling of by-products burden-free while the APOS system model includes the
impacts of handling the by-products. Although, the library switch function gives a warning that
some products for the switched processes does not contain an equivalent product in the replaced
library thus to make the comparison complete it would be best to create the processes from scratch
with APOS processes.
4.5
Life cycle inventory
The LCI was mostly found through ecoinvent for the feedstock types because it already provides
complete input of data. Although when there was no EU region a unit process from another region
outside EU was transformed to create the process for an EU context by mostly only changing the
electricity, heat, and tap water. This might be an issue because it is not necessarily true that the
inputs i.e. the production of the same crop will be the same from region to region.
The modelling of the share of the region for the imported feedstock types by using data from
WITS (2025) is also questionable. For example for palm oil the share of import from Malaysia is
28% so in the modelled unit process of the imported palm oil the process ’Palm oil, crude RoW|
palm oil mill operation | Cut-off, U’ is giving a share of the remaining 72% as in ecoinvent there are
only a MY, RoW and GLO palm oil processes. But the RoW process was not transformed for the
share of the other import countries from WITS (2025) such as Indonesia (25%), Papua New Guinea
(12%), and Colombia (11%). Thus, the input data from the RoW process might not be representative
for the other countries but it would be too time consuming to transform everything in the process.
For the feedstock types that was not included in ecoinvent the LCI was found from LCA studies
25 of 34
==PAGE:32==
relating to these feedstock types. It was a bit difficult to find studies with useful LCI for example for
tapioca, castor oil, and algae. Therefore, the first studies where it was possible to model the process
without issue was found. The LCI for algae relates to a cultivation of algae whereas the data in
BioMAT states that they consider algae originating from the sea so there is an inconsistency in that.
4.6
Sharing principles
The choosing of a sharing principle is essentially subjective and there is no consensus about which
principle is more right than others (Bjørn et al., 2025). The choosing of the impact grandfathering,
expenditure grandfathering, and the FCE only sharing principle in this study is because they are the
most easiest to use and the author have great knowledge of using these principles before in other
projects. There might be other principles in which it can be argued that it is better to use but more
time would be needed to review and compare different principles which is not the main goal of this
study.
4.7
Impact categories with the highest exceedence
As seen in Figure 6 the impact categories with the highest exceedence for all sharing principles are
climate change, phosphorous, and nitrogen. Thus, when considering using biomass as a transition
to a sustainable bioeconomy the focus should be to consider biomass feedstocks that have lower
impacts on these categories. Ocean acidification should also be in focus as it exceeds with the
expenditure grandfathering. The freshwater basin in dry regions are also exceeded thus if possible a
lower share of biomass produced in these regions should be considered.
Looking at the LCIA-PB results shows that a higher amount of impact categories are exceeded
(10 of the 15). This shows again the aspect of what type of method would be better to communicate
the results as the two different methods provide different results with their own interpretation.
For the bioproducts results 2 additional impact categories are exceeded compared to the baseline
with a higher transgression of the AESR. 3 additional impact categories are also exceeded depending
on the sharing principle. Thus, it indicates that the results will be different when the focus shifts
from biomass use to bioproducts production. In this study only two bioproducts were considered but
there are still 11 remaining semi-final products.
Finally, looking at the alternative scenarios show that no significant changes were found for the
RER and EU-27 scenarios. Thus, the transformation of the processes from RER to EU-28 might not
have been that necessary as it was also time consuming and the time to do the transformation could
be used elsewhere.
26 of 34
==PAGE:33==
For the local scenario the highest relative change is for the freshwater use and a significant
change in Land-system change - Tropic by between 274.5-277.8%. This can be a bit strange as in
this scenario the feedstock types produced in the EU-28 region was given a share of 100% for those
types that both have an EU region and import region. Thus, there should be more of a change in
the Land-system change - temperate impact category but the reason is because the characterization
factor for this impact category and also for the Boreal category have a specific substance for example
"Transformation, from forest, intensive, Temperate" or "Transformation, from forest, extensive,
Boreal to forest and taiga" whereas the Tropic category have the standard intensive, extensive,
and primary Transformation, from forest and an additional ’from tropical rain forest’. This means
that the input substances of land transformation should be changed to the specific region for each
feedstock types. This is also why there are no impacts for the Boreal and Temperate Land-system
change categories.
The changing of feedstock share shows very high relative changes across all impact categories
which shows that it is important to consider which feedstock types have the highest or lowest impact
across the different impact categories.
4.8
Other considerations
As seen from Figure 3 the highest feedstock types used are plant oils (32.7%), starch (29.2%), and
lignocellulosic from forestry (11.5%). The plant oils (castor oil, palm oil, soybean oil, rapeseed oil,
sunflower oil, coconut oil, palm kernel oil), starch (maize, potatoes, wheat, tapioca), and also sugar
(sugar beet, sugarcane) are so called first generation feedstock which can give a competition for
food production for example price of corn increased by around 100% between the years 2005-2007
because of the demand of it for biofuels production. These feedstocks also have higher impacts on
land use and freshwater use (Bardhan et al., 2015).
Lignocellulosic forestry, Lignocellulosic agriculture, and animal biomass can be considered
second generation feedstock these feedstocks reduce the land use required and do not compete with
food crops. Although, the waste streams of these feedstocks can be more complex and requires
additional pretreatment because they contain compounds such as lignin that reduces fermentation
efficiency which increases process time and costs (Cavelius et al., 2023).
The aquatic biomass which mainly originates from algae is a third generation feedstock. Algae
has a faster biomass formation compared to terrestrial plants and when cultivated does not require
arable land or freshwater. Additionally, efficient algae cultivation needs a direct CO2 supply which
can originate from industrial emitters or carbon capture (Cavelius et al., 2023). Although, as seen
from Table 2 algae only have a share of 0.2% of the total biomass use and only for the semi-final
27 of 34
==PAGE:34==
product ’Polymers for plastics’. Thus, the use of this resource is still underutilized.
A last feedstock type that can be considered are fourth generation feedstocks which uses genetic
engineering to increase wanted traits of organisms for biomass for bio-based chemical products
(Cavelius et al., 2023).
Another issue is that the future projections for the supply and demand of biomass from now
to 2050 listed in Kowalczewska et al. (2023) conclude that the demand will be higher than the
potential supply and a transformation of the agricultural and forest landscape of Europe needs to be
completely remodelled. Additionally only 10-15% of this increase in supply will come from forest
waste and residue so there will be some major environmental consequences to meet future demand.

5
Conclusions, Limitations and Recommendations
5.1
Conclusions
• 6 of the 15 impact categories exceeded its SoSOS for the PB-LCIA baseline scenario with
the highest being Biogeochemical flows - Regional P (11.2-112.5), Biogeochemical flows - N
(7.5-35.2), and Climate change - CO2 concentration (2.4-20.3).
• 10 of the 15 impact categories exceeded its SoSOS for the LCIA-PB method considering the
expenditure grandfathering sharing principle with the highest being Eutrophication, freshwater
(11-34.9), Ecotoxicity, freshwater (3.3-10.4), and Climate change (3.2-10).
• 8 of the 15 impact categories exceeded its SoSOS for the PB-LCIA bioproducts results
and 3 additional impact categories are exceeded depending on the sharing principle. The
highest transgression are Climate change categories - Energy balancce and CO2 concentration
(20.3-146.2), Biogeochemical flows - Regional P (7.6-112.5), and Freshwater use - Basin dry
(25.6-81.3).
• The alternative scenarios RER and EU-27 shows no significant relative changes compared to
the baseline.
• The alternative scenario local crop production shows significant relative changes for the impact
categories Freshwater use - Basin semidry, dry, and humid by -18.7 to 12.9% compared to the
baseline and Land-system change - Tropic by 288.4% compared to the baseline.
• The alternative scenario feedstock even share have a relative change between -29.5 to 70.8%
compared to the baseline across all the impact categories.
• The alternative scenario APOS have a relative change of -11.5 to 48.8% compared to the
baseline across all the impact categories except for Land-system change - Global and Biogeo-
chemical flows - Regional P
5.2
Limitations
• This study relied on the BioMAT database which is still in development so there can be some
uncertainties relating to the biomass quantities used.
• The reasoning for choosing the sharing principles used in this study is because of their ease of
use and the author’s knowledge of them but there might exist more relevant sharing principles.
29 of 34
==PAGE:36==
• This study only considered the impacts of biomass use but as shown in the bioproducts
example there might be higher impacts when the scope shifts to the semi-final chemical
products.
• The LCI mainly originates from ecoinvent where some were transformed with some found
from LCA studies. The transformed processes might not be correct in the context of a specific
region.
• This study aggregated impacts of many different feedstock types. Thus, it was not possible to
do a contribution analysis for each of them to check which processes, materials or life cycle
stages have the highest impact.
5.3
Recommendations
• Official statistics on bio-based chemical products or the BioMAT database should be developed
to limit the uncertainties.
• The AESA shows that the total impact depends on what feedstock types are used to produce bio-
based products as shown in the feedstock type even share scenario. Thus, it is recommended
to choose feedstock types that have lower impacts on the categories that are transgressed
• Another AESA where the focus is on the semi-final chemical products could be a good idea to
perform to see how higher the SoSOS are transgressed.
• A review to consider other sharing principles that might have more relevance could be
interesting to look at.
• Choosing how to handle multifunctional processes have shown to give different results i.e.
cut-off compared to APOS. Thus, choosing how to allocate the multifunctional processes
should have a focus.
• Other scenarios could be done of for example minimizing the use of first generation feedstock
to see by how much the impacts changes.
