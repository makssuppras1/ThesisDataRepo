Abstract
Ammonia is a widely produced synthetic commodity, primarily used in the fertilizer industry. However,
its conventional production process contributes to significant greenhouse gas emissions. To address this is-
sue, the concept of Power-to-Ammonia (P2A) technology has emerged, combining water electrolysis and the
Haber-Bosch process to produce green ammonia using renewable energy-based hydrogen. This study focuses
on a Power-to-Ammonia energy system that includes a wind farm, grid connection, battery energy storage
system, electrolyser, hydrogen storage, and ammonia synthesis plant.
The study utilises a two-stage stochastic mixed-integer minimum cost model to optimize both investment
and operational costs while considering hourly flows of electricity and products among system components.
Uncertainty in electricity generation from the wind farm and electricity market prices is also incorporated
using scenario-based techniques. The goal of this decision-making tool is to identify opportunities for cost
reduction and enhance the competitiveness of green ammonia compared to conventional production methods.
The tool is applied to the HØST PtX Esbjerg project, exploring various case studies. The results highlight
the significance of improving the electrical efficiency of the electrolyser and reducing the levelized cost of
electricity from the wind farm to enhance cost-effectiveness. However, despite these improvements, green
ammonia still faces challenges in achieving cost competitiveness compared to traditional ammonia produc-
tion. This underscores the need for additional cost and technology advancements or external market factors
such as subsidies to make Power-to-Ammonia systems economically viable.

1
Introduction
The year 2022 witnessed a series of unprecedented climate events, including wildfires, droughts, and extreme
temperatures, highlighting the urgent need to address climate change. To limit global warming to a safe
level, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has set a target of limiting the temperature
increase to 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels [1]. Achieving this target requires a significant
reduction in carbon emissions, and one promising solution is the adoption of Power-to-X (PtX) technolo-
gies. PtX technologies refers to a range of processes that convert renewable electrical power into different
forms of energy carriers or chemical compounds. These technologies play a crucial role in reducing carbon
emissions and transitioning to a sustainable energy system. They have the potential to produce low-carbon
fuels and chemicals, decarbonize hard-to-abate sectors, provide energy storage and flexibility, and enhance
energy security [2].
This thesis focuses on the Power-to-Ammonia (PtA) technology, which combines water electrolysis and the
Haber-Bosch process to produce green ammonia (NH3). Conventional ammonia production heavily relies on
the Haber-Bosch process and fossil fuels, resulting in significant carbon emissions. Green ammonia, on the
other hand, offers a carbon-neutral alternative by using renewable electricity for electrolysis and hydrogen
production [3]. Green ammonia has diverse applications and is primarily thought to be a carbon-neutral
solution for shipping as a maritime transport fuel as well as to be used as feed-stock for green fertilizers. It
can potentially also be considered for applications in fuel cells, long-term energy storage, fuel for industry
and peak power plants, or as an addition/mixture to conventional fuel among others.
2
Problem statement and objectives
Like many technologies at a similarly early stage of development, deploying PtA at scale brings significant
challenges in planning, investment, scheduling and operation.
Indeed, the variability of wind and solar
electricity generation poses challenges for renewable ammonia production because the Haber-Bosch process
process has traditionally been designed for steady state operation. Variability can be managed with storage
buffers, including batteries and hydrogen storage to manage short-term and long-term variability, respec-
tively, but nevertheless, most of the decisions to be made in a PtA project are subject to a significant level
of data uncertainty. In this regard, uncertainty handling is an ongoing issue for decision makers. Hence,
from operation and management perspectives, the need for accurate decisions is necessary to decide between
alternatives, calculate costs, estimate expected revenue, and mitigate possible risks safely and reliably [4].
To address these challenges, this research adopts a two-stage stochastic programming approach, which com-
bines deterministic and stochastic elements. This approach allows for decision-making under uncertainty
by considering two stages: the first stage involves decision-making before uncertainty is realized, and the
second stage involves decisions based on the actual realization of uncertain parameters. The model is set as
a two-stage stochastic mixed-integer minimum cost model that optimize both investment and operational
costs while considering hourly flows of electricity and products among system components. The sources of
uncertainties considered here are the renewable generation outputs and electricity prices from the grid.
The decision-making tool developed in this thesis aims to identify the key components and operational char-
acteristics of PtA systems that have the most significant impact on the total cost. By understanding the
cost reduction potential, the tool can guide the development of PtA technologies and make green ammonia

more competitive with conventional ammonia production. Additionally, this tool can serve as a preliminary
design tool for PtA systems in industry applications.

3
Theory
The purpose of this section is to provide a comprehensive understanding of the functionality and significance
of each component within the system by clarifying the specific roles and functions that each component
fulfills in order to contribute to the overall operation of the system.
3.1
Power-to-Ammonia energy system
The considered PtA energy system is intended to produce green ammonia for a specific yearly demand at a
specific location. It includes a wind farm, a grid connection to buy electricity at the spot market prices, a
battery energy storage system, an electrolyser, a hydrogen storage and an ammonia synthesis plant as shown
in the graph below.

Figure 1: Power-to-Ammonia energy system
The electricity produced by the wind-farm can be either directly used for producing hydrogen via the
electrolysis of water or be stored in the BESS in case of excess power. The grid and the BESS are meant
to supply electricity for the electrolysis in case of low power generation. Their role is to help balancing the
intermittency of the wind-farm power production. After the electrolysis of water, the hydrogen can either be
used as feed-stock for the production of ammonia, or be stored in the hydrogen storage. Ideally, the ammonia
synthesis process operates at steady-state operation, so the role of the hydrogen storage is to complete the
hydrogen flow coming out of the electrolyser to make it as constant as possible. Both BESS and hydrogen
storage act like buffers.
When considering this system, there are two sources of uncertainty. The first one is the wind-farm power
production, depending on the meteorological conditions, and the second one is electricity prices which are
determined by various factors in the market. These factors include supply and demand dynamics, fuel costs
or geopolitical events. In order to appropriately address these uncertainties stochastic optimization is used.

3.2
Wind farm
The wind-farm is the renewable production unit (RPU) of the system. Its role is to provide electricity for
the system. The quantity of power produced by the wind-farm depends on meteorological conditions such
as the wind-speed and the wind direction and is also reduced by various sources of losses.
Wake effect is considered the most influencing one [5].The wake effect is the trail left by each turbine. It
affects the airflow and wind patterns downstream, causing a reduction in the performance of downstream
turbines.
3.3
Grid
Usually, for large plant such as the one considered, the electricity is not bought directly from the grid at the
spot prices, because the volatility and the uncertainty on the future market prices would make the investment
in such a plant too risky. Electricity is bought via a Power Purchase Agreement (PPA). A PPA is a power
supply agreement between an investor/developer and a buyer on the purchase of a specific power production.
However, stochastic optimization techniques address the challenges posed by price volatility and uncertainty.
Therefore, in this study, spot market prices are utilized for electricity procurement instead of relying solely on
a PPA. This approach allows the grid connection to serve primarily as a backup option, providing additional
power from the grid when the wind farm’s generation is insufficient, rather than relying on it as the primary
source of electricity as in a PPA. Furthermore, by incorporating spot market prices, the system can optimize
its operation by considering fluctuations in electricity prices and strategically purchasing electricity when
prices are low.
3.4
Battery energy storage system
The battery energy storage system (BESS) allows the system to store electrical energy in the form of chem-
ical energy within rechargeable batteries for later use. Excess electricity from the wind-farm can be charged
into the BESS when it’s available and discharged when electricity is needed, helping balancing the renewable
production to meet the operational requirements of the ammonia plant. Battery storage is especially needed
for an off-grid system to power the other auxiliary systems when renewable energy is unavailable.
3.5
Electrolyser
The electrolyser uses the process of electrolysis to split water into its constituent elements, hydrogen and
oxygen, using an electric current. The chemical equation of water electrolysis is:
2H2O(l) →2H2(g) + O2(g)
(1)
For electrolysis, the output is hydrogen, oxygen and excess heat from the process. Oxygen is a by-product,
which can be used in various industries, such as paper and pulp production, glass manufacturing, water
oxygenation, fish farming, steel and metal industry, medical care industry, food, manufacturing, and many
more. The excess heat may be used for district heating if there is a suitable district heating consumer in the
area close to the electrolyser installation.
The electrolyser has two electrodes: an anode and a cathode. The anode is connected to the positive terminal
of the power source, while the cathode is connected to the negative terminal. The electrolyser contains an
7
==PAGE:9==
electrolyte, which can be an aqueous solution of an alkali metal hydroxide, such as potassium hydroxide
(KOH) or sodium hydroxide (NaOH) or also a solid polymer membrane. The electrolyte allows the flow of
electrically charged particles, ions, between the electrodes. Figure 2 illustrates an alkaline water electrolysis
system.
Figure 2: Electrolyser schematic
When an electric current is passed through the electrolyte, electrolysis occurs. The water molecules (H2O)
near the anode and cathode are decomposed into hydrogen (H2) and oxygen (O2) gases. Once the hydrogen
and oxygen gases are formed, they are typically separated to prevent them from recombining and forming
water. The separated hydrogen and oxygen gases are collected and can be further processed or stored.
For electrolysis, a high-water purity is desired, which is often provided by a desalination plant, especially for
large-scale installations that have larger demand than local freshwater sources can provide. A desalination
plant is a facility that removes salt and other impurities from seawater or brackish water, making it suitable
for water electrolysis.
Three water electrolysis technologies are already available commercially: Alkaline electrolysis cells (AEC),
polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM) and solid oxide electrolysis cells (SOEC). Those technologies are be-
ing described in the catalogue [6]. Megawatts scale systems are already being deployed for both AEC and
PEM, while SOEC electrolysis system are still in demonstration phase for large scale applications. PEM
technologies are widely regarded as the leading electrolysis technology for the future because it offers numer-
ous advantages over other electrolysis technologies. One of its key advantages is to operate at high current
densities, resulting in a high efficiency, which allows for the production of hydrogen with minimal energy
losses. Additionally, PEM electrolysers have a rapid response time, enabling quick adjustments to changes
in power input. This flexibility is particularly important when integrating intermittent renewable energy
sources such as wind, as it allows for efficient utilization of fluctuating electricity generation. Furthermore,
PEM electrolyzers have the advantage of operating at low temperatures and pressures, enhancing their safety
and reducing maintenance requirements. For those reasons, the electrolysis technology considered here is
the PEM.

3.6
Hydrogen storage
The hydrogen storage plays a crucial role in the green ammonia production because it helps to address the
intermittent nature of renewable electricity production. It ensures the continuous availability of hydrogen,
which is needed to fulfill the strict operational constraints of the ammonia synthesis loop. There are several
methods of hydrogen storage, such as high-pressure tanks where the hydrogen is compressed to pressures
ranging from 350 to 700 bar, underground storage where the hydrogen is stored underground in natural
geological formations or pipeline storage where the hydrogen is stored through a network of pipelines. High-
pressure tanks are expensive because of the need to compress hydrogen to high pressure, and underground
storage necessitate natural geological formations which can not be available. Hence the pipeline storage,
which is simplest and cheapest technology which is not tied to specific underground cavern formations, is
used.
3.7
Ammonia plant
The ammonia synthesis loop, referred to as the Haber-Bosch (HB) process follows the equation:
3H2(g) + N2(g) →2NH3(g)
(2)
The HB ammonia synthesis loop comprises a synthesis reactor, mixing units, compressors, heat exchang-
ers, and an ammonia separation unit. The feedstocks are hydrogen from the electrolysis units and nitrogen.
Pure nitrogen is required for the Haber-Bosch synthesis of ammonia. Pure nitrogen is produced by an Air
Separation Unit (ASU), which uses a cryogenic distillation process to separate ambient air into nitrogen,
oxygen, and argon.
Given the multiple constraints on operation, synthesis loops are typically optimised for steady state
operation at near full capacity with limited capability for reduced operation and the process is normally shut
down only for maintenance. The operation ranges of a green ammonia plant can be divided into [6]:
• Shut down – cold standby: Shut down/cold standby is when the plant is shut down and cooled to
ambient temperature. Cold shut down is generally only used for maintenance or other prolonged period
of no production. It can’t be used for a short stop of the plant due to no product demand or missing
feed availability because frequent pressure and temperature cycling can lead to catalyst damage and
equipment fatigue (reducing the lifetime of catalysts and equipment). Therefore, an ammonia plant
must be kept in hot standby mode in periods where it’s not not in operation.
• Hot standby mode: Hot standby mode is an operation mode where there is no production but almost
all units are kept at minimal operation condition (i.e. at minimal operation temperature and pressure)
to enable a fast ramp up in capacity. For an ammonia plant, the hot standby mode depends on the
duration, i.e. it may be still-standing for a couple of days, while circulating hot gas may be used to keep
the reactor warm for prolonged periods (weeks). Starting an ammonia loop from cold conditions can
take up to one day, while ramping up from hot conditions take usually around 2 hours. Hot standby
mode requires no feedstock.
• Operation at 30 – 100% of capacity: A conventional ammonia plant usually has an operating capacity
of 70-100%. However, as as a general rule of thumb, the minimum turn-down ratio of many transmitters
9
==PAGE:11==
and control valves are around 30%, hence these plants can often handle loads down to 30% without
major changes.
• Operation at 20 – 35% of capacity: If there is a need to reduce operations to 20%, this can usually be
achieved by additional CAPEX spending to buy equipment that can handle larger capacity ranges.
• Operation at 0 – 20% of capacity: For operation at lower capacity than 20% a significant increase in
CAPEX can be expected, as multiple valves, instruments and rotating equipment would have to be
purchased to manage the wide range of operating loads.
4
Methodology
This section is intended to outline the methodology utilized in developing the model.
4.1
Wind farm
In order to estimate the electricity production of the wind-farm the most accurately, the wake effects in taken
into account in the calculation of the electricity production of the wind-farm. The inter-distance between
wind turbines in the wind farm is closely linked to the wake effect since it is influenced by the proximity of
turbines and the flow of wind through the farm. In order to mitigate the negative effects of wake interactions
and optimize energy production, the inter-distance of the turbines is set to be superior or equal to five times
the rotor diameter as a typical rule-of-thumb.
The layout of the wind-farm is a simple grid, with the distance between the turbines being maximized subject
to the size of the site and the number of turbines while meeting the inter-distance constraint. The electricity
produced by the wind-farm is either stored in the battery energy storage system (BESS) or used to produce
hydrogen via the electrolysis of water. No transmission losses are considered.
4.2
Grid
The procurement of electricity in the system is based on spot market prices, which reflect the current market
conditions and fluctuate in real-time.
In addition to the spot market prices, a grid connection fee is included as part of the system costs. This
fee accounts for the expenses associated with maintaining the grid infrastructure and providing the grid
connection service. It is a fixed cost incurred for being connected to the grid, regardless of the amount of
electricity bought. The grid connection fee helps cover the operational and maintenance costs of the grid,
ensuring reliable access to electricity when needed.
4.3
BESS
When charging and discharging the Battery Energy Storage System (BESS), several factors are taken into
account.
These include a charge and discharge efficiency, which represents the energy losses during the
storage process. Additionally, there are limits imposed on the charging and discharging rates to ensure safe
and efficient operation. These limits are reflected in the maximal charge rate, which specifies the maximum
rate at which the BESS can be charged, and the minimal and maximal state of charge, which define the
lower and upper limits of the battery’s energy storage capacity.
10
==PAGE:12==
4.4
Electrolyser
Given the rapid response time of the Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolysis system, it has the
capability to quickly transition from no production to full production within a matter of seconds. Therefore,
there is no need to account for ramping up or down of the electrolysis process, as well as specifying minimum
or maximum load requirements.
4.5
Hydrogen pipe storage
In addition to the pipeline storage, a two-stage compressor to compress the hydrogen into the pipeline has
to be considered. The minimal pressure of the hydrogen storage is the outlet pressure of the electrolyser.
Using hydrogen from storage at below the electrolyser pressure would require another compressor to take
the hydrogen out of storage when the storage pressure is lower than the electrolyser pressure, which will
results in additional CAPEX as well as OPEX and power consumption. A maximal flow rate for filling up
the hydrogen storage in considered, as well as the power consumption of the compressor.
4.6
Ammonia synthesis loop
The ASU is modelled as an integrated part of the ammonia synthesis and its electrical consumption is
included in the electrical consumption of the ammonia plant. Indeed, there are numerous other equipment
items within the HB process that consume electricity, primarily rotating equipment such as pumps and
compressors. In order to keep the model linear, the specific electricity consumption of the ammonia plant is
taken as a constant value (per unit of ammonia produced).
The operational constraints are the main challenge for a PtA system, because the intermittency and high
fluctuations of renewable sources are not compatible with a steady state operation. However, implementing
the start-up time after a shut down or a hot standby mode would largely increase the complexity of the
model, so the assumption was made that the synthesis process is running continuously (meaning no shut
down) with a minimum load and a ramping rate.
4.7
Model formulation
The model is a two-stage mixed integer stochastic minimization cost model that optimizes both investment
and operational costs under constraints, by handling the hourly flows of electricity, products and byproducts
between all the system components, such as the wind-farm, the storage units, the electrolyser and the
ammonia plant. It results in optimal capacities for each components, hourly flows between components for
a year of operation and total investment, fixed and variable costs.
The two source of uncertainty dealt with are the power generation from the wind-farm and the electricity
market prices. Stochastic optimization is used to appropriately address these uncertainties. The uncertain
parameters are modeled via the scenarios based technique, which consist in generating a finite set of probable
realization of the uncertain set of parameters (known as scenarios).
Those scenarios are generated via
historical data.
Two-stage stochastic programs have two decision stages. The first-stage decisions are those that are taken
before the uncertainty is revealed. Also called here-and-now decisions. First stage variables are the same
for all scenarios. The second-stage decisions are those that can be taken after the uncertainty is revealed.
Also called wait-and-see or recourse decisions. Second stage variables are based on the scenario. The first
11
==PAGE:13==
stage variables of the model (scenario independent) are the choice of each components capacity. In order to
get closer to real-world decisions, the capacities of the different components are not continuous variables but
can only be chosen from a discrete set of values. It allows to choose only realistic sizes but also to account
for economies of scale. This approach allows for a more comprehensive analysis and decision-making process
within the system’s operation. The sizing of the components is thus made via binary variables αu,c ∈{0,1}
which represents the choice of the capacity. U stands for units and represent the different components in the
system mentioned above while C stands for choice and represent the different choice in the available set of
capacities.

4.8
Power generation profile
In the context of this system, each scenario represents a specific combination of wind data profile and
electricity prices profile. These profiles capture the variations in wind speed and wind direction and the
corresponding electricity prices over time. The wind data and electricity prices for each scenario are sourced
from the same year. By aligning the wind data and electricity prices from the same year, any potential
dependencies or correlations between these factors can be accurately captured in the scenarios. This allows
for a more realistic representation of the system dynamics. The wind data is then used to calculate the
power generation of the wind-farm for each capacities available in the set of choice.
4.9
Wind data
Wind data are download from the database ERA5 via an API. ERA5, or the Fifth Generation of the Euro-
pean Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) Reanalysis dataset, is a global atmospheric
reanalysis dataset. Data is available from 1940 onwards. ERA5 is produced by assimilating a vast amount of
observational data from satellites, weather stations, buoys, and other sources into a numerical weather pre-
diction model. The dataset covers a wide range of atmospheric variables, including temperature, humidity,
wind speed, precipitation, and surface pressure, among others. It has a spatial resolution of approximately
31 kilometers and is available at various temporal resolutions, such as hourly, three-hourly, and monthly.
From the ERA5 dataset, the 100m u-component of wind (u100) and the 100m v-component of wind (v100)
are extracted. These components represent the horizontal speed of air moving towards the east and north,
respectively, at a height of 100 meters above the earth’s surface. The speeds are measured in meters per
second. These wind components are extracted for a specific year and a defined area, which is characterized
by its maximum and minimum latitude and maximum and minimum longitude. The data is collected within
this geographical region to capture the wind patterns and variations specific to that area. To derive the
overall speed and direction of the horizontal 100m wind, the u-component and v-component are combined

4.10
Pywake and wake effects
To calculate the hourly electricity production of the wind-farm the python library PyWake is used. PyWake
is an open-source wind farm simulation tool used for studying the interaction between turbines within a wind
farm and its influence on the farm’s flow field and power production. It provides a set of tools and functions
to simulate and analyze the wake effects within wind farms. PyWake focuses on capturing the interaction
between individual wind turbines and the resulting wake effects, which can significantly impact the overall
power production and efficiency of wind farms. The library offers a range of wake models, including Jensen’s
model, Bastankhah-Gaussian model, and Larsen model, among others. These models are used to estimate
the wake effect and predict the power losses caused by turbine wakes.
The central component in the architecture of PyWake is the WindFarmModel. It is created by providing
a Site, a WindTurbines object, and a wake deficit model as input. The WindTurbines object is primarily
defined by parameters such as turbine diameter, hub height, nominal power, and wind speed cut-in and
cut-out. The wind speed cut-in and cut-out represent the minimum and maximum wind speeds required
for the turbine to start and stop generating electricity, respectively. The Site object requires inputs like
the geographical coordinates (in degrees) of the area’s center, the hub height, the roughness length, and
the turbulence intensity.
These parameters provide essential information about the site’s characteristics
and atmospheric conditions. The wake deficit model chosen is the Bastankhah-Gaussian. This choice is
motivated by its computational efficiency, accuracy, and suitability for large wind farm simulations.
4.11
Layout of the wind-farm
As explained in section 3.2, the layout of the wind-farm has a meaning-full impact on the wake effect and
thus on the power production. An optimized layout can increase greatly the power generation of the wind-
farm. However, performing an optimization layout on a large wind-farm requires a important computational
time and represent a whole optimization model by itself. Moreover, this level of accuracy in the power
generation calculation is not necessary. Hence, a simple grid layout is considered for the wind-farm. The
number of turbines is calculated from the turbine nominal power and the total capacity of the wind-farm.
The distance between turbines is being maximized according to the number of turbines and the available
area, while respecting the constraint 15 explained in 3.2.

A way to measure the electricity production efficiency of a wind-farm is to calculate its capacity factor.
The capacity factor of a wind farm is a measure of its actual power output relative to its maximum potential
power output over a given period of time. The capacity factor is calculated by dividing the total energy Etot
produced by the wind farm during a specific period T by the maximum possible energy that could have been
produced if the wind farm had operated at its full capacity for the same period:
CapacityFactor = (
Etot
Capacity · T ) · 100%

For a a specified area and a specific type of turbine, when the capacity of a wind farm is expanded, it
requires reducing the distance between turbines. As a consequence, this reduction in inter-distance intensifies
the wake effect, which leads to a decrease in the overall power output of the downstream turbines, resulting
in a reduction of the wind farm’s capacity factor, as shown in figure 7.

calculate the power generation of the same wind-farm in the same conditions (location, years for wind-speed
data), the capacity factor is 0.66, which represent a increase of 16% compared with when wake-effects are
included. Hence, understanding and accounting for this effect is crucial because it highlights a trade-off
between the size and efficiency of a wind farm. While increasing the capacity by adding more turbines may
seem beneficial, it comes at the cost of decreased efficiency due to the amplified wake effect. That is why
the optimal balance between maximizing capacity and maintaining a satisfactory capacity factor to ensure
the overall effectiveness and profitability of the wind farm needs to be considered.

5.2.6
Hydrogen storage
The decision has been made to implement a pipeline storage system operating at a pressure of 70 bars. This
pressure level strikes a balance between achieving efficient high-pressure storage and avoiding the need for
thicker pipes, which would increase costs.
To regulate the hydrogen storage process, a maximum ramp-up rate of 10% is considered, to limit the
needs for large compressors and thus limit the costs of the storage system. On the other hand, no specific
constraints are imposed on the ramp-down rate.
In the case of a pressurized PEM system, where hydrogen is discharged at a pressure of 28 bars, it is necessary
to maintain a minimum storage pressure of 28 bars. This requirement ensures that hydrogen can be efficiently
extracted from storage without the need for an additional compressor. Using hydrogen from storage at a
pressure lower than the electrolyser pressure would otherwise necessitate an additional compressor, resulting
in additional investment costs, operational costs, and increased power consumption.
Given the assumption of a storage pressure of 70 bars, the minimum utilization of stored hydrogen is then
40%. This storage system including the compressors have been modelled in Aspen+. Aspen Plus is a process
simulation software developed by Aspen Technology. It is widely used in engineering and chemical industries
for modeling, simulating, and optimizing various chemical processes.
Via this simulation, the electrical
consumption of the whole storage system has been evaluated to be 0,476 MWh per tons of hydrogen stored
in.

6.2.1
Expected value of wait-and-see approach
The wait-and-see approach involves solving each scenario independently, assuming perfect information and
no uncertainty. In this approach, the planner can determine the optimal design solution based on the specific
wind data and electricity prices for each scenario. However, in reality, it is impossible to have perfect forecasts
for uncertain parameters, making it impossible to make the perfect sizing decision.
The wait-and-see solution is obtained by solving the deterministic program for each scenario separately
and then calculating the weighted average of the solutions based on their respective probabilities.
The
difference between the wait-and-see solution and the stochastic solution represents the value of perfect
information, highlighting the potential benefits of having accurate and complete information about uncertain
parameters.
It is important to recognize that the wait-and-see approach is a simplified representation and does not
account for the inherent uncertainty and variability present in real-world scenarios. Nonetheless, by com-
paring the wait-and-see solution to the stochastic solution, valuable insights can be gained regarding the
potential advantages of having perfect information and the value it can bring to decision-making processes.

6.2.2
Expected value solution
The expected value approach consists in creating a scenario which represent the expected value (EV) of the
uncertain parameters, that is to say the probability-weighted average of every scenarios. In this case, since
all scenarios are equally probable, it means computing the average hourly electricity production of the wind-

farm and the average electricity spot market prices across the four scenarios. This expected value scenario
is then used in the deterministic model which gives the capacities of each component. The expected value
solution is obtained by solving the deterministic program for each scenario separately with the capacities of
the components being fixed to the solution of the expected value scenario, and then calculating the weighted
average of the solutions based on their respective probabilities. The difference between the stochastic solution
and the expected value solution represents the comparative benefits of employing stochastic programming
versus relying only on expected values.

7
Discussion
This section goes deeper into the discussions surrounding the results obtained in the previous sections,
providing a more detailed analysis of the limitations and potential improvements of the model.
7.1
Levelized Cost of Ammonia
In the analysis of the base case, the decision to use high-cost assumptions was intended to ensure a conser-
vative estimation of the LCoA. While this approach provides a robust perspective and accounts for potential
cost uncertainties and under-estimation, it is important to recognize that it leads to a LCoA in the upper
range of expected values.
However, the fact that even with the technology improvement considered, green ammonia is still not compet-
itive with traditional ammonia shows the challenges in making PtA cost-competitive without either others
significant cost/technology improvements or without external market factors such as subsidies.
Further routes that could be explored for cost competitiveness could be electrolyser cost reduction, increased
carbon taxes on grey ammonia or EU subsidies of green ammonia.
7.2
Electricity Prices
The electricity price assumption in this study put deliberately the electricity price at a relatively high level,
in order to enhanced the system’s resilience and grid independence. The objective was to push the model
towards a higher level of self-sufficiency, which succeeded by seeing that in most cases the grid connection
capacity was reduced. While this approach succeeded in moving the system towards grid independence, it
also contributed to the high LCoA obtained. To gain a more comprehensive understanding of the system’s
performance under different electricity price scenarios, further analysis could be conducted with alternative
assumptions, such as returning to pre-war price ranges or considering a more stabilized price range. This
analysis would also provide valuable insights into the sensitivity of the system’s economics to electricity price
variations.
7.3
Scenarios
A significant constraint encountered in this stochastic optimization study is the limited number of scenarios
that could be used. The computational resources available for the model were based on an AMD Ryzen 7
PRO 5850U with Radeon Graphics CPU, featuring 8 physical cores, 16 logical processors and 16GB of RAM
available. All cases study were solved using Gurobi under Python. The solving time for the model ranged
from 4 to 14 hours. Unfortunately, when attempting to include more than 4 scenarios, the Gurobi solver
reached its memory capacity. Consequently, the study was conducted with only 4 scenarios, which may have
influenced the quality of the results.
To address this limitation, one possible improvement is to implement a decomposition method, which can
enhance computational performance and enable the consideration of a larger number of scenarios. By utilizing
decomposition techniques, the computational burden can be distributed more efficiently. Another solution
could be implementing scenarios reduction techniques.
Scenario reduction techniques are computational
methods used to reduce the number of scenarios in a stochastic optimization model while still capturing the
important characteristics of the problem. By reducing the number of scenarios, these techniques enable the
model to be solved more efficiently without sacrificing the overall quality of the solution.
50
==PAGE:52==
7.4
Potential Improvements
Several potential improvements can be considered to enhance the model’s accuracy and broaden its applica-
bility.
An improvement that could be considered is the integration of other renewable energy sources, such as
solar. Indeed, some PtA projects actually rely on both solar and wind because it helps to mitigate the
fluctuations in power generation by leveraging the complementary nature of these sources. Due to seasonal
effects, when there is less wind, there is often more sunlight available, and vice versa. Hence, combining
these sources would results in a more stable and reliable electricity supply for the system operation. The
addition of a stable renewable power source such as geothermal or hydroelectric would also allow higher util-
isation and may be able to replace the need for a grid connection while minimising hydrogen and/or BESS
storage requirements. Others storage technologies such as pumped hydro-storage could also be considered.
Implementing those possibilities in the model would broaden its applicability, particularly in the location
of the project (location with more solar energy than wind energy for example) but also allow for a more
optimized choice of units (choice of renewable sources, choice of storage units).
In certain Power-to-Ammonia (P2A) projects, particularly in Denmark, there is a consideration of uti-
lizing the heat generated from the electrolyser by selling it to district heating systems. This presents an
additional revenue stream for the overall system and has the potential to significantly impact the LCoA.
However, it also introduces new operational constraints on the electrolyser, as it needs to meet the heat
demand required for district heating that would impact both the sizing and operation of the electrolyser.
Furthermore, an important improvement for the model would be the inclusion of the cold start-up time of
the ammonia plant, which is a crucial aspect of real-world ammonia plant operations. Incorporating this
parameter into the model would provide a more accurate representation of the system’s behavior and allow
the model to better capture the dynamics and constraints associated with the ammonia plant’s operations,
leading to more reliable and relevant results.

8
Conclusion
In conclusion, this thesis successfully achieved its main goals of developing a two-stage stochastic mixed-
integer minimum cost model and incorporating uncertainty in electricity generation and market prices. This
model was designed with the aim of closely approximating real-world conditions. An extensive and in-depth
literature review was conducted to gather cost data. Various measures were implemented to enhance its
accuracy, such as incorporating the wake effect in the calculation of electricity production from the wind
farm and utilizing binary variables to account for economies of scale in cost estimation. The developed
model enables accurate calculations of the levelized cost of ammonia and aligns closely with the evaluations
conducted by the IRENA.
The use of stochastic optimization instead of a simpler deterministic model has demonstrated its relevance
in this study. The expected value of the levelized cost of ammonia obtained from a single scenario is higher
than the LCoA calculated using the stochastic model. Additionally, it was found that design results from one
scenario cannot be applied to other scenarios, highlighting the significance of considering multiple scenarios
and employing stochastic optimization methods.
The application of this tool aimed to identify opportunities for cost reduction and enhance the competitive-
ness of green ammonia compared to conventional production methods. Through several case studies, it was
found that the most cost-effective improvements include increasing the electrical efficiency of the electrolyser
and reducing the levelized cost of electricity from the wind farm. These findings emphasize the importance
of continuous efforts to enhance the efficiency and cost-effectiveness of green ammonia production.
Despite these improvements, the levelized cost of green ammonia remains higher than that of traditional
ammonia, indicating the need to explore additional routes for cost reduction. Potential areas for further
development include reducing the cost of electrolyser technology and considering the implementation of sub-
sidies or other external market factors.
Looking ahead, several enhancements can be considered for the model. Firstly, the inclusion of the cold
start-up time of the ammonia plant would provide a more realistic representation of its operation. Addition-
ally, incorporating other sources of renewable energy, such as solar power, and exploring storage options like
pumped-hydro storage could further optimize the system’s performance and reduce costs.
