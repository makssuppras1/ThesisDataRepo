Abstract 
Anaerobic digestion is a sustainable source of bioenergy that can provide heat and power by 
treating organic waste. Some of the most commonly treated substrates are manure and lipid rich 
waste. These substrates contain high amounts of ammonia and long chain fatty acids (LCFA) 
respectively, which can be proven inhibitory for the process. In the present thesis, the interactions 
of the co-inhibition with ammonia and LCFA were examined in batch and continuous mode. It has 
been hypothesized that the combination of these inhibitors would cause a synergetic inhibition 
effect that is higher than the sum of the individual inhibitions. Therefore, several sets of batch 
reactors were examined, in which either ammonia, or LCFA or their combination was used. The 
results of some batches showed evidence of the above mentioned synergy. Moreover, three CSTR 
reactors were inhibited with increasingly levels of ammonia (RNH3), LCFA (RLCFA) or both (RCMB) 
for a period of 80 days. Even though RCMB showed the highest inhibition, the claims for synergy 
were not validated.

1. Introduction 
Climate change is indubitably the most crucial environmental issue that the planet is facing today. 
The rise in global temperature can cause serious effects on ecosystems, food chains and ultimately 
in human life (Appels, et al., 2011). It is clear that CO2 emissions have to be reduced and that 
renewable resources can play a crucial role in that direction.  
Some of the most promising renewable energy sources are organic refuse and biomass, since they 
are abundant (Mao, et al., 2015) and can support a continuous power generation, by simultaneously 
treating waste (Appels, et al., 2011; Rajagopal, et al., 2013). Waste materials such as manure and 
food residues are of major importance because they do not require agricultural land usage, which 
can otherwise be used for food generation. The different technologies that are available for electricity 
and heat production from biomass and waste can be categorized into thermochemical, 
physicochemical and biochemical conversion processes (Appels, et al., 2011).  
1.1 Anaerobic digestion 
Anaerobic digestion, classified as a biochemical process, is a technology that is gaining a lot of 
attention in recent years and has been projected to have a compound annual growth rate of 9.4% 
until 2018 (Hanft, 2014). The application of anaerobic digestion for waste treatment is continually 
emerging and the digested organic waste had an annual increase of 25% until 2016 (Buffiere, et al., 
2008; Appels, et al., 2011). The wide applicability and the benefits of anaerobic digestion make it 
an attractive alternative for waste treatment and energy production. 
Anaerobic digestion occurs in aqueous environments, with biomass sources that have high water 
content, without the necessity of pretreatments. On the contrary, technologies like combustion, 
==PAGE:12==
2 
pyrolysis and gasification cannot provide a net positive energy balance if the water concentration of 
biomass is higher than 40%. Anaerobic digestion is feasible in industrial and small scales, having 
various applications in rural areas and developing countries. For instance, households in India use 
small scale anaerobic digesters to provide energy for cooking. 
Biogas, the gas product of anaerobic digestion, consists approximately of 55-65% CH4, 35-45% CO2 
and trace amounts of gases such as N2, H2 and H2S. It can be upgraded to natural gas purity and used 
in the natural gas grid, or valorized in a combined heat and power (CHP) installation. This is an 
environmentally friendly process due to the low emissions of pollutants, such as volatile organic 
compounds (VOCs), which are oxidized during combustion (Appels, et al., 2011). As far as the 
liquid by-product of anaerobic digestion (digestate) is concerned, it is rich in nutrients, such as 
nitrogen and can be used as a bio-fertilizer and soil enhancer (Tambone, et al., 2009). 
1.2 Steps of anaerobic digestion 
Anaerobic digestion is a combination of biochemical processes, where different reactions are 
mediated by relevant microorganisms (bacteria and archaea). Biodegradable organic materials are 
ultimately converted into CH4 and CO2 under anaerobic conditions (lack of oxygen) (Divya, et al., 
2014; Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). The four steps of this “structured process” are hydrolysis, 
fermentation or acidogenesis, acetogenesis and methanogenesis (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). 
They are generalized in Figure 1.  
1.2.1 Hydrolysis 
The biological reaction of hydrolysis is used to describe certain reactions that lead to the 
solubilization of particulate compounds (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011) and to decomposition of 
organic polymers to oligomers (Gavala, et al., 2003). Hydrolysis is generally carried out by 
==PAGE:13==
3 
proteases, cellulases, xylanases and lipases (extracellular enzymes), which are excreted by different 
groups of fermentative bacteria (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011; Gavala, et al., 2003). The main 
depolymerization reactions that occur during this stage are the cleavage of proteins’ peptide bonds, 
lipids’ ester bonds and carbohydrates’ glycosidic bonds (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). 
 
Figure 1. Stepwise degradation of organics to CH4 and CO2 (Source: Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011) 
 
Proteins are hydrolyzed into polypeptides and amino acids, a process generally faster than that of 
the carbohydrates’ breakdown (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). Carbohydrates are the most oxidized 
substrate in anaerobic digestion and depending on their type, they break down into different 
oligomers or monomers. In the case of sugars, starch or pectin, simple saccharides are being released, 
while for lignocellulosic materials various branched or linear carbohydrate molecules can be 
==PAGE:14==
4 
produced. Lignocellulose (a combination of lignin, cellulose and hemicellulose) is difficult to 
degrade by microorganisms and their enzymes (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011), since the plant cell 
wall uses lignin as its defense mechanism. Finally, lipids break down to long chain fatty acids 
(LCFA) and glycerol. LCFA are carboxylic acids consisted of a long aliphatic chain with 13 to 21 
carbons (Cirne, et al., 2007). 
1.2.2 Fermentation/Acidogenesis 
Sugars and amino acids produced during the step of hydrolysis are further transformed into volatile 
fatty acids (VFA), hydrogen, alcohols and carbon dioxide in a process called fermentation 
(Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). Two separate groups of acidogens (bacteria) degrade the 
oligosaccharides and amino acids to VFA, H2 and CO2 (Batstone, et al., 2002). It is important to 
notice that ammonium is released during this step from amino acids, and that LCFA are not 
converted into VFA during this step (Angelidaki, et al., 2011). 
Fermentation is a fast process with many intermediates and end-products. The major VFA of this 
process are butyrate and acetate, and when a system is operating under stable conditions most of the 
substrate is converted to acetate and hydrogen directly (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). Nevertheless, 
when a system is inhibited, less oxidized products such as propionate, butyrate and ethanol appear 
in higher concentrations. Aromatic organic acids and valerate can also be produced, mostly from 
amino acids (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). 
1.2.3 Acetogenesis 
The products of the fermentation step, alongside LCFA, are oxidized to acetate, which is the main 
precursor for methane production (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011; Karakashev, et al., 2006). The 
electrons produced from this oxidation, form H2. The production of H2 is unfavorable in terms of 
==PAGE:15==
5 
energy, since it increases the free energy of LCFA and VFA oxidation (Angelidaki & Batstone, 
2011). In order to make the oxidations favorable energetically (negative free energy), very low 
concentrations of H2 should be present in the system. This is where hydrogenotrophic methanogens 
come into the frame, by converting CO2 and H2 to CH4, assisting to maintain low H2 concentrations. 
However, there is a very narrow range of pH, pressure and compound concentrations where this 
reaction is possible (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). 
 
Figure 2. Acidogenesis and hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis scheme (Source: Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011)  
 
During acetogenesis fatty acid molecules are degraded to VFA in the cytosol of the bacteria. This 
process is called beta-oxidation because the beta carbon undergoes oxidation to a carbonyl state 
(Houten & Wander, 2010). VFA such as propionate, lactate, valerate and butyrate as well as ethanol, 
are transformed into acetate and H2 by acetogenic bacteria (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). Certain 
homoacetogenic bacteria transform the H2 back to acetate, but it is not yet fully understood how 
these bacteria couple this pathway to adenosine triphosphate (ATP) synthesis (Angelidaki, et al., 
2011). 
1.2.4 Methanogenesis 
Methanogenic microorganisms belong to the Archaea domain and are very diverse with respect to 
cell morphology (Angelidaki, et al., 2011). Ultimately, methane is generally formed by two 
pathways; aceticlastic methanogenesis, which converts the acetate into CH4 and CO2, and 
==PAGE:16==
6 
hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis which converts H2 and CO2 into CH4 and H2O (Angelidaki & 
Batstone, 2011)  
Two mechanisms for methane formation from acetate have been discovered: direct acetate 
transformation to CH4 and CO2 which is carried out by aceticlastic methanogens (i.e. 
Methanosarcinaceae spp. or Methanosaetaceae spp.); and a two-step reaction where acetate is first 
oxidized to H2 and CO2 and subsequently converted to CH4. The last reaction is carried out by 
acetate-oxidizing bacteria (like Clostridium spp.., etc.) in a syntrophic association with 
hydrogenotrophic archaea (like Methanomicrobiales spp. or Methanobacteriales spp.) (Hattori, et 
al., 2000; Karakashev, et al., 2006). The acetate pathway is responsible (either direct methanogenesis 
from acetate, or from acetate oxidation to H2) for two thirds of methane production and is one of the 
most sensitive processes of anaerobic digestion (Angelidaki, et al., 2011). The remaining one third 
comes from other precursors like methanol and hydrogen/CO2. 
Some of the most important factors that influence the aceticlastic activity are temperature, organic 
acid and ammonia concentrations. At thermophilic conditions (~55 ºC), it has been estimated that 
acetate oxidation is favored by low acetate concentrations, while aceticlastic methanogenesis is 
favored at higher acetate concentrations (Karakashev, et al., 2006; Zinder & Koch, 1984). In general 
acetate oxidation is increasingly dominant at low VFA concentrations and increased temperatures. 
When ammonium and VFA are present, the syntrophic mechanism becomes the main pathway for 
acetate degradation (Schnurer, et al., 1999).

1.3 Factors affecting anaerobic digestion 
Anaerobic digestion is a delicate biochemical process and its efficiency is affected by several factors, 
such as the nature of the substrate, temperature, pH, nutrients concentration, C/N ratio, organic 
loading rate (OLR), hydraulic retention time (HRT) and reactor design. 
1.3.1 Substrate 
The substrates used for anaerobic digestion are municipal solid waste, agricultural by-products, 
seaweed, food waste, industrial by-products, glycerol, sewage waste, manure and slaughterhouse 
waste. The biogas yield of each substrate depends on its volatile solids (VS) content and on the 
nature of the existing VS. For instance 1 g VS of lipids can provide up to 1014 NmL CH4, 1 g VS 
of protein (assumed as (C4H6.1O1.2 N)x) up to 525 NmL CH4, while carbohydrates (assumed as 
C6H12O6) can yield up to 375 NmL CH4 g-1 VS (Miron, et al., 2000). 
Manure is the most commonly used substrate for anaerobic digestion. Its proper management 
diminishes odor problems, the release of pathogens, the contamination of surface and ground water 
and the attraction of rodents and insects (Sakar, et al., 2009). Thus, by treating manure via anaerobic 
digestion, environmental and health hazards are reduced and energy can be used for local needs 
(Nasir, et al., 2012). 
Cattle manure is mostly composed of water, where small concentrations of VFA are found. Its dry 
matter is approximately 12 to 16% of the total weight and crude fibers are the solid compound with 
the highest concentration. Those fibers have varying percentages of cellulose, hemicellulose and 
lignin depending on the animal and its diet. Proteins and ash are approximately 28 to 40% of the dry 
matter, while small amounts of lipids are also present (2-5%). Considerable amounts of metals and 
nutrients (N, P, K), which are essential for the microbial growth, exist (Amon, et al., 2007). N, P and 
==PAGE:18==
8 
K are present in the form of organic compounds and inorganic salts. Due to its composition, manure 
can provide a very strong pH buffer capacity (Georgacakis, et al., 1982). 
The methane yield of manure depends on many parameters such as the type of manure (poultry, 
cattle, pig, etc.), the animals’ diet and the reactor operating parameters (Nasir, et al., 2012). Several 
studies on cattle manure showed a wide range of methane yields. Namely, Omar et al (2009), Ahring 
et al (2001) and Harakishan and Sung (2003) observed methane yields of 184, 202 and 210 NmL 
CH4 per gram of VS, respectively; while Amon et al (2007) and Dubrovskis et al (2009) found yields 
of 166 and 168 NmL CH4 per gram of VS. The used systems were continuous reactors operating in 
thermophilic conditions. The CH4 content ranged from 56% to 65% in every case. 
Slaughterhouse organic residues and wastes can be used as a source of protein for animal feed. Due 
to legal restrictions, the high cost of treatment and the risk of transmittable diseases, slaughterhouse 
waste is increasingly treated via anaerobic digestion (Salminen & Rintala, 2002). The digestion of 
this substrate can be proven very challenging, considering the high amounts of proteins and lipids 
(Tritt & Schuchardt, 1992). The energy value of lipids and protein renders them an ideal substrate, 
especially on co-digestion concepts (Alves, et al., 2009). 
Depending on the animal and the body part that is discarded, the waste composition varies 
significantly (Tritt & Schuchardt, 1992). Organic nitrogen can be as high as 30 g/kg in the case of 
blood for example, while protein can be 70% of tissue’s dry matter. Lipids can be found in 
concentrations as much as 15% of the dry matter in the case of the slaughtering by-products in 
German slaughterhouses (Tritt & Schuchardt, 1992). As discussed in section 1.2, proteins break 
down to amino-acids and then to VFA, thus increasing nitrogen and ammonia concentrations, while 
lipids break down to LCFA, Both ammonia, VFA and LCFA are known inhibitors of the anaerobic 
digestion process.

1.3.2 Temperature 
Anaerobic digestion can take place under psychrophilic (<25 °C), mesophilic (25–40 °C), 
thermophilic (45-60 °C) and hyperthermophilic (>60 ºC) conditions (El-Mashad, et al., 2004). 
However, the two most commonly used temperature regimes are thermophilic (with an optimum at 
55 °C) and mesophilic (with an optimum at 37 °C). Thermophilic digestion holds a rate advantage 
over mesophilic and has a higher organic loading capacity, consequently exhibiting higher CH4 
production (Mao, et al., 2015). However, thermophilic digestion is more prone to inhibition caused 
by acidification and has decreased stability, higher energy input and poorer methanogenesis than 
mesophilic. Nevertheless, even though mesophilic digestion has higher stability and richer microbial 
communities, it sometimes presents low methane production due to poor biodegradability (Bowen, 
et al., 2014). 
As far as temperature variations are concerned, microorganisms are very sensitive to sudden changes 
and the methane and hydrogen production is affected. By decreasing the digesters temperature 
several phenomena can be observed. Namely, lower VFA production rate, reduced substrate 
consumption rate (Bowen, et al., 2014) and slower microorganism growth (Mao, et al., 2015). 
Increasing temperatures can result in increased pH, faster hydrolysis rate of organic matter but lower 
methane potential due to the death of microorganisms (Wang, et al., 2014).  
1.3.3 pH 
The optimal pH range for anaerobic digestion has been reported to be in the range of 6.6 to 8.2 (Mao, 
et al., 2015), although studies exist where anaerobic digestion performs adequately in higher pH 
values. In lower pH, around 6, the hydrolytic enzyme efficiency is at its peak, resulting to high VFA 
concentration and low VS content (Jiang, et al., 2013).  
==PAGE:20==
10 
The range of the pH is of great importance in anaerobic digestion because the growth rate of 
microorganisms depends on it. The relative abundance of microbes can increase more than twice 
when the pH is fixed to the optimum value (Mao, et al., 2015). Furthermore, pH is essential when 
coming to control free ammonia concentration (FA), which is toxic to microbes. VFA concentration 
is significantly affected by pH and vice versa (Fang & Liu, 2002). 
The importance of pH in anaerobic digestion can be highlighted by the fact that acidogenic and 
methanogenic microorganisms prefer to grow on certain pH levels. Methanogenesis is optimum in 
pH values between 6.6 and 8.2, but is inhibited at pH levels below 6.6; while acidogenesis has an 
optimum pH range between 5.5 and 6.5 (Mao, et al., 2015). In a study by Dinamarca et al. (2003), 
total soluble solids (TSS) and volatile soluble solids (VSS) were degraded in a high extent at pH 
levels of 7 and 8, without having a significant difference between these two values. 
1.3.4 Nutrients concentration 
It is known that less biomass is synthesized during anaerobic digestion compared to aerobic 
treatments. Thus, the nitrogen quantities required are low, but necessary for biomass synthesis 
(Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). Other nutrients such as calcium, magnesium, sodium and potassium 
can stimulate the fermentation step, but can be proven inhibitory in high concentrations. Digestion 
can be improved if the above-mentioned nutrients are in concentrations between 0.01 M and 0.005 
M (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). Other micronutrients, namely the heavy metals nickel and cobalt, 
are needed for the growth of anaerobic organisms, but can be inhibitory for fermentative bacteria 
and methanogenic archaea in high amounts (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). Iron promotes the 
degradation of organic matter (Mao, et al., 2015) and increases the precipitation of sulfides and 
phosphates, which would otherwise precipitate other trace metals (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011).

1.3.5 Carbon to nitrogen (C/N) ratio 
Anaerobic digestion systems are sensitive to the C/N ratio as it reflects the levels of the most 
abundant nutrients. A high C/N ratio can induce a state of low protein solubilization rate, low total 
ammonium nitrogen (TAN) and low FA concentration. Thus, the cell biomass cannot be maintained 
(due to insufficient nitrogen), resulting to reduced microbial populations and consequently to low 
biogas production (Mao, et al., 2015). On the contrary, excessively low C/N ratios increase the 
chances for ammonia inhibition, which is toxic for methanogenesis, causing poor utilization of 
organic sources (Mao, et al., 2015). The optimal C/N ratio has been reported to be between 20 and 
30-35, with the most commonly used ratio being 25. C/N ratios below 15 lead to significant ammonia 
inhibition, and biogas production can be approximately three times less, compared to a C/N ratio of 
25 (Wang, et al., 2014; Wu, et al., 2010). 
1.3.6 Reactor design 
1.3.6.1 Organic loading rate (OLR) 
OLR provides insight on the amount of organic matter (VS or COD) that is fed in a digester daily, 
under continuous feeding. As OLR is increased, biogas production can be increased to an extent, 
while the biogas yield per unit of biomass is rarely improved. Adding large volumes of new organic 
matter in a system may change the digester’s environment and inhibit the whole process (Mao, et 
al., 2015), causing organic overloading. High OLR leads to faster hydrolysis and acidogenesis 
compared to the methanogenic microbial activity, increasing the VFA production, which acidifies 
and inhibits the digester. As pH decreases even hydrolysis can be limited leading to an irreversible 
inhibition. The optimum OLR differs depending on the substrate, inoculum, reactor’s design, 
recirculation, pH and temperature (Mao, et al., 2015).

where V is the reactor volume and Q is the feed’s flow rate per day. 
A sufficient retention time is required to achieve an adequate degradation of organic matter. It is 
related to the microbial population growth and wash-out rate and is usually dependent on the OLR, 
temperature and type of substrate (Mao, et al., 2015). In general, 12 to 25 days are required for the 
sufficient waste treatment in thermophilic anaerobic digestion. Decreasing the HRT can result to 
VFA accumulation, biomass and microbes wash-out, while increasing the HRT can lead to 
insufficient utilization of the bioreactor (Mao, et al., 2015). Studies have shown that methane 
productivity is greatly reduced in HRTs below 10 days, in the case of algal biomass (Kwietniewska 
& Tys, 2014), while the digestion of food waste cannot be sustained at HRTs below 8 days (Kim, et 
al., 2006). Thus, it can be suggested that the maximal methane yield in continuous reactors can be 
achieved with a long HRT and a low OLR. Nonetheless, novel reactor systems can operate efficiently 
under low HRT (16 hours) and with high OLR (19 kg COD/m3/d) (Luo, et al., 2016). 
1.3.7 Reactor types 
In recent years, considerable attention is being given for the development of novel reactors for the 
anaerobic treatment of wastes (Rajeshwari, et al., 2010). Some of these systems are continuous 
stirred tank reactors (CSTR), batch and fed-batch systems, fixed bed reactors, fluidized bed reactors, 
upflow anaerobic sludge blanket (UASB) reactors and expanded granular sludge bed reactors. Focus 
will be given in the batch and CSTR system, since they were the ones used in the current thesis.

1.3.7.1 Batch reactor 
Batch reactors are some of the oldest systems used for the anaerobic treatment of waste. This reactor 
design is considered obsolete for industrial purposes, but it is frequently used in research 
(Angelidaki, et al., 2009), due to its advantages in experimentation and its simplicity and practicality. 
It can provide insight on the uncharted areas of anaerobic digestion and aid the determination of 
methane potential, inhibition levels, pretreatment efficiency and bio-augmentation strategies. 
A typical batch reactor is composed of an airtight vial. The vial usually contains inoculum, organic 
substrate and water. Before its incubation, the batch reactor is flashed with nitrogen and/or carbon 
dioxide gas to ensure anaerobic conditions. The incubation occurs at a constant temperature. The 
biomethane potential protocol of Angelidaki (2009) is based on batch reactors. Due to the lack of 
external disturbances (changes in temperature, constant feeding, and microbial wash-out) the 
methane yield in a batch reactor will more likely be higher than that of a CSTR reactor and inhibition 
levels can be underestimated (Girault, et al., 2012; Li, et al., 2014). 
1.3.7.2 CSTR reactor 
CSTR reactors are the most commonly used continuously fed reactor systems in industry and 
research. A typical CSTR reactor consists of a tank where anaerobic digestion takes place, under 
continuous or semi-continuous mixing, an influent and an effluent tank, an exit for the biogas and in 
certain cases a recirculation extension (Figure 3).  
A CSTR system can provide uniformity of system parameters, such as mixing, substrate 
concentration and temperature (Usack, et al., 2012). The optimum reactor configuration depends on 
the substrate, inhibitors’ concentration, moisture content and many other nontechnical factors 
==PAGE:24==
14 
(Usack, et al., 2012). As discussed above a CSTR reactor can hardly provide as high methane yields 
as a batch system.

1.4 Inhibitors 
Anaerobic digestion is a delicate process, vulnerable to inhibition by accumulating chemicals that 
can affect one or several microorganism groups. Some of those inhibitors are ammonia and 
ammonium ion (NH3 and NH4+), LCFA, VFA, sulfide, heavy metals, light metal ions (Na+, Ca2+, 
K+, Al3+, etc.), furfurals, chlorophenols and halogenated aliphatics. In the following chapter 
NH3/NH4+, LCFA and VFA are thoroughly explained as they are of great interest for anaerobic 
digestion. 
1.4.1 Ammonia and Ammonium ion 
Ammonia is of major importance for the performance and stability of anaerobic digestion, especially 
on N-rich organic feedstocks. It can be produced from the digestion of proteins, nucleic acids and 
urea (Gonzalez-Fernandez & Garcia-Encina, 2009). Low concentrations of ammonia are necessary 
for bacterial growth, but increased concentrations may lead to severe problems (i.e. low process 
==PAGE:25==
15 
stability and decrease of microbial activity (Zhang, et al., 2011)). Unionized free ammonia nitrogen 
(NH3) and ammonium ion (NH4+) are two main forms of inorganic ammonia nitrogen in the 
anaerobic digestion process (Rajagopal, et al., 2013). 
Early research by Wiegant and Zeeman (1986), claimed that acetate utilizing methanogens (like 
Methanosarcina spp.) are more resistant to ammonia inhibition than hydrogenotrophic methanogens. 
It was suggested that aceticlastic methanogens are more protected from ammonia due to their large 
spherical cells, with a higher volume-to-surface ratio than the smaller hydrogenotrophic 
microorganisms. Consequently, the diffusion of FAN into the aceticlastic cell should be less than 
into the hydrogenotrophic, leading to lower energy demands for NH3 removal from the cell (Wiegant 
& Zeeman, 1986). However, his hypothesis was disproved. More recent papers showed that 
aceticlastic methanogens are more susceptible to FAN than hydrogenotrophic microbes (Calli, et al., 
2005).  
Concerning tolerance of acetoclastic methanogens to ammonia, Methanosarcinaceae spp. seems to 
be the dominant aceticlastic at high ammonia concentrations, unlike Methanosaetaceae spp., which 
is sensitive to high FAN concentrations  (Karakashev, et al., 2005). Thus, it can be concluded that 
the resistance of microorganisms to ammonia inhibition is not related to their order (both microbes 
belong to the Methanosarcinales order), but their physiology. Nonetheless, an increase of ammonia 
concentration is often related to an increase of VFA (acetate, propionate). As discussed in chapter 
1.2.3, acetotrophic methanogenesis occurs in a very tight window, since elevated hydrogen or 
propionate amounts can increase the free energy of the reaction. Thus, the presence of ammonia can 
inhibit both methanogenic pathways either directly or indirectly. 
Other evidence showed that ammonia can affect the microbial community in another way, apart from 
the ammonia diffusion in bacterial cells that causes nutrient and energy deficiency. Gallert et al., 
==PAGE:26==
16 
(1998) demonstrated that certain enzymatic reactions in the interior and exterior of the cell are 
inhibited directly, showing lower rates of protein deamination. 
There is a wide range of inhibiting TAN concentrations (1500-7000 mg L-1). This can be attributed 
to the differences in the nature of the substrates, temperature, pH, acclimation of micro flora and 
inocula (Chen, et al., 2008). It has been reported that TAN concentrations of 2500 mg NH4+ - N L- 1 
could result in partial inhibition of the anaerobic digestion process, while concentrations of 3300 mg 
NH4+ - N L-1 could completely cease methanogenesis (Hobson & Shaw, 1976). However, if a process 
is adapted to high ammonia levels, the ammonia nitrogen tolerance is increased to concentrations 
above 3000 mg NH4+ - N L-1 (Angelidaki & Ahring, 1993), or even higher (Sung & Liu, 2003). 
Whereas, Lauterbrock et al. (2012) observed inhibition in TAN concentrations exceeding 6000 mg 
NH4+ - N L-1. 
It should be noted that inhibition by ammonia should not always be related to TAN levels, but mostly 
to the FAN concentration, which depends on TAN, pH and temperature (Fernandes, et al., 2012). 
There is a broad variation in literature about the FAN concentrations that can be proven inhibitory 
for a bioreactor. This is due to the differences in substrate, inocula, temperature, pH and acclimation 
periods (Rajagopal, et al., 2013). Concerning the digestion of cattle manure under thermophilic 
conditions there are several published results in literature. Angelidaki and Ahring (1994) noticed 
that FAN concentrations above 700 mg L-1 inhibited the reactor, resulting to high VFA 
concentrations and low methane production. A higher tolerance level was observed by Borja et al. 
(1996) where inhibition was observed at 995 mg FAN L-1. However VFA accumulated to such extent 
(>5000mg L-1) that pH dropped to 7.6 from 7.9, resulting to a reduced FAN level of 685 mg L-1. 
Tolerance to FAN concentrations up to 1200 mg L-1 has also been reported, at a pH of 7.6 (Nielsen 
& Angelidaki, 2008). 
==PAGE:27==
17 
1.4.2 Long chain fatty acids (LCFA) 
Lipid rich waste is mainly produced from the food processing industry, such as dairy food industry, 
slaughterhouses and edible oil processing industry (Cirne, et al., 2007). Lipids are an attractive 
substrate for anaerobic digestion due to their high-energy content. In several studies increased 
methane yields have been observed from lipids, especially in cases of co-digestion with manure 
(Ahring, 2003; Cirne, et al., 2007). The inhibitory effects of lipids can be attributed to LCFA, which 
are produced by lipids during hydrolysis (Angelidak & Ahring, 1992).  
LCFA can affect the lipid-water surface, thus inhibiting lipases. Lipases are the enzymes that 
hydrolyze lipids and need an interface in order to act. Thus, by disrupting the liquefaction of lipids, 
washout of biomass can occur (lipids have a low solubilization in water and tend to stay at the surface 
of the reactor) (Rollon, 1999). However, LCFA are most commonly reported to have an acute toxic 
effect on the microbial activity of aceticlastic and hydrogenotrophic methanogens (Angelidak & 
Ahring, 1992; Rinzema, et al., 1994; Koster & Cramer, 1986). Pereira et al, (2005) observed that 
high amounts of LCFA can be mineralized and they claimed that the inhibition was related to 
physicochemical transport limitations, rather than to microbial metabolic functions. 
Concerning the amounts where LCFA can be proven inhibitory to the anaerobic digestion process, 
a wide range can be found in literature. The factors that affect the inhibition levels are the type of 
LCFA (palmitic, oleic, stearic acids), the co-digestion substrate and ratio, pH and temperature, as 
well as the reactor configuration. Small amounts of LCFA can reduce the methane production rate 
(Ma, et al., 2015) and complete inhibition can be observed in values as high as 1.5 g oleic L-1 
(Angelidak & Ahring, 1992). 
==PAGE:28==
18 
Studies on the microbial composition of LCFA-degrading bioreactors can provide important 
information about the communities that are involved in the process. The families of Clostridiaceae 
spp. and Syntrophomonadaceae spp. appear to be essential for LCFA degradation. Microbial cultures 
on oleate and palmate revealed different microbial composition (Sousa, et al., 2008). This indicates 
the dependence of the microbial communities to the saturation degree of LCFA.  
1.4.3 Volatile fatty acids (VFA) 
VFA is an intermediate of anaerobic digestion that can affect the stability of the process at high 
concentrations. Several studies have observed inhibition of methanogenesis during increased VFA 
concentrations (Van den Heuvel & Beeftink, 1988; Fukuzaki, et al., 1990; Hill, 1989). If 
methanogenesis is inhibited, the removal of hydrogen and VFA is reduced, leading to organic acids 
accumulation, reduction of manure’s buffering capacity and low pH values that can inhibit 
acidogenesis and hydrolysis (Siegert & Banks, 2005). As already seen, VFA concentrations are an 
indicator of the robustness of anaerobic digestion and sudden increases can be associated with any 
kind of inhibition. 
1.4.4 Synergistic effects of inhibitors 
Nowadays, the scientific interest has been shifted towards synergistic inhibition effects in different 
combinations of inhibitors. Synergy, as defined by Oxford Dictionary is: 
“The interaction or cooperation of two or more organizations, substances, or other agents to 
produce a combined effect greater than the sum of their separate effects” 
The first studies that revealed such interactions with ammonia and other inhibitors were from Wang 
et al. (2016). Manure reactors operating under elevated ammonia levels, exhibited higher levels of 
inhibition when LCFA were added to the reactors. A study from Lu et al. (2013), showed similar 
==PAGE:29==
19 
results, where the combined effects of ammonium and organic acids were investigated. Nevertheless, 
the exact natures of these interactions (ammonia, LCFA and VFA), the inhibition mechanisms and 
the inhibitory levels that trigger the synergistic effects have not been deciphered yet. 
 1.5 Aim  
Even though the inhibition of anaerobic digestion is a topic that has received a lot of attention, there 
are still uncharted territories concerning combinations of inhibitors. The aim of the current thesis 
was to investigate the significance of the ammonia and LCFA synergistic inhibition effect in 
thermophilic conditions. One of the objectives was to quantify the inhibitory concentrations of 
ammonia, LCFA and their combination in batch systems. The main objective however, was to assess 
the relative methane production of manure-based CSTR reactors that operated under the same 
conditions but with elevated concentrations of inhibitors. Three CSTR reactors were used; one was 
inhibited with ammonia, another with LCFA and the third with the combination of the two.

content of the headspace was measured twice per week until the methane production was ceased. 
All tests were done in triplicate.

2.7.2 Inhibitory concentration (IC) 
The inhibitory concentrations (IC) were calculated with the GraphPad Prism 5 software. The IC

the CSTR reactors. This period was chosen, in order to have a more realistic approach to the methane 
yield, since the LCFA addition can affect the methane production rate instead of the final methane 
yield.

2.7.4 Relative methane production  
The methane production of each CSTR reactor during the 1st period was considered as the maximum 
uninhibited methane yield of manure for each respective reactor. Thus, the maximum expected 
methane production yields of RLCFA and RCMB were calculated by summing the yield of manure 
(during period 1) with the theoretical methane yield for lipids (1014 mL CH4 g-1 VS) (Lesteur, et al., 
2010). Ultimately, the relative methane production was defined as the percentage of the actual 
production of each reactor, divided by the maximum expected. 
Concerning the batch experiments; the maximum expected methane production was calculated as 
the sum of the methane production of the batch reactors with baseline LCFA and NH4+-N 
concentrations and the theoretical methane production for every amount of LCFA (1014 mL CH4 g-
1 VS) (Lesteur, et al., 2010). Therefore, the relative methane production of each batch was derived 
by dividing the actual methane production by the corresponding maximum theoretical production.

3.1 Preliminary information 
3.1.1 BMP of manure 
The ultimate methane potential of manure was investigated according to Angelidaki’s (2009) 
protocol. Results showed that the methane yield for cattle manure had with a value between 288 ± 22 
NmL CH4 g-1 VS and 301 ± 23 NmL CH4 g-1 VS (Figure 5). There was no statistical difference 
between the batches with 1.35 g VS L-1 manure and 2.70 g VS L-1, while the control batches (avicel) 
had a methane yield of 419 ± 39 NmL CH4 g-1 VS. This value however, is slightly higher than the 
theoretical value of carbohydrates, indicating an overestimation of the manure’s ultimate biomethane 
potential.

Literature results for the BMP of cattle manure indicate a high heterogeneity. This can be explained 
by the dietary habits and the different breeds of cattle (Nasir, et al., 2012). Similar yields as in the

The relative methane production was calculated in order to facilitate the comparisons between the 
two inhibitors and their combinations. The maximum methane production of the reactors with 
baseline concentrations of NH3 and LCFA was 60.3 NmL CH4 g-1 VS (VS from manure and 
inoculum), after 15 days of incubation and 78.4 NmL CH4 g-1 VS after 42 days. These values were 
considered as the maximum for the batches without any added LCFA. In the batches with added 
LCFA the maximum production was calculated as the sum of the theoretical yield of lipids 
(1014 NmL CH4 g-1 VS) (Miron, et al., 2000) and manure’s maximum production. Thus, the 
maximum methane production differed between batches with different LCFA concentrations.

3.2.2 Inhibitory concentrations calculations 
Inhibitory concentrations (IC) indicate the effectiveness of a substance to inhibit a biological (or 
biochemical) function. It is a quantitative measure that estimates how much inhibition would be 
caused by a certain concentration of an inhibitor. The IC methodology is mostly used in 
pharmacological studies and clinical trials (Volpe, et al., 2014); however it can be a useful tool in 
order to semi-quantify the expected inhibitions of anaerobic digestion. It is important to mention that 
the following values might not be represented in every batch experiment of anaerobic digestion; as 
they are merely values derived by a study performed with a certain inoculum and under specific 
experimental conditions.

3.3 CSTR reactors 
3.3.1 CSTR reactors performance 
During period 1 (P-I), the yield of all reactors was between 149 and 153 NmL CH4 g-1 VS and it was 
considered as the maximum yield in terms of VS coming from manure, for the respective reactors. 
Nonetheless, these values were significantly lower compared to the CH4 yield acquired from the 
BMP, probably due to FAN values above 500 mg L-1, a concentration that is characterized as 
inhibitory by Angelidaki and Ahring (1994). 
In P-II RLCFA and RNH3 were spiked with their respective IC5 inhibitors and RCMB with both. These 
values derived from the batch toxicity tests. In the first three days of P-II a sharp decline in the 
reactors’ performance was observed (Figure 15). RLCFA and RCMB presented the highest inhibition, 
but after ten days their relative methane production increased to almost 100%. However, it was 
temporary and the relative production decreased again. An inhibited steady state was achieved in the 
last ten days of P-II, with a relative methane production of 84% ± 6% and 83% ± 6% for RLCFA and 
RCMB respectively. The inhibited steady state is defined as a stable but suboptimal state of continuous 
reactor operation (Fotidis, et al., 2014). A similar trend was also observed for RNH3, and its relative 
==PAGE:52==
42 
production in the last 10 days of P-II was equal 82 ± 5%. It is worth mentioning that the inhibition 
was considerably higher compared to the expected, according to the batch experiments, and that 
there were no statistical differences between the relative methane production of the three reactors. It 
appears as if the methanogenic consortia were equally affected regardless of the inhibitor. The 
reduced methane production of RLCFA and RCMB could also be attributed to LCFA washout (Hwu, et 
al., 1997).

In the last examined period, RNH3 and RLCFA were spiked with 4.54 g NH4+-N L-1 and 
1.13 g LCFA L- 1, respectively, while RCMB was spiked with both. Those concentrations 
corresponded to the calculated IC20 values. It can be seen that in the first days of P-IV, RLCFA and 
RCMB struggled to convert the spiked LCFA to biogas. More specifically, the methane production of 
RCMB collapsed to values between 4 and 20%, while RLCFA followed a more robust trend with relative 
methane production between 24 and 70%. Since both reactors operated in the same LCFA levels, 
this difference is attributed to the higher TAN concentrations of RCMB. Free ammonia could have 
inhibited the microbes responsible for the β-oxidation of LCFA, leading to LCFA accumulation and 
consequently to inhibition of methanogenesis. However, RCMB recovered after ten days and produced 
a considerable part of the LCFA methane potential in four days, leading to relative productions above 
100% with values reaching up to 185% (Figure 15). In the last days of P-IV RCMB stabilized to 
69 ± 6%. RLCFA was restored, compared to the first days of P-IV, and its relative production was 
equal to 86 ± 4%.  
In this point it is important to mention that several studies in acetate or propionate, as the substrates, 
indicated that LCFA can induce high levels of inhibitions in smaller concentrations than 1.13 g 
LCFA L-1 (Angelidak & Ahring, 1992). This could be due to the adsorption of LCFA on the surface 
of manure particles, lowering the adsorption of LCFA in the cell membrane of methanogens. 
Consequently, the inhibition is reduced and methane production can be stimulated (Astals, et al., 
2014; Palatsi, et al., 2009; Kim, et al., 2004). Moreover Angelidaki et al. (1990), observed LCFA 
induced inhibition in batch experiments of cattle manure digestion only after adding more than 2 g 
L-1 of glycerol-triolate (GTO). Finally RNH3 presented approximately the same inhibition as in P-III 
(73 ± 3%). This is an indicator of the importance of acclimation of the reactor, and to the role of 
FAN in the inhibition of the biomethanation process (Fernandes, et al., 2012).

Instead of having greater inhibition than the other reactors as hypothesized, RCMB exhibited the same 
relative methane production as RNH3 and RLCFA in P-II. As the experiment progressed and the 
concentrations of the inhibitors increased, RNH3 and RLCFA demonstrated a significantly better 
resistance to ammonia and LCFA toxicity respectively, than RCMB. It seems that the combination of 
the inhibitors stalled RCMB more than in P-II. However, the initial hypothesis that RCMB would exhibit 
higher inhibition than the sum of the two separate inhibitions of RNH3 and RLCFA was rejected. In 
literature, there are indications that substances might cause synergistic inhibition effects, like 
VFA/NH3 (Lu, et al., 2013) and Mg2+/Na+ (Chen, et al., 2008). Previous studies hypothesized

caused by LCFA and ammonia, but in the present thesis the only period that synergy was indicated

VFA, the type of LCFA, the OLR and LCFA to organic loading ratio merit further investigation.

3.3.2 VFA accumulation and pH - FAN fluctuations 
In general, VFA concentration is a precursor for inhibition in anaerobic digestion and can affect the 
pH of the reactors and vice versa (Fang & Liu, 2002). It can be seen from Figure 17 that the VFA 
concentrations of all reactors were below 200 mg L-1 at P-I. The addition of LCFA in RCMB and 
RLCFA in P-II is correlated with a twofold increase of VFA, in the first days of this period. However, 
RLCFA recovered smoothly and its VFA ranged in approximately 200 mg L-1. The same occurred to 
RCMB with the exception of the 30th day (Figure 17). RNH3 on the other hand, followed a relatively 
stable trend throughout all periods, with a slightly increasing trend alongside increased ammonia 
concentrations. On the beginning of P-III a similar trend with P-II was observed for RLCFA and RCMB, 
only this time it was approximately 300% higher than the VFA concentrations of P-I (Figure 17). 
The VFA accumulation decreased and plateaued during the reactors’ steady states. It is worth 
mentioning that RCMB accumulated twice the VFA compared to RLCFA.  
Finally during P-IV, RLCFA demonstrated the same trend after the LCFA-spike; however RCMB did 
not. In this case, VFA increased in a stepwise manner, until reaching the highest observed 
concentration of around 1200 mg VFA L-1 after eight days. The VFA pattern of RCMB revealed that 
the β-oxidation of LCFA was inhibited by the increased free ammonia concentrations, as was 
methanogenesis in the first days of P-IV. Moreover, the increase of VFA was accompanied by 
decreasing values of pH (Figure 17 and 18). When pH was reduced from 7.7 to 7.35, so was the 
FAN concentration (Figure 18), leading to easier degradation of LCFA to VFA, and to increased 
methanogenesis rates due to the lower presence of free ammonia. The increased relative production 
in the middle of P-IV for RCMB can be explained by the transformation of the accumulated VFA to 
biogas. Nonetheless when pH was increased RCMB presented the same inhibition as in the previous 
period, probably due to the similar FAN levels (Figure 18).

4. Conclusions 
The results of the present thesis did not fully support the initial hypothesis. In the case of the batch 
toxicity assay, there was evidence of a potential synergistic effect, especially on the ultimate methane 
production of four out of fifteen sets of reactors. However, only two sets of batches presented higher 
inhibition than the sum of the potential inhibitors in 15 days of incubation, indicating a higher effect 
in the ultimate biomethanation potential, rather than in the methane production rate. The combination 
of ammonia and LCFA in the CSTR reactor increased the toxic effect compared to the individual 
effects of the inhibitors, but not to an extent to be characterized as synergistic co-inhibition effect. 
The only indication of synergy was observed in the beginning of the last period, where the relative 
methane production of RCMB was greatly inhibited. Nonetheless, as VFA increased and pH dropped, 
the FAN levels were reduced and the reactor was able to recover. In order to completely disprove 
the initial hypothesis further inhibition of the reactors should be applied. 
The knowledge received from these experiments can provide a better understanding of the mode of 
action of the co-inhibition by ammonia and LCFA. It could be potentially exploited by the biogas 
industry, in order to achieve high yields from lipid rich substrates in elevated ammonia 
concentrations. The free ammonia levels can be partially reduced by LCFA degradation to VFA, 
without necessarily alleviating the inhibition. Moreover, the net methane production can be 
increased even in elevated ammonia concentrations, due to the high energy content of lipids and to 
the potential pH reduction.
