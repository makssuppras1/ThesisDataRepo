Abstract
The effective numerical representation of amino acids is a critical prerequisite for applying
machine learning to peptide-level biological tasks. Conventional encodings such as one-hot
vectors or substitution matrices provide residue identity but lack explicit chemical struc-
ture, and they are not easily extended to post-translationally modified residues. This thesis
explores a representation based on two-dimensional chemical depictions of amino acids, ar-
ranged into peptide mosaics and compressed into latent vectors by a convolutional autoen-
coder.
To assess the utility of these image-based features, we apply them to the prediction of MHC
class I peptide binding, a biologically meaningful benchmark for which large curated datasets
exist. Performance is compared to one-hot encodings and a BLAST sequence-similarity base-
line under nested cross-validation across alleles. The results show that image-derived features
support competitive classification, particularly in the low false-positive regime, but do not
yet outperform one-hot encodings on global discrimination metrics. Performance was also
strongly shaped by class imbalance and limited coverage of phosphorylated peptides.
These findings demonstrate that chemically explicit 2D representations are a feasible alterna-
tive to hand-crafted encodings, though current formulations do not surpass simpler baselines.
The work highlights both the potential and the limitations of image-based embeddings, and
suggests future refinements such as end-to-end training, allele-conditioned feature fusion,
and graph-based encodings to better capture binding-relevant variation.

Introduction
A fundamental challenge in applying deep learning to proteins is how to represent amino acids
numerically, as neural networks require numeric input. This necessitates encoding schemes
that is able to distinguish between different residues while preserving biologically relevant
conservation patterns[1].
One-hot encoding, substitution matrices such as BLOSUM [2], and physiochemical property
based encodings like VHSE8 [3] are common approaches. While effective in certain contexts,
these traditional methods have notable limitations. One-hot encoding treats amino acids as
completely independent categories, providing no information about their biochemical prop-
erties, structural tendencies, or evolutionary relationships between residues.[4, 5]. BLOSUM
and VHSE8 incorportate prior biological knowlede, but rely on manually curated, fixed
feature sets that cannot adapt to specific predition tasks [1]. Moreover, all these schemes
generally lack the flexibility to represent post-translationally modified residues - such as
phosphorylated or glycosylated amino acids, which can critically influence peptide structure
and function [6].
The objective of this study is to design and evaluate a peptide encoding framework that
effectively captures sequence properties associated with phosphorylation, with the potential
to extend to other PTMs. To achieve this, we develop a deep learning approach based on
image representations of amino acids. In our framework, each amino acid is depicted as a
small 2D image of its molecular structure. We then train a convolutional autoencoder to
learn a compressed, unsupervised representation of these peptide images. The learned repre-
sentations are subsequently fed into a supervised model to predict whether a peptide binds
to MHC class I allele. MHC class I peptide binding is chosen as a biologically meaningful
and computationally challenging benchmark, as this problem is central to T-cell immune
response and vaccine design, and it presents a rigorous test of new sequence representations.
Notably, MHC I binding preferences depend on specific anchor residues at certain positions
in the peptide, yet tolerate high diversity at other positions. This makes peptide–MHC bind-
ing an excellent case to evaluate learned representations, the encoding must capture both

position-specific features and global similarities to succeed [7, 8].

Biological Background
3.1
The Immune System
The immune system protects the body from harmful pathogens such as viruses, bacteria
and parasites. It is typically divided into two interconnected branches: the innate immune
system, which provides a rapid but non-specific defense. and the adaptive immune system,
which delivers a slower but highly specific response tailored to individual pathogens.
The innate immune system acts as the first line of defense. It relies on physical barriers such
as skin, and on specialized cells and molecules that recognize general features common to
many microbes. Although it responds quickly, the innate immune system does not retain
memory of past encounter [9].
In contrast, the adaptive immune system is capable of recognizing specific molecular signature
of pathogens and generating a highly targeted response. It also retains immunological mem-
ory, which allows for faster and stronger responses upon re-exposure to the same pathogen.
This system replies on lymphocytes, including T cells and B cells, which recognize short
fragments of proteins - known as peptides - from pathogens [9].
These peptides must be presented on the surface of cells by specialized proteins know as
Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) molecules. MHC-mediated presentation enables
T cells to detect whether a cell is healthy, infected or abnormal. Since the recoognition
of peptide-MHC complexes is centraal to the activiation of adaptive immune responses,
understanding this mechanism is fundamental for both immonology and the development of
predictive models in computational biology [10].

is to bind short peptide fragments derived from intracellular or extracellular proteins and
present them on the cell surface for surveillance by T cells. this process enables the immune

• MHC class I molecules are expressed on nearly all nucleated cells and present peptides

ended groove with defined anchor residues at specific positions. MHC class I peptides

• MHC class II molecules, on the other hand, are primarily expressed on professional

of the MHC class I binding groove - particular its fixed length range and well-defined anchor
positions make it both biologically important and computationally tractable. These features
provide a consistent framework for evaluating alternative amino acid presentations, as even
subtle sequence differences can influence peptide-MHC binding.
3.3
Post Translational Modifications
Beyond primary amino acids sequences, Post-Translational Modifications (PTMs) play an
important role in expanding the functional diveristyu of proteins. These covalent modifica-
tion, which include phosphorylation, glycosylation, acetylation, ubiquitination and others,
are able to alter protein stability, localization, activity and interaction [13]. In the context
presentation, PTMs can give rise to modified peptides that are distinct from their canonical
counterparts.
The presence of a PTM can have mutiple consequences: it may alter the binding affinity
to the MHC molecule, modify the peptide’s conformation or affect T-Cell Receptor (TCR)
recognition. In some cases, PTMs can give the rise to neoantigens - peptide signatures not
previously encountered by the immune system, which may elicit strong immune repsonses
[14]. This is particular relevant in cancer, where abnormal phosphorylation patterns can lead

to the presentation of tumor-specific phosphopeptides that can become targets for cytotoxic
T cells [15].
Among PTMs, phosphorylation is one of the most prevalent and functionally significant.
It is involved in key regulatory pathways and is frequently dysregulated in disease [16].
Phosphorylated peptides are not only biologically meaningful but also pose computational
challenges due to their rarity, strutural differences, and the limitations of traditional encod-
ing schemes that ignore such modifications [17]. This thesis explores how a deep learning
approach leveraging 2D depictions of amino acids can enable more informative and flexible
peptide encoding schemes, with the potential to improve prediction of modified ligands such
as phosphorylated peptides.

Machine Learning Background
4.1
Methods for peptide embedding
In order to apply machine learning models to peptide data, each amino acid sequence must
be converted into a numerical format that models can interpret. Several encoding schemes
have been developed for this purpose, each with different assumptions and limitations.
One of the simplest and most widely used methods is one-hot encoding, in which each amino
acid is represented as a binary vector of fixed length, typically with 20 dimensions, one per
standard amino acid. This approach is straightforward and preserves the identity of each
residue, but it imposes no notion of similarity between amino acids, meaning all are treated
as equally distant in feature space.

A more informative alternative is to use substitution matrices, such as BLOSUM (Blocks
Substitution Matrix) [18]. These matrices are derived from alignments of evolutionarily re-

BLOSUM-based encodings thus provide insights into residue similarity grounded in biolog-
ical context. However, because they are derived from aggregated sequence data, they may
not generalize well to non-canonical residues or post-translationally modified peptides.

Another method, VHSE8 (Vectors of Hydrophobic, Steric, and Electronic properties), repre-

Machine Learning Methods
The machine learning approach in this thesis is formulated as a supervised classification
problem. In supvervised learing, a model is provided with labeled examples (X,y), where X
denotes the input features and y the target label, The model is a parameterized function fθ
that produces a prediction:

4.3
Convolutional Neural Networks
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are a class of deep learning architectures partic-

• Early layers capture low-level features such as edges or residue-level patterns.

• Deeper layers combine these into high-level, abstract representations of the input.
In many applications, including structural bioinformatics, CNNs form the front-end feature

maps suitable for classification, regression or other downstream tasks.

4.5
Regularization
In machine learning, it is important to develop strategies to avoid overly complex models that
may perform well on training data but poorly on unseen data. Without proper safeguards,
models risk overfitting - learning patterns that are specific to the training data, including
noise or artifacts. This results in poor performance on new inputs.
Several regularization strategies exist to combat overfitting. A commonly used technique is
dropout, which improves robustness by randomly deactivating a fraction of the neurons in
a given layer during each training iteration [23]. By doing so, the network is discouraged
from relying too heavily on specific pathways and instead learns redundant and distributed
representations. In practice, this means that each mini-batch effectively trains a different
sub-network, and the full model behaves like an ensemble of these sub-networks at inference
time.
To evaluate generalization ability reliably, especially when tuning hyperparameters, the use
of a separate, held-out test set is ideal. However, when data is limited, this may reduce the
amount available for training or result in test sets that do not adequately reflect the full
data distribution. Nested cross-validation offers a solution that maximizes data usage while
still yielding an unbiased estimate of performance.

hyperparameter search), and finally evaluated on the untouched outer test fold. This process
is repeated across all combinations of outer and inner folds. For example, in a 5-fold nested
cross-validation, each of the 5 outer folds serves once as the test set, while the remaining 4
folds are rotated internally, resulting in multiple independent models and predictions. Final
performance metrics are averaged across outer test folds, ensuring that no information from
the test data leaks into model selection or training.

4.6
Performance measures
There are many different ways to measure a model’s performance. A binary classifier can be
evaluated using several complementary metrics, each capturing different aspects of prediction
quality. In this project, we primarily rely on AUC, Confusion Matrix, AUC@0.1, and MCC,
with support from the confusion matrix to interpret model behavior.
• Area Under the Curve (AUC): The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC)
curve plots the True Positive Rate (TPR) (sensitivity) against the False Positive Rate
(FPR) (1-specificity) as the decision threshold that determines the cutoff at which
predicted probabilities are classified as positive or negative is varied. The AUC sum-
marize this curve as a single number ranging from 0 to 1, with 0.5 corresponding to
a random guess and 1.0 to perfect prediction. AUC is threshold-independent and is
useful for evaluating overall ranking performance of the mode’s prediction scores. It is
particularly informative in imbalanced scnarios because it considers how well positives
can be separated from negatives across all thresholds.
• Partial AUC at 0.1 FPR (AUC@0.1): In many immunological prediction contexts,
a low FPR is desired, as researchers may only tolerate a small proportion of false pos-
itives in exchange for higher precision in predicted binders. To emphasize performance
in the high specificty regime, we compute a partial AUC restricted to FPR ≤10% [24].
The AUC@0.1 effectively measures the model’s ability to correctly identify a subset of
true binders while making very few false positives. We normalize the partial AUC by
the 0.1 FPR region, so that a value of 1.0 corresponds to perfect classification up to
10% FPR. This corresponds to the standardized partial AUC definition, meaning
values are directly comparable only to other standardized partial AUCs computed with
the same cutoff, but not to the full AUC. To assess how an observed AUC@0.1 reflects
meaningful predictive power or could arise by chance, it is common to compare against
a null distribution obtained by randomly permuting the test labels while keeping the
predicted scores fixed.
• Confusion Matrix:A confusion matrix summarizes the outcomes of a binary classifier

(FN), and True Positives (TP). These metrics provide an overview of model behavior,
for example indicating what fraction of true binders are correctly identified or how
reliable predicted binders are.
• Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) MCC is a metric that captures the
balance between sensitivity and specificity in a binary classifier, it is defined as:

5.1
Dataset
The training data were obtained from Refsgaard et al. 2021 [10], comprising 63 phosphopep-
tide datasets collected across ten studies. Each dataset consists of phosphorylated ligands
identified in specific cell lines, supplemented with randomly sampled unmodified peptides
used as negative controls. All peptide sequences and their binding annotations were down-
loaded from the authors’ GitHub repository: https://github.com/mnielLab/NetMHCphosPan-1.
0.
The raw data were provided in files with the extension *.EL.sty, where each file contains
the ligands eluted from a particular cell line. These files were concatenated into a single
collection and subjected to several filtering and preprocessing steps:
• Peptide length: Only 9-mer peptides were retained. This length is the most frequent
among MHC class I ligands and ensures a uniform input size for the models.
• Host species: Only peptides derived from human cell lines were kept. Non-human
entries were discarded. The resulting set is referred to as D_Human9, formatted as
[peptide, label, cell line].
• Allele assignment: From D_Human9 we derived D_HLA9 by reassigning pep-
tides from cell-line labels to their corresponding HLA alleles using the MHCMotifDecon-
1.0 tool (https://services.healthtech.dtu.dk/services/MHCMotifDecon-1.0/). Since the
tool does not accept non-standard amino acids, all phosphorylated residues were re-
placed with "X" prior to submission. Allele information was taken from allelelist.bench
and allelelist.train (from the same repository). By specifying MHC class I as in-
put type and leaving other parameters at default, the tool outputs a mapping of each
peptide to a single HLA allele. The resulting dataset is stored as [peptide, label,
HLA allele].
• MHC pseudo-sequences: To provide structural context for the binding groove,

5.3
Model Architectures
The overall model pipeline consists of two main stages: (i) a representation module, im-
plemented either as a convolutional autoencoder that compresses peptide depictions into a
low-dimensional feature vector or as a one-hot encoding used for baseline comparisons, and
(ii) a feedforward neural network classifier that takes these feature vectors as input and
predicts MHC binding. The autoencoder serves as a trainable feature extractor, while the
classifier acts as the downstream predictor.

5.3.1
Autoencoder
Peptide depictions of size 28 × 252 × 3 were processed by a convolutional autoencoder.
The encoder comprised four convolutional blocks, each with a 3 × 3 convolution (padding
1), batch normalization, LeakyReLU activation (slope 0.1), and 2 × 2 max pooling. This
reduced the input to a 1 × 15 × 128 feature map, which was flattened and passed through a

5.4
BLAST Baseline Evaluation
As a non-machine-learning baseline, we implemented an all-vs-all sequence-similarity search
using BLASTp (Basic Local Alignment Search Tool). A single BLAST database was created
from the entire set of peptide sequences (binders and non-binders). Each peptide was then
queried against this database using the -task blastp-short option, configured for short
sequences with an E-value threshold of 1000. Self-hits were excluded, and the top non-self
hit for each peptide was retained.
For scoring, we used −log10(E-value), capped at 100 for hits with reported E-value 0. If
the best hit was annotated as a non-binder, the score was negated. Peptides without any
significant hit (E-value ≥1000) were assigned scores near 0. Binary predictions were derived
by thresholding at 0, corresponding to predicting “binder” if the best hit was a binder with
a stronger match than any non-binder. From these scores, we computed ROC curves, AUC
values, and confusion matrices.
This baseline reflects the performance achievable from raw sequence similarity alone, without
training a predictive model.

downstream classification layer (hereafter referred to as the classifier head ) for binary
binding prediction. During Stage II the encoder (if present) remains frozen.

– AE path (learned features). Train the AE end-to-end to minimise mean-squared

for context:
• Label shuffling: permute labels while keeping scores fixed; report mean ± SD pAUC@0.1.
• Top-10% score shuffling: randomly redistribute only the highest decile of scores;
report mean ± SD pAUC@0.1 and overlay the mean ROC within the zoomed region.
For each fold, a zoomed ROC figure (FPR ≤0.1) is produced that displays the model curve,
the random diagonal, and both baseline overlays.

5.7.6
Outputs and reproducibility
For each fold and representation the evaluation produces: (i) fold-wise ROC curves and

Sequence preferences were qualitatively assessed with position-specific sequence logos for

modifications to accept phosphorylated residues (additionalAA = {"s","t","y"}). The
resulting logos display per-position information content on a bit scale (maximum ≈4.32 for

5.9
Model development
We developed the system through six experimental configurations (hereafter, model vari-
ants), iteratively finalising the network architecture and then varying the input and par-
titioning schemes. Unless stated otherwise, architectural components are defined in §5.3,
partitioning is described in §5.5, training follows §5.6, and evaluation uses §5.7.
5.9.1
V1 — Prototype (random split; no SE; Linear DNN).
This initial prototype combined a random (non-clustered) split, an encoder without Squeeze-
and-Excitation (SE) blocks, and the Linear DNN head. Its main role was to validate the
end-to-end pipeline. Because the partitioning ignores cluster structure and the absence of
SE blocks severely limits the quality of the autoencoder features, V1 is not methodologically
comparable to later variants and is reported only as a proof of concept.
5.9.2
V2 — First exploratory step (clustered baseline with SE; Linear DNN).
In this configuration we changed both the partitioning and the encoder: clustered partitioning
was introduced to obtain more meaningful train–test splits, and SE blocks were enabled in
the encoder to make the autoencoder features competitive with the baseline. The Linear
DNN head was retained so that both representation paths (autoencoder features and one-
hot baseline) were evaluated under the same classifier. While in principle it is preferable to
vary only one factor at a time, the prototype (V1) was too limited to serve as a baseline. V2
therefore represents the first meaningful exploratory configuration, though the use of a non-
linear head complicates attribution of performance differences solely to the representation.
5.9.3
V3 — Architecture finalised (clustered partitioning; with SE; Linear SNN).
We replaced the multi-layer head with a single-layer logistic classifier to reduce the influ-
ence of classifier complexity and to ensure that performance differences could be attributed
primarily to the learned representations. From this point onward, SE encoder + Linear
SNN defines the final architecture used in subsequent variants.

5.9.4
V4 — Input augmentation with peptide + MHC pseudo (clustered parti-
tioning; with SE; Linear SNN).
Building on V3, the peptide input was augmented with an MHC pseudo-sequence (as defined
in §5.5) to incorporate allele-specific information. This step was conceptually important,
since without MHC data the model effectively reduces to a majority-vote classifier across
alleles. In development runs, however, the inclusion of pseudo-sequences did not lead to
measurable improvements over V3. V4 is therefore retained as a meaningful exploratory
variant, documenting that explicit MHC input did not confer an advantage under the present
setup.
5.9.5
V5 — Allele-specific clustered partitioning (with SE; Linear SNN).
To assess allele-specific generalisation, we constructed outer/inner folds per allele (see §5.5),
holding the architecture fixed (SE encoder + Linear SNN). This prevents cross-allele signal
sharing and probes whether performance persists under stricter generalisation constraints.
5.9.6
V6 — Allele-specific clustered partitioning with phosphopeptides in the
outer test (with SE; Linear SNN).
Finally, we stressed the allele-specific setting by ensuring that phosphopeptides were explic-
itly present in the outer test partition (phospho subset defined in §5.7). This variant evaluates
whether models trained predominantly on unmodified peptides generalise to phosphorylated
peptides under allele-specific constraints.

V5: Allele-specific clustered partitioning (with SE; Linear SNN)
Under the allele-specific partitioning of V5, we restricted analysis to a subset of alleles chosen
by a pragmatic criterion of data availability: more than 100 non-phosphorylated binders and
at least 15 phosphorylated binders. These thresholds were selected arbitrarily to ensure stable
estimates while retaining a representative spread of alleles. Within this subset, predictive
performance varied markedly across HLA alleles (Figures 29–30). ROC–AUC values (Figure
29) ranged from below 0.6 for alleles with limited training data (e.g. A11:01, B35:08, C16:01)
to above 0.9 for well-represented alleles such as B07:02 and B40:02 (see §6.1). Across most

7
Discussion
In this work, we investigated a 2D image-based deep learning approach for peptide encoding
in MHC class I binding prediction. Our method employed CNN-based autoencoders to de-
rive continuous feature representations from RDKit-generated 2D depictions of amino acids,
which were then assembled to represent full peptides. The objective was to capture richer
chemical and structural information for each residue, including post-translational modifi-
cations such as phosphorylation, with the aim of improving binding prediction accuracy.
In practice, the proposed representation yielded mixed outcomes. While the autoencoder-
derived features achieved competitive performance in certain metrics (notably ROC–AUC
and low-FPR partial AUC), the conventional one-hot encoding generally outperformed the
image-based approach across most evaluations. The following discussion interprets these find-
ings in relation to prior state-of-the-art methods, examines potential causes of the observed
performance gap, and outlines future directions for refining peptide encoding strategies.
Crucially, these results must be understood in the context of dataset biases. Class imbalance,
uneven allele coverage, and limited phosphopeptide representation fundamentally constrain
model performance and frame the scope of any comparison.
7.1
Dataset Composition, Class Imbalance, and Potential Biases
Model performance does not exist in isolation but is contingent on the data used for training
and testing; dataset composition and biases must therefore be carefully considered when in-
terpreting results. In this thesis, we curated a dataset (D_HLA9) comprising 9-mer peptides
with binding labels across multiple HLA class I alleles. A critical examination of this dataset
reveals several sources of bias that constrain both model development and evaluation.
Class imbalance is severe in this domain. According to the data analysis §6.1, on average
only about 15% of peptides in an allele’s dataset were labeled as binders. In such a class-
imbalanced setting, a naïve majority-class predictor can achieve deceptively high accuracy
by always predicting ‘non-binder,’ underscoring the limitations of accuracy as a performance
Page 63 of 79
==PAGE:69==
Jialu C. C.
August 24, 2025
metric. We utilized metrics like AUC, MCC and threshold selection (sensitivity = specificity)
that account for imbalance, they are not entirely immune. ROC-AUC remains insensitive
to class proportions but can mask poor performance on the minority class, whereas MCC
explicitly integrates all elements of the confusion matrix and is therefore well-suited for
imbalanced peptide–binder datasets. Threshold selection strategies further help to counteract
imbalance but cannot remove the fundamental skew in class distributions. Partial AUC in
the low-FPR regime to specifically evaluate performance where false positives are tightly
controlled, which is critical when non-binders vastly outnumber binders. The class imbalance
can also affect training dynamics: the loss function may be dominated by negatives unless
weighting or balanced sampling is used. In our case, we implicitly addressed this by focusing
on threshold-independent metrics and by examining confusion matrices to ensure balanced
error trade-offs. Still, the imbalance likely contributed to the models’ hesitance in calling
binders, especially for rare cases like phosphopeptides where the ratio of positives was even
smaller (only ∼8% binders among anchor-phosphorylated peptides §6.6).
Peptide–allele coverage was highly uneven. While the average allele in our dataset was as-
sociated with ∼1,400 peptides (Table 5), the distribution was heavy-tailed: common alleles,
such as HLA-A*02:01, had tens of thousands of peptides, whereas some alleles were repre-
sented by only a handful. This imbalance risks biasing model development, as alleles with
abundant data dominate overall metrics, while alleles with scarce data yield unstable per-
formance estimates. To mitigate this, we imposed a lower threshold for inclusion, requiring
at least 100 binders and 15 phospho-binders per allele. However, even this criterion is itself
quite limited for training data-hungry machine-learning models, and therefore does not fully
resolve the issue. Consequently, macro-averaged performance metrics across alleles must be
interpreted with caution, as they are disproportionately influenced by well-studied alleles
with extensive data. By contrast, the capacity of the models to generalise to alleles with lim-
ited coverage remains inherently constrained. This limitation highlights the need for future
methodological developments, such as data augmentation or transfer-learning strategies, to
improve predictive robustness for under-represented alleles.
As a case study in dataset bias, we examined peptides phosphorylated at anchor positions
Page 64 of 79
==PAGE:70==
Jialu C. C.
August 24, 2025
(P2 or P9). Within the V6 model allele subset (those meeting the ≥100 binder and ≥15
phospho-binder criterion), we identified 2,284 unique anchor-phosphorylated peptides, of
which only 184 (8.1%) were true binders (Table 6). This extremely low binder fraction makes
performance evaluation for this subset particularly challenging, as estimates are dominated
by class imbalance and limited sample size.
Finally, many immunopeptidomics datasets lack experimentally confirmed non-binders; in-
stead, negatives are often assumed (e.g. unobserved peptides or random draws). For example,
the NetMHCphosPan dataset used in this study introduced artificial negatives by sampling
random natural phosphopeptides from PhosphoSitePlus to balance positive examples across
lengths [10]. Such negatives may include true binders that were not detected or may differ
systematically from binders for reasons unrelated to binding, introducing additional uncer-
tainty into training and evaluation.
In sum, severe class imbalance, uneven allele representation, limited phosphopeptide cov-
erage, and uncertainty in negatives all influence both the development and the evaluation
of our models. These factors constrain absolute performance and limit generalisation, par-
ticularly for rare alleles and phosphorylated peptides. However, because all methods were
evaluated on the same dataset, the comparative analysis between one-hot and image-based
encodings remains valid. The broadly similar performance of the two representations should
therefore be interpreted as reflecting the shared limitations of the dataset: when data are
sparse, imbalanced, or noisy, improvements in representation alone cannot fully overcome
these constraints.
7.2
Model Comparison
One of the central findings of our evaluation is that the CNN autoencoder-based encoding
did not outperform a conventional one-hot sequence encoding on binding prediction. Across
multiple experiment variants (V2 through V6), the one-hot baseline showed equal or better
results on most metrics. In the initial benchmark (V2), both encoding schemes achieved
nearly identical overall accuracy (ROC–AUC) on the test folds, with no statistically signifi-
cant difference (see §6.3.1). This indicated that the learned 2D-image features were at least
Page 65 of 79
==PAGE:71==
Jialu C. C.
August 24, 2025
capturing relevant information to some extent. As the model architecture evolved (V3–V6),
a consistent pattern emerged: one-hot features provided a small but systematic advantage in
predictive performance. For instance, under the finalized architecture (V3), the one-hot rep-
resentation yielded higher ROC–AUC than the autoencoder in every fold of cross-validation
(ensemble AUC per fold), suggesting a robust if modest performance edge (see §6.3.2). Al-
though the difference in mean AUC was small (and only marginally non-significant by a
Wilcoxon test, p≈0.06), the direction was uniform, pointing to an inherent advantage for
the simpler encoding. This trend was mirrored in later variants: by the final integrated
model (V6), per-allele analyses showed one-hot encoding consistently outperforming the au-
toencoder features, with significance confirmed by paired bootstrap tests on some metrics
(see §6.5).
Notably, the autoencoder-based features did prove competitive in certain regimes. When we
focused on the high-specificity region of the ROC (false positive rate ≤0.1), the partial
AUC for autoencoder features was comparable to that of one-hot encoding. Both encodings
achieved a standardized pAUC@0.1 of around 0.74–0.76, indicating strong early retrieval
of true binders (see §6.3.1 - §6.3.3). In one experiment (V3), the autoencoder even slightly
exceeded the one-hot baseline in partial AUC on every fold, though the margin was minor
and not statistically significant. This suggests that the chemical image embeddings retained
some signal for identifying top binders with low false-alarm rates, potentially capturing broad
physicochemical tendencies that correlate with binding at stringent thresholds. However,
when considering the overall ranking of predictions (full AUC) and threshold-dependent
measures, the one-hot encoding remained as good as or better than the learned features. For
example, at a typical operating point (sensitivity = specificity), the one-hot model produced
confusion matrices with slightly more balanced outcomes than the autoencoder, yielding
marginally higher true positive and true negative counts (see §6.3.1 - §6.3.3).
Although the anchor-phospho subsets are heavily imbalanced and (see §6.6, in some cases,
dominated by recurrent peptides across alleles or folds, both models were evaluated on the
same distribution. Relative differences between encodings therefore remain informative, even
if absolute values are inflated. In fact, large performance gaps observed for certain alleles (e.g.
Page 66 of 79
==PAGE:72==
Jialu C. C.
August 24, 2025
HLA-A*01:01) point to fundamental differences in how the models capture phosphorylated
binders (see Table 7). These results should be interpreted as row-level performance on the
evaluation set, not as evidence of generalisation across a broader and more diverse set of
unique phosphopeptides.
In summary, autoencoder-derived representations never substantially outperformed the tra-
ditional encoding in our tests. At best, they matched the baseline on some folds or metrics;
at worst, they were modestly inferior across the board. This holds true for both the global
evaluation (all peptides) and the phosphopeptide-specific evaluation. When focusing on the
subset of peptides containing phosphorylated residues, both methods showed a clear drop in
performance, consistent with the increased biological heterogeneity and limited data avail-
ability of this category. The Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC) on phosphopeptides was
around 0.45–0.50 (see §6.4 - §6.5), compared to 0.55–0.60 on the full test set. Within this
more challenging regime, the one-hot encoding generally retained a slight advantage, match-
ing or modestly exceeding the autoencoder in most folds. Thus, the relative ranking of the
two encoding schemes remained unchanged in the phosphopeptide context: the image-based
features were competitive but did not surpass the simpler baseline.
7.3
Analysis of Autoencoder Feature Limitations
Several factors help explain why the autoencoder-based features underperformed the one-
hot encoding in our study. First, the limitation does not stem from an inability to repre-
sent phosphorylated residues in general. In V1–V4, where training covered nearly the entire
dataset (including phosphopeptides), reconstruction analysis showed that the autoencoder
reproduced phosphorylation accurately; the phosphate moiety was rendered correctly and
reconstruction error was not systematically concentrated on modified sites (see §6.7.1. Thus,
capacity to encode post-translational modifications was present when sufficient examples
were available. However, when data availability was low, the autoencoder struggled: recon-
structions of under-represented peptides were less accurate, and error maps became more
diffuse. This indicates that while the model can learn chemically meaningful features, its
ability to generalise is fragile under data scarcity (see §6.7.2 - §6.7.5).
Page 67 of 79
==PAGE:73==
Jialu C. C.
August 24, 2025
The remaining gap appears to arise from a mismatch between the unsupervised reconstruc-
tion objective and the downstream discriminative task. Reconstruction encourages strict
pixel-level recovery of amino-acid depictions, which does not necessarily align with the
position-specific and allele-specific signals that drive MHC binding. In particular, one-hot
encoding preserves position explicitly and presents a linearly separable representation for
anchor positions, whereas the autoencoder’s continuous features can blur or diffuse position-
specific information across channels. Consistent with this interpretation, training dynamics
indicated less stable optimisation for the autoencoder-based pipeline: in several folds the val-
idation loss declined more slowly and, within the same epoch budget, did not reach as clear
a plateau as the one-hot counterpart; classification accuracy also fluctuated more during
training (Figure 27 + 21). These behaviours suggest that the autoencoder learned features
sufficient for reconstructing peptide depictions, but not structured in a way that optimally
supports the downstream classification task. In particular, the representations were not well
aligned with the linear decision boundary required to separate binders from non-binders.
A second limitation lies in the allele-specific nature of MHC binding. Subtle motif differences
across alleles require abundant training data, yet many alleles are sparsely represented. Our
reconstruction analysis confirmed that autoencoder quality degraded sharply with decreasing
dataset size: reconstructions from large allele sets were recognisable, but those from small
sets collapsed to noise (see §6.7). This illustrates how latent features become unreliable
under data scarcity. By contrast, the one-hot encoding presents each amino acid identity
explicitly, which in principle allows the classifier to capture even rare allele-specific motifs.
The autoencoder’s chemically smoothed latent space may instead blur such distinctions, a
possibility that could explain its weaker performance under data scarcity.
Another important consideration is that our autoencoder was trained in an unsupervised
manner on the reconstruction task, separate from the binding prediction objective. This
meant the encoder was never informed which peptide features matter for MHC binding, and
instead sought to reproduce all visual details of the input. Consequently, its latent codes
may capture side-chain orientations or minor pixel-level differences without emphasizing an-
chor positions or other binding-relevant motifs, leaving the classifier to sift through largely
Page 68 of 79
==PAGE:74==
Jialu C. C.
August 24, 2025
task-irrelevant variance. In our setup the encoder was fixed after pretraining, and without
fine-tuning on the predictive task its features could not adapt to binding-specific decision
boundaries. Training the autoencoder directly on binder peptides could, in principle, align
the learned features more closely with the binding task. However, this was not feasible in
our setting: binders are relatively sparse, and pooling them across alleles would obscure
allele-specific motifs and reduce biological signal. The competitive performance of the au-
toencoder on some metrics (e.g. partial AUC) shows that it still captured general biochemical
properties, but a more effective strategy would require representation learning informed by
binding labels, or a joint training approach that balances reconstruction with task-specific
supervision.
In summary, the autoencoder-based encoding scheme was limited by its dependence on abun-
dant data, its unsupervised training objective, and the lack of task-specific fine-tuning. While
the model could reproduce phosphorylated residues when trained on the full dataset, feature
quality degraded under data scarcity, and the latent space was not optimally aligned with the
binding classification task. Training directly on binders might resolve this misalignment, but
data sparsity and allele-specificity made this infeasible in our setting. These factors together
explain why the one-hot baseline retained an advantage, while also highlighting potential
avenues for improvement through joint or supervised representation learning.
7.4
Comparison with State-of-the-Art Methods
To contextualize the performance of our models, it is helpful to compare them against both
simple baselines and established predictors. At the lower end, a non-learning approach such
as BLAST provides a practical reference point. In our evaluation, an all-vs-all BLASTp search
achieved only modest predictive power (AUC ≈0.66) (see §6.2), correctly flagging binders
only when near-identical peptides were present in the reference set. This highlights the limi-
tations of raw sequence similarity and defines a lower bound for predictive performance. Our
one-hot and autoencoder models clearly exceeded this baseline by learning position-specific
features, but they still fall short when compared to dedicated state-of-the-art predictors.
At the opposite end of the spectrum, NetMHCpan [7] and its phospho-specific variant
Page 69 of 79
==PAGE:75==
Jialu C. C.
August 24, 2025
NetMHCphosPan [10] are state-of-the-art pan-allele predictors for MHC class I antigen pre-
sentation. Both rely on sequence-based encodings and pan-allele modelling, but differ in
scope: NetMHCpan integrates peptide sequences with MHC pseudo-sequences to general-
ize across alleles, while NetMHCphosPan extends this framework by introducing dedicated
tokens for phosphorylated residues (pS, pT, pY) and jointly training on modified and un-
modified peptides. This mixed-data strategy enabled much stronger performance on phos-
phopeptides compared to earlier tools.
Our study differs in two key ways. First, we trained allele-specific models rather than pan-
allele models, meaning our methods cannot generalize to unseen alleles or benefit from cross-
allele information. Second, our focus was on representation schemes, comparing one-hot en-
codings to image-based autoencoders without integrating explicit allele features. NetMHC-
pan’s inclusion of allele pseudo-sequences is therefore a clear advantage, explaining its consis-
tently strong accuracy on broad benchmarks. Likewise, NetMHCphosPan’s targeted training
regime and large-scale data integration would likely outperform our allele-specific models on
phosphopeptides. It is also important to note that the dataset used here is derived from
the same NetMHCphosPan corpus (albeit filtered to 9-mers and human alleles, see §5.5), so
any numerical comparison would not be independent. We therefore restrict comparisons to
a conceptual level rather than direct benchmarking.
In terms of flexibility, our image-based encoding offers a general way to handle arbitrary
post-translational modifications, since any chemical structure can be represented as an im-
age without redesigning the alphabet. In contrast, NetMHCpan/NetMHCphosPan require
explicit token extensions and retraining for each modification. However, this flexibility did
not yet translate into superior predictive power in our experiments, where one-hot encodings
consistently matched or exceeded the autoencoder on this dataset. Achieving performance
comparable to state-of-the-art predictors will likely require combining flexible representations
with pan-allele training and larger, more diverse datasets, as done in NetMHCpan.
In summary, our methods explore new directions in peptide representation but are less pow-
erful in integration and data leverage. NetMHCpan remains the benchmark for broad accu-
Page 70 of 79
==PAGE:76==
Jialu C. C.

7.5
Future Directions for Improved Feature Extraction
While expanded and higher-quality datasets will always improve model performance, the
focus here is on methodological refinements. Our findings do not invalidate the concept of
using rich chemical representations for peptides; rather, they highlight that refinements are
needed to unlock their full potential. We propose several technical improvements (focused
on the feature extraction stage) that could be explored in future work.
Deeper and More Expressive CNN Architectures
The current autoencoder uses a modest CNN encoder (four conv blocks with BN/LeakyReLU
and max-pooling; latent size 256; see §5.3.1). Deeper encoders with residual connections or
attention can improve feature richness and gradient flow, enabling finer discrimination among
closely related side chains and modified residues. Classic depth/expressivity gains are well
documented (VGG [26], ResNet [27]), and modern reviews summarize attention mechanisms
in CNN pipelines [28]
Data Augmentation for Chemical Depictions
RDKit depictions are deterministic (see §5.2). Augmentations like random rotations, trans-
lations, mild scaling/jitter should help the encoder focus on structural essentials rather than
drawing idiosyncrasies. Image augmentation is a standard lever in vision CNNs and often
improves generalization [22, 28]
Multimodal or Hybrid Encodings
Rather than relying solely on image-derived features, future work could explore fusing them
with complementary sequence-based encodings (e.g., one-hot vectors, substitution matrices,
or physicochemical descriptors), or more directly incorporating allele-specific context. In this

study, the simple concatenation of peptide and allele pseudo-sequences at the input stage
(V4) did not yield measurable performance gains compared to peptide-only models (see
§6.3.3). This outcome suggests that naïve fusion strategies are insufficient. However, more
sophisticated integration approaches, such as conditioning the peptide encoder on allele
features via cross-attention or feature-wise modulation may allow the model to capture
interactions between peptide residues and the MHC binding groove more effectively.
Graph-Based Molecular Representations
Instead of 2D pixels, encode residues (or entire peptides) as molecular graphs (atoms =
nodes; bonds = edges) and learn embeddings with graph neural networks (GNNs) or mod-
ern learned fingerprints. GNNs preserve connectivity and stereochemical patterns, and can
make phosphate groups explicit in the graph, likely improving representation of PTMs.
While RDKit-image encoders are convenient (see §5.2), graph/message-passing encoders are
a natural next step for chemistry-aware features [29].
End-to-End Representation Learning
One lesson from our work and related studies is that learning representations in an end-to-
end fashion often yields superior results [1]. Instead of a two-stage autoencoder + classifier,
future models could train jointly to optimize a task-specific loss. For example, a peptide
image encoder could be trained simultaneously with the predictor, possibly using a multi-
task objective (reconstruct input + classify binding). This way, the representation focuses on
features that matter for binding. End-to-end learned embeddings have been shown to match
or exceed hand-crafted encodings even with fewer dimensions, and they easily accommodate
new symbols (e.g. a new modified residue) by expanding the vocabulary or input channels,
letting the model learn how it affects binding.
Scope note
It is important to note that all the above suggestions operate upstream of the classifier;
the intention is to improve the peptide representation itself. Any of these improved feature
extraction techniques could be plugged into our two-stage framework (as a replacement

isolation of the representation learning is valuable – it means future efforts can focus on
encoding peptides in more informative ways, confident that if the encoding is truly predictive,
even a basic classifier will be able to detect the signal. Ultimately, by making the peptide
features more expressive and aligned with binding biology, we increase the likelihood that
an image/structure-based approach could surpass traditional encodings in performance.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated two-dimensional chemical depictions of amino acids as a represen-
tation strategy for peptides, with MHC class I binding prediction serving as the evaluation
task. The study showed that autoencoder-derived features are viable for classification and
competitive in certain regimes, but overall did not surpass the performance of simple one-hot
encodings. The enduring strength of one-hot features underscores that new representations
must capture critical position-specific information at least as effectively as conventional en-
codings, even under data imbalance and limited coverage of modifications.
Despite these limitations, the work establishes a foundation for chemistry-aware peptide
representations. By embedding structural and modification information directly into the
encoding, this approach offers flexibility that sequence-based methods cannot easily accom-
modate. Future developments such as end-to-end supervised training, data augmentation,
or graph-based encodings may bridge the current performance gap. More broadly, as im-
munoinformatics increasingly confronts post-translational modifications and non-canonical
residues, flexible encodings of this type could become important complements to traditional
sequence-based methods.
