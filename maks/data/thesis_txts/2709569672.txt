Executive summary 
 
The steep upsurge of the cost of energy that followed the stagnation of the economy due 
to the Covid-19 epidemic should have made apparent the necessity for affordable and reliable 
energy sources to allow the advancement of society. This is an additional burden for low-income 
nations that have to balance the socio-economic growth and the investments necessary to contrast 
global warming and finance adaptations due to its consequences. Indonesia, the fourth most 
populous country, is one of these. The government set ambitious targets to transition their 
economy from being fossil fuels based to becoming more sustainable.  
This master thesis aspires to assist the diffusion of clean, affordable energy, especially in low pro-
capita income countries like Indonesia. Although the country has not abundant wind resources, 
wind energy may provide a feasible contribution to the cause, and public procurement via tender 
is one of the choices selected by the Government of Indonesia (GoI) to provide affordable wind 
energy. The tender process can be associated with a sealed bid auction where the lowest bid wins 
the auction. The GoI is oriented to hold First Price Sealed Bid Auction (FPSBA), where the winner 
is awarded the bidden offer, probably with an incentive to encourage the developers.  
 
The main result of the thesis is a game-theoretic model that provides insights to optimize the 
tender design. The optimization is based on the pursuit of the “rational” optimum bid that 
maximizes the profit of the FPSBA, interpreted as the product of the payoff multiplied by the 
probability of winning the auction. The model is supported by an estimate of the Levelized Cost 
of Energy (LCOE) of the existing wind farms in Indonesia, where the wind market is still 
immature, and Vietnam, where the wind market is more developed. Vietnam is a neighbouring 
country in good relations with Indonesia, as the two are both members of the Association of 
South-East Asian Nations and hence may represent an objective for the Indonesian wind market.  
 
The optimal bid provides insights regarding the possible rational outcome of the auction. 
According to game theory, FPSBA is seen as competitive games of incomplete information, also 
known as Bayesian games. For these games, the existence of the Nash equilibrium was proven 
theoretically. The author used a simple mathematical model to calculate the optimal bid starting 
from a triangular distribution of the costs among the bidders, assumed typical for a developing 
market. The LCOE of the two Indonesian wind farms of commercial size, Sidrap and Tolo1, was 
used to estimate the higher limit of the triangular distribution, while the lower is the average 
LCOE of Vietnam’s projects. 
 
==PAGE:4==
The model covers Single-Item and Multi-Item Auctions, as these options may both be useful for 
different purposes in the context, namely, Single-Item for projects in determinate locations, for 
high demand requirements or the remoteness from the grid, and Multi-Item for allocating large 
capacity at once. Furthermore, the model uses the optimal bid to compare the outcome of the 
auction for the possible pricing rules, not only the First Price for which the bid is optimized, i.e. 
the Second Price and Uniform Price pricing rule. The model was built on the consideration that 
the wind energy market is still immature in Indonesia, hence a triangular distribution of the 
development costs of the projects is used to simulate the presence of investors with different levels 
of development costs. The mode of the distribution can be varied to simulate improvements in the 
sector or general variations of the market. 
 
The model is conceived from the bidder perspective as it proposes a bidding strategy that 
maximizes the profit, and at the same time, also provides insights useful from the auctioneer's 
point of view, since it compares the economic results of different strategies allowing the creation 
of outcome scenarios according to the pricing rules and the number and type of bidders, that 
determine the capacity ratio. This is an important parameter for the success of the auction that 
the auctioneer is able to learn beforehand, but can control only in a limited way, hence it is 
advantageous to have a reference value for the bids connected to the capacity ratio before the final 
definition of the auction. The model in this way supplies information useful to the design phase 
of the tender.  
 
The optimal bid functions for the two intervals determined by the triangular distribution output 
the optimal bid and represent the Nash equilibrium for the FPSBA as a function of the number of 
players, the parameters of the triangular distribution. 
 
The mathematical optimum has been verified for FPSBA in Single-Item auctions for different 
values of the mode of the triangular distribution using simulations. A parametric study is needed 
since the domain of the optimal bid function is limited by the set of parameters of choice. 
However, the domain can be modified with a change of variables for the function. 
 
The optimal bids for the Single-Item auctions simulations with the cost range [30, 70 EUR/MWh] 
have been determined for different values of the mode of the distribution and the number of 
players. 
 
The model has then been applied to Multi-Item auctions comparing the optimal bid for Pay-as-
Bid pricing rule to other pricing rules (Vickery auctions and Uniform Price) for different numbers 
of bidders that result different capacity ratios.  
 
The results are coherent with the expectations.

Chapter 1: Introduction         
   
Denmark has a long-term cooperation program with Indonesia, which in recent years 
included a strategic sector cooperation programme supporting the Nationally Determined 
Contributions and the security of energy supply [1.1]. This thesis focuses on the design of wind 
tenders, as a tool to provisioning clean energy at a fair price, from a game-theoretic perspective. 
The dissertation is structured in five chapters: the first provides an assessment of the status of the 
energy market in Indonesia with a description of the electricity distribution corporation, PLN, 
and the current energy mix and resources, along with an excursus on Danish expertise in the wind 
sector from a technological and regulation point of view. This is followed by a characterization of 
the tender for energy in general and in the game theory context and concluded with the research 
questions investigated in the dissertation. The second describes the steps necessary to build a 
simple mathematical game-theoretic model based on a realistic Levelized Costs of Energy range, 
that has at the higher end the LCOE of currently existing wind projects in Indonesia and on the 
lower end the average LCOE for projects in Vietnam, where the wind market is more mature in 
comparison to Indonesia. A brief theoretical background allows subsequently to deduce the 
fundamental equations for the game-theoretic model and the main points of the implementation. 
In the third, the results of the dissertation are presented for the model and its background and 
the in the fourth the results are debated. Finally, the fifth chapter reviews the results in 
consideration of the research questions.  
 
1.1 Motivation 
 
The steep worldwide increase in the cost of energy that followed the stagnation of the 
economy due to the Covid-19 epidemic should have made apparent the necessity for affordable 
and reliable energy sources to allow the development of society. Renewable energies seem to 
promise a future of affordable and locally available energy, mostly disengaged by the fluctuation 
of the market and with the huge and very needed additional advantageous outcomes of cutting 
pollution, creating local jobs and positively contributing to the noble goal of preventing the 
worsening of global warming. 
  
==PAGE:7==
Indonesia, with a population of over 270 million and increasing energy demand, is one of the 
world's greater exporters of coal and the eighth largest CO2 emitter. At the same time, the 
Government, on one hand, has set ambitious targets for social and economic development, and 
on the other aims to deploy 23% renewable energy in their energy mix by 2025 (it was 12.5% in 
2020) and committed to going NetZero by 2060.  
Denmark is a major player in the diffusion of renewable energies, with world leadership in wind 
energy and, via several national programs has helped finance, design, and implement projects for 
improving energy efficiency and life condition all around the world. 
The two countries have a protracted collaboration and, already in 2005, have set the target on 
climate action, with the Government of Indonesia and the Government of Denmark binding in a 
dedicated partnership, INDODEPP. Since the Government of Indonesia embraced a full-
spectrum strategy to tackle their energy transition and decided to include wind energy via 
subsidized tender procurement, Denmark seems to be the perfect partner to provide help in this 
direction. PLN expressed the need of acquiring knowledge on the integration of renewables in the 
power system, also because, although the ambitious targets set, the wind energy market is growing 
at a slow pace in Indonesia because of inefficient auctions due to policy uncertainty, unclear 
auctions rules, low margins and sunken costs for developers. This master thesis aspires to assist 
the diffusion of clean, affordable energy, especially needed in low pro-capita income countries, 
with a mathematical model based on game theory which would meet a double function: on the 
one hand, it could help developers to orient their bids and reduce the possibility of sunken costs, 
and on the other, it could help PLN to establish more concise and fair pricing rules in the auctions, 
making more attractive for developers to participate. 
 
 
1.2 Background 
 
1.2.1 Indonesia 
 
The Republic of Indonesia is a country in Southeast Asia consisting of over 17,000 islands 
(6,000 populated), located on the border between the Pacific and the Indian Ocean. It is the fourth 
most populous country in the world with the largest Muslim population. The over 270 million 
inhabitants, half of which are concentrated in the island of Java where the capital Jakarta lies, are 
divided into over a thousand ethnic groups, over twenty of these with more than a million 
members but also including minorities. Even if there were cases of tensions among the groups for 
ethnic, social or religious reasons, the country is characterized by tolerance and peaceful 
coexistence and free election are regularly held. However, development has been uneven across 
the land and inequality is one of the challenges for the Government [1.2].  
 
Committing to the Paris Agreement, the GoI set ambitious greenhouse gas emission (GHG) 
reduction targets (29% unconditionally and 41% with international support, compared to 
business-as-usual scenario) for 2030. Although the country relies massively on fossil fuel, 
according to the IESR report this is technically and economically feasible pointing to increase 
sharply the renewable energy share in the primary energy mix (80% by 2040 and 100 by 2050) 
[1.3]. 
 
Moreover, Indonesia is undergoing serious climate-related hazards that have had harsh 
consequences on the most vulnerable part of the population that would require urgent 
adaptations and mitigations. The territory is especially susceptible to hydrometeorological 
==PAGE:8==
disasters (floods, extreme waves, land and forest fires, drought, and extreme weather) with severe 
health and socio-economic impacts. However, the national level policies appear not to be 
sufficient response at the provincial and local level [1.4]. Regarding GHG emissions, the GoI 
identified the main contributing sectors in land-use change, peat-forest fires and energy, and has 
taken actions for the preservation of primary forest and the employment of a more sustainable 
energy mix [1.5] 
 
In a joint effort with the other South-East Asian Countries (ASEAN), Indonesia set the national 
target for renewables to reach 23% by 2025 and 31% by 2050 [1.6]. Considering the growth of the 
electricity demand (the GoI estimated an increase of 77.8GW  electricity capacity in the period 
2016-2025)  [1.7], a massive effort is needed.  
 
 
 
1.2.2 PLN 
 
The joint-stock company Perusahaan Listrik Negara, or PLN, is the government-owned 
corporation entitled with the distribution of electricity in the country, it is also the dominant 
owner and operator of electricity. PLN has the vision to become the leading electricity company 
in ASEAN and the mission to using electricity to improve social conditions with environmentally 
sound activities. In particular, PLN has the strategic objectives of more than doubling its 
renewable energy capacity (from 7.8GW to 16GW) and extending the electrification ratio of the 
country to 100% [1.3]. These targets appear to be ambitious for the difficulties deriving from the 
extension and geographical fragmentation of the country and the diverse level of development 
across the archipelago. As apparent from the table 1.1 reported from [1.33], both the average 
production cost (updated to 2021 costs by the author) and the demand of electricity varies greatly 
from region to region. This is, of course also an opportunity, because the high cost of production 
in areas as North and South-East Sulawesi, Maluku and Papua may encourage the deployment of 
renewables instead of the conventional energy sources.  
On the consumers side, beyond the regional difference, there are four main categories (Houses, 
Businesses, Industries and Public offices), but the electricity prices highly depend on the capacity 
required and consumption. Since the population income and needs spaces very broadly, from 
rural and semi-rural areas to heavily industrialized, and even though the supplier is solely PNL, 
this results in a plethora of tariffs that from one hand suits population needs and from the other 
makes administration more complex [1.5].

1.2.3 Energy resources in Indonesia  
 
The country is the world’s largest archipelago, stretching over five thousand Km from the 
95˚E and the 141˚E meridian, lying on the intertropical convergence zone (ITCZ or Equatorial 
Doldrums) with weather conditions dominated by the monsoons. It is blessed by a variety of 
resources in form of minerals and energy sources and it is home to one of the world’s most ancient 
rainforest with high levels of biodiversity. These resources attracted during the centuries the 
attention of miners, loggers and merchants of spices, with the first reference of salt extraction 
dating two thousand years ago [1.9].  
 
Indonesia’s energy consumption increased steadily in the decade pre-Covid19, along with the 
increase in population and economic growth. The fossil fuels that historically supported the 
country’s development, coal, oil and gas, even if still crucial [1.10], seem to struggle to keep the 
pace. The GoI concern for the environment and the consequences of pollution on the populations 
is drawing more attention to other sources of energy, also in the attempt to diminish Indonesia 
exposition to the global energy market [1.10]. In 2020 coal became the first source of primary 
energy supply with more than 553 billion barrel of oil equivalent, (BOE), surpassing crude oil & 
products (472 billion BOE), with natural gas & products occupying the third place (251 billion 
BOE). These three constitute over 85% of the total. The remaining is provided in vast majority by 
Biofuel, Biomass, Hydropower and Geothermal [1.11] 
 
 
Coal  
Indonesia has had a long tradition of coal mining with large deposits in Sumatra and Kalimantan 
suitable for low-cost extraction. In modern times, commercialization saw a continuous increase 
under the First-Generation Coal Contracts of Work beginning in 1989 that brought Indonesia to 
become the first world exporter of coal (in weight). The broadening of mining activities to sub-
bituminous coal, lignite and blend expanded the use to the domestic market and other ASEAN 
countries. The increase in efficiency of the ultra-supercritical plants together with the fluctuation 
of export, brought the GoI to reserve a large part of the coal to the growing internal demand [1.12]. 
Additionally, this kind of plant could be designed with a reduced minimal load and start-up time, 
and increased ramp rate to make them convenient in combination with variable renewable 
energies. Especially because retrofitting existing plants is possible.  Furthermore, the plant could 
be re-designed to use natural gas or biomass [1.13] 
However, PLN committed to stopping building new plants after 2023 and began biomass co-firing 
with coal in 17 power plants [1.14] 
 
 
Oil and Gas 
Indonesia has been a pioneer in the commercialization of gas and oil, thanks for example to the 
production sharing contracts (PSC), and the sector helped the economic growth of the country as 
much as coal. [1.15] 
 A peculiarity of the sector, has been that the GoI has always relied on transportation via ships, 
partially because back to/in post-colonials times, foreign companies had operated in that way, 
partially to concentrate the quite modest investment in the exploration for new deposits  
 
The rise in domestic consumption pulled by the rapid economic growth, brought the country from 
being one of the biggest exporters to possibly become a net importer due to production decline 
==PAGE:11==
and the absence of significant new reserves discovery. Since 2009, the GoI imposed that one-
fourth of the natural gas produced from the PSC must provide for the national supply [1.15]. 
Indonesia declined also as an exporter of LNG, but it is still the seventh-largest producer [1.14]. 
To date, Indonesia misses a thorough gas pipeline network, with the few developed sections close 
to consumers centers, far from the gas reserves, and most of the transportation implemented via 
ship, as shown in the Fig 1.2. The long-time planned extension connecting the plant in East 
Kalimantan with the west of the island and Java had been cancelled  [1.29] 
 
 
In 2018 the replacement rate of the oil reserve in Indonesia halved due to the exploration 
diminishing and technological limitations. The reserves operated by the national energy company, 
PERTAMINA (47% of Indonesia production), are in mature fields that necessitate more advanced 
(and expensive) recovery techniques (Enhanced Oil Recovery), beyond the scope of Indonesian 
companies. GoI’s measure to impose 25% of oil production to domestic use, the unclear regulatory 
environment, and disputes with international companies limited foreign investments. In response 
to this, GoI set a target to increase crude oil and condensate production to one million barrels per 
day by 2030, with a task force dedicated to drilling a thousand development wells by 2025 [1.14].

Indonesia, with a large part of the territory lying on the “ring of fire” (Fig. 1.3), has the largest

resources and 17.5 GW of reserves [1.13] and a total capacity second only to the USA, with over 
2TW. However, with the 2021-2030 National Electricity Plan, GoI lowered the expectations in 
comparison to the 2019-2028 National Electricity Plan [1.14]. In Fig. 1.3, the locations with 
potential (red dots) lie on the Java trench (Sumatera, Java Bali) and from the Flores Sea towards

Hydropower 
The country has estimated 75GW hydropower resource and 19.4 GW classified as micro 
hydropower [1.13] but the installed capacity grew slowly in the last decade with only 2.2GW. 
However, hydropower generated a significant 8% of the total generation in 2020 [1.14] 
Biofuel 
The island of Java and Bali sustain a very intensive agriculture alternative, while the other islands 
are less prone to intensive agriculture. Nevertheless, the archipelago is a major producer of crops 
such as rise, corn, soybeans and one of the largest exporters of tree crops like rubber, copra and 
palm oil [1.16]. This is a potential source of biofuel and the GoI had launched a program in 2006 
to pursue it. Unfortunately, without support by local governments [1.17], after the initial 
development (the program peaked in 2009) the share of biofuel as primary energy consumption 
has declined [1.14].

With two-thirds of the land featuring yearly average GHI values between 1600-1800 kWh/m2,

Wind resources 
Wind is not the predominant energy source in Indonesia [1.13], as it results from the map derived 
from the wind resource assessment (WRA) that shows the LCOE of ASEAN countries [1.19], 
depicted in Fig. 1.3. The map shows in white the areas with a capacity factor lower than 15%. It 
must be noticed that the NREL report [1.19], revised in 2020, is focused on the areas with the best

improved wind technologies, as the observed trend of increasing hub height for the turbines and 
the commissioning of additional data, the WRA map may be upgraded. However, it is quite clear 
that Indonesia is not as windy as Denmark. 
 
Nevertheless, wind energy can contribute to increasing Indonesian renewable energy share, the 
electrification of remote areas and the transition to NetZero by 2060, since locations in Southern 
Sulawesi, South Kalimantan, Java, Bali and other islands have potential  [1.13]. Additionally, 
remote areas untouched by electricity have decent potential and wind power may be a viable 
solution. In 2014, based on the many studies conducted by numerous national and international

1.2.4 Wind Power in Denmark  
 
The history of electricity generated by wind energy in Denmark began with the pioneer 
physicist and inventor Poul la Cour, that produced electricity for the Askov Folk High School in 
1891. Improvements were developed at the time of World War I and II to supply electricity during 
energy shortages, until the 200 kW Gedser wind turbine generated 2.2GWh between 1956 and 
1967. However, due to the low cost of oil, the Danish Association of power station considered the 
turbine unfeasible to compete in the market. The situation changed with the energy crisis of 1973-
74 and the reluctance towards nuclear power, so a new turbine (Tvind-turbine) was built and by 
the end of the decade a test center for wind turbines was set in Risø, partially publicly subsidized 
until 1989, when it was turned into a Feed-in-Tariff. In the meanwhile, the energy sector was 
regulated with a long-term vision: nuclear power was definitely abandoned and, while the  
country’s energy backbone was sustained by coal, more importance was given to combined heat

==PAGE:15==
and power (CPH) small-scale plants and renewable energies. The penetration of wind energy and 
the liberalization of the power market drove power plant operators to try to attain operational 
flexibility of CPH plants. Nowadays, the most advanced of these plants can go from 10 to 100% of 
their nominal capacity. The passage from a few big capacity plants per region to fewer big plants 
surrounded by a multitude of relatively small (Fig. 1.5), decentralized ones required more control 
and regulation over the power system but allowed to cover the growing demand while diversifying 
the energy mix hence ensuring a higher degree of security and less exposition to the fluctuation 
of the market [1.8].  
Another key feature of the power system is the transmission grid. Denmark lies between the 
Scandinavian Peninsula and Central Europe, so the country is divided into two power systems, 
connected via a DC interconnection, one synchronized with the Nordic power system and the 
other with Continental Europe. Interconnector capacity with Germany, Sweden and Norway is 
almost as much as the peak load and electricity is traded via the Nordic Power Exchange, a series  
of international electricity trading markets. The state-owned TSO owns and operates the grid and 
is accountable for securing the supply and the transparency of the market [1.8]. 
 
The number of turbines in Denmark peaked in 2000 with more than 6200 units. The experience 
gained in the previous decades brought to plan systematically the decommissioning of the old 
turbine scattered around, incentivizing a more careful repowering for better land use and, thanks 
to the increasing size and capacity, reducing the overall number of turbines. Nevertheless, the 
shortage of feasible land and the abundance of shallow water conduced the politics to oblige power 
companies to go offshore. The companies were reluctant because of the higher costs and the 
uncertainty related to the “off beaten track”. However, after more than two decades, thank to that 
initial obligation, “small” Denmark is a leader also in the offshore wind market, keeping its 
position as a “giant” of wind power, and now the new farms are assigned with a tender process, 
with the producers awarded with a Feed-in-Tariff [1.8].

1.3 Tenders for energy 
 
In the sector of public procurement, a tender is a formal process of requesting quotations 
for the procurement of goods or services, where, differently from an auction, the winner is 
determined not exclusively by the economic value of the proposal, but also by non-price factors. 
However, being the two processes similar in all the aspects but the determination of the winner, 
in the relative literature the words are used almost as synonyms [1.21]. 
 
The motivation for the choice of a tender as a selection method for public procurement can have 
an economical or strategic base. As from one side the auctioneer can instigate the bidders to 
compete against each other, from the other it is appealing the control retained over volume, 
budget, location, or other variables. Nevertheless, there are risks of non-fulfilment of the targets 
in case of inadequate bidders’ pool and low realization rates: one of the basic requirements for 
successful auctions is to ensure sufficient competition. In a broader sense, there should be a 
balance between the risks from both sides: the auctioneer wants to avoid low competition, 
shortage of supply, non-realisation and high prices, while the bidders risk the failure of the 
projects in case of bids too low for the “winner’s curse” (the circumstance that leads a bidder to 
over-optimistic bidding, resulting in unfeasible economic conditions) or sunk costs [2.7].  If these 
conditions are not balanced, the chance of having unsatisfactory outcomes increases. From the 
auctioneer’s perspective, it is a delicate trade-off between creating the conditions for smaller 
bidders to participate and allowing bigger bidders to bid strategically, speculating on their true 
costs, and creating conditions that avoid collusive behaviors [1.21]. This is particularly true in the 
case of Indonesia, since there is necessity to broaden the wind market. 
 
In procurement auctions for renewable energy at a national level, the tender is held by an agency 
representing the Government, as a Ministry, a regulatory authority or directorate (usually related 
to energy, industry and/or economy), or an operator for the energy market or transmission 
system. Despite providing more control to the auctioneer, this is not sufficient yet to ensure the 
essential security level of investment needed for public procurement. Further preventive 
measures and remedial penalties come into play: material and financial pre-qualifications are 
quite common, as emerges from the AURES II database and as shown in brief in table 1.4 these 
were included in 98.06% and 85.16%, respectively, of the recorded wind auctions in Europe 
during the period under consideration (between 2011 and 2021) [1.22].

The pre-qualifications and the penalties are a precaution for both parties involved. The developer 
can gauge its potential to enter the competition in public procurement, and the auctioneer can 
prevent non-capable participants. Indeed, an uncontrolled process results in a serious threat to 
the developer, especially for smaller companies, but also to the auctioneer. The developer may 
become insolvent creating great trouble to the auctioneer and its aim to secure a contract to get 
installed a certain capacity or a certain energy volume at a fair price [1.23]. 
The auction can be single-item or multi-item (also called single-object or multi-object), namely 
concern one project, or a certain volume that will be fulfilled by several bidders, thus projects. In 
the single-item case, the auctions are usually designed for a specific location, often the project is 
defined to some degree (capacity, point of connection or other characteristics) and there could be 
studies issued by the auctioneer, as wind resources assessment, environmental impact 
assessment, geological studies. The vast majority of the auctions held in Europe during the period 
under consideration in the AURES II study [1.21] were multi-item, as evident from the figures in 
table 1.3. There could be different pricing rules, as, for example, a single-item auction awards 
the sole winning project with the price bid by the bidder (First Price) or the price bid by the 
second-best bidder (Second Price). The latter was thought to contain the risk of winner’s curse. 
The multi-item auctions allow the previous form of pricing and in addition, a common price for 
all the winners. More specifically, in the multi-item context, these are called also Pay-as-Bid, 
Vickery auction and Uniform Price. Pay-as-Bid correspond to the First Price, the Vickery auctions 
have a pricing rule related to the Second Price, with the obvious consequence for multiple winners, 
that each gets the price bidden by the successive bidder, and lastly, according to the Uniform Price 
pricing rule all the winners are granted the price of the last awarded bid of the pool of the auction’s 
winners.

1.4 Sealed Bidding in Game Theory 
 
An auction is a complex process to model since it is subjected to some variables that make 
it difficult to analyze according to deterministic methods, as the bidders have different goals and 
act strategically to pursue these. Game theory models the auction mathematically and logically on 
the basic assumption of players’ “rationality”, in order to determine, in a simplified version of the 
real process, the actions a player should take to win the game, or rather, achieve the best possible 
outcome. The work of brilliant mathematicians and economists came to help revealing useful 
insights even from a simple model. Auctions are characterized by format and information. Or in 
other words, the way the prices are announced and updated, the way participants bid, how the 
participants decide the evaluation of the auctioned object and the auction is closed. In 1950, John 
F. Nash, Jr, generalized the game theory approach to “not zero-sum games” (the auction objective 
is to maximize the value of an object assigning it to the buyer that can make the best use of it), 
then his work was extended to include some private information by William S. Vickery and the 
concept of equilibrium by John C. Harsanyi. The concept of equilibrium brought the theory closer 
to reality, especially with the work of Paul R. Milgrom, who introduced mathematically tractable 
conditions for bidders’ information and the distribution of values across bidders [1.27]. The 
concept was later rigorously demonstrated in the general form, in [1.30]. 
==PAGE:21==
  
 
 In the perspective of game theory, the auctions consist in a process that can be represented as 
a non-cooperative static game with incomplete information, also called Bayesian game. The 
normal form of this game can be derived by modifying the normal form of non-cooperative 
games of complete information with the concept of types and the distribution of beliefs, as 
depicted in the schematic in Fig. 1.6. A more formal explanation, without the presumption of 
considering it exhaustive, is provided in the methodology chapter. 
 
 
Figure 1. 6: Schematic of the Normal form of non-cooperative games with incomplete information 
 
In [1.28], a mathematical model based on bid optimization is described. The “optimal bid” is 
interpreted as the maximization of the product of the probability of winning the auction and the 
payoff. Aside from the fundamental concepts of Nash equilibrium and optimal bid, the model 
investigates the different outcomes due to the distribution of costs for single-item First Price 
Sealed-bid Auctions (FPSBA) for uniform and triangular costs distribution.  
 
This thesis broadens the target to single and multi-item Sealed-bid auctions, which are the 
primary mechanisms utilized for wind tenders, while specifying it for the immature Indonesian 
wind market with the perspective that the costs encountered by the developers will most likely 
decrease [1.31] hence choosing the triangular distribution [1.28]. The mathematical approach 
aiming to optimizing the bid in [1.28] has been used to implement a more comprehensive model 
that includes other awarding mechanisms. The winner of this type of auction can, in fact, be 
awarded according to different formats, namely,  First Price, Second Price and, for multi-item 
auctions, Uniform Price and Vickery auction, that is, the conversion of Second Price awarding 
mechanism for multi-item sealed bid auctions. Furthermore, the model proposed in [1.28] had 
structural limitations on the number of players, unsuitable to track a market in development and 
its mathematical formulation is unclear for the triangular distribution of costs. These limitations 
have been overcome by restructuring the model with a novel, more flexible architecture and 
providing a more robust mathematical background that supports the use of parameters, as a 
realistic costs interval and a broad number of bidders. Thus, aside from the existence of the 
optimal bid demonstrated theoretically for this type of games, this has to be verified in a practical 
way, and the range of parameters that ensure its existence has to be explored. 
 
 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
In this scenario, the investigation focused on answering the following research questions: 
 
1) What are the optimal bid functions expressions for the chosen set of 
parameters? 
 
2) Can the optimum bid function be verified for FPSBA? 
 
3) Does the optimum bid function exist for every set of parameters? 
 
4) What are the outcomes for single-item auctions using the optimal bid function 
for different values of the mode? 
 
5) What are the outcomes for multi-item auctions using the optimal bid function 
for different pricing rules?

2.2 Sealed Bid Auctions in Game Theory 
 
 
As anticipated, the mathematical background for game-theoretic modeling of the sealed-bid 
auction begins with the normal form representation of non-cooperative games with complete 
information and is then extended with the concepts of types and the distribution of beliefs to 
achieve the normal form representation of non-cooperative games with incomplete information. 
2.2.1 Static Games with Complete Information 
 
These simultaneous-move games have no private information: all players are aware of the 
timing of the game, the feasible moves, and the payoffs of all the others. The normal form of the 
non-cooperative game of complete information, labelling it GCI, can be represented, as in [1.28]: 
 
GCI = { A1, … , An; u1, … , un } 
 
where   
A1, … , An are the feasible set of actions of all the n players 
 
 
u1, … , un are the payoff functions  
 
And the game process [2.16]: 
 
1) Player i chooses an action ai from the set Ai                   (for each i) 
2) Player i receives the payoff ui(a1, .. , an), or utility        (for each i) 
 
The assumption for the model is that each player is rational, this means that all participants would 
play a move that maximizes their profit.  
 
There are different strategies in determining How to win a game, but the objective is to 
understand the best way to play, and, in the case of auctions, how to maximize the profit, 
expressed as payoff multiplied by the probability of victory. To achieve that, an indirect approach 
could be to determine the moves not to play. In other words, it is possible to analyze the game by 
looking at the set of feasible actions for all the players and excluding the less desirable: this is the 
approach of the Iterated Elimination of Dominated Strategies [2.16]. 
This method compares all the possible outcomes given by the combination of all feasible actions 
of each player. One player at the time is considered: in case a move results in better outcomes for 
any possible move of the other competitors, the action is a dominating strategy. On the contrary, 
a move that results in worse outcomes is defined as dominated. This comparison can be iterated 
further to find all the “irrational” moves and, according to the assumption of rational players, 
these moves, even if feasible, should not be played and can, thus, be eliminated from the analysis.  
Unfortunately, this approach has drawbacks: it would require players to become aware of other 
players’ dominated strategies, and the level of complexity increases with the number of players. 
Furthermore, there are cases when it is not possible to determine the dominated strategies[2.16].  
 
A more general approach, introduced by John F. Nash Jr., aims instead to determine the outcomes 
that can not be the solution. Once these are excluded, the remaining solutions can be analyzed, 
taking into consideration that there might not be a solution better than all the others. The best 
solution of the game would provide each player with a strategy that represents the best response 
to the other players’ strategies (mutual-best-response). This set of strategies, which is defined as 
==PAGE:31==
Nash Equilibrium (NE), is then “self-enforcing” (or strategically stable) because all the players 
would hold to the best response. From the opposite perspective, a set of strategies that would have 
even only one player deviating from its strategy, cannot be a NE. This, however, does not provide 
any information about the efficiency of the solution, in other words, whether the set of NE 
provides the highest payoff for all players. Moreover, in case more than one set of NE exist, the 
best has to be evaluated case by case [2.16].  
Games that require outguessing the other players do not admit a set of strategies that satisfy NE 
conditions (mutual-best-response). On the contrary, these games require players to conceal their 
strategies. This requirement sets the introduction for the idea of games with “Incomplete 
Information”, or Bayesian games [2.16]. 
 
2.2.2 Static Games with Incomplete Information 
 
These simultaneous-move games have some degree of private information: at least one 
player is unaware of another player’s feasible moves and/or the payoffs. The sealed bid auctions 
constitute a classical example. In this case, all bidders are aware of their own strategy, not of 
others’. 
 
 To represent the fact that a player knows its payoff function but is uncertain of others’, the payoff 
ui  can be written as ui (a1, .. , an; ti), where ti is the player i’s type,  belonging to the player i’s type 
space, Ti  (ti ϵ Ti). Each type ti  can be associated with a different payoff function that player i may 
have, because in an auction, the payoff depends on the bids of all players but also on the individual 
bidder valuation of the good, that is in this case the player’s type, ti. In these terms, the statement 
“player i knows its payoff function” is equivalent to “player i knows its type” and “player i is 
uncertain about others’ payoff function” is equivalent to “player i is uncertain about others’ type” 
(as in an auction each bidder is unaware of other’ valuation of the good). The uncertainty, or the 
player i’s belief about player j’s type, can be represented by a probability distribution p(ti  | tj  ). It 
is assumed that the players’ types are independent, so p does not depend on ti and thus, the player 
i’s belief about player j’s type can be expressed as p( tj  ). In the model presented here, the type 
distribution is assumed to be triangular. Harsanyi defined Static Bayesian games joining the 
concept of beliefs and types with the static game of complete information [1.28].  
 
The next step is the definition of equilibrium for Bayesian games, and in order to do so, first, the 
players’ strategy space must be defined.  In Bayesian games, the strategy space is a complete plan 
of actions, it can be defined as the action rule (not a single action, in general, but in this context 
the action rule coincides with the action since the players have one move to make, choosing how 
to bid once they are assigned their type – the cost deriving from the distribution), that specifies 
each action for each possible type and every possible circumstance. Thus, in games with 
incomplete information, the strategy space is built from the types and the actions space: 
 
Player i’s set of possible strategies is the set of all possible functions with range in Ai and 
domain Ti  [2.16]. 
 
It is not necessary for player i to specify actions for each of its possible types, or in other words, 
the player should not be concerned about other possible types since player i’s strategy is the best 
response to player j’s if, for each of player i’s types, the action specified by the strategy for that 
type maximizes i’s payoff, given player i’s belief about player j’s type, and player j’s action rule.  
 
At this point, a Bayesian Nash Equilibrium (BNE) can be defined as a set of strategies that is the 
best mutual response (so that, none of the players has reason to change its strategy) [2.16]

information can be represented as in [1.28]: 
 
GII = { A1, … , An, T1, … , Tn; b1, … , bn u1, … , un } 
 
where

T1, … , Tn are the type spaces  
p1, … , pn are the beliefs

The process of the game, in this case [2.16]: 
 
1) Nature draws type vector t = (t1 , .. , tn ), where each ti  is drawn from the probability 
distribution p( ti  ) over player i’s set of possible types, Ti 
2) Nature reveals ti  to player i but not to the others 
3) Player i chooses an action ai from the set Ai        (for each i) 
4) Player i receives the payoff ui(a1, .. , an; ti)           (for each i) 
 
The fictional entity “Nature”  is introduced in incomplete information games as an impartial being 
that has the function of revealing in secret the randomly extracted type to each player, with no 
interest in the outcome.

2.2.3 The simulation: From costs to payoffs 
 
 
As anticipated, the model here presented is a simplified version of this general approach.  
The strategy space is composed by the type space, represented by the triangular distribution of 
costs that simulates the evaluation of the good (the LCOE of the project), and the action rule, 
characterized by the bid function, bid = bid(costs). The optimal, or NE, is the strategy that 
==PAGE:33==
optimizes the profit, so in this case the function bid = bid(costs) that provides the highest chance 
of winning maximizing the payoff. The sample space of costs for the triangular distribution, [k,h], 
was determined by the LCOE study of the existing wind farms in Indonesia (for the highest cost 
allowed, h) and Vietnam, another country in ASEAN with more developed wind power markets  
(for the lowest cost allowed, k). This choice was driven by the perspective of strong development 
of wind power in Indonesia: the pronounced difference in the market of the two countries in the 
ASEAN region may provide a realistic costs spectrum for the years to come. The bidders’ 
estimated costs are simulated with a triangular distribution function, which better represents 
immature markets characterized by the presence of bidders with pronounced difference in 
strength, or in other words, of developers with different expertise levels, production techniques, 
management control, or other competitive advantages. The mode of the distribution is used as an 
indicator of the development of the market: the more the market is developed, the closer the mode 
is to the lower cost allowed and vice versa. This results in a higher number of bidders with costs 
close to the mode value. The results of the simulations for different modes will be compared to 
assess the impact of a growing level of competition.

The model was implemented in MATLAB in two versions for the single- and multi-item 
auctions. It is divided in four main sections: Input, Game, Storage of results and Analysis, the flow 
chart of the first two sections, common to both the versions, is shown in Fig. 2.12. 
The number of bidders had been used as a parameter of the analysis, considering cases of 15, 20, 
25 and 30 bidders, since the average deduced from the AURES II database resulted to be slightly 
above 20 bidders per auction [1.22].

2.3.2 Multi-Item 
 
This version allows more variations of the settings of the game and  can be used to compare 
the results for different categories of bidders, differentiated by the capacity of the project and 
strength of the bidders. As hinted above, the main difference is the introduction of the capacity 
vector. The pool of bidders was divided into two groups, big capacity and small capacity bidders. 
Furthermore, in the attempt of simulating different competitive pressure, the bidders can be 
divided in groups partitioned in “strong” and “weak” bidders adding the non-optimal strategies 
introduced in the single-item simulations or others elaborated in other contexts.  
 
The difference in capacity is defined in the input phase. Two categories of capacities have been 
used for the simulations: 75MW and 250MW with the capacity cap set to 750MW. These values 
were determined by the assumed capacity target of GoI, 1,5TW by 2025 [2.7], half of which to be 
assigned by direct appointment, while the remaining 750 in a number of auctions in the period 
2020-2025. Since the covid-19 epidemic put a stop to most economic projects and no wind tender 
have been announced until the moment of the finalization of this thesis, it was assumed that the 
full capacity was to be auctioned in a single event. Moreover, it has to be considered that the 
average realization period for multi-item auctioned projects deduced from the AURES database 
is close to three years [1.22] and that this may not include the time needed for the definition of a 
preliminary version of the project (as a reference, the auction for the Anholt offshore wind farm 
had one-year timespan between announcement and placing of the final bid [2.14]) 
 
The choice of the capacity size for small and big capacity bidders was instead decided based on 
the two currently existing commercial-size windfarms of Sidrap (75MW) and Tolo (72MW), and 
the optimistic perspective of an increase in the size of the plants. This set of parameters can easily 
be modified to adjust to more specific conditions. 
Three scenarios were considered with the number of small bidders being 1/3, ½ and 2/3 of the 
total number of players (so that the number of big bidders is, conversely, 2/3, ½ and 1/3).The 
choice of having fixed capacity for the two categories, in the sense that these are not related to the 
number of bidders, have the outcome of a varying capacity ratio (the quotient given by the total 
bid capacity to the capacity cap), an important parameter to evaluate the success of the auctions. 
This was considered a better alternative than fixing the capacity ratio and having the capacity of 
the projects changing on the base of the number of bidders and ratio of small to big capacity 
bidders. The results of different pricing rules in these setting can be compared, namely Pay-as-
Bid, Vickery and Uniform Price.

3.1 Single-Item Model 
 
This thesis proposed a game-theoretic model for FPSBA and tested the optimal bid 
function for the maximization of the bidder profit. The simulation used a triangular distribution 
of the costs, where the mode is used as a proxy of the development of the market. The CDF of the 
costs allowed to explicit the profit (eq 3) given by the probability of victory multiplied by the 
payoff. The optimum bid is then defined maximizing the profit function. This provided a “rational” 
strategy, in the game-theoretic sense.

size currently present in Indonesia, Tolo1 and Sidrap, which provided the higher limit of the costs 
range, h, while the lower limit, k, is the average LCOE for projects in Vietnam, where the market 
is significantly more mature, hence representing a future prospect. This value was further 
decreased to account for the developments of the market in the area.  The reference LCOE values

Chapter 4: Discussion              
                            
The main weak point of the model proposed in this master thesis lies in its purely theoretical 
base. However, due to the private nature of the information held by both the bidders, the projects 
developers and the auctioneers, the agencies that design, organize and manage the auction, this 
was not possible in the context of this master thesis. Thus, the results, although coherent with the 
expectations, cannot be considered reliable for an evaluation of actual auction, at least until 
compared with real-life data. Furthermore, the LCOE analysis used to derive the costs range was 
based on a combination of methods, results and data from various sources. This introduced 
uncertainty that, although not quantified here, has to be considered in case of further use of the 
results achieved. As said, the comparison with real data is crucial to verify the model, and this of 
course is not viable for wind tenders. Nonetheless, since First-Price sealed-bid auctions are used 
in many other fields, the model has the potential to find a more plausible field of application, for 
comparison tests or predictions, as the ad auctions for online platforms like Google, or social 
media like Facebook, TikTok and similar. Especially considering that the model can be easily 
implemented in an open-source syntax like Octave, or with some more adaptations, Python.

4.2 Effects of the extension of the cost range on the statistics 
 
The increase of the upper limit of the cost range allowed to ensure the existence of the 
optimal bid function for the cost range of interest. This came the price: on one hand, the optimal 
bid function provides slightly higher values as shown above, and on the other, the statistical 
considerations had to be dismissed from the current analysis. The reason for this is shown in the 
sketch in Fig. 4.1. The area below the probability density function has always a unitary value (that 
is also the value of the cumulative distribution function at the upper end of the distribution). Once 
the upper end of the distribution is increased, the height of the triangle must decrease, and so 
does the probability of the mode, 2/(h-k). Hence it was not possible for the parameters 
configurations considered draw information from the statistics 
However, it was also the consequence of the choice of using the real LCOE instead of normalized 
parameters. In this case, being possible different kind of transformation, a normalization that 
could provide a range of cost feasible for the analysis might have been found. A simple 
normalization based on the upper limit of the range, was used in [1.28]. This brought to unclear 
results generated by the VBA code developed, and since the author of this thesis has low 
experience with that programming language, was considered wiser to proceed with the real LCOE 
to achieve results that would allow a clearer analysis.

Chapter 5: Conclusions 
 
Indonesia expressed the determination to improve its socio-economic conditions in a 
sustainable manner and requested support from Denmark. This master thesis aspires to provide 
a little contribution to this support in the form of a game-theoretic model that proposes reference 
payoff values for the possible outcomes of the tendering process, in particular the economic 
outcome of the optimal bid intended as Nash equilibrium for First Price sealed-bid auctions 
(FPSBA) with a triangular distribution of the costs. Here are summarised the conclusions of the 
investigated research questions:  
 
1) What are the optimal bids functions expressions for the chosen set of 
parameters? 
 
The optimal bid functions for the two intervals determined by the triangular distribution are 
defined in eq 5 and eq 6, for costs below and above the mode, respectively. These equations 
express the optimal bid represent the Nash equilibrium for the FPSBA as function of the number 
of players, the parameters of the triangular distribution  
 
 
2) Can the existence of a concrete optimum bid function be verified for FPSBA? 
 
The results of the simulations suggest that the mathematical model provides indeed an optimal 
bid function, according to the conditions considered. This was confirmed via the verification of 
the convergency of the optimal bid over an increasing number of games, and via the comparison 
with non-optimal strategies, both optimum-based and costs-based, for different values of the 
mode of the triangular distribution. 
 
 
3) Does the optimum bid function exist for every set of parameters? 
 
The optimal bid function has a wide domain, but the existence of the solution is not guaranteed 
for every set of parameters. This was verified with a parametric study based on the costs range of 
interest. The domain can also be expanded using a normalization technique. However, this would 
require nevertheless a parametric study 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
 
4) What are the outcomes for single-item auctions using the optimal bid function 
for different values of the mode? 
 
The optimal bids are shown in table 3.2 with the relative payoffs in table 3.3 for realistic 
numbers of players for successful auctions. The payoffs for the FPSBA seem to confirm the 
conclusions in [5.1] available in short in [5.2]. For this kind of auction, the bidder earns a positive 
payoff only if it is skilled to precisely estimate its true costs, and nothing that may negatively affect 
the costs exceeds the expected uncertainties. For this reason, there is a risk that the bidders 
exaggerate the costs and the model seems to be coherent with this. The issue may be eliminated 
in case the additional payoff ensured by subsidies are confirmed also for future projects, because, 
assuming the information available about the PPA are reliable, as estimated in table 4.1 in the 
discussion, the theoretical payoff seem sufficient to satisfy any risk allocation analysis. 
 
 
5) What are the outcomes for multi-item auctions using the optimal bid function 
for different pricing rules? 
 
The results of the model are coherent with the expectations. From the auctioneer’s perspective, 
the best pricing rules appear to be, in the lower competition cases, the Pay-as-Bid pricing rule, 
equivalent to the First Price in the single-item case provides lower payoffs (table 3.4 A), while 
when decent competition is present, the Vickery auctions, equivalent to the Second Price pricing 
rule for single-item auctions, appear to award the lowest payoffs. Even more, as stated in [5.2], 
this type of auction incentives the bid of the true costs.  
In all cases, the uniform price pricing rule is greatly advantageous for the stronger bidders and 
may thus be desirable from the bidders’ perspective. From this point of view, the Pay-as-Bid 
pricing rule may still be seen as favourable, even for the lower competition cases, because 
although it may award lower payoff, at the very least it brings less uncertainty, which can make 
the bankability of the project more secure [1.26]. Finally, not much can be said about the weaker 
bidders, who would not have the benefit of the large payoffs in the case of the uniform price pricing 
rule in case of low competition when they have more chance to be awarded. In case the auctioneer 
has an interest in fostering this kind of bidders to widen the market to more developers, probably 
other kinds of solutions have to be pursued, such as a direct appointment or dedicated auctions 
with specific pre-qualifications [1.21].
