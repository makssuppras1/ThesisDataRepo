Summary
Depleting fossil fuels, rising energy demand and provision for a high-reliable power system
have motivated the adoption of DC microgrids. This concept has progressed signiﬁcantly after
its AC counterpart, in order to distribute power in a more eﬃcient and eﬀective approach.
As the implementation of such power systems are at their infancy, various challenges have
emerged. For instance, reliability and stability issues for operating these microgrids in diﬀer-
ent applications under diﬀerent circumstances. Therefore, several scenarios are considered in
this work to analyze some issues at a system level study.
For this purpose, a DC microgrid has been considered and diﬀerent analyses are performed
using MATLAB Simulnik software to present the results in form of system simulations. Also,
a lab experiment is carried out to verify some of the ﬁndings in the simulations. First, a
sensitivity study in occasion of a fault is executed to point out on how diﬀerent components
contribute to a fault characteristics. It is found that fault impedance is the dominant factor
which impacts the voltage variations on DC bus, due to the fact that fault impedance could be
of a high value depending on fault occurrence condition.
Next, DC microgrid performance is analyzed in presence of multiple power converters and
AC utility grid disturbances. Harmonic spectrum results have shown that due to increasing
penetration of PWM based power electronic loads in the DC microgrid systems, the DC bus
spectrum pattern is changing which is totally a new phenomenon compared to the traditional
AC grid. A harmonic study at the Point of Common Coupling (PCC) for a balanced and un-
balanced AC grid is executed to indicate the presence of 3rd harmonic and its multiple which
can be dangerous for distribution transformer. Lab experiment results conﬁrm that DC bus
voltage and harmonics ﬂowing through DC link capacitor can decrease the life-time of DC-link
capacitor of drives. Hence, because of possible disturbances in AC grid, care should be taken
into account once selecting the components for DC microgrid power electronic sources and
loads.
In the last case study, the performance of the DC microgrid is analyzed under fast vary-
ing load for two diﬀerent modes of operation, grid connected and grid isolated (island mode).
Therefore, an Energy Storage System (ESS) is integrated to the DC microgrid through a DC/DC
converter to create the island mode operation in the simulation. The simulation results have
inferred that the performance in DC bus is degraded in the two diﬀerent modes of operation
under pulse load condition. So, the need to investigate for a proper control alternative to
improve the voltage stability and system performance is of importance.

future, more concrete analysis is needed to ensure the system reliability and stability for

CHAPTER1
Introduction
Climate change has been one of the major concerns for human being in the 21st century.
Concentration of greenhouse gases and global warming has been increasing the temperature
of earth aﬀecting human health and a wide range of environmental impacts on the earth. The
conventional electricity power plants are the leading source of producing electric energy and
enormous eﬀort has taken place to increase the eﬃciency and reduce the respective environ-
mental impacts of these power generating units. It is admitted that extreme use of fossil fuels
will result in draining these mortal reserves, specially coal, beside its huge negative impact on
the environment [1]. The increasing demand of energy from developing countries has made
them to invest more and more on building more infrastructure for conventional power plants
and yet with a low eﬃciency. Hence this increase in energy demand has made the energy
sectors to provide the security of supply through usage of excessive amount of coal which
carries massive amount of carbon [2].
The emergence of renewable technologies has provided a clean alternative to the aforemen-
tioned issues, diminishing environmental impacts and a feasible replacement to the traditional
power plants. Renewable energy sources (RES) can fulﬁll a high volume of energy demand
by harnessing various sources of immortal energies. Bio energy, wind and solar power are the
prominent sources which have developed immensely over the past years and is forecasted to
advance and become more aﬀordable as time passes by. Many research is under development
to utilize RES to supply the energy requirements with almost zero emissions, and to make it
achievable and to solve the problems of supplying the energy locally, development of remote
areas etc [1]. However, the penetration and integration of these technologies has its own
respective technical challenges like grid interconnection, regulatory frameworks, managing
intermittent supply, market liberalization and so on.
Electric power systems have been under signiﬁcant changes due to variations in both supply
and demand automations. There is a large transition from supply with synchronous generators
and the penetration of renewables, and from demand side the rise of variable and distributed
generation sources as well as the progress of power electronic technology at large [3]. Due to the
intermittent feature of renewables, the traditional power distribution systems have undergone
substantial transformations to improve reliability, performance and aﬀordability.

1 Introduction
1.1
Background
The concept of microgrid was introduced to manage the distributed generation (DG) in an
eﬀective way. The primary application was to be AC and it is capable of operating in grid
connected as well as in isolated operation, known as island mode, thanks to the advanced
energy storage systems (ESSs) to back up the microgrid once disconnected from the utility.
DC microgrid has emerged after its AC counterpart to distribute power in a more eﬃcient and
eﬀective approach [4].
Progress in power electronics has contributed to a great extend to make DC microgrids
interesting for further research. In a DC microgrid, PVs, ESS, loads and generating units
which are DC in nature do not need DC/AC converters to connect at PCC of AC utility grid.
For instance, Power in a PV system must ﬂow through converter into DC bus and then dis-
tributed to loads or ESSs. The distribution to theses devices can be performed with or without
utilizing a conversion level depending on the DC bus voltage level which makes the system
even more economical. Similarly, as shown in ﬁgure 1.1, battery systems can be both directly
or indirectly connected through power electronic converter to the common DC bus provided
that the suitable voltage is interfaced with the batterys feature. In wind turbines, power has to
be converted from AC to DC and from that interface regulate to 50 Hz by a second conversion
for AC utilities. Hence, DC grid systems can perform eﬃciently by choosing an appropriate
voltage level so that some conversion stages can be removed [5].
Implementaion complexities of DC microgrids are still under research and yet, more
analysis is needed and it requires more considerations in such complex networks to ensure the
system reliability.

1.2 Objectives

1.1.1
Problem Formulation
At this moment implementation of DC microgrids is a hot topic for researchers and indus-
tries within various applications. With the fast pace of its development as smart and eﬃcient
distribution systems, and at the same time the rise in RESs penetration can make it as com-
plimentary where reliability, eﬃciency, performance and cost are of great concern. However,
there are many challenges raised to address diﬀerent issues of such implementation in diﬀerent
industries such as marine, Electric Vehicles (EVs), industrial and urban networks, data canters
and so on.
In a DC microgrid system if a heavy load is connected or disconnected from the system
(for any reason) like in case of a fault or use of pulse loads, DC bus voltage undergoes some
oscillations. These oscillations can propagate whole network including connected devices or
converters. Similarly, variety of disturbances from utility grid can cause abnormal operation
of DC bus system and may result in some stability issues. Hence, it is essential for the devices
to tolerate these abnormality at DC bus system. Therefore, it is important to analyze these
scenarios at a system level where a number of power generation units, storage systems and
nonlinear loads are connected to the same DC bus.
1.2
Objectives
The main idea of this thesis is to investigate diﬀerent scenarios and analyze how diﬀerent
disturbances aﬀect the performance (voltage stability) of a selected DC microgrid conﬁguration.
The considered three cases are as follow:
1. Fault sensitivity study in presence of diﬀerent component impedances.
2. The impact of nonlinear loads on DC bus and AC grid side.
3. The eﬀect of pulse load on DC bus for grid-connected mode and island mode.
The objective is to analyze the impact of some factors on the reliability of such hybrid
AC/DC networks. More speciﬁcally, the goal is to elaborate on the mentioned objectives by
studying three case studies which include running simulations on a given microgrid and lab
tests to verify some of the ﬁndings.

CHAPTER2
Literature Review
2.1
DC Micogrids
DC microgrids have proven to be an impressive technology by the recent developments in the
modern electrical grids and their utilization is widely argued [5]. In the following section
advantages and disadvantages of implementing this technology and a comparison with its AC
counterpart is brieﬂy presented, furthermore diﬀerent applications and issues are reviewed.
DC microgrid adoption will beneﬁt in a variety of perspectives:
• Using less number of power conversion units, converters, as well as skipping costly
centralized power generation units will both reduce the CO2 emission level and making
the system more economical [6],[7].
• Rural electriﬁcation is what many developing countries are lately dedicating large invest-
ments. In many regions there is not the capacity to build the infrastructure for centralized
power plants as well as the respective required transmission lines. DC microgrids are
expected to be one alternative to compensate for this issue, therefore research and devel-
opments are executed to analyze the implementation and life cycle cost of such systems
[6].
• System eﬃciency in DC system can be higher comparing to AC, utilization of less
semiconductor components in the power electronics used in LVDC leading to less losses
in the system [8],[9]. It is also addressed in [4] that the overall conversion between AC
and DC has a direct relation on the tradeoﬀamong the conversion losses and the reduced
conversions.
• In AC systems power is delivered in RMS value, on the contrary in DC systems power
is delivered in its peak value which makes it
√
2 times more than that of in AC [10].
Besides, there is no skin eﬀect in DC systems and current is distributed equally in the
cables cross section area leading to using smaller cable size for the same amount of
power which is transferred in AC. Skin eﬀect causes more losses in AC since current
passes through the outer section of the cable [10].
• Unlike AC system where the regulation is reached through frequency, in DC grids
voltage is the only essential concern to stabilize the system and there is no reactive power
regulation, which increases the stability and power delivery in the system [4].
All that being mentioned, there are several issues and challenges raised by the evolution of DC
microgrids that demand more research and attention which are indicated as follow:

2 Literature Review
•
One of the serious issues with DC systems is protection, thanks to the zero-crossing
nature of the AC systems which makes it straightforward to detect the arc and trip the
switches in occasion of the fault in AC system. But in DC systems the zero crossing
does not exist, thus it requires complicated approach to provide the appropriate safety
requirements which makes the system costly to implement DC circuit breakers [11].
Furthermore, system grounding is what makes the system design of the DC microgrid
complicated once compared to AC systems, particularly where the DC system is inter-
faced with AC and other RESs [12].
• There are various aspects which has to be reevaluated including earthing concept, cor-
rosion, fault detection, over current and over voltage protections in LVDC. Because the
nature of AC and DC diﬀer from each other it requires that new standardization and
same provisions and attitudes in AC to be implemented in DC systems considering its
requirements. For instance, voltage level standardization is the primary step to start the
DC implementation [13].
• Utilizing DC systems can decrease the system reliability due to the higher number
of implementation of power electronics.
A challenge arises considering the impact
of lifetime of the components in power electronics comparing to traditional network
elements resulting in reliability issues as well as higher cost. Although analysis and
research has taken place to evaluate the power electronics with modern reliability [14],
there is still more development required to assess the component and system level
reliability in DC grids [15].
• The coordinated control systems in DC is more complicated than its AC counterpart.
Control is assorted into local and various levels depending on the functionalities and the
communication method which makes its operation rather demanding in LVDC [16].
2.2
Applications of DC Microgrids
DC microgrids are expected to retain in stable operation within high power quality and pro-
vide diﬀerent performances such as coordination of internal units, grid support eﬀectiveness,
stability margin during transients, voltage stability in the main DC bus, coordinated control,
communication and grid support capability. Additionally, the system has to be designed in
such a way to comprise redundancy in power supply, ﬂexibility in conﬁguration under fault
condition and scalability [17].
Due to high assets of DC microgrids, there are many applications where DC microgrids
are utilized and because of this variation in applications, there are speciﬁc topologies and
architectures designed depending on the function area and system requirements to fulﬁll the
reliability, performance and economic speciﬁcations and aspects.

2.2.1
Shipboard DC power systems
Maine power systems have undergone considerable research and development over the past
years. More regulations are imposed on environmental friendly technologies to reduce the
emissions resulting in amendments in fuel eﬃciency strategies in the conventional systems.
Replacement of traditional mechanical systems with electrical propulsion systems in vessels
is an example of these developments which presents the new concepts of the All-Electric Ship
(AES). One of its new features is the Integrated Power System (IPS) which keeps the system
feeding the loads through a smart way of decreasing the number of generators [18]. This is
considered as an on-board DC microgrid distribution system as shown in ﬁgure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: An on-board DC grid example [19].
The interconnected topology in this system is designed based on improving the reliability
of feeders in case of a fault which is achieved by connecting the supplies through diﬀerent zones
in the main DC bus. It contains diﬀerent power converters for diﬀerent zones depending on the

2.3
Faults in DC Power System
It is imperative to study and analyze power systems under failure and fault conditions to en-
sure the stability and reliability of any power system as well as to fulﬁll the requirements for
selecting the protection equipment and the right rating of components in the system. The
occurrence of a fault could be at any time and at any location, it can inﬂuence the operating
condition of the system with diﬀerent impacts throughout the network and elements. Faults
could be described as a massive current ﬂow in an abnormal path within the network which
can create immense damage to equipment resulting in power interruptions, system imbalance,
system unavailability, ﬁre or explosion, individuals injury and so on. Faults can occur due
to insulation failure, physical damage, lightening, heavy winds, excessive loading, animals
shorting lines, falling trees, human error and so on.
Being relatively a new subject, LVDC microgrids are in their infancy stage comparing
to its AC counterpart and there are not many fault investigations and analysis performed.

2.3.1
Fault types
Possible faults in DC microgrids can take place at any points in the system and terminal units
like DC/DC and other types of converters, grid connected voltage source converters (VSC)
and connected loads. On the other side, network faults can occur at feeders, DC main bus and
transmission lines.
Pole-to-pole and pole-to-ground faults are the common types in a DC microgrid. The
former, also known as line-to-line(L-L) or short circuit fault, occurs once there is a path be-
tween the positive and neutral (in case of having a bipolar conﬁguration, symmetrically loaded
system) or negative pole of the system [28] as indicated in ﬁgure 2.7, and this type of fault
often has a low fault impedance.
The pole-to-ground fault, also known as line-to-ground(L-G) or earth fault, happens when
there is a path between ground and a conductor with either positive or negative polarity as
shown in ﬁgure 2.7. Voltage variation in this case depends on which method of grounding
is used [5],[17],[29]as well as the fault impedance being high or low. However, it should be
mentioned that fault impedance itself depends on diﬀerent parameters like cable arrangement,
fault point and so on.
Figure 2.7: Fault types in DC system: (a) Pole-to-ground-(b) pole-to-pole .
These faults are severe for the whole network, specially converters and batteries, hence the
investigation of these faults is crucial to understand their impact on the non-faulty devices in
the system. Faults occurring in the main DC bus can inﬂuence all the connecting loads and
sources depending on their operating mode. Faults happening in feeders also could have the
same impact, particularly on the loads, depending on the location of the fault.
As mentioned, there are diﬀerent possible fault zones in a DC microgrid like in distributed
generations (DGs), loads and DC bus. For each of the faults in these units, consequently the
fault goes under its respective zone as it is indicated in ﬁgure 2.8. Hence, the detection and
isolation of faults is another crucial factor to be considered in designing DC grids, and research

Harmonics
Power system harmonics has always been an area that attracts a great attention to both industry
and academia. This is mostly because of the non-linear (or harmonic producing) loads that
are increasing in various industrial and domestic applications. The escalation in proportion of
non-linear loads has made more recommendations in IEEE Std.519 (Recommended Practice
and Requirements for Harmonic Control in Electric Power Systems) and more limitation laid
down by utilities. The power grid can be easily polluted by a wide variety of consumers which
generate current harmonics. Consequently, other equipment which are connected to the same
network would be aﬀected. The knowledge on this area can help to develop power system
reliability and highlights the importance of keeping harmonic levels as minimum as possible
which cannot be neglected when it comes to considering optimum operation of grids.

2.5
Load Variation Effects on Voltage Stability
The next generation of future technologies which apply DC grids will be vastly diﬀerent, for
instance new ship designs include more complicated electrical infrastructures which will be
deployed in AESs. There are considerable number of research going on in order to accurately
investigate the behavior of such systems under various operation modes in a power system. All
the electrical networks are to operate safely and in a required stable situation in power systems,
and this stability comes into more consideration once dealing with loads and their mode of
operation in grids. Therefore, it is imperative to study how loads are integrated within a power
system and in dynamic coordination with other components.
The frequent connection and disconnection of loads in power systems result in transients
that make voltage level ﬂuctuation. These oscillations need to be within a standard range in
the network, and values beyond these range make the system unstable. In LVDC microgrids,
particularly in ship power systems, there are diverse types of loads such as electromagnetic
guns, launch systems, hotels and free electron lasers that cause current spikes in a periodic
style. Generator ramp rate is an important characteristic in designing AESs. Ramp rate is
the acceleration or deceleration of output power per minute which is often in MW/minute
[44]. The pulse load ramp rate in a ship power system could be several times higher than a
generator ramp rate, which will result in a highly unbalance power among loads and generation
units causing instabilities. This point highlights the importance of ESS to cover for these
abnormalities and compensate for the intense power demand of pulse loads.

Harmonic Analysis
With the emergence of modern power semiconductor devices, the area of power electronics
has made huge progress in recent years. As new technologies are evolving, the respective
issues from the applications will also arise for which new considerations should be taken into
account. As reviewed in chapter 2, one of the issues regarding the development of power
electronic converters is power quality which has huge impact on the reliability and economy
of any power systems. Harmonic distortion is one of the critical concerns in the modern
era of power systems. High harmonic distortion is added to grids due to the increasing use
of nonlinear industrial loads such as variable frequency drives and power converters. These
distortions cause the equipment to heat, disrupt circuit breakers, reduce lifetime of the power
factor correction capacitor and overheat transformers etc [57].
Impacts of power electronic converters are well known facts in AC grid systems, but it
is often overlooked in DC grids. As DC microgrid is an emerging technology, there is an
increasing number of researchers working in this area. In order to mature this technology, it
is important to carefully analyze all aspects of this technology including harmonics and power
quality.
In a DC microgrid, multiple power electronic converters (such as PWM inverters) are
connected at common DC bus. These power converters can have diﬀerent conﬁgurations,
Switching Frequency ( fsw) and load proﬁles which can create diﬀerent pattern at DC bus.
This kind of situation is totally new comparing to its AC counterpart, where drives having
their separate DC bus system. Therefore, it is important to analyze impacts of multiple power
converters system at common DC bus.
Most of DC microgrid systems are normally interfaced with AC utility grid in order to
ensure power ﬂow between AC and DC system in the event of increased load demands or
surplus generation from RESs in DC microgrid system. It is well known that AC utility grid
suﬀers from a number of power quality issues such as voltage sag, swell, unbalanced and so
on. Recent power quality surveys show that voltage unbalance is one of major power quality
issue in AC distribution networks [58]. These AC grid power quality disturbances can also
aﬀect DC microgrid operation.
To further investigate DC microgrid performance in presence of multiple power converters
and AC utility grid disturbances, a case study has been implemented in this chapter. For this
investigation, a DC microgrid system under consideration is shown in ﬁgure 5.1, where three

number of additional harmonic components that are not just integer multiple of these switch-

multiples. This phenomenon is called Intermodulation Distortion. This can be understood by
following mathematical expressions.
There are three motor drives in the considered DC microgrid system and each drive operates
at a diﬀerent switching frequency. Let us assume here fsw1, fsw2 and fsw3. The input DC-link
current signal generated by three motor drives will be:
S(t) = A1sin(2π fsw1t + ϕ1) + A2sin(2π fsw2t + ϕ2) + A3sin(2π fsw3t + ϕ3)
(5.1)
Where A and ϕ are the amplitude and phase angle of three diﬀerent components. The output
signal at DC bus as DC-line current will be:
I(t) = N(S(t))
(5.2)
Where N is a non-linear function. Signal I(t) contains the component of three frequencies of
input signal and linear combinations of their respective switching frequencies such as:
m1 fsw1 + m2 fsw2 + m3 fsw3
(5.3)

Due to its ﬂexibility, DC microgrids are widely used in a number of applications. In some
applications, load variation is very fast, such as military vessel and industrial applications
where a repetitive pattern of load is utilized such as drilling. This fast load variation can create
unwanted oscillation at DC bus, which may results stability issues for the whole microgrid
system. Impacts of fast load variation can be diﬀerent in grid connected and island mode.
Therefore, in this chapter, both conﬁguration of DC microgrid system have been employed and
analyzed under fast varying (or pulse) load conditions.
This analysis is performed to analyze how a DC microgrid behaves diﬀerently in grid-
connected or isolated mode (Island Mode) in the presences of pulse load condition. A com-
parative analysis is performed for each of the modes and time domain results are presented.
System consideration for this study includes three drives and a variable resistive pulse load
connected at the common DC bus. Furthermore, an ESS is included to feed the system during
the time when AC utility is unavailable. The ESS is connected to the DC bus through a
bidirectional isolated DC/DC converter, single phase Dual Active Bridge (DAB) topology has
been considered, which is very common in such applications. In grid connected mode, it is
assumed that the battery is fully charged and since the DC grid is fed by the AC utility only,
ESS is not connected as indicated in ﬁgure 6.1 and system speciﬁcations are given in table 3.1
(in chapter 3). Pulse load is applied with 150 ms duration, each is applied for three times.

CHAPTER7
Conclusion
This project has pointed out some challenges which will be common issues in future as the
demand for eﬃcient and reliable DC microgrid system is increased.
The main idea of this thesis was to investigate diﬀerent scenarios and analyze how diﬀerent
disturbances aﬀect the performance (voltage stability) of a chosen DC microgrid. Diﬀerent
analyses under diﬀerent assumptions were performed using MATLAB Simulnik software to
present the results in form of system simulations along with a lab test experiment to verify some
of the results. Prior to system simulations, an overview to the previous works and theories
related to DC microgrids and their applications in various industries have been reviewed. Also,
some theoretical approach to fault calculations, harmonic analysis and pulse load eﬀects have
been summarized.
In the ﬁrst scenario, the considered DC microgrid system is studied under a Line-to-Line
(L-L) fault on DC bus and a sensitivity analysis is performed to analyze the impact of four
diﬀerent impedance elements (cable impedance, fault impedance, internal impedance of utility
grid, internal impedance of diodes in rectiﬁer) on the voltage level and ﬂuctuations. It is found
that fault impedance is the dominant factor which impacts the voltage variation on DC bus.
Also, cable impedance is another important factor when it comes to the conﬁguration of the
grid which highly depends on its speciﬁc application. Hence, to manage the grid stability and
reliability, these considerations should be taken into account when designing DC microgrid
systems.
In the next case study, the impacts of multiple power converters at common DC bus have
been observed and results show that due to increasing penetration of PWM based power elec-
tronic loads in the DC microgrid system, the DC bus spectrum pattern is changing, which
is totally a new phenomenon compared to the traditional AC grid or microgrid. Therefore,
is important to analyze how this new pattern would impact DC microgrid stability and ﬁlter
design of power electronic converters such as in motor drives.
Furthermore, analyses are performed in grid connected DC microgrid system to show what
kind of changes can be seen at PCC when AC utility grid changes from balanced to various
unbalanced conditions. The harmonic performance at PCC has been analyzed and it implies
that 3rd harmonics and its multiple can be observed once AC utility enters to an unbalance
condition and it is intensiﬁed by the increase in unbalance magnitude from 3% to 10% which
can be dangerous for distribution transformer. Also, the impact of AC grid voltage unbalanced
on DC bus has been investigated and it is found that the unbalanced condition increases the
==PAGE:76==
62

ripple in DC bus voltage and current as the unbalance condition becomes higher. This can
create stability issues to DC microgrid operation and stress diﬀerent components of motor
drives such as DC-link capacitors, inductor, IGBT modules and so on. Lab experiment results
validate that DC bus voltage and harmonics ﬂowing through DC link capacitor can decrease
the life-time of DC-link capacitor of drives. Hence, due to possible disturbances in AC grid,
care should be taken into account once selecting the components for DC microgrid power
electronic sources and loads.
In the last case study, the performance of the DC microgrid is analyzed under fast varying
load for two diﬀerent modes of operation, grid connected and grid isolated (island mode).
An ESS is integrated to the DC microgrid through a DC/DC converter to create the island
mode operation in the simulation. The simulation results have inferred that the performance
in DC bus is degraded in diﬀerent ways under two diﬀerent modes of operation during pulse
load condition. Therefore, in island mode, implementing a concrete control over the DC/DC
converter is vital.
