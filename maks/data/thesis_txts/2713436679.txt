Abstract
This project presents theoretical and experimental investigations of
an alternative solution to Windar Photonics’ alignment setup to deter-
mine the remote focus distance of a wind LIDAR system. The proposed
system is a compact telescope alignment setup, which is based on a Shack-
Hartmann wavefront sensor, which has the ability to determine the focus
position of the beam. The proposed system has the upper hand in size,
since the current system used at Windar Photonics takes up over 1.5m
in length, while the compact telescope setup takes up less than 50cm in
length.
The performance of the proposed system would be tested and
evaluated
The project will work with both a He-Ne laser setup and a 1532 nm
infrared (IR) laser setup.
These two parts both have clear intentions.
The He-Ne setup will serve as ”practice” to the IR setup, since it will
show potential issues. The IR setup has the intention of showcasing the
performance of the system.
The experimental investigations consists of measuring the focus po-
sition from the baseline system that is used at Windar Photonics and
measurements conducted with the compact telescope alignment setup.
The data from the focus position measurements would serve as a refer-
ence data set and the compact telescope setup would be compared to this.
The Data will be compared to simulations which will give an indication
of how well the system performs compared to theoretical expectations.
The results gathered from the experimental investigations of the IR
setup are inconclusive on the performance of the system. The system has
a trend fairly close to the theoretical expectations, but there seems to
be various errors. This is noticeable for the Shack-Hartmann based setup
when the focus position is at a great distance, which are the distances that
would be important for Windar Photonics. An error could potentially be
due to incorrect models, which can especially be seen for shorter focus
distances when measuring the remote focus distance in a conventional
method, since the Gaussian beam becomes more truncated.
The results might be inconclusive, but the system does seem to have
potential, not only due to its size, but also the performance and the gains
that could be made from optimizing the system further.

Introduction
Aligning a laser system to great distance accurately, while also keeping the
alignment system compact is a challenge that Windar Photonics is facing. In its
current iteration Windar Photonics is using a fairly bulky system using a beam
profiler to determine the focus of their LIDAR system [1, 2]. This master thesis
has the intention of exploring an alternative solution to determining the focus
distance. The system to be explored is based on a Shack-Hartmann wavefront
sensor, which will be used to find the radius of curvature of a Gaussian beam
transmitted by the baseline LIDAR telescope and from this the remote focus
distance. The goal is both to provide a system that is smaller and provides
at least the same accuracy if not better. A conventional measuring method, a
beam profiler based setup and the proposed Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor
system can be seen in Figure 1 where the laser, circulator and the two lenses f8
and f216 (denoted L3 and L4, respectively) are what will be called the baseline
system throughout the thesis.
Figure 1: Schematics for a conventional method, a beam profiler based setup
and the proposed Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor based setup used for de-
termining the remote focus distance for a LIDAR system
The work that is to be conducted will be split up in two parts. The first
5

part will consist of working on a He-Ne laser setup. This section can be seen
as a practice for the second part, which is an infra-red (IR) setup as used by
Windar Photonics. The choice of working with a He-Ne laser initially is due to
the fact that it is visible light, which means that it will be easier and safer to
work with in a practical matter. The He-Ne setup will also give an indication
of what has to be done differently and potential issues when moving over to
the IR setup. The second part will be the IR setup, which will take advantage
of the knowledge gained from the first part with the He-Ne setup. This will
also showcase the performance of the system, and as such the viability of the
proposed system can be analyzed.
The two parts will include their own simulations that have the aim of setting
up models that can describe the focus position of the system. In section 2 the
theoretical considerations will be made. In section 3 the experimental setups

based on the theory presented in section 2. This includes some proof of concept
simulations and then simulations to the behaviour of the Gaussian beam. In
section 5 the results from the experimental work will be presented and discussed
for both the He-Ne and IR setup. This section will give an indication of the
performance of the proposed system to measure the focus position of the LIDAR
system. Section 6 will bring conclusions and a summary to the report based on
all of the prior sections and finally the last section will present some outlook
and recommendations to potential future work with the proposed system.

2.3
Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor
The Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor is a type of sensor that can be used
gather information on the wavefront of an incident beam. This is achieved by
using a microlens array, which divides the beam into an array of intensity spots.
The measured data can be analyzed with various methods and tools. In this
case the spot displacement and phase gradient will be the key parameters used
for the analysis. An example of the concept of a Shack-Hartmann wavefront
sensor can be seen in Figure 8.
(a) Planar Wavefront
(b) Distorted wavefront
Figure 8: A representation of a planar wavefront and a distorted wavefront
incident on a lenslet array and the image that a camera plane would capture in
these two situations. [7]
To measure the wavefront, the incident beam is aimed at the lenslet array.
Each lenslet collects the light and then focuses it onto a camera sensor (CMOS
for example), which is located at the focal plane of the microlens array. The
camera will capture a spot for each lenslet that is hit by the beam.
If the
wavefront is a plane wave, the focus spots are going to be centered relative

3.3
Shack-Hartmann He-Ne
The work with the He-Ne Shack-Hartmann setup had a few different versions
throughout the project. Initially the setup only made use of the first telescope
which consists of the L3 (f = 8 mm) and L4 (f = 216 mm) lenses.
L4 is
mounted on a translation stage, and with this the distance of the focus can be
manipulated, which will correspond to the radius of curvature. The IR setup
will of course also have L4 on a translation stage. This translation in the z-
direction will be denoted as ∆z and is the distance the L4 lens has been moved
away from its start setting, which is a state where the system is collimated.
The best case scenario would have been if this setup could be used to deter-
mine a wavefront curvature, but since this part of the setup is meant to have
an outgoing beam that is focusing very far away, it would have a large radius of
curvature for the wavefront and as such it would be very difficult to capture spot
displacements through an image. This basic setup would be used to measure the
distance of the focus in a long hallway, since these measurements would be the

reference to aim for with the Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor measurements.
An image of the setup that was going to be used for the focus test can be seen
in Figure 14 and a more detailed picture can be seen in Figure 15. The latter
picture includes the additional components for the Shack-Hartmann wavefront
sensor operation, but everything before the lenslet array is the baseline setup.
Figure 14: The baseline setup just containing L3, L4 and the 3x beam ex-
pander, which would be used to measure the distance of the focus in a hallway.
The beam expander was aligned to collimate the beam with approximately 1mm
radius. Since the hallway was only 40m long, and the region of interest includes
focus positions beyond 40m, the beam would be folded back using a mirror so
that a longer distance could be achieved.
The focus measurements were fairly simple. The focus point of the system
was adjusted to various axial positions, and then the focus was determined
visually. For farther beam focus positions this does increase the margin of error
since the depth of focus is stretched further.

Simulations (He-Ne and IR)
The simulation section will consist of the different simulations performed during
the project.
Some of the simulations are for demonstration purposes, while
others are meant to evaluate theoretical values for the various experiments, so
that the theory established can be compared to real world experimental data.
The various scripts for the simulations can be seen in Appdenix A.4 and A.5.
All of the simulations are based on the assumption of perfect Gaussian beams
and uniform wavefronts.
4.1
Lenslet spots (Plane wave and curved wavefront)
The lenslet array outputs an array of focus spots, where each spot corresponds
to a single lenset. This was simulated, so that experimental data could be com-
pared to the corresponding theory. It has to be mentioned that these simulations
do not include the Gaussian intensity profile. This means that all of the spots
will be of equal intensity, which is incorrect but is not an issue, since intensity
measurements are not key to the project. Initially a plane wave incident on the
lenslet array was simulated. The simulation corresponds to the He-Ne setup,
and the image plane corresponds to the ThorLabs Silicon camera used (See ap-
pendix A.1). The image that a camera would capture can be seen in Figure
28.

of the beam is aimed at the top right (Matlab plots y inversely) corner of the
camera plane. The intensity is normalized.
As mentioned in the theory section, the plane wave will produce an output

4.2
Intensity centroid simulation
A test for the intensity centroid calculations was also conducted. It made use
of the simulations in the previous section, by taking the plane wave image as
a reference, and comparing it to the curved wavefront. The simulation finds
the centre of the spot produced by the lenset array, both by using the intensity
centroid calculation as well as finding the peak of the lenslet spot. The intention
is to showcase that by finding the intensity centroid, the actual displacement of
the spot can be measured, rather than measuring which pixel has the highest
intensity. This is due the ”max” function used in Matlab to determine the peak
of a spot is bound to a pre-defined grid, which corresponds to the data from the
camera chip.
The simulation made for this purpose simulates one intensity spot, which
corresponds to the spot coming from the furthest corner lenslet. This will lead
to the largest displacement and the larger a displacement is, the higher the
accuracy of the measurement will be.
For the experimental part it will not
necessarily be possible to use an outer edge of the Gaussian beam since the
intensity might be too low to capture with the camera. The intensity spot for
a plane wavefront and a curved wavefront can be seen in Figure 31.

(a) Planar Wavefront
(b) Curved wavefront
Figure 31: Simulation of two intensity spots that originate from the same
lenslet, but where the incoming wavefront is either plane (left) or curved (right).
This leads to a displacement relative to the reference, which in this case is the
plane wavefront. This will lead to the largest displacement, which can be seen
on the images. It has to be noted that the order of magnitude for the images
are in millimeters, which means that the displacement will also be very small.
The intensity is normalized.
For these two images both the peak position was found and the intensity
centroid was calculated, the script can be seen in Appendix A.4.2. On top of

Figure 32: The output produced by the simulations in Matlab. The output
shows the position of the peak of the intensity spot, by using the Matlab ”max”
function. It also shows the intensity centroid of the intensity spot. The intensity
centroid should be able to provide a higher accuracy, and as such will be used
for the experimental data as well. Finally the displacement is also printed, and
this is based on the intensity centroid.
As it can be seen, the script is demonstrating that there will be a difference
between using a peak finding function and finding the centroid of the intensity
spot. The intention of using the intensity centroid calculations is that it has the
capability to determine sub-pixel displacement, while the peak finding function
does not, and as such the values that are going to be used for the radius of
curvature calculations will be more accurate.
The way the intensity centroid calculation works at the moment is a fairly

everything else than the region of interest 0, the intensity centroid will only be
based on the region of interest. Alternative solutions will be discussed later.

presented and discussed. Both the He-Ne and IR setups had a focus position
experiment conducted, where the intention was to visually determine the focus
position of the system. This type of measurement corresponds to the radius of
curvature of the wavefront. The conducted measurements will be a reference to
the second experiment conducted with both systems. The second experiment
made use of the Shack-Hartmann systems and would with the use of the lenslet
array capture images of the intensity spots. From these images the radius of
curvature could be calculated.

Conclusion
The goal of the project was to conduct a theoretical as well as an experimental
investigation on a compact system that could determine the focus position of a
laser beam, by using gathered knowledge on Gaussian beams. The intention of
the system was for it to be more compact and at least as accurate as the current
telescope alignment system used by Windar Photonics.
This was done for both a He-Ne (632.8 nm) laser setup and an IR (1532 nm)
setup, with the intention of having the He-Ne being a testing and learning phase
for the IR setup. Both of the setups went through various iterations, which were
either to improve performance or to simply solve issues that appeared during
the process. Simulations for both of the systems were set up and would be used
as theoretical models to describe how the systems function as well as presenting
some kind of expectation for the experimental system. The simulations included
proof of concept demonstrations, simulations with regards to the performance of
the system and conversions from the Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor system
to the baseline system.
For the experimental part of the project various experiments were conducted.
The initial experiment was a focus position measurement for both of the systems.
This was done visually and would work as a reference to the final data. This
data would also be compared to the theoretical simulations as an indication to
whether the physical system aligned with the expectations from the simulations
or not.
The experiment that would correspond to the Shack-Hartmann wavefront
sensor system was also conducted for both of the setups. The Shack-Hartmann
wavefront sensor system consisted of telescopes (4f systems) to shrink the beam
and as such the radius of curvature as well. This had the potential to contribute
to errors in the system, but was anticipated to be minimal. From the gathered
images of the intensity spots an analysis was conducted to extract the radius
of curvature of the wavefront and afterwards they were up-scaled through theo-
retical simulations so that they would correspond to a baseline system without
any telescopes to shrink the beam and radius of curvature. For the He-Ne setup
the results were not exactly good, but gave a lot of valuable information on
how to improve the system for the IR setup. The IR setup made use of the
lessons learned during the investigation of the He-Ne system, and as such it
also ended up with yielding better results, but still not fully satisfactory. Some
errors relative to the reference data were as high as 115m, which is an error of
over 150% for the corresponding reference data point at a focus of 75m. This
error did get minimized through the use of multiple intensity spots on various
locations of the image.
The results were not perfect but indicated that this setup has the potential
to be a viable solution, if the system was optimized further. On top of that
the system demonstrated a proof of concept to an alternative solution that is
definitely more compact that the current solution used at Windar Photonics.

Future Work
Based on the measured data and the conclusion, a lot of improvements could
be done to the system. The first recommendation would be to build a system
specifically designed to work as a Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor, rather than
the setups built during this project. The cameras used here did not allow a well
defined distance from the lenslet array to the camera chip plane, which has a
very high probability of leading to an image slightly out of focus and as such
the images will be less accurate for data gathering. Another recommendation
related to the setup itself would be creating a custom solution to the optical
components and their positioning, since it was discovered how sensitive the
radius of curvature was to even tiny displacements within the telescope of L5
and L6.
On the data processing end of the system, improvements could also be made.
The first one would be to increase the amount of intensity spots being analyzed,
as hinted to in the experimental data discussion. A larger quantity of data used
will lead to a more accurate average and as such the radius of curvature calcu-
lated will be closer to the real value. This would somehow have to be automated
based on the experience from working with the setup and the designation of re-
gions of interest, since the current method is just too slow and tedious if every
single intensity spot would be analyzed from an image. The other option would
be to operate the system exactly as a Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor. That
would be to use Zernike polynomials to recreate the whole wavefront. In a very
brief manner the Zernike polynomials work in a very similar manner to Fourier
series, just that these are not related to a frequency spectrum. The polynomials
when summed up can recreate a wavefront. Zernike polynomials are discussed
in various literature including [8].
As a final note the system proposed does seem to have potential, but would
need further investigation and optimization before it could be fully concluded
whether the performance can match that of the solution used at Windar Pho-
tonics.

Description  
Thorlabs Mounted Microlens Arrays are best suited for Shack-Hartmann sensor applications. All lenslets are 
made from fused silica for excellent transmission characteristics from the deep UV to IR and have a plano-
convex shape that allows nearly refraction limited spots. 
The lenses are formed using photolithographic techniques based on semiconductor processing technology, 
which allows for excellent uniformity in the shape and position of each microlens, unlike some microlens 
arrays produced from molded epoxy. 
The MLA150-5C-M has a chrome mask that blocks light from being transmitted unless it goes through a 
microlens and therefore increases image contrast. The MLA150-7AR-M and MLA300-14AR-M have a 
broadband AR coating to reduce surface reflections in the 400 – 900 nm spectral region to below 1%. 
These arrays are mounted in a Ø1” (25.4 mm) disk that is 3.5 mm (0.14”) wide that is compatible with our 
mounts for Ø1” optics.
