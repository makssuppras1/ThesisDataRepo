Abstract
One of the main strategies proposed by Denmark to help reduce Greenhouse gases is
to support the transition into electric mobility. However, the adaptation to electric vehicles
(EVs) generates new challenges that need to be faced. One of the unknowns is the
impact of EV charging on the total electricity demand and how it will affect the distribution
networks (DNs).
A model that can define a representative mix of EV charging profiles focused on private
charging has been developed in this thesis. The profiles have also been used to examine
the grid impact in a modeled DN of Frederiksberg.
First, research has been conducted to determine the different charging profiles and define
their characteristics. The four charging profiles defined in the research process have been
developed in Python: Instant charging, night charging, cost minimization charging, and
CO2 emission minimization charging. A load of a mix of charging profiles has been defined
by merging the four individual profiles, scaling them to the area under study, and adding
variability to the parameters used to describe them.
The impact of the EV load has been analyzed in the DN of an islanded region of Fred-
eriksberg. A probabilistic analysis randomly locates the house’s base consumption, and
the EVs with the corresponding load for each simulation have been defined.
The influence of the generated EV load in the DN has been measured according to the
simulated transformer and lines loading level, and the voltages in the buses, for four dif-
ferent scenarios. An initial scenario with a low penetration level and three possible future
scenarios with a higher penetration level but different charging profile distributions.
The main conclusion links instant charging with afternoon demand peaks, leading to grid
problems in the transformers and lines. However, future scenarios propose various strate-
gies that could reduce the afternoon peaks by modifying how users carry out EV charging.

Introduction
Creating a greener and more sustainable environment is included in every country’s agenda.
However, Denmark intends to be a leader in climate action that inspires and motivates oth-
ers, establishing a global climate action strategy called ”A Green and Sustainable World”
[1]. Global pollution is one of the environmental issues that has captivated everyone’s
interest and concern. The new long-term strategy proposed by Denmark is based on
increasing awareness and strengthening the focus on adaptation and sustainable devel-
opment.
Electric mobility has long been considered an essential strategy for reducing carbon emis-
sions. Since electric vehicles (EVs) do not use combustion engines, they help reduce
greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions in the transportation sector, improving air quality in
cities and other residential areas.
Every year, the transition to electric mobility accelerates tremendously. According to the
most recent Danish data [2], the number of EVs has climbed to 66.000 in 2022, up from
31.000 a year earlier. Furthermore, Denmark’s government plan to reduce GHG emis-
sions proposes a prohibition in 2030 on the sale of new gasoline and diesel cars [3]. As
a result, incorporating EVs into the power grid opens up new opportunities for innovation,
technology, and new challenges that must be handled.
Due to these technological advances, selecting an EV over a traditional internal combus-
tion engine in 2022 will save the EV owner a significant amount of money in the long run,
thanks to cheaper maintenance costs and efficient performance [4]. Furthermore, new
technology is customizing EV charging to the user’s preferences or even discharging the
EV’s battery to send energy back to the grid (V2G) [5]. These advancements make the
transition to electric mobility more prominent daily.
However, the transition into electric mobility has a direct impact on distribution networks
(DN), as these infrastructures must be able to supply all of the required energy. Due to the
additional electrical demand imposed by EV charging, an increase in load could result in
a rise in short-circuit currents, or a voltage level that is no longer within acceptable limits,
reducing the equipment’s lifespan [6].
In the short and long term, one of the primary challenges for distribution system opera-
tors (DSOs) is keeping the stability and proper functioning of DNs. The DSO’s forecasted
maximum consumption is used to design and construct a DN over a certain area. How-
ever, the DSO must predict and adjust to the daily and seasonal variations suffered by the
electricity consumption [7] as Figure 1.1 shows. The DSO must ensure that every client
directly connected to the grid gets the requested energy. Previously, these customers in-
cluded private homes, businesses, public lights, hospitals, schools, and the like, but now
the transition into electric mobility has introduced a significant new load: EV charging.
Consequently, grid impact analysis dealing with EV charging has lately been on the table.

Home charging and workplace charging account for 90% of all chargers worldwide but
only 60% of installed capacity because of the lower power rating compared to public charg-
ers [8]. However, the load generated by EV charging at home depends on the quantity of
EVs linked to the grid and how individuals behave. The charging profile of an EV over a
week can differ depending on the installation it uses, the type of management used, and
the EV owner’s charging pattern or motivation.
Consequently, predicting the EV load for home charging has become one of the most
significant unknowns for the DSO’s future.
1.1
Context & motivation for this thesis
This research has been conducted under the supervision of the Division for Electric Power
and Energy of Danmarks Tekniske Universitet (DTU). It is also part of FUSE, a collabora-
tion between various Danish companies to establish EV charging infrastructures for the
metropolitan area of Frederiksberg. This densely populated landscape was chosen for
the project because of the large number of businesses, households, and public spaces.
This thesis has also been co-supervised by the DSO Radius Elnet. DSOs are the orga-
nizations in charge of distributing and managing energy from generation sources to end
customers. Therefore, they must control the energy flow via the distribution network to
ensure proper operation by anticipating the end user’s behavior.
Despite the FUSE collaboration’s overall focus on public charging infrastructures, Radius
has underlined the necessity to construct a model that generates a realistic mix of home
charging behavior.
However, as previously stated, estimating the charging process of EVs is challenging due
to the many unknowns surrounding EV users, particularly those who charge their vehicles
at home. The electricity demanded by EV charging varies depending on the charging in-
stallation, the charging technique used, and the owner’s charging habits and motivations.
Furthermore, the charging services and user interfaces supplied by different companies
differ, and numerous types of chargers are available on the market. As the shift to electric
transportation progresses, more complex and powerful EV charging infrastructures are
being developed.
The decisions taken by the EV owner have a direct impact on the charging profile of the
vehicle. The house, type of EV, daily usage, level of knowledge, or preference when

1.2
Research goal and tasks
The main goal of this thesis is to build different accurate load profiles considering all of the
necessary factors for home charging, which are combined to estimate an overall aggre-
gated EV load. The generated load will be used to examine how it can affect Frederiks-
berg’s grid. Furthermore, this model must make the variables easily accessible to adjust
the EV load for different places or scenarios under investigation.
In order to achieve this, different tasks have been established.
• Investigation on EV charging procedures and strategies: This task aims to de-
termine how EVs are now charging and detect the variables that directly impact
charging profiles. Familiarizing with EV load characterization is critical, as it is re-
viewing prior work in this field and the various services Danish charging providers
offer.
• Definition of the EV load: The parameters that influence each type of charging
method should be established, as well as the different charging profiles. This work
also entails the creation of a representative load based on a combination of charging
profiles and the variability introduced by EV owners’ behavior.
• Development of simulations: The DN and the conditions under which the sys-
tem will be simulated must be determined once the model has been built. Different
scenarios must also be defined to validate the model, ensure it works correctly and
analyze the results.
• Analysis of the results: The goal of performing grid impact analysis is to test the
infrastructure’s performance and reliance. However, it is essential to quantify this
impact and adequately represent the different scenarios’ results to achieve reliable
conclusions.

2
Research methodology
This chapter delves deeper into the methodology used to learn about EV load characteri-
zation, the numerous EV profiles, the variables that influence them, and the different types
of charging services now available in Denmark. In addition, various research articles that
have developed EV charging loads are also described.
The research phase’s purpose is to thoroughly understand how EVs are currently charged
in Denmark to accurately represent a mix of profiles generated by EV users in the pro-
duced model.
2.1
Literature review
Lately, the transition to electric mobility is a topic that has been on the table. As a result,
various studies have been conducted to assess the influence of this EV implementation
process in the near future on topics like grid behavior, CO2 emissions, and charging strat-
egy optimization. The articles that have been examined attempted to establish charging
profiles based on user data, assumptions, or statistical analysis. This review aims to an-
alyze previous work on EV load characterization, determine what parameters are taken
into account, and get familiar with them.
There are three recent studies about driving patterns and charging procedures that DTU
has completed that are worth mentioning. The first study [9] collects hourly meter data
from households in Denmark that own an EV and then analyzes the influence on ag-
gregated hourly electricity consumption after predicting new profiles and constructing al-
ternative scenarios. The second study [10] leverages data acquired from driving habits
to create charging profiles for no-management charging, static-management charging,
and smart charging based on price minimization. Finally, the third study [11] presents a
method for developing unique charging profiles based on historical driving characteristics
of private Danish automobiles and the home plug-in behavior of EVs in Japan. After-
wards, the impact on the grid of these profiles is examined with varied penetration levels
and EV chargers. These publications are noteworthy because, in addition to describing
charging profiles using various methodologies, they include data obtained in Denmark,
which is relevant for learning about how EVs charge in the area covered by this thesis.
The research studies analyzed in this thesis, develop the charging profile mainly using
the following three methods [12]: simulation analysis, behavior analysis, and statistical
analysis.
• Simulation analysis is a method of calculation based on the probability of scenario
changes. For instance, generating itineraries and charging load profiles for a pop-
ulation of EVs based on real-world vehicle driving data. This data can be used to
simulate different future scenarios on a specific grid [13] or to compare how different
charging strategies affect the load of an area [14].
• Behavioral analysis involves scholars analyzing the travel characteristics of users
or vehicles in a specific time or space and then using this data to build a model of
corresponding characteristics. This model is afterward used to simulate different
future scenarios [12].
• Statistical analysis studies the historical data and uses mathematical statistics and
probability estimation to predict the future. Paper [15] develops a methodology to

This classification has made it easier to comprehend the data used to create the models,
but it has also made it easier to identify the different charging profiles modeled in the
research studies. Along with all the literature review analyzed in this thesis, three main
charging strategies have been characterized [13]. The terminology used varies in each
paper but can be divided into the following categories:
• No-management or uncontrolled charging: EVs are immediately connected to
the grid to charge as soon as arriving home until the battery is full.
• Static-management or controlled charging: EV owners shift charging load away
from expensive peak hours to a pre-defined and fixed hour.
• Dynamic management or smart charging: Thanks to a constant, coordinated
control with the charging operator and an intelligent control system that receives

However, it is essential to understand that EV charging is also directly affected by the
charging installation and the customer’s preferences.
The established charging installation at each home is the first factor that affects EV charg-
ing. The numerous charging options are already defined by how the EV is physically linked
to the grid. The following are the many charging infrastructures that are available on the
market for private households:
• ICCB (In Cable Control Box): There is no communication or control with this
charger, which comprises a single cable connecting the EV to the socket. This
charging installation is uncontrollable, and the charging process begins as soon as
the EV is connected to the grid. They plug into a regular household socket and are
limited to the amount of power they can supply (2.3 kW) [24].
• Dumb charger: This charging unit communicates with the EV, allowing for new
features such as scheduling and interrupting the charging process. These chargers
allow users to access real-time data such as the EV’s SOC via an app [25].
• Smart charger: The charging box and connection are still present, but this time the
box is in continual communication with the EV and has access to external informa-
tion via the charging operator. Different data, such as hourly pricing or CO2 emission
levels at electricity generation, can be transmitted, allowing an app to schedule, op-
timize, or interrupt the charging process. This software also displays the variables
that determine the charging profile and real-time data from the EV [26].
In addition, if a person has a smart charger, it will be possible to add an EV aggregator
(EVA) to the installation to control the EV’s charging process in the near future. The
function of the aggregator is to combine electricity demand from several users to achieve
a meaningful load and sell that capacity in the electrical markets [27], to provide flexibility
to the system and the market. In exchange for an incentive, it could assist the DSO by
disconnecting unnecessary demand if the electricity price or demand is excessive, adding
value to the smart charging installation and benefiting the customers economically.
Owning a specific charging installation does not entirely dictate how an EV will be charged.

The type of management chosen by the EV owner, based on the installed installation at
the household, is the second component that creates an EV charging profile. The selected
management technique is the control system the charger will use when charging the EV.
The approaches offered by Danish charging operators shown in Table 2.2, follow the same

• No-management: The charging process begins as soon as the EV is attached, with

• Static-management: The charging process is programmed to be scheduled at a

• Dynamic management: This control system is only available through smart charg-

Once the different charging installations and the management strategies have been de-

reasons people have while charging their vehicles must be determined.
Personal motivations for charging a car may be influenced by variables that can not be
controlled, like people’s working routines. Other factors, like electricity pricing or CO2
emissions, influence the charging process and are the primary reason people plan their
charging times. These objective traits are easy to track and can help understand people’s
charging motivation.
According to the research studies analyzed in the literature review and services offered
by the Danish companies, the main charging motivations people have when charging an
EV have been defined as:
• Instant charging: The EV starts charging when connected to the grid. This can be
due to the charging installation or because the EV owner needs to charge the car,
regardless of the circumstances.
• Cost minimization [29]: The car’s charging cost is minimized. The charging proce-
dure is fixed to off-peak periods when the tariffs are low in the event of static control.
In addition, the hourly spot price is considered in smart charging to optimize the
charging cost.
• CO2 emissions [30]: The EV charging strategy is optimized to reduce GHG emis-
sions. In the case of static control, the charging process is scheduled at night when
the total electricity demand is lower. The hourly CO2 emissions and power genera-
tion are assessed in smart charging to reduce pollution.

3.1
No Management
The first charging profile to be defined is immediate charging, which operates indepen-
dently of any management system. When the EV arrives at the residence, it begins charg-
ing and remains plugged in until the battery’s total capacity is reached. Instant charging is
the simplest method of charging an EV, yet, it has the most significant grid impact because
it occurs during high-peak times when power demand is high.
The plug-in threshold SOC and initial SOC of the model are two parameters that must be
defined. The first one determines the charging frequency and is defined as a function of
the SOC. The EV will be charged if the car arrives at the household with a lower SOC than
the defined one. The choice of this parameter has been based on a paper that studies the
coincidence factors for domestic EV charging from driving and plug-in behavior. It links
the remaining SOC at arrival time with the probability of plug-in [36]. The paper shows
how an EV has a 100% chance of charging when the remaining SOC is 0.5. The second
parameter is just the EV’s SOC at the beginning of the model and has been considered
60% for coherence purposes. This project has normalized the SOC but can also be found
as a percentage value in other research studies.

been simulated. Pandapower, a Python-based power system analysis tool, has been
used to model a low voltage (LV) DN and analyze the potential impacts of EV charging on
the distribution network.
In order to simulate the effect of the EV load, the first step is to model a DN, defining the
lines, cables, transformers, and all the necessary specifications. Then, once the model is

the EV load must be introduced into the model so the simulation can be carried out.

Results & discussion
The DN model has been constructed, several scenarios have been described, and the
simulation’s operation has been discussed. The outcomes from the four possible sce-
narios are presented in this chapter, along with some conclusive figures. However, it is
critical first to describe how the load’s influence has been quantified in this research.
The proposed probabilistic analysis has simulated three essential parameters: trans-
former loading, loading of cables, and voltage drops on the buses.
• The transformer supplies the final voltage in the DN, stepping it down to the con-
sumer’s consumption level. If the transformer is continuously overloaded, the DN
will deteriorate faster. Overloading produces overheating and, eventually, thermal
degradation, which can lead to the transformer failing due to cracks in the insulation
[63]. An increase of just 10ºC on the transformer can lead to the isolation lifetime
being cut by over 50% [64]. As a result, the parameter examined in the simulations
is the transformer loading, which is directly related to its longevity.
• Distribution cables transport the electricity from the transformer to the customer’s
housing and are limited by their maximum current. A blackout can occur if a line
is approaching its full capacity because it can no longer provide the current [65].
Therefore, the loading level of the lines has been studied in the simulations.
• Finally, the voltage drop in each bus reveals the supply voltages at those levels that
will immediately impact equipment life and performance. Voltage imbalance can
result in overheating and de-rating o all induction motor types of loads, as well as
mal-operation of protection relays and voltage regulation equipment, and generation
of non-characteristic harmonics from power electronic loads [66]. Radius follows
Green Power Denmark’s suggestion for this type of DN: to keep the voltage in the
buses at 400 V ± 10% in 100% of the time, based on the Europanorm EN 50160
[67].
The findings differ from one simulation to the next since each scenario has 500 separate
runs. As a result, a method for displaying the outcome must be proposed.
For each scenario, the results of one simulation will be shown first. Then, the current
and voltage fluctuation seen by all lines and buses during the week and the transformer
loading will be depicted. These results help to understand how the network reacts to each
scenario. First, however, the rest of the simulations must be considered to comprehend
its behavior completely.
For each time step in the simulations, the transformer, line, and bus results have been
gathered to understand the broader picture better. The key to analyzing the results has
been to group the values for every time step and apply a confidence interval to them, which
measures the likelihood that a value will fall within the resulting limits [68]. First, a nor-
malized distribution with the corresponding mean value and standard deviation must be
created from the analyzed values. The confidence interval (typically 95 percent) must then
be chosen, and the associated z-value applied to the mean value provides the range’s
lower and upper limits. Figure 6.1, shows a confidence interval illustration.

6.5
Overall discussion of the results
The modeled DN has effectively conducted a probabilistic analysis of 500 simulations for
the four defined scenarios. The outcomes of these simulations have been presented in
the corresponding figures and tables, showing the behavior of the DN for each scenario.
Scenario S0 has successfully recreated the current scenario with a low penetration level
to determine the DN’s overall operational range. This initial scenario has been used as a
starting point for validating the results from the remaining scenarios, which involved more
significant penetration levels.
Scenario S1 has presented the challenges caused by EV load by increasing the pene-
tration level while keeping the distribution of charging profiles as it currently is. If EVs
continue to charge when plugged in instantly, the demand peaks in the afternoon will rise.
The results from S1 have shown an increase in the transformer loading levels, reaching
values above 100% of loading, which can result in overheating, consequently reducing
the transformer’s lifespan. Furthermore, problems in the lines have also been detected.
The amount of time the DN experienced congestion problems has increased to 17.3%
of the week, with values up to 150% of line loading indicating that congestion difficul-
ties may arise. As a result, relocating the peaks to the evening appears to be the most
straightforward solution to this problem and is presented in the following two different
future scenarios.
Scenario S2 proposes a way to lower the afternoon peaks while maintaining the same
penetration level. This solution mainly concentrates on converting EVs that do not follow
any management strategy to static charging at night. This could be caused by increas-
ing consumer awareness through tariffs and encouraging EV owners to avoid charging
during peak hours. Since there has been a certain spread in the start times of EVs using
static management, the overall results suggest that the operational range of all the com-
ponents has improved compared to scenario S1. In addition, the transformer loading has
not reached higher values than 80% of loading, reducing heating problems and without
affecting the transformer’s lifespan. Line congestion time has also reduced to a maximum
value of 12.13%.
Scenario S3 offers another possible solution to reduce the peaks generated in scenario S1
while preserving the same penetration level. It envisions a future in which smart chargers
are more widely available to EV users, and dynamic management is primarily employed
to manage EV charging in the DN. Despite the encouraging results, which reduced the
operating range of the DN’s equipment, a new problem posed by smart chargers has been
identified. As new developments improve battery sizes and EV charger’s performance
advances, customers’ charging frequency will be lowered. As a result, if the charging
condition for the smart charger improves substantially, the DN will be asked for a large
quantity of power from the many EVs planned to charge at that time. Even though it only

==PAGE:62==
lasted a short period, the transformer’s loading level reached its maximum value of 125 %,
creating the worst peak seen by the transformer in all scenarios. This would undoubtedly
raise the transformer’s temperature, which would shorten its lifespan. The lines have been
found to follow a similar pattern. Although the number of lines that experience congestion
has increased significantly, with a maximum value of almost seven lines, the maximum
congestion time has decreased to 12.02% of the week. This is a new challenge that the
DSOs and charging operators will face as the transition into electric mobility continues. As
the shift to electric mobility proceeds and the number of EVs and smart chargers grows,
the DSOs and charging operators will have to face this new challenge.
Overall, removing EVs that charge instantly when plugged in has improved the DN’s total
operational range for scenarios S2 and S3 compared to scenario S1, indicating that shift-
ing EV load to night times is the best way to alleviate grid performance issues. However,
the DSO’s new challenge is to create or participate in a system that makes the EV load as
uniform as possible during the night, balancing demand peaks caused by unanticipated
changes in energy costs or CO2 emissions.
S2 has shown that implementing a tariff system, such as the Green Power Denmark’s
Tariffmodel 3.0, which encourages consumption at night, has improved grid performance
and produced the best outcomes. However, this is since the charging start times with static
management have been designed with a certain spread. This has avoided an extensive
fleet of EVs with the same charging day and time, avoiding peak values. Besides, even
if consumption shifts to night periods, the problem of peaks generated by EVs charging
simultaneously will continue to exist if the number of smart chargers in the system is
considerably big enough.
The implementation of EVAs can be considered a potential solution that could benefit
both the DSO and the user. The charging periods would be carried out at the optimal
time, usually at night, lowering the peaks generated in the afternoon. Additionally, the
EVAs could contribute to the market by offering a part of the connected EVs’ capacity to
provide flexibility to the system. Consequently, some EVs could disconnect in exchange
for an incentive if an unexpected peak occurs.
Finally, the simulations helped identify the DN’s additional two properties. First, identifying
the lines typically in danger of congestion has been made easier thanks to the classifica-
tion of lines. All classifications show that most lines work with safe loading levels, whereas
few cables reach congestion levels with scenario S0, which has a low penetration level.
This can help the DSO identify the lines they should be cautious of or possibly consider
improving. Second, there have been low fluctuations in the voltage values due to the sim-
ulated network. Figure 5.2 demonstrates that the transformer is positioned in the center
of the DN and that the lines connecting the buses are organized to minimize the risk of
voltage drops throughout long distances.

7
Conclusion & future work
This project has successfully built a Python model to develop representative EV load
profiles focused on home charging that considers all of the necessary factors and charging
profiles. The generated loads have also been favorably used to examine the grid impact
in modeled DN of Frederiksberg.
For this purpose, a research process has been carried out first, including a literature re-
view and a market analysis. This research process has helped in getting familiar with EV
load characterization, defining the variables that affect charging profiles, and understand-
ing the different charging services offered currently in Denmark.
The four different charging profiles identified during the research process have been mod-
eled in Python: Instant charging, night charging, cost minimization charging, and CO2
emission minimization charging. In order to create a model capable of providing an EV
load profile for a fleet of EVs, the defined individual profiles have been combined, scaling
them to the area under study, and introducing variability to the parameters, used to define
them. All variables’ values are assigned based on assumptions thoroughly justified and
discussed to be as accurate as possible. Nonetheless, this model provides easy access
to the variables, making it adaptable to different areas or scenarios.
The influence of the generated load has been investigated using an islanded section of
the Frederiksberg LV DN that had previously been modeled. In addition, this model has
been modified to include the defined EV load as an argument and perform a probabilistic
analysis of 500 simulations. This approach randomly locates the home base consumption
and EVs with the corresponding load for each simulation.
Finally, the influence of EV charging has been quantified, examined, and compared ac-
cording to the transformer’s loading, the loading in the cables, and the voltage level in the
buses for four different scenarios in this thesis:
• The first scenario represents the current situation, with a balanced distribution of
charging profiles slightly dominated by instant charging and a low penetration level
of EVs, which has helped confirm the model’s validity and get an overview of the
DN’s operational range.
• The second scenario represents a future scenario with a more significant penetration
level, in which the charging profile distribution remains unchanged from the current
one. The results have shown an increase in the afternoon peaks caused by instant
charging. The peaks have increased the transformer loading level, reaching values
over 100%. Besides, the DN has suffered from congestion problems for up to 17%
of the week, reaching values of 150% of line loading.
• The third scenario is also a future scenario with a high penetration level, in which EVs
that do not follow any management strategy are converted to static charging at night.
It has successfully lowered afternoon peaks and improved the overall operational
range of the DN. The maximum value of the transformer has not reached 80% of
loading, and the congestion time has been reduced to 12% of the week.
• The fourth scenario is a future scenario with a strong penetration level, in which
smart chargers, and hence dynamic charging, are the most widely utilized EV charg-
ing strategy in the DN. On the one hand, the data reveal that the afternoon peaks
Grid impacts from combinations of marked-based electric vehicle charging profiles
51
==PAGE:64==
have been reduced, and the DN’s overall operational range has improved. However,
on the other hand, the effective implementation of smart chargers has produced a
new problem for the DSO and charging operators. The EVs charged dynamically
have generated a secondary peak at night, caused by a sudden improvement of the
charging conditions. The secondary peaks have caused the transformer to reach
up to 125% of loading for a short period in the worst cases. Besides, the sudden
increase in the demand has also congested up to almost seven lines for the same
short periods. Consequently, the overall congestion time of the scenario has also
been reduced to 12% compared to scenario S1.
The simulation results have shown that instant charging increases afternoon load peaks
and could cause future grid problems in the transformers and lines. Overloading the trans-
former generates overheating, reducing the transformer’s lifespan considerably. Conges-
tion in lines can directly create blackouts or irreversible damages in the DN.
Therefore, a shift to night charging is required. It can be accomplished by encouraging
consumers to postpone the charging period using static management or by deploying
smart chargers that usually charge at night. However, if the charging circumstances sud-
denly improve, a secondary peak problem may result from the significant increase of EVs
charging simultaneously. The integration of EVAs in the system is proposed to deal with
the secondary peaks generated by smart chargers. The EVAs could offer part of the con-
nected capacity to add flexibility to the system, disconnecting the unnecessary EV load in
case an unexpected peak occurs.
7.1
Future work
Although the thesis’s initial goal has been successfully achieved, other aspects could be
investigated further in the future to improve and complete this research.
First, as stated in the conclusions, all variables in the given model have been assigned
values based on assumptions thoroughly justified and explored to be as accurate as pos-
sible. However, a comparison between the charging profiles of a fleet of EVs created by
the model and the load derived from actual data would be intriguing. This could be useful
for validating or modifying the model to improve its efficiency.
On the other hand, commercial loads should be considered to fully create a DN model, as
they are the most consuming loads in the model. Even though Frederiksberg is primarily
a residential region with limited industrial infrastructure, it would be interesting to include
it in the model if other localities were to be studied.
Furthermore, it would be very interesting and beneficial for the results to simulate the 
different scenarios in more than one modeled DN. The results obtained in this thesis may 
depend on the placement of the buses, length of lines, the complexity of the DN, or a 
different distribution of kinds of houses. Therefore, verifying the results achieved in this DN 
or examining how they differ from the outcomes acquired in other DNs would be helpful.
Finally, even if it were not established as one of the main objectives at the outset of the
thesis, based on the findings, it would be very enriching to continue examining potential
future scenarios, to find a solution to the challenge that smart chargers present. For
example, defining a Tariff model that optimizes the variations caused by the electricity
spot price or the deployment of EVAs to avoid the peaks generated in the evening and
stabilize the whole night charging process could be a feasible expansion to complete this
thesis.

and has been conducted throughout almost the whole thesis because the profiles have
been created from scratch based on many assumptions. As the project advanced, the
profiles and variables have been adjusted to make the EV load as accurate as possi-
ble. Besides, familiarizing with Python also took more time than expected, making the
development of the profiles a slower process.
As a result, the initial steps in the thesis proved to be more difficult than expected. How-
ever, as the profiles began to take shape, the final stages became more enjoyable and
had appropriate outcomes in terms of results and timing.
Therefore, if the tasks had to be divided into periods again, the thesis’s early phases
should have been scheduled for longer.

CHAPTER 2
Project Description
2.1
Background
Global pollution is one of the biggest current challenges the world is facing and the
development and implementation of electric mobility has been considered as one of
the main solutions to slow carbon emission. These kind of vehicles, don’t have com-
bustion engines and therefore help by reducing the emission in the transport sector
and therefore improving the air quality within cities and other residential areas.
However, this integration of EVs into power system is creating new opportunities,
but also challenges, particularly to distribution systems. The supplementary electric-
ity demand required by EV charging could lead to congestion or other problems in
the actual grids and that is one of the biggest issues that concerns DSOs regarding
short-term and long term period. The infrastructure needs to be ready to support
all the demanded electricity, so the DSOs have to be able to predict the way these
vehicles charge.
The impact EVs have on the grid depends on the amount of EVs connected and
their charging profile. This last pattern is one of the biggest unknown aspects that can
be expected for DSOs, specially for home charging were the behaviour may depend
on natural patterns (peoples need of charging) as well as the smart charging schemes
that more and more charging operators are implementing for their customers.
2.2
Prior work
This research is going to be carried out under the supervision of the Center for Electric
Power and Energy of DTU Elektro. The main objective of this center is to carry out
industrial research projects in close collaboration with companies’ R&D departments.
The company provides innovative solutions to clients and trains young researchers to
integrate themselves into the production system and provide value. The Thesis will
recall advancements made as a result of participation in one of the industrial projects
on which the electric group is working on.
==PAGE:76==
4
2 Project Description
This project is part of FUSE, a collaboration formed by different danish compa-
nies with the purpose of developing charging infrastructures for urban areas, that can
satisfy the charging needs of city residents while being future-proof by allowing com-
munication with the electricity grid and smart charging. The urban area selected for
the project is complex and densely populated environment in Frederiksberg Munici-
pality, due to its number of companies, households and public area. The companies
that collaborate in FUSE are the following ones:
• Spirii
• Tomorrow
• Electric car association FDEL
• Frederiksberg Municipality
• DTU
• COWI
• Danish Electric Car Alliance
• Radius Elnet
This thesis will also be co-supervised by Radius Elnet, a electricity grid company
and DSO that covers the Copenhagen area, in North Zealand and in parts of Central
Zealand.
Their job consists of operation and maintenance of the electricity grid
and the distribution of the electricity that their customers buy from their respective
electricity suppliers. It is a company that owns the cables in the ground and the
electricity meters that calculate the customers’ electricity consumption, and it is
them who are responsible for their customers having electricity in the socket every
day.
2.3
State of Art
As previously mentioned, predicting the load of an EV that is charged at a household
is a challenge due to the several charging techniques and strategies that customers use.
According to the charging techniques, an EV can be charged in different ways:
• No-control: The charging process starts when the EV is connected, without any
control system.
• Self-Control: The charging process is scheduled to off-peak tariff periods, de-
creasing the charging cost for the user.
• Smart-charging: An algorithm coordinates the charging process with a given
objective. Specifications like reducing congestion, minimizing the price or opti-
mizing the load curve can determine and vary the charging time. Some smart
chargers already include an aggregator that can help the grid with ancillary
services. In short-term and long-term period this service will be more common.
==PAGE:77==
2.3 State of Art
5
Regarding charging strategies, people can have different motivations when charg-
ing the car, here are some of them:
• Urgency: The customer needs to charge the car, regardless of the circumstances.
• Price: The car’s charging cost is minimized, by charging at off-peak periods.
• CO2 emissions: The EV charging plan is optimized to reduce greenhouse gas
emissions.
• Frequency regulation: Some customers helps the grid maintaining the frequency
or helping with congestion by connecting/disconnecting the EV, in exchange for
an incentive.
All these features, have direct impact on how people charge their EVs, and need
to take into account when defining a realistic load for an urban area, with private
households.
Some papers and studies in the field, have defined charging profiles of groups of
EVs while trying to resemble reality. As an example of characterization of EV loads,
Figure 2.1a shows the EV curve that a group of EV with no-control would create and
the paper studies the probability of the EVs charging at the same time [VP19]. On
the other hand, Figure 2.1b shows the characterization of an individual EV charging
profile by analyzing the behaviour of several vehicles [LM19]. The inputs that are
taken into account are the driven distance, the expected departure and arrival time
and the probability of charging every day.

These two publications demonstrate how EV loads in residential households have
been forecasted. They are, however, charging profiles that derive from pseudo-real
driving patterns or systematic assumptions. The distinctive idea of this project is
to properly analyze how private household vehicles are being charged by gathering
enough data to replicate reality in the most eﬀicient way possible.
==PAGE:78==

2 Project Description
2.4
Problem Statement
Even though the whole FUSE collaboration goal is to develop grid model and public
charging infrastructures on the best locations of Frederiksberg, there is a previous
analysis that needs to be performed, focused on private household EV charging. The
DSOs, need to work with a electricity grid that can handle the demand generated
by EVs added to the daily consumption. However, predicting how private EVs will
charge is quite a challenge.
There are several reasons why anticipating private EV charging behavior is dif-
ficult. First of all, it depends on the customer and the user interface (the charging
services are offered by different companies that may differ from each other). Apart
from that, there are also smart chargers that consider decision variables in order to
optimize the charging time and duration. It is important to define and understand
all this variables in order to perform a realistic simulation on the modelled grid.
Radius, as the DTU Elektro’s & FUSE’s DSO partner, has expressed the need
to simulate a realistic behaviour of private EV electricity demand, in order to carry
out the corresponding simulations to prevent congestion or other voltage issues in the
grid.
2.5
Research goals and tasks
The main objective in this thesis is to create a realistic load profile focused on
private household EVs and to analyze how may they influence the grid of
Frederiksberg. In order to achieve the main goal of this project, different minor
goals have been established.
• Investigate charging procedures and strategies: It is fundamental to
research on, which charging control system are operators offering currently (in
case they do).
• Define how a charging profile should be formulated: The variables that
affect in the EV charging process need to be detected and defined. Afterwards,
based of calculations a formal definition of a ”load profile” has to be made.
• Propose the final load curve: It is necessary to define a realistic mix of
charging profiles regarding the area of Frederiksberg.
• Analyze the impact on LV/MV system on Frederiksberg: Finally, it
is essential to determine how to quantify the impact on the grid. Once these
parameters are defined, the model can be simulated in the corresponding soft-
wares.

Plan
The activities that will be carried out in order to reach the imposed goals, will be
explained and shown in a GANTT Diagram.
3.1
Activities
• Project Definition [W1-W4]
The first step is to properly determine as precise as possible what the topic of the
Thesis will be and define the steps that will be followed. For that, this PDR has to be
written. This will also be used at the end of the project to compare the final project
and the initial though.
• Research [W3-W7]
First it is important to familiarize with the previous work done in FUSE prior
to this project and other similar researches focused on EV load characterization and
grid impact outside FUSE. It is also necessary to search what services the charging
operators are offering and gather enough information in order to properly define what
a charging profile is. The parameters that will have be defined are battery capacity,
load percentage and charging frequency.
• Load profile development [W7-W12]
After doing enough research, different charging profiles will be developed, mixing
EV charging techniques and strategies. Afterwards a realistic combination of those
charging profiles needs to be defined for the modelled system (grid of Frederiksberg).
In this step the amount of EVs that will use each charging profile has to be defined.
Finally, in order to better simulate reality, some variability will be added to the final
load, in parameters like the time of charging, charge percentage or charging frequency.
• Develop the models and insert the charging profiles on them [W12-W17]
Once the electricity demand for the grid is known, it will be simulated in the
model that is already created in the software. However, it is important to take some
time familiarizing with the model and with the software first.

CHAPTER 4
Intended Learning
Objectives
The intended learning objectives (ILO) of this Thesis are:
• Developing a solid understanding and firm base of knowledge in EV charging
and its consequences to the grid, while familiarizing with the current develop-
ment trends and opportunities within the field.
• Mastering technical scientific methodologies, theories and tools and use them
to view a complex, open issue from an academic point of view and propose a
variety of possible actions. Specifically in this project by creating a realistic
load profile as input for the modelled grid of Frederiksberg in Pandapower and
Powefactory and analyzing the results from the simulations.
• Being able to develop relevant models, systems and processes, via analysis and
modelling, for solving technological problems. In the case of this project, cor-
rectly analyzing the current market in order to develop a relevant demand for
the modelled grid that can process a solution to satisfy the need of understand-
ing the impact of EVs effect on the electricity grid.
• Being able to express the knowledge obtained via research both orally in the
presentation and in writing, both in the report and slides of the presentation.
• Being able to work independently and reflect on own learning during the Mas-
ter: academic development and specialization.
• Manage all phases of a project – including preparation of timetables, design,
solution and documentation.
