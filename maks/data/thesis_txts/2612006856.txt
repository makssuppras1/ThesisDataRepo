Abstract
Chronic diseases are a problem worldwide, and even though chronic diseases act diﬀer-
ently, studies show that one’s life habits, especially eating habits, inﬂuence some chronic
diseases’ symptoms. Furthermore, it has been proven that medication for these diseases
can be reduced or entirely removed by changing diet. However, ﬁnding out which food
aﬀects the diseases is no easy task, even with experts’ help.
The suggested solution for this project is a mobile app, which aims to help the user go
through a learning process about how certain food items worsen their symptoms. Food
tracking can be complicated, and manually entering everything is a tiring process that
can demotivate users. Therefore, the application allows for a simple food tracking scheme
based on taking pictures and writing notes. It is possible to enter other relevant informa-
tion, such as medication and symptoms.
The system and learning process for this project works based on the test results. This
shows that it could have potential in society, helping users change their lifestyle, and
thereby reduce symptoms and medication intake. However, the application needs further
testing and optimization before being ready to use outside of a testing environment.

Introduction
Technology is a subject researched all over the globe which is continuously becoming more
advanced. Technology is almost a part of everything in most people’s lives today, includ-
ing housing, communication, and healthcare. The advancement of technology doesn’t just
improve current solutions but also create entire new research areas. One of the more recent
areas which are currently growing is ”Personal informatics systems”. This ﬁeld’s initial
characterization was by Ian Li, Anid Dey, and Jodi Forlizzi in 2010, in a paper called
”A Stage-Based Model of Personal Informatics Systems” [1]. This paper was based on
interviews in the quantiﬁed self community and resulted in a 5 stage process (see ﬁgure 1) .
Figure 1: Stage-based model described in [1].
Creating personal informatics systems is possible on multiple platforms and can be done
through manual input as seen in, e.g., weight loss applications or automatically as seen
with smartwatches or other gadgets that automatically monitors GPS signal, heartbeat,
etc. Because of this, there are many possibilities within personal informatics systems,
and multiple areas beneﬁt from using them. Throughout the last ten years of personal
informatics systems, especially the healthcare area has been a large focus. Previously it
has been customary to focus on symptom treatment [2], instead of treating the cause of the
symptoms, especially in chronic diseases. However, with the help of personal informatics
systems, focus on treating the cause through lifestyle changes has grown [3]. Examples of
how treating the cause can reduce symptoms is seen in the Danish TV show ”Kan man
spise sig rask” on tv2 [4]. In this show, doctors and dietitians aid people with chronic
diseases to change their lifestyle. As the participants implement the new lifestyles, the
symptoms tend to decrease.
Besides dietitians and doctors, this process is aided by personal informatics systems, which
helps the users track data about themselves relevant to the disease and lifestyle changes.

Problem statement
For this project, the aim is to help users go through a learning process that teaches them
which food items worsen their symptoms. This learning process is often aided by experts,
as mentioned in section 1. However, it would be beneﬁcial if people could achieve this
knowledge by themselves.
One of the primary motivations for choosing this topic is that i have the chronic disease
colitis ulcerosa and wish to learn more about how i can reduce symptoms. Because of
these reasons, the following problem statement was formed:
”How can a personal informatics system in the form of a mobile app be developed to help
people with chronic diseases go through a learning process about how their food habits
correlate with their disease and thereby help reducing symptoms?”

Existing solutions
When considering a system’s design and functionalities, it is a good idea to look at simi-
lar, existing solutions. These solutions have probably already gone through a UX-design
process and therefore have an idea about what works. Furthermore, it is possible to look
at user reviews for the existing systems to see what the users thought worked and what
did not.
Throughout this chapter, multiple systems containing food, medication, or symptom
tracking are described.
2.1
Food tracking
Food tracking is a broad term, containing multiple methods (See section 4). One of the
more popular food tracking apps, especially in Denmark, is Lifesum [5], which has over
10 million downloads. Lifesum uses a high detailed version of food tracking, where users
enter the weight and brand of every consumed food item. To make food tracking easier,
Lifesum has implemented multiple tools to aid the user.
Lifesum uses a cloud-based
service where everyone can enter new food items into the database, with the number of
calories, sugar, fat, etc. Doing this allows the users to search through an existing database
to see if the food is already in the system and create it if it is not. The app also allows
for copying previously entered meals and scanning bar codes of products to search the
database to ease food tracking for the users. Lifesums’ primary focus is on helping people
eating healthier and losing weight.

Flaredown and Lifesum use many of the same design choices by being able to swipe

this project. Finally, it can be concluded that the users approve using a journal based

Disease and medication tracking
According to the CDC (Centers for disease control and prevention), a chronic disease
is a condition that lasts for one year or longer while limiting daily activity or requiring
medical attention [8]. There are many chronic diseases, which can be rooted in anything
from an unhealthy lifestyle to genetics. Chronic diseases have been split into three diﬀerent
categories to narrow down the aim of this project.

For this project, the main focus will be on people with diseases where diet aﬀects symp-
toms daily. These diseases are easier to evaluate based on daily intake since it is possible
to monitor symptoms on the same day as the food was consumed. Many diseases that
classify as ”functional diseases” lies within this category. A functional disease is a disease
that somehow impacts the patient, but it is not possible to ﬁnd the cause of the disease
through medical examinations [9]. It is known that some of these diseases can be aﬀected
by food intake and that some of them are triggered directly by certain food items. An
example of this is the disease ”irritable bowel syndrome”, where certain food items cause
diarrhea, abdominal pain, etc. [10].
Some of the diseases within the other categories could beneﬁt from the solution created
throughout this project. However, this will not be veriﬁed in this project and could be
subject to future research.

3.1
Tracking chronic diseases
January 2, 2021
3.1
Tracking chronic diseases
There are many reasons why someone would like to track themselves, regardless of having
a chronic disease or not. Some reasons are to become more healthy or, in some other way,
achieve a more profound knowledge about oneself. When it comes to tracking chronic
diseases, there are multiple possibilities, such as being able to manage the disease better
and reduce symptoms, but it can also be to have more informative communication with
doctors, which can help provide more accurate treatment to the patients [11]. For this
reason, it is essential to consider how chronic disease tracking should be performed and
what the purpose is. There are many aspects of one’s lifestyle which can aﬀect diﬀerent
chronic diseases, with the 2010 World health report stating some of them, such as smoking,
inactivity, alcohol intake and unhealthy diet [12]. For this project, the main focus is on
the users’ food intake and disease monitoring through symptoms and medication.
3.2
Problems with tracking chronic diseases
Even though studies show that changing aspects of your lifestyle can reduce chronic dis-
ease symptoms, it is common to treat a disease’s symptoms with medication instead of
the cause. One reason is that while it may reduce symptoms, many existing solutions try
to force a template about what the user should eat and avoid to improve. People tend
not to like being told what to do and prefer to control how the process should be and
which parts of the process to follow. Surveys with doctors show that many doctors praise
apps that help in chronic disease management (CDM), but they think patients would not
be willing to use them [13]. Another problem that prevents users from using the existing
apps is that it is easier and faster to take medication, than to go through a learning process.
Apart from the users not wanting to use the system for various reasons, there are some
more psychological issues with tracking chronic diseases. Studies have shown that con-
stantly reminding someone that they have a disease can create a nocebo-eﬀect, which
worsens the symptoms [14]. Therefore, it is important to consider possible solutions to
this problem when designing the application.

4
Food tracking
Food tracking is used for many purposes, such as managing symptoms for chronic diseases,
weight management, or improving performance, either physically or mentally. Because of
this vast potential uses, there are many diﬀerent types of people who use food tracking.
Doctors use it to help their patients, athletes use it to perform better and private users
can improve their self-knowledge.
Depending on the usage of the food tracking system, a diﬀerent amount of detail is
required. However, added detail often means that the system needs more user input.
Therefore, it is essential to think about the system’s requirements and the user’s expec-
tations. The reason for this is to ensure that the user can acquire the required knowledge
without burning out.
Diﬀerent types of food tracking will be described throughout this section, varying from
entirely automatic to requiring a large amount of manual input.
4.1
Automatic food tracking
In recent years, many solutions have tried to make food tracking automatic. An example
of this is the Automatic Ingestion Monitor, which is developed by Dr. Edward Sazonow
from the University of Alabama and his team [15]. With this technology, Dr. Edward
automatically tracks food and calorie intake by having a camera and a chewing sensor,
which, when combined, automatically takes pictures of food when the user is eating.
Figure 5: The Automatic Ingestion Monitor [15].

4.2
Manual food tracking
January 2, 2021
A problem with the current automatic food tracking technologies is that often, there is
some pay-oﬀ. Many food items resemble each other, but contain entirely diﬀerent nutri-
ents, e.g., bulgur and rice. Furthermore, certain food items can be hidden on the plate,
for example, in stews. Thus, solely using image recognition technologies and automated
image taking can not be used to achieve perfect details about the meals.
There are, other automated food tracking technologies that do not use images. One com-
pany that claims to have solved a part of automatic food tracking is HEALBE, which has
created the GoBe smartwatch. According to the description, this watch can automatically
detect calorie intake through spectroscopic sensors which can detect glucose levels [16].
When looking further into the product and reading reviews, it quickly becomes apparent
that this product comes with a pay-oﬀ. Some of these are the system not being accurate,
with multiple users having an inaccuracy of calorie intake of around 10-15 %. Further-
more, the watch can not detect calories from alcohol properly since it aﬀects the glucose
levels diﬀerently than food[17].
This shows that even though automatic, detailed, and accurate food tracking would be
an excellent tool for food tracking systems, it is not possible as of now. However, the
technologies within automatic food tracking are advancing over time, and automatic food
tracking is usable in some situations if the pay-oﬀis acceptable.
4.2
Manual food tracking
Manual food tracking, as opposed to automatic food tracking, requires manual input from
the users. Depending on the accuracy and detail needed for the diﬀerent applications, it is
possible to adjust the amount of user input. In applications using manual food tracking, it
is important to ensure that users do not lose motivation or spend too much time compared
to the results they get. One application that requires a lot of user input and provides a
high level of detail for the user is Lifesum as described in section 2.1.
A diﬀerent type of manual food tracking that requires no electronic devices is keeping
track of food intake using pen and paper. An example of this is food diaries, which, much
like Lifesum, help track all the user’s relevant information. When comparing the pen and
paper version to the app, there are pros and cons of either. The paper version creates
more freedom since users can create it as they see ﬁt, with only relevant data present.
However, the tools provided by e.g., Lifesum, can not be implemented in a paper version.
Furthermore, it is possible to implement analysis tools in apps, whereas the paper version
requires complete manual analysis. Both can be used in diﬀerent scenarios because of
these diﬀerences, depending on what the user wants to achieve.

Other manual food tracking schemes that do not require any technology are interview
methods used to get insight into peoples eating habits. One such method is ”24-hour
Dietary Recall (24HR)” [18]. In this method, the interviewer asks the respondent about
their food and beverage intake within the last 24 hours. This interview uses in-depth
questions to provide the interviewer with as much information as possible. This method
can be used by multiple people, such as doctors or dietists, to diagnose and help their
patients. Another potential user is researchers who can use it to understand eating pat-
terns in groups of people. An example of this is doing a 24-hr recall on multiple people
from the USA and Denmark, and then grouping the food items into diﬀerent categories
to compare the two countries’ eating habits [18].
This shows that there are many diﬀerent ways to do manual food tracking, depending on
the purpose and the detail required of the tracking process.
4.3
Food tracking for this project
For this project, the aim is to create a learning process where the user has to think about
their food habits and how they inﬂuence their symptoms. The reﬂection will primarily be
on the user and not the system, which means that the system does not need very detailed
data to do calculations. Instead, the system should help store the food details so that it
is understandable to the user. For this project, the food tracking will happen by allowing
the user to take images with their smartphones and writing a small text or a few tags
to the image. By doing this, the user can easily see what is in the picture and read the
text. This means that in theory, some automatic food tracking could be used, such as
the automatic ingestion monitor, to take the images of the food items. However, this will
not be included in this project since it would make the system less accessible and more
expensive. Using food tracking in this way allows for an accessible and cheap system,
which requires minimal user input to track one’s food habits.

Chronic disease management through learning
Specifying what learning is and when something has been learned can be challenging.
Diﬀerent philosophies and models have been created throughout time, which tries to de-
scribe diﬀerent stages of learning. According to psychologists, the deﬁnition of ”learning”
is described as ”a relatively permanent change in behavior, due to past experiences” [19].
However, learning a subject can be done to diﬀerent degrees, such as knowing tennis rules
and knowing how to play tennis. One of the most famous learning models of diﬀerent
learning degrees is Bloom’s taxonomy [20]. Bloom’s taxonomy consists of three hierarchy
models, the cognitive, aﬀective, and psychomotor domains. For this project, the one rel-
evant is the model for the cognitive domain. Bloom splits the cognitive domain into six
levels of understanding: knowledge, comprehension, application, analysis, synthesis, and
evaluation. A person achieves a higher level of education within an area by learning more
about it.
When developing an application, it is therefore important to think about which level of
learning the user should achieve by using it. Is it merely enough to know the answer to
a question, or should the user understand the reason behind this answer?
5.1
Learning process vs. getting the answer
Most of the personal informatics systems used today, such as those mentioned in section
2, often work in either of two diﬀerent ways. The ﬁrst is to help the user go through
a learning process that teaches the user about themselves and what they should do to
achieve their results. The other uses the user’s data to calculate the result and tell the
user how to achieve the desired results. Both of these ways of doing it are valid and
have diﬀerent uses. Most systems work in diﬀerent ways, however, those that calculate
the answer often use a higher level of artiﬁcial intelligence, while the ones facilitating the
users’ learning process are user-driven. One way to try to quantify how these systems
work in general is through the stage-based model (ﬁgure 1).

possible to detect which direction the user swiped.
Once a swipe is detected, the program either increments or decreases a value called ”cur-
rDay” by 1. When the application is initially opened, the current date is saved in an
”originaldate” value. The day the user swiped to is then calculated by adding the curr-

date = dateCalculate(originaldate, ”dd/MM-yyyy”, currDay);

Discussion
This section aims to discuss some of this project’s decisions and how the project could
have been shaped diﬀerently. Furthermore, the system’s potential in society and ideas for
future improvements will also be discussed.
During the ﬁrst stages of the test, it quickly became evident that there were some issues
with the chosen participants. The participants did not have the required motivation to
evaluate their data, and one participant turned out not to be in the target group (see
section 10.2). In papers which work within the same area as this project, it can be seen
that achieving motivation in these kinds of systems can be one of the most challenging
tasks. One such paper is ”Personal informatics in Chronic illness management” by An-
thony Tang [30]. In this paper, it is mentioned that most participants are not motivated
initially when using these kinds of systems. It is also documented how the participants’
stay unmotivated when presented with evidence that the learning process has worked in
previous cases. Here Anothony Tang mentions that one of the best ways to motivate the
users is to engage their curiosity and show that the process is beneﬁcial in their case.
Therefore, a re-performed test would require some alterations. One possible solution is
to perform the test through an expert within the area, such as a doctor, dietitian, or a
coach. These could help ﬁnd participants who are signiﬁcantly impacted by their disease
and therefore have a higher level of motivation to reduce symptoms. Furthermore, these
experts could help the participants evaluate the data and help them see the learning pro-
cess’s potential.
Besides the issues with the test performed in this project, future tests would also be re-
quired. This is due to the test focusing solely on features that aid the user during the
learning process.
In section 8.6, some bugs have been identiﬁed, however, there is a risk that more bugs
exist. Therefore it is necessary to perform an in-depth bug test in the future to get rid of
as many errors as possible. Furthermore, the interface was not optimized throughout the
project, and during the test, some participants mentioned that it was not intuitive to use
all features. Therefore UX-design testing would also be required if the project were to be
continued and released to the public.
One way the design could be diﬀerent was to hide some features from the application’s
front page. Based on the feedback in the last test stage (section 10.5), it can be seen that
one participant thought that the application became unmanageable with added function-

Cloud based system
During the implementation of this project, it was decided to not connect the application
to the internet. There would be several beneﬁts for allowing the user to connect to a
server and implement features from cloud-based systems. This would enable the user
to back up their data online to prevent data loss if the user switches to a new phone.
Another possibility that could greatly beneﬁt people with chronic diseases were to look
at results/ feedback from other users who have the same disease. These are only some of
the additions which internet access could provide. However, it would also be necessary to
look into potential consequences of doing this, such as data security, users being scared
of being monitored, etc.

The ﬁnal system has been created to allow for a lot of customizability. However, it would

element is created, it would be possible to add it to the timelines at any time.

11.2
Potential in society
As mentioned in section 3.2, medication is often used to treat chronic diseases symptoms
instead of treating the cause. Throughout this project, it has been proved that it is possi-
ble to learn some of the factors that inﬂuence symptoms. Therefore it would be possible
to reduce symptoms for many patients worldwide if it became customary to go through
this learning process and change the patients’ lifestyles. The learning process does not
necessarily have to be carried out through the application developed in this project. How-
ever, using aids seems beneﬁcial, with most test participants agreeing that the application
would ease the learning process. By reducing symptoms of people with chronic diseases,
it might be possible to decrease the medical capacity used for chronic diseases.
With this being said, it is not as easy to implement in reality. Creating a shift in how
medical treatment is performed globally is not possible, and the learning process would
not work on all patients. Some patients probably will not beneﬁt as much from the learn-
ing process as is seen in this project, and some will not have the motivation to track
themselves, evaluate the data, and change their lifestyle.
Therefore, the potential in the learning process and system developed throughout this
project would be on a smaller scale. A more realistic potential is to release the applica-
tion once ﬁnished, allowing people with motivation to use it and change their lifestyles.
Furthermore, medical experts could advise patients to go through this learning process
and show them the beneﬁts of doing so.

Conclusion
Throughout this project, a learning process and a system facilitating this process have
been developed and tested. Based on previous research, food can aﬀect chronic disease
symptoms, and the test of the learning process re-conﬁrms this. Based on this project,
it can be concluded that a lot of knowledge is achievable from going through a learning
process, however, it requires motivation and time from the user. During the test, only
one out of four participants beneﬁtted from the learning process. This was due to a lack
of motivation in the three other participants. Thus, some methods to increase user moti-
vation should be implemented, such as guidance from experts when evaluating the data.
The application proved to be suﬃcient enough to help go through this process and evalu-
ate the collected data. However, if the system were to be released and used by the public,
further work is required. The application’s user experience and interface have not been
optimized, and the current version contains several bugs that need solving. Furthermore,
additional improvements should be implemented, such as creating a cloud-based system
and allowing the users to create graphs of their data.
If the system were optimized and developed further, it could help reduce symptoms for
many users and thereby decrease their medicine intake. Furthermore, it could improve
these users’ life quality and help them have a normal life.
To summarize, it can be concluded that based on this project’s ﬁndings, the learning
process and application works, but needs future development to work ideally in society.
