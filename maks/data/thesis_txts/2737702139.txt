Abstract
This Master’s Thesis focuses on improving the accuracy of forecasting power
grid data, especially on a short-term horizon of three days with an hourly reso-
lution. It explores various machine-learning techniques to develop models that
take advantage of overlapping weather forecasts. This work has been done in
collaboration with Electricity Maps, using their data and models as a starting
point. The goal is to improve the performance of these models and provide
insight into effective forecasting strategies for efficient energy management.
The thesis presents an in-depth exploration of machine learning techniques for
forecasting hourly power system data. It proposes four model architectures and
custom feature engineering. This study shows that forecasting models can be
improved by incorporating overlapping weather forecasts and by using regres-
sors for narrower ranges of the forecast horizon.
Among the regressors that
have been studied, the LASSO, the Random Forest and the Extreme Gradient
Boosting have been the most relevant for the proposed architectures. The re-
sults underscore the importance of tailored modeling approaches and highlight
the potential of advanced machine learning methods to address the complex
challenges of power system data forecasting.
In addition, the thesis discusses limitations encountered and suggests avenues for
future research, including the exploration of neural network-based approaches
to further enhance predictive capabilities.

Introduction
As stated in the sustainability policy of DTU, "DTU’s mission is to develop
and apply the natural and technical sciences for the benefit of society.
This
means that in our work, we are committed to creating sustainable solutions that
improve people’s lives, create value for society, and promote a sustainable use
of resources". This Master’s Thesis aims to support these goals. The general
context that provides the purpose of this study is explained in the first section
of this chapter, and the prior work that provides the foundation for this study
is presented in the second section. The research questions to be explored are
then presented in the third part, and the thesis outlines are presented in the
last part.
1.1
Background
Climate change is primarily caused by CO2 emissions from the burning of fossil
fuels for energy. That is why in 2019, following the Paris Agreement, Europe
set itself the goal of achieving a carbon-neutral economy by 2050. A key part of
the solution is to electrify the world, but if the electricity is generated by fossil
fuels, it makes no difference. Europe’s target includes interim targets such as
a minimum of 42.5% renewables in the electricity mix by 2030 [1]. According
to the International Energy Agency, "renewables are set to provide more than
==PAGE:13==
1.1 Background
2
a third of total electricity generation worldwide by early 2025, overtaking coal"
[2]. However, these new energy sources are intermittent, making it difficult to
know where the electricity is coming from and how much carbon it generates.
Knowing when the electricity is less carbonized can be useful for many applica-
tions: accurately tracking emissions, devising decarbonization strategies, quanti-
fying the savings potential of such strategies, building tools that empower users
to make more informed decisions, helping users prioritize energy-demanding
tasks when the carbon intensity is at the lowest, increasing energy efficiency
(e.g. smart home), shifting consumption (e.g. electric vehicles charging), or
scheduling energy-intensive tasks such as data centers’ computational loads at
times when less carbon-intensive sources are available in the grid. Another use of
this data is for the companies to comply with new regulations: EU law requires
all large companies and all listed companies to disclose information on the risks
and opportunities arising from their activities on people and the environment.
This is done through a Corporate Sustainability Report [3] where the emissions
linked to the electricity consumption should be included in scope 2 [4]. This
helps investors, civil society organizations, consumers and other stakeholders to
evaluate the sustainability performance of companies, as part of the European
Green Deal [5].
In an always more electric world, there is a need for transparency and accu-
rate information about electricity consumption, CO2 emissions, and the actual
carbon footprint of the electricity consumed. Many parties will scrutinize and
challenge this real-time information on the physical origin of electricity. How-
ever, it is not always easy to trace electricity back to its source: so far, there are
2,500 distribution [6] system operators in Europe alone. Moreover, as renewable
electricity generation, such as wind and solar production, fluctuates throughout
the day this information needs to be dynamic and updated in real-time.
The company Electricity Maps uses the world’s electricity data to support
the transition to a decarbonized electricity system. Its data is used primarily
by companies with high electricity consumption. It provides historical, real-
time, and forecast data on electricity carbon intensity, generation, storage, and
imports for more than 200 zones around the world. Carbon intensity is a measure
of how clean our electricity is: it refers to how many grams of carbon dioxide
are released to produce one kilowatt-hour of electricity [7].
==PAGE:14==
1.2 Prior work
3
1.2
Prior work
To achieve its goal, Electricity Maps follows approaches to collect data from
official sources like the ENTSOE in Europe [8]. Most of the used data is open-
source and some parts of their models as well to allow contributions on their
website [9]. When some data is missing, various techniques to estimate it are
used. A flow-tracing pipeline, [10] and [11], is run to determine how electricity
and its carbon intensity flow between countries. This allows this company to
provide carbon intensity information for both production and consumption for
each country. The different steps of the methodology can be seen on Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Electricity Maps data pipeline [9]
Once the flow traced data is calculated, the breakdown of energy production
and consumption by source can be used in real-time and as input to forecast
their future values and to anticipate the carbon intensity of electricity.
The metric used to measure how clean a zone’s power consumption is at a
given time is carbon intensity. The carbon intensity factors use emission factors
calculated through life cycle analysis. These numbers come mostly from the
IPCC (2014) Fifth Assessment Report [12], which aggregates emission factors
estimated by several studies.
The emission factors are then applied to each
energy source and summed to give the final carbon intensity.
Electricity Maps currently only generate forecasts about production modes, stor-
age modes, and exchanges to compute carbon intensity and electricity produc-
tion up to 48 hours ahead using their models. Extending the forecast horizon
and improving the accuracy of the models could help users plan better decar-
bonization strategies and gain a better understanding of the carbon intensity of
electricity.

1.3 Research question and hypothesis
4
1.3
Research question and hypothesis
Currently, the company only forecasts 48 hours ahead and not 72 hours ahead
because of a large drop in accuracy after this horizon. The goal is to extend
the forecasting horizon of CO2 emission intensity in power grids with hourly
time granularity from 48 to 72 hours without seeing a large drop in performance
beyond 48 hours. The signals to forecast are all production, consumption and
storage modes as well as the exchanges. Since there are many different charac-
teristics to predict, such as more than a hundred per zone, the models must be
easily scalable and not target-specific.
Since the goal is to focus on the models, several assumptions are made, mostly
about the data:
• The data are not necessarily stationary.
• The data is ergodic. In other terms, it implies that over time, a system
explores all possible states and that the average behavior observed over
time is representative of the system’s long-term behavior.
• There are no computational time constraints other than the machine lim-
itation.
• The different grid zones behave more or less the same, i.e. a good model
on one zone will also perform well on another zone when trained on the
data from the other zone.
Some assumptions are questionable, especially the ones about scalability to other
zones and about ergodicity as discussed in chapter 6.
This master’s thesis does not focus on the implementation of the models. They
will be taken from some widely used Python packages like scikit-learn [13] since
the challenge is to find the most suitable model and the right features.
1.4
Thesis outline
This thesis explores various regression models and feature engineering techniques
for electricity grid data forecasting, with the goal of improving the accuracy
and reliability of predicting energy consumption and production patterns. This
study is structured as follows.

Chapter 2
Literature review
The goal of this chapter is to identify potential solutions to our problem. The
first section is a literature review of machine learning regressors and models
that may be useful for our applications. The second part delves into the feature
engineering required to make the best use of the models.
Among the vast diversity of machine learning, it can be challenging to find the
state-of-the-art models suitable for our time series forecasting. The methodol-
ogy used to have an extensive literature review of the models suitable for our
research question was: using Electricity Maps prior work, a search by keywords
similar to our topic on research papers, and looking into different recent time
series forecasting competitions to see the winning models. The most relevant
competitions are Kaggle competitions [14], M competitions [15] and the Energy
Forecasting competition [16] The M competition (Makridakis competitions) are
a series of open competitions to evaluate and compare the accuracy of different
time series forecasting methods. They are organized by teams led by forecasting
researcher Spyros Makridakis. The results of the 5th M competitions were pub-
lished in 2022, and the 6th competition is still ongoing. The Energy Forecasting
Competition is a Kaggle competition of ASHRAE (American Society of Heating
and Air-Conditioning Engineers). It was founded in 1894 to advance the arts
and sciences of heating, ventilation, air conditioning, and refrigeration. This
competition is interesting to see how physics-based exogenous variables can be
used for a forecasting model.

2.1 Models
7
The paper [17] presents a systematic review of electricity forecasting methods.
It maps many electricity forecasting methods and the metrics usually used to
evaluate the models: "Regarding the accuracy metrics used, it was observed that
the Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) (38%) was the most used error metric
among electricity forecasters, followed by the Mean Absolute Percentage Error
(MAPE) (35%)".
This has been confirmed by Electricity Maps.
Thus, the
metric used in this work will be the MSE normalized by the mean absolute
value of the feature to be estimated.
2.1
Models
Since the goal of this work is to improve the carbon intensity forecasting of
electricity, models that are close to the company’s current data architecture are
prioritized: i.e., regression-based models. Models such as ARIMAX (Autore-
gressive Integrated Moving Average with Explanatory Variables), GARCHX
(Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity with Explanatory
Variables), or SARIMAX (Seasonal ARIMAX) were not considered because sig-
nals to be forecast are not stationary. Stationarity is a key assumption for these
models, as they rely on the statistical properties of the time series, such as
mean and variance, remaining constant over time. Thus, alternative methods
that do not require stationarity, such as the ones explored in this thesis, are
more suitable for forecasting electricity grid data.

2.1.2
Random Forest
The Random Forest algorithm is a widely utilized supervised machine learning
algorithm developed by Leo Breiman and Adele Cutler [23]. It is an ensemble
learning method that can be employed for both classification and regression
tasks.
The algorithm employs the concept of ensemble learning by combining the pre-
dictions of multiple simpler models, namely decision trees, to produce a more
accurate and stable prediction. In the case of a regression task, the individual
decision trees are averaged, while in the case of a classification task, a majority
vote is used to determine the predicted class. The individual decision trees of
the Random Forest are based on a series of binary decisions about the features.
An illustration of a Random Forest composed of three decision trees of depth
three is shown in Figure 2.1.
==PAGE:22==
2.1 Models
11
Figure 2.1: Example of Random Forest model [24]
The algorithm uses bootstrap aggregating (bagging) and feature randomness to
train the individual decision trees. Bagging involves generating multiple subsets
of the training data by random sampling with replacement. Each subset is called
a bootstrap sample and used to train a decision tree. This helps reduce the risk of
overfitting by reducing the variance of the final model. It also increases stability
by smoothing out the predictions and reducing the impact of noisy data. The
feature randomness, also called feature bagging or "the random subspace" [23]
method, generates a random subset of features, which ensures low correlation
among decision trees.
This diversity lets the ensemble perform better than
individual models and leads to less bias.
Random forest algorithms have three main settings that need to be set be-
fore training. These include the number of trees and the number of features
sampled. Hyperparameters for ultra-short-term electricity load forecasting have
been studied. Several studies [25] [26] show that such algorithms can be used for
electricity load forecasting. However, it has not yet been proven to be scalable
for the many modes that we need to forecast to compute the overall electricity
carbon intensity and the time horizon we are looking at.

2.1.3
Gradient Boosting trees
As for the Random Forest explained previously, Gradient Boosting frameworks
combine multiple weak learners, such as decision trees, to create a strong final
model. In Random Forest, the trees are built independently and in parallel. In
Gradient Boosting, the trees are built sequentially, with each new tree correcting
the errors made by the previous trees. Each tree depends on the outcome of the
previous trees.
A Gradient Boosting framework has four main parts: the loss function, which
decides how well the model does; the base learners, which are usually decision
trees; the number of trees in this case; and the learning rate, which controls how
much each new base learner contributes to the ensemble.
In regard to the Random Forest, Gradient Boosting trees are usually more accu-
rate than Random Forest [27]. However, if the data are noisy, the boosted trees
may overfit and start modeling the noise. Both the Random Forest and Gradient
Boosting algorithms are difficult to interpret because of their architecture.
Several implementations are available with their own strengths and weaknesses.
The most popular ones are: XGBoost [28], LightGBM [29], CatBoost [30]
and AdaBoost [31]. In Kaggle competitions, LightGBM is the most widely
used. This is largely because its implementation results in a small decrease in
predictive performance for a much larger increase in training speed. This means
more opportunities for feature engineering, experimentation, and model tuning,
leading to larger gains in predictive performance. CatBoost is not used as much
because it is found to be much slower on average than LightGBM, XGBoost
or AdaBoost, even though it achieves the highest predictive accuracy.
This
framework is particularly good when dealing with large amounts of categorical
features.
2.1.4
Neural networks-based models
Since neural networks are widely used in time series forecasting, they have their
place in this literature review.
However, they are not used exactly like the
regressors seen in the previous parts. A major drawback of most of these models
is their lack of robustness to missing values and outliers - which are common
in the data used by the company. Their implementation can be quite different
from the regressors, and they tend to be more computationally expensive to
train. They are also not easily interpretable. For these reasons, they will not be
considered in the rest of this project. The goal of this part is just to give a brief
==PAGE:24==
2.1 Models
13
overview of these types of machine learning algorithms, rather than to explain
the theory behind them:
• Feedforward Neural Networks (FNN) [32]: the information moves
only in one direction, from the input layer through any hidden layers and
finally to the output layer. This is the simplest kind of artificial neural
network. Classic algorithms based on this architecture are:
– Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLP): a basic FNN primarily used to
recognise basic patterns.
– Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) which were developed
for computer vision and image processing are also used for time se-
ries applications. They are effective in parallelization, which reduces
calculation time, and captures local patterns in the data. A com-
monly used type of architecture is the Temporal Convolutional Net-
work (TCN) made to capture long-range dependencies by skipping
some elements in the input sequence.
• Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) [33]: they are good in capturing
sequential dependencies. When FFN assume that inputs and outputs of
a sequence are independent of each other, the output of recurrent neural
networks depends on the prior elements within the sequence. Among this
category, several architectures based on this concept of sequential data
processing are used in time series applications:
– Vanilla RNN which is the basic form of RNN but prone to issues and
not very suitable to be used directly.
– Long Short-Term Memory Recurrent Neural Network (LSTM) capa-
ble of learning long-term dependencies.
– Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) which is similar to LSTMs but with a
simplified architecture, often faster to train.
• Transformers [34] are a useful tool for time series forecasting because
of their flexibility and there ability to capture long-range dependencies in
the data. They use other mechanisms for sequential data without relying
on recurrence or convolution. The most common models are:
– Informer which reduces the computational complexity.
– Autoformer which is able to handle seasonal patterns
– Patch Time Series Transformer (PatchTST).
The above techniques can be used as final models or as blocks to form hybrid
models that offer better performance.

One model that stands out for its performance is the Neural Basis Expansion
Analysis for Time Series with Exogenous Variables (NBEATSX). This model
has been shown to perform particularly well in forecasting electricity prices [35]
(EPF) and could therefore be suitable for our application. NBEATSx provides
an approach to addressing the complex issue of multivariate regression by offer-
ing the following:
P(y[t:t+H]|y[: t], X[: t + H]) = P(y[t+1:t+H]|y[t−L:t], X[t−L:t+H])
(2.7)
Where:
• y[t:t+H] represents the time series forecasts
• y[:t] denotes the historical data up to the current time t
• X[:t+H] includes the exogenous information available at the time of the
forecast.
• L is the number of previous observations used (lags).
• H is the forecast interval (horizon).
The structure of NBEATSx decomposes the target signal into several local non-
linear projections through its various blocks, as shown in Figure 2.2. Each block
consists of a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) tasked with learning the coefficients
that influence both the past (backcast) and future (forecast) elements.
The
backcast helps refine the inputs for the subsequent blocks, and the accumu-
lated predictions from all the blocks produce the final forecast. These blocks
are organized into stacks, with each stack focusing on a particular type of basis
function.
For example, Figure 2.2 illustrates a scenario where the target variable is the
hourly electricity rate, with a 96-hour backcast and a 72-hour forecast horizon.
In this case, the matrix X contains data on wind power generation and electricity
consumption. For the Electricity Price Forecast (EPF) analysis [35], the forecast
span is H = 24, corresponding to a day-ahead forecast, while the backcast
duration is L = 168, corresponding to one week of data.
NBEATSx’s forecasting process is expedited using a localized input data vector
corresponding to the backcast duration. Despite its speedy computation, the
model can capture extended temporal relationships by integrating exogenous
variables, such as seasonal trends indicated by calendar data. As depicted in
==PAGE:26==
2.2 Feature engineering
15
Figure 2.2, the NBEATSx model is composed of S stacks, each containing B
blocks. The initial block receives the combination [y[t−L:t], X[t−L:t]], which in-
cludes L lags from the target time series y and the external variable matrix X.
Subsequent blocks then receive inputs incorporating residual connections from
the backcast results of the prior block.
Figure 2.2: NBEATSx Model Architecture [35]
The time vector is discretely defined, with polynomial degree basis vectors re-
flecting trend model coefficients and harmonic vectors akin to Fourier series
components.
Exogenous basis expansion resembles time-variant local regres-
sion with a matrix of exogenous variables, allowing models to adeptly reflect
structural assumptions with both interpretable and combined bases. The final
prediction is a weighted sum of the different stack predictions. NBEATSx en-
forces specific types of stacks, offering real interpretability by allowing us to
observe the different stack contributions to the final value, compared to the
classic NBEATS.

Scaling
The literature emphasizes the significance of scaling features prior to their uti-
lization in regression analyses for numerous reasons. In our case, the algorithms
for training the linear models use coordinate descent. This technique has been
proven to be sensitive to non-scaled data, especially in cases where the scales
of different variables in the optimization problem vary significantly. Another
reason is that penalized linear regressions impose constraints on the size of the
coefficients associated with each variable. However, this value is dependent on
the magnitude of each variable.
Consequently, it is imperative to normalize
the variables. In such models, data scaling can significantly enhance the inter-
pretability of coefficients or the ability to rank the coefficient importance based
on the relative magnitude of post-shrinkage coefficient estimates.
==PAGE:39==
3.3 Scaling
28
The most prevalent scaling techniques employed in this context are:
• Standard Scaler: the procedure involves subtracting the mean of each
observation and then dividing by the standard deviation to center the
variable at zero and standardizing the variance at 1.
• Min Max Scaler: in this approach, the data is scaled to a fixed range —
usually 0 to 1.
• Robust Scaler: the scaled values will have their median and interquartile
range set to 0 and 1, respectively. It consists of subtracting the median to
all the observations and then dividing by the interquartile difference.
The Standard Scaler was found to be overly sensitive to outliers, rendering
it unsuitable for the purposes of this study.
In contrast, the Robust Scaler
demonstrated greater robustness to outliers, making it a more suitable choice
for this work. The formula for this scaler is given:
xscaled = x −median(x)
Q3(x) −Q1(x)
(3.2)
where:
• x is the original feature vector.
• xscaled is the scaled feature vector.
• median(x) is the median of the feature values.
• Q3(x) is the 3rd quartile or 75th percentile of x.
• Q1(x) is the 1st quartile or 25th percentile of x.
• Q3(x) −Q1(x) is the interquartile range (IQR) of the feature values.

4.1
Cross-validation method
The purpose of using a simple training and testing framework is to easily evalu-
ate model performance and use those results to refine feature engineering. This
framework serves more as a tool for iterative improvement rather than a defini-
tive method for evaluating and comparing models. However, to properly assess
==PAGE:41==
4.1 Cross-validation method
30
and compare the models, a cross-validation scheme is essential. Since the two
frameworks are quite similar, only the cross-validation scheme will be detailed
in this report.
First of all, cross-validation is a technique that allows for better utilization of the
training data to train and evaluate the model compared to a simple train-and-
test split. For time series, cross-validation differs from other machine learning
applications.
Randomly selecting samples for the test and train sets is not
feasible because using future values to predict past ones is illogical and can lead
to data leakage when computing the features.
In our case, we use rolling cross-validation for the time-series models. The mod-
els were initially trained on the first 70% of the data, arranged chronologically.
Subsequently, 6 successive folds, each comprising 5% of the original dataset,
were used as cross-validation folds. Figure 4.1 illustrates this process. Although
traditional cross-validation typically involves 5 steps, using 6 steps provides
slightly finer granularity in the validation process, offering more updates at the
cost of a slightly higher computational load.
Figure 4.1: Cross-validation steps
Given the large size of the dataset, each of the 6 folds is sufficiently large to
produce statistically significant results. Therefore, 6 folds are preferred over the
traditional 5. In practice, the difference in results between using 5 and 6 folds
is minimal, but the additional fold provides a more detailed evaluation.
Figure 4.2 shows the cross-validation framework. Each box represents an im-
portant milestone in the process, and the arrows represent the next steps. The
scheme focuses only on the work done in this study, i.e. on the models and not

4.2
Regressors
The various regressors identified in the literature review are tested to see which
ones best fit these models: regular linear regression, LASSO, Ridge, Random
Forest, and XBGoost which represents the family of gradient-boosting trees.
XGBoost was chosen over other gradient-boosting trees because it is already
used by the company to predict some features and because it is easy to im-
plement.
The use of neural networks such as NBEASTX was explored but
abandoned due to the difficulties in handling missing values. In particular, the
lack of weather forecasts at the shortest horizons - from 0 to 5 hours ahead - was
a critical issue. This model was limited for our applications because it could
only produce the 72-hour-ahead forecasts when given inputs without missing
values for the weather data and lagged values, which was not always possible.
For the LASSO and Ridge regressors, the regularization strength must be se-
lected. This is done by a cross-validation strategy using the scikit-learn built-in
functions [13]. The same strategy is used for the ElasticNet regressors: a list
of values for the regularization strength and the L1 to L2 ratio is explored and
selected by cross-validation. This cross-validation strategy consists of training
and evaluating several regressors with the different hyperparameter values to be
explored, using a 5-fold cross-validation to select the best ones based on their
Mean Squared Error (MSE). It is interesting to note that several strategies can
be used to select the best hyperparameter, which may lead to slightly differ-
ent values according to the documentation. Other strategies include using the
Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) or the Bayes Information Criterion (BIC)
to select the optimal value of the regularization strength.
Since their hyperparameter search space is larger, the Random Forest hyper-
parameters were optimized using a different strategy. The Optuna framework
[44] was used because it is faster than the sklearn grid search. This framework
uses an intelligent search algorithm to explore the search space of the MSE ef-
ficiently. The hyperparameters to be optimized are the number of estimators,
==PAGE:45==
4.3 Model architectures
34
the maximum depth of the trees, the minimum number of samples to do a split,
the minimum number of samples to do a leaf, the maximum number of features
used to train the trees, and whether or not to use bootstrap when training each
tree. The hyperparameter values to be explored are available in Appendix B.
The other parameters, which are less relevant, are the default ones implemented
in the packages used.
The same framework has been intended to optimize the XGBoost hyperparam-

ones, a wide enough hyperparameter search was not possible on a regular com-

to hyperparameters than, for example, LASSO. Hyperparameters from the lit-

selected. Their values are available in Appendix B.
Finally, the regressors with the best hyperparameters are trained on the entire
dataset and used for testing.
4.3
Model architectures
Four different models have been tested, using several sub-models for some of
them. The idea developed through these several models is the sensitivity to the
horizon forecast. On a short horizon, typically the first hours to forecast, the
autoregressive forecasts are quite reliable, whereas on a longer horizon forecast,
only the weather forecasts are relevant for the models. Some long-term models
seen in the literature emphasize the importance of weather data for long-term
forecasting [45]. The chosen approach is to split the models into different re-
gressors for different forecasting horizons. This approach has been tried in [22]
with other models and showed some improvements in performance. However,
compared to the predictor and feature engineering developed by the company
and this study, the sub-models used were less reliable.

Coefficients analysis
Analyzing the coefficients of a Random Forest regressor, or any tree-based
model, is quite irrelevant and uninformative. Tree-based models, including the
following XGBoost, capture non-linear relationships between features and the
target variable. Unlike linear models, where coefficients represent the linear re-
lationship between a feature and the target, tree models create splits based on
feature values to reduce variance and improve prediction accuracy. Addition-
ally, tree-based models do not use coefficients to make predictions; instead, they
make decisions by traversing the trees based on feature values and conditions
at each node.

Chapter 6
Discussion
This discussion section aims to highlight the hypotheses and choices made during
this master thesis that are open to debate. These include decisions related to
feature engineering, hyperparameter tuning, selection of regressors, computing
time, and coefficient analysis.
6.1
Feature engineering and the curse of dimen-
sionality
Throughout the model development process, it became evident that adding cer-
tain features improved the performance of the estimators. However, it reached
a point where further feature addition ceased to enhance the results. In fact, as
depicted in Figure 6.1, adding more features did not lead to improved perfor-
mance and, in some cases, even degraded it. This issue underscores the challenge
of feature selection and the curse of dimensionality. Looking at the weights of
the coefficients, we can see that although they are more numerous than the
weather features, the weights of the electricity grid autoregressive features are
smaller. Therefore, some of them may be useless.

Regressor hyperparameters tuning
Due to computational power limitations, regressors requiring extensive hyperpa-
rameter searches, such as Random Forest and Extreme Gradient Boosting, were
not fully optimized. Consequently, their performance might be underestimated
compared to the LASSO regressors, which were more thoroughly optimized.
However, while better hyperparameter tuning could enhance the performance
of Random Forest and XGBoost, it is not expected to drastically change the
overall results. The improvement in performance from more precise hyperpa-
rameter tuning is likely to be beneficial but not transformative.
6.3
Choices of regressors
As seen in the literature, numerous other techniques can enhance electricity
grid data forecasting. Regressors with data processing approaches similar to

these insights, we can make more informed decisions about feature selection and

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This final chapter aims to present the main findings of this Master’s thesis, the
limitations encountered and finally the future work to improve the electricity
grid data forecasting.
7.1
Main findings
The primary objective of this thesis was to enhance the forecasting accuracy
of electricity grid data by exploring various machine learning estimators and
optimizing their performance through careful feature engineering and exploring
new model architectures. Several key findings emerged from this study:
• Model architecture: Models trained with horizon-specific predictors
outperformed those using a single model for all horizons. This approach
reduced residual correlations and improved forecast accuracy, particularly
for longer horizons.
• Regressor performance: Among the regressors evaluated, Random For-
est and Extreme Gradient Boosting demonstrated superior performance
compared to linear models like LASSO. These tree-based models excelled
in capturing complex, non-linear relationships in the data.
==PAGE:86==
7.2 Limitations
75
• Feature engineering: Incorporating additional features, such as over-
lapping weather forecasts and Fourier series, significantly improved the
model’s accuracy.
However, the inclusion of too many features led to
diminishing returns and even reduced performance due to the curse of
dimensionality.
• Computing time: While not a primary criterion in this study, computing
time varied greatly between models. Efficient training of hourly models
using LASSO was comparable to training fewer Random Forest regressors,
highlighting the trade-off between model complexity and computational
resources.
Overall, this thesis highlights the significant impact of incorporating overlapping
weather forecasts and reducing the horizon ranges of predictors for forecasting
hourly electricity grid data over a three-day horizon. It underscores the crucial
role of tailored feature engineering, appropriate model selection, and robust
hyperparameter tuning in enhancing forecast accuracy. The findings suggest
that further optimization and the exploration of advanced machine-learning
techniques could yield even more precise and reliable predictions.
7.2
Limitations
While this thesis provides valuable insights into forecasting hourly electricity
grid data, it also has several limitations.
Firstly, the analysis is based on a
specific dataset and may not fully generalize to other regions or time periods.
Additionally, the lack of extensive hyperparameter optimization for computa-
tionally intensive models like Random Forest and XGBoost likely led to an
underestimation of their potential performance. Optimizing these parameters
is expected to yield significant improvements.
Furthermore, the thesis primarily focuses on the technical aspects of modeling
and may not fully consider external factors such as policy changes or mar-
ket dynamics that could impact electricity grid data.
Lastly, computational
constraints may have limited the scope of the analysis, particularly in terms
of exploring more sophisticated techniques or conducting extensive sensitivity
analyses.
Although the traditional coefficient analysis was not applicable to the tree-
based models, the analysis performed on the linear LASSO models provided

7.3 Future work

more insightful interpretations, leading to better feature exploitation and model
improvement.
It is to be noted that the use of neural networks such as the NBEASTX described
in the literature review was intended during this thesis but was abandoned due
to the lack of weather forecasts at the shortest horizons - from 0 to 5 hours
ahead. This model was limited for our applications because it was only able to
produce the 72-hour-ahead forecasts when given inputs without missing values
for the weather data and lagged values, which was not always possible. It may
be possible to leverage the power of these models by using other data estimation
techniques.
7.3
Future work
Future work in this area could focus on several avenues for further exploration
and improvement. Firstly, conducting more comprehensive sensitivity analyses
could provide deeper insights into the robustness of the models and the model
performances under different conditions.
Furthermore, expanding the dataset to include additional features or incorpo-
rating external factors such as investigating the impact of market dynamics, and
emerging technologies on electricity grid data.
Additionally, exploring alternative approaches for feature engineering and di-
mensionality reduction could help address the curse of dimensionality and im-
prove model interpretability. Furthermore, integrating advanced techniques for
analyzing decision tree-based models, could provide deeper insights into model
behavior and feature importance.
Future work could also explore the potential of neural networks in electricity
grid data forecasting. Neural networks, particularly deep learning architectures,
have shown promise in capturing complex patterns and non-linear relationships
in various domains. Applying neural networks to electricity grid data forecasting
could lead to more accurate and robust models, especially for handling high-
dimensional data and capturing temporal dependencies.
Lastly, there is a need for further research into computational efficiency and
scalability, particularly for models with large hyperparameter search spaces.
Optimizing the computational resources and parallelizing the training process
could enable the exploration of more complex models and larger datasets, ulti-
mately leading to more accurate and reliable electricity grid data forecasts.
