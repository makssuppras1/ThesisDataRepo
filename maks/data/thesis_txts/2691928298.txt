Abstract
Climate change is causing changing precipitation patterns and an increase in extreme
rain events. Combined with urbanization this poses a still bigger risk to cities as urban
drainage systems are under growing pressure. Accurately modelling such drainage sys-
tems is of huge interest for both design purposes and real-time control, often using ad-
vanced physically based models, such as Mike Urban from DHI. These models are com-
putationally demanding, and a wide array of conceptual and data-driven models have
been developed to remedy this. Machine learning techniques such as artiﬁcial neural
networks are showing good results and interesting prospects for new ways to combine
data sets and models for this purpose.
This study investigates one such type of neural network, the Long-Short Term Memory
(LSTM), which has been used in hydrology for predicting rainfall-runoﬀcorrelations of
ungauged natural catchments with great success. The proposed method uses rainfall
series alongside data representing physical attributes of diﬀerent catchments as input to
predict the outﬂow of those same catchments. The neural network will learn the relation
between the physical attributes and the rainfall-runoﬀpatterns and be able to predict
runoﬀhydrographs of ungauged catchments based on their physical attributes.
The constructed model takes rainfall events of variable duration along with any number
of physical attributes as input and returns predicted hydrographs for those same events.
The tested models had four diﬀerent physical attributes as inputs in diﬀerent combina-
tions: impervious area, longest pipe length, mean pipe slope, and mean pipe diameter.
The LSTM network is shown to be able to predict runoﬀfrom the diﬀerent catchments
with a median NSE of 0.96 for single catchments and up to 0.85 for cross-catchment
predictions. The performance appears to be unaﬀected by backwater.
Adding the additional physical attributes was not shown to signiﬁcantly increase model
performance, although some evidence was found that it improved predictions for events
of higher intensity. The lack of improved performance is likely due to the physical attrib-
utes not having enough descriptive power or the selected catchments being too small.
The method is showing some promise as a tool for predicting outlet hydrographs al-
though additional trials are in order to test diﬀerent input data. This study is a ﬁrst try
at adapting this method for urban catchments, and it is the hope of the author that the
ﬁndings can be used to further the development of machine learning techniques in this
ﬁeld.

Resumé
Klimaforandringer forårsager skiftende nedbørsmønstre og en stigning i antallet af ek-
streme regnhændelser. Kombineret med urbanisering udgør dette en stadig større risiko
for byer, hvor de urbane aﬂøbssytemer oplever et stigende pres. Der er en enorm in-
teresse i at kunne modellere disse systemer nøjagtigt med henblik på design eller imple-
mentering af styring, og der anvendes ofte avancerede fysisk baserede hydrodynamiske
modeller til dette, såsom Mike Urban fra DHI. Disse modeller kræver stor computer-
kraft, og der er udviklet en bred vifte af konceptuelle og datadrevne modeller for at af-
hjælpe denne begrænsning. Maskinlæringsteknikker såsom neurale netværk viser gode
resultater og interessante perspektiver for nye måder at kombinere data og modeller.
Dette studie undersøger en type neuralt netværk, LSTM (Long-Short Term Memory),
som er blevet brugt inden for hydrologien til afstrømningsprædiktioner fra umålte natur-
lige oplande med stor succes. Den foreslåede metode anvender regnserier og fysiske
egenskaber fra forskellige oplande til at forudsige afstømningen fra de samme oplande.
Netværket vil der fra lære sammenhænge mellem de fysiske egenskaber og forskellige
afstrømningsmønstre og blive i stand til at forudsige afstrømning fra umålte oplande
baseret på disses fysiske egenskaber.
Den konstruerede model tager regnhændelser med variabel varighed sammen med et
vilkårligt antal fysiske egenskaber som input og returnerer hydrografer for disse be-
givenheder. De testede modeller benytter ﬁre forskellige fysiske egenskaber i forskellige
kombinationer: befæstet areal, længste rørføring, gennemsnitlig rørhældning og gen-
nemsnitlig rørbredde. LSTM netværket er i stand til at forudsige afstrømning fra de
forskellige oplande med en median NSE på 0.96 for enkelte oplande, og op til 0.85 for
umålte oplande. Det lader til at modellernes præstation ikke påvirkes af tilbagestuvning.
Det kunne ikke påvises at tilføjelsen af yderligere fysiske egenskaber var med til at øge
modellernes præstation væsentligt, men der blev fundet nogle beviser for, at det for-
bedrede forudsigelserne for begivenheder med højere regnintensitet. Manglen på øget
præstation skyldes sandsynligvis at de fysiske egenskaber ikke er tilstrækkelig beskriv-
ende for oplandene, eller at de udvalgte oplande er for små.
Metoden virker lovende som et værktøj til forudsigelse af aﬂøbshydrograﬁer, skønkt der
skal yderligere forsøg til for at teste forskellige inputdata. Denne undersøgelse er et før-
ste forsøg på at tilpasse denne metode til urbane oplande, og det er forfatterens håb, at
resultaterne kan bruges til at fremme fremadrettet udvikling af maskinlæringsteknikker
inden for dette område.

1 Introduction
Climate change is an established part of the challenges of modern society. The changes
to the climate are underway and the changes in climate extremes have been for a while
(Seneviratne et al. 2012). The changes in precipitation patterns is perhaps one of the
most fascinating and visually powerful elements of climate change, and while the change
in rain events might not be as extreme as portrayed in disaster movies, it will have a
profound impact on human society as well as natural ecosystems (Seneviratne et al.
2012).
Since 2007 more than half of the worlds population have been living in urban areas with
this fraction projected to reach two-thirds by 2050 (UNU 2014). The rise in urban pop-
ulation has led to larger cities and increased vulnerabilities to extreme weather events
with the most vulnerable cities being located in developing countries (Revi et al. 2015;
UNU 2014). Urban areas are susceptible to pluvial ﬂooding which can destroy human
livelihoods and cause large economic and environmental damages. This makes it more
relevant than ever to be able to correctly predict not only precipitation patterns but also
the relationship between rainfall and runoﬀin diﬀerent urban settings.
1.1
Use of models for urban hydrology
Rainfall-runoﬀmodelling is an old but still relevant ﬁeld of study and being able to
model the patterns between rainfall and runoﬀis of great interest to hydrologists and
urban water professionals (Beven 2012). In an urban setting it allows for the prediction
of runoﬀﬂows during rain events which is useful for designing and dimensioning urban
drainage systems. In combined sewer (CS) systems speciﬁcally being able to predict
sewer ﬂow can help prevent overﬂow (CSO) events and help management of wastewa-
ter treatment plants (WWTPs). These are the two over-arching uses of rainfall-runoﬀ
modelling for either running scenarios for design use or using the models for making
forecasts and implementing real-time control (RTC).
1.2
Modelling approaches
Modelling of rainfall-runoﬀrelations in urban catchments can be done in many diﬀerent
ways. There are three overall ways of doing this: physically based models, conceptual
models, or data-driven models.
The physical based models are detailed numerical models that simulate many diﬀerent
physical processes in the urban drainage system (Wolfs and Willems 2017). These mod-
els rely on detailed physical descriptions of the drainage system to solve the Saint-Venant
equations that describe the balance of mass and energy (Beven 2012) and combine the
rainfall-runoﬀmodels for the catchment surface with the 1D sewer model for subsurface
ﬂow. MIKE URBAN and MIKE+ from DHI (DHI 2019; DHI 2020) and SWMM from US
EPA (EPA 2020) are examples of software used to build such models. These models can
be very detailed spatially and temporally and are highly customizable, but they suﬀer
from being very computationally heavy.

Conceptual models take the physical world and describe it through a diﬀerent phe-
nomenon e.g. a linear reservoir where the runoﬀfrom an entire catchment is described
as a tank of water that can be ﬁlled up by rain and then will give a diﬀerent outﬂow as
a linear function of the storage in the tank (J. T. Pedersen et al. 1980). A more widely
used concept is the Nash cascade where a number of these linear reservoirs are placed in
series or cascade (Beven 2012). Another concept is the time area method which models
the runoﬀfrom a catchment with a curve describing how long it takes for diﬀerent frac-
tions of the total catchment area to contribute to the runoﬀduring a rain event (Winther
et al. 2011). These concepts are used in simulation software such as Kosim and Samba
(Kroll et al. 2017). Kosim has also been combined with WEST models to combine water
quantity and quality modelling (Solvi 2007).
Conceptual modelling approaches are often focused on simplifying the detailed phys-
ical models into computationally lighter conceptual models. Kroll et al. (2017) used the
conceptual model Cosimat in their work to simplify and mirror a detailed hydrodynamic
model. Ledergerber et al. (2019) used the Kosim-WEST combination to create concep-
tual models using the reservoirs in series approach to model the sewer system. Wolfs and
Willems (2017) showed that several diﬀerent ways to model ﬂow in conceptual models
such as the linear reservoirs in series could give very good approximations of detailed
hydrodynamic models.
Data-driven models are, as the name indicates, build to consider data from measure-
ments to calculate input and output relations. For rainfall-runoﬀmodelling this means
taking the rainfall data as an input and give runoﬀor ﬂow hydrographs as an out-
put without considering any internal catchment processes. Data-driven models include
deterministic methods such as the unit hydrograph method (Chow et al. 1988) and
stochastic models such as time series models like auto regressive integrated moving av-
erage (ARIMA) (Boyd et al. 2019) and machine learning methods like support vector
machine (SVM) (Granata et al. 2016) and artiﬁcial neural networks (ANNs) (Valipour
et al. 2013).
1.3
Machine learning models for urban hydrology
The rise of machine learning techniques has given a boost to data-driven models. By
applying a neural network approach Kratzert et al. (2019) were able to signiﬁcantly im-
prove predictions in ungauged catchments, outperforming calibrated benchmark mod-
els. The model used was of the type long short-term memory (LSTM) network which
was trained using rainfall data series but also given static physical data related to the
catchments. This allowed the model to map between speciﬁc physical attributes and the
rainfall-runoﬀpatterns of diﬀerent catchments.
The work of Kratzert et al. (2019) is interesting as it presents new possibilities for pre-
dicting ﬂows from ungauged catchments. The other data driven models all depend on
having historical input and output series available for training and optimizing paramet-
ers. The conceptual models often rely on existing physical models and both need to
be calibrated against historical data to be reliable. Constructing and training a model
to make predictions in ungauged catchments could help overcome this limitation or

Artiﬁcial neural networks (ANNs) or simply neural networks (NN) is a very hot topic
in engineering and the natural sciences with the methods being implemented in many
diﬀerent ﬁelds for a wide variety of uses. The theory and techniques presented here
is not an exhaustive review of the ﬁeld but a summary of some of the most important
and foundational parts. There is a large number of very practical online sources for

The theory section introduces the theory behind artiﬁcial neural networks and the some

Third section introduces recurrent neural networks, most importantly the LSTM network
used for all the models in this study. The fourth section continues the review of the use
of recurrent neural networks and the diﬀerent architectures that can be implemented

3.1.2
Layers
In a neural network, nodes are often placed in parallel in what is called a layer. Each
node in the layer takes the same input vector, X, and gives a single output, ai, which is
then an element in the layer output vector, a. A layer like this is called a dense or fully

as opposed to the input and output layers which are very visible. The values assigned in
the hidden layers are not directly visible and are often very diﬃcult to interpret. Adding
more nodes to each layer or adding additional layers increases the complexity of the
network.

3.2.2
Batching
The training set is split into smaller parts called batches or mini-batches which are fed
to the network one batch at a time (Brownlee 2017). The network then trains on this
batch and updates the weights. This gives a smaller memory requirement as less data is
held in memory at a time. It also gives better optimization as the weights are updated
more times. After all batches have been run through the network, the loss function is
calculated using the last set of weights. Running the whole training set through the
network once is also called an epoch. Both batch size and number of training epochs
are hyperparameters for the model training.
3.2.3
Optimization scheme
The parameters of the model are optimized using a stochastic gradient descent algorithm.
A number of such algorithms have been developed (Ruder 2016), and for this study the
Adaptive Moment Estimation (Adam) (Kingma and Ba 2015) has been used as it is a
commonly used and well-performing algorithm.
As the loss function goes towards a minimum value it is best practice to let the step size
of the optimization algorithm decrease with each epoch. This is called learning rate
decay and for this project it has been implemented with an exponential decay schedule
on the form
α(t) = α0(1 −r)t
(3.2)
where α is the learning rate at epoch t, α0 is the initial learning rate, and r is the decay
rate.
3.3
Recurrent neural networks
Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) are a special type of neural networks that can learn
pattern recognition in temporal sequences. RNNs are used especially in applications
such as text and speech recognition, machine translation, and, most importantly, time
series predictions. RNNs take an input, x, and uses it in the same way as the neuron
showed in Figure 3.1 by multiplying each of the elements with a weight, W, and adding
a bias term, b. However, the input to the RNN has an extra dimension which is that the
input is a sequence of time steps. The RNN does not only rely on these external inputs,
but also on its own output from the previous time step. So, to predict the ﬂow in time
step t the RNN would use the rain input for time step t along with the ﬂow prediction
for time step t −1. For prediction of ﬂow at time step t +1 the network would then take
the rain input for step t + 1 and the ﬂow from step t. In this way information is passed
along inside the model allowing it to recognize and predict patterns in time.

3.3.2
LSTM
A problem with the standard RNN is that long term dependencies get lost. This is due to
the problem of vanishing gradients which causes long term contributions to disappear
completely for deep networks (Britz 2015). As the dependencies of RNNs can be as long
as the data series that is being modelled e.g. rainfall or climate data for many years, it
can severely limit the models.
Fortunately the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model has been invented to overcome
the problem of vanishing gradients (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber 1997). The model is
speciﬁcally developed to model long time dependencies and will learn these as a default
behaviour.
The LSTM model has an extra ﬂow of information running through the network for each
time step along with the output, h. This is called the cell state, c, and is allowed to pass
through the network without any weighted summations and activations. This is what
lets the network learn long-term dependencies.
Where the standard RNN only performs a single operation with a weighted summation
and an activation function, the LSTM has four diﬀerent sets of weights and activation
functions. These are also referred to as gates. The diﬀerent gates allow for diﬀerent
ways for the input to inﬂuence the output and the state.

output at each time step. Through training the model can learn which patterns in the
input should change the cell state and how much the input should inﬂuence the output.
For rainfall series the cell memory could help the model remember how much rain has
been falling and how that should inﬂuence the runoﬀﬂow in later time steps when
combined with the rainfall in the current time step.
The LSTM has four times the number of trainable parameters of the standard RNN. The
parameters are the three matrices W, U, and b. They hold all the parameters that need
to be optimized in training.

3.4
RNN model setups
RNNs can be designed in many diﬀerent ways depending on the application. RNNs
can be used to make diﬀerent types of predictions and take many diﬀerent data set as
input and there are a number of diﬀerent ways to design the model accordingly. Some
relationships might be easy to model and only need a simple model setup, while other
patterns require more advanced models to predict. To extend the model from the single
LSTM cell as shown in Figure 3.3, either more cells or more layers can be added.
3.4.1
Multiple RNN cells
The RNN cells can be placed in parallel in the same way as the nodes in a feed forward
network. In this setup each cell is fully connected to the layer before and receives the
entire input and computes an output as described in the previous section. By placing
multiple cells in parallel it gives the network a better possibility for learning complex
relationships in the data set. For the LSTM model it gives the model more cell states and
makes it better at remembering past inputs.
Figure 3.4: RNN layer with multiple cells. The layer works just like a layer in a feed forward network as the input,
x, is passed to all cells. The recurrent input, h, is also passed back along to all cells as well as forward as an input to
a consolidating fully connected layer that gives the ﬁnal output, ˆy.
After a layer with multiple cells which increases the dimension of the input data, a fully
connected layer can be placed to consolidate the data into the desired output format.
E.g. in the diagram in Figure 3.4 a data structure of width 1 is passed into a layer with
3 cells in parallel. Afterwards the output from this layer which has width 3 is passed to
a fully connected layer with a single cell to give a ﬁnal model output with a width of 1.
The number of cells in the LSTM layer is used for naming the models so that a model
with 10 cells in the LSTM layer is called an LSTM(10) model regardless of input and
output dimensions.
3.4.2
Multiple RNN layers
There are multiple ways to make the RNN models deeper (Pascanu et al. 2014). Just as
the deep feed forward models the RNNs can have multiple layers feeding the outputs
to the next layer and the next time step. There is also the possibility of adding depth
to the input or output layers in the form of additional fully connected layers before or

3.4.3
RNN architechtures
For RNNs diﬀerent architectures can be used depending on the desired output. An RNN
can be used to predict either one or multiple time steps from either one or multiple
previous time steps. The ﬁve diﬀerent architectures can be seen in Figure 3.5.
Figure 3.5: Five diﬀerent RNN architectures. The bottom layer (red) represent an input, which could be either a

The one-to-one setup is a model that takes a single time step and predicts one value.
This model would not need to be a RNN as it only takes a single time step which would
cancel out the eﬀect of the recurrent part of the network resulting in a model more like
a standard ANN. For rainfall-runoﬀpredictions this model would take the rain from a
single time step and predict the runoﬀin a single time step from that. The input and
output time steps do not necessarily have to be the same.
In stead of predicting only a single value, the single input can be used to predict multiple
steps into the future. This is the one-to-many setup. Here a single rainfall value would
be used to predict multiple steps ahead. This could be used in a setup where only the
total depth of the rain event was known and the model was used to predict how the
runoﬀpattern would look in some time resolution.
The many-to-one setup would mean using rainfall data from several previous time steps
to predict the ﬂow of the next time step. This looks a lot like other time-series prediction
methods such as linear trend models.
The work for this study has been done using the many-to-many setup, type d in Fig-
ure 3.5. This setup would take a series of rainfall data and output a series of runoﬀ
predictions of the same length. This setup gives an output for each time step that is fed
as input and is in line with the target model of this project.
The last architecture, setup e in Figure 3.5, is an encoder-decoder setup where a variable
number of inputs maps to a variable number of outputs. The point of this setup is to

4.2.2
Splitting series into events
The rain series is used to split the rain and ﬂow series into matching events. A rain
event is deﬁned as starting when the rain intensity is more than 0.02 mm/min. After
the intensity has fallen below this value, the event continues for 240 minutes, or 4 hours,
to assure that the entire ﬂow from the event has left the catchment. Each event is given a
number, and since the same rain series has been applied to all the selected subcatchments
the events will match across subcatchments. Figure 4.4 shows how the series data looks,
and how the series are split into events. In Figure 4.5 the many diﬀerent event durations
can be seen.
This way of splitting into events ensures that the subcatchments are emptied completely
for excess water between events. This matches with the way that runoﬀis calculated
in MU when doing multiple-event simulations. The Bellinge model uses the time-area

method which applies an initial loss at the beginning of a rain event (DHI 2017). For
multiple event simulations the dry periods between events allow this initial loss to reset
when two conditions are met: all rain inputs are zero and runoﬀhas fallen to zero for
all catchments. When these dry period conditions are met the recovery of initial loss
capacity starts, and the initial loss capacity is recovered at a rate of 0.5 mm/h (default
value) (DHI 2017). As such the initial loss capacity is restored to full between the
individual events which can also be seen in Figure 4.4 where the initial peaks in rainfall
do not generate runoﬀ.

4.2.3
Padding and masking
After splitting the data series into events the individual events are padded to all have
the same length. This is done to make the events ﬁt into the ﬁnal input data tensor
and ensures that the data can be fed to the NN model in a single batch, which makes
the training setup much simpler than having to use 204 diﬀerent input data sets. The
padding of the input events is done by simply extending all the events to have the same
length as the longest event and then ﬁlling all the added time steps with a masking
value. A common masking value to use would be 0, but as the rain input series has a lot
of 0s, a negative value of −1 was used in stead since that would never occur in a data
set of physical attributes. The padding can be seen in Figure 4.6.
When training the model the input events are passed through a layer called a masking
layer that removes the padding again. From the masking layer the data can then be
passed on to the actual neural network layers such as the LSTM layer that can use the
data. In this way a large number of diﬀerent events can be passed as input very easily.
The process is described more detailed in the tensorﬂow documentation (Abadi et al.
2015).
Figure 4.6: Padding and masking of events. The padding is applied during data formatting to give the input data a
uniform shape. The masking takes place inside the neural network model as a ﬁrst step to extract the actual data.

5.2
Static input variables
The Bellinge model used to generate the training data set is a physically informed hy-
draulic model. It uses certain physical parameters to compute the runoﬀand ﬂow that is
generated in the simulations. To be able to simulate the same behavior the same or some
of the same physical attributes will be needed to inform the NN models. The MU model
is made up of two parts: 1) a surface runoﬀmodel that describes how the precipitation
is transported from the surface and into the drainage system, and 2) a 1D hydraulic
model that describes the drainage system.
The data used for this project, and indeed many other rainfall-runoﬀmodelling en-
deavors, is inﬂuenced by both these parts. As the runoﬀﬂow is extracted from a point
in the drainage system somewhere downstream of all the inlet catchments, the ﬂow is
not only inﬂuenced by the surface of the urban catchment. To correctly simulate the
rainfall-runoﬀpattern it is necessary to include physical attributes that can represent
characteristics of both model parts.

5.2.2
Input data formatting
A diagram of the data formatting can be seen in Figure 5.7. The static input features are
joined with the rainfall series, so that each event is recreated for each subcatchment with
the respective physical attributes concatenated as additional rows of data. The model
input data for each event ends up being a matrix containing one row of rainfall data
and a handful of rows each containing a single physical attribute. The events for each
subcatchment are stacked together and stacked together with the events from the other
subcatchments to form a 3D tensor containing the entire input data set.
The ﬁnal input data set ends up having the dimensions (no. events × no. subcatchments,
no. time steps, no. attributes). With 10 subcatchments and 204 events the total number
of input events is 2040.
To stack the many events into a combined 3D data structure, all the events need to be
the same length. This is a requirement for the data structure used (numpy ndarray)
but also for the tensorﬂow model since the model has to take the entire data set as a
single input with a single set of dimensions. This was achieved by padding the input
events as described in Section 4.2.3.

5.3.1
Single catchment training
The SCMs are not meant to predict across diﬀerent catchments, which means that they
have to be trained and tested on data from the same subcatchment. As such only 1/10
of the total data, or 204 events, is used for each model. The models were trained and
tested 10 times, one for each subcatchment, to see how they performed on diﬀerent
data. The split can be seen in Figure 5.8.
For the SCMs a simple hold-out setup is used for the training and testing. This means
the 204 events are split into two subsets, a training and a testing set. As the names
imply the training set is then used for training while the testing set is kept aside until
training is done. The model is then tested on the test set to give an estimate of how it
might perform on unseen data. For this work a 90/10 train/test split is used, meaning
184 events are used for training and 20 left out for testing.
Figure 5.8: Diagram of 90/10 train/test split. Each subcatchment is trained on by separate models.
5.3.2
k-fold cross validation
For models that are meant for cross-catchment predictions, the full data set is used and
the models are trained and tested using data from all 10 subcatchments.
The training and testing is performed as a k-fold cross validation with k = 10. For each
fold the model is trained on data from 9 of the 10 subcatchments, and tested using the
data from the last one. In this way each model version is actually trained 10 times and
tested each time on data from a subcatchment that has not been seen during training.
This gives a very good basis for evaluating each model performance.
In each fold, data from one subcatchment is set aside for testing. As with the SCMs, this
gives a 90/10 train/test split. For the MCMs however, a random selection of the training
data is withheld and used for validation of the training after each training epoch. The
amount of data kept for validation is the same as for training, 204 event, but randomly
selected across the 9 training catchments. This gives a train/val/test split of 80/10/10
as shown in Figure 5.9.

For the MCMs without subcatchments with backwater only 7 subcatchments are used,
however a whole subcatchment still needs to be set aside for testing, resulting in a
86/14 train/test split. It is not necessary for the validation set to have the same size

Using a validation set gives the model the ability to constantly validate how well the
training is going along the way. It also makes it possible to implement early stopping
in the training. An early stopping callback with patience 5 is included, meaning the
training is set to stop if the performance on the validation set does not increase for 5
epochs in a row. This allows for quicker training of the models as unnecessary epochs
are skipped and gives less overﬁtting.
5.4
Evaluation criteria
To evaluate the model performance several diﬀerent evaluation criteria are used. For
an urban rainfall-runoﬀmodel there are a number of diﬀerent criteria that can be in-
teresting to evaluate on as the model can be used to predict diﬀerent characteristics
of rainfall events. For this work the models have been evaluated using a measure for
goodness-of-ﬁt of the predictions and two diﬀerent error measures. The Nash-Sutcliﬀe
eﬃciency (NSE) is used to evaluate goodness-of-ﬁt. The NSE is a widely used measure
that describes the error variance of the model (Beven 2012). NSE takes on values in
the range [−∞;1], where 1 indicates a perfect ﬁt, 0 is a model ﬁt as good as the mean
value of the observations, and values below 0 indicate a ﬁt worse than the mean.
Two diﬀerent error measures are used. The relative volume error (RVE) gives an indic-
ation of the error of the total runoﬀvolume of the model compared to the observations.
The RVE gives no indication of the ﬁt, as the volume is summed over the entire dura-
tion of the event but not at speciﬁc times. The RVE can be an interesting hydrological
measure and the total runoﬀvolume can be useful in planning operations for sewage
treatment plants and dimensioning retention basins.
31
==PAGE:44==
5
Methodology
The second error measure is the percentage error in peak (PEP) which is the error at the

from the series to compare the model with. Both the error measures are relative values,
making them easily interpretable and comparable across events and subcatchments as
opposed to absolute or squared values. All three measures can be seen in Table 5.8.

The nature of relative measures is that they can have very high variance, for instance if
the peak ﬂow of a event is very low but the model prediction is high, the value of the
PEP can be quite high. The NSE can have even more variance than the error measures
as it has no lower bound. For this reason it will be necessary to use a robust measures to
aggregate the performance measures. This means using the median instead of the mean
and using the interquartile range (IQR) in stead of the variance or standard deviation.
The median and IQR are robust measures since they are not changed by the value of

1. LSTM(20) RIA
2. LSTM(50) IA

They have high median NSE and have important characteristics that have been touched

7 Discussion
The following section is a discussion that covers the data, methodology, and results. An
attempt has been made to keep data and model separate but it can be diﬃcult for a
data-driven model.
7.1
Modelling choices and results
A discussion of the choices made for the models, the setup around them, and the results.
7.1.1
Modelling setup
The modelling setup constructed and used has been a major part of the work done for
this thesis. It is a setup that has not been found in the related literature or teachings. It
has been a considerable part of the modelling eﬀort to construct and test this setup along
with the models themselves. The result is a modelling setup that can take rain and ﬂow
series from multiple catchments and split them into events which can then be combined
with physical attributes from the catchments, before being fed to a neural network one
event at a time for training.
This setup and the associated data structures are generalizable to ﬁt events of varying
lengths and from any number of subcatchments. It also provides an easy way to feed
diﬀerent physical information to the model. Any physical attribute that is quantiﬁable
as a single number can be input into the model this way. This gives an easy way to
test diﬀerent physical attributes as there is no need to work them into a physical model
context. In theory the model does not need to be told how each feature inﬂuences the
input-output relation, but will learn this through training alone. This is a clear advantage
of the NN models compared to other model setups.
The many-to-many architecture chosen for this thesis gives an easy way to predict the
hydrograph for an entire event in one go. However it gave some problems as the masking
involved for handling events of diﬀerent lengths was part of the data set. This had to
be handled during the model analysis making additional workarounds necessary. The
many-to-many setup also makes some examples (some events) very long. The long
events can make training slow since the gradients computed during training become
very long computations slowing down the optimization. A many-to-one architecture,
which takes a rain series as an input to predict ﬂow in a single time step, would have
many more examples but they would be shorter allowing faster optimization for each
example. Since this setup was not tested it is not possible to say if it would be faster, but
is worth consideration.
7.1.2
Eﬃciency of physical attributes
Deciding which physical attributes to add to the models was at the same time simple
and diﬃcult. Simple because there is a limited amount available when extracting them
from an MU model, and diﬃcult because they needed to be able to add information that
can help the models. The input attributes needed to actually represent a useful value
54
==PAGE:67==
7
Discussion
with respect to the outﬂow of the subcatchment. In theory the NN models should be able
to ignore data that does not help with the ﬁt by adjusting the weights during training,
but during training it was found that giving too many inputs was mostly bad for the
performance.
While it is diﬃcult to say exactly which models performs the best, some trends in per-
formance can be identiﬁed. The performance gets better for larger networks as well as
removing initial loss manually. A more surprising ﬁnding is that there is no increase in
performance from removing subcatchments impacted by backwater from the training
set. The models seem to be able to predict ﬂow in these subcatchments as well as in the
others, which is encouraging for future work.
The models with extra physical inputs did not show an overall better performance than
the models with scaled rainfall as the only input (RIA). The models with physical at-
tributes still do not systematically outperform the RIA models. This is an unfortunate
result as it was one of the main research questions and the overall goal of the thesis.
There is however some evidence that the models can learn to diﬀerentiate between sub-
catchments based on the attributes. This is an encouraging result as it indicates that
improving either the model structures or the quality of the physical attributes could
yield models that are better than the benchmark. At the same time it can be seen that
the physical attributes might make prediction better for events with higher intensities
which would be the most interesting events for design purposes.
Additional physical attributes have been considered as input, the most relevant would
perhaps be a sort of center of gravity of the subcatchment that could describe how far
the diﬀerent MU catchments are from the outlet where ﬂow i predicted. This might give
a better estimate of the time of concentration of the subcatchment. Another attribute
that can inﬂuence the ﬂow and which could be extracted directly from the MU model is
the pipe roughness, but it is secondary to the other attributes describing the pipes and
might not be very diﬀerent across catchments in an MU model.
A diﬀerent approach would be to compute additional physical attributes based on the
ones already extracted. It could be the maximum ﬂow computed using the Manning
equation as used by Nguyen (2020). It could also be the ratio between the outlet pipe
and the receiving pipe which might help describe backwater. This is a technique called
feature engineering where data features are combined to create new ones that have
more descriptive power.
7.1.3
Overﬁtting and randomized validation set
From Figure 6.12 it can be seen that the performance of the RIA model does not get bet-
ter for larger models. Actually the median NSE value is highest for the smaller models.
This could be a sign of the models overﬁtting when they become larger. For the models
with additional attributes as input the opposite is true and the median NSE increases
with size which is why the larger models were designed. It seems as if even large models
could have been tested as performance keeps improving.
This change in performance with size is very clear from the results, but some of this
change is also due to stochastic variations in the optimization and data selection. The

testing data is the same for all the models with each subcatchment being the test set
once for each model, but the split between training and validation data was random for
each model run. This was done to make the training less biased, but it has introduced

7.2
Data set
For data-driven models like neural networks the data set is an vital part of how the
model ends up performing and what kinds of data it will be able to use for predictions.
This section is a discussion of the data set used for this study, possible issues with it, and
how they might be remedied.
7.2.1
Model data vs. measurements
The data set used in this work is generated by a physical based MU model. This makes
the whole data set a bit limited in its representation of real world ﬂow. Using model
generated data sets in stead of measurement data gives a data set that has a much
more linear correlation between rainfall and ﬂow as the equations governing the model
will always give the same output when given the same input. The ﬂow in a real world
drainage pipe will be much more varied from event to event as it depends on a lot more
variables than the equations used in an MU model. For this reason the models trained
on data from an MU model will probably not have the same predictive capabilities when
presented with real world measurements.
The MU model used in this work uses the time-area method for calculating runoﬀwhich
is a very simpliﬁed method. The runoﬀgeneration from the surface is a lot more simpli-
ﬁed than the hydraulic model that governs the drainage system. A main concern is that
this method completely ignores runoﬀcontributions from green areas making it very un-
reliable for larger rain events. Of course ﬁtting a neural network to data from this model
will give a network that has these same ﬂaws when predicting ﬂow from measurements.
Another issue is that diﬀerent hydraulic structures and pumps have a huge impact on
the ﬂow patterns and in an urban drainage system this impact is often very hard to pre-
dict. As mentioned in the Data section the selected subcatchments have been specially
selected to have less complex hydraulic systems, but only straight pipes and manholes.
For larger subcatchments it might be diﬃcult to get around these types of structures and
their inclusion is likely to complicate the modelling eﬀort.
7.2.2
Data selection
A lot of the results suggest that the data set used was not ideal for modelling ﬂow from
all types of events. There are a lot of small events some of which do not even generate
runoﬀsince they are smaller than the initial loss. It was the hope that including these
in the training set would teach the LSTM networks to include the initial loss. The hope
was that the extra ﬂow of information of the LSTM networks called cell state, might be
able to handle initial losses, however this turned out not to be the case.
56
==PAGE:69==
7
Discussion
The smaller events are not only a problem because of the initial loss. Since the networks
can only learn what the available training data can show, having many small events
makes the models good at handling this type but not necessarily others. Since the rela-
tionship between rainfall and ﬂow is not linear, training on small events does not make
the models better at predicting large ones. The models systematically under-predicting
large ﬂows is most likely caused by this. This behavior is unfortunate as the larger events
are the most interesting for design purposes since they are harder to handle and have
unforeseen outcomes.
To get more large events with a chance of full running pipes, a data set for a longer
period could have been used with the smallest and least interesting events removed.
This would give the models a better chance of learning patterns for this type of event.
The data set used for this study was limited by only representing a single year. With a
longer data set it would also have been easier to remove training example without losing
too much of importance.
7.3
Ways forward
There are many ways to improve on the models and data sets used for this study, many of
which have been suggested in the sections above. Other ways forward are more broad,
these are discussed below.
7.3.1
Flow predictions from measurements
A good way forward would be to use the models for predicting measured ﬂow in stead
of simulated. This would be the most interesting use of the models, and could be done
by simply trying to use the current models for predictions on measurements and see
how well they perform. The next step would be to train the models using measurements
instead of simulated ﬂow. The physical attributes would still have to be available from
somewhere, either a physical model or some other type of database.
Most of the studies referenced in this report make predictions of ﬂow from natural catch-
ments. Most are streamﬂow predictions and this makes a lot more physical attributes
available and relevant. Streamﬂow is impacted by a lot of diﬀerent factors whereas it
has be diﬃcult to ﬁnd relevant attributes for this study. Using measurements of urban
ﬂows will open up for using a few more relevant attributes such as humidity and tem-
peratures. However, the most diﬃcult part will still be the hydraulic system which is
only impacted by very little by these environmental factors.
7.3.2
Additional network types
There are a wide selection of additional neural network types that can be tried instead
of or alongside the LSTM network. As mentioned earlier the neural networks are used
in many diﬀerent contexts and there are many ways to customize networks. Additional
architectures of interest could be the gated recurrent unit (GRU) models. These are
simpler versions of the LSTM, and have shown some potential for modelling time series
with better computational times than the LSTM (Zhang et al. 2018). It is doubtful that
the GRU network will perform much better than the LSTM models using the same data
57
==PAGE:70==

Discussion
set as used for this study. A diﬀerent way could be to enhance the LSTM with additional
fully connected layers as shown by Pascanu et al. (2014).
A diﬀerent setup could be a physically informed model that takes into account a physical
model of the system. Jiang et al. (2020) shows the use of such models for modelling run-
oﬀfrom natural catchments based on geophysical models and data. Khandelwal et al.
(2020) used a physical model to inform parallel LSTM networks to simulate diﬀerent
parts of the runoﬀgeneration in order to predict streamﬂow. These methods are inter-
esting as they can use the physical knowledge for cases when the input data is sparse
to best utilize that data. At the same time the data-driven model can learn additional
behaviors not included in the physical model and in this way the two parts can support
each other.
Lastly, convolutional neural networks are seen in many diﬀerent uses often involving
image data of some type. There are 1D convolutional networks that could be used for
series data instead of the RNNs reviewed here. Assem et al. (2017) used such networks
for predicting urban streamﬂow and showed that they out-performed the RNN models
used. The convolutional neural networks have a completely diﬀerent structure and have
very few trainable parameters possibly making the training even faster.

8 Conclusion
For this study the main purpose was to construct and evaluate a LSTM recurrent neural
network model for ﬂow predictions from gauged and ungauged urban catchments. The
work is meant as a ﬁrst step to apply this method in an urban setting and identify use-
ful techniques and potential pitfalls for further development. The following is a brief
summary of the work done in this pursuit.
1. Construction of a modelling setup for training and predicting entire series of rain-
fall and ﬂow data. The setup is made for running scenarios for design use and is
highly generalizable as it can handle any number of rain events of diﬀerent duration
through padding and masking.
2. A procedure for adding physical attributes as an input to a data-driven hydrological
model has been established. The modelling setup can handle any physical attributes
as input without a need for changing the model itself to accommodate it. The neural
networks are purely data-driven while still being able to take multiple physical
features as inputs.
3. Diﬀerent model structures and complexities have been tested along with diﬀerent
data inputs to try and identify the model and data characteristics best suited for
this purpose.
During the testing of diﬀerent models the following conclusions were drawn:
• The LSTM networks can ﬁt gauged single subcatchments well with a median NSE
of 0.96. Backwater does not aﬀect this.
• The best performing multi-catchment models had a median NSE of 0.85 when pre-
dicting on 10 ungauged subcatchments.
• Increasing model width gives increasingly better performance while also making
the models better at handling additional inputs.
• The tested model structures cannot learn to simulate the initial loss which is part of
the time-area method used in the physical model. A diﬀerent network structure is
needed to learn this behavior, but when the input data is changed to take the initial
loss into account, model performance is increased for all versions.
• The current setup cannot be used to train LSTM models with multiple layers. The
optimization scheme fails when this is attempted and additional work is needed to
adjust it.
• The models that had physical attributes added to the input data did not convincingly
outperform the models trained solely on rainfall scaled by impervious area. This
is likely due to the attributes not having enough descriptive power which could be
improved through feature engineering.
59
==PAGE:72==
• The data set used should have been more selective and have contained data from
a longer period to contain a larger fraction of events with high rainfall intensities.
Overall there are many interesting prospects for using neural networks for modelling
urban hydrology and supporting physical based models. The work done for this study
has been a ﬁrst step with many lessons for future endeavors in this ﬁeld.
