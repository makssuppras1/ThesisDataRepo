Abstract 
 
Intense and extreme precipitation causes increasing risks from pluvial flooding in cities, which, 
combined with growing urban areas, have massive economic and social consequences. For this 
reason, pluvial flooding has become a great concern, and planning adaptation strategies have 
become a high priority to local government authorities. This underpins the need for efficient models 
for predicting floods.  
 
Therefore, this thesis compares the performance two such models SCALGO Live (SCALGO) and 
MIKE Flood in order to assess use cases of SCALGO. In order to compare the models, it is tested 
how SCALGO can be configurated to a degree where a meaningful comparison with MIKE Flood is 
possible – if at all. The models are tested in a case area located in Ringsted, Denmark, using one-
hour CDS rainfalls with return periods of 20, 50, and 100 years and six indicators: flood extent, flood 
depth, water depth in sinks, flooded buildings, Critical Success Index (CSI), and Expected Annual 
Damage (EAD) and corresponding sub-indicators. 
 
SCALGO is configurated to include hydrological losses resulting from infiltration and drainage to 
make flood projections of SCALGO approximate the flood projections of MIKE Flood. SCALGO is 
configurated using the Soil Conservation Service (SCS) Curve Number (CN) method and tested in 
a case area for the first time. CNs are estimated applying multiple methods to identify those able to 
accurately estimate surface runoff (defined as the amount of water ending on surfaces), that will 
make the results comparable to MIKE Flood. Using CNs improves flood projections of SCALGO 
compared to MIKE Flood, taking into consideration that the SCS CN method is a simple method with 
certain limitations, which is reflected in the discussion. 
 
In order to assess use cases of SCALGO, the projections of SCALGO are compared to those of 
MIKE Flood using the six selected indicators. All indicators increase with return period regardless of 
model. When comparing SCALGO with MIKE Flood, the flooded area is smaller in the three CDS 
events, while flood volume is smaller in the 50 and 100 year events. However, significant routing 
differences between the models are identified, severely influencing the results. Firstly, in SCALGO, 
all runoff is routed directly into sinks following the main flow paths, whereas runoff in MIKE Flood 
can flow in any direction depending on the circumstances. Secondly, flooding along flow paths are 
not included in SCALGO, which, in contrast, is found to be a key driver for the observed increase in 
flooded area in MIKE Flood. Therefore, drawing conclusions from flood area and flood volume will 
not add much value to the study and for this reason, it is more reasonable to compare the models 
based on flooding in sinks. In SCALGO, a higher number of sinks contain surface runoff and are, 
additionally, filled with a greater amount of water compared to MIKE Flood. Moreover, more buildings 
are flooded in SCALGO compared to MIKE Flood and the models only to some extent predict the 
same flooded buildings. This is assumed to be because surface runoff is routed differently in the 
models and because the applied CNs result in too much water in the SCALGO model. 
 
Based on these findings, this thesis concludes that the use cases of SCALGO are 1) identification 
of areas prone to flooding and identification of critical flood hotspots in a catchment and 2)

1 Introduction 
Extreme rainfall has had massive economic and social consequences in recent decades (Åström, 
2015; DHI, 2020a; Kaspersen et al., 2017). In the future, Denmark will experience heavier, more 
frequent, and more intense rainfall (Olesen et al., 2014; IPCC, 2012). Simultaneously, urban areas 
are expanding, increasing the ratio of impervious surfaces, putting pressure on existing water 
infrastructures (Kaspersen et al., 2017). Consequently, as flood-inducing rains cause far-reaching 
damage to tangible as well as intangible assets, pluvial flooding in cities is a great concern in 
Denmark and the rest of the world (Spildevandskomiteen, 2017; Ten Veldhuis and Clemens, 2010). 
 
This creates a strong need for hydraulic models for analysing floods in cities, setting storm water 
management strategies, and enlightening political decision making (Paludan et al, 2010; Spilde-
vandskomiteen, 2017; Åström, 2015). In such analyses it is important to carefully consider the validity 
of each hydraulic model. This assessment depends on the concrete project, available data, and 
required level of detail, and accuracy in the results (Paludan et al., 2010; Thorndahl, 2008; Zhou & 
Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2018). The model should be as simple as possible, yet as complex as necessary.  
 
Hydraulic models range from simple to complex; from surface runoff models to sophisticated 
hydrodynamic models (Paludan et al., 2010). As it may be challenging to decide which hydraulic 
model to use in each individual case, the Danish Water and Wastewater Association (DANVA) has 
developed a flood tool, the Climate Meter, which is meant as a free supportive tool addressed at 
professionals. It recommends which model to use according to specific needs (Paludan et al., 2010).  
According to the Climate Meter, the simplest flood models should be used for simple flood modelling. 
Conversely, coupled flood models (the most complex models) comprised of a one-dimensional (1D) 
drainage model and a two-dimensional (2D) overland inundation model are recommended for 
detailed flood modelling purposes (Paludan et al., 2010). SCALGO Live (SCALGO) is an example 
of a simpler surface runoff model, while MIKE Flood is an example of a complex 1D-2D flood model. 
 
Complex models often become numerically unstable and computationally intensive (Jamali et al., 
2018; van Dijk et al., 2014). Furthermore, gathering data, setting up the model, and defining 
parameters in complex models is time consuming. Therefore, simpler models are often applied in 
practice. Therefore, it is relevant to consider when the simpler SCALGO model can be used to solve 
complex tasks normally performed applying MIKE Flood, or whether SCALGO’s limitations make 
such applications unfeasible. An interesting question is whether a simple model as SCALGO could 
be configurated to reach the same results as MIKE Flood, considered state of the art, while keeping 
the benefits of SCALGO’s simplicity.  
 
There are significant differences between SCALGO and MIKE Flood, the main being that the former 
is a static, the latter a dynamic model (SCALGO, 2020; DHI, 2017a). Several other factors result in 
differences in flood projection between the two models. The current version of SCALGO does not 
include hydrological losses such as infiltration and drainage systems. These losses may be included 
in MIKE Flood. Other significant differences are that runoff is routed differently in the two models

and that precipitation in MIKE Flood is in intensity while in depth in SCALGO. 
 
The considerations above have shaped the objectives of this study, which aims to explore potential 
use cases for SCALGO. This is done by evaluating and comparing the performance of SCALGO 
and MIKE Flood in a selected case area. For this purpose, it is investigated whether a SCALGO 
model can be configurated to a degree where a meaningful comparison with MIKE Flood is possible. 
 
The study objectives are: 
• Are there potential use cases where SCALGO is adequate when projecting pluvial flooding? 
• Is it possible to configurate SCALGO using surface runoff computations to bring SCALGO 
projections closer to those of MIKE Flood? 
• If the answer to the above question is positive, how is it done? 
 
This study is divided into three distinct parts structured around the steps outlined in Figure 1.1 below. 
The arrows indicate the workflow. Part 1 represents the preparatory phase and is comprised of an 
initial screening of Ringsted case area and interviews with four water professionals in order to define 
relevant indicators. The interviews are important because the choice of model is always based on a 
case by case evaluation depending on the concrete project, which requires experience. The 
professionals have the experience to choose the most suitable model in a specific case and to supply 
helpful knowledge when selecting indicators. 
 
Part 2 comprises setting up a MIKE Flood model and a SCALGO model and making them 
comparable. This includes defining the necessary hydraulic parameters. As there are no available 
validation data, the best approach is to compare SCALGO results with MIKE Flood results. 
 
Lastly, in Part 3, the models are evaluated and compared using six indicators selected on the basis 
of the conducted interviews in Part 1 in order to assess use cases of SCALGO.

2 Theory 
 
 
2.1.1 Extreme rainfall and time of concentration 
 
Precipitation is the driver of rainfall runoff and processes related to runoff in the hydraulic models 
(DHI, 2015). When precipitation reaches a certain intensity, it is termed extreme rainfall which is the 
primary driver of pluvial flooding (Zhou & Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2018). In relation to the Three Point 
Approach, Sørup et al. (2016) have defined rainfall within the extreme domain, Point C, in Danish 
context as rare events causing flooding in the urban environment. It is when the capacity of sewer 
system is exceeded and when overland flow becomes substantial (Sørup et al, 2016). This definition 
of extreme rainfall is adopted throughout this study. 
 
In terms of hydraulic modelling, a precipitation file will either contain a historical or synthetic rain 
event and is loaded externally to the model (DHI, 2015). Depending on the rainfall, both runoff 
dynamics and amount of runoff will vary, which is why an appropriate rain (in terms of event duration, 
peak intensity etc.) that represents the complexity of a case area and sewer system must be chosen 
(DHI, 2015; Spildevandskomiteen, 1999). Synthetic rains can be estimated from Intensity-Duration-
Frequency (IDF) curves. IDF curves are graphical representations of the probability of the 
occurrence of a given average rainfall intensity and is derived from statistical analyses performed on 
observed historical rainfall depths monitored by rain gauges during storms (Madsen et al., 2002; 
Madsen et al., 2009). Monitored annual maximum depths are ranked from the first year of record 
(year 1) to the n’th year of record in decreasing order representing return periods and their 
corresponding rainfall depths (Butler & Davies, 2004; Spildevandskomiteen, 2006). Rainfall intensity 
and duration are most often fitted to describe the IDF curves, as the utilization of rainfall depth 
presents some challenges when considering catchments with different characteristics and different 
time of concentration (henceforth TOC). 
 
TOC is defined as the time it takes surface runoff to flow from the most hydraulic remote point of the 
drainage area to the point in the drainage system that is under investigation e.g. an outlet (Winter et 
al., 2011; Spildevandskomiteen, 2005). TOC depends on the size of the investigated catchment, 
topography, land use, and geology, thus TOC represents the hydrological response of a catchment 
(Winter et al., 2011; Nielsen, 2019). For example, a steep slope may in general result in high water 
velocities (on ground surface as well as in pipes) thus low TOC while the opposite may be the case 
for a flat area (Thomason, 2019). Larger catchments are likely to have higher TOC compared to 
smaller catchments due to larger distance between most hydraulic remote point and outlet. At the 
This chapter describes the theory relevant for the present study namely extreme rainfall 
and time of concentration, theory on different methods of how to describe the complex 
process of modelling rainfall runoff from impervious and pervious surfaces in models, and 
last but not least, hydraulic modelling principles.

The Chicago Design Storm (CDS) is estimated from regional IDFs and is moreover the most popular 
synthetic rain due to its profile. The design rainfall is commonly used for design of urban drainage 
systems in Denmark (Madsen et al., 2002). The CDS is an idealised profile of the observed storms 
which tends to describe a statistically constructed return period (Butler & Davies, 2004). The CDS 
rainfall includes maximum average rainfall intensities for multiple durations for the specific return 
period (Spildevandskomiteen, 2005). Thereby, a CDS profile contains extreme rainfall of various 
durations occurring simultaneously, thus one single rainfall is able to test every part of the drainage 
system if the duration of the CDS rainfall is long enough for the water to penetrate even the most 
inert parts of the system (Spildevandskomiteen, 2005). Therefore, the duration of the CDS rainfall 
must be equal to or greater than the time of concentration. Only then the entire watershed contributes 
flow to the drainage system and the system is tested at the peak, which is when all catchments are 
contributing to the sewer system (Winter et al., 2011; Spildevandskomiteen, 2006; Winter et al., 
2011). If the duration of a CDS rainfall with a certain return period is increased, the intensity and 
peak will stay the same whereas the total rainfall depth and volume will increase. The CDS does not 
consider coupled rainfall, thus some rainfall dynamics are neglected, thus CDS profiles are 
consequently not suitable for very large or complex systems containing CSOs, basins, pumps etc. 
(DHI, 2019; Spildevandskomiteen, 1999; Thorndahl, 2015). 
 
2.1.2 Modelling rainfall runoff from impervious and pervious surfaces 
 
In relation to urban pluvial flood hazard, the dominant type of runoff is surface runoff (DHI, 2015). In 
an urban setting, impervious surfaces only allow little or no rainfall to infiltrate into the ground, making 
them a major contributor to surface runoff (henceforth runoff). Runoff from most impervious surfaces 
will be collected by the sewer system until its capacity is reached and the system is overloaded 
(Winter et al, 2011). When modelling runoff from impervious areas an infiltration capacity of zero is 
often assumed (Nielsen, 2019). Meanwhile, pervious areas generate runoff in case of extreme 
events where the infiltration and storage capacity of the soil is exceeded (DHI, 2020a; Kaspersen et 
al., 2017; Olesen et al., 2017). Although pervious areas constitute with less runoff than impervious 
surfaces considering smaller rainfalls, they can still constitute with a significant amount of runoff in 
case of bigger less frequent events as they often cover a large portion of the urban surface 
(Kaspersen et al., 2017). Even though pervious areas can contribute with substantial amounts of 
runoff to the sewer system, loads from pervious areas are often neglected in flood modelling (DHI, 
2015; Nielsen, 2019). This assumption can cause an underestimation of the amount of runoff being 
led to the sewer system. 
 
As impervious as well as pervious surfaces contributes to runoff in the case of an extreme event, a 
hydraulic model that can simulate the hydrological loads from different surface types is essential for 
the study of urban flooding (DHI, 2015). The below sections describe the theory behind methods of 
how runoff can be modelled in the two models used in this study, namely MIKE Flood and SCALGO 
Live (henceforth SCALGO), shown with respectively the Time Area Method with rainfall directly on 
2D surfaces and the Soil Conservation Service Curve Number method.

2.2 Hydraulic modelling principles 
In general, hydrological models considering urban catchments may be divided into two distinct 
classes; surface runoff models and coupled 1D-2D hydrodynamic models (DHI, 2017d). SCALGO 
falls under the former class and MIKE Flood under the latter. These model types will be presented 
in the following sections.  
 
2.2.1 Surface runoff models 
 
Surface runoff models are commonly used when analysing urban runoff and the general 
characteristic for these models are that only runoff appearing on the surface is accounted for (DHI, 
2017d). A surface runoff model basically breaks down a Digital Elevation Model (DEM) into sinks, 
the corresponding subcatchment, and the flow paths connecting them (see Figure 2.4) (Balstrøm & 
Crawford, 2018).

determined (refer to Figure 2.6) from counting the accumulated number of cells contributing with flow

elevation values (Balstrøm & Crawford, 2018). Water accumulates in sinks until the holding capacity 
is reached and water spills over to the neighbouring cell with the lowest z-value, as illustrated in the 
yellow catchment in Figure 2.4. Once water spills over, it will follow the downstream flow route 
defined by the flow directions, cell by cell, until it reaches the next downstream sink (Balstrøm &

for a short period of time during an event are not easily determined using this approach. 
 
2.2.2 Coupled 1D-2D hydrodynamic models  
 
The essence of a coupled 1D-2D hydrodynamic model is to couple a 1D drainage model and a 2D 
overland inundation model into one dynamically coupled modelling system (DHI, 2017a; Zhou et al., 
2013). This performs an integrated dynamic simulation of pipe flow and surface flow, including 
exchange of water between network and ground surface, which is useful when trying to understand 
the integrated system (DHI, 2017a; DHI, 2019; DANVA, 2016). In the present study, MIKE Flood 
serves as the coupled modelling system, comprising the 1D drainage model, MIKE Urban, and the 
2D overland inundation model, MIKE 21. 
 
As mentioned in section 2.1.2.1, MIKE Urban handles the rainfall-runoff relationship in impervious 
areas using Model A. Meanwhile, the MIKE 21 routes runoff depending on the topography of the 
terrain. Two parameters in the 2D model are controlling when a cell is considered or not in the 
calculations; drying depth and flooding depth. If the water level in a cell is below the drying depth, it 
is not considered in the calculations, whereas if the water level reaches the defined flooding depth, 
it is included (DHI; 2017a). 
 
As illustrated in Figure 2.7 below and in Figure 2.2 in section 2.1.2, two models are connected 
through selected manholes coupled to the 2D model. The manholes thus facilitate the interaction 
between the drainage system and the overland flow when water either flows from the 2D model into 
the 1D drainage model or when water surcharges from the network onto the surface as illustrated in 
the figure below (DHI, 2017a; DANVA, 2016; Zhou et al., 2013). Sealed nodes and closed structures 
are examples of nodes which should not be coupled to the 2D model. The 2D surface model is 
generated from terrain data, e.g. a DEM. Each grid cell has a surface elevation and will exchange 
water with the 1D model and neighbouring cells (van Dijk et al., 2014).

3.3 Resolution of digital elevation model 
The same DEM is used for all modelling purposes in this study. The model is based on data collected 
in 2019 and is the official Danish elevation model produced and provided by the Agency for Data 
Supply and Efficiency (SCALGO, 2020). The data is obtained from laser scanning of Denmark from 
airplanes where after the data is processed to maps containing the height of the terrain and area in 
a horizontal resolution of 0.4 m (Agency for Data Supply and Efficiency, 2020). For comparison, 
Mark et al. (2004) suggests a 5 x 5 m resolution for quick assessments and a 1 x 1 m resolution for 
thorough analyses in urban areas, since a finer resolution will cover sidewalks, roads, houses etc. 
Therefore, a high resolution improves the model accuracy, however, it also increases the 
computational time in MIKE Flood (Adeogun et al., 2015). From weighing computational time against 
accuracy, it is decided to change the resolution from 0.4 x 0.4 m to 2 x 2 m for application, as it is 
assessed that this will reflect the desired balance between these two considerations. In both 
SCALGO and MIKE Flood, the same resolution is used, as it improves the comparability of the 
models. In addition, several terrain edits are made so that both models take gateways and openings 
in buildings into account when simulating. This is done by utilizing the various terrain editing tools 
supported by SCALGO.

3.4 Rain events  
Flood-inducing rain events must be selected for modelling purposes and in order to compare and 
assess the performance of SCALGO and MIKE Flood. The intention is to select three rain events 
able to represent a wide yet still realistic range of extreme precipitation. Skrift 27 ‘Functional 
Practices of Drainage System under Rainfall’ is a paper by the Water Pollution Committee of The 
Danish Society of Engineers (SVK) (Spildevandskomiteen, 2005). Skrift 27 defines a minimum 
functional requirement of water on terrain of a return period of 10 years considering combined sewer 
systems in urban areas. This may be used to set the lower limit of selected CDS rainfalls for the 
analysis. Moreover, Zealand experienced on the 2nd of July 2011 a historical rainfall which the Danish 
Meteorological Institute (DMI) characterised as least a 100-year event (DMI, 2011). It resulted in 
damages of 4.88 million DKK (Mørk, 2016). The storm was termed ‘historical’ (Mørk, 2016), thus it 
is considered suitable for defining the upper limit. It is thus decided to use CDS rainfalls with return 
periods of 20, 50, and 100 years in the analysis. The CDS rainfalls are extracted from the 
spreadsheet ‘Regional Regnrække’ version 4.1 developed by SVK (Spildevandskomiteen, 2014). 
 
As explained in section 2.1.1, the duration of a CDS rainfall should always be estimated from TOC. 
This method is applied in the present analysis. The estimation and validation of TOC can be found 
in appendix A. Rainfall input to the hydraulic models are derived from symmetric CDS rainfalls with

4.2 MIKE Flood setup 
The technical drawing in Figure 4.1 below illustrates the main inputs to and settings in the MIKE 
Flood model, which will be described in this section. A description of the estimation of relevant 
coupling parameters, rainfall input, and bathymetry file can be found in appendix B. 
 
In the MIKE Urban model, all unsealed nodes of the type manhole within the case area are coupled 
to the DEM using the 2D model, MIKE 21. The terrain model has a rectangular grid with a resolution 
of 2 m x 2 m. All cells comprising buildings are inactivated for the sake of model stability. Precipitation 
is created from the one-hour CDS rainfalls described in section 3.4 and are applied to both pervious 
and impervious surfaces. As illustrated in the figure, it should be noted that MIKE Urban applies the 
CDS rainfall on impervious surfaces and MIKE 21 on pervious surfaces. The simulation time is 2 
hours, ensuring that the peak, being when all catchments are contributing to the sewer system, is 
modelled within the computational time. The time step is specified as 0.1 second to ensure model 
stability. All results generated by the flood model are updated every two minutes.

4.2.1 Effective rainfall 
On impervious surfaces infiltration is set to 0, while infiltration on pervious surfaces is included in the 
2D inundation model. Impervious and pervious surfaces in the model are defined as illustrated in the 
figure below. On pervious surfaces, each grid cell is given the constant infiltration rate of 29.3 mm/h 
as described in section 3.5. The rate will be fully effectuated if enough water is present in the cell, 
while runoff is generated in situations where the rainfall intensity exceeds the infiltration rate (DHI, 
2017c). Moreover, runoff may infiltrate during and after the rainfall events if flowing from fully 
saturated areas to areas with excess capacity. The infiltration dfs2-file is generated in the software 
Python using a script programmed and provided by Associate Professor Roland Löwe from DTU 
Environment, Denmark. In essence, the script creates a raster where cells covering pervious 
surfaces are allocated the infiltration rate.

4.2.2 Coupling settings 
In the MIKE Urban model, nodes are located in the middle of the road. In reality, rainwater would 
flow along the sidewalk and enter the sewer system through downspouts located along the curbs 
due to the roads sloping from their middle to the sidewalks. Consequently, the 2D coupled areas 
located in roads are modified to have the width of the relevant road, letting the model capture water 
flowing at the side of the road. A figure showing how the 2D coupled areas are modified can be seen 
in appendix B. Additionally, the coupling level between the 1D and 2D model is changed from the 
default highest level to lowest level as downspouts are located at the lowest level of the road. The 
coupling parameters are identical for all coupled nodes. Max flow defines the upper limit on the 
discharge that is able to flow through the node (DHI, 2017a) and is given a high value to ensure that 
this setting will not limit the exchange of water between surface and sewer system. The inlet area 
describes the transfer of water between surface and sewer system (DHI, 2017a) and is approximated 
from the average number of downspouts in Ringsted per node in the model (see appendix B).

4.3 SCALGO setup 
As described in section 2.1.2.2, the current version of SCALGO does not include any hydrological 
losses. Yet, these losses in terms of infiltration and sewer system can be accounted for by including 
the SCS CN method. However, including the SCS CN method in SCALGO is still under 
implementation. Currently, SCALGO is testing the method utilizing the CNs described in section 
2.1.2.2 (referred to as SCALGO default CNs), thus no standard values are available. This study will 
be the first time that SCALGO includes the SCS CN method tested in a case area. Consequently, a 
major part of setting up the SCALGO model is selecting a set of SCS CNs to represent pervious and 
impervious areas in Ringsted. 
 
It is necessary to underpin that the overall purpose is to construct two comparable models. This 
purpose decides the selection of CNs, based on the aim of reducing the difference in the amount of 
water that ends up on the surface in SCALGO and MIKE Flood respectively. Thereby, other 
differences between the models may be identified. In this study, the amount of water that ends up 
on the surface will be referred to as surface runoff. Surface runoff is equal to the amount that neither 
infiltrates nor enters the sewer system. The CNs are estimated from plotting rainfall depth versus 
surface runoff depth and selecting the curve that fits best. Figure 4.3 below serves as an example 
on how to select a CN from three points representing three sets of rainfall depths of three distinct 
return periods and corresponding surface runoff depth. In the example below, it is illustrated that CN 
86 is the best fit, thus, in this example, this CN should be selected. 
 
 
Figure 4.3: An example of how a curve number, CN, is selected from three sets of rainfall depths, P, and surface runoff 
depths, Q. It is seen that CN 86 best describes the points, thus should be chosen. 
 
Initially, it was not clear which method would give the best approximation of surface runoff 
contribution from impervious and pervious areas so that results are comparable to MIKE Flood and 
required a lot of consideration. Runoff estimates could easily be obtained by using the MIKE Flood 
model. However, a MIKE Flood model is not usually available and is, at any rate, rather time 
consuming. Therefore, the aim is to identify other and simpler methods for surface runoff calculations

pervious and impervious surfaces respectively in Ringsted. Compared to MIKE Flood results,

models the most and as being applicable for deriving the CNs in Ringsted. This is explained further 
in section 5.2.2 in the result chapter. All methods considered are listed and described below, along 
with considerations of using them.  
 
Methods for estimating runoff contribution from pervious surfaces 
 
Method 1 – SCALGO default CN representing pervious surfaces 
As described in section 2.1.2, SCALGO has adopted a CN (CN 74) from the SCS CN method 
describing the rainfall-runoff relationship on pervious surfaces. Since this CN is given by the SCS 
CN method and used by SCALGO in the implementation phase, it is reasonable to take into 
consideration. The surface runoff depths are found from the known rainfall depths by reading it 
directly on the y-axis from the plotted CN curve. As mentioned, the default pervious CN represents 
average urban green areas in Denmark. As the default is an average of all Danish urban green 
areas, it is unclear how well this CN is able to describe Ringsted case area. 
 
Method 2 – pervious surface runoff from hand calculations in Excel 
The reason for considering method 2 is to get a surface runoff estimate that is as similar to MIKE 
Flood’s as possible. As opposed to method 1, this method estimates surface runoff depths from 
pervious surfaces based on the assumed infiltration rate, also used in MIKE Flood. The method 
assumes infiltration to be the only loss of water, thus surface runoff is estimated through the concept 
of mass balance; rainfall being input and infiltration being loss. Surface runoff from a rainfall is 
thereby found by subtracting the infiltration rate from rainfall intensity in every time step. All data 
processing is done utilizing Microsoft (MS) Excel and the hand calculations can be found in appendix 
C.

The highest impervious CN identified by the SCS CN method is 98 and is moreover the CN used in

on impervious surfaces is – as also mentioned under method 1 – logical to take into consideration. 
It should be noted that the default impervious CN has a high value of 98 as it does not take the 
capacity of sewer system into account. Because of this, surface runoff from impervious surfaces may 
be overestimated resulting in a bigger portion of surface runoff ending on the surface causing flood 
damages. 
 
Method 4 – Impervious surface runoff applying the method of Kaspersen et al. (2017) 
Method 4 is included as it is used in a previous study in Denmark performed by Kaspersen et al. 
(2017). The method assumes no infiltration on impervious surfaces, thus surface runoff entering the 
sewer system will be the only loss. The sewer system is assumed to have a capacity corresponding 
to precipitation from a 5-year return period. Hence, a one-hour 5-year event is subtracted from the 
one-hour CDS rainfalls in every time step. Method 4 generalizes any sewer system by assuming 
that one size fits all. This assumption led to considering method 5. 
 
Method 5 – Impervious surface runoff from computing the capacity of Ringsted sewer system 
With inspiration from Kaspersen et al. (2017), method 5 is considered in order to estimate the actual 
capacity of Ringsted sewer system. The capacity is approximated from running MIKE Urban MOUSE 
runoff and network simulations applying one-hour CDS rainfalls of various return periods and 
examining when nodes surcharge to terrain. However, this is a difficult assessment as the capacity 
of the system because local bottlenecks might create upstream flooding, although the bigger main 
pipes may still have the needed capacity or more. Finally, the rainfall depth corresponding to the 
capacity of the system is subtracted from the rainfall input in the same way as in method 4.  
 
Method 6 – Impervious surface runoff from spilling volume 
Method 6 is considered as it is an alternative method where the CN is estimated directly from the 
spilling volume by using MIKE Urban. For this method, MIKE Urban which only considers impervious 
areas is used, thus the spilling volume is considered an expression for the amount of impervious 
runoff that the sewer system cannot handle. Moreover, it is anticipated that this method produces 
precise estimates, as the capacity and hydraulics at any given time of the sewer system are included. 
All nodes in the MIKE Urban model (except outlets and basins) are changed from normal cover type 
to spilling and the surface runoff depth is estimated from the 1D simulation result summary under 
continuity balance – spilling nodes. Method 6 is applied using the one-hour CDS rainfalls with return 
periods of 20, 50, and 100 years, presented in section 3.4.

5 Results 
 
 
5.1 Replies from water professionals 
Four interviews with engineers from EnviDan are conducted as means to obtain modelling insights 
and to get knowledge about how SCALGO and MIKE Flood are used in practice. Three main 
conclusions are: 
 
• 
Parameter settings and choice of model rely on the project and available data. However, 
default parameters are usually used. In addition to this, modelling parameters are also 
chosen based on field measurements, experience, literature, inputs from for example the 
utility, or adopted from comparable projects. 
 
• 
SCALGO is applied as a screening tool, which can identify potentially problematic areas 
to ensure particular attention to these areas going forward. MIKE Flood is useful when 
doing an integrated analysis of surface as well as drainage system.  
 
• 
SCALGO is useful for comparing the overland surface to flood damages to buildings and 
the sewer system, however, it is limited by being a stationary model and because it does 
not consider sewer systems. Advantages of the model is that it is quick and intuitive. 
Conversely, MIKE Flood is computation heavy and time consuming. MIKE Flood is 
dynamic, thus able to model sudden changes in the flow direction of surface runoff and 
able to model flow paths splitting into two. 
 
The first bullet point is useful in relation to this study, as especially MIKE Flood includes many 
parameters. Therefore, it should be kept in mind that in some cases it is better to keep the default 
values, because defining parameter values on insufficient basis may increase model uncertainty 
instead of decreasing it. SCALGO is by the interviewed widely used as a screening tool for analysing 
terrain as such as assessing flow paths, watersheds, and sinks, while the interviewed attach MIKE 
software’s ability to include sewer system great importance. The indicators should be chosen to 
challenge these statements to see if the applications of SCALGO can be expanded further. The full 
interview can be found in Appendix H. 
 
This chapter presents all findings from the analysis. The chapter commences with main 
conclusions from the conducted qualitative interview, followed by an evaluation of the 
estimated runoff depths from pervious and impervious surfaces, and the corresponding

5.2 Estimation of curve numbers for application in SCALGO 
The aim with this entire section is to select a set of CNs that will reduce the difference in the amount 
of water that ends up on the surface considering SCALGO and MIKE Flood. As declared earlier, 
amount of water ending on the surface is referred to as surface runoff. In order to select the CNs, 
surface runoff depths from pervious and impervious surfaces must be estimated. As previously 
mentioned, it was not evident which methods to use. Therefore, multiple methods are tested. As the 
CNs cannot be selected before surface runoff is estimated, this result section is divided into two 
parts. Firstly, the estimated surface runoff depths using the methods described in section 4.3.1 are 
presented and compared to MIKE Flood validation method. Secondly, the resulting CNs are 
presented. From evaluating and comparing surface runoff depths and CNs with MIKE Flood 
validation method, a set of CNs is selected. 
 
5.2.1 Determining surface runoff depths from pervious and impervious areas 
 
In this section, surface runoff depths found from using the methods described in section 4.3 are 
evaluated and subsequently compared. The table further below summarizes surface runoff depths 
from impervious and pervious areas estimated from the 20-, 50-, and 100-year one-hour CDS 
rainfalls utilizing the methods. 
 
Initially, it should be noted that all estimated surface runoff depths from impervious and pervious 
surfaces increase – as expected – with return period regardless of method. Moreover, due to the 
SCS CNs curves increasing exponentially, surface runoff in all methods increasingly constitutes a 
larger proportion of the rainfall depth. 
 
Methods for estimating pervious surface runoff depths 
 
Method 1 uses CN 74 to describe the rainfall-runoff relationship on pervious surfaces. In SCALGO, 
CN 74 is only used in the implementation phase, whereas, in this study, CN 74 provides the basis 
for method 1. Using CN 74, estimated surface runoff depths are based on empirical studies 
conducted in comparable catchments (USDA, 1986).  For this reason, CN 74 to some extent includes 
infiltration of lateral inflows. Consequently, by using this method low surface runoff depths are 
obtained, especially considering the 20-year event where surface runoff equals 17% of the rainfall 
depth. 
 
Method 2 is the numerical method assuming a constant infiltration rate of 29.3 mm/h, which is the 
infiltration rate used in MIKE Flood. Considering the 20-year event and the 100-year event, surface 
runoff is 37% and 47% of rainfall depth respectively. The method is static and estimates surface 
runoff on pixel level (estimations can be found in Appendix C, S1). For this reason, it does not take 
dynamic runoff routing into account and the estimated surface runoff depths are consequently 
higher.

MIKE Flood validation method considering pervious areas is estimated from MIKE Flood mass 
balances. Two simulations are conducted: one with a simulation period of one hour and one of two 
hours (estimations can be found in Appendix C, S3). This is done in order to assess the influences 
on surface runoff depths when rainfall duration is the same, while a simulation period is increased 
from one to two hours. As presented in Table 5.1, surface runoff decreases significantly when 
increasing the simulation period. This can be explained from that MIKE Flood is a dynamic model, 
thus water can flow further downstream and infiltrate. This will occur no matter the duration of the 
simulation period, however, a longer simulation period allows more water to infiltrate due to the 
constant infiltration rate. Moreover, the difference in surface runoff depth considering the simulation 
period of one hour and two hours respectively is reduced with increasing the return period, as there 
will be less cells with excess infiltration capacity. It is realistic that surface runoff may flow further 
downstream and infiltrate when entering cells with available capacity. However, this complicates the 
validation of methods 1 and 2, as more infiltration capacity is effectuated due to the dynamics 
included in MIKE Flood. These factors become very significant when applying a two hour simulation 
period making it hard to assess which duration should be used for the validation. Yet, this method 
as validation method applying one hour simulation period will be used. This is done further below. 
 
Methods for estimating impervious surface runoff depths 
 
Method 3 assumes an impervious CN (CN 98) adopted from the SCS CN method and simulated in 
USDA (1986). Applying method 3 results in high surface runoff depth estimates. This was expected 
as method 3 does not consider the influence of the sewer system. Consequently, 83% to 89% of 
rainfall is turned into surface runoff, when using this method. 
 
Method 4 adopting the method of Kaspersen et al. (2017), a one-hour 5-year return period is 
subtracted from the one-hour CDS rainfalls in every time step. Surface runoff depths are found to 
constitute 30%, 44%, and 52% of the rainfall depth considering the 20-, 50-, and 100-year event 
respectively. Estimations can be found in Appendix C, S1. 
 
Method 5 initially estimates the capacity of Ringsted sewer system. From running MIKE Urban 
simulations with different return periods, it is found that the capacity of Ringsted sewer system is 
approximately a 2-year event, despite water is surcharged to the surface in specific nodes (see result 
files in appendix D). Given a 2-year return period, Skrift 27 recommends combined sewers in urban 
areas to be running full, which Ringsted sewer system according to the analysis does not live up to 
(Spildevandskomiteen, 2005). This may be both due to the sewer system being an older system and 
due to the analysis, as the capacity was difficult to assess, as mentioned in section 4.3.1. Due to the 
latter, it may be that the system has a bigger capacity than found from the analysis. Appendix D 
contains result files presented in MIKE View showing flooded nodes when the model is loaded with 
a T1, T2, T3, T4, and T5 one-hour CDS rainfall respectively. From using this method, it is found that 
46%, 57%, and 63% of rainfall becomes surface runoff considering the return periods of 20, 50, and 
100 years respectively. Surface runoff depth estimations can be found in Appendix C, S1.

Method 6 estimates surface runoff directly from the spilling volume by using the MIKE Urban. The

Validation of methods with MIKE Flood validation method 
As outlined in the beginning of this section, the overall aim is to select a set of SCS CNs that will 
reduce the difference in surface runoff between SCALGO and MIKE Flood. Moreover, it should be 
kept in mind that surface runoff is defined as the amount of water that ends up on the surface. As 
mentioned, the estimated surface runoff depths from pervious and impervious areas are to be 
compared with the MIKE Flood validation method below. 
 
Considering pervious surfaces, methods 1 and 2 are candidates. Comparing the findings using 
method 1 with the validation method of a one hour simulation period reveals that method 1 
consistently underestimates the surface runoff. Conversely, method 2 consistently overestimates the 
surface runoff. But as return periods get bigger, the findings of method 2 come increasingly nearer 
the findings of the validation method, while the opposite is the case for method 1. As mentioned 
above, MIKE Flood is a dynamic model meaning that runoff may flow downstream and infiltrate, and 
the results point towards method 1 being able to empirically account for some of these dynamics. 
Meanwhile, method 2 uses the same infiltration rate as in MIKE Flood. It mathematically and 
accurately imitates the MIKE Flood computation. The only deviation in results using method 2 
therefore arises from the infiltration of lateral flows in MIKE Flood. Moreover, the findings using 
method 2 are closest to the validation method. For these reasons, it is decided to select method 2 
for estimating the amount of runoff ending up on pervious surfaces. 
 
It is observed that the validation results for the one hour simulation period are between the two 
methods, thus one could argue that both methods can be applied, and additionally that the answer 
may lie somewhere between the methods.

runoff depths compared to all methods. Method 3 is approximately 8 times greater than the MIKE 
Flood validation method (one hour simulation), method 4 approximately 4 times greater, method 5 
approximately 5 times greater, and method 6 approximately 2 times greater. Therefore, method 6 is 
the closest to the findings using MIKE Flood validation method, which indicates that method 6 should 
be used for estimating the amount of runoff ending up on impervious surfaces.

Rainfall depths and corresponding estimated surface runoff depths found from using method 2 and 
method 6, and SCALGO default CN curves (CN 74 and 98) are plotted in Figure 5.1. Plots of all 
other methods including the validation method can be found in appendix C, S4. The red (CN = 98) 
and green (CN = 74) dashed graphs in Figure 5.1 illustrate SCALGO default SCS CN curves 
describing average impervious and pervious surfaces respectively in Danish cities (Lerer, 2020). As 
shown in the graphic, the coordinates representing pervious (method 2) and impervious (method 6) 
surfaces nicely follow the SCS CN curves representing CN 86 and 73 respectively (black and blue 
solid curves). Method 6 results in a lower CN compared to SCALGO default impervious CN because 
of the inclusion of the sewer system. Basically, less runoff becomes surface runoff (the amount of 
water that ends on the surface) as some enters the sewer system and consequently the CN goes 
down. 
 
The pervious SCS CN curve representing method 2 lies above SCALGO default pervious SCS CN 
curve. SCALGO default pervious CN is based on the SCS CN method and found from the 
characteristics of the case area, while method 2 is estimated on ‘pixel level’. Consequently, method 
2 does not consider infiltration of lateral inflows, which SCALGO default pervious CN might, as 
explained above, to some extent include. As a result, method 2 has a greater CN compared to 
SCALGO default pervious CN of 74. As elaborated previously, the CN where surface runoff differs 
the least between SCALGO and MIKE Flood may lie between methods 1 and 2. Therefore, a 
suggestion for further investigation is to test longer and shorter TOCs. Changing TOC will 
subsequently change rainfall depth and amount of water that can infiltrate, thus changing TOC will 
also influence the CN that fitting the MIKE Flood results. 
 
When applying method 6, the impervious surface runoff depths are lower than the pervious surface 
runoff depths estimated from method 2. Subsequently, the estimated CN for impervious surfaces is 
lower than that of method 2 and SCALGO default CN. This may be explained by the sewer system 
capacity, which, according to the results, has a bigger capacity than the infiltration capacity of 
pervious areas.

The results obtained from using SCALGO are greatly influenced by the selected CNs, as the CNs 
are the only factor influencing the rainfall-runoff relationship in the model. For this reason, the results 
listed in the tables above can be used to identify differences between the models caused by 
modelling principles as well as help reveal if the estimated contributions of surface runoff are 
accurate enough for the models to be compared from the indicators. 
 
Table 5.3 indicates – as anticipated – an increase in flooded area and flood depth with return period 
regardless of model. When applying MIKE Flood, the flooded area increases with 58% from the 20-
year event to the 100-year event, while the flooded area projected by SCALGO only increases with 
20%. When considering the 20-year event, flooded area and flood volume projected by SCALGO is 
almost identical to MIKE Flood. Therefore, increasing return period increases the difference in 
projections between the models. This difference can be explained by different runoff routing and 
different properties of the models. Firstly, SCALGO routes all runoff directly into sinks unlike MIKE 
Flood as it is a dynamic model where runoff may freely change flow direction. Secondly, SCALGO 
does not consider flooding along flow paths, which in MIKE Flood is a key driver for the observed 
increase in flooded area. Consequently, the flooded area does not increase as much as in MIKE 
Flood, and additionally, sinks are filled much faster in SCALGO compared to MIKE Flood. The latter 
is moreover confirmed by indicator 3. Both the total maximal water depth in sinks and the number of 
sinks containing runoff is significantly greater in SCALGO compared to MIKE Flood. As SCALGO is 
a static model, flooding is observed as a final snapshot after all water is routed to and accumulated 
in sinks. In contrast, flooding in MIKE Flood is simulated continuously from the maximum inundation 
observed at any point in time within the simulation period. Therefore, while runoff is routed, it will 
contribute to and be registered as flooding in multiple cells in MIKE Flood. Consequently, flooded 
area and flood volume estimated from MIKE Flood are, opposed to SCALGO, not linked to the water 
mass balance.  
 
The above mentioned differences between SCALGO and MIKE Flood with regard to runoff routing 
and flooding along flow paths complicate the comparison of results of Indicator 1 and Indicator 2 of 
the models and make the indicators less valuable in this study. Considering these indicators, the 
starting point for comparing the models is complex as these differences means that it is to be 
expected that the results will also turn out very differently. A more valid comparison of the models is 
achieved when comparing the models from the sinks, as both models project the accumulation of 
water in sinks. Moreover, this way discrepancies, because of flooding along flow paths being 
projected by MIKE Flood but not by SCALGO, is avoided when comparing sinks.

surface, while the 1D drainage model in MIKE Flood applies MOUSE Model A. Consequently, runoff 
from impervious areas is connected to the drainage system as a direct load to the nodes of the 
network. Therefore, excessive runoff spreads on the 2D surface from the 2D coupled areas located 
in the roads when either the connection capacity is limited or when the sewer system becomes 
overloaded and subsequently surcharges. In reality, runoff would possibly surcharge at other points 
in the catchment such as the gutters, from which it could flow towards lower terrain following different 
flow patterns. Yet, in MIKE Flood, much runoff instead accumulates in the roads. Therefore, 
predicted flooding in roads may be overestimated.

including the CNs in the model does not improve the agreement between SCALGO and MIKE Flood 
significantly according to CSI. As EAD is estimated from the number of flooded buildings, and since

buildings identified by SCALGO is consequently 27% higher than MIKE Flood.

6 Discussion 
 
 
6.1 Indicator comparison 
Some key differences in flood projections between SCALGO and MIKE Flood are: 
 
• 
There is a significant difference in flooded area and flooded volume when 
considering the most frequent events. The projections by SCALGO are in general 
smaller than those of MIKE Flood. 
• 
A higher number of sinks contain runoff and are additionally filled with a greater 
amount of water in SCALGO compared to MIKE Flood. 
• 
Locations of flooding differ. 
• 
More buildings are flooded in SCALGO compared to MIKE Flood. The models only 
to some extent predict the same flooded buildings. 
 
The differences mentioned in the first bullet are greatly influenced by how flooding is estimated and 
presented by the models. As mentioned in the results section, the same water may be accounted 
for more than once in the flood estimations using MIKE Flood. Moreover, flooding along flow paths 
are included. Conversely, SCALGO creates flooding by filling up sinks, not including flooding along 
flow paths, thus flood extent and flood volume are displayed and estimated from the final 
accumulation of surface runoff in sinks. These differences in the models become very significant, 
especially when comparing projected flooded areas. As MIKE Flood does include flooding along flow 
paths – and as the results are greatly influenced by it – while SCALGO does not, it is expected that 
the results deviate. Therefore, this comparison will not gain much value to the study. For this reason, 
it is more reasonable to either compare the models based on flooding in sinks, or to include flooding 
along flow paths in SCALGO. The latter is discussed further in section 6.4. 
 
As mentioned above, due to major differences in how flood extent is simulated by the models, 
comparison of flood projections will be most valid when comparing flooding in sinks. However, the 
results still deviate. The second bullet addresses the observed difference of flooding in sinks: a 
higher number of sinks contain accumulated water in SCALGO compared to MIKE Flood. 
Additionally, the sinks contain more water. Two explanations for these observations may be that 
surface runoff is routed differently in the models and that the applied CNs result in too much water 
in the SCALGO model. SCALGO routes all runoff directly into sinks following the flow paths identified

circumstances, SCALGO continues to route surface runoff along the same flow paths. During an

Therefore, there is a need for further investigation on methods to more accurately estimate 
impervious and pervious CNs.

even though the models comply with flood maps in some situations, generally there is quite a 
difference in the locations of flooding. A major reason for this observation is the differences in how

neglection of flooding along flow paths as well as MIKE Flood accounting for the same water more

reason, it is hard to compare the models from the flood maps. Even though SCALGO is compared 
with MIKE Flood it should be kept in mind that MIKE Flood is still “just” an uncalibrated model 
sensitive to parameters. Therefore, validation data is needed in order to assess the flood projections.

have some false negatives in its identification of flooded buildings. However, as SCALGO does not 
include any sewer system, flooding caused by back up (in Danish: tilbagestuvning) of water is not

flows to the next downstream sink. In MIKE Flood the water may not reach this downstream sink if 
captured by a drainage grill or if routed by another flow path than the one identified by SCALGO. 
When buildings are located along sinks, SCALGO may project that all will be flooded, while MIKE 
Flood will not.

6.2 Time of concentration, rainfall duration, and the SCS CN method 
In SCALGO, the same rainfall depth is applied on the entire catchment, thus individual TOCs of 
subcatchments are not taken into consideration. For this reason, the furthest upstream 
subcatchment directing runoff to the furthest upstream sink is allocated a rainfall depth bigger than 
it ought to be, which in SCALGO gives a greater degree of filling of that sink. In reality, upstream 
sinks having smaller subcatchments have smaller TOC compared to downstream sinks having big 
subcatchments. Because of this, less water is lead to smaller sinks, thus smaller rainfall depths 
should be considered. In the present study, this is not accounted for when choosing rainfall depth, 
as the rainfall depths applied in MIKE Flood are derived from the CDS rainfalls, which are defined 
from the TOC describing the entire catchment. As a consequence, in SCALGO upstream sinks are 
exposed to a rainfall depth representing a much bigger catchment; a factor that this study neither 
takes into account nor explores when choosing rainfall depth in SCALGO.  
 
Like SCALGO, the SCS CN method only considers the total rainfall depth of any given event, as the 
method assumes that rainfall is continuous in time and uniform in each catchment. Therefore, the 
method does not take intensity as well as temporal distribution of rainfall into account. However, 
different TOCs will affect both rainfall depth and the amount of water that can infiltrate, and therefore 
the amount of surface runoff. In additional, the temporal variations of a rainfall may influence how 
much water will end up on surfaces. 
 
Considering pervious areas and based on the above, the SCS CN method will always estimate the 
same amount of surface runoff considering the same catchment (thus the same CN) and a certain 
rainfall depth. This means that the SCS CN method estimates the same the amount of surface runoff 
when considering events with the same rainfall depth, however, with different temporal distributions 
and different peak intensities. Figure 6.1 below illustrates that even though the rainfall depth is the 
same, intensity and temporal distribution can affect the resulting runoff volumes. 
 
The SCS CN method gives an average surface runoff depth, which will be incorrect when comparing 
it to each individual event, but the most accurate when considering the average event. However, in 
reality, a short and intense rainfall with the same rainfall depth as a long rainfall with low intensity 
will in some cases result in more surface runoff than the latter, as illustrated in the figure below. The 
reason is that the rainfall intensity of the prior exceeds the infiltration rate of the soil. As a constant 
infiltration rate is assumed in this study (as also illustrated in the figure), this relation between 
infiltration rate and rainfall intensity may be of minor importance. However, applying an infiltration 
varying with time, which can only be modelled in MIKE Flood, would probably lead to bigger 
differences in the results between the models. Therefore, it could be investigated whether a higher 
CN than would normally be chosen should be applied when considering events of very high 
intensities, an interesting topic for future studies. 
 
In addition, because of a higher soil water content, more runoff is generated from pervious areas 
during fall and winter compared to summer (Nielsen, 2019). Therefore, it would also be interesting 
to investigate the choice of CNs considering annual variations. This is, however, not a part of the 
present study.

Considering impervious areas, the capacity of sewer systems, and thus the amount of surface runoff 
generated, is less influenced by annual variations. Consequently, the amount of surface runoff from 
impervious areas will vary less than pervious areas throughout the year. Therefore, it may be 
reasonable to define one single impervious SCS CN representing impervious areas. Currently, the 
SCS CN method uses CN 98 as this method does not account for the effect of the sewer system. 
This CN may be correct when considering paved areas not connected to the sewer system since 
close to all rainwater will become surface runoff, but it will greatly overestimate surface runoff 
considering impervious areas in areas connected to the sewer system. 
 
In the present study, the overall aim is to construct a SCALGO model that is comparable to a specific 
MIKE Flood setup. This is the only consideration when estimating surface runoff and selecting CNs. 
However, it is found that the selection of CNs is complex and associated with many uncertainties, 
which could inspire many more studies. For this reason, it may be useful to have a set of CNs which 
represents average Danish conditions. 
 
 
6.3 Limitations related to the case area 
A key limitation of this study is that the models are only tested in one case area and the findings 
might thus not be representative for every urban catchment. Ringsted case area comprises 
apartments, detached housing, and green areas, has an older combined sewer system, and may 
furthermore be characterized as a steep catchment. These are some of the characteristics limiting 
the application of the findings in other areas. Yet, it may be discussed whether the behavior observed 
is only due to the characteristics and limitations of the Ringsted case area, or if SCALGO has some 
limitations of its own affecting the results. 
 
In general, more buildings are flooded in SCALGO compared to MIKE Flood. Several buildings in 
Ringsted are exposed as they are located in or near a depression in terrain, thus a high number of 
flooded buildings is expected. However, in relation to identifying buildings being flooded, it seems

that the modeling principles of SCALGO are a main limitation as well. In every catchment runoff will 
be routed according to the 8N approach, thus runoff will always flow directly into sinks no matter the 
catchment. Yet, it would be interesting to test case areas of different sizes and with other 
characteristics, such as less terrain slope, and with another composition of buildings. 
 
It is anything but simple to make general statements on where the models are likely to concur in 
catchments, as the models are only tested in one case area. However, a parameter that would most 
likely influence the results when testing other case areas is sewer systems, as not two are alike. As 
an example, a sewer system sloping in other directions than terrain would be likely to increase the 
differences in flood projections, as the sewer system would lead the water in another direction than 
the terrain. In such cases, as SCALGO only projects the terrain, flooding might occur differently 
compared to MIKE Flood. Moreover, flooding caused by an undersized sewer system could greatly 
influence the results, as such flooding would be captured by MIKE Flood and not by SCALGO. 
 
 
6.4 Application of SCALGO 
This section takes its starting point in the applicability of SCALGO. The assessment of when to apply 
SCALGO relies on the comparison of flood estimations carried out in this study. Below are listed 
three use cases in relation to flood analyses and SCALGO is benchmarked against them. 
 
Identification of exposed areas 
When performing a flood hazard assessment, one aim is to identify areas prone to flooding and 
critical hot spots. These are analyses that SCALGO can perform, as SCALGO identifies vulnerable 
areas in Ringsted. More accurate results are expected when using MIKE Flood, however, setting up 
a MIKE Flood model is time consuming and the simulation computation heavy. As told by the 
interviewees, in SCALGO, analyses are quickly obtained. Therefore, it may be advantageous to 
apply SCALGO for screenings for urban pluvial flooding. 
 
Accumulation of water in sinks 
Assessing the amount of water that accumulates in sinks is also relevant when performing a flood 
hazard assessment. This analysis can be performed in SCALGO. In this study, SCALGO 
overestimates the amount of water in each sink as well as the number of sinks containing runoff 
compared to MIKE Flood. However, the inclusion of CNs in SCALGO causes the results of SCALGO 
and MIKE Flood to approximate each other. It may be possible to reduce the aforementioned 
overestimation compared to MIKE Flood when better methods for selecting a set of CNs are 
identified. However, the SCS CN method will never be able to account for the dynamics in the sewer 
system, thus SCALGO may overestimate the water accumulating in a sink if the sink in reality is 
connected to the sewer system. 
 
Flood risk analysis 
Performing a flood risk analysis using SCALGO with the setup tested in the present study will be 
difficult. As flooding along flow paths are not included, the model does not project the full 
consequences of a flood inducing rainfall. SCALGO does include an add-on module to estimate

7 Conclusion 
Overall, it is difficult to configurate SCALGO using surface runoff computations in a way that brings 
SCALGO flood projections closer to those of MIKE Flood. It proved challenging to identify a method 
able to accurately estimate surface runoff, from which the Soil Conservation Service (SCS) Curve 
Numbers (CNs) were derived, that would make the projections close enough between the two 
models. Using the SCS CN method clearly improves the flood projections of SCALGO in the sense 
that they approximate those of MIKE Flood. However, this comes at the cost of many questions and 
unsolved issues. 
 
Firstly, upstream sinks, having smaller subcatchments, have smaller TOC compared to downstream 
sinks with bigger subcatchments. In this study, the rainfall depth representative for the entire 
catchment is applied, thus upstream sinks are exposed to a rainfall depth bigger than it ought to be. 
It could be valuable to further explore how rainfall depth should be chosen in SCALGO in order to 
make up for this issue. Secondly, different TOCs alter rainfall depth and the amount of water able to 
infiltrate, and therefore the amount of water ending up on the surface. A second suggestion for further 
work is therefore to investigate the effect of applying longer as well as shorter rainfall durations, and 
historical rainfalls, and how these factors would influence the choice of CN. In relation to this and 
when considering pervious areas, it could be investigated whether a higher CN than would normally 
be chosen should be applied when considering events of very high intensities. In addition, because 
of a higher soil water content during winter, more runoff is generated from pervious areas. It would 
therefore be interesting to investigate the choice of CNs considering annual variations. 
 
Runoff is routed differently in the models, which becomes significant when comparing the results of 
SCALGO and MIKE Flood. Therefore, the comparison should rely on comparing the water 
accumulated in sinks, excluding flooding along flow paths. Compared to MIKE Flood, SCALGO 
overestimates the amount of runoff in sinks. This is certainly due to the runoff routing differences, 
however it also indicates that the surface runoff estimations (from which the CNs are selected) are 
too high, hence there is more water in SCALGO than in MIKE Flood.  
 
It should be kept in mind that SCALGO is validated against MIKE Flood. Even though MIKE Flood 
is considered state of the art in flood mapping, it is after all an uncalibrated model built upon uncertain 
parameters, to which the model is very sensitive.  
 
Considering potential use cases where SCALGO may be adequate when projecting pluvial flooding, 
it is indeed found that SCALGO may be used for identification of areas prone to flooding and 
identification of critical flood hotspots in a catchment. SCALGO is moreover useful when assessing 
the amount of water that accumulates in sinks. Performing flood risk analysis using SCALGO is 
difficult, because SCALGO does not include flooding along flow paths. However, an existing add-on 
module included in SCALGO may enable the inclusion of flooding along flow paths, possibly making 
it possible to perform initial risk calculations in SCALGO. This is, however, yet to be confirmed.

A  Estimation and validation of time of concentration 
Ringsted is an urban area densely covered by downspouts connected to the sewer system. 
Therefore, time of concentration (TOC) representing Ringsted is estimated based on the assumption 
that surface travel time is sufficiently short and thus can be ignored (Winter et al., 2011). TOC is 
thereby solely estimated based on the travel time within the sewer system. In other words, the time 
it takes the water to flow from the most remote point in the sewer system within the case area to the 
most distant outlet in the case area. Consequently, equation a1 below can be used to estimate TOC 
(Winter et al, 2011).

B  Model parameter settings in MIKE Flood 
This appendix contains changes to parameters in the 1D-2D model set up. Parameters requested in 
the 2D interface that are not specified (thus keeping their default value) are not discussed in this 
section. Settings regarding the Time-Area Method used in the 1D drainage model are furthermore 
listed in a table below. 
 
Precipitation 
The CDS rainfalls are uploaded to the 1D drainage model as storm runoff catchment loads, applied 
only on impervious surfaces. Meanwhile, the 2D inundation model applies precipitation on pervious 
surfaces. In order to reduce both computational time and result files, the CDS rainfalls applied on 
pervious surfaces are reduced from 60 time steps (60 minutes) to six time steps of 10 minutes each. 
This is processed in Excel. The 2D precipitation dfs2-file is generated in the software Python using 
a script programmed and provided by Associate Professor Roland Löwe from DTU Environment, 
Denmark. In essence, the script loads a DEM where cells covering pervious surfaces are allocated 
the 2D precipitation. Consequently, precipitation varies in time as well as domain. 
 
Bathymetry 
The bathymetry file is developed from the DEM and illustrated in figure 0.4 below. It includes the 
physical form of any given terrain. Moreover, the bathymetry defines the computational domain with 
a 2 meter rectangular grid spacing and is conducted in MIKE Zero. The size of the bathymetry file is 
deliberately larger than the case area allowing excess water to exit the case area in the model. All 
orange cells in Figure 0.4 are inactive cells because they are set equal to the ‘Land value’ defined 
in the bathymetry editor, hence not considered in the calculation. For the sake of model stability all 
cells comprising buildings are also inactivated.

meters. The red cells are not considered in the calculation.
