Abstract 
This thesis evaluates 15 prominent green building frameworks (e.g. LEED, WELL, 
IEQ Compass, etc.) that include suggestions and/or qualifications for healthy design. 
Furthermore, it proposes a new framework for assessing health in early-stage 
building design from these 15 frameworks. The newly formed framework is 
comprised of 40 indicators which are indicative of possible health outcomes for 
building occupants. The second half of this thesis evaluates existing methods for 
translating these 40 building health indicators into quantitative health outcomes for 
the occupants. A tool concept is proposed utilizing these 40 indicators, including a 
feasibility evaluation of the indicators and a demonstration of the tool utilizing one of 
these indicators. The tool is intended to enhance the Iterative Design Process (IDP) 
through an automated health assessment of a digital building design.

Overview and Introduction 
This thesis is comprised of four main components: (1) an evaluation of health 
indicators from 15 sustainable building design frameworks, (2) a synthesized new 
health indicator framework, (3) a proposal for a BIM-compatible digital health 
assessment tool based on this framework, and (4) a proof of concept for the tool. 
This thesis aims to continue the convergence of understanding towards what 
constitutes a healthy building and propose actionable design solutions. 
 
Figure 1. This thesis is comprised of four phases from evaluating the current extent of health in green building 
frameworks to isolating specific parameters from these frameworks to develop a tool concept. 
15 Framework Assessment 
There were 15 frameworks selected to assess the current state of health in 
sustainable design. These frameworks include some of the most common or relevant 
building certification schemes, guidance documents, and regulations pertaining to 
sustainability and health within Europe and North America, with a focus on 
Scandinavia. These frameworks present inspiration or recommendations for 
sustainable and healthy design in a variety of ways. Some are more detailed and 
concrete while others are more ambiguous and abstract.  
Each framework’s suggested metrics were assessed to determine which may be 
used as potential health outcome indicators, based on the review of existing 
research. These metrics are then narrowed further to leading, objective, and indirect 
indicators of health. The indicators from these frameworks have been selected 
==PAGE:9==
2 
 
based on what they tell us about health and then grouped based on their 
characterization regarding their IEQ category and their link(s) to design decisions.  
Synthesized Health Indicator Framework 
The next component of this thesis utilizes the assessment of the 15 frameworks to 
synthesize the most relevant health-related Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ) 
indicators considered in building design today into an early-stage building health 
evaluation framework. A literature review was conducted to determine how these 
indicators are related to specific design choices and health outcomes. A new 
framework is suggested consisting of 40 health indicators that encompass the four 
main facets of IEQ – indoor air quality, thermal, visual, and acoustic comfort [1].  
Tool Concept 
The second half of this thesis consists of the conceptual development of a digital tool 
to assess, in the early design stages, a building’s potential impact on occupant health 
by utilizing the Industry Foundation Classes (IFC) file format of BIM and different 
Building Performance Simulation (BPS) tools [2]. IFC is intended to standardize the 
format of building data so that models can be exchanged between applications [3].  
The indicators from the new framework are connected to early-design strategies and 
an approach for implementation into an early-stage BIM tool is suggested. One of 
the main components of this phase was to quantify the relationship between design 
choices and potential health outcomes. 
The objective of this thesis is a proposal for a digital tool for early-stage design of 
healthy buildings that can be used as an additional consideration to factors such as 
cost, time, function, and sustainability, as opposed to an alternative. The tool follows 
the concept of analyzing building health through the connections between building 
design, the resulting indirect health indicators, and finally the potential health 
outcomes based on these indicators. Importantly, the tool can be utilized in a 
prescriptive decision-making manner as opposed to purely evaluative. 
Proof of Concept 
To demonstrate the potential functionality of the proposed tool, a proof of concept 
was developed for one of the 40 health performance indicators, volatile organic 
compound (VOC) concentration. The result is a Python script which requires an IFC 
file as the main input and outputs a Disability Adjusted Life Years (DALYs) report for 
the building based on that specific indicator. DALY is one way to measure harm to a 
population of people caused by an exposure. The overall input and output is similar 
to that of a building LCA software which requires a building model to gather all 
materials and outputs a Global Warming Potential (GWP) for the building. The 
==PAGE:10==
3 
 
DALYs can then be used to iterate the design within the tool by making adjustments 
and comparing the resulting DALYs. This is demonstrated in the final phase of this 
thesis through an evaluation of three different flooring options in a model and how 
they impact the DALYs output.

Background 
The Drivers of Building Design 
Indoor environmental concerns regularly come second to the outdoor environment. 
Design for healthy indoor spaces has not been the top priority of the building 
industry. Barring developments such as banning specific harmful materials like lead-
based paint and asbestos in some regions, the importance of indoor environmental 
health is grossly underrecognized. Historically, the design drivers behind 
architecture have been cost, time, material availability, structure, etc., often 
excluding health. Sustainability is a more recent consideration at the forefront of 
design but should not be separated from health. Arguably, nothing unhealthy is 
sustainable for the human population. Our environment is one of the biggest 
impactors of our health and people spend on average 90% of their time indoors, 
making our built environment one of the most impactful factors [4]. The movement 
of salutogenesis1 means that improving health is no longer solely focused on 
prevention of disease but also supporting optimal health. This concept translates 
from medical and lifestyle interventions to architecture. Building design can hinder 
health through exposure and poor environment or aid health by preventing exposure 
and fostering a supportive environment. However, the definition of a healthy building 
is largely disjointed across the industry so for the purposes of this thesis will be 
defined early on. 
Design tools have been developed to aid with traditional design drivers such as cost, 
time, materials, structure, and energy but we are lacking contiguous design tools for 
early health interventions. While it is essential to be mindful of oversimplification, 
people naturally veer towards simple tools to guide their decision making [5]. Thus, 
the development of simple but accurate BIM tools, BPS, and assessments effectively 
brings knowledge and theory into routine practice. An example of such a tool is Life 
Cycle Assessments (LCAs), used for sustainability evaluations. Software like 
LCAByg and OneClick have made conducting LCAs on buildings a relatively simple 
task. The material components of a building are input from a BIM file and a series of 
sustainability metrics are output. The main output of concern from building LCAs for 
assessing sustainability is the GWP in kilogram of CO2 per square meter. While there 
are many limitations to simplifying sustainability to a single number, the goal of 
decreasing GWP guides designers to make more sustainable choices. Additionally, 
our ability to make accurate assessments of sustainability are continuing to improve.

The current limitations of these metrics should not eliminate the tools as valuable for 
design. The same strategies can be translated to health of buildings. 
A simple tool is the easiest way to transmit the knowledge from decades of research 
into actionable design choices by architects. However, the metrics used by 
engineers for determining the health of a building may not translate into the language 
used by architects and designers. For example, the significance of metrics such as 
VOC concentration and spatial daylight autonomy (sDA) as results of design choices 
may mean little to the architect as compared to more universally understood metrics 
such as harm to health and wellbeing. Once this language is translated, the 
development of a decision-making tool bridging the gap between science and design 
is more feasible [6]. Another obstacle to healthy design is defining what a healthy 
building is and accurately determining connections to health outcomes. Thus, the 
first need is a clear understanding of the parameters which impact health.  
The Current State of Health in Buildings 
Health is a broad and complex term encompassing a state of optimal being and 
those factors that affect that state of being can be deemed as healthy, unhealthy, or 
neutral. As the theory of salutogenesis suggest, the scope of health continues to 
grow and evolve as our ability to optimize human longevity and life quality advance 
[7]. Today, the World Health Organization (WHO) defines health as “a state of 
complete physical, mental, and social well-being and not merely the absence of 
disease of infirmity” [8].  
At a point in architecture’s history the top ‘health’ priority of a building was simply to 
provide shelter from the natural environment and be structurally sound to ensure that 
the shelter itself did not cause harm to the occupants. This example encompasses 
the protective and preventative abilities of our built environment for our health. The 
point being that the link between health and buildings has existed since the 
beginning of architecture. Moreover, as our knowledge of this link has grown, so has 
our ability to control related health outcomes. Sick Building Syndrome (SBS) 
represented a shift in the way health and buildings were understood. The term SBS 
was first identified and coined in the 1970s after a series of cases in which office 
occupants were experiencing an array of symptoms, seemingly related to the 
building but unexplained. This spurred the rise of a contrast with the so-called 
‘healthy building.’ Leading to the study of how design could produce a healthy or sick 
building [9].  
==PAGE:13==
6 
 
 
Figure 2. Licina et. al. depicts how the focus on health has evolved within the green building industry [10]. 
There is an untapped market for healthy buildings, as detailed by Allen and 
Macomber in their book Healthy Buildings: How Indoor Spaces Drive Performance. 
Until the realization and conceptualization of SBS, health pertaining to buildings was 
approached with reaction as opposed to prevention. Documented cases of exposure 
to indoor environmental chemicals and substances of concern (COCs) like asbestos, 
lead, radon and bacterial contaminants like legionella, demonstrated the potentially 
fatal consequences of poor Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ). Naturally, changes 
have occurred in the industry as a response to such incidences. However, the market 
for healthy buildings is hindered by the lack of a common consensus on what a 
healthy building is; how to quantify health; varying standards for metrics; disjointed 
information between key stakeholders; a lack of follow-through from design, 
development to occupancy; and the misconception of health being a luxury that 
comes at a premium. Allen and Macomber make a case for why healthy buildings, 
particularly in the context of commercial office buildings are not only the right thing 
to do but also the financially smart decision [11]. 
For the sake of this thesis, a healthy building is defined as one that is designed 
through the lens of occupant health as the top priority and to the healthiest extent 
possible given the state of health knowledge at the time. This means that what may 
be deemed as a healthy building designed today may not be the healthiest building 
in 50 years as the relationships between our built environment and health continue 
to be better understood. But, each building should be optimized according to the 
status of health knowledge at the time of design.

As depicted in Figure 2, healthy buildings have also taken a backseat to energy 
savings and environmental sustainability. Since this recognition of a building’s 
impact on occupant health, frameworks for healthy design have been developed, but 
largely as a subcategory of sustainability. These frameworks come in the form of 
certification schemes, regulations, guidance, etc. Certification schemes like 
BREEAM and LEED arose in the 1990s and have increased their focus on health 
but schemes like WELL are entirely focused on the experience of the occupants [12]. 
Certifications schemes are intended to improve the overall quality of buildings and 
incentivize developers with the ability to market the quality of their product as a 
sustainable and healthy choice [13]. Some drawbacks of these certifications are their 
expansiveness, complexity, and the labor required to obtain.  Hence, one of the aims 
of this thesis is ultimately to reframe the lens through which health in building design 
is approached, by making it simpler to assess and thus implement. 
Reframing the Process with BIM, Early-Stage Design, and IDP 
The aim of this section is not to suggest a transformation to the building design 
process but to evaluate the existing technologies and processes that are already 
innovating the industry through a new lens. Design has become increasingly 
complex, due both to our demands and to our growing abilities to build more complex 
structures. This means that there is likely no definitive optimal solution. Instead, a 
more realistic result is a series of continuous iterations of improvement [14]. As the 
design process evolves, it is relevant to continue assessing why, when, and how 
solutions can be implemented to result in the best outcomes.  
Why. With an increasing demand for high performing buildings, both in terms of 
environmental sustainability and in human health, there is a need for interdisciplinary 
work between experts across many fields. It is not possible to expect designers to 
have the abilities of an architect as well as a structural engineer, energy engineer, 
HVAC specialist, etc. The result of this new reality is a modern design framework 
called the Integrated Design Process (IDP). The principal of IDP is that the individual 
designer is replaced by a team of designers, each with specific abilities [15]. This 
richness in knowledge can enhance the overall quality of design but requires 
effective communication between counterparts and requires an iterative process of 
compromise. IDP is often applied to optimize the energy usage of a building. The 
tool proposed in this thesis intends to introduce a digital solution to more easily 
incorporate health into the IDP process. 
When. The earlier in the design stage considerations to health are made, the more 
cost effective and likely the health interventions are to be implemented into the 
design. Consider the McLeamy curve in Figure 3, the most effective time to make 
changes to a design is early.  
==PAGE:15==

By shifting the traditional design process, the more likely they are to be implemented 
in the final building and the more cost effective. Thus, conducting an early-stage 
evaluation of health, while not necessarily comprehensive or entirely conclusive, 
begins steering the project towards health with actionable suggestions. 
How. Digital tools are pivotal to turning the abstract into actionable and the 
qualitative into quantitative. They transform once menial and tedious tasks into 
automated and detailed systems. One technology rapidly shifting the building 
industry and making collaboration easier is Building Information Modelling (BIM). 
BIM is defined as “the virtual representation of the physical and functional 
characteristics of a facility from inception onward. As such, it serves as a shared 
information repository for collaboration throughout a [building’s] life cycle” [17]. BIM 
can be combined with simulation software to make assessments early in the design 
process [18][19]. The premise of BIM is that with enough data, we can essentially 
create a digital twin of any building. However, there is a lack of data and tools for 
assessing the health of buildings, and of the existing tools, most are limited to the 
research domain, making their integration into actual design difficult [14]. 
Additionally, current frameworks are generally based around measurements and 
occupant surveys of an already constructed building, leaving minimal to no time for

Hypothesis 
This thesis aims to follow the hypothetico-deductive scientific method from both a 
qualitative and quantitative research approach. The hypothetico-deductive method 
requires that the hypothesis is able to be falsified by a test on observable data [21]. 
From the established thesis methodology and objective, the following overarching 
hypothesis is considered: 
Within the current knowledge framework of green buildings, a digital building health 
assessment tool can be designed which quantifies a building’s potential health 
implications in such a way that the tool outputs actionable information for iterating 
the design towards a healthier outcome. 
This thesis is limited in testing this hypothesis because a fully developed tool would 
require knowledge, resources, and time beyond the capacity of this thesis. 
Additionally, the testing of this hypothesis would require the validation of the 
proposed tool over the course of a building and its respective occupants’ lifetimes 
which would be best substantiated by evaluating a large quantity of buildings and 
their occupants through a longitudinal epidemiologic study. Hence, this thesis does 
not test this hypothesis in its entirety. This hypothesis 
has many existing sub-hypotheses which can be

may be unable to predict reliable health outcomes 
from a digital model and thus cannot provide valuable

Scope 
As stated, the extent of the relationship between the built environment and health is 
too vast to be explained or summarized within any single paper or tool. This makes 
defining the scope for this thesis crucial to understanding where its value lies. 
 
Figure 6. This graphic depicts the healthy building DNA framework developed by H. Liu et al. The yellow highlighted 
areas are the selected areas of focus for this thesis [22]. 
Location. This thesis evaluates 15 commonly utilized frameworks that are driving 
healthy building design, particularly in Europe and North America. It should be noted 
that for many areas of the world, the luxury of designing buildings to optimally serve 
the health of occupants is not feasible until more basic needs of the population have 
been reached. Thus, the tool this thesis argues for developing is most presently 
relevant in the parts of the world where certification schemes, guidance, and 
regulations are already steering designers towards healthy design choices. 
Building Stage. This thesis is focused on strategies and interventions for the early 
design phases of a building. Human health is relevant at all stages of building design 
from conception to construction, occupancy, and deconstruction. But, as previously 
discussed, the stage of a building where interventions are the most valuable is in the 
earliest stages of design. Hence, the goal is ultimately to provide structure and 
guidance for how to most effectively address health in the earliest stages. 
==PAGE:19==
12 
 
Health outcomes. Buildings impact health and wellbeing in a variety of ways 
[23][24]. There are some impacts to health from the indoor environment that are 
strongly corroborated by research literature while others remain less clear. For 
instance, the impact of indoor radon on the risk for lung cancer is heavily studied 
and clear. Other health and wellbeing metrics such as stress, productivity, and 
comfort are more complex to quantify. For application into a tool quantifiable metrics 
are necessary, so this thesis mainly focuses on health outcomes with concrete 
measures. 
Building typology. Another important boundary for applicability of this thesis is the 
type of building. While theoretically, the resulting tool from this thesis can be applied 
to all building types, the most applicable is multi-family residential, schools, and 
commercial offices. These are the buildings of focus for the 15 frameworks evaluated 
and are places of high occupancy, resulting in a significant impact on people.

Methodology 
The methodology for this thesis consisted of the four previously described phases, 
each building upon the last.  
Phase I: 15 Framework Analysis 
The first step of this phase was to select the frameworks that would be utilized for 
the synthesis. The top green building certification schemes in Europe and North 
America were first selected. This included DGNB, LEED, BREEAM, and WELL. An 
additional 11 frameworks were selected based upon the recommendation of indoor 
environment specialists and desktop research of the most common healthy building 
guidance in North America, Europe, and more specifically Denmark.  
The first phase reviews and analyzes the 15 selected frameworks to develop a better 
understanding of how they are influencing building design. With the end goal of this 
thesis being the beginning stages of development for an early-phase digital building 
health design tool, the frameworks were also assessed for health indicators that can 
potentially be incorporated. The most optimal indicators for this type of tool are 
indirect, leading, and objective measures of health, the meaning of which is further 
explained in this section. An underlying assumption is that collectively these 15 
frameworks converge towards a more holistic measure of building health. 
This section details the approach for evaluating these frameworks holistically and 
then for determining how the relevant indicators were selected. The method for 
evaluating these frameworks was first to categorize them as either a certification, 
regulation, or guidance, as each has a different level of power and influence on the 
design of a building. For example, regulatory guidelines must be followed in order 
for the building to be legally constructed whereas guidance simply acts as a source 
of inspiration. The scope of the standards and certification schemes were limited 
mostly to Europe and North America but have been applied beyond these regions. 
The existing frameworks range from abstract to concrete design guidance and span 
across many dimensions of sustainability. Some also focus on health in a very broad 
sense while others are linked to more specific health outcomes. 
Considering the future development of a digital tool, the goal of this phase was to 
evaluate these frameworks and narrow the focus to actionable, direct design 
considerations for health. Thus, the effort is also limited to an array of direct health 
outcomes of the building design, such as high PM2.5 concentration from poor kitchen 
ventilation, excluding indirect health outcomes on the population level, such as those 
resulting from climate change due to inefficient energy usage. While important, the 
health considerations pertaining to these population level effects are beyond the 
==PAGE:21==
14 
 
bounds of the concrete preliminary design tool focused on a building’s direct impacts 
on its occupants’ health.  
Indirect, Leading, Objective Health Indicators 
In an evaluation of green building and health, Allen et al. proposes a framework for 
categorizing the health performance indicators (HPIs) of buildings. The framework 
is recommended as a tool for designing studies that aim to attribute certain building 
parameters to enhanced or harmed occupant health and wellbeing. The proposed 
categories were direct versus indirect health measures, leading versus lagging 
indicators of health, and objective versus subjective. These categories intend to help 
explain what the performance metrics tell us about health, whether they elicit reactive 
or proactive interventions, and the strength of these metrics, respectively [4].  
 
Figure 7. In an assessment of green buildings and health, Joseph Allen et al. generated this 4-quadrant framework 
for categorizing health performance indicators in studies on building design and health outcomes [4]. 
To generate a framework with actionable design recommendations, this 4-quadrant 
model was utilized in this thesis for assessing the 15 frameworks and selecting 
specific indicators. Health performance metrics relating to design decisions fall into 
the indirect, leading category [4]. Indirect insinuates that the metric is not a direct 
measure of occupant health but is an indicator of possible health outcomes. Indirect 
measures of health performance are most applicable to the proposed tool because 
==PAGE:22==
15 
 
they correspond to indicators measurable from the design as opposed to the 
occupants. The goal of the tool, however, is to predict direct measures of health 
based on these indirect measures. A leading measure is one that is a precursor to 
health outcomes and thus interventions are possible in the design process. Lastly, 
objective (or quantitative) measures are better suited than subjective (or qualitative) 
measures for incorporation into a digital assessment tool, because they more easily 
allow for repeatability and comparison. Thus, the 15 frameworks were assessed for 
metrics fitting into this 4th quadrant.  
The selected indicators are related to health of the occupants as a result of the 
building design, as opposed to the result of non-design related parameters that 
impact the building conditions, e.g. selected cleaning products, maintenance 
schedule, user-behavior. Then they are evaluated for how abstract (subjective) or 
concrete (objective) they are. It is specified to which extent the indicators can be 
determined or evaluated in the design stage of the building. For example, the 
subjective and qualitative nature of a post-occupancy survey is not well-suited to the 
desired tool outcome because it cannot be conducted in the design stage. 
The following section characterizes each of the 15 frameworks evaluated. The 
indicators selected from each of the 15 frameworks are included as Appendix A. 
The frameworks are categorized into inspirational guidance, certification schemes, 
or regulatory standards. Most of the frameworks are applicable to a variety of 
building types but have a focus on commercial office spaces. The percentage of a 
framework that is focused on occupant health as opposed to other green building 
parameters was determined. A similar strategy to that of Rohde et al. was followed 
[25]. Each framework is divided into multiple categories. For example, the Velux 
Compass is comprised of 7 categories: flexible, quality, environment, healthy, 
community, local, and affordable. One out of these seven categories pertain directly 
to occupant health. For a framework like DGNB, the percentage of health was 
determined by the percentage of possible awarded points that are related to 
occupant health. Each of the frameworks was evaluated based on the level of 
concrete guidance it provides for designers, relative to the other frameworks. For 
example, some frameworks are comprised primarily of subjective and abstract 
indicators like ‘beauty’ while others provide concrete indicators such as a filter PM10 
removal rating. 
Framework Summaries 
Below are introductions and summaries to the 15 frameworks assessed in the first 
phase of this thesis. Most of the information is taken from the published guidance 
provided by the framework organization.  
==PAGE:23==

The goal behind “The 9 Foundations of a Healthy Building” is to provide a clear and 
actionable document that summarizes the top 9 foundations of health in the built 
environment in 2 pages each. The framework acts as guidance to stakeholders 
involved in the building design process. Each of the 9 foundations is supported by 
some of the most relevant and well understood evidence regarding the relationship 
between the design criteria and health outcomes [26]. 
Nordic Swan Ecolabelling is a sustainability certification system for new buildings, 
particularly in the Nordic region. The focus of the certification is within resource 
efficiency, reducing climate impacts, non-toxic circular economy, and conservation 
of biodiversity; however, there is a sub-focus on indoor climate [27].  
DGNB is the most commonly utilized sustainability certification framework for 
buildings in the world, with a strong focus on Europe. The three essential paradigms 
are life cycle assessment, holistic approach, and performance orientation with 
focuses on environmental, economic, and sociocultural and functional quality. The 
latest version includes ‘DGNB Hjerte’ which acknowledges buildings with a particular 
focus on human health and wellbeing [28]. 
The WELL certification framework is devoted entirely to human health and well-
being. The certification provides strategies for not only designing healthier buildings 
but also for the operations and policies of these buildings. With the latest version of 
the certification, each of the WELL features must be evidence-based, verifiable, 
implementable, and presented for outside input. The ten well concepts include air, 
water, nourishment, light, movement, thermal comfort, sound, materials, mind, and 
community [29]. 
The Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design or LEED certification is the 
most widely used green building certification in the world. LEED “provides a 
framework for healthy, highly efficient, and cost-saving green buildings. The 
certification scoring system is broken into the following categories: integrative 
process, location and transportation, sustainable sites, water efficiency, energy and 
atmosphere, materials and resources, indoor environmental quality, innovation, and 
a regional priority [30]. 
The Living Building Challenge is a philosophy framed as a building certification which 
aims to raise the standard for both building design and construction’s impact on 
people, community, and nature. The certification is routed in an iterative process of 
improvement with the goal of all aspects of buildings making the world a better place. 
The framework is made up of the following ‘petals’: place, water, energy, health + 
happiness, materials, equity, and beauty [31]. 
==PAGE:24==
17 
 
The IEQ Compass is a design tool developed to better understand IEQ’s importance 
for comfort, health, and well-being. The tool is comprised of 4 main components: 
Indoor Air Quality (IAQ), thermal, visual, and acoustic IEQ. The objective is to 
evaluate a building based on these criteria and obtain a score corresponding to an 
IEQ label, similarly to that of energy performance ratings [1].  
The TAIL framework is a rating scheme developed for rating the IEQ of buildings 
undergoing major energy renovations but can be translated to other building 
typologies and stages. The focus of the framework, like the IEQ compass, is on the 
thermal, acoustic, indoor air, and luminous environment. The levels within these 
different categories are compared to Standard EN-16798-1 and WHO guidelines to 
determine the quality [32]. 
Building Research Establishment’s Environmental Assessment Method (BREAAM) 
is a rating scheme focused on sustainability in all stages of a building. The main aims 
of the framework are to reduce the life cycle impacts of buildings on the environment, 
recognize buildings by their environmental performance, be a credible source, 
stimulate demand, and create value for sustainable buildings. The sections are 
management, health and wellbeing, energy, transport, water, materials, waste, land 
use and ecology, pollution, and innovation [33]. 
The Velux compass is a tool that provides a framework for the pre-construction 
phases of a building. The tool is comprised of strategic drivers, design drivers, and 
performance drivers that building upon each other, in that order. The overarching 
drivers are flexibility, quality, environment, healthy, community, local, and affordable 
[34]. 
The Active House guidance is a set of specifications for design an active building 
which aims to integrate health and comfort with energy efficiency and environmental 
performance. The active house radar is comprised of 9 criteria divided among the 
categories of comfort, energy, and environment – daylight, thermal environment, 
IAQ, acoustic quality, energy demand, primary energy performance, energy supply, 
sustainable construction, and freshwater consumption [35].  
Bygningsreglementet (BR18) is the Danish building code which details the 
requirements of all buildings in the country. The regulations are extensive and 
include some parameters relating to occupant health including, pollution, moisture 
and wet rooms, sound conditions, light and visibility, thermal indoor climate, and 
ventilation [36]. 
The New European Bauhaus Compass is a framework developed to provide 
guidance to building designers to create buildings that are focused on aesthetics,

sustainability, and inclusion. The compass is intended to be used in conjunction with 
self-assessment tools to evaluate a design [37]. 
According to the European Union, “Level(s) is an assessment and reporting 
framework that provides common language for sustainability performance of 
buildings. Levels has six main objectives relating to greenhouse gas emissions, 
resource efficiency and material circularity, water resource efficiency, health and 
comfortable spaces, adaptation and resilience to climate change, and optimized life 
cycle cost and value. Each objective has associated indicators that can be used to 
assess a building’s quality within that objective [38][39] [40] [41]. 
The Indeklimahjulet or Indoor Climate Wheel is a tool that is part of a larger project 
from RealDania aiming to design healthy homes based on the latest indoor climate 
research. The wheel divides 15 indicators into visible indoor climate and invisible 
indoor climate and each indicator is linked to particular health outcomes [42].  
Phase II: Synthesized Indicator Framework 
The next phase of the thesis utilizes the indicators extracted from the 15 frameworks 
to established a new framework focused on early-stage design. This newly 
developed framework consist of 40 indicators which are comprised of objective and 
measurable metrics that are impacted by specific design choices and are predictors 
of occupant health outcomes. What separates this new framework from the 15 
frameworks previously evaluated is that all indicators are objective and/or 
measurable. The goal of this is not to reduce the comprehensiveness of extensive 
health frameworks like the WELL certification and EU levels or minimize the value 
of big-picture frameworks like New European Bauhaus. Instead, the aim is to provide 
a list of concrete indicators that can be incorporated into a modelling tool which can, 
theoretically, output a direct exposure-response health implication.  
This newly proposed framework is broken into the same four categories as the IEQ 
Compass and TAIL rating schemes: IAQ, thermal indoor environment, acoustic, or 
visual. The indicators from the 15 frameworks that met the criteria of leading, 
objective, and indirect health measures were classified by their IEQ category, design 
choices, and health outcomes. The associated design choices and health outcomes 
are not necessarily comprehensive of all possibilities but include at minimum the 
most relevant possibilities from the most up-to-date healthy building research. The 
WELL framework and 9 Foundation of healthy buildings were developed from the 
most comprehensive literature reviews of relevant research on the connection 
between buildings and health. Hence, the basis for much of this framework’s 
description of the relationships between health and building design comes from 
these two sources. Additional literature review was conducted on each of the 40 
indicators to further characterize them by health outcomes and determine the extent

Phase III: Tool Concept and Development 
The third phase of this thesis consist of turning this framework into a conceptual tool. 
In a similar way to life cycle assessments (LCAs) for building designs, the idea of 
this tool is that it essentially acts as a health assessment of the building. In the early 
stages of a building’s design, LCA can be used to identify hotspots based on 
sustainability indicators like Global Warming Potential (GWP) and suggest 
adjustments to the design. This tool intends to have a similar output from health 
indicators like VOC concentrations, lighting, thermal comfort, etc. Similarly to the IEQ 
Compass, the goal of this tool is to evaluate the potential health implication of a 
design. However, this proposed tool differs in its representation of the potential 
health implication. While the IEQ Compass utilizes indirect metrics such as 
overheating or emissions, the proposed tool utilizes both indirect and direct health 
metrics. 
The objective of phase 3 is to answer the following questions:  
1. Can we predict these health indicators based on a digital building model and 
relevant data, if so, how?  
2. How might the output of these predictions be translated into a quantitative 
potential health impact? 
The conceptualized process to answer these questions consists of simulating the 
indicators from the four IEQ categories using BIM files in the IFC format as an input. 
The output of these simulations are then used to quantitively estimate potential 
health outcomes using health metrics such as DALY or harm [43]. These health 
metrics are based on a review of existing research which have established a 
conclusive dose-response relationship between different building parameters and 
associated health outcomes. 
Simulating the Indicators from IFC 
IAQ 
To answer the first question, the capability of available modelling and simulation 
software for these indicators were assessed. An evaluation was also made of the 
software’s compatibility with IFC. In terms of simulating IAQ and ventilation, there 
are several existing software such as IA-Quest, SimScale, Airscan, and CONTAM. 
Estimates of each of the 14 indicators within the IAQ category have been 
demonstrated either experimentally or routinely in practice with simulation software 
or existing databases. For example, CONTAM has been utilized to model indoor 
radon contamination to optimize ventilation within a space [44]. CONTAM is

designed to predict occupant exposure to airborne contaminants for risk 
assessments. Additionally, Airscan simulates concentrations for more than 650 
volatile organic compounds, particulate matter, NO2, and Ozone [45]. CONTAM 
could be utilized for the conceptual development of this tool, based on its significant 
use in research on the topic of IAQ simulation. A translator has been proposed and 
developed for making IFC file types compatible with the CONTAM program [46]. 
Thermal 
Thermal comfort also has many existing modelling software – EQUA’s IDA ICE, 
SimScale, CBE for ASHRAE-55, and RayMan. The seven indicators within the 
thermal environment category are the most common outputs from thermal comfort 
simulation software. The latest version of IDA ICE supports zone modelling with a 
visualized output for air temperature, air velocity, operative temperature, draft risk, 
PPD, and PMV. IDA Climate and Energy is also compatible with the IFC file format 
[3]. Many of the software model multiple IEQ categories such as thermal comfort and 
visual. Software like IDA ICE will also provide some IAQ related outputs like the CO2 
concentration. 
Acoustic 
The thermal environment, air quality, and visual environment are often assessed 
together while the auditory environment is rarely simulated during the design 
process, except for complex acoustic projects like music venues. The availability of 
acoustic simulation software is less than that of the other three IEQ categories; 
however, software like Treble and Siemens Acoustic simulation are being more 
regularly used in practice for all different building typologies. The quality of acoustic 
simulation has also improved in recent years with the advancement of technology 
and increasing data. Acoustic simulations play a vital role in aiding designers and 
acousticians in creating acoustically healthy spaces for people [47]. Treble is a 
program being used by some of the largest building design and engineering 
companies in Denmark and is IFC compatible [48]. 
Visual 
The visual environment is largely a factor of daylight. There are many simulation 
tools including the Velux Daylight Visualizer, Radiance, DaySim, Dial+, EQUA and 
more. The output of the Velux Daylight Visualizer includes a visual representation of 
luminance, illuminance factor, and daylight factor [49]. The tool already allows 
designers to iterate their design based on whether the output of the simulation is 
within the ranges suggested by regional standards. Many of these daylight tools are 
part of larger simulation software for energy which also include simulations of the 
thermal environment. That is because there are often trade-offs between optimizing 
for the different IEQ categories, which is why it is important to define the categories 
that are of the most concern early in the design process.

A New Future for Building Simulations 
There are some limitations to automating the process from IFC file to simulation 
software output. Presently, the IFC files must be directly uploaded to the different 
simulation software and the simulations often require additional input data, such as 
location-specific data like weather files. Figure 10 depicts the process flow for the 
tool with the use of existing modeling and simulation software. However, in theory, 
individual building modelling and simulation software will be replaced by a single 
software like Spacio, an early-stage modelling tool. Spacio is an in-browser BIM tool 
that combines parametric modelling of buildings with simulation capabilties to 
generate early-stage designs. The use of artificial intelligence has allowed Spacio to 
generate designs at speeds much quicker than most available software [50]. The 
desired results of a Spacio model and simulations can predict many of the 40 health 
indicators from the framework proposed in this thesis. For example, Spacio can 
automatically simulate indoor lighting parameters like daylight availability based 
upon the selected site and building geometry. The next step is to use the data from 
this type of model-simulation to predict potential health outcomes for occupants. 
From Indicators to Health Outcomes 
Answering the second question of phase three is particularly difficult in comparison 
to the first. The desired final output of this proposed tool is quantifiable health 
outcomes which can guide the designer to consider certain design choices. 
However, defining quantitative methods of estimating the health effects of buildings 
is particularly challenging [51]. Quantifying health outcomes requires extensive 
epidemiological and toxicological data that is beyond the scope of this thesis to 
acquire. Many of the suggested indicators do not have sufficient data to establish a 
dose-response relationship required for determining potential health outcomes. 
However, the overarching concept behind this tool can be demonstrated with the use 
of health outcomes that are currently predictable from the 40 indicators, e.g. radon 
exposure, heat mortality risk [51].  
Environmental health is commonly evaluated through risk and harm assessments 
[52]. These assessments are normally done for exposure to specific chemicals but 
can also be applied to other exposures such as daylight levels, noise, heat etc. The 
Odds Ratio (OR), Relative Risk (RR), and Correlation Coefficients (COR) can be 
used to quantitatively describe how a design parameter will affect these exposures 
and influence the effect on the occupant [51]. Measures such as PMV also quantify 
the impact certain building parameters have on occupants’ satisfaction and overall 
wellbeing. However, the focus is on the quality of the thermal environment and less 
on the potential health implications. One of the biggest drawbacks in quantifying 
occupant wellbeing is that quality is a subjective measure that varies from occupant 
to occupant.

Considering there are many different methods for quantifying health, the different 
IEQ categories, or even the individual indicators, may not be best expressed through 
the same quantified health metric. A potential output considered in the development

each of the indicators. DALY is used to estimate chronic harm to a cohort of people 
[53]. The next phase of this thesis formulates a method for establishing a health

VOC Box-Model  
The next part of the script utilizes these extracted IFC elements and quantities to 
conduct a simple box model to determine the concentration of VOCs in each room 
of the building [54]. VOCs are one of the most prevalent indoor air pollutants, being 
up to ten times higher than outdoor concentration, and present many health 
concerns. VOCs include an array of different chemicals that are emitted from a 
multitude of sources including but not limited to, paint, sealants, and varnishes [55]. 
Different materials have area-specific emission rates for VOCs which can be tested 
in a controlled environment. Material emission databases are becoming more 
common, with databases like Health Product Declaration Collaborative (HPDc) 
standardizing reporting methods [56]. 
A box model can be either single-zone or multi-zone and the model is based on the 
assumption of complex mixing in a confined space. The concentration is then a 
function of room volume, ventilation, source emissions, and chemical-physical 
interaction of the emissions (i.e. deposition, transformation, etc.). Box models can 
account for other variables like filter removal and multiple emission sources [54]. For 
the purposes of this tool, a steady-state box model is assumed, which means that 
variation in the source emissions are not accounted for. Additionally, the 
concentration in of VOCs is assumed to be negligible. The simplified mass balance 
for determining the concentration of VOCs in the room follows:

Discussion 
Phase I 
Of these 15 frameworks, some are more focused on health and wellbeing while 
others are more focused on the larger context of sustainability. The Nine 
Foundations of Healthy Buildings, WELL, IEQ Compass, TAIL and the RealDania 
Indeklimahjulet target specific health outcomes in their design guidance. These six, 
while focused on health, approach design suggestions from varying degrees of 
specificity. Additionally, this thesis was limited to the evaluation of the 15 selected 
frameworks. Future evaluation of other health-first frameworks like the Fitwel 
certification and Assessment standard for health building (ASHB) may add to the 
extent of healthy buildings defined by the frameworks evaluated as a part of this 
thesis. Fitwell and ASHB are similar to WELL in their focus specifically on health in 
buildings. However, these two frameworks were not selected for evaluation in this 
thesis due to the latest Fitwel guidance, version 3, only being available through an 
online project portal and ASHB being outside the scope of North America and 
Europe. 
These health-specific frameworks are more recently established than the more 
general sustainability frameworks, but the general frameworks have also recently 
developed health-focused sections. For example, in the latest version of DGNB 
(version 2023), a new additional sub-certification focused on health was introduced 
– DGNB Heart. This indicates that the general trend within the green building space 
is towards an increased attention on importance of building’s impact on our health, 
which results in a new market for healthy design assistance. 
Most of the frameworks are used for evaluation at different stages of the building – 
from design to occupancy. WELL is a performance-based certification and requires 
continuous post-occupancy review. When considering this and what each of these 
frameworks are lacking in their design guidance, it was clear that most of these 
frameworks provide goals and requirements without many strategies for achieving 
the set targets. Hence, the leading healthy building frameworks provide the designer 
with limited guidance on how to attain these targets.  
The highest priority consideration in the stages before building construction is 
determining the health target performance indicators [22]. To develop better 
guidance for designers, the framework analysis phase of this thesis also involved 
selecting the health indicators potentially valuable in early-stage design. Repeatedly, 
the frameworks categorized health in buildings by IAQ, thermal comfort, lighting, and 
acoustics. Within each of these categories, are select indicators that meet the criteria 
==PAGE:44==
37 
 
of objective, leading, indirect measures of health. The results of this selection are 
included in Appendix A. 
IAQ 
Indoor Air Quality is by far the most studied out of the three IEQ categories [22]. 
Hence, the IAQ category has the most well-established relationship between design 
strategies and health outcomes. The IAQ of a building is affected by the outdoor air, 
ventilation system, material selection, household activities, and occupant behavior 
[1]. The quality is defined largely by the concentration of different chemical and 
biological pollutants within the indoor environment. Some of these pollutants have 
stronger established dose-response relationships than others. Radon for example 
has been studied for its connection to a significantly increased risk of lung cancer, 
especially for tobacco smokers. Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) are another 
pollutant of concern that has garnered attention in the research community. They 
are emitted from many sources within a building but are commonly emitted by certain 
types flooring and paints. However, dose-response relationships are still unclear for 
many VOCs. Many indoor pollutant sources are also the result of factors out of 
control of the designer, e.g. cleaning supplies, personal care products, etc. Thus, 
both prevention (e.g. optimizing material selection) and mitigation (increased 
ventilation) strategies for IAQ are crucial in the design phase. 
Thermal Health 
Another heavily studied area of IEQ is the thermal environment, largely due to its 
significant impact on energy use of a building, but increasingly due to the 
understanding of its impacts on both human health and comfort [74][75]. The overall 
thermal environment can impact human wellbeing in many ways (e.g. discomfort, 
productivity, sleep) but it is less clearly defined how much each individual thermal 
health indicator may contribute to these health outcomes. Traditionally, each 
indicator is evaluated together to holistically assess thermal comfort, indicated by 
the Predicted Mean Value (PMV). This method is limited in its capacity to explain 
wellbeing metrics beyond comfort, such as links to obesity and diabetes. 
Additionally, there is a synergistic nature between the thermal environment and other 
IEQ parameters like emission rates from material and mold growth which are not 
considered by comfort metrics.   
Acoustics 
The acoustic environment has been considered by some research on residential 
buildings as more important to IEQ than air quality. The value of high-quality acoustic 
design may be recognized by some but nonetheless it is understudied and 
underrepresented in healthy building frameworks. The intricacies of the relationship 
==PAGE:45==
38 
 
between our health to noise are not entirely understood, there is strong evidence for 
the link between noise and lower productivity, mental fatigue, stress, and increased 
sick leave. There are many components that make up the acoustic environment 
including external noise sources (e.g. traffic, aircraft, neighbors, etc.), internal noise 
sources (e.g. mechanical equipment, occupants, etc.), and the building parameters 
that influence the perception of these sounds [76]. These components are largely 
determined in the site selection and design phases of the building, making early-
stage decisions even more critical. 
Visual Environment 
The visual environment can be categorized by lighting (natural and artificial) and 
views (in and out). It is another IEQ category under-researched in comparison with 
thermal comfort and IAQ. Light is essential to our visual and circadian systems [29]. 
The circadian system in turn impacts many other aspects of human health from our 
metabolism to the psyche. However, lighting design is traditionally focused on more 
practical aspects like workspace visibility and does not consider design for well-being 
aspects like circadian rhythm [29]. Lighting design needs also vary depending on the 
building typology, for instance studies have shown a link between hospital patient 
recovery time and lighting [77]. Aside from lighting, the views both in and out of a 
building also impact the occupants’ health and well-being. Views-out provide 
humans with a semblance of our innate connection with nature, which is pivotal to 
health and wellbeing [26]. Views-in impact feelings of privacy and safety. Together 
lighting and view design have the ability to significantly impact the health and 
wellbeing of building occupants.  
Future Considerations 
As noted by Liu H et al., extensive research has been undertaken regarding the 
design stage of buildings, particularly with IAQ, neglecting the construction and 
operation stages as well as other potentially health impacting building attributes (e.g. 
aesthics, tidiness, social aspects, etc.) [22]. Hence, the objective of Phase I and II of 
this thesis was not to reiterate the copious amounts of research already conducted 
but to synthesize the most studied and relevant information through a selection of 
indicators. Of the 15 frameworks, DGNB, WELL, and EU levels contain the most 
indicators meeting the criteria of the 4th quadrant in Figure 7. Other frameworks such 
as New European Bauhaus provide no indicators for evaluation. In the third phase 
of this thesis, the selected indicators from the frameworks were cross-checked for 
repetition and a new, synthesized framework was defined. 
==PAGE:46==
39 
 
Phase II 
Together the 40 indicators make up a new healthy building framework for early-stage 
design assessment and iteration across IAQ, thermal, acoustic, and visual 
environments. Respectively there are 14 indicators for IAQ, 7 for thermal 
environment, 7 from acoustic, and 13 from visual, some indicators are repeated 
between IAQ and thermal environment. This synthesized framework is not the first 
time that these four categories have been evaluated together, but it is novel in its 
aim at focusing specifically on the interventions possible in earliest design stages for 
each category. 
Table 3 summarizes the links between these 40 indicators, design choices, and 
health outcomes based on the 15 frameworks assessed as well as additional 
research. It is clear from the literature review that the links between IAQ and health 
have been heavily studied while the dose-response relationships between the 
thermal, acoustic, and visual environment and occupants are less clear. 
Most of the 15 frameworks refer to standards for dose exposure values pertaining to 
air quality, light, noise, and temperature [75]. Amatkasmin et al. [78] emphasizes that 
safe dose exposure should not be the only metric and that occupant-focused metrics 
should be more utilized. This thesis explored translating dose exposure metrics into 
quantified health outcomes through dose-response relationships. With the 
understanding that health cannot be fully encompassed individually by either 
subjective occupant metrics or by quantitative dose-response relationship, the aim 
of this thesis was to establish a new method for assessing health in the design phase 
from a human focus (direct) as opposed to the standard indirect indicators. The 
established framework focuses on the physical aspects of healthy buildings and less 
on the psychological elements of health. 
Limitations 
The biggest limitation to further developing this framework is that some of the design 
decisions, like site selection, and corresponding indicators do not have such clear 
links to health outcomes. For example, in the case that the site is located on a 
contaminated site then there may be clear health links depending on the 
contaminant, however there is significant variability in this indicator. There are also 
many more indirect health outcomes that stem from site selection. Additionally, this 
framework does not reflect the potential of tradeoffs or relationships between the 
different IEQ categories. For instance, an increase in daylight in the building may 
result in better visual environment at the expense of the thermal environment.

Future Considerations 
As research interest in these connections increases, developing the conceptual 
basis for the development of a tool that could be used to assess health in the future 
is valuable to expediting the process of incorporating healthy design into buildings. 
The convenience of a tool that is up to date and evolving with research is that much 
of this interpretability is removed because the tool is less dependent on the 
designer/users’ level of knowledge or capacity for attention to detail. Many of the 
current frameworks used to steer design are open ended and provide unclear 
direction to the designer, which leaves room for interpretation. On top of this, the 
more detailed and extensive frameworks are very comprehensive, but they require 
a significant amount of reading and evaluation on behalf of the designer or whoever 
else is responsible for implementing the framework. A tool can help to maintain the 
comprehensive nature of the frameworks without requiring assessment and self-
education on part of the designer. 
An early-stage design tool is inherently indirect because there is not yet a building 
to measure any lagging or direct metrics from. As stated in Allen et. al., the goal of 
categorizing the indicators examined in the 15 sustainability frameworks is not to be 
definitive but to be used for evaluation. This sentiment was used as the guide for 
determining which metrics in these 15 frameworks may be used to develop a 
technology that can guide design choices through objective, leading, and indirect 
metrics.  
From Framework to Tool 
The aim of the synthesized framework was not to recreate a weighting system for 
‘scoring’ the quality of the 4 IEQ categories that is described by certification schemes 
like TAIL, IEQ Compass, WELL, etc. but to generate an overview of the indicators 
that are important and how they are linked to design, building parameters, and health 
outcomes. Based on the literature, these connections are more easily made for some 
indicators than others. The third phase of this thesis focused on how these 
connections may possibly be demonstrated with the use of digital design services 
like BIM and BIM-based performance simulations [22]. Many of these indicators have 
existing tools for modelling or simulating in the design phase of a building. In bridging 
==PAGE:48==
41 
 
the gaps between design and health outcomes, this phase clarified the relevant 
indicators and associated design choices, answering the question of ‘what are the 
valuable health indicators for early-stage design?’ Phase 3 investigates how the 
values of these indicators can be determined with digital modeling and simulations.  
Phase III 
A tool concept like the one proposed adds value to the building industry by utilizing 
the existing technologies, enhancing IDP collaboration, and implementing early-
stage design considerations. Quantifying sustainability holistically is a nearly 
impossible task, similarly is quantifying health. However, the value of a sustainability 
indicator like GWP is instrumental in terms of steering the direction of design. The 
idea is similar to the utilization of a metric such as DALY. While a single number 
does not encompass health, it provides a method for guiding decision-making. 
Governments often use DALY to direct investments into treating or accommodating 
specific diseases and disabilities by comparing the DALYs gained per dollar spent. 
The same method can be applied to decision making in building design. The more 
DALYs gained per cost of design changes, the more likely the change is to be made. 
DALY has been used to establish the cost of different exposures through not only 
mortality but also the burden of disease. However, the Institute for Health Metrics 
and Evaluation has the largest database on the Global Burden of Disease (GBD) for 
determining the DALYs of different causes. This includes health outcomes 
considered more within the realm of wellbeing, such as depression, anxiety, 
violence, etc. Counter to a metric like PMV, a drawback of DALYs as a health metric 
is a limit on its ability to encompass wellbeing. As defined in the background of this 
thesis, health is beyond just the absence of disease or harm. Ideally, to encapsulate 
health, parameters like comfort and wellbeing should also be considered. 
Benefit of Digital Tools 
The relationship between building design and IEQ is better understood than the 
relationship between building design and health or IEQ and health [4]. The 15 
frameworks are the foundation for the tool because they are the current drivers of 
healthy building design. Many of the indicators included in these frameworks are 
already routinely simulated in different software programs throughout the design 
process and then used to manually assess a particular aspect of health or wellbeing, 
such as thermal comfort, circadian rhythm design, or harmful air pollutants. Digital 
design solutions have been revolutionary for automating many of these tasks. 
The quality of design, resource use, and labor efficiency required is improved by the 
use of digital tools. For example, energy simulation tools such as IDA ICE allow for 
predictions of heat and electricity use early in the design process based on the input 
==PAGE:49==
42 
 
of an IFC model and a few other parameters. In the case that a building design would 
use large amounts of energy to cool a certain section due to solar heat gains, the 
simulation will indicate this and the designer can implement a solution such as solar 
shading. BIM technology, modelling/simulation software, and the concept of healthy 
building design are independently not novel concepts. The novelty lies in the ability 
to assess the health of a building in the early design stages by generating a design 
in a BIM software and utilizing a simple tool.  
For many health outcomes, predictions like this are not yet possible. Currently, 
health assessments are difficult to make until later stages of the design or post 
construction and require a team of specialists to conduct individual evaluations. This 
is why most of the certification schemes require ongoing post-occupancy testing by 
a trained professional. In terms of design stage assessments for health and 
wellbeing, thermal comfort and daylight simulation software are some of the few 
available for making reasonable predictions about the health of buildings. However, 
the outputs from these simulations then require additional knowledge to detail their 
links to health outcomes. Reducing the impact of this knowledge gap for designers 
is the key value in the conceptual tool proposed in this thesis. Further research is 
needed to expand on the specific input variables, datasets, and procedures required 
for determining these health outcomes from indirect indicators. 
This tool concept enables iterations to the BIM, based on the output from a partially 
automated health assessment as opposed to a team of many experts. Most 
innovation begins with a limited group of experts who have the knowledge to execute 
a skill, product, or idea. As time goes on, the experts in a field grow and sometimes 
the abilities of an expert can be compiled into a tool. For example, at a point in 
history, mathematicians were required for complex algebra problems, but as 
calculator technology has improved, we have distilled the skills of entire 
mathematicians into a single tool. 
Tool Feasibility 
There are currently several main limitations to developing a comprehensive 
assessment tool such as this one. The first being that accurate simulations which 
output each of these indicators do not exist entirely. Second being that many of these 
health indicators do not have an established exposure-response relationship with 
health outcomes, despite this, it is clear there is some sort of relationship. For the 
development of an effective quantitative tool, this dose-response relationship is 
beneficial. Additionally, there are many indicators that do not have a clear method 
for quantification. These are two factors that are essential for incorporation into a 
BIM to health outcome tool. One of the limitations of using a dose-exposure-based 
==PAGE:50==
43 
 
evaluation is that the psychological and physical health impacts are not holistically 
considered [22]. 
To continue developing this tool in the future, the relationship between indicators 
and health outcomes must be established. The link between these indicators and 
design decisions must also be clear. Thus, a relationship between design decisions 
and health outcomes can be determined and later quantified. The next and final 
phase of this thesis aims to clarify the relationship between the indicators and health 
outcomes by demonstrating how the VOC concentration indicator can be simulated 
and converted to DALYs.  The health outcomes related to VOC concentrations are 
not entirely clear, but our understanding is growing [79]. The next section details the 
functionality of a Python tool developed to predict the potential health outcomes, in 
DALYs, of the VOC concentration simulated from a digital building model 
Phase IV 
To have some perspective, the proof-of-concept in phase four only calculates the 
DALYs from one building component, for one pollutant of concern, which is just one 
of 14 indicators within the air quality category, which is one of four main components 
of IEQ. This demonstrated tool only covers a small fraction of what this thesis has 
deemed as the framework for healthy building design. Nonetheless, it demonstrates 
the benefit of a tool that automates a health assessment that is a time- and labor-
intensive process, if performed at all. On the surface, the tool seems as simple as 
inputting a building design and then obtaining the output of a simplified potential 
health impact, DALY. However, the intermediary steps include complex simulations 
and modeling of different indoor environmental measures that a combination of 
different design choices have a synergistic relationship on. For example, the 
concentration of a VOC in a room is dependent on a multitude of factors including 
but not limited to ventilation rate, material choices, temperature, etc. The 
simplification of these complex processes into this tool should be heavily considered 
when using the output for design changes. Future research warrants extensive 
sensitivity analysis to assess how the DALY output is affected by other parameters.     
Another concern with the use of DALY is that it frames everything in the lens of harm 
[80]. An interesting consideration for the future is how some building parameters may 
increase healthy life years as opposed to causing harm. This could be represented 
by the metric Quality Adjusted Life Year (QALY) which is ‘a measure of the state of 
health of a person or group in which the benefits, in terms of length of life, are 
adjusted to reflect the quality of life’ [81]. For example, some studies are showing 
that concrete can actually absorb indoor air pollutants [82]. In the case of assessing 
harm from design choices, this could mean that the use of pollutant absorbing 
concrete may actually provide a health benefit as opposed to a health harm.

Conclusions 
Sustainability and health are luxuries in the building market right now. This is 
because many certifications require large budgets. Making affordable and useable 
tools available will enable more designers to implement health and sustainability 
strategies and collectively push the industry towards more sustainable and healthy 
design practices. Designing healthy buildings is an interdisciplinary challenge 
between epidemiology, engineering, architecture, and legislation.  
In many ways this tool is doing exactly what a certification assessor will manually do 
post occupancy, but digitally automated in the design phase. The ability of a digital 
BIM tool to speed up this assessment allows for faster iterations that are more likely 
to be implemented in the end. It is important to make clear that these health metrics 
are not all-encompassing or perfectly indicative of a healthy building. Likewise, that 
is not the intent behind developing a tool with simplified metrics. The tool is designed 
to incentivize designers and other decision makers to consider some key factors in 
designing a healthy building. The best incentive is often to make something simple 
and easy to use. However, for this tool to be effective, it must be accurate in that the 
design decisions result in healthier buildings. The simple utilization of a select few 
quantifiable metrics does not inherently make the building healthy.  
The problem of health in the built environment is still much more complex than any 
singular tool could solve. One could argue though that the complexity and abstract 
nature of the relation between building design and health is precisely the reason 
simple tools are necessary. BIM and simulation tools are the future of building 
design. The closer digital models and simulations of buildings get to real life, the less 
guess work and manual calculations that have to be used and our design abilities 
increase.  
A significant amount of research needs to be conducted to make the extensive 
nature of this framework useful and hence this tool possible. Much like that of BIM, 
as technology improves this tool can evolve with it. But each aspect considered in 
this thesis is part of a quickly evolving architecture industry. BIM has rapidly shifted 
the way architects and engineers work together through the design process. 
Research on health related to buildings is continuing to be conducted and establish 
clear relationships. Thus, the incorporation of these powerful tools and knowledge 
together can progress the healthy building industry forward. 
Additionally, the use of BIM and a quantitative health assessment from models 
should not be treated as entirely trustworthy indicators of a building’s health. The 
value of the tool is not in the accuracy or precision of its predictive nature but in its 
ability to provide information to a design team that would otherwise require extensive 
==PAGE:52==
45 
 
labor hours and knowledge to evaluate the same indicators. It also make it more 
possible to do an evaluation of whether or not there is a burden shift between 
sustainable and healthy buildings. If a health assessment is conducted alongside 
LCAs or other forms of sustainability assessments during the early design stages, 
optimization strategies balancing health and sustainability can be implemented.  
It is also valuable to remember that the role of a designer is equally qualitative as it 
is quantitative [5]. The human component to building design is invaluable and one 
not replicable by any digital tool, yet. As architecture continues to become more 
complex, utilizing tools to assess the quantitative aspects of a building’s design 
allows designers to focus more on the qualitative traits of architecture that provide 
immeasurable benefits.
