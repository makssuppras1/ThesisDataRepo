Abstract 
Heart failure (HF) is a significant global health concern, primarily caused by ischemic 
heart disease (IHD). Current treatments focus on symptom management because of in-
adequate regenerative capacity in adult human cardiac tissue. Human pluripotent stem 
cell-derived cardiomyocytes (hPSC-CMs) offer a promising regenerative therapy for 
HF, but their low viability post-transplantation remains one of many challenges needed 
to be overcome before stem cell-based therapy can become widely available. 
The aim of this thesis was to develop an in vitro ischemia model using hPSC-CM 3D 
clusters to investigate the effects of environmental stressors such as hypoxia and nutri-
ent deprivation on cell metabolism, viability, and functionality.  
The model was established by culturing hPSC-CM clusters under varying conditions of 
metabolic and hypoxic stress. Metabolic stress was induced using three different media 
compositions, while hypoxic stress was simulated with low oxygen levels (1% O₂). The 
effects of these conditions were applied to hPSC-CM of two maturation stages (CM15 
and CM22). Glucose/lactate measurements, viability assays, and calcium imaging were 
assessed. Results indicated that hPSC-CMs maintained metabolic activity even under 
glucose deprivation, suggesting activation of alternative metabolic pathways to obtain 
energy. Hypoxia generally had a protective effect on cell viability, except in glucose-
deprived conditions with insulin supplementation, where a significant decline in viabil-
ity was observed in the early CM15 stage.  
The studies highlight the complex interplay between maturity, nutrient availability, hy-
poxia, and growth factor signaling in hPSC-CM survival and function. These findings 
provide insights into optimizing the culturing conditions for hPSC-CMs in ischemic 
conditions, enabling a deeper understanding of the low viability of hPCS-CM post 
transplantation and potentially propose procedures which in the future can enhance ther-
apeutic efficacy for cardiac regeneration.

4 Introduction 
Heart failure (HF) is a prevalent cardiovascular condition characterized by the heart's reduced abil-
ity to efficiently pump blood to the body's organs. As a significant global health concern, HF leads 
to deteriorating physiological capabilities among affected individuals and imposes a substantial eco-
nomic burden on healthcare systems. A leading contributor to HF is ischemic heart disease (IHD), 
which results from the narrowing or blockage of coronary arteries, reducing blood flow and oxygen 
supply to the myocardium. IHD remains the primary cause of death from non-infectious diseases, 
responsible for nearly 10 million fatalities in 2021 (Figure 4-1) [1]. 
 
 
Figure 4-1: Global Prevalence in Ischemic Heart Disease 
Illustration of an artery with plaque blocking blood flow to myocardium (a) leading to heart with ischemic heart disease 
(b). Global prevalence of IHD by country [2].  
 
HF currently has no cure, largely due to the human heart's limited regenerative capacity to repair 
myocardial damage. Existing medical therapies focus on alleviating symptoms through pharmaco-
logical interventions that reduce fluid retention and enhance cardiac contractility, often comple-
mented by device-based solutions, such as implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICDs) and car-
diac resynchronization therapy (CRT). Despite these treatment options, the prevalence of HF con-
tinues to rise, underscoring the need for innovative therapeutic approaches that address the underly-
ing cause of the condition and offer the potential to restore normal cardiac function [3], [4].  
==PAGE:12==
 
 
12 
 
In this context, therapies utilizing human pluripotent stem cell-derived cardiomyocytes (hPSC-
CMs) have emerged as promising candidates due to their regenerative potential. However, even 
with ongoing clinical trials, this approach faces several challenges, including issues related to cell 
maturation, functionality and integration into host myocardial tissue. A large number of hPSC-CMs 
transplanted to IHD myocardium die within the first 24h. Getting a better understanding of the rea-
sons for this low viability and potentially to be able to apply mitigation strategies could enhance the 
therapeutic potential of hPSC-CMs. Culturing these cells in environments simulating nutrient depri-
vation and hypoxia similar to the stressed conditions present in ischemic myocardium could lead to 
increased understanding and has also emerged as a promising conditioning strategy that could in-
duce metabolic adaptation and improve the functional capacities of the cardiomyocytes [5]. 
 
Furthermore, identifying the role of specific stressors, vital nutrients or supplements may facilitate 
the development of strategies for the co-delivery of supportive elements alongside hPSC-CMs. This 
approach could significantly enhance the survival and effectiveness of transplanted cardiomyocytes 
by providing essential support for their growth, metabolism, and overall viability in the challenging 
environment of IHD. 
 
The aim of this master thesis is to develop an in vitro ischemia 3D cluster model utilizing hPSC-
CM with following objectives: 
• To establish in vitro culturing conditions suitable for culturing hPSC-CM 3D clusters. 
• Investigate the effects of environmental stressors, including hypoxia and nutrient depri-
vation, on cell metabolism, viability and functionality of these cells. 
• Employ advanced cell imaging techniques to monitor the behavior, survival, and real-
time function of the hPSC-CM 3D clusters. 
• Evaluate impact of environmental conditions on cellular morphology and function in 
hPSC-CM 3D clusters of different maturity.

perimental part of this thesis. Metabolic stress was induced using three different experimental media 
increasingly deprived for nutrients and supplements compared to a control media, while hypoxic 
stress was induced by low (1% O2) and compared to standard normoxic (21% O2) oxygen condi-
tions. The impact of these conditions were assessed using various read-outs, including metabolism

5 Background 
5.1 Heart failure 
HF is a serious condition characterized by the heart's inability to pump sufficient blood to meet the 
body's demands for oxygen and nutrients. This condition affects more than 64 million people world-
wide leads to significant morbidity and mortality, with 45–60% of affected individuals dying within 
five years of diagnosis [6] [7]. It represents a critical public health challenge, with profound impli-
cations for individual patients and a substantial economic burden on healthcare systems[1] . Be-
cause of an aging population and increasing prevalence of risk factors such obesity, diabetes and 
hypertension, including in newly industrialized and developing countries, it is projected that this 
number will continue to rise [3], [4], [8]. 
 
5.1.1 Definition and Classification of Heart Failure  
 
HF can manifest acutely, often due to injury or infection, or it can develop chronically, worsening 
over time [9] [10]. Symptoms include shortness of breath, fatigue, and chest pain, but some individ-
uals may remain asymptomatic until their oxygen demands increase, such as during physical activ-
ity [11], [12].  In 2021 a new comprehensive definition of HF was introduced. The classification in-
cludes four HF stages emphasizing the symptomatic nature of HF as a clinical syndrome:   
At risk for HF (Stage A), Pre‐HF (Stage B), Symptomatic HF (Stage C) and Advanced HF (Stage 
D). Additionally, a classification based on left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) was intro-
duced.[3]. 
 
5.1.2 Ischemic Heart Disease 
IHD also known as coronary artery disease (CAD) or coronary heart disease (CHD), is a leading 
cause of HF (Figure 4-1). IHD occurs when the coronary arteries become narrowed or blocked, re-
ducing blood flow and oxygen supply to the myocardium. The lack of oxygen due to ischemia af-
fects cardiomyocytes (CMs), the heart's contractile cells. Prolonged oxygen deprivation can lead to 
necrosis and myocardial infarction (MI) also known as “heart attack”. [13] IHD is the primary cause 
of death among noninfectious diseases, accounting for almost 10 million deaths in 2021 [1]. 
 
5.1.3 Scar Tissue Formation  
While many organs of the body, e.g. skin and bone, can regenerate after being damaged, the same 
isn’t true of the adult heart. In contrast to human embryonic and fetal development where heart cells 
==PAGE:15==
   
 
15 
 
undergo cell division to form the heart muscle, most mammalian CMs withdraw permanently from 
the cell cycle within a short period of time after birth and enter a terminal state in which they can no 
longer divide. Instead of regeneration following IHD, the heart initiates a reparative process that re-
sults in scar tissue formation over several weeks [5], [14]. This process unfolds in three phases: The 
inflammatory phase, the fibrotic phase, and the remodeling phase [15]. Over time scar tissue ma-
tures, resulting in a stable but non-functional fibrotic area [15]. The dense extracellular matrix 
(ECM) of scar tissue can further obstruct blood flow, leading to additional limitation in oxygen and 
nutrients supply for surviving CMs. This hostile environment contributes to impaired electrical con-
ductivity and HF [5]. 
 
5.1.4 Current treatments: 
HF management includes pharmacological treatments, device implants, and cardiac transplantation. 
Pharmacological therapies, including angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors, beta-block-
ers, angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs), and diuretics, are fundamental in managing HF. These 
medications primarily aim to alleviate symptoms, reduce fluid retention, and enhance cardiac func-
tion [4]. In addition to medications, implantable devices such as ICDs and CRTs play an important 
role in reducing hospitalizations and mortality in specific patient populations. Cardiac transplanta-
tion remains the definitive treatment for patients with end-stage HF, however, the limited availabil-
ity of donor organs significantly restricts its widespread use [4]. 
Despite the current treatment options HF continues to present substantial challenges. Current phar-
macological treatments are not curative, they treat symptoms only and the disease often progresses 
despite intervention. The rising prevalence of HF and the limitations of existing treatments highlight 
the necessity for developing novel therapeutic strategies [4]. One promising approach is stem cell-
based therapy, which aims to restore damaged myocardial tissue and improve heart function by uti-
lizing stem cells [16], [17]. 
 
5.2 Stem Cell Based Cardiac Therapy   
Stem cells are unique, undifferentiated cells capable of developing into various specialized cell 
types with regenerative potential [18]. This makes them promising candidates for therapies aimed at 
repairing damaged tissues, which is very relevant in conditions like IHD [19]. Stem cells can be ob-
tained from different sources with very distinct differentiation potential. While adult stem cells, like 
mesenchymal stromal cells and cardiac progenitor cells have very limited potential to form CMs de 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
16 
 
novo, pluripotent stem cells (PSC) have the capacity to form essentially unlimited number of any 
somatic cell, including CMs [18], [20].  
 
5.2.1 Pluripotent Stem Cells (PSCs)  
PSCs can differentiate into all cell types in the body, including CMs [21]. PSCs can be derived from 
either embryonic stem cells (ESCs) or induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs). 
ESCs are derived from blastocyst inner cell masses and are pluripotent [22], making them suitable 
for cardiac regeneration. However ethical concerns regarding their source persists. iPSCs are repro-
grammed from adult somatic cells, e.g. fibroblasts or blood cells, a process first achieved in mice 
and later in humans (hiPSCs) in 2007 [23], [24]. 
The development of hPSCs represents a significant achievement in stem cell research for regenera-
tive therapy. Their self-renewal capabilities provide a scalable, patient-specific cell source, reducing 
immune rejection and hPSC derived CMs (hPSC-CM) have become the most preferred cell source 
for cardiac stem cell research [20], [25], [26]. 
 
5.2.2 Challenges with Cardia Stem Cell Based Regeneration Therapy   
Although the concept of regenerative therapies based on stem cell has been a very active field of re-
search for two decades and holds great potential for HF patients there is still considerable improve-
ments and optimization needed before stem cell-based therapies for HF and IHD will become 
widely available [20], [27]. The regenerative process is highly complex comprising many barriers 
and challenges to stem cell-based therapy for cardiac disease and the number of clinical trials is still 
low [20], [28]. In the earliest clinical trials, the approach was inefficient, with limited evidence that 
transplanted cells could effectively reconstitute the myocardium in HF patients [20], [29]. In recent 
years more promising approaches aiming at mitigating these barriers are currently being tested in 
clinical trials [30]. It will be interesting to follow the outcomes of these trials in the future. In Figure 
5-1 are illustrated some of the procedures relevant to address when generating hPSC-CM intended 
for transplantation in vivo to animal models or patients.

5.2.2.1 Cultivation and Maturation  
Producing a sufficient number of high-quality CMs for transplantation is essential. Ensuring the pu-
rity of hPSC-derived CMs is crucial to prevent potential teratoma formation if these highly prolifer-
ative stem cells are transplanted prematurely [32]. Effective differentiation and cultivation protocols 
including optimal conditioning, combined with functional assays and RNAseq to assess heterogene-
ity, maturation, purity are vital for generating robust CMs with a higher chance of survival post-de-
livery [11], [20]. 
 
5.2.2.2  Delivery and Retention 
The far majority of transplanted hPSC-CMs are typically lost post-transplantation [20], [33], [34]. 
Delivery methods such as intravenous infusion have a large waste and struggle with endothelial bar-
riers. Direct injection in heart has higher retention but is susceptible to retrograde leakage [20]. To 
address these challenges, approaches such as spheroid formation have been implemented to im-
prove retention. Additionally, engineering solutions, such as tissue-cell-seeded patches and hydro-
gel injections, can help retain cells in place [20], [26]. 
  
5.2.2.3 Survival and proliferation 
The ischemic myocardium presents a harsh microenvironment characterized by hypoxia, inflamma-
tion, and fibrosis, which compromise cell survival and lead to high post-transplantation cell death 
rates (17.8% within 24 hours) [13]. Delivering the CM with a support package of vital nutrients and 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
18 
 
growth factors, potentially in a hydrogel delivery option could potentially increase survival rate of 
transplanted cells [20]. Additionally, immune rejection is also a risk which may necessitate pro-
longed immunosuppression [35]. Using autologous cells can be a solution to reduce immunological 
issues but is expensive and has the disadvantage of not being readily available if needed acutely 
[20].  
  
5.2.2.4 Electromechanical integration 
Arrhythmia risk from immature CMs post-transplantation remains a critical concern, as immature 
CMs due to suboptimal calcium handling are more prone to causing arrhythmias. These cells typi-
cally exhibit higher spontaneous beat frequencies compared to matured and adult [36].  To mitigate 
this risk, various CM maturation strategies have been developed, including electromechanical stim-
ulation. This approach aims to enhance the structural and functional properties of CMs, promoting 
their maturation and reducing the likelihood of arrhythmias [37], [38].  
  
5.2.3 In vitro hPSC-CM models 
While stem cell-derived CMs offer significant promise for HF therapy, further research is crucial to 
improve efficacy. In vitro models are essential and can be applied for different aspects of research 
and development of future improved therapies (Figure 5-2). Mechanistic studies using hPSC-CM 
can aid exploration of molecular pathways and enhance understanding of acute to chronic IHD tran-
sitions. Patient-specific iPSC technology can be used to study disease modelling and facilitate the 
investigation of genetic forms of IHD and new therapeutic targets. In drug development in vitro CM 
models can be valuable for screening and mechanistic understanding of cardioprotective agents. In 
regenerative medicine, in vitro systems are used for production of hPSC cells intended for trans-
plantation into in vivo models or patients. While isolation and differentiation protocols are rela-
tively well established, media formulations and understanding of environmental conditions that sup-
port subsequent cultivation and maturation of CMs that prepare them best for surviving transplanta-
tion remain a research area with much focus. Mimicking the hypoxic environment is important and 
recent studies highlight the importance of metabolic conditioning and growth factor withdrawal in 
promoting adult-like phenotypes of CM [39], [40].

5.2.4 In vitro h-PSC-CM Ischemia Models 
Developing in vitro models that accurately replicate the challenging conditions of IHD myocardium 
is essential for identifying the key factors that enable hPSC-CM to survive and thrive. This under-
standing will enhance the likelihood of optimizing the survival rates of transplanted hPSC-CM. 
 
5.2.5 Cell Source and Structural Formation 
Depending on the purpose, needs and costs of the ischemic in-vitro models, it’s important to con-
sider the suitable cell source, such as a cell-line, ESC, PSC [42]. 
The formations of the cells can either be in 2D monolayer or 3D culturing. 2D allow for easier at-
tachment and histological assessments. However, 3D cultures more closely mimic the in vivo envi-
ronment, providing more accurate representation of cellular interactions and ECM, enhancing phys-
iological relevance and complexity of the model [43], [44].  
 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
20 
 
5.2.6 Media composition including Nutrient Deprivation:  
Source and type of base media, growth factors and small molecules, metabolic supplements and 
supportive cells are all relevant factors to consider. Reducing key nutrients and other blood supply 
supporters mimics ischemic conditions, allowing for studying possible metabolic shifts and cellular 
responses, including necrosis and apoptosis. 
 
5.2.7 Hypoxia  
Oxygen levels are relevant to consider when simulating ischemic environment, where oxygen levels 
such as 1-2% O2 oxygen levels are needed to mimic ischemic conditions [45], [46]. 
 
5.2.8 Mechanical and Electrical stimulation 
Bioreactor systems allowing for controlled application of mechanical and electrical stimuli can en-
hance maturation leading to improved contractile properties and structural organization [38], [41].  
 
5.2.9 Assessment Techniques:  
Techniques such as live-cell imaging, fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS), and various 
readouts e.g., calcium handling, contractility, hypoxia and cell death markers and RNAseq can be 
used to evaluate the cellular responses to ischemic conditions. 
  
5.3 In-Vitro Model Systems Related to Thesis 
The experimental section of this thesis specifically investigates the metabolism and viability of 3D 
clusters of hPSC-CM cultivated under conditions of nutrient deprivation and hypoxic stress. The 
aim is to elucidate the metabolic responses and viability of hPSC at two different CM stages. 
 
5.3.1 3D Culture Systems  
3D culture systems represent a significant advancement in the cultivation of PSC-derived CMs. The 
hPSC-CM clusters are typically generated using aggregation media combined with orbital shaking 
regimens. The 3D cell clusters more accurately replicate the architecture and microenvironment of 
the heart [47], [48]. The systems allow for better cell-cell interactions, and mechanical loading, 
thereby mimicking the structural and functional properties of heart tissue[49], [50]. Studies have 
shown that 3D culture enhances sarcomere length and improves gene expression related to cardiac 
contractility and calcium handling, which are critical for proper heart function [36], [48]. In the con-
text of ischemic HF, 3D cultures systems allow for improved simulation of hypoxic conditions that 
mimic the low-oxygen environments encountered in vivo during myocardial infarction. By studying 
==PAGE:21==
   
 
21 
 
PSC-CMs in a 3D hypoxic environment it is possible to gain insights into the cellular mechanisms 
underlying ischemic injury and recovery more effectively than the standard 2D cultures [51], [52]. 
 
 
5.3.2 Metabolism of different age hPSC-CMs  
The metabolism of hPSC-CMs is dynamic and complex, differing from immature to mature hPSC-
CMs [53]. Immature hPSC-CMs, paralleling the fetal-like metabolic profile, primarily rely on glu-
cose and glycolysis as energy supply, while mature hPSC-CMs shift towards oxidative phosphory-
lation and fatty acids metabolism paralleling the metabolic profile of adult CMs [54]. Despite using 
systems without addition of maturation enhancers, the metabolic transition can be influenced by ox-
ygen levels, nutrients and hormonal signals, all factors which are crucial for cell survival in both 
healthy and ischemic environments [55].  
 
5.3.3 Metabolic supporters 
Basal media used for cell cultivation are designed to include essential components that support cel-
lular metabolism. As glucose serves as the primary energy source for the hPSC-CMs this is a key 
component of most basal media. To further enhance cell viability and metabolic activity, these me-
dia are often supplemented with insulin, growth factors, and vitamins.  
5.3.3.1 Glucose 
As illustrated in Figure 5-3, the glycolytic pathway in hPSC-CMs begins with glucose conversion to 
pyruvate, generating ATP and NADH essential for cellular energy. In immature hPSC-CMs, py-
ruvate is mostly converted to lactate due to limited mitochondrial activity, producing only 2 ATP 
[11]. As maturation progresses, pyruvate can enter pathways for complete oxidation in the TCA cy-
cle, producing 36 ATP in the absence of glucose, glycogen, amino acids and lactate can serve as en-
ergy supply if oxygen is present [11], [56], [57], [58]. 
 
5.3.3.2 Insulin 
Insulin is very important for glucose metabolism. It enhances glucose transport and promotes hPSC-
CM proliferation by activating glucose transporter proteins (GLUT) to the cell membrane [59]. In-
sulin also acts as a growth factor and anti-apoptotic, essential for cell survival and maturation [36], 
[60]. In hypoxic environment insulin has also been shown to up-regulate expression of Hypoxia-
Inducible Factor 1 alpha (HIF-1α) a major regulator of cellular adaptation to low oxygen environ-
ment [61]. 
  
==PAGE:22==
 
 
22 
 
5.3.4 Oxygen Level 
Oxygen is a critical element for cellular metabolism and biological processes essential for cell sur-
vival. However, the required oxygen level can vary depending on the metabolic stage of the hPSC-
CM which can make it complex to determine the correct oxygen settings [62]. Too high levels of 
oxygen (hyperoxic conditions) can induce oxidative stress and hinder maturation[63], [64]. Con-
versely, too low oxygen levels (hypoxia), as seen in ischemic heart conditions, can also induce oxi-
dative stress and cause cell death[65]. However, studies have shown that short-term hypoxia and 
alternating between hypoxia and standard cell culturing conditions (21% O₂) during growth can en-
hance metabolic maturation and mitochondrial function, improving resistance to oxidative stress 
[43], [52], [66], [67]. When hPSC-CMs are exposed to hypoxia, certain cellular mechanisms can be 
activated to aid in adaptation and survival. One such mechanism involves HIF-1α, a key regulator 
that stabilizes under low oxygen conditions and promotes glycolysis, enhancing cell survival. How-
ever, prolonged exposure or imbalance of HIF-1α can also be harmful, as it balances between pro-
apoptotic and anti-apoptotic effects, depending on the cellular context [68]. See Figure 5-3 for illus-
tration. Adapting oxygen levels and understanding the metabolic nuances are pivotal for optimizing 
hPSC-CMs to better reflect in vivo CM properties in ischemic environments.

6 Materials and Methods 
This section provides a description of the materials and methods used to establish an ischemic in 
vitro model being exposed to a stressful condition and to characterize and analyze the hPSC-CMs 
response to this environment mimicking ischemic conditions in IHD. Metabolic stress in form of 
three different starvation media as hypoxic stress will be applied. The three selected media compo-
sitions, the CM ages and culturing duration were chosen based on CM viability observations from 
several pilot studies (Appendix A.1A). 
 
6.1 hPSC-CMs 
The differentiation of hPSC-CMs was conducted by the department of Cardiomyocyte Biology & 
Biomarkers in the Cell Therapy Unit at Novo Nordisk using established protocols. This process in-
cluded expansion of the cells followed by differentiation in 1L stirred tank bioreactors using a mod-
ified version of a published protocol. Obtained CMs were purified and cryopreserved as single cells 
12 days post differentiation induction (this stage is referred as CM12) and stored in liquid nitrogen 
tanks until use. The CM12 batch was analyzed for CM purity by flow cytometry using fluores-
cence-labeled cTnT and NKX2.5 antibodies. The same batch of CMs was used for all experiments 
to avoid batch differences arising from variation in differentiation processes.  
 
6.1.1 Media compositions 
Two media were used for providing the hPSC-CMs for aggregation culturing and thawing: 
1. Aggregation medium: Optimized by NN for CM culturing through the addition of various 
supplements (specifics are NN proprietary). 
2. Thawing medium: Aggregation medium + DNase I + Rock inhibitor. 
 
6.1.2 Thawing hPSCs 
For thawing, a cryovial was removed from the nitrogen tank and thawed for 9 minutes in a 37°C 
bead bath. To minimize osmotic stress, 1 mL of thawing media was added to the cells before trans-
ferring to a 50 mL Falcon tube containing thawing media. The cryovial was rinsed with 4 mL of 
thawing media to maximize cell recovery, and the contents were transferred to the Falcon tube, fol-
lowed by centrifugation at 300g for 3 minutes. The cells were resuspended in 40 mL of pre-heated

Aggregation media with 10uM-Rock inhibitor. A 200 µL sample was collected for cell counting us-
ing NucleoCount-202.

million cells/mL in aggregation medium supplemented with 10uM rock inhibitor in two Able Biott 
bioreactors. The bioreactors were maintained at 60 RPM for 3 days in an incubator set at 37°C, 5% 
CO2 and 21% O2.  This allowed for the formation of homogenous clusters, progressing the cells

6.2 hPSC-CM Ischemia In Vitro Model

6.2.3 Hypoxic Stress 
Two oxygen levels were used: 1% O2 (hypoxia) and 21% O2 (normoxia). Hypoxic incubation was 
achieved by connecting a gas regulator (Agilent) to a Cytation C10 microplate image reader (Ag-
ilent). The regulator, connected to CO2 and N2 gas outlets, controlled gas concentrations based on 
user-defined O2 and CO2 levels.  N2 and CO2 displace O2 in the chamber, to effectively reduce the 
available O2 [52]. A mixing fan ensured even gas distribution, maintaining a consistent hypoxic en-
vironment. This setup, installed and calibrated by Agilent, ensured gas accuracy of ±0.1%. For the 
experiment, gas levels were set at 1% O2 (mimicking ischemic MI) and 5% CO2 (same CO2 as in a 
standard cell-culturing incubator). See Figure 6-3 for illustration. A heating system maintained the 
plate at 37°C, functioning as a hypoxic incubator. To prevent evaporation and contamination the 
plate was covered with a non-sealed lid which allowed for gas exchange although this solution 
could involve a risk of delay in gas exchange.  The plate remained in this environment for 24 hours, 
between 48 and 72 hours of culturing (Figure 6-2 d)

6.3.3 Beating activity (Ca2+ imaging) 
A functional assay of intracellular Ca²⁺ flux was conducted to evaluate the beating activity response 
between CM stages under different conditions. The assay utilized the Ca²⁺ -binding dye Fluo-4 
(ThermoFisher F14201), which fluoresces upon binding to Ca²⁺ ions, allowing for the visualization 
and measurement of Ca²⁺ transients. Calcium transients in CMs serve as a surrogate measure for 
beating activity, as the influx of Ca²⁺ during an action potential triggers muscle contraction, and its 
removal leads to myocyte relaxation. 
Ca²⁺ transients were measured 72 hours after exposure to either hypoxia or normoxia. A volume of 
100 µL of Fluo-4 dye was added to a minimum of three wells in the 96-well plate, followed by in-
cubation for 30 minutes. The intracellular Ca²⁺ flux was then video recorded using the Cytation C10 
imaging system at 10 frames per second (FPS) for 30 seconds in the GFP channel. 
 
6.4 Limitatons in experimental procedures: 
The experimental procedures faced some limitations. Cell count was only performed at time 0 
meaning there could be a difference in cell concentrations after 48 h culturing in different media in 
the 6-well plate. This means I am not certain I have the same number of cells for all conditions. Dif-
ferent cell concentrations could potentially impact metabolic measurements for lactate and glucose. 
Additionally, transferring cells from separate 6-well plates to 96-well plates could introduce varia-
bility. Using use of two different Cytation C10 units, each with varying gas control setups and po-
tential instrumental differences, may have affected the consistency of the viability results.

Data Visualization and statistical tests for the experiments were performed using GraphPad Prism 
(version 10.4.1). Tables, plots and bar charts shown as mean±SEM.  Significance level was set at 
p<0.05 (*), p<0.01 (**), p<0.001 (***), p<0.0001(****). 
For Viability calculations (VCA%) each data point included in the mean value consisted of at least 
3 replicate measurements for each timepoint. 
The two tested CM stages were consistently denoted as CM15 and CM22 for all time points.

6.5.1 Brown-Forsythe Test 
The Brown-Forsythe test was used to assess the equality of variances among groups. This test deter-
mined whether replicates from all repeats could be pooled or should be kept separate for viability 
data.  
 
6.5.2 Robust Regression and Outlier Removal (ROUT) Test 
The ROUT test, with a Q value of 0.5%, was used to identify extreme values considered errors and 
unsuitable for analysis.  
 
6.5.3 Shapiro-Wilk Test 
The Shapiro-Wilk test and Q-Q plots assessed data normality for each group (4 media conditions, 2 
oxygen levels, 2 CM stages), totaling 16 groups. For the viability test, an additional group with two 
time points (48h, 72h) was included, resulting in 32 groups. 
 
6.5.4 Unpaired T-test 
The unpaired t-test compared the means of two independent groups. It was used to determine signif-
icant differences in glucose and lactate concentrations between normoxia and hypoxia conditions 
[69]. 
 
6.5.5 ANOVA Tests 
The one-way ANOVA test compared the means of three or more independent groups, identifying 
statistically significant differences in VCA%. 
The two-way ANOVA test examined the interaction between two combined effects of media type 
and CM stages on lactate production, and to assess significant differences in VCA% across various 
experimental conditions and their interactions. 
 
6.5.6 Post-hoc Tukey or Fisher’s LSD test 
The post-hoc Tukey and Fisher’s LSD test was conducted following a significant ANOVA result to 
compare differences in pairs.

8 Discussion 
This thesis aimed to understand the responses of hPSC-CMs to ischemic stressors. Specifically, it 
investigated metabolic stress induced by three different media compositions and hypoxic stress by 
comparing low (1% O₂) versus standard oxygen (21% O₂) incubation. Additionally, it examined 
whether there were differences in responses between two stages of hPSC-CMs, separated by a one-
week interval in age (CM15 vs. CM22), mainly focusing on lactate metabolism and cell viability, 
but also discussing the examples of functionality and where RNAseq could have provided better un-
derstanding. 
 
8.1 Metabolism 
 
8.1.1 hPSC-CMs Lactate Metabolism in response to Metabolic stress 
The results demonstrated that glucose was metabolized into lactate, following glycolytic metabo-
lism even in the presence of oxygen. These findings align with other studies indicating that in-vitro 
cultured hPSC-CMs maintain an immature metabolic phenotype compared to adult in-vivo CMs. In 
immature CMs, the majority of ATP is produced through glycolysis, producing lactate, rather than 
oxidative phosphorylation in the mitochondria, which are not fully developed [70]. Lactate produc-
tion was especially seen in glucose-containing media, with a clear inverse relationship between glu-
cose decrease and lactate production. Interestingly, even in glucose-deprived media, there was still a 
detectable increase in lactate production, although it was about 5-10 fold lower than in glucose con-
taining conditions. This suggests that hPSC-CMs can produce lactate without glucose, potentially 
through alternative metabolic pathways that produce lactate, like glycogen. While direct evidence 
specifically linking glycogen storage to in-vitro hPSC-CMs under starvation conditions is still 
emerging, findings from in-vitro studies of hPSCs have shown that glycogen can be utilized to sup-
port energy needs during periods of nutrient deprivation [71], [72]. Additionally, in-vivo studies of 
CMs suggest that glycogen storage can support CMs' energy needs during periods of nutrient depri-
vation [72], [73]. This indicates that glycogen breakdown may be activated to sustain energy pro-
duction in the absence of glucose. Alternative energy sources, as amino acids and lactate could also 
have been supporting metabolism. However, the higher lactate concentrations observed after 72 
hours of culturing compared to 48 hours suggest that lactate is not the primary energy source. An 
interesting observation was seen in the glucose deprived media with insulin supplementation after 
48 h. In this media lactate levels were lower than in the complete starvation media indicating that 
==PAGE:45==
   
 
45 
 
insulin can affect lactate metabolism. Insulin is known to be able to stimulate Coris cycle, a meta-
bolic process happening especially during starvation where lactate is used as a substrate to generate 
pyruvate as part of gluconeogenesis [74], [75].  
 
8.1.2 Lactate Metabolism in different CMs stages 
The lactate production was overall similar between CM15 and CM2. However, CM22 exhibited 
lower lactate production compared to CM15 in glucose-containing media. This suggests that the 7-
day older CM22 may have initiated a metabolic shift from glycolysis to mitochondrial TCA cycle 
activity leading to lower lactate production. This observation is consistent with a study from Ergir et 
al, who reported a metabolic switch in long-term cultured 3D hPSC-CMs [76]. However, in this 
study there was about 4 weeks interval between CM stage and other studies indicate that a signifi-
cant differences in maturity are not evident until at least two weeks of culturing [77]. It could be 
speculated if a time interval of 7 days between CM stages as used in this study might have been too 
short to achieve a more mature metabolic switch in the hPSC-CMs. If a metabolic switch had oc-
curred in the CM22, a larger difference in lactate production between normoxic and hypoxic condi-
tions would have been expected due to reduced lactate production under oxygen-rich conditions. 
However, there was no significant difference in this variation between any of the CM stages or me-
dia, further suggesting that at CM22 the matured metabolic switch had not occurred yet. RNA se-
quencing could have provided insights into maturity factors and difference in metabolic pathways 
between the two CM stages. 
 
8.1.3 hSPC-CMs Lactate metabolism responses to Hypoxic Stress 
It was indicated that lactate production was higher under hypoxia compared to normoxia across all 
media and stages of CMs, however likely due to small sample size and large variation this was only 
significant for CM22 in Ctrl. Media. A hypoxia induced metabolic shift towards increased glycoly-
sis is consistent with the known cellular response to low oxygen availability [78]. Hypoxia also 
stimulates HIF-1α which upregulates glycolytic enzymes and promotes lactate production [79].  
 
8.2 Viability 
8.2.1 hPSC-CMs Viability in Response to Metabolic stress 
Having explored the metabolic responses of hPSC-CMs to various stressors, it is interesting to ex-
amine how these stressors affect hPSC-CM cluster viability. After 48h of culturing in normoxic 
conditions the CM viability was significantly decreased in the Starv. Media compared to Ctrl for 
both CM15 and CM22 stage. Although both supplemented media (Starv. + Glucose + Ins and Starv 
==PAGE:46==
 
 
46 
 
+ Ins.) had absolute values below the Ctrl media this was not significant different fro, Ctrl. Further 
decline in viability was seen for all media over the following 48-72 h time interval where hypoxia 
was also added as additional variant.  
These findings of reduced viability in Starv. media are consistent with other studies that have re-
ported apoptosis and necrosis in glucose-starved cells [80]. 
This rapid decrease in viability under glucose and insulin deprivation highlights the critical role of 
metabolic support in maintaining cell survival and function. The observed cell death is likely due to 
the inability of hPSC-CMs to sustain energy production through alternative metabolic pathways 
when deprived of glucose. This is supported by studies showing that hPSC-CMs primarily rely on 
glycolysis for ATP production, and glucose deprivation can lead to an energy crisis and cell death 
[70]. Despite being affected the majority of the hPSC-CMs were still alive after 72 h of culturing 
indicating availability of alternative nutrient sources such as lactate, amino acids or glycogen [72]. 
This is supported by the lactate production observed in glucose-deprived conditions, indicating that 
alternative metabolic pathway, as glycogen, may be sustaining some level of energy production and 
viability. 
Interestingly, in glucose-deprived but insulin-present conditions under normoxia, hPSC-CMs exhib-
ited significantly higher viability. This suggests that insulin acts as a rescuer even under nutrient 
stress, potentially by promoting cell survival pathways and inhibiting apoptosis. Studies have shown 
that insulin enhances cell growth, stability, and anti-apoptotic mechanisms in various cell types, in-
cluding hPSC-CMs [58], [81]. The PI3K/Akt pathway, activated by insulin, plays a crucial role in 
this process by inhibiting pro-apoptotic factors and promoting the expression of anti-apoptotic pro-
teins [82] 
Moreover, insulin can also bind to Insulin like Growth Factor-1 (IGF-1) receptors. This is a potent 
growth factor and insulins cross-reactivity to these receptors may contribute to the observed protec-
tive effects of insulin under nutrient stress conditions, as IGF-1 signaling is known to promote CM 
survival and reduce apoptosis [83]. The insulin containing starvation media had an effect of 48 h 
viability similar to the glucose containing media demonstrating the rescuing power of insulin after 
48 h culturing in normoxia 
 
8.2.2 hPSC-CMs Viability in Response to Hypoxic stress 
Generally, the study showed that hypoxic stress did not have a harmful effect on hPSC-CM sur-
vival. The viability of hPSC-CMs under hypoxic stress exhibited a pattern almost similar to that ob-
served under normoxic conditions. 
==PAGE:47==
   
 
47 
 
These findings align with previous studies indicating that hPSC-CMs can adapt to hypoxic environ-
ments by shifting their metabolic pathways to rely more on glycolysis, which is less dependent on 
oxygen [83]. HIF-1α plays a crucial role in this adaptation by upregulating glycolytic enzymes and 
promoting lactate production, which also was supported by the trend to higher lactate production in 
hSPC-CMs under hypoxic conditions [79]. Interestingly, hypoxia even appears to provide a better 
survival condition particularly for hPSC-CMs in glucose and insulin-deprived media. Several fac-
tors may contribute to this observation. Firstly, the fetal in-vivo environment is characterized by 
low oxygen levels [55], and hPSC-CMs often share metabolic and functional similarities with fetal 
CM [70]. This resemblance might explain why hPSC-CMs exhibit better viability under hypoxia, as 
it offers a more physiologically relevant environment for these cells. 
Additionally, the standard cell culture oxygen level, referred to as normoxic conditions (21% O₂), 
might be hyperoxic for hPSC-CMs, leading to increased oxidative stress. Hyperoxia can result in 
the excessive production of reactive oxygen species (ROS), which can damage cellular components, 
induce apoptosis, and impair cell viability [84].  HIF-1α also plays a crucial role in cellular adapta-
tion to hypoxia. Besides upregulating glycolysis, HIF-1α activation has been associated with re-
duced apoptosis by increasing the expression of anti-apoptotic proteins, which help prevent cell 
death [85]. These findings highlight the robustness of hPSC-CMs to hypoxic stress and suggest that 
hypoxia may provide a protective effect under certain nutrient-deprived conditions, which is very 
relevant for therapies to survive in an ischemic environment. However, a remarkable observation 
was made regarding CM15 under glucose-deprived but insulin-present conditions in hypoxia. In 
this scenario, a significant drop in cell viability (50% over 24 hours) was observed, particularly be-
tween 60 to 72 hours, where the highest viability decline across all conditions was seen. This effect 
is surprising, as the presence of insulin is supporting growth and anti-apoptosis. During hypoxia in-
sulin can upregulate HIF-1α expression in various cell types, including CM [74] [80] which could 
perhaps be relevant for the observed response. The role of HIF-1α in CM is complex; while it can 
promote survival under hypoxic conditions, excessive HIF-1α activation can also lead to apoptosis 
in severe hypoxia, indicating that the balance of HIF-1α activity is critical for CM health and sur-
vival [67], [87]. The simultaneous and combined stress of hypoxia-induced HIF-1α upregulating 
glycolytic activity, along with insulin's role in stimulating growth and enhancing glucose uptake in 
glucose-starved environment might be highly stressful for the hPSC-CMs [58], [81]. Overall, these 
results underscore the complex interplay between hypoxia, nutrient availability, and growth factor 
signaling in determining the survival and function of hPSC-CMs. The findings suggest that while 
==PAGE:48==
 
 
48 
 
insulin and hypoxia can be protective under separate conditions, their combination can worsen 
stress and induce cell death, highlighting the importance of carefully modulating these factors in 
therapeutic applications. Where the experiments performed in normoxia indicated that delivering 
CM with an insulin supplement alone could significantly increase viability, the hypoxia data sug-
gest that insulin as stand alone could have detrimental in an oxygen deprived ischemic environment.  
 
8.2.3 Viability in different CMs stages 
The results do not demonstrate an overall difference in viability between the two CM stages. How-
ever, they do show that CM22 under complete metabolic stress (absence of glucose and insulin) ex-
hibit a viability decline of 30% for CM15 and a 50% decline for CM22. This suggests that CM22 
are more sensitive to the switch from high glucose culturing media to glucose deprived and no insu-
lin media. It is interesting that with only 7 days interval between CM stage eigns of a maturity 
switch was observed, as this is different from what has been previously published where at least two 
weeks are needed to find differences [77].  
The significant decline in viability for the CM15 stage in Starv + Ins media has been discussed ear-
lier, and it is interesting to observe that the same dramatic pattern in viability decline does not seem 
to apply to the CM22 stage in similar media. The difference could perhaps be explained by a differ-
ence in growth factor effects as IGF-1 and insulin receptors are typically expressed in higher levels 
in younger cells.[88], [89] Having a higher expression of HIF-1α  and pushing stronger for growth 
in anaerobic nutrient deprived environment might push the CM over the edge. Looking further into 
RNA sequencing data for these genes could further elucidate this hypothesis, but unfortunately 
these data were not available so obtaining a deeper molecular understanding of the mechanisms be-
hind these findingd was unfortunately not possible to pursue in these experiments. 
It could be argued that the CM22 in Starv + Ins media exhibit a similar trend of viability drop be-
tween 66 to 72 h, as CM15 do between 60 to 66h h, suggesting that CM22 may simply be delayed 
in responding to the toxic environment. It would have been interesting to follow the CM22 for a 
longer period to clarify if they will show a similar but delayed profile as the CM15 stage in Starv. 
media supplemented with insulin. 
 
8.3 hPSC-CM Beating frequency changes 
Despite published data [77] and the above described metabolic data indicate their limited sign of 
maturation from CM15 to CM22, beating frequency data indicate a notable difference between 
CM15 and CM22 in terms of Ca2+ flux patterns. 
==PAGE:49==
   
 
49 
 
The included examples show that for both normoxia and hypoxia, CM15 exhibit higher beating fre-
quencies and more active Ca2+ flux compared to CM22, which is consistent with their immature 
state as mature CM display more stable and regular beating patterns [28]. The limited data included 
also suggests that hypoxic stress impacts the beating function of hPSC-CMs, resulting in less stable 
frequencies compared to normoxia. This observation aligns with studies indicating that hypoxia in-
duces stress responses in CMs, leading to irregular beating patterns [90][26]. However, it is im-
portant to note that data was only generated in Ctrl media and for only two clusters for each condi-
tion. Therefore, while the observations provide valuable insights, they cannot be considered defini-
tive. Further studies with better analytic processes are needed to explore these findings and the im-
pact of environmental stressors on ca2+ flux and beating activity. 
 
8.4 Stressors as preconditioning factors for hPSC-CM 
Snyder et al. identified that exposing hiPSC-CMs to controlled hypoxic conditions before subject-
ing them to severe hypoxia can improve their resistance to ischemic stress [91]. It has also been pro-
posed to use maturation media with high fatty acid and calcium concentrations to condition hiPSC-
CMs demonstrating an enhancement of their metabolic efficiency and stress resistance [54]. Other 
studies have shown that intermittent starvation can enhance the maturation of hPSC-CMs by adapt-
ing their metabolic pathways in response to nutrient availability. This adaptation likely includes the 
utilization of stored glycogen to meet energy demands during periods of starvation [25], [72]. Addi-
tionally, research by Lees et al. highlighted that oxygen levels can influence glycogen synthesis in 
hPSC-CMs, suggesting that environmental factors play a critical role in regulating glycogen storage 
[92]. 
Other treatments, such as heat shock, can induce the expression of protective proteins that help cells 
cope with stress. Also, external factors such as mechanical stress and electrical stimulation have 
been shown to enhance contractility and overall maturation [93].

9 Conclusion and future perspectives 
This study successfully established a 3D ischemic in vitro model to investigate the metabolic and 
viability responses of hPSC-CMs under conditions of metabolic and hypoxic stress. The model pro-
vided a robust platform for examining these responses, revealing critical insights into the metabolic 
flexibility and survival mechanisms of hPSC-CMs.  
 
The results showed that glycolysis is actively producing lactate under all conditions, even in the ab-
sence of glucose as an energy supply, indicating that the cells can remain metabolically active with-
out glucose which is normally the main energy source. 
Overall hypoxia seemed protective except in CM15 cultured in glucose deprived media supple-
mented with insulin. While insulin initially appeared to be rescuing, it induced dramatic cell death 
in CM15 under low oxygen conditions. The findings in this thesis underscore the complex interplay 
between hypoxia, nutrient availability, and growth factor signaling in determining the survival and 
metabolism of hPSC-CMs and highlights the importance of carefully modulating the combined set 
of factors relevant for therapeutic applications when studying conditions aimed for enhancing the 
viability and function of hPSC-CMs of cardiac regenerative therapies. 
 
Future research should focus on extending the maturation of hPSC-CMs by increasing the culturing 
duration or using maturation enhancers, such as short-term hypoxic and metabolic stress. Applying 
RNA sequencing and proteomics, should be employed to gain a comprehensive understanding of 
the molecular pathways involved in CM responses to ischemic stress. It could also be interesting to 
employ genetic engineering with CRISPR techniques knocking down HIF1- α, insulin or IGF1 re-
ceptors to get a deeper understanding of the role of these biologies. Follow up with in vivo studies 
to validate the findings from the in vitro model and assess the translational potential is also relevant.  
Overall, the in vitro ischemia model developed in this study provides a valuable foundation for ad-
vancing our understanding of CM biology and developing innovative therapies for heart disease.

A.1. Pilot-Study  
Prior to the initiation of the main experiments outlined in this thesis, several pilot studies were con-
ducted to optimize conditions for the comprehensive testing included in subsequent investigations. 
These pilot experiments were limited in scope, with a small number of trials and numerous varia-
bles, resulting in insufficient data for robust statistical analysis. Consequently, while the findings 
may not be entirely accurate, they serve as a valuable guide for selecting the conditions for the main 
study.  
  
Aim:   
The primary objective of these pilot studies was to select three distinct experimental starvation me-
dia for the main study, each exhibiting varying degrees of nutrient deprivation compared to the con-
trol media. Additionally, the studies aimed to determine suitable culturing durations and cardiomyo-
cyte (CM) maturation stages.  
  
Materials and Methods:   
Media Composition:   
Ten different media formulations were tested during the pilot experiments (Table 1). The control 
media consisted of a glucose containing Dulbecco's Modified Eagle's Medium (DMEM) optimized 
by Novo Nordisk for CM culturing through the addition of various supplements, including albumin 
and insulin, further specifics are Novo Nordisk proprietary. The most nutrient-deprived starvation 
media was a glucose-free DMEM. Variations in glucose, albumin, and insulin concentrations were 
investigated to assess their effects on CM viability. To maintain osmolarity between glucose-con-
taining and glucose-free media, the impact of the inactive isomer L-Glucose was evaluated.

Discussion:  
The pilot studies demonstrated that media manipulation could successfully create nutrient-limited 
environments that better mimic ischemic conditions without inducing excessive cell death. The ad-
dition of insulin appeared to enhance CM viability in starvation media, while albumin showed no 
==PAGE:64==

visual effect on viability but helped prevent clumping in nutrient-rich media, resulting in morphol-
ogy similar to control media clusters. The use of L-Glucose to maintain consistent osmolarity across 
the media was validated, showing no impact on CM viability.   
   
The pilot experiments provided valuable insights into the conditions for the main experiments. 
Given that the starting material was cryopreserved CM12, which requires three days of culturing to 
form 3D cell clusters, the youngest CM stage selected was CM15. To ensure a difference between 
the chosen maturity stages while avoiding older CM stages that appeared less viable, CM22 was se-
lected as the second maturation age. A one-week interval was also practical from an experimental 
point of view. As minimal media effects were observed after 24 hours of culture, but clear effects 
were noted after 48 hours, this duration was chosen prior to transferring to 96-well plates.  
  
Besides the control media the starvation media without any additions (media 10) was selected as the 
most deprived media. To represent various degrees of nutrient deprivation, starvation media with 
added glucose and insulin, as well as albumin (media 2) to prevent clumping, was chosen as the 
second experimental media. Finally, starvation media with insulin only (media 7) was selected as 
the third experimental media. These conditions represent different levels of nutrient availability, 
from optimal to severe starvation, enabling a comprehensive analysis of cardiomyocytes under var-
ying growth and survival environments. Since L-Glucose did not affect viability, it was included to 
maintain similar osmolarity across all experimental media.

A.2. Statistical Evaluation 
To assess the robustness of our experimental data, we conducted a series of statistical tests on both 
the metabolism and viability datasets. 
 
A ROUT Q=0.5 test was performed, which detected no outliers in the metabolism data; however, it 
identified one outlier in the viability data (Ctrl. CM22 at the 48-hour and 72-hour time points). This 
outlier was excluded, while all other data were retained for analysis. 
 
The Shapiro-Wilk test indicated that all groups, except one in the metabolism dataset and 28 out of 
32 groups in the viability dataset, were normally distributed. Based on the visual assessment pro-
vided by the Q-Q plot, and considering the robustness of t-tests and ANOVA to minor deviations 
from normality, we treated all data as normally distributed to facilitate parametric analysis. (It is im-
portant to note that the Prism software does not offer a non-parametric option for two-way ANOVA 
[94].  
 
For the viability data, the Brown-Forsythe test revealed significant differences in variance in one 
group. Consequently, the data are presented as Mean ± Standard Error of the Mean (SEM) from 
four independent experiments, each including at least three replicate measurements
