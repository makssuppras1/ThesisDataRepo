iii
Abstract
The evolution of computing techniques plays an increasing important role in engineering
due to the large complexity in modern industry. Classical methods such as the finite vol-
ume and finite element method each fall short in their own way due to low convergence
order and global operators respectively. This thesis therefore presents a nodal discon-
tinuous Galerkin finite element method (DG-FEM) to discretize the spacial part of the
coupled vibro-acoustic transient PDE problem, which governs many physical interactions
such as microphones and vibrations in cars. The linear acoustic and isotropic linear elastic
hyperbolic systems are discretized separately to reach a semidiscrete strong formulation,
and a new generalized numerical flux formulation is presented for both single-physics and
multi-physics element couplings, which is independent of the chosen flux method. The for-
mulation is a function of a local 1D coeﬀicient matrix, which computes the single-physics
numerical flux in a local edge-aligned coordinate system. The coeﬀicient matrix is deter-
mined for both a central flux and upwind flux, and a material variation across an element
interface as well as boundary conditions are considered for the upwind flux. A low-storage,
five-stage, fourth-order Runge Kutta method is used for time integration which has a larger
stability region and lower storage requirements. A computational analysis in performed
which shows that DG-FEM is well-suited for implementation in a parallel environment,
due to its linear behavior and good communication properties between elements. The
linear acoustic and isotropic linear elastic single-physics schemes are validated against a
known solution and a commercial software respectively, and shows perfect agreement. The
coupled vibro-acoustic scheme is validated for both a central flux and upwind flux multi-
physics coupling via two benchmark cases, and is also shown to match with the solution
of commercial software, and further testing is done to showcase its capabilities. DG-FEM
has a large potential both commercially and for research, where new adventures can be
found in non-linear problems, cut-elements and optimization problems.

Sammendrag
Udviklingen af modellerings teknikker spiller en stigende rolle i ingeniørbranchen på grund
af kompleksiteten i moderne industri. Klassiske metoder som finite volume og finite ele-
ment har deres mangler såsom lav konvergens orden og globale operatorer respektivt set.
Denne tese præsenterer derfor en nodal diskontinuert Galerkin finite element methode
(DG-FEM) til at diskretisere den stedslige del af det koblede tidslige vibro-akustiske PDE
problem, som dækker over interaktioner som mikrofoner og vibrationer i biler. De lineær
akustiske og isotropisk lineær elastiske hyperbolske ligningssystemer er diskretiseret hver
for sig for at opnå en stærk formulering, og en ny generaliseret numerisk flux formulering
er præsenteret for element koblinger ved enkelt-fysik og multi-fysik, som er uafhængig af
den valgte flux metode. Formuleringen er en funktion af en lokal 1D koeﬀicient matrice,
som udregner den den numeriske flux for enkelt-fysik i et lokalt koordinatsystem som er
normalt på elementranden. Koeﬀicient matricen er udregnet for både en central flux og
en upwind flux, og materiale variation over en element interface samt randbetingelser
er givet for upwind flux.
En fjerde-ordens Runge Kutta med god plads udnyttelse og
større stabilitetsregion er brugt til tidsintegration, og en effektivitets analyse viser at DG-
FEM ville være god i et paralleliseret miljø på grund af dens lineære skalerbarhed og
gode kommunikation mellem elementer. De lineær akustiske og isotropisk lineær elastiske
problemer er respektivt valideret mod en kendt løsning og kommercielt software og viser
perfekt samhørighed. Det koblede vibro-akustiske problem er valideret for både en cen-
tral og upwind multi-fysik kobling mod to benchmark cases og er også vist at passe mod
en kommerciel løsning, og yderligere undersøgelser viser dens egenskaber. DG-FEM har
et stort potentiale kommercielt og i forkning, hvor nye udfodringer kommer til at ligge i
ikke-lineære problemer, cut-elements og optimering.

Introduction
1.1
Background
It is assumed that the reader has a basic knowledge in the areas of applied mathematics.
1.2
Motivation
Modern paradigms in scientific computing and the evolution of computing techniques and
capabilities play an increasing important role in vast number of engineering applications.
Amongst are vibro-acoustic interactions which are important to many physical phenomena
and engineering tasks, and covers several length scales, e.g. from MEMS microphones to
the design of concert halls. Experimental testing is a key design activity to ensure well-
made products, but manufacturing costs and turnaround time for tests make it unfeasible
to conduct more than just a few. Supplementing tests with accurate physics simulations
make design iterations faster and cheaper.

The importance of simulations can for example be found in the automotive industry, where
cabin noise from engine vibrations, turbulent flow over side mirrors and road contact,
etc., affect passengers’ comfort immensely and can make one car brand superior to the
other. Manufacturers can test and iterate on the comfortability of the car, but simulating
the acoustic impact of various sources is far more time- and cost-eﬀicient in achieving
a finished product, and has the potential of dramatically improving development times.
The simulations are done by discretizing the governing partial differential equations by a
mathematical approximation and solving them computationally. The governing equations
must be representative of real life, the approximation determines the accuracy and solving
speed is achieved through computations.
There are several ways of doing a mathematical approximation and choosing is often not
trivial. Among the methods are the widely used Finite Difference Methods (FDM), Finite
Volume Methods (FVM) and Finite Element Methods (FEM), which all does a discrete
representation of the spatial derivative. But these well-studied methods each fall short
in their own way. The FDM has the advantages of being easy to implement and allows
for hp-adaptivity1.
One can also formulate the FDM using upwinding such that it is
well-suited for wave-dominating problems where propagation of information is directional.
However, FDM falls short when it comes to discretizing complex geometries which require
unstructured grids. The FVM is, unlike the FDM, well suited for complex geometries.
1Being able to converge by reducing the mesh size (h) or increasing the polynomial order (p).
==PAGE:14==

The FVM consists of a grid of cells that are connected through fluxes, which are meant
to conserve energy in each cell, and a problem-specific upwind flux can be derived and
give high accuracy in wave-dominating problems, like for the FDM. The FVM is therefore
often used when solving real-life wave-dominating problems, such as the Navier-Stokes
equations.
However the FVM is unable to increase the polynomial order in each cell,
giving the method a low order of convergence2. The FEM has both hp-adaptivity and
is geometrically flexible and seems to be the natural choice for most problems. However,
because the FEM is a global method, the discrete operators become very large for large
problems. In a semi-discrete scheme where time integration is employed, one must invert
the potentially very large operators, making the scheme computationally non-eﬀicient.
Furthermore, the FEM can have stability issues in wave-dominating problems due to the
structure of the basis if not dealt with properly. All these properties are summarized in
Hesthaven and Warburton, 2007, Table 1.1.
1.3
Aim of thesis
A method that has geometric flexibility and hp-adaptability along with being well-suited
for wave-dominating problems and computationally eﬀicient, is therefore sought-after for
solving problems like vibro-acoustics. This leads to the aim of this thesis, which is to
implement the discontinuous Galerkin finite element method (DG-FEM). This method
has over the last decades been researched and developed, and combines the FVM with the
FEM. The problem is solved on each element using the hp-adaptability of FEM, making it a
local method with relatively few degrees of freedom, and elements are connected through
a flux, like in the FVM, where upwinding is a well suited choice for wave dominating
problems (Hesthaven & Warburton, 2007), like vibro-acoustics.
This thesis is intended as an introduction to DG-FEM for readers without knowledge of
the method. Vibro-acoustics is an interesting case-study to work with, but the theory
presented could also be applied to other coupled hyperbolic problems. Relatively basic
theory will therefore be presented with relevant citations to material, which go further
into detail.
1.4
Review
DG-FEM was proposed for the first time in Reed and Hill, 1977 to solve a steady-state neu-
tron transport equation. Since then, DG-FEM has been used for single-physics problems
multiple times and is thoroughly explained in the book Hesthaven and Warburton, 2007
which gives the theoretical background for the DG-FEM framework by Nunn and Warbur-
ton, 2008 used in this thesis. However, the book does not treat any of the relevant physical
problems that this thesis require, concerning acoustics, elasticity and multi-physics cou-
plings. The hyperbolic system of linear acoustic equations are on standard form and can
be seen for example in Pind et al., 2020; Shaposhnikov et al., 2019, whereas the isotropic
linear elastic equations required some research. The equations are not on its standard
form, in that they always are on a first order velocity-stress or velocity-strain formula-
tion for DG-FEM, such they form a linear hyperbolic system of equations. Examples of
velocity-stress formulations are plentiful where e.g. Feng et al., 2007; Martin and Dumb-
ser, 2006 consider 2D and e.g. Dumbser and Käser, 2000; Sihar and Hornikx, 2019; Wilcox
et al., 2010 consider 3D. Anisotropic materials are furthermore investigated in Zhan et al.,
2018; Zhan et al., 2020, however this is out of scope in this thesis.
2The order of convergence is a description of how fast a method goes, or converges, towards a true
solution when decreasing the cell/element size.

Chapter 1. Introduction
3
When considering the exchange of information between elements, also known as numerical
flux, all papers utilize the so-called upwind flux which is derived from a Riemann formu-
lation of a hyperbolic system of equations. The upwind flux is also well-known in finite
volume schemes, and was treated early by Randall J. LeVeque, 2002; Toro, 1999. Two
different versions of the upwind flux exist, one in a generalized formulation (Dumbser &
Käser, 2000; Martin & Dumbser, 2006; Miryaha et al., 2015), which is hard to understand
mathematically, and one where a mathematical derivation is made using the solution of
advection equations via the domain of dependence (Shaposhnikov et al., 2019; Sihar &
Hornikx, 2019; Wilcox et al., 2010; Zhan et al., 2018; Zhan et al., 2020) which will be
considered in this thesis. Both methods are probably equal, but in this thesis the math-
ematics will be explained thoroughly. All of them however treats the multi-dimensional
problems as an edge-aligned 1D problem, also called an x-split, for much easier derivation.
This was originally also proposed in Toro, 1999.
Finally considering vibro-acoustics and the multi-physics coupling, most papers do what
is called a unified coupling, meaning that the linear acoustic equations are governed by the
isotropic linear elastic equations. In the stress-velocity formulation, two Lamé parameters
are introduced such that when setting the second parameter two zero, the linear acoustic
equations are achieved (Wilcox et al., 2010; Zhan et al., 2018). This obviously simplifies
some derivations, but also constrains the possibilities in that it becomes harder to im-
plement more advances physics. Another coupling is done in the x-split formulation by
Zhan et al., 2020, who treats the equations separately and invokes the coupling conditions
through their mathematical formulation. It is however hard to understand exactly what
is done in the paper, and in this thesis I present things in much more easy-to-understand
formulation, even though it is probably a lot similar to what is done in their paper.
Boundary conditions to satisfy linear elasticity are not found in many articles, but Sihar
and Hornikx, 2019; Zhan et al., 2018 presents these briefly through the weak formulation
of the PDE. A mathematical approach is presented in Feng et al., 2007 such that the
upwind flux formulation mathematically satisfies the boundary conditions. This approach
will not be considered in this paper, but could be of interest if more complicated conditions
were to be implemented.
1.5
Structure of thesis
The thesis is organized as follows. In chapter 2 the governing equations are derived for lin-
ear acoustics and isotropic linear elasticity, including boundary and coupling conditions.
The theory of discretization by DG-FEM is presented in chapter 3, which is necessary since
the reader is not expected to be familiar with DG-FEM. The discretization is applied to
the governing equations in chapter 4 and a generalized numerical flux scheme is derived in
chapter 5, which is independent of the chosen flux method. Local formulations for linear
acoustics and isotropic linear elasticity are derived for central flux and upwind flux which
fit into the generalized formulations. In chapter 6 the time integration is looked upon in-
cluding a stability and scalability analysis. Chapter 7 concerns the implementation in code
and the solvers are validated separately in chapter 8. Several case studies are investigated
in chapter 9 and finally a conclusion and future work is presented in chapter 10.

boundary condition in eq. (2.12). An illustration is seen in fig. 5.6a where the external

5.5.3
Open face
The open face condition allows for information to slip out of a boundary without reflecting
any information no matter the incoming angle. This is somewhat of a diﬀicult problem
if working with a standard finite element method, since it is invoked through a first or-
der approximation to a Sommerfeld condition. In DG-FEM however, we simply set the
external state to zero, such that no information of the incoming state is blocked. This
implementation however only works for the upwind flux, as far as I can tell, since the
upwind flux knows the direction in which information travels. In other words, the central
flux does not know that information is moving out of the boundary and therefore expects
there to be an external state. Whereas the upwind flux knows that information is mov-
ing out of the boundary and therefore does not need an external state. For the acoustic
variables, this is done to simulate an open face in a channel for example as
p+ = 0, on Γa,out,
v+ = 0, on Γa,out,

6.4
Computational scalability
In this section the computational scalability of the right hand side function will be inves-
tigated. Recall that DG-FEM is a local method where each element solution is evolved in
time separately. The kth element is only concerned about its neighbouring elements when
evaluating the flux contributions to the right hand side function. And this may sound
similar to a standard FEM where the system bandwidth1 determines the computational
eﬀiciency. However in DG-FEM, the connection of elements is merely an index call to
neighboring solutions and the element index will not change the computational effort. A
good example is periodic boundary conditions. This is always very non-eﬀicient in stan-
dard FEM because elements on opposite boundaries are far from each other resulting in
a large bandwidth. But in DG-FEM, the distance between elements will not affect the
computational effort due to the locality of the method, and communication to neighboring
elements is just an index call. It does not matter if the neighboring element is right next
to it or on the other boundary in a periodic condition.
The locality of DG-FEM also makes it well-suited for parallel computing since almost all
operations work on local element level, meaning that each element essentially could be
given its own CPU. A connection between these CPU’s will then have to be made when
needing the state of the neighboring elements.

The purpose of this chapter is to provide an overview of the framework and the changes
made in the development of the coupled vibro-acoustic solver.
A nodal DG-FEM framework (Nunn & Warburton, 2008) has been used as a basis in the
development of the coupled vibro-acoustic solver in Matlab (MATLAB®, 2021). The key
part of the framework is the development of the local grid, the local operators, global
coordinates and the Jacobians.

the development of boundary node mappings, such that nodes on the boundaries can
easily be coupled to neighboring element nodes for flux formulations. There a different
mappings for both the internal flux as well as for boundary conditions. Some of this has
been altered. The framework has given a routine for LSERK4 time stepping which has
not been changed. And lastly, the main setup for the right hand side function has been

flux is chosen as the method for multi-physics coupling, the additional coeﬀicient matrix
terms that the upwind flux contribute with is set to zero, such that the coeﬀicient matrix
for a central flux in eqs. (5.14) and (5.15) is achieved. All edge nodes therefore has its own
coeﬀicient matrix, and a transformation to global coordinates using eqs. (5.6) and (5.7) for
the single-physics and eqs. (5.11) and (5.12) for the multi-physics can then be performed.
The whole routine is found in appendix H.

all variables and the user chooses which time steps are needed in the output. Mappings,
global information and operators are saved in structure arrays and imported in the right

that importing structure arrays and unpacking them, reduced the computational time by
a factor of 30. A last addition to the time integration scheme was the calculation of elastic
displacements, ˜u, from elastic field velocities, ˜v. In eq. (6.1) the field velocities was simply
to set the right hand side function, L, resembling the relation ∂˜u

mesh, mappings, initial condition, temporal field plot of stresses and pressures, and the
probe time series.

DTU computers through Thinlinc. The software winSCP can then be used to extract
results from the external PC to my local PC such that studies can be made.

Conclusion
This chapter will conclude upon the discoveries and achievements made in the thesis titled
”Transient vibro-acoustics using the discontinuous Galerkin finite element method” and
discuss the possibilities of future work.
In this thesis the discontinuous Galerkin finite element method (DG-FEM) was used as
a mathematical approximation to solve transient physics involving linear acoustics, linear
isotropic elasticity and the coupled vibro-acoustic problem.
In chapter 5, a simple generalized numerical flux formulation was derived for single-physics
and multi-physics, which was independent of the chosen flux-method, to complete the
semi-discrete spacial discretizations in chapter 4. The formulations utilized an x-split of
the numerical flux, such that the coupling conditions could be implemented, making the
formulations a function of a flux-specific 1D coeﬀicient matrix. For the central flux, this
was easily determined directly from the x−directional Jacobian, A. For the upwind flux,
a Riemann problem was considered from which the solution became a function of inde-
pendent advection equations. The solution of each advection equation was a propagation
of the initial state from either the left (considered) or right (neighboring) element. The
upwind flux gave the possibility of dealing with interfaces of varying materials, and al-
lowed for easy implementation of boundary conditions due to its directional features. The
upwind flux was therefore chosen as the method for single-physics couplings while both
flux methods could be used for multi-physics.
In chapter 6, investigations of time-integration schemes was conducted.
It was found
that the low-storage, five-stage, fourth-order explicit Runge Kutta method lead to the
largest stability-region, while needing to save four times less data between time steps,
which made it a well-suited method for time integration. Using a central flux for both
single- and multi-physics showed all imaginary eigenvalues, confirming that the method is
non-dissipative. Contrary to this, the upwind flux had negative real eigenvalues making
it dissipative. A general CFL condition ensured stability for all single-physics simulations
with C = 1. For multi-physics, a numerical experiment was conducted where a subset of
the stable simulations could be categorized by a criteria, while a conservative material-
dependent piecewise expression for C (timestep factor) would ensure stability for other
simulations.
However this was far from perfect, and an idea that one could compute
the largest eigenvalue of the system before doing simulations was discussed.
Finally,
the schemes was found to scale linearly with the number of elements and have good
communication between elements, thus being well-suited for parallel computing.
DG-
FEM is also much more well-suited for periodic boundary conditions, since the distance
between elements does not alter the computational eﬀiciency, like it does for classical FEM.
In chapter 7 implementation of the scheme in code was presented, showcasing the changes
made to the exciting Nunn and Warburton, 2008 framework to allow for complicated
geometries with multiple materials and vibro-acoustic couplings. The implementation of
the generalized flux-formulation scheme was shown in appendix H to be 1:1 with the
derived equations, and a change from global to local variables reduced the computational
effort with a factor of 30.
In chapter 8 the linear acoustics was shown to achieve spectral convergence as expected.
Isotropic linear elasticity was compared against COMSOL and shown to get the same dis-

placement solution, thus validated but not with any convergence rate. The two coupling
methods were validated against COMSOL and shown to be exact in the acoustic domain.
In the elastic domain, the absolute displacements deviated from each other after 25 mil-

Finally in chapter 9 case studies showed the capabilities of the upwind flux DG-FEM. A

10.1
Future work
• To have the two hyperbolic sets of equations separated, instead of in a unified scheme,
one has the possibilities of adding physics to either the acoustic domain or elastic
domain. For the elastic domain, one could work with non-linear stress-strain re-
lationships, anisotropic materials, damping in the material and energy absorption
when interacting with acoustic waves.
• Vibro-acoustic metamaterials are meant to block acoustic waves.
Many of such
have already been made, and it would be interesting to see if DG-FEM is better at
modeling such materials.
• Cut elements can be used as an adaptive method to update an interface region be-
tween for example an acoustic domain or elastic domain. This allows for complicated
modeling with either moving domains or just large deflection which would otherwise
be neglected (Gulizzi & Saye, 2022).
• Topology optimization is still getting increasingly popular, especially for transient
problem where one might want to optimize the acoustic properties of an object. In
combination with cut elements, this could be a very strong tool
• It was hypothesized that the upwind flux was better the central flux at handling
interfaces with a change in single-physics properties, such as air and water. This
would be a nice thing to test.
• In this thesis, it was shown that a standard FEM and DG-FEM not always achieves
the same solution. To investigate the accuracy of problems with harder clamping
condition, it would be interesting to compare results to real-life vibro-acoustic tests.
• Doing a large-scale parallel 3D DG-FEM solver in a language like c++ which allows
for high code eﬀiciency.
• Not all papers use a Runge Kutta time integration scheme. Some papers use what
is called an ADER integration scheme, which can achieve arbitrarily high order
convergence in time. One might find advantages here, such that a higher polynomial
order can be utilized, without timestep reduction (Dumbser & Käser, 2000; Martin
& Dumbser, 2006). Another approach is made by Tavelli and Dumbser, 2018 who
use a staggered grid to reach a higher temporal convergence. This is state-of-the-art
within finite difference, and due to the method being semi-implicit, allows for large
time steps while still being stable.
