Abstract
Cancer is one of the most serious diseases globally. The tumor microenvironment is char-
acterized by highly heterogeneous spatiotemporal chemical gradients. Microﬂuidic methods
oﬀer signiﬁcant potential for generating realistic spatiotemporal chemical gradients, enabling
a deeper understanding in invaluable insights into their impact on cancer progression. This
thesis contributes to the ongoing Microcrad research project, focusing on establishing a mi-
croﬂuidic platform to study chemical gradients in cancer cells, with a focus on pH and oxygen
gradients. The aim is to conduct additional microﬂuidic experiments to explore pH gradi-
ents further and to develop and optimize numerical simulations of oxygen and pH gradients
within the microﬂuidic platform. The latter can be used to assist in the future validation of
the experimental results and the design phase of diﬀerent microﬂuidic platforms.
A microﬂuidic experiment was conducted to establish a pH gradient ranging from pH
6 to 7.4 along with the relevant numerical simulation. Quantitative ﬂuorescence imaging
using a pH-sensitive dye enabled the acquisition of pH values where an additional calibration
experiment was conducted to correlate intensity values with speciﬁc pH values. Investigation
of the oxygen gradient included further development on the current numerical simulation
of the existing microﬂuidic device.
Furthermore, diﬀerent designs of the platform were
considered including reducing the thickness of the microﬂuidic chip and positioning the gas-
supply channels closer to the region of interest by the design of a 2-layer microﬂuidic chip to
achieve a broader oxygen range. Finally, two oxygen inﬂow ranges were mainly examined:
0-21% and 0-10% of oxygen.
The comparison between the experimental results and numerical results in terms of the
pH gradient indicated a notable discrepancy. Speciﬁcally experimental data revealed a pH
gradient ranging from 7.45 to 7.2, while the numerical simulations indicated a range of 6.1 to
6.67. The comparison between the oxygen gradient numerical simulation of the microﬂuidic
device with the relevant experimental results presented a good agreement in the resulting
oxygen range with an approximately higher lower bound of oxygen percentage, by 0.5% in
the case of the numerical simulation. The investigation of the diﬀerent microﬂuidic device
designs indicated that the most crucial factors in the resulting oxygen gradient are the
distance between the gas channels and the area of interest, as well as the total surface area
of the device which is exposed to atmospheric air. In general, the 2-layer design achieved
broader oxygen gradients in the observation area of the device. However, when lower inﬂow
ranges of oxygen were applied the lower bound of the resulting oxygen gradient remained
relatively high for studying severe hypoxia. A device design with a smaller thickness would
be better suited for this case.
Overall further considerations should be taken in the investigation of the pH gradient.
The initial step should involve the recreation of the calibration experiment, where certain
errors were introduced during the procedure. The simulation of oxygen gradient has shown
a good agreement with the experimental results, indicating a potential for predicting oxygen
distribution in diﬀerent conﬁgurations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Cancer is considered the second leading fatal disease in the Western world after cardio-
vascular diseases [1]. Although extensive research has been conducted on cancer types and
genetics, the tumor microenvironment, a crucial component in cancer development, remains
inadequately explored.
The tumor microenvironment presents a highly spatial heteroge-
neous proﬁle and is characterized by restricted blood supply, deprivation of nutrients, and
waste product accumulation [2]. The accumulation of waste products leads to a decrease in
pH across the tumor area, making the environment acidic and generating pH gradients [3].
Additionally due to the limited blood supply, hypoxia, or low levels of oxygen, is observed
in most tumor microenvironments, with the oxygen levels presenting a spatiotemporal pro-
ﬁle across the tumor area [4]. This harsh environment promotes cancer’s progression and
metastasis while it presents increased therapeutic resistance [5][6]. Especially the created
hypoxic environment is reported to increase the malignancy of tumors by the development
of cells with more aggressive forms [7].
It is therefore important to further understand the mechanisms behind tumor microenvi-
ronment and test cancer treatments that could overcome these challenges. Currently, in vivo
studies of the tumor environment are not feasible since it is diﬃcult to control the interac-
tion between the chemical gradients and the cancer cells. At the same time, conventional in
vitro methods are not realistic enough to mimic the tumor microenvironment. Microﬂuidic
methods can be used as pivotal tools for conducting realistic in vitro studies since they can
generate spatiotemporal gradients.
The present Master’s thesis will contribute to the ongoing research of the collaborative
project "Micrograd" between the group of Nanoﬂuidics and Bioimaging from DTU with two
other groups at the University of Copenhagen and BAM in Berlin. The project concerns the
1
==PAGE:18==
1.2. Purpose
establishment of a microﬂuidic platform for the cultivation of cancer cells in the presence of
one or more chemical gradients, with a focus on pH and oxygen gradients. The thesis will
contribute to the further development and improvement of the microﬂuidic platform through
the development and optimization of the numerical simulation of the platform in COMSOL
Multiphysics 6.1 and the conduction of additional microﬂuidic experiments.
1.2
Purpose
The purpose of this master’s thesis is to further develop the numerical simulation models
of the microﬂuidic platform for the pH and oxygen gradients, which are set in COMSOL
Multiphysics 6.1. The numerical simulation will be used to support the validation of the
pH and oxygen gradient experiments in the microﬂuidic platform. Additionally, microﬂuidic
experiments will be performed using the current microﬂuidic device, and the outcomes will
be analyzed through ﬂuorescence imaging using a speciﬁc dye. The end goal of this thesis
is to contribute to the validation of the microﬂuidic platform through numerical simulations
and supplementary microﬂuidic experiments.
Currently, a speciﬁc design of the microﬂuidic device has been fabricated, and the pH
gradient experiment has been successfully conducted. The time-depend simulation models
have been set up already as 2D models for the pH and oxygen gradients. Hence in terms
of the investigation of oxygen gradient in the microﬂuidic platform the aim is to optimize
the simulations and investigate diﬀerent designs of the microﬂuidic device, to achieve a
broader range of oxygen in the area where the cells will be seeded. During the numerical
simulations mainly two inﬂow oxygen ranges will be applied: 0-21% and 0-10%. The ﬁrst
is the currently usable range in the experimental set-up. The relevant numerical results of
the oxygen gradient simulation will be compared with the experimental results, conducted
by Yanfang. The shift to a lower inﬂow range will be applied since oxygen levels within the
tumor microenvironment are usually below 2% [8].
Regarding the investigation of pH gradient, the microﬂuidic experiment of the pH gradient
will be recreated and it will be characterized by ﬂuorescence imaging of the SNARF-1 ﬂuo-
rescence dye, which changes color depending on the pH [9]. The applied pH values will be 6
and 7.4. This range is selected since the exctacellular pH (pHe) in tumors is usually acidic,
while normal tissue typically has a slightly alkaline extracellular pH (pHe) [10]. A diﬀerent
ﬁlter cube will be used in the microscope compared with the initial experiment. A calibration
experiment will then be conducted to correlate the intensity values of the SNARF-1 to pH
values. This will be done through data analysis, by constructing a calibration curve, based
on the results of the calibration experiment. Finally, a comparison of the simulation results
and the experimental results will then be conducted.

Chapter 2
Background
This chapter provides a brief introduction to the fundamental principles utilized through-
out this thesis. Additionally, it provides an overview of the approaches that are employed in
the development of microﬂuidic devices for chemical gradient generation. Finally, it presents
the current microﬂuidic platform developed by Yanfang for the Micrograd project.
2.1
Theories and relationships
2.1.1
Physics of microﬂuidics
Microﬂuidics is the ﬁeld of study and technology that manipulates small volumes of ﬂuids
ranging from 10−6 to 10−18 by employing channels with dimensions typically in the range
of tens to hundreds of micrometers [11]. Due to their beneﬁts such as the reduced amount
of required samples, reduced cost, reduced footprint, high resolution and sensitivity, and
increased reliability, this ﬁeld has quickly become a revolutionary technology with widespread
applications across various disciplines, including biology, chemistry, information technology,
and optics [12][11].
In general, fundamental ﬂuid mechanisms and governing equations applicable to bulk
ﬂuid motion can also be applied in microﬂuidics. However, as the scale decreases, viscous
forces become more inﬂuential in microﬂuidics, unlike macro-scale scenarios where inertial
forces typically dominate ﬂow behavior [11].
This results in low Reynolds numbers and
a prevalence of laminar ﬂow.
Consequently, the primary mode of mass transport shifts
from convection to diﬀusion. A characteristic of laminar ﬂow that is greatly employed by
microﬂuidic applications is that two liquids can ﬂow next to each other and mixing is only
achieved by diﬀusion unless special structures are incorporated.

2.1. Theories and relationships
Below basic relationships that are applied to microﬂuidics and are utilized in the numerical
models in COMSOL Multiphysics are presented.
Gases
Gases ﬂowing within microchannels are typically assumed to behave according to the ideal
gas law [13]:
PV = nRT
(2.1)
Where P and T are the absolute pressure and temperature of the gas respectively, V is the
volume of gas, n is the number of mols of the gas and R is the ideal gas constant.
Henry’s gas law was utilized during the numerical modeling of oxygen gradient simulation
in COMSOL Multiphysics. The law states that at a constant temperature, the amount of

Fluorescence microscopy
The microﬂuidic experiments outlined in the thesis were analysed using ﬂuorescence mi-

The ﬂuorescence dye that was utilized was the Carboxy
SNARF-1, which changes color with pH [9]. A brief introduction regarding the fundamental
concepts behind ﬂuorescence microscopy is provided below.
Fluorescence microscopy is based on the principle concept of ﬂuorescence, where ﬂuores-
cent molecules (ﬂuorophores) absorb photons in a speciﬁc wavelength range and emit photons
with lower energy, at wavelengths that are longer than those of the absorbed photons [18].
This process is illustrated by the Jablonski diagram in Figure 2.1a, where it can be seen that
as the electrons in the ﬂuorescent molecules return to their ground state after the excitation,
the energy of emission is lower than that of absorption. This diﬀerence between the exciting
and emitted wavelength is known as the Stokes shift (Figure 2.1) and it outlines the great
advantage of ﬂuorescence in providing high contrast and sensitivity [19]. This feature is ap-
plied during ﬂuorescence microscopy by using speciﬁc ﬁlter cubes that obstruct the reﬂected
excitation light while enabling only the emitted ﬂuorescence to proceed through.
Figure 2.1: a)Simpliﬁed Jablonski diagram for ﬂuorescence. b)Illustration of stokes shift
between absorption and emission wavelengths.
The Motorized Nikon Ti2-E microscope conducts ﬂuorescence microscopy based on the
epiﬂuorescence conﬁguration. In this setup, the sample is illuminated with excitation light
from above, and the resulting ﬂuorescence is gathered by the same objective lens used for
directing the excitation light. This conﬁguration has the beneﬁt of directing the majority
of the excitation light away from the detector [18]. Therefore the sensitivity of accurately
capturing the emitted ﬂuorescence is enhanced. The conﬁguration of a typical ﬁlter cube
for epiﬂuorescence microscopy is depicted in ﬁgure 2.2 [18]. It is composed of an excitation

2.2
State of the art
As mentioned previously, employing microﬂuidics provides a signiﬁcant advantage in ac-
curately mimicking spatiotemporal gradients, a procedure that is proven to be extremely
challenging in conventional in vitro experimental setups.
Currently, microﬂuidic devices
for gradient generation can be classiﬁed into two main categories based on their leading
mechanism.
The ﬁrst category exploits the laminar ﬂow in microﬂuidic channels and the formulation
of the concentration gradient is based on controlled diﬀusive mixing through diﬀusion and
ﬂow. Tree-shape networks and Y-shape device designs are alternatives to this category [20].
Figures 2.4a and 2.4b provide an illustration of tree-shape networks and Y-shape designs
respectively [20]. Speciﬁcally, the tree-shape gradient generation microﬂuidic devices are
one of the earliest developed systems [20] and have been widely employed by many studies
[21][22][23][24]. The tree-shape microﬂuidic devices are consisted by multiple channels which
are split and eventually merged. The main advantage is their simple design and their ability
to formulate controllable and stable concentration gradients in a relatively short period
[25][20]. Although this design requires a large surface to be designed, due to the multiple
mixing channels, and confronts issues with ﬂow blocking and leakage.
Additionally, the
exposure of cells to ﬂow and shear stresses can cause damage or induce undesirable responses
10
==PAGE:27==
2.2. State of the art
from cells [20][26]. To alleviate shear stresses, cells have been embedded in hydrogels [2] or
additional diﬀusion channels have been included between mixing channels and chamber area
[27]. In another study [24] bubble trappers were included upstream of the gradient chamber
to address the issue of bubbles, which can signiﬁcantly increase shear stresses and aﬀect the
reliability of the gradient. The Y-shape design is based on two channels which eventually
lead to one converging channel [28]. It does not include the disadvantage of ﬂow blocking
and leakage and the need for a large surface but still, it introduces shear stresses and it
highly depends on the ﬂow rate and the diﬀusion coeﬃcient of molecules [20].
The second category is solely based on diﬀusion, where a porous membrane is used between
the ﬂow stream and the gradient formulation area, ensuring only speciﬁc molecules are
allowed to diﬀuse (ﬁgure 2.4c).
This approach alleviates the previously reported issues.
The main disadvantage is the long time that is required to generate a stable gradient. [20].
Additionally, the gradient proﬁle is inﬂuenced by the thickness of the membrane, as the
diﬀusion rate decreases due to resistance, leading to a reduction in concentration variance
within the gradient area [29]. Commonly used materials for the porous membrane include
PDMS, nitrocellulose, hydrogels, polyesters, and PEGDA [20]. Speciﬁcally, PDMS has been
extensively used in the fabrication of microﬂuidic devices due to its excellent gas permeability,
optical transparency, cell compatibility, ﬂexibility in fabrication, and chemical stability [30].
A common conﬁguration in this category is the use of a single-layer device with a central
gradient chamber and channels that supply the speciﬁc molecules that are studied are placed
at each side of the gradient chamber. Chang et al.[31] have developed a PDMS microﬂuidic
device for oxygen gradient generation where the gradient chamber was ﬁlled with cells ﬂoating
in medium and at the sides of the gradient chamber two gas channels were included, one
was supplying the chamber with air and the other with pure nitrogen. However, placing the
gas channels so close to cells can induce evaporation and formation of bubbles in the cell
culture chamber. To alleviate this other studies have also included liquid channels between
the gradient chamber and the pressured-gas supply channels [4][32]. Other studies have also
included multiple layers in the microﬂuidic device and the supply channels were placed in
a diﬀerent layer than the cell culture or gradient channel [33]. Additionally, cells are often
embedded in hydrogels which further provide structural support and mimic the physiological
environment of cells [4][32].

2.3
Design of the microﬂuidic device
The research of Nanoﬂuidics and Bioimaging from DTU is responsible for the fabrication
and characterization of the microﬂuidic platform in the Micrograd research project. One
of the primary design objectives, in addition to ensuring the generation of stable chemical
gradients, is to enable the retrieval of cells after exposure to the gradient proﬁle. This aspect
is crucial because most microﬂuidic platforms are sealed, making it challenging to eﬀectively
study the eﬀects of gradients on biological processes such as gene expression. To address
this concern, a reversible sealing microﬂuidic device was developed. The original design of
the microﬂuidic device was a tree-shaped network, made by PDMS, with integrated bubble
traps. Additionally, a collagen-based hydrogel was incorporated into the gradient area, where
cancer cells were seeded. As pH gradient studies are currently less explored compared to
other chemical gradients, the initial emphasis was on developing a pH gradient along the cell
area. However, the microﬂuidic platform can also be employed for generating other chemical
gradients. Speciﬁcally, investigations into oxygen gradients will also be conducted.
Due to the high dependency on the ﬂow rate, and the shearing eﬀect which leads to
the disruption of cells, it was decided to replace the current design with a diﬀusion-based
microﬂuidic device. The new microﬂuidic device was made by PDMS as previously. Brieﬂy,
the 4 mm thick PDMS chip was manufactured using the SU-8 mold process (PDMS soft
lithography) [34] in the cleanroom, allowing high control over the dimensions. The 5x5 mm
gradient area is designed in the center of the PDMS chip, to match the dimensions of the
Visium spatial transcriptomic slide, where the cells will be seeded. To prevent collapse during
assembly, an array of pillars with a diameter of 200 µm was incorporated into the gradient
observation channel. The 100 µm wide liquid channels are oriented horizontally along the
length of the device, one on each side of the gradient area, and ﬂuidic connections to the
channels were made by punching holes through the PDMS. Two pH solutions, 6 and 7,4,
are supplied, one in each liquid channel for the generation of the pH gradient. Additionally,
two 500 µm wide gas channels were designed vertically along the device’s length, one on
each side of the gradient area, and gas connections to the channels were made again by
punching holes through the PDMS. For the generation of the oxygen gradient, one gas
channel is supplied with a high percentage of oxygen, while the other gas channel receives
gas with zero percentage of oxygen. The microchannels have a thickness of 150 µm. Figures
2.5a and 2.5b illustrate the fabricated microﬂuidic chip. Speciﬁcally ﬁgure 2.5a provides
an enlarged view of the PDMS area where the microchannels and gradient area are placed.
This conﬁguration has the advantage of allowing the investigation of both pH and oxygen
gradients.
To assemble the reversible sealing microﬂuidic device, the process begins by placing a
PMMA holder, which was previously manufactured in the lab, beneath the PDMS chip.
On top of the PDMS chip, precisely over the gradient area, a 150 µm thick collagen-based
hydrogel is positioned. This hydrogel is prepared on a microscope glass slide, with the length
and width of the PDMS chip cut to match the dimensions of the glass slide (76x26 mm). A

This section provides a description of the setup procedure for the pH and oxygen gradient

3.1.1
Selected physics
The mechanisms for the establishment of both pH and oxygen gradients are diﬀusion
and convection. H+ ions and oxygen molecules diﬀuse due to the concentration diﬀerence
while new H+ ions and oxygen molecules are transported through the movement of a liquid
and/or gas. Therefore from the available physics in COMSOL Multiphysics, the Transport
of dilutes species along with a laminar ﬂow ﬁeld is chosen for each case.
The Reacting
Flow, Diluted Species feature was used to couple the two physics, where the velocity in the
Transport of Diluted Species is synchronized to the one computed in the laminar ﬂow. In
the merged simulation, two branches of Transport of diluted species are coupled with one
laminar ﬂow. The dependent variable for the transport of the diluted species is the molar
concentration. In this speciﬁc case, the concentration of H+ ions and oxygen molecules in
each solvent/material is taken into account.
The equation utilized in the Transport of dilutes species multiphysics is the convection-
diﬀusion equation 2.7, with zero reaction term, and it is coupled with the incompressible
form of Navier-Stokes equations 2.9 and 2.10 in the laminar ﬂow. It must be noted that
both liquid and gas ﬂow was treated as incompressible ﬂow. In the case of the gas ﬂow, this
assumption was taken since the Mach number, the ratio between the velocity of the ﬂuid
and the velocity of sound, was far below 0.3 and under this condition, it is possible to take
the assumption of incompressible ﬂow [35].

3.1.5
Mesh and study set up
The mesh of the geometry was based on the user-control mesh option in COMSOL Mul-
tiphysics. A free tetrahedral mesh optimized for ﬂuid dynamics was generated for all cases
and the number of elements was adjusted. In the areas of the gradient area, hydrogel, and
liquid and gas channels a ﬁner mesh was generated to ensure high precision in these areas.
To ensure mesh independence in the simulated results, the number of elements around the
area of interest was increased until no further changes were observed in the gradient area.
The lowest number of mesh elements, at which the results remained unchanged, was selected
to optimize computational time.
As the phenomenon of gradient creation is time-dependent and the goal is to investigate
it during its formation, a time-dependent study is chosen for all cases. The total duration
was initially set to 1200 minutes to ensure that the steady state would have been reached.

Oxygen plasma treatment of the PDMS chip
Before conducting the experiment it is necessary to impose oxygen plasma treatment on
the PDMS chip. PDMS is a hydrophobic material due to its methyl (-CH3) groups on the
surface [42]. This is an issue during the assembly of the device since liquid is placed on the
PDMS surface, and if the surface remains hydrophobic the liquid will not eﬃciently spread.
Through plasma treatment, polar silanol groups (SiOH) are generated by the interaction of
oxygen plasma with silicon-containing groups on the PDMS surface. This transformation
alters the surface properties of PDMS, converting it from hydrophobic to hydrophilic.[42].

3.2. Experimental study
3.2.3
Calibration experiment
To quantify the intensity values of the pH gradient experiment it is necessary to conduct a
calibration experiment. Through the calibration experiment, a calibration curve will occur,
establishing a correlation between the recorded intensity values and the corresponding pH
values.
For the calibration experiment, four pH solutions mixed with the relevant volume of
SNARF-1 ﬂuorescence dye were prepared. Speciﬁcally pH 5.95, pH 6.52, pH 7.12 and pH
7.43. Similarly to the pH gradient experiment, the hydrogel was prepared the previous day
and was soaked in the pH 7.43 solution, mixed with SNARF-1, overnight. The assembly
of the device follows also the same procedure as previously. The main diﬀerence was that
initially both of the inlets were supplied with the pH 7.43 solution. The device was then
placed in the microscope, and the same parameters as previously were set. The goal was to
obtain the intensity value that corresponds to pH 7.43. Therefore, after waiting 30 min an
image was taken. After visualizing the image in ImageJ it was checked whether the intensity
proﬁle along a drawn line was almost the same along the length of the gradient area. The
line was drawn at various heights along the gradient to ensure that the distribution was
uniform along the vertical direction.
Initially, the intensity values presented small variations, and therefore the experiment
continued to run with the pH 7.43 solution and an image was taken again after 30 min. This
iterative process continued until the intensity values were almost the same along the length
of the gradient area. Once uniform intensity was observed across the gradient area, the pH
7.43 solutions in the two supply tubes were replaced with the pH 7.12 solutions. The same
procedure was conducted and when the intensity values stabilized in a new value the p.H
7.12 solutions were replaced with the pH 6.52 solutions. Then the experiment continued
until the intensity values stabilized once again. Finally, the pH 6.52 solutions were replaced
with pH 5.95 solutions, and the experimental procedure concluded when the intensity proﬁle
along the length of the gradient area became nearly identical for the pH 5.95 solution.
3.2.4
Data post-processing and visualization
After acquiring the intensity proﬁle for each pH solution from the calibration experiment,
the calibration curve must be constructed through data analysis. The curve will serve as
a tool for correlating the intensity values with their corresponding pH levels, providing a
systematic method of translating intensity measurements into accurate pH values.
Initially, through imageJ, the four images that correspond to pH 5.95, 6.52, 7.12, and 7.43
were merged into one image stack. Then a horizontal line was drawn along the gradient area.
By making the images as a stack, this line was transferred in all the images simultaneously.
The intensity values along the drawn line were then plotted against the length of the gradient
39
==PAGE:56==
3.2. Experimental study
area. The intensity values from each image were extracted and imported into MATLAB for
further analysis. It’s important to mention that the line was drawn in a position with fewer
defects in the hydrogel and less noise in the image. However, various positions along the
height of the gradient area were visually checked to ensure that the intensity values in each
image were similar, regardless of the height of the gradient area.
In MATLAB, the mean intensity value was computed for each of the four datasets. The
intensity values that presented a big variation compared to the others were excluded from
each corresponding data set since they were referring to hydrogel’s discrepancies or noise.
This mean intensity value is therefore assigned to the relevant pH value.
Based on the calculated mean intensity values and their corresponding pH values a ﬁt curve

is further described in the results section.

Chapter 5
Discussion
In this chapter, the obtained results from the simulations and experiments are further
examined and compared. Additionally, the limitations and possible future improvements of
the methods employed in this study are outlined.
5.1
pH gradient
The pH gradient investigation included the generation of a pH gradient simulation along
with the performance of a microﬂuidic experiment for generating a pH gradient in the fabri-
cated microﬂuidic device of 4mm thickness. The result from the numerical simulation of the
pH gradient indicates a resulting pH range in the gradient area of the device between 6.1
to 6.67, with a time for reaching the steady state to be approximately 50 minutes. On the
other hand, the experimental results from the pH gradient experiment indicate a pH range
between approximately 7.45 to 7.2. The gradient was almost constant by 80 minutes of the
experiment, but small variations were observed until 100 minutes. Therefore a relevant large
diﬀerence is observed between the experimental and numerical results. Speciﬁcally, the nu-
merical results indicate a shift towards the pH 6 value while the experimental results indicate
the opposite. Additionally, the time for reaching the steady state is also approximately 50%
longer in the experimental results. To investigate this diﬀerence the diﬀusion coeﬃcient in
the hydrogel was changed to lower values compared to the gradient area which was assumed
to possess water properties. Despite this change, the pH gradient was almost unchangeable,
and only the time for steady state was prolonged in the case of unrealistic large values.
There is no obvious reason for this relatively big diﬀerence between the experimental and
numerical values of the pH gradient but several possibilities will be discussed. Regarding
the simulation of the pH gradient system, as has already been mentioned, only the geometry
of liquid channels, hydrogel, and gradient area have been included in the simulation. It
was assumed that H+ ions do not diﬀuse through the PDMS chip and therefore it was not
included in the simulation. In reality though, since the liquid ﬂow applied in the experimental

procedure is relatively slow even though the PDMS is neutrally charged, the permeability of
PDMS in small molecules could potentially cause the diﬀusion of H+ through its structure.
This can result in a smaller concentration of H+ ions in the gradient area and therefore to
higher pH values.
In terms of the experimental results, initially, it must be noted that the experimental
results of Yanfang regarding the pH experiment indicate approximately the same pH range
along the gradient area, despite the use of a diﬀerent microscope and ﬁlter cube. Although
this is a positive indication of the validity of the present experimental results, during the
experimental procedure and the interpretation of SNARF-1 ﬂuorescence dye some errors
were induced. One of the most crucial concerns and limitations during the experimental
procedure was that the relevant intensity proﬁle of 7.4 pH was from a calibration experiment
conducted the previous day. Due to closely matched intensity proﬁles with the relevant pH
experiment, and the ease of introducing bubbles to the system during the exchange of pH
solutions (which was the reason for the failure of the ﬁrst calibration experiment), I proceeded
with the calibration experiment using the next pH solutions under identical experimental
conditions as before. Therefore inevitably I induced an error in the system and if more
time was available the calibration experiment must have been recreated. Additionally, even
though I have left the pH solutions out of the fridge to reach room temperature in both the
pH gradient and calibration experiment, the time that the solutions were left out of the fridge
was not precisely measured in both cases. Especially during the calibration experiment, since
all the pH solutions were prepared simultaneously and were out of the fridge at the same
time, the lower pH solutions were left longer time without being used outside of the fridge.
On the contrary in the pH gradient experiment the pH 6 solution was used at the same time
as the pH 7.4 solution. Furthermore, the particular carboxy SNARF-1 dye that was used
for the characterization of pH value is particularly sensitive in the detection of pH between
7 to 8 [44]. Therefore in lower pH solutions, the ﬂuorescence intensity did not present a big
diﬀerence. Thus, there might be an overestimation of the pH values in the gradient area for
the lower pH solutions, which subsequently leads to a swift toward higher pH values in the
lower bound of the pH gradient. Finally, for more accurate quantiﬁcation of pH values using
the SNARF-1 ﬂuorescence dye, ideally, the ratio of ﬂuorescence between the two emission
wavelengths should be used. However, in our case, only the TRICT ﬁlter was employed,
which covers an area from both emission wavelengths.
Thus a future improvement, since there is no available time during this thesis, is initially
the recreation of the calibration experiment considering the above observations, with ideally
more pH solutions as reference intensities since currently, I used only four datasets of intensity
values-pH values. Additionally, apart from ensuring that the parameters in the microscope
are identical between the calibration and pH experiment, environmental parameters, such
as temperature should also be considered since pH is temperature-depended. Finally, the
validity of the pH gradient experiment could be further examined by using the ratio of
ﬂuorescence intensities for the determination of the pH values, since it provides more accurate
results.

simulation still does not match, the possibility of including the diﬀusion of H+ through the

5.2
Oxygen gradient
Regarding the oxygen gradient in the microﬂuidic platform, the thesis focused on opti-
mizing the current numerical simulations and investigating diﬀerent designs for achieving a
broader gradient in the observation area of the microﬂuidic platform.
The simulation results of the already-existing 1-layer microﬂuidic device of 4mm thick-
ness indicate an oxygen range between 7.88% to 16% in the gradient area when the inﬂow
boundary condition of oxygen was between 0% to 21%. It was anticipated that this design
would not fully reach the boundaries of the inﬂow of oxygen concentration due to the gap
between the gas channels and the gradient area. However, the initial 2D simulations have
indicated a much lower loss in the gap between the gas channels and the gradient area, as the
total length, width, and thickness of the PDMS chip were not included in the simulations.
Additionally, the air boundary in the side boundaries of PDMS was also not considered.
Therefore, this result highlights the inﬂuence of the distance between the gas channels and
the gradient area on the three-dimensional diﬀusion of oxygen through the PDMS. Further-
more, the higher diﬀerence between the lower bound of the gradient, aiming for 0% oxygen,
with the higher bound, aiming for 21% oxygen, outlines the eﬀect of the PDMS’s exposure
to the surrounding air on its side borders.
Speciﬁcally, less than 50% of the initial inﬂow oxygen range reached the gradient area.
To alleviate this the microﬂuidic device was simulated with a thickness of 2 mm instead of
4 mm. The results indicate an oxygen gradient between 5.6% to 15.6% and therefore an
increase of approximately 23%. However, this diﬀerence is solely concentrated in the lower
bound of the gradient. This indicates that the stronger contributor to this change is the
available exposed PDMS surface to air. In the case of the device of 4 mm thickness, even
though the oxygen diﬀuses in a larger volume of PDMS, the double exposed side surfaces
to air compensate this eﬀect, resulting in a slightly higher upper bound compared with
the device of 2 mm thickness. On the contrary in the 2 mm thickness device, the smaller
exposed surfaces, restrict the diﬀusion of the air, and the supply of the 0% gas was more
eﬃcient. A diﬀerent set of simulations was also conducted with an inﬂow boundary condition
of oxygen between 0% to 10%. This change was applied since the biologist collaborators of
the Micrograd project highlighted the importance of this range for studying the impact of
low oxygen in cancer cells. Especially the lower bound of the range is particularly important
for studying severe hypoxia in cells since most of tumors present oxygen levels below 2%
[8]. The results from these simulations indicate an oxygen range from 5.61% to 9.48% and
3.35% to 8.12% for the 4 mm and 2 mm thick microﬂuidic device respectively. In the case
of the device of 2 mm thickness even though this range is closer to the desired range, ideally
it should be broader, especially in the lower bound, for the conduction of a more eﬃcient
study of hypoxia in cells.
Regarding the time required for the gradient proﬁle to reach
60
==PAGE:77==
5.2. Oxygen gradient
steady state, the device with a thickness of 2 mm reached steady state in approximately 8
hours, whereas the device with a thickness of 4 mm reached steady state in approximately
12 hours. Therefore, the design with a thickness of 2 mm outperforms in this aspect, which
is attributed to the lower volume over which oxygen diﬀuses and the more limited access of
the surrounding air to the system.
In order to reach a broader oxygen gradient compared to the above, a diﬀerent design of
the microﬂuidic platform was investigated, where the gas channels were placed in a diﬀerent
PDMS layer, and were positioned closer to the gradient area. Each PDMS had a thickness
of 2 mm and therefore the whole device was 4 mm thick. By this conﬁguration, the gas
channels are closer to the gradient while ensuring no disturbance to the hydrogel.
The
simulation results of the 2-layer device indicate an oxygen range in the gradient area from
5.24% to 17.2% and from 3.52% to 9.28% for the inﬂow oxygen boundary condition of
0% to 21% and 0% to 10% respectively. Therefore an improvement in the obtained range
by 48% and 20% was achieved compared with the 4mm and 2mm thickness 1-layer device
respectively. Despite the broader oxygen ranges achieved by the 2-layer device, it is observed
that the lower bound of the oxygen gradient has not signiﬁcantly changed compared to the
1-layer device of 2 mm thickness. Speciﬁcally, the 20% increase in the oxygen range is mainly
based on the upper bound of the range. Especially in the case where the inﬂow of oxygen is
between 0 to 10%, the lower bound of the 2 mm, 1-layer device is slightly lower. Therefore
it can be concluded, that even though the gas channels are positioned closer to the gradient
area, the inﬂuence of the surrounding air is greater in the case of the 2-layer device due to
the total thickness being 4 mm. This can explain the minimal diﬀerence in the lower bound
of the oxygen gradient between the 2-layer device and the 1-layer device with a thickness
of 2 mm. On the contrary, in the case of the 1-layer device with a thickness of 4 mm, the
most signiﬁcant eﬀect of switching to the 2-layer device was observed in the lower bound of
the oxygen gradient. This is because, in this case, both devices have a thickness of 4 mm.
Therefore the eﬀect of the surrounding air is the same, and the change of placing the gas
channels closer to the gradient presents a clear eﬀect in this case.
The additional set of simulations that were conducted where the upper limit of the inﬂow
oxygen boundary condition varied between 9,7,6,5 and 4% indicated that the lowest per-
centage of oxygen that was achieved in the gradient area was 2.5% by the inﬂow boundary
condition of 0-4% oxygen. Nevertheless, in the case of the inﬂow boundary condition of
0-10% oxygen, the lower bound was approximately 3.5% and therefore it was observed that
lower oxygen levels were diﬃcult to reach even though the inﬂow of oxygen was greatly de-
creased. This is attributed to the 2 mm vertical distance between the gas channels and the
gradient area, which inevitably aﬀects the resulting oxygen gradient, and most importantly
in the surrounding air, which diﬀuses through the side PDMS boundaries. Speciﬁcally due
to this eﬀect it was observed that in all the cases the upper bound was almost the same or
even above the relevant upper bound of inﬂow oxygen percentage. It must be also noted that
in a diﬀerent set of simulations that were previously conducted, where the surrounding air
was not considered and the total length and width of PDMS were restricted in the area that
surrounds the microchannels, the resulting oxygen gradient for the 0-10% oxygen inﬂow was

between 1.7% to 8.1 % compared with the current 3.5% to 9.3% which further highlights the
eﬀect of the air boundaries in the resulting gradient. This set of simulations can be found

In terms of the time required to reach steady state, the 2-layer device reached steady
state at approximately the same time as the 1-layer device of 2 mm thickness(8 hours) and
therefore faster than the 1-layer device of 4 mm thickness (12 hours). This can be attributed
to the total lower distance between the gas channels and the gradient area, which contributes
to a faster equilibrium in the gradient area.
Overall, based on the above results, it can be concluded that both the distance between
the gas channels and the gradient area, as well as the total exposed PDMS surface area to
air, play critical roles in the resulting oxygen gradient range. In case the goal is to have a
broader oxygen range in the gradient area, compared with the relevant inﬂow oxygen range it
can be concluded that the 2-layer device is overall superior. However, in our case, apart from
a broad oxygen range it was crucial to reach low oxygen levels in the gradient area. In this
case, the 2-layer device is not considered ideal due to the larger total surface area exposed
to air. The results of the 2 mm, 1-layer device indicate that although the distance between
the gas channels and the gradient area was larger, in the lower oxygen inﬂow range (0-10%
oxygen) the lower bound of the oxygen was slightly lower than the relevant bound of the
2-layer device, since the eﬀect of the surrounding air was lower due to the smaller exposed
surfaces. Therefore in studies where the goal is to reach very low oxygen levels, it is crucial
to consider the total surface area of the exposed borders. Since it is practically diﬃcult to
construct a PDMS layer with less than 2 mm thickness, the modiﬁcation of the thickness of
the 2-layer device in the numerical model is not possible. Therefore in case oxygen levels
below 2.5% need to be studied an alternative approach would be the conduction of numerical
simulation where the gas channels are slightly closer to the gradient to investigate if the eﬀect
would be important in studying hypoxia with below 2% of oxygen.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This thesis focused on the further development of the numerical simulations of the mi-
croﬂuidic platform, concerning the generation of pH and oxygen gradients in COMSOL
Multiphysics 6.1. Diﬀerent designs of the microﬂuidic platform have been considered in the
numerical simulation of oxygen gradient to investigate conﬁgurations that achieve broader
oxygen gradients compared with the current 1-layer device with a thickness of 4 mm. Addi-
tionally, a microﬂuidic experiment regarding the generation of pH experiment was conducted.
The pH values were acquired through quantitative ﬂuorescence imaging of the SNARF-1 ﬂu-
orescence dye, which is sensitive to pH. Speciﬁcally, an additional calibration experiment
was performed to correlate the intensity values with speciﬁc pH values.
Regarding the investigation of the pH gradient, the numerical and experimental results
present a relatively big discrepancy. Speciﬁcally, the numerical results indicate a pH gra-
dient between 6.1 to 6.67 while the experimental results indicate a pH gradient between
6.45 to 7.2. This diﬀerence suggests that further examination should be conducted in both
numerical simulation and experimental procedures. In terms of the experimental procedure,
future improvements include the recreation of the pH gradient and calibration experiments,
ensuring that all the conditions during the procedures are identical, and by constructing
the calibration curve with more pH values as reference intensities. Additionally, the ratio
of SNARF-1 intensities in two wavelengths could be used, since it is reported that achieves
higher accuracy in the determination of pH. In case by further validating the experimental
results, the numerical results still do not match, a reconsideration regarding the involved
physics of the system should be applied.
Regarding the numerical simulation of the oxygen gradient in the microﬂuidic platform,
overall they presented a satisfactory correlation with the experimental results generated
by Yanfang, with an approximately higher lower bound of oxygen percentage, by 0.5%.
This result is a positive indication that the present simulated model can be further used to
assist in the prediction of oxygen gradient in diﬀerent designs of the microﬂuidic platform,
after further optimisation is conducted. In terms of the diﬀerent designs of the microﬂuidic
platform that were investigated, it was proved that the most important factors in the range
65
==PAGE:82==
of the resulting oxygen gradient in the observation area of the device were the distance
between the gas channels and the total exposed surface area of PDMS to atmospheric air.
The latter was proven to have an important eﬀect when lower inﬂow ranges of oxygen were
examined. Speciﬁcally for the diﬀerent designs that were examined overall the 2-layer device
design, achieved the broader oxygen ranges. However when lower inﬂow ranges of oxygen
were applied the lower bound of the resulting oxygen gradient remained relatively high,
in case severe hypoxia aimed to be studied. In the design of the 1-layer device of 2 mm
thickness, despite the larger distance between the gas channels and the gradient area, the
lower bound of the resulting oxygen was slightly lower. Thus, a microﬂuidic device with a
smaller thickness and a relatively shorter distance between the gradient area and the gas
channels, to an extent where the assembly of the device is doable, could be considered as a
future improvement for studies of severe hypoxia.

According to the protocol, the speciﬁed quantity for the 10X PBS is 20 µL, and for the
NaOH is 4.5 µL. The amount of NaOH is calculated to achieve a ﬁnal pH of the hydrogel
within the basic range, speciﬁcally between 7.2 and 7.5.
Based on the above quantities, and considering the desired ﬁnal volume of the solution is

Protocol’s procedure
1. Place in a box ﬁlled with ice the listed reagents, the PureTM 200G Pipet Tips, Molec-
ular BioProducts, 200 µl, and a pre-cold Eppendorf tube for mixing the reagents.
2. Add the needed amount (as calculated above, in the methods section) of 10x PBS, 1N

NaOH, and Milli Q water to the pre-cold Eppendorf tub, and mix thoroughly with a
pipette.
3. Use the PureTM 200G Pipet tips to pipette the stocking collagen solution (Collagen I
High concentration, Rat tail, 100 mg) and add slowly to the above mixed solution. It
is crucial to remove the collagen solution from the pipette very slowly to prevent the
solution from sticking to the inner walls of the pipette tip.
4. Vortex the solution for 15 s. Put the tube back on the ice to cool down again, and
then vortex for another 15 s. Repeat this procedure three times in total to ensure an
eﬃcient mixing of the reagents has been achieved.
5. Centrifuge the mixed collagen solution for approximately 15 s to remove the bubbles
that are generated during the vortex process. Use an identical pre-cold Eppendorf
tube ﬁlled with PBS or water for the certiﬁcation process. In case the bubbles are not
removed after 15 s, return the collagen solution to ice and repeat the process after it
has cooled down.
6. Take 10 µL prepared collagen solution from three diﬀerent positions in the tube (top,
middle, and bottom) and check the pH with the pH stripe indicator to see if the
solution is homogeneous. The pH should be the same in all three positions in a value
between 7.2 to 7.5.
7. Add 40 µL of the collagen solution to the prepared Pll-coated glass slide with the cut
frame and cover it with the FEP ﬁlm. Add a normal glass slide on top. Press gently
the borders of the top glass slide to make the FEP ﬁlm ﬂat. This will result in a
homogeneous hydrogel in terms of thickness.
8. Put the above structure into the oven at 37-42 °C for one hour.
9. After one hour remove it out of the oven and observe the collagen-based hydrogel ﬁlm,
which should be white.
10. Remove the top glass slide and FEP ﬁlm gently and use a tweezer to remove the
remained collagen solution from the borders of the hydrogel.
11. Soak the prepared collagen-based hydrogel ﬁlm into a 4-well plate. Add 4 ml of the
prepared 7.4 pH solution, which is already mixed with 8 µL SNARF, and completely
cover it with aluminum foil to avoid bleaching of the dye.
12. Leave the hydrogel overnight and use it the next day, for the pH gradient experiment.
Note: If it is not feasible to conduct the experiment on the following day, soak the hydrogel
in PBS to preserve it. The day before the experiment, soak it in the relevant solution with
the ﬂuorescence dye.

A.2
Assembly of the microﬂuidic device
The steps for assembling the microﬂuidic device are listed below:
1. Open the pressure-based ﬂow controllers and connect them with the cable that supplies
the air, which is available in the lab.
2. Connect each pressure-based ﬂow controller with one of the tubes where the low and
high pH solution will be placed, through the the black cable which is already connected
to the ﬂow controllers.
3. Connect each ﬂow sensor with one of the tubes where the low and high pH solution
will be placed through the connecting tubings.
4. Finally, connect the ﬂow sensors with the pressure-based ﬂow controllers.
5. Place the holder on top of the black plastic plate, for better view.
6. Screw the ending of the connecting tubes (Of supply tubes) to the corresponding inlets
of the holder.
7. Screw the ending of the outlet connecting tubes
8. Make three holes with the tweezer in the disposal tube. Place the outlet connecting
tubes in the two holes. The third one allows air to escape.
9. Place the 4 o-rings in the inlets and outlets ending of the holder.
10. Transfer the prepared 5ml high and low pH solutions (Mixed with the SNARF) in
the tubes. Aluminum foil should constantly cover the tubes to avoid bleaching of the
SNARF.
11. Before placing the PDMS chip above the holder, impose a ﬂow of around 10 µL/min
and wait until the liquid reaches the surface of the holder, to avoid having trapped air
later in the device.
12. Place the surface-treated PDMS chip above the holder. Align the inlet and outlets of
the PDMS chip with the corresponding o-rings on the surface of the holder.
13. Impose again a ﬂow of around 10 µL/min and wait until the liquid reaches the top
surface of the PDMS chip.
14. After the above is veriﬁed, take the well plate with the hydrogel, and from the mixture
of the pH 7.4 solution and SNARF that it is soaked take around 1 ml and spread it to
the top surface of the PDMS chip. This will help to protect the hydrogel when it will
be placed.

15. Carefully take the hydrogel layer and place it slowly onto the PDMS surface to prevent
any trapped air between the hydrogel layer and the PDMS chip. If the hydrogel is not
correctly aligned with the gradient area of the PDMS chip, use the tweezers to gently
move the glass slide until the hydrogel and the gradient are aligned.
16. Place carefully the metal frame above the hydrogel area. Take the regular screwdriver

solutions from the pressure-based ﬂow controllers.

Fluorescence Imaging of the pH gradient
The protocol below outlines the necessary steps for utilizing the Motorized Nikon Ti2 mi-
croscope to conduct ﬂuorescence imaging of the pH gradient experiment.
1. Open the microscope and then the camera. After the camera stops blinking open the
computer, and the relevant software of the microscope (Micromanager).
2. Initially, the focus must be found. Turn on the LED, remain in ocular mode, activate
ED1, and push the ND ﬁlter.
3. From the micromanager choose 4x magniﬁcation, BF (bright ﬁlter) as the ﬁlter cube
and Green LED 1%.
4. Move the sample to the lowest position and the ﬁnd focus.
5. After the focus has been found close the ED1, push back the ND ﬁlter, and set the
speciﬁc settings for the experiment. Choose the TRICT ﬁlter cube, which is suitable
for the SNARF ﬂuorescence dye, Green LED with 9% intensity, auto shutter, and
exposure time of 100ms.
6. Change to camera mode and press "Live". Correct the focus if needed.

7. Set the ﬂow rate in the micropumps to 3 µL/min and adjust the inlet pressure to 1030
mbar. The pH experiment has now started.
8. It is essential to take a single image at the beginning of the experiment, where the
