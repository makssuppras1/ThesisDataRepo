Abstract
Glioblastoma multiform is the most common and aggressive type of brain tumor, as it
has a highly heterogeneous tumor microenvironment. According to the WHO only 5 % of
patients survives over 5 years after diagnosed. Exhausted and inactive T cells is a hall­
mark of GBM, characterized by upregulation of various inhibitory molecules and immune
checkpoints. As the name implies, glioblastoma is multiform through various biological
mechanisms and origin, therefore, no successful treatment have been found yet. How­
ever, multiple clinical trials are ongoing, one of them being immune checkpoint inhibition
with Nivolumab. This master thesis is part of a clinical trial with Nivolumab and 25 patients
diagnosed with GBM were enrolled. The aim was to analyze immune cells present within
the brain and blood of GBM patients treated with Nivolumab and Bevacizumab. Analysis
was done by staining cells with various molecules, in order to define T cells, Treg cells,
NK cells, and myeloid cells, along with characterize whether they were activated or in­
hibited. The results showed some patients had T cells, NK cell and myeloid cells within
the tumor as well in the blood, along with Nivolumab entering the brain. T cells and NK
cells showed stable frequencies over time in the blood, which indicated a negative effi­
ciency of Nivolumab. Furthermore, T cells showed to upregulate inhibitory markers such
as TIM­3 over time as Nivolumab was administered, along with a high frequencies of PD­1
co­expressed with TIGIT in both blood and tumor. The NK cells showed CD56dim CD16+
and CD56dim CD16­ NK cells dominate in the blood and digest, respectively. Their results
indicated NK cells with cytotoxic functions were activated in the blood, whereas NK cells
in the digest indicated to be exhausted. Myeloid cells was detected in low frequencies in
the tumor, but with significantly higher levels of G­MDSCs, pDCs, M1 macrophages and
M2 macrophages. In conclusion, there indicates to be a difference between circulating
immune cells in the blood and immune cells siting i the tumor. But further investigation
would have to be done to verify functions of these immune cells into depth.

Introduction
Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is one of the most aggressive types of brain tumors with
very low survival and no treatment to remove the tumor cells completely. However, several
clinical trials are ongoing testing various immunotherapies in GBM patients, along with
investigating importance of the immune system and its cells within the tumor.
1.1
The immune system
The immune system is the body’s defense mechanism towards foreign substances. It is
a broad network of molecules and cells working together to detect disease, ranging from
external pathogens to cancer, and distinguish between healthy and infected or cancerous
tissue. The immune system can be divided into two: The innate immune system and the
adaptive immune system [1]. Figure 1.1 shows the immune cells in focus of this project
including their lineage markers.
Figure 1.1: Overview of the immune cells of interest of this project; Innate and adaptive.
For each cell the molecules of identification is presented and used within this project, as
well as sub­populations.
1.1.1
The Innate
The innate immune system is the body’s first­line defense mechanism against non­self
which provides a broad range of protection. The components of the innate immune system
consist of the physical barrier defense (e.g. skin), chemical barriers (e.g. antimicrobial
peptides), humoral immunity (e.g. complement system, inflammatory chemokines), and
a cellular immunity. The cellular immunity consist of myeloid and lymphoid cells. Within
these are monocytes, macrophages, dendritic cells (DCs), and natural killer (NK) cells [2],
which will be described in detail in the following sections.
Page 1
==PAGE:11==
Upon inflammation, the innate immune response is among others activated through the
antigen­presenting cells (APCs) macrophages and DCs. The APCs contain pattern recog­
nition receptors (PRRs) that binds to pathogen­associated molecular patterns (PAMPs)
and damage­associated molecular paterns (DAMPs) on pathogens and dying cells or tis­
sue, respectively. Hereafter, activation of phagocytosis is initiated through engulfing of
the pathogen in order to present it on the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class­II
and further present it to immune cells from the adaptive immune system [1].
1.1.1.1
Monocytes
Monocytes are white blood cells which circulate the blood and can differentiate into monoc­
yte­derived macrophages and DCs, hence very heterogeneous. Three different popula­
tions have been described; Classical monocytes (CD14++, CD16­), Intermediate mono­
cytes (CD14+, CD16+), and Non­classical monocytes (CD14+, CD16++) [3]. 80­95% of
circulating monocytes consist of the classical monocytes, which proves to be extremely
phagocytic [4]. The intermediate monocytes cover 2­8% of circulating monocytes and
have several functions, such as production of reactive oxygen species (ROS). Lastly,
also only 2­11% represents non­classical monocytes of the circulating monocytes which
secretes cytokines [4].
1.1.1.2
Macrophages
Macrophages are remarkable APCs due to their many functions in regard to recogni­
tion and respond to a wide range of receptors and stimuli. They are leukocytes residing
in tissues throughout the body, but they can also be derived from circulating monocytes.
Phagocytosis, endocytosis, and secretion of molecules, such as ROS, are their main func­
tions [5]. Furthermore, macrophages exhibit phenotypic heterogeneity depending on the
tissue they reside in. Macrophages can be defined as CD11b+ CD45+ CD68+ monocytes
[6, 7]. CD11b is expressed on the surface of some APC immune cells and modulates var­
ious biological functions, such as cell adhesion and phagocytosis [8]. CD45, also known
as leukocyte common antigen, is expressed on the surface of all leukocytes and regu­
lates different cellular processes including cell growth and differentiation [9]. CD68 is an
intracellular marker for macrophages and other mononuclear phagocytes, but its function
is poorly investigated [10]. It has been hypothesized to participate in peptide transport
and its over­expression in tumor tissue is correlated with poor prognosis [11].
Macrophages can differentiate into either M1­type or M2­type, being pro­inflammatory or
anti­inflammatory, respectively [12]. M1 macrophages are stimulated by tumor necrosis
factor (TNF)­α and interleukin (IL)­17A and secrete different pro­inflammatory cytokines
(e.g. IL­6, IL­12, TNF­α). These are also referred to as ”fight” macrophages, associ­
ated with good prognosis in the context of cancer. The M2 macrophages are stimulated
by anti­inflammatory cytokines, IL­13 and IL­4, and known as ”fixer” macrophages [13].
This phenotype can repair tissue damage, however, in a cancer context this cell type
support angiogenesis i.e. formation of new blood vessels in the tumor [14] by secreting
vascular epithelial growth factor (VEGF) among others and expressing immunosuppres­
sive molecules, such as programmed death­ligand 1 (PD­L1). In addition to the molecules
described above, the M1 and M2 phenotype can be defined based on the expression of
CD86 and CD163, respectively [13].
As mentioned earlier, macrophages are highly phenotypic heterogenous depending on
the environment in which they reside. Specifically, in the central nervous system (CNS)
the special phenotype of tissue­resident macrophages is called microglia. Microglia acts
as the first­line defence APCs, which is unique for the CNS. In the CNS glial cells are
found, which are specific CNS­cells such as astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, and microglia,
with 5% to 20% in the CNS consists of microglia. Microglia and macrophages share
Page 2
==PAGE:12==
some adhesion molecules, thus making it difficult to differentiate them based on surface
molecules. Upon CNS injury, microglia changes into active APCs with increased motility,
phagocytic activity, and release of cytokines and ROS. In their active form, MHC class­II is
upregulated together with co­stimulatory molecules like CD86 and CD40 [15]. Microglia
cells can be defined as CD11b+ CD45low. Similarities between macrophages from the
peripheral immune system and microglia from the CNS is their ability to both transform
into the M1 or M2 phenotype, hence express CD86 or CD163, respectively [13, 16, 17].
1.1.1.3
Dendritic cells
DCs are considered to be the most efficient APC, due to their ability to effectively activate
naive lymphocytes, along with their phenotypic heterogeneity. In the ”immature” state, be­
fore recognition of antigens (PAMPs, DAMPs, pathogens or cytokines), the expression of
MHC class­II, co­stimulatory molecules, and secretion of pro­inflammatory cytokines are
decreased, thus, making them poor inducers of naive T cells. However, once in contact
with a presented antigen, they become activated ”mature” DCs and migrate to the lymph
node. DCs are highly effective at antigen capturing due to their high endocytic capacity via
receptor­mediated endocytosis [18]. DCs can develop into several phenotypes, two being
myeloid DCs (mDCs) and plasmacytoid DCs (pDCs). Both are first identified by the hu­
man MHC class­II marker HLA­DR+, and secondly can be differentiated into mDCs being
CD11b+ CD33+, whereas pDCs are CD11b­ CD33­[19, 20]. Besides the linage markers
mentioned above, it has been described that pDCs express CD123+, whereas mDCs are
defined as CD11c+, and they both lack expression of CD14 [19–21].
The mDCs originate in the bone marrow from common myeloid progenitor cells. mDCs
capture soluble antigens by phagocytosis, meaning uptake of antibodies through various
receptors (e.g. mannose receptor) [22]. According to a study by Kim et al. (2007), in­
duction of proliferation of naive T cells, antigen­presentation, and expression of MHC and
costimulatory molecules, are greater in mDCs than pDCs.
The pDCs originate from both common myeloid and lymphoid progenitors in the bone
marrow. Immune tolerance is highly controlled by pDCs as they upon inflammation accu­
mulate at the inflammation site to take up antigens. Hereafter, they migrate to the lymph
node where upregulation of MHC class­I and MHC class­II occurs upon maturation. Af­
ter maturation, pDCs can present antigens exogenously to both CD8+ and CD4+ T cells,
and hereby induce immunologic response [20]. Furthermore, another role of pDCs is to
release interferon (IFN)­α, which gives an anti­tumor effect by reducing angiogenesis and
inhibiting proliferation of tumor cells [23].
1.1.1.4
Natural Killer cells
NK cells are granular lymphocytes and is important for the early defense of the innate im­
mune response. Around 10­15% of all lymphocytes are considered to be NK cells, which
are defined as CD56+ CD3­. NK cells are well­known to have cytotoxic functions along
with being able to secrete cytokines, such as IFN­γ, as well as to interact with both innate
and adaptive immune cells. Thus, NK cells are contributing to the immune­surveillance
[24]. They can recognize and detect foreign pathogens through activating and inhibitory
receptors on their surface. A constant interaction with MHC class­I in healthy tissue will
establish tolerance to self molecules, so when change occur, NK cells will become toxic to­
wards stressed cells [25]. Upon stimulation, NK cells becomes activated, and the markers
CD38, CD69, and CD107a can be expressed on the surface. CD38 is an early activa­
tion marker that remains in mature NK cells. Interaction with its ligand CD31 on T cells
results in secretion of various cytokines (e.g. INF­γ, TNF­α). High expression of CD38 in
NK cells indicates an enhanced killing­phenotype [26]. CD69 is also an early activation
marker, however, its expression on surface of NK cells decreases rapidly after 4­6 hours
Page 3
==PAGE:13==
of activation [27]. Binding of CD69 to its ligands triggers activation of extracellular signal­
regulated kinases (ERK) to induce degranulation and cytotoxicity [28]. Lastly, CD107a,
also referred to as lysosome­associated membrane protein­1 (LAMP­1), is a functional
marker for NK cell degranulation, which is the release of granules containing molecules
like perforin and granzymes. As granules are released, CD107a is transported to the
surface of NK cells, hence a marker for activated NK cells mediating degranulation [29,
30].
Five sub­populations of NK cells have been characterized based on the expression of
the adhesion molecule CD56+ and the antibody­dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC)­
mediating FcyRIIA receptor CD16+ [31, 32].
These NK sub­populations are defined:
CD56Bright CD16­, CD56dim CD16­, CD56Bright CD16+, CD56dim CD16+, and CD56­ CD16+
[33]. The CD16­ NK cells produce cytokines, whereas CD16+ NK cells are cytotoxic. Fur­
thermore, CD16+ NK cells have the ability to mediate ADCC by binding to various anti­
bodies on foreign cells, which results in lysis. The CD56dim CD16+ NK cells make up ap­
proximately 90% of total NK cells in the blood, while 10% consist mainly of the CD56Bright
CD16­ and CD56Bright CD16+ NK cells [34]. The remaining populations (CD56dim CD16­
and CD56­ CD16+) are only present in small percentages. However, upon stimulation
with cytokines such as IL­2, the activity of CD16­ NK cells increase and becomes more
cytotoxic [33]. This increase in cytotoxic capacity is caused by upregulation of CD16 in
those subsets being CD16­/low [34].
1.1.1.5
Myeloid­derived suppressor cells
The myeloid­derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) are a heterogenerous group of immature
myeloid cells, such as macrophages, granulocytes, and DCs. Under healthy conditions
immature myeloid cells differentiate into mature immune cells, but under chronic inflam­
mation the immature myeloid cells are prevented from entering differentiation into mature
cells, and instead into MDSCs. MDSCs are characterized by expressing the myeloid dif­
ferentiation markers, CD11b and CD33, but does not express HLA­DR. Furthermore, they
can differentiate into two subsets in humans, mononuclear MDSCs (M­MDSCs) or granu­
locytic MDSCs (G­MDSCs), defined by the expression of CD14+ CD15­ or CD14­ CD15+,
respectively. Through several mechanisms MDSCs can induce immunosuppression and
promote tumor development. One mechanism is their secretion of ROS to suppress T
cells and NK cells. Another is their function to modulate macrophages in brain tumors
towards the M2 phenotype. This occurs through cell­cell contact and decreasing IL­12
production of macrophages together with stimulation of IL­10 and IFN­γ [35].
1.1.2
The Adaptive
The connection from the innate to the adaptive immune system is the APCs. The adaptive
immune system is dependent on antigen­presentation from APCs in order for the adaptive
cells to become activated. Unlike the innate response, the adaptive response is specific
towards foreign cells and provide long lasting memory. The main cells of the adaptive
immune system are the B cells and T cells, which are both lymphocytes [1].
1.1.2.1
T cells
The T cells give the primary immune response via a cell­mediated response. They mature
in the thymus from precursors to naive T cells and migrate to the lymph node to search
for antigens presented by APCs. Naive T cells are divided into subtypes, the helper T
cells and the cytotoxic T cells, defined by expression of the co­receptors for the T cell
receptor (TCR), CD4 and CD8, respectively. When naive T cells have located an APC,
their TCR will interact with the MHC molecule presenting the antigen. Additionally, a
co­stimulatory signal is needed through the binding of CD28 binding CD80/CD86 (B7­1
and B7­2, B7), respectively expressed on the T cell and the APC. This interaction result
Page 4
==PAGE:14==
in release of the cytokine IL­2, which stimulates the T cell to increase proliferation and
differentiation into effector cells [1, 36]. Hereafter, T cells travel through the bloodstream
to the site of inflammation [1]. Migration of effector T cells depends on various integrins
and chemokines, such as CD183 (CXCR3). CXCR3 is a chemokine receptor for the
interferon­inducible chemokines CXCL9/10/11 secreted by APCs. Interaction between
CXCR3 and its ligands guides T cells towards the inflamed tissue [37].
Based on the expression of CCR7 (CD197) and CD45RA, four differentiation stages of T
cells have been characterized; CD45RA+CCR7+ as naive T (TN) cells, CD45RA­CCR7+
as T central memory (TCM) cells, CD45RA­CCR7­ as T effector memory (TEM) cells, and
CD45RA+CCR7­ as T terminally differentiated effector memory (TEMRA) cells. CCR7
is a homing chemokine receptor to the secondary lymphoid organ, hence essential for
lymphocyte migration to the lymph node [36, 38]. TEMRA cells re­express CD45RA,
which allows them to differentiate and proliferate again. TEM cells mediate protective
memory by migrating to the site of inflammation, whereas TCM cells migrate to secondary
lymphoid organs to proliferate and differentiate into effector cells in response to antigen
stimulation [39].
When T cells have engaged activation through TCR’s recognition of a specific peptide
MHC complex along with activating cytokines, the molecule CD69 is upregulated. Thus,
CD69 is described as an early activation marker on T cells, but it is reduced after 4­6
hours of activation as for NK cells. Furthermore, it has been shown to be important in
stimulation of a T helper 1 and 17 immune respond, due to the interaction with sphingo­
sine 1­phosphate receptor 1 (S1P1) and the amino acid transporter complex LAT1­CD98.
These two receptors together with CD69 controls migration and cell metabolism. How­
ever, if CD69’s ligand galectin­1 interacts with CD69 instead of binding of S1P1, it will
promote Treg cells’ development instead [27]. Upon activation CD39 is upregulated as
well, which hydrolyze ATP into adenosine and phosphate. Stressed or damaged cells
secrete ATP, hence it acts as DAMP and can trigger an inflammatory respond. However,
when ATP is hydrolyzed to adenosine with help from CD73, an anti­inflammatory envi­
ronment is created. If too much ATP is hydrolyzed, it will cause an immunosuppressive
environment [40].
Interestingly, CD39 have shown to be co­expressed with CD103, which characterize a
unique population of CD8 T cells within the tumor microenvironment (TME). CD103, also
known as integrin αE, is expressed in DCs, but also in a small population of T cells in
the peripheral tissue defined as tissue­resident memory T (TRM) cells [41]. CD103 is
upregulated by transforming growth factor (TGF)­β, which is secreted by other immune
cells, such as macrophages. Upregulation of CD103 and other molecules such as CD69
and CD39 characterize these cells [42]. According to research by Duhen et al. (2018),
CD103 co­expressed with CD39 on TRM cells have shown to expand in tumors and have
high killing abilities along with better overall survival (OS).
T cells can be divided into two types based on the chains of their TCR. Either the recep­
tor consist of α and β chain or γ and δ chain, respectively being the conventional αβT
cell or the unconventional γδT cell. The αβ­TCR is responsible for the MHC­recognition
and is expressed by the majority of T cells, whereas the γδ­TCR do not need MHC anti­
gen presentation to perform its function, and further γδ­TCR represents only 1­5% of the
overall T cell population [44, 45]. An important differences is their recognition of antigens.
γδT cell recognize lipid antigens, which are presented by e.g. CD1 molecules, whereas
αβT cell recognize antigens presented by MHC molecules [46]. However, common for
both T cells is the expression of the co­stimulatory molecule CD3. The γδT cell can also
Page 5
==PAGE:15==
express CD56, which is associated with the NK cells [47, 48]. Expression of the surface
receptors NKG2D is induced by soluble mediators, such as perforin, and indicates γδT
cells display cytotoxic potential. When activated they can secrete anti­microbial proteins
as well as express TNF­related apoptosis­inducing ligands. In addition, they can secrete
IFN­γ to inhibit angiogenesis and enhance the presentation of MHC class­I expressed by
tumor cells. Other molecules like DNAM­1 have also shown to be involved in activation
and cytotoxicity of γδT cells [49]. When referring to T cells throughout the report it is the
αβT cell, unless otherwise specified.
Another unique immune cell of the T cells is the Treg cells. Treg cells maintain immune
equilibrium using a broad spectrum of mechanisms, and maintaining self­tolerance is one
of their main functions. They are highly immunosuppressive, and generally downregulate
proliferation of effector T cells. Treg cells arise from either naive CD4+ T cells or CD4+
thymocytes, respectively upon stimulation with FoxP3 or TGF­β [50]. These cells pro­
duce the cytokines TGF­β and IL­10, which can inhibit T cells and APCs [1]. The main
lineage marker for Treg cells is FoxP3 along with high expression of CD25, the IL­2 re­
ceptor alpha, and low expression of CD127, the IL­7 receptor, [51]. Interaction with APCs
and other immune cells is important for Treg cells’ immunosuppressive functions. After
migration to the inflammatory tissue, Treg cells mature or adapt to the microenvironment.
Various inhibitory markers are expressed by Tregs, such as T cell immunoreceptor with
Immunoglobulin and ITIM domains (TIGIT) and cytotoxic T­lymphocyte­associated pro­
tein 4 (CTLA­4) [51]. Expression of CTLA­4 on the surface of Treg cells and other T cells
compete with CD28 for binding of B7 on APCs, resulting in inhibition of the co­stimulatory
signal of naive T cells and suppress the activation of T cells. Another suppressing effect
is the release of cytokines TGF­β, IL­10, and IL­35 [51, 52].
1.2
Cancer
Cancer is defined as a large group of individual diseases caused by cellular change, which
results in abnormal and uncontrolled cell growth. Cancer cells are typically formed when
the control mechanisms that in healthy cells are applied during cell division and growth get
damaged. If a cell fails to repair these control mechanisms or undergo apoptosis, the cell
can develop into a cancer cell. This process of healthy cells transforming into cancer cells
is termed oncogenesis. The speed of growth varies between cancer types, and so does
the potential of the cancer cells to invade other tissues in the host [53]. To understand
the biology of cancer, ten hallmarks have been proposed; Sustaining proliferative sig­
naling, Evading growth suppressors, Resisting cell death, Enabling replicative immortal­
ity, Inducing angiogenesis, Avoiding immune destruction, Tumor­promoting inflammation,
Genome instability & mutation, Deregulating cellular energetics, and Activating invasion
and metastasis. These hallmarks helps explain how tumors are able to grow and spread
throughout the body, as seen in figure 1.2. The most aggressive cancer types display all
ten hallmarks [54].
1.2.1
Glioblastoma multiforme
GBM is one of the most aggressive brain tumors and is therefore, rated as grade IV tu­
mors. According to the World Health Organization (WHO) only 5 % of patients survives
over 5 years after diagnosed [52]. The tumor mainly arises from normal glial cells, such
as astrocytes or oligodendrocytes, through multiple steps of oncogenesis in the CNS. As
the name implies, glioblastoma is multiform as they have various biological mechanisms
and origin, therefore, no successful treatment yet have been found [55]. The brain has
for a long time been considered an immune privileged organ due to the presence of the
blood brain barrier (BBB), making drug delivery challenging [56]. Furthermore, a tumor
Page 6
==PAGE:16==
Figure 1.2: Illustration of the ten hallmarks of cancer. Modified from [54].
within the brain has a very unique immune environment consisting of immune cells driv­
ing hypoxia, angiogenesis, and triggering production of VEGF [52]. At the moment, the
treatment for patients with GBM is surgical removal of the tumor followed by radiother­
apy and Temozolomide chemotherapy, also called Stupp’s regimen [55]. Treatment with
Stupp’s regimen shows progression­free survival (PFS) of 6.7 month, and an OS around
16 months [57]. There is currently a poor understanding of the underlying factors for
long­term survival chances.
However, two molecular factors have been identified as an indicator of progression associ­

transferase (MGMT). MGMT is a DNA repair gene, that when methylated results in in­
activation of this tumor­suppressor gene. This results in low expression of MGMT and
longer survival, thus making it a prognostic biomarker for survival prognosis [58, 59]. The
second molecular factor of interest is the isocitrate dehydrogenase­1 (IDH­1), an enzyme
involved in Krebs cycle, which catalyzes the production of α­KG from isocitrate. If IDH­1
is mutated another product will be produced resulting in enhanced TME formation, such
as upregulation of VEGF. Therefore, IDH is also used as a prognostic biomarker [60].
The TME of GBM consists of various immune cells, as described above, along with other
cells such as T cells. The migration of T cells to the brain and cross the BBB is poorly
understood. Different theories of the mechanism have been investigated, one plausible
model being that T cells enter the brain through the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). A study
identified T cells coexpressing CCR5 (CD195) and CXCR3 within a sample from the CSF,
therefore, CCR5 is a marker to further help identify if T cells are homing towards the brain
upon inflammation [61].

1.3
Immunosurveillance
”Immunosurveillance” was first time discussed in relation to cancer back in 1909 by Paul
Ehrlich. The hypothesis was the immune system can differentiate between healthy and
cancerous cells, and eliminate cancerous cells [62, 63]. Later a new theory was called ”Im­
munoediting” by Dunn et al. (2002). Immunoediting explains the host­protecting actions
­ immunosurveillance, together with the tumor­sculpting actions in the immune system
when tumors are developing. The sculpting of tumors results in selective growth caused
by the pressure from the immune system, which promotes better armed tumors to es­
cape elimination [62, 64]. Cancer immunoediting sums up the events which takes place
in a immune response to cancer and is divided into three phases, the three ”Es”: ”Elim­
ination”, ”Equilibrium”, and ”Escape”, as seen in figure 1.3 [64]. The elimination phase
encompasses the original concept immunosurveillance, which is destruction of cancer by
the host immune system before the tumor becomes clinically apparent. If one cancer
cell differentiate into a new type, the immune system and tumor will enter the equilibrium
phase. In this phase, the cancer cell is suppressed by T cells and certain molecules so it
does not outgrowth. Here the process of immunoediting can occur, as the tumor immuno­
genicity is edited due to the immune system selective pressure and promotes the tumor
cell variants. These cancer variants may enter the escape phase due to being constant
under specific immune pressure as unstable tumors. Thus are no longer suppressed by
the immune system and their outgrowth can cause clinically apparent disease [62, 65].

1.3.1
The Tumor Microenvironment and Glioblastomas
The TME of GBM is composed of several cell types including glial cells and various im­
mune cells. The glial cells, such as microglia, secrete soluble factors to induce an immune
response and attracts T cells (CD4, CD8, Treg), NK cells, DCs, macrophages, and MD­
SCs [66].
Even though, an immune respond is occurring in GBM patients, there have not been
found any evidence of the elimination process to taking place. Whereas, the equilibrium
phase of GBM have been suggested to occur, which have been shown in patients with
transplanted organs that showed developed glioma metastases after transplanting from
a ”healthy” donor. These cases suggest that donor­patients may have been in a resting
state, hence the equilibrium phase, of GBM without diagnosed [67]. In addition, tumor
cells of GBM employ various methods to escape immune surveillance. One mechanism
is the expression of various immune cell receptor ligands to avoid detection by inhibiting
the immune cells [68]. Expression of these inhibitory receptors can identify T cells be­
ing exhausted, due to loss of T cells’ effector functions as a result of prolonged antigen
stimulation [69].
As mentioned earlier, NK cells express both activating and inhibitory receptors in general
immune response towards cancer. NK cells express a heterodimer receptor consisting of
C­type lectin NKG2A/D together with CD94. CD56Bright NK populations have been shown
to express high levels of the inhibitory receptor NKG2A, whereas CD56dim express low
levels of NKG2A [70]. In humans, healthy cells express normal amounts of the non­
classical molecule human leukocyte antigen (HLA)­E, which NKG2A detects. Therefore,
when activated NK cells patrol tumor cells, the expression of the ligand HLA­E will rescue
tumor cells from cell­mediated killing [25]. Additionally, studies have shown that both NK
populations of CD56Bright and CD56dim express the activating receptor NKG2D. Engage­
ment of NKG2D stimulates cytokine production together with cytotoxic molecules if bound
to its ligand, such as MHC class­I­related chains A/B (MICA/B). Under healthy conditions,
these ligands are poorly expressed or absent, while in diseased tissue, such as cancer,
they become upregulated on stressed or damaged cells [71, 72]. Immune elimination
could occur via the NKG2D­MICA/B interaction which lead to release of perforin, IFN­γ,
or other pro­inflammatory cytokines. However, immune escape is also a possibility with
this interaction, as high level expression of MHC class­I antigens by tumor cells, allows
binding of the inhibitory NK receptors, hence masking NKG2D ligands [73].
As previously described, macrophages can differentiate into two types, but in the TME a
third type termed tumor­associated macrophage (TAM) can also develop. These macroph­
ages have been converted by either tumor cells or MDSCs to the M2 phenotype and be­
come tumor promoting. TAMs promote tumor growth, invasion, angiogenesis, and tumor
metastasis, as well as inhibit T cell activation and proliferation. Furthermore, tumors have
evolved to adapt the cells in the TME, such as TAMs, to express high levels of PD­L1 [74,
75].
Other interesting receptors that are over­expressed in GBM tumors, include the CD155
pro­tumorigenic antigen, which has been shown to promote migration, invasion, angio­
genesis and proliferation of tumor cells. CD155 is rarely detected in normal tissue but is
upregulated in human malignancies [76]. Several receptors of interest have been investi­
gated recognizing CD155, in order to identify cells targeting cancer, one being the activat­
ing receptor DNAM­1 (DNAX accessory molecule­1, CD226). DNAM­1 is expressed on
NK cells and its interaction with CD155 generates cell­mediated cytotoxicity, accompanied
by an increased production of cytokines [77]. A second receptor of interest is the inhibitory
Page 9

receptor TIGIT, which is expressed on both T cells and NK cells. Studies indicates that
TIGIT inhibits T cell proliferation and activation. Furthermore, its expression correlates
with the expression of programmed cell death protein 1 (PD­1), suggesting TIGIT can be
used as a marker for exhausted tumor­infiltrating T cells (TILs). In addition, TIGITs up­
regulation in Treg cells creates a very suppressive phenotype [78]. Figure 1.4 gives an

DNAM­1, causing TIGIT to out­compete DNAM­1 from interacting with CD155 [77, 78].

Exhaustion of T cells results in constantly antigen stimulation and loss of T cell functions.
Exhausted T cells are defined by expression of various molecules, such as the T­cell
immunoglobulin and mucin domain 3 (TIM­3) and the lymphocyte activation gene­3 (LAG­
3). TIM­3 is an inhibitory molecule expressed by T cells, Treg cells among others. It has
been suggested that expression of TIM­3 is upregulated on immune cells in a series of
cancers. Furthermore, like other inhibitory markers TIM­3 can be co­expressed with PD­
1, which is observed in T cells that are very exhausted and express significantly lower
amounts of effector cytokines IFN­γ, TNF, and IL­2. Together, PD­1 and TIM­3 can identify
exhausted T cells, and blocking of both results in tumor regression and improve anticancer
Page 10
==PAGE:20==
T cell response in patients [79]. LAG­3 is also an inhibitory marker expressed on CD8+ T
cells, CD4+ T cells and Treg cells upon antigen stimulation. LAG­3 have shown to bind to
MHC­class II in the same manner as the co­receptor CD4, along with LAG­3 have shown
a greater binding affinity to MHC class­II than CD4 [80].
Another molecule that is known to be associated with exhausted T cells is the transcription
factor T cell factor 1 (TCF­1). TCF­1 normally is expressed early TCM cells and stem cell
memory T cells, however in checkpoint blocking in cancer, its expression correlates with T
cell exhaustion. During expression of PD­1, TCF­1 is also highly expressed, and hereby
represents a marker for pre­exhausted T cell. But as several other inhibitory molecules,
such as TIM­3 and CTLA­4, are upregulated on the same cell, the expression of TCF­1
decreases, which represents an exhausted T cell [81].
The molecule PD­1 is expressed on many cells and is one of a well­known inhibitory
molecules which interacting with various cancers, including GBM. PD­1 binds to its cog­
nate ligands PD­L1 and PD­L2, leading to inhibition of activated T cells. PD­1 is expressed
on activated T cells, whereas PD­L1 and PD­L2 are expressed by IFN­γ on APCs and can­
cer cells. This molecule has been highly studied as a potential target in treatment against
many types of cancer do to its expression on various immune cells e.g. T cells, which will
be elaborated in section 1.4.1 [52].
1.4
Cancer Immune therapy
Cancer immune therapy is a collective term for treatments in cancers, that helps the im­
mune system fight cancer. These treatments have been investigated intensively over the
last decades with varying results. Immune therapies shows better responses towards
some cancer types than others and sometimes immunotherapies seem to provide a bet­
ter response in combination with other types of treatments. The different types of im­
munotherapy consist of stimulation of the immune system via cells, such as lymphocytes,
vaccines, and antibodies among others [82].
Two bigger groups of immune therapy have been developed; Active and passive. Both
provide a tumor­antigen specific response. Active immunotherapy aims for long­lasting
effect and includes cytokine therapy and vaccines. Cancer vaccines function like nor­
mal vaccines and cytokine therapy injects cytokines to stimulate immune cells to attack
cancer. Passive immunotherapy is short­termed, thus requiring repeated applications and
includes antibody­based or effector T cells [82, 83]. Furthermore, administration of expan­
sion of active effector T cells within the tumor, also known as to as adoptive cell therapy,
is another immune therapy, which uses the patient’s own immune cells to induce tumor
response [84]. Another impressive immune therapy is immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI),
which will be described in the next section.
1.4.1
Checkpoint inhibition and immune activation
ICI are monoclonal antibodies targeting specific molecules expressed on immune cells or
cancer cells for blocking of an inhibitory axis present in the tumor. CTLA­4 and PD­1 are
two of the most used ICI molecules in various cancer. ICIs blocks immune checkpoint
proteins, such as PD­1, from binding to its ligand on cancerous cells and have shown to
be evolutionary drugs in many cancer­treatments, such as melanoma [85]. Melanoma
was the first cancer to show an improved OS of 10 month for patients responding to the
treatment [86]. Recently, in melanoma patients receiving Nivolumab showed an OS of 36
month [87]. CTLA­4­blocking antibodies, such as Ipilimumab, have shown an improved
OS of 10 months either by itself or in combination with other treatments in melanoma
studies [88]. Recently, blocking with anti­CTLA­4 antibody in adoptive cell therapy for
Page 11
==PAGE:21==
ovarian cancer improved the activity of CD8+ TILs [89]. Furthermore, treatment with anti­
CTLA­4 in melanoma patients showed an increase in activated CD4 and CD8 T cells,
while decrease in naive T cells [90].
Recently a study by Xiong et al. (2020) examined blood from oral cavity squamous cell car­
cinoma patient, which is patients with mouth cancer treated with the Nivolumab. Promis­
ing results was observed, in regard to an increase in CD8+ T cells, while a decrease in
CD4+ T cells and additionally no change in NK cells. Another study by Jiang et al. (2018)
investigated patients with various advanced metastatic tumors treated with Nivoulmab or
Pembrolizumab, both PD­1 blocking antibodies. Here 25% of the patients showed an im­
provement by having an increase in CD8+ T cells and no change in NK cells, along with a
decrease in CD4+ cells. Meanwhile, a melanoma study by Ribas et al. (2016) also treated
with Pembrolizumab showed remarkable results of tumor biopsies. NK cells, Treg cells
and monocytes did not change, whereas CD8 TEM cells showed the greatest expansion.
Additionally, CD4 TEM cells decreased, while CD4 TCM cells and MDSCs increased.
1.4.2
Immune therapy and other therapies for GBM
A revolutionary treatment of GBM has yet to be discovered, however, several drugs has
been approved in order to stall the cancer and extend the life­time of patients. Meanwhile,
immune therapy is still ongoing in clinical trials.
As mentioned earlier, tumor resection is performed if possible and is a hallmark in the
treatment of malignant brain tumors. It is often not possible to remove the entire tumor,
as it grows into the normal and vital tissue of the brain. In 2005, the US Food and Drug
Administration (FDA) approved the chemotherapy Temozolomide for treatment of newly
diagnosed GBM. The mechanism of action is alkylation of the DNA, which trigger the
tumor­cell to undergo apoptosis. Surgery followed by radiation and chemotherapy with
temozolomide is today the current standard therapy [58, 94]. Later in 2009, the drug Be­
vacizumab (Avastin) was approved by FDA as a single agent for patients with progressive
GBM. It is a recombinant humanized monoclonal IgG1 antibody that selectively binds to
and neutralizes the human VEGF. VEGF is a protein promoting angiogenesis as a re­
spond to hypoxia i.e. reduced oxygen in tumors [14]. Neutralization of VEGF can result
in reduced tumor growth due to decreased vascularization [95].
Various immunotherapy have been developed and some approved as a treatment of GBM.
Especially, vaccines are an ongoing method for immunotherapeutical targeting, one being
DCVax, approved for treatment of glioblastoma in Switzerland. DCVax is a DC­based per­
sonalized cancer treatment with purified tumor­specific antigens. However, the challenge
with some vaccines is immune escape due to lack of peptide­expression. An example of
such escape is the peptide vaccine Rindopepimut. It targets the protein EGFRvIII which
has been shown to be upregulated on tumor cells in GBM patients. Nonetheless, those
with a recurrent tumor were analyzed afterwards and showed 82% of the tumors had lost
expression of EGFRvIII [52].
The engineering of patient T cells, Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cells, is in another
ongoing clinical trial for GBM. CAR T cells are constructed of a single chain variable frag­
ment specific to a tumor cell target which enable activation of T cells without need for a
co­stimulatory signal. An example of such is the expression of the Fas receptor on CAR
T cells, which induces apoptosis. Its ligand FasL has been found upregulated on GBM
tumors, hence a target for CAR T cells to focus upon [52].
A new interesting technique has been generated using oncolytic viruses to selectively kill
and infect tumor cells. Oncolytic viruses function by not infecting non­transformed cells,
Page 12
==PAGE:22==
and the genes are either knocked down or knocked in to enhance an immune response.
Herpes simplex virus T­VEC is one example of an oncolytic virus, being transfected with
the gene granulocyte­macrophage colony­stimulating factor (GM­CSF). The secretion of
GM­CSF increases DCs’ recruitment into the TME, and hereby increase the antigen pre­
sentation together with T cell activation. However, because of the BBB only a very small
amount of viruses can enter the brain, which is one reason for combination with other
therapeutics [52].
Last, the ongoing trial of ICI for GBM patients. Since the TME of GBM expresses several
immunosuppressive checkpoints, investigation using ICI as treatment has started many
clinical trials. Even though the tumor cells may evade the immune system, ICI have shown
to generate T cell response and enhanced anti­tumor immunity. As mentioned earlier,
numerous cancers are treated with ICI and result in an immune response, why there is an
interest of using it in GBM patients as well [96]. Nivolumab is an engineered humanized
IgG4 monoclonal antibody that targets PD­1 [97]. Mechanism of action is visualized in
figure 1.5. It was the first ICI targeting PD­1 and to be approved by FDA in 2014 for the
treatment of melanoma. Hereafter, the treatment with Nivolumab was approved in various
cancer, however, there are still multiple ongoing clinical trials using it in GBM patients [98,
99], one being part of this project.

Materials and Methods
In this project multicolor flow cytometry was performed by using four panels of antibodies
specific for various surface and intracellular molecules. This was used to identify and
characterize the cell populations and expression of activating and inhibitory molecules in
GBM patient samples.
3.1
Methodology
Flow cytometry is used to analyse the optical properties of single cells as they flow past
single or multiple laser beams. Each cell is analyzed for visible light scatter, measured as
forward scatter (FSC) and side scatter (SSC), indicating the relative size and granularity
of the cell. Furthermore, the cell is analyzed for the expression of cell surface and intracel­
lular molecules to for example determine the cell types within a heterogeneous population
[100]. The expression of molecules are detected using panels of fluorochrome­conjugated
antibodies specific for the molecules of interest. The flow cytometer is composed of three
main subsystems; fluidics, optics, and electronics, which works together to measure the
physical characteristics of single particles. The fluidics system’s main function is to trans­
port the cell for interrogation by using hydrodynamic focusing. Hydrodynamic focusing
uses sheath fluid to surround the sample core in order for single cells to pass through
the laser beam. By narrowing the stream of the fluid the event rate of cells is decreased,
hence single cells can be analysed. The optical system consists of excitation and collec­
tion, by direction of laser beams towards the flow cell and directing the emitted light to the
right detectors. The electronics system’s main function is to convert the light signals via
photodetectors, such as photomultiplier tubes (PMT) into electrical current which is con­
verted into numerical data, such as frequencies, and further be analyzed. PMT values
can further help display the data in software such as FlowJo [101]. In this project four
existing panels were modified and new antibodies were titrated as described below. The
panels were tested prior the staining of patients samples.
3.2
Patient samples
Patients diagnosed with GBM who have relapsed after standard treatment were included
in the clinical protocol CA9UP. Blood and tumor samples were collected between Novem­
ber 2018 and March 2021. Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were isolated
from blood, and tumor samples were enzymatically digested to obtain a single cells sus­
pension prior to the start of this project. The patients have received Nivolumab and Beva­
cizumab according to the scheme presented in figure 3.1. The analysis of patient samples
was only based on half of the patients included in the clinical trial. Appendix A.4 shows
an overview of clinical data collected from Rigshospitalet.
The patients were split in two arms; A and B. Patients in arm A had their tumor surgically

T2: 57 days after treatment, and T3: 113 days after treatment. Arm A received Nivolumab
7 days before surgical removal of the tumor, whereafter arm A received Bevacizumab in

3.3
Antibody staining of cells
The cells were thawed in pre­heated R10 (RPMI with 10% fetal calve serum (FCS)), and
hereafter washed in FACS buffer(PBS with 2% FCS) by centrifugation at 1500 rpm for 5
min with the setting acceleration 9/deceleration 9. Supernatant was discarded carefully
to preserve cell pellet after centrifugation. The washing step was repeated once more.
Cells were stained with the antibodies of interest according to section 3.3.1 and 3.3.2
and incubated in the dark for 30 minutes at 4◦C. After incubation, the cells were washed
twice in FACS buffer as described above. Hereafter, the cells were fixed using 1% PFA
(PBS + 4% PFA) and incubated at 4◦C in the dark between 1 hour and overnight. If cells
were additionally stained intracellularly, the kit eBioscienceTM Foxp3 / Transcription Factor
Staining Buffer Set (Thermo Fisher, cat# 00­5523­00) was used according to the manu­
facture protocol. Finally, cells were washed two times in FACS buffer and resuspended
in FACS buffer.
3.3.1
Antibody preparation for Titration
In order to obtain the optimal separation of the negative and positive population detected
via antibody staining and the optimal antibody concentration, titration of antibodies was
performed. The staining panels for this project includes antibodies specific for both sur­
face (SF) and intracellular (IC) molecules. Cells were stained with lineage markers ac­
cording to the cell type the given antibody was titrated for; CD3 for T cells, CD56 for NK
cells, and CD11b for myeloid cells. The antibody was titrated from the recommended vol­
ume 1:1, 1:2, 1:4, 1:8, 1:16, and a negative control stained only with the lineage antibody.

3.3.2
Antibody preparation for analysis
Four antibody modified panels contains various markers chosen for identification of sev­
eral cell types: T cells, Natural Killer (NK) cells, and myeloid cell, and for determination of
expression of various inhibiting and activating molecules. Appendix A.1 presents the four
panels. Appendix A.2 shows an overview of antibodies information, including the titrated
volume for a total staining volume of 100 µl.
3.3.3
Resting of digest
The tumor digest was rested over night for the lymphocytes to recover after the enzy­
matic digestion of the tumor samples. The samples were thawed in pre­heated R10 and
resuspended in R10, with 30 U/ml DNAse to reduce cell clumping, and incubated at room
Page 16
==PAGE:26==
temperature for 5 minutes. The cell suspension was centrifuged at same setting as for the
washing step and supernatant was discarded. The cells were resuspended in X­VIVO™
15 media with 5% Human serum (HS) and incubated overnight in a humidified C=2 incu­
bator at 37◦C and 5% CO2.
3.3.4
Compensation
Compensation setup was made for each antibody using 1 µl of the fluorescently conju­
gated antibody together with 1 drop of OneComp compensation beads. PBS was added
after 10 minutes of incubation at room temperature in dark. The compensation for NiR
was made with 1 µl near­IR viability dye and 1 drop Arc amine reactive beads, and incu­
bated for 30 minutes at 4 ◦C in the dark. The NiR stained beads were washed twice in
PBS and 1 drop of ArC amine negative beads was added together with PBS.
3.4
Analysis
3.4.1
Flow data
The samples were run using the instrument BD LSR­Fortessa™and collected data was
analyzed using FlowJo v10.7 software. Uniform manifold approximation and projections
(UMAP) were constructed in the software FlowJo.
3.4.2
Statistics
Statistical data was composed using the software GraphPad Prism, and unparameteric
statistical tests, unpaired t­test (Mann­Whitney test) and one­way ANOVA (Kruskal­Wallis
uncorrected Dunn’s test), were applied for comparison of means. By doing a normality
test on different samples from the data using GraphPad Prism, it showed that some of the
populations were unparameteric. Furthermore, as only one factor, time, was investigated
between the samples, one­way ANOVA was applied. Additionally, unparied t­test was
used to compare to independentl groups.

Discussion
The aim for this thesis is to investigate immune cells present within the brain and PBMCs
in GBM patients treated with Nivolumab and Bevacizumab.
The age distribution of this patient cohort had a median of 57 years old, where from 12
patients were above that age. According to Tamimi AF (2017) the age distribution in most
patients is diagnosed at older ages with a median age of 64. In addition, it is only 7
patients that was females, which correlates with the incidence of GBM is higher in males
than females [102].
Recruitment of lymphocytes across the BBB has shown great interest in the research field
of drugs as well as in the immune system. One entrance for the lymphocytes to the brain
is via the CSF using the expression of specific chemokines, such as CCR5 and CXCR3.
The study by Giunti et al. (2003) observed an increase of T cells in multiple sclerosis (MS),
which is a disabling disease of the brain and the CNS. The study obtained peripheral blood
and CSF from 21 patients with MS, where they used flow cytometry to detect whether
CD4+ and CD8+ T cells’ co­expressed CCR5 and CXCR3 in blood and CSF. Their results
showed the mean percentage value in CSF of CD8+ T cells co­expressing CCR5 and
CXCR3 was 54%, whereas it was 6.55% in the blood. Meanwhile, CD4+ T cells co­
expressing CCR5 and CXCR3 showed a mean percentage in CSF of 23.4 %, whereas it
was 2.2% in the blood. Increased numbers of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells were thus detected
in the CSF compared to the blood. In this project, the single expression of CCR5 and
CXCR3 using flow cytometry was detected, however, this was done for T cells in the
digest not the CSF. The results of the study can only imply whether T cells are migrating
to the brain using the same molecules. CXCR3+ T cells was found at high frequencies
in the digest and blood, which was also the case for CCR5+ T cells. The observations
of both molecules could imply that upregulation on cells helps them homing towards the
brain via CSF. It was investigated whether patients of this cohort co­expressed CCR5 and
CXCR3 in the digest, which only three patients showed (data not shown).
Additionally, research by Chow et al. (2019) discovered that CXCR3 is part of a chemokine
system with DCs, which can be a biomarker for sensitivity to PD­1 blockade. The study in­
vestigated the expression of CXCR3 in CD8+ T cells using flow cytometry in tumor­bearing
mice treated with anti­PD­1. The tumor­bearing mice were either wild­type, expressing
CXCR3+, or mutated mice not expressing CXCR3­/­. The results showed a better survival
rate in wild­type mice treated with anti­PD­1 with a higher frequency of CD8­T cells, along
with reduced tumor growth compared to CXCR3­/­ mice. In contrast to CXCR3­/­ mice,
reduced tumor growth was also detected, but the responds were significantly reduced
compare to wild­type mice. Furthermore, a lower frequency of CXCR3­/­ mice treated
with anti­PD­1 was detected in CD8 T cells. RNA and protein levels of CXCR3 ligands
(CXCL9 and CXCL10) were detected while treated with anti­PD­1, which showed an in­
crease of both ligands after treatment. According to the study, the ligands are expressed
by CD103+ DCs in the tumor which attracts CXCR3+ T cells, and these chemokines in DCs
were further examined in tumor­bearing mice. The results showed a higher frequency of
CXCL9 after anti­PD­1 treatment in CD103+ DCs. In relation to this project, CXCR3 was
investigated using flow cytometry in CD8+ and CD4+ T cells, which showed a higher fre­
quency in the digest compared to the blood. Taken this into account, this could suggest
the Nivolumab bound CXCR3+ T cells in the digest is cytotoxic CD8 T cells migrated from
blood to the brain with an efficacy PD­1 response. However, there was no increase in
Page 43
==PAGE:53==
CXCR3+ T cells in the blood over time, which suggest Nivolumab did not affect migration
of T cells to the brain. It was not possible to compare the DCs’ results as CD103+ DCs
were not analyzed. Nonetheless, the results imply that these DCs present in the TME
of this project might not express more ligands as Nivolumab was administered, since no
increase was seen in CXCR3+ T cells in the blood.
The overall population of CD4 and CD8 T cells showed that the fraction of CD8 T cells
decreased over time in the blood, and a lower frequency was detected in the digest com­
pared to the blood. Meanwhile, the CD4 T cells had a tendency to increase over time
in the blood, but showed a significant low level in the digest. In the blood this suggests
that as Nivolumab was administered a significant increase of CD4 T cells was observed,
whereas the fraction of CD8 T cells showed a tendency to decrease. In addition, the
fraction of Treg cells also significantly decreased over time in the blood and showed sig­
nificantly lower levels in the digest. According to other studies involving Nivolumab and
other anti­PD­1 drugs, in the blood an increase of CD8 T cells should occur as a posi­
tive respond to the treatment, along with some implying a decrease in CD4 T cells and
Treg cells [91–93]. This does not seem to be the case in this project, as the opposite is
detected. This could mean the treatment with Nivolumab is not working as efficient as it
should.
The detection of differentiation sub populations of CD4 and CD8 T cells in the blood and
digest showed an overall stable frequency over time in the blood as Nivolumab was given.
The differentiation sub populations of CD4 T cells, TCM, TEM, and TEMRA, showed to
have a higher frequency of CD4 TCM cells compared to CD8 TCM cells. Meanwhile,
CD8 T cells showed a higher frequency of TEMRA cells compared to CD4 TEMRA cells.
The high frequency of CD4 TCM and CD8 TEMRA cells in the blood could indicate an
active immune system, since these cells differentiate into effector cells when inflammation
occurs and reside in tissues waiting to be activated.
The study by Manjarrez­Orduño et al. (2018) investigated melanoma and non­small­cell
lung carcinoma (NSCLC) patients’ frequencies in differentiated sub populations of CD4
and CD8 T cells in the blood to show an ongoing immune respond in patients not treated
with anti­PD­1. Hereafter, NSCLC patients were treated with Nivolumab and had a blood
sample taken 3 month after. The samples were analyzed using flow cytometry, along with
compared to the control, which was blood from healthy donors. It was first investigated
how the distribution of T cells were in non­therapy patients’ blood. For CD4+ T cells, their
results showed CD4 TCM cells were detected at high frequencies compared to CD8 TCM
cells, CD4 TEM and CD4 TEMRA cells. Meanwhile, CD8+ T cells showed in NSCLC pa­
tients a higher frequency of CD8 TEMRA cells compared to CD4 TEMRA, CD8 TCM and
CD8 TEM cells. In addition, both CD4 and CD8 TEM cells were seen at lower frequencies
than CD4 TCM and CD8 TEMRA cells. Even though, T1 is the only sample of non­healthy
non­treated patients in this project, the stable frequencies over time suggests Nivolumab
did not give a efficient immune response when compared to the higher levels of T cells in
NSCLC and melanoma.
NSCLC patients treated with Nivolumab were compared with the same control. The re­
sults showed high frequencies of CD4 TCM cells with a significant high level compared
to the control, along with a high frequency of CD8 TEMRA cells compared to the control.
Furthermore, detection of CD4 TEM and CD4 TEMRA cells in treated NSCLC showed
significant high levels compared to the control, but at low frequencies. However, CD8
TCM and CD8 TEM cells showed no significant difference compared to the control, but
also low frequencies. Compared to the results of this project, similar high fractions of CD4
Page 44
==PAGE:54==
TCM and CD8 TEMRA cells were detected in the blood along with significantly lower lev­
els in digest compared to untreated T1 in CD4 TCM cells. According to the study, a higher
frequency was detected in the blood compared to healthy patients, suggesting Nivolumab
should show an increase in the blood over time. However, as only CD4 TEM, CD8 TEM,
and CD4 TEMRA cells showed significantly higher levels in digest compared to blood, it
implies Nivolumab could have a positive impact on those cells in the digest compared to
those circulating in the blood.
Another study by Ribas et al. (2016) investigated tumor biopsies obtained from 53 melano­
ma patients treated with Pembrolizumab. They used multicolor flow cytometry to charac­
terize T cells in the tumor and peripheral blood, and inspected if a change occurred from
before treatment and on treatment (taken at mean time of 74 days). In the tumor, they
found a significant high level of CD8 TEM cells on Pembrolizumab therapy compared to
before therapy, whereas a significant low level was detected in CD4 TCM cells on Pem­
brolizumab therapy compared to before therapy. Furthermore, the blood samples before
and on Pembrolizumab treatment only showed an overview of CD3+ TEM cells, which
showed a stable frequency. Compared to the results of this project, Nivolumab treatment
implies a high fraction of CD4 and CD8 TEM cells in the digest compared to in the blood,
however, we did not have a digest sample before Nivolumab treatment to compare with.
Furthermore, there was a stable frequency in both CD4 and CD8 TEM cells in the blood,
which correlate with their results, thus Nivolumab did not have an effect on circulating
TEM cells in the blood.
Even though only a small number of digest samples were investigated, the results still
gives an indication if there is any upregulated molecules on the immune cells within the
tumor. The activating molecules presented in the results revealed almost no difference
within the blood over time, but significantly higher levels in the digest samples. The early
activation molecule CD69 was detected in high frequencies in the digest compared to
the blood, meaning T cells in the tumor could had been activated recently. However, the
ligand of CD69, galectin­1, is a radioresistance marker found in e.g. cervial cancer and
GBM [105, 106]. Research by Chou et al. (2018) investigated 45 patients with GBM, which
showed a high frequency of galectin­1 on tumor cells in GBM patients. In this project, it
was not possible to investigate if galectin­1 was present, however, the high frequency of
CD69 in the digest could also be negative if galectin­1 is found in GBM patients. This is
just an observation for CD69+ T cells in the tumor.
A recent study by Davidson et al. (2019) explored PD­1’s expression in tumor­infiltrating
lymphocytes (TILs) and peripheral blood from malignant glioma patients. The samples
were analyzed using flow cytometry. The results indicated higher frequencies of PD­1
in TILs vs the blood, along with high frequencies of the activating molecule CD69, and
the inhibitory molecules TIM­3, CTLA­4, LAG­3 and TIGIT. PD­1+ TILs showed higher
frequencies of TIM­3, CTLA­4 and TIGIT compared to in the blood, whereas LAG­3 was
seen at stable frequencies. CD69 also showed high frequencies in TILs compared to
blood, but this was not commented on in the study. Nevertheless, due to the high expres­
sion of various inhibitory molecules on PD­1+ TILs, the cells represented an exhausted
phenotype. Whereas low frequencies of inhibitory markers in the PD­1+ T cells in the
blood could indicate a more activated phenotype. These results almost correlate with the
results within this project, however, the study looked at overall CD3+ PD­1+ TILs, whereas
this project examined the differentiated sub populations of CD4 and CD8 T cells’ expres­
sion of single molecules in digest, and not further PD­1+ T cells’ expression of molecules.
The results of this project suggest, the frequencies of TIM­3, CTLA­4, LAG3, and TIGIT
in the blood and digest represents exhausted T cells.

Additionally, another study by Koyama et al. (2016) investigated mice, that were recently
resistant to anti­PD­1, the effect of other inhibitory molecules TIM­3, CTLA­4, and LAG­3
in lung cancer. Tumor samples were analyzed using flow cytometry and untreated mice
were compared to resistant anti­PD­1 mice. The results showed a significantly higher
frequency of TIM­3 in CD4 and CD8 T cells in resistant anti­PD­1 mice, along with high
frequencies of CTLA­4 and LAG­3 in resistant anti­PD­1 mice compared to untreated.
This suggest that upon resistance towards anti­PD­1, other inhibitory molecules are up­
regulated. Resistance of Nivolumab was not investigated in this project, however, an in­
creasing frequency is observed for TIM­3, LAG­3 (except in CD8 TEM cells), and CTLA­4
over time in the blood in both CD4 and CD8 T cell as Nivolumab was given. Thus, as PD­1
becomes blocked other inhibitory molecules were upregulated on CD4 and CD8 T cells.
Furthermore, the inhibitory molecules also showed to be present at high frequencies in
the digest.
The study by Woroniecka et al. (2018) also explored T cell exhaustion in 21 patients diag­
nosed with GBM looking at some of the same inhibitory molecules as this project LAG­3,
PD­1, TIGIT, TIM­3, and CTLA­4. TILs and blood samples were analyzed using flow cy­
tometry. The results detected low frequencies of single expression of LAG­3 and TIM­3
in CD8+ T cells isolated from TILs in the blood. Whereas CTLA­4, TIGIT, and PD­1 also
showed low frequencies in the blood, but still higher than LAG­3 and TIM­3. Additionally,
higher frequencies were observed in TILs for LAG­3, TIM­3, TIGIT and PD­1, while CTLA­
4 showed a little higher frequency compared to its detection in the blood. Moreover, the
co­expression of TIM­3 or LAG­3 with PD­1 were also analyzed in TILs and blood sam­
ples. The results indicated that TIM­3 and LAG­3 was accompanied by PD­1 expression,
meaning TIM­3 and LAG­3’s frequencies were almost the same as when they were de­
tected as single molecules. These observations were also investigated in this project,
which showed that co­expression of TIM­3 and LAG­3 with PD­1 resulted in almost the
same frequencies in CD8 T cells in the digest (data not shown). This suggest upregula­
tion of inhibitory molecules could occur alongside PD­1’s upregulation in the digest and
the blood of GBM patients.
Another co­expression with PD­1, which showed to be in cancers was TIGIT. According to
research by Josefsson et al. (2019) a high frequency of PD­1+ TIGIT+ can be found in CD4
and CD8 TEM cells in Hodgkin lymphoma tumor samples analyzed with flow cytometry.
Their results showed blocking of both PD­1+ and TIGIT+ would result in better survival
rate than only blocking one. Further, co­expression of PD­1+ and TIGIT+ showed a more
exhausted phenotype. The results in this project suggests co­expression of PD­1 and
TIGIT was highly abundant, as seen for CD8 TCM cells which almost expressed the same
frequency for TIGIT alone compared to PD­1+TIGIT+. Accordingly, the high PD­1+TIGIT+
frequency indicates CD4 and CD8 T cells being exhausted. Furthermore, another study
investigated the co­expression in Treg cells, which showed that if PD­1 was block, TIGIT
would be upregulated in Treg cells [111]. This will be discussed later in this chapter. This
mechanism could support the results in this project, showing an increase over time in the
blood in TIGIT+ T cells as Nivolumab was given.
The molecule TCF­1 was not seen in high frequencies, but its expression correlates with
how exhausted cells might be in the blood and digest [81]. CD8 TEMRA cells showed
small frequencies with significantly higher levels over time as well as expression in di­
gest, whereas the other T cells only showed TCF­1 in the blood (data not shown), how­
ever, its upregulation on CD8 TEMRA cells in the blood and the digest suggests they are
exhausted as Nivolumab was administered.

==PAGE:56==
The co­expression of CD39 and CD103 in CD8 T cells within the TME have been high­
lighted as unique TRM cells. The results showed a significant high level in the digest
compared to in the blood in almost all differentiated sub populations of CD8 and CD4 T
cells, and CD8 T cells appear to have a higher frequency in the digest compared to CD4
T cells. Research by Duhen et al. (2018) explore various cancers (e.g. melanoma and
lung among others) for presence of TRM cells in tumor and peripheral blood. The results
clearly showed these cells were in very small percentages in the blood compared to the
tumor, which correlates with this project’s data. According to the study, these cells have
the ability to kill tumor cells through a restricted MHC method, which was not described in
the study. Thus, their presence in the results of this project indicates possibly activated
CD4 and CD8 TRM cells with cytotoxic functions. Nevertheless, this phenotype also ex­
press PD­1 and other inhibitory molecules, which can cause outgrowth of tumor and result
in the escape of the immune system [43].
In regard to these observations a change could be seen in immune cells after administra­
tion of Nivolumab in GBM patients, along with detection of various T cells in the digest.
Specifically, CD4 T cells increased, as CD8 T cells decreased over time in the blood,
along with both T cells were detected in the digest. The T cells in the digest suggest to
contain both activating and inhibitory molecules, along with upregulation of homing re­
ceptors suggesting recently activated T cells migrate to the brain. TRM cells shows high
frequencies in the digest of especially CD8 T cells, which imply a cytotoxic response could
occure in the brain. Furthermore, several inhibitory molecules showed an increase over
time as Nivolumab was administered compared to activating molecules showing stable
frequencies. Especially, TIGIT showed high frequencies as single molecule but also as
co­expressed with PD­1, which according to research represents exhausted T cells.
Although there is a high frequency of PD­1+ T cells in the digest compared to in the blood,
the frequency of IgG4, which detects Nivolumab in the digest indicates differently. IgG4
was added to the panel to visualize expression of PD­1 indirectly by binding Nivolumab
bound to PD­1 molecules. The results indicated Nivolumab bound to T cells were present
in the tumor. However, not all PD­1 molecules were bound to Nivolumab, which could
be explained by various factors. Cells of the digest were rested over night, where the
cells could have internalized bound Nivolumab. Some studies have investigated whether
this occurs for monoclonal antibodies (one being Nivolumab [112]), where from outcome
showed PD­1 bound to Nivolumab can cause internalization from the surface of T­cells
[112–114]. In addition, an intact BBB does not allow penetration of monoclonal antibodies
due to their size [115]. A possible mechanism for Nivolumab to enter the tumor is through
already activated T cells homing to the tumor. Nivolumab’s binding to PD­1 on peripheral
circulating T cells can penetrate the BBB [115].
One of those cells being very immunosuppressive in tumors are Treg cells. As mentioned
earlier, they showed a decrease in blood and low levels in digest, however, they showed
high frequencies of the molecules TIGIT and PD­1, while showing low frequency of CTLA­
4. CTLA­4 is one of the molecules contributing to Treg cells’ suppressor functions, thus
a low expression of it can mean a less immunosuppressive Treg cell [116]. Upregulation
of TIGIT is associated with a very immune suppressive phenotype, and together with
expression of PD­1, Treg cells can further enhance their immunosuppressive functions
and contribute to tumor progression in GBM patients [117]. Research in head and neck
squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) by Wu et al. (2019) showed blocking of PD­1/PD­
L1 resulted in increased frequencies of TIGIT on Treg cells, hence suggesting blocking
with Nivolumab increases frequencies of TIGIT. Accordingly, the high frequency of TIGIT
in Treg cells could suggest escape of Nivolumab. Yet, as only one patient sample was
Page 47
==PAGE:57==
possible to detect Treg cells, nothing can be concluded for their upregulation of inhibitory
molecules as Nivolumab was given.
γδT cell were found at very low levels in the blood and digest, which was expected as these
cells only constitute 1­5% of all T cells [118]. γδT cells are involved in tumor surveillance
due to their great effector functions in many cancers. However, it has been discussed
whether these cells are a positively or negatively outcome when detected in tumors [119,
120]. In cancer it has been discovered that γδT cells are IL­17+. They induce angiogene­
sis and recruit neutrophils. Neutrophils secrete tumor­promoting agents, such as growth
factors and ROS, which have shown to be involved in a poor prognosis in cancer [119,
121, 122]. In a study by Lee et al. (2019), γδT cells were explored in blood and tumor of
GBM patients using next­generation sequencing­based TCR sequencing. The cells were
matched with other immune cells characteristics (e.g. macrophages, Treg, T cells) to ver­
ify their functions. One of their conclusions was γδT cells appeared to have anti­tumor
activity. Upon these observations, it was not possible to verify whether the presence of
γδT cells are positive or negative. Further analysis would have to be done to verify such.
Another small population examined in this project was the NKT cells. NKT cells also have
the ability to kill and secrete cytokines to stimulate other immune cells, however, they only
seemed to appear in the blood samples of this project. As many other cells, NKT also
express inhibitory molecules like PD­1 and CTLA­4, therefore the injection with Nivolumab
could enhance their activity, however, as it was not able to detect NKT cells in the digest,
nothing could be concluded [124].
The NK cells showed the cytotoxic CD56dim CD16+ NK cells being in very small fraction in
the digest compared to the blood, and the non­cytotoxic CD56dim CD16­ NK cells showed
being the largest fraction in the digest compared to the blood. The research by Close et al.
(2020) investigate GBM diagnosed patients’ blood and tumor for NK cells using some of
the same molecules DNAM­1, CD69, and NKG2D. They showed significantly lower levels
in digest compared to in the blood of DNAM­1+ and NKG2D+ NK cells, which could have
been the caused by TGF­β. TGF­β is secreted in GBM and can reduce the expression of
those molecules on NK cells, hence NK cells become inactive [126]. In this project, the
frequency of NKG2D in digest compared to blood is significantly lower. Yet, DNAM­1 were
detected with higher frequency than NKG2D in the digest, but still with lower compared to
the blood. This observation could suggest the NK cells being active in the blood, but as
they enter the TME, molecules like TGF­β could inhibit the NK cells’ activity. Moreover,
as described in section 1.3.1, TIGIT and DNAM­1 compete for the same ligand, CD155,
on GBM tumor cells. Examining the data could suggest the high frequency of TIGIT in
CD56dim CD16­ NK cells was associated with the lower frequency of DNAM­1 in the
digest, whereas CD56dim CD16+ NK cells showed a higher level of DNAM­1 compared
to TIGIT. Additionally, the results in the digest also showed lower frequency of CD107a+
CD56dim CD16­ NK cells while TIGIT+ CD56dim CD16­ NK cells showed higher frequency
levels. Compared to CD56dim CD16+ NK cells in the digest, the cells here showed a high
frequency of CD107a and a low frequency of TIGIT. Upregulation of TIGIT in CD56dim
CD16­ NK cells along with low levels of CD107a indicates poor killing capability. In a
study investigating myelodysplastic syndrome (bone cancer) using flow cytometry, the
expression levels of TIGIT in NK cells suggested to be associate with low levels of CD107a
and DNAM­1, hence TIGIT+ NK cells inhibit activated cytotoxic NK cells [127]. The other
inhibitory molecule NKG2A was expressed at low frequencies in both populations in blood
and digest, which support the theory of NKG2A being present at low levels on CD56dim
NK cells [70].

Investigation of myeloid cells showed an increase of G­MDSCs over time in the blood, as

suppressing them via mechanisms, such as PD­1 and the release of ROS. A possible

of DCs and macrophages differentiating into M2 macrophages [130]. Interestingly, in this
project the majority of MDSCs in GBM patients was G­MDSC+ which also have been
observed in other studies [131, 132]. Generally, an increase of MDCSs in the blood was
associated with poor prognosis, which perhaps could imply the same with high level of
G­MDSC in the digest in relation to this project [132, 133].

6
Conclusion
In this project, it was hypothesized if change could be detected in the immune compo­
sition and activation of cells after administration of Nivolumab in GBM. Additionally, that
Nivolumab bound to lymphocytes could be detected in tumor samples. The hypothesis
was investigated by analysing immune cells present within the brain and blood of GBM
patients treated with Nivolumab and Bevacizumab. The results showed that T cells, NK
cells and various myeloid cells was detected in the tumor samples as well in the blood.
Furthermore, it was shown that Nivolumab enters the brain, though it was not shown how
it happens. The administration of Nivolumab over time in the blood showed the frac­
tion of CD8 T cells significant decreased, whereas CD4 T cells showed a tendency to
increase. Furthermore, T cells’ expression of activating molecules did not change over
time, whereas the inhibitory molecules showed to increase over time in the blood. Es­
pecially, the inhibitory molecule TIGIT was present in the digest at high levels as single
detected but also as co­expressed with PD­1, which could represent exhausted T cells.
Moreover, the detection of TRM cells in the digest could indicate a cytotoxic immune
response in the tumor. A clear difference in the blood and digest samples is detected be­
tween the CD56dim CD16+ NK cells and CD56dim CD16­ NK cells. The results indicated
NK cells with cytotoxic functions were active in the blood, whereas NK cells in the digest
indicated to be exhausted along with a small fraction of CD56dim CD16+ NK cells per­
forming degranulation. The presence of myeloid cells in the digest samples were not very
high. The G­MDCSs, pDCs, M1 macrophages and M2 macrophages were observed in
higher levels of the digest compared to the blood. Furthermore, all myeloid cells showed
to be present in the blood at low levels, except DP macrophages, which was detected
at high levels. In general, there seemed to be a difference between cells circulating the
blood and cells siting in the tumor. However, further studies needs to be done in order to
verify functions of cells into depth.
