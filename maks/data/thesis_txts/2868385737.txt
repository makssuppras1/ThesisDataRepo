Abstract 
Integration of RNA-seq in somatic variant calling has the potential to contribute to a more comprehensive 
understanding of disease mechanisms and improve targeted treatment. However, somatic variant calling 
using RNA-seq data faces challenges due to post-transcriptional RNA processing, which modifies transcripts 
from the complementary copy of the DNA template. Tools like RNA-Mutect, RNA-SSNV, and IMAPR attempt 
to filter away false positive variant calls due to RNA processing. This study developed and evaluated the 
pipeline Mutect-IMAPR, which quantifies the recall of somatic variant calling using tumor RNA-seq and 
normal WGS input data. Results indicated no notable differences between Mutect2 and IMAPR 
performances, with only around 10% of DNA variants confirmed via RNA-seq. IMAPR's own reported high 
performance was deduced to be due to stringent filtering and WGS confirmation of RNA variants, differing 
from this study's RNA validation of WGS variants. 
To achieve an improved sensitivity of RNA-seq integration in somatic variant calling, the pipeline Variant 
Extraction and Expression Reporting (VEXER) was developed. VEXER integrates RNA-seq data into standard 
DNA-based variant calling pipelines by annotating DNA VCF files with RNA-level information. This enables 
clinical interpreters to easily access RNA single nucleotide variant (SNV) expression and utilize it in clinical 
decisions. VEXER was applied to a case study of BRAF V600E patients and provided insights into RNA 
expression patterns during targeted BRAF treatment. Results suggested that no RNA coverage observed 
pre-treatment might be associated with a worse treatment response. Results further showed that RNA-seq 
samples tend to decrease in support of the alternative allele in later patient biopsies. It is considered if this 
decrease could be related to the known BRAF resistance mechanism and therapy-induced tumor evolution.  
A broader analysis across 1752 patients revealed a positive correlation between total SNVs in DNA and the 
amount of SNVs expressed in RNA, and significant differences in SNV RNA expression percentages among 
eight cancer types. Some cancers demonstrated lower SNV RNA expression, possibly due to cancer survival 
mechanisms, while others, like ovarian and esophageal cancers, revealed higher expression, potentially 
increasing the SNVs available for targeted treatment. In this project VEXER demonstrates its potential for 
enhancing somatic variant interpretation, contributing to treatment strategies, and guiding future research 
in understanding tumor evolution by analyzing variant expression changes in RNA. Further studies are 
needed to determine if an increased RNA sequencing depth could improve variant calling from RNA-seq as 
well as the coverage of BRAF and increase confidence in the data. 
 
The code is available at the GitHub project page: https://github.com/HelenePetersen/Integration-of-RNA-
and-DNA-omics-data-to-support-clinical-practice/

1 INTRODUCTION 
Integration of omics data could provide a more comprehensive understanding of disease mechanisms 
resulting in more accurate diagnoses and targeted treatments. Precision medicine has initially focused on 
DNA-based technologies and identification, but its full potential cannot be realized without the RNA 
transcriptome (Horton et al., 2024). In clinical practice today, DNA and RNA sequencing data are used to 
answer different questions related to the genome and transcriptome. From DNA, disease-causing variants 
are identified from germline or somatic mutations, while RNA is the key to assessing gene expression 
changes and detection of gene fusions (Barbitoff et al., 2024; Maher et al., 2009; Wang et al., 2009). 
Germline mutations are carried in germ cells (egg and sperm) and can be passed on to the next generation, 
while somatic mutations are new changes to the germline background that occur in somatic cells and only 
affect the cells originating from the cell where the mutation occurred (Gilchrist, 2024; Hurle, 2024). By 
including RNA in variant calling, it can ideally be a resource to reliably distinguish mutations that are 
expressed and can affect cellular functions (Long et al., 2022). A study by Horton et al., 2024 found that 
RNA sequencing identified previously undetected germline pathogenic variants, resolved inconclusive 
results, and strengthened confidence in classifications with limited evidence from likely pathogenic, to 
pathogenic. 
In a pilot study, I tested somatic variant calling tools on RNA-seq data, to see if current methods could 
effectively use RNA for variant detection. The results, which are described in A. Supplementary Material, 
showed big differences between the tools, leading me to question whether the patient data used was ideal. 
This made it clear that the findings needed to be validated on a larger scale. As part of the pilot study, I 
looked more closely at a subset of variants to understand when and why a tool would call a variant—or fail 
to. One key issue observed was the very low RNA coverage compared to at the DNA level, which often did 
not provide enough support for a variant to be confidently called. This raised the question: is there enough 
data available to predict whether a variant will be expressed? To explore this, it would be useful to gather 
information about the total RNA coverage for each variant and its support for the alternative allele. Based 
on these findings, this project focuses on the three main aims: 
1. Understand and describe variant propagation from DNA to RNA. 
2. Quantify variant recall in RNA with respect to DNA, as well as potential in improving sensitivity from 
combined analysis. 
3. Propose a method for variant annotation that takes into account both DNA and RNA variant 
information. 
1.1 SOMATIC VARIANT CALLING FROM RNA-SEQ 
To shed light on the status of research within somatic variant calling from RNA-seq data, recently published 
tools attempting this and their authors’ considerations of the major obstacles within the area are 
presented. RNA-MuTect, RNA-SSNV and IMAPR, are all tools published in recent years, trying to perform 
somatic variant calling from RNA-seq data instead of the commonly used DNA-seq data. There have been 
reports that when applying a standard somatic mutation calling pipeline developed for DNA to RNA data, 
65% of DNA-based mutations are not detected in the RNA and 92% of RNA-based mutations are not found 
in the DNA (Yizhak et al., 2019). Reasons for this could be that RNA data can have poor read depth 
uniformity due to variation in expression levels (Xu, 2018). Hence, if genes are highly expressed there can 
be high read support by RNA but if the genes are lowly expressed, the coverage might not be sufficient to 
detect mutations (Xu, 2018; Yizhak et al., 2019). Yizhak et al., 2019 reports for a typical RNA sample only

55% of the transcriptome has sufficient coverage to detect mutations. Tang et al., 2024 believe the false 
discoveries in RNA variant calling are associated with lower read depth at the variant site than adjacent 
nucleotides and determined a read depth surrounding the variant site to exclude sites with uneven read 
coverage. Similarly, RNA-Mutect only considers mutations supported by at least 3 or 4 read coverage 
(Yizhak et al., 2019). The main limitations for variant calling at the RNA level are believed to be due to 
alternative splicing, allele-specific expression1, RNA editing and post-transcriptional processing (Long et al., 
2022; Quinones-Valdez et al., 2022; Xu, 2018). Furthermore, RNA has lower accuracy than DNA, due to 
increased error rate during reverse transcription and alignment errors near splicing junctions (Xu, 2018). 
RNA-MuTect, RNA-SSNV and IMAPR apply different strategies in their pipelines to overcome the obstacles 
in RNA variant calling. In 2019 RNA-MuTect was published from the Broad Institute. The high number of 
mutations only detected in RNA when applying the standard somatic mutation pipeline, was addressed by 
applying several filtering steps, including removal of alignment and sequencing errors and RNA editing sites 
(Yizhak et al., 2019). On a validation dataset RNA-MuTect achieved a sensitivity of 0.72 and precision of 
0.87 (Yizhak et al., 2019). 
In 2022 the tool RNA-SSNV was published, this tool focuses on somatic single nucleotide variant 
identification. Long et al., 2022 argues that RNA-MuTect is limited by its unsatisfying precision-recall 
performance. RNA-SSNV includes a multi-filtering strategy to remove doubtful calls and a supervised 
machine learning model to identify somatic mutations and artifacts. The area under the curve (AUC) for the 
RNA-SSNV precision-recall curve was 0.94. (Long et al., 2022) 
The tool Integrated Mutation Analysis Pipeline for RNA-seq data (IMAPR) was published in May 2024. 
IMAPR differs from existing methods by applying a strategy to avoid both bias towards individual aligners 
and variant callers, that could otherwise potentially result in high rates of false discoveries. To account for 
the documented low accuracy (high amounts of false-positives) when calling variants from RNA-seq, IMAPR 
has implemented eighteen mutation filters to reduce the false discoveries, including filtering of RNA editing 
events. IMAPR reports having the best performance compared to RNA-MuTect and RNA-SSNV. IMAPR 
achieves an F-score of 0.372 compared to 0.317 and 0.339 for RNA-MuTect and RNA_SSNV, respectively. 
An ROC analysis with an AUC of 0.950 was reported for IMAPR compared to AUC = 0.913 for RNA-SSNV, 
while RNA-MuTect has a true positive rate of 0.844 and false positive rate of 0.224. (Tang et al., 2024) 
1.2 PROCESSING OF RNA 
To elaborate on the difficulties in calling variants from RNA and how they arise, I will in the following 
sections go into depth with current knowledge of RNA processing in areas selected to highlight how RNA 
can deviate from its DNA template. The genetic code stored in DNA is transcribed to pre-mRNA, but 
through processing, the transcripts are altered from the complementary copy of DNA to mature mRNA. 
These alterations result in a diversity of protein functions but errors in the processes might be disease-
causing. This raises the question of how the variant calling will distinguish RNA diversity from RNA variants.  
Alternative transcription initiation 
The core promoter is a region that directs transcription initiation by serving as a binding platform for RNA 
polymerase II and its associated general transcription factors. The core promoter is located upstream and 
downstream of the transcription start site (TSS), where transcription is typically initiated (Haberle & Stark, 
2018). The DNA elements making up the core promoters can vary considerably (Kadonaga, 2002). In a study

and evolvable CpG-rich promoters. 92% of protein-coding transcriptional units that have at least two 
recognized TSSs are predicted to use different start codons (Carninci et al., 2006). Carninci et al., 2006

exons or varying lengths of 5’ untranslated regions (5’-UTR), which can influence translational regulation 
(de Klerk & A.C. ’t Hoen, 2015). Enhancers are cis regulatory elements (CREs)2 increasing the transcriptional 
activity of a gene by recruiting transcription factors and RNA polymerase II (Haberle & Stark, 2018; 
Pennacchio et al., 2013). Enhancers also seem to influence the use of alternative tissue-specific TSS (de 
Klerk & A.C. ’t Hoen, 2015). 
Alternative splicing 
Richard J. Roberts and Phillip A. Sharp were the first to make the discovery of split genes, that the genetic 
information in a gene is discontinuously organized. They documented different segments of RNA joined 
together during the synthesis of mature RNA (Berget et al., 1977; Chow et al., 1977). In 1978 Walter Gilbert 
suggested describing the regions as introns which alternate those expressed, exons (Gilbert, 1978). The 
split gene discovery led to what is now known as alternative splicing, describing the process of which 
transcripts end up containing different subsets of exons. de Klerk & A.C. ’t Hoen, 2015 describes in their 
review of alternative mRNA processing that it is not clear how the spliceosome decides which exons to 
include in the transcript. They further point out that the selection of a splice site depends on factors 
including the presence of splicing regulators, strength of splice sites and structure of exon-intron junctions. 
Supporting splice factors can enhance or suppress the alternative splicing by interfering with elements such 
as splice donor and acceptor sites, branch sites, polypyrimidine tracts and other sequence motifs (de Klerk 
& A.C. ’t Hoen, 2015). Transcriptome analysis has shown that most protein coding genes have one 
transcript dominating the expression level and often the same across tissues (Gonzàlez-Porta et al., 2013). 
Alternative polyadenylation 
The first detection of poly(A) tail synthesization dates to 1960 (Edmonds & Abrams, 1960). Colgan & 
Manley, 1997 document in their review of mRNA polyadenylation, the progress done from the 70’s to 90’s. 
Describing how polyadenylation was identified as a post-transcriptional modification to the 3’ termini and 
how transcription was seen to proceed past the polyadenylation site, revealing that transcription 
termination did not create the poly(A) tails. In the mid 90’s results showed that endonucleolytic cleavage of 
the synthesizing RNA and poly(A) addition occurs in a coupled reaction and is now together known as 
polyadenylation and closely related to transcription termination (Colgan & Manley, 1997; Tian & Manley, 
2016). In a Nature review by Tian & Manley, 2016 of alternative polyadenylation, polyadenylation is 
described to be controlled by CREs which defines the strength of a polyadenylation site (PAS). A gene can 
produce transcripts with multiple PASs leading to distinct mRNA isoforms, this is known as alternative 
polyadenylation (APA) (Tian & Manley, 2016). In Figure 1 A, tandem 3’-UTR isoforms are generated by an 
APA in the terminal exon, which is commonly known as 3’-UTR APA (Mitschka & Mayr, 2022). Most APA 
sites are in the 3’-UTR region, which contains CREs involved in various aspects of the mRNA metabolism. 
APA sites in the 3’-UTR can considerably impact the post-transcriptional gene regulation, including mRNA 
stability, translation, nuclear export, cellular and protein localization. In Figure 1 B, APA sites are located 
upstream of the last exon, most commonly in the introns. Upstream APA sites produce alternative terminal

RNA editing 
RNA editing is a post-transcriptional modification that alters the sequence at the RNA level (Licht & Jantsch, 
2016). This modification was first described in 1988, during an activity that unwinds double stranded RNA it 
was observed that base-pairing properties were permanently altered. The modification was documented to 
make irreversible change of many, but not all adenosine residues into inosines in Xenopus laevis (Bass & 
Weintraub, 1988). In mammals two major types of RNA editing are present: adenosine to inosine (A-to-I) 
and cytidine to uracil (C-to-U) (Licht & Jantsch, 2016). The A-to-I editing mostly occurs within introns and 
UTRs of protein coding genes, it can affect RNA stability, localization, regulation of gene expression, 
translation and produce or delete splice sites affecting the production of protein isoforms (Christofi & 
Zaravinos, 2019; Licht & Jantsch, 2016). RNA editing is simply described as reversing harmful mutations or 
providing protein abundance, but its emergence has been debated (Christofi & Zaravinos, 2019). 
Nuclear export 
The 5’-capping, splicing and polyadenylation are coupled with nuclear export, ensuring only mature 
transcripts reach the cytoplasmic translation (Stewart, 2019). The transport through the nuclear pores is 
mediated by nuclear export factors, which are afterwards removed to prevent transcripts from returning to 
the nucleus (Stewart, 2019). Nuclear processing steps are involved in delivery to ribosomes, regulating 
mRNA stability and mediating translation (Stewart, 2019). 
Alternative translation initiation 
Regulation of the translation impacts both the abundance of a protein but also the amino acid composition 
through use of different translation initiation sites (TIS) (de Klerk & A.C. ’t Hoen, 2015). In 1984 the Kozak 
consensus sequence was discovered as the optimal context for initiation of translation in mammals (Kozak,

mechanism of initiation, initiate translation at the first AUG codon encountered, but if the context is weak 
the ribosome might bypass the first AUG and start initiation downstream, this is known as leaky scanning

or truncated protein isoforms will be created (Kozak, 2005). However, if the TIS is out-of-frame with the 
pORF the effects can be various; block translation of the pORF reducing protein production, or production 
of different peptides (de Klerk & A.C. ’t Hoen, 2015; Kozak, 2005). In a study using global translation 
initiation sequencing, more than half of the detected TIS used AUG as the translation initiator, while around 
half of the transcripts contained multiple TIS (Lee et al., 2012). 
 
Fusion 
Structural changes in the genome can be caused by breakage of DNA and afterwards erroneous rejoining 
and replication, producing altered transcripts and fusion proteins (Paratala et al., 2016). This was first 
observed in 1960 when the Philadelphia chromosome was discovered (Rowley, 1973). The Philadelphia 
chromosome was a result of genetic material exchange between chromosome 9 and 22, where genomic 
rearrangements caused generation of the fusion gene BCR-ABL (Wong & Witte, 2004). Genomic 
rearrangements include inversions, tandem duplicates, and deletions that occur within one single or two 
adjacent genes. Larger genomic rearrangements can also occur as inter- and intra-chromosomal 
rearrangements. Apart from genomic rearrangements, fusions can occur at the transcriptional level by 
trans- and cis-splicing. Trans-splicing splices exons from two separately encoded transcripts, while cis-
splicing is splicing of adjacent genes (Dai et al., 2018). Fusion events result in activation, reduction, or 
elimination of the original gene function, or generate a chimeric (fusion) protein (Paratala et al., 2016). 
 
Variant impacts 
Figure 2 illustrates different impacts that variants can have depending on their location in the genomic 
sequence. Variants in the gene regulatory domain like promoters, will often disrupt binding of trans-acting 
factors and reduce the expression of a gene (Bagger et al., 2024). Changes in promoter regions and TSSs 
cause alternative transcription initiation (de Klerk & A.C. ’t Hoen, 2015). Variants located in splice donor or 
acceptor sites can introduce errors in the process of alternative splicing. Variants in the introns can also 
disrupt splicing by interfering with enhancer or silencer motifs, potentially caused by RNA-editing or APA. 
Variants in a coding exon can result in nonsense or frameshift variants leading to premature translation 
termination and shortening of the encoded protein (Bagger et al., 2024). Missense variants in a coding exon 
disrupt protein function by reducing enzymatic activity, stability, localization, or structure assembly (Bagger 
et al., 2024). Changes in the coding exon could be caused by APA, different TIS and fusions. 
To avoid disease-causing impacts, control mechanisms exist to degrade abnormal transcripts and proteins. 
Following deadenylase activity on the mRNA, resulting in poly(A) tail shortening, the mRNA can be 
degraded by two pathways (Garneau et al., 2007). Either decapping removes the 5'-cap, or the exosome 
attacks the now unprotected 3'-end, resulting in degradation (Garneau et al., 2007). The exosome has 
important roles in regulating mRNA metabolism. By performing pre-mRNA quality control, 3'-end 
processing, and efficient transcriptional termination, the exosome affects transcription, splicing, 
termination, and export. The proteasome controls cellular protein levels through indirect control of gene 
transcription and degradation of irregular and misfolded proteins (Komili & Silver, 2008). During 
translation, nonsense-mediated decay detects transcripts with premature termination codons, arising from 
mutations, frameshift, and extended 3’-UTRs, and triggers degradation (Garneau et al., 2007).

1.3 RNA AND DNA SEQUENCING TECHNOLOGY 
Variant calling is currently mainly done using DNA-seq data, so it is important to understand the differences 
between DNA and RNA at the level of sequencing method. Presented below are the specific sequencing 
methods used by the department of genomic medicine (GM) to perform RNA and DNA sequencing. DNA 
sequencing for variant interpretation was previously done in GM using whole-exome sequencing (WES) but 
is at present performed using whole genome sequencing (WGS). 
WGS was performed using Illumina DNA PCR free prep and were paired-end sequenced. This method 
eliminates the need for PCR amplification during library preparation and PCR-induced bias is removed, 
resulting in highly accurate sequence information. In the WGS PCR-Free library preparation workflow, 
tagmentation combines tagging and DNA fragmentation in a single reaction, see Figure 3. When the bead-
linked transposomes are saturated with DNA, further tagmentation is blocked, resulting in consistent 
library yield and uniform library insert sizes (Illumina, 2023). RNA sequencing was done using Illumina 
TruSeq Stranded Total RNA Library Prep Kit and run as paired-end sequencing. Whole-transcriptome or 
total RNA-seq enables expression analysis of both coding and noncoding RNA. See Figure 4 for total RNA-
seq library preparation workflow. First rRNA is removed to minimize ribosomal contamination and 
maximize uniquely mapped reads covering the RNA of interest (Illumina, 2017c). The RNA is fragmented, 
then copied using reverse transcriptase and primers into the first strand cDNA. After the second strand 
cDNA synthesis, the 3’-ends are adenylated to prevent ligating to each other during adapter ligation. The 
adapters contain a corresponding T to the A on the cDNA strand, ensuring complementary overhang for 
ligating the adapter to the fragment. The final cDNA library is obtained after PCR fragment enrichment and 
purification (Illumina, 2017b).

1.4 SEQUENCING IN CLINICAL PRACTICE 
To have a better background for designing improvements to the somatic variant calling pipeline at GM, I 
will describe some of the considerations regarding designing and running a clinical pipeline used for 
sequencing data. Bagger et al., 2024 argue that WGS is preferred over panel and exome sequencing for 
genetic analysis. WGS detects more variants both in noncoding parts but also in exons due to better 
mapping quality. WGS can further be revisited and reanalyzed if new clinical insights or disease causes are 
discovered along the patient journey. WGS generates much more raw data, which results in variant files 
being between 13,000- and 24-fold larger than those from gene panels and exomes. The large amount of 
data, sets requirements for computational and storage infrastructure, along with a need to process data 
within a clinical timeframe ranging from patients with stable conditions to neonates and patients with 
unstable and severe conditions. The data handling pipelines are automated using pipeline managers like 
Snakemake or Nextflow and run in parallel on a high-performance computer cluster to increase speed of 
processing. The two main bioinformatics steps in the data analysis pipeline are read mapping and variant 
calling. The open-source Genome Analysis Tool Kit (GATK) has established standardized workflows of 
variant calling, while commercial solutions like DRAGEN and Sentieon have created hardware-accelerated 
solutions. (Bagger et al., 2024) 
Sequencing in clinical practice is an intradisciplinary accomplishment, see Figure 6. Somatic variant calling 
requires a whole blood (normal) sample and a tumor sample, germline variants are then subtracted from

The production pipeline at GM performs alignment of RNA-seq data using the tool STAR (Spliced Transcripts

which due to genomic variations and sequencing errors, contain mismatches, insertions, and deletions. In 
addition, alignment of RNA has the challenge of reconstructing the spliced RNAs, to perform reference 
mapping (Dobin et al., 2013). The two alignment algorithms are presented below. 
2.2.1 
BWA-MEM algorithm 
BWA-MEM single-sequencing alignment follows the seed-and-extend paradigm. BWA-MEM seeds7 an 
alignment with supermaximal exact matches (SMEMs), which at each query position finds the longest exact 
match covering the position, see Figure 9. While seeding, seeds are connected into chains that are close to 
each other and colinear. Chain filtering aims to reduce unsuccessful seed extension later. Short chains are 
filtered out which are largely contained within a long chain and much worse than the long chain. (Li, 2013) 
Seeds are ranked by the length of the chains they belong to and by the seed length. Seeds are dropped if 
they are contained in an already found alignment or extended if it potentially leads to a new alignment. 
BWA-MEM uses a gap extension penalty to avoid extension through a poorly aligned region with good 
flanking alignment. BWA-MEM chooses between local and end-to-end alignments by comparing the 
difference between the best score reaching the end and the best local alignment score. (Li, 2013) 
During paired-end mapping, BWA-MEM processes a batch of reads at a time and estimates the mean and 
variance of the insert size distribution. The top 100 hits of forward and reverse reads are checked and if the 
mate is unmapped in a window [𝜇−4𝜎, 𝜇+ 4𝜎] from the mapped mate, the Smith-Waterman (SW) 
alignment is performed. Pairing is performed on hits found from the single-sequence alignment and SW 
rescuing. BWA-MEM computes the distance of the first and second read in a pair if they are in the right 
orientation, otherwise the distance is set to infinity. The scoring of a pair includes the SW score, matching 
score and probability of observing an insert size larger than the distance. The pair that maximizes the 
pairing score is chosen as the final alignments for both ends. (Li, 2013)

applies machine learning to correct base quality scores for systematic errors, arising from biochemical 
processes during library preparation and sequencing, or defects in chips or sequencer. (GATK Broad

2.4 VARIANT CALLING AND VCF FILE FORMAT 
After finalizing data preprocessing, the variant calling can be performed. The variant call format (VCF) is the 
standardized file format used to store sequence variation, this includes SNPs, insertions, deletions, and 
structural variants. The VCF file consists of a header section with meta-information. Each header line starts 
with “##” except the tab-delimited field definition line, that starts with “#”, see Figure 11 (Danecek et al., 
2011). The field definition contains according to Danecek et al., 2011 eight mandatory columns: 
Chromosome (CHROM), a 1-based position of the start of the variant (POS), unique 
identifiers of the variant (ID), the reference allele (REF), a comma-separated list of alternate 
non-reference alleles (ALT), a phred-scaled quality score (QUAL), site filtering information 
(FILTER) and a semicolon separated list of additional, user extensible annotation (INFO) 
(Danecek et al., 2011). 
After the mandatory columns FORMAT and sample ID columns can be present, FORMAT defines genotype 
information separated by colons. The sample ID column values are separated by colons matching the 
genotype information, which includes genotype (GT), phase set (PS), read depth (DP), genotype likelihood 
(GL), and genotype quality (GQ). Usually, VCF files are stored in a compressed binary form and an index file 
is created which allows for fast data retrieval of variants. (Danecek et al., 2011)

2.4.1 
Mutect2 
The tool Mutect2 is recommended for calling short somatic variants according to GATK best practices 
workflow (GATK Broad Institute, 2024e). Somatic mutations might have low allele fractions and can 
therefore be difficult to distinguish from sample preparation artifacts, sequencing and mapping errors. 
Mutect2 assigns each site’s (genomic position) read pileup8 a log odds for somatic activity, if it exceeds a 
threshold, it is considered an active site (Benjamin, Sato, Cibulskis, et al., 2019). An active site has sufficient 
evidence of variation for Mutect2 to proceed with local reassembly and realignment (Benjamin et al., 
2021). Mutect2 then determines the assembly intervals which surround each active site (Benjamin, Sato, 
Cibulskis, et al., 2019). The local assembly creates a de Bruijn-like graph of kmer reads, see Figure 12, and 
prunes the chains from the graph using an adaptive pruning algorithm (Benjamin, Sato, Cibulskis, et al., 
2019; Benjamin & GATK team, 2018). In the first round of the pruning algorithm, the empirical error rate is 
determined using chains below the active region likelihood ratio threshold, and then in the second round it 
uses the learned error rate to recalculate the likelihoods to use for pruning. Pruning in this adaptive way is 
effective for samples with non-uniform read depth like RNA data (Benjamin, Sato, Cibulskis, et al., 2019).

2.5 VCF COMPARISON 
VCF comparison covers the comparison of VCF files from Mutect-IMAPR with the standard DNA-based 
procedure which is here referred to as the true VCF file. The true VCF file is created by input of both WGS 
==PAGE:24==
HELENE BRASCH LIND PETERSEN 
29-01-25 
Integration of RNA and DNA omics data to support clinical practice 
Page 24 of 82 
 
tumor and normal samples to the variant caller Strelka2, and the files are present in the GM phase 1 data 
repository. Strelka2 is by GM at present preferred over Mutect2 for the standard DNA-based variant calling, 
when both a tumor and normal sample are present, because of reduced computation time. Strelka2 uses a 
variant probability model and a final empirical variant scoring (EVS) producing the probability of an 
erroneous variant call (Kim et al., 2018a, 2018b). The EVS score is partly based on pre-trained random 
forest models, with important features being genotype probability, root-mean-square mapping quality, 
strand bias, and fraction of reads consistent with locus haplotype model (Kim et al., 2018a). Variant filtering 
is primarily based on the EVS score, assigning LowEVS filter if below threshold, but if tumor depths are 
below two, a somatic variant will be assigned a LowDepth filter (Kim et al., 2018b). If the locus depth is 
greater than 3x the mean chromosome depth in the normal sample a HighDepth filter is assigned. When no 
variant filtering is applied, the PASS filter is assigned. 
To make the fairest comparison, the DNA (WGS) VCF file is subset according to the RNA coverage, so only 
variants covered by at least one RNA read remain in the true VCF file, see Code block 10. The coverage of 
each RNA file is found using the genomecov -bg command from bedtools, see Figure 13. The true VCF 
file is then subset using the coverage information as the regions-file (-R) for bcftools view. Bcftools norm 
apply left-alignment and normalization (Li et al., 2024). The definition of VCF normalization is according to 
Tan et al., 2015: 
A VCF entry is normalized if and only if it is left aligned and parsimonious. A VCF entry is left

if the entry has the shortest allele length among all VCF entries representing the same

Normalization ensures that variants in different VCF files are represented in the same way, so the

2.6 PIPELINE DESIGN USING SNAKEMAKE 
The workflow management system Snakemake is argued to contain the necessary properties for a data 
analysis to become reproducible, adaptable, and transparent. A full reproducible data analysis must be 
automated, scalable and portable. To ensure transparency, a data analysis must be readable and well-
==PAGE:26==
HELENE BRASCH LIND PETERSEN 
29-01-25 
Integration of RNA and DNA omics data to support clinical practice 
Page 26 of 82 
 
documented, the parameters, code, and components in the software should be traceable through all 
involved steps, to ensure that the validity of the results can be fully assessed. A valid and reproducible data 
analysis is more valuable to the scientific community if the analysis can be reused, and therefore its ability 
to extend, replace or modify steps, and adjust parameters. Adaptability falls in the intersection of 
reproducibility and transparency, where scalability, portability, and readability are important, see Figure 14. 
(Mölder et al., 2021) 
 
Figure 14. Hierarchy of properties to ensure sustainable data analysis. From (Mölder et al., 2021). 
A Snakemake workflow is saved in a Snakefile where each step is represented as a rule that has a set of 
input files and a shell command describing how to obtain the output files. Each rule is a job that is executed 
to generate the defined output files. Snakemake goes on recursively, searching for the job to execute that 
generates the needed input file for the current job, and checks if the file is already present. Snakemake’s 
search for dependencies among the rules is saved in a directed acyclic graph of jobs (DAG), showing the 
execution plan. The ability to skip execution and directly use the already generated output files reduces 
computation time. (Mölder et al., 2021)

3.1 CHANGE OF GENERAL FUNCTIONALITIES IN SNAKEMAKE  
For the final development steps of the pipelines, the input directory is changed from a personal working directory (where the test dataset was moved 
to) to the GM phase 1 data repository, where the BRAF patient data is located. This directory change resulted in some challenges regarding consistency 
of file naming patterns and file types. The main challenges were the following: 
- 
Not all patients have a WGS sample, many are sequenced as WES. 
- 
VCF files had either been performed using Strelka2 or Mutect2. 
- 
Different biopsy preservation methods are used. 
- 
Multiple tumor WGS/WES and RNA-seq samples can be present for each patient.  
Compared to the test dataset which only had one tumor WGS and its matching RNA sample, it was now necessary to ensure that the WGS and RNA 
data from the same biopsy were matched. I was considering using the newest samples, since if the sequence analysis was redone, this should be the 
most recent knowledge about the patient. However, this did not seem to be without flaws. If e.g. two WGS samples but only one RNA sample are 
present, there would be a risk of matching WGS and RNA data from two different biopsies. I chose instead to design the Snakemake workflow to run 
per patient-ID_glass-number (PidGn). If three sequencing analyses have been done for a patient, three biopsies have been performed, and each are 
assigned a glass number. Instead of excluding data by selecting some of the samples, I will in this way ensure getting the VCF file that has been used to 
recommend the BRAF target therapy and match the correct WGS and RNA data. For the normal sample input, it is not enough just to run it per PidGn 
because the normal sample will not have the same glass-number as the tumor samples. Therefore, it is first ensured that both the WGS and the RNA 
tumor samples for the same glass-number are available, and then the pipeline is run for those PidGn that, when split by a hyphen, matches the normal 
sample patient ID. The principles behind the modifications can be seen in Code block 15. The implementation of the two main pipelines is a bit 
different, see GitHub project page for further information.

recall is extracted for Mutect2 and IMAPR, see Figure 17. The recall is chosen as the performance metric 
because it tells how many of the true DNA variants are found by the variant caller. It should be noted that 
when referring to the true variants it is the variants found in the Strelka2 generated VCF file and the 
variants are not further validated. The VCF comparison could be improved if the ground truth VCF file for 
each patient was known. The performance output is separated into four different categories, indels, MNPs 
(Multi-Nucleotide Polymorphisms), SNVs, which are different types of variants, and record, which is the 
performance across all variant types. The performance of Mutect2 and IMAPR is very similar, with an 
overall recall below or around 0.12. This similar performance can be justified by some of the insights I 
learned when implementing the IMAPR code. The IMAPR algorithm focuses on removing the false positive 
variant calls and does not do much differently from Mutect2 regarding the variant calling step. In my 
opinion the main difference between the two is the realignment step done with HISAT2 after the first 
variant calling and repeated Mutect2 variant calling. Therefore, if Mutect2 cannot find the variants, it is 
unlikely that IMAPR will perform better when considering the recall performance.

3.2.3 
Performance comparison with original IMAPR pipeline (ORG-IMAPR) 
The ORG-IMAPR tool was selected to be tested because the authors claim it outperforms other tools 
designed for RNA-seq variant calling. The performance of IMAPR seen in this study is not persuasive enough 
to recommend it for variant calling from RNA-seq data. Tang et al., 2024 identified with ORG-IMAPR a total 
of 9203 candidate RNA somatic variants (RNA-SMs) from 100 cancer samples covering lung, head, and neck 
cancers, where 7990 (86.8%) were confirmed by high-coverage WGS (Tang et al., 2024). Tang et al., 2024 
report a recall of around 0.64 for validation datasets, which is much higher than what I experienced in this 
study when running the implementation with Mutect-IMAPR. It was realized that Tang et al., 2024 evaluate 
the variant calling performance differently. I consider the variants called from DNA to be true if they have 
RNA coverage and evaluate how many of these variants can be confirmed by calling variants from RNA. 
While Tang et al., 2024 first call their RNA variants, then attempt to filter away the false positives and finally 
check how many of the remaining variants can be found in DNA. Their reported overlap between WGS, 
WES, and RNA-SMs (output from ORG-IMAPR) can be seen in Figure 19. The aim of my analysis was to test 
how many DNA variants could be confirmed by RNA and the filtering step of ORG-IMAPR was therefore 
considered irrelevant. It has, due to the missing filtering step, not been possible to confirm if the Mutect-
IMAPR implementation could have reached a similar recall as ORG-IMAPR, by evaluating how many RNA 
variants were confirmed by WGS. 
  
Figure 19. a) Overview of which ORG-IMAPR mutation filters filter away RNA-SMs and b) Overlap between RNA, WGS and WXS data. 
From (Tang et al., 2024). 
From my study of BRAF patients, six unique patients with a total of 9 true VCF files were tested. The total 
somatic variants from the true VCF files when considering those with at least 1 RNA read is around 25,000, 
giving an average per patient around 3200 variants. The total somatic variants with at least 4 RNA reads 
coverage from the true VCF files is around 12,000, giving an average per patient around 1400. For ORG-
IMAPR, a total of 9203 RNA-SMs were found across 100 samples, giving an average of 9 somatic variants 
found per patient. This indicates that ORG-IMAPR considers very few variants per patient to be a real 
variant from RNA, compared to the numbers of true variants I see from the DNA VCF files. With 7990 out of 
9203 RNA-SMs confirmed by WGS, the tool appears to perform very well. However, my interpretation is 
that the filtering is so hard on the RNA data that it looks like almost all the variants called can be confirmed 
in WGS, but in reality we never confirm more than around the 10% found by Mutect2. The IMAPR filtering 
strategy is applied to overcome the challenges of RNA-seq data diversity, but this study shows that IMAPR 
does not perform better than Mutect2 when considering the use-case of being able to confirm if DNA 
variants are expressed in RNA. IMAPR has therefore not solved the obstacles related to accurate variant 
calling from RNA-seq data.

3.2.4 
Why does the variant calling from RNA-seq not work? 
With an inexperienced mindset it could be considered straightforward to just change the input data from 
DNA to RNA and expect to see variants called which are truly expressed. This is however not the case, and 
we know that several processing steps diversify the genetic code from DNA to mature mRNA. In addition, 
more technical deviations from the DNA-seq data, not touched upon in detail in this project, cover reverse 
transcription during RNA-seq and alignment errors near splicing junctions. I believe the focus of future tools 
trying to call somatic variants from RNA-seq data should rather consider RNA to be supplementing 
information and not a stand-alone tool to replace DNA-based variant calling. We know from current tools 
that it is possible to filter away false positives due to e.g. RNA-editing, but how about improving the 
fraction of true variants confirmed by RNA. This could potentially be done by exploring parameter 
optimization of Mutect2 of selected parameters. A limitation to this is, what is a true RNA variant? Filtering 
away DNA variants found by Strelka2 with low or no RNA coverage gives my best estimate for true RNA 
variants. 
The low sequencing depth observed in the pilot study has not been investigated further in this part of the 
study, but it cannot be excluded that a higher sequencing depth could provide the information necessary to 
improve the variant calling from RNA. The RNA sequencing depth is decided for use in gene expression 
analysis or fusion gene detection, and not for variant calling. 
3.3 INVESTIGATION OF DNA AND RNA COMBINED ANALYSIS 
If it is not possible with high recall to predict variants directly from RNA sequencing data, then let us add 
the RNA information in another way. This is the motivation behind my design of the VEXER pipeline. The 
pipeline is designed to extract from a DNA tumor and normal VCF file, positions where somatic SNVs have 
been found and include the information from the RNA level. SNVs are chosen for RNA coverage lookup in 
VEXER because it is the simplest variant format. It is the hope that SNVs with low evidence from the DNA 
could be confirmed present or absent by RNA information and possibly help to point in the direction of the 
correct targeted therapy for that patient. By collaborating with GM interpreters, who formulate the genetic 
clinical report (mentioned in section 1.4), about the pipeline design, it is believed that this extra layer of 
information can be beneficial in clinical practice. 
In Figure 20 an overview of the folder structure for VEXER is provided. All files with a white background 
except the metadata files are used as input to the Snakemake pipeline. The script files are executed in the 
snakemake rules of the Snakefile, described further in section 3.3.1. The yaml files specify input folders, 
where to put the output and error logs, and define Snakemake command line arguments. After the 
Snakemake pipeline has finished, the summarize_results.sh is run, it is described further in section 3.3.1. 
Patient data are investigated in the BRAF and Cancer type analysis, using the supplementing scripts in the 
corresponding color shown in Figure 20. These analyses are presented in section 3.3.2 and 3.3.3. Further 
information about the specific scripts and metadata files can be found on the GitHub project page.

Pileup format 
The samtools mpileup command takes a BAM file as input and produces a pileup format from the 
alignment, see Code block 16 above. Each line in the pileup format represents reads at a single genomic 
position, the line is tab-separated and contains the following information (Li, 2024):  
• 
Chromosome name 
• 
1-based position on the chromosome 
• 
Reference base, N if no reference is provided 
• 
Number of reads covering the position 
• 
Read bases 
A “^” character followed by alignment mapping quality tells that this position is the first covered by 
a read. A single character indicates base and strand of alignment, see Figure 22. If an insertion is 
present a “+” is followed by an integer giving the length of the insertion and the inserted sequence. 
Similarly, for a deletion a “-” is followed by the length and the deleted reference bases, and a “*” 
will indicate the deleted bases. A “$”indicates that this is the last base covered by a read. (Li, 2024) 
• 
Base qualities encoded as ASCII characters

3.3.1.3 
Deciding threshold for RNA_PASS 
After running the VEXER pipeline on the 10 patients from the test dataset, the SNVs were first investigated 
by plotting each patient against the quality metrics; PERCENTAGE, QUALITY_SUPPORT_AVG, RNA_TOTAL, 
and RNA_ALT_COUNT, see plots in C. Supplementary Material. From the percentage plot Figure 29, most 
SNVs are located close to 100% support, and in Figure 31 and Figure 32 there is a tendency for the SNVs to 
only have one total count and one count of the alternative allele. This could indicate that many of the SNVs 
only have one supporting read and a few total counts resulting in a high percentage of support. The 
relationship between thresholding the SNVs on the different quality metrics and the decrease in SNVs was 
investigated. The total number of SNVs was plotted against different thresholds for the parameters: 
RNA_TOTAL, RNA_ALT_COUNT and PERCENTAGE, see plots in D. Supplementary Material. It was observed 
that both for the RNA_TOTAL and RNA_ALT_COUNT, that the number of SNVs decreases fast and then 
stabilizes. When thresholding the PERCENTAGE, the number of SNVs keeps decreasing. I decided to assign a 
filter label RNA_PASS to those SNVs that show a higher degree of RNA support towards the alternative 
allele. The threshold for assigning the RNA_PASS label was decided to be where the differential quotient is 
45 degrees for RNA_TOTAL and RNA_ALT_COUNT. This was calculated using the formula for average rate of 
change of the function over an interval, see equation 3.1. A 45-degree slope corresponds to an equal 
change where ℎ= 𝑓(𝑥+ ℎ) −𝑓(𝑥). Since it will not make a difference if the threshold is a decimal 
compared to an integer, the change has been calculated using h = 1.

3.3.1.4 
Filter categories 
With the threshold for RNA_PASS determined, four filter categories are created to inform the GM 
interpreter of the degree of RNA support for each variant. It was considered if only SNVs with the FILTER 
output PASS from Strelka2 should be in the analysis. However, it was decided to increase the amount of 
information the most and therefore not filter out any SNVs based on Strelka2’s algorithm. In this way it is 
possible to have a high support of the variant from RNA-seq data while it might not be classified as PASS 
from Strelka2, and this information could be beneficial for the GM interpreters. Each of the categories are 
described below using as an example, the FILTER output LowEVS from Strelka2 with the different RNA 
filters appended. 
1. LowEVS;RNA_PASS 
o 
SNVs with this filter show high support for the variant in RNA according to the threshold 
criteria. 
2. LowEVS;RNA_NOPASS 
o 
SNVs with this filter do not have enough RNA support to pass the threshold criteria. 
3. LowEVS;RNA_NOALT 
o 
SNVs with this filter have coverage of RNA but do not show any reads supporting the 
alternative allele in the RNA. 
4. LowEVS 
o 
SNVs with this filter do not have coverage of RNA. 
With these categories of RNA filter, it is possible for the GM interpreter to easily incorporate an extra layer 
of information about the degree of RNA support. Information showing high RNA coverage, but no variant 
support could help indicate that the variant is not expressed. Furthermore, knowing there is no RNA 
coverage also tells that there is no extra layer of information on this variant. The thresholds should not be 
taken very strictly, in the end the GM interpreters have a large amount of knowledge about the pathogenic 
variants and the information about SNVs with RNA_NOPASS can still have an interest. The distribution of 
SNVs in the different filter categories are visualized in F. Supplementary Material. Here most of the SNVs 
from the test dataset are in LowEVS;RNA_NOALT, followed by LowEVS;RNA_NOPASS, LowEVS;RNA_PASS, 
and LowEVS. PASS;RNA_NOALT, PASS;RNA_NOPASS, and PASS;RNA_PASS show a similar decreasing pattern 
of assigned SNVs as the filtering categories with LowEVS.

3.3.2.1 
Low RNA coverage of variant position 
The low RNA coverage observed for BRAF V600E has raised doubts about how certain we can be about the 
BRAF results seen, and if it is even possible to decide a common lower limit for trustworthy RNA evidence. 
The RNA filter categories were also previously argued to be more an indication of evidence than a strict 
threshold. It is investigated to see if the low RNA coverage observed could be due to the somatic 
environment, that is, a biological reason we do not see a high RNA expression of BRAF V600E. This was 
done by comparing the average RNA coverage per gene between somatic and germline SNVs for the BRAF 
patients completing the VEXER pipeline (57 PidGn). To retrieve the information about the germline SNVs 
the VAR_prefix wildcard in Code block 15 is replaced with the specific VCF prefix _snv_germline and

RNA coverage is performed for both all genes and clinically relevant genes (including BRAF) using 
Relative_RNA_cov.R, and results are shown in G. Supplementary Material. From this comparison, no clear 
suppression in the somatic RNA expression is seen, so this is likely not the cause for the low TOTAL_RNA 
coverage experienced on the position of the BRAF variant. The following analysis is therefore done only 
looking at the somatic BRAF data.

4 CONCLUSION 
Biological processes altering DNA transcripts to mature mRNAs and technical limitations in RNA sequencing 
and alignment, all contribute to the RNA diversity seen in RNA-seq data. The solution for how to distinguish 
between non-harmful diversity and disease-causing variants in the RNA-seq data remains an unsolved 
challenge. Post-transcriptional processing, including alternative splicing and RNA-editing, is believed to be a 
major obstacle for accurate RNA-seq variant calling. The tools RNA-Mutect, RNA-SSNV, and IMAPR attempt 
to address this issue by filtering out false positive variants that arise from RNA processing. 
The pipeline Mutect-IMAPR was developed in this project to quantify the recall of somatic variant calling by 
Mutect2 and IMAPR, with tumor RNA-seq data and normal WGS data as input. While Mutect2 was not 
designed for RNA-seq input, IMAPR was specifically developed for this purpose and relies on Mutect2 for 
variant calling. Results showed minimal differences in recall performance between the two tools. 
Approximately 10% of DNA variants with RNA coverage were confirmed by RNA-seq variant calling, 
indicating limited recall performance for both tools. Because IMAPR relies on the Mutect2 algorithm it is 
unlikely that IMAPR can achieve a better recall performance than Mutect2. The reported good performance 
of IMAPR-ORG likely results from stringent filtering, which reduces the RNA somatic variants to a small 
subset of true positives. Additionally, the differences in performance arise from the methodology: this 
project sought to confirm WGS variants with RNA-seq data, contrasting with IMAPR-ORG’s approach of 
confirming RNA variants with WGS data. Future algorithms should aim to improve the fraction of WGS 
variants confirmed by RNA rather than solely focusing on filtering false positives. Developing an accurate 
truth set of RNA variants would significantly aid this effort. Moreover, exploring parameter optimization of 
Mutect2 could further improve RNA-seq variant calling performance. 
The VEXER pipeline was developed as an alternative strategy to improve sensitivity when incorporating 
RNA into the variant calling pipeline. VEXER identifies somatic SNV positions from a DNA tumor and normal 
VCF file, then integrates RNA-level information for these positions and creates a new DNA and RNA 
annotated VCF file. The resulting annotated VCF file includes details such as RNA read coverage, alternative 
allele support (counts and percentage), average Phred quality of supporting reads, and an updated FILTER 
column with an RNA filter category summarizing RNA quality at each SNV position. By cooperation with GM 
interpreters the VEXER output is ensured to be presented in a clear way. VEXER adds a layer of information 
and brings the variant interpretation a step closer to the protein level, which potentially can help to 
elucidate if a variant is expressed and navigate the expected benefit of a targeted treatment. In the ideal 
case this could result in earlier identification of effective treatments, reduce costs by avoiding ineffective 
personalized therapies, and minimize unnecessary side effects. 
VEXER was applied in a case study of BRAF patients with the variant V600E, here VEXER showed potential in 
understanding how patients respond to BRAF targeted treatment. The study showed low RNA coverage of 
BRAF V600E which complicated the analysis. The low RNA coverage does not seem to be due to a general 
suppression of RNA expression in the somatic environment, but it is unclear whether another biological 
reason or a technical limitation is behind. Further studies are needed to confirm if an increased RNA 
sequencing depth would improve the coverage of BRAF or whether the BRAF expression does not provide 
sufficient RNA reads to increase confidence in the data. RNA-seq samples are present both pre- and post-
treatment and the context of time point needed to be considered when the conclusions in the BRAF study 
were formed. Results indicated that no RNA coverage of V600E pre-treatment could be associated with a 
worse treatment response. Additionally, it was observed that RNA-seq samples tend to decrease in support 
of the alternative allele, when comparing the first sample showing support of the alternative allele and last

sample for a patient. This decline may relate to the BRAF resistance mechanism and therapy-induced tumor 
evolution, but further studies are needed to investigate this hypothesis. VEXER has in this BRAF study 
shown its potential by providing information that was used to track the RNA expression changes of BRAF 
V600E during BRAF targeted treatment.  
In a broader analysis, VEXER was run for 1752 unique patient IDs to investigate data patterns linking 
expression of SNVs in RNA and cancer types. Results revealed a positive correlation between the total 
number of DNA SNVs and the number of SNVs with RNA expression of the alternative allele. Furthermore,

reflecting the effect of cancer survival mechanisms hiding the variant expression. Conversely, ovarian and

A. SUPPLEMENTARY MATERIAL 
The tools Strelka2, Mutect2 and IMAPR were selected to be studied in a three-week pilot study. Strelka2 is used in GM’s pipeline for somatic variant 
calling on DNA and preferred over Mutect2 because of shorter computing time. Mutect2 is recommended by GATKs best practices for somatic variant 
calling on DNA. IMAPR was newly released when the project started and promised good performance using RNA-seq data and better performance 
than other candidate tools like RNA-MuTect and RNA-SSNV. 
The output VCF files when using RNA-seq as tumor and DNA-seq as normal input were compared to the GM production VCF from Strelka2 run on 
tumor and normal DNA samples. The performance was not promising for either of the tools and it was considered if the comparison tool was 
performing accurately. Investigation of the som.py tool showed that giving the same input file as truth and query did result in a 100% performance. 
Normalizing the VCF files did not improve performance, but it was possible to include variants from the truth that did not pass the filter requirement 
of Strelka2, resulting in a larger intersection of the truth and query for Mutect2 and Strelka2. IMAPR called 18 variants in total where zero of them 
were found in the true VCF file, this comparison was done manually since the output format of IMAPR is not compatible with som.py. It did not seem 
sensible that IMAPR could only find 18 variants while Mutect2 finds more than 20,000 variants. These results need to be confirmed on a larger scale.
