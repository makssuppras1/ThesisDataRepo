Abstract
Hemolysis is the rupture of red blood cells and the presence of hemolysis in a blood sample can
indicate medical conditions such as autoimmune disorders or parasitic infections. When hemol-
ysis occurs the cell contents are released into the blood plasma interfering with measurements of
e.g. potassium and calcium levels.
This thesis is part of a larger project to develop a solution for reliable and fast detection of
hemolysis which can be integrated into the existing Radiometer ABL blood gas analysers. The
thesis has investigated the use of evanescent wave absorption to detect hemoglobin. A future
device will include both the sensor and a way to separate the cells from the plasma, and this
thesis has focused on the sensor itself.
A theoretical study on the ﬁeld distribution in a square waveguide of cured OrmoComp on a
glass substrate using Marcatili’s method has been conducted. This showed that the optical power
extends, depending on waveguide thickness, approximately 240 nm from the waveguide surface
and that approximately 2 % of the total optical power extends into the surrounding liquid. In
addition to this waveguide study a grating coupler has been designed that will couple light with
a wavelength of 420 nm into the waveguide. When illuminated from the from the back of the
glass substrate, this coupling will occur at approximately 42 degrees.
Using Guided Mode Resonance Filters (GMRFs) has been investigated. A GMRF is a pho-
tonic crystal structure that reﬂects light at a certain resonant frequency when illuminated by
broadband white light. The reﬂected resonance wavelength is highly dependent on refractive in-
dex of the probed solution and the with of the resonance peak is dependent on light absorption in
the device. However, GMRF measurements proved to be problematic since a large response was
observed in refractive index change but with little to no observed absorption, which is required
to selectively measure the hemoglobin concentration.
Absorption measurement on a square waveguide proved however to have a strong response.
For a 5 mm long device, a detection limit below 0.5 g/L was obtained. In general for both
GMRF sensors and waveguides, a system of an injection moulded COC chip attached to the
devices using double sided tape was used to control the various samples. Both types of sensors
also experienced adsorption of hemoglobin on the surface resulting in, especially for smaller
concentrations, a gradual change in signal response. This is a common trait in biosensors and
consistent with the Langmuir adsorption model. This adsorption increases the sensitivity of the
device by agglomerating the hemoglobin in the sensing region.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Hemolysis is the rupture of red blood cells and the detection of hemolysis in a blood sample can
indicate medical conditions such as autoimmune disorders or parasitic infections. When hemol-
ysis occurs the cell contents are released into the blood plasma interfering with measurements
of e.g. potassium and calcium levels. Additionally incorrect handling and storage of the blood
sample can cause up to 15 % of detected hemolysis.
Critically ill patients in e.g. operation rooms, emergency rooms or neonatal wards must have
their blood tested quickly using Point of Care (PoC) devices to ensure the correct treatment.
Measuring the level of hemolysis is therefore an important check for the quality of other blood
sample measurements, and no fast PoC method currently exist for the detection of hemolysis,
and performing this quality check is currently only possible at central labs.
Traditionally, hemolysis is measured by centrifuging the blood sample and measuring the
hemoglobin1 concentration of the separated plasma. Using hemoglobin as a marker is possible
because it should only be present inside the red blood cells. Detecting any in the blood plasma
means that hemolysis has occurred. The Hb concentration is measured by light absorption of
transmitted light through the separated plasma. Centrifugation is not possible in PoC devices
due to time and sample size constraints and using this method on on whole blood would result in
both intra- and extracellular hemoglobin being detected preventing the determination of degree
of hemolysis.
This thesis is part of a larger project to develop a solution for reliable and fast detection of
hemolysis which can be integrated into the existing Radiometer ABL blood gas analysers. The
thesis will investigate the use of evanescent wave absorption to detect hemoglobin. A future
device will include both the sensor and a way to separate the cells from the plasma, and this
thesis will focus on the sensor itself.
1.1
Sensor requirements
For the sensor to be useful the desired detection limit is 0.5 g/L ± 0.1 g/L corresponding to a
hemolysis degree of 3 . In addition, the sensor should be compatible with the ABL 90 platform,
so measurements must be completed on a sample of 65 µL in under 35 seconds while having a
lifetime of minimum one month. These are the current measurement parameters for the ABL
90 system and they are critical to the performance and usefulness for e.g. the medical staﬀat
hospitals relying on this apparatus.

1.2
State-of-the-art
This section presents state-of-the-art methods for hemoglobin detection that either attempt to
achieve the same goal or utilises some of the same methods used.
Hemolysis detection
For use during extracorporeal circulation therapies, a device for detecting the free hemoglobin
level has been developed by [1] in which plasma surface reﬂectance spectroscopy is used.

2.1
Hemoglobin
The role of red blood cells is to bind and transport oxygen from the lungs to body tissue where
it is needed and to assist in transporting CO2 from the tissue to the lungs. This is done through
the protein hemoglobin which consist of four heme groups, each containing an iron atom and
four polyeptide chains[4] in a structure as seen in Figure 2.1.
In order for hemoglobin to transport oxygen, the iron atoms must be in their ferrous Fe(II)
state. However, the iron atoms oxidises steadily into the Fe(III) state which cannot bind oxygen
and the protein is known as methemoglobin in this state. The body can reduce the ions back
to the ferrous state, but in pure solutions the oxidation does not reverse. Since no reduction
reactions has been conducted in this thesis it must be assumed that most hemoglobin detected
in this thesis has been methemoglobin. No eﬀort to reduce the iron ions has been made since the
impact on the refractive index has been shown to be negligible[12] and the absorption spectrum
has a similar shape to both oxygenated and deoxygenated hemoglobin[13].

Whole blood separation
For the entire project to be a success, two obstacles must be overcome.
One is the actual

Inertial microﬂuidics
Particles, such as red blood cells, in a Poiseuille ﬂow experience inertial lift forces causing the
particles to migrate towards an annular region around the center of the channel[15], causing an
area at the channel walls to be free from particles. This is known as the F˚ahræus Eﬀect from
an article in 1929 by Robin F˚ahræus[16] and appears in capillary tubes with a radius under 300
µm[17] at high ﬂow rates. Because of the inertial nature of the lift forces, the Reynolds number,
i.e. the ratio of inertial and viscous forces, must be high resulting in a high ﬂow rate for this
eﬀect to materialize. It could be a very convenient way of creating a cell-free layer on the sensor
as long as the ﬂow rate can be maintained throughout the measurement such that the red blood
cells does not sediment. This eﬀect has been exploited in a novel evanescent wave device for use
during extracorporeal circulation therapies as described earlier[1].
Other ways of using inertial microﬂuidics could be a version of Pinched Flow Fractionation as
in [6] where the channel width increases very rapidly and by the spreading of the ﬂow proﬁle the
large cells can be separated from the plasma. A drawing illustrating this can be seen in Figure

Chapter 4
Waveguide device design
This chapter describes the design considerations of an evanescent wave sensor using a square
waveguide along with the relevant parameters.
4.1
Material choices
Before any design process can begin, a consideration of the materials has to be made. First of
all, the device is a waveguide sensor and the waveguide itself must be transparent at the relevant
wavelength and with a higher refractive index than the surrounding materials. It must also be
pattern-able to enable easy fabrication.
Because light coupling cannot be done through the plasma as the sensor is measuring ab-
sorption, the substrate should also be transparent. Surrounding the waveguide on other sides is
blood plasma with a refractive index of around nplasma ∼1.3.
These limits can be fulﬁlled by choosing the substrate to be boron glass, nsubstrate = 1.47
and the waveguide itself in the hybrid polymer Ormocomp from micro resist technology GmbH
with a refractive index of normocomp = 1.518 [25]. This is a negative tone photoresist that can
be patterned using standard UV lithography and thereby enables easy fabrication. It also has a
higher refractive index than the other materials so that guiding can occur.
Since hemoglobin has its maximum absorption, depending on whether it is oxygenated or
deoxygenated, at approximately 420 nm the sensor is designed to work optimally at this wave-
length.
4.2
Waveguide parameters
In an evanescent wave absorption sensor only the optical power in the liquid contributes to the
absorption detection. For deciding the dimensions of the waveguide we must therefore be able
to estimate the ﬁeld from such a waveguide since an exact solution to the wave equation to this
case has not been found [9]. Such a method has been developed by Marcatili [26] by dividing
the system into diﬀerent regimes with diﬀerent governing equations. Using this convention, the
waveguide is divided into 5 diﬀerent sections as seen in Figure 4.1. In the case of the device
in this project the waveguide core consists of the hybrid polymer Ormocomp, n1 = 1.518, the
substrate is Pyrex borosilicate glass, n4 = 1.47, and the surrounding areas is the liquid to be
analysed, in this case mostly water n2 = n3 = n5 ∼1.338[27]. It is assumed that the light is
well guided and therefore decays exponentially outside the waveguide. In relation the ﬁelds in
the shaded areas are assumed to be small enough to be neglected. Inside the waveguide the
ﬁeld is assumed to be described by a sinusoidal distribution. With these assumptions Maxwell’s
equations can be solved by matching the boundary conditions on the sides of the waveguide.

4.3
Light coupling
Getting light into the waveguide in a reproducible and simple manner is important for the ﬁnal
device and its incorporation into the existing sensing chip. There are several ways of doing this,
as discussed shortly below:
End face coupling Simply aligning the end of a ﬁber to the end of the waveguide will, if the modes are
suﬃciently matched, couple the light into the waveguide and can be done quite eﬃciently
depending on the alignment and mode mismatch[28]. This method is very sensitive to slight
misalignments that will result in a signiﬁcant reduction in the amount of coupled light and
will therefore also be harder to incorporate into a chip. The advantage of this method is
its simplicity and that it is relatively easy to set up measurements. Another version of
this coupling method is using collimated light and a microscope objective to focus on the
end face. This method allows for a larger distance between objective and the end face
compared to a ﬁber end where the light diverges very quickly. The microscope objective
end face method is therefore been used in this project for measurements on waveguides.
Prism coupling Using a prism positioned above the waveguide will by diﬀraction cause some of the light
to be coupled into the waveguide. This is a rather simple method but for good results, an
index matching liquid is required which again will be a problem for easily interchangeable
chips and will increase the number of required components. [28]

Chapter 5
Device fabrication
This chapter describes the fabrication process of both the GMRF sensors and the waveguide
device, including the stamp for coupling graters. The proposed stamp design is based upon the
concept in [29], where a grating structure is etched in a glass substrate onto which a chromium
pattern is deposited. Through this process a combined stamp and photomask. By imprinting and
illuminating by UV light simultaneously the designed waveguide structures with nano-patterned
grating structures will be created without the need for any long processing or alignment.
Included in this chapter is also the implemented solution for ﬂuid sample control.
5.1
Waveguide fabrication
In this section the process for fabricating simple rectangular waveguides is outlined and the
purpose of each step described. For a detailed process ﬂow, see Appendix A. All the waveguide
devices in this thesis have been fabricated using this process.
Spinning
The hybrid polymer OrmoComp is diluted with the thinner OrmoThin in the ratio 1:3. Undiluted
OrmoComp is quite viscous so the dilution both eases handling and allows the desired thickness
to be reached. According to the manufacturers speciﬁcations a spin speed of 4000 rpm for 30
s should for this dilution yield a resist thickness of just under 400 nm[25], and these are the
parameters that have been used. The resist is spin on a pyrex wafer using a WS-650 spinner
from Laurell Technologies Corporation and prebaked on a hotplate for 3 minutes at 110◦C.
Exposure
To deﬁne the pattern, the resist is exposed in a S¨USS Mask Aligner MA6 with a dark-ﬁeld mask
using a proximity exposure. The dose used is 400 mJ/cm2 and a postexposure bake to evaporate
the solvent is conducted on a hotplate for 10 minutes at 130 ◦C.
Development
After the postexposure bake, the pattern is ready to be developed. This is conducted in OrmoDev
from Micro Resist Technology. Development for the thickness used in this project is 60 seconds.
Following development, a hardbake is done for 3 hours at 150◦C. The hardbake is not a critical
step but is included to ensure that all solvent is evaporated and that the waveguide is as stable
as possible.
The waveguides are after development ready for the ﬁnal dicing step and appear as shown in
Figure 5.1. Heights of the waveguides used in this thesis is between 350 nm and 400 nm.

5.3
Stamp fabrication
In the following section, the major steps of a proposed fabrication process is outlined and a
detailed process ﬂow can be found in Appendix B. This particular process has only been partially
completed, but the entire proposed process is outlined here.
E-beam grating deﬁnition
The ﬁrst main process step is the grating deﬁnition using e-beam lithography. The stamp is
fabricated using a fused silica wafer because it needs to be UV transparent. The grating pattern
is deﬁned in the e-beam resist CSAR which is spun on the wafer in a manual spinner after which
a softbake is conducted. Because the fused silica is not conductive, a charge compensating layer
for dissipating the deposited charge is required. For this, ESPACER, which is a liquid that is
spun on the wafer, is used.
The actual grating pattern is deﬁned in a e-beam writer, the charge dissipating layer is rinsed
oﬀby water and the resist is developed. The wafer now has a grating deﬁned in e-beam resist.
A preliminary dose test of the gratings gave the best results at 212.5 µC/ cm2 ± 20 %, but
no further processing of the stamp was conducted.
Grating etch
A layer of aluminium is deposited on the wafer and a lift-oﬀprocess is performed. This gives a
wafer covered in metal with openings where the valleys of the grating in the stamp will be. This
is etched in a Reactive Ion Etcher until the desired depth. A reactive ion etch is used because
we wish to obtain a very anisotropic etch.
Waveguide deﬁnition
To deﬁne the waveguide, a UV lithographic process is conducted. The resist AZ5214E is used,
which is a positive image reversal resist. Performing this process leaves a positive of the ﬁnal
waveguide desired. The gratings are at this point valleys, and an additional RIE process will
cause them to become protrusions.
Mask and ﬁnal processing
Depositing a layer of chromium on the wafer will now allow for a lift-oﬀprocess such that the
entire wafer is covered in metal except for openings deﬁning the waveguide and gratings. This
means that the stamp now also functions as a mask for a UV cured imprint. Finally the stamp
is coated in an anti-stiction coating to be ready for imprinting. The coating is obtained through
the use of FDTS3, which results in a monolayer of a teﬂon-like molecule with −( CF2) x CF3
chains.

7.3
Method comparison
While it could be interesting to measure the refractive index of blood plasma while measuring
the hemoglobin concentration, the absorption measurement is the key parameter. For GMRF
sensors the refractive index changes signiﬁcantly and also more than would be expected for the
measured concentration, but any response of the resonance peak width, which is the measure of
light absorption, is below the noise level. Repeatable and stable measurements with the GMRF
sensors has not been obtained and this method does not seem to be the best solution. The
waveguide device shows that signiﬁcant absorption is present and that a detection limit of below
0.5 g/L is obtainable with a 5 mm waveguide. The same eﬀect of Hb adsorption on the surface
is seen in both sensors, but with the use of TritonX the surface can be cleaned and thus the
adsorption only enhances the sensitivity of the device.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This thesis has examined the use of evanescent waveguide structures for the detection of hemoglobin.
A theoretical study on the ﬁeld distribution in a square waveguide of cured OrmoComp on a
glass substrate using Marcatili’s method has been conducted. This showed that the optical power
extends, depending on waveguide thickness, approximately 240 nm from the waveguide surface
and that approximately 2 % of the total optical power extends into the surrounding liquid. In
addition to this waveguide study a grating coupler has been designed that will couple light with
a wavelength of 420 nm into the waveguide. When illuminated from the from the back of the
glass substrate, this coupling will occur at approximately 42 degrees.
Measurements have been conducted on both Guided Mode Resonance Filters (GMRFs) as well
as on square waveguide to investigate the absorption of light in hemoglobin. GMRF measure-
ments proved to be problematic since a large response was observed in refractive index change
but with little to no observed absorption. The absorption is required to selectively measure
the hemoglobin concentration and refractive index has been used only as a measure of device
performance.
Absorption measurement on a square waveguide proved however to have a strong response.
For a 5 mm long device, a detection limit below 0.5 g/L was obtained.
In general for both GMRF sensors and waveguides, a system of an injection moulded COC
chip attached to the devices using double sided tape was used to control the various samples.
Both types of sensors also experienced adsorption of hemoglobin on the surface resulting in, es-
pecially for smaller concentrations, a gradual change in signal response. This is a common trait
in biosensors and consistent with the Langmuir adsorption model. This adsorption increases
the sensitivity of the device by agglomerating the hemoglobin in the sensing region. The grad-
ual change is however currently the limiting factor in determining the measured concentration
accurately, but this could be improved by advanced data analysis.

8.1
Outlook
The next step in the development of this device would be the fabrication of a grating coupled
waveguide device. This will enable more stable measurements and an exhaustive calibration
curve can be completed in order to determine the detection limit with better accuracy and
precision.
Having all measurement optics below the detection surface will free space for the development
of the separation mechanism for the red blood cells, such that measurements on whole blood can
be characterized. Whole blood is a complex liquid and several additional possible contributions
to the signal response is introduced.
The waveguide device could be a promising solution to the detection of hemoglobin and mil-
limetre size devices could be small enough to obtain detection limits below the desired value. To
increase the detection limit, a model for the adsorption of hemoglobin on the waveguide surface
can be developed and applied to the data analysis.
