Abstract
In this thesis the magnetic properties of LiCoPO4 are studied by Monte Carlo Simulations of a spin
Hamiltonian containing Heisenberg exchange interactions and single-ion spin anisotropy terms. A
Monte Carlo code is developed and tested. The parameters of the spin Hamiltonian are then obtained
through literature studies in combination with analysis of measurements on the spin wave spectra of
the material. The Monte Carlo simulations yield a low-temperature magnetic susceptibility ╬Š(T) for
’¼üelds along b, and a magnetic contribution to the heat capacity in fair agreement with experimental
data.
The N┬┤eel temperature is underestimated by 30%.
Similar underestimates was seen from
simulations on LiNiPO4 (32%) and LiFePO4 (30%). Greater discrepancies are found between the
simulated magnetisation M(H) and experimental data in terms of the phase transition ’¼üelds, just as
the agreement with the experimental high-temperature susceptibility is unsatisfactory. Two potential
explanations of these discrepancies are discussed, both related to a hypothesised strong spin-orbit
coupling; an anisotropic g-factor and a non-negligible Dzyaloshinky-Moriya interaction.
Further
work is needed to understand the implications of including these contributions.

2.2.2
Magnetocrystalline anisotropy
In crystalline materials, the arrangement of ions and the crystal ’¼üeld from neighbouring ions can give
rise to di’¼Ćerent kinds of magnetocrystalline anisotropy, that will make the spins align in preferred
directions.
This makes the magnetic properties of such materials direction dependent, and the
spins can have a preferred easy-axis along which they will tend to align, but they can also have an
intermediate and hard axis, for which the spins will try to avoid aligning towards.
In the lithium metal phosphates, this phenomenon is prominent and details of the theory will be
elaborated upon.

In a material with a single preferred easy-axis along which the spins will
tend to align, the anisotropy energy can be described by

2.2.3
Superexchange
In magnetic materials, the magnetic ions interact through what is called the exchange interaction.
This is a quantum phenomenon and relates to the fact that the total wavefunction, considering both
spin and spatial variation, for electrons and other fermions must be antisymmetric. Most often this
interaction is described by a Hamiltonian of the form
Hexch. = JijSi ┬Ę Sj.
(30)
for two ionic spins Si and Sj.
The simplest form of exchange is direct exchange where the spatial part of the wave function of
two di’¼Ćerent atoms overlaps. This e’¼Ćect, however, cannot explain the large exchange interactions in
the lithium orthophosphates as the transition metal ions are too far apart for the direct exchange to
have any e’¼Ćect.
In some materials, an intermediate ion can mediate the exchange interaction between the metal
ions. A typical example of this is that of oxygen ions in MnO, where the pz-orbital of the oxygen
ions mediate between the d-orbitals of the Mn, to create a strong antiferromagnetic interaction.
Because the mediation of the interaction, is through a non-s orbital, it can depend strongly on the
angle between the mediating ion and the metallic ions. The shell occupations and bond angle can
in many cases be enough information to tell whether a superexchange interaction is ferromagnetic
or antiferromagnetic. In the 1950s J. Goodenough and J. Kanamori developed a set of rules, which
could well predict whether a superexchange interaction would be weak ferromagnetic or strong
antiferromagnetic, corresponding to either small negative values or large positive values of Jij. These
have come to be known as the Anderson-Goodenough-Kanamori rules [6].

Metropolis algorithm
The method described until now is called direct sampling, and is probably the most naive form of
Monte Carlo simulation. A better method for sampling of con’¼üguration space is a method known
as Markov Chain Monte Carlo, or a speci’¼üc implementation of it, the Metropolis algorithm. In
this method, samples are not independently sampled, but instead are sampled in sequence.
In
this way, the iŌĆÖth sample xi+1 depends solely on the sample preceding it, xi. We thus start with
some initial con’¼üguration or sample xi, and generate new samples x1, x2 . . . by iterative changing
the con’¼üguration xi. First a new random sample xi+1 is created by some random change to the
preceding sample xi. Now either the new proposed sample is accepted or rejected, and the probability
of doing so (the transition probability), is denoted P(xi ŌåÆxi+1).
In the Metropolis algorithm, the choice of the transition probability is set to be

condition of ergodicity of sampling. The ’¼ürst condition of detailed balance, requires that for any
transition between two con’¼ügurations a and b with the probabilities p(a), p(b), we must have that
p(a)P(a ŌåÆb) = p(b)P(b ŌåÆa). The Metropolis algorithm ensures that this is the case, see [8],

If these two conditions are met, then this guarantees that the Markov chain sampling will converge to
the right probability distribution, see [8]. However, the number of steps needed for the convergence
of the distribution is not guaranteed to be computationally manageable. Thus it could be that the
required number of steps could be very large. To understand this better we will look at an example.

To be able to run a simulation, some parameters are required from the user. These are the number
of iterations K per step, the dimensions of the array of unit-cells Nx, Ny, Nz, and the type of
element to simulate. Before beginning a simulation in the program, the user will have to input these
parameters. The number of iterations is not directly input by the user. Instead, a number called the

with a varying ’¼üeld, with the temperature held constant; if more scans are input, these will run

a simulation with the given parameters.

Boundary conditions
In an earlier Bachelor project [10] a similar program for Monte Carlo
simulations was developed. Here it was reported that implementing periodic boundary conditions
for these systems was non-trivial. Because of this I will here explain my method of implementing
both periodic and open boundary conditions.
For calculating the exchange energy of a spin at an index u = (i, j, k) of the array, a list of lists
of three-dimensional displacement indices (Ōłåi, Ōłåj, Ōłåk) are iterated through. Each element in the
outermost list corresponds to an exchange coupling Jij, i.e. an enumeration has been chosen such
that the list at the nŌĆÖth index of the outermost list, corresponds to the neighbours of the enumerated
nŌĆÖth exchange coupling.
Now for each element (Ōłåi, Ōłåj, Ōłåk) in the innermost list, it is checked
whether the index (i + Ōłåi, j + Ōłåj, k + Ōłåk), is outside of the array. Now two cases can occur
Periodic boundary conditions If in the case of periodic boundary conditions, the index is wrapped
to lie in the array by use of the modulo operation, and the corresponding spin looked up.
Open boundary conditions If open boundary conditions have been chosen, and the index is
outside the array, then the look up is skipped. This ensures that no problems will occur with
indexing out of the bounds of the array.
For each considered neighbouring spin v = (i+Ōłåi, j +Ōłåj, k+Ōłåk), and each corresponding exchange
coupling the term JijSu ┬Ę Sv is calculated and added to the current Hamiltonian energy.

5.1
Determining parameters
To be able to simulate the magnetic behaviour of LiCoPO4, by using the Hamiltonian of (31), a set of
parameters for the ’¼üve exchange interactions Jb, Jc, Jab, Jac, Jbc and the two single-ion anisotropies

5.1.1
Literature study
First parameters were sought in literature. In ref. [13] Vaknin et al. performed neutron di’¼Ćraction
experiments and magnetometry measurements on LiCoPO4. From their data, it was argued that the
coupling in the material could be explained to be in between that of an ideal 2D and 3D Ising-magnet.
In other words, the material was seen as consisting of weakly antiferromagnetic planes coupled by an
inter-layer coupling JŌŖź. Although the authors do not give an estimate for this inter-layer coupling
JŌŖź, they give an estimate for an easy-axis single-ion anisotropy along the b-axis of Dy = ŌłÆ2.5meV,
assuming that Da = Dc = 0.
In ref. [18] neutron di’¼Ćraction measurements and magnetometry measurements on LiCoPO4 were
used to determine the magnetic structure in the range of 11.9T ŌłÆ20.5T. It was found that the
magnetic structure present in this phase, is a combination of two components, a cycloid component,
where the spins ordered into a spiral phase, with the turn angle rotating in the (bc)-plane, and a
ferromagnetic component along the b-axis. Using this data, and the magnetometry measurements,
an Ising model was proposed, where the cycloid structure was simpli’¼üed. All spins were assumed
parallel to the b-axis, and that the material ordered into ferromagnetic (ac)-planes. From this model
it was stipulated that the jumps in magnetisation arose from stacking faults in the di’¼Ćerent (ac)-
planes.

0.23 meV. It was also found that the value of Jbc was reasonably close to the exchange coupling

5.1.2
Attempt at determining the single-ion anisotropy
In need of values for the single-ion anisotropies, two di’¼Ćerent unsuccessful methods were attempted.
Although the methods did not work, a huge part of the project time went into these attempts, and
they gave valuable information, hence their reproduction here is deemed important.
Using analytic methods
First an analytic model for how the single-ion anisotropies in’¼éuence the
susceptibilities were derived. By ’¼ütting this to measurements, values for the single-ion anisotropies
could potentially be obtained.
Thus a simple model was set up, with no exchange couplings and only one single-ion anisotropy
present along an easy b-axis. Using this model, I derived expressions for the susceptibility tensor
elements Žćxx, Žćyy, Žćzz, along the three axes, which can be seen in A. It was found that the suscep-
tibilities were di’¼Ćerent, but that the slope of the inverse susceptibility with respect to temperature,
which is equal to the inverse of the Curie constant, were equal. This made it hard to ’¼üt the model
to the experimental measurements of the inverse susceptibility, as these have di’¼Ćerent slopes. It
nevertheless showed an important point, which was also discussed in [13], namely that a single-
ion anisotropy can give a ’¼ünite Curie-Weiss temperature, even in a model without any exchange
interactions.
Using simulations
In ref. [21] the angle dependence of the magnetic moment of a single crystal
of LiCoPO4 of 32.47mg was measured. The applied ’¼üeld was H = 16kOe, and measurements were
performed at T Ōēł4K, T Ōēł280K and T Ōēł/296K. The result of their measurement is shown in
’¼ügure 9. A high level of anisotropy is seen, and that the anisotropy is more pronounced at low
temperatures than at high temperatures.
To determine the single-ion anisotropies from this, di’¼Ćerent simulations were run at low temper-
ature, with the two single-ion anisotropies set to be equal, but varying the value of Da = Dc. In
each simulation, the magnetic ’¼üeld was held constant, but at an angle, ╬Ė varied in the (ab)-plane,
the results can be seen in ’¼ügure 10.
From these simulations, it can be seen that for a value of
D = Da = Dc between 0.02meV and 0.03meV, the ratio of maximal to minimal magnetisation is
around 2.7, the same as in the experiments, see ’¼ügure 9. This value is very small compared to the
value of 2.5meV, obtained in [13]. This was also backed by later ’¼ündings, where measurements on
spin-wave dispersion were used to ’¼ünd reasonable values for the anisotropies. It was thus decided,
that this method was not useful to obtain these values.
Later on, it was found from simulations, that from the model used in the simulations, the suscep-
tibility along the b-axis at low temperatures approached zero, following the textbook behaviour of
antiferromagnets, see [5]. Thus it was impossible with this model, to obtain the nonzero susceptibility
at low temperatures, that has been seen experimentally in [20] and [21].

5.4
Boundary conditions and structure determination
As described in section 4.2, at temperatures below 11 K, and for ’¼üelds By in the interval 11.9 T-
21.5 T, the magnetic unit-cell in LiCoPO4 triples in size along the b-axis, and the structure is a
combination of cycloid component and a ferromagnetic component, see ’¼ügure 6.
From simulations it was found that when periodic boundary conditions were chosen, then at a
’¼üeld of around 22 T, a transition would occur, where the magnetisation would jump to a value
of M = 0.5Ms. To understand this phase transition, the structure was studied above and below
the transition by manual visual inspection. Below the transition each (bc)-plane consists of a two
sublattice antiferromagnetic structure, A-sites being spin up and B-sites being spin down, see ’¼ügure
24a. Above the transition half of the spins of one of the sublattices ’¼éips, and the magnetic unit-
cell doubles in size, see ’¼ügure 24b. In experiments this is not what has been seen. This does not
correspond to the structure seen in the experiments and thus, it was chosen to switch to use periodic
boundary conditions. Doing this did however pose a problem. Considering that my lattice consists
of a cube of 63 = 216 unit cells, the number of unit-cells which are at the surface are 152. For all the
unit-cells which are at the surface, one or more neighbours will be absent. This makes these atoms
di’¼Ćerent from the ones in the bulk. A simple consideration shows that the number of unit cells for
an N 3 cube will be 6N 2 ŌłÆ12N +8, such that the ratio of surface-cells is 6N2ŌłÆ12N+8
N3
. This expression
decreases very slowly with N, and would require a large number of atoms, for surface e’¼Ćects to be
negligible, and would take a huge amount of time. Thus it was not attempted to use larger system
sizes. It could also be argued, that if system sizes large enough that surface e’¼Ćects were negligible,
then results from using periodic and open boundary conditions should be equivalent. In a future
study this, might be of interest to study.

Discussion
As the determination of parameters for LiCoPO4 proved more di’¼ācult than ’¼ürst anticipated, the
values for the parameters were only obtained within the last month of the project. In addition to
this, it was also within the last month that the hysteresis behaviour at the low temperature was
observed, and that it was found that the method for changing spins in the algorithm was found not
to work that well.
Because of time constraint, some assumptions for the simulations were made. For all simulations,
the system size was chosen to be 6x6x6 unit-cells. This choice was based upon earlier studies by co-
supervisor Ellen Fogh, who found that the energies depended on system sizes, but seemed to converge
to a unique value for system sizes of 5x5x5. To make sure that this convergence was obtained, a
system size larger than this was chosen.
With more time a proper study of the dependence on
system size should be performed. The number of Monte Carlo steps was likewise chosen to be 105.
Although at the beginning of the project, tests were performed for determining an ideal number of
Monte Carlo steps, these tests were discarded when it was found that the iteration method used,
was poor compared to other methods, as was described in section 5.5. In this section, the ŌĆØhysteresis
behaviourŌĆØ of the simulations was also described. This behaviour persisted through all the methods
and for all the materials simulated. As the check of the code together with the visualisation program
showed that all terms of the Hamiltonian were implemented correctly, it is believed that this e’¼Ćect
is not a bug in the code. Instead, it is believed that the simulation choice of only changing a single
ion spin at a time, is not able to explore the con’¼üguration space su’¼āciently. To be able to do this,
cluster methods might be able to provide more e’¼Ćective and e’¼ācient simulations.
Despite these di’¼āculties, it was still reached within the timeframe, to both obtain a set of reasonable
parameter values for LiCoPO4 and to perform simulations with these.

Further work
Iteration method
In section 5.5, the e’¼Ćect of ŌĆØsimulation hysteresisŌĆØ was described; the results
of the simulation depended on whether the applied ’¼üeld was swept up or down. It was also found
that for the three methods for generating proposal spins, this hysteresis e’¼Ćect did not disappear.
As has already been mentioned, in future studies it would be interesting to study whether using
alternatives such as clustering methods, ref. [8], would be able to diminish or remove this e’¼Ćect.
g-factor
In all the simulations, the g-factor has been set isotropic and constant as 2. In many
materials, the value of g can, however, vary greatly from 2. For Co2
+-ions, the experimental value
52
==PAGE:53==
of the e’¼Ćective magnetic moment in paramagnetic salts is in between that obtained when assuming
orbital quenching with L = 0, and that obtained when considering both L and S, see e.g. [5] or [32].
Thus it would seem that the assumption of orbital quenching, and disregarding the orbital angular
momentum, is not possible for the Co+2-ions in LiCoPO4. Because of this, in future studies it could
be necessary for the value of g to be changed or replaced by a tensor with anisotropic values, gxx,
gyy and gzz, along the three di’¼Ćerent crystallographic directions.
Anisotropic exchange interaction
In my simulations, the energy contribution from each ex-
change interaction is isotropic and of the form Hexch. = JijSiSj; it does not change if the two spins
are rotated together by some angle. In some materials, the exchange can be more complicated and
of the form Hani.exch. = SŌĆĀ
iJijSj, where Jij is a three by three tensor. This results in the di’¼Ćer-
ent coordinates of the spins to coupled with di’¼Ćerent strengths. This could potentially have been
included in the simulations but would introduce many unknown parameters into the model, and it
was thus chosen not to do. In future studies, the e’¼Ćect of introducing this e’¼Ćect could be studied.
Dzyaloshinsky-Moriya interactions
For the exchange interaction of equation (30), the energy
is invariant to exchange of the spins for all neighbouring pairs, the dot products Si ┬Ę Sj and Sj ┬Ę Si
are equal.7 If the crystal lattice is such that it does not have an inversion symmetry around the
centre between a neighbouring pair, an interaction between the pairs of the form
HDM = D ┬Ę (Si ├Ś Sj)
(80)
can exist, where the vector D is either parallel or perpendicular to the axis between the ions. This
interaction is known as the Dzyaloshinki-Moriya (DM) interaction and the e’¼Ćect is that the ions
will try to be at right angles to each other to minimize the energy, and the result of this is that the
spins will be slightly canted, giving a weak ferromagnetic response. Although the DM-interaction
could have been implemented in the simulation code, it was chosen not to do so by two reasons.
Firstly often the DM-interaction is weak compared to the other exchange interactions, and it is
suspected that the e’¼Ćect of introducing it in the simulations, would be to cant the spins slightly,
giving a small ferromagnetic response, an e’¼Ćect known as weak ferromagnetism. Secondly including
this e’¼Ćect would introduce even more unknown parameters, which would have to be determined
by some methods, and no e’¼ācient method for determining these are known.
Despite this, it is
known however that the strength of the DM-interaction depends on the spin-orbit coupling, which
for LiCoPO4 could be strong, as evidenced by the rather large value of Ōłåg Ōēł0.3 [23]. It has also
been shown by Fogh in ref. [9], that only two DM-interactions are possible, and that the value of
one of these DM-interaction in LiCoPO4 could be as large the single-ion anisotropy Dc. This gives
reason to believe that the DM-interaction could play a crucial role in the magnetic structures of this
material. In future studies, the e’¼Ćect of including the DM-interaction into the simulations could also
be studied.

Conclusion
In this project, a program for doing Markov Chain Monte Carlo simulations was developed. This
program was developed to be able to simulate the magnetic behaviour of LiCoPO4 in response to
changing temperatures and applied ’¼üelds, using a model for the Hamiltonian.
Before simulating the program was tested on simple solvable examples and by reproducing results
of co-supervisor Ellen Fogh and former Bsc. stud. Rasmus Eilk├”r Hansen. In these tests, it was also
shown that both the magnetic contribution to heat capacity and the magnetic susceptibility could
be obtained through the simulations.

To be able to simulate LiCoPO4, a set of parameters were sought for the model Hamiltonian, ’¼ürst
through a literature study. In this literature study, the ’¼ürst three values for the parameters Jbc, Jb
and Jc were obtained. The literature study also revealed that the material was believed both to be
highly anisotropic, and to have stronger intralayer exchange-couplings in (bc)-planes than interlayer
couplings.
For the found parameters for Jbc, Jb and Jc, the parameters Jab and Jac were set to zero, and
simulations were run with single-ion anisotropies Da = Dc = 1 meV. These simulations showed, that
non-zero values for Jab and Jac were needed for the simulation to obtain the right low-temperature
magnetic structure Cy, which had been seen experimentally.
To obtain values for these, measurements of the spin-wave dispersion of LiCoPO4 taken by co-
supervisor Rasmus Toft-Petersen was analysed.
A model for the dispersion relation derived by
Thomas B. S. Jensen in [22] was adjusted to ’¼üt the data, and to obtain values for Da, Dc, Jab.
Although the model was not able to ’¼üt all the data points, a simple consideration of the model,
showed that some of the measurements were not able to be explained by this model.
Using these parameters simulations were performed, with both varying temperatures and magnetic
’¼üelds. It was found that with low temperatures a ŌĆØsimulation hysteresisŌĆØ-e’¼Ćect was seen, which
complicated the data analysis. It was however found that the choice between periodic and open
boundary conditions had a large e’¼Ćect on the results of the simulations, for the chosen system sizes.
It was seen that the simulations were able to fairly well produce results of magnetic heat capacity and
low-temperature magnetic susceptibility seen experimentally. It was also seen that a ’¼üeld-induced
phase in the simulation, were of a di’¼Ćerent magnetic ordering than a magnetic-’¼üeld-induced phase
seen experimentally.
In an attempt to eliminate or decrease the unwanted e’¼Ćect of ŌĆØsimulation hysteresisŌĆØ, di’¼Ćerent
algorithms for generating proposal spins in the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm was simulated and
analysed. It was found that one of the methods was particularly bad, but that none of the methods
was able to eliminate the e’¼Ćect.
To get insight into the predictability of the model, simulations using parameters found in the liter-
ature for the other lithium orthophosphates were carried out and analysed. It was found that for all
these, the N┬┤eel temperatures of the simulations T sim.
N
, were fairly below the value seen experimentally
T exp.

, with a ratio T exp.

It was also seen that the other lithium phosphates all had a spin-’¼éop transition at some ’¼üeld, where
the spins spontaneously reoriented, into some new con’¼üguration. No such spin-’¼éop phase was seen
in the simulations of LiCoPO4.
A simulation of the phase diagram of the model was obtained through a series of simulations
in (T, By)-space, and phase transitions were identi’¼üed, using numeric di’¼Ćerentiation of the basis
projections Fy and Cy, with respect to T and By. The phase diagram was found to have a very
similar shape to that found from experiments, although still with essential di’¼Ćerences. Based upon
this, it has been shown that although simulations with the found parameters are able to reproduce
some of the experimental data, not all features are reproduced. The simulations using the parameters
of the other LiMPO4 has however shown that even for these this is not possible.
