Abstract
Mono- and poly-cyclic carbon chains containing aromatic rings (BTEX and PAHs) naturally occur
in crude fossil hydrocarbons. They are produced by incomplete combustion of fuels or biomass and
cause organic pollution when released into the environment. Aromatic compounds undergo long range
atmospheric transport, as evidenced by their detection in the raw water in Greenlandic water plants.
They pose a mounting hazard to Greenland’s supply of drinking water because of their toxicity,
reflected by low legislative concentration limits. This is a challenge for Nukissiorfiit – the municipal
water company – which does not possess the equipment to efficiently treat organic pollutants, such as
BTEX and PAHs.
This study analyses two sets of data to understand the scope of the contamination in Greenland :
data gathered in two sampling campaigns (conducted in the Angmassalik and Disko areas) and data
from the National boringsdatabase (Jupiter, GEUS).
First, analysis results are discussed. Despite the sampling taking place in usually contaminated ar-
eas, pollutant concentrations were below the detection limit. Second, the historical data in GEUS
is reviewed. This data exhibits seasonal patterns and a tendency towards increasing concentrations,
despite anthropogenic emission reduction. Mixing ratios and detection frequency of compounds in this
database differ from standard values in other studies and give insight on likely sources. Third, the
impact of extreme weather events on pollutant concentration is discussed. It was found that the sam-
pling campaigns coincided with extreme weather events – unusual precipitation and wind patterns.
Extreme weather events will play a disrupting role in the future of pollution modelling in the Arctic.
To investigate the extent of and climate-change influence on organic polluting, long-term observations
of specific compounds are required. These should include meteorological data, changes in the regulatory
framework, and cover all of Greenland.
The field work was conducted with the support of Nukissiorfiit and the Greenlandic governmental
department of Environment. The samples were analysed in Denmark Tekniske Universitet (DTU)
laboratories.

Motivation and outline
Aromatic carbons in drinking water resources in Greenland have become an amounting problem in
the recent years. The aim of this project is to analyze available data and reveal the tendencies and
sources of these contaminants.
This project was first proposed by Pernille Erland Jensen, PhD and Associate Professor at the Depart-
ment of Environmental and Resource Engineering in Denmarks Tekniske Universitet (DTU). After a
preliminary discussion concerning the academic aspects of the project, Mads Albæk Sørensen, Oper-
ations consultant in water supply at Nukissiorfiit was brought onboard to state what the company
would expect from such a project, the needed preparatives before bringing more people onboard, and
which kind of support they would provide.
With a better scope of the project, a meeting was held with representatives from Nukissiorfiit, Landslæ-
gen and Natur og Miljø. Each party had an area of focus (raw, drinking, and surface water respectively)
that they wanted addressed in the subsequent field work. Susanne Berglund, from Natur og Miljø, and
Judith Mar´echal, post-doc at DTU Arctic, joined the project on a supervisory capacity.
Existing data from the Jupiter database (presented in section 2.1) was gathered and analyzed to
outline potential investigation sites. Two sites were covered : the Angmassalik area and the Disko
area. Further samples were gathered by thesis supervisors, researchers and Natur og Miljø employees
to cast a wider net. Analysis of the collected samples was done in DTU laboratories by technicians.
After the field work and analysis, the GEUS database was analysed in its entirety to compare campaign
results to past data.

Introduction
1.1
Organic pollutants : BTEX and PAH
BTEX and PAH are aromatic hydrocarbons. They are chains of carbon and hydrogen containing one
or more benzene ring. Aromaticity gives the compounds an increased stability, meaning they do not
break down as easily as aliphatic compounds. BTEX are monoaromatic hydrocarbons (MAH), whereas
PAH compounds have several aromatic rings.
Both types are considered Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs). POPs are toxic chemicals negatively
affecting humans and the environment. PAHs are different from many other POPs in that they are
byproducts of combustion (i.e. unintentionally produced) and their emissions are ongoing (Friedman
et al. 2014). Because of their persistence, they can be transported by wind and water, affecting
another area entirely from the one they originated from. BTEX and PAH pollutants are mutagenic,
carcinogenic, immunotoxic, and teratogenic to all forms of life (i.e. microorganisms to humans) (Varjani
et al. 2017). Concentrations above 0.1 or 0.01 µg/L (depending on the compound) in drinking water
are harmful to human health.

It is used in paint thinners, fuel, resins and rubber. During the high season of snowmobiling

comparable to those of medium European towns. 2-stroke engine emissions make up a large part

• Xylene : Xylene is a general name for three isomeric compounds (ortho-xylene, meta-xylene
and para-xylene, called o-, m-, and p- Xylene, respectively). Like the other BTEX, it is naturally
occurring in crude oil and coal tar. It can be released in the environment naturally though forest
fires.
Potential anthropogenic sources are fuels, paint, treated leather and wood/coal combustion.
Xylene is moderately toxic, affecting primarily the human nervous system (Krewski et al. 2004).
Presence of hydrocarbons (particularly BTEX) in lakes, rivers, and coastal waters has been intensively
studied in the past (Gschwend et al. 1982). The major emissions source was found to be emissions of
unburned fuel from two-stroke-engines mainly used in small motor boats (Schmidt et al. 2004). Some
Greenlandic towns get their drinking water directly from coastal waters (Oqaatsut, Kitsissurarsuit
among others), which exposes them to the emissions from local boat traffic. In the Disko Bay and the
West coast in general, this traffic is expected to grow with the decrease of sea ice cover.
Snowmobile engines are similar to motor boats (two-stroke) and are used extensively around Green-
landic towns. Although both the local communes and Nukissiorfiit prohibit the use of snow-mobiles
and dogsleds in the vicinity of the catchment area, this still occurs. Dog-sleds are not expected to
have any impact on aromatic hydrocarbon contamination (the concern is mainly biological pollution),
however snowmobiles have demonstrably been linked to organic pollution (Reimann et al. 2009).

1.1.2
PAH
PAHs are organic compounds composed of more than one aromatic ring. They are emitted as gases
by the combustion of organic matter – be it in engines and incinerators or during forest fires. Natural
sources include bitumen and oil deposits. PAHs are carcinogenic and have been linked to negative
effects on the cardiovascular system and fetal development.
More than half of the annual emissions of PAHs in the world comes from burning of biomass, partic-
ularly bio-fuel burning in India and China (Banks et al. 2019).
PAHs are typically at low concentration in drinking water and humans are exposed to contamination
through air pollution, tobacco smoke (smokers are among the most exposed groups) and diet (smoked
or grilled meat) (WHO 2010).
Low heat combustion, such as road traffic and tobacco smoke, result in smaller PAH compounds with
limited stability and range. These tend to dominate local pollution.
PAHs have been observed in remote sites, indicating a tendency to long range transportation (LRT).
Sources for LRT are mainly wildfires and biofuel burning – notably because of the high temperatures
of such combustion and the increased stability of the compounds formed (J. Keyte et al. 2013).
Besides the primary emission, PAHs are subject to re-volatilisation after condensation and gas ex-
change with vegetation, soils, and water surfaces. PAHs persist for decades in soil once deposited and
can be re-emitted after interacting with organic matter and black carbon. Their natural degradation
in soil and water is temperature dependent.

PAH in the Arctic
Greenlandic legislation limits the sum of PAHs (ΣPAH) concentrations in the drinking water at 0.1
µg/L, with a tighter limit for Benzo(a)pyrene (0.01 µg/L) (Pedersen 2021). This compound is
commonly used as a marker of PAH mixtures.
Local sources of PAHs in the Arctic are the limited road traffic, forest fires, and fuel combustion.
PAHs that undergo LRT are stored in Arctic soil and water where their degradation is slow to null,
because of the low temperatures. The transportation occurs in plumes of open fires, whether they
are biofuel burning in India or boreal forest fires. Because PAHs are subject to photo-degradation,
emissions reaching the Arctic in winter are usually higher than those reaching in the summer (J. Keyte
et al. 2013).
In 1993, a study at Dye 4 (Kulusuk) investigated the partitioning of PAHs in the atmospheric and
particulate phases. Jaffrezo et al. (1993) finds an absence of gaseous fraction and a particulate phase
limited to heavy PAHs (more than 3 rings). These results can be explained by the lower stability
of lighter PAHs and the absence of degradation during LRT for the particulate phase. This suggests
that any contamination in the Arctic of PAHs with low molecular weights ( ≤3 rings) would be from
local sources. These less volatile PAHs follow the ”one-hop” pathway. If a compound is emitted to the
atmosphere, transported and deposited to the Earth’s surface, never to return to the atmosphere (i.e.
one-hop), the distribution of this contaminant could be determined by knowledge of its initial source
distribution, atmospheric circulation and its lifetime in the atmosphere (Macdonald et al. 2000).

1.2
Greenland’s drinking water supply
1.2.1
Current State
Greenland holds, in its ice cap, 8% of the world’s freshwater reserves (Greenland’s Ice Island Alarm
2007). For the 60 000 people living on the island, tap water is almost exclusively supplied by Nukissior-
fiit – save for a few bases and airports which have their own supply. Nukissiorfiit is a government-owned
energy and water company.
Tap water is produced from surface water everywhere in Greenland, except for the Disko island where
groundwater is available. This surface water can either be seawater; melt water from yearly melting off;
or rain water, collected from freshwater lakes and rivers in the vicinity of towns (Schoenemann-Paul
2022), (Nukissiorfiit 2022). Compared with biomes at lower latitudes, Greenland – and the Arctic in
general – lack a clay and humus layer that would slow or stop organic pollution. This renders drinking
water supplied from surface freshwater more sensitive to changes in the pollution. Because it lacks a
buffer layer, any changes in pollution will be reverberated in the drinking water.
The seawater is desalinised and microfiltered in reverse osmosis membranes. This is an expensive and
time consuming process reserved to isolated, sparsely populated towns.
Figure 1: Taphouse in Kuumiut
Nukissiorfiit’s standard treatment of the water is a succession of
sand filters and UV light. Depending on the quality of water,
its end use – namely if the vandværk supplies a fish factory or
not – and the size of the population, the treatment will include
chlorine and PAX (aluminum polychloride used in floculation and
aggregation). These chemical treatments are subject to varying
use, meant to adapt to the changes brought about by the seasons
and weather conditions. This treatment does not affect the BTEX
and PAH concentrations in the water.
In towns – with larger population and grid – the water is delivered
directly to the houses via pipes. Some houses are equipped with
a water tank that Nukissiorfiit periodically refills. In settlements,
the water is supplied with a grid of ”imertartarfik”, taphouses
where the inhabitants come to collect water (Fig 1).

1.2.2
Challenges
Arctic amplification
Arctic amplification is the phenomenon for which any change in the net radiation balance tends to
produce a larger change near the poles than in the planetary average. Despite enjoying the reputation of
being a pristine environment, the Arctic is a receptor of Eurasian and American continental pollution.
The specific weather conditions of the Arctic and its biome contribute to the capture and storage
of these pollutants. Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs) are cold-trapped at high latitudes because
they are unable to re-enter the atmosphere and efficiently degrade. This leads to their accumulation
in the boreal regions.

The Arctic Haze is an optical and meteorological phenomenon. It enables its chemical ingredients
to persist in the atmosphere for significantly longer than other pollutants. The haze is caused by
temperature inversions during the Arctic winter, trapping pollutants in a stable atmosphere where
sunlight is nearly completely lacking. The result is a visible reddish-brown haze, with maximum
occurrence in late spring/early spring (Shaw 1995). The phenomenon of arctic haze in the winter half
of the year is a clear manifestation of hemispheric pollution reaching the north (Barrie et al. 1992).
Contaminants, if deposited in surface waters, are directly transported along watercourses. Xenobiotics
(i.e. chemical compounds unknown to the local organisms, which are metabolised but not needed
for their normal functioning) are deposited on the soil surface and migrate via the drainage system.
Pollutants deposited on the snow reach the soil and groundwater through percolating water. They
are then drained into surface waters (Kozak 2016). Persistent pollutants in the snow constitute a

secondary emission source. POPs enter the Arctic freshwater environment through river run-off and
atmospheric deposition, and accumulate in lake sediments and catchment area (Vorkamp et al. 2022).
Some pollutants now banned may be trapped in permafrost and prone to gradual resurgence in the
years to come. Thawing leads to the release of old carbon compounds and heavy metals from organic-
rich soils (Stern et al. 2012).
Arctic amplification and the other phenomena explained above have been identified as risk factors
for arctic communities regarding drinking water contamination and food contaminants (Previdi et al.
2021). As a result, Greenlandic Inuits have the most elevated levels of POPs in their blood compared
to any other population, due to their consumption of seal meat and other marine mammals (Martinez-
Swatson et al. 2020). The lipophilicity (affinity for fatty compounds) of many POPs, including PAHs,
accelerates their accumulation in plant and animal tissues, magnifying in concentration as the trophic
level rises (Franzaring & van der Eerden 2000).
The warming of average temperatures allows the POPs to have a greater volatility and affects the
mass balance between water and sediments. Thus, some POPs that were previously accumulated in
the sediments might partition from the particle phase to the liquid phase and enter the freshwater
environment.
Climate change and regime change
Climate models project a 35-60% increase in Arctic precipitation over the coming century (Stocker
2014). Advection of moist air masses from lower latitudes will become more frequent in the future as
pole-ward transport of atmospheric moisture is expected to increase with climate change, especially
during spring, summer, and autumn (Christensen et al. 2021). Another cause is the enhanced surface
evaporation associated with sea-ice retreat (Bintanja & Selten 2014), Figure 3.

Climate change also brings about a positive feedback loop with forest fires in the circumboreal forest.
The new weather regime is anticipated to come with a 50% increase in fire occurrence as well as a
doubling of area burned (Flannigan et al. 2009). This exacerbates climate change by disturbing the
carbon cycle and permafrost ecosystems. It also means that emissions from local sources of Black
Carbon (BC) and aromatic hydrocarbons will increase.
Glacial meltwater
Despite Greenland holding a consequential portion of the Earth’s fresh water, most of this water
does not end up in the taps of Greenlandic people. Water discharged from the melting of Greenland
Ice Sheet contains sediment, solutes, total phosphorus (TP), dissolved inorganic nitrogen (DIN), and
other macro- and micronutrients. Due to the powerful interactions between glaciers and bedrock,
glacial meltwater is rich in glacial flour (fine-grained, silt-sized particles of rock) rich in heavy metals
(Burpee et al. 2018). This renders the water unsuitable for drinking purposes in many cases. In Ilulissat
for example, the water running off from the hydropower dam in Paakitsup Akuliarusersua (45km north
of the town) is too high in Aluminum to be used as tap water (John & Stocker 2022).
Even though ”continental” towns – understood as the ones on the main island, not smaller islands
detached from the inland ice – have access to a large quantity of water with the glacial meltwater,
this water is used almost exclusively for hydropower generation, not for local homes. For many towns,
this limits the drinking water supply to meltwater from yearly precipitations.

Recent scientific publications (2018 and onward) focus on biomonitoring and biodegradation of PAH
in marine environments. Older publications study global trends through ice cores and glacial studies.
There are not enough drinking water studies in Greenland. Nonetheless, PAH drinking water studies
in Poland, China, the US and Canada cite Greenlandic data as background to compare results or
historical trends.
Martinez-Swatson et al. (2020) analyses the exposure of Greenlandic people to transported PAHs, but
the route studied is the consumption of marine mammals and wild game, not the drinking water.
Some studies on transportation and deposition include BTEX under the umbrella term VOC (Volatile
Organic Compounds), like Pernov (2018) or Meyer & Wania (2008). Other scholars focus on the impact
in the marine environment and its capacity to remediate spills and contamination (Fritt-Rasmussen
et al. 2013); or on the soil (Sørensen et al. 2010).
BTEX have been studied in the Russian (Kosyakov et al. 2021), Norwegian (Brakstad et al. (2018),
Reimann et al. (2009)), and American Arctic (Richmond et al. 2001). There is a strong focus on
biodegradation and biological treatment of BTEX surrounding oil spills.
Conclusion
Aromatic hydrocarbons are subject to some degree of monitoring in the Arctic. This monitoring is
conducted in ice cores, marine environments, and through bio-monitoring species.
PAHs are more studied than BTEX. Recent studies have focused on the biodegradation of aromatic
hydrocarbons and potential oil exploitation externalities on the Arctic biome.
Although the mechanisms of arctic amplification, long range transportation, bio-accumulation, and
global warming are well studied, I find there is a lack of studies on Greenland’s exposure to Northern
hemisphere aromatic compounds emissions. There is further a lack of data monitoring the contami-
nation of Greenlandic drinking water – even though such studies of aromatic hydrocarbons exist in
the Arctic. This is critical given the need for ascertainment of contamination in the Arctic and the
changes that climate change brings about.

2.1.1
Areas of concern
In order to select areas where field work would be conducted, a discussion was held with Nukissiorfiit
and the Ministry of Environment of Greenland. The areas where pollution is highly concentrated are
densely populated or areas where towns and settlements are clustered, namely :
• The Angmassalik region (East Greenland) : Tasiilaq, Kuumiut, Kulusuk, Sermiligaaq, Tiniteqi-
aaq and Isortoq.
• The Disko bay : Ilulissat, Aasiaat, Qiasannguit
• Western Kujalleq region (South Greenland) : Narsaq, Qaqortoq...
Out of these areas, East Greenland was chosen. The relatively low accessibility of the region has
limited past field work by other actors. However, due to the low amount of personnel and low logistic
costs, this study circumvented this obstacle. A focus on East Greenland coincided with the priorities
of Nukissiorfiit and Naalakkersuisut (the Greenlandic Government), which wanted to investigate the
region due to high levels of pollution in an area with a lower population density. 3000 people live
in Tasiilaq, the largest town in the region. Surrounding settlements are occupied by no more than
100-300 people. In comparison, many Western towns exceed 1500 people (4500 in Ilulissat, 3000 in
Aasiaat, 1500 in Narsaq, and 3000 in Qaqortoq). This field work took place in July 2022.
After the first field work was completed, all parties agreed to an extension of the covered area supported
by remaining funding. The Disko area was chosen because of its high pollution levels. This field work
spanned between August and September 2022.
2.2
Sampling campaigns
2.2.1
Generalities
Field work was accompanied by Nukissiorfiit and followed DTU guidelines. In each town, a typical
procedure was followed. It stared with discussing the local catchment and hydrology with Nukissiorfiit
employees, analysing maps to identify the waterways feeding the water treatment plant (vandvaerk in
the text), and finally taking water samples between the different water bodies.
Nukissiorfiit provided logistical support whenever the water catchment areas were not accessible by
foot.
The tests conducted by Nukissiorfiit are only done on raw water at the vandværk. Conducting tests
throughout the course of the water provides useful knowledge to determine where pollution first enters
the water. This in turn gives information on the origin and type of deposition of these pollutants.
For each vandværk, samples were taken of raw water, treated water, and surface water (where the
water is pumped). Additional samples were taken, when relevant, at lakes, rivers, and other reservoirs.

Furthermore, background data samples were gathered from more remote water bodies and bodies not

Each research location was divided into zones, marked in red on Figure 5. Sample locations were
identified as shown by the black arrows.
Sampling before and after the piping, catchment lake, and river helps identify the contribution of each
zone in the total contamination. Each zone has a likely source of pollution and remediation.
• Pipe : if detection is low for the pumped water sample but high in the raw/treated water,
the pollution occurs within the piping system itself. Therefore pollution can be reduced by
cleaning/changing the pipe. In this case, the pollution is local.
• Lake : if the water entering the lake is clean but the water sample at the vandvaerk is polluted,
there is likely a source of pollution within the lake itself. This could either be due to litter or re-
suspension of pollutants within seasonal cycles. Litter indicates local pollution and re-suspension
can be linked to long range transportation (LRT) and regime change.
• River/ snow/ ice : if the pollution detected in the melt water sample is comparable to the one
in the treated water, the pollution entering the water between the river and the vandvaerk is
negligible. Rivers and other water bodies in this zone are usually far from the human activity.
Pollution present in the water is thus likely remote, but snowmobile activity can also be a source
of contamination in such cases.
In order to maximise the efficiency of the study and minimize the number of sample analyzed, other
samples were only analyzed if pollution was detected in the ”raw water” sample.

2.3.1
Analysis of BTEXn from water samples by HS-GC-MS
BTEXn, that includes the compounds mentioned above, are analysed from water samples by headspace-
GC-MS (HS-GC-MS).
Water samples are collected in containers with no headspace where after a certain volume is transferred
to a GC-MS vial containing internal standard (fluorobenzene). Heated and agitated incubation of the
samples is done automatically by the autosampler of the GC-MS prior to injection. Calibration and
control solutions are prepared in the same type of containers as the samples are collected. Two levels
of calibrations are typically used; low (0.04 – 40 µg/l) and high (20 – 3500 µg/l). Analysis time on the
GC-MS is 28 min per sample including GC cool down time.
Incubation and Chromatography
Sample vials are incubated in a rotary shaker at 500 rpm 90 °C for 5 min and 250 µl headspace
is injected in pulsed splitless mode at 20 psi until 0.2 min at 80 °C. Chromatographic separation
is achieved on a 30 m x 0.25 mm I.D x 1.0 µm film thickness HP-5 ms capillary column (Agilent
Technologies). The initial column temperature is set to 60 °C then ramped at 15 °C min−1 to 75 °C
then 3 °C min−1 to 90 °C for 2 min then 25 °C min−1 to 170 °C for 5 min then 50 °C min−1 to
300 °C. The final temperature is held for 4 min and the total run time is 28 min with Helium (0.868
ml.min−1) as carrier gas (Appendix C.2).
Detection
Detection is achieved using an Agilent 5975C electron impact (70 eV) triple-axis mass-selective de-
tector. The mass-selective detector temperatures are 230 °C for the EI source and 150 °C for the
quadropole with the transfer line held at 250 °C. The spectra are monitored in selected ion monitoring
(SIM) mode (Appendix C.3).

reporting template (Appendix C.8).

2.3.2
Analysis of PAHs in water using solid-phase extraction (SPE) and GC-MS.
Naphthalene, acenaphthylene, acenaphthene, fluorene, antracene, fluoranthene, pyrene, benzo(k)fluoranthene,
benzo(b)fluoroanthene, benzo(a)antracene, benzo(a)pyrene, chrysene, phenantrene, indeno(1,2,3-cd)pyrene,
dibenzo(a,h)anthracene, benzo(ghi)perylene are extracted using 1 g / 6ml ENVI-18™(Supelco) SPE
columns and analyzed by GC-MS. Using 100 ml sample the quantification limit is 0,25 µg/l.
Sample preparation
The pH of the sample is adjusted to 2 using 1 % 4 M phosphate buffer pH 2,2 and pyrene-d10 [20
µg/l ] and perylene-d12 [20 µg/l] in acetone are added as internal standards.
SPE
The SPE column is conditioned using 2x6 ml toluene:methanol (9:1) followed by 6 ml methanol and
6 ml deionized water. The sample is loaded using vacuum with a maximum flow rate of 10 ml/min
through the column and the vacuum applied for an additional 1 hour to completely dry the column.
The PAHs are eluted using 2x1 ml toluene:methanol (9:1) and a small amount of anhydrous Na2SO4
is added to each sample vial to remove any residual water. When the two phases have settled the
upper phase is transferred to a GC-MS vial.
GC-MS
Chromatographic separation is achieved on an Agilent 6890 gas chromatograph equipped with a 60 m
x 0,25 mm i.d x 0,25 µm film thickness ZB-5ms column with a 5 m GuardZ column (Phenomenex). 2
µl sample is injected in splitlesss mode with the sample inlet held at 300 °C. The oven is programmed
to 70 °C, then 20 °C/min to 300 °C, then 50 °C/min to 325 °C held for 10 min. Helium is used
as carrier gas with a 1 ml/min constant flow. Detection is achieved on an Agilent 5975C triple-axis
mass-selective detector operated in SIM mode with the MS source at 230 °C and the quadropole at
150 °C.

3.1
Analysis results
General Limitations
Due to the general heavy duty of the laboratory and the plurality of samples sources, some samples
spent a relatively long time in DTU’s refrigerator. Communication with laboratory staff was also
hindered by the overwhelming workload at the time of the analysis.
There generally is long travel time between Greenland and Copenhagen. The west coast is reached
through Kangerlussuaq and the east through Reykjavik. The long flights and transfer time negatively
impact the preservation of the samples. However, the analysis also included samples that spent a short
amount of time in both transport and storage, offering comparison between the two.
None of the samples returned results above the detection limit, in either of the two
rounds of analysis. The detection limits were :
• 1st round - BTEX : 0.02 µg/L
• 1st round - PAH : 0.25 µg/L
• 2nd round - BTEX : 0.017 µg/L
These unexpected results are not in agreement with those obtained in yearly analyses by Nukissiorfiit
and the ones in literature cited in the introduction. This triggered an intense review of the analysis
process from the sampling campaign to lab analysis, including logistic conditions. Results of the quality
controls (QC) and spiked samples lead to the conclusion that the results are trustworthy and that
concentrations in the water at the time of the field work were low.
Concentration below detection limit is not unusual in the yearly testing. What is surprising is the fact
that, out of all the contaminants tested, none gave a result. The following sections detail the findings,
the QC values, and the spike recovery.

3.1.2
Second round of analysis and Spike recovery results
As mentioned in 2.3, this round focused on BTEX at low concentrations. Since the first analysis
showed no contamination, and the most widespread organic pollution in the Greenlandic drinking
water is BTEX at low concentrations, the analysis of PAH was not re-conducted. A lower detection
was achieved by using a more modern and sensitive machine.
In order to test the robustness of the method and rule out a default in the analysis, a spike recovery
was included. A spike recovery is a similar process to the one of the QC samples. A known quantity
of pollutant is added into a sample to spike it. The recovery percentage here indicates the robustness
of the method.
Due to poor communication with the laboratory staff, the exact concentrations of the spike were not
recorded. However, in the lab’s standard procedures, spike concentration are typically 5 µg/L. A spike
of this concentration is plausible for all compounds in Table 10 except Toluene. m+p-Xylene likely
had a 10 µg/L spike since 5µg/L of each isomer (meta- and para-) was added. Toluene likely had a
20µg/L spike, which is not in accordance with standard procedure in the lab.
This only affects the quantification, rendering it inexact, but not the sensitivity of the machine. For
all compounds except Toluene, the spike recovery percentage (the amount of compound detected
compared to the spike) is 110 +- 10% – which is satisfactory. An average recovery percentage above
100% indicates a slim presence of pollutants in the sample. However the confidence interval is too
large for this to be decisive.

To explain the outcome of the study, potential changes affecting concentrations, transportation, and
local and remote sources of pollutants need to be addressed. As seen in sections 2.2.2 and 2.2.3, the
meteorological conditions for both sampling campaigns conducted for this study were exceptional.
West Coast
The West coast had its wettest year in 2022. As seen in Table 11 displaying the GEUS data, the average
detected concentrations across Greenland were low. In general, BTEX concentrations in groundwater
reflect the outcome of multiple processes such as source dissolution, dispersion and dilution (Ramos
et al. 2013). This means that increased precipitations would have diluted the average concentrations
of GEUS to levels below the detection limit. In observing the meteorological conditions and results
for the West coast sampling campaign, dilution of concentrations by precipitations appears to be a
worthy hypothesis for the outcome of this study.
This is supported by data in the GEUS database. The spring and summer seasons of 2019 were
particularly dry, and many places were very low on water capacity before the rains started in 2020
(Sørensen 2022). This coincided with high concentrations of pollutants detected in the water. The peak
in Figure 32.(b) corresponds to sample 1507687, taken on the 04/06/2019. 0,57 µg/L of m+p-Xylene
were found, along with concentrations around 0.20 µg/L of toluene, o-xylene, and ethylbenzene.

East Coast
Although the argument presented above applies well for the West coast, dilution is not a viable
hypothesis for the Angmassalik region (East coast). As field work took place during the driest month
of the year, another explanation needs to be explored for the East. Studies have shown that atmospheric
BTEX and PAH compounds are prone to wet deposition (Liu et al. (2018), Zahed et al. (2010)). Since
the precipitations were low, the deposition from long range sources would have been low as well.
This suggests that local sources in the Angmassalik region, dominating this period, had a negligible
contribution to drinking water contamination.
Local sources of aromatic compounds in the Arctic include snowmobile activity, waste incineration,
and spillage. Snowmobiles are only used in the winter and there are no incinerators in the East.
Contaminants released by snowmobiles are stored in the snow and percolate into the water during the
melt season. July was the second month of the 2022 with an average temperature above 0°C (Figure
33) and there was little to no snow left around the drinking water catchment area, signifying the
melting season had passed. This suggests that any local pollution that might have been detected had
already dispersed from the area at the time of the field work.

4.2
GEUS database
This section analyses the trends and values in the GEUS data and compares it with other studies.
Frequencies
Table 11 shows that detection the frequencies of BTEX and Benzo(a)pyrene in Greenland are high
(12-48%). Although the frequencies are high compared to other drinking water studies (Zhao et al.
(2020) and Li et al. (2013), China ; Moran et al. (2005), USA ; Wycisk et al. (2003), Germany),
the concentration ranges are much lower. This suggests a slower, more diffuse contamination of the
Greenlandic drinking water than that of lower latitudes.
The spatial distribution of contamination observed in Figures 22 and 25 is correlated with the distri-
bution of the human population in Greenland – caution is needed for this conclusion, since more tests
are conducted in these areas. This correlation suggests that local anthropogenic sources play a role in
the organic contamination of the drinking water. However the large detection frequencies in remote
areas show that local sources alone cannot account for the contamination.
Concentrations
The detection profile of PAHs presented in GEUS is not in accordance with other studies in Greenland.
There have been no studies conducted on Greenlandic drinking water. The occurrence of PAH levels
in snow, sediments, soil, berries and other elements have received more scientific attention :
• Two studies analyzed snow collected at the Summit research station, on the Greenlandic in-
land ice. Lebedev et al. (2015) found higher levels of fluoranthene (0.023–0.457 µg/L) and
benzo[a]pyrene (0.0011–0.187 µg/L). Masclet et al. (2000b) found fluoranthene and pyrene con-
centrations ranging from 0.1 to 4.7 µg/L.
• In a sediment study, Elmquist et al. (2008) found ΣPAH (sum of PAHs) concentrations ranging
from 0.024 to 0.454 µg/L in the surface sediments of seven Pan-Arctic Rivers (spanning Eurasia
and America).
• Crowberries around Ilulissat were found to contain 0.69 to 93.01 µg of PAH /kgdryweight (Kronik
et al. 2021).
The detection ranges of the aforementioned studies are notably higher than the ones in GEUS (as
seen in Figure 21). There are some possible explanations for this difference. Although PAHs reach
and are deposited in the Arctic, they degrade or become volatile before reaching the drinking water
catchment. Due to their lipophilia, PAH accumulate in fat and organic tissues. Greenlandic people
are heavily exposed to PAHs through their consumption of higher trophic marine animals for this
reason (Martinez-Swatson et al. 2020). PAH also tend to accumulate in sediments (marine or riverine)
(Dong et al. 2015). Concentrations in such studies can thus be expected to be higher than in drinking
water values. Studying the sediments in the water catchment areas of exposed towns will clarify the
pollution that the drinking water is in contact with.

Ratios
Recent studies have examined how the ratios between the BTEX and PAH compounds can provide
insight into source origination, as well as the photochemical age of the air mass transporting them
(Miller et al. 2011) :
• A specific range of the toluene to benzene ratio (T/B) has been used as an indicator of traffic
emissions (Miller et al. 2011).
• The m+p-Xylene to ethylbenzene ratio can act as an indicator for the photo-chemical age of the
air mass (Nelson & Quigley 1983).
• Ratios of concentrations between PAH compounds are indicative of the source of the pollution
(Elmquist et al. 2008).
As shown by Gelencs´er et al. (1997), toluene and benzene concentration are prone to reduction through
interactions with OH radicals, with a typical T/B ratio of 5:1. Given that all concentrations of Green-
landic pollutants are low, the typical toluene/benzene ratio gives insight on the concentration of
benzene. If the toluene concentration in a sample is 0.039 µg/L (average value from Table 39) and
the ratio is respected, the sample also contains 0.008 µg/L of benzene. This concentrations would not
reported because it falls under the detection limit (0.02µg/L). This potentially explains the absence
of benzene in the ratios shown in section 3.2.4.
The typical m+p-Xylene to ethylbenzene ratio (mp/E) reported by Nelson & Quigley (1983) and
Monod et al. (2001) is 3.6:1. This is not in accordance with the mp/E ratios found in the GEUS
database (1:1 - 1.6:1). mp/E ratios similar to those in GEUS have been found in urban environments
in Rome and Tokyo (Brocco et al. 1997) (Wadden et al. 1986). Wadden et al. (1986) break down
several mp/E ratios observed for different sources : vehicle exhaust, gasoline vapor, petroleum refinery,
paint solvent and petrochemical paint. All these sources have a ratio in the 1:1 - 1.6:1 range in this
study, beside the paint solvent, which has a ratio of 2.8:1. This excludes paint solvent as a source for
Greenlandic m+p-Xylene and ethylbenzene pollution.
Further studies are needed to explain why the ratios observed in Greenland differ from the global
range.

General Discussion
4.3.1
Influence of climate change on organic pollution in the Arctic
Temperatures will inevitably rise with climate change. In the Arctic, they will rise faster than anywhere
else in the world (Rantanen et al. 2022). This temperature increase comes with myriad effects on the
Arctic environment. From changes in precipitation patterns and form, to more extreme weather events,
the whole meteorological system will be altered. Among these changes, organic pollution in Greenland
will be strongly impacted as well.
Evidence suggest that the long range transportation of pollutants will decrease with higher temper-
atures. PAH degradation is temperature dependent. Lebedev et al. (2015) found that PAH levels in
snow in polar regions and high mountains decreased in the winter of 1989/1990 due to the exception-
ally warm weather. This suggests that PAHs would degrade before depositing in remote areas such as
Greenland. Additionally, emissions from mid-latitudes have decreased and are expected to continue
decreasing with the passing of emission regulations (for reference, see : Indonesia (Huboyo et al. 2019);
Japan (Xing et al. 2022); China (Wu et al. 2021); France and Germany (Bessagnet & Allemand 2020);
Korea (Myung et al. 2020)). Warmer wintry temperatures will thus hinder the transportation of al-
ready abating emissions. Because of global warming and emission regulations, the Arctic can expect
a decrease in the load of long range contaminants.
However, when it comes to organic pollution, there are also negative impacts to consider with rising
temperatures. Most notably, the new weather regime is anticipated to come with a 50% increase in
circumboreal forest fire occurrence, as well as a 100% increase in the area burned (Flannigan et al.
2009). Forest fires are one of the largest sources of Black Carbon and PAHs in the Arctic (Figure 34).

Additional factors will also increase the load of boreal organic pollution. The Arctic population is
expected to grow and heightened human activity in the Arctic comes with an upsurge in combustion
and potential pollution. Furthermore, global warming will greatly reduce the sea-ice cover in the Arctic,
opening the Northern routes and increasing ship traffic in the Arctic Ocean. Without the proper
regulation frame, heavy fuel burning by cargo ships will inevitably release organic hydrocarbons into
the atmosphere and the sea. Lastly, the melting of the ice cap releases ”legacy pollutants”, deposited
long before legislation was put into place controlling these emissions. Pollutants – even those that
have been banned since – are stored in the Arctic ice, and their dispersion threatens to offset efforts
in reducing emissions. The Arctic can thus expect higher emissions released within the polar region.
PAHs and BTEX respond differently to climate change depending on their volatility. Their future
behavior in the climate will be controlled primarily by competition between decreasing deposition and
increasing re-emissions (Friedman et al. 2014). Higher occurrence of extreme weather events, such as
the unusually dry and wet month of the fieldwork of this study, will make modelling of future trends
more complex and less specific. Liu et al. (2015), in their study of volatile organic compounds, explain
that the model-measurement discrepancy is caused by physical dilution, transport, and heterogeneous
processes. At the time of writing, Tasiilaq and the Angmassalik region are experiencing the sixth
Piteraq (a particularly capricious and violent wind phenomenon) since September. The rate and the
strength of these storms are drastically increasing (Damberg 2020), worsening living and research
conditions.
Climate change will strongly influence the organic pollution in the Arctic. Studies predict contradictory
trends for the aromatic hydrocarbons polluting the region. Further studies are needed to establish
which phenomena will dominate. The results in section 3.2 show an increase of organic pollution in
Greenland in the recent years. Additionally, significant decline of PAH concentrations was not observed
by Yu et al. (2019) and Luo et al. (2020) (Figure 34), despite the expected decline due to PAH emission
reduction. The study conducted in the European and Greenlandic Arctic by Yu et al. concludes that
the expected decline in arctic concentration is offset by the increment in volatilizing components,
caused by global warming. Luo et al. (2020) point at boreal forest fires as a cause for the PAH levels.
This suggest that climate change has an overall malign effect on the organic pollution of Greenland.
4.3.2
COVID-19 and air pollution
Towards the end of 2019, the first cases of pneumonia linked to the COVID-19 viral strain appeared
in Wuhan, China. What ensued was a global pandemic and extensive lock-downs across the globe.
The curtailment of personal travel and economic activity was accompanied by a 30-60% reduction
in atmospheric levels of nitrogen dioxide, carbon monoxide, particulate matter, and volatile organic
compounds (VOC) (Adam et al. (2021), Mulkay et al. (2020)). Organic hydrocarbons are a subgroup
of VOC, and particulate matter (PM) is a vector for long-range transport of particle-bound PAHs
(as opposed to volatile PAHs). Particle changes have a great impact on the transport of PAH to the
Arctic (Friedman et al. 2014).

Conclusion and outlook
Pollution in the Arctic has been the subject of studies for decades and provided useful global insight
on pollution. Studies conducted in the Arctic have been used to determine the efficiency of regulations
passed in industrialized countries and to compare historic records of global pollutants. Aromatic
hydrocarbons (AH) are not only a concern of international pollution with the Arctic as a measuring
station. Organic pollution directly affect health and livelihoods of Arctic communities. POP pollution
can be comparable to the theory of humanist environmentalists like Nixon (2011), who discuss the
slow violence of pollution, detrimental to its victims only decades after its release. As Robert Nixon
would put it, these pollutant are ”an attritional catastrophe, overspilling clear boundaries in time and
space – temporal, geographical, rhetorical, and technological displacements – that underestimate the
human and environmental costs”.
Contamination of Greenlandic drinking water by organic pollutants exhibits seasonal patterns and a
tendency towards increasing concentrations. Because the field work’s results were below the detection
limits, it can be inferred that this increase of pollution is irregular. This irregularity is caused by
extreme weather events, fluctuations in volatility of the compounds, and changes in the global emissions
landscape. Although emissions in mid-latitudes are expected to decrease, Arctic pollution sources are
projected to increase in the form of boreal forest fires and re-suspension of ”legacy pollutants”.
The GEUS database offers insight on the past and present contamination of Greenland, however it
lacks consistency regarding testing. Due to this, trends deducted from the GEUS database lack density
and repeatability. This is a problem in cases such as this study, where no evidence of pollution was
found. In the study of aromatic hydrocarbons in the Arctic, three main action items are suggested.
First, long-term observations are needed targeting AH and their tracers. Increased re-suspension and
emissions within the Arctic will produce signal enhancements, which will be hard to detect within the
inherent natural variability. Hence, new trends might only emerge in the years to come and be currently
invisible. These trends are important indicators of feedback mechanisms in Arctic amplification.
Second, the location of an observatory matters in determining the significance of pollution trends.
Future research needs to be more localised, as not every observatory will exhibit all types of aromatic
hydrocarbon changes clearly.
Third, consolidating the GEUS database and the regularity of testing is essential. Only long-term
time series can document Arctic change and further our understanding of the Arctic climate and its
biogeochemical processes. Standard operation procedures for the measurement properties are needed,
such that they can be efficiently compared (e.g. consistent analysis of the same parameters analysed,
of every town and settlement) (Schmale et al. 2022).
In summary, organic pollution is an important topic, relating to changes in anthropogenic and natural
atmospheric chemical influences on the Arctic and its population. This study presents several worrying
trends in aromatic hydrocarbon contamination that need to be further researched.

Recommendations
6.1
Drinking water treatment
Greenland’s drinking water is particularly exposed to aromatic hydrocarbons and organic pollution be-
cause the geology of Greenland lacks a clay and humus layer that would naturally buffer contaminants.
Given the increasing trends in BTEX and PAHs emissions, including a carbon filter into Nukissiorfiit’s
standard treatment would recreate the natural buffer present in other biomes, and reduce the exposure
of Greenland (Gong et al. 2007).
6.2
Verification of hypothesis and monitoring in the coming years
Conducting another similar study in the Angmassalik or Disko areas would help prove or disprove the
hypotheses brought forward in this report. Namely, the influence of extreme weather events on the
deposition of organic pollutants and the influence of COVID-19 lockdowns on anthropogenic emissions.
Recent developments raise concern for a sharp rise in BTEX and PAH pollution from combustion.
In many countries, deconfinement was accompanied by measures to stimulate an economic recovery.
These measures include the re-opening of coal plants and the relaxation of emission quotas. Rapidly
rising levels of air pollution have consistently been observed after deconfinement (Ikhlasse et al. 2021).
Furthermore, the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine exacerbates the diminishing concerns for emissions.
The energy crisis that ensued led many countries to resort to more polluting sources of energy (coal,
peat and domestic wood burning).

6.3
Regime change, precipitation, and organic pollution
Extreme weather events and disruptions in the weather regime have been brought forward as causes
for unusual contaminant concentrations in Greenland. By comparing DMI’s archives and the GEUS
database, a statistical study could be conducted to highlight correlations between detection meteoro-
logical conditions. Highlighting correlations helps determine the transportation and deposition modes
of pollutants and corroborate or disprove arguments brought forward in section 4.1. Additionally, the
influence of Piteraq on contamination is also an urgent topic given that the occurrence of these storms
is on the rise.

Ratios between BTEX are widely used as indicators of source and origin. For example, comparison of

+ BgP) and the Flu/(Flu + Pyr) ratios (seen and explained in Figure 38).

It is imperative to investigate the overall trends in Nuuk, especially after such high detections occurred.
At the time of writing, a new, international airport is under construction in the capital city. This large

6.7
Micro-plastics in the East
Micro-plastics are not a part of this study. Visual plastic pollution was observed around the drinking
water lakes in the Angmassalik region. To determine whether this pollution has an impact on the
drinking water and if remediation is needed, a study should be conducted in the region.
6.8
Bio-monitoring of aromatic hydrocarbons in Kulusuk
This study stands out compared to the literature, because of the low pollution concentrations found
during the field work. As mentioned in section 4.2, other studies have focused on carriers where BTEX
and PAH tend to accumulate (organic tissues, fat, river sediments). Kulusuk raised concerns regarding
the exposure of its drinking water to contamination (road traffic, airport, old mining equipment among
other sources). Data is lacking from the GEUS database to investigate the effects of this exposure.
Due to the presence of trout in the last lakes connected the catchment one, a bio-monitoring study of
BTEX and PAH levels in the fish can be conducted. Such a study would investigate potentially higher
concentrations of organic compounds than in water, and inform on the safe consumption of both the
trouts and the drinking water in Kulusuk.

6.9
How to keep the water clean
Despite the ominous tone of this study, the results of the sampling campaign are great news for the
Greenlandic people. Drinking water in 15 towns, covering the water supply of 75% of the population
of Greenland, have been analysed as free of toxic BTEX and PAH compounds. Further research is
needed to establish which parameters of these sampling campaigns were dominant in the results and
how they can be replicated. Greenland does not have direct influence on the amount of precipitation
is receives, but the arguments regarding the global emission landscape can be used as a starting point
for international discussion. If COVID-19 did play a role in the clean water in Greenland, what can
be learned and applied ? Can mid-latitudes countries durably quell their emissions ?

C.1
Calibration standards and controls
Stock 1 Calibration mix
Add 100 µl of each compound (and 116 mg solid naphthalene) to 100 ml methanol. Store at -20 °C.
Stock 2
Add 1 ml of Stock 1 calibration mix to 115 ml water in a 118 ml crimp sealed vial. Add the water
and crimp seal the vial before adding Stock 1 calibration mix through the septa. Incubate at room
temp under gentle shaking for 2 h. If necessary store upside down at 4 °C.
Stock 3 (low range only)
Add 10 ml Stock 2 to 1100 ml water in a bottle with gas tight septa. Add water and close the cap
before adding Stock. Store upside down at 4 °C.
Calibration standards no. 1-11
Calibration standards no. 1-11 are prepared in 40 ml vials with a Teflon lined septa in the cap.
First add water and then Stock 2 or Stock 3 (depending on range) by submerging the needle below
the water surface, with the cap closed as much as possible (Appendix C.5). Store upside down.
Quality control Stock 1 (BTEX)
The Quality control stock 1 is prepared from the BTEX Mix, High Concentration 2000 µg/ml
(Sigma no CRM47993). It contains all the compounds except naphthalene.
Break the ampoule and dissolve 1 ml in 9 ml methanol. The final concentration is 200 µg/ml. The
mix is stored as 1 ml aliquots at -20 °C.
Quality control stock 2 (BTEX)
Add l ml Quality control stock 1 to 115 ml water in a 118 ml crimp sealed vial. Incubate at room
temp under gentle shaking for 2 h. The final concentration is 1.73 µg/ml
Quality control standard high (BTEX)*
The Quality control standard high is prepared in a 40 ml vial with a Teflon lined septa in the
cap. Add 40 µl Quality control stock 1 to 40 ml water. The final concentration is 200 µg/l.
Quality control standard low (BTEX)*
The Quality control standard low is prepared in a 40 ml vial with a Teflon lined septa in the cap.
Add 40 µl Quality control stock 2 to 40 ml water. The final concentration is 1.73 µg/l.
*The concentrations of the quality control standards can be changed in if necessary to cover a higher
or lower calibration range.

Add 100 µl pure fluorobenzene to 100 ml methanol in a 118 ml crimp sealed vial. Store upside down

C.6
Guide to preparation of samples and standards
Preparation of fluorobenzene internal standard
1. Add 100 µl pure fluorobenzene in 100 ml methanol in a 118 ml crimp seal vial (Internal
standard stock 1). Fill the vial with methanol, seal the cap and add the fluorobenzene through
the septa using a 100 µl Hamilton glass syringe. [A]
2. Add 10 ml of stock 1 to 1100 ml dH2O in a 1 l flask with crimp seal cap (Internal standard
working solution 1). Fill the flask with water, seal the cap and add stock 1 through the septa
using a 10 ml plastic syringe. [B]. Incubate at room temp under gentle shaking for 2 h.
3. Low range only. Add 10 ml of Internal standard working solution 1 to 1100 ml dH2O in a 1 l
flask with crimp seal cap (Internal standard working solution 2). Fill the flask with water,
seal the cap and add stock 1 through the septa using a 10 ml plastic syringe. [B].
4. Wrap the flask in aluminum foil and store horizontally at 4C.

(Stock 2). Fill the flask with water, seal the cap and add stock 1 through the septa using a 10
ml plastic syringe.

vial loosen the screw cap a fraction when holding the vial upside down to equalize the pressure.
Turn upside down until analysis. [F]

C.7
Start up procedure – Details
HS-GC-MS start up procedure
The following parameters should be checked in the indicated order before running a sample series.
Background check
1. Empty vial
2. Water
3. Blank (water + internal standard)
Performance check
1. Standard 10 in the calibration curve
2. Standard 1 in the calibration curve
3. Blank
No peaks should be detected when running the background check and the lowest standard in the
performance check should give a clear peak. The highest standard in the performance check should
have good peak symmetry and show no carry-over to the performance check blank.
Background check
If any background signal is detected change the following part in the autosampler and GC.
1. Injection syringe
2. Inlet septa
3. Glass liner
If the background signal persists run the lowest calibration standard and compare the signals. If the
background signal is at least 10 times lower than for the lowest standard you may continue with the
performance check.

Performance check
The lowest standard should give clear peaks well separated from the background and the highest
standard should give good peak symmetry. The following examples for the highest standard is not ok.
1. Cut off peak due to detector saturation
2. Fronting peak due to column overload
3. Split peak due to column overload or poor column properties
If the detector is saturated or the peaks are fronting less sample has to be injected and/or less sample
has to be put in the GCMS vial. Split peaks can occur from an overloaded column but if split peaks
are observed even at lower concentrations it is an indication that the column needs to be replaced.
