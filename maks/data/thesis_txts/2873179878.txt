Abstract
The rise of mobility-on-demand (MoD) services offers new opportunities for eﬀicient and
sustainable urban transportation, but it also introduces challenges related to supply-
demand imbalances and competitive dynamics between providers. This thesis focuses
on autonomous mobility-on-demand (AMoD) systems, where self-driving vehicles operate
without human drivers and can be dynamically repositioned and priced. A multi-agent
reinforcement learning (MARL) framework is proposed, in which two independent agents
manage separate fleets in a shared environment based on real-world taxi demand data from
Brooklyn, New York. Each agent jointly learns rebalancing and pricing strategies using
the Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) algorithm combined with graph neural networks (GNNs) to
capture spatial structure. Rebalancing actions are translated into vehicle flows using a
Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) formulation that minimizes rebalancing costs
subject to operational constraints. Passenger decisions are simulated using a multinomial
logit model sensitive to price and travel time. While the jointly learned strategies do not
outperform a centralized baseline, they offer a more realistic view of how independent
operators behave in a competitive setting and show how agent decisions adapt to changes
in parameters such as fleet distributions, customer wage levels, and demand conditions.

Introduction
As cities grow and urban populations increase, the number of people who have private
vehicles also rises. This increase makes maintaining effective transportation systems more
diﬀicult.
According to the United States (U.S.) Census Bureau, 91.6% of households
in U.S. own at least one private vehicle, and 58% of those own two or more[1]. This
trend is alarming because the transportation sector accounts for approximately 28% of
greenhouse gas emissions[2]. In addition, owning a private car can be costly for owner
due to limited parking areas and maintenance expenses. These challenges bring the need
for more sustainable mobility solutions.
One promising solution is the Mobility-on-Demand (MoD) model, where users request
transportation services through digital platforms. MoD systems include ride-hailing ser-
vices (such as Uber and Lyft), car-sharing, and shared micromobility options like e-bikes
and scooters.
These services allow people to access transportation without owning a
vehicle.
In recent years, MoD services have become an increasingly important part of urban trans-
portation systems. According to the North American Bikeshare and Scootershare Associ-
ation (NABSA), the number of cities in North America with shared micromobility services
increased by 47% in 2023 compared to 2020 [3]. Their popularity can be related to their
convenience, affordability, and environmental benefits. Since MoD systems rely on shared
vehicle fleets, they reduce the need for individuals to own cars. This leads to lower emis-
sions, decreased traﬀic, and less urban space devoted to parking. For users, these services
eliminate costs related to maintenance, parking and insurance, and offer fast and flexible
access to transport, especially in areas public transit does not reach.
Even though MoD systems have a lot of benefits, they face a major challenge: the imbal-
ance between supply and demand across different parts of a city. This creates a situation
where vehicles tend to accumulate in high demand areas while others are left with too few.
If not managed, this imbalance leads to longer waiting times for passengers, lower vehicle
utilization, and ineﬀicient operations. To manage this problem, two main strategies are
being investigated:
Rebalancing: moving idle vehicles to places where demand is expected to increase.
Dynamic pricing: adjusting ride prices based on real-time demand to influence when and
where passengers choose to travel.

While both strategies are studied, managing them jointly is still challenging. Demand
is uncertain, and pricing effects on user behavior can vary across time and location.
Moreover, in many cities, multiple MoD providers compete with one another, which makes
the system even more complex.
This thesis investigates Autonomous Mobility-on-Demand (AMoD) systems, which rely
on self-driving vehicles that operate without human drivers. These autonomous fleets can
reposition themselves, respond to ride requests, and make decisions about pricing without
human intervention. The study explores how joint rebalancing and pricing strategies can
be learned within such systems using modern machine learning techniques. Specifically, it
applies multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) to train artificial agents. Each agent
manages a separate fleet within a shared environment. These agents learn to set prices
and reposition vehicles to eﬀiciently meet demand while adapting to the strategies of com-
peting agents. The proposed approach integrates graph-based models to capture spatial
relationships between regions with the Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) reinforcement learning
algorithm.
To evaluate this approach, a simulation environment is developed using real-world demand
data from Brooklyn, New York. Two independent agents operate in the same city, each
managing its own fleet and setting its own prices. A passenger choice model is used to
simulate user decisions based on travel time and price, which allows demand to be split
between competing agents. Graph neural networks (GNNs) are applied to model spatial
and temporal relationships across the network. Experiments are conducted under varying
fleet sizes and wage levels, and performance is assessed based on the total reward achieved
by the system.

2.1.2
Markov Decision Process
In order to understand MDPs, Markov chains should first be understood.
A Markov chain is a stochastic model that describes a sequence of possible events in which
the probability of each event depends only on the state obtained after the previous event
[6].
A Markov chain consists of the following.
• A set of states S, where s ∈S
• An initial distribution of events µ0
• A transition probability matrix P where the probability of moving from state i to
state j is Pij
Pij = P(Xt+1 = sj | Xt = si)
(2.3)
A Markov Decision Process (MDP) extends the structure of a Markov chain by introducing
actions and rewards [7, 8].
In an MDP, an agent interacts with an environment and
makes decisions over discrete time steps to maximize the reward. An MDP consists of
the following.
• A set of states S, where s ∈S
• A set of actions A, where a ∈A
• A transition probability function P : S × A × S →[0, 1] indicating the probability
of moving to state s′ from state s after taking action a
P(s, a, s′) = P(st+1 = s′ | st = s, at = a)
(2.4)
• A reward function r : S × A →R indicating the expected reward received after
taking action a in state s
r(s, a) = E[rt | st = s, at = a]
(2.5)
• A discount factor γ ∈[0, 1] which trades off immediate and future rewards
MDPs provide a structured way to represent situations where decisions must be made
under uncertainty over time.
By including both the consequences of actions and the
rewards they produce, MDPs make it possible to analyze how different choices affect the
evolution of a system. This framework is useful in many applications that require making

RL is used to learn decision-making strategies through interaction with uncertain and
dynamic environments.
It is particularly effective in problems where decision-making
must adapt over time based on feedback from the system. RL has been applied in a
variety of areas, including game playing, robotics, recommendation systems, and resource
management, where learning through trial and error improves long-term performance.
Deep Reinforcement Learning
Deep Reinforcement Learning integrates RL with deep neural networks to handle complex,
high-dimensional, continuous environments.
In deep RL, neural networks are used to
estimate value functions and determine optimal actions through policies. Data is collected
from interactions with the environment and stored in a replay buffer. Then, the neural
networks are trained on this data using gradient-based optimization techniques.
Some methods aim to learn a value function Q(s, a), which estimates the expected return
of taking action a in state s. The agent then selects the action with the highest estimated
value. An example of this approach is the Deep Q-Network (DQN). Other methods learn
a policy π(a | s) directly, which gives the probability of selecting action a in state s. These
policies are trained to maximize expected return, as in REINFORCE and Proximal Policy
Optimization (PPO). Another group of methods includes both a policy model (actor) and
a value function model (critic). The actor chooses actions, while the critic evaluates them.
Examples include A3C, Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG), and SAC [9]. SAC
method is used in this project.

Multi Agent Reinforcement Learning
MARL extends traditional RL where multiple agents coexist in an environment and in-
teracts with it simultaneously. Each agent learns a policy to maximize its own expected
return independently.
In MARL, agents can have different type of interactions. In a cooperative setting, they
share a common goal that increases the importance of collaboration. In a competitive
setting, they have opposing goals, success of one agent may affect the success of the
other negatively. In mixed settings, agents can have partially aligning or conflicting goals,
which requires a balance between cooperation and competition. These different modes
of interaction make MARL suitable for modeling a wide range of real-world multi-agent
systems.

Choice Models
Choice Models are predictive models used to estimate decision-making behavior among a
set of discrete alternatives. It is a widely used approach in many fields from transportation
to economics to understand how individuals choose among options.
The main idea behind choice models is based on Random Utility Theory (RUT) and it
assumes that individuals assign a utility to each option and choosing the one with the
highest utility. Since there is always factors that are unobservable, utility is modelled as
a combination of a deterministic component and a random error term.

2.2
Related Work
As the use of smart mobility systems increases, managing the balance between vehicle
supply and passenger demand remains a major challenge. Imbalances between the origin
and destination of trip requests can lead to ineﬀicient vehicle distribution, long waiting
times, and reduced service quality. Addressing these issues requires strategies that can
respond to spatial and temporal variations in demand. Two important methods to address
this problem are vehicle rebalancing and dynamic pricing, which help adjust how vehicles
are redistributed across the network and how passengers make travel choices.
Rebalancing and pricing in mobility-on-demand systems have been approached using a
range of methods. Optimization-based techniques, such as fluid models [11], queueing
networks [12], and model predictive control (MPC) [13], have been widely applied to
compute rebalancing flows and vehicle assignments. For pricing, some studies have used
equilibrium-based models to analyze steady-state supply and demand balance under spa-
tially differentiated pricing [14]. Others have proposed approximate dynamic optimiza-
tion frameworks for joint pricing and rebalancing decisions in shared vehicle systems [15].
While these approaches are grounded in structured mathematical models, they often rely
on simplified assumptions about demand or driver behavior, may require solving large-
scale optimization problems, and can face challenges in scalability or adaptability to highly
dynamic environments. RL has emerged as a promising alternative due to its ability to
handle high-dimensional inputs, stochastic demand, and support fast decision-making in
real time.
RL has been used to handle different areas in mobility-on-demand systems, such as re-
balancing, pricing, dispatching, and charging. One of the initial applications of RL for
rebalancing was by Wen et al. [16], who used an action space with only 8 cardinal direc-
tions. In 2019, Fluri et al. [17] proposed a two-stage reinforcement learning framework
where the agent predicts a target vehicle distribution over the network and it is realized
with an optimization algorithm. More recently, Gammelli et al. [18] introduced a graph-
based RL approach, using GNNs for better generalization and transferability. Building
on this, Schmidt et al. [19] proposed an offline GNN-based RL framework trained solely
on historical fleet data without any simulator interaction.
In parallel, in order to influence demand distribution by adjusting trip fares, RL has been
applied to dynamic pricing. Song et al. [20] applied Q-learning to design surge pricing
strategies for late-night taxi services in Seoul, which aims to improve supply in underserved
areas and reduce driver refusal rates. Chen et al. [21] used Proximal Policy Optimization
(PPO) to develop a dynamic pricing strategy for ride-sourcing platforms with human
drivers by adjusting both trip fares and driver wages to better match supply and demand.

Turan et al. [22] also applied PPO to set real-time prices in electric autonomous vehicle
fleets, which focuses on profit optimization while accounting for vehicle availability and

Lei and Ukkusuri [23] introduced a deep reinforcement learning
framework with adaptive pricing policies that respond to dynamic demand patterns in a
one-sided mobility market.
Li et al. [24] propose a reinforcement learning-based framework that jointly addresses
vehicle rebalancing and dynamic pricing in AMoD systems. The method applies a bi-

tested in big cities such as New York City and San Francisco.
This thesis extends the framework of Li et al. The centralized architecture is replaced

Several studies have also studied MARL in AMoD systems. Hoppe et al. [25] model
each possible vehicle-request pair as an agent that decides whether to accept or reject
an assignment. On the other hand, Wang and Turan [26] assign each network node as
an agent responsible for local vehicle routing and dynamic pricing decisions. While these
works show the benefits of multi-agent control in AMoD, they do not consider interactions
between independent service providers.
Choice modeling is being used in smart mobility systems to capture user preferences and
simulate behavior in response to pricing and service quality.
For example, Yang and

system. Unlike prior work that focuses on centralized control or multi-agent coordination

3.1
Problem Definition
This section provides a general overview of the AMoD system studied in this thesis. The
system models two independent smart mobility companies operating in the Brooklyn area
of New York City. The service area is partitioned into a set of regions, and each company
manages an equal-sized fleet of autonomous vehicles. Vehicles pick up passengers in one
region and drop them off in another. Trips are defined between region pairs, and travel
distances and times are measured between the centroid of each region.
Time is divided into fixed intervals. At each timestep, for each OD pair, passenger demand
is sampled from a distribution fitted to historical trip data. This distribution captures
patterns in real world mobility behavior. Passenger decisions are based on a discrete choice
model: given the prices and estimated travel times offered by each company, passengers
select a service provider probabilistically or may choose to reject both options.
The system operates over a finite time horizon.
At each step, each company makes
decisions about pricing and vehicle allocation. The interaction between the two companies
is modeled as a multi-agent decision problem, where both companies share the same
passenger demand and vehicle dynamics.
This study focuses primarily on the joint control of vehicle rebalancing and pricing. To
evaluate the performance of this approach, two control modes are also considered as
benchmarks: one where companies only rebalance their fleets, and one where they only
control prices.
• Rebalancing only (benchmark): Both companies receive their demand shares and
must reposition their vehicles optimally to meet future demand.
• Pricing only (benchmark): Companies can set OD-specific prices to influence cus-
tomer choice, but cannot rebalance their fleets.
• Joint control (main model): Companies control both pricing and rebalancing.

3.3
Assumptions and Constraints
To keep the simulation manageable and realistic, several assumptions are made. The
system is modeled as a competitive multi-agent decision-making problem, where each
company interacts with shared demand while acting independently. Each company op-
erates a fixed-size fleet of autonomous vehicles, with no additions or removals during the
simulation. Vehicles serve one passenger at a time, and all trips, including rebalancing
movements, are delayed according to the travel time between origin and destination. The
system runs in time steps of three minutes over a limited time period. At each timestep,
passenger demand between OD pairs is sampled from a stochastic distribution based on
historical taxi data. This demand is not known in advance and must be anticipated by
the agents. Agents can set OD specific prices, but pricing actions are bounded to prevent
unrealistic fare values and ensure stable learning. Rebalancing actions are constrained by
current vehicle availability and regional travel times. Agents cannot observe the strategy
of their competitor.

This chapter presents the methods used to build and train the multi-agent AMoD sys-

4.1
Data & Preprocessing
In this section, the data sources and preprocessing steps used to generate inputs for the
AMoD environment are described. The goal is to transform raw data into a structured
format ready for simulation.
This section begins by presenting the data sources used in the study. Then it explains how
the service area is divided into a set of predefined regions. Following this, the process of
extracting and aggregating demand from the raw trip data is described. The method used
to estimate travel times for rebalancing empty vehicles between regions is also explained.
Finally, the main assumptions and simplifications applied during the data preparation

4.1.1
Data Sources
This study uses two primary data sources to construct a realistic scenario for an AMOD
system in Brooklyn, New York.
For the partition of the city, The NYC Department of Sanitation (DSNY) District Map is
used[29]. This dataset contains geographic boundaries for districts, provided in GeoJSON
format. With predefined districts, demand aggregation, vehicle flows, and graph-based
representation becomes easier.
For ride demand and fare information, the New York City Taxi dataset provided by the
Illinois Data Bank is used[30]. This dataset contains detailed records of taxi trips from
2013, including pickup and drop-off coordinates, timestamps, trip durations, and fare
amounts.

These two data sources form the basis for preparing the inputs to the AMoD simulation

4.1.2
Spatial Partitioning of Brooklyn
The study focuses on the Brooklyn borough of New York City and aims to partition it in
a structured way. To achieve this, predefined regions from the DSNY District Map are
used. Only the geographic data corresponding to Brooklyn is extracted, and the borough
is divided into 18 regions. Each region represents a distinct spatial unit used for demand
aggregation, vehicle tracking, and decision making.
To enable network based operations, a topology graph is also constructed. This graph
shows which regions are directly connected and is used by the AMoD simulator for rebal-
ancing and by the reinforcement learning agents for graph-based message passing.
4.1.3
Demand Extraction and Aggregation
To prepare the raw trip data for simulation, only trips where both the origin and desti-
nation are located within Brooklyn are extracted from the initial dataset, which contains
taxi trip records from across New York City. The data is further filtered to include only
trips that occurred on March 8, 2013, between 19:00 and 21:00. This time window was
selected to capture evening rush hour dynamics.
After filtering, each trip is assigned to an origin and destination region based on the
predefined 18-region partitioning of Brooklyn. For each origin-destination pair, the total
number of requested trips is calculated in addition to attributes such as the average fare
and travel duration.
4.1.4
Rebalancing Travel Time Estimation
Another core input for the AMoD simulation is the travel time estimates used for rebal-
ancing empty vehicles between regions. These travel times are essential for computing
optimal rebalancing flows using CPLEX. To obtain these estimates, average travel dura-
tions are calculated for each origin-destination pair using taxi trip records from the entire
year of 2013. Using a full year of data increases the likelihood that at least one trip was
recorded for each region pair, which ensures more reliable travel time values.
4.1.5
Scenario File Construction
All processed inputs, which are ride demand, rebalancing travel times, vehicle availability,
and region connectivity, are combined into a single scenario file in JSON format. This file
is the main input to the AMoD simulator. The full preprocessing is implemented through
a series of Python notebooks.

4.1.6
Limitations and Assumptions
In order to facilitate the simulation setup, several simplifications and assumptions are
made during the data preparation process. Firstly, rebalancing times are averaged and
treated as fixed during simulation, ignoring temporal fluctuations such as congestion.
Additionally, because the historical dataset reflects only completed trips and not the total
number of attempted ride requests, demand is scaled during simulation. A 50% rejection
rate on the customer side is also assumed and implemented through the passenger choice
model during the simulation.

Critic Networks:
Each agent maintains two critic networks that estimate the expected
return (Q-value) of a state-action pair. Like the actor, the critic begins with a GNN
that processes the input graph and node features. Then, it combines the GNN-encoded
state with the selected action for each origin region. These combined features are passed
through MLP layers. Then, a sum over all origin regions produces a single scalar Q-value
representing the expected return.
The critic’s target networks are updated using Polyak averaging (soft updates), which
improves stability during training by smoothing target value updates.
4.4.3
Training
Each agent is trained independently using the SAC algorithm, introduced in Section 2.1.3.
Key parts of the SAC training process are:
• Replay buffer: Each agent stores past experiences in a buffer. These experiences are
in the form (st, at, rt, st+1), representing the state, action, reward, and next state.
During training, the agent samples random batches from this buffer to learn more
eﬀiciently and avoid overfitting to recent experiences.
• Actor and critic updates: The actor network learns a policy that selects actions
based on the current state. The critic networks estimate how good those actions
are by learning Q-values. The actor is trained to choose actions that maximize the
critic’s value estimate while also staying exploratory. Two critics are used to reduce
bias and make training more stable.
• Target networks: Each critic has a corresponding target network, which is a slowly
updated copy used to compute stable target Q-values during training. Instead of
replacing the weights all at once, the target networks are updated gradually using
a weighted average of the current and previous critic parameters. This slow update
helps reduce training instability.
• Entropy regularization: The SAC algorithm encourages exploration by adding an
entropy term to the objective function. This helps the agent remain flexible and
not settle too early on a narrow strategy. This regularization is controlled by an
entropy coeﬀicient that balances exploration and exploitation.
• Discounting future rewards: A discount factor γ = 0.99 is used to give more im-
portance to immediate rewards, while still considering the effect of future outcomes.
Overall, this training setup allows each agent to learn pricing and rebalancing strategies
that perform well over time, even in a changing and uncertain environment.

Experiments and Results
This chapter presents the experiments conducted to evaluate the proposed learning-based
control strategies for AMoD systems. First, a monopolistic setting where a single agent
controls the entire fleet and serves all passenger demand is studied. This scenario is used
as a benchmark to assess the benefits of centralized control in comparison to decentralized
learning.
Then, a multi-agent setting is studied, where two independent agents operate within the
same environment. Each agent has its own fleet and and makes decisions independently.
This setting reflects a competitive market and shows how agents learn to compete or
coordinate over time.
Lastly, additional experiments are performed to examine system behavior under varying
conditions.
These include changes in total fleet size, wages, and vehicle distributions
between agents.
5.1
Experimental Setup
All experiments are conducted using a discrete-time simulation environment representing
an AMoD system in Brooklyn, New York. The city is divided into 18 regions based on a
predefined spatial partitioning.[29] The simulation horizon is one hour, spans from 19:00
to 20:00, and is divided into 20 time steps of 3 minutes.
Passenger demand is generated from real taxi trip data and added to the environment
using a Poisson distribution to capture stochasticity. A utility-based passenger choice
model is used, where passengers select between available travel options or reject service.
Rebalancing and pricing decisions are made at every time step and directly influence the
future state of the system.
Three control modes are implemented in the environment:
• Mode 0: Rebalancing only, where agents control vehicle repositioning while fares
remain fixed.
• Mode 1: Pricing only, where agents adjust trip fares while vehicle positioning is not
actively controlled by the agents.
• Mode 2: Joint control, where agents control both rebalancing and pricing simulta-
neously.

6
Conclusion
MoD systems often face challenges caused by imbalances between vehicle supply and pas-
senger demand. These imbalances can lead to longer waiting times, low vehicle utilization,
and overall ineﬀiciencies in the system. To address these issues, reinforcement learning has
shown promise through adaptive strategies for rebalancing and dynamic pricing. Building
on this, this thesis explored how competition between multiple service providers affects
system performance by developing and evaluating MARL methods for joint control of
rebalancing and pricing in AMoD systems.
The study modeled two competing mobility service providers, where each agent inde-
pendently learned how to operate its fleet of autonomous vehicles through pricing and
rebalancing decisions.
An SAC reinforcement learning algorithm was used, combined
with GNN architectures to process spatial input data. The simulation environment was
based on real world taxi demand data from Brooklyn, New York, in 2013 and used a
multinomial logit model to represent passenger choices in response to price and travel
time.
Experimental results showed that introducing a second agent into the system reduced over-
all system reward compared to the single-agent (monopoly) baseline. In the single-agent
case, full control over pricing and rebalancing allowed for globally coordinated decisions
that maximized eﬀiciency. However, in the multi-agent setup, each agent followed its own
objective independently, which led to overlapping strategies, ineﬀicient rebalancing, and
direct competition for the same passengers. This competition not only limited coordina-
tion but also pressured agents to lower their prices, reducing profit margins compared to
the monopolistic case. Even though both agents used identical learning algorithms and
operated under symmetric conditions, the combination of strategic competition and lack
of collaboration reduced the system’s ability to serve demand effectively. This indicates
the limitation of decentralized learning in competitive environments, where individual
optimization may come at the expense of system-level eﬀiciency.
Additional experiments explored how the system responded to changes in key parame-
ters. Varying the total fleet size revealed that there is an optimal number of vehicles
for effective operation, and increasing the fleet size beyond this point did not necessar-
ily improve performance. The way the fleet was distributed between agents also had a
significant impact, as agents adapted their behavior based on their share of available re-
sources, indicating a strong sensitivity of learning dynamics to resource allocation. When
stochasticity was introduced into passenger wage levels, while keeping the average wage
Joint Rebalancing and Dynamic Pricing Policies with Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning for
Mobility-on-Demand Systems

==PAGE:55==
constant, system performance remained largely unchanged. This suggests that using fixed
wage assumptions is a reasonable approximation in this context. Increasing the average
wage level did improve results up to a certain point, but the benefits became smaller after
a certain threshold. Finally, assigning different wage levels based on where passengers
started their trips led to better performance, likely because it reflected more realistic
differences between regions.
These results suggest that uncoordinated competition between service providers can have
mixed effects. On the one hand, competition can benefit customers by lowering prices
and increasing available options. On the other hand, it can reduce profits for the service
providers, especially if they continuously undercut each other to attract demand. Without
regulation, such as setting a minimum price or ensuring that all areas receive some level
of service, this kind of competition may become unsustainable for companies. Over time,
it may lead to instability in operations and make it harder to maintain consistent and
reliable services.
Managing mobility systems with multiple providers requires careful planning.
While
competition can improve responsiveness to passenger demand, it also introduces challenges
that may need to be addressed through regulation.
Cities may need to balance the
outcomes of competition with goals such as broad service coverage, economic sustainability
for operators, and fairness across regions. In addition, the results underline the importance
of testing learning-based approaches under a variety of conditions, such as different fleet
sizes and pricing sensitivities, to better understand system behavior and avoid unintended
outcomes.
6.1
Future Work
While the current framework provides valuable insights into competitive dynamics in
smart mobility systems, several extensions could make it more aligned with real-world
conditions.
One promising direction is to include public transportation as an option in the passenger
choice model. Public transport is a common and affordable travel mode for many people,
and it naturally competes with smart mobility services. Adding this option would better
represent the choices passengers make in cities and give a more accurate picture of how
demand is shared between different transport modes.
Another useful extension is to include vehicle charging limits, especially for electric au-
tonomous vehicles. In real-world operations, battery levels and charging station avail-
ability are important factors that affect how vehicles move and when they are available.
Including these constraints would make the system more realistic and help understand

how charging needs affect rebalancing and service decisions.
Another direction for future work is to include basic regulations in the system to help
ensure fairness between mobility providers. In competitive settings, companies may focus
only on increasing their own profit, which can lead to uneven service across different areas
or unfair pricing. Adding fairness rules could help ensure that all regions get enough
service and that prices stay within reasonable limits.
It would also be useful to represent different types of passengers in more detail.
For
example, people may have different travel needs based on their income level, the purpose
of their trip, or how urgent the trip is. Including these differences in the model would
allow the system to better understand how passengers make choices.
Overall, these extensions would make the framework more realistic and relevant for practi-
cal decision-making. By capturing important factors such as public transport use, charg-
ing needs, fairness between providers, and differences in passenger preferences, the model
can offer more meaningful insights to guide the design of smart mobility systems that are
eﬀicient, inclusive, and responsive to urban needs.
