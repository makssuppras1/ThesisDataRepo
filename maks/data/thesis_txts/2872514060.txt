Abstract
Artifact­driven process monitoring was developed to address the limitations of traditional
approaches, including lack of automation and inflexibility to evolving multi­party processes.
The SMARTifact platform demonstrates its real­world applicability, however, it currently
faces challenges in its deviation detection and aggregation capabilities, core aspects of
effective monitoring. In the thesis, we enhance the deviation classification by adapting
a mismatch taxonomy from related literature and incorporating historical states of the
model into the detection algorithms. In addition, we integrate well­founded techniques for
process­level aggregation to combine data from multiple executions. For the validation of
our solution, we make use of a real­world dataset to verify detection accuracy and bench­
mark the performance of both contributions. These improvements result in actionable
monitoring insights and promote real­time decision­making, as well as long­term process
optimisation.

Introduction
1.1
Context and Motivation
In today’s highly competitive business environment, process monitoring has become a
central piece in ensuring operational excellence and continuous workflow improvement.
It helps businesses to detect real­time deviations from the intended execution flow, iden­
tify areas for improvement, and automate many of the formerly manual tasks, leading to
significant cost savings.
However, companies often need to adapt to changing factors such as seasonality, evolv­
ing customer demands, and dynamic market conditions. To remain agile and competitive,
many organisations have embraced the servitisation paradigm [1], shifting from strict in­
house processes to a cross­company collaboration with specialised service providers.
This transition has significantly changed how business processes are carried out and
monitored.
In contrast to traditional single­party processes, where all activities were executed and
monitored within the same organisation, this transition has led to the emergence of multi­
party processes. These introduce an additional layer of complexity for process monitoring,
as company assets are now handled by third parties and the underlying data becomes
fragmented across various systems. Cross­integration of these systems is often costly
and inflexible, undermining the purpose that the servitisation had in the first place.
Artifact­driven monitoring [2] was suggested as a solution to this issue, leveraging the
Internet of Things (IoT) paradigm to equip the assets with sensors and communication
devices. Such artifacts, denoted as “smart objects”, can then be tracked across organi­
sational boundaries to determine the state of the process in real­time without the need for
manual tracking. Importantly, the artifact­driven monitoring makes use of the Extended
Guard­Stage­Milestone (E­GSM) modelling language, which allows for a less strict, rule­
based monitoring approach. However, the main drawback of E­GSM is that its models are
less intuitive and more difficult to understand. To address this limitation, [3] introduced a
method for translating the commonly used standard for process modelling, Business Pro­
cess Model and Notation (BPMN), to E­GSM, bridging the gap with existing modelling
practices.
Building upon this research, the SMARTifact platform was developed and later improved
upon in [4] to show the system’s viability in real­world scenarios. This work refined the
system by introducing deviation detection as well as the visualisation of its results over a
BPMN model. Additionally, the platform’s architecture was redesigned to support the de­
ployment of microservices, which improved system modularity and enabled more effective
processing of complex multi­party operations.
1.2
Problem Statement
While artifact­driven process monitoring provides significant advantages over traditional
BPMN­based monitoring, several issues currently remain unsolved.
In particular, the current deviation detection mechanisms are restricted by the structure
of E­GSM, where historical data may be lost. This is especially problematic for the loop
structure, where, by design, the inner stages are reset at the beginning of every itera­

tion. This effectively erases traces of previous execution flows, making it difficult to track
collective process behaviour over multiple iterations.
Additionally, multiple distinct types of deviations can result in the same E­GSM stage
attribute pattern, making it impossible to tell them apart. For example, consider a delivery
process where trucks should follow the route: warehouse →customer A →customer
B. In the first situation, a truck visits customer B first, then backtracks to customer A,
going against the intended sequence (warehouse →B →A). In the second case, a truck
that initially goes to customer B makes multiple trips to customer A due to unsuccessful
delivery attempts (warehouse →B →A →A). Even though both cases reflect different
types of operational issues, they result in the same final stage pattern in E­GSM, making
them indistinguishable under the current system.
Moreover, the previous work covered aggregation on a per­stage basis, where monitoring
data is analysed for individual process stages. However, another relevant approach that is
yet to be implemented is to consider the aggregation results on a per­process basis. The
current model lacks the ability to combine and analyse data from multiple executions of
the same process type, which would offer key insights into overall process performance,
comparative analysis across different process instances, and identification of issues that
only become apparent when examining multiple process executions at once.
Solving these limitations would provide stakeholders with more detailed and interactive
insights into the performance of the processes, ultimately improving decision­making and
process optimisation.
1.3
Project Scope
To address the current challenges in artifact­driven process monitoring, we aim to refine
the deviation classification and detection to improve monitoring precision and support
actionable decision­making. Additionally, we plan to address the aggregation of deviations
on the process type level.

1.5
Methodology
To address the challenges explained previously, the thesis will make use of established
tools and methodologies within process modelling and monitoring, focusing on devia­
tion detection improvements (RQ1), and per­process monitoring aggregation and insights
(RQ2). The methodology is centred on the research questions to ensure systematic re­
search, implementation, and evaluation.
To solve the limitations of deviation detection in artifact­driven process monitoring, our
study begins with a literature review to identify common types of deviation in processes
and their applicability to our case, addressing RQ1.1. The survey of literature will help us
determine whether any other classes should be considered. Consequently, an analysis of
current limitations will be performed to recognise the primary factors contributing to inac­
curate detection, addressing RQ1.2, including an examination of historical snapshots of
the E­GSM model as a potential solution, related to RQ1.3. In case the approach proves
useful, the research will focus on incorporating these improvements into the existing plat­
form in a computationally efficient way, aligning with RQ1.4.
Beyond deviation detection, the study also aims to introduce per­process aggregation
to provide a broader overview of process execution and performance, addressing RQ2.
In particular, we will employ data aggregation techniques across process executions to
gather performance measures which can provide a detailed insight into the process be­
haviour, relevant to RQ2.1. To this end, we will employ both daemon and event­driven
aggregation methodologies. Daemon aggregation periodically calculates the measures
at preset intervals based on stored data and then saves them into the database. In con­
trast, event­driven aggregation is performed as a response to certain trigger events and
does not store any new data. Both of these types are able to notify the stakeholder as a
reaction to certain thresholds or conditions. Furthermore, we will explore extensions to

Background
This chapter provides the foundational concepts needed to understand the contributions
of this thesis.
2.1
Business Process Management (BPM)
All organisations have to manage a number of processes with a series of steps they per­
form to add value to themselves and their customers. This process­centric thinking first
emerged in the 1980s, supported by the technological advancements of the time, with the
adoption of a centralised database at Ford. [5] Since then, many empirical studies have
confirmed that organisations that are process­oriented demonstrate higher efficiency and
perform better compared to those that are not. The discipline that focuses on supervising
these processes is called Business Process Management (BPM). [6]
BPM is concerned with a wide range of aspects related to the process, such as planning,
coordinating, and monitoring, as well as the execution itself. The design of the business
process is especially important, as it determines the success of its execution. BPM pro­
vides a unique perspective that ensures consistency across executions while also iden­
tifying opportunities for redesign. Such changes can often give companies an edge over
their competitors by reducing the number of errors or optimising the workflow, saving both
time and financial resources. [6]
2.1.1
Offline vs Online Process Monitoring
Process monitoring can be broadly divided into two main categories:
• Offline Process Monitoring: Assesses the process post­mortem, or once the ex­
ecution has finished. This type of monitoring evaluates event logs to summarise
deviations, analyse performance, and provide overall statistics over one or multiple
executions.
• Online Process Monitoring: Also referred to as runtime process monitoring, this
approach assesses running instances of the process. It analyses partial traces to
visualise the execution and can trigger alarms for process owners in case of devia­
tions. In many cases, encountering deviations within the execution can lead to cost
savings and workflow optimisation, ensuring the desired quality of service. Runtime
process monitoring is therefore often more desirable due to its ability to detect and
respond to a violation as soon as it happens. [6]
While online process monitoring offers clear advantages, traditional Business Process
Management Systems face several challenges in implementing effective runtime moni­
toring.
2.2
Traditional Approaches
Businesses use BPMSs (Business Process Management Systems) to design, implement,
automate, and monitor internal processes. While BPMSs come in many variants, they
generally consist of the following modules, as illustrated in Figure 2.1:
• Execution Engine: Main part of the system that runs process instances, distributes
work, manages data, and coordinates activities.

• Process Modelling Tool: Allows creation of processes including annotations, stor­

• Worklist Handler: Interface that keeps track of due work items and offers them to

• Administration and Monitoring Tools: Handles exceptions and provides monitor­

• External Services: Integrates external systems, such as other organisations’ BPMS,

Nonetheless, the traditional approach suffers from various shortcomings, which we can
relate to the components of BPMS. The first is the Worklist Handler, through which the
participants interact with the system to advance the execution of the process by carrying
out work items, such as a delivery driver picking up and delivering packages. The issue
here is the lack of automation, as the human operators need to manually send notifications
to the system, which may often be postponed or forgotten. In our example, the driver may
first deliver all the packages and only mark them as delivered at the end of the day. As
a result, the execution trace of the process is highly inaccurate, possibly losing important
insights into the execution. [2]
Another problem arises from the External Services portion of BPMS in combination with
the current trends in servitisation, where companies increasingly rely on third­party service
providers that are contracted to perform parts of the business process. [1] While External
Services enable the integration of the service provider’s system into the BPMS itself,
this may often be costly and ineffective. For example, if a logistics company decides to
outsource their delivery management to a third­party, but the provider’s IT infrastructure is
incompatible with the organisation’s BPMS, it could take many resources to integrate the
two systems. Moreover, the logistics company could later decide to contract a different
provider due to better conditions, invalidating the previous work and leading to the same

The process modelling component of traditional BPMSs typically relies on standardised
modelling languages, with Business Process Model and Notation (BPMN) being the most
widely adopted approach.
2.3
Business Process Model and Notation (BPMN)
Business Process Model and Notation (BPMN) is the current standard for the modelling
language used in BPMS. BPMN is developed and maintained by the non­profit Object
Management Group (OMG) and provides a graphical representation of business pro­
cesses. Figure 2.2 illustrates a set of typical BPMN elements, including activities, events,
gateways, and sequence flows. In practice, the model may be extended with additional
elements, depending on the complexity of the modelled process.
Figure 2.2: Overview of core BPMN 2.0 elements (adapted from [7])
The advantage of BPMN is that it can be easily understood by computer scientists as
well as professionals from other domains, thanks to its simple visual representation that
resembles the real­life process. Additionally, it can effectively model multi­party processes
via its hierarchical structure, making it suitable in the context of the mentioned servitisation
trend.
2.3.1
Limitations of Imperative Modeling in BPMN
However, BPMN is what we refer to as an imperative modelling language, which means
that it specifies exactly how a process should be executed. In practice, the steps of a
process may often be interchanged due to unforeseen circumstances, such as human or
technological errors or other environmental factors. The rigid structure and strict execution
order of BPMN can have a negative effect on process monitoring tasks. As a result,
deviations require human intervention through the Administration and Monitoring Tools to
recover and continue the process monitoring. [2]
2.4
Artifact­driven Process Monitoring
To solve the mentioned problems, Meroni [2] introduced artifact­driven monitoring, a novel
approach to business process monitoring. It is centred around the so­called artifacts,
which are the physical objects that participate in the process extended with Internet of

Monitoring

Things (IoT) devices in order to make them self­aware. This effectively addresses the
first discussed issue, as there is no more a need for manual notification sending in multi­
party process execution. Instead, the artifacts utilise computing devices, sensors, and
communication interfaces to autonomously track process execution. The resulting data
are reliable, accurate, and immediately available for monitoring tasks.
Furthermore, artifacts can be dynamically bound and unbound to process executions,
enabling convenient integration with third­party operations. It is no longer necessary to
implement software changes to receive data from each contracted external party, as the
flexibility of artifacts ensures adaptability to evolving business process requirements.
The implementation of artifact­driven monitoring requires a modelling language that can
handle the flexible nature of artifact behaviour, leading to the adoption of Extended Guard­
Stage­Milestone (E­GSM).
2.5
Extended Guard­Stage­Milestone (E­GSM)
Artifact­driven monitoring uses E­GSM, a declarative modelling language centred around
artifacts. E­GSM was created by extending the existing Guard­Stage­Milestone (GSM)
language with two new constructs, process flow guards and a fault logger, that make it
possible to detect deviations from the intended behaviour.
Unlike imperative languages such as BPMN, E­GSM focuses on what needs to be achieved
within the process without enforcing an exact execution order or specific steps. This
makes the models resilient to deviations, which can be easily detected without disrupting
the monitoring flow.
2.5.1
E­GSM Constructs
The primary construct of E­GSM is a stage which, similar to BPMN’s task, represents
an activity related to an artifact within the process. Each artifact also has an underlying
information model, keeping track of its associated data. An information model consists
of:
• Data attributes: Describe the properties of the artifact itself.
• Status attributes: Track the execution of related stages.
Stages in E­GSM are oftentimes nested inside one another to represent more complex
scenarios, such as parallel execution or iterative behaviour. Figure 2.3 shows an E­GSM
stage with its associated constructs, which include:
• Data Flow Guard (DFG): Inherited from GSM (originally “Guard”), it describes a
condition that determines when a stage should open. The condition is expressed
as an Event­Condition­Action (ECA) rule: “an [on e] [if c] expression that is
triggered when an event e occurs and the condition c is true” [2]. It is mandatory to
have at least one DFG on each stage.
• Milestone: Describes the condition that causes the associated stage to close. Op­
tionally, another condition that invalidates the milestone can be defined, reopening
the stage. Similarly to DFG, at least one milestone per stage needs to be present.
• Process Flow Guard (PFG): Represents a boolean expression based on DFGs
and milestones, intended to ensure the correct order of execution. It is assessed
10

Monitoring
==PAGE:21==
upon evaluation of the DFG of the associated stage before it opens, and its value
determines whether the stage activation complies with the expected process flow.
• Fault Logger: Another ECA rule that signals faults detected during the execution of
the stage. Regardless of these faults, which may be caused by external factors, the
termination of the stage remains controlled by the achievement of its milestones.

2.5.2
E­GSM Execution Perspectives
Together, the constructs govern three important execution perspectives:
• Status: Represents the current execution status of a stage within its lifecycle. Sta­
tus can assume one of the three values:
– unopened: The default status before any of the stage’s DFGs have been trig­
gered.
– opened: When a DFG is triggered and the stage becomes active.
– closed: When a milestone is achieved or when the parent stage is closed.
• State: Describes whether any faults were encountered during the execution of the
stage. The possible values are:
– regular: The default state.
– faulty: When the boolean expression of the fault logger evaluates to true during
execution.
• Compliance: Represents whether the stage adheres to the required execution or­
der. The possible values are:
– onTime: The default compliance, indicating that the stage was opened accord­
ing to the expected flow.
– outOfOrder: When a stage opens while none of its PFGs hold.
– skipped: Assigned to stages that should have been executed earlier, based on
the expected flow, but were bypassed when a later stage was opened outO­
fOrder.

2.6
Translations between BPMN and E­GSM
Despite the advantages that artifact­driven monitoring provides, E­GSM can often be dif­
ficult to interpret for users unfamiliar with declarative modelling languages. To address
this, Meroni [2] developed a framework to allow the user to adopt artifact­driven moni­
toring without E­GSM specific knowledge. Instead, it leverages the well­known BPMN
collaboration diagrams to semi­automatically construct the necessary E­GSM models,
requiring only the addition of artifact­related information to the BPMN diagram.
The process monitoring results are then translated back into an enriched BPMN diagram,
including an overlay with aggregation data and detected deviations. In this way, the pro­
cess owner can interact with the system primarily using BPMN diagrams, and the hidden
complexities of E­GSM are handled internally. This approach allows the user to benefit
from the advantages of artifact­driven monitoring while being simple to use and easily
adaptable. The specific rules for BPMN to E­GSM conversions are presented in Ap­
pendix F.
2.6.1
E­GSM Blocks
The translation from BPMN to E­GSM results in structured block hierarchies that model
different patterns and flows within business processes. There are multiple types of blocks,
each with distinct properties and typically organised in a nested structure. While these
blocks can be directly modelled in E­GSM, for the purpose of this paper, we introduce them
specifically in the context of the mapping that results from BPMN to E­GSM translation.
The following critical block types are relevant for our study:
• Sequence Block: This is the most common parent block, as it is always present
upon BPMN to E­GSM transformation, representing the high­level process flow. It
consists of at least 2 child blocks that are intended to run exactly once and in a
12
Enhancing Aggregation and Deviation Detection through Historical Data in Artifact­driven Process
Monitoring
==PAGE:23==
sequence, which is ensured by their PFGs. The purpose of a sequence block is to
model sequential flows.
• Parallel Block: It is designed to model concurrent execution where all branches
must be completed. It is a type of block in which all children, meaning sub­processes
or branches, are intended to run in parallel and exactly once, coordinated by PFGs.
The parallel block is obtained from a pair of diverging and converging BPMN parallel
gateways, where each path between the gateways becomes a branch in the block.
• Exclusive Block: The purpose of an exclusive block is to model decision points
where only one path should be taken. It consists of multiple branches, exactly one
of which should be executed once, based on the associated condition defined by
PFGs. The exclusive block is derived from a pair of diverging and converging BPMN
exclusive gateways, creating a branch for each of the paths between the gateways.
• Inclusive Block: It is designed to handle scenarios where one or more paths may
be executed based on conditions. This is a block in which at least one of the children
is supposed to be executed exactly one time, defined by the PFGs. It is obtained
from the translation of a pair of diverging and converging inclusive BPMN gateways,
resulting in one branch per path.
• Loop Block: The purpose of a loop block is to model iterative behaviour and re­
peated execution patterns. Loop block is the only parent type that allows for re­
execution. Its only child is the iteration block, which is reopened for each iteration of
the loop. The iteration block, in turn, has two child branches, acquired from translat­
ing the paths between a pair of diverging and converging BPMN exclusive gateways.
The intended execution sequence is either a full iteration, executing both branches
one after the other, or a half­iteration, executing one branch before leaving the loop,
a behaviour based on PFGs.
Individual blocks are visualised in Figures 6.1 to 6.5, which provide detailed illustrations
of each block’s structure and execution flow. The deviations in E­GSM are specific to
the block type, a topic we examine in detail in Section 6.1 where we study the deviation
classification and explore how different blocks exhibit distinct deviation patterns.

2.8
Internet of Things (IoT)
IoT refers to a network of interconnected sensors that measure various conditions within
their environment, generate data, and communicate with other systems. Recent advance­
ments in sensor technology, embedded computing, and low­power wireless communica­
tion substantially simplified the deployment of IoT solutions, as well as their cost. These
factors contributed to making the IoT technology widely available and practical for many
industrial and business use cases [8].
In the context of artifact­driven process monitoring, smart objects are equipped with such
sensors to serve as the foundation for monitoring of the physical artifacts involved in the
process execution. The collected data is transmitted as a stream of events, allowing for
a scalable, real­time monitoring setup across organisational boundaries.
2.8.1
Message Queue Telemetry Transport (MQTT)
MQTT is a lightweight communication protocol designed for scenarios with constrained
resources, such as computational power and bandwidth, making it ideal for IoT. It oper­
ates over the TCP/IP protocol, allowing for bidirectional communication through a central
queue called a message broker. MQTT is based on a publish/subscribe model, meaning
that the clients do not communicate with each other directly. Instead, they publish mes­
sages to specific topics, which the broker then distributes to clients subscribed to those
topics [9]. MQTT is utilised by the SMARTifact platform to enable efficient communication
between smart objects and the other components of the platform.

4.2.1
Deviation Detection and Categorization
Conformance checking is a widely researched area within process mining [10, 11, 12].
The main goal is to determine how well the observed behaviour in event logs conforms to
the intended process model. Additionally, an analysis and classification of the detected
deviations is often performed. In this thesis, we apply these concepts to the domain
of artifact­driven process monitoring, emphasising visual representation of the obtained
results and accurate deviation classification.
In a broader sense, deviations can be divided into two groups: unfitting behaviour, where
log behaviour violates the model constraints, and imprecise behaviour, where the model
allows behaviour that is not observed in the log [11]. In our research, we are interested in
studying unfitting behaviour.
Alignment­based conformance checking is currently the state­of­the­art method for de­
tecting deviating behaviour in process mining [12]. Various metrics obtained from align­
ments, such as fitness and precision, support assessing how well a model captures the
actual behaviour recorded in the event log [11, 13]. The behavioural alignment approach
Enhancing Aggregation and Deviation Detection through Historical Data in Artifact­driven Process
Monitoring
17
==PAGE:28==
provides further classification of deviation patterns, such as relation mismatches or event
mismatches. In our work, we adopt the taxonomy originally proposed by García­Bañuelos
et al. [14], and referenced in [15].
Several researchers have adapted conformance checking techniques for specific do­
mains, each presenting unique challenges. In healthcare, [16] applies conformance check­
ing to clinical treatment processes using Data Petri Nets. However, this work highlighted
difficulties in defining cost functions that produce medically meaningful alignments, sug­
gesting instead declarative approaches and rule­based encodings via Medical Logic Mod­
ules (MLMs). Educational applications have also emerged, with Nguyen et al. [17] propos­
ing sequence alignment methods to compare student training processes against ideal
learning traces. In multi­agent systems, Mecheraoui et al. [18] proposed compositional
conformance checking, which projects event logs onto system components to localise
deviation analysis.
Declarative approaches have also been explored [19, 20], where encoding of guidelines
is done via rules, using rule compliance to interpret alignment results. In particular, the
review by Klessascheck et al. [20] shows that both qualitative (compliant/non­compliant)
and quantitative (fitness­based) categorisation of deviations are essential for actionable
insights. It further highlights that declarative tools make use of such measures more of­
ten than their imperative counterparts. In addition, they frequently include visualisations
to relate deviations back to the process model. However, most of the work in the declar­
ative method is limited to the healthcare domain, with only one solution supporting online
monitoring. Additional relevant contributions include weighted artificial negative events
that improve the estimation of precision and generalisation in incomplete logs [21], and
component­based conformance checking [18], which decomposes nested Petri Nets into
components for localised deviation analysis.
Our work builds on these foundations but adapts the deviation model to the specific nested
block structures of artifact­driven processes.
4.2.2
Visualisation and Aggregation of Conformance Results
Visualisation methods are critical in making conformance results understandable to end
users.
Common techniques include colour­coded process models, deviation rate bar
charts, and interactive filtering [12, 22, 23, 24].
Rehse et al. [12] surveyed academic and commercial tools, highlighting trends such as
fitness visualisation at the activity level, deviation breakdowns per case attribute, and
interactive drill­down. Kirchner et al. [23] applied alignment­based colouring to BPMN
diagrams in hospital settings, while Kalenkova et al. [25] demonstrated how alignment
results can be mapped back to BPMN diagrams to enable visual exploration of confor­
mance at the activity level. Similarly, Caballero et al. [24] introduced SepVis, which offers
trace filtering by deviation type, temporal filtering, and interactive highlighting of traces.
Carmona et al. [10] emphasised the importance of aggregating alignment results to sup­
port overview insights by colouring activities according to the proportion of conforming and
deviating traces. In Inductive visual Miner (IvM), Leemans [26] leveraged Projected Con­
formance Checking (PCC) to support activity­level aggregation of fitness and precision
and visualise deviations as dashed edges. PCC further supports per­activity approxima­
tion of fitness measures and provides highlighting filters to focus attention on specified
behaviours [13].
Nagy et al. [22] proposed real­time monitoring visualisations with deviation filtering based
on alignments, using colour­coding and tooltips to manage interface complexity. Pour­

Earth Movers’ Distance and performance spectrum. Similarly, Rinderle­Ma et al. [28] sug­
gested enhancing process models with runtime annotations to support domain experts in

Our work adopts similar design principles through BPMN overlays, per­stage tooltips and

4.2.3
Filtering and Variant Exploration
While filtering is not part of the scope of the thesis, it represents a valuable direction for
future work. Filtering allows stakeholders to focus on relevant process variants or sub­
logs.
Salas­Urbano et al. developed both LoVizQL [29], a specialised query language for filter­
ing and analysing event logs, and a UI framework for exploring collections of DFGs [30].
This work emphasises the need to support multiple filters simultaneously and enable in­
teractive exploration of DFG variants.
Schuster et al. [31] presented the advantages of interactive filtering by variants and control
flow patterns in Cortado, resulting in fine­grained temporal analysis capabilities. Leemans
et al. [32] integrated filtering into DFvM, supporting both pre­ and post­mining filtering
based on attributes or model elements.
Zhang et al. [33] used activity projection to explore model simplification and deviation
patterns in different sub­logs. Furthermore, user­driven filtering is supported in Business
Miner [34], where non­technical users can explore process variants and performance de­
tails through question trees, which could inspire future interactive enhancements to devi­
ation analysis.
Finally, Klessascheck et al. [20] noted that explicit filtering capabilities often lack runtime
compliance tools. Our work contributes to this direction by proposing precise deviation
categories. Similar filtering capabilities could be integrated into our framework in future
work to allow more flexible exploration of deviations across different process perspectives.
4.2.4
Summary
Previous research provides a strong foundation for deviation detection, visual aggrega­
tion, and variant exploration in process mining. Our analysis suggests that declarative
methods are highly desirable, as they can better accommodate certain domains.
While the use of declarative approaches has gained momentum, to our best knowledge,
none of the related work utilises the artifact­driven approach for real­time deviation detec­
tion and visualisation. Our contribution addresses this gap by defining a deviation clas­
sification system tailored to artifact­driven process structures, implementing correlation
analysis capabilities, and enhancing stage­level aggregation techniques.
Moreover, we integrate this functionality into an intuitive and interactive visualisation frame­
work that builds upon the established design while introducing novel features for deviation
analysis. Filtering methods that enable more fine­grained analysis remain relevant as a
direction for future work.

Deviation Detection Enhancements
In the current SMARTifact implementation, the deviation detection is performed by apply­

as the process tree. Each node in such a tree corresponds to an E­GSM stage, and its
structure reflects the nested nature of E­GSM models. In particular, leaf nodes repre­

predetermined range of possible deviation types which dictate the deviation logic for its
children during detection.

of the mentioned process tree, with some characteristics of Breadth­First Search (BFS)
at the analysis level. For each visited node, the algorithm first considers its type before
applying the relevant logic to evaluate the behaviour of its children and determine what
deviations are present based on their status and compliance (see Figure 5.3).

5.4
Aggregation Enhancements
The baseline SMARTifact system supports per­stage, real­time aggregation in the form
of an overlay that appears when hovering over the BPMN activities. This includes counts
for compliance, status, and state perspectives, however, these measures exclude past
process executions. Furthermore, no dedicated user interface exists to show combined
data from multiple instances of the same process type, as the current implementation
functions within the instance view.
The current platform uses colours to represent real­time process execution, where green
symbolises fully executed stages and orange shows open stages. We will therefore need
to consider how to leverage colour­coding while maintaining the option to track real­time
execution of process instances. Our approach will thus introduce process­level aggrega­
tion in a specialised view, further supported by stage colouring based on deviation rates,
as well as additional metrics.
We aim to improve the per­stage aggregation with more useful measures beyond simple
counts, such as rates and percentages. We plan to implement these improvements as
daemon­based jobs to ensure efficient background processing.
The enhanced aggregation will provide stakeholders with actionable insights derived from
both the current execution state and historical performance patterns.
5.5
Assumptions and Input Models
To design and implement our proposed enhancements, we rely on various assumptions
in relation to the input models and the overall monitoring setup.
First, we assume that a BPMN collaboration diagram is readily available for each moni­
tored process type. This diagram serves not only as the basis for defining the structure of
the process but also for the visualisation of monitoring results. The BPMN model should
define the interactions among participants via pools, the flow of the executions via se­
quence flows and gateways, and the communication between parties via message flows.
The diagram must further be enriched with artifact­specific information through data ob­
jects that identify relevant artifacts and specify the conditions under which activities use
them.
Importantly, the BPMN diagram must be block structured, meaning its control flow can
be decomposed into nested blocks that are free of overlaps, such as sequences, loops
and branches. This property is crucial to ensure proper structural transformation of BPMN
blocks into their E­GSM equivalents. On a related note, we assume that the corresponding
E­GSM model is available for the same process and that there is a clear mapping between
the two. This mapping is possible due to naming conventions, allowing us to determine
the type of each block and maintain traceability during the monitoring process.
Lastly, we assume that the monitoring platform continuously receives a stream of events
that describe the process’s evolution over time, either from physical sensors or from an
emulated environment. In addition to being essential for process monitoring, these events
also serve as input for both historical and real­time aggregation and conformance check­
ing.
These assumptions form the foundation for our improvement strategy and guarantee that
our suggested improvements can be successfully integrated into the current SMARTifact
architecture while maintaining system reliability and performance.

Deviation Detection and Aggregation
Deviation detection is arguably one of the most important aspects of process monitoring.
In order to provide stakeholders with the most informative and detailed overview of how
the process execution deviates from the intended model, the discovery must be reliable
and capture any possible diversion. This chapter first describes the current classification
of deviations in artifact­driven monitoring along with the limitations of the implemented
approach. We then introduce a related paper that is concerned with process model repair
using different categories and compare the two approaches, including a mutual mapping.
Lastly, we synthesise the results in a new classification that includes deviations that cover
a broader range of cases, and we explain the role of engine history in achieving finer­
grained results.
6.1
Deviation types in artifact­driven process monitoring
In this section, we summarise the deviation classification introduced in [4] and further
refined in [3]. Since the classification is organised by block type, we first refer readers
back to Section 2.6.1 for a recap of the characteristics of E­GSM blocks. Deviations are
determined based on a combination of status and compliance perspectives of the block
itself and its child elements.
Additionally, for each type, we include the corresponding E­GSM model and BPMN dia­
gram, as well as a process tree. These representations serve multiple purposes: to clarify
how the algorithm operates over the tree structure, to better understand the relationship

6.1.1
Sequence Block
The sequence block represents a process structure where children should be executed
exactly once in a strict order. Figure 6.1 illustrates the different representations of this
block. Currently, the following deviations are recognised:
1. Incomplete process blocks: Indicates that a stage had opened status at a point
when it should have been closed instead. This deviation can manifest itself in one
of two ways:
(a) A stage opens with outOfOrder compliance due to its PFG expression, because
its predecessor was opened instead of closed and hence incomplete.
(b) The parent sequence block should have closed but did not due to some chil­
dren having opened status (instead of closed), and therefore being incomplete.
While this may overlap with the first scenario, it may also occur in cases where
the last stage in the sequence remains opened despite not having any succes­
sor to violate.
2. Skipped process blocks: Indicates that a stage or a series thereof should have
been executed but was not. This deviation can be detected in two cases:
(a) A stage has skipped compliance, in which case a successor stage exists with
outOfOrder compliance due to its PFG expression. In this situation, all stages

(b) The parent sequence block should have closed but did not due to some children

6.1.6
Limitations of Previous Deviation Detection
The current approach to deviation detection faces several limitations that restrict its ef­
fectiveness. Due to the nature of E­GSM, reopening a parent stage causes all children
stages to reset their attributes to a default value of unopened status and onTime compli­
ance. As the algorithm only considers the current value of each stage’s attributes, this
severely limits the potential to detect the deviations that happened further in the past. The
loop block is especially affected by this limitation, as it is intended to be re­executed and
therefore resets the attributes by design. Moreover, the “Incorrect execution sequence”
deviation could be more fine­grained if the exact order of execution was known instead of
just temporary values of status and compliance.
In the case of the exclusive block’s “Incorrect branch” deviation, there exists a special
case due to PFG of the branches, which states that no other branch should be active
upon opening. In practice, this may cause the correct branch to be outOfOrder, leading
the algorithm to mistakenly classify the chosen branch as incorrect. This occurs because
outOfOrder branches are automatically tagged as incorrect, making the deviation less
intuitive for stakeholders to understand.

between the two based purely on their contemporary compliance.

6.2
Deviation Classification Based on Behavioural
Misalignments
This section will explore deviation classes from a related approach to try to identify op­
portunities for improvement of the baseline classification used in SMARTifact. Garcia­
Bañuelos et al. [14] present a conformance checking method that summarises the dif­
ferences between the model and the log in the process mining context. This approach,
denoted as behavioural alignment, first translates both the model and the log into the
same type of Prime Event Structures (PES) and then proceeds to generate a set of oper­
ations needed to make the two equivalent, making use of a Partially Synchronized Product
(PSP).
The main difference from our approach to deviation detection is that the authors present
possible enhancements to the process model, whereas our work assumes that the model
is correct by design. Therefore, we only aim to capture the deviating log behaviour which
we always consider as non­conformant without revising the model. Furthermore, in our
approach, we do not compare equal data structures symmetrically as [14] does in the case
of PESs. Nevertheless, the deviation classification presented in the paper can provide
valuable insight into possible deviating behaviour.
6.2.1
Binary relationships
To classify mismatch patterns, we first need to introduce the three possible relations be­
tween two events in the PES. These relationships form the foundation for understanding
how events can deviate from their expected interactions:
• Immediate causality: Indicates that an event always comes immediately before
another event, similar to the idea of PFGs in the sequence and iteration blocks’
children, creating a strict flow order.
• Direct conflict: Shows that two events never appear in the same execution and
are both enabled from the same configuration. This is analogous to the E­GSM
exclusive block.
• Concurrency: Indicates that the two events may appear in any order, related to
parallel or partially inclusive E­GSM blocks.
6.2.2
Deviation types
The deviations in the paper are divided into two main categories:
• Unfitting behaviour: Describes log behaviour that does not conform to the model.
It is further divided into:
– Relation mismatch: Based on the event relationships described in Section 6.2.1,
this deviation signals that a different relation exists between events in the two
PESs.
Enhancing Aggregation and Deviation Detection through Historical Data in Artifact­driven Process
Monitoring
33
==PAGE:44==
– Event mismatch: Indicates that an event in the log cannot be matched to the
model.
• Additional behaviour: Describes model behaviour that does not appear in the log
but is allowed.
For our purpose, we will only consider unfitting behaviour, as we assume model cor­
rectness. Consequently, we are only interested in identifying deviations observed in the
execution that violate the constraints imposed by the model.
Relation Mismatch
Since both the log and the model are translated into a PES, this category of deviations
considers each combination of two of the possible event relations. In our context, it makes
sense to consider these as permutations instead, as our model and log come in different
structures.
1. Immediate causality/Concurrency (CausConc): Denotes that in one of the PESs,
two events come one after another, while in the other one, they happen in parallel.
This is an example of a deviation where concurrent execution violates sequential
requirements, or vice versa.
2. Immediate causality/Direct conflict (Conflict): Indicates that events directly fol­
low one another in one PES, but only a single one of them is executed in the other.
This describes situations in which exclusive behaviour takes the place of sequential
flow.
3. Concurrency/Direct conflict (Conflict): Indicates that two events are executed in
parallel in one of the PESs, and only one happens in the other. This illustrates cases
in which exclusive selection takes the place of concurrent execution.
In behavioural alignment, the last two are treated as the same deviation since, in the
context of PSP, they have very similar characteristics. We discuss these further in Sec­
tion 6.3.6, where we perform a mutual mapping with the existing work’s deviations.
Event Mismatch
This group covers all instances of behaviour that deviate from the model but cannot be
adequately explained through relation mismatch.
1. Task skipping (TaskSkip): Refers to a case where a task appears in the model’s
PES but is omitted from the log’s PES, and both then converge on the same following
task. This is an example of expected activities being skipped.
2. Unmatched repetition (UnmRepetition): Indicates that there is a repeated event
observed in the log, even though the model does not allow such looping behaviour.
This captures unapproved repetitions of activities.
3. Task Substitution (TaskSub): Occurs when a task is replaced by another task with
a different label but in the same execution context, following another matched event.
This represents cases where different activities are carried out other than those that
were specified.
4. Task relocation (TaskReloc): Occurs when the events in the log appear in a swapped
order, being enabled by a different previous event. This illustrates reordering of ac­
tivities beyond what the model allows.
34
Enhancing Aggregation and Deviation Detection through Historical Data in Artifact­driven Process
Monitoring
==PAGE:45==
5. Task absence/insertion (TaskAbs): Acts as a final class where all other unmatched
deviations fall into. A basic example describes a situation where an event appears
in the log but not in the model.
This classification provides a comprehensive framework for understanding process de­
viations from a behavioural perspective, complementing the structural approach used in
artifact­driven monitoring.

Conflict version, where the model allows for exclusive choice but the log exhibits concur­
rent behaviour. In E­GSM, this is manifested as a single correct execution of the entire
exclusive block, followed by an incorrect one where only branch A2 is executed. This
pattern is best represented as an “UnmRepetition” of the parent block, in addition to an
incorrect branch deviation, as was done in the previous classification.
Another distinct detail of E­GSM compared to Behavioural Alignment is that the correct­
ness of a branch is explicitly defined by guards and deterministically evaluated during
runtime. We can easily distinguish between correct and incorrect branches, whereas in
PES, all branches are equally valid. This difference can be seen in Cases 7 and 9, where
we map the behaviour to “TaskSkip”, but mark it with a “conditional” qualifier marking
since either branch would be considered correct in the PES approach. Furthermore, the
incorrect branch is left opened in Case 9, which motivates the extension of the current
incomplete deviation detection to all branches instead of just the correct one.
Cases 16 and 17 show the specific scenario where the correct branch is opened with
outOfOrder compliance because of the DFG constraint stating that no other branch may
be active simultaneously. Looking at the histories presented in Appendices C.7 and C.8,
this can be identified either by the initial skipped compliance of the correct branch or by
directly observing the value of the condition.
6.3.4
Inclusive Block
Appendix D contains the deviations of the inclusive block. It shares characteristics with
both exclusive and parallel blocks, allowing multiple branches to be executed at once
based on their individual guard conditions. This dual nature creates a unique deviation
landscape where patterns observed in both exclusive and parallel blocks can manifest
within the same structure.
6.3.5
Loop and Iteration Blocks
The final set of deviating flows for loop and iteration blocks is presented in Appendix E.
Loop blocks present unique challenges due to their iterative nature and the inherent diffi­
culty of distinguishing between intended and unintended repetitions. Conceptually, a loop
can be seen as a sequence of two stages or a single stage followed by the next block in
the sequence, depending on the loop condition.
Cases 1, 8 and 9 are labelled with an “UnmRepetition”, which may initially seem unintu­
itive. While loops are designed to allow for repeated behaviour, the way they are struc­
tured in E­GSM does not entirely align with the BPMN semantics, as the repetition must
occur within the controlled iteration logic.
In Case 1, the entire loop block is reopened after a completed execution, which is more
related to the parent sequence block. In such scenarios, it is reasonable to reset the in­
ner iteration counter. In contrast, Cases 8 and 9 illustrate re­execution of a single branch

In Case 3, the entire loop block is completely skipped from the point of view of the parent

the implementation of historical snapshots.
6.3.6

6.5.6
Iteration Context and Temporal Filtering
The deviation algorithms described in the previous sections are more precise thanks to
the use of temporal boundaries defined by iteration blocks. Specifically, when a stage is
contained within an iteration block, the analysis is limited to the time frame corresponding
to each individual iteration execution. This prevents historical data from different itera­
tions from influencing the deviation detection, making sure that each cycle is analysed
independently.
The high­level traversal algorithm from Section 6.5.1 keeps track of the context of the
closest parent iteration stage. This context is characterised by open­close timestamp
pairs, where each pair represents one complete iteration cycle. The algorithm uses such
time constraints to filter the analysis of child stages so it only considers the activities
executed within the boundaries of the iteration.
For each iteration, the analyzeStage function creates a filtered context and invokes the
block­specific detection logic. This ensures that the algorithm can correctly identify which
iteration index a deviation belongs to and that histories that persist across iterations do
not get mixed up.
In practice, the algorithm filters stage histories to only include entries within the time con­
straints, while the current status and compliance values, usually considered in the detec­
tion algorithms, are replaced with their latest value within the filtered context. The same
detection algorithms are then invoked repeatedly, once for each iteration, utilising the
filtered data, as illustrated in Figure 6.8.

7.1
Technology Stack
The technologies used in this work align with the stack used in the original SMARTifact
platform, which was selected based on the system requirements, such as efficiency and
the ability to run in low­resource environments, as well as support for a scalable, modular,
cloud­based architecture. The main components include:
• Backend: Node.js was chosen as the main framework for the platform, and all back­
end modules are implemented in it. Node.js is compatible across many platforms,
lightweight in nature, and well­suited for event­driven applications. Furthermore, it
offers parallelism and asynchronous execution, making it ideal for this monitoring
platform. Although JavaScript is not natively object­oriented, which would make the
system more extensible and type­safe, it provides enough flexibility for our purposes.
Additionally, its use across all modules makes code reuse and maintainability sim­
pler.
• Frontend: The frontend is developed in Angular, a TypeScript­based framework
that supports the development of Single Page Applications (SPAs). Such applica­
tions are advantageous as they minimise the need for client­server communication,
resulting in a smooth user experience. Angular is designed around components,
which can be reused in multiple views and maintain consistent design, in our case
for both instance and aggregation views.
• Communication: The system makes use of MQTT for communication between the
backend components, as well as with the smart objects. MQTT is very lightweight
and supports microservice architecture by allowing modularisation via asynchronous
messaging. For the backend­frontend interaction, WebSocket connections ensure
quick two­way communication and UI updates, needed for real­life monitoring.
• Database: A non­relational database, specifically AWS DynamoDB, is used as the
persistence layer of the application. A Docker image of DynamoDB is used for
the local deployment and testing purposes, but a migration to the cloud version for
real­world application would be seamless. The main advantage of a non­relational
database is its scalability potential and schema flexibility, which can accommodate
data structures that evolve over time.
• Visualisation: The main libraries used for monitoring result visualisation are:
– GoJS: Used for E­GSM diagrams.
– BPMN.js: Used for BPMN diagrams.

Contributions

7.3.1
Engine Worker Modifications
The Engine Worker application contains significant complexity, as it houses the E­GSM
engine, whose underlying logic is non­trivial. Given that the module already behaved as
expected and no major architectural modifications were necessary to support the new
capabilities, only two targeted changes were implemented.
First, the event emission logic of E­GSM state changes was refined so that each internal
model transition only emits a single event, eliminating redundant messages. This optimi­
sation reduces the load on the central MQTT Broker and avoids additional processing in
the aggregation jobs, where these events trigger historical tracking of status, state, and
compliance attributes.
Second, a new type of event was added to emit the PFG condition when the value changes
during evaluation. This information is then utilised by various deviation detection algo­
rithms in the Aggregation Agents. These condition updates are published to an existing
“eGSM ENGINE INSTANCE” MQTT topic, which is already subscribed to by the relevant
aggregation jobs, since it is also used to deliver the E­GSM state updates.

8.2
Deviation Detection Performance Analysis
We have now validated the correctness of our deviation detection, so we can turn to eval­
uating the impact on the system performance. This analysis compares the performance
of two versions of the deviation detection algorithm ­ the original version developed by
Garda in [4] and the new version introduced in Section 6.5. Our version incorporates a
wider range of deviation types with more defined criteria for identifying them. This compar­
ison aims to evaluate end­to­end processing performance in terms of speed and message
generation within the system.
8.2.1
Cross­System Event Tracking
To be able to track the message flow through the system accurately, correlate messages
across different modules, and track the overall performance, it was necessary to tem­
porarily extend the existing codebase. To this end, we integrated an in­memory tracker of
events sent and received in multiple parts of the platform to avoid the overhead of write
operations.
The tracker associates each event sent from the emulator with a unique correlation ID
and stores the related process type, as well as a timestamp of when the event was sent.
The tracker only outputs the data to a file after finishing the entire experiment.
Moreover, the correlation ID is maintained in the engine, which processes the E­GSM
model state changes, and forwarded in the resulting messages directed to the aggre­
gators. After receiving each event from the worker, the aggregators run the deviation
detection algorithm and track the same information as the emulator to allow for message
association.
It is important to note that the system creates a one­to­many relationship between em­
ulator events and aggregator messages. A single emulator event causes the worker to
send multiple state changes in accordance with the evaluation of its inner model. There­
fore, a single emulator message may result in multiple correlating aggregator messages.

For our performance validation, we made use of the dataset introduced in Chapter 3,

8.2.3
Deviation Comparison Metrics
We examined the event timestamps generated by the emulator and the aggregator to
evaluate the performance of both algorithm versions, focusing on five metrics that provide
a thorough picture of the algorithms’ performance:
• Average difference between emulator and last aggregator message per event:
This measure determines the total processing time for each event, from initiation by
the emulator to the final message generated by the aggregator. It represents the
real­world impact of the new, more comprehensive processing logic and is the main
indicator of end­to­end processing speed. The reported value is the average of all
events of the respective process type.
• Number of aggregator messages generated per emulator message: This indi­
cates how many algorithm runs are caused by a single event from the emulator. If
we can reduce the quantity of messages per event, it will in turn reduce network
traffic, system load, and log clutter. The system will therefore become cleaner and
more stable.
The remaining three metrics are calculated based on the end­to­end processing time (i.e.,
the difference between the emulator and the last aggregator message per event):
• Standard deviation: This demonstrates how constant processing times are be­
tween runs. Lower numbers indicate more consistent system behaviour. It is crucial
for detecting issues with performance and ensuring reliable operations.
• Median: This measure displays typical performance and the middle value of pro­
cessing times. This is particularly helpful in systems where averages are affected
by occasional outliers.
• 90th percentile (P90): This illustrates the worst­case scenarios by providing the
value below which 90% of processing times occur. Monitoring P90 helps to under­
stand how the system reacts to high processing demands and ensures that delays
stay within acceptable ranges.

8.4
Aggregation Performance Analysis
Our goal in this analysis was to measure response times in order to assess the system’s
performance under different numbers of concurrent process instances, providing insights
into scalability and identifying potential bottlenecks.
To clarify, concurrent instances refer to multiple process instances running simultane­
ously, each generating and managing its own set of events in parallel. In particular, we
measured the change in the total processing time for each individual event as the number
of concurrent instances increased. The purpose was to understand how system behaviour
scales under higher load and identify points where performance changes become more
visible.
8.4.1
Emulator Adjustments and Cross­System Tracking
To make tracking possible across the system, we employed the same concept of corre­
lation IDs as in 8.2.1, with one modification. Instead of logging completion right after the
execution of the deviation detection algorithm, the correlation ID is forwarded into our new
aggregator, where the messages are logged after the computation of aggregated metrics.

To properly test the system under load, we decided to only perform the validation with non­
compliant process instances, as they cover more complex situations. The emulator was
therefore further enhanced to support emulation of the same process instance multiple
times in parallel.
One issue that arose during the initial testing was that under a major load (100 simul­
taneously emulated process instances), the MQTT broker would very occasionally drop
messages, which is unacceptable in a precise monitoring scenario. The reason was that
the same event was sent 100 times at once, every few seconds, overwhelming the queue.
While some parallel event sending is expected in a real­life scenario, it may be unreason­
able to assume such a high overload from a shipping company in our running example,
especially since hours worth of real data are compressed to just a few seconds of em­
ulation. To prevent message loss, which was observed during the initial testing, a fixed
waiting time of 75 ms was introduced between consecutive events. In order to effec­
tively stagger outgoing events, the waiting time was applied gradually, with the first event
occurring instantly, the second after 75 ms, the third after 150 ms, and so on.
Although a certain amount of artificial delay is introduced this way, it ensures that the
system functions consistently across all test sizes and allows for reliable comparisons.
Nonetheless, this is only a targeted fix for test scenarios, and the limitation needs to be
addressed in the future.
8.4.2
Validation Data and Approach
As mentioned, we conducted the experiment by duplicating emulation of the same deviat­
ing process instances. Specifically, we decided to focus on the LHR­AMS process type,
emulating instances p14 and p15 and duplicating them in a 1:1 ratio.
We conducted two different test scenarios. The first involved a clean system where each
test run started with no historical data stored in the database. The second used a pre­
loaded database, simulating a situation where the system already contains 100 historical
processes along with their deviations before starting. The latter scenario reflects a more
realistic environment where, along with continuously writing data during operation, the
database already contains past executions relevant to the aggregation.
The test was conducted in both scenarios by repeatedly running the new version of the
algorithm, increasing the number of concurrent instances from 2 to 100. As in the analysis
done previously, multiple aggregator messages are logged per one emulator message.
The comparison was made between the emulator and the final message generated by
the aggregator, measuring the total processing time for each event.
Each test configuration was run four times to reduce the impact of outliers and random
performance fluctuations, and the results were averaged. The system was tested with 2,
10, 50, and 100 concurrent instances. These values were selected to represent minimal,
low, medium, and high concurrency levels, naturally introducing increasing performance
impact as we move across this scale and making the progressive jumps in expected re­
sults.
Additionally, a single­instance test was conducted to verify baseline system functionality
and initial performance. This test recorded an average processing time of 88.62 ms (with­
out database) and 90 ms (with database), but it was excluded from the main analysis as
it does not reflect concurrent behaviour.

Conclusion
In this work, we conducted an extensive literature review of related work to gain insights
into visualisation techniques and deviation classification in the field of conformance check­
ing and process monitoring. By analysing a pivotal related paper on the behavioural
alignment approach to model repair, we refined the baseline deviation taxonomy of the
SMARTifact platform to cover a broader range of cases. We formalised the algorithms to
detect these deviations in pseudocode and developed various visualisations to present
process­level aggregation metrics. To evaluate our results, we performed an empirical
analysis using a real dataset, along with benchmarking to assess the performance of the
implemented solutions.
9.0.1
Answers to Research Questions
This section provides answers to the research questions introduced in Section1.4 which
served as the guide for this thesis:
RQ1: How can the different classes of deviations be detected in artifact­driven process
monitoring?
The different classes of deviations can be detected algorithmically by applying DFS­
based traversal over a tree representation of the E­GSM model. Specifically, the set
of deviations relevant for each analysed node depends on its block type, and the
deviations themselves are collected by observing the status and compliance per­
spectives of each individual child stage. These attributes are evaluated both in iso­
lation for each stage and in relation to other stages (e.g., sibling and parent stages).
For different detection logic, either current or historical values or their combination
is used. In addition, we take into account the runtime evaluated value of PFG con­
ditions. We also apply temporal filtering based on the iteration context to correlate
deviations with iteration cycles.
• RQ1.1: What classes of deviations are relevant to detect in artifact­driven pro­
cess monitoring?
We reviewed the related works and identified research which categorises mis­
match patterns between two Prime Event Structures, one for the model and one
for the observed log. To identify the gap between our baseline SMARTifact de­
viation classification and the related paper, we performed mutual mapping and
applied the related taxonomy to specific deviating cases. The results motivated
a new derived classification that achieves more expressiveness and takes into
consideration both control­flow and event­based anomalies.
• RQ1.2: What are the challenges in achieving finer­grained deviation detection
in the current methods?
We demonstrated that the main challenge was the lack of historical E­GSM
state snapshots, which limited the potential to tell apart two deviations with
the same current execution perspective attributes. For the same reason, the
overtime process development could not be analysed to determine the exact
behaviour. Furthermore, the unavailability of the evaluated PFG condition at
runtime in the deviation detection algorithm limited the ability to evaluate the
correctness of the execution of branches and iterations.

• RQ1.3: How can historical states of the model execution contribute to more
accurate deviation detection?
By storing historical snapshots of each stage’s state, status, and compliance,
as well as PFG evaluations, our new algorithms can reconstruct the exact exe­
cution flow. Thanks to this integration, we can accurately detect more specific
and complex deviation classes, such as multi­executions (including the num­
ber of re­executions), exact incorrect execution orders, and precise stages in­
volved in overlaps, and also confidently determine branch correctness. These
insights are not possible by studying the final state of the model alone.
• RQ1.4: How can we incorporate finer­grained deviation detection into the ex­
isting algorithm while maintaining efficiency?
We managed to leverage the existing DFS­based algorithm by extending it
with our new detection logic and to limit the amount of redundant event send­
ing and processing across the system while maintaining the real­time detection
capability. The benchmarks show acceptable ranges of performance time in­
crease, as well as more predictable end­to­end processing times compared to
the baseline approach, which can be attributed to more robust detection logic.
RQ2: How can artifact­driven process monitoring results be aggregated to provide an
overview of the entire process instead of a specific instance?
Our approach combines historical and real­time process executions to calculate var­
ious deviation­related aggregated metrics. Furthermore, a BPMN overlay is gener­
ated to update the UI in real­time, making use of the established icon system ex­
tended to the case of multiple instances. Aggregation is applied on both the overall
process level and stage level to provide multiple monitoring perspectives.
• RQ2.1: What techniques can be used to consolidate monitoring data across
multiple executions of the same process?
We implemented an event­driven aggregation job capable of receiving data
from deviation detection jobs of running process instances through MQTT, as
well as retrieving data from past executions from the database to combine them
in unified data structures. One job per process type is instantiated, and each
may be deployed on a different machine, supporting the established distributed
and scalable architecture.
• RQ2.2: What changes need to be applied to the current implementation to
achieve per­process monitoring?
The required changes included:
– Enabling MQTT broadcast within the same module to correctly route de­
viation data and making use of asynchronous topic subscriptions to avoid
race conditions.
– Adding a new component responsible for writing deviations to the database
to persist the data for the future.
– Implementing a new event­driven aggregation job type which calculates
the metrics.

level and process­type monitoring.
– Refactoring of existing BPMN functionality to allow for code reuse and sim­
plify maintenance of the platform, along with extending the overlays to ac­
commodate multiple process instances.
• RQ2.3: What visualisation techniques best support per­process monitoring?
We designed and implemented new visualisations in a dedicated UI view that

Future Work
This work improved the SMARTifact platform by refining and extending the deviation clas­
sification and improving the aggregation capabilities. While these contributions are sub­
stantial, many promising avenues remain open for future research and development.
The aggregation view could be further enhanced by incorporating filtering and drill­down
functionalities, inspired by those used in the field of data warehousing. Such options
are often cited in literature as essential for enhancing flexible analysis of variants and
drawing more concrete conclusions about the execution ([29] [30], [31]). Filtering criteria
could include various data attributes, deviation types, or even control flow relationships.
Another improvement of the aggregation visualisation could be to allow the stakeholders to
customise the severity brackets used for stage colouring in the BPMN overlay. Currently,
the severity classes are split uniformly into 25% intervals, but the user could adjust the
ranges based on the specific needs. Additionally, weights could be assigned to specific
deviations and stages to further emphasise critical, domain­specific risks.
The validation strategy could overall be expanded. While the current evaluation provides
a good basis, it could be extended with more real­life or artificially generated data to further
confirm the system’s applicability and reliability across unexpected scenarios. This would
involve acquiring BPMN models along with a set of respective execution logs, translating
them to E­GSM, and replaying the logs as we did in Section8. In addition, our design
choices are rooted in principles adapted from related work, usability testing with domain
experts and end users should be carried out to guarantee qualities such as clarity, usabil­
ity, and effectiveness.
Finally, a more pressing technical issue was encountered toward the end of the devel­
opment phase. Emulating a high amount of smart objects at once sometimes caused
message loss, which was temporarily patched for the testing purposes by introducing
artificial delays. However, this workaround does not carry over to real­world scenarios,
limiting the usability of the platform. To solve this, further work at the architectural level is
needed, potentially introducing multiple message brokers or implementing caching tech­
niques. Furthermore, while MQTT is appropriate for event emission from sensors thanks
to being lightweight, the inter­module communication could be handled by other types of
message brokers, such as RabbitMQ or Apache Kafka, as was already proposed in [4].
