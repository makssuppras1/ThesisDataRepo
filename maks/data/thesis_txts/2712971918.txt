Abstract
The Roll on Roll off (RoRo) stowage problem is a crucial issue for marine logistics as
RoRo ships are used very commonly. The RoRo stowage problem is known to be an
NP­hard problem and that is why different algorithms have been tested before on various
prior works. Nevertheless, there are not many papers on the RoRo stowage problem
even though it has an huge impact on marine logistics. Therefore, for this thesis, differ­
ent solution methods for the RoRo stowage problem are analyzed. Column generation
with Dantzig­Wolfe decomposition, the Kernel search matheuristic and another problem­
specific heuristic algorithm were tested. The column generation method gave very good
results for the test sets while the performance of the Kernel search algorithm is not as im­
pressive. The custom heuristic algorithm produced very impressive results, since it was
developed for the specific RoRo stowage problem in this thesis.

Introduction
Maritime transport is the most common method of transportation for international trade and
today’s global market. Specifically, the most convenient mode of transport for international
vehicle transportation is maritime transport using Roll­on/Roll­off (RoRo) ships. RoRo
ships are designed to carry wheeled cargo which includes all kinds of vehicles. ”RoRo”
describes how cargoes are loaded and discharged from the ship, so each cargo roll on
and off on their own wheels.
RoRo ships follow predefined routes by doing several stops at different ports either to take
or deliver cargoes all over the world. The stowage plan for RoRo ships is very critical since
changing the location of a cargo on the ship to deliver another cargo is a very costly task.
This is due to the fact that the cargoes are mostly large vehicles, and it is time consuming
and challenging to change their locations. Since RoRo ships operate for a very long time
to finish their routes, the most cost efficient way is to carry as much cargo as possible.
Therefore, a good stowage plan can save a lot of money.
Each RoRo ship has its route plan beforehand, so it is certain which cargo will be received
from which port or which cargo will be delivered to it. Thereby, it is possible to make a
stowage plan in advance. A stowage plan consists of several decisions such as which
cargoes to carry, how to stow each cargo during the trip, and how many vehicles there
will be in each cargo. While making the plan, lots of constraints should be taken into
account. For instance, there are mandatory cargoes bounded by long­term contracts,
so there is no option not to carry them. A second constraint is that some parts of the
ship are not suitable for stowage, so there are certain locations to place cargoes. Another
constraint is the stability of the ship which plays an important role because the ship should
be in balance to be able to continue its trip. Since stability equations consist of differential
equations, it is very hard to add them into the model.
RoRo stowage problem is a real­life problem and it is one of the main challenges for
maritime logistics companies. A poor stowage plan can cause operating at a loss. There
are some works conducted on this problem in the literature which will be discussed in detail
in the following section. In all of the papers, and there are not many of them, researchers
came up with different solution methods taking into account different constraints along
with the main ones because the problem cannot be solved in a reasonable time. It is
proven that the problem is NP­Hard in Øvstebø et al. [1].

1.1
Prior Work
X. Sun et al. came up with a semi­automated approach to solve stowage problem on
RoRo ships [2]. They initialize the problem with any feasible solution and generate better
stowage plans by improved lowest horizontal line searching algorithm. They tested their
algorithm for both solving homogeneous and heterogeneous decks. Homogeneous decks
are allowed to stow only one type vehicle while heterogeneous decks can have several
types of vehicles on it. They also made detailed analysis on stability of the ship and ship
floating.
In one of the recent papers published in 2020, Hansen and his colleagues worked on an
adaptive large neighborhood search (ALNS) heuristic for the stowage problem [3]. They
came up with an MIP model and they considered a generalized version of planar storage
location assignment problem (PSLAP). They considered the case where the decks are
2D planar surfaces and all cargoes must be delivered. Thus, all cargoes are mandatory.
Therefore, the objective is to minimize the undesirable shifting of cargoes, which was
introduced to the model using a cost penalty. They used the ALNS introduced by Ropke
and Pisinger [4]. To construct a feasible initial solution, they used greedy randomized
adaptive search procedure (GRASP). They used Dijktra’s algorithm to calculate shifting
costs by turning the storage plan into a network graph. They conclude that the model
and the heuristic work well for a stowage plan of 1 deck with an average of 1 undesirable
shift. However, in reality there are many decks on the ship. They also did not consider
the stability constraints regarding the balance of the ship.
Unlike Hansen, Øvstebø et al. considered a ship with several decks and included the sta­
bility of the ship in the model [1]. For the stability constraints they make sure that torque
from all sides of the ship is in the allowable torque limit. Their MIP model handles both
optional and mandatory cargoes and there is a revenue for carrying an optional cargo.
Therefore, the objective is to maximize the revenue, which is the difference between the
profits made from the optional cargoes and the penalties due to the undesirable shift­
ing of cargoes. They came up with a heuristic to solve the model, which consists of 3
parts: selection of optional cargoes, construction of stowage plan and improvement of
the plan. They compared the commercial MIP solvers and the heuristic that they came up
with. When there are no optional cargoes, commercial MIP solvers work very well unless
there are many instances. By introducing optional cargoes, the computational time for the
MIP solvers gets higher and the heuristic starts to outperform them. Note that the paper
was published in 2011 and current commercial solvers perform better. For my thesis, I
specifically worked on the model introduced in Øvstebø’s paper and I had a chance to
work with same data set they made their research on. I used the Gurobi solver for two of
the methods that I worked on. Gurobi can handle complex problems well so that it can
find the optimal solution for the MIP model for small instances in no time. However, for
larger instances it can not find an integer solution. Luckily, I had a chance to measure the
performance of different solution methods that I worked on. Results will be compared in
chapter 6.

1.2
Motivation
As international marine trade relies heavily on RoRo ships, finding optimal stowage plans
for them is of great importance. Despite its significance in real life applications, there are
a surprisingly limited number of works on the RoRo stowage problem in the literature.
Therefore, I believe that there may be different solution methodologies or different mod­
elling approaches to find better solutions for the RoRo stowage problem. As an example,
heuristic methods that have previously been applied to other problems may produce good
results for this problem as well. In short, my research question is: ”Are there any solution
methods for the RoRo stowage problem that outperform the existing ones?”.
1.2.1
Summary of my work
In the scope of my thesis I tried some solution methods on one of the variations of the
RoRo stowage problem. The model that I worked on is proposed in Øvstebø et al. as
mentioned before [1]. I tried three different methods to solve the problem. First one is
column generation which is a very well known algorithm for complex problems. It is an it­
erative algorithm which iterates between a master problem and one or more subproblems.
It creates columns or variables as they are needed. I used Dantzig­Wolfe decomposition
along with column generation. It is a widely used algorithm for large problems along with
column generation. The algorithms will be discussed in chapter 3 in detail.
The second method I implemented is a heuristic solution that is my own idea inspired by
column generation. I came up with a new MIP model which is equivalent to the model in
Øvstebø et al. [1]. The new MIP model uses previously created solution samples, namely
’patterns’, and tries to come up with a solution by combining some of the patterns. The
number of pre­computed patterns in the new MIP model is adjustable by some coefficients
so that it is possible to solve even the biggest instances in a reasonable time. By using
these coefficients, it is also possible to create the patterns that are most likely to be in the
solution. So, the heuristic can eliminate bad solutions before they are considered in the
model. The advantages and disadvantages of the heuristic algorithm compared to the
original MIP model will be discussed in section 4. The heuristic will also be explained in
detail.
The last solution method that I implemented is a matheuristic called the Kernel search
method, which was explained in Boschetti et al. [5]. The matheuristic promises to find
integer solutions quickly by solving many small MIP models instead of solving a big one.
The algorithm considers different decision variables at each iteration and keeps track of
the most promising ones. The algorithm will be discussed in chapter 5 in detail.
All solution methods are evaluated on the same data sets. The results for each solution
method will be presented, discussed and compared with the others in chapter 6.

Problem Description
In the scope of my thesis I focused on solving a specific RoRo stowage problem introduced
in Øvstebø et al. with both optional and mandatory cargoes [1]. I will refer to this model
as ’original MIP model’ in the following chapters for simplicity.
2.1
RoRo Stowage Problem
The RoRo ship has several decks and the decks are not identical. They have different
lengths and widths. They also have an adjustable height, so the height of each deck is
a decision variable as well. The ship has a predefined route and each cargo has to be
picked up and dropped of at its corresponding load and unload port, respectively.
There are two types of cargoes, namely optional and mandatory cargoes. Cargoes consist
of several identical vehicles and if a cargo is to be transported, all vehicles of that cargo
type must be located on the ship. So, there is no option to carry only some of the vehicles
of a cargo type. Mandatory cargoes must be carried to their destination, but this is not the
case for optional cargoes. Instead, there is an assigned revenue of carrying each optional
cargo, which are typically different from each other.
Once a decision has been made to place a cargo on the ship, there are two different
additional considerations. The first one is to decide how many vehicles of that cargo
type will be stowed on each deck and the second one is to decide where on the deck it
should be placed. To decide where to put the cargo on the deck, each deck is divided into
lanes. Number of lanes are fixed for each deck but the width of each lane is also a decision
variable. Lane can be interpreted as columns such that all the vehicles placed on the lane
are one after each other but they cannot be placed side by side. Once these two decisions
are made, the cargo is placed at the very end of the deck. For the specific problem at
hand, cargoes can only be loaded and unloaded from one side of the deck and they can
only move forwards or backwards. Since all of the cargoes are placed as far as possible
to the loading edge, it might be the case that one or more additional cargoes must be
moved before the desired cargo can be unloaded. In order to minimize undesirable cargo
movement in a stowage plan, a penalty cost is introduced for each undesirable move.

Small Example
Let us take a look at a small example with only 4 mandatory cargo types (blue, green,
purple and red) and 4 ports in order to explain to problem more clearly. Assume that
there is only 1 deck and the deck is divided into 3 lanes. Vehicles of each cargo type
have the same length and width, and the deck is long enough to fit at most 4 vehicles and
wide enough to fit at most 3 vehicles. The loading and unloading ports and the number
of vehicles in each cargo are shown on figure 2.1 below.
Figure 2.1: Small example
There are multiple feasible solutions to this small example. Since the green cargo is
loaded before the purple cargo and its unloading port is before the purple cargo’s unload­
ing port, some of the feasible solutions will result in an undesirable move. To avoid this,
all ports should be taken into account while solving the problem.
Let us examine two different feasible solutions of this example. In figure 2.2 the stowage
plan when the ship leaves the port is illustrated for each port. Since there is only one
deck, all cargoes must be stowed on that deck. On port 1 there are 4 blue cars on the first
lane, 4 green cars on the second lane and there is only one green car on the third lane.
Width of the first lane equal to the width of the blue vehicle and total width of the lanes
must be equal to the width of the deck. On port 3, there is one undesirable move for the
purple cargo because it is the unloading port of the green cargo, which was placed before
the purple cargo. If the green cargo was an optional one and had a penalty cost higher
than its revenue, it would not be transported. It is obvious that this solution is feasible but
not optimal.

2.2
Original MIP Model
In this section, the MIP model introduced by Øvstebø et al. will be explained in detail [1].
Other models in the following chapters are variations of the original MIP model. Therefore,
the comparison of different solution methods are done between the original MIP model,
the LP Relaxed solution of the original MIP model and the solutions obtained from different
methods.
Original MIP model aims to find the optimal stowage plan explained in section 2.1.1 small
example for real data sets. Note that the original MIP model in section 2.2.2 is missing
stability constraints which ensure that the ship is in balance when cargoes are stowed.
All analysis are done by neglecting stability constraints. As mentioned in Øvstebø et al., it
is not easy to add stability constraints into model since it makes the model non­linear [1].
These calculations are quite complex and differential equations are needed. They used
some linear approximation to avoid non­linearity but since it does not reflect the reality, I
decide not to put them in my thesis. More detailed research on stability of the ship can be
found in X Sun et al. [2].

In this section Dantzig­Wolfe decomposition method and column generation approach will
be explained in detail. Theory for both of the applications will be discussed and explained
thoroughly. Master and subproblem formulations of the decomposed original MIP model
will be given for the column generation method.
3.1
Dantzig­Wolfe Decomposition
Dantzig­Wolfe decomposition method is proposed by Dantzig and Wolfe in 1960 [6]. It
is first used in reformulation of LP problems but now it is also used to reformulate MIP
problems as well.
By applying Dantzig­Wolfe decomposition to an MIP model, we transform it to an equiv­
alent MIP model with many more variables and fewer constraints compared to original
problem. LP relaxation of the new model often provides a better lower/upper bound com­
pared to the LP relaxation of the original model. Since there are many variables, they
need to be added into the model dynamically. That is why column generation is used to
solve the transformed model.
Dantzig­Wolfe reformulation will be explained in detail with an example. Before explaining
it in detail some background information is needed. Needed theorems and definitions will
be explained in section 3.1.1.
3.1.1
Minkowski­Weyl Theorem and Definitions
For the theory of Dantzig­Wolfe decomposition, definition of a polytope should be given
and the Minkowski­Weyl theorem should be explained [7].
A polyhedron is a set of x variables such that X = {x ∈Rn|Ax ≤b}. A Polytope is a
bounded polyhedron.

Method
In this chapter a heuristic solution approach to the original MIP model discussed in Chapter

Each pattern represents a possible solution for a lane to be placed on a deck. Unlike other
solution approaches, this heuristic does not limit the number of lanes that can be placed on
a deck. However, this can also be added by introducing an additional constraint. I came
up with a MIP model that represents the original problem in a different way using the
generated patterns. The model takes all the patterns as an input parameter and decides
on which patterns to use and how many of them will be in the solution for every deck and
every port.

4.3
Reducing the Number of Patterns
When solving the pattern MIP model with all possible patterns, we get the optimal solution
since it is equivalent to the original MIP model. However, even for the smallest data
instances, there are more than 200000 possible patterns and for the larger instances,
the pattern number goes up to millions. Therefore, for most of the instances, the pattern
MIP model cannot be solved to optimality within reasonable time as expected. So, in
order to reduce the computation time, one option is to reduce the number of precomputed
patterns. It is obvious that some of the patterns are better than the others, so I came up
with 4 different ideas to reduce the number of patterns. In the following subsections, these
ideas will be explained in detail. How I used different approaches to reduce the number
of patterns will be explained in chapter 6.
4.3.1
Removal of infeasible lanes due to length
The first approach to reduce the number of patterns is to limit the total length for the
patterns. We already know that patterns with lengths higher than the length of the deck
with the maximum value are not feasible patterns. So, limiting the length of the pattern is
a great way to reduce the number of patterns. One further improvement for this idea that
may also help is to remove constraints in equation 3.2. To do that, we can limit the length of
the patterns such that it can never exceed the length of the deck with the minimum value.
Note that this may leads to solutions where one or more mandatory cargoes cannot be
transported, since by introducing the length limit for the patterns, the problem gets more
constrained than the original MIP model. But the solution time decreases since many
constraints from the pattern MIP model are removed.
4.3.2
Length utilization of decks
The second way to reduce the number of patterns is to remove all patterns with length
utilization below a certain threshold. The utilization is calculated by dividing the total length
of the pattern by the length of the deck. For instance, some of the patterns consists of only
one vehicle which can almost never be in the solution. If the total length of a pattern is half
of the length of the deck it means that the pattern only utilizes the half of the deck. Such
patterns will most probably not be in the optimal solution. So, including those patterns in
the model is a waste of time. Therefore, I decided to introduce length utilization parameter
while creating the possible patterns. The results for different values of length utilization
parameter will be discussed in chapter 6.
4.3.3
Periodic decks
The third approach to reduce the number of patterns is to assume periodic solutions. I
realized that the parameter that increases the number of patterns most is the number of
vehicles in cargoes. So, I decided to reduce the number of vehicles in such a way that

I also do not change the problem itself significantly. I decided to divide the number of
vehicles for all cargoes and the lengths of all decks by the same coefficient. By doing
this I assume that there is less space on decks and also there are less vehicles in each
cargo. Note that it did not affect the width or the height constraints since the reduction
of vehicle number does not affect the width or the height of the pattern. This is because
only the length of the pattern is calculated by summing all the vehicles on that pattern
while the height and the width is calculated by taking the maximum value. By reducing
the number of vehicles in each cargo type and the deck space, only solutions that are
periodic in length are assumed to be feasible. Depending on the scaling factor, number of
patterns can be reduced greatly. However, higher values of the scaling factor assumes a
shorter period for the solutions, which would mean that it is repeated more often, resulting
in a model further away from the original problem. Below, in figure 4.2, the relationship

4.3.4
Clumping cars together
Another way to reduce the number of patterns is to clump the cars together. Having only
1 vehicle from a cargo type on a pattern is usually not cost efficient. So, the model already
invests in patterns that consists of several vehicles of a cargo type. That is why clustering
cars together works very well to reduce the number of patterns and find a better solution.
To cluster cars, I divide the number of vehicles in each cargo type by a coefficient and
multiply the length of these vehicles by the same coefficient. So, we have fewer cars but
they are longer. Let us assume that we have 100 vehicles in cargo type c and each vehicle
is 5 meters long. If we choose the coefficient to be 5 we would have 20 vehicles in cargo
type c with each of the vehicles being 25 meters long. This could also be done by adding
a constraint while creating the patterns, which ensures that each pattern must have either
0 or at least n vehicles from a cargo type. However, this results in high computational time
for creating patterns. Therefore, I decided to do it with introducing another scaling factor
for clumping cars together. Figure 4.3 shows the relationship between the scaling factor

4.3.5
Limiting the number of different cargo types
The last idea to reduce the number of patterns is to limit the number of cargo types on a
pattern. Since having high number of cargo types increases the risk of paying penalty, the
optimal solutions to the model tend to avoid such patterns. That is why it is very logical

Kernel Search Method
In this chapter, Kernel Search Method and how it is implemented for the original MIP
model will be explained in detail. The algorithm introduced in Angelelli et. al, as a heuristic
method [9]. Kernel search is an iterative matheuristic method to find a heuristic solution
for MIP problems. The method is based on solving several smaller MIP problems instead
of solving a big one.
5.1
Kernel Search Algorithm
The algorithm goes through two phases: initialization and expansion. To initialize the algo­
rithm, some of the variables should be chosen as promising variables. In the initialization
phase of the algorithm, multiple methods can be used. I used LP relaxation to initialize
the algorithm as suggested by Boschetti et al. [5]. After solving the LP relaxation of the
original problem, the non­zero decision variables are put in a subset, namely a kernel.
They are the most promising variables. Reduced cost of the rest of the variables is cal­
culated using the shadow prices of the corresponding nonnegativity constraints and they
are partitioned into buckets. Partitioning process works such that the variables with the
highest reduced cost are in the first bucket and the rest of the buckets are filled with the
same logic. Therefore, the first bucket consists of the most promising decision variables
while the last bucket has the least promising variables.
Before moving on to the next stage, the optimal solution x∗and the optimal objective value
z∗must be initialized. To do so, the reduced problem MIP(K) is solved. This is equivalent
to setting all variables outside of K to 0 and solving MIP without any relaxation. If a
feasible solution can be found within the time limit, it is used to initialize x∗and z∗.
After the initialization, the expansion phase of the algorithm starts. This part is the iterative
part and at each iteration an extended version of the initial MIP model is solved. In the
first iteration, the reduced version the original MIP only contains the variables in the kernel
and the variables in the first bucket. After solving the reduced MIP we must check if the
new variables in the bucket have improved the objective. If that is the case, the non­zero
variables in the bucket are added to the kernel. If the objective values is not improved,
none of the variables in the bucket are added to the kernel. In order to ensure this, I

have added this as a constraint to the reduced MIP as suggested in Boschetti [5]. If the
objective value has improved, x∗and z∗are updated accordingly. At each iteration, the

6.2.1
Final Test
Among periodic decks and clumping cars methods, I decided to use clumping cars method
along with cargo type limiting method. There are couple of reasons for this. First one
is clumping cars is something that is already done by the original MIP model. Since
the original MIP model can be solved to optimality for instances, I was able to examine
the optimal solutions and realized that the model tries to put the vehicles from the same
cargo type together as much as possible to avoid penalty cost for relocation of cargoes on
unloading ports. The second reason is that clumping cars reduce the number of patterns
more than assuming periodic decks. That is why it usually has less computational time
for all of the instances. The last reason is that in general clustering cars makes more
sense than assuming periodic solutions. Periodic decks method is more likely to be too
constrained compared to clustering the cars together.

Discussion
In this section the results covered before will be further discussed and comparison of

To make the comparison easier, visualization of solution of each method for the same
data instance will be given. The chosen data instance is ’AVERAGE1’ since it only has

consists of 3 mandatory and 3 optional cargoes and 6 decks. Each cargo will be shown

of brown vehicles and none of the decks has more than 3 cargo types on port 2. Note
that there is not only one stowage plan that gives the optimal solution. Different stowage
plan may result in same objective function value. On deck 2, one of the green vehicles is

7
Conclusion
7.1
Discussion
For my thesis I applied 3 different methods to solve the RoRo stowage optimization prob­
lem proposed in Øvstebø et al. (2011). The theory behind all of the methods I analyzed
and the results for all of them are given in previous chapters in detail. To conclude, I will
summarize the results shortly for each of the methods that I implemented.
First, I implemented column generation method along with using Dantzig­Wolfe decom­
position method to solve the problem. Then, I compared the results with the LP relaxation
results of the original model. The column generation method did very well and found a
better upper bound for almost all of the instances compared to the upper bound found by
using LP relaxation. However, run time for column generation is always higher than the
LP relaxation time since the LP relaxation found results in less than a second for all of the
instances that I worked with. Different decomposition methods may work better but the
column generation method with Dantzig­Wolfe decomposition gave promising results.
Secondly, I implemented a heuristic algorithm that uses previously created patterns and
then takes these patterns as parameters to decide which pattern will be included in the
solution. The heuristic worked very well for almost all of the instances. It could solve some
of the instances to optimality. Creating patterns beforehand is a very good idea to identify
the bad solutions, so that these bad solutions can be removed from the pattern sets before
they evaluated in the model. This helped a lot with computational time especially for
the larger instances. However, the largest instance that I made my tests on consists of
12 cargo types. That is why pattern generating model might not be the best model to
solve even larger instances. A larger instance means higher number of patterns and
higher computational time. Even removing the bad patterns might not work well for larger
instances.
Finally, I implemented a matheuristic called the Kernel search algorithm. The algorithm
worked very well for small instances, but it could not find feasible solutions for some of
the larger instances. Note that I implemented the algorithm in MATLAB. Therefore, it uses
MATLAB’s optimizer which is much slower than other optimizers like Gurobi or Cplex. In
spite of this, the run time for the algorithm for almost all of the instances was short. The
RoRo Stowage Optimization

==PAGE:47==
advantage of the algorithm is that the MIP model is divided into a number of smaller MIP
models, which are easier to solve. That is why the method always finds a reasonable
solution. Therefore, I believe that the Kernel search algorithm gave promising results and
if it had been using better solvers such as Gurobi or Cplex, it would result in even better
solutions. The algorithm can also be used along with other heuristic approaches since in
the worst case scenario, it gives a good initial point to start with.
7.2
Conclusion
To sum up, all of the solution methodologies that are mentioned in section 1.2 were tested
on a variety of data instances. Column generation with Dantzig­Wolfe decomposition,
pattern generating heuristic and the kernel search matheuristic are explained in previous
chapters in detail and the results are given and discussed in chapter 6 with visuals and
tables. All of the solution methods for the RoRo stowage problem gave promising results.
Therefore, the answer for my research question is: yes, there are some solution methods
for the RoRo stowage problem that outperforms the existing ones. Since only one type of
RoRo stowage problem is considered, these solution methods may not work well for other
versions of the RoRo stowage problem or they may give even better results. So, further
investigation of the methods is required to draw more concrete conclusions on different
versions of the RoRo stowage problem.
