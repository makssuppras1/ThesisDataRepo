Abstract
This study examines retrieving snow depth using optimal estimation based on satellite passive microwave
radiometry (PMR) observations from the Advanced Microwave Scattering Radiometer 2 (AMSR2). The radiative
transfer model Snow Microwave Radiative Tranfer is used as forward model, and reference data is obtained
from the Round Robin Data Package using NASA Operation Icebridge Data from 2013-2019.
The analysis of a synthetically created dataset shows that the potential for retrieving snow depth is very
promising if we have accurate covariances between model parameters, while the retrieval performs signi-
ficantly worse if the covariances are unavailable.
Our analysis of observations from AMSR2 highlights challenges linked to incomplete ”real-world” data.
This involves not knowing the prior state and uncertainty covariances correctly and lacking information
about the correct model setup. The retrieval suffers from non-uniqueness and high inter-correlations. On
top of this, the high sensitivity to several poorly understood model input parameters complicates the re-
trieval process.
The best-performing retrieval of snow depth from observations is obtained when only using model input
parameter variances in the prior state covariance matrix. This resulted in a correlation coefficient of 0.72, a
coefficient of determination of 0.50 and a root mean square error of 0.03 m.
However, this result is expected to be due to a lack of knowledge regarding the true covariances. Based
on the analysis of synthetic data it is expected that significant improvements can be obtained if these are
accurately defined.
Therefore, a crucial next step is to obtain a large-scale dataset with coinciding values of all the necessary
model input parameters.

Introduction
Snow on sea ice is important in the climate system due its insulating effect, which affects sea ice growth
and melt rate by altering the heat exchange between the atmosphere and the ice [1]. Furthermore, accurate
snow depth retrievals are crucial in the determination of sea ice thickness from remote sensing and as of
now snow depth measurement are one of the main sources of uncertainties [2]. Snow is also important due
to its effect on the ice albedo feedback, with (especially new) snow having a significantly higher albedo than
the underlying sea ice [3].
In situ measurements of snow depth (SD) over sea ice are scarse. Drifting buoys, such as the Ice Mass
Balance Buoys (IMB), provide SD measurements from the entire winter, but these measurements are of
limited value, as they are local observations and therefore only representative of a small area around the
buoy [4].
Airborne measurements of coinciding snow and sea ice from NASA’s Operation IceBridge (OIB) are an
invaluable source, but these are mainly constricted to multi year ice and measurements are only conducted
in March and April. Furthermore, these campaigns are expensive and the last flight was conducted in 2019
[5].
A still commonly used SD product is a modified version of the Warren Climatology [6], which is based on
observations from the North Pole drifting stations from the 1950s to 1990s. However, as the Arctic climate is
changing rapidly, this climatology is outdated and simple modifications have been made to attempt to fit it
into a changing climate, such as by halving the SD over FYI [7]. Studies have shown that significant errors
in sea ice thickness estimation can result from the use of the climatology [8]
Other SD products are based on atmospheric reanalysis data as input to a snow accumulation models in
combination with snow redistribution by sea ice drift [9, 10]. However, these models still need further
development and an intercomparison study by Zhou et. al. showed significant differences in snow depth
derived products from reanalysis data when compared to reference data [11].
Satellite remote sensing has a great potential for providing continuous, up to date, polar scale, estimations
of snow depth. Satellite microwave remote sensing is independent of sunlight and can penetrate through
clouds, which provides a clear advantage compared to infrared and visible radiation. Furthermore, the
substantial coverage of Passive Microwave Remote Sensing instruments, such as the Advanced Scattering
Microwave Radiometer 2 (AMSR2), means that measurements of the entire arctic can be obtained several
times a day [12].
Estimation of snow depth by using observations from a passive microwave radiometer (PMR) is not new.
Previous studies have shown that emitted thermal radiation, also called brightness temperatures (TB), in the
microwave region can be linked to SD. Several of these studies have been based on an empirical relationship
between the amount of scattering in the snowpack and the snow depth [13, 14, 15]. These provide a simple
relationship but are very sensitive to other effects that can lead to similar behaviour such as snow grain size
variations, wetness and sea ice type [2]. Other studies have used the thermally insulating effect of the snow
cover to make snow depth predictions based on temperature of the snow/ice interface [16].

In recent years, several deep learning approaches to snow depth estimation has been made using PMR
observations. These have shown promising results when compared to classical empirical relationships [17,
18]. However, they lack separation of sea ice types. Failing to separate firstyear ice (FYI) and multiyear
ice (MYI) is expected to cause a falsely based link between snow depth and brightness temperatures as the
scattering effect caused by snow depth is similar to that observed in MYI. However, the MYI signal is often
stronger and is therefore expected to dominate the retrieval [2].
On top of this, neural networks (NN) are limited by their lack of explainability. Deep learning techniques are
largely a black box, as the deep learning model updates itself simply based on observations. Nevertheless,
this process of learning by observing also means that the deep learning model can repeatedly learn and
become more robust, as more data becomes available. However, most deep learning models require target
values, meaning that fairly accurate snow depth measurements are required to train the networks.
Both NNs and regression algorithms fall into the category statistical retrieval algorithms as they primarily
use statistical inference [19]. On the other hand, physical retrievals use and invert a forward model by an
iterative process to converge to a solution considering prior knowledge. Physical retrievals can provide
more insight into the nature of an inverse problem, such as quantifying the information content [19]. They
have two main advantages when compared to statistical retrieval methods. The physical processes are
clearly evident at each point in the retrieval, and they do not require a large training dataset. Both of these
are very notable advantages in terms of estimating snow depth on sea ice, as a lack of accurate, large-scale
reference data is one of the main constraints.
Furthermore, the complex interactions between parameters in the snow pack makes it very advantageous to
understand what the retrieval learns and how. Therefore, this study examines the use of a physical retrieval
method known as optimal estimation (OE). This particular retrieval method also has the great advantage of
retrieving probabilistic outcome, hereby providing uncertainty estimates [19].
1.1
Project purpose
In this study we aim to solve the inverse problem of estimating feasible model parameters to the snow mi-
crowave radiative transfer model (SMRT), from observed Brightness Temperatures. We focus on estimating
snow depth due to the evident need and the proven sensitivity of TBs to this parameter. To retrieve relevant
parameters we use optimal estimation (OE) which is a probabilistic physical retrieval method, that uses a
probabilistic version of least squares to find the optimal solution of the parameters along with an estimate
of their uncertainties in the form of an posterior state covariance matrix.
To test the potential of SMRT we perform tests using a dataset from Cambridge Bay obtained in April 2022,
with all SMRT model input parameters measured.
Subsequently, we retrieve estimates of snow depth with OE on a larger dataset, provided by the Round
Robin Data Package [20]. This dataset contains measurements of SD and SIT from OIB, that has been co-
located to TBs from the Advanced Scanning Microwave Radiometer 2 (AMSR2), reanalysis data from the
European Medium Range Weather Forecast (ECMWF) and electromagnetic backscatter from the Advanced
Scatterometer (ASCAT).

2
Theory
In this section, the relevant theoretical background of this project will be provided. The theory is split into
four main topics. The first section describes the relevant physical and electromagnetic characteristics of
snow and sea ice. The second section describes what microwave remote sensing is, along with terms that
are important for the passive microwave signatures of snow and sea ice. The third section focuses on the
Snow Microwave Radiative Transfer (SMRT) model and the linkages between the model input parameters
to the model and different ice and snow characteristics. The fourth section briefly explains the basis of OE,
focusing on the assumptions and the key equations of the algorithm.
2.1
Sea Ice and snow physical characteristics
Snow-covered sea ice is the media from which we attempt to deduce information in this study. To un-
derstand the electromagnetic signal that we observe and the related challenges, we must have significant
knowledge about the physical and electromagnetic characteristics of the media involved.
Sea ice forms from ocean water, and hence one of the significant differences compared to freshwater ice is
that sea ice contains salt. The salt is located in highly dense liquid pockets in the ice called brine pock-
ets. These are formed during sea ice formation and start to drain immediately afterwards, signifying that
the salinity of sea ice decrease with age. The salinity profile typically has a so-called ”c-shape”, with the
highest salinity near the interfaces with the snow and the ocean. Sea ice is split into two primary categories
first-year ice (FYI) and multiyear ice (MYI). MYI has lasted through the summer melt and hence contains
significantly different characteristics. It contains very little salt compared to FYI, has a rougher surface and
contains a larger amount of air due to mainly summer surface melt [21]. In appendix A.1 is a more thorough
explanation of FYI and MYI. This study focuses on FYI due to its less complex nature than MYI.
The snowpack on Arctic sea ice is a complex media, which makes modelling it a complicated process. Snow
on sea ice shows large variability over small and large geographical and temporal scales. Furthermore, it is
highly impacted by wind, temperature, time of snowfall and the thickness of snow cover beneath it. Espe-
cially wind storms have a large effect as they accumulate snow in sheltered areas while leaving other areas
bare. Wind deposition results in compacting, increasing the snow’s density and hardness. Furthermore,
snow storms break up snow grains and change the snow structure. The combination of windstorms and
snowfall events makes snow a layered medium, but the layering can be highly anisotropic even when the
snow surface looks fairly smooth [22].
One of the key processes which impact the snow cover is snow metamorphism, which happens due to a
temperature gradient between the warm sea ice and the colder atmosphere during Arctic winter. Snow
metamorphism generally requires low-density snow and a strong temperature gradient [22]. Therefore, this
process is strongest when the snow cover is thin due to the highly insulating effect of snow cover, making the
temperature gradient smaller. This signifies that snow metamorphism mainly happens in the early winter
[23].
The temperature gradient changes the microstructure of the snow, as the gradient leads to an upward water
flux, which, combined with sublimation-condensation cycles, leads to grain growth. The sublimation hap-

Apart from this, snow on FYI contains salt. During the formation of sea ice, a small amount of brine is
expelled upwards, resulting in a thin brine layer on the sea ice surface. The subsequent snowfall accumula-
tion causes an upward brine flux from the sea ice surface into the snow. This process produces saline snow,
primarily within the bottom 6-8 cm, with salinities ranging from 1 to 20 parts per thousand, also known as
the practical salinity unit (PSU) [24].
Snowcovered sea ice is modelled by assuming only variations with depth [25]. Horizontal spatial variations
are not considered, although they are known to be present [22]. The impact of snow on microwave emission
depends highly on both the number of layers and the type of layers. On top of this, several studies have
shown [26], [22], that layering and snow types are far from constant over both spatial and temporal scales.
In order to model the snowpack, we must therefore attempt to describe the general snowpack, although, as
mentioned above, this is no trivial task.
Based on observations [22], [26], it is expected that both snow on FYI and MYI will generally be character-
ised by a depth hoar layer at the bottom of the snowpack. Above the depth hoar layer are typically so-called
windslab, or wind-packed layers, which are formed by redistribution of snow by wind [22]. These are typic-
ally higher-density layers with smaller grains than depth hoar layers. These layers become more prominent
during the winter; hence, highly dense windslab layers are expected by the end of the Arctic winter [1].
On top of these layers, new snow layers may form. However, the low precipitation in the Arctic and the
strong winds, signify that these new snow layers are expected to be short-lived [1]. Therefore, they are
neglected when modelling the snowpack in this study.

Dielectric properties of snow and sea ice
This section is based on information from chapters 4.3 to 4.6 from Ulaby et al. [30]
To understand the propagation of radiation through snow-covered sea ice, we must first examine the dielec-
tric properties of the materials involved. A parameter of uttermost importance in this regard is the average
dielectric constant (denoted ϵ). This is generally a complex number given by: ϵ = ϵ′ −jϵ′′. Here ϵ′ is
known as the relative permittivity, which has to do with the propagation of radiation when encountering
an interface. ϵ′′ is the dielectric loss factor and determines the absorption and emission of radiation of a
material.
Sea ice and snow are not pure materials but are instead mixtures consisting of pure ice, salt and air. The
dielectric constant of a multi-phase mixture is related to the dielectric constants of the individual substances,
along with their volume fractions, their spatial distributions and their orientations with respect to the ori-
entation of the incident electric field vector [30].
The substance with the highest volume fraction is commonly known as the host material, whereas the others
are known as inclusions.
For sea ice, the host material is pure ice, and the inclusions are brine pockets and air pockets. For FYI, the
air fraction (also known as porosity) tends to be small, and hence FYI is frequently modelled as a two-phase
medium of pure ice and brine.
The salinity in the brine causes the dielectric constant to be very large compared to both pure ice and air,
with especially the loss factor being much higher. This signifies that the brine inclusions have a significant
impact, especially the imaginary part of the effective permittivity of sea ice. This is illustrated in figure 1,
where we observe that the loss factor of FYI is much larger than that of MYI.
Dry snow is generally thought of as a two-phase medium consisting of pure ice and air. Nevertheless, as
previously mentioned, snow on sea ice is expected to have some degree of salt within it, concentrated close
to the snow/ice interface [24]. The salinity is typically modelled by including salinity in the bottom of the
snowpack in the form of brine inclusions. One of the mixing models that are commonly used to model dry
snow is the Tinga-Van-Blossey model, here shown for spherical inclusions [30]:

2.8
Atmosphere and roughness corrections
The SMRT model estimates only the self-emission component of the brightness temperature. However, the
quantity measured by the radiometer will be a sum of several contributions as described in section 2.3. To
compare modelled TBs from SMRT with measured TBs from AMSR2, corrections must be applied, which
include the frequency depended expected contributions from the upwelling/downwelling radiation and
which computes the transmissivity of the atmosphere at AMSR2 measurement frequencies.
2.8.1
Atmosphere correction
The Arctic atmosphere is generally dry, and during winter, even more so. This is partially due to the cold
temperatures, making the air have a lower capacity to hold water and partially due to sea ice cover, which
blocks the evaporation from the underlying ocean [38]. In this study, we only examine sea ice covered
regions during winter months; hence we can assume that the atmosphere will be dry and, therefore, the
atmospheric impact on the TBs will likely be low [39]. Based on an empirical study by Leif Toudal Pedersen
using yearly measured brightness temperatures from AMSR2 for the entire Arctic, a simple linear relation-
ship between the transmissivity and the up/downwelling temperatures as a function of total columnar wa-
ter vapour (TCWV) was found. This study is available in appendix A.2. Due to the high correlation found
in the study and the fact that the atmosphere is cold and dry, it is estimated that this simple correction is
sufficient to account for the atmospheric influence.
2.8.2
Rough snow/ice interface correction
Another highly important correction is due to the rough interfaces between especially the snow and the sea
ice. As previously mentioned, SMRT assumes layers to be plane parallel interfaces; hence they are expected
to be perfectly smooth. Within the snowpack, this is considered a reasonable assumption, but both the
snow/ice interface and the air/snow interface are likely, not smooth.
The main effect of the interface roughness is that the polarization-specific signals are being mixed, with
increased roughness causing increased mixing. Hence, the total reflectivity consists of not only a coherent
component but also an incoherent component. The surface roughness is characterized by the surface root
mean square (rms) slope (m), which in turn depends on the surface height and correlation length.
In this study, a rough interface correction is applied to the simulated SMRT TBs, which is based on a study
by Shi et al. [40] in combination with empirical relationships from Chapter 12 Emission Model And Land
Observations by Ulaby et al. In appendix A.3 the correction is described in more detail.
The correction is only done to the snow/ice interface.

In this study we aim to solve the inverse problem of estimating feasible model parameters to SMRT from
observed TBs. To do so we use optimal estimation (OE) which is a probabilistic physical retrieval method,
that uses a probabilistic version of least squares to find the optimal solution of the parameters along with
an estimate of their uncertainties in the form of an posterior state covariance matrix [19]. It combines ob-
servations, prior information, and the corresponding uncertainties to find an optimal solution. The python
package called pyOE is used for the optimization, more information about this package is available from
[19].
OE works by trying to find an optimal solution xop given an observation and prior information by using an
iterative process. The prior is described by the mean xa and the covariance matrix Sap. The iteration process
is initialized with a first guess (or start guess) of the state x0. For the snow covered sea ice this involves
providing a guess of the snow depth, sea ice thickness etc. The first guess can be based on reanalysis data
or likewise but in absence of this xa is typically used. The forward operator F transforms the first guess
from model space to measurement space, hence giving F(x0). The subsequent step involves computing the
difference between F(x0) and yobs and using this difference to update the first guess to the next guess x1.
Here yobs is the observations belonging to the model parameters which we aim to retrive. Finding the next
guess is done weighting the differences by the measurement uncertainty covariance matrix Sy and the prior
state covariance matrix Sa [19]. The process of updating the first guess is shown in equation 21. As Sy and
Sa are covariance matrices this implies that the errors of the model parameters xa the observations yobs can

Bayes theorem
OE uses bayesian statistics, where probability describes a degree of belief in an event. As a result, bayesian
probabilities are updated whenever we obtain new knowledge. The mathematical form of this belief is
given by Bayes theorem [41]:
P(x|y) = P(y|x) · P(x)
P(y)
(24)
This states the probability of getting x, here our model parameters, given y, which is essentially the solution
to the inverse problem. Bayes theorem states that the solution can be obtained by multiplying the probability
of y given x (P(y|x), also known as the likelihood, with the prior probability density of the state P(x). Here
P(x) is based on some prior knowledge, such as a model forecast or measurements obtained at a different
time or distance. P(y|x) represents the uncertainty in the forward model and the measurement process. P(y)
is a normalisation factor [41].

3.1
OE challenges
A central challenge linked to OE is non-uniqueness, which describes that there is a distribution of states
around x which map to y. However, it can also imply that significantly different states map to y. This lead
to inversion problems more often than not being ill-posed; meaning that the solution is not unique or that
no solution exists. Mathematically non-uniqueness can be described as follows [19]:
F(x) = F(x’) = y
Furthermore, OE retrievals are complicated by the fact that, observations contain some noise component and
similarly, models also contain uncertainties, which can be either in the physics themselves or in additional
datasets required to drive the model [19].
Therefore, initially, using a synthetic dataset to examine the retrieval problem is often suggested. Synthetic
observations come with several advantages. Forward errors cancel each other out, and we know the true
state, e.g. the actual model input parameters, which we can compare to the retrieval [19].
3.1.1
Uncertainties related to the prior
The prior dataset serves as a constraint for the retrieval, as the uncertainty in the retrieval must be smaller
than the uncertainty in the prior; otherwise, no information from the observations has been added by the
retrieval. Nevertheless, if we lack information about the variability and covariance of the true state, then
the retrieval can be biased. In cases where the information content in the observations is high, the prior only
serves as a slight constraint. However, in most cases, the prior is essential and using the wrong prior can
lead to erroneous retrievals [19].
3.1.2
Uncertainties related to the forward model
Uncertainties in the forward model are also crucial. Forward models are typically built on first principle
understanding, but many approximations are still used due to lack of knowledge or lack of computational
power. An example applicable to this study is using a 1D radiative transfer model instead of a 3D radiat-
ive transfer model. Typically forward model uncertainties are ignored, thereby assuming that the forward
model is 100% accurate. Nevertheless, this can lead to a significant underestimation of the error in the re-
trieval [19]. Adding forward model uncertainties shifts the balance between Sy and Sa to put more emphasis
on Sa.
Furthermore, not including the forward model uncertainties can lead to reduced information content re-
trieved from the measurements, as described by the degrees of freedom of the signal. The degrees of free-
dom (d) describes the amount of independent information obtained from the observations given the prior
state. If the observations have perfect information content, then d is equal to the number of observations or
model parameters [19].
3.1.3
Uncertainties related to the observations
The observations vector y is typically multidimensional as there are observations from multiple instruments
or, as in this case, observations from several channels of the same instrument. Typically both uncorrelated
and correlated errors of y exist. These are defined in the measurement covariance matrix Sy [19].

The main goal of this study is to attempt to retrieve snow depth (SD) using an optimal estimation retrieval
algorithm with SMRT as the forward model. A requirement for successful retrievals is that the forward
model can accurately replicate the observations. If we have a bias in the model caused by imperfect know-
ledge of underlying physics or likewise, then OE will never provide us with accurate results as the bias will
be transferred into a retrieval bias through the optimization process.
If the forward model (SMRT) is provided with the true state vector, e.g. with model parameters we know
to be true, the model should simulate TBs, which coincide with the observations to a degree decided by the
measurement uncertainty. However, this is the ideal case, where the measurements of the true state vector
are error-free, and the forward model is perfect. In reality, this is likely not the case.

4.5.1
Summarizing remarks
The discussion above highlights one central limitation: the scale difference between a satellite footprint and
field measurements. Measurements of salinity, temperature and roughness are only made in the four pits.
Snow depth, correlation length and density are also measured in the vicinity of the pits, taking measure-
ments up to 111 meters away from the pit sites. This means that especially salinity, temperature and rough-
ness lack information regarding spatial variability, which is a major issue, as the footprint of AMSR2 is so
large. Snow depth, correlation length and density are also limited by their spatial distribution. However, if
all these measurements are combined, then the spatial extent becomes somewhat reasonable compared to
AMSR2’s footprint. Therefore, in the following section, all measurements are combined, and only the result-
ing signal based on observations from all four sites is examined. This should hopefully cause the simulated
values to be less sensitive to small-scale variations, and better describe the area’s larger-scale properties, as
observed by the radiometer.

5.1
NASA’s Operation Icebridge
Snow and sea ice data from OIB includes SDs obtained with the CReSIS snow radar, along with SIT obtained
using a combination of laser and radar altimetry. Here it is assumed that the Ku-band radar penetrates to
the snow/ice interface, whereas the laser altimeter (UTIG LiDARs) measures the surface elevation. By
calibrating these measurements using leads (ice-free ocean segments), a geoid model and the principle of
Archimedes, these measurements can be converted into a total sea ice thickness [47].
In this study, measurements from OIB are used as reference data and treated as ”the truth”. However, OIB

From ECMWF, the ERA5 reanalysis dataset is used. This reanalysis dataset combines vast amounts of his-

The skin temperature is an estimate of the temperature of the surface of the Earth, calculated as a theoretical
temperature, which is required to satisfy the energy balance. It represents the temperature of the uppermost

6.2
Synthetic data
To do this investigation, we need a suitable dataset. Nevertheless, no larger datasets contain the amount of
information, as was available for CB. Instead, a dataset with coincident measurements of snow depth and
sea ice thickness from Operation Icebridge and brightness temperatures (TBs) from AMSR2 is used. A more
thorough description of this dataset is available in section 5
This dataset lacks measurements of snow salinity, snow/ice interface roughness, correlation length of the
snow and the ice, density measurements and snow layering information. To do optimal estimation, these
parameters must therefore be described through a distribution based on information from other sources.
Before examining the actual AMSR2 observations we examine a synthetic dataset. Examination of a syn-
thetic dataset has several advantages, with the most important one being that we know the true values of all
model input parameters; hence, allowing us to validate the retrievals. When examining the real observations
the true values of several model input parameters are unknown and even for those where we have reference
data, we cannot consider it to be the truth, as reference data is linked to uncertainties. Furthermore, when
examining synthetic data, we are sure that we can replicate the simulated observations perfectly; hence, that
no bias is present.
As an initial examination, a synthetic dataset using values from the CB dataset is created. This dataset is not
expected to be ideal, as CB is located in a narrow fjord with little variation. It is, therefore, unlikely that the
values provided from here will provide the required variation. Furthermore, it is possible that snow found
in CB can be significantly different from snow found in the Beaufort Gyre area. Even so, CB allows using
real data to make qualified guesses of unknown values.
To make the synthetic dataset, several choices are made. Firstly, it is decided to use a two-layer snow
model, as snow with higher salinity and correlation length is expected to be present at the bottom of the

for this is that including more layers increases computation time and provides more parameters, which
makes the behaviour between variables more complicated to assess. A more substantial discussion of the

As mentioned in section 3, optimal estimation is a probabilistic inversion method. This signifies that prior
probability distributions of model input parameters and observations must be defined as initial estimates
before the inversion.
The prior state covariance matrix describes the estimated covariances between input model parameters.
The diagonal make up the estimated variance of each parameter, and the off-diagonal elements provide
expected covariances between parameters. In figure 18a, an example of such a covariance matrix, which
will be used later in this study, is shown.

Figure 18a shows that the prior state covariance matrix is diagonal, which is caused by a lack of knowledge
regarding real covariances between parameters. We can compute the covariances between model paramet-
ers in the synthetic dataset, but these are not expected to coincide with real-world covariances. As the goal
is to examine actual observations, we, therefore, do most tests using only the variances, as shown in figure

For the measurement uncertainty covariance matrix (figure 18b), the AMSR2 channels are expected to be

not known and are therefore neglected.
Nevertheless, this matrix contains information regarding the uncertainty of both the actual observations
and the forward model. We have no information regarding the uncertainty of SMRT, and therefore we do
not know the variances or covariances that might arise from imperfect forward modelling. Based on this
lack of knowledge, the measurement uncertainty covariance matrix is therefore kept as a diagonal matrix.

would be the ideal case.

for variation, which ensures that the other variables must not compensate for changes in the TBs stemming
from other sources. Hence, we wish that the SD will not change due to a faulty correlation length start
guess. At the same time giving more room for variation of secondary parameters also signify that these are
more likely to compensate for changes in TB, which are actually stemming from changes in the parameter of
interest, e.g. SD. If all input parameters were uncorrelated or we knew the accurate correlations, this would
not be an issue, as the impact of each parameter could then be easily separated. However, as this is not the
case, it provides a significant limitation.

by decreasing the SIT.
Figure 22c shows that increasing the snow/ice interface roughness shifts the polarization-specific TBs to-

make incoherent components, hence, mixing the signal at the two polarizations.
Understanding the impact of changing the snow salinity, as observed in figures 23c and 23d, is not trivial.
When the salinity increases, the absorption increase due to the increase in the dielectric loss factor. This, in
turn, causes the penetration depth to decrease, as is shown in figure 24b. Intuitively this should cause the
TBs to decrease, as a larger portion of the radiation measured by the radiometer will stem from the colder
snow than from the warmer snow/ice interface. However, this temperature difference is expected to be
relatively small.
The saline snow layer is above the most significant reflection interface, which is the snow/ice interface. Fig-
ure 24a shows that the reflected part is much more extensive for horizontal polarization than for vertical,
which is why the effect of having more emission from above the snow/ice interface affects horizontal po-
larization to a much higher degree. Effectively, this is expected to result in a larger fraction of the radiation
measured by the radiometer being from the snow/ice system than from the cold atmosphere; as a result
increasing the simulated TBs. This analysis also fits with observations in figure 23d. Here we observe that
changes in salinity impact the TBs most while the salinity value is low, which fits with the impact of the
salinity on the penetration depth being much more prominent for low salinities. The smaller impact of the
salinity in the top layer (figure 23c) compared to the bottom layer (figure 23d) is expected to be due to the
fact that the default salinity of the bottom snow layer is 8.67 PSU. This signifies that a more significant part
of the emission already stems from above the snow/ice interface as a default, and therefore the impact will
be less significant.

The majority of the emission from the snow/ice system stems from the sea ice. However, the amount of
this radiation transmitted across the interface between the sea ice and the snow depends on the relationship
between their permittivities. The permittivity of snow is mainly determined by the density, as the density
decides the mixing ratio between the permittivity of air and the permittivity of ice (see section 2.1). Hence,
when the density increases, the permittivity of the snow comes closer to that of the ice.

tion 13. Similarly, the angle of refraction can be obtained from Snells Law (see equation 15). Examination of
these two formulas shows that more of the incoming radiation is reflected when the permittivity difference
is large. Furthermore, the amount which is reflected is polarization specific, with horizontal polarization
being more sensitive than vertical.

7.2
Omitting parameters or considering them known
Our main goal is not to examine the average parameters but to retrieve individual SDs and possibly SITs.
Hence, we attempt to estimate SDs and SITs based on individual TBs but use average values as start guesses.
The covariance matrices are consistent with those used for the average TB analysis performed above.
The results obtained when doing this are available in appendix C.2.1. However, due to the correlations
between model input parameters, as evidenced from tables 10 and 9 this retrieval does not perform well.
The retrievals of both SD and SIT are biased towards their start guesses. Nevertheless, much unwanted
variation also occurs when the start guess is correct.
Therefore, we aim to test whether some additional parameters can be omitted or considered known. We
have decent surface temperature estimates from the reanalysis data ERA5 [48], which are available hourly, in
a gridded product with resolution 0.25◦× 0.25◦; hence, this is readily available data which can be considered
known. As evidenced by the sensitivity studies omitting other parameters when using both TBs at vertical
and horizontal polarization will likely result in some errors. As the primary goal of this study is to obtain
as accurate as possible estimates of SD and possibly SIT, we test the impact on the retrievals of these two if
we omit parameters.
It should be noted that this analysis uses logarithmic units for SD and snow correlation length. This is done
as SD exhibits a slightly more normally distributed behaviour when using logarithmic units. As normally
distributed variables are a condition for OE, it is, therefore, better to use. However, the improvement is only
minimal, and the primary reason for using logarithmic units is that it constrains OE from trying to optimize
by finding unphysical negative SDs and correlation lengths. This signifies that the optimization is obtained
faster, as OE does not examine these unphysical parts of model space. In appendix C.5, it is shown that
using logarithmic units shows little impact on the retrievals. Here is also shown that SDs are slightly more
normally distributed when using logarithmic units.

8.0.1
SMRT setup
Deciding on a setup is no trivial task, as we have no information from our data regarding the necessary
amount of layers. Furthermore, it must be considered that a more complicated setup signifies that computa-
tional time increase and that it gets more cumbersome to understand the processes happening in the SMRT
model.
As mentioned in section 2.1 it is highly likely that a depth hoar layer will be present in the bottom of the
snowpack, which was created during the early winter. Furthermore, observations from other sources [24]
and from CB have shown that the salinity in the snow is likely located at the bottom of the snowpack. This
fit with the expected mechanism leading to saline snow, which is brine rejection into the snow during sea
ice formation. Studies have shown that depth hoar layers in FYI can vary significantly, but that an average
value could be around 25% [26]. The top part of the snowpack is hard to quantify, as it depends on the
amount of time since the last snowfall and the amount of re-distribution by the wind. Yet, it has been shown
that wind-distributed layers of rounded or faceted crystals make up a large part of the snowpack on FYI.
The new-fallen snow will have low density and small grains and will therefore be practically transparent
for the radiation at frequencies used in this study. Even so, a low-density snow layer could greatly impact
the heat transfer, as empirical relationships have shown that a low-density snow layer is linked to low
thermal conductivity. Hence, low-density snow layers effectively protect the sea ice against fast variations
of the atmosphere temperature [28]. In terms of modelling snow on sea ice, a lower thermal conductivity
of the snow leads to a gentler slope, signifying that if the remainder of model parameters are kept constant,
then the snow/ice interface temperature gets shifted towards the warmer ocean (this can be seen in the
sensitivity study in appendix D.2). This signifies that if such a snow layer is present, the retrieved SD could
be overestimated compared to the reference SD from OIB. Nevertheless, the strong winds in the Arctic cause
such new snow layers to be short-lived.

8.0.2
Sensitivity study
For some variables, the sensitivity study in the new setup mimics the behaviour observed for the synthetic
dataset. An increase in salinity causes the simulated TB at horizontal polarization to increase, whereas the
vertical remains almost unchanged. Similarly, the snow/ice interface roughness causes increased mixing
and changes in the surface temperature shift the simulated TBs up or down. Figures showing this are
available from appendix D.2.
For sea ice thickness, we observe that the simulated TBs increase when the SIT decrease, which fits with the
expectation. Even so, the increase is much more significant for higher frequencies when the sea ice becomes
sufficiently thin. This is likely linked to two factors. Firstly, the penetration depth decreases with frequency,
signifying that a larger part of the emitted radiation will stem from within the snowpack. As this is above
the snow/ice interface, less of the emitted radiation will be reflected at the snow/ice interface. Hence, a
Optimal estimation of snow and ice parameters from microwave remote sensing
65
==PAGE:75==
larger part of the radiation measured by the radiometer will stem from the snow/ice system compared to
the contribution from the cold atmosphere.
Furthermore, the SIT has the most significant impact on the snow/ice interface temperature when the sea
ice is thin (see figure 67a in appendix D.3). Hereby the temperature of the snow layers will differ more from
the snow/ice interface temperature (hence the sea ice temperature) when the SIT is low.
Changing the density of the bottom snow layer leads to a massive impact on the simulated TBs at horizontal
polarization, as we observed for the synthetic dataset too (see figures 34d and 23f). Yet, the simulated TBs
at vertical polarization seem to be impacted to a more significant degree than for the synthetic dataset. This
is likely linked to the setup having a larger snow/ice interface roughness value, which signifies that more
of the polarization-specific signals are mixed.
The impact of the snow correlation length on the simulated TBs is much larger in the new setup compared
to the old one (see figures 34c and 23a). This is expected to be linked to how the correlation lengths are
defined here, where changing the top value automatically changes the bottom value too. Hence the impact
of changing either is linked to a change in all layers. Furthermore, default correlation lengths are much
higher for this setup.
The most interesting change in sensitivity is observed for the snow depth. Here we observe a very signi-
ficant change in sensitivity caused by the change in setup (see figure 34a versus figure 22a). This change is
expected to be highly linked to the fact that the default value of correlation length is much higher for the
new setup when compared to the old. A higher correlation length means that more radiation is scattered
within the snowpack, which mainly affects the higher frequencies. Hence, two competing processes hap-
pen when the snow depth change. Firstly, a snow depth increase causes an increase in the TBs based on the
impact on the temperature profile. At the same time, a higher snow depth results in more volume scattering
in the snowpack. This is expected to be the reason for the behaviour observed in the snow depth sensitivity
study in figure 34a.

OE Results when using real data
Apart from updating our starting guesses, we must also update our input covariance matrices when using
real observations. Firstly, as previously mentioned, the variances used for snow salinity, density and cor-
relation length in the examination of the synthetic dataset are expected to be too low. In real observations,
we expect much more variation than the variances present in the synthetic dataset; hence, as an alternat-
ive estimate, we use the variances of the original CB dataset. These are still expected to be too low due
to the location of the test side in a fjord, but they do show larger variation and provide a more realistic
guess. Ideally, we would have a larger dataset from Arctic snow-covered sea ice with all necessary input
parameters. However, as we do not have this, using the information from CB provides a decent estimate.
The measurement uncertainty matrix, which was used for the analysis in the synthetic dataset, also needs
revision. This uncertainty matrix provided a best estimate using only the assumed uncertainty of the obser-
vations themselves. In reality, we do expect some uncertainty from the model, but we do not know to what
extent. What we do observe, though, is that the horizontally polarized simulated TBs are much more sens-
itive to model input parameters, especially to those which are poorly known. This was shown both through
sensitivity studies and the CB test case, where we observed that the difference between simulated and ob-

Outlook and comparison to other algorithms
To put the results obtained in this study into context, we make a comparison to other PMWR based retriev-
als. Here we do a direct comparison to classical GR based empirical models from Rotosky et al. [15] and
Cavalleri et al. [13], along with a comparison with the multilinear regression model proposed by Kilic et
al. [14]. Furthermore, we compare to deep learning based models from Folkman et al. [17] and Dong et al.
[18] but this is done qualitatively as these models are more cumbersome to apply to the dataset used in this
study. Lastly, a brief discussion of deep learning based SIT retrievals are made.
Folkman et al. [17] obtained high R2 (0.7) and R (0.9) values when comparing to OIB data by using a relat-
ively simple feed-forward neural network (NN) with four hidden layers, which was trained using AMSR2
and Soil Moisture and Ocean Salinity (SMOS) data. In Dong et al. [18], a more complicated deep learn-
ing algorithm was created, which also outperformed the retrievals it compared to, including the modified
version of the Warren climatology [6] and the NN by Folkman et al.
However, these deep learning approaches examine a mix of FYI and MYI, which is expected to be why
they perform so well. The negative emissivity gradient with frequency seen for MYI (see figure 40 from the
theory section), is similar to that resulting from increasing the snow depth. Therefore, it is expected that the
inclusion of MYI mainly causes the good performance of the deep learning approaches.
To test this hypothesis we do an initial performance comparison where we use the same empirical al-
gorithms by Cavalleiri, Rostosky and Killic [13, 15, 14] that Folkmann et al. compared with and test how
well they do on the dataset used in this study. Table 15 shows the comparison’s performance measures and
in figure 39 the retrievals are shown. Here it is seen that both the methods by Rostosky et al. and Kilic et al.
fail to capture any significant trend. Cavallieri et al. performs better but the spread in the retrieved values
is very large, which is also reflected in the RMSE being larger than the standard deviation of the input data.
In Folkmann et al., the empirical algorithms performed reasonably well, obtaining correlation coefficients
between 0.7 and 0.9, and Rostosky et al. obtained an R2 value of 0.58. However, these values are far from
those seen in table 15, heavily indicating that the deep learning algorithms would perform significantly
worse on only FYI.
It should be noted that although [17] used the same dataset, they validated their model on only data from
2015. In appendix, figure 75, it is shown that the correlations look very different if only examining data from
2015. However, very little data over FYI is available for this year and examining only one year provides little
testing of robustness.

The slight sensitivity, combined with the measurement uncertainty, makes the impact of SIT negligible.
However, studies predicting SIT from satellite PMR radiometer using deep learning approaches have been

The results obtained in this study highlight that Sea Ice Thickness is highly correlated with several para-

the results we obtain are caused by a direct sensitivity of the observations to the variable of interest or if the
result is due to correlations to other parameters.

Discussion
Results obtained in this study have shown that if we have accurate prior information, there is good potential
for retrieving SD using OE with SMRT as forward model. However, several things should be examined
further to fully unlock the potential. Furthermore, obtaining accurate results when using actual observations
requires additional measurements of model input parameters obtained either from field measurements or
from reliable reanalysis data. In this section, we examine what could have been done differently to improve
the results and what information from other parts it would require.
10.1
Creating a more relevant synthetic dataset
Examining a synthetic dataset is a natural first step of a retrieval process. It provides a baseline for the
retrieval’s behaviour when not considering ”real-world” problems, such as biases and faulty priors. How-
ever, to obtain the maximum potential from the analysis of the synthetic dataset, it ought to replicate the
observations as well as possible.
However, the synthetic dataset used in this study produced simulated TBs, which differed so much from
the shape of the actual AMSR2 observations that the covariances and average values of the synthetic dataset
were of little use. As a result, much work had to be redone when examining the real observations.
Therefore, using theoretical and empirical relationships to create a synthetic dataset with linked model input
parameters could be advantageous. This would ensure that covariances were available, and the synthetic
dataset could be tuned to ensure that it produced simulated TBs which were distributed around the mean
of the actual observations, and with a spread that allowed them to reproduce the spread of the actual obser-
vations.
Furthermore, we could have ensured that the parameters were normally distributed, which would help us
avoid unwanted errors resulting from this. Comparing a dataset with normally and not-normally distrib-
uted parameters could help us assess to what extent the retrievals differ if this core assumption of OE is not
fulfilled.
According to [19], the error of having non-normally distributed parameters is only significant if the inform-
ation content from the observations is limited. This, however, is the case for several SMRT model input
parameters, and therefore it is expected that the lack of normal distribution of model parameters could cre-
ate significant errors. The examination in section 7.2 supports this expectation, as it was shown that the
retrieval of salinity was biased towards a normal distribution, although the true distribution was far from
normal.
Nevertheless, It should be noted that the issues with non-uniqueness imply that even if we create a synthetic
dataset that can replicate the observations, this does not imply that the covariances will be correct.

be parameterised using three layers. Furthermore, we have assumed that it is feasible to examine several
years of data simultaneously. On top of this, it is assumed that the defined prior describes the true variation
and that the bias correction found in section 8.0.3 is accurate for the data. However, all these assumptions
could be faulty. Non-uniqueness is a significant issue; therefore, it is highly possible that other combinations
of model parameters could be more accurate than those found here (see, e.g. 8.0.3). Furthermore, using a
three-layer setup is possibly too simple to describe the complex snowpack.
Therefore, more time should be spent examining how the snowpack is best constructed. Firstly, the shallow
penetration depth into the saline snow could signify that having more layers and, hereby, a more continu-
ous temperature profile would be more accurate. Using more layers could also involve using theoretical
and empirical relationships to compute profiles of the snow parameters, as was suggested for creating the
synthetic dataset too. This would provide a physical basis for linking more parameters, which due to the
limited information content, as defined by the few degrees of freedom, could help solve the issue of non-
uniqueness.
However, using more layers also comes with the cost of a higher computation time. Therefore, the goal is to
describe the snowpack accurately but using a setup that is as simple as possible.
10.3
What data do we require to obtain accurate results?
Section 7.5 showed that if we know the true covariances, there is a great potential for estimating snow
depth. Hereby, a central limitation to improving the results is to improve our priors. However, this is
no trivial task. Currently, no larger-scale datasets are available with coinciding measurements of SD, SIT,
snow correlation length and snow/ice interface roughness, which we require for examining TBs at only
vertical polarisation. For examining observations at both polarisations, we require coinciding snow salinity
and density measurements too. On top of this, obtaining such datasets would be cumbersome as snow
correlation length, salinity and density, and snow/ice interface roughness currently cannot be accurately
measured remotely.
As mentioned previously, priors could be created based on theoretical and empirical relationships, possibly
in a data assimilation setup, where model parameters are constrained by observations. However, basing co-
variances on theoretical relationships comes with some concerns. Firstly, if the theoretical relationships are
incorrect or inapplicable for the specific situation, then significant errors and faulty correlations could result
from it. The non-uniqueness issue signifies that even though we replicate the observations, the combination
of parameters could be erroneous, hereby leading to faulty parameter links.
Therefore, it would be ideal to base prior covariances on a combination of real measurements and modelling
results. Below is a suggestion as to how a dataset of significant size could be created, which would provide
the required covariances for examining TBs at vertical polarisation, as shown in section 7.2.
During the MOSAIC campaign Dr. Martin Schneebeli created a larger dataset of snow micropen meas-
urements, that will soon be published (this information is obtained from communication with Dr. Martin
Schneebeli). From snow micropen measurements snow correlation lengths can be obtained [26]. As the
measurements were obtained with a snow micropen, they will provide correlations with, as a minimum,

snow depth and snow density. No correlations with sea ice thickness will be available. Nevertheless, the
correlation with SD is expected to be more important as the main processes altering the snow correlation
length are snow metamorphism, and wind re-distribution [23]. The temperature gradient in the snow pack
is more influenced by the SD than by the SIT (see appendix D.3) and therefore the SD is expected to have a
larger impact.
Radar measurements at, especially C-band, are highly linked to the roughness of the measured surface[53],
which for sea ice ideally is the snow/ice interface. These measurements could be used to estimate the
snow/ice interface roughness used in this study. As the roughness value is based on the interface sur-
face root mean square (RMS) and correlation length [40], it is possibly not straightforward to translate the
backscatter to this. However, studies have been performed aiming to quantify sea ice RMS from SAR and
imaging spectrometer measurements [54]. Nevertheless, the backscatter measurements should be translated
to a 25x25 km snow/ice interface roughness value, and it is not clear how exactly small-scale roughness is
translated to larger scales. Ideally, the part of SMRT created to model active MR could be used as forward
model to do OE on radar observations and, from this, obtain a snow/ice interface roughness parameter,
which could then be used in the passive part. This would provide a great advantage as several satellite
radar measurements are readily available, and these could be co-located with other relevant measurements.
If the above measurements were combined and co-located with suitable snow, sea ice and surface temperat-
ure datasets, then it is possible that an observation-based prior dataset with great potential could be created.
As mentioned in this study, either the skin temperature or the 2 m above ground temperature from ECMWF
[48] are good candidates for surface temperature estimates.
OIB is probably the best suited snow depth and sea ice dataset. However, it cannot be expected to be applic-
able for periods other than March and April, and as will be explained in the subsequent section, it is linked
to its own uncertainties. Alternatively, snow accumulation models such as NESOSIM or SnowmodelLG
[9, 10] could provide first guesses of snow depth, and ice growth models such as The Los Alamos sea ice
model (CICE) [55] could provide sea ice thickness data. This would provide data that is applicable for the
whole winter and for the whole arctic. It would also be possible to use sea ice and snow depth products
which are based on differences in laser and radar freeboards, such as from Cryo2Ice, which is a snow depth
and sea ice product based on overflights of IceSat2 and CryoSat2 [50]. However, it has been shown that
the radar cannot be expected to penetrate to the snow/ice interface as SD, density and snow/ice roughness
influence the radar penetration, and therefore this dataset is likely biased to show shallower snow depths
than the truth [56].
10.4
The accuracy of our reference data
Data from OIB are often used as reference data for comparison and validation [17, 15, 14, 18]. However,
this data is linked to its own uncertainties. King et al. [5] compared OIB quicklook data over level and
deformed FYI with in-situ measurements. They found a mean bias of −4.5 cm over level FYI and −6.6 cm
over deformed FYI. In this study, the majority of data is quicklook data, as the IDCSI4 product was only
available until 2013. At Beaufort Gyre, the majority of the FYI is expected to be deformed due to the strong
Optimal estimation of snow and ice parameters from microwave remote sensing

==PAGE:93==
ocean currents [28]. This underlines that the data from OIB cannot be considered the truth and should only
be considered reference data.
10.4.1
Issues with the spatial scale
Another relevant issue, which was mainly discussed with regard to the CB dataset, is the issue with spatial
scale. Although several OIB measurements make up individual grid points, the satellite footprint variation
is not guaranteed to be accounted for. SD measurements from OIB are also linked to their own uncertainties,
and the same is true for the SIT and surface temperatures.
These two issues highlight that the results obtained in this study should be considered with a certain level
of scepticism. It is far from guaranteed that our reference data is fault-free, and it cannot be guaranteed
that it is representative of the satellite footprint. Hereby, the retrievals can be more or less valid than the
performance metrics account for. To quantify the uncertainty, it would be appropriate to filter the data
to ensure that only data points with a certain amount of OIB measurements are included. Furthermore,
the uncertainty of a given data point could be used for comparison. Here, it would be most appropriate
to combine the measurement uncertainty and the standard deviation, as the standard deviation points us
toward the variability within the grid segment.
10.5
Improving the measurement uncertainty covariance matrix
Previously, we have focussed on discussing how the prior state covariance matrix can be improved. Nev-
ertheless, it is also essential to improve the measurement uncertainty covariance matrix. In this study,
off-diagonal elements were ignored, but this is expected to be inaccurate. Correlated errors caused by, for
example, using the same receiver, subject to common amplifier elements, are expected to be present. How-
ever, determining correlated errors is typically much harder, but it can be done [19]. Model uncertainties
should ideally also be included in the prior uncertainty covariance matrix. These are more complicated to
obtain, but they are not expected to be zero.

12
Conclusion
In this study, we have examined retrieving snow depth from satellite PMR observations from AMSR2 using
optimal estimation. The radiative transfer model SMRT was used as forward model, and reference data were
obtained from the Round Robin Data Package using NASA’s Operation IceBridge Data from 2013-2019.
As a test case, a dataset from Cambridge Bay was analysed in which coinciding measurements of all model
input parameters to SMRT were available. Analysis of the dataset provided a baseline for the performance of
SMRT. It showed that even when all model input parameters were known, significant discrepancies existed,
which are mainly expected to be due to spatial scale differences.
Our analysis of a synthetically created dataset showed the importance of knowing the covariances between
model input parameters. We found that the retrievals suffer from non-uniqueness and significant correla-
tions. This biases retrievals towards the start guesses if only variances are included in the prior state covari-
ance matrix. Nevertheless, it was found that the potential for retrieving snow depth is very promising if we
have accurate covariances between model parameters.
Furthermore, a highly limited sensitivity to SIT was found, which shows that retrieving SIT from PMR
observations is unlikely. However, the high correlations between retrievals signify that SIT could possibly
be estimated as a secondary parameter.
Our analysis of observations from AMSR2 highlighted challenges linked to ”real-world” problems. This
involved not knowing the prior state and uncertainty covariances correctly, lacking information about the
correct setup and issues regarding whether reference data can be considered the truth. The examination
showed that correctly defining uncertainties and priors is crucial. The retrieval suffers from non-uniqueness
and high inter-correlations; hence, there are generally several retrieval options that will lead to the same
outcome. On top of this, the high sensitivity of simulated TBs to poorly understood model input parameters
complicates the retrieval process.
The best-performing retrieval of snow depth was obtained using only model input parameter variances in
the prior state covariance matrix. This resulted in a correlation coefficient of 0.72, a coefficient of determ-
ination of 0.50 and a root mean square error of 0.03 m. This is a reasonable result, with a performance
comparable to other retrieval algorithms over FYI. It is expected that the reason for the best performance
being when not including covariances, is linked to a lack of knowledge of the true covariances. Hence, the
retrieval was given incorrect covariance information leading to incorrect retrievals.
Nevertheless, as was shown when examining the synthetic dataset, the potential, when accurately defining
the model input covariances, is much higher than when not including them. Therefore, a crucial next step
is to obtain a large-scale dataset with coinciding values of all the necessary model input parameters. If
examining only TBs at vertical polarisation, this analysis showed that having coinciding measurements of
sea ice thickness, snow depth, snow/ice interface roughness, surface temperature, and snow correlation
length should be sufficient. Hence, measurements of snow salinity and density are not required.

A.1
Relevant characteristics of sea ice
This section is previously evaluated work done in a special course with Leif Toudal in 2021 [35]
This section explains in further detail the characteristics of FYI and MYI.
A.1.1
Ice formation and FYI characteristics
During sea ice growth lumps of salty water called brine are trapped within the ice floe. Once the sea ice
reaches a thickness of approximately 0.3 m first year ice (FYI) is formed [21]. The incorporation of salt and
air into the ice floe are extremely important with regard to remote sensing. Both the permittivity and volume
and surface scattering are heavily dependent on the distribution of liquid brine and air within the ice. The
amount of brine within the ice floe is determined by the requirements of phase equilibrium. This means
that changes in the temperature will be accompanied by changes in the size of the brine pockets resulting
from freezing or melting.
Because the most rapid freezing occurs at the top of the ice floe it can be assumed that the highest salinities
will be found here. Yet, it must be noted that brine draininge starts immediately after the entrapment. [57]
A.1.2
Desalination
Sea ice undergoes radical changes during the melt season, with the most obvious one being a large decrease
in salinity and increase in porosity. This desalination happens mainly due to an increase in temperature
throughout the ice. As the largest change in temperature occur at the surface, the largest desalination is also
seen here. As the temperature increases ice melts causing the brine inclusions to contain more water. This
in turn increase the volume and cause brine pockets to form into vertical drainage channels, which causes
gravitational drainage to occur faster.
In the Arctic this process is further enhanced due to the melt of snow during the summer. The summer melt
causes the ice to be flushed with freshwater, as the meltwater filter through the top ice layers. The summer
melt can furthermore create meltponds[57].
This desalination process causes MYI to be remarkably different from FYI. MYI is known for having higher
air concentrations, especially in the upper layers. Furthermore, the salinity of MYI is lower than FYI, though
the differences between the two are most pronounced in the top layers of the ice. Apart from this, the surface
layer of the MYI is also very distinct. It is rougher with a hummock like structure and great inhomogeneity
throughout ice floes[57]. Furthermore it is known to have thicker snow layers than the FYI, as snow can
accumulate on MYI throughout the entire freezing season. Figure 40 shows a model of MYI versus FYI with
their differences in surface roughness, snow depth, air content and salinity highlighted.

A.1.3
FYI and MYI depth profiles
Both FYI and MYI characteristics also have a strong depth dependency.
In winter when the atmosphere is colder than the ocean, the temperature in snow and sea ice increase with
depth. Studies have shown that the temperature profile is largely linear within the snow and the ice. The
temperature gradients are determined by the SD and SIT along with the temperatures of the air, the ocean
water and the snow/ice interface [28]. Calculations of the snow/ice interface temperature are shown in
section 2.7.
The summer melt causes MYI to have a high porosity especially in the upper layers, which are typically the
ones that has lasted through summer melt. The lower layers of MYI typically have lower porosities. MYI
tend to have a constant salinity profile with low salinities around 0ppt to 2ppt [28].
FYI contain few air bubbles and the porosity is generally low. A constant porosity profile is expected for
FYI, though slightly higher porosity is likely for the top layers of FYI.
FYI has a varying salinity profile, that has some degree of c-shape. The shape is more pronounced for
thinner FYI. FYI is characterised by high salinities close to the snow/ice interface and close to the ocean/ice
interface [28].
The bulk salinity can vary greatly from around 25PSU for thin ice (< 5cm thick), but the salinity decrease
rapidly and FYI typically have a bulk salinity around 2-4PSU for ice more than 150cm in the end of the
winter. Yet, the emitting layer where measurements are obtained will be located close to the snow/ice
interface for FYI and this layer is therefore likely to have a higher salinity.
Salinities in FYI can vary from around 20PSU in the top of young sea ice down to approximately 2PSU in the
bottom. Yet, The salinity depend heavily upon the age of the ice with older sea ice typically having lower
salinties [28]

D.1
Ice concentration filtration and separation of FYI and MYI
This section is previously evaluated work done in a special course with Leif Toudal in 2021 [35].
It explains how the separation of FYI and MYI is done, using both the Gradient Ratio, The backscatter and
the variability of the OIB datapoint.
Removing the possible impact by water in the brightness temperatures are crucial due to the large emissivity
differences between ice and water. The filtration is performed by using sea ice concentrations from ECMWF.
All datapoints with ice concentration less than 90% is removed. This threshold is chosen to assure that water
has a minimal impact on the measured TBs.
To separate FYI and MYI a number of criteria are used.
Firstly data linked to 10% largest standard deviations of SIT is excluded, as these symbolize that large
variability is present within the footprint and that several different ice types or ice characteristics might be
present. In this study that is unwanted, as all ice within one category should preferably be similar, in order
to be able to obtain an average value of the unknown variables, which will fit well with all measurements
within the category. Furthermore, the SMRT model can only model either FYI or MYI. Hereby it is important
to try and exclude measurements that could contain both ice types.
On top of this, SIT limits of below and above 2m are used to separate FYI and MYI respectively. This
separation is used as it is expected that sea ice grow less than 2 m within one winter.
Other separation criteria uses the so-called gradient ratio (GR) and backscatter values. The gradient ratio
(GR) separation uses the knowledge that more scattering occur within ice at higher frequencies, especially
when looking at MYI due to the high porosity. For FYI this tendency is almost non-existing within the
microwave range. The gradient ratio measures emissivity changes with frequency and is dependent upon
the polarization. Calculation of the GR(36.5/18.7)V is as follows:
GR(36.5/18.7)V = TB(36.5GHz)V −TB(18.7GHz)V
TB(36.5GHz)V + TB(18.7GHz)V
(32)
