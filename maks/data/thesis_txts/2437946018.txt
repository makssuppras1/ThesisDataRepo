ABSTRACT
Various methods to estimate battery degradation have been documented on the hypothesis
that “PFR frequency regulation method permits deadband of 20 mHz, enhancing battery
life vis-à-vis FNR method of frequency regulation operations.” State of Health (SoH) is a
crucial metric to determine the useful/productive life of a battery pack which undergoes
degradation due to frequency regulation services such as FNR and PFR. This has a direct
impact on the operational cost for service providers dependent on battery storage systems
that maintain grid frequency.
Analysis of SoH for a grid-connected battery is the central theme of our thesis.
A
real-time scenario of the grid-connected battery installed in Nordhavn (460 kWh/630 kW
capacity) and the frequency regulation method such as FNR based on the DK1 energy
market and PFR based on the DK2 market in Denmark have been analyzed. We created a
scaled model using a battery with a capacity of 288 Wh, emulating this context.
We adopted two capacity testing methods along with an existing mathematical degradation
model to validate the hypothesis-
1. Normal capacity test
2. Sequence capacity test, a new proposed testing method to estimate capacity in shorter
time duration with 99% accuracy of the normal capacity test.
Besides, a Battery Management System (BMS) to estimate State of Charge (SoC) of the
battery was developed using coulomb counting technique.
The proposed sequence capacity test achieved promising results in determining the SoH
of the battery, and this test could be used in the future SoH analyses. Based on the
hypothesis, the FNR was expected to contribute higher degradation compared to PFR, but
the test results gave the opposite results. Two weeks of FNR operation showed degradation
of 0.77% whereas it was 1.8% degradation after two weeks of PFR. The reasons for this is
inferred to be caused due to cell imbalance occurrence. The BMS developed in this thesis
could not include the cell balancing method as this was not possible with the given test
battery. Thus, it was concluded that cell balancing forms a critical part in SoH analysis.
Extending these results to the Nordhavn battery, the lifetime of Nordhavn battery is
expected to serve 5.77 years of FNR and only 2.56 years of PFR.
Based on results of a mathematical degradation model the Nordhavn battery degraded
faster-performing FNR compared to PFR. The lifetime of grid-connected battery per-
forming FNR and PFR was determined to be 3.7 years and 11.16 years respectively. An
economic analysis based on the measured degradation showed that batteries look feasible
with a revenue of 7.68 €/h and an operational cost of 7.07 €/h.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
The transformation from an environmentally hazardous and carbon-dissipating energy
sector to more green climate-friendly sector is quite challenging.
Batteries are often
considered a part of the solutions to accommodate this transformation. Batteries have,
because of their decrease in cost, in the last decade experienced signiﬁcant interest in
grid-connected scenarios when it comes to stabilizing a power grid based on ﬂuctuating
energy sources such as wind and sun. This thesis presents a state of health (SoH) analysis
of how grid-connected battery degrades when performing frequency regulation, which is
one of the critical ancillary services when stabilizing a power grid. The SoH is an indicator
to quantify the aging and degradation in the battery. Battery degradation becomes an
important parameter when estimating the cost of operation and to calculate how to make
the best use of battery energy storage systems (BESS) and thereby to determine whether
or not a grid-connected battery is a feasible solution for a speciﬁc service.
1.2
Formulation of the problem
As shortly presented green energy sectors face challenges when it comes to keeping a stable
grid, because of non-controllable energy sources. Batteries are controllable units that
can act as both power loads and power sources, which make them suitable for providing
ancillary services to a grid. Batteries are easy to operate and can be deployed on a large
scale within a reasonable time frame [10], but the cost of batteries is one factor that
slows down the continued investment of grid-connected batteries worldwide. With a very
high investment cost, it is of great interest to keep the batteries operating for as many
years as possible and hence of great interest to have precise calculations of a battery’s
lifetime, when delivering various services. This thesis investigates how a battery is aﬀected
while providing frequency regulation in two diﬀerent frequency markets, DK1 and DK2 in
Denmark.
Based on an experimental test, this thesis aims to measure the battery degradation caused
by two frequency regulation methods such as FNR and PFR and thereby calculate the
cost of operation. Also, the number of full battery cycles occurred during the test are
calculated. With a cycle estimation, the results from this thesis can be used in other cases
and on real case battery installations.
Based on the results from the experiments carried out in PowerLab DK, the lifetime of
a real case scenario that of the grid-connected battery at Nordhavn in Copenhagen, is
analyzed.
The thesis enlightens and clariﬁes the following questions:
• How does frequency regulation aﬀect the degradation of batteries?

1.3
Battery technology and applications
Figure 1.1: Battery Applications [1]
A lot of diﬀerent battery technologies are available in the market. In the present day,
lithium batteries are considered more eﬀective than the other available technologies due to
its various merits that would be discussed in detail in this thesis. The battery capability

1.5.1
Battery degradation and methodologies for estimation
The quest for this began by knowing the causes behind the degradation of the Li-ion
battery. Degradation of the battery takes place when the combination of various stress
factors such as current severity (C-rates), operating temperature, state of charge (SoC),
depth of discharge (DoD), overcharge, undercharge, idle condition takes place [3, 11]. It
3
==PAGE:18==
1. Introduction
was observed that the performance behaviour of grid-connected battery model had been
discussed extensively over the past few years. Modelling of performance, battery aging, and
degradation issues have also attracted attention in recent studies. Estimating SoH is one of
the critical parameters of a battery and for this SoC needs to be estimated. Determining
the SoC is complicated, but there exist various methods available such as using OCV
method, coulomb counting method, and fuzzy logic method[12, 13, 14]. To determine
exact degradation is not straightforward, complicated and not wholly understood yet[15].
As mentioned earlier, SoH indicates the aging and degradation of the battery[11]. SoH
is determined by calculating the capacity fade and power fade. Capacity fading is the
reduction in the original capacity of a battery, while power fading is the increase in a
battery’s internal resistance. The causes of capacity fading is a result of cyclic aging and
calendar aging. Calendar aging occurs when the battery is idle without any operation,
and the cyclic aging occurs when the battery is in operation. The rate of calendar aging
doubles with an increase in temperature by 10 ◦C.[13] The SoH is then calculated to know
the End of Life (EOL).
The degradation of the battery is an extensive topic and can be addressed in diﬀer-
ent ways. It can be determined using diﬀerent electrical battery models[16] or thermal
battery models or electro-chemical battery models[6, 17] or a combination of the models.
Paper [11] uses all the three models to predict the SoH of the battery. According to
most studies, degradation analysis is based on capacity fade instead of power fade as
the EOL is clearly deﬁned as a function of capacity fading. The EOL occurs when the
capacity reduces to 80% of its original capacity[2, 5, 18].[19] uses power fading to determine
the SoH. It deﬁnes the EOL as a function of power fade, and EOL occurs when the
power capacity reduces to 60% of its original power capacity. Contrarily, [5], mentions
40% as the EOL concerning capacity reduction. Uncertainty in power fading level might
be a reason why most of the studies use capacity fading for degradation analysis, as
EOL, in this case, is well deﬁned. Diﬀerent studies use a diﬀerent combination of stress
factors to estimate SoH. Rizzoni et al.[11] perform SoH estimation as a function of the
charge/discharge current, battery voltage, SoC and operating temperature. [19], does not
use SoC for estimation, whereas [3] only uses SoC as a function of DoD to calculate the SoH.
In most studies [11, 17, 20, 21, 22, 23?
], where SoH is experimentally determined,
use simple capacity tests on the battery to determine SoH. The battery is made to com-
pletely charge/discharge with a very low C-rate and determines the charge throughput.
Again, there is no particular standard to perform the capacity test with a standard C-rate.
The capacity test is repeated after some cycles to measure the loss in the charge through-
put. The same technique has been implemented in this thesis to estimate capacity fading.
Diﬀerent papers use diﬀerent C-rates such as C/25, C/5, C/3 to perform the capacity test.
Based on the manufacturer’s test report [21], this thesis uses the same C/5 rate to perform
the capacity test on the test battery. The other methods of capacity estimation are listed
and described by Waag et al.[12].
The SoH of the battery, when determined using an electrochemical method, estimates the
capacity fading on a chemical level. The primary reason for capacity fading in chemical
level is due to the following: 1. Loss of accessible active material (LAAM) and 2. Shift

FREQUENCY REGULATION
Power grids are facing a dramatic change from traditional power plants such as centralized
coal plants to more decentralized power production from various renewable sources such
as wind, biomass and solar. This transformation challenges some of the fundamental
aspects of a power grid because the behavior of these new energy sources is much diﬀerent
from the conventional power plants. The new era of power production depends more on
non-controllable energy sources, such as weather conditions. This dependency on weather
conditions makes their power production ﬂuctuating and challenging to control. It is
known that the power production must be equal to the power demand, making it very
important to have controllability of either production or consumption because a mismatch
will introduce deviations in the system frequency. Deviation in system frequency is one
of the challenges that an eco-friendly power system face and this thesis look into how
batteries can help to restore system frequency and how battery performance is aﬀected by
contributing to this frequency stabilization.
3.1
System frequency
Frequency along with voltage levels are the key indicators when analyzing a power grid.
The frequency must stay within a certain level near the nominal frequency, often 50 Hz
(EU) or 60 Hz (USA). The frequency depends on the balance between power production
and power demands; this is a result of Newton’s law of motion.

them to react on the system frequency making them a possible solution for stabilizing
the frequency in green power grids. How units, among these batteries, are supposed to
react on the frequency, depends on the frequency market in the area of which they are

3.3
Frequency regulation by batteries
This section introduces some of the pros and cons that batteries have as a unit-performing
frequency regulation. Batteries have an excellent response time and can react within
seconds. It can meet the demand almost simultaneously, and it instantaneously follows the
demand needed for the frequency variations [30, 38, 39]. Batteries are also relatively easy
to install and can be placed in areas where a mismatch in the production and demand are
known to occur.
On the negative side, the limited energy capacity is a major issue, as of now many frequency
markets have no ’relax’ time and primary frequency units are expected to be online at all
times, this is also the case for DK1 and DK2. The nature of a battery makes it impossible to
deliver power continuously, and batteries, therefore, need special regulations or rules before
batteries can participate on a large scale. E.g., diﬀerent areas of the United States address
this issue by introducing some new regulations. Reference [40] explain the regulations in
California and [28] compares various markets in the US. Most of the regulations focus
on better payment for fast responding units such as batteries while other regulation are
discussed in [27], which investigates the battery degradation based on diﬀerent power
bidding techniques in Denmark.
The degradation is economically signiﬁcant as unit performing frequency regulation should
be paid at least the cost of operation, and the degradation of the battery is a big part of
this cost of operation.

Capacity Test
The normal capacity test and the sequence capacity test are two diﬀerent methods used to
determine the capacity of the battery.
Normal capacity test
The detail of the normal capacity test is the same as mentioned in the preconditioning test.
This test is performed after the frequency regulation test. The outcome is to measure the
capacity and compare with the preconditioning test results to determine the degradation
of the battery. The time taken by the normal capacity charge/discharge test is around 5-6
hours.

Sequence capacity test
To minimize the time used for calculating the actual capacity and thereby the capacity
loss, a speciﬁc charging/discharging proﬁle is adopted, called the sequence capacity test.
Also, considering the FNR and PFR market as seen in section 3.2, performing the normal
capacity test would hold the player from not bidding for 2 blocks in the day-ahead market.
Thereby the player losses 8 hours of revenue by not participating. Instead by performing
the sequence test, the time taken for charge/discharge test would be 3-4 hours. Thus, it
greatly helps the player to increase the participating time in the market. The outcome
of the test is to validate with the normal capacity test and to see if it is possible to get
similar results in a shorter testing time. The sequence test will also be compared to the
results of the preconditioning test. After each frequency regulation, this test will be carried
out. From the sequence test, it is also expected that one can see in which SOC regions the
degradation is worst.

4.4.1
Battery Management System (BMS)
Under this section, the BMS developed for this thesis is described. The BMS developed
is used for measuring and monitoring the cell pack voltage and temperature. The other
function of this BMS is to determine the SoC. This is done based on Coulomb counting
technique. It is to be noted here that this BMS unlike the other BMS available in the
market does not predict the SoH of the battery and based on the complexity of the battery
pack assembly, the individual battery pack voltage balancing was not performed.
This BMS program will be used in all the algorithm developed - Capacity Test, Sequence
Test and Frequency Regulation.
The algorithm used for the BMS, coulomb counting and to determine the initial SoC are
described in detail with their ﬂowchart.
As mentioned above, the BMS measures and monitors the voltage of the 4 battery packs
connected in series and measures the temperature in the center of the battery. To measure
the voltage and temperature, the user needs to set the LXI channel number. The instrument
then measures the voltage, and temperatures in the channels speciﬁed and the readings are
displayed in the LabVIEW environment. Simultaneously with the voltage and temperature
measurements, the SoC is calculated. A parallel branch is seen in ﬁgure 4.4 for the SoC
estimation. Based on battery rated current, the charge current at time t and the initial
SoC, the SoC is estimated and displayed on the front panel. The process of SoC and initial
SoC estimation are described in detail further.
For the protection of the system, the BMS monitors the voltage and temperature. If the
voltage is less than the minimum voltage of the batteries, in this case, 3V, the BMS STOPS
the experiment. Also if the temperature is above 45 ◦C, the BMS pauses the experiment
for 1 hour and allows the battery to cool. The power supply and the load outputs are
turned oﬀduring this time.

for batteries as they dictate the current state of the battery and whether they need to
charge or discharge. They assist in determining the battery life enhancement. The BMS
systems available in the market when connected with the battery determines the SoC and
SoH and displays them. Due to the shortage of time, it was not possible to procure a
conventional BMS system and thus BMS system was needed to be developed to determine

4.4.2
Normal Capacity Test
The capacity test is performed to determine percentage degradation of the battery. The
battery is fully charged and fully discharged with a constant current rate of 0.2 C to
perform the normal capacity test. The 0.2 C corresponds to 4.72 A. The C-rate is cho-
sen because the capacity test performed by the manufacturer used 0.2C [21]. During
the capacity test, a sequence of pulse-train with a duty cycle of 0.983 is applied to the
charge/discharge of the battery. The battery is ON continuously when either charging
or discharging with 0.2C for 3 minutes after which the power supply or the electronic
load is switched OFF for 3 seconds, where the battery OCV is measured. Preliminary
tests were performed to determine the OFF time. Based on the report of [20], the OFF
time used was for 1 minute. Starting with this, it was tried to measure the OCV initially
for 1 minute, followed by reducing the time to 10 seconds and further reducing the OFF
time to 3 seconds. In all the three cases the variation in the recorded open circuit voltage
was the same for the last 4 decimal places. Thus it was decided to use 3 seconds as
OFF time to measure OCV. This sequence is applied continuously during the complete
charging and discharging of the battery. The algorithm for the normal capacity test and se-
quence capacity test are the same and is seen in ﬁgure 4.8 and 4.9 which are described below.
The program begins by initializing the power supply, the electronic load, the multi-
meter and the data acquisition system. As seen in the front panel diagram of the capacity
test shown in ﬁgure 4.11, the channels for voltage and temperature along with the rated
current are to be set. These are the inputs for the BMS embedded in this algorithm. After
setting these inputs, one needs to select the option whether to charge or discharge. Based
on the selection the programs gets either into charging or discharging.
Charge Control Mode
First, the charge control will be described followed by the discharge control. Figure 4.8
shows the charge control ﬂowchart. Once the charge mode is selected, the algorithm enters
the while loop of the charging control. With every iteration, which executes every second,
the voltage from the multimeter (VBAT ) is read, the electronic load is disabled, and the
voltage (VPS) and current (IPS) from the power supply is read. The voltage and the
current are displayed in the front panel. A timer function turns on the timer to implement
the ON-OFF sequence. The program checks for any error in the systems and stops if any.
The timer function turns ON and sends a true logic signal until reset. Upon receiving the
true signal, the required current level to charge the battery is set, i.e., 4.72 A and the
output of the power supply is enabled. The reset of the timer function occurs every 3
minutes and stays for 3 seconds providing a logic false signal output. Upon receiving the
false signal output, the power supply output is disabled. When the battery is charging from
the minimum voltage of 12 V, the battery is charged with Constant Current (CC) mode.
Once the power supply voltage has reached the maximum battery voltage of 16.8V, the CC
mode changes to Constant Voltage (CV) mode. The program continuously checks if the
power supply is in CC or CV mode. When the mode shifts to CV mode, the program no

4.4.4
Frequency regulation
This chapter presents the LabVIEW program that executes the frequency regulation
algorithm, the LabVIEW ﬁle is to be found in Appendix D. The algorithm is based on the
description of the considered frequency markets in chapter 3. The overall concept is to
emulate a BESS which participates in frequency markets, contracting an amount of power
that the unit keeps at its disposal.
FNR
This section presents the implemented algorithm that performs the frequency regulation
in DK2. The power bid in this simulation is determined by the limitations of the power
supply and the load, which have a current rating of 20 A. 20 A in power when operating
at the minimum battery voltage (12 V) gives the following power.
Pbid = 12 V · 20 I = 240 W
(4.11)
Setting Pbid to 240 Watt the battery should deliver power in the range of −240W to 240W
depending on the frequency.
Batteries have contrary to conventional power plants a capacity limit regarding energy,
and when this capacity is reached - either fully charged or discharged, the battery can no
longer deliver the contracted power. When these limits are reached the algorithm forces
the battery to recharge or re-discharge back to 50 % SoC. This upper and lower capacity
limit is set based on the battery voltage, when the system reaches constant voltage mode
doing charging, corresponding to 16.8 V at the power supply, the battery acts as if it is
fully charged. This is reasonable since the battery is no longer able to charge with the
contracted power bid. The battery is considered fully discharged when the battery voltage
is 13.2 V; this value is based on testing regarding the battery voltage level as well as the
voltage levels at the individual cells. The cell voltages should never go below 3 V and,
because of variations between the individual cell voltages, 13.2 V was set as a reasonable
minimum voltage. The precondition test will also show that the voltage drops fast below
13.2 V and this value is therefore at a very low SoC.
When the battery is either fully charged or discharged, the algorithm restores the SoC back
to 50 %, this recharging/discharging current is set in order to live up to the rules described
in chapter 3, saying that a unit should be back online after a maximum of 30 minutes.
Charging 50 % of a battery on 30 minutes corresponds to a C-rate of 1. The maximum
charge/discharge current is 20A and the maximum oﬀtime is calculated in equation (4.12)

The result presented in chapter 5 showed that the battery had lowered its capacity
performing frequency regulation. This section will discuss the result in details and describe
some of the issues and challenges the experimental setup had.
As discussed earlier the preconditioning had issues while performing the normal discharge
and both sequence tests. This made the comparison between the 2nd normal discharge
and 2nd sequence test, close to impossible. The comparison would give misguiding results

operating before the test. The importance of having the same operation before performing

go above 16.8 as long as the battery voltage is below. This problem may have forced
undesired degradation.

• Low battery quality
• The imbalance between cell voltages

Ambient temperature:
The ambient temperature increased during the test period; this may have increased
the battery degradation more during the end of the test period. On the other hand, the
battery operating temperature during PFR operation was relatively lower compared to
FNR, as shown in 5.14. Although, ambient temperature is a factor for degradation, in this

stress factor. Thus the temperature is therefore not likely to be the main reason for the
unexpected degradation after performing PFR.
Battery Quality
There is a high probability of chance that the test battery could have some manufacturing
defect. Since two cell-packs showed higher cell imbalance during PFR operation, this could
show variation in the quality of the individual cell-packs. The battery with defects or lower
manufactured quality could have unexpected chemical reactions within, during operation
which causes faster degradation of the battery. Thus, the cell-pack quality could be a
potential reason for the sudden degradation of the battery.
Studies performed in [6] showed that batteries of the same type could act diﬀerently
(seen in Figure 6.1). In this ﬁgure, test on battery #7 showed degradation as expected
compared to the remaining batteries that gave unexpected results. This clearly shows that
each battery can behave diﬀerently.
If the test batteries are of bad quality, the degradation can behave diﬀerently from
what is expected,and degradation can result in a shorter lifetime. With this hypothesis,
the FNR should have resulted in more degradation, unlike the test results compared to
the PFR operation. A battery of lousy quality could have a diﬀerent degradation curve
and would reach its EoL sooner than indicated by the degradation curve provided by
the manufacturer. The only way to test the battery quality and whether or not this has
aﬀected the degradation negatively is to perform the test on multiple batteries. As stated
earlier testing on two diﬀerent batteries could not be done as one of the batteries failed to
operate. Simulating the frequency regulation on several batteries would have improved the
test setup and strengthened the conclusion of the experiments.

CONCLUSION
This thesis investigated how frequency regulation aﬀects the SoH of a Li-ion battery using
real-time experiments. The state of health was analyzed by emulating frequency regulation
in two diﬀerent regulation markets and measuring the battery capacity before and after
the experimental tests. To estimate how a real case grid-connected battery storage system
would be aﬀected by delivering these two services, the results describing the capacity loss
caused by the two frequency regulations were up-scaled by assuming a linear degradation
curve for the batteries. This thesis furthermore estimated a cost of operation based on the
up-scaled results; this showed the economics behind providing frequency regulation.
The major work in this thesis was to set up the test bench, assemble test batteries, electrical
equipment and create a battery management system that enabled measurements of battery
voltages and temperatures. The thesis shows how to set up a communication between
the test battery, the electrical instruments, and the computer. Via this communication, a
production pattern is created that simulates the operations patterns for the two frequency
markets.
From the capacity tests before and after providing frequency regulation in two weeks, the
capacity loss was measured to be 1.8 % in DK1 and 0.77 % in DK2. Based on these values
the battery would reach End of Life after 22.4 and 52.0 weeks respectively. These lifetime
predictions were compared with an already known degradation model, that estimates
a lifetime of 32 weeks of FNR service and 253 weeks of PFR. Comparing the lifetime
estimations for the FNR indicates that using the degradation model on the test batteries is
estimating a to short lifetime. Looking at PFR the estimated 253 weeks was much longer
than the test results which indicate that the degradation caused by emulating PFR was
too high, and the battery has faced degradation from external factors. The measurements
describing FNR did, on the other hand, look promising and is therefore expected to give a
reasonable estimation of how FNR aﬀects batteries. The fact that the PFR results where
misleading is discussed throughout the thesis and the possible reasons for this greater
degradation is expected to be the imbalance between the battery cells and a battery in
unnatural poor condition.
The test result was up-scaled to estimate how grid-connected batteries are aﬀected by
frequency regulation. This up-scaling is based in these two degradation values, and it is
therefore expected that up-scaling FNR would give the most reliable results. Based on the
FNR results, the lifetime of the real case battery storage system was estimated to be 5.77
years, with a cost of operation of 7.07 €/h.
7.1
Future work
The experimental work done in this thesis was based on only one battery, and validation of
the test result was therefore not possible. To validate or improve the results it is necessary
to repeat the test on multiple similar batteries. In order to improve the estimations on the

grid-connected battery, the down-scaled test should be carried out on the speciﬁc battery
type of the grid-connected storage system, which in our case is the NMC type. Doing this
would remove the uncertainties linked to up-scaling the results, results that were based
on an LCO battery. For future work, the test period should also be extended since a two

It is also suggested that for future tests a more advanced BMS system is implemented.
This should be done in order to have a better SoC estimation that would enable analysis

The test bench could also be used to investigate how diﬀerent bidding strategies would

INCREMENTAL ANALYSIS PLOT
Figure A.1 shows the incremental analysis performed on the capacity test of the battery.
The incremental analysis is performed by using the equation A.1
IC (W/V ) = Qt+1 −Qt
Vt+1 −Vt
(A.1)
where Q is the charge fed into the battery and V is the battery voltage. As the voltage
curve is steep during the initial and ﬁnal SoC region, the IC value is low. The high peak is
seen as the voltage diﬀerence is very minimal. The peak in the ﬁnal SoC region is seen as
an outlier as the system gets into CV mode. Thus they are ignored. The incremental test
is performed after every capacity test. The shift in the peak of the curve, or developing of
new peaks or reduction in the peak height indicates the reduction in the capacity due to
internal chemical changes in the battery.
