Abstract
Drug development is slowed by the limited efficacy of current testing practices namely
animal testing, due to an inability to predict the behavior of native tissue. Scalable micro-
physiological systems (MPS) offer a solution to these limitations. This thesis aims to create
an eutectic gallium-indium (eGaIn) based ink that can be DIW (direct ink writing) printed
into an already designed flexible membrane cardiac 2-D microphysiological system to mon-
itor cultured cardiac cell’s force and frequency. Several design specifications are presented
by this application: thickness of the sensor (<10 µm), available area of the sensor (9x8.5
mm), activation of eGaIn without removal from the printing platform, an operating range
of 0-20% strain, and long term stability on the order of weeks to months.
Three methods of producing liquid metal particle (LMP) elastomer composites were
attempted: Sonication of bulk eGaIn in acetone followed by mixing with styrene-ethylene-
butylene-styrene (SEBS) solution, overhead mixing in acetone followed by mixing with SEBS
solution, and direct sonication in hydrophillic polyurethane (HPU) solution. It was found
through SEM analysis that direct sonication in HPU provides the most uniform particles.
Additionally, it was found using a 3D microscope that HPU offers better ink uniformity with
LMPs on the single micron scale. This HPU ink could be printed with an average maxi-
mum thickness within the design specifications (7.28 µm). Different methods of mechanical
activation (compression, sonication, and strain) were tested to render these inks conductive.
It was demonstrated that compression activation was the most effective way to rupture the
eGaIn LMP and render the ink conductive. The conductivity of the mechanically activated
HPU based ink was found to be 2.61x105S/m, which is one order of magnitude lower than
that of pure eGaIn.
Due to the favorable results of the HPU ink, this ink was used to print sensors. These
sensors were evaluated for drift, gauge factor, linear fit, and hysteresis.
Through cyclic
testing on an Instron Universal testing machine with strains of 0-20% for 1800 cycles at
1 Hz the sensors were characterized in conditions that would be seen on the MPS. The
sensors were found to have an initial drift toward lower resistance ranges, and then more
stable readings over hundreds of cycles. This shows a small initial drift, but long term small
resistive hysteresis. All replicates tested showed high linear correlation (R2 > 0.7). The
gauge factor for three samples was within the range of 0.5-0.65 with one outlier with a gauge
factor of 0.09. Mechanical hysteresis of 7.0% was observed.
In order to access the sensor the MPS housing was also redesigned. A sensing insert and
a lid to prevent contamination were designed, taking into account hygienic design. This was
integrated into the design and implemented using magnets. This allowed for connectivity
to the designed strain sensors. An initial attempt to integrate the sensor was made, and
it was observed that activation of the sensor failed, as well as irregular texture caused by
degradation of the substrate. It is unclear if the cause of the failure to activate is due to the
degradation, or if the two are unrelated.
A new eGaIn based ink was successfully created and characterized. This ink was then
printed into a strain sensor which met all the specification of the intended application. The
redesigned MPS housing allowed for monitoring of the printed strain sensors.

Introduction
Nearly 9 of every 10 drug candidates fail in the human trial phase [1]. A significant
contributor to this high failure rate is the inability to reliably predict toxicity in preclinical
trials.
It was found that only approximately 43% toxicity studies in rodents accurately
predicted toxicity in humans [2].
Cardiac toxicity is the leading cause of drug toxicity in preclinical, clinical, and mar-
keting stage drug failure [3]. In addition to the need for cardiotoxicity screening, drugs for
cardiac disease research also need accurate models, as it is the leading cause of death globally
[4]. Due to the lack of efficacy of animal studies to mimic native tissue, organs-on-chips and
MPS are innovative alternatives. These systems take advantage of the recent advances in
stem cell technology to more accurately model human tissue. Recently, induced pluripotent
stem cells (iPCS) are programmed to replicate native organs in 2D or 3D scafolds. The
micro-environment surrounding the cell as well as the cellular heterogeneity are tailored to
mimic in-vivo conditions [5].
While these solutions have advanced significantly due to the ability to differentiate stem
cells at scale, these systems are limited by the techniques used to observe these cultures. In
the case of cardiac models, the contractile function of the cells is an important indicator
of tissue health and maturity [6]. Strain sensors are a common method used to quantify
the contractile function of cultured cardiac cells.
While these sensors can track beating
rate and contractile force many are constrained by resistive hysteresis limiting the lifetime
of the studies [7] [8]. Many state of the art examples of these sensors are made through
patterning inks which use micron-scale conductive particles, which demonstrate changes in
resistance when strain is applied on the sensor by cells. Under repeated strain these particles
can migrate within the sensor overtime contributing to hysteresis, and a lack of long term
efficacy. While other methods such as optical tracking can also quantify contractile functions
of cultures they can be difficult to scale, or require long alignment procedures[6]. The need for
reliable tracking of cardiac behavior coupled with the demands for longevity and scalability
of a tracking system create a complex problem.
3D printed liquid metal sensors present a viable solution to the current limitations [9].
eGaIn, a highly conductive liquid metal, is proposed as a solution to prolong sensor use
by limiting resistive hysteresis. As this conductive additive is in a liquid state it can flow
and fill in gaps, or spaces created through repeated sensor stretching, unlike solid particles
which migrate changing from the initial morphology. In order to fabricate strain sensors
with eGaIn, DIW 3D printing allows for reliable manufacturing at sufficient resolution of
the circuitry for a microphysiological device. Using a liquid metal strain sensor on a flexible
membrane platform, it is hypothesized that cultured cardiomyocyte contractility can be
tracked at scale.
The aim of this thesis is to design and test a 3D printed low hysteresis strain sensor that
can be integrated into an already designed cardiac 2-D microphysiological device in order
to track contractile functionality of cultured cardiomyocytes. These aims have been divided
into the following objectives:
• Determine the requirements for composite ink formulation and activation, taking into
1

consideration the spatial fabrication requirements of the envisioned cardiac microphys-
iological device.
• Characterize the conductivity, strain sensitivity and hysteresis of printed strain-sensors
embedded in elastomer composites.
• Design, fabricate and test tissue culture membranes with integrated strain sensors for
quantifying membrane displacements.

Background
2.1
Cardiac Tissue Culture Physiology
2.1.1
Overview
Cardiomyocytes, the muscle cells of the heart, make up 80-90% of the heart, by volume.
These cells are rod like shaped approximately 100-150 µm long and 20-30 µm in width,
and can be seen in Figure 1 B [10]. Adjacent cardiomycotes assemble to form intercalated
disks creating an electrical and mechanical connection between the cells using gap juctions.
These junctions enable synchronous contraction between the cells, beginning to form a tissue
[11]. The Frank Starling relationship shows a relationship between myocardial fibers and
force generated by the contraction, in native conditions [12].
The preload conditions of
these myocardial fibers can be varied a on MPS by increasing strain and observing the
resulting force of contraction. The resulting force can be recorded, and evaluated for integrity
compared to native conditions. As cardiomyocytes mature and form connected fibers, the
force generated by their contractions grows as seen in Figure 1 A. A single cardiomyocyte
can generate at least 1 µN while cardiomyocyte clusters can generate 1 to 4 kPa of force
[13].
Figure 1: A) Length dependent activation of myocardial fibers. Increasing cell length will
increase the force created during contractions in a predictable fashion until a plateau is
reached. This preload can also be increased by increasing the strain applied to the cells.
[14]. B) Microphysiology of the heart. Cardiomyocytes arrange themselves to connect via
intercalated disks, allowing for fibers to form. These fibers make up the myocardium of the
heart wall, causing the heart to beat. Figure B comes from BioRender.

2.2
Strain Gauges
2.1.2
Effect of Strain On Cardiac Cells
There have been many attempts to recreate a cardiac environment to study disease and
effect of drugs. In order to effectively model the native habitat many aspects must be taken
into account. One such aspect for cardiac cells is the native strain experienced. In the heart
increased pressure load (directly correlated to beating force) is related to increased wall ten-
sion [15]. This can be simulated by stretching cardiomyocyte tissues allowing the cells to pull
on each other, as seen in the native environment. Strain similar to the native environment
(10-20% strain), increased cell proliferation in cultures [16]. Many types of strain have been
imposed often multi-axial. This is meant to represent the native environment of the heart
[15]. However, the cultures seen on the MPS are of a smaller scale than cardiac tissue in
the heart. Therefore, a number of groups have proposed uni-axial strain for an effective
modeling of micro-cardiac tissue [17] [18] [19].
2.2
Strain Gauges
Strain sensors transduce mechanical deformations into electrical signals [20].
There
are several types of strain sensors such as resistive, capacitive, transitive, pizeoelectric, and
triboelectric [21]. Resistive strain sensors operate off of an elongation of the wires in the
sensor. This simple operational principle allows for easy fabrication and prototyping of the
sensor compared to other options.
A strain sensor’s capabilities are evaluated by analyzing its gauge factor, durability,
linear range, transient response, and stability [22].
Gauge factor (GF) is the change in
resistance found in relation to the strain applied to the sensor relative to its initial state, for
resistive sensors as seen in equation 1.
GF = (∆R/R0)/(∆L/L) = (∆R/R0)/ϵ

rubber giving a geometrical gauge factor around 1.6-2 [23] [24].

2.3
Cardiac Tissue Models Based on Deformable Membranes
The change in resistance of resistance strain sensors is directly related to Poisson’s ratio.
Meaning a strain will increase length and decrease the cross sectional area of the material,
and thereby increase its resistance. This relationship is demonstrated by the equation .
R = ρL/Ac
(6)
Where (ρ) is specific resistance, (R) is resistance and (Ac) is the cross sectional area. Ob-
serving the change in resistance when strain is applied allows for the gauge factor to be
calculated.
The ideal gauge factor will allow for clear differentiation during strain within a measur-
able range. The change in resistance should allow for the range of interest to be surveyed,
with enough response in order to differentiate varying strain. Durability is also an important
factor for strain gauges. In order for a gauge to be effective it must withstand the range of
strain it is intended to measure. Reaching the desired strain without deformation is crucial
to having an effective design. Linear range is a sensors’ correlation to a linear regression pre-
diction to imposed strain. A high R2 value of sensor data compared to the regression shows
strong correlation which is desirable over the sensors’ intended range. A sensor’s transient
range is defined by its response and recovery times. A response time is the time it takes for
a sensor to reach a steady state response [25], while a recovery time is time it takes for the
sensor it return to its baseline after a strain is applied. Having a small recovery and response
time is ideal to be able to interpret signals quickly and effectively. Lastly, stability is how
consistent a response is as a strain is imposed over time [22]. If the value says fairly con-
stant then it can be assumed that the sensor is stable. Stability is related to hysteresis.Both
mechanical hysteresis which is deviations from expected readings over time, due to warping
of the sensor, and resitive hysteresis which is caused by particle migration. This is often due
to repeated strain causing small particles migrating within the sensor matrix, changing the
paths in which electricity is transferred throughout the sensor, known as percolation paths.
Both forms of hysteresis can lead to drifts in the sensors gauge factor, limiting the lifetime
and usefulness of strain sensors.

eGaIn Material Properties
eGaIn, is a metal alloy that is in a liquid state at room temperature and shows low
toxicity [29]. A ratio of 75% gallium and 25% indium by weight is most commonly used due
to its low melting point of 15 °C [30]. eGaIn shows high conductivity of 3.4x106S/m, which
is 58% as conductive as copper [9; 30; 31].
Another notable feature of eGaIn is its ability to easily oxidize, under ambient condi-
tions. This 1-3 nm oxidized shell allows for self containment of the liquid center, which can
create support structures such as the wires seen in Figure 4. Utilizing the oxidized shell,
micro or nano scale LMP can be created which stabilize the interface between the LMP
and the surrounding media[32]. These particles can be created in an array of sizes through
methods such as sonication and mechanical agitation [29]][9]. Modifications to the shell can
be made through the solution the LMPs are formed in. These modifications can help further
stabilize the particles in solution as well as bind them together [33].

2.5
Rupturing the Oxide Shell of eGaIn
Due to the minimally conductive oxidized shell coating each LMP the conductive liquid
metal cores of oxidized eGaIn particles cannot connect to each other to create a conductive
path. When a conductive path is formed it is known as percolation which occurs when these
shells are ruptured allowing for connected paths of liquid metal to form. These paths allow for
the flow of electrons throughout the material, rendering it conductive or "activated". There
are several ways to rupture the oxidized shell. Strain, compression, and acoustic waves are
all effective published methods of activating LMP [29][35]. While there are other methods
to activate eGaIn LMP, such as thermal or chemical, these options are less applicable to this
project.
2.5.1
Compression
Compression exerts force on the particles causing the LMPs to rupture and flow. There
are several factors that can effect the pressure needed to activate LMPs. The material the
LMP is suspended in has a large effect. If it has a large Young’s modulus then the material
will absorb much of the force reducing the force felt by the LMPs themselves. The elastic
potential energy of material (ue) is defined by a variation of Hooke’s law. This refers to the
energy that can be stored in the configuration of a given material before deformation occurs.
In the formula below E is Young’s Modulus, and ϵ is once again strain.

As seen above the higher the Young’s Modulus the more energy the material can absorb.
When making a composite with LMP additives the modulus of the bulk material should be
taken into account.
The dispersion of LMP in the material also has a large effect.
If the particles are
closer together the ruptured material has to travel a shorter distance to perculate, making
activation more likely. This tighter spacing can be achived through higher loading volumes
of LMPs in the ink, or through allowing settling of LMPs as the ink dries [35]. If the loading
volume is higher then the concentration throughout the ink will be higher allowing for more
pathways to form. When LMPs are allowed to settle the concentration at the bottom, of
the print increases locally allowing for the added benefit of high local concentration, without
the disadvantage of difficult printing that comes with high solid loading volumes. The size
of the particles also effects the force needed for activation.
Larger particles require a smaller force to activate partially due to faster settling. This
particle sedimentation can be described by Stoke’s equation which approximates a sphere
settling in fluid. Here settling velocity (vs), can predict how quickly a particle will settle

2.5.3
Strain
eGaIn LMP networks can alternatively be rendered conductive through strain activation.
A strain placed on the material harboring a LMP based ink or on dried ink itself can rupture
the oxide shells, creating a percolation network, rendering the system conductive as seen in
Figure 6 [37] [33] [38]. In the case of chemical modifications of the eGaIn LMP shell in
the ink, strain for activation can be as low as 50% or 1.5%, by creating strong interaction
between the eGaIn LMPs [37] [33]. For Wang et al. [33] the interactions between the droplets
were much stronger than the force needed to break the metal oxide shells due to the presence
of PVA-AD and PVA-CD on the LMP shells. This allows for an extremely low activation
strain of 1.5%. Thrasher et al. [37], also uses chemical modification of n-acrylate-containing
phosphonic acid ligands, to create attraction between particles allowing for strain activation
in a low regime. Alternatively chemical modifications such as thiol can be added in order
to compete with oxygen on the surface of the eGaIn particles, creating a thinner shell. This
technique is used by Vellum et al. [38], who further limit the formation of the oxide shell
through introduction of HCl, which is a strong acid known to remove the oxidized shell
observes activation beginning around 100%, but not stabilizing until around 400%. Stable
or full activation meaning that the conductivity plateaus is not reached until approx 200%
for Thrasher et al. and 100% for Wang et al., despite the early percolation formation [37][33].

2.7
DIW
Traditionally this pneumatic based 3D printing method utilizes visoelastic materials to
print at high resolution up to single microns. The inks used change rheology to a "flow"
state when pressure is applied allowing the ink to be extruded. Once the ink is deposited
and is no longer under applied pressure it is at "rest" state and will remain in its printed
shape [47]. Lind et al. [48] uses this method, but also utilizes the evaporation of solvent
within the ink. The presence of solvent allows the ink to spread once printed reaching sub
10 micron prints. As this ink spreads and the solvent evaporates the solid content of the ink
is left behind in a thin print.
These inks can be pure elastomer or an additive can be used to give the ink desired
properties. Conductive additives are common in many applications such as flexible circuitry
and sensing. Popular additives for this application tend to be silver particles, carbon black,
and carbon nanotubes [49] [50].Through the choice of elastomer, additives, and solvent the
ink can be catered to different applications.
Two elastomers are used in this thesis, SEBS (Styrene Ethylene Butylene Styrene Block
Copolymer) and HPU (Hydrophilic Polyurethane). These elastomers use butyl acetate and
elthanol or water and solvents respectively. These combinations fit the application due to
both elastomer and solvent low toxicity [51] [49].

The deformation of the flexible membrane can be described by plate theory. Since the

2.8.3
Sensor applications
Through applying a known force on the membrane, a change in resistance can be ob-
served. Through this the sensor can be characterized, so that a change in resistance relative
to the initial resistance can be correlated to a force, over a known area. This allows for gauge
factor and a linear correlation to be calculated. From here the membrane can be seeded with
cells. Once these cells mature they will contract creating a change in resistance which will be
lower than the neutral state, due to the shortening of the membrane,and contraction of the
sensor. With different strains imposed by the membrane these changes in force imposed by
contraction will vary. Collecting this data, a plot can be made of strain vs force of contrac-
tion. To demonstrate that the cells cultured are representative of native culture this curve
should show the Frank Starling relationship. The scope of this thesis consists of making the
initial characterization of the change in resistance due to an imposed strain.

4.1
Ink Formulation
This thesis began with an exploration of an eGaIn based DIW 3D printing capable ink.
This ink was to be created using eGaIn LMP in an elastomer. Based on similar applications
in literature we planned to first create LMPs and then mix them into an elastomer [29] [33].
Due to the high failure strain, bio-compatibility, and non-toxic solvent formulation SEBS
with low PS content was chosen as the first elastomer to investigate [49]. A protocol was
established to make eGaIn LMP and varying loading concentration of these LMP were tested,
using SEBS as an elastomer (see Appendix Section 7.1.2). While this SEBS based eGaIn ink
formulation initially yielded promising results, large batch variation and incompatibility with
insulation due to a common solvent, limited the usefulness of this ink. Due to the significant
limitations of the SEBS based ink, a hydrophilic polyurathane, HPU, was also tested. With
the change in elastomer a change in LMP synthesis was also attempted, a promising option
to combat the inconsistencies seen with the previous method. The HPU also no longer had
a common solvent with the membrane material. These possibilities seemed promising to
overcome both issues seen with the SEBS based ink.
4.1.1
LM-Composite Inks Using Pre-Formed eGaIn Particles and SEBS
In order to produce the LMPs required to create an effective ink, two methods were
tested: sonicaiton of bulk eGaIn in acetone, and mixing of bulk eGaIn in acetone. Both
of these methods have been shown to produce micron sized particles in literature [32] [29].
Sonication was the first method tried. To investigate the sizes of the particles formed SEM
images were taken (Figure 14). This method yielded small powdered particles, with small
clumps visible to the eye. There was large variation between batches and some displayed
larger and more frequent clumps. In general, this method formed 1-20 micron-scale wrinkled
spheroid eGaIn aggregates, as seen in Figure14 A. It is hypothesized that the poor unifor-
mity of the sonicated LMPs is likely due to the local nature of LMP formation, within one
millimeter of the probe.
The observed non-uniformity lead to the decision to attempt LMP formation with over-
head mixing, to give a higher uniformity alternative that is ideally more reproducible. While
overhead mixing has been shown to create larger particles than probe sonication, there have
been reports of single micron particles created using this method [29] [35]. An experiment
was planned to test if using an overhead mixer to create LMPs was more appropriate for
our application. Using this method, LMP formation occurred on a much longer time scale
than expected from literature, as demonstrated by Ford et al. [35]. Additionally, the particle
sizes appeared much larger to the eye than those seen in probe sonication. The experiment
was stopped after 92 minutes as this was far past the desired time frame with Ford et al. of
five minutes. As seen in Figure 14 the overhead mixing created spheroid-like structures that
were on the order of hundreds of microns, much larger than those created using sonication.
While neither method of producing particles created spheroids in the targeted size range
we moved forward with the sonicated particles based on their smaller size because it was

4.2
Activation
Once the eGaIn inks were printed they had to be activated, a stimulus must be applied
to rupture non-conductive oxide shells and create a percolation network, in order to be
conductive. The initial systematic studies on activation were conducted using the SEBS ink.
The results from these studies were then used to develop an activation protocol for the HPU
ink. The Young’s modulus for HPU and SEBS share much of the same regime (5-10 MPa),
but HPU with high water content can have a lower modulus around 1 MPa [29]. Due to
the low ratio of water in the solvent (30%) it is estimated that these inks should perform
similarly under compression and strain. The affinity for water absorption and swelling of
the HPU could be problematic when submerged for sonication activation.
Initially the SEBS based eGaIn ink was printed onto a PS slide using a 400 µm nozzle.
In order to render the ink conductive it had to be "activated’. Different activation methods
were chosen based off of what was seen in literature. Compression activation was tested
using an Instron with compression plates. The samples were compressed and then checked

When the thin layer of SEBS was printed onto these slides there was a
visible texture. The SEBS layer separated when exposed to water as intended, but the slide
appeared to have small bubbles formed inside and a texture left behind, shown in Figure 24

was printed onto the insulation and then was covered with the central insulation layer as
well as the top layer, leaving the pads exposed. On the slide the print could not be activated
using the hammer. With hindsight it is likely due to the lack of strain on the print that the
test samples experienced being removed from the glass substrate (see Section 4.2). A print
of the eGaIn sensor alone was printed on a glass slide which could be easily activated when
gently hammered. This can also be from the shear experienced on the top of the eGaIn as
the PDMS sheets that were placed between the hammer and the print were moved.
The alignment of the print was correct for the sensing system and then a resistance
could be measured across the sensor. Meaning that the system as a whole was functional,
despite membrane integration experiencing complications. As seen in Figure 24 A the pogo
pins were placed properly to monitor the printed sensor. The system could be properly
assembled together, however time did not permit for optimization of the sensor into the
membrane.

Discussion
5.1
Ink Development
5.1.1
LMP Formation in Acetone
LMPs were sucessfully created, however both methods deviated from what is seen in
literature. Lee et al. [29] observes particles of an average size of 2.29 µm when using a
similar sonication procedure. The deviation is hypothesized to be due to the use of a vial
rather than a wider mixing cup as used in the method to fabricate the LMP. The larger
distance between the probe and the bulk eGaIn can create inconsistencies. Ford et al. [35]
observed a similar particle size as seen below with just 10 seconds of mixing. The stirring
shaft in the method from Ford et al. was not specified, so the difference in shearing between
the two shafts can likely explain the change. With further optimization of processing time
and more optimal equipment such as a discoidal stirring shaft which would provide more
shear, it is likely that an overhead stirrer for formation of LMP would be a viable option.
However, with material and time constraints it was decided to continue with eGaIn LMPs
created through sonication. It is also possible that particle settling over time allowed for
coalescence of the particles, between sampling and SEM measurement.
5.1.2
Elastomer Comparison
While initially a SEBS based eGaIn ink allowed for fair printing and activation, it
became clear that uniformity both within and between batches was a problem. Aggregates
formed in all batches, however some were more extensive than others. The common solvent
between the eGaIn ink and the SEBS insulation layers allowed the printed eGaIn traces
to be pulled deforming the trace. Due to these limitations the decision to use HPU as an
elastomer was made.
This change allowed for the bulk eGaIn to be sonicated in the polymer solution rather
than the solvent. While this could have been attempted with the SEBS based ink there was
concern over solvent loss, as this had been observed while printing and storing the ink. The
created LMP are more uniform than those seen with the SEBS based ink. This is likely
due to the more uniform control over the local environment around the sonication probe.
The more viscous HPU solution prevented large flow around the probe. This allowed for
the mixing and small probe movements to create a more uniform ink. The thinner vial
used opposed to a wider mixing cup as required with the sonication in acetone (to prevent
material loss) also allowed for more contact within the effect range of the probe sonicator.

can be estimated as follows.

For eGaIn σA is equal to 3.4x106S/m and has a volume percentage of 70% in both inks [9].
Substituting these values into equation 10 the theoretical effective conductivity of both inks
is 2.38x106S/m.

5.1
Ink Development
The conductivity of both inks was one order of magnitude less than the expected. This
is likely due to the fact that all of the eGaIn additive does not interact in the percolation
network, creating a smaller volume actively involved than the theoretical. The SEBS ink
had large particle clumps which hold a larger volume of the ink than the smaller particles
throughout the ink. This uneven distribution of particles could lead to problems creating a
peculation network, possibly explaining why this ink was determined to be approximately
half as conductive as the HPU ink.
5.1.3
Ink Printing
The HPU ink allowed for most particles to be within the optimal range, and printed
very well. The average thickest part of the prints tested were found to be 7.28 µm, while the
SEBS ink was shown to be 10.94 µm. While these do not represent the average thickness
it does represent the threshold required for the application. The surface toughness data
(Table 3) all signals that the SEBS ink is rougher than the HPU ink, and that the average
deviation from the surface is larger. The maximum peak height from the base also shows
that the SEBS ink can have peaks largely out of range, while the HPU ink has peaks within
the desired thickness. From the LEXT images shown in Figure 16 A & B, it is clear that
one ink is far more homogeneous than the other. The transition to the HPU based ink also
eliminated the issue of common solvent disrupting printed layers. The HPU ink remained
in its printed orientation when insulated. Further experiments can optimize the printing of
the HPU ink, allowing for thinner prints in height and width. Increasing printing speed as
well as lowing pressure could both help achive this.
5.1.4
Activation
The activation parameters found to be effective on the un-insulated SEBS based ink
aligned with literature. The pressure needed to activate the prints was found to be 0.373
MPa which aligns with the less than 1 MPa needed to activate particles with a diameter
more than 1 µm reported in literature [36]. Sonication activation following a protocol es-
tablished by Lee et al. [29] worked effectively under the same conditions. Strain activation
began to show leaks of eGaIn droplets and partial percolation pathways as strain approached
100%, following results seen in literature [37].
The insulated print activation for the HPU ink behaved unexpectedly for probe soni-
cation as well as compression. The damage seen during probe sonication could be due to
swelling of the hydrophilic ink as water diffused through the insulation, exacerbated by the
local heat of the probe. The compression issues on the Instron can be explained by the thin
nature of the print (0.15 mm in height), causing any uneven nature of the plates to only
partially compress the sensor. However, changing the sample preparation raised concerns
that the strain of removing the sensors from a substrate that they clung to lowered the
force needed for activation of the sensors. When the sample preparation was changed to a
substrate that allowed for the sensors to detach easily the sensors no longer activated when
hammered. If stretched and then hammered these sensors could be activated. More trials
are needed to tell if the cause of inactivation when hammered is caused by the difference in

strain when removed from the substrate or batch variation. Ideally to determine the cause
two batches of prints with six sensors each would be created. Three of each of these prints
would be placed on the glass substrate and three would be placed on PS. Activation would
be attempted on all of these sensor. If the sensors from both batches activate only if they
were placed on the glass substrate then it is likely that this is the cause. If the activation

5.1.5
Sensor Integration
Due to complications in the membrane integrity of the device without implemented
strain sensors, and equipment failure only one attempt of integration could be implemented
into the membranes. Due to the variation of the substrate the membranes lost integrity
nullifying the experiment.
In the future this can be solved easily by a change of slide
substrate to glass, or a thicker layer of PVA could be applied. It is unknown if this variation
was the cause of the inability to activate the sensor on the slide or if the activation method
is flawed. Further testing can clarify the cause.
If activation is in fact the limiting factor several additional experiments can be run to
find a more applicable alternative. Using a smaller nozzle will increase the shear felt on the
eGaIn particles embeded in the ink, perhaps activating the ink while printing. This could
eliminate the separate activation step all together. Using a sub 60 µm nozzle could present
a viable solution, as long as clogging does not become an issue. Alternatively a longer 60
µm nozzle can be used as more shear will be applied to the ink as there is longer contact
with the needle.
5.2
Sensing Design
5.2.1
Sensor Characteristics
There is large variation between replicates. This is likely due to printing variation or
incomplete activation, but the variation it difficult to identify. Replicate 4 appears to not
be fully activated as there is a drop in resistance when it is first placed under strain, as
seen in Figure 20 A. The sensor does still seem to stabilize over many cycles. Replicate
1 has a much smaller range of resistance changes than the others. This could be do to a
thinner percolation channel within the sensor. Strain would only be seen across this thinner
area, as it is the limiting section which would agree with the small curve seen in Figure
20 B. However, it is difficult to pin point the issue, as well as determine what a "normal"
sensor’s behavior is. In order to clarify the same experiment should be repeated, ideally
with replicates made from three different batches of ink. The sensors over all tend to have a
degree of drift in early cycles. This can be from further activation and percolation network
formation during the strain, which stabilizes over the same strain. The change in resistance
over a cycle also slightly decreases over time, but it is thought that the larger effect in the
changes seen in Figure 21 B is the drift to lower resistance values.
The gauge factor of the sensor is lower than the hypothetical gauge factor for liquid metal

path rearrangement, which can allow for higher conductivity. It appears that this competes
with the narrowing of the material until high strains (>400%) when Pouillet’s law begins

5.3
Apparatus Prototyping
5.3.1
Sensor Integration
The initial attempt to integrate this sensor design into the cell-culture device was un-
successful, and only one attempt was able to be made. The problem with apparent solvent
damage to the polystyrene substrate can likely be solved through the use of glass slides or
a thicker layer of PVA solution to prevent the solvent from eroding the PS. Activation of
the device is a larger issue. It appears that strain may in fact be a necessary precursor to
compression activation at the pressure applied. Several approaches can be taken as noted in
section 5.1.4. Any of these will likely assist the activation of the printed sensor. It would be
wise to initially test these changes on the smaller sensors seen in Figure 18 to save material
and use a robust print.
5.3.2
Housing Design and Fabrication
An effective housing for the device was created and fabricated. The housing exhibits
hygienic design and follows all of the restrictions of the design. Ideally upon further reflection
the slots on the back of the sensing insert should be replaced with holes barely larger than
the wires themselves. This would limit the condensation that can occur on the inside of
the lid, leading to possible contamination or disruption of the sensing system. The final
design performed well, but future iterations can improve upon the functionality. The screw
mechanism used to secure the housing top and bottom together can be tedious and difficult
in gloves. It is important that this fit is very tight as this seals the printed device in place
ensuring alignment with the sensing system. Ideally a snapfit design can be implemented, if
this will provide sufficent stability.

6
Conclusion
A new eGaIn elastomer ink was created by directly sonicating eGaIn in elastomer so-
lution. Thin traces of approximately 7 µm of this ink could be printed and mechanically
activated using a combination of mechanical strain and compression. Sensors of this ink
were fabricated and evaluated for drift, gauge factor, linear fit, and hysteresis which were all
found to be favorable to the application. An initial attempt at integration was performed
but suggested a need for re-formulation of the sensor print protocol. Future work on this
project will require deeper investigation into the mechanical activation requirements of this
ink. This can be done by testing multiple batches of sensors, half dried on a glass substrate,
half dried on PS slides. It is also needed to reformulate the MPS fabrication procedure. We
believe this will require a thicker layer of PVA or transition to glass to prevent degradation
of the print substrate.

eGaIn was added under the silver pad and the insulation was placed 0.5 mm over the pad,
eliminating any path for liquid eGaIn to escape (5). This combination allowed for the ability
to measure resistance once again, and in a closed system that was reliable. This design was
used for all tests.
