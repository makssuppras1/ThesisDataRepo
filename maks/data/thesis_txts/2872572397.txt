Abstract
This study presents a comprehensive techno­economic analysis of heat pump configurations
designed for waste heat recovery from data centers. The analysis was conducted in coopera­
tion with an industrial partner and is grounded in a real­world case study located in Denmark.
The analysis evaluates system performance through design and operational modelling using
Engineering Equation Solver (EES), followed by financial analysis employing detailed cost eval­
uations in Microsoft Excel. Two primary configurations, parallel and series, were investigated
using natural refrigerants, with a particular emphasis on two­stage systems to fulfill the high
temperature lift required. Although R600 consistently demonstrated the highest coefficient of
performance (COP) across almost all simulated design models, it was ultimately excluded from
operational and financial assessments due to the limited commercial availability of compatible
components.
Transitioning to operational models, reflecting fixed component characteristics and real­world
constraints, resulted in the selection of the best three two­stage models. In the base case
scenario, a R600a­based parallel compression system with a flash tank and internal heat ex­
changer achieved the highest COP (4.5). This operational modelling confirmed the robustness
and adaptability of the selected configuration under realistic variable conditions, enhancing con­
fidence in its practical implementation.
Financial analysis also indicated strong economic feasibility, yielding a positive net present
value (€169 244), a favourable internal rate of return (9.7%), the lowest levelised cost of heat
(€6.35 per MWh), and the shortest discounted payback period (14.2 years). This extended pay­
back period could be considered the main significant financial drawback. Sensitivity analysis
highlighted heat sale price and electricity costs as pivotal factors influencing economic viability,
emphasising the necessity for market stability and secure, long­term commercial agreements.
Additionally, risk associate with refrigerant choice was evaluated and a two­stage ammonia­
based heat pump system with an intercooler is suggested for applications with high safety re­
quirements.
The research underscores the strategic relevance of natural refrigerants combined with ad­
vanced two­stage heat pump systems, providing a viable pathway towards sustainable, efficient
and financially attractive waste heat recovery solutions for the data center industry.

Introduction
1.1
Energy Consumption in Data Centers
In early 2024, more than 11 000 data centers were registered worldwide. Between 2010 and
2020, this number rose by an average of 4% per year. At first, better cooling technologies and
cloud­based infrastructure stabilized the energy demand, but lately, electricity consumption has
spiked as the demand for data services has grown faster than efficiency improvements. In 2022,
data center consumption was estimated to be between 240 and 340 TWh, representing 1­1.3%
of global electricity demand (excluding data networks and cryptocurrency mining) [1, 2].
In Denmark, data center energy consumption in 2021 is projected to increase sevenfold by 2030,
reaching nearly 28 PJ [3]. Large financial investments are driving this rapid rise; in the last five
years, over USD 225 billion has been spent on AI startups, considerably exceeding clean energy
investments. Between 2019 and 2023, capital expenditures by US tech giants like Microsoft,
Amazon, Alphabet, and Meta quadrupled to USD 150 billion, or roughly 0.5% of the US GDP.
Similar patterns are also emerging in China, India, and other expanding economies, signaling
that the data center sector urgently needs effective and sustainable energy [1].
Given this rapid growth in energy demand, improving the thermal management of data centers
has become increasingly important, not only to maintain operational efficiency, but also to en­
able energy reuse. Effective cooling strategies play a pivotal role in achieving both these goals,
particularly in view of stricter environmental regulations and the broader push for decarbonisa­
tion. Understanding how cooling systems are configured is therefore essential, especially when
considering opportunities for waste heat recovery and integration into district heating networks.
In this context, it is useful to distinguish between primary and secondary cooling systems. Pri­
mary cooling solutions include air­cooled, water­cooled, and two­phase systems. Among these,
air­cooled systems remain the most widespread, relying on structured airflow control, typically
through cold aisle/hot aisle configurations. Water­cooled systems make use of water’s high heat
capacity, achieving elevated coolant temperatures of up to 60°C. More advanced two­phase
cooling systems are capable of handling high heat fluxes at lower mass flow rates, reaching
coolant temperatures of up to 80°C [4, 5]. The secondary cooling cycle decouples the internal
cooling process from the final heat sink, ensuring redundancy and thermal stability.
1.2
Environmental Impact of Data Centers
The increasing global reliance on data centers has raised significant environmental concerns,
particularly due to their growing electricity demand and associated carbon emissions. The ICT
(Information and Communications Technology) sector was responsible for over 2% of global
greenhouse gas emissions in 2018, a share that continues to rise with rapid digitalization. De­
spite advancements in energy efficiency, the expansion of artificial intelligence, cloud comput­
ing, and data storage has led to higher overall energy consumption, intensifying the environ­
mental footprint of data centers. While Nordic countries benefit from colder climates that reduce
energy requirements for cooling, the industry’s reliance on vast amounts of electricity remains
a challenge, especially when sourced from carbon­intensive power grids. Additionally, the heat
generated from server operations, if not efficiently managed, contributes to thermal pollution,
further intensifying their environmental impact. The integration of renewable energy sources,
optimization of cooling technologies, and improved energy management strategies are essen­
tial to mitigating the growing environmental burden of data centers. However, the increasing
scale and geographic spread of data infrastructure necessitate systemic solutions to reduce
carbon emissions while maintaining the sector’s rapid expansion [6].

1.3
Energy Consumption of District Heating networks
District heating (DH) remains a cornerstone of the global heating sector, delivering centralised
thermal energy to residential, commercial, and industrial consumers through expansive pipeline
networks. As of 2024, more than 17 000 operational DH systems supply over 70 million Euro­
pean citizens, with total annual energy consumption exceeding 450 TWh. Heating accounts for
approximately 50% of the total final energy consumption in Europe, with nearly 70% of energy
use in residential and commercial buildings dedicated to heating and cooling services.
Figure 1.1: Annual global energy supplies to district heating networks
Historically, traditional DH networks have been heavily reliant on fossil fuels, particularly coal,
oil, and natural gas. As illustrated in Figure 1.1, this dependency continues to define the majority
of existing systems. However, in response to increasing pressure to improve energy efficiency
and decarbonise the heating sector, alternative heat sources, especially industrial waste heat
and renewable energy are gaining traction. Recent studies suggest that up to 50% of DH energy
demand could feasibly be met by waste heat recovery [6]. Key sources include data centers,
power generation facilities, and wastewater treatment plants. When combined with heat pumps,
these sources allow for the effective utilisation of low­grade thermal energy, substantially reduc­
ing primary energy consumption and reliance on fossil fuels.
To accommodate such recovered heat, DH systems themselves must evolve. District heating
networks are commonly categorised into five generations, differentiated by temperature levels
and system integration capabilities:
• 1GDH: High­temperature steam­based systems (>150°C).
• 2GDH: Pressurised hot water systems (>120°C).
• 3GDH: Lower­temperature pressurised hot water systems (<100°C).
• 4GDH: Systems integrating renewable energy and smart control technologies.

Environmental Impact of District Heating networks
While district heating provides a more efficient alternative to individual heating systems, its
environmental impact is largely determined by the energy sources used for heat generation.
Currently, district heating accounts for 36% of total greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions in the
European building sector [7], with fossil fuels still playing a dominant role in many regions.

DH systems based on solid fossil fuels, in particular, contribute significantly to CO2 emissions,
with average emission intensities reaching 95 gCO2/MJ for coal and 108 gCO2/MJ for peat
[6]. Figure 1.3 illustrates the CO2 emissions intensity index for district heat and heat production

by 2050. Studies show that incorporating waste heat into district heating networks can reduce
emissions by up to 84% compared to conventional fuel­based systems, particularly in regions
where coal and peat are still widely used [8]. For example, integrating data center waste heat
into DH networks has been demonstrated to lower annual emissions by up to 720 tCO2 per
10 MW of recovered heat, while simultaneously improving the overall energy efficiency of both
data centers and district heating infrastructure [8]. Additionally, fifth­generation district heat­
ing and cooling (5GDHC) systems enable bidirectional energy flows, allowing heat recovery
and redistribution among various consumers, further reducing the carbon footprint of heating
networks. As regulatory frameworks tighten and carbon pricing mechanisms incentivize low­
emission technologies, waste heat utilization will play an increasingly vital role in decarbonizing
urban heating systems and aligning with broader sustainability objectives [7].
1.5
Legislative Drivers
The energy efficiency, waste heat utilization, and integration of data centers into the district
heating network is growing significantly within the European Union’s regulatory framework. Data
centers with power demand higher than 500 kW must report their key sustainability metrics,
including energy consumption, renewable energy use, water footprint, and waste heat reuse by
15 May 2024. These reports will be assessed by the European Commission by 15 May 2025
and may introduce new legislative measures to improve efficiency.
An essential aspect of this directive is that data centers with a total rated energy input exceeding
1 MW should integrate their waste heat into the district heating network unless they can prove
that it is technically or economically unfeasible. Additionally, a cost­benefit analysis is required to
assess the feasibility of waste heat connection to district heating systems or other demand points
(such as industrial sites or agricultural facilities) when significantly refurbishing or planning new

Following these regulations, the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) reinforces
the obligation for companies to account for energy efficiency and waste heat recovery in their
sustainability disclosures [9]. The Energy Efficiency Directive (EED) also gradually increases
commitments toward a greener district heating and cooling network, ensuring a 75% utilization
rate of renewable energy or waste heat, achieving 100% by 2050 [10].
In addition to the EED and CSRD, which describe efficiency and waste heat recovery for sus­
tainable data centers, other regulations apply to refrigerants used in cooling systems. The F­gas
Regulation (EU) 2024/573 sets strict rules for the containment, use, and recovery of fluorinated
greenhouse gases (F­gases), which are commonly used in data center cooling systems. The
regulation aligns with international commitments under the Kigali Amendment by promoting re­
frigerants with low global warming potential (GWP) and ozone depletion potential (ODP) [11].
Key parameters are the inclusion of mandatory leak detection, recovery and recycling of refrig­
erants, and restrictions on high­GWP gases for servicing and maintenance of cooling systems.
By following these regulations in this study, the focus is going to be on sustainable cooling so­
lutions that comply with regulations, thereafter, using refrigerants with a GWP <3 and ODP =
0. The refrigerants considered include six natural alternatives: R718 (water), R600 (n­butane),
R600a (isobutane), R717 (ammonia), R290 (propane), and R744 (CO2).
Denmark’s government has set ambitious targets for district heating. The 2018 Energy Agree­
ment requires that by 2030, at least 90% of district heating comes from sources other than coal,
oil, or gas [12]. This initiative complements the Climate Act introduced in 2019, which aims
for total climate neutrality by 2050 and a 70% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by 2030
(compared to 1990 levels) [13]. To accelerate the transition, Denmark eliminated the surplus
heat tax on January 1, 2022, making waste heat recovery more economically viable [14].
1.6
State of Art of Heat Recovery Systems in Data Centers
1.6.1
Previous research studies
Data centers have emerged as significant prosumers in district energy systems, given their
substantial electricity consumption and corresponding waste heat generation. Huang et al. em­
phasize the dual role of data centers as both consumers and producers of thermal energy.
Data centers typically reject low­grade waste heat, generally below 40°C, yet advancements in
heat pump technologies now allow efficient upgrading of this waste heat to temperatures be­
tween 70­90°C for district heating applications, potentially reducing energy waste by up to 60%.
This study underscores the critical gap in the coordinated management of renewable energy
integration and downstream waste heat utilization, advocating comprehensive global controls
to optimize energy efficiency comprehensively [15]. Davies, Maidment, and Tozer further ex­
plored the feasibility of integrating data centers with district heating systems, showing that cou­
pling data center cooling with district heating networks using heat pumps significantly increased
heat reuse efficiency. Their findings suggest that such integration can achieve potential annual
CO2 emission reductions of approximately 3 000 to 5 000 tons per data center [16]. Similarly,
Wahlroos et al. demonstrated through simulation analyses that integrating data center waste
heat recovery with district heating remarkably decreased fossil fuel dependency by nearly 20%,
simultaneously reducing operational hours of traditional heating plants by approximately 40%,
thereby substantially promoting sustainability [17].
The environmental impact of refrigerants used in heat pump systems is critical. Refrigerants
such as CO2 (R744) and propane (R290) have emerged as environmentally friendly options
with significantly lower Global Warming Potential (GWP < 20 for R290, GWP = 1 for CO2) and
zero Ozone Depletion Potential (ODP) compared to conventional refrigerants (typically GWP

> 1000). Karimov, Korotkova, and Pavlov investigated the practical applicability of CO2 heat
pumps, noting their superior safety and negligible environmental impact. Despite high oper­
ating pressures up to 73.8 bar, CO2 heat pumps demonstrated efficiencies (COP values of
3.0­3.5) suitable for domestic hot water and space heating, particularly in moderate climates
[18]. Propane (R290), another prominent natural refrigerant, has thermodynamic properties al­
lowing high performance at very low refrigerant charges, typically 40­50% lower than traditional
refrigerants, due to its high latent heat of vaporization of approximately 425.4 kJ/kg. Investiga­
tions by Mateu­Royo et al. indicate systems using R290 achieve COP improvements of around
13% compared to traditional refrigerants like R22. Additionally, operational benefits include
approximately 50% reduced refrigerant mass flow and notably lower compressor discharge
temperatures (10­15°C reduction), positively impacting system durability and efficiency [19].
For data center applications where waste heat temperatures typically range between 30­50°C,
advanced high­temperature heat pump (HTHP) configurations have become crucial. Mateu­
Royo et al. conducted a comprehensive comparative analysis of various advanced HTHP con­
figurations employing low­GWP refrigerants. Their findings indicate two­stage cascade config­
urations are optimal for temperature lifts above 60 K, whereas single­stage cycles with econo­
mizers or parallel compression suit lifts below 50 K. Specifically, HCFO­1233zd(E) and HCFO­
1224yd(Z) emerged optimal, balancing COP (3.5­4.0) and volumetric heating capacity ( 450­550
kJ/m³) [19]. Zühlsdorf, Jensen, and Elmegaard extensively studied refrigerant selection, em­
phasizing zeotropic refrigerant mixtures’ advantages. These mixtures align temperature glides
effectively, optimizing thermodynamic cycle efficiency and potentially reducing heat exchanger
dimensions by approximately 20­30%, thus substantially decreasing investment costs [20].
Economic considerations are crucial for evaluating advanced heat pump systems feasibility
for waste heat recovery.
Studies by Cao et al. and Tan, Feng, and Wang provide exten­
sive economic analyses comparing different heat pump configurations and refrigerants. Cao
et al. demonstrated that two­stage heat pump systems employing flash tanks or economizers
achieved approximately 15­20% higher COP and exergy efficiencies and shorter payback peri­
ods ( 2­3 years less) compared to conventional single­stage systems [21]. Similarly, Tan, Feng,
and Wang compared heat pump technologies, concluding that mechanical heat pumps (MHP)
typically demonstrated 20­30% superior economic performance for high heat demand scenar­
ios, whereas absorption heat transformers (AHT) proved economically favorable (with roughly
10­15% cost savings) in scenarios with lower heat demand [22].
The reviewed literature underscores the importance of integrating data center waste heat recov­
ery with district heating through advanced heat pump technologies using natural refrigerants.
Strategic refrigerant selection, notably CO2 and propane, along with advanced configurations
like two­stage and cascade systems, provides robust solutions upgrading low­grade data cen­
ter waste heat to district heating standards. These approaches significantly enhance thermo­
dynamic performance and environmental sustainability. Economic assessments validate these
configurations’ feasibility and cost­effectiveness, aligning directly with this thesis’s objectives
to optimize waste heat recovery from refrigeration plants in data centers for sustainable dis­
trict heating integration. Collectively, these studies provide a robust theoretical and practical
foundation for further research and implementation in this domain.
1.6.2
Similar Case Studies
In Europe, particularly in Scandinavian countries, the integration of waste heat from data cen­
ters with district heating networks is becoming increasingly common. This trend is driven by
technological advancements in heat pump efficiency and the need to utilize excess heat effec­
tively within urban energy systems to reach sustainability targets. Several successful imple­
mentations across Sweden, Finland, Norway, and Denmark demonstrate the feasibility of such

projects.
One of the pioneering examples is the Bahnhof Data Centers in Stockholm (Pionen, Thule,
St Erik), where heat recovery systems were installed as early as 2014, providing a combined
capacity of 600 kW + 500 kW + 1 500 kW. The return on investment (ROI) for these systems has
been estimated at 3 to 5 years, highlighting their economic viability. In addition to these existing
systems, Bahnhof has planned the Elementica data center in Stockholm, which is expected to
supply up to 112 GWh of heat annually to the district heating network [23].
Finland has also implemented large­scale heat recovery projects. The Espoo Clean Heat initia­
tive in Espoo, Kauniainen, and Kirkkonummi aims to recover annually up to 75% of the waste
heat from Microsoft’s data centers, covering approximately 40% of the total district heating de­
mand in the area. The heat is captured year­round, with seasonal heating demand variations
affecting utilization rates [24, 25]. Thanks to the Espoo Clean Heat project a decrease of almost
400 000 tonnes of CO2 is expected, contributing to Espoo’s carbon­neutrality target by 2030
[25].
Another significant Finnish case study is in Mäntsälä, where Yandex’s data center integrates
a 4.0 MW heat pump system to supply waste heat to the local district heating network. The
system extracts heat at 40°C and boosts it to 85°C, achieving a coefficient of performance
(COP) ranging from 2.7 to 4.0 [23, 26]. Currently, it provides heating for 1 500 homes, with
plans to expand capacity to 4 000 homes. The project reduces CO2 emissions by up to 11 000
tons annually, covering most of the city’s district heating demand, with only peak loads relying
on other fuels [23, 26]. The total investment was approximately 3.5 million euros [23].
Norway has also begun leveraging data center waste heat for district heating. In Oslo, the
OSL01 data center, operated by Stack Infrastructure, has been connected to the city’s district
heating network managed by Hafslund Oslo Celsio. Originally started by DigiPlex in 2018, the
project was completed in 2022 and now exports approximately 3.5 MW of heat, equivalent to
25 GWh per year, to heat up to 5 000 homes [27]. The facility uses specially designed heat
exchangers to capture and transfer waste heat into the municipal district heating system [28].
The OSL01 data center is located in the Økern district of Oslo and was originally built as a gov­
ernment communications hub. Today, it operates as a modern data center with a total capacity
of 8 MW, and future expansions are planned to increase its heat recovery capabilities. Addition­
ally, Stack has designed its OSL03 and OSL04 facilities to support heat reuse, although these
projects are still seeking district heating customers [27, 28].
These case studies illustrate how data centers can function as integral components of sus­
tainable urban heating systems. By leveraging heat pump technologies and district heating
infrastructure, cities across Scandinavia are successfully turning excess heat into a valuable
energy resource. The scalability and efficiency of these systems provide a compelling model
for other regions seeking to decarbonize their heating sectors.
1.7
Economic Feasibility
Recovering waste heat from data centers in Denmark is both a smart financial investment and
a sustainable solution. Integrating waste heat recovery can reduce district heating costs and
overall energy system expenses by 5.1% [29]. Additionally, it is expected to cut carbon emis­
sions by 1.4%, preventing 180% of the emissions that would be generated by a non­integrated
data center [30].
Recovering low­temperature waste heat from data centers using heat pump systems and in­
tegrating it into district heating networks is emerging as an economically and environmentally
viable solution.

Regulatory trends in the European Union are pushing for better integration of data centers into
district heating systems, making waste heat recovery a future­proof investment [29]. Companies
that adopt this technology can enhance their ESG performance and attract environmentally
conscious investors. Financial incentives, such as green energy subsidies, can also help offset
initial investment costs [29]. By recovering and reusing waste heat, a data center can shift
from being a passive energy consumer to an active contributor in Denmark’s renewable energy
transition. The combination of cost savings, emission reductions, and long­term regulatory
support makes it an investment that is both strategic and responsible.
The economic feasibility of such projects is crucial for attracting investments and ensuring long­
term sustainability for both data center operators and district heating companies. Several key
economic aspects contribute to their viability:
• Cost Savings and Efficiency Benefits
Integrating waste heat recovery into district heating networks can significantly reduce opera­
tional expenses and result in system­wide cost savings of up to 5.1% [29]. Studies have shown
that utilising heat pumps for waste heat recovery can lead to cost reductions in district heat­
ing networks, improving energy efficiency and decreasing dependence on conventional heat
sources [31]. Wahlroos et al. highlighted that data center waste heat integration can lower total
heating costs between 0.6% and 7.3%, depending on the district heating configuration [31].
Additionally, Zhang et al. found that economic feasibility is further enhanced when waste heat
is incorporated into district heating networks, as it can reduce peak load demand and stabilize
energy pricing [32]. Heat pump systems provide an efficient means to upgrade low­temperature
waste heat to useful levels suitable for district heating applications, making them a viable solu­
tion for sustainable and cost­effective heat supply [31, 32].
• Return on Investment (ROI) and Net Present Value (NPV)
Economic analyses suggest that a medium­size data center investing in waste heat recovery
project, as the one analysed in this study, can achieve a Net Present Value (NPV) of €1.04M,
while large­scale data centers can reach an NPV of €16.3M [33]. The payback period for these
investments varies: vapour compression cycle­based heat pump systems can achieve simple
payback periods of 2.76 years for a 4.5 MW data center and 2.56 years for a 13.5 MW data
center [34].
• Subsidies and Financial Incentives
Several European and Danish funding programs enhance the financial feasibility of waste heat
recovery projects.
For example, the Danish District Heating Pool (Fjernvarmepuljen) provides subsidies to district
heating companies, which can indirectly benefit data centers by improving the financial terms
of heat supply contracts [35]. By partnering with district heating providers, data centers can se­
cure more favourable long­term contracts and improve their sustainability profile, making them
eligible for other green incentives. The program offers subsidies of up to 20 000 DKK per con­
verted fossil fuel heating system, with an allocated budget of DKK 265 million in 2024 and DKK
200 million in 2025.

2.1
Overview of the Methodology
This study follows a structured techno­economic methodology to evaluate the feasibility of inte­
grating two­stage heat pump systems for the recovery of waste heat from a data center and its
utilization in a district heating (DH) network in Denmark. The methodology comprises three se­
quential phases: (i) thermodynamic design modelling, (ii) design and operational performance
evaluation, and (iii) technical and financial analyses, including sensitivity assessment. The tech­
nical modelling is conducted in Engineering Equation Solver (EES), while the financial evalu­
ation is carried out in Microsoft Excel. The general aim is to identify the most technically and
financially viable system configurations using natural refrigerants under site­specific boundary
conditions provided by an industry partner.
The initial design phase involves the development of multiple heat pump configurations using
six natural refrigerants. Two principal integration strategies are considered: series and parallel
connection to the existing ammonia­based cooling system. In the series configuration, a second
stage is added on top of the existing single stage ammonia chiller, either as a booster system
(when ammonia is used in both stages) or as a cascade arrangement (when using different
refrigerants). In contrast, the parallel configuration represents a completely independent two­
stage system operating alongside the existing installation without any direct interconnection.
This allows for greater design flexibility, enabling the implementation of various internal heat
exchangers, different intercooling techniques, and alternative placements of flash tanks and
internal heat exchangers within the cycle.
All configurations are initially assessed under design conditions using thermodynamic perfor­
mance indicators, with the coefficient of performance (COP) serving as the primary criterion
for selection. The top­performing systems based on COP are then carried forward for detailed
operational modelling, which accounts for real boundary conditions and annual performance.
The system is modeled for integration into the local district heating network, where the forward
temperature ranges from 63 to 65 °C in summer and 68 to 72°C in winter, while return tem­
peratures range from 48 to 52°C in summer and 42 to 45°C in winter. These values define the
operational boundaries and influence the system sizing and component design. Following this,
the most promising configurations undergo a comprehensive financial evaluation that assesses
long­term financial viability over a 25­year system lifetime.
The financial model considers capital expenditure (CAPEX), operational expenditure (OPEX),
and revenues from heat sales to the local district heating network to calculate the net present
(NPV) value and internal rate of return (IRR) of the investment. A base discount rate of 6% is
assumed, and environmental externalities such as CO2 pricing are excluded from the revenue
model.
Finally, sensitivity analyses are performed to evaluate the robustness of the technical and finan­
cial outcomes against key uncertainties. This allows identification of risk factors and boundary
conditions under which the investment remains viable.
This integrated methodology ensures a systematic comparison of different heat pump designs
from both technical and financial perspectives, with the goal of forming optimal system selection
for the recovery of industrial waste heat in data center applications.

R717, or ammonia, also shows a wide saturation dome in the log(p)­h plot, indicating a high

R290, or propane, displays a favourable balance between environmental sustainability and per­

signals high flammability, propane is already used in commercial and residential systems with
proper mitigation strategies, such as charge limits and secure housing. Its saturated vapour line
is fairly steep compared to R600 and R600a, reducing the sensitivity to evaporation pressure
variations and simplifying superheat control in the evaporator.
R600 (n­butane) and R600a (isobutane) exhibit thermodynamic behaviour similar to R290, oper­
ating at low pressures with high efficiency. However, their saturated vapour lines in the log(p)­h
diagram bend more to the right, especially when compared to R744. This geometric feature im­
plies that pressure fluctuations during operation can more easily shift the vapour state toward
the saturation curve, increasing the risk of liquid droplets reaching the compressor. To avoid
this, higher superheating must be applied, which is known to reduce heat pump efficiency by
introducing greater irreversibility in the cycle. Like R290, these fluids are highly flammable and
must be handled with strict safety design constraints, making them less favourable in systems
where refrigerant containment and leak prevention cannot be guaranteed.
R744, or carbon dioxide, has a distinctive thermodynamic profile. The narrow saturation curve
and very low critical temperature of 31.1 °C could limit its ability to operate in standard two­
stage cycles with the temperature levels considered in this study. While its non­flammability and
extremely low GWP make it attractive from an environmental point of view, its high operating
pressures complicate system design. Components must withstand intense mechanical loads
and maintain tight sealing under transcritical conditions, especially in the gas cooler. These
conditions also limit control over temperature glide during heat rejection, potentially reducing
system performance when high­temperature district heating is required.
Thus, the decision of which refrigerant to adopt involves a careful trade­off between thermody­
namic performance, safety, environmental impact, and technical feasibility. The characteristics
visible in the log(p)­h diagram, such as vapour line curvature and enthalpy span, must be con­
sidered together with physical and chemical constraints like critical temperature, flammability,
toxicity, and pressure level. Each refrigerant presents its own set of benefits and drawbacks,
and the final selection will depend on how these factors are prioritised in relation to system
objectives and regulatory boundaries.

Detailed Description of Modeled Configurations
The two­stage parallel configuration models primarily differ in their choice of intermediate pres­
sure management and internal heat exchange mechanisms. The model TS 1 employs a closed
intercooler between the two compressors and includes an internal heat exchanger (IHX) that
facilitates heat exchange between the refrigerant exiting the closed intercooler at high pressure
(Pc) and the low­pressure (Pe) stream exiting the evaporator. This configuration is evaluated
using refrigerants R600, R600a, and R290, R718.
In contrast, the TS 2 model uses an open intercooler, which allows direct mixing of the refrigerant
at the intermediate stage. It also includes an IHX that exchanges heat between the medium­
pressure (Pm) stream exiting the open intercooler and the low­pressure evaporator exit. The
same set of hydrocarbons (R600, R600a, and R290) and R718 are assessed in this model.
The TS 3 configuration replaces the intercooler with a flash tank operating at the intermediate
pressure, where liquid and vapor phases are separated. Similar to TS 2, an IHX transfers heat
between the flash tank saturated liquid stream (at Pm) and the evaporator exit stream (at Pe).
This model is also tested with R600, R600a, R290 and R718.

IHX.
The TS Ammonia model mirrors the TS 2 setup with an open intercooler and IHX, but differs by

A distinct parallel architecture is introduced in the TS Parallel model, where two compressors

the refrigerant. This model includes an IHX between the condenser outlet and the evaporator
outlet. The SS Water NO IHX version omits this component to evaluate its performance impact.

system efficiency during the design phase. Based on COP values under the defined conditions,
the most promising configurations were selected for further operational modelling and technical
and financial analyses.

can indicate errors in energy balance equation, temperature assignments, or boundary condi­
tions in the model.
Numerical considerations
In any numerical model, it is important to assess the influence of numerical error on the results,
particularly when solving systems of nonlinear algebraic equations. In this study, all thermody­
namic models were implemented in Engineering Equation Solver (EES), which applies iterative
solvers with specified convergence criteria. The convergence tolerance was set to a relative
residual of 10−3, representing a balance between solution accuracy and computational effi­
ciency.
While it might appear beneficial to use the lowest possible solver tolerance to minimize resid­
uals, this approach is not computationally efficient. Reducing the solver tolerance significantly
increases the number of iterations required to achieve convergence, with diminishing returns
in terms of result accuracy. A simple sensitivity test was carried out on based on tolerances.
The resulting changes in outlet temperatures and heat loads were negligible beyond the third or
fourth significant digit, indicating that excessively tight tolerances yield no practical benefit for
engineering calculations with typical input data uncertainties.

In order to improve the overall efficiency of the heat pump system and to increase the refrigerant

The IHX facilitates heat exchange between the cold, low­pressure vapor (leaving the evapora­
tor), and the hot, high­pressure subcooled liquid (leaving the condenser).
This internal energy recovery improves the cycle by:
• providing additional superheating at the compressor inlet (protecting the compressor from
liquid droplets),
• increasing subcooling of the liquid before entering the expansion valve, therefore improv­
ing the Coefficient of Performance (COP).

2.3
Operational Modelling of Configurations
The Operational modelling builds directly upon the outcomes of the design model to evaluate
the performance of selected heat pump configurations under realistic operational conditions.
Although the primary purpose of the design model was to determine the ideal component sizes
based on a set of fixed input parameters, such as the heat source and sink temperatures, the
heat loads and the target temperature lifts, the operational model evaluates how these systems
perform when exposed to actual variable operational conditions. In practice, these conditions
often deviate from nominal design points due to seasonal fluctuations, partial load operation,
and the unavoidable selection of commercially available hardware components whose charac­
teristics differ slightly from idealized calculations.
In the design model, each component was ideally dimensioned to meet predefined performance
criteria precisely. This resulted in idealized UA­values for heat exchangers and specific com­
pressor characteristics such as swept volume. Conversely, the operational model involves the
selection and implementation of real components, based on catalog data and manufacturer
specifications, thus introducing a degree of mismatch between the ideal design and the practi­
cal configuration. This approach allows for more accurate predictions of system performance,
providing insights into how the three best­performing configurations from the design stage be­
have under realistic conditions.

The following sections describe in detail how each major component was transitioned from de­
sign to operational modelling, highlighting both the methodological changes and the implications
for system performance evaluation.
Heat­exchangers
In the design modelling phase, the heat exchangers (evaporator and condenser) were sized
using an idealised approach. These values were obtained from predefined pinch temperature
differences and target thermal capacities, ensuring precise control over the design conditions.
However, during operational modelling, the heat exchanger areas determined in the design
phase were used as a basis for selecting commercially available heat exchanger units from
manufacturer catalogues. After matching the design­calculated heat exchanger area with a
suitable real component, the physical heat transfer surface area from the chosen component
replaced the idealised UA­values within the model.
To achieve greater accuracy in simulating actual heat exchanger performance under variable
input conditions, the modelling methodology was refined. The previously used simplified heat
exchanger approach was replaced by a discretised heat exchanger model. This method divides
the heat exchanger into multiple discrete segments or control volumes, solving energy balances
and heat transfer equations iteratively along the flow path. This segmented modelling enhances
the precision of heat transfer calculations, particularly under operational conditions where local
temperature gradients, varying flow rates, and thermodynamic property changes significantly
affect performance.

When selecting the plate heat exchangers for the heat pump system, gasketed plate heat ex­
changers (GPHE) were preferred and chosen where commercially available in the required
sizes, as determined from the system design models with the selected compressors already in­
tegrated. This preference was based on several technical and operational considerations. First,
the district heating network connected to the system is expected to expand over time, which may
lead to increased heat loads or changes in operating temperatures. GPHEs offer the advantage
Techno­Economic Analysis of Heat Pump Configurations for Waste Heat Recovery in Data Centers

==PAGE:33==
of modularity, allowing additional plates to be added to the frame if higher capacity is required
in the future, without the need to replace the entire unit.
Second, although both the data center cooling loop and the district heating system operate
with clean water, long­term use may still necessitate occasional cleaning or inspection of the
heat exchangers. GPHEs can be opened for maintenance, whereas BPHEs are sealed and do
not permit internal access once installed. This serviceability is particularly valuable in district
heating applications where operational continuity is important.
Third, the potential use of either R600a or R717 refrigerants influenced the decision. While
R600a is compatible with standard BPHEs, R717 (ammonia) is not compatible with copper­
brazed exchangers due to material compatibility issues. In contrast, GPHEs with stainless steel
plates are suitable for both refrigerants, providing greater flexibility in refrigerant selection and
future system upgrades.
Finally, the high­temperature operation of the system, delivering heat up to 75°C to the district
heating network, further supports the use of GPHEs, which are well suited for such temperature
levels. In cases where GPHEs were not commercially available in the required size, alternative
heat exchanger types were considered to ensure the necessary heat transfer performance could
be achieved. In sum, the use of GPHEs where possible enhances the system’s adaptability,
maintainability, and long­term reliability.
Compressor
During the design phase, the compressor was modeled based on idealized assumptions, includ­
ing constant isentropic and volumetric efficiencies, to precisely match the required cooling load
and mass flow rates. In contrast, the operational model implements a compressor chosen from
actual commercial offerings. This real compressor is selected based on the swept volume and
nominal capacity derived from the design model calculations. Instead of relying on idealized ef­
ficiency assumptions, the performance characteristics of the selected compressor are obtained
from manufacturer­provided compressor maps or empirical polynomial correlations. These cor­
relations link the isentropic efficiency, volumetric efficiency, and power consumption to critical
parameters such as suction and discharge pressures, suction temperatures, and compressor
speed.
The incorporation of a real compressor introduces more accurate and realistic behavior into
the operational simulation. This approach allows for modelling of variations in compressor per­
formance due to partial loads, off­nominal operating points, and constraints like maximum dis­
charge temperatures, allowable pressure ratios, and speed limitations. Consequently, the oper­
ational model captures the degradation in compressor performance and efficiency that naturally
occurs when operating conditions deviate from the design reference points.
When transitioning from a design to an operational compressor model, additional equations
must be introduced to accurately capture the compressor’s performance under varying oper­
ating conditions. Specifically, polynomial expressions are employed to estimate the isentropic
efficiency and volumetric efficiency of the real compressor as functions of relevant parameters,
including rotational speed, suction pressure, and inlet/outlet temperatures.

2.5.2
Financial Sensitivity Analysis
In parallel to the technical sensitivity analysis, a one­way (univariate) financial sensitivity analy­
sis was performed to explore the influence of key economic and operational parameters on the
NPV of each system configuration. This analysis focuses on financial viability and investment
risk, rather than technical performance, and seeks to identify which variables most strongly
affect project profitability, as well as how each model responds under changing external condi­
tions.
The parameters analysed include:
• Heat price
• Electricity price
• Capital Expenditure (CAPEX)
• Operation and Maintenance (O&M) costs (excluding electricity)
• Discount rate
• Full Load Hours (FLH)
The selected variation ranges reflect realistic uncertainties, policy­driven dynamics, and con­
servative assumptions based on market conditions and industry benchmarks:
• Heat price: ±50%, chosen due to the difficulty in forecasting long­term district heating
tariffs and the central role of heat sales in project revenue. This broad range ensures
coverage of both favourable and pessimistic market scenarios.
• Electricity price: ±50%, reflecting its volatility and significant influence on OPEX. Since
electricity is the main operational input, changes in its cost can drastically affect NPV.
• CAPEX: −20% to +20%, accounting for uncertainty in capital cost estimates. The lower
bound is justified by the use of list prices in the baseline, which may overestimate real
investment costs due to potential supplier discounts or economies of scale.
• O&M costs (excluding electricity): −20% / +10%, due to the conservative baseline
derived from Danish Technology Catalogue [44] for a 1 MW heat pump. While some
operational optimisation may yield minor savings, cost increases are more probable in
practice.

• Discount rate: Varied from −1% to +3% around the baseline, resulting in an absolute
range of 5­9%. This captures a realistic spread of financing conditions and investor ex­
pectations in the energy infrastructure sector.
• Full Load Hours (FLH): −25% to +20%, building on a base case that already assumes

Fjernvarme Fyn’s data center project) to a highly optimized case with only 4% downtime

In addition to the one­way analysis, a two­way sensitivity analysis was also conducted for NPV
as a function of both heat price and electricity price simultaneously. This was motivated by the
interdependence of these two variables, which are often affected by common market forces,
policy decisions, or energy transition dynamics. Since heat revenues and electricity costs are
the dominant components of cash inflow and outflow, respectively, their combined variation
offers a more realistic picture of financial risk than either parameter alone. The results were
visualised using a heatmap, which highlights regions of economic feasibility or vulnerability
depending on the joint movement of these key prices.

• Combo charts, combining parameter variation bars with NPV trend lines for each model,

• Cartesian spider plots, to show the continuous sensitivity of NPV across each parame­

• Tornado diagrams, to rank the parameters based on the maximum deviation they pro­
duce in NPV, indicating their relative importance.
• Heatmaps (for two­way sensitivity), to illustrate how combined fluctuations in heat and
electricity prices influence NPV and model competitiveness.
This financial sensitivity framework complements the technical analysis by addressing the investment­
side uncertainty and provides actionable insights for stakeholders, particularly in terms of market
exposure and economic risk mitigation.

4.1
Design Models
The resulting performances obtained for each design model are shown, respectively for Parallel
and Series Configurations, in Table 3.1 and 3.2.
The higher power consumption displayed by the Parallel Configuration models in these tables,
approximately double that of the Series configurations, is linked to the distinct design capacities
of each system. The Series models were developed with a cooling load of 100 kW each, with
an expectation that two such units would be implemented in the actual plant to meet the overall
demand. In contrast, the Parallel configurations are designed to individually handle the full
200 kW cooling load of the process due to their placement and operational capabilities within
the system.
It is important to note that the results for the TS CO2 and Cascade CO2 design models are
not included in these tables, and the corresponding cells are marked as ’n.d.’ (not determined).
This exclusion is due to inherent thermodynamic limitations encountered during their simulation.
Specifically, the cascade system with CO2 (R744) in the high stage was found to operate in a su­
percritical state already at the evaporator side. This condition leads to substantial inefficiencies,
primarily because heat transfer occurs solely as sensible heat, without phase change, which is
detrimental to the overall cycle performance in a heat pump application aiming for efficient latent
heat utilisation.
Attempts were made to mitigate this problem by increasing the pinch temperature difference
(∆Tpinch) between the low and high stages within the evaporator­condenser component. The
objective was to force the CO2 evaporator into a subcritical regime. However, achieving subcrit­
ical CO2 operation in this context would require evaporator temperatures below its critical tem­
perature of 31°C. Such a low­temperature range proved unfeasible, as it would bring the high­
stage evaporation temperature too close to the low­stage evaporation temperature, thereby
critically compromising the overall system’s efficiency and practicality.
Additional attempts were made to model the TS CO2 configuration in order to address a similar
issue. In this case, the problem emerged at the intermediate pressure level. Applying the same
modelling principles used for the other refrigerants led to an optimal pressure situated in the
supercritical region. However, this rendered the cycle configuration unfeasible, as phase sep­
aration between liquid and gas is not possible in the supercritical domain at this intermediate
pressure. This absence of phase separation fundamentally disrupts the intended mass flow
distribution of the two­stage cycle. Consequently, the system would not operate as designed;
all mass flow would be directed through the smaller compressor (from intermediate pressure Pm
to high pressure Pc), with virtually no refrigerant flowing to the low­pressure evaporator. This
effectively prevents the system from providing the necessary cooling to the data center. To miti­
gate this issue, the same approach was employed by lowering the intermediate pressure below
the critical point. This resulted in a configuration where the intermediate and evaporation pres­
sures became closely aligned, due to the evaporator operating near the critical pressure. This
iterative modelling process highlighted the challenges associated with TS CO2 and ultimately
indicated that a single­stage configuration is the most viable under the given conditions.
A better comparison can be done by looking at Figure 3.1, which displays the resulting yearly
average COP of the design models evaluated, excluding the ones that turned out to be highly
inefficient.
Techno­Economic Analysis of Heat Pump Configurations for Waste Heat Recovery in Data Centers

==PAGE:62==
A primary observation from Figure 3.1 is the significant variability in COP across the evaluated
designs. COP values range from approximately 3.40 (or as low as 2.50 when highly inefficient
models are included) to over 4.10 under identical boundary conditions, specifically the source
and sink temperatures.
This broad range underlines the critical influence of both system architecture and refrigerant
selection on the overall energy efficiency of a heat pump system of this kind. Generally, two­
stage (TS) systems demonstrate a tendency towards higher COPs compared to the cascade or
single­stage (SS) configurations, particularly evident in the superior performance achieved by
hydrocarbon refrigerants within many TS setups. This suggests an inherent efficiency advan­
tage of two­stage operation under the specified conditions.
The cascade and single­stage (SS) systems, including Cascade_other_refrigerants, SS CO2,
SS Water, and SS Water NO IHX, consistently exhibit relatively lower COPs, predominantly
remaining below 3.60. This indicates that these configurations, under the defined operating
parameters, are less energetically efficient than the more advanced two­stage designs.
In contrast, the two­stage (TS) system configurations (e.g., TS 1, TS 2, TS 3) generally demon­
strate a notable improvement in COP. Several configurations within these groups achieve COPs
exceeding 3.90, highlighting the efficiency benefits inherent to two­stage operation for the spe­
cific refrigerants assessed. Within these TS categories, the hydrocarbon refrigerants, namely
R290 (Propane), R600 (Butane), and R600a (Isobutane), consistently yield the highest COPs,
frequently exceeding 4.0. This compellingly suggests their particular suitability for two­stage
heat pump applications in terms of energy efficiency under the prevailing design conditions.
Ammonia (R717) based systems, such as TS Ammonia and TS Ammonia series, present com­
petitive yet typically slightly lower COPs when compared to the peak­performing hydrocarbon
two­stage systems. The COPs for R717 in these categories generally range from 3.90 to 3.95,
affirming its robust performance potential as a highly efficient refrigerant, although not reaching
the absolute maximum observed in this analysis. Furthermore, the TS Parallel configurations,
especially when employing R290, R600, and R600a, achieve some of the highest COPs (e.g.,
>4.0), suggesting that parallel compression or similar architectural enhancements can further
optimise the energetic efficiency for specific refrigerants.
A detailed examination reveals the impact of specific design features. Comparing TS 1 with TS
1 NO IHX, TS 2 with TS 2 NO IHX, and TS 3 with TS 3 NO IHX, it becomes apparent that the
incorporation of an Internal Heat Exchanger (IHX) generally leads to a marginal, but consistent,
improvement in COP for most refrigerants (e.g., for R290, R600a, R600). This incremental
enhancement substantiates the theoretical advantages of subcooling liquid refrigerant before
expansion. However, a notable exception is observed for R718, where the removal of the IHX
appears to slightly increase COP, in all the models except ’TS Parallel’ and ’TS Parallel NO
IHX’.
The behaviour of water and ammonia models differs significantly from that of the other working
fluids when an internal heat exchanger (IHX) is introduced. Specifically, as shown in Figure 3.1,
the addition of the IHX leads to a decrease in the average annual COP of the system. This is
primarily due to the increased inlet temperature at the compressor (the low­stage one for TS
systems), resulting from the IHX raising the refrigerant temperature after the evaporator outlet.
For water and ammonia at these high temperatures in the superheated vapour region, the isen­
tropic lines lean further to the right on the log(P­h) diagram. This characteristic means that
an increase in the inlet temperature to the first compressor directly results in a larger enthalpy
difference for the compression. As a result, the real work that needs to be provided by the
compressor rises, ultimately causing a decrease in the system’s COP.
Techno­Economic Analysis of Heat Pump Configurations for Waste Heat Recovery in Data Centers
57
==PAGE:63==
In models such as TS Ammonia, TS 1, TS 2, TS 3, and SS Water, with an IHX, the internal heat
exchanger is placed between the low pressure (Pe) at the exit of the evaporator and the inter­
mediate pressure (Pm) after the first expansion valve. This placement ensures superheating
after evaporation. However, it may also contribute to the observed COP reduction in systems
utilising the IHX compared to those without it.
Increased superheating in heat pump systems generally enhances cooling capacity but raises
compressor work, particularly for water and ammonia, as explained above, but does not con­
tribute to increased heating capacity. As a result, it does not improve (and even reduce) the
system’s COP.
In terms of refrigerant performance within systems, R290, R600, and R600a consistently emerge
as dominant performers across the high­performing TS systems. Their ability to deliver the high­
est COPs highlights their significant energetic advantages. Mid­range performers include R717,
which demonstrates strong competitiveness, and R718 (Water), which exhibits more varied per­
formance depending on the specific system. Although these last two may not always reach the
peak COP of pure hydrocarbon systems, they often provide robust performance and may offer
practical advantages in terms of pressure, flammability, or toxicity, making them relevant for
holistic design considerations.
In summary, the highest COPs (exceeding 4.0) are consistently achieved by the two­stage
systems utilising pure R290, R600, and R600a. These configurations represent the most ener­
getically efficient designs under the evaluated conditions. R717 (Ammonia) also demonstrates
strong performance, with COPs closely approaching those of the leading hydrocarbons. These
findings provide compelling evidence for the strategic selection of specific system architectures
and refrigerants to maximise energy efficiency in industrial heat pump applications, particularly
emphasising the superior energetic performance of hydrocarbons and ammonia for sustainable
design.
It is crucial to acknowledge that these COPs are derived from model­based calculations and do
not inherently account for practical considerations such as pressure drops, control complexities,
or the safety regulations associated with flammable or toxic refrigerants. A comprehensive and
practical design discussion would necessitate the integration of these crucial aspects with the
presented energetic performance. Future research could extend this analysis through detailed
exergy evaluations to identify sources of irreversibility.
In Table 3.3, the best 10 performing design models are presented based on their annual average
COP. The top 3 performing configurations were TS 3 (with R600 and R600a) and TS Parallel
NO IHX (with R600), all achieving an average annual COP higher than 4.1 .
When proceeding to operational models, other factors such as commercial availability, safety
concerns, and specific operational constraints are also crucial to take into account; a single de­
cision based on energetic performance is not sufficient. While evaluating these broader factors,
specific models were critically reviewed. All models utilising R600 (n­butane) were ultimately
not selected for further operational modelling due to the scarce commercial availability of com­
pressors specifically designed for R600. Additionally, the TS Parallel NO IHX configuration
presented a high risk of droplet intake in the compressor. This is because the lack of an internal
heat exchanger at the intermediate pressure before the compression of the saturated vapour
coming from the flash tank could cause the initiation of compression in the two­phase region,
even with slight variations in pressure or temperature. Such two­phase compression can lead
to severe damage to the compressor and reduced operational longevity. To avoid such risks
and ensure safe and reliable operation, the integration of an IHX is therefore considered indis­
pensable for these designs.
Techno­Economic Analysis of Heat Pump Configurations for Waste Heat Recovery in Data Centers

==PAGE:64==
The isentropic efficiency regression model yielded lower R2 values for certain compressors (0.7
lowest), indicating a poor fit between the predicted and actual values. This discrepancy can be
attributed to several factors. Firstly, the regression model is based on a fixed polynomial struc­
ture that may not fully capture the true efficiency behavior of compressors exhibiting nonlinear,
stepwise, or threshold­dependent performance trends. Secondly, the model does not account
for compressor­specific characteristics such as internal valve dynamics, oil interactions, or con­
trol strategies, which can significantly influence performance but are not represented in the input
features. Additionally, for compressors operating over a narrow range or having nearly constant
efficiency across conditions, the signal­to­noise ratio becomes too low for the model to identify
meaningful patterns. In such cases, the use of more flexible modelling techniques, such as
piecewise regression, refrigerant­specific models, or non­linear approaches like Gaussian pro­
cesses or neural networks, could potentially improve predictive accuracy. Moreover, expanding
the input parameter set to include additional thermodynamic or geometric variables could allow
for better compressor­specific model calibration and generalization.
So, the final models developed for operational analysis are TS 3 with R600a (iso­butane), TS
Parallel with R600a (iso­butane), and TS Ammonia NO IHX with R717 (ammonia).
Although the configuration TS Ammonia Series NO IHX exhibited strong performance, with an
average annual COP close to that of the top­performing models, it was deliberately excluded
from further development into operational modelling. This decision was driven by several in­
terrelated practical and strategic considerations. Firstly, implementing this configuration would
require significant modification of the existing ammonia­based chillers in the plant to enable a
high­stage series connection. Such modifications inherently introduce greater system complex­
ity and increase safety risks. From a reliability standpoint, the series arrangement introduces
potential single points of failure, which could translate into longer system downtimes during
maintenance or unexpected shutdowns. For data center operations, where continuous uptime
and high reliability are of paramount importance, such risk profiles are generally unacceptable.
In contrast, the selected parallel configurations provide inherent redundancy, as the data center
can remain operational with the original chillers while the parallel system is serviced, thereby of­
fering an additional safety layer. Given these factors, the practical drawbacks in terms of safety,
operational risk, and data center reliability outweighed the benefits, rendering this configuration
unsuitable for further operational development.
This exclusion of the absolute top­performing models (which were primarily R600­based) had
a slight negative effect on the chosen best 3 models’ performance compared to the original
highest­ranking configurations. However, considering the strong similarity in thermodynamic
properties and performance between R600 and R600a, the difference in COP caused by this
decision is quite small. Even for the lowest chosen configuration (TS Ammonia NO IHX, with an
annual COP of 4.02), it only meant a 3.05% reduction in its average annual COP compared to
the best design model (TS 3 with R600 at 4.15). This trade­off is deemed acceptable considering
the enhanced practicality, safety, and commercial viability of the selected models, which are
essential for the successful long­term implementation and operation of the industrial heat pump
system.

Operational Models
As noted in the results section, TS 3 exhibits distinct performance characteristics compared to
the other models when evaluated against their respective design configurations. This deviation
is primarily linked to the compressor selection and its associated isentropic efficiency. Under
operational conditions, TS 3 demonstrates a noticeably lower isentropic efficiency than under
design conditions. This reduction is largely due to the fact that, for the low­stage compressor
in TS 3, the nearest commercially available unit with suitable operational parameters was over­
Techno­Economic Analysis of Heat Pump Configurations for Waste Heat Recovery in Data Centers
59
==PAGE:65==
sised by nearly 50%. Consequently, the compressor must operate under part­load conditions
with significantly reduced rotational speeds, which leads to an efficiency drop to approximately
60%. Improving TS 3’s performance would therefore require identifying a better­matched com­
pressor for the low stage, ideally one that operates closer to its optimal load conditions. En­
hancing compressor efficiency in this manner would directly reduce power consumption and
lead to a higher overall coefficient of performance (COP) for the system.
In both TS 3 and TS Parallel, similar behaviour is observed at the intermediate pressure level.
Since the subcooling is maximised after the condenser in both configurations, the state entering
the two­phase region lies close to the saturated liquid line. This results in a low vapour frac­
tion and, consequently, a reduced mass flow of saturated vapour from the flash tanks. This
phenomenon is particularly relevant to the intermediate flash gas bypass and will be examined
further through technical sensitivity analysis to better understand its impact on system perfor­
mance.
In TS Parallel, the compression lines on the log(p)–h diagram appear to overlap due to dif­
ferences in isentropic efficiency between the compressors. The smaller compressor exhibits
significantly lower efficiency, which causes its performance line to converge toward that of the
larger, more efficient compressor. This behaviour resembles the condition in TS 3, where the
only commercially available low­stage compressor with suitable characteristics was approx­
imately 50% oversized. As a result, the compressor operates continuously under part­load
conditions, leading to reduced efficiency. The difference in mass flow rate is also evident when
comparing the internal heat exchanger (IHX) segments between state points 4–5 and 8–9. The
path between points 8–9 is wider, reflecting the higher mass flow rate on the hot side and the
smaller temperature difference between point 8 and point 4. Despite the relatively small tem­
perature increase achieved, the IHX performs a critical role. This is supported by the log(p)–h
diagram, which shows that the IHX successfully ensures that the compressor inlet conditions
are shifted toward the superheated vapour region, thus preventing liquid entrainment and en­
hancing system safety and stability.
In contrast, TS Ammonia does not require an internal heat exchanger to prevent droplet forma­
tion prior to compression. This is attributed to the favourable thermophysical properties of am­
monia. Furthermore, the orientation of the isenthalpic lines in the superheated region, leaning
more steeply to the right, indicates that additional superheating would result in a proportionally
higher compressor work input compared to R600a­based configurations. In practical applica­
tions, a small degree of superheating is typically always present as a natural consequence of
heat transfer processes and system layout. This helps ensure that the refrigerant enters the
compressor as a superheated vapour, thereby reducing the risk of liquid entrainment and im­
proving operational reliability. However, it is important to note that ammonia systems operate
at significantly higher pressure levels than hydrocarbon­based alternatives. This may introduce
greater mechanical and sealing stresses, making component durability a critical consideration.
Over time, material fatigue, joint integrity, and valve wear could become key design and main­
tenance concerns in high­pressure ammonia systems.

tween 6.35 and 6.9 EUR/MWh for a 25­year lifespan) remain significantly below the estimated
heat sales price of approximately 34 EUR/MWh, providing substantial margins for project suc­
cess. However, the long payback periods suggest that project success will depend on securing
stable, long­term commercial agreements and potentially accessing available financial incen­
tives to improve short­term cash flows.
The impact of various market and operational factors on these financial outcomes is further
explored in the subsequent sensitivity analysis.
4.4
Sensitivity Analyses
Technical Sensitivities
The results from the technical sensitivity analysis provide valuable insights into how key param­
eters influence system performance, particularly the coefficient of performance (COP). Among
the investigated variables, ˙Qe shows a strong and direct correlation with COP within the anal­
ysed range. An increase in ˙Qe decreases COP by increasing the total compression work. Given
that the heat load from data center is expected to only increase in the future, this trend suggests
a likely negative impact on COP over time if capacity is increased.
The subcooling temperature difference, ∆Tsc, emerges as a particularly critical parameter. Al­
though its sensitivity per unit change is relatively low, the broader range over which it is varied
makes it a highly influential factor in overall system performance. A reduction in ∆Tsc leads to
a notable decrease in specific condenser heat, sQc, which falls disproportionately compared to
the corresponding decrease in sWtot. This is primarily due to the increase in vapour content
entering the flash tank, resulting in a higher mass flow rate entering the high­stage compres­
sor. While this condition shifts the compression process closer to the saturated vapour line,
slightly improving isentropic efficiency, the benefit is negligible when compared to the losses
incurred. Specifically, the enthalpy difference across the condenser decreases as a result of
the elevated condenser outlet temperature associated with lower subcooling. The net effect is
a major reduction in sQc and a minor decrease in sWtot, ultimately reducing the COP.
In the case of TS Parallel, this negative effect is even more pronounced. The increase in mass
flow rate is directed towards a smaller, over­dimensioned compressor, which operates at lower
efficiency and consumes more electricity. As a result, the COP for TS Parallel decreases by
almost 0.4 over the analysed ∆Tsc range, whereas the other configurations exhibit a significantly
smaller reduction, in the range of approximately 0.2. These findings clearly demonstrate that

maximising ∆Tsc is beneficial for system efficiency, and it should therefore be prioritised in both
system design and operational control strategies.
Another noteworthy observation is the pronounced impact of varying heat load from the data
center on the performance of TS Ammonia. This sensitivity is primarily driven by the system’s
compressor power consumption. Compared to the other configurations, the compressors in TS
Ammonia exhibit a significantly larger increase in power demand when

in Figure 3.5f. This behaviour can be attributed to the thermodynamic properties of ammonia,
as illustrated in its log(p)–h diagram in Figure 3.2c. The isentropic lines in the superheated
region tend to lean more steeply to the right, indicating that even slight reductions in isentropic
efficiency can lead to substantial increases in compressor work. As a result, TS Ammonia is
more susceptible to performance losses under suboptimal compression conditions, particularly
when subject to fluctuating data center heat loads.
Regarding Tsink,in, its absolute effect on system performance is relatively small, as shown in
the tornado diagrams. The system demonstrates good robustness to moderate variations in
sink temperature. Nonetheless, the observed trend consistently shows a decrease in COP with
increasing Tsink,in values. This behaviour is attributed to a combination of factors: the rise in
condenser outlet temperature leads to a lower enthalpy difference across the condenser, while
the increased vapour quality entering the flash tank raises the high­stage compressor mass
flow rate insignificantly raises its efficiency. In parallel, the increase in temperature difference at
the condenser pinch point (∆Tpinch) also contributes to a reduction in system efficiency. Overall,
while the effect of Tsink,in is less pronounced compared to ˙Qe and ∆Tsc, it follows a consistent and
unfavourable trend with increasing sink temperatures. This trend aligns with thermodynamic
expectations, as a higher sink temperature increases the temperature lift, thus requiring more
compressor work for the same heat output.
Based on the overall sensitivity trends, TS 3 appears to be the most robust configuration with
respect to COP sensitivity to variations in the key technical parameters. While it shows mod­
erate sensitivity to changes in ˙Qe and ∆Tsc, the absolute impact on COP is smaller compared
to TS Parallel and TS Ammonia. In particular, the response of TS 3 to subcooling decrease is
less severe, and the system maintains relatively stable performance across a range of ˙Qe. In
contrast, TS Parallel exhibits the highest sensitivity to subcooling, due to the inefficiencies in­
troduced by its over­dimensioned low­stage compressor. Similarly, TS Ammonia demonstrates
high sensitivity to changes in evaporator heat load, which can be traced to the steep isenthalpic
behaviour of ammonia and its resulting compressor work increase under non­ideal conditions.
Therefore, when technical robustness is a design priority, particularly under conditions of oper­
ational uncertainty, TS 3 offers the most stable and reliable performance among the evaluated
configurations.

4.4.1
Overall Sensitivity Findings
Integrating the technical and financial analyses provides a comprehensive foundation for as­
sessing the most suitable heat pump configuration for waste heat recovery from data centers.
Technically, the TS 3 model demonstrates notable robustness against parameter variations
such as heat load from data center ( ˙Qe) and subcooling temperature difference (∆Tsc). Despite
its stable performance under fluctuating operational conditions, its oversized low­stage com­
pressor significantly reduces energy efficiency and consequently financial attractiveness. On
the other hand, the TS Ammonia configuration, while thermodynamically effective, exhibits high
sensitivity to cooling load variations due to ammonia’s isentropic lines inclination to the right,
resulting in substantial increases in compressor power consumption and decreased operational
stability.
Financially, TS Parallel consistently emerges as the superior model, delivering favourable eco­
nomic indicators such as higher Net Present Values and shorter payback periods, compared to
the other models, under varying market and operational conditions. This advantage stems from
an optimal combination of lower capital expenditures, high operational COP, and reduced sen­
sitivity to fluctuations in key cash flow parameters, including heat and electricity prices. Thus,
from a strictly economic perspective, TS Parallel represents the most robust and financially
sustainable option.
However, practical considerations, particularly safety and operational reliability, must heavily in­
fluence model selection for data center applications. While R600a­based systems (TS 3 and TS
Parallel) excel in energetic and financial terms, they inherently pose significant safety risks due
to the flammability and explosion hazards associated with the refrigerant itself and the accumu­
lation of it in the flash tank. These configurations necessitate tighter risk mitigation measures,
sophisticated monitoring, advanced control systems, and rigorous adherence to safety proto­
cols. The critical nature of data center operations, prioritising uninterrupted functionality and
utmost reliability, highlights the importance of thoroughly addressing these safety challenges.
Ammonia­based systems, despite avoiding flammability issues, introduce alternative complex­
ities, including higher operating pressures, material fatigue concerns, and toxicity risks.
All things considered, the TS Parallel configuration employing R600a arises as the most bal­
anced choice, delivering robust financial performance, moderate sensitivity to technical param­
eter changes, and high energy efficiency. Nonetheless, the implementation of this model must
be accompanied by meticulous safety planning and operational risk management strategies to
mitigate the associated hazards effectively. Alternatively, when absolute reliability, safety, and
risk minimisation are the primary considerations, as is typically the case in data center applica­

5
Conclusion
This thesis has explored the technical and economical viability of advanced heat pump con­
figurations for the recovery of waste heat from data center operations, with the specific aim
of reintegrating the recovered energy into existing district heating infrastructure. By modelling
a realistic case study based in Denmark, the study offers a robust and transferable method­
ology applicable to similar industrial settings. Through an extensive design evaluation, two­
stage configurations, particularly those employing hydrocarbon refrigerants such as R600a and
R600, were found to deliver the highest coefficients of performance (COP), with several con­
figurations exceeding COP values of 4.0. Ammonia­based configurations also demonstrated
strong performance. Three configurations were selected for detailed operational modelling: TS
3 (R600a), TS Parallel (R600a), and TS Ammonia (R717). Operational analysis revealed that
TS 3 maintained robust thermodynamic performance across varying technical conditions but
was hindered by reduced isentropic efficiency due to persistent part­load compressor opera­
tion. This limitation arises from the unavailability of a commercially suitable compressor with
a closer capacity match, resulting in the selected unit being significantly oversized and there­
fore operating under part­load conditions even in the base case scenario. TS Parallel exhibited
both strong technical behaviour and the highest financial viability, largely due to its favourable
CAPEX­to­performance ratio. In contrast, TS Ammonia delivered reliable operation with mini­
mal risk of droplet entrainment but at a cost of reduced profitability. Technical sensitivity analysis
identified the subcooling temperature difference (∆Tsc) and evaporator heat load ( ˙Qe) as the
most impactful parameters on COP, while financial sensitivity analysis confirmed the dominant
role of heat price, electricity price, and system availability in determining project profitability. TS
3 emerged as the most technically stable model, while TS Parallel proved to be the most finan­
cially robust. Despite the strong technical and financial performance of R600a­based systems,
their flammability presents a significant safety concern, especially in flash tank configurations.
This is particularly critical for data center applications, where system reliability and risk mitiga­
tion are non­negotiable. As such, while TS Parallel with R600a may offer the best technical and
financial performance under controlled conditions, it must be paired with rigorous safety sys­
tems. In cases where safety and reliability are the overriding priorities, TS Ammonia represents
a strong, even if less profitable, alternative. Overall, the findings of this thesis advocate for
the careful balancing of technical performance, economic returns, and operational safety in the
design of industrial heat pump systems. The proposed methodology, combining rigorous mod­
elling with empirical data and sensitivity analysis, provides a solid foundation for future waste
heat recovery projects in data­intensive sectors.
