Abstract
The effect of roving on monaural and binaural localization ability of single­sided deaf
(SSD) listeners and the interplay of monaural and binaural cues was examined. Seven
SSD patients localized sound coming from seven locations between ­90° and 90°. Lo­
calization was evaluated with no roving, level roving, spectral roving, and level­spectral
roving. All four roving conditions were evaluated without and with activated cochlear im­
plant. In the monaural condition, roving only had an impact on patients being able to use
monaural cues. In the binaural condition, performance of six patients was impacted by
at least one of the roving conditions. Furthermore, it was shown that roving can impact
localization without being captured in the root­mean­square error. If monaural cues com­
plement binaural cues remained unclear. However, better monaural localization ability
resulted in better binaural performance.

Introduction
Everyday we rely heavily on binaural cues to understand each other in noisy environments
or to orientate ourselves in acoustic scenes by localizing different sound sources. These
binaural cues are the interaural level difference (ILD) and the interaural time difference
(ITD) which dominate horizontal sound localization (Macpherson & Middlebrooks, 2002).
People with single­sided deafness (SSD) miss these binaural cues and have to rely on
monaural cues which for people with normal hearing (NH) mainly facilitate elevation judg­
ment of a sound source. Therefore, horizontal localization is especially degraded for SSD
patients (Slattery & Middlebrooks, 1994). A cochlear implant (CI) is a potential technical
solution to help SSD patients in horizontal sound localization by restoring binaural hearing.
Multiple studies suggest that on average CIs improve sound localization for SSD patients
(Bernstein et al., 2021; Oh et al., 2022; Sullivan et al., 2019). Generally, their binaural per­
formance is similar to bilateral CI users (Dorman et al., 2016). The binaural benefit mainly
relies on restored access to ILDs as the signal processing in CIs encodes the envelope
information rather than the temporal fine structure (Dorman et al., 2015; Seebacher et al.,
2022).
Predicting the created ILD perceived by an SSD patient with a CI (SSD­CI) is challenging.
The CI maps the dynamic range of incoming sound to the dynamic range of the electric
stimulation. This mapping and the exact placement of the electrodes is patient­specific
and each single electrode often has a different mapping. Additionally, CIs incorporate
different signal processing which historically rather focuses on improving speech intelli­
gibility than spatial hearing and can be adaptive to the particular incoming sound (Zeng
et al., 2008).
While on average the benefit of a CI for sound localization of SSD patients is clear, findings
by Litovsky et al. (2019) show high inter­individual differences in monaural and binaural lo­
calization performance. Some patients are able to accurately localize monaurally and/or
binaurally, while others show no monaural localization ability or worse binaural perfor­
mance than others. This suggests different use of monaural level and spectral cues and
different interactions with binaural cues between subjects. Previous work has been done
to group patients based explicitly on their monaural and binaural performance (Buss et al.,
2018; Dillon et al., 2017). However, they did not conclude on an individual level on how
monaural and binaural outcomes might relate to each other.
One goal of the present study is to investigate which role level and spectral cues play in
monaural sound localization and how monaural cues might complement binaural cues
achieved through the CI. As SSD­CI patients represent a heterogeneous group, the
present study tries to group patients based on their performance to investigate whether
there are systematic patterns between monaural and binaural results within groups.
Better characterizing the relationship between monaural and binaural localization ability
in this specific clinical subpopulation will help to clarify the benefits of cochlear implan­
tation in SSD patients and has the potential to facilitate better expectation management
between clinicians and patients before implantation.
To investigate the role of monaural cues, the consequences of the head acting as an
acoustic obstacle have to be considered. Spectrally, the head attenuates high frequencies
more effectively than low frequencies. Therefore, in a monaural listening condition, a
stimulus will sound bright when played from the ipsilateral side and muffled when played

from the contralateral side. Additionally, a sound will create a higher level at the ear
coming from the ipsilateral side than when the sound is coming from the contralateral
side. If the level and spectrum of a stimulus is held constant in a localization task, subjects
can learn to utilize these spectral and level characteristics. In reality, the properties of
sounds to be localized vary markedly. Therefore, to measure the localization ability more
realistically the level and spectrum of the stimulus needs to be varied which is referred to
as ’roving’. By introducing either level, spectral, level­spectral or no roving the influence
of monaural level and spectral cues for localization can be entangled. That is for example,
if subjects can only localize accurately when no level roving is applied, they most likely
base their perception on the level at the normal hearing ear (Wanrooij & Opstal, 2004).
When SSD patients localize sound with activated CI, they rely on binaural cues while still
having access to monaural cues. In normal­hearing sound localization, monaural cues
play a negligible role (Macpherson & Middlebrooks, 2002) and localization is based on
binaural cues. For SSD­CI patients monaural cues might still be helpful as binaural cues
are only partly restored.
The present study investigates the localization ability under different roving conditions
without and with activated CI. In the monaural case, it is hypothesized that the perfor­
mance of patients who can accurately localize when no roving is applied deteriorates with
introduced level or spectral roving depending on which monaural cue is dominating for
monaural localization. Patients who cannot accurately localize monaurally without ap­
plied roving will stay unaffected by introduced roving. In the binaural case, the outcomes
and potential effects of roving are a central point of this study as, to the author’s best
knowledge, this has not been systematically studied. This is of special interest as hear­
ing in SSD­CI is unique due to the acoustic and electric stimulation. This investigation
will help clarifying the benefits of cochlear implantation for SSD patients and might help
guiding counseling on implantation decisions.

Methods
2.1
Participants
A group of SSD patients with a unilateral CI implanted at the Copenhagen Hearing and
Balance Center (CHBC), Rigshospitalet, participated in this study. They were recruited
postoperatively to participate in a larger study. This included the assessment of speech­
in­noise, which is not part of the present study. The patients were invited if they were
18 years or older, native Danish speakers, had a pure­tone average (PTA4) ≥70 dB HL
at the poor ear, and a PTA4 ≤30 dB HL at the better ear, had at least six months of CI
use, and were willing and able to participate (i.e., without severe comorbidity and within
driving distance to Copenhagen). The PTA4 is defined as the mean thresholds at pure­
tone frequencies of 0.5, 1, 2, and 4 kHz. The research procedures were approved by the
Science­Ethics Committee for the Capital Region of Denmark (reference F­23034539).
Table 2.1 outlines the demographic information of the seven patients in the study. The
mean age of all patients was 60 years ranging from 40 to 72 years. Two patients were
implanted in the right ear (R), and five were implanted in the left ear (L). All results in the
following sections are normalized as if all patients had normal hearing on the right side
and the CI on the left side. None of the patients had residual hearing in the implanted ear.
Figure 2.1 shows the audiograms of the non­implanted ear for all patients.
2.2
Setup
The sound localization ability was assessed in the Spatial Hearing Lab at CHBC. The Spa­
tial Hearing Lab consists of a loudspeaker array with 41 KEF Ci160RR­THX loudspeakers
(KEF, Maidstone, United Kingdom) with custom­made enclosures built into the walls of
a sound­treated but otherwise ordinary clinical room (see title page). The loudspeakers
were driven by Sonible d:24 amplifiers (Sonible GmbH, Graz, Austria). All loudspeakers
were equalized in level, delay, and magnitude response as measured at the listening po­
sition. The patients were placed in an ordinary chair with their ears approximately at the
level of the 0° elevation horizontal loudspeaker array. They wore a microphone clipped
to their collar to enable communication with the facilitator sitting outside of the Spatial
Hearing Lab.

2.3
Localization
The sound localization task consisted of short noise bursts presented randomly from a set
of loudspeakers, and listeners were asked to identify the location of origin. The specifics
of the paradigm were based on the guidelines proposed by Van de Heyning et al. (2017).
The stimuli were played from loudspeakers spaced between ­90° (left) and +90° (right) in
steps of approximately 30°, with minor adjustments to the placement of the loudspeakers
due to the constraints of the room. The exact angles and distances to the position of the
patient are listed in Tab. 2.2. Due to the dominating role of the visual cue in this setting, the
spacing between loudspeakers is assumed to be constantly 30° in any further analysis.
The loudspeakers were labeled from 1 to 7 from left to right. The task of the patient
was to indicate the loudspeaker from which they perceived the sound by entering the
corresponding number on a keyboard. Furthermore, the patients were instructed to refrain
from turning their heads during stimulus presentation, and no feedback was given about
correct and incorrect responses. A short training session with activated CI was conducted
for familiarization in which the experimenter was present in the room and ensured that the
patients did not turn their heads. In the actual experiment, the patients were alone in the
lab.
The stimulus consisted of three sequential noise bursts of a duration of 0.3 s with gaps of
0.2 s to limit the effects of head movement during stimulus presentation (Perrett & Noble,
1997). Squared cosine ramping of 15 ms length was applied on the on­ and offset of
each burst. Within each presentation, the same noise burst was used but regenerated
between presentations. The noise was speech­shaped CCITT­noise (Comité Consultatif
International Télégraphique et Téléphonique; ITU­T Rec. G.227) filtered by either the ipsi­

SR, LR, or LSR condition, respectively. All conditions were tested in a block with (+CI)
and without (­CI) activated CI. The starting CI condition was preassigned to the patients
in alternating order and the CI was set in the daily program of each patient. The order of
the roving conditions was randomized within patients and CI conditions. The patients had
a small break of 30 s in between the roving conditions and a larger, unrestricted break
between the CI conditions.

3.1
RMSE Performance
First, the performance, as measured by the RMSE, is evaluated on a group level and after
that on an individual level. Figure 3.1 shows the overall performance, as measured by
the root­mean­square error (RMSE) between the actual and the perceived angle, for each
roving and CI condition. The exact values of the median RMSEs are stated in Tab. 3.1.
RMSEs decrease when the CI is activated indicating a benefit of the CI. Without activated
CI (­CI), level roving leads to the highest RMSE followed by level­spectral, spectral, and
no roving. With activated CI (+CI), the highest RMSE was found in the level­spectral
roving condition while the other conditions perform relatively similar.
Figure 3.2 depicts the RMSE for each patient in each roving and CI condition. P02 re­
mains unaffected by introduced roving while the RMSE decreases for P07 when roving
is introduced. Monaural performance of all other patients deteriorates with roving. While
P03 is mainly affected by spectral roving, level roving has a larger impact on P01, P04,
P05, and P06. Moreover, P01, P03, P05, P06, and P07 show unexpected monaural re­
sults. The more challenging conditions, i.e., more introduced roving, were found to lead
to a lower RMSE (P01/P05/P06: LSR < LR; P03: LR<NR; P07: LR,SR,LSR < NR). The
+CI results reveal a noticeable impact of roving on binaural localization performance for
all patients except P05. Deteriorated results are either driven by level roving (P01, P04),
spectral roving (P02. P03) or the combination of both level and spectral roving (P06, P07).

3.2
Profiling
The following section concerns the profiling of all patients first in the monaural and after
that in the binaural condition. As monaural localization represents a challenging task, the
patients are divided into groups based on the non­roved condition. Without applied roving,
basing the perceived location on level and spectral cues will lead to the most accurate
performance compared to the other roving conditions and, therefore, reveal which patients
are able to extract monaural cues at all.
Three groups could be determined:
1. Responses constantly towards the normal­hearing side (’NH­side’): P02, P07
2. Responses on the deaf side (left) are mainly limited in a range around 0° while
patients have some localization ability on the normal­hearing side (right) resembling
a rectifying function (’rectified’): P01, P03, P06
3. Responses based on localization over the whole range (’location­based’): P04, P05.
The response patterns correlated with an RMSE of around 100°, 50°, and 30°, respec­
tively. Figure 3.3 shows the response patterns for one representative of each group in
each roving condition. While the first group stayed unaffected from introduced roving
(P02), the RMSE of the latter two groups deteriorated and the response patterns showed
larger variations (P01, P05). The response patterns of all patients in all conditions can be
found in the Appendix, Fig. A.1.

3.3
Extended Performance Analysis
In the final results section, an extended performance analysis is carried out. The RMSE
in the roved conditions was compared to the non­roved condition. If the RMSE was no­
ticeably higher in the roved condition, an effect of roving was assumed. Furthermore,
the response patterns in the roved conditions were split up into their subconditions, i.e.,
divided into different presentation levels (LR), spectral shaping (SR) or both (LSR). An
effect of roving was concluded if responses tended more to the CI side (left)
• at a lower presentation level compared to a higher presentation level and/or
• in the contralateral shaping compared to the ipsilateral.
Visually this leads to a downward shift in the response pattern.
The result of this analysis can be seen in Tab. 3.2. Five different cases occurred:
1. No effect of roving was visible neither based on the RMSE nor in the response
pattern (white; Fig. 3.5a)
2. An effect based on the RMSE was visible but not in the response pattern (dark blue;
Fig. 3.5b)
3. An effect in the response pattern was visible but not based on the RMSE (light blue;
Fig. 3.5c)
4. An effect based on both the RMSE and the response pattern was visible (green; Fig.
3.5d)
5. No effect based on the RMSE. In the response pattern, an effect was visible but not
systematically as described above (grey; Fig. 3.5e).
A representative pattern for each case can be found in Fig. 3.5. The pattern of each
patient in each subcondition is stated in the Appendix, Fig. A.2 to A.4.

Discussion
4.1
RMSE Performance
One aim of this study was to investigate the role of level and spectral cues in monaural
sound localization of SSD­CI patients. Moreover, the effect of roving on binaural local­
ization was of interest as well as how monaural cues might complement binaural cues.
Lastly, patients should be grouped based on their monaural and binaural performance to
examine potential systematic patterns between monaural and binaural localization.
The results of the overall performance in the present study align with other studies but
show some differences. Litovsky et al. (2019) investigated localization ability with applied
level roving and found a drop in the mean RMSE from 56.3° without activated CI to 29.3°
with activated CI. Comparing this to a drop from 75.5° to 32.6° in the present study reveals
high congruence in the +CI condition. As monaural performances are subject to high inter­
individual differences, ­CI results are highly dependent on the particular patients which
explains the difference in the ­CI condition between the present study and Litovsky et
al. (2019). Moreover, Litovsky et al. (2019) used a narrower loudspeaker spacing which
leads to a lower or equal estimate of the RMSE due to its mathematical properties.
Seebacher et al. (2022) used a similar setup as the present study in the level­spectral
roving condition and reported a drop of the median RMSE from 83.6° (­CI) to 38.9° (+CI)
compared to 63.6° to 41.1° in this study. The +CI results are congruent while ­CI re­
sults reveal differences due to the performance of the particular patients. To the author’s
best knowledge, no other study investigated localization ability similar to the no roving or
spectral roving condition of the present study.
The results of the present study support the hypothesis that performance deteriorates
with introduced roving for patients being able to use monaural cues (P01, P03, P04, P05,
P06). P03 seems to rely on spectral cues as spectral roving has a larger impact on the
RMSE. Consequently, the other four patients depend on level cues as level roving reveals
a larger effect. This shows that both level and spectral cues can be utilized for localization
as measured by the RMSE. Which cue is the dominating one is subject to inter­individual
differences.
The results also align with the second hypothesis that patients who are not able to use
monaural cues (’NH­side’) stay unaffected by introduced roving even though the RMSE of
P07 decreases with introduced roving. The lower RMSE is driven by more responses to
the extremes on the CI side (left; see Fig. A.1). Analyzing these extremes as in Sec. 3.3
revealed no systematic pattern. However, this patient suffers from other health issues
disturbing the monaural performance. Moreover, this patient started with the non­roved
condition, so it can be assumed that the impact of these health issues increased over time
in the roved conditions. This suggests rather an effect of his health issues in combination
with the experimental time than an effect of roving.
The unexpected monaural performances of patients P01, P05, and P06 (RMSE in
LSR<LR) might indicate an interplay of level and spectral cues as the spectral roving
might counteract the level roving to some extent. For all three patients, a higher presen­
tation level leads to responses tending to the normal­hearing side (right). Even though
the effect of spectral roving is not apparent for all three patients, the contralateral shaping
might result in responses tending more to the CI side (left) and, consequently, to more
Sound Localization in Single­Sided Deaf Listeners with Cochlear Implants
13
==PAGE:22==
accurate localization when a high presentation level is combined with the contralateral
shaping.
Splitting up the level­spectral roving and the level roving condition into the
different presentation levels and comparing them supported this explanation to a certain
extent. Finally, the 5° lower RMSE of P03 in the level roving compared to the no roving
condition might be in the range of test­retest deviations.
Another important outcome of this study is that binaural performance was affected by
roving for all patients except P05. This leads to the following two possible conclusions
concerning the interplay of monaural and binaural cues. 1) Assuming that ILDs for SSD­
CI patients do not get altered by introduced roving and are therefore constant throughout
roving conditions, patients also rely on monaural cues. These monaural cues are less re­
liable when any roving is applied, explaining the differences between not roved and roved
conditions. 2) If ILDs are affected by roving due to adaptive algorithms in the sound pro­
cessor of the CI or the individual mapping of input to output dynamic range, these changes
in ILDs are responsible for differences between two roving conditions and no statement
about the use of monaural cues in the +CI condition can be made. The results of P02 and
P07 suggest that roving affects the restored ILDs as these patients did not use monaural
cues and it is unlikely that they would do so when access to ILDs is restored. Moreover,
Potts et al. (2019) found that the setting of the adaptive automatic gain control algorithm
has a significant influence on sound localization in bilateral CI users. To get more insight
into the present case, the experiment could be repeated with the same patients using a
clinical device in which all adaptive algorithms are turned off. Nevertheless, the results of
this subsequent experiment would be confounded by the fact that the patients are used
to localize with the adaptive algorithms turned on requiring quick adaptation to the new
input. Another approach would be simulating the signal processing chain of the CI with
the different stimuli as an input to analyze the potential differences of ILDs between the
stimuli.
4.2
Profiling
The group patterns found in the present study align with those of Dillon et al. (2017) and
Buss et al. (2018) who based their grouping on level­roved stimuli. For monaural local­
ization, they also reported patients only responding to the normal­hearing side (’NH­side’)
as well as patients being able to localize accurately (’location­based’). They found no pa­
tients localizing only on the normal­hearing side (’rectified’) but instead stated a ’midline’
and ’random’ behavior. The former group perceived the sound originating from 0° primar­
ily independent of the actual location. This group was not found in the present study. The
latter group responded randomly where some patients revealed a level­dependent pat­
tern as explained in Sec. 3.3. This group was partly detected in the two ’location­based’
patients P04 and P05. Their patterns in the level roving condition fall on a continuum
between ’location­based’ and ’random’ according to Dillon et al. (2017) and the analysis
in Sec. 3.3 revealed a level dependent pattern for these patients. Binaurally, Dillon et al.
(2017) and Buss et al. (2018) reported the same grouping as in the present study. More­
over, they stated two groups that were not found in the present study: a ’compressed’
group not spanning the full range of speakers and a ’NH bias’ group not including the
speaker at ­90° on the CI side. The reason why some monaural and binaural patterns
were not found in the present study but in the other studies and vice versa remains un­
clear. Arras et al. (2022) found that cognitive feedback, i.e. top­down information, affects
monaural localization ability. Therefore, one potential reason might be that patients use
different strategies to solve the task in the absence of reliable monaural and binaural
cues. Furthermore, the present study only examined seven SSD­CI patients compared to
20 patients in the studies by Dillon et al. (2017) and Buss et al. (2018). Consequently, the

missing patterns could appear if more patients are solving the task of the present study.
The analysis of the connection between the groups in the monaural and binaural task
might argue for the use of monaural cues in the +CI condition. Patients not relying on
monaural cues (’NH­side’) rather lateralize binaurally than actually localize. Patients re­
lying on monaural cues to a varying amount (’rectified’, ’location­based’) gain actual lo­
calization ability (’correlated’, ’best’). This suggests that the ability to use monaural cues
in the ­CI condition results in better binaural performance. Even though some RMSEs of
binaural ’lateral’ members are about the same as in the ’correlated’ group, the later still re­
veals a better localization ability in the response pattern. Analyzing the response patterns
of Litovsky et al. (2019) shows the same connection. Assuming that all patients receive
benefit in an equal manner through the CI, the only explaining difference between these
groups is the use of monaural cues. That would mean that for the ’NH­side’ group the
quality of the restored ILDs is good enough to differentiate between left and right instead
of monaurally constantly replying to the NH side. For the ’rectified’ and ’location­based’
group the qualitative side information is already transmitted via monaural cues so that the
ILDs help to refine localization. Validating this connection with more patients would be
very insightful as this connection would facilitate better expectation management.
4.3
Extended Performance Analysis
The RMSE is insufficient as a measure of localization ability. While in the monaural condi­
tion, grouping based on response patterns and the RMSE was congruent, the RMSE was
not expressive to group the patients in the binaural case. As the RMSE is a mean overall
response, different patterns can lead to the same RMSE value. Also, Buss et al. (2018)
argued that the RMSE does not fully capture a patient’s ability to localize. They compared
the number of performances below the chance level with the amount of significant Spear­
man correlation coefficients calculated between the actual and perceived location. While
8 out of 20 patients performed below chance level, 14 revealed a significant correlation
coefficient.
The extended analysis delivers a clearer picture of the effect of roving than the RMSE
alone. The most patients are affected by level and spectral roving. Even though the
RMSE remains constant between a roved and an unroved condition, patients can still be
impacted by roving (light blue in Tab. 3.2). Moreover, it suggests that if level or spectral
roving hast an effect, this effect also remains in the level­spectral roving condition. All this
together might explain why in the +CI condition the RMSE of P06 and P07 only deterio­
rates in the level­spectral roving condition. Both patients are affected by level roving and
spectral roving which is not captured in the measured RMSE due to the averaging over all
responses. When both kinds of roving are combined this leads to an RMSE­measurable
deterioration of performance.
Likewise, the RMSE can capture an effect of roving which is not visible in the response
pattern (dark blue). Monaural and binaural localization in SSD­CI patients is character­
ized by an insufficient amount of information transmitted to the brain leading to worse
performance than people with normal hearing. This might lead to some measurement
noise as the patients have to decide on the perceived location based on this insufficient
information in each trial again. In this simple model, roving might increase this random
noise so that no clear pattern is visible while the RMSE is still increased.
Lastly, the cases where neither an effect in the RMSE was visible nor a systematic effect
in the response pattern could be clarified with a larger sample size and a third spectral
roving condition, e.g., pure CCITT­noise without spectral shaping. The sample size per

sometimes becomes only visible when the two extreme presentation levels are compared.
4.4
Limitations
The present study is subject to some limitations. First of all, the number of patients (n=7)
is quite low. Especially the profiling would benefit from more patients and gain more confi­
dence about the connection between monaural and binaural groups. Moreover, this study
did not investigate confounding factors such as years of deafness prior to implantation,
the age of implantation, the type of CI implant, and years of CI experience. All these
factors might impact monaural and binaural outcomes.

5
Conclusion
This study investigated the monaural and binaural localization ability of seven SSD­CI
patients in four different roving conditions. Monaural localization ability deteriorated with
introduced roving for patients being able to use monaural cues. Patients unable to do
so stayed unaffected by roving. Moreover, roving impacted binaural performance but it
remains unclear if this originates from the use of monaural cues or because ILDs get
altered. It was shown that the RMSE is insufficient as a measure to capture localization
ability and the influence of roving. Furthermore, it could not be clarified if patients rely on
monaural cues in binaural localization However, based on profiling, patients being able
to use monaural cues perform better binaurally. Future work will try to gain data from
more patients for the present localization paradigm trying to validate the current findings.
Additionally, it should focus on investigating the influence of roving on the CI and the
created ILDs.
