Summary
The increase in anthropogenic activity have increased the environmental impact and resulted in more fre-
quently damaging events as a consequence. The need for optimizing and reducing the environmental im-
pacts of the processes that have a high emission is evident. Furthermore, the consumption rate of different
resources have increased, leading to scarcity or potential scarcity in the near future. Thus, optimization of
resource management i needed. Regarding to this, cement production is roughly responsible for 8% of the
global CO2 emissions, and a small reduction of its impact could lead to influential benefits. Combing a re-
duction in environmental impact from cement production, with resource optimization is of great desire. The
currently primary management of mine tailings, a waste product from mining, is impoundment in which the
material is an unused resource with potential for leaching and polluting the nearby surroundings. Therefore,
utilizing mine tailings (MT) in cement as supplementary cementitious material SCM could potentially be
beneficial in multiple ways. However, the issues of these, are the complexity of the chemical composition of
the mine tailings.
Various aspects ought to be investigated, but in this study, the sulfide content in mine tialings and the poten-
tial influence on mineralogy, have been investigated. The technical aspect of the mineralogic change have
been examined through two experimental setups for 308 days. The analytical measurements tools XRD,
TGA, IC and ICP-OES, have been used to determine the mineralogical effects utilizing MT in cement have.
To investigate the influence of a higher sulfides content, additional sulfides were added to samples containing
mine tailings, to see if they provide any influence in reactivity. The cement SCM ratio for the experiment
was 70/30. The high content of MT have been chosen to investigate a worst case scenario.
From these it was clear that the specific chemical composition for each MT used, influenced the cement
mineralogy. Tendencies of potential for sulfate attack in form of later formation of ettringite of suggestion
based on the results. A change in the common cement components such as increasing of dolomite content
was observed for sample containing mine tailings. The higher sulfide content seem to influence the avail-
ability of chloride ions. This could have damaging effect when used in reinforced concrete. The use of MT
in cement is suspected to have potential for damaging the properties of concrete. Limitations for what type
of construction cement containing MT can be used for could be a requirement in the future.
An LCA have been conducted on 13 scenarios to investigate if the use of MT in cement is environmentally
beneficial. In this a comparison of the SCM fly ash (FA) have been used, as it is currently a common SCM in
cement. MT from China and Sweden have been used in the experimental part of this study, and thus the LCA
have modelled scenarios in these locations. Furthermore, the amount of SCM used have been modelled, and
the difference in impact between 15% and 25% SCM have been included. In this LCA, focus have been on
the impact category freshwater ecotoxicty and environmental dissipation. In this, the environmental impact
have been modelled for short-term emissions with and without End-of-Life (EoL) leaching and long-term
emissions. A clear importance on the geographical location and the chemical composition can be concluded.
The Swedish scenarios have a benefit from using SCM regardless of the SCM type, time frame of emissions
and leaching. this was not the case for China.
The overall conclusion of this study based on the two parts, is that MT utilized in cement could be envi-
ronmentally beneficial, but the influence of the geographical and chemical specific composition of the MT
greatly influence and potentially limit the extent of the use.

Introduction of the project
The anthropogenic origin and severity of climate change and the related issues are no longer a question,
but a fact supported by scientific evidence. The increase in frequency of damaging events can be observed
globally, and the cause is the increase of greenhouse gas emissions related to various processes deemed
necessary for our society to function. The construction industry play a crucial part, and especially production
of cement is an important contributor as a CO2 emitter. Roughly 8% of the global CO2 emissions can be
traced to the production of cement. This is including extraction and production of the necessary components
and the energy required to obtain the necessary high temperature [3, 9].
3.1
Utilization of mine tailings
Due to the global increase in consumption and the economical growth, it is only natural that the mining of
metals and mineral have increased, and is most likely to continue this trend [6]. Proper and more sustainable
management of mine tailings have become more essential for the mining industry [6].
When mining the recovery rate will never be 100%, and a percentage of the wanted mineral or metal remains
in the tailings. When mining metals, the majority of the mined materials ends as tailings [16]. Furthermore,
a decrease in ore grades have been observed due to an increase in demand of various minerals and metals,
increasing the volume of tailing produced per extracted mineral [6].
The mining process consist of various steps producing high quantities of different waste materials. The main
waste produced are waste rock, tailings from the ore beneficiation process and slag from mineral extraction.
The MT are then in a form of slurry, ready to be disposed in storage facilities. The process water used in the
extraction process are separated from the tailings by filtration and recycled.
Currently, the most common storage method of MT is impoundment, but it can also be stored otherwise such
as deposited in old mines in which the tailings are bound in cement. The impoundments can have different
designs, but for this project the centreline design is relevant. As illustrated in Figure 1 a starter dyke is
installed which is the base for the impoundment, and will indicate the direction of the embarkning. Then,
multiple dykes are placed upon the starter dyke in which the center of each dyke is alined, thus the name
centreline. Other designs are upstream and downstream [6].
Figure 1: Common embankment construction methods for storage of mine tailings [6].

3.2
Scope of the project
With the impoundment method of mine tailings, follow different major and minor environmental impacts,
such as spillages and failures due to the structural stability. Air and soil pollution are of concern due to dust
particles dispersing, causing harmful exposure to humans and ecosystems. A common concern is the mine
tailings contaminating the ground and surface water. To avoid this, various initiatives are put in place, such
as different liner systems and filers, depending on the nature and hydrolics of the tailings. However, the
complexity of tailings and the potential chemical reactions, may cause breaches of the initiatives [16]. The
list of environmental impacts goes on, and it is clear that mine tailings pose an environmental threat when
not manged correctly: Therefore, sustainable solutions on how to dispose of MT are needed.
Prior to utilization of MT as SCM, different kinds of treatments should be taken into consideration, such as
drying, grinding and calcining, depending on the properties of the specific tailing in question.
3.2
Scope of the project
This project aim to investigate the potential use of sulfide containing mine tailings in cementitious binders.
The experiment has been conducted for 308 days in which varies analytical methods have been used to best
understand the sulfide oxidation mechanisms. These have been chosen, to investigate some of the issues that
may follow, depending on the sulfide content. One issue could be sulfate attack, which affect the properties
of concrete as describes in Section 4.1.1. MT tend to have a high content of sulfide compounds, thus poses
a potential to influence the mineralogy of the cement in a certain direction. Thus, before utilization of MT
is possible the chemical mechanisms needs to be understood. This study is only concerning the effect of
higher sulfide content. Some of the challenges from utilizing MT is stated in Section 4.3.
Life cycle assessment (LCA) is used as a tool to assess the environmental impact of a product or a process.
When conducting a full LCA, every life stage is taken into account, from the extraction of raw materials used
in various processes, through the manufacturing and use phase, until the final disposal. However, depending
on the purpose of the LCA, system boundaries may be defined to exclude certain life cycle stages, if assessed
not to be of relevance. The scope of an LCA and how the results are used varies greatly, from a hot spot
analysis to improve a specific product or processes, comparison of to systems to determine the better option
to establishing of new policies.
The LCA in this study will be scenarios based on which different composition of cement will be investigated,
as well as the influence of geographical location. As will be discuss in details in section 8 and 9, the chosen
processes will mainly be generic and will provide an initial screening of the environmental impact related to
utilization of MT in cement.
The structure of the project
This thesis is based on two parts (1) an experimental part and (2) an LCA. the first part focus on the sulfide
oxidation mechanisms and how it effects the mineralogy when utilizing mine tailings in cement. In regards
to this, relevant theory will we included, followed by the methodology used to conduct the experiments.
Results will be presented and discussed first for each analytical tool used, and then a discussion which
include using the tools complementary to interpretation of the experimental results. The first part will be
completed by a conclusion of the main findings and remarks on how this is to be included in the second part
of the thesis the LCA. The LCA will include the typical main parts of an LCA. Goal and scope definition

4.1
Cement chemistry - Portland cement
As mention in section 3 the scope of the project is the potential utilization of MT, containing sulfides, to use
in cementitious binders and the related environmental impacts of this utilization. Thus, the chemical aspect
will mainly focus on the cement chemistry. However, since the composition of the cement influences the
properties of concrete, the concrete aspect should not be disregarded. Issues, such as sulfate attack in the
concrete, will be discussed in section 4.1.1, and is an example of this.
Portland rapid cement is the most common cement, and assessed to be most relevant to use in this study.
The primary constituents of portland cement are calcium silicates, in which materials such as limestone is
a natural source of calcium. Also, clay is a source of aluminium, silica and other minerals. The chemical
composition of clinker consist of alite (C3S), belite (C2S), aluminate (C3A) and ferrite (C4AF).
In solid hydrated cement paste four solid phases are; (1) calcium silicate hydrates (C-S-H) which makes up
50-60 % of the volume in hydrated cement, (2) calcium hydroxide (CH) known as portlandite, constitute
20-25% of the volume, (3) calcium sulfoaluminates hydrates occupying 15-20% which at an early stage
in hydration favors the formation of ettringite (C6A ¯S3H32) and (4) unhydrated clinker grains in which the
volume depend on the degree of hydration and the particle size distribution of the anhydrous cement [14].
Due to the hetreogeneous mixture of portland cement, several hydration processes occurs simultaneously,
though, not at the same rate. The C3A tends to hydrate at a faster rate than the silicates and largely influ-
ence the stiffening and setting characteristics of the cement paste. Meanwhile, the silicates dominates the
hardening. Because the hydration of C3A is an immediate reaction, it is necessary to add a retarding agent,
usually gypsum, for the portland cement to be used in most construction applications. When the aluminate
ions and sulfate ions have a high availability during the hydration, large amounts of ettringite are formed
quickly compared to what is considered as a normal rate for portland cement. This is also expected to be
clear form the experimental results of this study. When hydration of C3S and C2S occur, C-S-H are formed.
The specific chemical composition of C-S-H varies depending on water-cement ratio, temperature, and the
duration of the hydration. Besides forming C-S-H, CH is produced.
4.1.1
Sulfides and the related issues in concrete
Sulphate attack in concrete occurs, primarily, due to sulphide mineral oxidation: This results in a crystalliza-
tion of new products with a higher volume, causing stress by expansion, and appears as cracks. Furthermore,
the products from sulphate oxidation reacts with compounds in the hydrated cement, which then forms new
products [7]. The most common form of sulphate attack is the reaction between sulphate ions and CH or
(C3A) forming gypsum and ettringite. These reactions occur at relatively moist conditions and at a room

4.1.2
Relative humidity and its effect on carbonation
Carbonation occurring in concrete is influenced by various factors, including the relative humidity (RH).Water
need to be present for the atmospheric CO2 to dissolve before it is able to react with CH, forming Calcium
carbonate. For which the RH should exceed 40-50%. However, at saturation, with a RH above 70-80%,
carbonation is significantly slowed due to the low solubility of CO2 in water. A balance of water content,
thus, create a RH optimum for carbonation at 65%, disregarding the cement type that is used [8, 22]. The
carbonation is influenced by both the external humidity, which the concrete is exposed to by the environ-
ment, and the internal humidity from the water/cement ratio. However, the internal humidity is dependant
on the moisture transport in the concrete, which is affected by the microstructure of the concrete. Therefore,
pore size plays a vital role, as well as the type of cement.
4.2
SCM
Mineral admixtures are often added to the cement for various reasons such as cost reduction, environmental
benefits, and enhancement of the workability in the final concrete. These enhancements include increased
resistant of thermal cracking and chloride or sulfate attack. The mineral admixtures act as a supplement
of the properties and are referred to as a supplementary cementing material (SCM). Thus, it can replace a
certain amount of clinker in the cement [14]. Common SCM are the by-products "fly ash" from coal com-
bustion and slag from iron blast-furnaces.
The environmental benefits arise, when using material that does not require additional clinkering process,
which is the main contributor to the CO2 emission from cement production. Thus, it has the potential for
significantly reducing the environmental impact. Furthermore, utilizing waste products from other industries
and processes also entails multiple benefits, as will be demonstrated and discussed throughout the life cycle
assessment sections 8-??. However, by using SCM the complexity of the cement increases. The kinetic
reactions of the SCM in the cement are depending on the specific chemical composition, the physical prop-
erties (such as fineness), and the amount used. The interaction between the cement and SCM will, thus,
vary between the SCM used and the formation of different phases, such as ettringite and portlandite [15].
When using SCM as clinker replacement, limitation and issues can occur such as; reduction in early strength,
limited amount of reactive SCMs and limited replacement by non-reactive SCMs [9].

4.3
Observation of mine tailings influence in cement
When using mine tailings (MT) as a SCM, the MT generally substitute the fine aggregate in the cement due
to the particle size. The MT properties impact various aspect of the concrete, including workability, density,
strength, and stability. Tailings containing metals also tend to modify the hydration process in the mortar.
It has been observed, that the use of MT decrease the workability, due to the finer particle size increasing
the surface area, which then results in a higher water demand. However, this issue can be eliminated by
excluding the finer particle sizes of the tailings. The heavy metals in the MT tend to retard the hydration by
forming a low permeability layer on un-hydrated grains, extending the setting time. An increase in density
for both fresh and hardend properties have been observed. When testing the compressive strength of concrete
containing MT, an increase in strength have been observed, but at a limit of clinker replacement. Beyond
a certain substitution percentage, the compressive strength decreases mainly due to the retardation effect
of heavy metals in the MT. To balance the workability and compressive strength, there should be no more
than a 30% substitution of fine aggregate. To add to the complexity of utilizing MT as a SCM in cement,
MTs chemical composition varies significantly depending on the mine. Table 1 illustrate a small selection
of examples on how the high the variation of compounds can be, even in tailings of the same type[12].

Many factors have to be taken into consideration in relation to the effects MT can have. Observation on

Experimental Method
5.1
Materials chosen
To investigate the sulfide oxidation mechanisms in cement when using MT as SCM two different types of
MT, from different locations, were used. A MT from the Tianbaoshan mine, located in China, and one from
the Zinkgruvan mine in Sweden. Furthermore, to test how the samples would react if a higher sulfide content
were present, one additional sulfide was added to each type of mine which complemented the chemical
composition of each mine (described below).

5.1.1
Mine tailings used in cement paste
Tianbaoshan
The Tianbaoshan deposit is located in the north eastern part of China in the Jilin-Heilongjiang region, as
seen in Figure 2, in which large reserves of Pb, Zn, Cu and Mo are being extracted.Tianbaoshan is mainly
referred to as a Pb-Zn deposit with an average grade of 1.72% Pb and 2.56% Zn, while Cu and Mo have an
average grade of 0.31% and 0.21%, respectively [25]. For reference, the global average ore grade in 2016
was 0.5% for metals [16]. Smaller amounts of Ge, Ga, Cd, and Ag, amongst others, are also present in the
Tianbaoshan deposit [26].
The chosen additional sulfide to investigate a higher sulfide content effect on the oxidation mechanisms were
based on the composition of the mine. Some sulfide minerals to be predominantly in Tianbaoshan are galena
(PbS), sphalerite (ZnS), pyrite (FeS2) and chalcopyrite (CuFeS2). Pyrite was chosen as the additional sulfide
in the sample containing MTs from Tianbaoshan.
Zinkgruvan
The Zinkgruvan deposit is located in south-central Sweden as seen in figure Figure 2. The main minerals
mined are zinc, lead and silver with an average grading of 9.3%, 3.9%, and 90 g Ag/t, respectively. The
dominant minerals are sphalerite and gelena, with smaller amounts of chalcopyrite (<0.2% Cu) as well as
other iron sulfides such as pyrite and arsenopyrite in concentrations below 0.1% [23].

5.2
Design of the experimental setups
Due to the high content of zinc in the deposit, sphalerite were chosen as the additional sulfide to use in

5.2
Design of the experimental setups
When designing the experimental setup, kinetic tests were the chosen over static tests. The static tests would
provide a measurement at a very short period of time (<24h), and would be relevant if only an overview of
the composition in the sample was wanted. Since investigating the sulfide oxidation mechanisms over time
was the study objective, kinetic testing were more appropriate. However, for analysing the samples extracted
over time, from the kinetic test setup, static test methods have been used to identify the chemical composition
and to quantify the elements at the different time steps during the project. These methods include ICP-EOS,
IC, XRD and TGA as described in ??. The design options were, however, limited by several factors such
as the amount of materials, the budget, and the equipment available. This meant that compromises were
made, and the recommended sample volume of 1kg was not met. The amount of materials available were a
limiting factor in this, as well as the equipment and space needed. However, it was evaluated to have little
to no influence for the scope of the project.
To gain the best understanding of the sulfide oxidation mechanisms when using MT as SCM in the construc-
tion industry, it was decided to make two different setups. One setup investigating the reactions constantly
submerged in water and one in which the samples were placed in air with changing humidity.
For the setup with changing humidity, referred to as humidity setup (HS), a common kinetic test called "The
humidity cell test" was used as initial inspiration as illustrated in Figure 3a. However, due to the mentioned
limitation, a smaller sample size than described in the test protocol ASTM D5744-13E1 was used [21]. In
stead of an inlet for the air being pumped in, it was decided to place the samples in a climate chamber, in
which the RH could be controlled. Because of this, the sample container had an open top. The setup was,
thus, a merge of two kinetic test methods. The two RH used in this study were based on theory described
in Section 4.1.2, where 45% and 80% were chosen as the lower and the upper value, respectively. This was
done to mimic the weathering conditions that might occur in the environment, but within a range that made
sense for the experiment.
For the setup in water, the sub-aqueous column was used as inspiration along with a batch setup used to
investigate sulfide mineral oxidation in tailings [11, 21]. This setup will be referred to as the sub-aqueous
setup (SAS). The setup was used to assess the release of elements when deposited under water. As with the
case of HS, a smaller volume of samples were used as a results of the previouly mentioned limitations.
By this, the mineralogical change when the samples were exposed to water and to change in humidity, as
would be the case in reality, could be investigated.

The method XRD is used as a characterisation tool of fine-grained materials and to identify different phases
in the sample by taking advantage of the specific symmetry and size of the unit cell. By the diffraction angle
of the X-ray, the different compound and the intensity can be determined in the sample.
TGA is used as a complementary analytical tool for XRD to evaluate the reactivity of SCM. TGA takes
advantage of the different phases’ mass loss at different temperatures. At temperatures between 50-200·C
the mass loss is used to determine the amount of to C-S-H compounds as well as AFm (Al2O3 −Fe2O3-
mono) and AFt (Al2O3 −Fe2O3-tri) phases. Portlandite is determined at temperatures between 400-500·C
and CaCO3 between 550-800·C.

cement such as CH, CaCO3, dolomite and quartz are generally easy to determine in this study. The decrease

some minerals various subsets have been used because of the variations amongst them for better determining
the mineral causing of the peak. This increases the difficulty and limit the precision for determination of the
minerals present, and the uncertainty this cause, is taken into account when analysing the XRD results.

Conclusion of investigation of sulfide oxidation mechanisms
Different conditions were investigated for their influence on the mechanism occurring in cement, and how it
effect the use of MT as SCM through experiments. These were designed to mimic the potential weathering
if used in construction. The overall tendency for both setup (effect of change in humidity and if submerged
in water) was an apparent increase in reactivity for samples containing MT. Besides the additional amount
of increase in sulfide minerals, e.g. ettringite, the results suggested that it had an influence in common
cement compounds such as dolomite. This is suspected to have, if somewhat small, influence on the changes
observed when utilizing MT. An increase in chloride concentration is evident for both setup, and could create
issues if cement containing MT is to be used in reinforced concrete.
A main concern of utilizing MT is the increase potential for sulfate attack due to the increase of available
sulfides. Increase in formation of ettringite is observed from XRD results and DTG suggest that MT and a
higher sulfide content influence the formation. Some evidence of later ettringite formation was found, but
whether or not the increase is enough to cause sulfate attack require additional research and testing, which
were not included in the scope of this study. Besides ettringite, later formation of gypsum were suspected
for Zinkgruvan for both HS and SAS, but could not be determined with certainty. This was however less
evident when adding sphalerite.
The leaching potential from the different sample types were investigated by results obtained with ICP. Po-
tential leaching for HS of when utilizing MT were higher than the reference, in special regards to Cr, Sb
and Se, causing different health issues. For SAS Pb exceeded the limit values for almost all measurement
with MT, and non for the reference. Thus if utilizing the cement containing MT for constructions, the in-
creased leaching potential should be taken into account to avoid pollution of soil, nearby freshwater and
groundwater.
From this study, it is clear that the chemical composition in MT influence the carbonation, hydration and
formation of minerals potential for sulfate attack amongst others. Furthermore, it is evident that the sulfides
role in this is important, when comparing with and without. However, the extent to which it effect the
properties when used for concrete, require additional studies of testing these. The increase of chloride
concentration and formation of agents for sulfate attack suggest some limitation of using cement containing
MT. The limitations, however, will vary depending on the specific chemical composition of the MT, and
potential restrictions should reflect this. With that said, there are evidence for utilizing MT in the cement
production.

Goal of the Life Cycle Assessment
This project objective is to investigate the potential use of MT as SCM in cement. In the experimental
part, described in Section 4-7, the technical aspect is investigated. This part of the project will conduct an
environmental assessment of the use of MT as SCM in cement production.
8.1
Intended Application, reasoning for carrying out the study and decision context
The LCA aims to evaluate the potential change in environmental impact when utilizing the waste product
MT as SCM in cement and concrete production. It is, thus, a hypothetical study which will investigate and
compare different "what-if" scenarios for more sustainable solutions to a process which is a high environ-
mental burden on a global level.
The reasoning for carrying out the study is closely related to the application of the results. The comparison
of different scenarios, to investigate if any environmental benefits arise when using MT as SCM in cement,
could be used as reasoning and guidance for future studies and decisions. The decision context of the study
will, thus, be situation A, which support decisions on a micro level.
Potentially, the nature of the study could influence a decision entailing a change in how some cement is
produced and would, thus, be a large change on global level. However, this specific study is not extensive
enough to be included in such a large scale consequence. Decisions taken, based on this study, will most
likely be related to whether any further studies and experiments should be conducted to gain more knowledge
and understanding for potential use of sulfide containing mine tailings in cementitious binders. The study
will, thus, provide guidance for future decisions, and investigate the potential for using the technology in the
future.
The decision context is thus determined to be situation A.

on the criteria A and B stated below, is taken from this article.
In short, the environmental dissipation category applies characterization factors of van Oers et al. (2021)
to those metal emissions which meet the dissipation criterion (criterion A) of Owsianiak et al. (2021). The
criterion state "determines whether an emission flow reported in a life cycle inventory actually contributes
to loss of accessibility of a given element when environmental fate mechanisms are considered" [20] (the
criterion reads: "An elementary flow must represent an anthropogenic emission of an element for which the
current annual rate of total anthropogenic emissions results in a steady state concentration in the receiving
environment that is below a reference concentration reflecting what is accessible for humans within the
considered time span"). Average element concentration in the upper continental crust is the reference for
long-term accessibility.
The second criterion (criterion B) "determines whether the element comes from a source that would be con-
sidered as a mineral resource for any generation living between the present and the time frame of assessment"
[20] was not considered in the present work as it requires substance flow analysis to be made, which was
outside the scope of the study. In the thesis it was, therefore, assumed that any metal presents in emission
flow is a metal resource in the long term, irrespective of its origin (e.g. fly ash or mine tailings). [20]
The results will mainly be presented and discussed excluding long-term emissions, however a brief inclusion
of the differences when including these will be presented.

Observation for specific impact categories
Two impact categories, freshwater ecotoxicity and environmental dissipation, have been selected to inves-
tigate further because of the relevance of these. This is due to the nature of the SCM used and the current
applied disposal methods MT. As mentioned in Section 3, leaching and, thus, pollution of freshwater is of
great concern related to disposal of MT. Thus, the impact category, freshwater ecotoxicity, is highly rele-
vant for this study. Environmental dissipation model the decrease of accessibility of a mineral resource. As
this is taken into account, the emissions of elements which cannot be recovered for different reasons [20].
Figure 34 illustrate the impact categories in percentage normalised to the scenario with the highest absolute
value. For freshwater ecotoxicity it is S11 while for environmental dissipation S8 have the highest absolute
value. From Table 14 and Figure 34 it is observed that the majority of the scenarios perform worse in en-
vironmental dissipation than in freshwater ecotoxicity, based on the amount of scenarios with negative and
positive values.

Interpretation of the Life Cycle Assessment
12.1
Significant issues
Due to the construction of the model with different scenarios investigation the influence of different param-
eters, it is seen as a sensitivity analysis in it self. This section will highlight some of the main environmental
important issues found in this study, based on the substance and process contribution analysis conducted.
SCM treatment or the avoidance of such, is evident an important factor of how well the scenario performs.
In this, the type of SCM greatly influence the potential benefit achieved. This was observed when comparing
scenarios at the same location, with the same cement/SCM ratio, but the two different SCM types, FA and
MT. A general tendency observed from process contributions is a higher benefit from avoided processes
from using FA. However, the processes used for FA and MT treatment varies on how representative they
are for their respectively SCM. The process used for FA treatment is more accurate to the specifics of FA,
probably due to FA have been used in the modern cement industry as early as the 1930s [14]. Detailed
inventories for FA in different applications are therefore available. Besides the limited amount of processes
concerning MT, the high variance of composition from mine to mine pose a complication for collecting data
and constructing the model. This is suspected to influence the result, but it uncertain to what extend. The
compositional influence is evident from the substance contribution analysis conducted on the sulfidic tailing
treatment process used. The issues related to this is that the processes are based on average values for one
type of mine for the entire country. As described in Section 4.3 the chemical composition varies significantly
even within the same types of mines.
Geographical location is evident an important factor related to the environmental impact. From the charac-
terized results, it could be observed that the Chinese scenarios had the overall poorest performance. This
is highly related to location specific processes. From process contribution processes related to electricity
highly influenced the impact. China’s electricity impact is greatly related to the high content of fossil fuel,
while the main electricity impact from Sweden is related to their nuclear power. In this regard, the future
composition of the electricity mix should be taken into consideration. Due to the increase pressure of reduc-
ing climate impacts and the joining of the Paris agreement, a reduction in the impact related to electricity is
likely for both location. However, a dramatic change of electricity mix is necessary for China to compete
with Sweden, and would be unlikely to occur in the near future. Never the less, it should still be taken into
account in future studies.
The transport processes used for the different location does not vary significantly, but still influence the
observed higher impact in China. This is due to higher transportation assumed necessary in China, based on

the size and thus greater distance from the mining site to the cement factory. This increases the emissions of
various elements increasing the impact observed. The increased emission of metals have an important role
in the two impact categories focused on, freshwater ecotoxicity and environmental dissipation.
When including EoL leaching it is evident that the level of details in leaching greatly influence the outcome.
For this reason the process used for FA leaching were adjusted, to have a similar level of details as possible
to the MT. However, without specific knowledge on the obtaining of the FA data, it was limited on what to
adjust. The data on MT were based on experimental measurements, and thus for a more precise comparison
between FA and MT, the same experiments should be conducted for FA. However, this was not in alignment
of the scope of this project which investigate the sulfide oxidation mechanism in MT and whether or not it
was environmental beneficial. It was however an influence factor on the results when including EoL leaching
in which the scenario with MT came to outperform the one with FA at the same location.
12.2
Conclusion and limitations
The use of SCM can be more environmentally friendly than using none. However, the benefit varies depend-
ing on the type and amount of SCM, whether or not leaching is to be included in the assessment, and the
time scale (short-term or long-term). One general tendency regardless of these, is that the SCM scenarios
outperform the non-SCMs of the same location. The benefit of utilizing SCM in cement varies significantly
depending on the geographical location. For China, an environmental benefit from utilizing SCM is only
observed for a 25% content for the short term emissions, while all scenarios for Sweden are environmentally
beneficial. For the long-term emissions, however, all MT scenarios, regardless of the geographic location,
are environmentally beneficial. Furthermore, the FA scenario with 25% MT from Sweden also has a neg-
ative impact score. Though not as high as the MT. This tendency of MT outperforming FA scenarios on a
long-term basis, can be observed for both freshwater ecotoxicity and environmental dissipation. It can, thus,
be concluded that if looking on the short-term scale, both FA and MT can be environmentally beneficial, but
it greatly depends on the location of the cement and concrete production and the amount of SCM used.
Limitations
The main limitation of this study is the level of specific detailed inventory. However, this is influencing
the scenarios and processes in different significance. The generic inventory for cement production for the
locations is assessed not to be greatly influencing the results, while the composition of the MT and, thus, the
emissions related to the treatment as well as the leaching in EoL, pose a great importance. Thus, the limiting
level of detail in these processes will influence the representativeness of the results. With that said, this LCA
is scoped to provide initial indication of the possible environmental benefit from MT in cement.
69
==PAGE:77==
13
Conclusion of this Study
A key point from both the experimental investigation of MT the potential of sulfide containing MT as cemen-
titious binders and the LCA, is the importance of the chemical composition of the MT. From the experiments
conducted, variance depending on which MT were used and the sulfides content were evident. Potential for
sulfate attack increased by the use of MT in cement, but to what extent could not be concluded in the chosen
scope. Another relevant issue related to a higher sulfide content and an increase in complexity of the miner-
alogy, were the increase of chloride, potentially damaging if used for reinforced concrete. This will restrain
the use of MT used in cement. However, this is for a 30% substitution, and using less amount of MT in the
cement, might be a solution for the increase of chloride. This is also the case for the increase in formation
of ettringite observed.
If less MT were utilized in cement, environmental benefits could still be achieved compared to the scenario
with no SCM used. This is however depending on multiple factors such as geographic location, inclusion
of EoL leaching and the time frame (short-term or long-term). Whether or not it is more beneficial than the
reference scenario, varies between the impact categories. Thus, by decreasing the amount of MT to avoid
specific chemical compounds, the environmental benefit could disappear. A reduction from 25% to 15%
seem to still pose some environmental benefit when using MT, both for Chinese and Swedish scenarios.
However for the Chinese it is very limited of the benefit achieved especially for short-term emissions both
with and without EoL leaching. Overall, the Swedish scenarios (Zinkgruvan) containing MT perform better
than when no SCM is used, regardless of including EoL leaching and the time frame of emissions or amount
of MT.
The difference in environmental benefit between the two location modelled in the LCA, and the influence of
the chemical composition regarding the origin of the tailings, suggest that the use of MT in cement are to be
used with some limitations and constrains. Never the less, potential for reducing the environmental impact
from both cement production and the mining industry is evident from this study.
14
Further work
Various aspect of both parts of the study could be investigated further, and is mentioned here.
Further Experimental Investigation Data for the pH development over time in the samples is highly rel-
evant. The pH greatly influence the stability of cementitious compounds. Thus investigating if the MT and
sulfides content influence the pH would be of or importance on determine if MT is suitable as SCM in ce-
ment. In this regard it would be suitable to examine if there is a threshold to the amount of MT used before
the change in pH becomes to much. The pH would also provide information to be used for determining
mineralogical changes. This would be the case of the suspected presence of sodium and magnesium sulfate.
Since there is a pH decrease, it would indicate potential reaction of magnesium sulfate forming magnesium
hydroxide. This should of course be used as a complementing tool for more precise determination of the
mineralogy.
The change of reactivity when sub-merged in salt water would be of relevance for future use. The additional
chemical complexity of the MT could be likely to even further influencing the reactions in cement in the
presence of various minerals and ions found in salt water.
70
==PAGE:78==
Since these changes have been observed for cement paste, it would be of use to investigate how it would
affect the properties when used in concrete. Thus examination of physical properties such as strength,
porosity and density would be of relevance before utilizing MT is realistic.
Optimizing the LCA As described this LCA study have the intend of providing an initial assessment on the
potential environmental benefit from utilizing MT in cement. Throughout the study it have become evident
that some aspects should be optimized for a more realistic assessment. This entails:
• Optimizing the geographical representiveness. This is especially the case for the Chinese scenarios
in which many generic processes have been used. These were often processes based on average to
be used for rest-of-the-world, when no other processes would fit. Even though adjustments have been
made after best ability of the practitioner, an increase of geographical specific inventory would provide
a more realistic assessment.
• More detailed inventory for EoL leaching. If experimental data are to be used, leaching measurements
should be obtained for all types of scenarios. Thus, leaching of fly ash with the same supplement
percentage should be conducted for best comparing options.
Besides optimizing the LCA further studies to be of relevance would be a deep analysis of the remaining
impact categories that were not discussed. This would provide a better understanding on the difficulties and
issues arising when using MT in cement. Furthermore, scenarios in which the MT are used as aggregate in
concrete manufacturing instead of cement, would articulate the issues regarding sand as a resource, and the
potential depletion.

Portland cement is made by heating, in a cement kiln, a mixture of raw 
materials (mainly limestone or chalk) to a calcining temperature of above 600 
°C and then a fusion temperature, which is about 1,450 °C to sinter the 
materials into clinker. To achieve the desired setting qualities in the finished 
product, a quantity of gypsum or anhydrite is added to the clinker and the 
mixture is finely ground to form the finished cement powder.
