Abstract
With an increasing focus on environmental issues and sustainable development in the
society, improvement must be done in all industries. As the building industry presents
40% of the energy consumption, this industry represents a great responsibility to im-
prove their methods. Prefabrication is considered more eﬃcient than traditional on
site building techniques. Therefore the main aim of this thesis is to improve developed
prefabricated building components.
The building regulations in 2018 have an optional building class 2020 for NZEB build-
ings. This requires a maximum energy consumption of 20 kWh/m2. This thesis will
follow the optional building class 2020. The ﬂoor and foundation are rarely prefabri-
cated, therefore the main focus will be to develop the wall and roof constructions. A
general solution to the decreased energy demand is to increase the insulation thickness.
The ﬁrst analysis conducted in this thesis is therefore to check how thick insulation
thickness is required in diﬀerent wall types with mineral wool class 0,37. The aim is
then to check if there is a diﬀerence between the wall types. The next step was to
see if other insulation materials could decrease this thickness. The result naturally
shows that the insulation thickness may be reduced with other materials, but most
of these are too expensive to be adequate, and some does not meet the requirements
concerning ﬁre.
Thermal performance is an important aspect to reduce the energy consumption. Ther-
mal bridges were therefore calculated to see what areas have potential for improvement
and if any wall types performed better than others concerning thermal bridges. Tim-
ber frame had one of the best values in most connections, except the connection of
modules. This may be because timber frame has long traditions in being build on site,
before prefabrication was normal, and therefore are not so developed in prefabrication.
Other than that, few wall types stood out. In the analysis of windows, it was chosen to
include the whole assembly of the window, to see if it was potential for improvement.
The result showed that the window had great potential for improvement, with high
values of heat loss. The construction detail between wall and roof showed a substantial
diﬀerence, not between wall types, but concerning the usage of parapet or not. Two
areas were thereby chosen as main focus for improvement, window assembly and roof
construction.
The window assembly is analysed concerning removal of the frame solution or using
windows that can‘t be opened, giving the option to decrease the thickness of the frame
and also to build it in the wall. Also, the placement for the window in the windowsill
was analysed, showing that this decreases the heat loss greatly if the architectural
expression is accepted. To decrease the frame thickness and build the frame inside the
ii
==PAGE:5==
wall showed an improvement. Further, a new design is made for the whole assembly.
The roof analysis is made to see if a parapet should be used, concerning thermal
bridge, the results showed that parapet should not be used. Also concerning assembly
on building site, this presents an issue. The analysis, therefore, results in an eave
instead of a parapet. The load bearing construction material was also analysed, re-
sulting in wood framing with I-beam as the best result.
Further, a basic LCA analysis was made of the diﬀerent wall types to ensure all of
them to have an acceptable value. A basic description is made on a method to achieve
proper air tightness. Including some step by step illustrations on window assembly
and connection of modules.

Introduction
For the following thesis prefabricated wall elements and existing ﬂat prefabricated roof
elements will be simulated and investigated. The simulations are based on a ﬂat single
family type house. This will result in some optimized areas of a building envelope.
The optimization will be based on energy consumption, air tightness and moisture
control, thermal performance, indoor environment, LCA, and cost.
1.1
Motivation
As the world is facing climate change, several international governmental policies aim
to reduce the greenhouse gases to limit the climate change. The European Union’s
Energy Roadmap 2050 is committed to reducing greenhouse gas emissions to 80-95%
below 1990 levels by 2050 in the context of necessary reductions by developed coun-
tries as a group. To achieve this, the focus is mainly on energy eﬃciency.[15] As the
building industry present 40% of the energy consumption, there is a pressure for the
industry to search for new ways to improve the buildings. To prefabricate structures
is a growing work method, that has a potential of improving the energy eﬃciency in
addition to the high possibility of cost reduction. [13]
The choice between prefabricated or build on site is dependant on the situation. Pre-
fabricated elements are however a method more used in later times, with potential for
improvement, and therefore used for this thesis. A reason for prefabricated compo-
nents to be considered a provident way of building houses are the cost savings and
reduced time of mounting on site. As the elements are produced on a factory before
arriving the building site, the time on site will be shortened, it is almost no need for
storage space on site, and the workforce required is signiﬁcantly reduced. By develop-
ing prefabricated solution, the heat loss may be reduced by minimizing the amount of
work on site, the opportunity of errors might be minimized.
For the new building class 2020 in the building code 2018 for Denmark, the energy
regulation is a maximum annual energy consumption of 20 kWh/m2.[3] The building
envelope serves as the interface between the indoor and exterior environment. As the
climate conditions diﬀer throughout the year, the result is often a great heat energy
loss through the envelope. The potential for development is, therefore, to reduce this
energy loss by improving the building envelope.[16]
State of the art literature presents the most recent prefabrication products and process.
As much have developed during these years, there is a great motivation for this thesis
to compliment prefabrication to the development in energy demand in the building
industry.

State of art
An essential part of analysing the building envelope is to have a clear overview of
what’s on the market and what has recently been analysed. This chapter will present
the literature study on state of art.
2.1
Development of prefabricated elements
Following is some recent research that have inﬂuenced the work on development in
this thesis. DTU have done a lot of research on improving building envelopes, in 2000
a report on the topic was published. The primary focus of this research was to ﬁnd
a methodology for assessing a total economic point of view on insulation thickness.
A method has been developed to determine which level of insulation is the absolute
economic optimum for a given building envelope. The process is based on making up
the changes in the operational energy costs/heating costs and the cost of construction
due to a change in the insulation thickness during 30 years of the building life. A
particular focus is on thermal bridges, as these are assumed to increase with increased
insulation thicknesses.[10]
The research has stated that it is technically possible to build exterior walls with
less heat loss than those just complying with the requirements of the current building
regulations with no considerably added use of material apart from insulation. The
insulation demands may expediently increase in the new energy demands. A lot of
research is conducted on the thermal bridges between wall-foundation, wall-roof and
wall-window. It reveals that there is a lot of heat loss between foundation and wall,
and this is an area with potential for improvement. It is therefore suggested to ﬁnd
new components for the upper part of the foundation, possibly larger elements of light
concrete with suitable insulation. The thermal loss by the foundation reduces propor-
tionally with the insulation thickness. Windows also lead to heat loss; a goal may,
therefore, be window placement around the center of the insulation in the window sill,
or new windows that decrease two-dimensional heat ﬂow. [10]
Building technologies oﬃce of US Department Of Energy have published window and
building envelope research and development; a roadmap for emerging technologies.
The aim is to map the challenges and possibilities to improve the building envelope.
The result is that quick and easy prefabricated building envelope solutions and meth-
ods of predicting/conﬁrming behavior and performance of materials are essential for
the development. As a lot of heat loss is a leak in the connecting areas between walls,
foundations, and roofs, a solution is to improve these areas. A suggestion is that insu-
lation materials that are applicable to walls also ideally would be appropriate to other
parts of the envelope, such as the roof and foundation. Another critical area is the air
tightness.[32]

Vapour barrier
A vapour barrier is a material layer preventing water vapor in the indoor air getting
into, and wet, the building structure. Airtight joints prevent water vapor molecules
to diﬀuse into the structure and moisture entering the building due to diﬀerences in
air pressure between the inside and outside. Another perspective is airtightness of the
vapour barrier, which reduces energy consumption due to unintentional loss of hot air
into the environment, it improves indoor climate and comfort as cold outside air does
not ﬂow through the structures.[35]
The get a tight vapour barrier, the main aim is to have minimal connections and dis-
turbances caused by pipes or electrical wires. Therefore, it is expedient to place the
vapour barrier extracted some centimetres in the insulation area, maximum 1⁄4 of
the total insulation (usually approximately 4-5 cm). The vapour barrier is measured
with an Sd value. A material layer with an Sd value of 0,5 m equals that the material
layer has the same resistance to water vapour diﬀusion as a 0,5 m thick layer of still air.
Polyethylene foil with a thickness of 0,2 mm is most commonly used as vapour barrier
in wooden houses. This is because the product is delivered in large formats, which
gives few joints and thus less risk of leakage. The plastic ﬁlm has good tear strength,
is easy to process and shape in corners and joints. Transparency makes it possible to
inspect the insulation behind the vapour barrier.[2]
Wind barrier
A wind barrier is a material layer that prevents cold outside air, for example behind
ventilated façade cladding, to blow through the insulation. The barrier must be capa-
ble of withstanding and deriving moisture penetrating the rain screen. When selecting
a wind barrier, special attention should be paid to how long the selected material can
be left without cover/rain screen.[36]
The wind barrier has functions both during the construction and in the building when
it is ﬁnished. Before the external cladding is mounted, the wind barrier shall help close
the building and protect the insulation. Some wind barriers also have some mechanical
strength that may help wind bracing. [38].

Connective products
Gluing becomes more and more relevant as sealing and jointing method, this is how-
ever mostly for wind barriers and underlayer in roofs. Clamping between ﬂat surfaces
should still be the primary method of sealing, particularly for vapour barriers. For
proper sealing by clamping, it is important to use clamps that are suitable. Also, it is
crucial to not have too much distance between each nail or screw. [2]
Glue products can typically be used for additional sealing of conversion joints, and for
repairing holes and rifters. They can also be used to fasten the wind barrier to the
frame and to seal around the passages. Flexible sealing bands can be used for window
frames and pipe of various sizes can be used to seal ﬁnishes. For windows, clamping
should be included in addition, in order to have double sealing. Sealants can be used
in joints where one can expect larger movements. Sealing foam may be used where
other methods are complicated; this may be between elements, as thermal insulation
and air tightening in joints with small movements (the size should be minimum 5 mm
wide and maximum 50 mm deep for the foam to work correctly). [2]
Clamping

Methodology
There are many existing solutions for prefabricated building envelopes, and in recent
time there has been a development concerning energy performance and economic op-
timization. In this thesis, the ﬁrst step is to clarify the requirements for the building
system according to the nearly-zero energy buildings in the 2020 energy frame. The
next step is to do a thorough investigation of the already existing solutions, and then
make further development of the solutions for both roof- and wall element, that satis-
ﬁes both economical and energy performance.
Once the requirements for energy and indoor environment performance and the pro-
duction and assembly is decided, the geometry of a typical Danish type house is
decided, and the chosen building envelopes are analysed based on these requirements.
The analyse is performed using the software IDA ICE, where the building is modelled
and then optimised such that the energy consumption is close to the requirements
for low energy class 2020; 20kWh/m2.[3] The insulation thickness of the envelope is

The diﬀerent building components are compared with each other and then further

Requirements
Following are the requirements forming the basis for further analysis in the thesis.
5.1
Energy and indoor environment performance
5.1.1
Energy consumption
The new building regulations 2020 in Denmark states that the energy regulation for
a type house should have a yearly energy consumption of no more than 20 kWh/m2.
In building class 2020, the calculation of total energy consumption must be weighted
by the individual forms of supply. The factors are 1.8 for electricity, 0.60 for district
heating and for other forms of heat use a factor of 1.0 and the relevant utility. At
this point, this is a voluntary requirement for low energy buildings and will be the
requirement for these simulations. This energy consumption includes the yearly sup-
plied primary energy for cooling, heating, ventilation, electric energy and domestic hot
water. The electrical energy use does not include energy from artiﬁcial lighting. For
installations where the unit and duct system only serve one dwelling, the speciﬁc elec-
tricity consumption for air transport must not exceed 800 J/m3 at maximum pressure
loss. Ventilation plants where the unit and duct system only serve one dwelling shall
be carried out with heat recovery with a dry eﬃciency of at least 85 percent.[4]
5.1.2
Air tightness and moisture control
It is essential for the heating and cooling demand to minimize the air leakage. Inﬁl-
tration through the envelope will allow cold air in and warm air out, this increases
the demand on the heating system, and thereby also the energy demand. Less evident
is the transport of moisture in the air through these leaks, that can cause condense.
When it is cold outside, the warm and moist indoor air will be transported through
the leaks to the cold air. Then the moisture will condense, and if not dried, it will ac-
cumulate over time. This makes the vapour barrier an important part of the building
envelope. [34]
For quantitative requirements, the Danish Building Regulations building class 2020
require that volume ﬂow through leaks in the building envelope must not exceed 0.5
l/s per m2 of the heated ﬂoor area at a pressure diﬀerential of 50 Pa. This is, however,
a minimum demand, as a solution to achieve the requirement have been to ﬁx energy in
other ways, as PV-systems. For the following thesis, where the goal is to improve the
elements, the leakage shall be even less. The local council makes requirements for how
often documentation must be provided of the volume ﬂow through leaks in pressure
tests. The result of the pressure test must be expressed as the average of measurements
using overpressure and underpressure. With increased energy demands, a solution has
been to increase the insulation thickness. This has lead to a lower temperature of the
outer part of the wall, and thereby an increased chance of condensation. [4]

Buildings must be designed, carried out and maintained so that water and moisture
do not endanger the health of persons or damage to the building. Buildings must
therefore be protected from accumulation of moisture due to moisture transport from
the indoor air. Thermal bridges in the climate screen must not cause problems with
eg. condensation and mold growth. Building structures and materials must not have a
moisture content, which in turn leads to the risk of growth of mold. Buildings must be
secured against the penetration of water from groundwater and surface water. Build-
ings must also be secured against the absorption of moisture from the underground.
Following is strategies for moisture control of the diﬀerent elements. [4]
Wall
There are several reasons to avoid condensation in the wall cavity, as moisture has
several consequences. Both the potential for moisture accumulation and growth of
mould is signiﬁcantly increased, in case of moist. For timber structures, the risk is
wood rot, and for steel, the risk is corrosion. The climate screen must be designed,
carried out and maintained to cover the penetration of rain and meltwater, and so
that it can run smoothly. [34]
In addition to proper sealing, drainage cavity should be considered for the external
walls. A drainage cavity consists of water repellent house wrap, a ventilated cavity
under the exterior cladding and a continuous vapour-retarding barrier towards the
interior.[55]
Roof
The roof construction is a critical location for moisture control; two main reasons are
leakage precipitation or vapour diﬀusion condensation. The leakage may be avoided
with a water-tight exterior layer. In cold climates, however, a water vapour barrier
on the inside of the construction is also necessary. Moisture that inﬁltrates past the
vapour barrier to the center of the construction is then captured and will accumulate
over time. The moisture must either be prevented from inﬁltrating into the construc-
tion, or the roof must be routinely ventilated. [55]
It may be advantageous to develop a vented roof system that can detect the presence
of moisture and provide ventilation to keep the relative humidity consistently below
levels that would lead to mould growth. Another solution that is theoretically possible
but more diﬃcult to realize in practice is a warm roof system. A warm roof means that
the structural layer is kept above the dew point temperature during the entire year
so that condensation cannot occur in the layer. This can be managed by providing at
least half of the roof construction thermal resistance outside of the main load bearing
structure.[55] Roof water must be diverted via drains and/or tagnets. [4]

5.1.3
Thermal performance
The thermal performance of a building refers among others to the envelope insulation
and window performance that controls the indoor environment from the outdoor en-
vironment and climate ﬂuctuations. The minimum insulation requirement is not only
a response to the requirement for energy savings, but also for providing comfort and
avoid the risk of condensation [3]. Generally, increased thermal insulating decreases
heating and cooling demand to obtain a comfortable indoor environment, to a certain
limit.
Heating and cooling demand plays an essential part of the overall energy consumption
within the 2020 Energy Frame, and a reduction of both the heating and cooling de-
mand is of high importance when designing the building for nearly-zero energy level.
The thermal performance of a building envelope is contributed by the following subsec-
tion; thermal transmittance, thermal bridge and thermal capacity. These subsections
are explained and explored below.
Thermal Transmittance
The insulation on a nearly-zero energy building is of crucial importance due to its
vital role in the building thermal losses. Housing must be carried out so that the
dimensioning transmission loss per m2 climate screen does not exceed 3,7 W when the
building is on one ﬂoor. But this is only a minimal requirement to ensure that the
building envelope has an acceptable thermal performance. The area of windows and
doors, and transmission losses through these are not included in the calculation. The
speciﬁc thermal loss of the diﬀerent components is calculated, and the total speciﬁc
thermal loss is then found as the sum of the thermal losses from the bearing surfaces
and the ventilation loss for the entire building. [7]
The thermal coeﬃcient, the U-value, describes the rate of transfer of heat through
one square meter of structure for every one degree of temperature diﬀerence across
the structure. The thermal loss through the building is dependent on the thermal
transmittance coeﬃcient. Therefore, the U-value has to meet the requirements of the
current building regulations, which in this case is Building regulations 2018.

Thermal Bridges
Thermal bridges within the envelope require structural details that often result in
greater heat ﬂow than the typical cross-section. The heat ﬂow is normally perpendic-
ular to the surface and is changed as a result of either decreased or increased heat ﬂow
density. Thermal bridges can be classiﬁed into two categories; point or linear thermal
bridges. As the thermal insulation increases, the eﬀect of the thermal bridges becomes
more substantial. This means that thermal bridges can play a crucial role regarding
building energy eﬃciency in a well-insulated building. [28]
The thermal losses of the building from the thermal bridges are signiﬁcant and can
be minimized. The solutions to reduce the thermal loss are chosen according to the
type of thermal bridge, materials from which the elements are made oﬀ, and economic
and physical limitations. One solution is an extrusion of a construction element at the
location where heat is most likely to escape due to conduction. Another solution is
to insulate the sensitive junctions with materials of low thermal conductivity. Using
vapour barriers or eliminate insulation piercing should be applied. [28]

Increased light transmittance and glazing creates better daylight conditions, but it
also allows more solar radiation into the building which can lead to reduced space
heating demands and an increased in overheating periods. It is therefore, necessary

BR18 require adequate access to daylight to be documented by the glazing area with-

speciﬁed glass area must be corrected for any shady surroundings, reduced light trans-

a glazed area of 20% of the ﬂoor area is used, due to a focuse on suﬃcient daylight.

If the light transmittance is lower, the glazed area must be increased correspondingly.

nal illumination intensity of daylight is 300 lux or more at least half of the relevant
ﬂoor area for at least half of the daylight hours. [4]
The daylight factor is the ratio of the illuminance on a surface in a room to that on
an external unobstructed horizontal surface, taking only the diﬀuse radiation from an
overcast sky into account. Daylight in workrooms etc. may similarly be taken to be
suﬃcient if a calculation or measurement can show that there is a daylight factor of
2% at the workspaces. [3]

be developed to counteract the visual impact of the thick walls and the reduction of the
daylight entering the rooms. There are no speciﬁc recommendations on this subject,
but splayed windows allow more daylight into the rooms and create an appearance of
a larger opening.
An advantage can be to place the additional needed insulation in the roof construction,
rather in the exterior walls. Increased insulation thickness in the roof is less inhibitive
than increasing the thickness of the exterior walls, and will not impact the daylight
entering the room or the appearance.

5.2
Production and assembly
Successful production and assembly of a building system require a complex plan that
eliminates any chance for error in the process from start to ﬁnish. If properly conceived
and implemented, prefabrication reduces the risk of problems arising on-site.
Still, prefabricated systems require signiﬁcant amounts of on-site work to complete the
building. This includes installation of technical service, claddings, vapour barrier and
roof water membrane among others. Improvisations by the contractor or a job hurried
due to inclement weather may result in expensive maintenance or replacement. To
minimize the risk of mistake on-site two main principles are used:
1. Reduce the amount of work to be performed on-site
2. Ensure that the on-site work is simple, intuitive, fast, and impossible to perform
incorrectly
In order for the elements to be properly transported, there are some techniques/re-
quirements. One way of transporting items is standing or in so-called A-frames. The
elements shall be protected to weather. The elements must be limited to standard
shipping dimensions, which are approximately 12,0 m long x 3,0 m wide x 3,6 m high,
with some variation depending on the speciﬁc transportation route. Special permits
may be given if necessary, if cost-beneﬁt analysis allows it.[16]
Wall elements should be mounted in full wall height, without horizontal seams. The
walls must be connected to the static system, based on the calculations in the speciﬁc
system. A fast mounting procedure is desired to quickly close the building envelope
which reduces the risk of moisture built-in to the construction or delays in the work
due to inclement weather. This can be achieved by connection details that are well-
designed and easy to make. Preferably envelope elements arrive on-site and are ready
to lift at the proper moment.[16]
It is essential to the performance of the building that the elements cannot be ﬁxed in
an incorrect position. Small errors such as this can occur and cause a larger problem
at the ﬁnal joint. Connections between elements must be designed in such a way that
the connection cannot be made unless both elements are in the exact correct posi-
tion. Elements should only be allowed to be ﬁxed when they are in the right position.

Durability and maintenance
The lifetime of a building is mainly based on what materials used in the construction,
and the climate the elements are exposed to. To chose the right material for the given
climate and to limit the exposure is therefore a basis of a well-designed system. The
overall goal is to reduce errors in the construction. These will, however, occur to some
extent. A focus is therefore, to prepare for repair, to consider the maintenance during
design will then be a more cost-eﬀective manner.
5.4
Cost
The insulation thickness of the envelope is optimized using a method based on the
concept Cost of Conserved Energy (CCE). The unit of CCE is "cost per saved energy
unit", and a measure is considered economically eﬃcient if the CCE is lower than
the weighted average cost of primary energy in the useful time of the measure [50].
The method ﬁnds the minimum cost when fulﬁlling the BR15 energy frame, and the
CCE in all the individual energy-saving measures is identical and minimized. The
output is a qualiﬁed estimate of an economically optimal solution. The CCE is not a
requirement, but only a tool for relative optimization. [50]

Vacuum insulated panels (VIP)
Vacuum insulation panels consist of a porous core, sealed of a vapor and wind barrier
making a vacuum. The positive aspect of this material is that it has a very low ther-
mal conductivity, making thinner walls possible. VIPs are however expensive and have
a short lifetime of 25 years. The panels are fragile and need mechanical protection
against punctures, and as all edges are wrapped in foil, it may lead to an increased
thermal bridge in all the connections. As the material is not ﬂexible, it is not very
applicable for a timber frame or similar. The same counts for concrete walls, as the
alkaline environment might damage the foil. There is, however, a positive aspect in
northern countries with its cold climate, because research shows that the panels de-
crease the lifespan even further in warmer climates. VIP is therefore mostly applicable
in certain areas where maintenance is easily accessible, and it is necessary with a thin
wall. If the panels are improved, making them less fragile and cheaper, it might be a
good replacement of traditional insulation. [45]
Polyurethane Foam (PUR/PIR)
Stiﬀpolyurethane foam (PUR/PIR) is used as factory-made thermal insulation mate-
rial in the form of insulation boards or foam blocks. It is one of the most eﬃcient and
high-quality insulation materials, which provides highly eﬃcient energy saving with
minimal space requirement. The material of rigid polyurethane foam (PUR/PIR) is
highly versatile with its optimum insulation ability. The products include everything
from insulation boards to rooﬁng, walls, ﬂoors and internal ceilings, to window insu-
lation. It is also stable and durable, with a lifespan of over 50 years.[39]

Extruded polystyrene (XPS)
XPS is an insulation type that is commonly used in a variety of installations for the
entire building envelope. It is a rigid insulation that is formed with polystyrene poly-
mer, manufactured using an extrusion process.
The extrusion process results in a
closed cell structure with a smooth surface on the top and bottom of the board. The
closed cell structure prevents water penetration and gives a long-term strength and
durability. XPS has a good thermal conductivity and a high compressive strength.
It has a higher water vapour diﬀusion resistance than EPS and is more suitable for
wetter environments. [49]
Polystyrene (EPS)
EPS is an insulation type formed with polystyrene polymer, manufactured using beads
of foam within a mould. These beads expand and fuse together after steam or heat is
directly applied to them. This expanding process results in a closed cell structure, not
a closed cell insulation board as voids can occur between the beads. These voids will
conduct heat, and gives a lower thermal performance than XPS. EPS also has a lower
compressive strength than XPS. For EPS to achieve the same compressive strength
as XPS, the density of EPS needs to be increased, which often results in a greater
thickness of the board. [49]In the ﬂoor a white EPS is used, this is the cheap version.
For the optimisation a gray EPS is chosen. The gray type is improved with graphite
in the mixture. Graphite is a radiation-reducing material, it reduces heat transfer
and decreases the thermal conductivity. The result is a gray EPS with 20 % better
insulation than white EPS. This type is, however, more expensive. [25]

7.3.2
Conclusion of insulation material
Many insulation materials present a greater ﬁre hazard, being more combustible, less
eﬀective ﬁre barriers and having higher ﬁre toxic. Glass wool and stone wool are clas-
siﬁed as non-combustible or limited combustible.[1] The fumed silica core in VIP is
not combustible, but the polymer barrier is. However, additional layers added can
reduce the ﬁre rating.[5] PUR, PIR, XPS, and EPS are ignitable and must have spe-
cial precautions. They are acceptable in buildings below four storeys but must have
suﬃcient cower. The cover may be mineral wool in connections, and this will ensure
that the insulation does not get involved in the early phase of a ﬁre. For a single story
type house, these insulations are acceptable concerning ﬁre precautions.[29] Most ﬁre
deaths and injuries result from inhalation of toxic ﬁre eﬄuents, and ﬁre gas toxicity
is recognized as a major factor in the assessment of ﬁre hazard. Toxic product yields
depend on the material composition and the ﬁre conditions. The insulation materials
have an order of increasing ﬁre toxicity, from stone wool (least toxic), glass wool, VIP,
EPS/XPS, PUR, and PIR. Burning PIR foam has a toxic hazard factor just below the
threshold for immediate danger, implying a toxic hazard.[1] Even though PIR is more
ﬁre toxic than PUR, it has better ﬁre resistance properties[19].
XPS and VIP are much more expensive than the other insulation types and has a very
high CCE seen in Table 7.6. Even though VIP gets a much slimmer wall, the CCE is
still so high that the reduction in insulation thickness does not add up for the large
increase in price. Both XPS and VIP may in some cases be expedient to use in some
areas. There are, however, other issues that needs to be considered. For VIP it seems
to be too fragile at this point of time. It has a short lifetime and is not very applicable,
but in the future, it may be a good alternative to the mineral wool.

Analysis of air tightness and moisture control
Air tightness is important to achieve a good energy performance for obvious reasons.
Moist is, however, just as important. If the thermal insulation in the building is wet,
insulation capacity decreases and the heat and cooling requirements will increase. Wet
wood gives fongus and mold. Most materials contain more moisture when building
than when the building is in use and the moisture level in the materials is in equilibrium
with the surroundings. This moisture overload is called building moisture and must
be dried out. If it is set too short for drying up building moisture, it is diﬃcult and
costly to compensate for it later. Delays in the building process, building damage
and undesirable indoor enviroment, can often be the most expensive consequences of
building moisture.[44]
11.1
Achieving good airtightness.
Good airtightness is dependent on planning and good detail drawings. General guide-
lines to achieve good airtightness is: [38]
• Both joint and sealing methods for internal or external air tightening must be
well planned and described.
• Connections should be drawn in a large scale (1:10, 1:5).
• Complicated or diﬃcult 3-D corners, such as bottom/side frame/window sill
should be considered outlined in perspective.
• Diﬃcult assemblys should be described step by step, preferably as drawings.
• Use products with documented adhesion and resistance, such as tape or adhesive
tape.
• Both the wind barrier and the vapour barrier should, if possible, be run contin-
uously around the entire building.
• Large formats for both wind barrier and vapour barrier will reduce the number
of joints.

Moisture control
There are two main options to avoid moisture problems. Either to avoid moist at all or
to have an acceptable drying time so that the moist do not cause any problems. Many
analyses are made in order to see how the latest development in building envelopes
aﬀect the drying time. SINTEF have therefore tested diﬀerences in drying time in 150
mm insulation and 400 mm insulation in a timber frame construction.[42]
It is observed that with a more open vapor barrier, the diﬀerence in drying rate be-
tween insulation thickness of 400 mm and 150 mm is less. This is due to the fact
that this vapor barrier allows for a certain drying inward, especially when the outside
temperature is high, a reduced proportion of the total moisture dries beyond the tem-
perature diﬀerentials.[42]
For the case of a more vapor-proof windshield, it may appear that the diﬀerence in
drying rate between the insulation thickness of 400 mm and 150 mm has decreased.
Then the diﬀerence is about the same if we look at the drying rate down to 80% RH -
which is interesting concerning mould risk. As the most common indoor growth molds
require over 80-85% relative humidity (RH) on the material surface or in pores in the
material surface to grow.[42]
The test executed for these results shows that this diﬀerence in mould risk between
insulation thickness is mostly caused by increased thickness of studs. So in case of the
improved timber frame with I-beam studs will give a smaller mould risk. Connection
areas between two items of wood or other materials are generally more critical. This
is, for example at the assmbly are of windows. In these interface between the two
materials, mold can grow. [42]
In addition to the membranes and studs, the insulation materials have diﬀerences
concerning moisture control. XPS has, for example, better moisture properties than
EPS, as it has a closed cell structure. [17]

LCA
LCA analysis of the diﬀerent wall types is executed in the program LCAbyg. LCAbyg
is a tool that calculates life cycle assessments for buildings. Based on this, an en-
vironmental proﬁle and resource consumption of the diﬀerent wall types is made.
Information about the building components and the building’s energy consumption is
input in the program. The input is based on Environmental Product Declarations.
The tool automatically calculates the LCA.
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a method that can provide a picture of the potential
environmental impacts and resource utilization of a building. This is calculated over
the entire life cycle of the building and therefore includes the procurement of raw ma-
terials, the production of building materials, the energy and resource consumption of
operation and maintenance, as well as the disposal and possible recycling of building
parts and building materials.
The analysis is based on nine factors. GWP measures the impact of the global warm-
ing, measured in CO2 equivalents. As the amount of greenhouse gases in the atmo-
sphere increases, the air close to earth will be heated, leading to climate change. ODP
measures ozone layer degradation by R11-equivalents. The ozone layer protects ﬂora
and fauna against harmful UV-A and UV-B rays, making it important to limit degra-
dation.
POCP measure the photochemical ozone formation, by ethen-equivalents.
This contributes to UV rays that form ground-level ozone, which is harmful to air-
ways. AP is the amount of SO2 equivalents, measuring the acidiﬁcation. SO2 reacts
with water and falls like "sour rain" which break down root systems and wash the
plants nutrients. The nutrient load is called an EP factor, based on PO4 equivalents.
Too high nutrient inputs promote undesirable plant growth in delicate ecosystems, for
example algal growth which lead to ﬁsh deaths. PEtot is the primary energy use.
This is important as high consumption of primary energy resources (before conver-
sion) from fossil and renewable sources can contribute to resource shortages. ADPe is
discharge of Abiotic resources - chemical elements, measured in Sb-equivalents. ADPf
Discharge of abiotic resources - fossil fuels, measured in MJ. PEtot is the primary
total energy consumption. It is measured in MJ and is included as high consumption
of resources in primary energy forms from fossil and renewable sources can contribute
to the depletion of natural resources.
The last one is secondary fuels, SEK, mea-
sured in kWh. Secondary fuels (eg. waste) are in principle a limited resource, and
therefore high consumption of secondary fuels can indirectly lead to resource shortages
Cradle-to-gate, i.e., no impact from operation or recycling. Based on the numbers
from LCAbyg a total score may be calculated. Two time periods are evaluated: 50
years and 120 years. They are weighted with 70 % and 30 %.

Discussion
For a general comparison of the wall types, there is diﬃcult to say one that is clearly
better than others. To chose the best wall type is a complex decision including a sub-
jecting perspective of the desirable architectural appearance. But a clear improvement
is to use I-beams instead of massive timber stubs. This has been analysed in previ-
ously published work, the building industry however, still often use the traditional
massive timber studs. The traditional Danish brick wall is the clearly thickest wall, so
all other wall types will be an improvement concerning thickness. The CCE analysis
shows that it is a cheap choice, but this is however only the cost for the insulation,
making an reasonable assumption that it would not be the cheapest when all materials
are included. This principle also counts for other wall types with massive back walls.
The analysis of diﬀerent insulation types shows that it is a complex area for improve-
ment, therefore is it challenging to achieve a deﬁnite conclusion. It seems that the
mineral wool is the most optimal in most cases, as the other is both expensive and
some lead to risk of considerable damage in case of ﬁre. There is a signiﬁcant diﬀerence
in cost between the diﬀerent insulation types, and if some of them become cheaper, a
diﬀerent conclusion could be made. There may, however, raise a discussion is special
cases. If it is advantageous to choose a more expensive system, special compositions
may be used. If there is particularly important to have a thinner wall, the materials as
PUR, XPS or even VIP may be used, and then rather protect the material against ﬁre
with an additional mineral wool layer either outside the whole wall or in connections.
Also, if there are small areas with speciﬁc needs, the expensive materials may be used
at this exact point.
There will also be limitations in the choice of insulation material for which wall type
that is chosen. With a timber frame, and also Termowood, there will be issues getting
the system completely tight. In constructions like Rockwool and CLT, the limitation
will be opposite. In these cases the will be necessary with a stiﬀinsulation material in
order to be able to connect the insulation to the back wall without the need of studs
in addition. With SIP all options are however possible.
The thermal bridges are also calculated to see if there is a diﬀerence in wall types and
how large thermal bridges they have. Timber frame with I-beam has in general very
low thermal bridge values. In connection of modules, it is however among the highest
values. This may be because timber frame is the most traditional wall type and is
therefore not as well developed for prefabrication. Systems like Termowood and CLT
are developed as prefabricated elements and are therefore a well-developed system of
connections. SIP also has a high thermal bridge in connections, this should be pos-
sible to minimize. For the connections of roof and wall, there are some considerable
diﬀerences. This, however, is one of the reason that there are diﬀerent connection
principles. It was during the analysis phase chosen to use the one common connection

to the others. The analyses of this theses show that the best option is the one without
parapet, and then the details must be executed precise to not get moist problems. An
eave must be included in the size necessary to get the water removed, and the airways
must be adequately protected against water and moist. There are many options on
how this may be done, but detailed drawings and instructions must be included for
the carpenters. One option is shown in Figure 9.2.
Another discussion concerning roof is the material used for beams. The analysis show
the same result as for the timber frame studs; the I-beams is a much greater option
than the massive wood studs. Some have improved by using thin steel elements, which
they claim enhances the performance. The analysis in this theses, however, show that
this gives great thermal bridges.
The analyses show that there is a great potential for improvement in the window as-
sembly. There are many choices and diﬀerent perspectives in the assembly of window.
The analysis shows that the greatest improvement is to both use a solution with a thin
frame, or better, without frame and a placement in the middle of the wall. To place
the window in the middle of the wall lead to an architectural issue. It is optimal for
thermal bridge purpose, but if the architectural perspective is too important, a great
improvement is also to just use a solution with a optimized size of the frame. An-
other perspective of the window assembly is prepare for repair. It should be possible
to replace the window without too many issues. In Figure 10.4 is however a possible
solution to achieve this. In this solution, it is only necessary to remove the water board.
Good airtightness is dependent on planning and good detail drawings.
A general
improvement to the assembly of the building is the detailed instruction on how the

Conclusion
For the simulations and analysis in this thesis, eight diﬀerent wall types have been
tested. First the insulation was analysed. Both the energy consumption for the dif-
ferent wall types and diﬀerent insulation types was investigated.
A CCE analysis
was done for each wall type. SIP had the thinnest wall thickness when the insulation
thickness was optimized, but the CCE value lies among the mean value, considering all
the wall types. Both the Rockwool walls, CLT and Termowood had a thin insulation
thickness with a low CCE-value. The brick wall, which has been used as a reference
wall, and the timber frame with traditional studs had the thickest insulation thickness
and a high CCE. In total wall thickness the brick wall and Rockwool is the thickest.
The same CCE analysis was done for diﬀerent insulation types. The analysis showed
that decreasing lambda values gives decreasing wall thickness and increasing CCE.
The price is too high when considering the insulation types as VIP and XPS, even
though VIP provides insulation with a thickness reduction of 80% compared to mineral
wool, class 37. VIP is also very fragile and has a short lifetime, it is not applicable to
be used as insulation in the wall. Even though mineral wool gives a thicker wall, the
CCE is much lower, and provides a more acceptable result.
The next analysis was for the thermal performance. The thermal bridge was calcu-
lated for diﬀerent connections of the building for the various wall types. The timber
frame was among the wall types with lowest linear heat loss coeﬃcient for almost all
the diﬀerent connections, especially the timber frame with I-beam. However, for the
connection of modules, the timber frame has much higher values than the rest of the
wall types. Rockwool had a high linear heat loss coeﬃcient for most of the connection
tested.
The thesis also includes a analyse of roof construction. This is for ﬂat roof, where
the principle connection of roof and wall was investigated. This analysis showed that
the parapet is not a good solution concerning the thermal bridge. It is also easier to
assemble the roof to the wall on site without a parapet. Diﬀerent structural materials
in the roof construction were analysed for timber frame and steel proﬁles. The steel
framing showed a greater thermal bridge and U-value. The timber frame with I-beam
gave a signiﬁcant advantage compared to the timber frame with traditional frames.
An analysis for windows was performed, where diﬀerent frame solutions were investi-
gated. The thermal bridge of the assembly of the window may be reduced with 60%
be minimizing the frame. The best solution will be to build in and insulate the frame
in the wall. This will give a considerable reduction in the supplied primary energy and
insulation thickness. Quadruple windows were investigated for the purpose of using
larger windows in combination with a removed frame and still achieve the requirements
for the indoor climate. The analysis showed that the there is an improvement with

quadruple glazing, of reducing approximately 10 cm of insulation.
A aim of the thesis was to improve the the building construction from the traditional
brick wall. This is achieved, by choosing another wall type and prefabrication. The
analysis of windows and roof will be an improvement on both brick walls and prefab-
ricated walls. On the perspective on environment, the LCA show that brick wall has
one of the lowest total scores, as for concrete walls.

Future work
Optimization of the ﬂoor and foundation was not considered to be within the scope of
this thesis, and could therefore be improved in future work.
The improvement of window assembly was analysed in this thesis. To further develop
the idea of total removal of the frame will be a subject for future work. An idea is
suggested in this thesis to use a gasproof insulation material to achieve the same U-
value as for the window. This may be analysed, and speciﬁc product may be developed.
The insulation types may also be further improved. For this point of time, there is
many products with good ⁄ values, but they must be produced cheaper and more
robust to be competitive to the classical mineral wool.
Timber frame and SIP constructions show good result on many aspects, they however
doesn’t have an optimized connection between modules, concerning thermal bridge.
Therefore these may be improved in this area to be even better suited to prefabricated
construction.
