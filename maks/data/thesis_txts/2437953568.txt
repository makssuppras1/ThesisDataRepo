Abstract
Due to the rapid growth of our consumerist society, plastic has become one of the major pollutants
of the oceans. The extent of this pollution is estimated to range in the millions of metric tons based
on the plastic production, but when looking at the actual ﬂoating plastic debris concentrations,
only 1% of it is found. The size distribution of this fraction shows a clear selective process that is
removing fragments smaller than 2.5 [mm] from the surface. One of the potential explanation is
biofouling. Algae and other marine life colonize the plastic surface and by doing so, increase the
speciﬁc density of the particle. Considering that the available surface limits growth on the plastic
and that smaller particles have a higher surface/volume ratio, there is a size threshold where
biofouling by microorganisms is not suﬃcient to make the plastic sink. Leaving only particles
above 2.5 mm ﬂoating in the water. This project is looking at the spatial distribution and fate
of plastics undergoing biofouling in the North Sea. For this, an Individual-Based Model (IBM)
was developed where plastic particles are generated at a speciﬁc source and experience growth
biofouling that is dependent on the surrounding chlorophyll concentration, temperature, and light.
The pollution modeled are Dolly Ropes (DR), which are strings of high-density polyethylene used
in beam-trawling ﬁshing. The sources used in the model is set to correspond to a zone of intense
beam-trawling ﬁshing pressure, and the scenarios look at beaching, plastic density and size, time
of release, and source localization. The results show that the particles sediment in more shallow
areas, along the residual currents, near the coast and have more chances to beach during the
winter than the summer. From September to March, the chlorophyll concentration is too low for
the particle to sediment. Therefore, all particle emitted during this period drift at the surface
until beaching or experiencing the next spring bloom. Most of the time, the particles sediment in
a diﬀerent exclusive economic zone than their source which could lead to some political issues.

Introduction
Plastic is essential to today’s society. Due to its low cost and capacity to be produced in any
texture, color, and shape, it is found in almost every product and now, in every places on earth
too. It has become one of the most common and persistent pollutants found in the ocean [53].
Even the most remote places of the ocean such as the Arctic and the deep sea are polluted [21][77]
[74]. Estimates of the pollution based on the global plastic production range between 4.8 and 12.7
million metric tons in 2010 and continues to increase. Surprisingly, only ∼1% of this amount is
perceptible at the surface [20][33][75][13]. Still, most of the plastic produced has a lower density
than water [6] and should ﬂoat. The missing plastic can only leave the ocean’s surface by 4 means:
beaching, entering the food web, being degraded or sinking and entering the sediments. Probably
all of these processes account for a fraction of the missing plastic, but some studies showed that
sedimentation is most likely the dominant one [25][77][13].
C`osar et al.[20] looked at the size distribution of the surface plastic particles in every ocean,
expecting to ﬁnd an exponential occurrence with a decrease in particle size due to fragmentation
(ﬁgure A.1a). However, the distribution looked more like a Gaussian with a median around 2.5
mm, which means that most of the missing plastic particles are smaller than 2 mm (ﬁgure A.1b).
A few theories explaining this gap were emitted: [20]
• Washing ashore
• Chemical photodegradation of the plastic (photolysis)
• Consumption by microorganisms
• Sinking due to biofouling (colonization by marine organisms with a higher density than sea-
water)
• Eaten up and defecated by ﬁsh
However, none of these theories can explain alone the disappearance of 99 % of the ocean plastic
from the surface, and some of them are very unlikely. Washing ashore is probably removing more
lage plastic pieces than small ones due to their exposure to wind, and once in the ocean gyres,
they are not likely to be brought back to shore. Also, there is no evidence that photolysis and
consumption by microorganisms are happening faster on smaller particles than on big ones [20].
Eaten up and defecated by ﬁsh is also unlikely. A study by Beer et al. [9] showed no increase over
the last 3 decades in plastic concentration in the digestive system of the Baltic ﬁsh population
even though the production of plastic increased signiﬁcantly.
Other studies [63] demonstrated that biofouling could be the most important of these vectors and
SINTEF Oceans [13] stated in 2017 that about 91.63% of the marine plastic is already being stored
in the sediments. They also stated that the shorelines and coastal sediments have on average a
microplastic concentration one order of magnitude higher than the deep-sea sediments even though
they account for only a small percentage of the global sediment area and volume. Fazey et al.
showed evidence that smaller plastic items sink faster than large ones [28]. Another study by Ryan
et al. [63] showed that larger particles tend to account for a higher proportion of the total plastic
particles as the distance from the shore increases. Since the litter source is land-based, this means
that smaller particles are disappearing from the surface faster than bigger ones.

Ryan et al. [63] explains that 4 main factors can describe this phenomenon:
• Biofouling can increase the speciﬁc density of the particle
• Biofouling is related to the available surface of the particle
• Buoyancy is linked to the volume and density of the particle
• The surface/volume ratio is bigger for smaller particles
These 4 statements mean that smaller particles should sink earlier than bigger particles when
colonized at the same rate. Another critical factor to take into account is the carrying capacity
of these particles. When looking at bioﬁlms [58], the thickness of them usually reaches a steady
state or carrying capacity that depends on available nutrients and ﬂow regime. This means that
the maximal volume of bioﬁlm on a particle would depend on this thickness and the surface of the
plastic. This carrying capacity can explain why the size distribution of the particles increases as
expected until a certain size [20] (ﬁgure A.1).
Bioﬁlms are heterogeneous matrix made out of extracellular proteins, sugars, and DNA with algae
and microbes living in it [64]. Most of the time, the organisms feed on the available substrate
such as sugars found in the water but also the ones produced by the resident algae. Since au-
totrophs are the ones producing the sugar, it is relevant to say that the growth of a bioﬁlm is
linked to the surrounding chlorophyll concentration. Furthermore, algae are producing what is
called Extracellular Polymeric Substances (EPS) which are sticky molecules that can provoke the
formation of aggregates but can also constitute the matrix of the bioﬁlm. This matrix can contain
phytoplankton, plastic particles and heavier molecules such as suspended sediment. They are also
responsible for marine snow aggregates that have recently been identiﬁed as a signiﬁcant sink for
microplastics [60].
Artham et al. [7] showed that the biofouling growth rate is highly correlated to the environment
productivity. Which is why an IBM model is necessary when modeling biofouling and sedimenta-
tion of plastics in the environment.
The reason why it is essential to understand the behavior and fate of plastic in the ocean is due to
its potential impact on life. Some of these plastics contain PCB’s and other toxic molecules but
can also serve as transport vectors for some hydrophobic pollutants that adsorb on them [73]. If
ingested by marine life, these pollutants can enter the food chain. Polyethylene especially since it
can accumulate more pollutants than other plastics [50].
Many particle tracking models have been focusing on ﬂoating plastic pollution which helped conﬁrm
their behavior at the sea surface [38][31][54][43][49][42]. Some of them included beaching which
an easy and inexpensive way to conﬁrm the accuracy of the model [22] but none of them were
considering sinking of the plastic particles. On the other side, a few studies focused on the sinking
behavior of microplastics due to biofouling [44][18][39][35], but none of them looked at their spatial
distribution using realistic hydrodynamic and environmental data. Taking all these aspects into
account is important due to the diversity of ﬂow regimes, and environmental conditions that
support the growth of the bioﬁlm itself. In order to conﬁrm the sinking behavior of plastic particles
due to biofouling in the environment, it is essential to be able to model it.
The aim of this study was hence to explore the possibility to simulate the sinking of plastic particles
in environmental condition using some established biofouling models in an individual-based model
(IBM). The latter includes biofouling growth on the plastic particles in the context of the North Sea

conditions. Biofouling is implemented in the model as the main sinking process, and the pollution
initially chosen for this model are Dolly Ropes (DR). This model allows highlighting the inﬂuence
of seasonality, particle size and source placement on the sinking potential of plastic particles.
Such a model is a useful tool to understand what is happening to the plastic when reaching the
sea. Some plastic pollutions might be crossing borders when other might sediment in a delicate
area such as a mussel-farm. It can be used in the context of political decision making when dealing
with the topic of environmental conservation.

Materials & methods
2.1
Modeled plastic: Dolly Ropes (DR)
The DR were chosen as support to model the bioﬁlm growth because they are often encountered
during plankton surveys around the North Sea, and they are easily recognizable. DR are also
very representative of the general pollution in a few diﬀerent aspects. Although the marine plastic
debris main source is land-based, at least 10% and up to 30% of the items found in the oceans
are related to ﬁshing activities [48][12]. This percentage is even higher in the North Sea. The
most common marine debris found are ropes and nets (30%) [12] but smaller synthetic ﬁbers, from
clothing mainly, are responsible for a very important yet less visible plastic pollution in the ocean.
Most of these items are also made out of polyethylene plastic. Modeling ﬁbers instead of spherical
particles represent a challenge but is also interesting since most of the plastic pollution has this
shape [8].
The DR are used in industrial beam trawling ﬁshing to protect the nets from being ripped oﬀby the
seaﬂoor (see ﬁgure B.1). They are long high-density polyethylene (HDPE) ﬁbers with a diameter of
about 0.6 mm. According to Sicor[4], these DR are made out of ”virgin”, HDPE (density ranging
from 930 to 980 kg m−3) but more precise information are diﬃcult to ﬁnd. A Dutch study [9]
estimated that European ﬁsheries use on average 100’000 kilograms of DR annually. The Dutch
ﬁshing industry accounting for 40% of it. On average it is estimated that 10-25% of the DR are
being ripped oﬀby the seaﬂoor. On a European scale, this represents 10’000-25’000 kilograms
which end up in the sea every year. Besides DR getting lost at sea, an unknown amount is cast
overboard during maintenance work. It is the most common marine plastic debris encountered on
the Dutch coast (more than 100 pieces of DR per 100 meters of beach) but also in some areas in
the North Sea.
Bastian Huwer et al. [30] took the opportunity during the yearly herring larvae survey on board
with the International Bottom Trawls Survey (IBTS), to sample and register the encountered
marine litter. The lengths of the DR noted as monoﬁlaments were registered and analyzed. Addi-
tionally, they were looked under the microscope to detect if biofouling was present.
The net used to collect the free-ﬂoating plastics in the water is called a MIK (Methot Isaac Kidd).
It has a diameter of 2 m, a ﬁnal mesh of 500 µm and is initially designed to sample ﬁsh larvae
(technical description of the MIK in the appendix). During the survey, the MIK goes from the
surface to 5 m above the sea ﬂoor and back up. The plastics encountered could, as a result, be
anywhere in the water columns or at the surface.

The beam trawling ﬁshing pressure has been assessed in Europe and reported in the study from Ole
R. Eigaard [26] (ﬁgure appendix B.4). When compared to their free-ﬂoating spatial distribution
in the North Sea (ﬁgure appendix B.5) assessed by the IBTS [30], it is evident that the pollution
travels with the currents and aﬀects places where this type of ﬁshing is restricted, such as Denmark.
In 2017, the IBTS reported 115 DR found during the winter (February), the sizes were ranging
from 0.5 cm to 117.7 cm, but most of them were smaller than 20 cm.
2.2
Biofouling theory & assumptions
Biofouling is changing the speciﬁc density of the particles turning it into a dynamic density (chang-
ing over time). In the model, the bioﬁlm is constituted of identical algae in a layered structure.
The biofouling model was inspired from the publication Ups and downs in the ocean: eﬀects of
biofouling on vertical transport of microplastics by Kooi et al. (2017) [39] with some changes and
adaptation to the available data.
2.2.1
Carrying capacity & its calibration
One major feature added onto the biofouling is the carrying capacity of the plastic particle.

2.3
TRABMAP (TRAcking Biofouled MArine Plastics) model
The IBM used to develop the TRABMAP model was created by Asbjørn Christensen, Patrizio
Mariani and Mark R. Payne in the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). It is an open source
model coded in Fortran that can be adapted to a high variety of cases. The TRABMAP model
is a new ”biology provider” but was initially developed from a ”simple settler” model by adding
some characteristics and attributes to passive particles. The most important feature added is the
dynamic buoyancy that the biofouling creates.
A Prezi presentation available online was created to oﬀer a more visual understanding of the plastic
biofouling model. >> Link

2.4
Vertical velocity
The spherical particles models can induce signiﬁcant errors [40] since Stokes’ formula is assuming
a very low Reynolds number which accounts for extremely small particles. Thus, overestimates the
sinking velocity of low-density spheres. It can represent a 10-fold overestimation (ﬁgure B.14) for a
sphere with a diameter of 1 mm. Which is why the use of Dietrich formula on the cylinder-shaped
particles of length l and diameter r is interesting to explore [24]. For shapes that are very diﬀerent
from a sphere, such as ﬁbers with l >> r, Dietrich formula needs some adjustment which is why
Khatmullina explored diﬀerent sinking behaviors depending on the shape of the most common
plastic litter encountered [35]. The use of one of her empirical formula for ﬁber-shaped particles
having the same diameter as the DR (0.6 mm) is used in this model (Eq. 30 to 32).

Results
3.1
Dolly ropes analysis
When the sampled DR were looked under the microscope, biofouling was noticed. Macro-algae
but also biomineralizing organisms such as sponges and shellﬁsh were growing on the DR. Other
types of debris were attached to the DR (ﬁgure C.1). These types of biofouling have the potential
to change the buoyancy of the particle. Most of the DR showed traces of some bioﬁlm, but since
they were dried, and went through some manipulation, it wasn’t possible to get more information
out of them. Another reason why it is diﬃcult to get some information from the sampled DR is
that the amount of time they have spent in the water is unknown.
3.2
Model Results
3.2.1
Maps
The data obtained from the simulations were uploaded into QGIS to extract maps of the spatial
distribution of the sedimented DR. The projection used was WGS 84 (EPSG:4326), and the land
map was taken from the website >> Natural Earth data [3]. To examine if there was a particular
accumulation zone, the heatmap function in QGIS was used. For all locations on the map, the
function counts the number of sedimented DR within a radius of 1◦(approximately 110 km) and
divides it by the surface of the obtained circle. The color scale is going from the highest density of
particles to the lowest but these values might diﬀer from one map to another. Some extra maps

Data analysis
To identify the key factors in the location of the accumulation zones, the data were analyzed in
detail using Matlab R2017. For each particle, the time of sedimentation was considered to be
when the particle coordinates (latitude and longitude) and the depth stopped changing, meaning
that the particle has touched the bottom. The time of settling on the other side was calculated
as the ﬁrst time the particle reaches 1 m depth. The chlorophyll concentration and density of the
seawater at the time of sedimentation were extracted to see how much they are playing a role in the
sedimentation of the DR. The distance traveled by each of the particles was calculated using the
sum of the distances covered each day until sedimentation. For this, a Matlab function allowing
the transformation of a pair of latitude-longitude data into a distance in km using the Haversine
formula [2] was used.
In the legends of the ﬁgures ”Ref”, ”may”, ”980”, ”S2”, ”cm” stand for the respective simulation:
reference, release in may, particle density of 980, release from source 2 and particle size of 1 cm.

Now, if considering that the most susceptible places for sedimentation of biofouled plastic particles

timescale for biofouled plastic particle to sediment [28] [39] [34] [63] but they are deeply correlated
to the environmental conditions and the model highlights this. A particle released in May will
take on average only 18.5 days to start sinking and will sediment in 24.4 days. Which is six times
faster than a release in January. Even within each month of release, the last particle created was
sedimenting at a younger age (ﬁgure C.4). Figure 9 shows well the importance of the algal spring
bloom in the increase in density. Therefore, the settling time of the particle depends almost only
on the chlorophyll concentration in the water and the initial density of the particle. The growth
modeled on the plastic accounts just for a small part.
The model emphasizes the fact that the amount of time the particle will spend in the water is
closely related to the season of release. A particle that is released in September, for example, will
stay at the sea-surface until the next year spring bloom starts. In this sense, we can only put a
”surface lifetime” according to the release date and yearly variation of chlorophyll concentration.
When considering the beaching and sedimentation, it is essential to keep in mind that the bound-
aries are set to ”sticky” which means that in the model, the particles cannot go back into suspen-
sion, but in reality, this phenomenon occurs. This model is showing the most extremes scenarios
when looking at beaching and sedimentation. The time to sedimentation is higher than the time
to beaching for the release in January but not for the release in May. Meaning that the sedimen-
tation/beaching ratio is much higher in summer. In winter the particles have more chances to
end up on a beach and in summer in the sediments. More scenarios analysis and studies on the
topic are needed to be able to give a number on this proportion and on the associated timescale.
Nevertheless, the model gives a reasonable estimation of where the released DR might be found
on the coast. The high concentration of DR found on the Dutch beaches (100 m2 of beach) [78] is
conﬁrming the heatmap produced by the simulation (ﬁgure 5f).
As we can see on the heatmaps ﬁgure 5a and 5e, the diﬀerence in size doesn’t aﬀect so much the
settling area. Both simulations look very similar, and this is probably because large particles have
a higher kernel encounter rate that is balancing out the higher buoyancy. Since they have a higher
volume, their sedimentation velocity will be higher [39] and the particle will touch the bottom
faster. This explains why they sediment at the same time even though the bigger particle reaches
the sea water density up to 20 days after the reference simulation (1 mm particle).
Therefore, most of the particle emitted near the English channel should sediment close by if
emitted in summer or travel along the southern part of the North Sea and then up the Danish
coast until meeting the favorable conditions for sedimenting. This also means that they do cross
many diﬀerent exclusive economica zones (EEZ) .
The particles released in May are crossing up to 4 EEZ (ﬁgure C.12) and the ones released in
January crosses up to 6 of them. Most of them end up in the sediments of a diﬀerent EEZ than
where it was released. Furthermore, the source of the DR is mobile which means that it has even
more chances to cross these economic boundaries. Alternatives to the use of the DR exist [78] and
have already been adopted by many European countries. The Netherlands being the primary user
of them in the North Sea, they play an important part in the pollution of the sediments by DR in
other countries.

Potential sources of error
The experiment made by Kaiser et al. [34], had similar environmental conditions than the ones
found in the model. At their coastal station, the chlorophyll concentration ranged between 1.7 to
9.9 mg m−3. The plastic particles used were cylindrical (1 mm diameter and length) polyethylene
(PE: 955 kg m−3) and the sedimentation time for these particles was six weeks (Baltic sea water:
1005 kg m−3) for the ones that were biofouled by Macrozoobenthos organisms. The timescale of
the sedimentation and all parameters seem to ﬁt the ones present in the model which is a good
sign but if we try to run the model with a release on the 19th of July (2012 instead of 2016) at
the coordinates of the experiment (near Heiligendamm: 54 ◦846.11N, 11◦5036.19E), the particles
are sedimenting within only 10 days. This diﬀerence could be explained by the following source of
inaccuracy:
The chlorophyll is compiled in the IBM using the biomass (in Nitrogen) of Diatoms, ﬂagellates,
and cyanobacteria. Going from phytoplankton biomass to chlorophyll and back to the biomass of
phytoplankton are some unnecessary steps that are increasing the uncertainty of the model. Also,
the chlorophyll concentration used in the ”Kooi model” should be only chlorophyll a which is not
available. Although the chlorophyll a is accounting for most of the total chlorophyll, this practice
is probably overestimating the biofouling rate. Which is coherent with the above. Therefore the
use of this chlorophyll is probably the most important source of inaccuracy in the model. Another
source of inaccuracy comes from the model itself. Not all algae that encounters a particle will stick
to it. It depends on the type of algae (Diatoms tend to stick more easily for example), on the
plastic surface properties (surface charge) and on other parameters.
The kernel encounter rate was designed for a smaller scale of particles (size range of bacterias or
ﬂaggelates) [38], and might be overestimating the encounter rate when used with big particles.
Ongoing work and ideas for improvement
Increasing the number of scenarios would help better understand the full extent of the pollution.
For example, a particle released in September might travel much further since it just missed the
high chlorophyll bloom of summer. Testing the inﬂuence of the carrying capacity on the ﬁnal
destination would be interesting. Analyzing more sources or use a 3D source and increasing the
emissions would also give more representative results but it was not possible with the available
computational power.
Generally, the conﬁrmation of the equations used for the growth on the particle and the oscillating
behavior of the biofouled microplastics is a necessary step for approval of the model.
Together with the GEUS institute (Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland), some research
are being done on the topic of sinking behavior of biofouled nano and microplastics. Physically
weathered DR fragments were put into a settling chamber in diﬀerent water conditions (Batlic and
tap water) to see if they were settling. The chlorophyll a concentration was registered to assess
the potential for aggregate formation due to EPS. Unfortunately, the results are not available yet
but pictures of the experiment are available in appendix D.
As highlighted by Fazey et al. [28], and Kaiser et al. [34], the type of biofouling that increases
signiﬁcantly the density of macroplastic particles is usually macrozoobenthos. Kaiser et al. even

concluded that the development of a microscopic bioﬁlm does not change the particles speciﬁc
density [34] but it is known to trigger the settlement of heavier biofouling organisms [80].
In
this context, it would be interesting to couple the present work with some data on mussel larvae

The choice of DR as support for biofouling growth is challenging since the source is mobile most of
the time and the input is not so important. It would be interesting to model other types of plastic
particles such as car tires fragments or fabric ﬁbers that are supposed to be some of the primary
sources of plastic pollution in the ocean [14] and use a river outlet as the source point.
To improve the accuracy of the model, it would be better to skip all steps that depend on chloro-
phyll and use the data for phytoplankton concentrations (Diatoms, ﬂagellates, and cyanobacteria
available in the OPEC database) if possible using biomass mgC or mgN instead of the number
of algae. This way, it would be possible to have three diﬀerent biofouling densities and see more
accurately the impact of a seasonal bloom for example.
As mentioned earlier, the Encounter rate could be improved, and it is one more reason why it
is essential to keep on studying how biofouling is forming and how it is impacting the vertical
distribution of plastics.
5
Conclusion
This project is the ﬁrst 3D model that uses real environmental data to look into the potential
for biofouling on free-ﬂoating microplastics. It allowed putting into perspective the knowledge
acquired so far on the topic of marine plastic fate and biofouling. It conﬁrms the fact that most
of the missing plastic should be located in shallow areas near the coast but also highlights the
importance of the source, the plastic properties, and the environmental conditions when trying to
predict a surface lifetime and a ﬁnal destination. More research on the growth rate of biofouling
on microplastic particles is needed to be able to increase the accuracy of such a model, but the
uses and applications are appealing. It could be a decisive tool in the conservation of our oceans.
As seen in this project, the particles are crossing some EEZ and end up polluting the sediments
from diﬀerent states which could lead to some political issues especially if the benthic environment
represents an economic value to the country. In the case of DR, the pollution could be avoided.
Alternatives to DR already exist, and many states have already stopped using them.

MIK technical description used by the IBTS
The MIK net consists of a rigid ring frame to which the net is attached, a conical net made of
nylon gauze, a cod-end bucket, a pair of bridles for towing, a depressor with bridles, a ﬂowmeter
for ﬁltered volume determination and a depth gauge for monitoring net depth. In particular, the
parts of the gear, as shown in ﬁgure B.2 are:
a) Ring of 2 meter diameter. This should be reasonably strong, preferably made of two connected
34
==PAGE:46==
Modeling of plastic fragments transport
steel tubes (ﬁgure B.2). When the ring is heavy ( 100kg) there is no need for a depressor, but only
a weight to stabilize the ring (see ”d” below)
b) Black net of 1.6 mm pore, 13 meter long, strengthened by nylon or canvas straps. In the last
meter of the net a 500 m net is inserted (b1)
c) Straps (with nuts, bolts and plates) or a strong line for mounting the net on the ring.
d) Saddle shaped weight or depressor of approx 25kg; weight dependent on weight of the 2m ring.
e) Pair of 10 meter long bridles to the gear. This length in order to keep the bridles away from
the path into the net.
f) Pair of 3.0 meter long bridles to the weight or depressor. This length is needed in order to keep
bridles away from touching the net.
g) Cod-end bucket (Ø 11 cm) for collection of the plankton sample. Use a netting of 500 µm or
less for the cod-end (see examples)
h) Flow meter mounted on a string crossing the ring, positioned in the centre of the ring. Keep
string slack so that ﬂowmeter is approx. 0.5 m inside the net. Suggested ﬂowmeter types are
General Oceanic 2030R or the newer types of Hydrobios 438 110.
i) In order to monitor the depth of the gear (and thereby calculate the distance to bottom) a depth
sounder with acoustic transmission should be mounted in the upper part of the ring.

D
GEUS experiment
The GEUS experiment consist of physically degrading the DR to nanoparticles sizes fractions
and test their buoyancy within diﬀerent water conditions. For each size fraction, the amount of
particles in the water can be assessed using a ﬂow cytometer [45]. The particles are mixed in
some ”condition” water (Baltic, tap or other) and kept into suspension by a constant steering for
a desired period. When the desired period is over, the steering stops and the particles start to
sediment. When passing in from of the Pcam that is a submersible laser sheet camera system,
they are illuminated by a the laser which allows to see them clearly in the video and to register
their diameter and settling velocity. The chlorophyll concentration of the ”condition” water is
registered to be able to assess the potential for aggregation on the particle.
