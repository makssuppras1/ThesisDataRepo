Abstract
Accurate water level prediction in streams is essential for flood management and wa-
ter resource planning in Denmark. This thesis develops a deep learning approach for
multi-station water level forecasting that can generalize to previously unseen monitoring
locations. We collected and processed 254GB of weather data from the Danish Mete-
orological Institute alongside daily water level measurements from 15 stream gauging
stations across Sjælland and Jylland, covering the period 2015-2025.
Our approach uses Transformer models enhanced with learnable station embeddings -
8-dimensional vectors that capture location-specific hydrological characteristics without
manual feature engineering. Through extensive correlation analysis and feature selec-
tion, we identified four key predictors: previous day’s water level, Antecedent Precipita-
tion Index (API), 14-day cumulative rainfall, and 4-day lagged temperature. This minimal
feature set balances predictive power with computational efficiency.
The model achieved strong performance across diverse hydrological conditions, with R²
values ranging from 0.75 to 0.93 for 7-day ahead predictions. Remarkably, the relative
RMSE remained below 8% of each station’s typical water level range, even for stations
completely excluded from training. This consistent accuracy - from small streams like
Værebro (2.24-2.98m range) to larger rivers like Gudenå (49.63-51.03m range) - demon-
strates robust generalization.
Error analysis revealed that prediction uncertainty grows rapidly for 1-3 day horizons as
the model transitions from relying on water level persistence to weather-driven dynamics,
then increases more gradually for longer horizons. The model maintains useful skill even
at 14-day horizons, suggesting operational value for both immediate flood response and
longer-term water management planning.
While limited by data availability (particularly the lack of vegetation management records)
and computational constraints, this work establishes that relatively simple architectural
choices combined with domain-informed feature engineering can effectively model com-
plex hydrological systems. The station embedding approach offers a promising frame-
work for deploying models to new, unmonitored streams based solely on their proximity
to weather stations.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Høje-Taastrup Municipality aim to develop a ”model that can predict flooding of streams
and help optimize its plant cutting management. With such a model it will be possible to on
a short basis alert farmers in the risk of getting flooded and on a longer basis take measure
to prevent flooding. Høje-Taastrup Municipality are currently cutting stream plants along
its public streams several times a year, to ensure that excess water is better diverted.
Stream plants however increase biodiversity by functioning as habitat for many species.
With the knowledge about flooding risk in hand, areas of low flooding risk can be identified,
and plants can be left uncut here thereby increasing biodiversity.”
Water level prediction in rivers and streams is a critical challenge with significant implica-
tions for flood prevention, water resource management, and environmental protection. In
recent years, extreme weather events have become increasingly frequent due to climate
change, making accurate water level forecasting more important than ever. Traditional
physics-based hydrological models, while valuable, often struggle to capture the complex,
non-linear relationships between meteorological conditions and water levels, particularly
in systems with multiple interconnected water bodies.
The advancement of machine learning techniques, particularly deep learning approaches,
offers new possibilities for improving water level predictions. These data- driven methods
can potentially capture intricate patterns in historical data that might be overlooked by
traditional models. However, most existing approaches focus on predicting water levels
for single water streams, with the created models not being general enough for use on
streams in a wide geographical area.
This research is motivated by the need for a more comprehensive approach that can:
• Predict water levels in stations across a wide geographic area, specifically Denmark.
• Incorporate diverse meteorological factors affecting water dynamics.
• Provide reliable predictions with sufficient lead time for preventive actions — in our
case, 7 days.
Accurate forecasts enable better water resource allocation, support agricultural planning,
facilitate navigation in waterways, and help protect critical infrastructure. In Denmark and
similar regions with extensive water networks, even modest improvements in prediction
accuracy can translate to significant economic and environmental benefits

1.4
Delimitations
This research is conducted within the following boundaries:
Geographical Scope: The study focuses on river and stream networks in Denmark, uti-
lizing data from selected water gauge stations. While the methodology is designed to be
generalizable, validation is limited to Danish hydrological conditions.
Temporal Scope: The analysis uses historical data spanning 2015 - 2023, with daily mea-
surements of water level data and meteorological data aggregated from multiple sources.
Predictions are limited to a 7-day forecast horizon.
Meteorological Variables: The study considers a specific set of weather-related features
(rainfall, temperature, pressure, wind speed, solar radiation) available from meteorologi-
cal stations. Other potentially relevant factors such as soil moisture, groundwater levels,
or human interventions are not included due to data availability constraints.
Model Architecture: The research focuses specifically on LSTM and Transformer based
architectures with station embeddings. While other deep learning approaches might offer
advantages, they are outside the scope of this investigation.
Prediction Type: The model predicts absolute water levels for use in grass cutting op-
timization rather than anomalies or flood events. Classification of flood risk levels is not
directly addressed, though the predictions could be used for such purposes.
Data Quality: The study assumes the reliability of the provided sensor data. While basic
preprocessing is performed, extensive data quality assessment and sensor calibration
issues are not addressed.
Computational Constraints: Model complexity is balanced against practical deployment

Theory
This chapter establishes the theoretical foundations necessary for developing a machine
learning system that predicts water levels in Danish streams. The accurate prediction of
stream water levels requires understanding from two distinct but interconnected domains:
the physical processes that govern water movement and accumulation in natural systems,
and the computational methods capable of learning complex patterns from historical data.
By bridging hydrological science with modern machine learning techniques, we can build
models that capture both the underlying physics and the site-specific variations present
in real-world stream networks.
The chapter begins with an examination of flood prevention strategies (Section 2.1), fo-
cusing on vegetation management practices that directly influence water levels through
their effects on channel hydraulics. Understanding these management interventions is
crucial because our prediction system must account for human modifications to the nat-
ural stream environment. This knowledge also provides context for the practical appli-
cations of our predictions—namely, optimizing grass cutting schedules to balance flood
prevention with ecological preservation.
Section 2.2 presents the machine learning foundations, with particular emphasis on Long
Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. These deep learning architectures have shown
remarkable success in capturing temporal dependencies in sequential data, making them
well-suited for modeling the time-lagged relationships between meteorological conditions
and water levels. We also discuss statistical baseline methods to establish performance
benchmarks for our deep learning approach. The quality of any prediction model depends
fundamentally on the quality of its input data. Section 2.3 therefore reviews water level
measurement methods, from traditional staff gauges to modern IoT sensors. Understand-
ing the capabilities and limitations of different measurement technologies helps us assess
data reliability and design appropriate preprocessing strategies for handling measurement
uncertainties.
Finally, Section 2.4 examines the various factors that influence river gauge height, includ-
ing precipitation, evaporation, temperature effects, and seasonal patterns. This compre-
hensive view of the hydrological system ensures that our feature engineering captures
the most relevant predictors while acknowledging the factors we cannot directly measure,
such as groundwater flows or upstream dam operations.
Together, these theoretical foundations provide the necessary background for developing
a robust, multi-station prediction system. By grounding our machine learning approach
in established hydrological principles, we aim to create models that are not only accurate
but also interpretable and trustworthy for practical deployment in water resource manage-
ment.
2.1
Flood prevention
Vegetation management, particularly grass cutting along stream corridors, represents
a critical yet often debated component of integrated flood risk management strategies.
This chapter examines the theoretical foundations underlying vegetation management
practices around water streams, exploring the complex relationships between vegetation

characteristics, hydraulic efficiency, and flood conveyance capacity. The management
of streamside vegetation involves balancing multiple objectives: maximizing flood con-
veyance, maintaining ecological integrity, ensuring bank stability, and meeting societal
expectations for landscape aesthetics and accessibility.
Hydraulic Theory and Vegetation Resistance
The fundamental hydraulic principles governing flow in vegetated channels stem from
the Manning-Strickler equation and its modifications to account for vegetation-induced
roughness. When vegetation is present in or adjacent to stream channels, it increases
hydraulic resistance, thereby reducing flow velocity and potentially increasing water levels
during flood events.
The relationship between vegetation height, density, and hydraulic resistance is non- lin-
ear and depends on several factors including flow depth, vegetation flexibility, and sea-
sonal variations. Theoretical models developed by Kouwen and Unny [1] and later refined
by Järvelä [2] demonstrate that vegetation resistance varies with the degree of submer-
gence. When flow depth is less than vegetation height, resistance increases dramatically
due to form drag on individual stems. However, when vegetation becomes fully sub-
merged and bends with the flow, the effective roughness may actually decrease.
Recent work by Luhar and Nepf [19] has shown that flexible vegetation exhibits a complex
drag-discharge relationship, where the reconfiguration of plants under increasing flow
can lead to a self-limiting drag that plateaus at higher velocities. This phenomenon is
particularly relevant for grass management, as the flexibility of grass species means that
cutting height decisions must account for both the immediate resistance reduction and
the potential for rapid regrowth.
Vegetation Management Strategies and Flood Conveyance
The theoretical justification for grass cutting along streams centers on the concept of con-
veyance capacity optimization. Conveyance capacity (K) is defined as the discharge that
would pass through a channel section under unit energy gradient conditions. By reduc-
ing vegetation height through regular cutting, the hydraulic radius of the channel can be
effectively increased, thereby enhancing conveyance capacity during high flow events.
However, the relationship between cutting frequency, cutting height, and flood risk re-
duction is complex. Research by Darby [3] suggests that while aggressive vegetation
removal can increase conveyance capacity by 20-40%, the benefits may be partially off-
set by increased flow velocities that can lead to enhanced erosion and downstream flood
peaks. The optimal cutting regime must therefore balance immediate conveyance bene-
fits against long-term geomorphological stability.
Västilä and Järvelä [20] developed a practical methodology for assessing the impact
of vegetation management on flood levels, demonstrating that strategic cutting in high-
velocity zones can achieve 60-80% of the flood reduction benefits of complete vegetation
removal while maintaining most ecological functions. Their work emphasizes the impor-
tance of spatial optimization in cutting strategies.
Ecological Considerations and Trade-offs
From an ecological perspective, riparian (alongside rivers) vegetation serves multiple
functions that must be considered within flood management frameworks. The root sys-
tems of streamside vegetation provide crucial bank stabilization, reducing erosion rates

by up to 80% compared to bare banks (Abernethy and Rutherfurd [4]). Additionally, ripar-
ian vegetation creates important habitat corridors, provides shade that regulates water
temperature, and filters pollutants from surface runoff.
Recent studies by Gurnell [21] have highlighted the role of riparian vegetation in support-
ing biodiversity, with over 200 species of invertebrates and 50 bird species depending on
Danish stream corridors. The vegetation also plays a crucial role in nutrient cycling, with
riparian buffers capable of removing 50-90% of nitrogen from agricultural runoff [22].
The theory of ecological flood management advocates for a more nuanced approach to
vegetation management that recognizes these multiple benefits. This approach suggests
that selective cutting strategies, which maintain a mosaic of vegetation heights and densi-
ties, can achieve acceptable flood conveyance while preserving ecological functions. The
concept of ”hydraulically effective” vegetation management focuses on removing vegeta-
tion from key flow paths while maintaining buffer strips along banks
Temporal Dynamics and Seasonal Considerations
Vegetation growth rates and seasonal variations introduce temporal complexity to flood
management strategies. In temperate climates, grass and herbaceous vegetation can
grow 2-5 cm per week during peak growing season, potentially negating the hydraulic
benefits of cutting within weeks. The timing of cutting operations relative to flood season is
therefore critical. Theoretical models suggest that cutting should be scheduled to ensure
minimal vegetation height during periods of highest flood risk, while allowing recovery
during low-risk periods.
The phenological cycles of different plant species also affect management strategies.
Research in Danish streams by Hansen et al. [24] found that native grass species show
distinct growth patterns, with peak biomass occurring in late July. This information can
be used to optimize cutting schedules, targeting interventions just before peak growth
periods.
The life cycles stages of vegetation also influence hydraulic resistance. During winter
months, many herbaceous plants die back naturally, reducing resistance without inter-
vention. Conversely, woody vegetation may pose greater resistance in winter due to the
absence of flexible leaves. These seasonal dynamics must be incorporated into manage-
ment planning to optimize cost-effectiveness and flood protection benefits.
Cost-Benefit Analysis and Optimization Theory
The economic theory underlying vegetation management for flood prevention involves
optimizing the balance between management costs and flood damage reduction benefits.
The marginal benefit of each cutting event typically follows a diminishing returns curve,
with the first cut of the season providing the greatest reduction in flood risk. Optimization
models developed by Babovic et al. [5] suggest that the economically optimal cutting
frequency depends on several variables including:
• Local flood frequency and magnitude
• Vegetation growth rates
• Potential flood damages
• Cutting costs and accessibility
• Environmental regulatory constraints

Integrated Management Approaches
Contemporary flood management theory increasingly emphasizes integrated approaches
that combine vegetation management with other measures. The concept of ”green in-
frastructure” for flood management recognizes that strategically managed vegetation can
complement traditional engineering solutions. This includes creating floodplain storage
areas with managed grasslands, constructing vegetated swales for flow attenuation, and
maintaining riparian buffers that slow overbank flows while providing ecological benefits.
The European Water Framework Directive [27] requires member states to achieve ”good
ecological status” in water bodies while managing flood risks. This has led to the de-
velopment of integrated assessment frameworks that evaluate vegetation management
options against multiple criteria including hydraulic performance, ecological quality, and
socio-economic factors. The theory of adaptive management is particularly relevant to
vegetation management strategies. Given the uncertainties in vegetation response, cli-
mate variability, and changing flood patterns, management approaches should be flexible
and responsive to monitoring data. Pahl-Wostl et al. [28] propose an adaptive cycle that
includes:
1. Planning phase: Setting objectives based on flood risk assessments
2. Implementation phase: Executing cutting regimes with built-in variability
3. Monitoring phase: Tracking water levels, vegetation growth, and ecological indi-
cators
4. Evaluation phase: Assessing outcomes against multiple objectives
5. Adjustment phase: Modifying strategies based on learned outcomes
Climate Change Considerations
Climate change is bound to alter preciptation patterns over the world, including Denmark,
necessitating a re-evaluation of traditional vegetation management strategies. The in-
creased frequency of extreme events may require more frequent interventions, while
longer growing seasons could accelerate vegetation recovery between cutting cycles.
Research by Riis and Biggs [30] suggests that climate change may also alter species
composition in riparian zones, with potential increases in invasive species that have dif-
ferent hydraulic properties than native vegetation. Management strategies must therefore
be robust to both changing hydrological regimes and evolving vegetation communities.
Conclusion
The theoretical foundations of flood prevention through grass cutting around streams re-
veal a complex interplay between hydraulic efficiency, ecological integrity, and economic
optimization. While vegetation removal can demonstrably increase channel conveyance
capacity and reduce flood levels, the magnitude of benefits depends heavily on site-
specific conditions and must be weighed against ecological impacts and long-term sus-
tainability. Effective flood management requires a nuanced understanding of these theo-
retical principles, combined with practical knowledge of local conditions and constraints.

2.2
Machine learning models for Stream-Level prediction
Water levels are influenced by a variety of meteorological factors, including rainfall accu-
mulation, evaporation, and atmospheric pressure. Traditional hydrological models often
rely on conceptual assumptions to simulate the transformation of rainfall into runoff. In
contrast, machine learning (ML) methods—particularly sequence-based models—offer a
data-driven alternative by learning direct mappings from historical meteorological and hy-
drometric time series to future water levels. This approach avoids many of the strong
parametric assumptions typically required by conceptual models.
2.2.1
Machine Learning
Machine learning (ML) is a sub-field of artificial intelligence that studies algorithms able
to improve their performance at a task T through experience E-commonly phrased after
Mitchell [6] as “a computer program is said to learn from experience E with respect to some
class of tasks T and performance measure P if its performance at tasks in T, as measured
by P, improves with E.” In practice this means fitting a statistical model to data so that
future, unseen inputs can be handled automatically with minimal human intervention.
What distinguishes ML from classical deterministic programming is this capacity to adapt;
instead of writing explicit rules for every situation, we supply data and let the algorithm
infer relationships that generalise beyond the observed examples.
ML projects, regardless of type, follow a relatively generic process. They begin with data
acquisition and cleaning—removing sensor glitches, aligning timestamps, and imputing
gaps in hydro-meteorological records. Feature representation comes next; sometimes
domain expertise suggests variables such as antecedent rainfall indices, but modern deep
models often prefer to learn features end-to-end. The core training phase searches pa-
rameter space for a minimiser of an objective like mean-squared error. Validation on
held-out stations or time periods guards against overfitting, while final testing provides an
estimate of generalisation.
2.2.2
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) Networks
Overview
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks are a specialized subclass of recurrentneural
networks (RNNs) introduced by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber [7] that address the limi-
tations of traditional RNNs when modelling long-term dependencies in sequential data.
These limitations are primarily due to thevanishing and exploding gradient problems that
hinder effective backpropagationthrough time (BPTT) in deep or long sequences.
LSTMs mitigate these issues by introducing a memory cell and a gating mechanism

2.3
Water-Level Measurement Methods
Accurate water-level data are the backbone of flood warnings, ecological studies and the
safe design of bridges and dams. Over the past century the tools used to collect these
data have ranged from simple rulers in the river to sensors on satellites. This section
explains how each method works, what it does well and where it struggles.
2.3.1
Conventional Gauge Stations
Staff Gauges
Staff gauges are marked boards fixed to a stable support, representing the oldest and
most fundamental method of water level measurement. Because the marks do not change,
they are still the legal reference at many monitoring sites. First documented use of staff
gauges dates back to ancient Egypt along the Nile River, where nilometers measured an-
nual flood levels [31]. Modern staff gauges typically consist of porcelain-enameled steel
or fiberglass-reinforced plastic plates with graduations at 0.01 m intervals, mounted verti-
cally on bridge piers, walls, or purpose-built stilling wells [32]. A trained observer reads the
water line—often once or twice a day. The downside is that nothing is recorded between
visits, potentially missing peak flood levels or rapid fluctuations. Human reading errors,
estimated at ±5-10 mm under good conditions, can increase to ±20-30 mm during turbu-
lent flow or poor visibility [33]. Despite these limitations, staff gauges remain essential for
calibrating automated systems and resolving disputes, as they provide an unambiguous
visual reference that requires no power or complex electronics.
Float-and-stilling-well systems
Float-and-stilling-well systems add a float attached to a pulley mechanism, representing
the first successful automation of water level recording. As the water rises and falls, the
float moves a pen or an encoder, creating a continuous record. The stilling well—a vertical
pipe or chamber connected to the stream through intake pipes—dampens wave action
and turbulence, allowing accurate measurement of the mean water level [34]. Traditional
mechanical systems use a counterweighted float connected via steel tape to a rotating
drum recorder, achieving accuracies of ±3-5 mm under ideal conditions [35]. Modern
digital shaft encoders have improved resolution to ±1 mm while eliminating mechanical
chart drives. They work well in quiet water but can jam on driftwood, ice or silt. Ice
formation poses particular challenges in Nordic climates, potentially lifting floats above
the true water level or blocking intake pipes entirely [36]. Regular maintenance including
debris removal and clock synchronization (for mechanical recorders) is essential for data
quality.
Pressure-Based Systems
Since the 1990s most new stations have used pressure sensors, which measure water
level indirectly through hydrostatic pressure. Two primary technologies dominate: gas-
purge systems and submersible pressure transducers. Gas-purge, or ”bubbler,” systems
measure the air pressure needed to push bubbles out of a tube. A precision pressure
regulator maintains a constant, low flow of dry nitrogen or compressed air through an

orifice line extending to a fixed point below the stream’s minimum expected level. The
back-pressure required to maintain bubble flow equals the hydrostatic pressure at the ori-
fice, which is directly proportional to water depth above that point [37]. Modern differential
pressure transducers achieve accuracies of ±3 mm over ranges up to 10 meters [38]. Key
advantages include keeping sensitive electronics on shore away from flood damage, easy
sensor replacement without entering the water, and inherent temperature compensation
since gas density changes are negligible. However, orifice lines can clog with sediment or
biological growth, requiring periodic purging at higher pressures. In streams with high sed-
iment loads, self-cleaning orifice designs or multiple redundant lines may be necessary
[39]. Submersible pressure transducers sit in the water and measure the weight of the
water above them directly. Modern piezoresistive sensors with temperature compensa-
tion achieve accuracies of ±0.05% of full scale, typically ±5 mm for a 10-meter range [40].
Vented cable designs automatically compensate for atmospheric pressure changes by
referencing a dry air connection at the logger box. Non-vented sensors require separate
barometric pressure measurement and post-processing correction, introducing potential
errors of ±10 mm per millibar of atmospheric pressure change [41]. Both pressure meth-
ods meet the common accuracy target of ±10 mm when they are calibrated and cleaned.
Installation depth must consider the trade-off between capturing low flows and avoiding
burial by sediment. Danish EPA guidelines recommend mounting at least 0.1 m below
expected minimum levels while maintaining 0.5 m clearance above the streambed [42].
Biofouling from algae and biofilms can drift sensor readings by several centimeters over
months, necessitating regular cleaning intervals determined by local conditions [43].
2.3.2
Non-Contact Sensors
When it is unsafe or impractical to put equipment in the channel, measurements are taken
from above the streams. Non-contact methods have gained popularity for their reduced
maintenance requirements and ability to operate during extreme floods that would dam-
age in-water sensors.
Microwave radar gauges
Microwave radar gauges send a short radio pulse to the water and time its echo, utilizing
frequencies between 10-80 GHz where atmospheric attenuation is minimal. Time-of-flight
measurements with modern phase-processing techniques achieve distance resolutions
better than ±3 mm [44]. Because the antenna sits high above flood level, it is rarely dam-
aged. Frequency-modulated continuous wave (FMCW) radar has become the preferred
technology for hydrological applications. By transmitting a swept frequency and analyzing
the beat frequency between transmitted and received signals, FMCW radar can resolve
distances with millimeter precision while using low transmit power suitable for battery op-
eration [45]. K-band (26 GHz) and W-band (78 GHz) frequencies offer optimal balance
between antenna size and performance in precipitation. Tests by the U.S. Geological
Survey show that modern radar gauges reach the same ±10 mm accuracy as pressure
sensors under most conditions [46]. Performance degrades during heavy precipitation,
with rain attenuation causing measurement noise proportional to rainfall intensity. Snow
accumulation on the antenna radome can block signals entirely, though heated radomes
mitigate this issue in cold climates. Surface waves and turbulence create measurement
uncertainty as the radar averages returns from its beam footprint, typically 0.5-2 m diam-
eter at the water surface [47].

Laser-Based Measurements
Laser gauges work the same way but use visible or infrared light, typically semiconductor
lasers at 650 nm (red) or 905 nm (near-infrared). Phase-shift and time-of-flight meth-
ods both achieve sub-centimeter accuracy, with phase-shift offering better precision (±2
mm) but ambiguous range, while time-of-flight provides unambiguous measurement over
longer distances [48]. They can be extremely accurate on calm water, such as reservoirs,
where laboratory tests demonstrate ±1 mm precision [49]. However, fog, heavy rain and
spray can block the beam. Atmospheric scintillation—refractive index fluctuations from
temperature gradients—introduces noise proportional to measurement distance and tur-
bulence intensity. Water surface characteristics also affect returns; clear water may pro-
vide weak signals requiring averaging multiple pulses, while turbid water gives strong
returns but may measure suspended sediment rather than true surface [50]. Both sensor
types can send data every minute by cellular or satellite link, making them popular for real-
time flood monitoring. Integration with SCADA systems allows automatic alert generation
when predetermined thresholds are exceeded.
2.3.3
Satellite Measurements
Ground gauges cover only a tiny part of the world’s rivers, with gauge density in Denmark
of approximately one station per 50 km² compared to WMO recommendations of one per
1000-2500 km² [51]. Satellite altimeters help fill this gap, though with different capabilities
than in-situ sensors.
Radar Altimetry
The Surface Water and Ocean Topography (SWOT) satellite, launched in December
2022, represents a revolutionary advance in space-based water level monitoring. Using
Ka-band radar interferometry, SWOT measures rivers wider than about 100 m every 21
days with unprecedented spatial resolution [52]. Early calibration/validation studies show
errors smaller than 10 cm on large rivers, meeting or exceeding pre-launch requirements
[53]. Previous altimetry missions like Jason-3 and Sentinel-3 provided along-track mea-
surements with 10-35 day repeat cycles, achieving 10-20 cm accuracy on rivers wider
than 200 m [54]. The transition from conventional nadir-pointing altimeters to SWOT’s
wide-swath interferometry increases spatial coverage by orders of magnitude while im-
proving height precision through averaging multiple looks.
Limits remain: the revisit time is too slow for flash floods which can rise and fall within
hours; narrow channels may be missed if below the 100 m width threshold; and heavy
storms can reduce accuracy through atmospheric delays and surface roughening. Steep
terrain creates layover and shadow effects that corrupt measurements near valley walls
[55]. Additionally, the satellite cannot distinguish between water surface and ice cover,
complicating winter measurements in Nordic regions.
Optical Remote Sensing
Optical satellites like Landsat and Sentinel-2 cannot measure height directly but provide
complementary information about water extent. Clear-sky images let scientists map the
water’s edge and estimate stage using local cross-sections, a technique called ”hydraulic
geometry” [56]. The Normalized Difference Water Index (NDWI) reliably distinguishes
water from land in multispectral imagery, achieving classification accuracies above 95%
in validation studies [57].
Temporal resolution depends on satellite revisit time (5 days for Sentinel-2 constellation)

2.3.4
New and Combined Approaches
Low-Cost IoT Sensors
Low-cost IoT sensors—small ultrasonic or pressure nodes under €200 with LoRaWAN or
LTE-M radios—now let cities and farmers cover many sites cheaply. Commercial exam-
ples include ultrasonic sensors achieving ±5-10 mm accuracy with power consumption
under 50 mW, enabling multi-year battery life [60]. LoRaWAN protocols provide 2-5 km
range in urban environments and up to 15 km in rural areas with elevated gateways [61].
Main hurdles are powering the sensors for years and keeping data quality high. Solar
panels as small as 5W can indefinitely power nodes in Danish latitudes, though win-
ter performance requires careful energy budgeting [62]. Data quality challenges include
temperature drift (±2 mm/°C for uncorrected ultrasonic sensors), vandalism, and biologi-
cal fouling. Edge computing capabilities increasingly allow on-device quality control and
anomaly detection before transmission [63].
Camera-Based Methods
Camera-based methods fix a camera on a bridge or pole and use image processing to
find the water line against a staff or wall. Traditional approaches used edge detection
and Hough transforms to identify water surface boundaries. Modern deep-learning mod-
els reach errors under 2 cm in good light, with convolutional neural networks (CNNs)
trained on thousands of labeled images [64]. The Large-scale Particle Image Velocimetry
(LSPIV) technique extends camera monitoring to simultaneous flow velocity measure-
ment by tracking surface features. When combined with surveyed cross-sections, this
enables discharge estimation without rating curves [65]. Thermal cameras operating in
long-wave infrared (8-14 μm) can distinguish water from land even at night, exploiting
temperature differences between water and surrounding surfaces [66]. Challenges in-
clude glare from low sun angles, rain or dirty lenses reducing image quality, and the need
for stable mounting to prevent vibration-induced errors. Lens heating elements and hy-
drophobic coatings partially mitigate weather-related degradation. Privacy concerns may
restrict camera deployment in urban areas despite technical advantages.
Sensor Fusion and Data Integration
Sensor fusion combines local sensors, cameras and satellite data in one model so the
strengths of one method cover the weaknesses of another. Kalman filtering and particle
filter approaches optimally combine measurements with different accuracies and sam-
pling rates [67]. For example, continuous low-accuracy IoT sensors can be periodically
corrected using infrequent but precise satellite overpass data.
Machine learning approaches, particularly Random Forests and neural networks, increas-
ingly handle sensor fusion tasks. These methods can learn complex non-linear relation-
ships between multiple data streams and automatically weight inputs based on reliability
[68]. Operational systems like the European Flood Awareness System (EFAS) already
integrate gauge data, radar precipitation, and satellite observations to provide coherent
continental-scale monitoring [69].

2.3.5
Data Quality and Uncertainty
All measurement methods produce data with inherent uncertainties that must be quan-
tified for proper use in prediction models. The Guide to Hydrological Practices (WMO,
2008) establishes uncertainty estimation procedures based on error propagation theory
[70]. Total measurement uncertainty combines:
• Systematic errors from sensor calibration drift
• Random errors from environmental noise
• Representation errors when point measurements approximate areal averages
• Temporal sampling errors when discrete measurements represent continuous pro-
cesses
Modern practice requires reporting confidence intervals alongside measurements. For
a typical pressure transducer installation, combined uncertainty might be ±15 mm (95%
confidence) accounting for sensor accuracy (±5 mm), installation effects (±8 mm), and
datum uncertainty (±10 mm) [71].
2.3.6
Summary
In practice, hydrologists mix tools: staff gauges lock down the reference height; pressure
sensors give fast local data; radar or laser keeps hardware out of the water; satellites
give a wide-area view; and cheap IoT or camera systems fill gaps in places that lacked
gauges. Each technology offers distinct advantages and limitations, requiring careful se-
lection based on site characteristics, accuracy requirements, and operational constraints.
The trend toward automated, non-contact, and networked sensors reflects both techno-
logical advancement and the practical demands of maintaining extensive monitoring net-
works with limited personnel. For machine learning applications like those developed
in this thesis, understanding measurement characteristics is essential. Different sensor
types exhibit distinct error patterns—pressure sensors may drift slowly over months while
radar gauges show increased noise during precipitation. By incorporating measurement
uncertainty into model training and validation procedures, we can develop prediction sys-
tems robust to real-world data quality variations.
2.4
Factors Influencing River Gauge Height
Understanding the multitude of factors that influence river gauge height is fundamental
to developing accurate prediction models. Water levels at any given monitoring station
represent the integrated response of a catchment to meteorological forcing, modified by
physical characteristics of the landscape and anthropogenic interventions. This section
provides a comprehensive examination of these factors, their relative importance, and
their temporal dynamics, establishing the theoretical basis for feature selection in machine
learning models.
2.4.1
Direct Precipitation
Rainfall directly entering the river channel provides immediate contribution to water lev-
els, though this component typically represents less than 1-5% of total discharge in most
catchments [13]. The relationship between precipitation and water level response is com-
plex and highly non-linear, governed by multiple interacting factors:

The rate of precipitation delivery fundamentally controls the partitioning between infiltra-
tion and surface runoff. When rainfall intensity exceeds the soil’s infiltration capacity—a
threshold that varies from 5-10 mm/hr for clay soils to 50-150 mm/hr for sandy soils—
Hortonian overland flow occurs [14]. Green and Ampt [15] developed the foundational
model for infiltration, showing that infiltration rate decreases exponentially with time as:

High-intensity rainfall events (>50 mm/hr) can generate immediate runoff even in perme-

• Raindrop impact: Sealing of soil surface by particle rearrangement, reducing infil-
tration by up to 90% [16]
• Air entrapment: Rapid wetting fronts trap air in soil pores, creating temporary bar-

• Hydrophobic conditions: Dry soils may repel water initially, enhancing runoff [18]
Duration and Temporal Distribution
Extended precipitation events saturate soils progressively, increasing the runoff coefficient—
the fraction of rainfall converted to streamflow—from near zero to approaching unity.
Dunne and Black [19] identified saturation-excess overland flow as the dominant mecha-
nism in humid regions, where the water table rises to intersect the ground surface.
The temporal distribution of rainfall within an event significantly affects peak water levels.
The Soil Conservation Service (now NRCS) developed standardized rainfall distributions
showing that front-loaded storms produce 20-40% higher peaks than uniformly distributed
rainfall of equal volume [nrcs1986]. Danish studies by Madsen et al. [20] found that 60%
of extreme water levels resulted from convective storms with durations less than 6 hours,
despite these events contributing only 15% of annual precipitation.

Localized storms may affect only portions of a catchment, creating complex water level
responses. The areal reduction factor (ARF) quantifies how point rainfall measurements

2.4.3
Snowmelt Dynamics
In Nordic regions, snowmelt represents a critical component of the annual water balance,
contributing 30-50% of annual runoff in Denmark despite the country’s relatively mild win-
ters [stisen2011]. The complexity of snow accumulation and melt processes introduces
unique challenges for water level prediction.

The degree-day method remains the most widely used snowmelt model due to its sim-
plicity and reasonable accuracy:
M = DDF × (T −T0)

• Season: Early season values of 2-3 mm/°C/day increasing to 5-7 mm/°C/day in late

More sophisticated energy balance approaches account for:
• Solar radiation: Provides 60-80% of melt energy during clear conditions [34]

• Latent heat: Usually negative (condensation) in spring, adding 5-10% to melt
• Ground heat flux: Supplies 1-5% of energy, important for shallow snowpacks
• Rainfall heat: Negligible except during rain-on-snow events

Rain-on-Snow Events
Rain falling on snow can cause rapid melting through multiple mechanisms:
• Direct heat input: Each mm of rain at 10°C can melt 1.2 mm of snow [36]

• Albedo reduction: Wet snow absorbs 20-30% more radiation than dry snow [37]

Elevation Effects and Melt Sequence
Temperature decreases with elevation at approximately 6.5°C/km (environmental lapse
rate), creating sequential melt from low to high elevations. This extends the melt season
and moderates peak flows. Danish catchments show limited elevation ranges (0-200 m),
but even modest relief creates detectable melt timing differences of 1-3 weeks [39].
Snow redistribution by wind creates spatial heterogeneity in snow water equivalent (SWE)
that can vary by factors of 5-10 over distances of 100 m [40]. Depressions and lee slopes
accumulate disproportionate snow, creating prolonged melt sources.

Integration and Synthesis
The multitude of factors influencing river gauge height operate across different spatial
and temporal scales, creating a complex, interacting system. Understanding these inter-
actions is crucial for developing robust prediction models:
Temporal scales:
• Minutes-hours: Precipitation intensity, channel routing
• Days-weeks: Snowmelt, soil moisture evolution, pressure systems
• Months-seasons: Vegetation growth, evapotranspiration cycles
• Years-decades: Land use change, channel evolution
Spatial scales:
• Local (meters): Bed roughness, gauge installation effects
• Reach (kilometers): Channel morphology, tributary inputs
• Catchment (10s km): Precipitation patterns, geological controls
• Regional (100s km): Climate patterns, large-scale pressure systems
Machine learning approaches must capture these multi-scale interactions while remaining
computationally tractable. Feature engineering that incorporates lagged variables, accu-
mulations over different periods, and rate-of-change metrics can help models learn these
complex relationships from data.

State of Research
Reliable water-level forecasting has been an active research topic for more than half a
century, but the last decade has seen a marked shift from physically based rainfall-run-
off models to data-driven approaches. Early statistical techniques (e.g., ARIMA or GLM)
offered incremental improvements yet were limited to short memories and single-station
deployment. The introduction of sequence-learning architectures—especially Long Short-
Term Memory (LSTM) networks—opened the door to exploiting long temporal dependen-
cies and heterogeneous meteorological inputs. Table 3.1 3.1 summarises representative
LSTM studies published between 2018 and 2024, ranging from local agricultural drains in
Inner Mongolia to continental-scale experiments on the CAMELS catchment set. main
To make sense of this fast-growing body of work, it is helpful to distinguish three research
streams:
• Single-stream models focus on reproducing the dynamics of one stream at a time.
Even though they focus on one stream, they often use data from multiple measuring
gauges alongside the stream. The resulting models are usually more accurate in
their predictions than the more global counterparts, but at the expense of poorer
transferability to toher streams and catchment areas.
• Wide-area (regional) models train on dozens of gauges within a coherent hydro-
climatic zone. By sharing parameters across stations they can leverage data-rich
sites to support data-poor ones, but they still rely on regional homogeneity assump-
tions that may not hold at national scale.
• Global models push generalisation further by pairing large Earth-observation datasets
(e.g., ERA5, SWOT) with neural networks that learn shared hydrological represen-
tations across countries continents. While promising, global studies typically work
with discharge rather than gauge height and still struggle with sub-daily resolution.
Across all three categories, three observations stand out:
• Dominance of rainfall-driven features. Most studies limit predictors to precipitation
and temperature; few explore pressure, wind or solar radiation even though these
variables influence evapotranspiration and antecedent conditions.
• Short forecast horizons. The majority of LSTM papers report 1- to 2-day lead times;
multi-day horizons remain the exception despite their practical value for operational
planning.
• Limited evaluation on unseen stations. Only recent global experiments explicitly
test spatial transfer, leaving open questions about how well models trained on one
hydrological regime adapt to another.
This chapter sumarises the current state of research. In 3.1 Section 3.1 reviews single-
stream studies and highlights lessons on feature engineering and sequence length. 3.3Sec-
tion 3.2 turns to wide-area models, examining how shared representations improve data
efficiency. 3.4Section 3.3 surveys global approaches and discusses open challenges in
scaling, transferability and physical interpretability. Collectively, the literature motivates

3.3
Wide area models
3.3.1
Motivation
Deep learning has moved well beyond proof-of-concepts at individual gauges. Today’s
research frontier is the development of wide-area models— networks that learn rainfall–
runoff relationships across whole regions or even continents, drawing on globally available
forcings such as the ERA5 reanalysis. A successful wide-area model should
1. generalise to ungauged basins with minimal or no local retraining,
2. provide discharge or stage at multiple temporal resolutions, and
3. quantify prediction uncertainty for risk-based decision making.
The ten articles reviewed in this chapter collectively demonstrate how the community is
addressing those requirements and where gaps remain.
3.3.2
Overview of Recent Studies
3.3.3
Methodological Trends
Reanalysis-driven modelling.
Several studies (Wilbrand, Tarek) rely solely on ERA5
meteorology; their results show that modern reanalyses can replace sparsely distributed
rain gauges when training continental networks.
Multi-timescale learning.
The timescale-consistent LSTM of [92] and the multi-timescale

Zero-shot and few-shot transfer.
[98] demonstrate that an LSTM trained on 200 basins

Physics-guided constraints.
Adding differentiable water-balance terms, [101] reduce

deep networks.
Operational hybridisation.
Rather than replace existing systems, [99] and [100] wrap
LSTMs around continental models or hydraulic solvers to accelerate runtime and correct
systematic errors.
3.3.4
Synthesis and Open Questions
• Data coverage. Global meteorological products now enable large-sample training,
but static attributes such as groundwater withdrawals and dam operations remain
coarse or absent.
• Temporal coupling. Multi-timescale LSTMs show promise, yet integration with sub-
hourly radar rainfall and seasonal climate outlooks is still experimental.
• Robustness. Networks like LSTNet withstand non-stationary inputs, but systematic
evaluations under climate change scenarios are scarce.

4.3.1
Weather data preprocessing
Weather ingestion and aggregation
The format of the weather data is obviously not suitable for training models, alongside it’s
enormous size and redundant data, so the text files had to be processed and combined
into a single .csv file containing the data only for the stations we specify, in a format which
more easily readable both for humans and machines. The data contains all weather sta-
tions, is split by date into thousands of files, and is of enormous size. It was needed
to extract the data for a single (or group of) stations, and consolidate the data into sin-
gle, per weather station, files.The implementation of this process can be found in the file
weather_processor_multithread.py. The original implementation had a simple iterative
process going through all the files, but since the data included 5.458 files totaling 254 GB,
this process took 11h to complete. In case any weather weather stations were missed, or
we want to extract data for new waether station, the process would need to be repeated
again. As this was extremely time consuming and underutilizing computer resources, a
solution leveraging multithreading was implemented which significantly improved the pro-
cessing time, to 2.5h. This was ran on an old 4 core CPU, so processing times would be
greatly reduced on CPUs with higher core counts.

Data Quality Control and Missing Value Treatment
Real-world hydrological data often contains gaps due to sensor failures, maintenance, or
transmission errors. We developed a comprehensive approach to handle these missing
values while preserving data integrity. First, we analyze missing data patterns in each
station’s time series. Our analysis (implemented in Analyzer - Missing data.py) identifies
consecutive missing periods and calculates gap durations.
We use a hybrid approach based on gap size:
Small gaps (1-3 days): We apply linear interpolation. These short gaps often result from
temporary sensor issues, and water levels change gradually enough that linear interpo-
lation provides reasonable estimates. For a 2-day gap, the interpolated values smoothly
connect the known points before and after.
Medium gaps (4-7 days): Here, simple interpolation becomes less reliable. Instead,
we use regression-based imputation leveraging correlated meteorological variables. The
algorithm identifies the best predictor variable (typically precipitation, with correlations
around 0.3-0.5), trains a linear regression model using available data and predicts missing
water levels based on the available weather data This works because even when water
sensors fail, weather stations usually continue operating.
Long gaps (>7 days): We remove these periods entirely. Imputing over such long gaps
would introduce too much uncertainty for reliable predictions. In cases where such big
(or much bigger) gaps exist, we simply take the longest sequence without long gaps, and
use that as our training data, discarding the rest.

4.5
Data preparation
4.5.1
Data Preparaion and Preprocessing
The multi-station data preparation pipeline processes time series data from multiple wa-
ter monitoring stations while maintaining station-specific characteristics. The system im-
plements flexible data selection strategies, allowing experiments with different temporal
ranges through either recent data selection (last_n_days) or specific date ranges (date_range_days).
4.5.2
Feature Normalization Strategy
The preprocessing pipeline supports two normalization approaches to handle the diverse
scale of meteorological features:
Per-Station Normalization: Each station’s data is normalized independently, preserving
location-specific variations in feature distributions. This approach maintains local patterns
that may be important for station-specific predictions.
Global Normalization: Features are normalized across all stations using type-specific
scaling strategies:
• Rainfall features undergo log transformation (log(1 + x)) followed by robust scaling
to handle their skewed distribution
• Pressure and humidity features use min-max scaling to preserve their bounded na-
ture
• Temperature and other standard features employ standard scaling
• Cyclical features (derived from temporal encoding) remain unnormalized as they are
already bounded by sine/cosine transformations
4.5.3
Sequence Generation
For each station, the pipeline generates overlapping sequences of length sequence_length
(default: 30 days) with corresponding target windows of prediction_days ahead. Each se-
quence i s associated with its originating station ID and optionally a seasonal indicator.
The station-to-ID mapping enables the model to learn station-specific patterns through
embeddings. Missing values are handled through feature-specific interpolation strategies
before normalization. The processed data preserves temporal continuity while ensuring
all features are properly scaled for neural network training.
4.5.4
Dataset Implementation
The MultiStationDataset class implements PyTorch’s Dataset interface to efficiently han-
dle multi-station time series data. The dataset stores:
• Input sequences as floating-point tensors of shape (sequence_length, num_features)
• Target values as flattened windows for multi-step ahead prediction
• Station identifiers as long tensors for embedding lookup
• Optional seasonal identifiers for capturing periodic patterns
The dataset supports both station-only and station-with-season configurations, returning
appropriate tuples based on the initialization parameters

4.6
Model Architecture
We implement two main models for training and prediction: LSTM [7] and Transformer
[12]. The selection of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and Transformer ar-
chitectures for water level prediction stems from the inherent temporal dependencies in
hydrological systems. Water levels at any given time point are influenced by historical
weather patterns, previous water levels, and seasonal variations - making this fundamen-
tally a sequence modeling problem.
Integration with Station Embeddings Both architectures complement the station embed-
ding approach implemented in this work. The 8-dimensional station embeddings capture
location-specific characteristics that modulate how the temporal models process weather
inputs. For LSTMs, these embeddings can be concatenated with input features or used to
initialize hidden states. In Transformers, station embeddings can be incorporated as po-
sitional encodings or additional attention keys, allowing the model to learn station-specific
response patterns.
4.6.1
LSTM Implementation
Station Embeddings: Both architectures complement the station embedding approach
implemented in this work. The 8-dimensional station embeddings capture location-specific
characteristics that modulate how the temporal models process weather inputs.
For
LSTMs, these embeddings can be concatenated with input features or used to initialize
hidden states. In Transformers, station embeddings can be incorporated as positional en-
codings or additional attention keys, allowing the model to learn station-specific response
patterns.
Seasonal Embeddings: (optional): When enabled, 4-dimensional embeddings repre-
sent seasonal quarters, capturing periodic variations in water level dynamics.
Network Architecture
The model concatenates weather features with embedding representations, creating an
enriched input representation. The architecture consists of:
• LSTM Layers: A multi-layer LSTM network (default: 3 layers, 256 hidden units) pro-
cesses the augmented input sequences. Dropout between layers (0.2-0.5) provides
regularization during training.
• Output Network: The final hidden state passes through a two-layer fully connected
network with ReLU activation and dropout. The first layer reduces dimensionality
by half (256 -> 128), and the final layer projects to the prediction horizon size.

Rolling Prediction Mechanism
The implementation creates predictions for every possible window in the timeline, moving
forward by a configurable stride. With a default stride of 1, the model generates daily
predictions, creating a dense coverage of the evaluation period. Each prediction uses the
preceding sequence of days as input to forecast the specified prediction horizon. This
approach generates overlapping predictions - for instance, predictions made on consec-
utive days will largely overlap in their forecast periods. While this creates some statistical
dependencies in the evaluation metrics, it provides valuable insights into prediction sta-
bility and how forecasts evolve as new information becomes available. The overlapping
nature also mimics operational deployment, where new predictions would be generated
daily using the most recent data.
Anchoring Predictions to Reality
A distinctive feature of the evaluation is the anchoring mechanism, which addresses a
common challenge in multi-step ahead predictions. When forecasting multiple days into
the future, models may produce predictions that are systematically shifted from the actual
values, even if they capture the correct temporal dynamics. This shift can arise from
accumulated errors in the scaling/unscaling process or from the model learning a biased
representation.
The implementation offers three anchoring strategies:
Replace Method: The simplest approach directly substitutes the first predicted value
with the actual observation at the prediction start time. This ensures continuity between
historical data and predictions but only adjusts the initial point.
Adjust Method: This strategy computes the difference between the predicted and actual
values at the start time, then applies this offset to the entire prediction sequence. This
maintains the model’s predicted dynamics while ensuring the forecast begins from the
correct level.
Blend Method: The most sophisticated approach applies an exponentially decaying ad-
justment across the prediction horizon. This creates a smooth transition from the an-
chored starting point to the model’s natural predictions, balancing accuracy at the start
with the model’s learned dynamics for longer horizons.
Temporal Filtering and Flexibility
The evaluation framework supports flexible temporal filtering through start and end date
parameters. This capability enables focused evaluation on specific periods of interest,
such as analyzing performance during historical drought conditions or flood seasons. Re-

6.1
Key Findings and Interpretation
Our multi-station Transformer model achieved remarkable accuracy in predicting Dan-
ish stream water levels, with relative RMSE values below 8% across all test stations.
This performance is particularly impressive considering that four of the five test stations
(Værebro, Hove, Gudenå, Vorgod) were completely unseen during training. The consis-
tency of results across diverse hydrological conditions - from small streams like Værebro
(2.24-2.98m range) to larger rivers like Gudenå (49.63-51.03m range) - demonstrates
the model’s robust generalization capability. The success can be attributed to three key
design decisions. First, station embeddings effectively captured location-specific hydro-
logical characteristics without manual feature engineering. The station similarity analysis
(Figure 4.10) reveals that embeddings learned meaningful geographical patterns - sta-
tions sharing weather data sources cluster together with correlations above 0.95, while
cross-region similarities drop to 0.85-0.90. This suggests the model learned that prox-
imity to weather stations matters more than absolute geographic distance. Second, our
feature engineering approach successfully balanced hydrological memory with meteoro-
logical drivers. The dominance of 1-day water level lag (r=0.979) confirms that stream
systems exhibit strong temporal autocorrelation. However, the importance of the An-
tecedent Precipitation Index and 14-day cumulative rainfall shows that the model also
captures the delayed rainfall-runoff response characteristic of Danish catchments. The
4-day lagged temperature feature likely captures evapotranspiration effects, which oper-
ate on slower timescales than direct precipitation. Third, the global normalization strategy
with feature-specific transformations preserved both relative differences between stations
and the physical properties of each variable. Log-transforming rainfall handled its skewed
distribution, while bounded scaling for pressure and humidity respected their physical lim-
its.
6.2
Feature Selection Analsis
Our best performing model used the following features for training and predictions: Water
level lag from 1 day before, API - Antecedent Precipitation Index, Cumulative rainfall of
last 14 days, Mean temperature of last 4 days lagged by 1 day.
Rainfall Integration: Why API Outperforms Simple Accumulations
The rainfall features present an interesting pattern. While all cumulative rainfall measures
show positive correlations with water levels (3-day: 0.245, 7-day: 0.329, 14-day: 0.331,
30-day: 0.245), the Antecedent Precipitation Index achieves the highest correlation at
0.344. This 5-10% improvement over simple accumulations is significant and reveals
important hydrological insights. API’s superiority stems from its exponential weighting
scheme (k=0.9), which mimics how catchments actually process rainfall:
Recent rainfall receives high weight (reflecting immediate runoff), while older rainfall con-
tributes with exponentially decreasing influence (reflecting soil moisture decay)
The inter-feature correlation matrix (Figure 4.4) shows cumulative rainfall features are
highly correlated with each other (0.70-0.91), confirming redundancy. API captures the
essence of all these features in a single, physically-motivated variable.

Despite API’s effectiveness, including 14-day cumulative rainfall (correlation: 0.331) im-
proved model performance. This apparent redundancy actually captures different aspects
of the rainfall-runoff process: API responds quickly to recent rain but ”forgets” past events
exponentially, while 14-day accumulation maintains equal weight for all rainfall within the
window.
This combination allows the model to capture both fast (surface runoff) and slow (ground-
water recharge) pathways.
Temperature’s Lagged Effect on Water Levels
Temperature features show consistently negative correlations with water levels (-0.469 to

relationship reflects two mechanisms:
• Evapotranspiration: Higher temperatures increase water loss from the catchment
• Seasonal patterns: Summer months have both high temperatures and low water
levels

Pressure features (-0.227 correlation): While low pressure indicates incoming storms,

correlation): Highly correlated with temperature (0.65) and provides similar information
about evapotranspiration potential. Temperature is preferred as it’s more reliably mea-
sured and directly interpretable. Wind speed (0.231 correlation): The positive correlation
is surprising but likely spurious, reflecting that both wind and water levels are higher in
winter. The effect is indirect and better captured through temperature and rainfall. Longer
water level lags: Despite significant correlations (7-day: 0.733, 14-day: 0.598), these are
redundant given the 1-day lag and add computational complexity in sequence models.
6.3
Prediction Horizon Analysis
The error growth pattern (Figure 5.6) reveals two distinct regimes. From 1-3 days, pre-
diction error increases sharply as the model transitions from relying primarily on water
level persistence to incorporating weather-driven changes. Beyond 3 days, error growth
becomes more linear, suggesting the model has fully transitioned to weather-based pre-
diction. This inflection point at 3 days aligns with our feature engineering choice of 4-day
lagged weather variables, indicating the model correctly learned the typical lag between
weather events and hydrological response in Danish streams. The maintained predictive
skill at longer horizons (R² > 0.75 even at 14 days) suggests that seasonal patterns and
long-term weather trends provide useful information beyond immediate conditions. This
has practical implications for water management - while day-ahead predictions can sup-
port immediate operational decisions, week-ahead forecasts remain reliable enough for
planning purposes.
6.4
Comparison with Previous Approaches
Our model significantly outperforms the basic LSTM implementation from Zhang et al.
[87], which achieved R² values around 0.6-0.7 on similar prediction tasks. The 20-30%

improvement in explained variance demonstrates the value of our architectural innova-
tions. Station embeddings allow the model to implicitly learn catchment characteristics
that Zhang’s single-station approach required manual encoding. The Transformer archi-

predictions, avoiding the sequential processing limitations of standard LSTMs. The com-

6.5
Regional Patterns and Transferability
The model’s ability to generalize across regions suggests it learned fundamental hydro-
logical principles rather than location-specific patterns. Sjælland stations (Sengeløse,
Værebro, Hove) showed slightly better performance than Jylland stations (Gudenå, Vor-
god), possibly due to the higher density of training stations in Sjælland providing better
regional coverage. The station similarity matrix indicates that models could be further
improved by ensuring balanced geographic representation in training data.
6.6
Limitations and Uncertainties
Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, our model was trained on data from
2015-2023, a period without extreme flooding events in Denmark. Performance during
rare extreme events remains untested. Second, the static weather-to-water station map-
pings assume catchment boundaries align with nearest weather stations, which oversim-
plifies complex drainage patterns. Third, we excluded stations with extensive missing
data, potentially biasing our results toward well-maintained monitoring locations. The
feature correlation analysis revealed strong negative correlation between temperature
and water levels (r = -0.52), but this likely conflates direct evapotranspiration effects with
seasonal patterns. Future work should explicitly separate these mechanisms, possibly
through physics-informed architectures that encode water balance equations.
6.7
Practical Implications
For operational deployment, our results suggest a tiered prediction system: 1-3 day fore-
casts for immediate operational decisions (RMSE < 2.5cm), 4-7 day forecasts for short-
term planning (RMSE < 5cm), and 8-14 day outlooks for strategic water management. The
model’s consistent performance across diverse stations indicates it could be deployed for
new monitoring locations after collecting just a few months of calibration data.
6.8
Future Research Directions
Several avenues for improving and extending this research emerge from the current
study’s limitations and findings.
Computational Resources and Model Scaling
Our experiments were constrained by limited computational capacity, which restricted
both model architecture exploration and hyperparameter optimization. With access to
more powerful GPU clusters, future work could investigate larger Transformer models
with increased attention heads and deeper architectures. More importantly, comprehen-
sive hyperparameter searches could be performed. The current grid search explored only

==PAGE:80==
100 configurations - with better resources, thousands of combinations could be evaluated,
potentially revealing non-intuitive parameter interactions.
Data Quality and Quantity Improvements
While our model performed well on available data, several data-related improvements
could enhance predictions. Expanding the temporal coverage beyond 2015-2025 would
capture more extreme events and long-term climate variations. Implementing more so-
phisticated quality control algorithms could reduce noise in the measurements. Our sim-
ple interpolation for gaps under 7 days could be replaced with physics-based imputation
methods that consider upstream/downstream stations. Additionally, incorporating addi-
tional data sources such as soil moisture sensors, groundwater levels, or satellite-derived
precipitation could provide complementary information about catchment conditions.
Automated Station Mapping
Currently, weather-to-water station mappings are manually defined based on geographic
proximity. Future work should develop an automated mapping algorithm that considers
catchment boundaries and drainage networks, elevation gradients and topographic bar-
riers, correlation analysis between historical weather and water data, and weighted com-
binations of multiple weather stations. This would not only reduce manual effort but could
improve predictions by better representing the spatial distribution of precipitation across
catchments.
Integration of Management Actions
A significant limitation of the current model is the absence of human management effects.
Grass cutting operations significantly impact stream hydraulics, but our dataset contained
only 20 records from 2024 - insufficient for model training. Future work should establish
systematic recording of grass cutting dates across all monitored streams and develop a
standardized format for management action data. With 2-3 years of consistent manage-
ment data, these human influences could be explicitly modeled through embeddings or
binary indicators, potentially reducing prediction errors during summer months when veg-
etation management is most active. The model could also learn the temporal decay of
grass cutting effects on water levels.
Real-time Deployment Considerations
Transitioning from research to operational deployment requires addressing several prac-
tical challenges. Developing robust pipelines for real-time data ingestion and quality con-
trol would ensure model reliability under operational conditions. Implementing ensemble
methods to quantify prediction uncertainty would provide water managers with confidence
intervals for decision-making. Creating adaptive learning mechanisms to update models
as new data arrives would maintain prediction accuracy over time. Finally, building intu-
itive user interfaces would enable water managers to effectively interact with and interpret
model predictions. These engineering challenges, while outside the scope of this thesis,
are crucial for translating research advances into practical benefits for Danish water man-
agement.
