Abstract 
Fungi have the huge ability to produce a tremendous number of secondary metabolites. These secondary 
metabolites could have an important role in drug discovery. The fungi that were used in this study is a 
newly discovered fungus, Aspergillus sulphureoviridis. Which were isolated in Denmark, in 2016. it was 
possible to analyse the chemical profile of the fungi. This was done by doing a dereplication of the MS data. 
Moreover, it was possible to elucidate the structure of 3 isolated compounds. It was discovered that 
compound 1 was a known compound, Cladobotrin IV. Compound 2 proved to be an isomer of Cladobotrin 
IV. Lastly, compound 3 was found to a derivative of Cladobotrin IV.

Introduction 
Secondary metabolites 
Fungi are an important source of secondary metabolites that can be very useful for drug discovery. 
However, secondary metabolites, unlike primary metabolites are not essential to the growth or survival of 
the fungi. Especially in laboratory conditions, it is hard to trigger the production of these compounds 
because these compounds.  
Secondary metabolites are small molecules that can be useful to the survival of the fungi by for example 
acting as signal molecules..

Fungi 
Fungi produces a lot of secondary metabolites. Some of them have a significant importance to the 
humankind. Some of those secondary metabolites falls into the category of drugs, pigments industrial 
chemicals and biofuels. On the other hand, fungi are also known to produce mycotoxins and contaminants 
in food. Even more, they can also cause life threatening infections. Therefore, discovery of secondary 
metabolites from fungi is very important and most of these compounds are still unknown. [2]. 
One of the most researched fungi, are from the species Aspergillus nidulantes. It has been determined that 
a lot of aspergillus species produce mycotoxins but also have. The A. Sydowii and A. Sulphureoviridis are 
subgenus nidulantes.

Chromatography 
Chromatography is a powerful separation technique used in analytical chemistry making it possible to 
separate compounds in simple or complex mixtures for further analysis. There are several types of 
chromatography methods such as gas chromatography(GC), liquid chromatography (LC), ion-exchange 
chromatography etc. Although, these are different methods, they all operate on the same principle which 
uses the knowledge of the chemical characteristics of the compounds in a mixture and their interaction 
between the stationary phase and the mobile phase. Compounds that are of interest for chemical analysis 
are referred as analytes. All chromatography methods consist of a stationary phase which is the immobile 
component and a mobile phase that moves through the stationary phase. The mobile phase carries the 
mixture of analytes through the stationary phase [3]. As the mixture moves through the stationary phase, 
analytes will be partitioned between the stationary phase and the mobile phase. If the analyte favours the 
mobile phase more, then the compound will elute quickly and if it favours the stationary phase then the 
compound will be retained and migrate through the system much slower. As a result, analytes elute at 
different times with different velocities[4].  
The choice of separation technique is highly depended on the chemical characteristics of the compound 
However, in this study only LC will be discussed.  
High performance liquid chromatography 
In LC, the mobile phase is a liquid that consists of a solvent or a mixture of solvents. The mobile phase 
moves the analyte through a column with the force of gravity. High pressure liquid chromatography (HPLC) 
and ultrahigh pressure chromatography (UHPLC) are more innovative versions of LC and as the name 
suggests, they use high pressure instead of the force of gravity used in LC.  Also, this allows the use of 
smaller-particle sized column resulting in better resolution. In natural product discovery the methods 
reverse phase preparative-, semi-preparative- and analytical liquid chromatography methods are usually 
used to analyse and purify compounds.  Preparative HPLC is usually used to make fractions of the crude 
extracts. The column diameter ranges much bigger compared to the columns used in analytical HPLC. The 
semi-preparative HPLC is usually a preparation for analytical HPLC. Both the preparative and semi-
preparative work under lower pressure compared to analytical HPLC that operates under very high 
pressure. 
Generally, an HPLC system consists of a solvent system, a column, an injection site, a pump, a detector, and 
a PC data system. Figure 3 illustrates this set-up.

There are different separation modes of HPLC. Selecting a suitable mode of HPLC depends on the chemical 
profile of the compound in query such as size and affinity to the stationary and mobile phase. However, 
only normal-phase chromatography (NPC) and reversed-phase chromatography (RPC) will be discussed. 
Normal-phase chromatography 
NPC is a traditional mode of separation and is usually known as liquid-solid chromatography or adsorption 
chromatography. It features a polar stationary phase and a nonpolar mobile phase. Because of this setup, 
this mode of separation is optimal for the separation of nonpolar compounds. Nonpolar compounds will 
elute first where polar compounds will elute later as they will interact with the stationary phase. Generally, 
the stationary phase is made of silica gel with silanol groups. The mobile phase is a low-polarity and 
solvents such as hexane dichloromethane and methane are used. Figure 4 shows a simplified illustration of 
the stationary phase for both NPC and RPC. In NPC, the analytes are retained by dipole-dipole and 
hydrogen bonding interactions[4].

Reverse-phase chromatography 
In natural product discovery, the most common technique that is used to separate compound is RPC.  In 
RPC, the polarity is reversed. The stationary phase is nonpolar, and the mobile phase is polar thus polar 
compounds will elute much quicker than nonpolar compounds as illustrated in figure 4. The stationary 
phase typically consists hydrophobic groups that will interact through Van der Wall forces. Typically, C8, 
C18, CN or phenyl hexyl  are the choice of columns used in RPC. The columns have different characterises. 
For example, C18 is very hydrophobic and therefore more retentive compared to a C8 column that is similar 
but less retentive. [6]. 
In In this study,  reverse phase C18 columns were used as well as phenyl hexyl reverse phase columns. 
While the C18 column is usually used for natural product discovery. The phenyl hexyl reverse phase column 
can retain aromatic compounds better. This is due to the π-π bindings that occurs between the column and 
the aromatic solutes[7]. The mobile phase is mixture of organic solvents like methanol or acetonitrile and 
water. Moreover, in this study, the separations are performed under gradient conditions where the 
strength of the mobile phase is increases with the time during the elution of compounds. 
 
Detectors 
Diode array detector 
Some compounds have an absorption in the ultraviolet (UV) and in the visible(VIS) region, especially 
secondary metabolites with conjugated bonds. Therefore, a detector that can measure the absorbance in 
both UV/VIS spectrum is favourable. In this study a diode array detector is used because it can detect 
absorption in UV to VIS region.  
The diode array detector (DAD) consists of a series of photodiode detectors, where each photodiode 
detector is responsible for the detection and recording a specific band area in the spectrum. The 
photodiodes are all connected which makes it possible to record the entire spectrum at the same time[8]. 
Because of this, it is possible to produce a total wavelength chromatogram (TWC) and more specifically 
extracted wavelength chromatograms (EWC).

Mass spectroscopy 
Mass spectroscopy (MS) is a very powerful tool, that allow the identification of molecular mass of a 
compound. A mass spectrometer has generally five components. It consists of sample inlet which leads to 
the ion source, where the ions from the solution will be transformed into gas phase ions. Next is a mass 
analyser that separates the ions based on their m/z values and lastly a detector. 
Electrospray ionization 
One of the common methods used to ionize molecules is electrospray ionization (ESI). This technique 
allows ions in a solution to be transformed into the gas phase. In this method, the solution that contains 
the compounds is sprayed out into the taylor core into the heating chamber. This heated chamber is at 
approximately atmospheric pressure. As seen on figure X, the taylor cone have a high voltage across the 
surface. Here, small, charged droplet are passed through into the ionized chamber and exposed to the 
drying gas which will evaporate the solvent from the droplets. This will change the charge density of the 
droplets. The density will continue to increase until the electrostatic forces surpass the surface tension in 
which the droplets will again be broken into much smaller sizes[8].   
 
 
Figure 5 Schematic presentation of ESI[9] 
 
Time of flight and tandem  
One of the common mass analysers used is, the time of flight (TOF). The technique behind this is based on 
the difference in velocities of the ions. When the ions are accelerated forward at the same time with the 
same kinetic energy, the ions will have different velocities because they have different mass.  The greater 
the mass the slower it will travel down the flight tube.

MS/MS  
MS/MS spectre can be generated by using a quadrupole. The quadrupole consists as the name suggest of 
pour parallel poles position to the direction of the ion beam. A specific current is applied in order to 
generate a magnetic field with a chosen radio frequency that can only specific ions with specific m/z value 
to pass through a collision cell where the ions will be fragmented and detected to be analysed[8].

Nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy 
A very powerful spectroscopic method is the nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy because it 
provides important information that makes it possible to elucidate or identify the chemical structure of 
compounds[11]. This technique uses magnetic field and electromagnetic frequencies to study the structure 
of compounds. The theory behind this technique is to take advantage of the nuclear spin states that many 
atoms have, because with some atoms, their nuclei behave as if they were spinning.  Because of this 
feature, the nucleus behaves like magnets around a magnetic field. The nucleus will align on the magnetic 
field and will change its spin state when an energy impulse is applied. The spin state changes back again 
when the energy pulse is gone. As a result, an NMR spectre will show different peaks because each type of 
proton and carbon will have different chemical environments and will therefore align differently on the 
magnetic field; consequently, each type of proton and carbon will have different chemical shift values. 
However, this spin phenomena are only seen in atomic nucleus that have an odd mass, odd atomic number, 
or both. The most common nuclei used in NMR spectroscopy are 1H - and 13C. Both of their atomic nuclei 
have a spin state of ½ and – ½[8].   
      
From a 1H-NMR spectrum, three types of information can be gathered. Every peak represents a different 
type of protein. Another important information can be gathered, is the total number of protons. This can is 
done by analysing the integral values under each peak. Lastly is the splitting pattern also called spin-spin 
splitting. This can be explained as the n + 1 rule to predict the splitting pattern. Each proton can sense the 
number of protons on the carbon next to the one it is bonded which give a split of n+1. The chemical shift is 
on the x-axis and is measured in ppm.  
In 13C NMR spectre, the number of peaks usually indicate the total number of carbons. A table representing 
approximate chemical shift ranges for selected types of protons and carbons atoms can be used to 
determine the type of proton and carbon in each spectrum. See figure XX in Appendix X. One of the most 
used solvents in 1H and 13C-NMR is deuterated chloroform (CDCl3). Interestingly, CDCl3 have a unique 
pattern in the 13C spectrum and usually gives rise to a three-peak pattern that have the same intensities at 
around 77 ppm[8].   
The information obtained from both the 1H-NMR and 13C-NMR is not adequate to confirm the structure of 
the compound. The Heteronuclear Single Quantum Correlation spectrum also shortened to HSQC is used to 
determine the carbon single bonds, 1J 1H-13C couplings,  correlated with the 1H-NMR spectrum[12]. This 
type of spectrum is a correlation of the 13C and 1H spectre. Here, the 1H-NMR is on the x-axis while the C-
NMR is on the y-axis. This spectrum will make it possible to assign each peak in the 1C NMR spectrum to the 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
corresponding proton from the 1H-NMR spectrum[8]. Like the HSQC, the heteronuclear multiple bond 
correlation, HMBC, also is a 2D NMR that show the correlation between the 1H-NMR and the 13C-NMR 
spectrum. However, this technique shows the correlation for 2J and 3J 1H-13C coupling. 
Databases 
In natural product discovery, the use of databases is a very important step in dereplicating known 
compounds. This will allow an efficient dereplication of known compounds that have been characterized 
from previous studies. Therefore, it will make it possible to focus on identifying and isolating new or 
unknown compounds. In this project, the MS spectre were searched against the in-house MS/MS database 
of DTU Bioengineering[13] is used along with the Reaxys online database. Reaxys, provides generally 
information from previous published studies. The in-house database is however much more reliable as the 
compounds were analysed with LC-DAD-HRMS and therefore contain accurate mass and fragmentation 
patterns for each compound in the library. 
 
Techniques for analysing compounds 
Dereplication 
MS data from LC-DAD-MS can be processed using dereplication. The dereplication makes it possible to find 
known compounds using databases to focus on discovering new compounds. Using the software, Agilent 
Masshunter, the output data is visualized as a total ion chromatogram (TIC). From this, the base peak 
chromatogram (BPC) is extracted which illustrates the peaks with the highest intensities. From here, 
different compounds will have different retention time. Compounds can be found by looking at [M+H] + 
peaks and the adducts that are related to [M+H]+. The chemical formular is then found by the m/z value 
and researching various databases and literature can often confirm whether the discovered compound in 
known.

Method and instrumentation1 
UHPLC-DAD-QTOF-MS 
The LC-MS analysis were performed using ultra-high liquid chromatography diode array detection 
quadrupole time-of-flight mass spectrometry (UHPLC-DAD-QTOF-MS). This was performed on an Agilent 
Infinity 1290 UHPLC system  (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, Ca, USA) that is equipped with a DAD. The 
separations were achieved on an Agilent Poroshell 120 phenyl-hexyl column with the dimensions of 250 
mm x 2.1 mm and particle diameter of 2.7 µm at 60 ⁰C with a flow rate of 0.35 mL/min. This was done by 
using a linear gradient of 10% acetonitrile (MeCN) in Milli-Q water buffered with 20mM formic acid (FA) 
that was increased to 100% in 15 minutes and held for 2 minutes. It was then returned to 10% for 0.1 
minute and held for 3 minutes. The MS detection were performed using Agilent 6545 QTOF MS that is 
equipped with Agilent Dual Jet Stream electrospray ion source with a drying gas temperature of 160 ⁰C with 
gas flow set on 13 L/ min. The temperature of the sheath gas was 300 ⁰C and the flow was 16 L/min. Other 
conditions are all set as previously described in Kildgaard et al. 2014[13].  
The fractionation of the crude extract from only the DCM were fractionated using Isolera One automated 
flash system (Biotage). This was done on a 25 g RP C18 20-35µm 100A (flash spherical AQ, Agela 
technologies) flash column (Biotage) with a gradient of 10-100% MeOH with a flow of 25mL/min. The 
fractions were automatically collected based on the UV signals, 254 and 320 nm which resulted in 13 
fractions.  
Fraction 5 was further fractionated by Agilent Infinity 1290 HPLC-DAD (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, 
CA, USA) system. The fractionation was done using a linear gradient from 25 to 45% MeCN in Milli-Q water 
over 30 minutes with a flow rate of 4 mL/min at the maximum pressure 450 bar and temperature at 40 ⁰C. 
The UV was monitored at 240, 320 and 430 nm. This was resulted in 14 subfractions. 
From subfraction 2, the purification of compound 1 and compound 2 were performed on a Gemini C6-
Phenyl column with the dimensions of 5µm, 100Å, 250 x 10 mm (Phenomenex) using a linear gradient from 
20 to 30% MeCN in Milli-Q water over 30 minutes. For compound 3, a RP C18 column with the dimensions of 
5µm, 100Å, 250 x 10 mm (Phenomenex). The linear gradient that was used was from 15 to 20% MeCN in 
Milli-Q water over 30 minutes[14].

Experimental 
The experiments are divided into two categories: a small-scale cultivation and extraction for profiling the 
secondary metabolites; and a larger scale cultivation and extraction for purification of compounds. 
Fungal strain 
The fungi to be investigated were an isolate of the fungi, aspergillus Sulphureoviridis, and were obtained 
from the IBT2 collection from DTU. The in-house id for A. Sulphureoviridis is 21686.  
Small scale cultivation 
Inoculation  
A. sulphureoviridis were grown on different media for both characterization and analysing purposes. 
Therefore, inoculation of the fungi was done using a three-point technique in which fungal spore were 
transferred to the agar plates with different media. This was sone by using 2mL of spore solution that is 
poured into the fungal culture agar plate. A drigalski spatula is used to spread the spore solution and to 
make sure to get the spores in the solution by scraping and rubbing the surface of the fungi. The 
suspension with spores is collected using a pipette and transferred to a 5 mL vial. An inoculation loop 
needle is then dipped into the vial with the spore suspension and poked into the agar plate in three 
different spots. The different media were, YES, CYA and OAT. The fungi were incubated at 25 ⁰C in the dark 
for 1 week and 6 weeks respectively for secondary metabolic profile analysis using UHPLC-DAD-QTOF-MS. 
This inoculation method was also used again for the larger-scale extraction.

Plug extraction  
Since the method of extraction is highly depended on the chemical profile of the target compounds, this 
extraction method is a qualitative method that targets specifically the secondary metabolites from fungal 
cultures grown in agar plates for HPLC and LC-MS. 
Using a plug drill, 3 plugs of 5 mm in diameter were taken from each colony making sure to plug from the 
oldest to newest growth. 2.0 mL eppendorf tubes were used for the extraction. The plug extraction involves 
two extraction steps. One with ethyl acetate with 0.05%(w/w) formic acid and the other with iso-propanol. 
Both solvents were HPLC grade. In the first extraction, the plugged samples are extracted with 1mL of ethyl 
acetate with 0.05%(w/w) formic acid and placed in an ultrasonication bath(Sonorex) for 45 minutes and 
centrifugated for 3 minutes. After this step the extract is transferred to another vial and was dried using 
nitrogen evaporation. After the evaporation step, the dried extracts were dissolved in 150 µL HPLC grade 
MeOH which were ultrasonicated, centrifuged and then carefully transferring 80 µL to HPLC vials for LC-MS 
analysis. Extraction with iso-propanol follows the same procedure.  
 
Large- scale cultivation and extraction 
YES media preparation  
2L of YES media is prepared for a large-scale culturing of A. Sulphureoviridis for further extraction. The 2L 
media resulted to 140 YES agar plates in total. The following method describes the preparation of 1L YES 
media.  
The following reagents were added to a 1L volumetric flask: 20 g yeast extract; 150 g sucrose; 0.5 g MgSO4, 
7H2O; 20 g agar; 1 mL tracemetal solution; 885 mL Milli-Q water. The ingredients were mixed using a 
magnetic stirrer until all reagents were dissolved. Since it is required for the media to be at a pH of 6.5 ± 
0.1, a few drops of 1M HCl were added with a 1 mL pipette to lower the pH. The volumetric flask with the 
media were covered with aluminium foil and autoclaved at 121 ⁰C for 15 minutes. The media were 
distributed to the agar plates where approximately 4 - 5 mm of media were poured into each agar plate. 
Once the media has cooled off, the agar plates were covered and refrigerated for cultivation of the fungi. 
The same inoculation method described in the small-scale cultivation were also applied for the large-scale 
cultivation.

Extraction 
The cultured A. Sulphureoviridis were cut and placed into 2x 1L Buchner flasks and extracted twice with 
EtOAc acid(1% formic acid).  A total of 2.5L EtOAc and 25 mL formic acid was used. The fungi were left to be 
extracted for 1 h and 24 h respectively. A filter paper with a diameter of 185mm (Whatman) was used to 
filter the big particles and the crude solution was evaporated using a rotary evaporator resulting in 7.02 g 
crude extract. A liquid-liquid extraction is performed to give 3 different phases: a dichloromethane (DCM) 
phase, a MeOH/Milli-Q water phase and a heptane phase. The crude extract is dissolved in a volume of 9: 1 
MeOH: Milli-Q water and extracted with heptane using the same volume. This gave two separated phases, 
a heptane phase that contain low polarity extracts and an  aqueous phase.  Milli-Q water is  added to the 
Milli-Q water-MeOH phase to get a ratio of 1:1. DCM is added to achieve a ratio 1:1 and the phases were 
separated.   Since, medium polarity compounds are found in the DCM phase, the DCM phase were dried  to 
be fractionated for further analysis. 
Preparative fractionation  
The DCM phase from the liquid-liquid extraction were dried using rotary evaporation (Rotavapor R-215, 
Buchi) and resulted in 4.13 g dry crude extract. The crude extract was transferred to the top of the cartridge 
( Sax Biotage snap, 25g). The column used was a RP C18 flash column (20-35µm 100A). The fractionation was 
done on the Isolera one automated flash system that resulted into 13 fractions. The solvents used were 
Milli-Q water and MeOH(HPLC grade, ≥ 99.9%). The gradient was 10-100% MeOH with a flowrate 25 
mL/min. All fractions were dried using nitrogen evaporation and measured. Samples from every fraction is 
collected for LC-DAD-MS analysis. 
 
Semi-preparative fractionation  
For further fractionation, fraction 5 yielded 0.081 g dry extract.  This dry extract was dissolved in a solvent 
containing a mixture of 250 µL MeCN and 250 µL Milli-Q water. This dissolved extract was transferred into a 
an eppendorf tube and centrifugated for 3 minutes. From this, 200 µL is transferred into a HPLC vial to be 
analysed using the method described in earlier sections. 
Final purification was described in under the method section.

Results and discussion 
Initial analysis of A. sulphureoviridis 
Crude extract is used for the initial investigation of the secondary metabolite profile of A. sulphureoviridis. 
To study this, the fungus was inoculated for 1 week and 6 weeks on three different media and then 
extracted as described earlier. From figure 1.a, in Appendix, it was roughly evaluated that YES media 
resulted in more compounds compared to CYA and OAT. Therefore, it was selected to only work further 
with the YES media. The aim of this experiment was to examine the chemical profile after 1 week and 6 
weeks. From a preliminary observation of the BPC scans, a rough comparison of the major peaks with the 
highest intensities showed that the chemistry has changed from week 1 and week 6. On figure 6,  the major 
peaks are assigned with numbers, and shows that only two peaks, no. 1 and 31, are still present from week 
1 to week 6. Moreover, new peaks are present in week 6. The disappearance of compounds from week 1 
can maybe indicate that some compounds were precursors for the new compounds in week 6 or that they 
were important for the initial growth of the fungus. Also, the compounds from week 6 can be compounds 
that can only be synthesized later in the growth process. Note that for week 6 the extraction solvents are 
different and therefore they result in extracting different compounds. Table 1.b in Appendix gives an 
overview of the peaks and their related m/z values.

HSQC 
The HSQC spectrum is shown in figure 13 where the 1H-NMR is on the x-axis and 13C-NMR on y-axis. From

because there are diastrophic protons that have two different chemical environments because of an 
asymmetry in the molecule. From this, it can be confirmed that there are two methyl carbons, two olefinic 
methine carbons, one carbonyl group. The remaining carbon atoms are non-protonated sp2 carbons. On

Compound 2 
The same analytical procedure of the NMR data for Cladobotrin IV is also applied for compound 2. 
Compound 2 have the same chemical formula as Cladobotrin IV but has shown to have different retention 
time. It should be noted however that some of the NMR signals for this compound have been weak and 
was not recorded. A more detailed analysis of the NMR data showed that compound 2 could be a structural 
isomer of Cladobotrin. Calculating the coupling constant of the olefinic protons is a method that can be 
used to determine the isomerism. For Cladobotrin IV, the coupling constants are calculated to be 15.28 and 
1.76 Hz and for compound 2, 7.36 and 11.84 Hz. Since the coupling constant is smaller in cis compared to 
trans isomers, it is suggested that compound 2 is a cis isomer of Cladobotrin IV. The proposed structure of 
compound 2 is found on figure 16 
Compound 3 
From the MS analysis, it is found that compound 3 has a m/z value of 255.0865 [M+H]+ with the chemical 
formula, C12H14O6. From the 1H-NMR spectrum, the integral values shows that there a total of 14 protons in 
the molecule. Moreover, there are two olefinic protons at 6.78 and 7.57 ppm, one methyl group at 2.15 
ppm, two methoxy groups at 3.83 and 3.99 ppm and one oxygenated methylene group at 4.62 ppm. From 
the 13C-NMR  spectrum, it can be confirmed that there are a total of 12 carbons. Together with the data 
from the HSQC showed that there are four sp2 carbons, a methyl group, two carbonyl groups and two 
olefinic methine carbons. Table 7, shows the HMBC correlations in compound 3. From the HMBC data, it is 
found that compound 3 has the same α-pyrone as in compound 1 and 2. Therefore, it can be assumed that 
compound 3 can be a derivate of compound 1 or 2.

Conclusion  
From the newly discovered fungi, Aspergillus sulphureoviridis, it was possible to analyse the chemical 
profile using LC-MS data from crude extracts. This was done by doing a dereplication of the MS data. 
Furthermore, the structural identification of 3 isolated compounds was done by analysing the NMR data for 
each compound. It was discovered that compound 1 was a known compound, Cladobotrin IV. Compound 2 
proved to be an isomer of Cladobotrin IV. Lastly, compound 3 was found to a derivative of Cladobotrin IV. 
Further studies 
Since A. sulphureoviridis is a newly discovered compound, many secondary metabolites are yet to be 
found. Further addition could be testing for antimicrobial activities against bacteria.
