Abstract 
 
This project addresses the difficulties in winter concreting. High humidity, rain and significant 
number of passages at low temperatures are the characteristics of Danish weather in wintertime. 
Frozen concrete leads to cracks, smoldering and spalling and shows problems with durability. Current 
in Denmark engineers are following winter concreting guidelines likewise other countries as e.g. 
Norway, which concerns an adequate temperature during curing, protect the concrete from freezing 
and avoid thermal shocks.  Following the guidelines’ recommendations, extra resources is used due 
to machinery and manual labor. In Denmark the cold weather concreting procedure has remained 
constant for several decades while in e.g. Norway which recently added an alternative approach to 
the usual costly arrangements. The alternative approach is to use antifreeze admixtures in the concrete 
that allow the concrete to gain strength at low temperatures. This master thesis seek to investigate the 
properties of two different antifreeze admixtures and to what extend these antifreeze admixture will 
benefit the Danish building industry in wintertime. This was investigated doing following: First, the 
heat development of all samples were measured using a semi-adiabatic calorimeter. Second, the 
compressive strength development of samples exposed to 5℃ and −10℃ was measured after 1, 2, 3, 
7 and 28 days of curing using a compression testing machine. Third, the freezing point of water mixed 
with antifreeze admixtures was measured using a micro calorimeter. The results showed that the heat 
and compressive strength development is affected positively by the antifreeze admixture. In addition, 
the antifreeze admixture was able to lower the freezing point of water when added. The parameters 
suggested by cement manufacturers using to simulate the heat development showed to be too 
conservative and to not be conservative.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Previous studies 
Concrete is a composite material made up of cement, aggregates and water. Concrete gains its strength 
through the chemical reaction between water and cement. The reaction creates a gel that sets and 
binds the aggregates together. The chemical reaction known as the curing process, depends on several 
different parameters, one of which is the temperature conditions. (Korhonen 1990) The best 
temperature for casting concrete is 15-16 ℃. At temperatures below +5 ℃ the chemical reaction rate 
between water and cement significantly decreases. (Çullu & Arslan 2013) 
The curing process of concrete is delayed in cold weather conditions. Cold weather conditions are 
defined according to Chairman et al. (1989) as “a period when, for more than 3 consecutive days, the 
following conditions exists: 1) the average daily air temperature is less than 40 F (5 ℃) and 2) the air 
temperature is not greater than 50 F (10 ℃) for more than one-half of any 24-hr period.” Delaying the 
curing process increases the risk of the concrete freezing before initial set (Chairman 1989). 
According to Korhonen (1990) the hydration process of plain concrete stops at temperatures below 
−4 ℃ because all free water in the concrete freezes and the concrete becomes dormant.  
A proper casting of concrete in winter conditions is when no permanent damages, e.g. early 
freezing, happen during the casting process. A hardening concrete will be permanently damaged if 
the water inside the concrete freezes in the first fifteen to twenty hours of maturity. The maturity of 
the concrete is stated as the number of maturity hours at 20 ℃, resulting in the same maturity 
condition. (Hansen & Pedersen 1999) According to Chairman et al. (1989) the concrete will not be 
damaged by one cycle of freezing and thawing if it has attained a compressive strength of 500 psi 
(3.5 MPa). Casting concrete in cold weather conditions results in two main problems: 1) Placing the 
concrete at low temperatures (below 5 ℃) delays the strength gain, and 2) later deterioration caused 
by cycles of freezing and thawing. (Polat 2016)  
Several options are available for protection of the concrete preventing the water from freezing, 
including heating the water and aggregates, covering the casted concrete with insulation material, the 
use of high-early strength cement which provides additional hydration heat and use of chemical 
admixtures to accelerate concrete hardening and increase early-age strength development.” 
(Demirboga et al. 2014; Hansen & Pedersen 1999; Chairman 1989) 
Countries with cold climate aim at concrete construction occurring in the warmer seasons. 
Extending the construction season demands special precautions. Many cold weather countries, 
including Denmark, are using the mentioned methods and thereby spend a large amount of money on 
the production of concrete during wintertime. “The cold weather concreting is usually expensive 
because of the need for equipment to protect the fresh concrete from frost damage” (Sakai et al. 2008). 
The choice of which method or methods to use is thereby dependent on the costs and benefits 
involved. The methods mentioned may be too expensive or may not provide sufficient protection. An 
alternative method, which in some construction projects may be economically or practical beneficial, 
is to use antifreeze admixtures, such as urea, sodium nitrite and calcium-nitrate (Korhonen 1990; 
Demirboga et al. 2014). The antifreezing admixtures works in two ways: 1) “They lower the freezing 
point of the liquid phase of concrete”, and 2) “they accelerate hydration of cement and also possess 
antifreezing properties” (Ratinov & Rozenberg 1995). Earlier studies demonstrate that antifreeze 
==PAGE:12==

admixtures will allow concrete to gain strength at below-freezing temperatures and lower the freezing 
point of the mixing water compared to control samples without antifreeze admixtures (Korhonen 
2004; Karagol et al. 2015; Dong et al. 2013; Korhonen & Cortez 1991; Sakai et al. 1991; Polat 2016). 
The literature encourages the use of antifreezing admixtures in concrete placed in low temperatures. 
However, adding antifreezing admixtures are not a common method in Denmark compared to other 
foreign countries. Laboratory tests supported by field demonstrations are required to develop this 
technology for Danish cements and construction practices.

1.3 Scope of research 
The aim of this project is to clarify the influence of antifreezing admixtures on the curing process of 
concrete exposed to cold weather conditions. In this way, the use of antifreezing admixtures in 
concrete will gain more value in Denmark and be part of the Danish standard of winter concreting in 
the long term. Two commercial antifreezing admixtures is investigated, one from a cooperation 
between the Danish concrete factory DK Beton and the German chemical firm BASF called 
MasterSet-AC and one from the Italian company MAPEI called Antifreeze N.  
To achieve these aims, experimental and theoretical research are being made: 
 Specimens will be cast and the heat development will be investigated by testing the specimens 
in a semi-adiabatic calorimeter. From these results, a correlation between the antifreeze 
admixture and the heat of hydration will be found and used to determine the curing process 
of the specimen exposed to cold weather conditions.  
 The heat development will be compared to computational hardening simulation in 4C –Temp 
and Stress. The simulation tool will be studied and evaluated when using antifreezing 
admixtures. 
 Standardized specimens (Ø100 ×  200 𝑚𝑚) will be cast and exposed to two different 
temperatures. The strength development of these will be investigated by compressive strength 
tests. 
 Samples of water with and without antifreezing admixture will be tested in a micro 
calorimeter. The antifreezing admixture’s ability to lower the freezing point will be 
determined.

2 Theory 
 
2.1 Freezing of concrete 
Literature investigating freezing of concrete as a construction practice shows a possibility for the 
concrete to freeze without losing strength, but ice is not usually thought of as a desired component of 
fresh concrete (Suprenant 1985). As mentioned in the introduction the concrete gains strength by the 
reaction between water and cement. When the water freezes, it is not available for the hydration 
process. In fresh concrete, eighty percent of the water is free water. Therefore, in the situation where 
the outdoor temperature becomes below the freezing point, a great amount of ice will form. 
(Kristiansen et al. 2015) 
The formation of ice affects the decrease in the compressive strength of concrete frozen at an early 
age in two ways. The first is directly related to the formation of ice lenses. The ice lenses are formed 
from the water found around the aggregates. Further water is extracted from the cement paste and 
enlarge the ice lenses. When the ice lenses formed melt and leave large openings, the aggregates are 
separated from the surrounding mortar. The second effect of ice formation is related to the stresses 
created by the volume dilation of the ice lenses. The volume expansion of the mixing water leads to 
a fracturing stress. The concrete needs a sufficient amount of strength to resist this stress. If the 
strength is not gained the compressive strength of the concrete decreases. (Yi et al. 2011) According 
to Karagol et al. (2014) “When fresh concrete freezes, the strength of such concrete is lowered by 20-
40%, its resistance to freeze-thaw cycling as given by the durability factor is lowered by 40-60% and 
the bond between reinforcement and concrete is lowered by 70%”.  
On top of the amount of ice lenses, the location of the ice may also be critical. Here the cooling 
rate influence the place where the ice forms and the type of ice that forms. Concrete which freeze 
rapidly after casting forms ice crystals uniformly distributed in the concrete. In concrete frozen slowly 
after casting, the water migrates to the cold surface where it forms as ice lenses, which can disrupt 
the concrete. Either way may damage the concrete but some literature indicates that slow cooling is 
more critical than rapid cooling in some cases. (Korhonen 1990)  
Irreversible damage caused by the formation of ice may be avoided if the concrete has a maturity 
of 15 to 20 hours or a strength of 3.4 MPa before the concrete is exposed to one freeze-thaw cycle 
(Hansen & Pedersen 1999; Korhonen 1990). For multiple freeze-thaw cycles the concrete need a 
strength of 24 MPa before they occur (Chairman 1989). It is, however, very hard to identify when the 
concrete has thawed. The air temperature can be positive while the concrete structure is still frozen. 
To prevent irreversible damage, a number of steps to follow are proposed (Dansk Beton 2011). The 
steps are presented in the following section.

2.2.1 How to mitigate the freezing of concrete at early age 
 
Traditional Danish winter concreting includes following precautions (Dansk Beton 2011): 
 Change thermal boundary conditions by covering the concrete. 
 Change concrete start temperature and/ or mix design by using heated concrete or using rapid 
hardening concrete or reduce water to cement (w/c) ratio. 
 Change “air temperature” by using an external heating source. 
 
Additionally, the following points should be checked on the day of casting: 
 Quality of the concrete 
 Concrete temperature 
 Weather e.g. wind and temperature 
 Possible insulation in place. 
 
In the days after casting, the temperature of the concrete should be documented, and it should be 
ensured that the temperature does not get below 5 ℃. 
Winter conditions increases the costs of the structure. The cost increase is primarily caused by a 
drop in the efficiency of the machinery and manual labour. The machinery costs increase two times 
and the manual labour work takes the twice as long when work is done during winter time. The 
heating requirements and need of protection cause most of the increase. (Korhonen 1990)  
Using antifreeze admixtures, the need for special protection is reduced. The price of the concrete 
mix will raise but it is still economically competitive with heating and protecting concrete (Korhonen 
1990).  
 
2.2 Chemical admixtures 
Chemical admixtures are shorting the project completion time and improving the concrete 
performance. In this context, chemical admixtures are an appellation for chemicals that provides 
protection of plastic concrete. The protection shall ensure durability properties and an adequate 
development of strength. Chemical admixtures are primarily two different substances - 1: accelerators 
and 2: antifreezing admixtures. An accelerator is added to the concrete for the purpose to reduce the 
setting time and accelerating early strength. Accelerating admixtures can be classed into four 
categories (Nmai 1998): 
 
1. 
Calcium chloride 
2. 
Accelerating admixtures containing calcium chloride 
3. 
Non-chloride accelerating admixtures 
4. 
Non-chloride accelerating admixtures for use in subfreezing ambient temperatures 
 
Accelerators increase the hydration rate and reduce the need for the considerations presented in 
subsection 2.1.1 How to mitigate the freezing of concrete at early age. Accelerators falling into the 
fourth category are the admixture for winter concreting. (Nmai 1998) 
==PAGE:17==

Antifreeze admixtures are thought of as chemicals which function in two ways: first “they lower 
the freezing point of the liquid phase of concrete; they are either weak accelerators or retarders of 
cement setting and hardening” and second “they accelerate hydration of cement and also possess 
antifreezing properties” (Ratinov & Rozenberg 1995). In literature, it is possible to find antifreeze 
admixture as an overall term for both accelerators and antifreeze admixtures.  
The main reason for mixing antifreeze admixture with the fresh concrete is to prevent the mixing 
water from freezing, in order for the reaction between water and cement to go on at low temperatures. 
According to Korhonen (1990) “the effectiveness of an antifreeze for reducing the freezing point of 
water is related to its eutectic point”. The freezing-point depression phenomenon is related to the state 
of freezing where the system goes from disordered state to ordered state. This requires a removal of 
energy from the system. An antifreeze admixture has a larger disorder than the mixing water, and 
therefore, more energy needs to be removed from the system to create order compared to the case 
without the antifreeze admixture. Thereby, water containing the antifreeze admixture has a lower 
freezing point than the pure water. (Chang & Goldsby 2014) This means that the eutectic point defines 
the freezing point of the different antifreeze admixtures. The following table shows the eutectic point 
of several chemicals, and at which temperature the freezing point cannot be lowered by adding further 
quantity of antifreeze admixture. (Korhonen 1990)

2.5 Concrete strength 
The strength is the ability of the concrete to resist stress without failure. The strength of concrete 
consists of several types, such as compressive, tensile, flexural, shear and biaxial strength, all of which 
are closely related. The flexural, shear and biaxial strength will not be discussed here. The tensile 
strength is low and therefore often ignored and instead reinforcement steel is used to pick up tensile 
load. On the other hand, the compressive strength is an essential property of concrete and the uniaxial 
compressive strength is often used as a general index of the concrete strength. From this point only 
the uniaxial compressive strength will be defined. 
The compressive strength of concrete can be measured by performing a uniaxial compression test 
defined by e.g. EN 12390-3:2009. In this test, the specimens are compressed and the failure occur 
within 2-3 minutes of progressively increased loading. In reality, the concrete structure is loaded 
differently (Mehta & Monteiro 2014). It is therefore important to know the difference between the 
conditions in the laboratory and the conditions on the construction site and know that the measured 
compressive strength is not necessarily comparable to the compressive strength of the structure on 
the construction site.  
The failure of concrete is related to the porosity and the strength depends on various parameters 
influencing the porosity. The complex web of various parameters that influence the strength of 
concrete is shown in following diagram Fig. 2 (Mehta & Monteiro 2014).

The stress-strain curve shows a linear-elastic behaviour up to about thirty percent of the maximum 
strength. This behaviour is caused by the micro cracks in the interfacial transition zone remain 
undisturbed. The interfacial transition zone is a narrow area around the aggregate particles with a 
higher w/c ratio. The higher w/c ratio is caused by, the cement particles in fresh concrete cannot pack 
as efficiently when they are near a larger solid object – this is also called the wall effect. Above this 
point, the curve gradually curves up to seventy-five to ninety percent of 𝑓𝑐 where the curve becomes 
flat and continue until the specimen fractures. Between thirty and fifty percent of the maximum 
strength the cracks in the interfacial transition zone elongate a little but no cracks occur in the mortar 
matrix. The crack propagation seems stable until here. Between fifty and seventy-five percent of 𝑓𝑐 
the system of cracks become unstable as the cracks in the interfacial transition zone grow again. 
Above seventy-five percent a complete fracture of the test specimen occurs. The reason for this is 
that the cracks between the matrix and the interfacial transition zone are bridging. (Mehta & Monteiro 
2014) 
The cracks in the matrix and the interfacial transition zone joins and a failure surface on the 
specimen develops. The failure surface develops at about twenty to thirty degrees from the direction 
of the load. The failure modes is presented in following Fig. 4 (CEN 2009).

failure modes are formed as an hourglass.

3 Material and methods 
3.1 Overview of experiments 
The practical work in this project concerns three parts: First a semi-adiabatic calorimetry investigating 
the hydration heat of the concrete specimens inspired by NT BUILD 388 (NORDTEST 1992), NT 
BUILD 480 (NORDTEST 1997) and EN-196-9 (CEN 2010). Second, a compressive test of the 
hardened concrete specimen exposed to different environmental temperatures inspired by EN-12390-
3 and carried out based on guidance from the technical personnel at DTU. Third, a adiabatic 
calorimetry investigating the antifreezing admixture’s ability to lower the freezing point.  
Beside the tests mentioned above, the temperature at test setup, the semi-adiabatic calorimetry and 
the temperatures inside the freezer and fridge are registered. A small experiment investigating the 
temperature difference between freezer and inside two specimens with individual concrete mix is also 
conducted.  
 
3.2 Concrete casting 
The properties of concrete mixed with the two different antifreeze admixtures are thoroughly 
investigated by casting the concrete specimens with 19 different concrete mixes. For every concrete 
mix 22 specimens are cast for the uniaxial compression test and 1 for the heat development test. The 
specimens are shaped as cylinders and the testing is carried out on these concrete cylinders. The 
dimensions of the cylinders for uniaxial compression test are 200 mm in height and 100 mm in 
diameter and for the heat development test are 314 mm in height and 134 mm in diameter. The moulds

3.2.2 Casting procedure 
Due to the large amount of concrete to be cast and for the sake of the heat development test procedure, 
the specimen is cast following the schedule shown in Appendix 3.  
The moulds for the specimen used in the uniaxial compression test are kept in the climate 
controlled room in the foundry. This room is used for another project but is kept at temperatures 
below 0℃.  This ensure that the moulds are cold and that the climatic chambers available for this 
project do not need to cool the moulds down as well as the concrete.  
The temperature of every constituent (cement, sand and aggregates) is measured once using a 
liquid thermometer once assuming the temperature of these is the same through the period of mixing. 
The aggregates, sand and cement, is stored in silos or boxes in the foundry. The tap water is measure 
once per mix also with a liquid thermometer. The water at 20℃ is taken directly from the tap in the 
foundry. The water at 5℃ is stored in the climate room in the foundry where it freezes and then the 
water is melted. The melted water is approximately 5℃. The temperatures are used for calculation of 
the starting temperature of the concrete mix which is calculated by dividing the total heat storage with 
the heat capacity of concrete. This is described in following equation.

holding concrete is put into the semi-adiabatic calorimeter and stays there until the measurements are

3.4 Temperature control 
The climate rooms available to store specimens for this project are a climatic chamber and several 
chest freezers. The climatic chamber is the model Skanfrys which was originally used for measuring 
displacement of salty porous materials during varying relative humidity. The equipment for 
measuring the displacements gently removed and the climatic chamber is only used for controlling 
the temperature at +5℃. The control of the climatic chamber and the appertaining controlling 
programme is carried out as prescribed in the manual for Skanfrys (Nielsen 1992). The documentation 
for the temperature during the storage of specimen is attached in Appendix 5.  
The chest freezer is controlled using a simpler method. The temperature of the chest freezers can 
be controlled by a knob on the side of the freezer. The knob is set to the minimum which makes the 
chest freezer cool as little as possible. A chest freezer is normally programmed to freeze the content 
to −18℃ and the knob on the side is set depending on the amount of food placed for freezing. 
Therefore, the freezer will try to achieve −18℃ even at this setting. Working against this, the chest 
freezer is connected to a turn-off timer, which allows the freezer to be turned on in 15 minute cycles 
followed by a period of 30 minutes where the freezer is shut down. These cycles ensure a mean 
temperature inside the chest freezer at approximately −10℃. The documentation of the temperature 
during the storage period is done using a small datalogger, Tiny Tag, which is shown in Fig. 7.

5 Discussion 
 
5.1 Concrete casting 
 
5.1.1 Antifreeze admixture  
The producers of the studied antifreeze admixture are both giving a maximum value of the content of 
alkali. This is important in cases with increased risk of alkali-silica reactions, which involve alkali 
ions and certain reactive siliceous minerals. The given maximum value is given to be on the safe side. 
The calculated content of NaNO3 in both antifreeze admixtures is based on the maximum value of 
alkali, which may not be that high. Calculating the content of alkali based on the given dry matter 
content, the content of alkali is 15 and 14 % in Antifreeze N and MasterSet-AC respectively. Trusting 
the given dry matter content, the content of NaNO3 is almost the same in both antifreeze admixture, 
which is 42 and 40 % in Antifreeze N and MasterSet-AC respectively. 
This project discusses antifreeze admixtures which are commercially available products on the 
Danish market. The selection of doses is based on the recommendations on the product data sheets 
and the Norwegian guideline “Vintervejlederen” (Kristiansen et al. 2015). The data sheet for 
Antifreeze N and the documentation of Antifreeze N Powder in “Vintervejlederen” are not close. The 
chosen doses of Antifreeze N are close to the doses documented as suitable for −5℃ and −15℃ in 
“Vintervejlederen”. The choice of these doses is based on the temperature in the chest freezer aiming 
at −10℃. Since the documented admixture is in powder form, the given dry matter content is used to 
calculate the final amounts of admixture. This is done with the conviction that the powder is the 
amount of dry matter content in Antifreeze N. This may be not the case. Dissolving Antifreeze N 
Powder with e.g. water does not necessarily result in water and a dry matter content. Thereby, the 
choice of doses of Antifreeze N may be too high. The advised doses of Antifreeze N in the data sheets 
is 4 and 10 % of the cement weight which is much lower doses than used.  The doses of BASF’s 
products follows their advised doses at air temperatures −5℃ and −15℃ (BASF 2015).  
 
5.1.2 Start temperature of the concrete mixes 
The temperature difference is calculated to be 5 ℃ which is only a small temperature difference. 
Creating a larger temperature difference could have been achieved by warming or cooling all 
constituents or by using warmer water in the mixes with the high water temperature. A larger 
temperature difference would also assumingly vary the results depending on the starting temperature. 
The little influence of starting temperature can be seen in the results from testing the heat development 
and compressive strength. During the casting of samples containing Antifreeze N, the admixture was 
not cooled down to 5℃ as the water was. Therefore, the total heat of liquids has been larger in these 
mixes. The MasterSet-AC and the accelerator MasterX-Seed is kept in the climatic chamber at 5℃ 
until using it in the mixes with low liquid temperature. This change in procedure is chosen to be okay 
when the procedure is the same for all mixes containing Antifreeze N and MasterSet-AC seperately.

5.1.3 The bearing of the difference in slump measurements 
The slumps vary between 250 and 50 mm with no visible pattern except the needed superplasticising 
admixture is less for mixes containing antifreezing admixtures, compared to mixes without 
antifreezing admixture. The plasticizing effect of other chemicals used as antifreeze admixtures is 
described in literature. “Antifreezing admixtures, especially the calcium salts, behave as weak 
plasticizer of concrete mixes” (Ratinov & Rozenberg 1995). The effect of chemical admixtures on 
the workability is not examined in this project but the variation of slumps indicates an effect of 
antifreeze admixtures.  
 
5.1.4 Change in transportation time 
The transportation time between the climate controlled environment and measuring site is important 
to know as the procedure is aiming at being the same for every mix. It is important to keep the period 
prior to placing the specimen in the hay box the same, or to know the difference between samples. 
This is the only way for the samples to be comparable. In addition, the longer the period where the 
specimens are stored outside the chest freezer and climatic chamber, the higher rate of hydration 
resulting in higher compressive strength. The transportation time was decreased when possible for 
practically reasons. The transportation time to the hay boxes stays approximately the same through 
the period of casting and therefore, the results are not edited in correlation to this. The change in 
transportation time to the chest freezer may have had a small impact on the compressive strengths.  
The total effect of the reduced transportation time may be little as the time difference is small 
compared to the entire length of the experiment.

5.3 Evaluation of the compressive strength 
The compressive strength of the specimen was calculated as defined in EN 12390-3 (CEN 2009). In 
processing the results, several reservations are made to avoid errors. The number of samples for each 
measuring point was increased to the largest possible depending on the space in the climatic chamber 
and chest freezers. The specimens of one concrete mix was distributed over 3 and in some cases 2 
different chest freezers and the specimens tested on the same day was coming from at least 2 different 
chest freezers, to minimize temperature changes in the chest freezers. All specimens are ejected from 
the mould 24 hours after casting and placed in a plastic foil bag of approximately the same size for 
each sample. The stagnant air inside the bag may perform as an insulation layer, and the bags were 
made as small and identical as possible. Due to shortage of the plastic foil available, some bags were 
reused. Reuse of the bags was equally distributed over all mixes.

5.4 Estimation of the freezing point 
The measurements using the micro-calorimeter show a number of things deviating from 
measurements presented in literatures. The freezing point of clean water is measured to be at block 
temperature (see “Procedure for the Micro calorimeter”) of −9.76℃ which is rather low temperature. 
The freezing point of water is referred to in literature to be 0℃ (Chang & Goldsby 2014). The low 
calculated value may be caused by the ability of transferring the cold to the sample. The transfer may 
not be good because the samples are not fitted closely to the metal container. Even correcting the 
measurements to having first peak of clean water at 0°C, the graph still shows two peaks, which is 
strange. The size and number of peaks is related to pores, which is not present in the samples, as the 
samples contain only liquid (Fontenay & Sellevold 1980). Looking at the clean water sample, it is 
expected for all the water to freeze at one temperature, as no substance affecting the freezing 
temperature is dissolved in the water. In addition, the fact that the first peak is very small compared 
to peak number 2, this appears also deviates from the expectations. The expectation for the clean 
water sample is 1 peak at a temperature close to 0℃ . Looking at, the sample with water and Antifreeze 
N the first peak is wide which may be correlated to the antifreeze admixture. When the water starts 
freezing, then the concentration of antifreeze admixture increases in the remaining liquid. The 
freezing of the sample containing water and Antifreeze N will therefore happen over range of 
temperature.  
The temperature difference between the freezing point of the two samples is different for the 
measured and the calculated values. The difference is 7.8℃ for the measured freezing points, which 
is twice the difference for the calculated freezing point. The antifreeze admixture’s effect on the 
freezing point of water was investigated by Sakai et al (1991). They found a temperature difference 
between zero and maximum added antifreeze admixture to be 3 to 4 ℃. According to measurements 
by Fontenay and Sellevold (1980), the heating of samples only gives rise to one peak on the graph. I 
heated the samples to 5℃ after measurement and this was not warm enough to melt the samples. The 
difference in between the melting points of the two samples may have been interesting to compare to 
the difference between the freezing points.

6 Conclusion 
 
Winter concreting needs special arrangements, all of which affects the curing process to a smaller or 
larger degree. It has successfully been proved that antifreeze admixtures have an effect on the heat 
development and compressive strength development of concrete has successfully been proved. 
Different admixture and dosage combinations have been investigated. Based on the experimental 
work and the discussion of the results, the following conclusions have been reached. 
The study of antifreeze admixtures in experimental work has been interesting as the properties of 
concrete containing antifreeze admixture have been found possible to visualize using simple test 
setups: Semi-adiabatic calorimeter, compression testing machine and micro-calorimeter, which made 
it possible to visualize and document the heat development of concrete, compressive strength of 
concrete and freezing point of water containing antifreeze.  
Both manufacturer’s products, Antifreeze N and MasterSet-AC and MasterX-Seed, showed an 
improvement of the hydration process of concrete. The investigation of the heat development showed 
a faster increase when using antifreeze admixture. The best mix design with the best performance 
was R-B8-20 while the worst was the reference sample L-Ref-20. Generally speaking, using the 
products from BASF gives the best performance for both Rapid and Low-alkali cement. This may be 
caused by the use of a combination of two different chemical admixtures as recommended by the 
manufacturer. The heat- and strength development is connected, even though no direct correlation 
was shown for the different mix designs. At 5℃, the highest compressive strength was obtained for 
R-B8-20 and the lowest was for L-B7-5, while at −10℃ the highest compressive strength was 
obtained for R-M7-20 and the lowest for L-M4-20, all exposed for 28 days. Overall, the highest 
compressive strength was obtained for the samples containing BASF’s products exposed to 5℃ and 
for the samples with MAPEI’s products exposed to −10℃ at 28 days, which may be caused by a 
shutdown of the chest freezers (see discussion). When looking at the compressive strength after 3 
days, the results are mixed, with no clear favorite amongst the two manufacturers. The antifreeze 
admixture’s ability to decrease the freezing point of water was clearly seen in both the measured and 
calculated result. The two types of results were not close, as would have been expected and further 
experiments may be needed to define the freezing point of water mixed with Antifreeze N. The 
measured difference between the freezing point of water and the freezing point of water mixed with 
Antifreeze N is 7.8℃ and the calculated difference is 3.9℃  The investigations of the antifreeze 
products align with literature investigating other chemicals used as antifreeze admixture and magnify 
the understanding of these products and supports the effects described by the manufacturers.  
Computational simulation of heat development is using the parameters described in the 
mathematical equation for the heat development. The parameters may be taken either from the given 
constant values from the cement manufacturers or from experimental results as in this project. Using 
the suggested parameters in simulated heat developments may give results which are not conservative 
or too conservative. This was examined doing a comparison between the suggested parameters and 
the measured which is shown in Fig. 34 and Fig. 35. This study illustrated, the suggested heat 
development was too conservative from 8 mh until 50 mh for Rapid cement and from 4 mh until 45 
mh for Low-alkali cement. In addition, the suggested heat development was not conservative when 
the heat development approximated 𝑄𝑚𝑎𝑥 during the large increase of heat compared to heat 
==PAGE:70==
| 62 
 
 
 
development of concrete containing antifreeze admixture. The used computational simulation may 
therefore be adjusted such as the suggested values for the parameters depend not only on the values 
for the cement but also for the chemical admixtures. The investigated heat development shows; the 
dependency of cement type on the parameters are large. Disregarding 𝑄∞ the average values of the 
parameter 𝜏𝑒 and 𝛼 is 8.41 hours and 1.31 for Rapid cement and 11.01 hours and 0.84 for Low-alkali 
cement. The effect of antifreeze admixtures on the parameters is also visible. For Rapid cement, the 
largest difference between the highest and lowest 𝜏𝑒 is 3.17 hours and 𝛼 is 0.28, and for Low-alkali 
cement, the largest difference between the highest and lowest 𝜏𝑒 11.89 hours and 𝛼 is 0.28, all 
antifreeze admixture taken into account.  
Antifreeze admixture is not a current standard in winter concreting in Denmark today as it is in 
e.g. Norway (Kristiansen et al. 2015). Based on this study the antifreeze admixture may be beneficial 
for certain construction projects during winter time in Denmark. Further study based on experimental 
work in the laboratory and especially in the field may be needed to introduce antifreeze admixture in 
e.g. the Danish guide for winter concreting SBI 125 “Vinterstøbning af beton” and Dansk Beton 
“Vinterstøbning” (Hansen & Pedersen 1999; Dansk Beton 2011).

1 Scope and field of application 
 
This method is used to determine the amount of heat developed during the hardening process of the 
concrete. The test procedure is inspired by NT BUILD 388 and NT BUILD 480. The heat of hydration 
is described as the produced energy as function of the maturity. The definition of the heat 
development is based on the knowledge of the heat capacity of the individual components in the 
concrete. 
The information obtained in this test method can be used to predict the heat development and 
temperature distribution in a structure where concrete hardening is taken place.  
 
2 Definitions 
Semi-adiabatic calorimetry:  
Determination of the temperature development in a specimen, where heat loss to the surroundings is 
possible and is taken into account. In this method the test specimen consists of concrete casted into a 
thin metal cylinder box.  
Semi-adiabatic calorimeter: 
Equipment which is also called a hay box, which is an insulated metal drum equipped with devices 
to determine the temperature in the concrete and the heat loss through the insulation of the box. The 
correlation between a recorded signal and the heat loss is defined by the calibration factor. See test 
procedure 
Transducer: 
A system of serial connected thermos elements that generate a voltage proportional to temperature 
difference over a section of insulation with a specified thickness.

3 Sampling 
A thin-walled metal cylindrical container is chosen according to fit in the hay box. The container 
may contain around 5 liters. The net weight of the fresh concrete sample, placed and compacted in 
the metal container, shall be determined within +/- 1% by weighing the container with and without 
concrete. The container is closed with a lid with a drilled hole in the center (in which the device 
determining the accumulated heat is placed). A proper drill is 10 mm in diameter.  
 
4 Test method 
4.1 Test setup 
The filled metal container is closed with the lid with the drilled hole where the temperature transmitter 
is placed. The metal container is gently placed in the hay box, which is shown in Fig. 1.

temperature increase in the concrete is used as a measure of the accumulated heat.

4.3 Calibration of the calorimeter 
The calibration of the Semi-adiabatic calorimeter is carried out with use of water. The thin-walled 
metal container is filled with water with temperature of approximately 40 degrees and is thereafter 
placed in the calorimeter. The metal container is closed with the lid with the drilled hole in the center. 
The temperature transmitter is placed in the drilled hole and the wire is connected to the hay box 
shown in Fig. 1. 
The transducer output, the temperature of the laboratory and the temperature of the water are recorded 
once per 15 minutes during 3 days.
