Abstract
The wake of a scaled wind turbine rotor is measured in a wind tunnel experiment in two diﬀerent
turbulence conditions. The experiment is validated with a 1-D momentum method Actuator Disc
model using URANS Segregated Solver. The characteristics of the wake of the ADM wake present
better results in lower turbulent conditions, and lower degree of accuracy in the high turbulent
conditions. The latest simulations, run with a Coupled Solver and using a lower value for the SST
closure constant Cµ (0.03) showed a better matching of the results, and sensitivity studies are to
be taken into account for future work. Furhter analysis are made on the ADM using the DTU
10MW rotor through a comparison with the CAD geometry simulation in absence of wind shear.
Results show high accuracy in the far wake although an underestimation of the wake deﬁcit of up
to 20% in until 7R distance from the rotor. The diﬀerent behaviour in the close wake is addressed
to the absence of the vortex structures in the ADM, which applies constant body forces on the
whole rotor disc and doesn’t present tip corrections.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Over the last decades, the reliance on wind energy as a source of electricity has risen sensitively:
due to government regulations and incentives, onshore wind power has become the cheapest source
of electricity. Technology and development done in composite materials and aeroelasticity of the
structures allowed the development of wind turbines generating up to 10MW power.
One of
the problems of wind farms that engineers and researchers are dealing with is the wake of wind
turbines. Indeed, the wake is characterized by lower wind velocity, also called wind velocity deﬁcit,
and higher vorticity caused by the presence of the blades and the whole wind turbine structure.
The velocity deﬁcit and turbulence in the wind turbine wakes are respectively the reason why in
wind parks the turbines are placed at a distance which ranges typically from 3 to 10 rotor diameters
[13] between each other. Such layout of wind park is designed by engineers so that turbines are
not standing in other turbines wakes. Smaller distances would result in a decrease of the overall
park eﬃciency and limited production of energy, but also in a decrease in the life of the rotors due
to problematic fatigue loading. Indeed, turbulence in the wakes creates dangerous oscillations in
the turbine structures leading to fatigue increases that in some extreme cases are up to 80% [11].
Therefore, there is an increasing interest in studying rotor wakes and their interaction in wind
farms. The ultimate goal is the best possible exploitation of the limited sites available for wind
energy production.
Although there is plenty of theoretical and numerical investigations, there is an increasing demand
for experiments testing the wake of wind turbines, for understanding what are the relations that
determine its characteristics and what are the mutual inﬂuences of wind turbines, also positioned
in diﬀerent arrangements. Experimental data on ﬁeld is very complicated to obtain because of
the diﬃculty of generating perfectly reproducible results due to, among other things, inconstant
natural atmospheric conditions. In this regard, laboratory experiments using small models of the
rotors operating in wind tunnels or water ﬂumes have become an important alternative to ﬁeld
measurements.
1
==PAGE:14==
On the other hand, technology is becoming more and more essential for research and for gaining
knowledge on the matter. Computers’ processing power has increased, bringing new engineering
techniques and CFD is becoming one of the leading sectors in the ﬁeld of aerodynamics. Indeed,
CAD simulation are excellent for performing highly accurate aeroelastic analysis for rotors or tur-
bines, and for large scale simulations, mathematical models like Actuator Disc Model or Actuator
Line Model can be accurate models for understanding the interaction between diﬀerent turbines
in a site.
One of the present relevant concern is the study of the tip vortices which are a problem in many
types of rotor.
Tip vortices are created by the diﬀerence of pressure on the two sides of the
rotating blade, the upwind side (high pressure) and the downwind side (low pressure). It creates
a radial ﬂow along the blade, towards the tip in the high pressure side and away from it in the
low pressure side. This ﬂow is maximum close to the tip and it is unwanted because it deviates
the ﬂow from being perfectly tangential to the blade. It tends to balance the pressure diﬀerence
on the two sides without generation of torque on the blade, causing loss of energy and the above
mentioned vortices in the near wake. Tip vortices are relevant for the wake length because they
create a boundary layer which limits the momentum exchange between the outside and the inside
of the wake and therefore are responsible for the slow recovery of the wind speed. It is possible
that, with a detailed understanding of the behaviour of the wake through experiments and the
employment of high resolution numerical techniques, in the future we will ﬁnd solutions to increase
the velocity deﬁcit recovery rate. This would lead to less sparse and more energy dense wind
farms. Computational Fluid Dynamics is employed at the moment for the study of blade add-
ons like winglets, spoilers or vortex generators with promising results for improving overall blades
performances and might be common in future generation wind turbines.
The present work illustrates the wind tunnel experiments done for the analysis of the wake behind
a scaled wind rotor. This experiments are the follow up of other experiments done in 2005 [14].
Moreover in this study a validation of the experiment through numerical simulation is performed.
This study is going to be followed by further studies focusing on the wake meandering and additional
computational analysis testing diﬀerent solvers and turbulence models.
1.2
Project Structure
The content of the work presented is divided in two main parts:
1. Experimental work
The ﬁrst part describes the experimental work done in the Research Institute of Applied
Mechanics, at the University of Kyushu, Fukuoka. It consists of wind tunnel measurements
of the wake of a MEL rotor. The study takes into account the wake generated by the scale
rotor measured at diﬀerent probe lines perpendicular to the ﬂow, in the near wake, 0.5D and
4D, and in the far wake, 6D, 8D and 10D. The experiments are assessing also the behaviour
of the wind turbine (CP and Torque) and the ﬂuctuation of the wind speed at the boundary
of the wake at 0.5D. The ﬂow has been sampled with a singular hot wire y-probe. The study
is done in order to assess the behaviour of the wake in smooth and turbulent conditions and
see what is the eﬀect of the turbulence on the wind deﬁcit and wake recovery. It is followed
2
==PAGE:15==
with a CFD validation done using the 1-D momentum method ADM (called also Virtual
Disc in the descriptions) of the commercial code Star-CCM+.
2. Comparison of the 1-D momentum method ADM with other models
The second stage covers the comparison analysis of the Virtual Disc on the DTU 10MW
Wind Turbine. For this last model, the performance of the ADM is going to be compared
with the CAD geometry of the rotor in NoWS (No Wind Shear) conditions. The simulation
is also showing a comparison with the new ADM developed by Sørensen in previous studies
[12]. Such model is presented only as a comparison for the eﬀect of the ABL on the wake
development.
1.3
Project Objective
The research objectives that this project is trying to reach are:
1. Description of the inﬂuence of turbulence on the acceleration of the wake and
dissolution of the tip vortex structure
The experimental investigation focuses, among other things, on the tip vortex development
and the relation between their dissolution and the acceleration of the wake of the turbine.
2. Investigations of tip vortices and other wind speed ﬂuctuations in the wake
boundary in time and frequency domain
The study carries an investigation in time and frequency domain of the velocity ﬂuctuations
found at the wake boundary at 0.5 diameters behind the rotor. The study examines the
frequency and magnitude of the ﬂuctuations in high and low turbulence conditions.
3. Investigations of the eﬀect of the tower on the horizontal, vertical and azimuthal
wake velocity distributions
The simulated results are going to be used also to obtain information on the wake rotation,
and to investigate on the asymmetry of the wake.
4. Investigation on the degree of accuracy of the 1-D momentum method ADM for
the representation of the near and far wake of a rotor
The 1-D momentum method actuator disc is very simple to apply because it only requires
knowing the diameter of a rotor, the Thrust and Power coeﬃcient curve. Information on the
airfoil geometry are not needed. In this study the ADM’s degree of accuracy for the near
and far wake is compared with the one of a simulation using a CAD geometry.

Chapter 2
Related Theory
2.1
Theory of the wake
A general review of wind turbine aerodynamics is given by Sanderse [1]. In his article "Aerody-
namics of Wind turbine wakes" it is described that the wake behind a HAWT is rotating and
surrounded by a highly turbulent shear layer as a transition zone to the undisturbed ﬂuid. The
shear contains also the the tip vortices created by the downwash eﬀect, described previously, at
the tip of the blade due to the diﬀerence in the pressure on the surfaces of the airfoils. The shear
layer is caused by the diﬀerence in velocity between the inner and outer region of the wake and
it contains the formation of turbulent eddies while it thickens moving downstream. The lowest
velocity in the wake is reached around one to two diameters downstream of the rotor and lies in
the near wake region, whose ﬂow ﬁeld is determined mostly by the turbine’s geometry. This region
is between two and ﬁve diameters, ending when the shear layer expands from the wake’s boundary
to its center. The tip vortices will fade away roughly at 4D. The wake expands because of the mo-
mentum exchange between the turbulent and undisturbed ﬂuid. There is empirical evidence that
in presence of atmospheric shear layer [5] the maximum velocity deﬁcit point tends to shift below
the rotor axis resulting in an asymmetric distribution of atmospheric turbulence. This distribution

has lower turbulence level closer to the ground. Another study reveals that the wind tower eﬀect
might create asymmetry also horizontally, moving the center on the side of the wind turbine axis

Indeed higher turbulence can lead to an expansion region length of only 1D (instead of roughly
2D) and to sensitively shorter wakes. Other factors aﬀecting the development of the wake are the
surface constraint eﬀect and wind shear eﬀects: this eﬀects are very relevant to our experiment
because it will be done in presence of asymmetric wind shear and most importantly the wall eﬀects
will inﬂuence our results, although only marginally.
2.2
One-Dimensional Momentum Theory
In this section the One-Dimensional Momentum Theory is brieﬂy introduced. The ﬁnal equations
derived in this descriptions are going to form the source terms in the Virtual Disc implementation
used for this study. It is the ﬁrst mathematical model used to describe the aerodynamic of wind
turbines and their general behaviour. In this model the rotor is conceived as a permeable, ideal and
friction-less disc which applies a force on the ﬂow of air passing through it. By itself the disc does
not apply a rotational velocity to the wake, therefore the latter is modelled separately, for example

drop, and the pressure gradually recovers to the atmospheric value p −0 further downwind. The

2.4
CFD modelling and simulation
In this Chapter there is a general introduction to part of the theory behind CFD simulations and
the methods used for this project. Computational Fluid Dynamics is used extensively to study ﬂow
behaviour for diﬀerent applications. In wind energy engineering CFD is used, among other things,
to see wind turbines’ wakes interactions in wind farms. Such big scale simulations can include
diﬀerent details like topography and atmospheric conditions and would otherwise be very diﬃcult
to study with experiments in wind tunnels. Due to the very heavy computational capacity needed
wind farms are mainly modelled using simpliﬁed mathematical models such as the Actuator Disc
Model or Actuator line Model.
Numerical Analyses are also implemented to study the turbulence behind a single wind turbine, to
perform blade design, and to optimize blades and their characteristics both from an aerodynamic
but also aeroelastic and structural point of view.
As mentioned in the project objective section, in this project the study of the wake is performed
from diﬀerent perspective, comparing the accuracy of the built-in 1-D momentum method Virtual
Disc function of STAR CCM+ with the CAD geometry simulation.

==PAGE:25==
2.4.1
Fundamental equations
STAR CCM+ models a diﬀerent physics phenomena that range from ﬂuid mechanics to chemical
reactions. For aerodynamics applications the basic fundamental laws that govern the mechanics of
ﬂuids, solids and their interaction are:
1) Continuity equation:
Continuity equation expresses the mass conservation across a control volume. It can be expressed
as:
∂ρ
∂t + ∇.(ρv) = 0
(2.39)
with ρ equal to density and v equal to the ﬂuid velocity.
2)Linear Momentum Conservation:
∂ρv
∂t + ∇.(ρv ⊗v) = ∇σ + fb
(2.40)
This law expresses that the variation in time of the linear momentum is deﬁned by the forces
acting on the continuum. In this equation ⊗indicates a vectorial product, fb is the resultant of
all the forces acting on the continuum per unit volume, such as gravity and centrifugal forces and
σ denotes the stress tensor.
3) Angular Momentum Conservation:
σ = σT
(2.41)
This law imposes the symmetry of the stress tensor.
4) Energy Conservation:
Lastly, the energy conservation of energy law:
∂(ρE)
∂t
+ ∇.(ρEv) = fb.v + ∇.(v.σ) −∇.q + SE
(2.42)
In this equation E is the total energy per unit mass, q is the exchange of heat with the boundaries,
and SE denotes an energy source per unit volume.
These equations alone are not enough to solve the PDE system and deﬁne results, as the number
of unknown quantities are bigger than the equations. For this reason there are other equations or
laws that need to be added such as boundary conditions, initial conditions, constitutive equations.
Moreover, these represent just the general equations, which will be then modiﬁed, since diﬀerent
cases would necessitate diﬀerent assumptions.
In CFD discretization methods convert equations in the continuum, which would be diﬃcult to
solve, into discrete algebraic equations, which are easier to solve numerically. The ﬁrst step of
the process of the discretization is the division of the domain, which in this case is the space
13
==PAGE:26==
in which the ﬂow of air is passing into a ﬁnite number of cell, which constitutes the mesh. All
the values calculated numerically are therefore contained in a particular position inside the cells,
depending on the application and the design of the problem. It could be for example vertices, cells
or centroids. In these locations an integral or weak form of the diﬀerential equations is employed
for spatial discretization.
After discretizing the time derivative, a coupled system of algebraic
equations (nonlinear, in general) is the result that needs to be solved at each time-step. Depending
on the model, the software uses the Finite Volume or Finite Element method for the discretization
of such continuous equations.
2.5
Time discretization
As previously introduced, in unsteady simulations, together with the three spatial variables there is
the time variable. Therefore, particles will move in space and time. Therefore the above mentioned
equations have to be calculated together in every deﬁned time step. The time step is the numerical
discretization of time. Every timestep includes diﬀerent iteration in which the values are calculated
using mathematical schemes in order to give accurate enough values of the calculated unknowns.
The errors in the calculations are stored in the residuals, and generally the more the iterations per
time-step and the ﬁner the timesteps, the more accurate is the solution. The minimal threshold
for choosing small time-steps, and therefore to reach high accuracy solutions, is the computational
cost of the simulation and therefore the duration of the simulation. On the other hand, the upper
limit to the choice of the time-step is the Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy condition, which is theoretically
necessary to the the convergence of the residuals. This condition can be explained by the Courant
number which generally has to be smaller or equal to 1.
c = uδt
δx < 1
(2.43)
In this equation u is the ﬂow velocity, δt and δx are respectively the dimensions of the time and
spatial interval. This conditions states that the change of position for a generic particle uδt has to
be smaller than the mesh dimension δx. It is important to notice that the CFL condition is just a
general rule, which is not always followed in practical applications.
2.6
Turbulence Model
In the analysis of ﬂuid dynamics there are two main diﬀerent types of ﬂows: laminar and turbulent
ﬂows. The Reynolds number, deﬁned as the rate between inertial and viscous forces is something
that can help understanding the diﬀerences of such behaviour. It can be written as:
Re = ρUL
µ
(2.44)
where ρ is the density of the ﬂuid, µ is the dynamic viscosity, L is the characteristic length of the
geometry in analysis.
14
==PAGE:27==
For low Reynolds number the ﬂow is laminar but when it increases it becomes turbulent. Turbu-
lence is characterized with ﬂuctuation in time of the velocity, and the streamlines are characterized
by vorticity. In order to implement this in the RANS equations some mathematical models have
been developed: some very common models are the Spalart-Allmaras, k−ϵ, k−ω. In the following
simulations the model used is the k −ωSST. The model was developed by Menter [8] and it is an
improvement of the original k −ω. The model adds 2 equations to the RANS equations. These are
the speciﬁc rate of dissipation and the turbulent kinetic energy. This model guarantees very good
results in cases in which the ﬂow presents boundary ﬂow separation and pressure oscillations.

4.1
General set-up
In this project all the cases are run using Unsteady RANS k −ωSST turbulence model with
Segregated ﬂow approach. The time integration scheme chosen is the implicit unsteady, for its
capability of handling higher Courant Number compared to explicit methods, with ﬁrst order time
discretization. [6],[9] . The simulation is done with air taken as ideal, incompressible gas at ambient
temperature conditions for density and dynamic viscosity.
For the simulations done in open space, the domain’s dimension has to be big enough to avoid the
results to be inﬂuenced by its boundaries. In this project the dimensions, and other characteristics
like the placement of the turbine are chosen following the work previously done at DTU in the same
department on the same software,[7]. The position of the geometry in the domain has to be done
always following some important considerations like the presence of Wind Shear, the induction
zone of the turbine and the length of the wake. The decisions taken are explained in the following
sections for the diﬀerent cases.
4.1.1
Mesh
The choice done for the meshing in each of the cases is the hexahedral isotropic trimmer mesher of
STAR-CCM+. The trimmer mesher results to be an optimal solution, very low in computational
complexity to generate and it can also successfully reproduce complex geometries. Because of the
23

domain dimensions for both the simulations, the mesh is nested, having concentric volume controls
that reﬁne the mesh. The result is a very ﬁne mesh close to the geometry, where it is important
to reproduce the details of the turbulent eddies in high accuracy, and quite coarse outside of the
area of interest, where lower levels of accuracy are permitted.
4.1.2
Boundary Conditions
In order to successfully represent the behaviour of the analyzed turbines in the diﬀerent simulations,
the correct boundary conditions are to be implemented. In this next chapter a description of the
boundary conditions used in the simulations is given:
1) Velocity Inlet: Velocity inlet is used to deﬁne the condition of ﬂuid entrance from the boundary,
specifying the direction and the velocity magnitude of the ﬂuid. Such condition is used in all the
simulation performed from the boundary in front of the rotor.
2) Pressure Outlet: It is the condition generally used to impose a speciﬁc pressure exiting the
domain from such boundary.
3) Slip Wall: This condition is applied to the walls at the sides of the domains both in the Virtual
Disc simulation and on the sides of the cylindrical domain. This condition describes a wall, but
does not apply any friction, and therefore any boundary layer, to the ﬂuid.
4) No-Slip Wall: This other boundary condition is used instead for modelling any boundary which
presents friction or any other object inside the domain. In this analysis the condition is used for
the wind turbine blades.

5.1.1
Velocity proﬁle without grid
As previously mentioned, the second part of the experiment consist with the measurement of the
wind velocity proﬁle of the wake at diﬀerent downwind distances both in the near wake (0.5D,4D)
and in the far wake (6D, 8D, 10D). All the measurements are made at the rotor axis height, with
samples distributed on an horizontal line perpendicular to the air ﬂow. In Figure 5.3 the near and
far wake velocity proﬁles measured by the probe in steady condition are illustrated in a scatter
plot. On the y axis there is the non-dimensional distance y/D and on the x axis there the measured
speed over the initial speed of 8m/s.
The 0.5D velocity proﬁle is shown in green, in the picture. The points of the samples are connected
for clarity. The 0.5D proﬁle is the only one which was not measured on both sides of the turbine
for simplicity. In the picture it is possible to observe that the 0.5D wake proﬁle is aﬀected by two
main phenomena: On one hand the wake from the hub and wind shear from the 50mm diameter
shaft and on the other hand the tip vortices. For the ﬁrst phenomenon it is worth mentioning that
the hub is not provided with a spinner but consists solely of a rotor disk with a diameter of 130mm
that due to the non-aerodynamic geometry is creating a bluﬀbody wake. The wake from the disk
aﬀects the wind speed that recovers gradually to 0.8Ui at the radial distance of 0.2D. Beyond 0.2D
the velocity starts to drop gradually due to the tip vortices. The lowest velocity due to the tip
vortices is reached around 0.4D where the velocity reaches 0.68Ui. At the tip radius the graph
show a steep, full recovery of the initial velocity without a parabolic shear distribution.
From the 4D curve it is possible to observe other phenomena: The velocity is generally slower than
the one at 0.5D. The wake is diﬀusing to larger radii creating lower velocity gradients. The diﬀusion
allows the wake to have momentum exchange which increases the wake recovery. Considering the
4D curve it is possible to see that the wake develops asymmetrically, balancing its center 0.2D on

Chapter 7
Conclusions
In the current study, the wake proﬁle of a MEL scaled rotor was measured in a wind tunnel for
both high and low turbulence ambient condition. The results of the simulations were validated
with a CFD simulation with the commercial code STAR-CCM+ using the built-in ADM based
on the 1-D momentum method with rotational forces included. The experimental results show a
good agreement with the empirical evidence and previous studies(see e.g.[11]): the wake velocity at
0.5D resulted generally lower in higher turbulent condition (Ti=15%), but developed faster due to
better turbulent mixing and momentum exchange at the wake boundary in presence of turbulence,
resulting in a shorter wake and up to 5% higher velocity at 10D. The results showed walls blockage
eﬀects, resulting in a maximum 10% higher velocity outside the wake, and a sensitive a-symmetry
of the wake.
The validation of the experimental results showed generally a better agreement with the simulations
in the low turbulence scenario than in the high turbulence one, but they all show a better wake
expansion in the experimental results. In the latest results, the use of the coupled solver with
Cµ=0.03 showed some partial improvements compared to the segregated solver done with Cµ=0.09
for which further studies are to be made.
The analysis on the azimuthal velocity showed an
interaction between the tower and the wake rotation, creating asymmetry conﬁrming the results
of Blomhoﬀ[3]. The diﬀerence in shape complexity, the bigger distance and dimension of the of
the structure holding the turbine is explanation given for the higher asymmetry of the wake in the
experimental results.
The model was further tested with comparisons on the DTU 10MW reference wind turbine. This
last comparison is done in absence of ABL with a simulation on the same software using the CAD
geometry of the same turbine. The results are compared with a simulation done by Sørensen in
Ellypsis3D with wind shear using the new model developed in [12] to understand the inﬂuence
of the wind shear on the wake development. The ADM model showed a limited accuracy in the
representation of the complex behaviour of the far wake, presenting an underestimation of the
normalized velocity deﬁcit up to 20% at 2R and 7R distances from the rotor. The same trend is
seen for the azimuthal velocity in the comparison with the CAD model. On the far wake, instead
49
==PAGE:62==
it showed instead a quite accurate prediction of the proﬁles.
In conclusions, in terms of computational resources, compared to the very simpliﬁed CAD geometry
simulation (only blades, rotating mesh, NoWS), it results in a reduction of the mesh cells of over
1/5. The ADM is therefore a good compromise for simulating large scale wind farms.
7.1
Future work
1. Additional investigation on the experimental validaion
The results are obtained mostly with the implicit URANS Segregated ﬂow solver and without
changing the K-Omega SST parameters, but the latest simulations done with Coupled Solver
and lowering Cµ to 0.03 showed better results. The results could be investigated with a
sensitivity analysis to understand which of the changes done plays a major role in the better
prediction.
The study could be extended to the trial of other turbulence models, as for
example LES. Moreover the diﬀerence in the behaviour found between the ADM in the
experimental validation of the MEL rotor and the validation of the DTU rotor using diﬀerent
models, leads to think that the CT is overestimated in the MEL rotor simulation.
The
simulation could be performed for diﬀerent thrust coeﬃcient values.
2. Sensitivity analysis on tower characteristics for wind turbines
The ﬁndings regarding the diﬀerent tower shapes need more accurate analysis. Since the
results suggested that diﬀerent shapes lead to diﬀerent behaviours, in the future a sensitivity
analysis will be done with the aim of including the characteristics of the tower in forecasting
the wind turbine wake. The analysis could further extended for including the tower in the
generation of momentum exchange in order to shorten the wake.
Diﬀerent add-ons, like

3. Implementation of the comparison ADM in Star-CCM+
Another future work is the implementation and validation in Star-CCM+ of the model lately
developed by Sørensen, described in [12]. Compared to the 1-D momentum method, the
model includes a very accurate representation of the behaviour of rotors, it includes also tip
and root corrections but diﬀerently from the more complex BEM ADM and ALM does not
only need the power curve and the Thrust Coeﬃcient as an input.
