ABSTRACT 
An investigation is carried out into the losses and effects, that voltage and current un-
balance has on distribution transformers in general, and specifically laboratory tests of 
this on an ASEA TOH 630kVA 10/0,4kV transformer. Simulations are carried out in 
MagNet to have another source of verification for observations and behavior in general. 
The losses are measured and calculated to get an idea of the size of the extra losses that 
occur, but there are no local data to get an idea of the real extra losses in the grid. A few 
ways of reducing the losses are discussed and their cost held up against the losses.

INTRODUCTION 
 
1.1 
Background 
With the constant search for points to optimize and costs that can be avoided in the elec-
trical power system, a look at the possible imbalances in the low voltage system and the 
extra losses and problems this can cause, as well as the possible ways to mitigate this is 
a subject of interest.  
An imbalance of load or local production will cause increased conducting losses in the 
three phases as the current isn't divided evenly, since the conducting losses are in-
creased with the current squared. An imbalance will also cause losses in the neutral 
conductor which is not needed under ideal balanced conditions. [1] 
Any imbalance from the feeders summed together will also cause increased losses in the 
transformer. This will in the case of a load imbalance result in increased conducting 
losses in the transformer, while a voltage imbalance or distortion (harmonics) will also 
cause increased losses in the form of core losses in addition to the increased conducting 
losses and skin effect losses. [1]  
The distribution of the load on the 3 phases of a feeder depends on what is connected to 
what phases at the customer’s installation, and the consumption pattern of the customer. 
With the increased popularity of single phase photo voltaic units in the distribution grid, 
and the possibility of single phase plug in electrical vehicles and heat pumps increasing 
in number, these might contribute to an even greater unbalance. 
The unbalance can also cause the voltages in the phases to exceed the +/- 10% voltage 
limit that is required in the customers connection point, or the 2% voltage unbalance 
factor. [2]   
The unbalanced conditions under which a transformer operates increases the energy 
losses in the device. These losses happen as an increase in energy dissipated in the 
transformer, thereby causing the temperature to rise. Not only does this in itself cause 
increased operating costs, the increased temperature also has an impact on the lifetime 
expectancy of the transformer.  
The magnitude of the unbalance and the associated losses and costs mentioned could 
increase, if care is not taken when new loads and distributed generation is connected at 
the customers.

1.2 
Investigation 
The investigation will look into the increased losses and effects that occur, as a result of 
unbalanced conditions for a transformer. This will be done through theoretical work, 
simulations in the finite element program Magnet, and actual tests in the laboratory.  
1.3 
Project goal 
This project will aim at investigating the increased losses that occur when a 10/0.4kV 
distribution transformer is subjected to an uneven load, compared to an ideal balanced 
distribution on its three phases. To further expand this investigation a look at the effects 
of an uneven voltage on the primary side will be performed. 
So in short this project aims at answering: 
- 
What are the increased losses and effects on a transformer under unbalanced condi-
tions? 
- 
What technical and economic benefits are available by balancing the conditions? 
 
1.4 
Method 
Papers and books related to answering the project goals will be investigated and dis-
cussed, as will their validity and relevance for the project. 
The transformer under investigation will be an ASEA 10/0.4kV ONAN (oil filled natu-
rally cooled) transformer which can be subjected to tests in the High Voltage Lab at 
DTU Elektro.  
A simulation model of a 3-phase 3-legged transformer will be subjected to different 
unbalances in Magnet to see the effects.  
Unbalances of the same kind will then be used on the ASEA transformer and held up 
against theory. 
The test conditions are an uneven load on the secondary side and uneven voltages on the 
primary side. The possibility of subjecting the transformer to harmonics in the laborato-
ry is still an open question, as this will require setting up the 3-phase four-quadrant am-
plifier to simulate different conditions. 
 
These tests will be carried out as open-circuit and short-circuit tests with a degree of 
modification. Because the lab does not have equipment to fully load a transformer of 
this size at rated voltage, these simplifications have to be made.  
 
So in short the method for the project can be summarized in the following points: 
- 
Gathering and investigating information relevant to the project goals. 
- 
Simulating a 3-phase 3-legged transformer in MagNet. 
- 
Verifying and investigating conditions on the transformer under tests in the High Volt-
age Lab.

2.2 
Magnetic materials 
The materials used in transformer cores are ferromagnetic materials. These materials 
have the characteristic that the magnetic moments of the atoms are randomly oriented, 
but when an external field is applied they start aligning, producing a much larger flux 
density than the external field would be able to produce on its own. This increases with 
the strength of the external field until all the moments are aligned, at this point the mate-
rial cannot help to increase the flux density further, and the material is said to be satu-
rated. [4]  
When the external field is removed the magnetic moments will move towards their orig-
inal orientation, but not completely. Thus a net magnetization in the direction of the 
previously applied field will still exist causing what is known as magnetic hysteresis. 
This causes the B-H curve for ferromagnetic materials to be nonlinear, and depending 
on the applied field it can actually take on a whole range of values. This is often illus-
trated with a B-H curve for each material or alloy, which can illustrate its properties 
found during testing. 
==PAGE:20==
Theory

Figure 2 - B-H Curve for a ferromagnetic material [5] 
As can be seen from the top and bottom of the curve, at a certain magnetic field intensi-
ty the flux density doesn’t increase anymore, at this point the material is said to be satu-
rated.  
Modern transformers often make use of cold rolled silicon steel, with a higher permea-
bility than normal steel, or hot rolled silicon steel (in which the magnetic moments are 
more randomly oriented than the cold rolled version). [1] 
These improved ‘H’ grades of steel have been available since 1968, and also display 
lower losses per unit weight compared to standard grade silicon steel. 1,12W/kg com-
pared to 1,32W/kg at a flux density of 1,7T and a frequency of 50Hz. [1]  
The transformer core is typically made up of laminations of this silicon steel with a 
thickness of around 0,3mm to minimize the eddy currents in the core. This is done by 
applying a layer of insulation coating to each lamination.

Flux and windings 
A transformer works by using Faraday’s law: That a time varying magnetic flux en-
closed by a wire will induce a voltage in the wire.  
This is explained with the formula:

Where e is the induced voltage in the wire (or windings) enclosing the flux  , N times. 
This means that if you have a flux enclosed by two separate sets of wires, then the volt-
age induced in the wires will depend on how many times each set runs around the flux, 
that is N. Meaning that the induced voltage doesn’t have to be the same or different, but 
depends on the number N for each. So for a given transformer there is usually a turns 
ratio, meaning the ratio of these two numbers. 
 
The windings are made of a conducting material with a low resistivity; this is typically 
copper or aluminum. The current running through the windings cause heating losses 
which are equal to the resistance times the current squared (I2R), because of this the 
windings need to be suitably dimensioned by giving them a sufficient cross-section.  
It can be shown that these losses are minimized for a given transformer, when the cur-
rent densities in the high and low voltage windings are equal. [6]  
 
The flux can also induce eddy currents in the windings, these are proportional to the 
height squared of the windings. To minimize this it is common to either create the wind-
ings like thin discs running around the core, or to divide the windings into insulated 
strands with a suitable transposing scheme. [6]

2.6 
Core design 
Transformer cores come in a lot of different designs. These depend a lot on the rating of 
the transformer, and the amount of phases or windings which are to be placed on the 
core. A simple torus can be used for a single phase in a low-power circuit, but it isn’t 
easy to manufacture or very space efficient compared to a hollow core. 
 
A ‘Y’ core has a low loss and exciting current compared to an ‘E’ core, but has the dis-
advantage that the assembly of the core and winding of the coils is a difficult process. If 
care is not taken the benefits of the design can be lost in the construction process. [1] 
Part of the advantage of the design is that there is no division of the flux paths, meaning 
that there is not an increase or decrease in the reluctance like in an ‘E’ cores center ver-
sus side legs. 
 
Figure 4 - Y-core design [1] 
 
For large power transformers either 3- legged or 5-legged ‘E’ cores are used depending 
on the space and transportation restrictions, in some special cases a transformer bank of 
3 hollow core 1-phased transformers are used. [1] In a 3-legged core there is no path for 
zero sequence flux to run, and thus it is forced outside the core to a path with a higher 
reluctance. By making a fourth or fifth leg the zero sequence flux will instead run in this

2.9 
Transformer connections 
There are a variety of transformer connections that all behave differently some of the 
most used connections are described here and reflected on. The illustrations are taken 
from Franklin and Franklin [1] while the information is from both Franklin and Frank-
lin, and Rusch and Good. [7] 
 
In a star connection one end of the three coils is connected in a common point to which 
a neutral can also be connected. The other ends of the coils are connected to the three 
phases. 
 
In a delta connection the three coils are connected end to end like in a triangle. Each of 
the three phases is then connected to a joint between 2 coils. 
 
2.9.1 
Star-star 
When a load is connected between two phases only, the current will run through the two 
phases on the secondary side and their coils, and their two associated phases on the pri-
mary side and their coils. The neutral points are relatively stable, and the voltage drop is 
only caused by the impedance of the transformer, leading to normal voltages. The neu-
tral point of the secondary side can be earthed without any problems. 
 
When the load is connected from a phase to neutral, without a neutral wire on the pri-
mary side, the current will be split up between the 3 phases and their coils on the prima-
ry side. If the transformer is not of a core-type design, but is instead a shell-type or a 
bank of 3 single phase transformers, a considerable deflection of the neutral point will 
occur, and the voltages will not be close to their rated value. This can also happen for 
the core-type but only under very unbalanced loads. 
 
In the above scenario where there is a neutral wire, the current will return through this 
instead of being divided between the phases meaning that only one coil on the second-
ary and its associated primary will be loaded, this resolves the problem described with 
non-rated voltages and a deflection of the neutral. 
 
The star secondary means that it is possible so deliver a 4-wire service to the consumer. 
So that both line-voltage and/or phase-voltage can be used if needed.

WORK BY OTHERS 
In this section a description of the work which has been done by others prior to the work 
carried out in this report, and which has acted as an inspiration and guide for the work.  
 
Electric Machinery by Fitzgerald [4] does a good job of describing the workings of elec-
tric machinery with a basis in Maxwell’s Equations. It explains the principles and work-
ings of magnetic circuits and the way this is utilized in transformers and different gener-
ators and motors.  
 
The J&P Transformer Handbook 11th edition [1] is a book by Franklin and Franklin 
describing the details of transformer construction and operation. The first edition was 
published in 1925 and the 11th in 1983. It starts with the basic transformer principles 
regarding flux and magnetic materials, before going into the different ways of construct-
ing transformers. It covers a lot of details regarding testing, operation, and the different 
ways a transformer can be coupled. Especially the different currents which will be pre-
sent under extreme unbalance for different couplings (like delta-star and star-star), 
where a single load could be placed between two phases, or a phase and neutral.   
 
“The Unbalanced Load Cost on Transformer Losses at a Distribution System” by Rog-
erio Salustiano et.al. [8] concerns measurements of the increased unbalanced losses 
measured in a sample of transformers from a greater population in Brazil. The samples 
are fitted to a Weibull distribution to find the average losses in the balanced and unbal-
anced case. These losses are then used as an average to find the total annual losses 
caused by unbalanced transformer loads, and calculated to a U.S. dollar figure. The 
measurements have been gathered from 3-phase power transformers rated at 30, 45 and 
75 kVA.   
 
“Evaluating and Derating of Three-phase Distribution Transformer under Unbalanced 
Voltage and Unbalance Load Using Finite Element Method” by Atabak Njafi et.al [9] 
describes the increased losses that will occur in a three-phase transformer that is sub-
jected either to an unbalanced voltage, or an unbalanced load. For the finite element 
simulation a model of a 34,5/0,4kV 100kVA Dy11 three-phase transformer is used. 
An increase in both core losses and conductor losses is noted when the transformer is 
subjected to an unbalanced voltage, but only an increase in conductor losses when it is 
subjected to an unbalanced load. 
==PAGE:30==
Work by others 
28 
 
In the paper “Asymmetry During Load-Loss Measurement of Three-Phase Three-Limb 
Transformers” [10] R. Escarela-Perez et.al. describe that the cause for unbalanced cur-
rents and powers during open-circuit and short-circuit tests of a three-phase three-limb 
transformer, is that the transformer as a 3-D structure is not completely symmetrical, 
neither in space nor in physical values.  
This means that the mutual inductances between the phases are not symmetrical, and 
this is described as the major source for the unbalances.  
A three dimensional finite element model of a transformer is made, and then a 6-port 
network impedance model is made from the finite element model to describe its behav-
ior during open-circuit and short-circuit tests. From the impedance model it can be seen 
that neither the self-impedances nor the mutual impedances are equal. 
  
Rusch and Good have made a paper with the clever title “Wyes and Wye Nots of Three-
Phase Distribution Transformer Connections”. [7]  In this they give insight on the pros 
and cons of different transformer connections and the operational issues which can 
arise. They also briefly address the issue of harmonics, and 3 phase core type transform-
ers with different numbers of legs.

This section will contain general information about the transformer, and the resistance 
measured in the windings.  
 
4.1 
The transformer under test 
The transformer which is used for this project and the tests is an Asea TOH 630. It is a 3 
phased 630kVA transformer used for transforming the voltage down from 10kV to 
400V, which is the voltage supplied to most customers. It is connected in what is known 
as Dyn11, meaning the high-voltage 10kV side is connected in delta, and the low-
voltage 400V side is connected in a star, with a neutral connection to the center of the 
star. The 11 means that if you compare a phase on the primary and secondary side, then 
the phase is shifted 11 times 30 degrees, or 330 degrees. Which is the same as saying it 
is shifted -30 degrees.  
It is possible through an off-load tap changer to change the voltage on the primary side 
from 10500V to 9500V in steps of 250V, when the transformer is disconnected from its 
supply.   
 
It is rated at 36,4A per phase on the primary side, and 909A per phase on the secondary 
side. The cooling for the transformer is ONAN, the ON meaning that the oil is cooling it 
by flowing naturally instead of being forced past the windings. The AN means that the 
air is also flowing naturally past the transformer, instead of being blown past it with a 
fan. To help with this cooling choice the transformer is surrounded by a large amount of 
metal fins to give it a larger surface, like a heat sink.  
 
The transformer itself was built in Odense, Denmark in 1981. At what started out as a 
company called Thomas B. Thrige, it later changed name to Thrige-Titan, then to Asea, 
before being closed down as ABB.  
After contacting ABB regarding construction details such as the dimensions of the core 
and the number of windings and their cross-section, Erik Koldby senior engineer at 
ABB wrote that all their construction drawings and archives regarding the transformers 
constructed in Odense where lost when the factory was closed down in 1995. What he

has said in an e-mail is that a guess would be that around 50% of the low-voltage distri-
bution transformers in Denmark are of the same design made in Odense, though the 
ratings and details differ depending on what year it was produced.  
 
The information which could be gathered about the insides of the transformer where 
thanks to pictures and information sent by Energimuseet in Tange, from their cut-open 
TOH 400 display model, and from Esben Larsen and Flemming Juul Petersen who vis-
ited the factory while it was still in operation. 
 
The core is a three-legged core made out of thin laminations of silicon-steel, it is fully 
rectangular without a cruciform design. The high-voltage windings are made with a 
copper wire would around the legs as a coil, while the low-voltage windings are made 
out of sheet-aluminum. This made for an easy way of getting a high cross-section on the 
low-voltage side with a minimum of work, since the sheet could just be rolled from one 
drum onto another where spacers and insulation was applied. The draw-back of this 
design is that the flat of the sheet is parallel to the flux running in the core, maximizing 
the eddy-currents in the winding. This was apparently seen as a good sacrifice compared 
to the easier construction of the windings.

5.1 
A-eberle PQ-Box 100 
The voltage and current measurements regarding the input to the transformer are made 
with an A-eberle PQ-Box 100. According to the manufacturer this device meets the de-
mands of the IEC 61000-4-30 (2008) standard for class-A measurements devices. [11] 
 
 
Figure 15 - A-eberle PQ-Box 100 
 
The device uses clamps to measure the voltage for up to 3 phases as well as the neutral 
and earth. It uses Rogowski coils to measure the current in the 3 phases as well as the 
neutral. The Rogowski coils supplied with the box have a measurements range from 5 to 
3000A with an accuracy of 1%. Because of the low currents used in the tests compared 
to the maximum of the coils, it was decided to coil the supply cables 10 times around 
the Rogowski coil to get a more accurate reading.

tive and reactive power, angles between the different currents and voltages, harmonics 
for both voltages and currents. It also has an oscilloscope mode where it can show the 
waveforms together.

Observations and limitations 
The PQ-Box showed some strange looking waveforms when it was first used, this was 
verified by connecting a voltage probe and a rogowski coil to an Agilent oscilloscope to 
compare the waveforms. The current in the figure is showing a double-peak, where the 
first peak is not quite in phase with the voltage, but the second peak is clearly 90 de-
grees shifted as it falls right on top of the zero-crossing of the voltage. This weird shape 
could be a result of the mentioned phase supplying 85% of the active power consumed 
by the transformer and roughly 31% of the reactive power, if the PQ-Box is to be be-
lieved during this test.

As mentioned previously the rogowski coils for the PQ-Box have a rating from 5-
3000A. A quick test was performed with a handheld clamp-on current meter to see if it 
was in accordance with the PQ-Box. They were showing the same values while the ac-

During some tests the active power on a phase was shown as negative by the PQ-Box. 
Since it did not seem correct at the time, the phase was also connected to a single phase 
Zes Zimmer power meter. Both the power meter and the PQ-Box were showing an ac-
tive power of the same magnitude with a negative sign, so it was deemed correct. A 
theoretical explanation for this was later found from a paper, [10] being explained with 
the position of the windings not being identical in 3 dimensional space, and the core 
material not being completely homogeneous. This results in the mutual-impedances 
between the phases not being equal, and the self-impedances of the coils not having the 
same values either. This causes the unbalanced currents and powers, and even negative 
power on some phases. [10]

in Magnet, a finite element modeling program focused on electric and magnetic circuits. 
 
9.1 
Balanced versus unbalanced voltage 
As it was shown in the paper by Njafi [9] an unbalanced voltage will result in a larger 
core loss, even though the average voltage is still the same. To test this claim a simula-
tion was set up with a model of a 3-phase 3-legged transformer, and the core losses cal-
culated by Magnet was compared in the two situations. 
 
 
Figure 35 - Magnet model of a 3-phase 3-legged transformer 
 
The model is a lot larger than the ASEA transformer, but since it was impossible to get 
construction details about the ASEA transformer, and opening it would be a costly affair 
since it would have to be closed again, it was more convenient to take a working model 
and ignore the size difference since the overall design method is the same. 
This means that the losses cannot be converted directly between the model and the 
ASEA transformer, but the theory can be tested in a simulation. 
 
==PAGE:56==
Open-circuit simulation 
54 
The transformer was connected in a star with an open-circuit secondary, the circuit was 
fitted with 3 ac voltage sources to simulate a 3-phase system exciting the transformer. 
This was done with a balanced voltage and an unbalanced voltage. 
This first simulation resulted in a 4,3% voltage unbalance factor (VUF) increasing the 
core losses by 1,6%. Which is not a lot since the core losses are usually smaller than the 
conductor losses at full load. [1] In this case the more pressing problem would be the 
VUF being above the recommended 2%. [2] 
 
An important detail was simulated which was verified in a short test on the ASEA trans-
former. If a single phase is disconnected during the open-circuit test, then the trans-
former will still have the rated voltage at the transformer terminal, because the remain-
ing two phases are still magnetizing the core, thus creating an alternating flux through 
the leg of the disconnected phase.   
 
This is most apparent in the example of a delta-star connected transformer where one of 
the primary phases is disconnected. The voltages on the secondary side will still behave 
normally and be able to deliver up to 70% of rated current without any noticeable 
changes. [1]  
 
9.2

Several simulations have been made to try and give a better understanding of the impact 
of voltage and current on the core. From Faraday’s Law it is clear that the voltage and 
the flux in the core are connected, and through Ampere’s Law that current and mmf are 
connected. What makes the situation difficult is the core. The core has a hysteresis 
curve that can have a multitude of values depending on what values the fields have at 
that moment in time, and what they were shortly before.

The core in the simulations has been excited with both balanced and unbalanced voltage 
and current. The result is that because of the higher impedance on the center leg a lower 
magnetizing current will run in this phase if ideal balanced voltage is applied. In the 
case where a current source is used so that the currents are of the same magnitude and 
also balanced with 120 degrees between them, the center coils will a have a slightly 
larger voltage than the coils on the side legs.  
In the case where a non-balanced current is applied there will be an increase in the H-
field around the coils, outside the core. This is the zero-sequence flux that has to find an 
alternate path since it cannot run in the core. This also gives an unbalance in the flux in 
the core legs, and therefore the B-field in the legs. This causes the voltages in the coils 
to assume drastically different values.  
In the case where an unbalanced voltage is applied the H-field will still be stronger out-
side the core, but the voltages on the coils will be stable as the B-field has the same rms 
value in the core legs.

BALANCING OPTIONS AND ECONOMY 
There are a number of ways to balance the loads on the 3 phases, a few of them are 
mentioned and described here as are the economic outlook. 
 
12.1 
Transformers with mixed connections 
As described earlier certain transformer connections passively balance the loads drawn 
through them by way of their design. This is often the reason why distribution trans-
formers are chosen to have a mixed connection like a delta-star or star-delta, instead of a 
delta-delta or star-star.  
 
12.2 
Phase rotating unit 
A unit actively rotating the phases at a household’s fuse box is one idea of balancing the 
loads in a low-voltage grid. An implementation of this is described in a report by Troels 
Vantore [17]. It needs either an interface with a 3-phase power meter or a meter of its 
own, as well as a way of communicating with the other installations in the same low-
voltage radial. The idea is then for 3 3-poled contactors to disconnect the power and 
rotate the phases to the optimal setting, before connecting the power again. By doing 
this, a piece of control logic can rotate the phases in the households so that an optimum 
of balance is achieved in the radial.  
 
12.3 
Static load balancer 
A static load balancer is another name for a 3-phase interconnected star static balancer. 
It is actually a 1:1 autotransformer, where two identical windings are placed on each leg 
of the 3-legged core. These are then interconnected and a neutral wire is run to the star 
point. Another use for this design is adding a neutral wire to a 3-wire system.  
 
==PAGE:73==

Because it is basically an autotransformer it can also act as a step-up or step-down trans-
former in addition to balancing the loads and providing a neutral wire, if the situation 
calls for it.  
 
It is described as being mostly used at the end of long feeders in rural areas supplying a 
mix of loads. This could be phase-ground loads for lighting, and phase-phase or 3-

12.4 
Reflections on solutions and economy 
The cheapest solution and the one already widely in use, is using mixed connections 
like delta-star which passively balances some of the load on the transformers used in the 
grid. One of the drawbacks is that this solution only balances the loads at specific junc-
tions in the net, and in some cases leaves long transmission paths with unbalanced con-
ditions. But the advantage is that since a transformer would have to be installed anyway, 
it is a good choice to choose one that helps with the balance. 
 
The solutions which require the purchase and installation of additional equipment is 
quite another case. The proposed phase rotation units are at a quick glance cheaper than 
the static balancer2 [17], and it can balance the consumption quite close to the consum-
er. Their downside is that they require logic and communication to work, and that they 
need to disconnect the supply for a few milliseconds to make the switch. 
The static balancer handles the balancing without any interaction, but it does so in a 
point in the line, and is basically another transformer with all the purchase and installa-
tion costs.  
 
In a given case the consumption patterns for the loads connected to a transformer would 
have to be analyzed to determine which solution would best fit the situation. 
It was not possible to get any data when an inquiry was sent to a distribution company, 
so the only source of information about the size of the unbalance is hear-say, and the 
paper by R. Salustiano et.al. [8] in which 3 different ratings (30, 45 and 75kVA) of 
transformers are investigated. They determine that the average annual losses due to un-
balance in the transformers is 125-156kWh per transformer per year.

DISCUSSION 
The problem with unbalanced loads comes from an inability to divide the loads evenly 
at the customers. Large single phase loads that aren’t coordinated at the customer or in 
the feeder they are connected to. This gets worse once the customer starts producing 
electricity and sending it back into the grid without doing so with balanced 3-phase 
units. Both of these problems have the same source: Producing single phase units is 
cheaper than producing 3-phase units. This can be motors consuming power, or power 
electronics generating power from photo voltaic arrays.  
The question then becomes a question of who should foot the bill for this unbalance. 
Should it be the customer consuming or producing power because he buys the available 
equipment?  
Or should it be the distribution company who needs to balance the load to reduce costs 
and unwanted situations? 
It could also be required that all devices over a certain power rating have to be 3-phase 
units, or that consumer installations are designed to be more evenly balanced.  
These questions would need to be answered either through standards or through legisla-
tion depending on whether common ground can be found between the different stake-
holders. 
 
It is an open question whether the effects seen when the 4-quadrant amplifier was con-
nected to the transformer also will happen when a load is applied. This is an interesting 
question because the amplifier is actually a 3-phase converter, although a bit more ad-
vanced than the ones used in a photo voltaic unit. 
 
Unbalanced voltages can lead to an unbalanced flux in a 3-legged core. If this flux con-
tains a zero-sequence component it will flow outside the core in whatever is present, 
this can be oil, the transformer tank, or the metal fittings keeping the core together. This 
can in a worst case scenario cause localized heating and degrade the transformer, short-
ening its lifespan. One way of answering this problem is to use a 4 or 5 legged core in-
stead so that there is a path inside the core where the zero-sequence flux can flow.

CONCLUSION 
 
14.1 
Results 
While it has been seen through simulation that a voltage unbalance will cause increased 
core losses, it has not been possible to verify this through experiments. The simulated 
losses were very small, but it was not performed on a core of the same size as the one in 
the ASEA TOH 630 transformer.  
The conduction losses in a 3-phase transformer will be smaller when the current is di-
vided evenly between the phases, versus when there is an unbalance, because the losses 
increase with the current squared. 
The amount of extra losses an unbalance will cause depends a lot on the load of the 
power transformer and the size of the unbalance. On the ASEA transformer it was found 
that in some theoretical cases the extra losses are large enough to warrant an investiga-
tion, into using devices to balance the whole, or part of the load. If the only available 
data from Brazil is used, the losses will instead be too small to warrant any action.  
  
While it was possible to re-create the unbalanced currents in the open-circuit test which 
were observed with the Spitzenberger Spies to a certain degree, it was not possible to 
pin down their exact influence, or if they would be present while the transformer is 
loaded. It is still an open question as to whether these will be present during a voltage 
unbalance.  
  
The extra losses and adverse effects caused by the unbalanced is a challenge for the 
distribution company responsible for the grid. The increased losses will hurt their oper-
ating costs and will have to be covered in some way. The same is true if any adverse 
effect cause the transformer lifetime to deteriorate.  
They are also responsible for delivering a 3-phase voltage with a maximum VUF of 2% 
at the customer’s connection point. While this will not hurt them directly, it is not keep-
ing up with the specified recommendations.

14.2 
Perspectives 
The work done in this report focusses mostly on the ASEA TOH 630 transformer, but 
the parts of the investigation which cover 3-phase transformers in general can still be 
used for other cases. The same holds true for the parts concerning 3-legged cores and 
Dyn connected transformers. The benefit of using the TOH 630 is that a large amount of 
the transformers in the Danish low-voltage grid are of the same general design, and in 
that way can be used as a general rule of thumb for the low-voltage grids in the country. 
In the same way it is not very accurate to use the estimates for unbalanced losses in 
Brazil for transformers in Denmark, but they do indicate that the losses happen, and that 
an investigation into such matters in Denmark would be beneficial. 
 
14.3 
Further work 
As noted this work mostly covers the ASEA transformer under test, transformers of 
other designs or newer models might not have the same losses or behave the same way. 
This would need to be considered or investigated in a given situation, or some more 
general principles will have to be found. 
If a true simulation of the ASEA transformer is wanted then one will have to be taken 
apart to measure the construction details. 
 
Another needed step is to collect data from the Danish low-voltage grid and to analyze 
it to get an overview of the degree of unbalance in the grid, to figure out whether it is 
feasible to improve on the current situation. 
 
With the observations done with the 4-quadrant amplifier and the transformer, it might 
be worth looking into what is causing these unwanted results. Is it the amplifier specifi-
cally or is it a general problem with the transformer and converters.
