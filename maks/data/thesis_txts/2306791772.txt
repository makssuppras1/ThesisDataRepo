Abstract
In recent years there has been an increased discussion regarding intermodal prioritisation
in Copenhagen.
This was enhanced by The Danish Commission on Congestion, who
suggested improvements to the public transport system in central parts of Copenhagen.
However, they did not have the tools to model the eects of such improvements: The
available microscopic models are too specic to model eects of larger infrastructural
changes, and the available macroscopic models handle private and public transport sep-
arately. Thus, is a clear need for a model that is detailed enough to realistically model
interaction between private and public modes, while being suciently ecient to cover a
large area. This thesis establishes such a transport model that can handle the assignment
of car trac and public transport simultaneously. It is based on the MATSim modelling
framework and applied to a network of the Greater Copenhagen Area, with the purpose
of modelling bus running times. The thesis starts by reviewing literature on modelling
of bus delays as well as mesoscopic and queue-based models capable of handling com-
bined assignment of public and private transport. This is followed up by a thorough and
critical examination of the various elements of MATSim. In order to use MATSim in a
Danish context suitable input is created based data from traditional transport models.
The thesis models both public and private transport for an entire day, and extracts bus
running times from the model. Compared to extensive delay data provided by Movia,
the models manages to capture the travel time variation of the observed data, however
with a mean value that is below the observed data. It is concluded that the model has
potential to model bus operation realistically, but that calibration is needed in order for
this to happen. The usability of the model as a policy support tool is discussed and a
variety of suggestions for future studies is proposed.

1.1
Motivation
This section will start by briey explaining the setting of the thesis, regarding trac

of the political setting, before nally motivating why there is a need for a new type of

1.1.1
Transport as a Derived Demand
Transport is an essential part of modern-day life. Every day people travel between dif-
ferent places in order to perform activities that provide them with utility. This can be
going to school to get educated, to work to earn salary, to shops to buy food, or to visit
places that can satisfy leisure needs. All of this is done, even though travelling in itself is
something that most people would like to do without. But as long as people have needs
that cannot be fullled without travelling, travelling as such cannot be avoided. This
fact has led to research indicating that travel models must include the underlying reasons
why people travel, such as Arentze and Timmermans, 2009; Axhausen, 2006.
However, despite being known since the 1970's (Jones, 1979; Jones, 1983), most trac
models runs according to  or heavily inspired by  the traditional four stage model,
dating back to the 1960's. Such models only consider the travel demand from people as
a mass, and not as individuals with desires that need to be fullled. Desires that have
only expanded as continuous economic development has increased the disposable income
among people and the production of companies, which have led to an even greater demand
for transport (Mallard and Glaister, 2008). This tendency is expected to increase,
and according to Schafer and Victor, 2000 the trac volume per person is expected
to double worldwide by 2050 on average. This is primarily due to growth in non-OECD
countries (Gudmundsson et al., 2015). A sign of this has been visible in Beijing, the
average number of daily trips by car doubled from 2000 to 2010 (Zhao, 2014).

1.1.2
The Rise of Congestion in the Copenhagen Area
The increase in trac in Denmark is not comparable to what is witnessed in countries
with much higher growth rates.
However, since the capacity of the road network is
limited, the trac growth has in fact led to an increasing congestion, that has become
evident in urban parts of Denmark  and especially in the Greater Copenhagen Area.
Kristensen et al., 2009 estimated that approximately 120,000 hours were wasted in
congestion every day in this area in 2001. By including the congestion of the rest of the
country as well, the losses add up to about 1% of the GDP; a gure that is expected
to increase dramatically over the next couple of decades (The Danish Commission
on Congestion, 2013). Besides congestion, the trac growth also imposes increased
emissions and trac related casualties (Kristensen et al., 2009).
Because of the economic and environmental disbenets of congestion, it has become a
hot political topic in Denmark. Without going into too much detail, a congestion toll
ring was in the pipeline during the late 2000's with support from the Social Democrats
(Kommuneforum, 2008). It all culminated rather anticlimactically in early 2012 when
the Social Democratic prime minister nally rejected the congestion toll ring altogether
(Sønder Tuxen, 2012).
Instead The Danish Commission on Congestion were formed to clarify the extent of the
problem, and to come up with solutions to reduce it (The Danish Commission on
Congestion, 2013). Their nal report, The Danish Commission on Congestion,
2013, concluded that with business as usual the car trac volume in the Greater
Copenhagen Area would increase by 10% from 2012 to 2025, but that the delay time for
cars would almost double. By implementing an unrealistic amount of congestion-reducing
initiatives, the delay time growth for cars could be lowered to 68%.
The report also noted that the concurrent available transport models could not predict
the eect on accessibility for buses in 2025, why this was let out of the equation. At the
time, however, buses had 12% of the total delay time, even though their trac volume was
only 5% of the total, indicating that buses are in fact severely inuenced by congestion.

1.1.4
Need for Contemporary Policy Support Tools
In order to accommodate and facilitate the discussion regarding intermodal prioritisation,
contemporary policy support tools are needed. As mentioned earlier and in The Dan-
ish Commission on Congestion, 2013, the current tools for predicting bus-related
consequences of infrastructural changes are unsuitable.
The current available tools fall into two cases. First, traditional large-scale models that
model passenger ows of private and public transport without considering interactions
between the two. These models, such as the Danish National Transport Model (LTM)
(Danish National Transport Model 2016) and the Øresund Transport Model (OTM)
(Sørensen et al., 2002), works on a rather aggregated level and are static (within time
bins of the day). This means that it is not possible to model the interaction between
individual vehicles in such models, and generally buses are assumed to follow the timetable
regardless of the trac load on the roads, just like cars are assumed unable to be delayed
by buses.
On the contrary, there are microscopic models, such as PTV VISSIM (Fellendorf,
1994), DRACULA (Liu, 2005), etc., that fully model the interaction between vehi-
cles, even including lane changing. Due to the detail level, they can only model a very
small geographical area, which dramatically reduces their applicability, since they do not
model the derived eects of the remainder of the transport system.
Thus, there is a clear need for models that can operate between the macroscopic and the
microscopic levels. It has to be detailed enough to handle interaction between private
and public trac, yet ecient enough to cover a meaningful geographical area, such as
Eastern Denmark. 1

2.1
Bus Delays
This rst section presents studies which sole purpose is to consider bus running times or
delays.
2.1.1
Purely Statistical Approaches
As briey mentioned in the introductory chapter, traditional transport models are not
capable of modelling interaction between private and public transport. As such, they
cannot be used to model bus delays.
To overcome the task, a variety of alternative methods have been suggested to model
bus running times along single corridors. In this section some studies regarding models
designed merely for this purpose is presented.
McKnight, 2003 investigated bus running times along two routes in New Jersey. A
simple linear model was constructed to estimate the bus running times. Besides a constant
term, the model consisted only of the free-ow travel time by car, the number of boardings,
and the number of bus stops. The model was based on data primarily collected by people
travelling in one of the two buses.
Tétreault and El-Geneidy, 2010, on the other hand used a great deal of AVL (Auto-
matic Vehicle Location) and APC (Automatic Passenger Count) data, to come up with
a model for prediction of a new bus route in Montréal, Canada. The model was based
on data collected from roughly 15% of the operating buses in the city. The aim of the
study was to select which stops should be included in a new route on an already used
corridor, and  given the stops  determine the running time of the bus. The statistical
model included 18 variables, including precipitation data, bus design, and time of day.
The model had a precision similar to that of McKnight, 2003 (R2 ≈0.6), but was
based on a wider variety of bus lines.

Vu and Khan, 2010 used a statistical pattern recognition technique to determine bus
running times. The study used data from two bus-routes in Ottawa, onto which the
statistical pattern recognition technique was applied. The model predicted the travel
times better than two standard techniques, and was also stated to behave well under
irregular conditions.
2.1.2
Separated Bus Simulation
Due to the complexity of the transit assignment problem, some modellers have taken
on a simulative approach. This allows for huge exibility, and for modelling personal
as well as vehicular attributes, and interaction between the individual vehicles of the
model (Gentile and Noekel, 2016).
In such a framework it is possible to model
capacity constraints, let passengers have dierent preferences for comfort (and thus not
boarding a near full bus), and so on. However, it does not explicitly provide functional
relationships between key variables, e.g. travel speed, number of vehicles in the network,
etc., and the corresponding passenger ows (Gentile and Noekel, 2016). Because of
this, simulation approaches have to simulate the whole system  a task that can be rather
cumbersome  in order to predict the passenger ows.
Gibson et al., 1989 simulated bus running times in Besançon, France and Santiago,
Chile using the simulator IRENE. The focus was on bus stop layouts, and how congestion
at the bus stop (created by other buses) aects the bus running times. They divided the
total travel into three components: Time spent moving, time spent waiting in junctions,
and time waiting at bus stops. Whereas the time waiting at bus stops was modelled
in quite great detail, the travel time along the road was assumed constant, and not
inuencing the delays. Thus the study only included delays caused by boardings and
other buses at the bus stops.
The microsimulation model TRAF-NETSIM was used in Abdelfattah and Khan, 1998
to estimate bus running times of a single bus route in Ontario, Canada. The model was
used to predict the bus running times under normal conditions, as well as with reduced
capacity and with signal prioritisation for buses. In addition to the micro-simulation
approach, it also developed a regression model, which was able to duplicate the micro-
simulated results of the scenario reasonably well (R2 ≈0.8).
Fernández, 2010 builds upon the study of Gibson et al., 1989 and uses microsim-
ulation to model public transport stops. The simulation was carried out in PASSION
(Parallel Stop Simulation), which was developed in the twenty years that followed since
Gibson et al., 1989, adding larger exibility regarding bus stop layouts and arrival
patterns. Neither did this study consider congestion along the road.
Ingvardson et al., 2015 simulate bus running times using a mixture of micro- and
microscopic approaches in an event-based model. The handling of passengers is simulated
microscopically as well as trac signals, whereas the travel time on the link is drawn from
a normal distribution distribution based on actual GPS data. The model is validated with
delay data from Movia. Furthermore, the study shows that completing the dedication of
bus lanes along a section of 5A in Copenhagen reduces the travel time variability and

2.2
Macroscopic Intermodal Prioritisation
The problem regarding intermodal prioritisation does not necessarily has to be dealt
with using a project to project fashion. Some studies have investigated the optimal
split between modes in a more general sense on a macroscopic level. A few of these will
be presented below.
Gonzales and Daganzo, 2012 consider the Vickrey single bottleneck problem, see
Vickrey, 1969, extended to a multimodal setting for the morning commute. The study
builds upon the theory of the multimodal macroscopic fundamental diagram and the cor-
responding network exit function. It shows that as the demand increased, public trans-
port gets relatively more attractive compared to car trac and will eventually become
the dominant mode.
Zheng and Geroliminis, 2013 also consider the multimodal macroscopic fundamental
diagram. In a idealised toy network with two regions, it is shown that the passenger travel
time can be reduced signicantly by dedicating 10% of the road space in the central region
to buses. The optimal proportion of dedicated bus lanes is shown to increase during peak
hours.
Another variation of the macroscopic fundamental diagram is found in Chiabaut, 2015.
The study uses a small meshed urban network and shows that the passenger ow using
public transport can surpass the car ow under dense conditions.
2.3
Disaggregated Combined Network Loading
The macroscopic considerations of the previous section can be a good guide post for
decision makers. However, if public transport is already widely available, it does not tell
whether or not dedicating a road for buses will reduce the passenger hours. In order to
provide policy support tools in such cases, less aggregated approaches are needed.
This section present studies on disaggregated multimodal network loading models. First
some microscopic approaches are presented, before moving on to some studies on meso-
scopic modelling. After this, the queue-based MATSim is presented.
2.3.1
Microscopic Models
Liu et al., 2006 used DRACULA (Dynamic Route Assignment Combining User Learn-
ing and Microsimulation), see Liu, 2005, to model the eects of dedicated bus lanes in
Leeds. DRACULA has a lot of similarities to MATSim. The day-to-day evolution gives
predetermined network routes for each individual as input to the actual simulation. The
routes are then scored and used as input for the generation of routes in the following

iteration. The vehicle movement is in accordance with car-following, lane-changing and
gap-acceptance rules, and the model also obeys trac signals. The model was applied to
a small network (less than 100 links) and had data given to it from an aggregated model.
Barceló and Casas, 2005 propose the AIMSUN (Advanced Interactive Microscopic
Simulator for Urban and Non-Urban Networks) model. This model also simulates trac
according to the three fundamentals: Car-following, lane changing, and gap acceptance.
As an example of an application, it replicated trac ows satisfactory when applied to a
365 link network in San Sebastian.
Cortés et al., 2007 present the MISTRANSIT (Microsimulation Transit) model, which
focuses on public transport. It has been applied to corridors and stations in Santiago,
Chile. For instance, it was used to test a holding strategy for buses aiming at reducing
passenger waiting time.
VISSIM (Fellendorf, 1994) is yet another microscopic transport model. It is the most
widely-used microscopic transport model in Europe with more than thousands of licenses
distributed worldwide (Fellendorf and Vortisch, 2010).
2.3.2
Mesoscopic Models
Whereas the microscopic model oers great realism at a high level of detail, due to their
high needs for detailed data and computational requirements, they are inappropriate for
large scale applications (Burghout et al., 2005).

been developed. One such model was proposed by Burghout et al., 2005, who combines
the microscopic MITSIMLab with the (back then) newly developed Mezzo model. The
model was applied to a Stockholm network with great results.
Mezzo was later extended in Cats et al., 2010 to also include a public transport module.
The model was applied on a Tel Aviv-network in order to validate the headways of a single
bus line. The modelled headway tted the observed data very neatly, indicating that a
bus operation can be modelled satisfactorily within the framework.

but also increased regularity.

Not all too dierent from BusMezzo, the nal type of model to be considered is MATSim,
Horni et al., 2016. MATSim has elements from both microscopic modelling and meso-
scopic modelling, and is sometimes referred to as queue-based. However, there seems to
be no clear consensus on how to categorise it. Lefebvre and Balmer, 2007, Flügel
et al., 2014, and Charypar et al., 2009 all use MATSim in their studies, but refer
to it as microscopic, mesoscopic, and queue-based, respectively. This study will use the
term queue-based to avoid the micro/mesoscopic question.
MATSim dates back to the start of the millennium. It was developed by Kai Nagel 
and later joined by Kay Axhausen  with the aim of creating an open-source transport
simulator (Horni et al., 2016). More people joined the group along the way, and today
the MATSim community spans across the world, primarily at universities, with the two
leading centres being TU Berlin and ETH Zurich, though.
Initially, the model only supported car trac. In 2009, Rieser et al., 2009 added mode
choice to MATSim, but without a realistically modelled assignment of public transport.
At this point public transport was modelled separated from the car trac and was simply
assumed to follow some predetermined factor of the free ow travel time for cars.
Rieser, 2010 presented a full implementation of schedule based public transport to
MATSim, where public transport vehicles were assigned alongside private trac on the
same network. Not only did the model simulate private and public vehicles, also the
passengers of the network were simulated in a realistic fashion boarding and unboarding
public transport vehicles along the way.
The model was applied to the large Zurich
network and showed realistic ow of both cars and public transport.
Also, expected
changes in the mode split were happening in a sensitivity analysis where a public transport
line was removed from the netowrk.
Neumann et al., 2012 later extended the model and used it on a network of the
Berlin/Brandenburg Metropolitan Region. At this point the model had had its boarding
and alighting times calibrated with data from a study, later being published as Neumann
et al., 2014.
In 2013, Grether and Nagel, 2013 presented an implementation of trac lights to
MATSim. The implementation was later applied in Grether, 2014, where car trac
in and around Cottbus were successfully modelled and simulated in dierent illustrative
scenarios whilst also simulating trac lights. The model did not include proper public
transport, though. The signalling module is now an optional extension to the default
MATSim settings. It requires quite specic trac light data to be used though.
Also in 2013, Ordóñez and Erath, 2013 suggested improvements to the public trans-
port part of the model. The main idea was to change the public transport assignment
model from being schedule based to being event based. The extension was implemented in

a modied version of the Singapore scenario with a simplied road network. The study
improved calculation times without adding much memory consumption to the model.
Also, it was much more realistic in a Singapore setting, and allowed passengers to choose

3.1
Overall Structure of MATSim
MATSim can best be explained according to the so-called MATSim Cycle, see Figure 3.1.
In more general terms, such type of process is known as an evolutionary algorithm.
The MATSim Cycle begins with an initial demand, that is a population consisting of
individuals with a selected day plan containing activities at certain places in the network
and trips connecting these places.
Figure 3.1: The MATSim Cycle. From Horni et al., 2016.
Afterwards, the cycle runs for several iterations beginning with the mobility simulation,
mobsim. It is in this module that trac runs through the network and activities are
performed, and is as such almost equivalent to a network loading. The mobsim results in
a series of events, which together describe what has happened during the mobsim. Based
on these events, the score of each person's day plan is determined according to the scoring
module. The scoring can then be used in the following re-planning module that allows
a randomly selected subset of the population to modify a day-plan, and every person to
select a day-plan from his/her choice set.
Various methods for determining when the cycle should end can be chosen, but the most

3.2.2.1
Routing Networks
Two types of routing networks exist in MATSim: A routing network for private transport,
and one for public transport.
The reason for the existence of two separate routing networks, is that a private trac can
use any link in the network that supports his/her mode. Thus, the routing network for
private modes is simply the input network that is parsed to the model. It may include
links where the chosen mode is not allowed, but this is handled in the algorithm.
The routing network for public transport is quite dierent, since public transport users
do not use the links of the networks as such, but travel via public transport vehicles. In
order to support such behaviour, links and nodes are created for each route of all lines in
the public transport schedule. For any such route, a link is placed for every stop on the
route, and with links connecting consecutive stops (Rieser, 2010). However, additional
links and nodes are not created for every departure of the route, why the network has
no temporal dimension, and is thus strictly speaking not a diachronic graph as known
from several other schedule based public transport models, e.g. Nuzzolo et al., 2004;
Nielsen and Frederiksen, 2008. The temporal dimension does exist when routing,
but is added by the algorithm internally on the go.
Transfer links are used to connect the various routes of the public transport schedule.
Since connecting all stops of all routes of the transport network would cause the transit
network to explode, MATSim only creates transfer links between stops that are within
a general predened distance, the so-called maximum beeline connection distance.
2
However, transfer links are not created between stops of the same line, neither are stops
created between the same stop of dierent routes, since no transfer is needed in this case.
Also, transfers are not inserted to stops ending a route or from stops starting a route.
Consulting Figure 3.2 to see an example might be helpful.
Notice that the exclusion of transfer links between stops of the same line can be inexpe-
dient. Chakirov and Erath, 2011 showed that in the highly congested metro system
in Singapore a fair a share of passengers travel in the opposite direction before travelling
in the desired direction. This is done in order to be able to get a seat or simply being
able to enter the metro.

The actual routing algorithm for cars is based on the A∗Landmarks Algorithm. The

is familiar with the traditional Dijkstra's Shortest Path Algorithm (Dijkstra, 1959). A
description on this can be found in any introductory book on algorithms, e.g. Cormen
et al., 2001.

considerably lower than what it may seem at rst glance.

Algorithm), whilst only using 100MB of additional memory to store the pre-calculated
shortest travel times to the landmarks from the nodes. The superior computation times
are backed up by Goldberg and Harrelson, 2005.
In order to use the algorithm, each car has to travel between two nodes. They actually
want to travel between two facilities, e.g. a household and a work place, but since every
facility is assigned to a link, the desired path is found between the to-node of the link of
the origin facility and the from-node of the link of the destination facility.

3.3.1.1

The link transmission model in MATSim has a theoretical foundation that dates back to
the 1950's. The derivation of the model is based on Horni et al., 2016 and Osorio

the link (the receiving function, R) and leave the link (the sending function, S). When
the density is close to zero, there is also very little ow. When the density is close to the
maximum, ˆρ almost no ow can pass through the link, on the other hand.

3.3.2
Node Model
So far, we have only looked at what happens inside each link. In order to have ow across
the entire network, the model also have to handle transmission between links. This is
handled by the so-called node model.
As it was the case for the link model, the theoretical derivation is presented before
providing a miniature example.
3.3.2.1
Relation to Kinematic Wave Theory
In MATSim nodes have no size, and their sole task is to send ow from in-links to out-links
in a fair way. This includes two major subtasks. The rst is to prioritise in-ows. Since
the model contains no direct right-of-way the model has to determine, which vehicles are
allowed to enter out-links rst. This is necessary, since ow entering a node might not be
able to move to the next link, why the node can not simply accept all the ingoing ow.
This naturally leads to the second task of the node-model, which is to prevent spatial
hypercongestion on outgoing links.
==PAGE:47==
3.3 Mobsim
31
The implemented node model in MATSim stems from a publication on fair intersections
in Cetin et al., 2003 (Horni et al., 2016). After nearly a decade, the theory was
linked to kinematic wave theory in Tampère et al., 2011 among others. This paper
listed seven requirements that a node model has to full in order to be consistent with
kinematic wave theory:
1. The model has to be general in the sense that it should be able to handle intersec-
tions with any number of in- and out-going links.
2. The ows must be maximized, e.g. if there is available space in front of a vehicle it
should make use of it.
3. No ows can be negative.
4. There must be ow conservation, such that no ow arises or is lost within a node.
5. The ows must obey demand and supply constraints, meaning that the ow can
occur without demand for that particular ow, and the ow can not exceed infras-
tructural constraints.
6. The model must obey turn fraction conservation, which implies that the rst-in-
rst-out principle is obeyed (contrary to what would be the case for maximizing
ow of uids for instance). See Daganzo, 1995.
7. The model must be compatible with link ow dynamics.
Presumably, many solution algorithms would be able to satisfy the above requirements.
A stochastic variant of the iterative solution algorithm suggested by Flötteröd and Ro-
hde, 2011 is implemented in MATSim. The algorithm for the node model is formalised
in the following algorithm, which holds for every node N ∈N.

3.3.3
Public Transport
MATSim started out as a uni-modal model that only allowed car trac. Rieser et al.,
2009 added mode choice to the model by allowing agents to assign plans with dierent
modes. At that point only pseudo-legs were assigned for non-car trips, as the legs were
calculated by the free ow speed of car times a coecient (2 in this case). In his Ph.D.
dissertation (Rieser, 2010), he improved the mobsim dramatically to support schedule-
based public transport running in the actual network. The rest of the section on public
transport is based on the dissertation, unless stated otherwise.
In order to allow public transport, a lot of extra features had to be added to the model.
It had to include dierent types of vehicles, dierent types of links (bus lanes, railway
tracks), allow transfers, allow accessing and egressing public transport vehicles, etc.
The key dierence is that car-users and the car vehicle itself can more or less be considered
equal (parking and walking to/from it is not included by default in MATSim). For public
transport users, this is signicantly dierent. Users have to walk to a nearby stop/station.
Once they are here, they have to enter the bus/train. But the bus/train should not
increase in size just because additional persons enter it. Also, they should be able to run
without any passengers. Finally, users should be able to leave the bus/train and walk to
their activity facility.
This led to the addition of two new transport modes for trip legs: public transport (pt)
and transit walk. The rst is a common name for any transport vehicle (bus, train, etc.),
and the latter is any stretch of walk that has to be performed during the trip. Secondly,
due to the alternating structure of day plans (leg, activity, leg, ...) a special activity, pt
interaction, had to be added to the model. This is used every time the public transport
user changes mode from transit walk to a vehicle, but also when changing between lines.
3.3.3.1
Timetables
Lines are the top of the hierarchy for scheduled public transport vehicles. A line corre-
sponds to a bus line (5A, 300S, 78, etc.), train lines (regional trains between Copenhagen
Central Station and Nykøbing F, S-train line A, metro-line M1), etc.
Within any of these, there will be a number of routes. Routes are the various line-variants
that exist for the line. Often there is a route in each direction (for 5A one towards Husum
Torv, one towards the airport). It can also be a route that terminates before other routes
(5A sometimes only goes to Sundbyvester Plads). It can also be a route that has fewer
stops than normal, whenever this does not result in a new line. A route consists of a
series of stops (see below) and the network route connecting them. A network route is
simply a series of links.
Routes have a nal attribute, which is the planned arrival and departure times for any
stop of the route, given in seconds after leaving the initial stop. This makes the concepts
of routes quite more confusing. For instance, for buses a line will often have multiple
routes with the same series of stops. This is because buer time is put into the time table

during congested periods of the day, forcing the arrival times (relative to departure) to
be dierent throughout the day.
Finally, a every route has at least one departure. A departure is simply a bus leaving

to congestion-induced buer times during the day, a substantial number of routes only

3.3.3.2
Physical Infrastructure Related to Public Transport Vehicles
Transit Stop Facilities or simply stops, are facilities that allows interaction between pub-
lic transport vehicles and public transport users. Each stop has a link assigned to it, and
the stop can only be serviced from this particular link. Remember that links are unidi-
rectional, forcing two opposite bus stops to be assigned to two dierent links. The stops
are also where interaction between buses and cars can occur. The stop has an attribute
that tells whether or not a lane is blocked, when the bus is dwelling at the stop. This
can force cars to wait behind buses, which is a major dierence compared to traditional
models. Unfortunately, data about bus bays have not been available, why this has not
been utilised in this thesis.
Buses are running just as any other vehicle in the network, except when they run on
dedicated bus lanes. These are simply links, which are situated at the same location as
the corresponding link, but they are treated as dierent links in the model. The fact that
buses and cars (generally) share the same infrastructure, means that buses are (almost)
just as prone to get stuck in trac as cars.
When a bus/train enters a stop, it dwells if:
a Passengers need to get o.
b Passengers need to board.
c It is scheduled that the bus/train has to wait until the planned departure time, and
it arrives before this.
It takes a predetermined amount of time for each passenger to access/egress the bus/train.
This can be adjusted for each vehicle type, so that a train with 10 doors handle passengers
more quickly than a bus with three doors. Once every passenger has access/egressed, it
is checked if any additional passengers have arrived at the stop and want to board the
vehicle. If not, the vehicle runs towards the next stop.
If a passenger has to make a transfer at a bus stop, it can only happen if a transfer-link
is available. These are automatically created when a simulation start, and they allow
transfers between lines at a bus-stop, see also Section ??. It is possible to control the
radius within which all stops will be connected by transfer links. MATSim does not
provide any other way of assigning transfer links in its original form. If one desires to
have transfer links created in a dierent way  and we do  the source code needs to be
changed. More on this in Section 4.6.

3.4.1
The Charypar-Nagel Scoring Function
The theory underlying scoring of day plans is somewhat similar to random utility theory
from discrete choice models as known from Train, 2003; Ben-Akiva and Lerman,
1985; McFadden, 1974.
However, random utility theory does not consider entire
day plans, but only the trips as such (Horni et al., 2016). For this reason, the scoring
function in MATSim is normally referred to as the Charypar-Nagel Scoring Function, and
not as a utility function. The name arises from the authors of Charypar and Nagel,
2005 in which a model framework for scoring day plans was created. The ideas of the
model can be dated back to Vickrey, 1969, but are also closely related to commonly
used utility theory.
As mentioned in Chapter 1 trips are only conducted if there is an underlying purpose
for the travel, and since MATSim is an activity based model, it is natural that MATSim
traditionally scores activities rather than trips. The default variables that inuence the

3.5.1
Plan Mutation
Plan mutators are replanning strategies that create new plans based on an existing plan.
As the initial plans were created relative to an empty network, they might turn out to
be performing horribly due to congestion and the very likely risk that a lot of other
individuals also chose (parts of) the same route.
Luckily, plan mutators allow the agents to be inventive, so that they can come up with
dierent plans by considering existing plans. The following describes the rerouting plan
mutation, before providing a brief node on plan mutation strategies that are not used in
this study.
3.5.1.1

Since this study is built on trips at set locations at set times, this study will not utilise
the vast majority of the plan mutations possibilities that MATSim oers. Actually, the
only plan mutation that makes sense for this study, is the rerouting strategy. This is due
to the fact that the trips did not have any information regarding routes.
The Reroute module allows the agent to basically follow the same day plan: The same
activities at the same locations, and the same legs with the same modes and departure
times. But the routes along these legs will (almost certainly) change, so that they are the
shortest, based on the previous iteration. The link travel times used for re-routing are
aggregated to time bins of 15 minutes (Balmer, 2007). Thus, even though the route
is based on the actual travel times of the last iteration, it is not completely accurate.
Both because the travels times are average times considered over a time period, but
also because the loading on the links presumably will be dierent in the next iteration.
Because of this the aggregation error caused by the 15 minute time bins are very likely
to be neglected compared to the noise created by agents changing plans.

Destination and mode choice, was already handled by the data from The

For most MATSim models, it would be appropriate to consider time mutating, so that the

strategy, it is not possible in traditional models, why using it helps emphasising the
strength.
MATSim also oers mode choice mutation and destination mutation, or combinations
with these and time mutations.
Only route choice is considered in this study though due to the above explanation. This
turns the MATSim model into a pure assignment model.

Analyses

3.6.1
Convergence
Since the type of model that is used in MATSim is a dynamic activity based model, equi-
librium is a much more diuse term than in traditional transport models. For dynamic
models there is a denition for the dynamic stochastic user equilibrium (DSUE) (Han,
2003). However, since MATSim uses scoring of entire days instead of only looking at
the travel times, in general an equilibrium is much harder to determine.
One way to come around this, would be to dene it as a situation, in which every agent
chooses the same day plan two times in a row. In this situation we would clearly have
an equilibrium-like situation. But will that ever happen in an evolutionary algorithm?
Perhaps in an idealised model. But since the model tries to reproduce reality, it can
be argued that the model ought not to ever end in such a situation. It is extremely
unlikely to happen in reality (considering days as iterations). In reality, people never
stop learning, and if a plan performs bad they will not use it again.
==PAGE:65==
3.6 Analyses
49
In this model we only consider a time-xed assignment. Had this been done in a tradi-
tional transport model, the aim would have been to nd the stochastic user equilibrium
(SUE). This can be approximated in MATSim by allowing people to choose their plan
randomly (according to some distribution  logit in this study) from their choice set.
However, there is a big dierence. In aggregated models, the stochasticity accounts for
random heterogeneity across the population. But since every individual has his/her own
choice set in MATSim from which they choose their plans, the randomness of choices in
MATSim is somewhat dierent and harder to interpret.
Due to the fact that no obvious convergence criteria exist for MATSim, the most intuitive
one would probably be to terminate the algorithm when the changes in the average score
of the executed population are under a certain tolerance.
However, the default way of dealing with termination in MATSim, is simply to let the
algorithm one for a predetermined number of iterations. This is also the approach chosen
in this study.
3.6.2
Model Output
As touched upon in the previous section, the scores of the agents' executed plans are
key output from the simulation. MATSim allows the user to easily see how the scores
has developed, by exporting a text le and a graph. It contains the value of the average
(across agents) best (across choice set) score, average worst score, average average score,
and average executed score. This can be used to see if the agents takes increasingly better
choices.
Another output is the time the model has spent on various phases of the model. This
can be used to determine if changes should be made due to computational reasons.
The most central out put though, is a le that contains all events that happened during
the simulation. This information is stored in a .xml le and can be massive. For full scale
tests, even when using the compressed .xml.gz format, the le still has a size of several
gigabytes. This is not surprising though, as it may contain hundreds of millions of events.
In order to analyse the events le in a useful way, handlers are available in MATSim.
These can be used to extract specic information from the model, for instance the planned
and modelled arrival for each time a bus arrives at a bus stop.

The remaining part of the section is a walk-through of the algorithm. Figure 4.3 can be
useful for keeping an overview.
The initial step of the algorithm simply initialises the values of the facility sharing infor-
mation. These values will be discussed in step 3.
Step 1 performs a grouped sum of the trips data. The only tricky part about this step, is
that it is not just a simple grouped sum. If that has been the case, the algorithm would
have created way too many facilities. This is due to the fact, that it is very likely that
each person visits a facility more than once during a day. From the data it is not possible
to determine if two destinations of the same person is identical. But chances are, that if
a person visits two activity locations with the same activity option in the same (OTM)
zone, it is probably the same place. This has been assumed in the algorithm, why every
person can at most cause one additional facility per activity option per zone.
A similar thing holds for all households. This is why all persons of a household in total
can only cause one additional household.
Step 2 takes the grouped trips from OTM zones, and turns them into LTM zones. No
direct conversion key has been available. 2 Instead a conversion key from micro zones to
OTM zones was available, in the sense that for all micro zones, their primary was OTM
zone could be found. It did not contain any information of how large portion of the OTM
zone it constituted, though. Such information was available when converting from micro
zones to LTM zones, though.
The conversion from OTM zones to a LTM zone was done by looking at the number of
microzones having each OTM zone as their primary OTM zones. This number is most
likely inaccurate, since it does not consider the sizes of the micro zone, as all micro zones
have equal weight. Thus, the weight of small micro zones are overestimated and vice
versa.
Step 3 utilises the initialised facility sharing information from the initial step. These
numbers have been used in order to reduce the number of needed facilities to a more
realistic level. Omitting this information would be very wrong, and would assume that
every trip corresponded to a unique facility. This would create way too many schools,
shops, work places, etc.
Regarding households, we already know the number of needed households, since house-
holds are contained within the trip data. However, there are normally more than one
household per location, since we only consider two-dimensional coordinates. Even though
such two households are two dierent facilities in the real world, they can be assumed the
same in the model without loss of information, when not having any further information
about the household, except for location. 3
The numbers are essentially guesstimates, and has not been given much attention. This
2Presumably, such a key exists, but the chosen method was deemed adequate, why a search for such

is primarily due to the fact that the method would never be that realistic anyway. In
order to do so, one would have had to assume dierent sizes of the facilities, and probably
also connecting the size of a facility with the location (urban, rural, etc.). In reality, there
are huge facilities such as universities or shopping malls, that attracts thousands of trips
every day, but this is not mimicked by using this approach.
Having found the needed number of facilities for each LTM zone in the previous step, step
4 distributes the facilities proportionally to the micro zones according to their sizes. The
D'Hondt Method used in the step, is normally used for allocating seats in parliaments,
and are used for general elections in many countries. The idea is that facilities (normally
seats) are spread proportionally, such that if zone A is between three and four times
larger than zone B, zone A will get three facilities, before zone B will get its rst. It
has the advantage of assigning an integer value for each zone, while still maintaining
proportionality as well as possible. See D'Hondt, 1878 for the original paper, or Elklit
et al., 2011 for more information.
Finally, step 5 simply draws coordinates inside each microzone, to get the location of
the facilities. It should be emphasised, that these draws are random, and as such most
certainly do not corresponds to real-life locations of facilities. Although the micro zones
used in this study is very small (at least in the inner part of Copenhagen), this could
create some problems, since the road network and facility locations are not independent
in reality.
This is especially the case for major real-life facilities such as universities or shopping
malls. However, as mentioned earlier, the facilities are only given a medium amount of
trips (by assuming the same average number of daily visitors for all facilities of the same
type), why this problem is probably limited. For instance, big problems such as a facility
attracting ten thousands of daily visitors being placed on a very small street are avoided.
It should be noted that after the coordinates have been drawn, the facilities have been
places on a link of the network, so that agents will be able to visit it in MATSim. In this
process, links with a free speed exceeding 80 km/h were not included in order to prevent
facilities being assigned to motorways.
The rst 3 steps of the algorithm were implemented in SAS. The D'Hondt Method was
programmed in Java, whereas the coordinates were drawn from the micro zones using
ArcGIS. Finally, the facilities were placed on a link by using methods of MATSim.

The remainder of this section will explain the algorithm in detail. Figure 4.3 can be used
to give an overview of the process as well.
Since step 0 is trivial, step 1 is explained rst. How the conversion from OTM zones to
LTM zones is done has already been touched upon in step 2 of Algorithm 4.2.1. However,
it is important to emphasise, why zones have to be pre-assigned to for person/zone/option
combinations. Had this not been done, there is a chance that two activities for the same
person with the same activity options at the same OTM zone. This would obviously be
the case, if the ζ-function had mapped the same OTM zone to two dierent LTM zones.
Although, the person might have performed the activity at two dierent places, chances
are that the two activities were performed at the same activity facility. Because of this,
each combination has to map deterministically to a predetermined LTM zone to overcome
this problem.
Step 2 is a crucial step of the algorithm. It is the step where each activity of the plan
goes from being assigned somewhere within a OTM zone, to be assigned at an activity
facility with a specic coordinate. This is done once for any household in the dataset,
and once per individual for any zone/option combination. The facilities are handled such
that when a facility has been used, it is put to the back of the queue again. This is
necessary, since facility sharing require facilities to be used more than once. By putting

Step 4 gives each activity an end time (and the rst activity a start time equal to 0). This
also means that any trip leg is given a start time, since their start times are equal to the
end time of the proceeding activity. Start times for subsequent activities have not been

considered time being late. This would however be quite articial, since we only know
when the activity began. We do not know when the activity should have begun, or in
which time period it was considered okay to begin.

Otherwise, there will be holes in the plan, where an individual have a trip to activity

The algorithm is explained in the following.
After the initialisation of step 0, step 1 extracts start- and endpoints from the links of the
shapele. This creates a new shapele containing nodes. They are actually only points,
but were given a unique id, making them full the criteria of a MATSim node. After
extraction, a cleaning of the points were needed in order to remove the many duplicated
points. Luckily, ArcGIS has such a tool readily available.
Step 2 joins each link with a set of shapele nodes. That is, for every link, for every node
it was checked if the node had the same coordinates as the start point or end point of
the link. If so, the id of the node was given to the link as well as a from-node or to-node
attribute, respectively.
Step 3 simply adds every node of the shapele to the MATSim network. Since a node
only contains an id attribute, and a coordinate, these could be taken directly from the
shapele.
Step 4 adds the links to the MATSim network. This is simply done by connecting the
corresponding to- and from-nodes of each link with a (straight) link for the link in the
forward direction (if it is open), and an opposite link in the backward direction (if it
is open). Notice that MATSim uses unidirectional links, why most road segments of the
shapele (those that are open in both direction) expands to two links in the MATSim
environment. To achieve a unique link for each road segment per direction, ids were
created by adding _Link_For or _Link_Back to the link ids of the shapele, depending
on whether the link corresponds to the forward or backward direction of the shapele
link.
In MATSim all links are straight lines from their start point to their end point, whereas
the original links had the possibility of containing some sort of curvature. Due to this,
the appearance of the links in MATSim is somewhat dierent from the original ones when
it comes to the geometry of the road segments. This is only a visual problem, though,
since the length attribute of the link secures that all links had the correct link  possibly
longer than the pure Euclidean distance from the start point to the end point.

4.4.3
Bus Stop Matching
We now move on to bus stop matching. It is here stops are assigned to a link of the network
and given a direction. The section will start with a brief description of alternative ways
of doing it.
4.4.3.1
Other Approaches
In order to overcome the problem, a variety of methods can be used. One would be to
manually assign each of the thirteen thousands stops. It would almost certainly be a very
reliable method, but it would take days weeks to go through all the stops, why this was
never really an option.
The intuitive approach would be to simply project each stop onto its nearest link. De-
pending on the angle between the road and the stop, the stop would then be assigned, so
that it would corresponds to being at the right side in the forward direction of the link.
This was not a good technique though, as there was discrepancies between the bus stops
locations provided by Movia and the navteq network used. It often led to stops being
assigned at the wrong side of a road.
Inspiration was then found in Li, 2012. Here it is proposed to do the following,
1. Determine candidate links for each stop.
2. Calculate the shortest projected distance between two adjacent bus stops.
3. Develop a network model that utilizes such relational information.
However, this was seen as a very complicated technique compared to the problem at
hand. Instead a dierent approach was chosen.
4.4.3.2
The Selected Approach
It was chosen to assume that the letting each stop be projected to its nearest link,
was actually the right thing to do. Although this is not always true, by investigating
the network, the success rate was seen to be so high, that it could not be justied to
construct an algorithm that could (possibly) increase it.
An early solution involved comparing the angle between the previous stop and the next
stop with the angle of the link onto which the current stop was projected. The idea was,
that if the two angles diered by less than 90 degrees, the stop should be assigned in the
forward direction. It turned out to be unsuccessful, see Figure 4.12a. It was especially
sensitive to sudden changes in the route.
Instead another approach was selected, that minimised the Euclidean distance between
start and end points of the associated link, and the previous and next stop, see Figure
4.12b. This method was included as a part of a larger Bus Stop Matching algorithmfor
determining the direction and location of every bus stop. The algorithm is presented

Step 6 assigns a direction for all stops having a certainty above 90%. 90% has been chosen
rather arbitrarily, but the success rate of the succeeding method where expected to be
below this value, why it seems to make sense to trust the method based on the shortest
Euclidean distance as long as the certainty is above 90%.
As mentioned earlier, determining the direction solely based on the location of the stops
are not perfect due to discrepancies between the reported locations and the actual location
of the road network. Nevertheless, in the cases where the certainty from the method using
the Euclidean distance is below 90%, it was deemed a better option to assign the direction
based on location. This is done in Step 7.
Step 8 is the nal step. It corrects the direction for stops assigned by the previous step in
those cases where two stops are assigned to the same link in the same direction. Since a
very detailed navteq network is used, the length of the links are generally very short. As
such, if two stops are assigned to the same link, they probably should have been assigned
with opposite directions (one stop on each side of the road). In order to correct for this,
the stop closest to the link will have its direction reversed.
Note, that this is only done for stops assigned a direction in step 7. Two signs assigned
to the same link in the same direction can occur, and it was chosen that if stops had a
higher certainty than 90% using the method based on the shortest Euclidean Distance,
the two stops should probably be assigned in the same direction.

4.6.2
Outcome
The number of transfer links created by the original method was so high, that when
searchin for the number dedicating 8gb of memory, the server ran out of memory while
nding the transfer links. In the deprecated version, we used a maximum connection of
667 metres, and only included stops whose stop groups were used as transfer points in
The Danish National Transport Model. This gave 77,037,786 transfer links.
By only including the transfer links between stops whose stop groups shared a transfer
link (or their stop group was the sme), the number was reduced to 14,690,789. By also
removing all stop groups happening outside the HT-area, the number was reduced to
14,633,429.
In order to reduce the number of transfer links even further, it was decided to merge all
routes with the same journey pattern (as the footnote says in Algorithm 4.5.1). This
reduced the number to 133,229. It also lead to a huge reduction of the transit router
network, which was reduced to 183,743 links and 52,605 nodes.

Chapter 6
Discussion
This chapter will discuss the model, and how it aects the obtained results. Since this is
an initial study of a larger process that intends to enable the use of the model for various
purposes, the discussion will focus on areas that inuence the bus delays, as well as things
that could generally improve the realism of the model.
The rst section will discuss the validity of the model. Here the theoretical reections
will be held against the modelled results when possible. Section ?? will briey mention
the shortcomings of the chosen approach for evaluating delays. Section 6.3 will discuss
to which degree the current model is suited for policy support, and which improvements
should be made to enhance its applicability and realism. Finally, Section 6.4 seeks to
prioritise among the potential improvements in order to suggest a guideline for future
work. The criteria for which the various areas of the model have been prioritised will be
presented initially, before pointing out the key areas for consideration in future studies.
6.1
Validity of the Current Model
This rst section of the discussion tries to determine the validity of the model. It will go
through the various elements of the model, beginning with the mobility simulator.
6.1.1
Mobsim
The mobility simulator (mobsim) is the key element of the model to consider, since this
study investigates operation of buses and is concerned with travel time.
The link model presented in Section 3.3.1 has some properties that seem more realistic
than a traditional aggregated model. Firstly, the varying time-dependent travel time is a
major improvement compared to macroscopic models where all users of the link (within
a time period of at least an hour or so) are assumed to experience the same travel time.
Another directly related realistic feature, is the fact that the travel time of a vehicle is
independent on behind vehicles.
A downside of the link model is that it is highly sensitive to the capacities. Having a
correct storage capacity is quite important, as having too low storage capacities would
lead to a high probability of preventing vehicles from entering a link. On the other hand,
having too high capacities allow too many vehicles to pass through nodes, reducing the
spill-back eects. Storage capacity ought to be easy to extract from the road network,
==PAGE:134==
118
Discussion
since it basically only depends on the area of the road. They are even handled by MATSim
internally. Thus the problem should not prove as big as it may sound at rst. In fact,
storage capacities are handled internally in MATSim.
Flow capacities, on the other hand, can not be extracted directly from the width and
length of the road, and must be given as input to the model for every link. Despite
the increased uncertainty regarding the values, having correct ow capacities seems to
be even more crucial than storage capacities. Too low ow capacities will increase the
general travel time signicantly under congested conditions, as links will let out too few
vehicles, whereas having too high capacities would cause nearly all vehicles to go by free
speed.
Furthermore, the ow capacity is the only parameter aecting the probability of a link
to get chosen next among the remaining out-links in a node for each time step. This will
be discussed shortly.
If the right values are used, the model is probably quite realistic regarding travel times
on links. The BPR formula has been used widely, because it is a continuous function that
has performed well compared to empirical measurements. However, it is not perfect, and
the fact that it predicts dierent travel times when there is 1 car per kilometre and 5 cars
per kilometre can be questioned. Cars would probably tend to go by free speed as long
as it is possible, although empirical data  at least for large roads  indicate otherwise
(Nielsen and Jørgensen, 2008). This could indicate that the speed is assumed a
little too high for close-to-breakdown situations in MATSim. This is also seen in the
modelled bus delay data, where buses seems to go a somewhat faster than the observed
data. However, since intersection modelling is not included in the current study, this
alone could explain the low travel times.
No matter whether the speed is to high or not, it is sure that the changes in travel
time happens much more much more sudden than with the BPR-formula. For a further
discussion on this, see Horni and Montini, 2013.
This shows that there is a big
dierence between having a soft constraint of capacity (as in the BPR formula), and
having a hard capacity as in this model.
The soft capacity values from the Danish
National Model has been used directly as hard constraints in MATSim without any
theoretical justication. However, the empirical results seem to indicate that realistic
travel times can actually be obtained in this way.
The node model was clearly created to support the theory underlying the link transmission
model in the most computational ecient way  and in a quite ow maximising way. As
such, it builds on some assumptions that do not seem all too realistic.
For instance, the node model chooses the next link to be considered by a probability based
on the ow capacity of the link. Arguably, cars arriving from a larger road (indicated by
the capacity) are most likely have right of way in this situation, one could also argue that if
there are more cars on another link, it is probably most likely that a vehicle from that link
will move next. By choosing capacity instead of ows makes the probabilities condition-
invariant, making it possible to pre-process the order in the next iteration. Whether this
==PAGE:135==
6.1 Validity of the Current Model
119
is utilised in the model remains unexamined, but due to the heavy utilisation of threads
(where there could be some idle time while the other threads are still running), it is very
likely to be the case.
Also, due to the short time steps of the model there is indeed a lack of continuity at
intersections. In reality, it is unrealistic to see very few cars entering the intersection at a
time if there are many cars on an in-link. In order to be realistic (on a micro level) they
should cluster more than is the case for the current node model. However, on average
(considering minutes instead of seconds) a reasonable number of vehicles enter the node,
why this is probably not that big of a problem.
Also, all moves are considered equal in a node. This means that there is no priority given
to trac travelling straight. It is also ignored in the model that right turning vehicles
are in less contact with the rest of the vehicles in the intersection, and vice versa for left
turning vehicles. This is far from realistic.
A realistic feature of the node model, on the other hand, is that cars have to wait in
queues before being allowed to enter the node. This means that even though a car user
has to turn right (down an empty link), he has to wait  possible for a long time  if
the car in front of him/her cannot move to his/her desired link. This feature seems very
realistic and helps creating spill-backs in the network. This is most likely one of the
greatest advantages of this model over a traditional aggregated model, where loads of
congestion can be contained within one single link.
Spill-backs are most likely over-estimated in the current model, though, since the model
does not consider turn lanes.
Lanes can be applied using an extension provided by
Grether and Nagel, 2013. However, it requires detailed data on intersection layouts
and signalling in order to be applicable. Thus, it has not been included in the study. It
should be noted though, that the node model is extremely suitable for using trac lights,
as the only real modication needed, is to exclude links facing a red light from the set of
possible in-links.
The lack of trac lights denitely aects the model. The node model is ow maximising,
in the sense that in each time step, it will reach the out-ow capacities as long as there is
a suciently large number of vehicles to move, and there is available space on the links
where they need to go. In real life, this is not the case for most intersections in urban
areas. This can both be due to trac lights that might prevent vehicles from moving
even though there is space, but it can also simply be because the movement they want
to do, has to cross transverse trac. These things are captured in microscopic models,
but is an area where the MATSim model falls short.
Since this study focuses on bus delays, the lack of properly modelled cyclists and pedes-
trians might not be a very big problem. Especially since the model does not consider
proper intersection modelling. Had such been modelled, however, the exclusion of cyclists
and (to some extend) pedestrians in the model would not have been fully negligible.
The results that was obtained with the full scale run may be explained by the occurrences
of breakdown certain places in the network, possibly even gridlocks. This is supported

a new plan will be chosen the following iteration. If the plan is the only one in the choice
set, the plan cannot be changed until the agent is chosen for plan mutation. And since
this probability is constant at 20%  not dependent on the scores of his/her choice set 
on average this will take four iterations to happen.

car trac is allowed, are so short that they will never be able to realise their full ow

not be that surprising after all.

is important to emphasise, that there does not seems to be any gridlock situations when

to lacking convergence.
6.1.2
Public Transport
One of the great things about MATSim, is that it allows public transport to be aected by
all types of trac, especially the trac induced by car. But it also allows modelling cars
to wait behind halting buses. Unfortunately, the necessary data regarding bus parking
type at each stops was not collected for this study, why no bus stop was assumed to block
trac. For the sake of this study  where we consider bus running times  it is not a
major concern. Buses seldom wait behind other buses, since they most often has to halt
at the stop themselves. Had the aim being evaluating the eects of implementing bus
lane, on the other hand, this feature would have been vital.
Since the model is expected to be multi-purpose during future studies, the model has been
set up to be compatible with buses blocking lanes already. As such, it can be implemented
once the data has been collected. If there were no intentions on utilising this feature in
the future, every bus-stop could simply have been snapped to the nearest node of the
network, and assigned to a loop link in this node, as done in Weiss et al., 2014. This
could have superseded the majority of the bus stop matching algorithm (Algorithm 4.4.2)
and the link splitting Algorithm 4.4.5.
When discussing the public transport model, it becomes clear that the expression agent
based modelling might be an exaggeration regarding the public transport users. The
public transport users choose a plan according to the schedule (more on this in Section
??, after which they decide upon a set of stops to go by. When reaching a stop, they
blindly enter the rst vehicle servicing the next stop on their route, and continue like this
until their reach their destination.
This approach is unrealistic in many ways. For instance the agents do not consider other
bus stops than the predetermined ones. To illustrate why this is a problem, consider two
bus stops located within 100 metres: Stop A with an hourly express bus with a travel
time of 30 minutes, and the stop B with a bus with a travel time of 35 minutes. If the
==PAGE:138==
122
Discussion
agent misses the connection for the express bus, the agent will wait a whole hour on the
next bus at the stop, even though he/she could have saved nearly an hour by using the
nearby bus stop. This situation can easily be made even more obscure, if the agent misses
the last bus of a bus stop. In this case he/she will simply stay at the bus-stop until the
simulation runs out.
Another pitfall can occur if an agent has two or more potential stops to which he/she can
walk to get to the public transport network.. Only the stop appearing in the day plan
will be considered, even though they in reality might be equally reasonable to use.
Yet another unsatisfactory behaviour can be found if an express bus arrives just behind
a regular bus at bus stop. Then the agent will once again enter the rst suited vehicle,
which in this case is the regular bus rather waiting a couple of seconds for the express
bus.
6.1.3
Replanning
The fact that a meaningful rerouting for public transport users cannot be done is clearly
a weakness. Plan mutation for public transport users have been done in other studies
by manipulating the plans in the temporal dimension, or by changing modes. However,
the model should still be able to support realistic rerouting for public transport users.
A large proportion of the public transport users most likely do not have a car available,
why mutating their plans to include cars would be meaningless. Such car availability
information was not provided in the trip data used in this study.
Whereas the temporal dimension is extremely exible for car user, it is not necessarily
the case for public transport users. For instance, if an agents has to travel a long distance
by train, there will most likely only be a departure every half hour or so. Thus, even
quite large time mutations does not necessarily change the plan.
Thus, there seems to be a need for extending MATSim to include a meaningful plan
mutation strategy for public transport users. This would allow the public transport users
to choose between dierent plans in their choice set. The problem with only having a
single plan, is that the agents cannot respond to how their executed plan turns out to
score. Their only option is to use the same plan the following iteration. This actually
means that the public transport users do not behave as agents, but as subservient entities.
It should be noted, however, that for some individuals it might be realistic to only have
one possible plan in their choice set. This will especially be the case in rural areas or in
the outskirts of the city, where public transport is sparse.
Before considering the scoring of plans, it is worth discussing the replanning framework
as a concept. The choice sets in MATSim is very dierent from what is normally used in
transport model. It consist of a plan that has just been executed in an iteration (except if
this plan has just been removed), and then a series of plans that were executed in previous,
outdated iterations. One could argue that the previous iteration is also outdated, though,
since its score most likely cannot be reproduced directly in the following iteration due to
==PAGE:139==
6.1 Validity of the Current Model
123
the choice of others and the stochasticity of the model.
By having agents being allowed from suboptimal plans (with some probability), makes it
very hard to talk about an actual equilibrium at any point. In a traditional SUE, a large
portion of individuals (those of a class between the same OD-pairs) are spread out on
various routes according to their perceived travel time, and equilibrium is reached once
no individual can improve his/her perceived utility (Daganzo and She, 1977).
In order for such an equilibrium to happen in the day-plan framework, where no indi-
viduals are equal, and thus cannot be spread out across dierent routes, equilibrium can
only happen if agents at some point shifts to a score-maximising strategy. Then equilib-
rium could say to be found when every agent chooses the same plan for two consecutive
iterations. This will never happen when constantly allowing the agent to choose between
the various day plans of his/her choice set. However, not allowing the agent to choose
suboptimal day plans may not seem as a good idea either, since the reason why the plan
executed badly in the rst place, could be due to the network being ill conditioned.
Thus, there seems to be a trade-o between wanting a balanced equilibrium-like situa-
tion in the end, and having the correct day-plans in the choice set when reaching the
equilibrium-like situation. There is most likely a need for changing strategies throughout
the iteration process, such that initially all day-plans are likely to be chosen (almost re-
gardless of their score), but in the end only the best-scoring plans should be considered.
Since the choice set is dynamic, the need for this is probably not that big after all, as
only current (planned) optimal plans can enter the choice set. The choice set itself will
thus also reach an equilibrium-like situation for each agent.
6.1.4
Scoring
Regarding scoring there seems to be a general need for calibrating the parameters. Even
this study only uses travel time and number of transfers 1 to score the executed day plans,
there still is a need for calibrating these parameters. The ratio between the parameters
for travel time, βt, and the number of transfers, βn, have an impact on the number of
transfers being present in the nal plans. The β parameter of the logit model controls
variation in selected plans within the choice set of an agent.
In MATSim arriving late at a facility is normally scored with a very big negative score.
However, in this study no data was available regarding the desired arrival time of the
individuals in the trip data. Only the actual arrival time was available. The actual arrival
time could have been used as desired arrival time. In that way the model would punish
additional travel time (compared to the real-life trip) harder than the travel time that
was also observed within the real-life trip. This could have been used as some sort of
incentive to force the travel times of the model to not exceed the observed travel times,
but such approach was not chosen.

6.3
Usability as a Policy Support Tool
This section discusses how the model can be used for policy support. Both how and to
which extend the existing model can be used, but also to point at how the model through
minor (or possibly larger) improvements can be used for policy support in various area
of transport planning.

First of all, it seems as if the model has the potential to correctly model bus running times.
The deviations from the observed data can most likely be reduced a lot without changing
the model as such. Changes to the road network should alone shift the mean value of the
bus running times to a level that is more in correspondence with the observed data. The
too low mean value put aside, the model seems to pick up the general uctuations of the
bus running times.
However, it seems as if the mobility simulator of the model may be a little too sensible to
congestion. The congestion occurs suddenly, and vehicles remain unaected in conditions
close to capacity limits. As previously discussed this is due to the underlying Fundamental
Diagram of the model which deviates from the norm. This might cause the model to be
unsuitable for accurate time predictions, but is hard to tell at the moment based on a
uncalibrated model that also does not include proper intersection modelling.
Regardless of whether the bus running times are exactly correct, or deviates a few minutes,
the model still seem to have a lot of potential as a tool for policy support in other areas.
Despite the rather poor transit (re)routing technique of the current model, it still allow
following passengers throughout their day. This gives important insight to transfers, and
will allow to model the interaction between public transport modes much better than in
traditional macroscopic models where then private and public transport are decoupled,
but also better than microscopic models that typically will only feature one corridor, and
ignore the interaction with the rest of the network.
It can also be used as the basis for simple appraisals of transport projects for projects
that are too small to be suitable for a traditional cost benet analysis. This could be the
implementation of long stretch of dedicated bus lane for instance.
As a nal note, it should be stressed that the computation time of the model when using
a full population is less than satisfactory. That the model is not able to reach some sort
of steady state within two days of calculation time using 20 cores on a high performance
computer, is not very desirable. Such a model should be able to run a variety of dierent
scenarios, why it would be preferable to have a considerably faster model.

6.4.1
The Road Network
Since the bus running times were generally too low in periods without congestion, this is
a sign that the used road network had free ow times that implied an unrealistically fast
bus operation. Thus, it seems as a good idea to try and nd more appropriate free speed
travel times than the ones used in the current model.
Another problem is the sheer size of the network. It is extremely detailed, and as already
described previously, includes a lot of small links that gets an articial low ow capacity.
This makes the network very sensitive to local congestion in some areas of the network.
And due to the fact that spill-backs are modelled, a false congestion may spread across
the network due to the short links.
Also the size of the network contributed to the model being extremely memory demand-
ing. An agent based model should have a detailed road network, but a lot of the links in
the model does not provide much added realism to the model, compared to the amount
of memory the network is using. It should be possible to reduce the network through
intelligent heuristics.
6.4.2
Intersections and Trac Signals
A more realistic handling of intersection, and an inclusion of trac signals would be
ideal. The model would probably be used to mostly model changes in or close to the
central parts of Copenhagen. Here trac signals are numerous, and intersections take a
substantial amount of time to travel through due to the high trac volume.
Also, such an implementation has already been developed by Grether, 2014, why it
will be expected to be easier to implement, than to build improvements from scratch.
Thus, it could in all probability be used to increase the realism within a reasonable time
horizon.
It was also initially intended to be included in the thesis, but was dismissed due to lack
of of time.
An obvious weakness, is that it would increase the size of the network quite dramatically.
This might lead to computational challenges  possibly too big to overcome. Also, it has
 as to the knowledge of the author  never been used whilst simulating public transport

All in all, the implementing intersections and trac signals to the model should be given
a high priority in future studies. It is hard to judge whether the computational burdens
would be too large, but it denitely has the potential to increase the realism of the model
a lot.
6.4.3
Rerouting for Public Transport
Implementing a rerouting for public transport users is absolutely key. The current situ-
ation, where public transport users do not behave as agents because they only have one
plan to choose from is not viable. The question is what to be used instead.
It could be done by implementing a whole new way of routing. This would probably be
hard to do. Nevertheless, Ordóñez and Erath, 2013 proposed an event-based approach
to replace the schedule-based transit routing, but it is not a part of the default MATSim
package.
A second way of doing it, could be to use the event of the previous iterations to modify
the schedule, in the next iteration, so that it corresponds to what occurred in the previous
iteration. This strategy is denitely not bullet-proof either, as the public transport would
(at least in the initial iterations) follow a (most likely too) pessimistic schedule, and also
possess some challenges involving plans of the choice sets possibly no longer being oered
by the public transport schedule. However, Neumann et al., 2015 uses an adaptive
schedule to determine minibus routes in South Africa, why an implementation similar to
that is possible within the MATSim framework.
A third way, and probably the best way, to change this, would be to allow the reroute
module to nd more than one shortest path. In that way, each user could have a choice set
of k-shortest paths (Eppstein, 1997), between which they could then select according to
some sort of distribution. It could also be through link elimination (in a public transport
context, probably a departure elimination), see Azevedo et al., 1993, or link penalty,
see Barra et al., 1993.
Another approach involving extending the choice set, can be found in Oliveros, 2013.
However, instead of including suboptimal plans, the plans were included by changing the
priorities of the elements in the plan. Thus, a plan minimizing walking, a plan minimising
travel time, and a plan combining the two (as a trade-o) were used. Presumably this
idea could be extended by randomly mutating the utility parameters of each person to
create a new perceived shortest path. This would be similar to a normal error component
logit mixture as presented in Walker et al., 2007, but due to the agent-based setting of
MATSim where agents cannot be grouped into classes between an OD-pair the resulting
model would not be the same. The method could be implemented in a parallel fashion,
though.
A very ambitious approach would be to use equilibrated choice sets as developed in
Rasmussen et al., 2015. No matter what, in order to get realistic responses for public

6.4.4
The Entities of Public Transport
In future studies, it should be considered whether it is possible to decouple the arrival
and departure times from the public transport route. The current coupling creates a
trade-o between realism on one hand, and memory consumption and computation time
on the other hand. It should be possible to allow for heterogeneous scheduling on the
same route throughout the day without causing the transit router network to explode.
This study did not distinguish between dierent bus types, nor did it consider the fact
that each bus is not only used on one departure. In reality, the same vehicles handles
multiple departures, and if it is late on one of them, it is bound to run late on the following.
Vehicle data can be extracted from the currently available data, but there simply was not
enough time to include these in the model. It would not require additional calculation,
and neither would it require substantially additional memory. More vehicle types would
be used (dierent sizes of buses), but on the other hand, less actual vehicles would be
used.
6.4.5
Cyclists
Due to the geographical location of the model, it could also make sense to study how
cyclists can be integrated in the model. MATSim has generally be developed in Berlin
and Zurich, where cycling is nowhere near as popular as in Copenhagen. This would not
only be interesting solely for the sake of modelling cyclists  which would be interesting
in itself  but also because the heavy trac on bicycle lanes impose increased travel time
on the surrounding trac in connection with turns.
Although some study has been done concerning cyclists in MATSim, the literature on
the topic is quite sparse. A model of the city of Patna in Eastern India (Agarwal et al.,
2015) included cyclists using the same infrastructure as the rest of the trac, but with
a signicantly lower speed. A similar approach was used in Dobler, 2013 which neither
featured interaction between cyclists and other modes.
This means that, most likely, a whole new way of including cyclists in the model will
have to be developed in the process if it is going to be implemented. Because of that it
must be deemed as an extremely ambitious task to carry through.
6.4.6
Delay Analysis
Future work should include a proper analysis. This initial analysis is very sparse, and does
only consider the temporal dimension by having time of day as a variable. Actually, in
order to study the delays thoroughly an analysis must also include the internal temporal
dimension, i.e. how far the bus is on its route. This is obvious the bus will most likely
be on time on the rst bus stop regardless of time of day.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
As the time trac volume increases in urban areas around the world, there will be a
continuing debate regarding which mode to favour when making infrastructural changes.
In Copenhagen, a variety of experts have argued for upgrading the public transport
system, and the debate is steadily gaining ground on the corridors of Copenhagen City
Hall. However, without appropriate policy support tools to back up the putative benets
of such improvements, comprehensive reprioritisation of the limited road-space in Central
Copenhagen is unlikely to happen. This thesis has sought to take the initial steps of
allowing such a model to be available for Eastern Denmark, and to test whether such a
model is suitable for modelling bus bus delays.
The thesis began by briey outlining the increasing trac in Copenhagen and the ac-
companying congestion.
This led to a summary of some political tendencies towards
reprioritisation of the streets in Copenhagen, followed by an explanation of the need for
modern policy support tools. Such tools should be able to model the interaction between
various modes, realistically, and do it for a reasonably sized area.
Chapter 2 reviewed the available relevant literature. A series of statistical approaches
as well as separated simulation-based studies to model bus delays were presented. A
few studies dealing with intermodal prioritisation in a more general perspective was also
presented, before turning towards a series of micro- and mesoscopic models capable of
simulating simultaneously assignment of public and private transport. Finally, a short
review of MATSim was conducted, including relevant extensions to the default core model
along the way.
Chapter 3 went into detail with the various components of MATSim. The chapter ex-
plained the routing algorithms, the link transmission model, the nodel model, and the
replanning module among other things. Some key insights from this chapter was that
the routing module was very weak for public transport, and that no suitable replan-
ning strategy could be used for passengers of public transport in the study. The link
transmission also gave rise to discussions as the underlying fundamental was critically
analysed through theoretical considerations and a small example. It was argued debated
the link transmission was too discrete too be realistic, just as the assumption regarding
link length-dependent shock wave speed was found to cause troubles in high-resolution
networks by never allowing short links to reach their maximum ow capacity. The node
model also gave rise to concerns, as the ow capacity dependent priorisitations of in-links
in a node, and the lack of dierentiating between the disturbance caused by dierent
turning moves was found to be a shortage.
==PAGE:148==
132
Conclusion
Chapter 4 dealt with development of the various input needed to get a MATSim model
up and running. A large variety of dierent algorithms were developed in order to provide
suitable input. Two of them  dealing with demand generation  created activity facilities
and daily plans based on trip data on a somewhat aggregated level. The development
even included methods from political science in order to assign realistic locations for
facilities between which the trips could be created. Bus stop matching turned out to
be quite a challenge, and the nal algorithms used three sub-algorithms to establish a
hierarchy from which a direction was determined. The key sub algorithm utilised the
start and end points of the associated link of a stop, and choosing the direction that
minimised the Euclidean distance between these and the proceeding and following stop.
Schedules were created from delay data, whereas providing transfer links without causing
the transit router network to explode required changes to the source code of the software.
Results of the model was presented in Chapter 5. The model was run using a 1%, 10%
and 100% population. The two smaller tests managed to reach a steady state like level,
whereas the full scale test seemed to have large uctuations, and did not nd a steady
state within 2 days of calculation time. Bus delays were extracted from the model for
each of the three runs. They were visually compared with observed bus delay data as a
function of time of day. This was both done for all buses at once (fully aggregated level),
for all runs of bus 5A, and for a single run of bus 5A. The 10% test gave very promising
results, whereas the 1% had the buses moving too fast, and the full scale tests suered
from severe periodic congestion.
The underlying reasons for the obtained results, and the assumptions of the model were
discussed in Chapter 6. The slight tendency of buses moving a little to far was found to
likely be caused by the lack of proper intersection modelling and lack of trac signal. The
severe congestion in the full scale test could partly be explained by the high-resolution
navteq network with many short links that articially lowered the capacity of the net-
work. On a more general level the chapter discussed how the model could be used for
policy support and which improvements should be made. It was concluded that given
that the thesis is an initial study, the results were very promising, and that the model
has potential of being used for policy support regarding infrastructural changes regarding
public transport. Future studies should try to implement an already existing intersection
and trac lights extension, as well as implementing some way of increasing the current
solo plan choice set for public transport users, when the model is used for assignment
purposes only. Further studies should also make a thorough statistical comparison be-
tween the observed and modelled data. If such veries that the model can model bus
operation realistically, the model could indeed be a refreshing new possibility to use when
predicting the eects of projects aiming at improving public transport.

Appendix B
Project Plan
B.1
Learning Objectives
• Organize and carry out a project work with a clear structure including project
statement, relevant theory, analyses and discussions.
• Review and discuss existing methodologies used for estimation of bus running times.
• Review, systematize and discuss existing research on agent-based multi-modal mod-
els.
• Convert data regarding activity day-plans, physical infrastructure, bus running
times and timetables to a detail-level and format compatible with MATSim, and
describe the data conversion process critically.
• Describe the assignment model used for simulating vehicles and their interaction,
and discuss the underlying assumptions.
• Propose how the model is used to choose between agent day-plans, and suggest
possible improvements.
• Apply MATSim to approximate the dynamic stochastic user equilibrium on a week-
day for Eastern Denmark.
• Make an initial analysis and evaluation of the validity of the simulated bus running
times, by comparing with real-life arrival data.
• Discuss the applicability of the model for policy support, including possible pitfalls
with the applied methods.
• Write a technical report and give an oral presentation, both in an understandable,
well-structured and well-documented form.
• Submit an extended abstract to an international conference within the eld of study.

Extended Abstract for hEART 2016
Bus and Passenger Delay Micro-Simulation within a
Combined Agent-Based Multi-Modal Assignment Model
Mads Paulsen†∗, Thomas K. Rasmussen†, Otto A. Nielsen†, Carlo G. Prato‡
†Department of Transport, Technical University of Denmark,
Bygningstorvet 116B, 2800 Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark.
‡School of Civil Engineering, The University of Queensland,
Brisbane 4072, Queensland, Australia.
Motivation
Most large-scale transport models are modelling passenger ﬂows of private and public trans-
port independently of each other and mostly without considering punctuality. However,
there is an increasing competition of road-space in dense urban road corridors, where car
traﬃc inﬂuences the operations of buses and vice versa, and where passenger ﬂows also
inﬂuence the dwell time for buses. The Danish Commission on Congestion, 2013 dis-
cussed the prioritisation between buses and cars in the central built-up areas of Copenhagen,
however, without having modelling tools to evaluate the impacts of suggested policies. The
idea of the present study originated from this debate, and has been supported by data from
both the bus and road authorities. Moreover, the purpose of the study has been to eval-
uate bus running times within a disaggregate micro-simulation model capable of capturing
the interaction between private and public modes. The model has been implemented for a
large-scale case study, and a large dataset containing observations of bus delays have been
used to calibrate the bus operation.
Background and Prior Studies
Traditionally, in large-scale transport models the assignment of car traﬃc and public trans-
port has been separated, ignoring the congestion caused by the other modes. Even though
the added congestion caused by buses is relatively small in some developed countries (Gib-
son, Baeza, and Willumsen, 1989), there are examples of road corridors in cities like
Copenhagen with a large share of bus transport that substantially aﬀect the car traﬃc.
Bus running times, on the other hand, are highly aﬀected by car traﬃc during peak hours
(McKnight, 2003). Hence, traditional large-scale transport models are inappropriate for
evaluation of bus running times – and the variation of these.
This topic is of great interest for bus authorities, since it can be a useful assisting tool
when scheduling timetables and thus increase the robustness of the operation. It also has

wider applications, e.g. to support decisions on establishing separate bus-lanes, implement-
ing signal prioritisation, etc.
To overcome the task, a variety of alternative methods have been suggested to model bus
running times along single corridors. Among these are Gibson, Baeza, and Willumsen,
1989, who simulated bus running times by splitting the travel time into components of
time spent moving, waiting in junctions, and waiting at bus stops; McKnight, 2003, who
proposed a simple statistical approach relating the bus running times to car travel times,
boardings, and number of bus stops; and T´etreault and El-Geneidy, 2010, who created
an extensive general statistical model to predict the running time of a new bus route based
on existing routes.
However, none of the aforementioned methods model the car traﬃc internally.
For
some analyses an integrated approach may seem unnecessary, but it is highly needed when
considering building new infrastructure such as dedicated bus lanes, by-pass roads, etc., in
order to accurately predict the eﬀects. Several models are now handling this by simulating
public transport as well as car traﬃc within a large-scale agent-based assignment framework,
including MILATRAS (Wahba and Shalaby, 2005), BusMezzo (Cats, 2013) and –
the model used in this study – the open-source software MATSim (Horni, Nagel, and
Axhausen, 2015).
Starting out as a uni-modal model, Rieser, Grether, and Nagel, 2009; Rieser
and Nagel, 2010 added mode-choice to MATSim and augmented the software to handle
combined assignment of car traﬃc and public transport for the area of Zurich.
Neu-
mann, Balmer, and Rieser, 2012 later extended the model and applied it to the
Berlin/Brandenburg Metropolitan Region with calibrated boarding and alighting times for
various public transport modes based on data acquired from a large sample collected man-
ually (Neumann, Kern, and Leich, 2014). A full combined model also exists for Sin-
gapore (Future Cities Laboratories, 2016). Additionally, Grether and Nagel, 2013
presented an implementation of traﬃc lights to MATSim, whereas Grether, 2014 success-
fully modeled and simulated the car traﬃc in and around Cottbus in diﬀerent illustrative
scenarios whilst also simulating traﬃc lights.
So far, no studies have used extensive bus running time data to evaluate the bus running
times in such a model. The open source nature of MATSim allows adjusting it to model new
functional relationships related to the simultaneous simulation of cars, buses, and passengers
in the same corridor, if such are needed to replicate observed running times. This task is
important since inaccurate running times of public transport lead to unrealistic passenger
ﬂows.
Method and Case Study
The purpose of this study has been to develop a micro-simulation model for bus operation
within a combined private and public transport assignment model for Eastern Denmark.
The study uses data collected by the regional bus authority, Movia, to evaluate the bus
running times retrieved from the model. The data consists of scheduled and actual arrival
and departure times at every bus stop for the majority of all operating buses in the area.
This allows evaluating the simulated running times with a very high level of detail.
The study furthermore includes simulation of traﬃc lights within the combined assign-
ment of car traﬃc and public transport.
The addition of traﬃc lights simulation adds
substantial realism to the combined simulation of car traﬃc and public transport.
Road networks and traﬃc lights (and other intersections) were received from the Danish
National Transport Model.
The ACTUM research project (The Danish Council for

Strategic Research, 2016) delivered daily activity patterns, whereas Movia provided
information about bus stops, timetables and vehicle types.
The study initially calibrates the model for selected corridors. The corridors include
congested narrow-space corridors (“Street Canyons”) where buses and cars share lanes, cor-
ridors with full or partly bus-lanes, and wider roads with and without bus lanes. Experiences
gained concerning large-scale simulation of the Copenhagen network are also presented, in-
cluding the experiences transferring data from more traditional models into the very detailed
simultaneous simulation-based model-framework. The study also addresses the level of ran-
dom noise in such a simulation, and discusses the applicability of such models for policy
support.
Results show that the microsimulation approach simulates the traﬃc satisfactory by
obtaining bus delays consistent with the observed data.
Furthermore, the model shows
realistic changes in bus running times when exposed to minor alterations to the physical
infrastructure, indicating that the model is suitable for predicting the impacts of small
actions concerning the prioritisation of shared spaces.
