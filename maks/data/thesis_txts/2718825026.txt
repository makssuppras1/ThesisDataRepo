Preface
The following is a Master Thesis project representing 30 ECTS points. It is a product of experimental
work carried out by Mette Røkke Bjørnson. The experimental work took place in the autumn and win-
ter of 2022/2023 at the department of Health technology at the Technical University of Denmark. This

project is part of the immunotherapy cancer research done by the SRH group (T cell and cancer) and
under the subsection of using CRISPR/cas9 in T cells lead by Postdoc Kristoffer Haurum Johansen.

Abstract
Immunotherapy entails therapies that modulate the immune system to fight diseases like cancer. Using
immunotherapy as a cancer treatment has revolutionized ways cancer is treated. Adoptive cell ther-
apies where the autologous T cell receptor (TCR) is replaced by cancer-specific TCRs have proven
to be an effective treatment. Using the gene editing method, CRISPR/Cas9 to insert cancer-specific
TCRs have several advantages compared to traditional viral transductions, including reduced off-
target effects, increased target specificity and reduced mispairing between the endogenous and trans-
genic TCR.
In this thesis, I present optimized CRISPR/Cas9 knockin (KI) of cancer-specific TCRs into primary
human T cells (CRISPR TCR-T cells) using different DNA templates for homology-directed repair. I
furthermore tested the possibility of expanding cancer-specific T cells using Antigen-presenting scaf-
folds. Lastly, I performed a killing assay to validate the efficiency of CRISPR TCR-T cells in vitro.
I have found that several HDRT types can be used to efficiently KI cancer-specific TCRs into primary
human T cells. Furthermore, I have shown that these T cells can kill target cells. Lastly, I have found
that scaffold expansion of specific T cells results in very high percentages of specific cells with similar
phenotypes to cytokine-expanded cells.
These results emphasize the potential use of CRISPR/Cas9 to insert cancer-specific TCRs for use
in immunotherapy. Furthermore, the result suggests the potential use of scaffold-expanded CRISPR
TCR-T cells to reach a higher amount of antigen-specific T cells.

Introduction
According to estimates made by World Health Organization (WHO), cancer is the first or second
leading cause of death before the age of 70 in 112 out of 183 countries. Both the burden of cancer
incidents and the mortality is increasing, probably due to increased life span, growth on the worlds
population and the changes in distribution and prevalence of the main risk factors [1]. In 2020 there
was an estimated 19.3 million new cases of cancer worldwide, and almost 10 million deaths caused
by cancer [2]. By 2040 it is expected that the global burden has grown to 27.5 million new cases each
year, and mortality to 16.3 million cases yearly [3]. Traditional cancer treatment includes chemother-
apy and radiotherapy. Even though these treatment options have come a long way, there are several
side effects associated with them. Chemotherapy is toxic. Both for the cancer cells and for our
healthy cells. This lack of specificity can lead to severe side effects and in the worst cases even death
for some patients [4]. Radiotherapy can be delivered with great precision, but will still target some
healthy surrounding cells. Some tumor types remain resistant to radiotherapy and in some cases can
the tumor recur shortly after treatment due to acquired resistance [4, 5]. Immunotherapy is a therapy
form in which the immune system is manipulated to fight diseases like cancer more efficiently. Adop-
tive cell therapy (ACT) is a type of immunotherapy where the T cells of a patient’s own immune cells
is used to fight cancer cells. The patient’s tumor infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) are either expanded
ex vivo or healthy T cells modified by either inserting a new cancer-specific T cell receptor (TCR) or
inserting a chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) that targets extracellular targets on cancer cell without
recognition of pMHC. CRISPR/Cas9 can be used to insert tumor-specific TCR into primary human
T cells (CRISPR TCR-T cell) and it is used in ACT today [6]. Transduction of tumor-specific TCRs
using CRISPR/Cas9 usually results in about 10% KI. Cell sorting can therefore be used to remove
homologue T cells from the population. To increase the specific cell count for ACT, Ag-scaffold can
be used as they only expand tumour-specific T cell [6, 7].
The overall aim of this Master Thesis project is to improve CRISPR/Cas9 KI of tumor specific TCRs
into primary human T cells, and following KI expand only CRISPR TCR-T cells with an Ag-scaffold.

1.1
Immune system
The immune system is the system of the body that can recognize and fight harmful pathogens. The
immune system consists of 3 lines of defence. The first line of defence is the skin and mucosal
surfaces that prevent pathogens from entering the body. The second line of defence is the innate or
natural immune system, while the third line of defence is the adaptive or acquired immune system.
An important factor of both adaptive and innate immunity is the ability to distinguish between self
and non-self [8–10].
1.1.1
Innate Immunity
The innate immune system is fast-acting and has a response time of minutes to hours [10]. It is known
as the ”natural” immune system since it, contrary to the adaptive immune system, doesn’t use somat-
ically recombined antigen receptors or memory to recognize and kill pathogens. The innate immune
system consists of different classes of soluble molecules including antimicrobial enzymes, antimi-
crobial peptides and plasma proteins known as the complement system. These soluble molecules
are present in extracellular fluids, blood, and epithelial secretion and can either kill or weaken the
pathogen. If this defence isn’t enough, innate immune cells become activated by pattern recognition
receptors (PRRs) that detect pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs), which are particu-
lar molecules present in or on the pathogen. If the second line of defence is insufficient to kill the
pathogen, the third line of defence is activated [9–11].
1.1.2
Adaptive Immunity
The purpose of the adaptive immune system is to recognize non-self antigens presented on major
histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules and distinguish them from self-antigens. The lym-
phocytes of the adaptive immune system use somatically recombined antigen receptors to recognize
peptides on MHC molecules and thereby generate an immune response to eliminate the pathogen.
Because the adaptive immune system needs to create antigen-specific lymphocytes, there is a lag time
between exposure of the pathogen and until the adaptive immune response kicks in, usually, days
to weeks [12]. The two primary immune cell types of the adaptive immune system are B cells and
T cells. Both of these cell types use somatically recombined antigen receptors. One key feature of
adaptive immunity is that B and T cells following activation can create memory cells that support a

1.2
T cells
1.2.1
T cell development
T and B lymphocytes originate from common lymphoid precursors (CLP). CLPs are generated in the
bone marrow from hematopoietic stem cells. Some CLPs enter the bloodstream, where they migrate
to the thymus to develop into T cells. The rest will stay in the bone marrow to develop into B cells.
CLP will enter the thymus through the cortical-medulla junction of the thymus. In the thymus, the T
cell progenitor must first commit to the T cell lineage. This is done when the earliest intrathymic T

T cell development requires 2 checkpoints. The first is the commitment to either CD4+CD8+ αβ T

1.2.2
T Cell Receptor
The T cell receptor (TCR) is expressed on the surface of all human T cells [21]. It is a glycosylated
heterodimeric protein consisting of an extracellular α and β chain. These are connected to trans-
membrane regions and short intracellular tails. The α and β chain of the TCR consists of a variable and
a constant region connected by disulfide bonds. The variable extracellular region is immunoglobulin
(Ig-)like, and binds to peptide:MHC complexes on antigen-presenting cells to activate the T cell.
The extracellular constant domain non-covalently interacts with the CD3 molecules [21, 22]. CD3 is
composed of 3 polypeptides, CD3E, CD3δ and CD3γ. The polypeptides are extracellular, and consist
of an Ig like constant domain, a transmembrane domain, and intracellular domain much longer than
the TCRs. Another CD3 subunit, CD3ζ, consist of a long intracellular region, a transmebrane region
and a short extracellular region (figure 2) [21].

T cell activation requires stimulation through the TCR and costimulatory signals. This results in

pathway can be influenced by cytokine signals from the environment, other T cells or itself. It there-

Signal 1: TCR-pMHC
The TCR on na¨ıve T cells scans peptides presented on MHC class I (rec-

presenting cells. Once the TCR binds to pMHC, the extracellular domain of the co-receptors CD4 (for

The intracellular domain binds to Lck, which is then recruited to the TCR. Lck then phosphorylates
immunoreceptor tyrosine-based activation motif (ITAM) on CD3 and the ζ chain of the TCR com-
plex. Once phosphorylated, the ITAM motif contains two binding sites where Zap70 can bind and
become activated. Zap70 thus propagates signalling events from the TCR resulting in downstream
activation [29–31].

For the T cell to fully activate and proliferate, a co-

human CD4+ T cells and on about half of the circulating CD8+ T cells, since CD28 is downregulated
after TCR activation [32, 33]. CD28 binds the ligand CD80 and CD86, which are members of the
B7 family, on antigen-presenting cells. The co-stimulatory signal prevents the T cells from becoming
activated by self-antigen and triggering an unwanted autoimmune response. If only signal 1 is initi-

Signal 3: Cytokine Stimulation
The differentiation of T cells is dependent on signals from the mi-

phenotypes, depending on which cytokines the T cells receive. If a T cell only receives signal 1 (TCR-

and won’t create a memory population. Presence of cytokines will result in survival, effective effector
function, and memory formation towards the antigen [28, 29, 31]. For CD4+ T cells, the cytokine
milieu it receives determines which effector cell it will become. This can either be TH1, TH2, or
TH17 CD4+ T cells. CD8+ T cells require cytokines to become effective cytotoxic T lymphocytes
[28, 29, 31].
1.3
T cell phenotypes
CD8+ T cells consist of different subtypes, ranging from na¨ıve precursors to fully developed effec-
tor T cells. Two extracellular receptors can be used to broadly classify T-cell effector and mem-
ory phenotypes. CD45RA is only present on na¨ıve or effector T cells and can therefore be used
to distinguish memory cells (CD45RA-) from non-memory cells (CD45RA+).
The lymph node
homing receptor, CCR7, can be used to distinguish cells that migrate to the secondary lymphoid
organs (SLOs) (CCR7+), from cells that do not home to SLOs (CCR7-). Where CCR7+ cells are
na¨ıve (CD45RA+CCR7+) or central memory (CD45RA-CCR7+). CCR7- T cells are effector mem-
ory (CD45RA+CCR7-) or effector (CD45RA-CCR7-) T cells. Na¨ıve T cell uses the CCR7 receptor
to home to the SLOs where they can encounter dendritic cells (DC) presenting antigen on their MHC
molecule. If the na¨ıve T cells become activated by the DC, they differentiate into effector T cells.
Effector T cells do not have CCR7 on their surface and thereby lose the ability to adhere to the SLO.
Instead, they acquire the expression of other adhesion molecules and chemokine receptors that will
help them migrate to inflamed tissue [33–35].
Other extracellular markers can be used to determine the subtype and effector functions of the T cells.
These include, but are not limited to CD27, CD28, PD-1, CD39, and CD57. CD27 and CD28 are co-
receptors regulated during TCR activation. After activation through the TCR, CD28 is downregulated

1.5
Immune Invasion
Once the immune synapse is generated, multiple activating and inhibitory pathways within the lym-
phocyte are triggered. It’s the sum of these pathways that determine the outcome of the interaction. To
survive, malignant or infected cells will try to evade the immune response by e.g., downregulating the
activating signals and upregulating inhibitory signals. This is done by excreting immunosuppressive
cytokines, reducing antigen recognition, triggering immune checkpoint molecules, and excluding the
immune cells from the tumor microenvironment (TME). Several immunotherapies have been devel-
oped to target these escape mechanisms (see section 1.6). However, for the cytotoxic mechanisms to
occur, active apoptotic signalling within the target cell is required, and this mechanism is often sup-
pressed in cancer cells or will become downregulated through resistance after immune cell activation
[43].
1.6
Immunotherapy
Immunotherapy is a type of biologic treatment that modifies the immune system to treat different dis-
eases. Some immunotherapies can help the immune system fight chronic infectious diseases or cancer
while others treat autoimmune responses. There are several types of immunotherapies that all target
different aspects of the immune system. (i) Host failing to identify tumor antigens, (ii) negative im-
munoregulatory mechanisms and (iii) tolerance to self. Many different cancer types use the immune
system for their development and growth. Furthermore is the TME immunosuppressive [44, 45]. Be-
cause this thesis is mainly focused on adoptive cell therapy (ACT), it is the only immunotherapy that
will be described.
1.6.1
Adoptive cell therapy
ACTs are therapies where immune cells (most often T cells) are transferred to the patient intra-
venously. Either tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TIL) which are isolated, expanded and reintroduced,
or gene-modified T cells which express novel T cell receptors (TCR) or chimeric antigen receptors
(CAR) are transferred as a treatment for cancer. ACT aims at modifying the immune system into rec-
ognizing tumor cells and thus carries out an anti-tumor effector function. The cells are intravenously
injected into the patient, where they will migrate to the site of cancer infection. ACT has proven to be
useful in cases where checkpoint inhibitors and other immunotherapies have failed. ACT is divided

1.8
Gene editing
Gene editing tools can be used to alter DNA sequences in living cells into new desirable sequences
with high efficiency and precision. This can be used to both eliminate genes and insert new ones.
Several gene editing tools exist, including, zink-finger nucleases (ZNF) and transcription activator-
like effector nucleases (TALEN). However, the clustered regularly interspaced short palindrome re-
peats (CRISPR) with the CRISPR-associated (Cas) protein, Cas9, has the lowest cost, highest effi-
ciency, and is less complex in its application. Furthermore, the rapid adoption and development of the
CRISPR/Cas method make it a great candidate to use in precision medicine going forward [50–52].

1.9.1
DNA Damage Response (DDR)
The DNA damage response (DDR) is triggered by the induction of a double-stranded break (DSB).
DDR is an interconnected network that maintains cell viability and genomic stability. It consists of
several different DNA repair and checkpoint mechanisms that respond to both endogenous and exoge-
nous sources of DNA damage. If the DNA is not correctly repaired, it can lead to apoptosis, genomic
instability and damage to the cell’s development process which could lead to cancer, neurological
disorders or immunodeficiency [56, 57]. There are two repair mechanisms that are activated by the
induction of a DSB. The error-prone non-homologous end-joining pathway (NHEJ) which often is

1.9.2
Non-homologous end-joining
The non-homologous end-joining repair (NHEJ) pathway is an efficient but error-prone repair mech-
anism. It is the predominant DNA repair pathway used in mammalian cells, and it plays an essential
role in V(D)J recombination of both T and B cell receptors. NHEJ is working at all stages of the
cell cycle. During NHEJ the blunt DNA ends are altered by either inserting or deleting bases result-
ing in insertion/deletion (indels) mutations. This often causes premature stop codons or frameshift
mutations that eventually cause knockout of the target gene. NHEJ is initiated by the binding of
the heterodimer Ku70/Ku80 (KU) to each end of the blunt DSB. This recruit the catalytic subunit
of DNA-dependent protein kinase (DNA-PKcs) that together with KU creates a stable complex that
is bound to each DNA end. DNA-PK phosphorylates DNA damage response proteins and thereby
recruits Artemis nucleases to the site of the break. Artemis has a 5’ to 3’ exonuclease activity where
it deletes nucleotides and a DNA-PK dependent 5’ to 3’ endonuclease activity where it inserts new
nucleotides on hairpins and single-stranded DNA overhangs. DNA polymerases then add missing
nucleotides to fill the gap in between the DSB. Lastly, the two DNA strands are ligated together, and
the DSB is repaired (figure 8). NHEJ following a CRISPR/Cas9-induced DSB will, in most cases,
result in a knockout of the target gene [51, 58–60].

1.10
Delivery of template DNA
1.10.1
Transfection
Transfection is a way to introduce exogenous nucleic acid into the cell. Different methods of trans-
fection exists, including; chemical, biological and physical methods to transfer DNA into cells. A
transfection can either be transient or stable. A transient transfection is where the nucleic acid is
not integrated into the host DNA, and therefore is not replicated and as a result is lost following cell
division. Stable transfection is where the nucleic acid is integrated into the host DNA and therefore
will be part of the genome of the host cell [64]. Different viral and non-viral methods can be used to
get the cargo (Cas9, sgRNA and HDRT) into the cells [54].
Chemical Transfection
In chemical tranfection, the negatively charged nucleic acid forms a com-
plex with either cationic polymers, calcium phosphate or cationic lipids. When the complex gets into
close proximity of the cell membrane, it will be taken up by either endocytosis or phagocytosis [64] .
Physical Transfection
To physical deliver nucleic acid into a cell, special tools and equipment is
needed. These include microneedles, that can inject DNA or RNA directly into the cells cytoplasm
or nucleus. Electroporation which applies electric pulses to creates pores in the cell membrane (see
section 1.10.2), laser-mediated transfection which uses laser pulses to create transient permeability of

January 22, 2023
the cells and lastly gold particles coated with nucleic acid and then delivered into the cell using a high
velocity gene gun [64].
Biological Tranduction
Biological transduction is the use of viruses to transfer nucleic acid into a
cell [64]. Due to viruses’ high cellular uptake and editing efficiency, they have been used as a delivery
method for CRISPR cargo [54].
Adenoviral Vectors (AdV)
Adenoviral Vectors (AdV) are double-stranded non-enveloped DNA
viruses meaning viruses without a lipid bilayer membrane on the outer surface. AdV can pack around
8kb of DNA. Adenoviral Vectors (AdV) can limit off-target effects and unwanted mutations. This is
due to the fact that it remains extrachromosomal following injection, and therefore doesn’t integrate
with the host DNA. AdV transduce both dividing and non-dividing cells. Since AdV limits off-target
effects, scientists have tried to eliminate the immunogenic effect of AdVs. This has resulted in the
development of second-generation AdVs, where all viral genes are eliminated, which also allows for
a higher packing capacity of DNA delivery for the CRISPR/Cas9 system. However, the viral capsule
can still cause acute phase immune responses in patients [54, 65, 66].
Adeno-Associated Viruses (AAV)
Adeno-Associated Viruses (AAV) are small non-enveloped ss-
DNA viruses from the Parvoviridae family. It is the most commonly used virus vectors for CRISPR/Cas9
genome editing. This is due to the fact that AAVs are less immunogenic than other viruses, and have
already been used in several clinical trials. AAV can target both dividing and non-dividing cells and
can exist long-term in non-dividing cells. After the virus has entered the cell, the AAV DNA remains
mostly episomal, meaning it doesn’t integrate within the cell’s own DNA. The main issue with using
AAV as a viral delivery vector for CRISPR/Cas9 is its small packing capacity of 4.7 kb [54, 65, 66].
Lentiviral Vectors (LV)
Lentiviral Vectors (LV) transduce both dividing and non-dividing cells.
It is a ssRNA virus part of the Retroviradae family. LV is an enveloped RNA virus and can pack
up to 10 kb of RNA genome. The retroviral family can retro-transcribe its genome into the DNA
of a host cell, resulting in a persistent expression of the gene of interest. However, the LV vector
system can integrate randomly into the host cell’s genome leading to mutations that are associated
with carcinogenesis. To reduce the risk of off-target integration, non-integrating lentiviral vectors
have been designed for CRISPR/Cas9 delivery [54, 65–67].

1.10.2
Cell Electroporation
Cell electroporation is a method where a short electric pulse creates pores in the cell membrane which
allows DNA, RNA and proteins to enter the cytoplasm of the cell. One of the major advantages of
electroporation is that it can be used on a large variety of cells, including both pro- and eukaryotes.
Since DNA enters the cytoplasm directly and not via endosomes and lysosomes, they are not exposed
to acidic conditions that can cause damage to the DNA. If the cells are exposed to irreversible electro-
poration, the cells are damaged beyond repair. This can happen for several reasons but the most likely
one is that the cell is unable to restore the membrane structure and barrier function. The cell dying
from electroporation is a result of the duration of treatment and field strength. Different cell types
require a different electric field to create pores. For instance, mammalian cells require a lower electric
field, approximately ∼7.5-15.0kV/cm whereas bacteria or yeast requires 12.5-16.5kV/cm [68, 69].
1.11
Template types
One of the major obstacles to successful gene editing using HDR is the toxicity of DNA. For that
reason, different types of DNA have been tested to find the optimal template type with the lowest
toxicity and highest KI efficiency. The HDRT type has to be optimized per cell type as some cells are
less sensitive. The donor template consists of flanking regions homologous to the target locus [70].
1.11.1
Toxicity of DNA
The major issue with using exogenous DNA as a template for HDR is that free exogenous DNA is
toxic to the cell. This is because the DNA sensing pathways use PRRs placed both inside and outside
the cell to recognize PAMPs. Therefore, when DNA is added as a template for HDR, the PRRs in
the cytosol will recognize them as foreign DNA. The cell will then activate the cell’s immune system
resulting in cell death [71].
1.11.2
dsDNA
Large linear dsDNA templates are toxic at high concentrations for T cells [6]. This problem can
be circumvented by introducing the template together with CRISPR-Cas9 ribonucleoprotein (RNP)
[6]. Another major issue with using linear dsDNA as HDRT is that they rapidly multimerize once
they enter the nucleus. As a result, they clump together and block their usage as HDRT. This can be
Master Thesis F22
Page 20 of 76
==PAGE:34==
Mette Røkke Bjørnson
January 22, 2023
circumvented by modifying the 5’ end of the donor dsDNA by e.g., adding biotin to it [70]. Higher
KI efficiency can also be reached by adding a Cas targeting sequence (CTS) to the end of the dsDNA.
This sequence is complementary to the gRNA, but with 3-5 mismatched bases, and thereby the Cas9
RNP can bind the template, but not cut it. As a result, the Cas9 nuclear localization signal that aids
Cas9 in getting into the nucleus can further aid in shuttling the template into the nucleus [72].
1.11.3
ssDNA
ssDNA can be used as a template for HDR. Compared to dsDNA, ssDNA is associated with lower
toxicity in T cells which could induce higher KI efficiency [72]. Short ssDNA (up to 500 nt) can
be synthesized while larger ssDNA have to be made from dsDNA [73]. Just like with dsDNA, the
attachment of CTS on the homologue arm of the template results in higher efficiency. However, as
Cas9 only binds to dsDNA, the attachment of the complementary DNA strand of the CTS sequence
is necessary [72].
1.11.4
Plasmid DNA
Another type of DNA template that can be used for HDR is a plasmid. Plasmids are favourable to use
since they can easily be manipulated in the lab [73]. One of the elements that affect the efficiency of
an HDRT is the length of the homologue arm. To create large sequence changes in mammalian cells
using plasmids as a template, 1-2kb of homology is usually used. Even though plasmids are used
as HDRT linearized plasmids have in some cases shown to have a higher KI efficiency compared to
circular plasmids [74].
1.11.5
ccDNA
Closed circular DNA (ccDNA) is a dsDNA template with covalently closed ends. ccDNA is believed
to have higher KI efficiency and lower cell death than normal dsDNA. A company that produces
ccDNA, GenScript, have compared it with their PCR-produced dsDNA and found that ccDNA gives
higher KI efficiency and lower cell toxicity [75].

1.12
Application of CRISPR/Cas9 in T cells
In the following section, several pre-clinical and clinical applications of CRISPR/Cas9 in T cells will
be presented.
A preclinical study by Schober et al. [76] tested non-viral KI of TCR compared to viral delivery and
KO of the endogenous TCR and viral transduction of transgenic TCR alone. They found that CRISPR
KI into T cell receptor α constant locus (TRAC) resulted in harmonized surface expression and func-
tional capacity similar to what is observed in non-edited T cells. This was not observed for the viral
tranduces TCR both with and without KO of the endogenous TCR. They showed the following KI of
trangenic TCR, the TCR is downregulated post stimulation in a similar manner to what is obseved in
non-edited T cell population. Transdued TCR did, however, show a different downregulation patterns
compared to non-edited T cells. In conclusion, Schober et al. showed that transgenic TCR workes in
a similar way to what is observed for endogenous TCR when the TCR is inserted by CRISPR/Cas9
KI. This is useful for the usage of gene-editied T cell in ACT [76].
A clinical study by Roth et al. aimed at creating a viral-free delivery method for CRISPR/Cas9 that
can induce rapid and efficient insertion of large DNA fragments into the genome. One of the major
challenges in creating a virus-free delivering system is the toxicity of long linear dsDNA at high con-
centrations. However, the use of recombinant viral vectors slows down the research and clinical use,
and the manufacturing and testing of the viral vectors are an expensive and lengthy process. Roth
et al. used the transfection method, electroporation to transport RNPs and HDRT into the nucleus
of the cell. They optimized the system by lowering the toxicity of dsDNA and finding the optimal
electroporation pulses to achieve the highest KI efficiency with lowest cell death. Roth et al. proved
the clinical application of their method by inserting the IL-2α (IL-2RA) receptor into T cells of IL-
2RA deficient children. The non-viral genome editing showed a correction of the IL-2RA deficiency
and the IL-2 receptor was expressed on the T cell’s surface. Roth et al. then developed a strategy
that could replace the endogenous T cell receptor. One of the issues with targeting the T cell receptor
locus is that the TCR must express the paired TCRα and TCRβ chain to create a functional TCR. They
solved this issue by inserting the entire β-chain together with the variable part of the α chain directly
into the locus of the constant α-chain. They successfully inserted NY-ESO-1 TCR into the T cells of
healthy donors and proved that they respond to NY-ESO-1 antigens presented on MHC [6].

Another phase I clinical trial by Foy et al. aimed at making an efficient approach for creating multiple
TCRs that target neoantigen. A target library of neoantigens for patients with different cancer types
were generated all targeting HLA class I alleles. Neoantigens were then matched with the patients
HLA class I alleles before being used to identify patient-specific neoTCR. The TCR gene sequence

which not all patients have. This new approach targets all of the patients up to 6 HLA class I alleles.
Furthermore, this clinical trial uses a viral-free gene editing system which speeds up the process of
creating personalized cell therapy [77].
These study shows the potential of using CRISPR/Cas9 as a gene editing mechanism of ACT using
TCR gene therapy. However, these methods are time-consuming and expensive. Personalized therapy
is also very donor-dependent and the quality of tumor biopsies can effect the outcome [77].
1.13
Challanges and possibilities of using gene therapy in immunotherapy
Adoptive cell therapy is an effective and promising tool in cancer immunotherapy. Several gene edit-
ing methods can be used to insert tumor-specific TCR or CARs into T cells. The gene editing method
CRISPR/Cas9 has proved a notable advantage over e.g., ZFN and TALEN. The main reason for this
is that the CRISPR/Cas9 system easily can change its target by altering the gRNA sequence. This is
a lot more lengthy and challenging process in other gene editing methods [4, 78].
Several different methods of transportation can be used to get the gene-editing method into the cell
(see section 1.10). An issue with using the CRISPR/Cas9 is the size of the system, and that all ele-
ments, Cas9, gRNA and HDRT, need to be moved into the nucleus of the cell for the gene-editing to
work. Therefore the packing capacity sets a limit to some delivery methods like AAV. Another isssue
with using viral vectors is the long-term expression of sgRNA and Cas9 which potentially could cre-
ate off-target effects [79].
A main issue with gene editing is the chance of off-target effects (OTE). Nowadays in silico methods
can be used to predict OTE of the gRNA, thereby limiting the chance. However, these methods
cannot detect epigenetic changes to the genome. Some methods can be implemented to prevent OTE
Master Thesis F22
Page 23 of 76
==PAGE:37==
Mette Røkke Bjørnson
January 22, 2023
like alternations to the gRNA. Changing the gRNA might decrease the OTE, but the same is observed
for the on-target effect [79, 80].
CRISPR/Cas9 edited T cells for ACT has the opportunity to redirect the immune system to cancer
cells. However, there is also an issue with OTE in the body were cross reactivity has been observed
leading to on-target off-tumor toxicity. The endogenous receptor can furthermore form a mispaired
TCR variants with the transgenic receptor which can lead to off-target toxicity [4, 81].
All diseases have a genetic component and using gene editing technologies to treat diseases opens a lot
of opportunities but also rises some ethical issues. These include off-target effects and the possibility
that the gene-modified organism might cause more harm than good. There is a chance that cells with
damaged DNA escape the DNA repair process. These cells could have proto-oncogene mutation or
unwanted DNA rearrangements which could lead to an increased risk of cancer development [4, 82].
Long term ethical issues should be included before exploring the possibilities for using gene-editing
methods to treat a disease. This can be if the genetic manupulation is performed on germline cells,
then the genetic changes can be inherited [4, 82].

Cryotubes containing frozen buffy coats (BC) were collected from the liquid nitrogen tank and thawed

T cell Enrichment
The T cells were isolated from the BC by negative selection using magnetic-activated cell sorting.
This was done using the MojoSort (Mojosort™Isolation Kits Protocol - 1) kit from Biolegend. This
enrichment method uses antibodies that bind to surface markers on non-T cells and then a magnet
(STEMCELL Technologies EasyStep magnet, cat. no 18000) fixates the negative fraction allowing
one to collect T cells. The enrichment was performed according to the manufacturer’s protocol.
The MojoSort protocol was used to isolate T cells for all TCR KI experiments. The cells were resus-
pended to a final concentration of 1x108 cells/mL in Isolation Buffer (PBS + 2% FCS + 1mM EDTA).

incubated on ice for 15 min. The solution was then moved to FACS tubes and Isolation buffer was
added up to 2.5 mL. The tubes were placed on a magnet for 5 min, after which the solution, containing
the T cells, was poured into a new 15mL falcon tube. The Streptavidin-Nanobeads were washed again
in 2.5 mL Isolation Buffer and placed on the magnet for 5 min. The solution was then poured into
the Falcon tube containing the rest of the T cells. The second step was conducted to ensure a higher
concentration of T cells. After enrichment of the T cells, they were resuspended in X-Vivo-15 with
5% Human Serum to a final concentration of 2 ∗106 cells/mL.

2.8
CRISPR/Cas9 design
Two different HDRT templates were used. One containing the TCR specific to the NY-ESO-1 peptide
SLLMWITQC and one containing the TCR specific for the human Merkel Cell Carcinoma (MCC)
peptide EWWRSGGFSF. Both HDRTs encode transgenic T cell receptors. To KI these new TCR, the
HDRTs were introduced as both, ssDNA, dsDNA and ccDNA to test the KI efficiency. The HDRT
were designed with homologous arms of 300bp. The homologous arms match the endogenous T cell
receptor alpha constant (TRAC) locus which integrates the template into the first exon of the constant
alpha chains locus (figure 14).
Both the MCC and NY-ESO-1 HDRT were designed in a similar same way. They contain T2A and
P2A which are self-cleaving peptides ensuring that both chains are expressed independently. The
β-chain was introduced at full length, whereas the α-chain only contained the variable part. The
homologous ends contained a cas targeting sequence (CTS) (see appendix A.1 and A.2).

3
Result and data analysis
The results presented in this master thesis are divided into 5 subsections. This first section shows the
DNA production for HDRT where the yield of both NY-ESO-1 and MCC DNA is shown together
with the characterization of ssDNA and dsDNA. The second section contains titration of ssDNA as a
template for CRISPR/Cas9-mediated KI. The titration is presented with different template conditions
and the attachment of CTS oligos to see how they affect the KI procent. The third section contains a
comparison of different DNA template types, ssDNA, dsDNA, and ccDNA compared to each other.
The fourth section is a flow cytotoxicity based killing assay that testes if the CRISPR TCR-T cells
can kill target cells. Lastly, this result section presents the results of scaffold expansion of CRISPR
TCR-T cells. Here different time points for scaffold expansion were tested together with different
methods of primary activation to test how they effects the phenotype, KI efficiency and cell count.
All of these results are important to both improve the CRISPR KI of TCR and to create the highest
number of specific T cells that target the cancer antigens for CRISPR/Cas9 cancer immunotherapy.

4
Discussion
T cells are essential in creating a functional anti-tumor response. The use of ACT where neoantigen-
specific TCRs (neoTCRs) are inserted could be a promising solution to creating a more efficient im-
munotherapy treatment for cancer [77]. In this thesis I have shown that KI of NeoTCRs into primary
human T cells using CRISPR/Cas9 (CRISPR TCR-T cells) are possible with several different tem-
plates types for HDR. I have furthermore shown that different templates have variable KI efficiency.
Furthermore, have I also shown that CRISPR TCR-T cells can kill target cells and lastly, that scaffold
expansion of CRISPR TCR-T cells results in a very high percentage of specific cells compared to
cytokine controls of CRISPR TCR-T cells. These methods emphasise the potential for further testing
and improvement of this method, which could be used as a cancer treatment and potentially increase
the overall survival of cancer patients. This method also provides a useful tool for in vitro studies of
T cell receptor and antigen-specificity of T cells.
4.1
ssDNA production
ssDNA was produced to test its efficiency in CRISPR/Cas9 KI of TCRs as the literature states that it
is less toxic in T cells than dsDNA and therefore a more favourable template type [72]. The method
used to create ssDNA was implemented from the protocol Wakimoto et al. [85], but with some
modifications (see section 2.6). The protocol of Wakimoto et al. explains two methods to remove
unwanted PCR products. I used the method of gel extraction but observed that most of the dsDNA
was lost in the process. Part of this could be due to human error in cutting out the gel band without
getting excess gel as these gel fragments are hard to remove completely. Furthermore, two different
DNA bands were observed on the gel. I, therefore, optimized the PCR reaction using different PCR
polymerases and at different annealing temperatures to find a PCR method that only showed one band
on a gel. From this point, a method to remove PCR reagents were then needed, and precipitation was
then chosen to isolate the dsDNA product. The ssDNA isolation from dsDNA was done according
to the protocol and the ssDNA quality was analyzed using the restriction enzyme EcoRI-HF (figure
16). The restriction enzymes proves that ssDNA is in fact produced but with traces of contaminating
dsDNA. The same is observed from the protocol of Wakimoto et al. I then tested whether the produced
ssDNA was a better HDRT than dsDNA, but ssDNA did not prove to be a much better HDRT (figure
19), as described by Shy et al. [72]. They also produce ssDNA from the Wakimoto et al. protocol, but
Master Thesis F22
Page 57 of 76
==PAGE:71==
Mette Røkke Bjørnson
January 22, 2023
with other modifications. Following ssDNA separation from dsDNA they use SPRI cleanup beads to
purify and concentrate ssDNA. It is therefore possible that the purification and concentration methods
of ssDNA influences how well ssDNA works as a HDRT. If the purification method used by Shy et al.
removed more or all dsDNA from the mixture, it could explain why they get better KI results using
ssDNA as a template from what I observe, given that ssDNA should be less toxic than dsDNA [72].
It should also be noted that Wakimoto et al. ssDNA production method is not the only method to
produce ssDNA. Shy et al. uses another ssDNA production method for large-scale production using
a proprietary isothermal enzymatic reaction process performed by Genscript Biotech [72] but other
methods also exist [86–88].
ssDNA were produced for the NY-ESO-1-, and MCC-specific TCR HDRTs. I observed a large differ-
ence in the yield of these two templates. This could be a consequence of human variation as they have
been produced at different timepoints. It could also be due to differences in the plasmid quality, as this
is the only aspect of the ssDNA production that differs between the two PCR reactions. A repeat of
the ssDNA production of both HDRTs simultaneously could eliminate human variation. If the DNA
quality from the ssDNA preparations were not optimal, it could further explain why ssDNA doesn’t
show a higher KI efficiency compared to dsDNA when comparing these, as I used the MCC-specific
TCR for the comparison (see section 3.3).
4.2
ssDNA titration
ssDNA titration was tested using different HDRT concentrations. It was found in earlier studies that
the optimal ssDNA template concentration for CRISPR/Cas9 KI in T cells was 80nM for HDRT at
similar size to the once I use (∼2.2 kb) and 40nM for HDRT larger than mine [72]. From this, 3
HDRT concentrations of 20nM, 40nM, and 80nM were compared. Contrary to what Shy et al. ob-
served, I found that the optimal HDRT concentration for CRISPR/Cas9 KI was 40nM with 20nM as
the concentration that provided the highest amount of CRISPR TCR-T cells. There can be several
reasons for this difference. Firstly, they produced their ssDNA in a slightly different way than what I
optimized. Secondly, they KI IL-2RA which is an IL-2 receptor and a CAR construct whereas I KI
a TCR [72]. This could influence the KI efficiency and prefered HDRT concentration, as the KI of
TCRs in general are lower as both an alpha and a beta chain have to be expressed and paired for the
tetramer to bind [6, 72]. Flow plots shown in figure 3.2 indicate a difference in KO efficiency using
different HDRT concentrations. The higher the template concentration, the lower the KO percentage

(as observed by CD3- T cells). This could be due to technical variation between the samples as this
was one of the first KI experiments I performed, and it should therefore be repeated to see if this is
observed again. In the case that is not a technical variation, an explanation could be that the high
template concentration results in a higher concentration of HDRT bound to Cas9 since the HDRT all
have CTS attached to them. This might inhibit the function of Cas9 RNPs which could possibly be
circumvented by increasing the RNP concentration. If Cas9 is inhibited by higher concentrations of
templates, it would likely also influence the KI efficiency.
The oligo attachment to CTS was also tested. The oligos were attached at the same concentration and
as stated in the literature [72]. The results (figure 18) show that the highest KI efficiency is observed
when the oligo is attached to the 5’ end, but with similar KI efficiencies as oligos in both the 3’ and
5’ end. These findings support findings by Shy et al. This difference between CTS oligos in 5’ and
3’ ends are not fully understood, and why two CTS oligos are not better than one, however it could
possibly be a result of the extra oligo making the DNA more toxic resulting in a lower KI efficiency.
4.3
Optimal HDRT
To test whether ssDNA was prefered over dsDNA in our setup I tested these HDRTs against each
other. They were further compared to ccDNA which possibly produces higher KI efficiencies and
live cell count compared to dsDNA [75]. Contrary to what Shy et al. observed, ssDNA did not pro-
vide significantly higher KI compared to dsDNA although there was a trend towards increased KI
efficiency. The overall highest KI was observed for ccDNA which matches what one of the manufac-
tures found [75]. ccDNA was commercially produced by Twist Bioscience. It must therefore also be
expected that the ccDNA HDRT used is of a better quality than the dsDNA and ssDNA used, which
could affect the KI efficiency. It would therefore also require additional tests to replicate and validate
these results.
4.4

The concept of using CRISPR/Cas9 to KI NeoTCR into primary human T cells is to use the CRISPR
TCR-T-cells for cancer treatment with adoptive cell therapy [4]. CRISPR TCR-T cells, therefore,
need to be able to kill target cells and spare healthy cells. A flow cytotoxicity killing assay was

24 hours. This experiment could be repeated with longer killing time to test if the lower E:T ratio
results in a higher killinger over longer periods. Other types of killing assay could also be performed

4.5
Scaffold expansion of CRISPR TCR-T cells
I have found the concentration of CRISPR TCR-T cells following non-viral KI of NeoTCRs to be
∼5-10 procent. If these cells were expanded to reach a specific cell concentration of >1010 cells
[47], then a lot of unspecific cells would be transferred to the patient which could create unwanted
side effects like a cytokine-storm [92]. To prevent this, the concentration of specific cells needs to be
higher. This can happen by cell sorting where unspecific cells are removed [6] or by expanding only
specific cells in the population by Ag-scaffolds [7]. The goal of the scaffold optimization experiment
was to expand CRISPR TCR-T cells to find the optimal resting time between electroporation and first
scaffold feeding and to furthermore see how the initial activation method affects the T cells. Surpris-
ingly I found that the rest period and initial activation didn’t effect the concentration of specific cells
that much and all had very high concentration of specific cells. The total amount of specific cells did
however change. This means that the rest period can be chosen depending on the goal of the scaffold
expansion. If a high specific cell concentration is the goal, then a short rest period is sufficient. If
high specific cell counts are the goal then a longer rest period is better. High specific cell counts were
also observed for cytokine control.
The phenotype panel I stained the scaffold-expanded cells with was used to determine if the initial
activation method and resting period affected the general phenotype of the T cells. This is not at all an
exhaustive phenotype panel, but included the most markers of memory and effector T cells. The stains
did indicate some minor differences between the samples that underwent different resting times be-
fore scaffold stimulation, while no difference was observed for the two primary activation methods.
Further I observed some differences between the scaffold expanded cells and the cytokine control,
such as a reduction in na¨ıve-like T cells (CCR7+CD45RA+) in the scaffold-stimulated T cells. CCR7
is a lymph node homing receptor whilst CD45RA modulates TCR signalling [33, 34, 93]. In T cells

The phenotype panel I used is merely a scratch on the surface of determining the T cell phenotype.
For a better and more complete phenotypical analysis, additional staining markers including intracel-

matory cytokines such as TNF-α and IFNγ by blocking their release from the golgi apparatus [94].
these factors are important to determine the functionality of the CRISPR TCR- and scaffold expanded
T cells to determine their ability to kill.
The scaffold-expansion method uses scaffolds to expand only the specific T cells. Other reactivation
methods could also be used (see section 1.7). Tvingholm et al. compared scaffold-expansions with
free peptide stimulations, together with the cytokines, IL-2 and IL-21. This free peptide expansion
functions similarly to the scaffold-expansion, however here the elements are not connected to a dex-
tran backbone, and the peptide is not presented on MHC on a backbone, but is instead presented
between cells in the culture. Tvingholm et al. did find that scaffold-mediated expansions gave higher
frequency of specific cells with younger phenotype and greater cytotoxic potential than free peptide
expansion [7]. This should be tested in CRISPR TCR-T cells to see if this is true for these cells as well.
Scaffold expansion of CRISPR TCR-T cells can also be used as a research tool to study different
TCRs. A pool of different CRISPR TCR-T cells could all be expanded with scaffold to see if they
expand equally.

expansion to test the feasibility of using scaffold-expand CRISPR TCR-T cells for ACT (figure 27).

4.7
Future perspectives
I have optimized both CRISPR/Cas9 KI of trangenic TCRs and scaffold expansion of the CRISPR
TCR-T cell. However, I have only scratched the surface of what could furthermore be optimized for
both CRISPR/Cas9 KI, but especially also for scaffold expansion of TCR KI T cells.
The next step in CRISPR/Cas9 KI I would test would be to titrate dsDNA as HDRT and following
that repeat the HDR template comparison experiment now with the optimal dsDNA concentration. I
would furthermore also try to optimize the ssDNA production by e.g., by using SPRI cleanup beads
like Shy et al. to see if this would result in higher KI with ssDNA as a template. I would also repeat
the ssDNA production with both NY-ESO-1 and MCC in parallel to rule out human errors as a reason
for low MCC DNA yield.
For scaffold expansion, I would start by testing scaffold expansion in comparison to free-peptide ex-
pansion to evaluate whether the phenotypes and T cell properties of the scaffold expanded TCR KI
T cells were favorable to peptide expanded TCR KI T cells. Then I would make a larger pheno-
typic staining panel including intracellular staining to better identify the phenotype of the scaffold-
expanded transgenic T cells, and lastly, I would perform a killing assay to test if the scaffold expanded
T cells would kill target cells.
Following this, I would KI two different TCR into the same pool of T cells, and following CRISPR/Cas9
KI, expand both transgenic TCR. This could e.g., be both NY-ESO-1 and MCC that were KI into the
same T cells population. This could be used to expand the potential use of this method. By KI several
TCR into autologous T cells, then you could target several cancer antigens in the same batch of ACT.
By scaffold expanding each of these trangenic TCR, you would probably have a very high concen-
tration of cancer specific T cells in ACT cell population that you could give to a patient as a cancer
treatment. This method could furthermore also be implementet to treat other disease than just cancer,
e.g., by attacking virus epitopes.

Conclusion
Personalized adoptive cell transfers as therapy for cancers have shown great promise in the clinic,
and developing novel techniques for expanding these therapies is highly sought after. In this Master’s
thesis I have presented CRISPR/Cas9 KI of cancer-specific TCRs which could be used in ACT. I
have used ssDNA as a HDR template as the litterature has shown higher KI efficiencies with ssDNA
compared to dsDNA. I have optimized the TCR KI using ssDNA as a template and compared it to
other template types. I found that ssDNA provides higher KI concentration than dsDNA as previ-
ously described, but also found that ccDNAs are favorable to ssDNAs in our experiments. Following
CRISPR/Cas9 KI of CRISPR TCR-T cell I have expanded only NY-ESO-1 specific T cell in a popula-
tion using antigen-presenting scaffolds. I have shown that primary activation with dynabeads creates
a higher live cell count of NY-ESO-1-specific T cells compared to plate-bound activation, and I have
shown that the primary activation method and resting time before scaffold stimulation does not affect
the overall phenotype of the T cells. Lastly, I have shown that CRISPR TCR-T cell can kill target
cells efficiently in an in vitro setting.
Many further optimiziation and testing experiments should be conducted to assess if this method
could be implemented for cancer treatment in the future and clinical applications of scaffold expanded
CRISPR TCR-T cells are still some way in the future. However, these initial optimization experiments
show proof that it is indeed feasible.
