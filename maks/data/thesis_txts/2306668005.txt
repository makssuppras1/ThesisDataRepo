Summary (English)
Since the invention of the PCR technique, the eld of population genetic has
been growing extensively and the eld helps to understand the evolution and
ecology in nature. Population genetic is among other uses, used to determine
factors inuencing the population structure and is used as a tool within con-
servation and management. This master thesis demonstrates the population
structure of the roach (rutilus rutilus) in a suite of Danish and Swedish lakes
and streams, and the factors determining population structure in regards to
historical and present eects, with a special focus on the Jutland ridge.
The results show that the population structure amongst the roach is determined
by both historical and present factors. The results show that there is an over-
all structure among the samples, wherein the Gudenåen is genetically dierent
from the rest of the samples, which might be caused by dierent re-colonisation
groups.
In addition to this, there is a hierarchical structure showing that the Jutland
ridge seem to act as a separation line between the eastern and western running
rivers, and this structure could have formed during the last glacial period.
More local and present factors such as connectivity, isolation-by-distance and
human dispersal by anglers are also observed to have an eect on the population
structure.
Some samples are outliers in regards to the structure, and in order to get a
better understanding of these outliers, more samples would be necessary.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Population genetics is the study of the genetic structures amongst populations
and, especially after the development of the PCR, the number of population
genetic studies has increased (Sclötterer, 2004).
Population genetics helps in understanding evolution and ecology in
nature and to understand the working of evolutionary forces. It helps to under-
stand the natural forces that determines the evolutionary changes, but also to
understand how genetic variation arises, how it is lost and the amount of varia-
tion within a population (Frankham et al., 2002). In addition to understanding
the natural forces, population genetics is also used as a tool within conservation
and management. It is shown that the release of genetically similar individuals
has a lesser negative eect on the overall population tness than genetically
not similar individuals (Hindar et al., 1991). Studies on population structures
can therefore be used for management, such as stock enhancement, for declin-
ing, vulnerable or endangered species. In the studies of the North Sea houting
(Coregonus oxyrinchus), Northern pike (Esox Lucius) and European grayling
(Thymallus thymallus), the population structure is used in order to determine
the best management strategies (Hansen et al., 2008; Bekkevold et al., 2015;
Meldgaard et al., 2003).

The genes consist of exon and introns (gure 1.2). The exons are parts

Genetic drift
Genetic drift is an evolutionary force reducing genetic diversity within a pop-
ulation. It is random transfer of genes from parents to their ospring. This
random transfer of genes, are eventually changing the allele frequencies, making
them dier from the originally allele frequencies (Hartl et al., 2007; Freeman
et al., 2007), and may eventually result in a loss of alleles. A consequence of
genetic drift is therefore that all individuals in a population are homozygote for
the loci having lost variation due to genetic drift (Hartl et al., 2007; Frankham
et al., 2002)(gure 1.5).
Genetic drift occurs in all populations, but the rate at which alleles

Natural selection
Natural selection is a process altering the allele frequencies within a population
(Freeman et al., 2007). Selection works in many ways. Selection sort in muta-
tions, where unt mutations are removed and the t mutations are selected for,
while neutral mutations is ignored (Loewe, 2008). Natural selection is occurring
in all populations, but if population sizes are too small, genetic drift is going to

improving the chances of survival and reproduction, can therefore be lost by
chance in a small population (Freeman et al., 2007).

that have an eect on the allele frequencies can either be positive or negative

Positive selection selects for advantageous mutations, and acts to
increase the allele frequencies. The negative selection act to decrease the delete-
rious mutation, and is a force that decrease the allele frequencies. There is also
selection that aect the genetic diversity such as balancing selection and puri-
fying selection. The balancing selection increases the genetic variability within
a population, where the purifying selection removes genetic variation (Nielsen,
R., 2005).
All individuals within a population dier in their ability to survive and repro-
duce due to their dierences in alleles. Those alleles that makes an individual
more adapted and better at surviving and reproducing are improving the tness
of that individual. Such alleles are due to positive and balancing selection in-
creasing in frequency over generations (Hartl et al., 2007; Freeman et al., 2007).

Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium
Hardy-Weinberg is a principle specifying the genotypic frequencies in a popula-
tion. When the observed alleles of a population correspond to the expected pop-
ulations, the alleles in the population is in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (HWE).
The expected allele frequencies are calculated as p2,2pq and q2, with the alleles
p and q (Freeman et al., 2007; Hartwell et al., 2011). In order for a population
to be in HWE, the population has to meet the assumptions that there is no
selection, no mutations, no gene ow and no genetic drift (Freeman et al., 2007;
Hartwell et al., 2011). When one or several of these assumptions is violated,
the allele frequencies are changing from one generation to the next, and the
genotypes will no longer correspond to the expected frequencies, p2, 2pq and q2
(Freeman et al., 2007; Hartwell et al., 2011).
Deviations from HWE could indicate that there is no random mating
within the population. Nonrandom mating could result in selection for some
alleles, and they will dier in frequency from the one with random mating
(Freeman et al., 2007; Hartwell et al., 2011; Frankham et al., 2002). Another
reason for deviations from HWE could be that the samples consists of more
than one population, referred to as the Wahlund eect (Karlsson et al., 2005).
Deviations from HWE can also be caused by errors in genotyping (Morin et al.,
2009).
However, no populations meet the criteria for HWE completely, as no population
size is close to innity, most populations experience some degree of gene ow and
many populations experience nonrandom mating (Hartwell et al., 2011; Hartl
et al., 2007).

Population structure
Population structure is the genetic structuring of populations, and it is a way
to describe the genetic relationships between populations. The structuring of
populations occurs when individuals within a population do not have random
mating (Laikre et al., 2005).
Without random mating, the populations are
divided into small groups, having dierent allele frequencies, and eventually,
the genetic distance between the groups increases (Hartl et al., 2007).
This
genetic dierentiation is caused by evolutionary forces such as natural selection,
genetic drift, gene ow and mutations (Laikre et al., 2005; Hartl et al., 2007).
The eect of gene ow is shown in the European grayling and Atlantic salmon
(Salmo salar), that both has a population structure aected by reduced gene
ow (Meldgaard et al., 2003; Vähä et al., 2007).
Reduced gene ow may be caused by physical barriers or mismatches
in space or time, preventing intergroup mating, but it can also be caused by
selection, local adaptations and ock or schooling behaviour of the examined
species (Hartl et al., 2007).
Both the genetic dierentiation between populations and the genetic clustering
can be estimated by various programs and tests (Hartl et al., 2007; Raymond
et al., 1995; Pritchard et al., 2009) able to identify homogenous groups of indi-
viduals. This can e.g. be important for conservation and management purposes
(Laikre et al. 2005; Bekkevold et al., 2015, Hansen et al., 2008).
Population structures is in Laikre et al., 2005, divided into three types
of structures (gure 1.11). The rst population structure described is that of
no dierentiation between individuals. The second is described as isolation-by-
distance, wherein genetic dierentiation is increasing with increasing geographic
distance.
The last type is where two geographically distant populations are
genetically more similar than with populations located in between.
The main factor aecting how the population structure is shaped, is the amount
of gene ow between groups, and it can be dicult to distinguish the three types
of structures (Laikre et al., 2005)(gure 1.11). A study of genetic dierentiation
amongst roach showed that this dierentiation did not aect the structure of
the roach (Laroche et al., 1999). However, in the study Bekkevold et al., 2015
several structural types were observed amongst Northern pike.
Populations may also have hierarchical structures. This means that
the groups of individuals can be subdivided into even smaller groups which are
genetically dierent (Hartl et al., 2007). A hierarchical structure like this has
been observed in Atlantic salmon population in Vähä et al., 2007.

(Schlötterer, C., 2004; Freeman et al., 2007).
Microsatellites are not equally spread in the genome. They tend to
form in clusters, and are more abundant in the non-coding DNA regions than
in the coding regions (Oliveira et al., 2006). This is most likely due to the high
repair rate in the coding region in order to avoid disruption of the reading frame
and malfunctions of the gene (Oliveira et al., 2006).
Compared with other genetic markers, microsatellites are highly informative,
abundant and easy to isolate, and they are good markers for distinguishing in-
dividuals. In addition to this they also have high mutation rates increasing the
number of alleles. These properties make them suitable for studies in popula-
tions genetics (Oliveira et al., 2006; Schlötterer, C., 2004).
However, primers developed for every species are needed, which is a disadvan-
tage of using them (Schlötterer, C., 2004; Franham et al., 2002; Oliveira et al.,
2006). Primers for roach has already been developed in other studies, and these
was used in this study (Hamilton et al., 2008; Witt Nielsen, L., 2012). In ad-
dition to this it can be challenging to combine data across dierent studies due
to dierences in genotyping. This was also an issue experienced in this study,
and was resolved by calibrating data as shown in Appendix A.

Other markers
Besides microsatellites other genetic markers are available for measuring genetic
diversity. These are e.g. allozymes and SNP's (Laikre et al., 2005; Franham et
al., 2002; Schlötterer, C., 2004) (gure 1.12). The dierent markers dier in
popularity and microsatellites and SNPs are currently among the most widely
used (Schlötterer, C., 2004).
Allozymes were the rst type of genetic marker available, and they
can be used to detect dierences in proteins, such as changes in amino acids
(Schlötterer, C., 2004). Compared with microsatellites, the allozymes are less
informative. This is because not all mutations result in a change of the amino
acid. Therefore, only some mutations in the coding region are discovered by this
method (Schlötterer, C., 2004). After the introduction of the PCR method, that
helps amplify DNA sequences, the allozyme method has been used less than the
new methods (Schlötterer, C., 2004; Franham et al., 2002).
SNP's is one of the new method that has been developed since the
invention of PCR, and is also used to identify genetic variation within the DNA
sequence (Freeman et al., 2007; Schlötterer, C., 2004). SNP's are the simplest
genetic marker and consist of just one base pair (gure 1.13). They are very
frequent compared to microsatellites, and almost 200 times more SNP's than
microsatellites have been discovered (Freeman et al., 2007). Compared to mi-
crosatellites, SNP's are highly abundant with low mutation rates. On average,
microsatellites are more informative per locus, as they have more alleles, how-
ever, more loci can be analysed at the same time when using SNP's (Schlötterer,
C., 2004; Liu et al., 2005).
In addition to this, there are several other genetic markers, but these are less
used and less popular than microsatellites and SNP's. All genetic markers pro-
vide information about the dierences in the DNA sequence in some way. The
marker used depends on the question of interest, and is often a trade-o between
sensitivity and marker price (Schlötterer, C., 2004; Sunnucks 2000).

Aim of study
During the last glaciation, a European ice sheet covered most of Denmark,
except from the Southwestern part of Jutland. Most organisms present today
dispersed south during this period, and dispersed back north with the retreat of
the ice sheet (Taberlet et al., 1998). The roach was one of the organisms living
in Denmark in last inter-glaciation (115.000-113.000 years ago) and after the
last glaciation it returned to the streams and lakes as well (Carl et al., 2012).
The edge of the ice sheet went from Hald near Viborg and through mid-Jutland,
and after the ice retreated 13.000-8300 B.C., it had created the Jutland ridge
(Iversen, 1973)(gure 1.15).
Previous studies have shown that the Jutland ridge seems to be a sep-
aration line between the eastern and western populations in Jutland (Bekkevold
et al., 2015), as the genetic dierence between them is higher than would be
expected from the geographical distance. Physical obstacles in river systems,
e.g. dams, weirs and geology, can obstruct the dispersal of sh, and this can
reduce or even remove the gene ow across it (Ovidio et al., 2002; Lundberg
1993 in Sivasundar et al., 2001).
In a previous master thesis, a genetic dierence between roach popu-
lation in the two streams Gudenåen and Skjern å was found (Witt Nielsen, L.,
2012). The data obtained in that study was utilized in this study, along with
new samples, in order to expand the data to observe the population structure.
In order to use data from Witt Nielsen, L., 2012, some conversion was needed
(Appendix A).
The aim of this master thesis is to investigate the genetic dierences
amongst roach populations in Danish and Southern Swedish streams and lakes.
This study focuses mainly on the role of the Jutland ridge as a driver for pop-
ulation structure in roach, but also explores the role of other factors, such as
environmental barriers and migration potential, in explaining population struc-
tures in roach. The results are then compared to similar studies in other species,
e.g. Northern pike (Bekkevold et al., 2015) and North Sea houting (Hansen et
al. 2008), to examine if the same patterns of populations structure are observed
in roach populations.

The roach is an omnivorous sh, living
in most lakes and slow running river
systems. They have a broad range of
food choices, and its growth and size,
is highly dependent on the ecologi-
cal status of the lake (Kottelat et al.,
2007; Arranz et al., 2015; Jacobsen et
al., 2004). They are known to migrate
during spawning season and in order to
nd feeding grounds, and may migrate
up to hundreds of kilometres (Lucas et
al., 2001).
The aim of this study is to in-
vestigate genetic dierences and popu-
lation structures amongst roach popu-
lations in Danish and Swedish streams
and lakes, using microsatellite mark-
ers. The population structure of roach
was investigated by Witt Nielsen, L.,
2012, who found a dierence between
the two river systems (Gudenåen and
Skjernå, Denmark).
The data obtained in Witt Nielsen, L.,
2012 was utilized in this study along
with additional samples from Jutland,
Zealand and Sweden, in order to iden-
tify factors inuencing the population
structure, with focus on inuence of
geographical factors, i.e. the Jutland
ridge and geographical distance.
Methods
A total of 15 locations were sampled
for roach, and, between 22-50 indi-
viduals were collected and analysed
from each site (table 2.1).
Since fo-
cus was on exploring the inuence of
the Jutland ridge, sampling was fo-
cused on east  and western Jutland
(11 sites).
Samples from Zealand (1
site) and from Sweden (3 sites) were
included to increase the sampling size
from the east side of the ridge and to
explore the inuence of geographical

were caught by electroshing, except

samples for microsatellite analyses was
either gill or muscle tissue.

Molecular analysis
For molecular analysis, DNA was ex-
tracted using Chelex Resin 100, with
proteinase K (Walsh et al., 1991). The
extracted DNA was amplied in PCR
at 55 degrees for 25 cycles, with 13
dierent microsatellite primers. Their
size ranges and primer sequences are
listed in gure 2.2 (Hamilton et al.,
2008). The DNA fragments were anal-
ysed on an ABI Prism 3110 Genetic
Analyser (Applied Biosystems), with
the LIZ 500 standard, and hereafter
genotyped in the program GeneMap-
per.
Statistical analysis
Deviations
from
Hardy-Weinberg
equilibrium (HWE) were tested in
the program Genepop on the web
(http://genepop.curtin.edu.au/) along
with Wier and Cockerham's Fis value
(Raymond et al., 1995; Rousset, F.,
2008; Wier & Cockerham, 1984). As
the sample Sövdeborgssjön was devi-
ating from HWE in most of the loci
and the loci Ca3 and Ca5 deviated
from HWE in most samples, they were
removed from the data.
In addition,
all individuals missing more than ve
genotypes were removed.
Hereafter
the allele frequencies and the allelic
richness for each locus and samples
were estimated using the program Fs-
tat (Goudet, J., 1995).
The allelic
richness is independent of sample size
and can therefore be used for compar-
ison between the samples of unequal
sizes. The genetic dierences between
the samples were estimated as Fst be-
tween pairs of samples using Genepop
on the web along with the level of sig-
nicance with an exact G test for all
loci (Raymond et al., 1995). The Fst
matrix was then used to visualize the
genetic dierences between the sam-
ples in a multidimensional scaling plot
in R.
A Factorial Correspondence analysis
was visualized in a AFC 3D plot in
the program Genetix, as an additional
way to identify grouping among the
individuals (Belkhir et al., 1996).
The genetic clustering was analyzed
using
the
Bayesian
clustering
ap-
proach
implemented
in
Structure
(Pritchard et al., 2009).
The prob-
ability of clustering was estimated
with
10.000
Markov
Chain
Monte
Carlo
steps,
and
10.000
Burn-ins,
with 20 replicates for each k, and
the
most
likely
clustering
identi-
ed as LnP(k).
Structure Harvester
(http://taylor0.biology.ucla.edu/struct
ureHarvester/) was then used to ana-
lyze the results from Structure, and ad
hoc statistics were used to estimate ∆
k as an estimation of clustering popu-
lations, k (Evanno et al., 2005, Earl et
al., 2012).
When sampling individuals within a
relative small area and catching small
individuals, there is a possibility that
the individuals are relatives, leading to
bias in the allele frequencies (Hansen
et al., 1997). Here, the number of sib-
lings were estimated for all sampled
locations in the program COLONY
(Jones et al.,2010; Wang, J. 2016).

Discussion
This study describes the population
structure in roach in a suite of Dan-
ish and Swedish lakes and streams,
and the results suggests that roach
has a hierarchical structure with a to-
tal of 4 clusters.
I argue that this
pattern relates primarily to geograph-
ical relationships, i.e the occurrence
of the Jutland ridge.
This is espe-
cially supported by the secondary hi-
erarchical structure which shows that
the groups west of the ridge (Skjern å,
Kongeå and Vidå) group into one clus-
ter, the Jutland group east of the clus-
ter (Gudenå), whereas the third and
fourth cluster consists of populations
from Sweden/ Zealand and MOL, re-

Conclusion
The aim of this study was to investi-
gate the population structure amongst
roach in a suite of Danish and Swedish
lakes and streams, with a special focus
on the Jutland ridge.
The analysis demonstrated that the
population

suggests

eects
seems to have an inuence on the pop-
ulation structure.
The overall struc-

period, as well as the second hierarchi-

Jutland ridge. The second hierarchical
structure also seem to be aected by
more present eects, such as high con-
