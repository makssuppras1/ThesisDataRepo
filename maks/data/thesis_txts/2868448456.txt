Abstract
Biological nanocoatings can be found on the surface of the eyes of insects. The nanostructures of
these carry information about their physical and optical properties, which manifest in their influ-
ence on their reflectance under the incidence of light with varying polarisations and wavelengths.
This study introduces first the Method of Auxiliary Sources (MAS) in a simpler setting, focusing
on nanowires on a dielectric substrate, before extending it to nanostructures represented by curves.
The incident light is modelled as a plane wave, with a brief comparison to Gaussian beam repre-
sentations. Auxiliary sources, represented by electric or magnetic line currents, replace the interior
and exterior fields at the boundaries between media. Boundary conditions at the interface are then
enforced in chosen test points. Additionally, a theoretical approach to model nanostructures as
planes is presented. The implementation of MAS for nanostructures represented by curves is car-
ried out in C++ using OpenMP for CPU computations and NVIDA CUDA for GPU computations,
enabling high-performance simulations. This allows for fast computations, making it possible to
achieve high accuracy results within reasonable timeframes. The MAS results are compared with
COMSOL simulations, validating both the theoretical foundation and the implementation. Further
investigations focus on reducing runtime while maintaining accuracy. The observance of resonances
within the nanostructures is briefly noted, and comparing it to larger nanostructures shows an ap-
proach to possibly minimise these. The considerations of the MAS-implementations are applied to
the inverse model, where the forward solver (MAS) is used iteratively. Synthetic data generated in
COMSOL, designed to mimic real-life experimental data, is used, thus avoiding the inverse crime.
The inverse model employs Bayesian inference with a Gaussian process prior, generating curve real-
isations evaluated at specified grid points. Bayesian inference is in this project implemented using
the pre-conditioned Crank-Nicholson scheme, where proposals are either accepted or rejected based
on an acceptance probability. The inverse model is tested under various parameters, demonstrat-
ing the ability to reconstruct nanostructures with a relative observational error of 2 % when using
noise-free synthetic data. With noise added, the reconstructed curves deviate more from the true
structures but still capture essential features, highlighting the method’s robustness. In conclusion,
the presented theoretical and numerical framework successfully reconstructs nanostructures under
the given assumptions. These results pave the way for extending the method to nanostructures
represented by planes and incorporating real-life measurement data in future work.

Introduction
Figure 1.1: Illustration of an artificial and
a natural biological nanostructure surface.
Biological nanocoatings are found in numerous settings
in nature. More specifically these can be found on the
eyes of insects such as for example spiders, flies, beetles
and moths. These nanostructures may exhibit different
properties, that could be used for concepts like navi-
gation and communication with relation to the polar-
isation and wavelength of light [1]. They may also be
formed by the environment, that they are found within.
This could for example be, that they are specifically
designed for living above or below water [2]. The re-
flectance of such nanocoatings hold considerable value,
and the study of such nanostructures allow for design of
bio-inspired antireflective coatings [3, 4, 5]. An exam-
ple of two different nanostructures can be seen in Figure
1.1.
The objective of this project is to apply mathe-
matical methods to study these biological nanostruc-
tures. Measurements in real-life scenarios can be ob-
tained either by directly studying the surface of the
nanostructures or by observing how they interact with
incident light across different wavelengths and polarisa-
tions. The interaction of incident light and a nanostruc-
ture can be considered as an electromagnetic scattering
problem. Thus, if some nanostructure is given, the elec-
tromagnetic field and hereby the reflectance would be
determinable. This allows for comparisons of the reflectance for different nanostructures, that pos-
sibly could disclose some features of the environment in which the insect could be found. Though,
if these reflectances instead are the only identity that can be measured, it might be possible to
compute approximations of the nanostructures, that the reflectances are found of.
Computing the scattered electromagnetic fields of nanostructures can be accomplished using
a wide-range of methods, though due to the complex surface the use of numerical methods may
be most suitable. For this project, we will study the the Method of Auxiliary Sources (MAS) to
compute the electromagnetic fields. MAS is a well-established numerical method with relatively
low time and space complexity [6]. The method works by introducing auxiliary sources both in
the interior and exterior of interfaces between different media and then employing the boundary
conditions in chosen test points.
To implement this, we study the theory presented in [7] and
expand it a more general case, that can be applied for this problem. Now, whenever the solution is
obtained the method allows for evaluation in any given observation point. The incident light may be
chosen to be represented by a plane wave with some given polarisation and wavelength. A benefit
==PAGE:7==
2
of this method is the possibility to evaluate the scattered field for any polarisation of incident light
by a linear combination of two solutions, thus minimising the number of times the solver has to
be applied. The computation of the scattered electromagnetic fields of some known nanostructure
under incidence of a plane wave will be known as the forward problem, which will be considered in
Chapter 2.
The inverse problem will then be the setup, where we have reflectances measured for different
wavelengths and polarisations in some given observation points, and then the nanostructure has to
be estimated. The inverse model will be an application of Bayesian inference that utilises Gaus-
sian process priors and the pre-conditioned Crank-Nicholson scheme (pCN) to formulate proposed
nanostructures. Inspiration for these choices were found in [8] in which Bayesian inference has been
used to estimate source trajectories and intensities from wave field measurements. The setup of this
project though differs in several aspects, most significantly by the fact, that they have an analytical
solution to the forward problem, which does not apply in this context.
To gather data similar
to real-life measurements, the forward problem has to be solved multiple times in each iteration
in pCN. Thus low time complexity of our forward solver is essential for the success of the inverse
solver. Now, the data used in the inverse model will be computed in COMSOL, while the MAS is
the forward solver used in every iteration of pCN, thus avoiding the inverse crime. The theory of
the inverse problem will be considered in Chapter 3.

The results of the forward problem for these curves
will be presented in Chapter 5.
The results will be
assessed to verify that the model works as expected
and the performance of the implementation will be pre-
sented. The results of the inverse model is then presented in Chapter 6.
Lastly, the results will be considered and discussed in Chapter 7, where also possibly further
work will be presented.
The discussion and the work of this project will then be concluded in
Chapter 8.

frequency domain. The scattered field is to be computed for relatively complex structures. Due
to this complexity, we will resort to the use of numerical solvers rather than analytical solutions.
There are countless of approaches, the easiest of which may be to use a tool such as COMSOL to
compute the scattered field. COMSOL is based on the finite element method and can be perceived
as relatively slow compared to other methods, but it presents reasonably accurate results. Though,
we need to consider an inverse scattering solver in which the forward problem has to be solved
repeatedly.
Thus, in order to realistically being able to solve the inverse problem, we need to
consider a method, that can both solve the problem accurately and fast.
We therefore instead
introduce the Method of Auxiliary Sources (MAS) due to it being a well-established numerical
technique that has a low cost in terms of both memory and runtime [6].
COMSOL will then
afterwards be used as a tool to validate the results.
MAS works by placing auxiliary sources in the interior and exterior of some boundary and then
enforcing the boundary conditions in each case. These boundary conditions result in a, perhaps
relatively large, linear system.
Solving this linear system results in coefficients, that allow for
evaluating of the scattered field in any given observation point. This is favourable due to the fact,
that real-life data is measured only in the far field, and thus the fields must also be determined
there.
In order to introduce MAS, we consider first an application with relatively simple structures. We
will study a single nanowire on a dielectric substrate after which we will include multiple nanowires.
The implementation of this will be conducted in Matlab and the results can be used to get insights
into how MAS works.
Next, we will consider curves in R2 as a representation of a biological nanostructure. To address
the form of real-life measurement data, we need to consider a more complex scattering structure
and multiple polarisations. This will be the forward model, that is used repeatedly in the inverse
model, which will be considered in Chapter 3.
Therefore, the implementation has to be done

2.2
Nanostructures: Curves
The next step is to consider the biological nanostructures represented by curves. In theory one
might be able to approximate a curve to the circles, that the nanowires can be expressed by. An
example could be the loosely approximated curve, that can be seen in Figure 2.6. Thus, one might
be able to utilise the previous computations to expand the problem to these curves. It though
presents the problem of how these nanowires should be chosen, such that we may get a close enough
approximation to data of the form seen in Figure 1.2. In the end, this might not even be favourable
due to the amount of nanowires one may need to do this approximation. Instead we will consider
the curves of the nanostructures themselves. This leads to increased complexity of the boundary
conditions and the choice of test points and auxiliary sources, but theory and implementation can
then easily be applied to any given nanostructure without considering how, we can approximate it
with nanowires.

Now that we are studying curves representing biological nanostructures, it is only reasonable to
consider them in contexts that resemble real-life applications. Normally, these measurements are
done with several wavelengths and polarisations of incident light for which the resulting reflectance
is measured at different angles. The incident light could be chosen such that either normal or oblique
incidence is obtained on the smooth dielectric substrate, though for our case, we consider normal
incidence only. The reflectance can easily be obtained in any observation point in the MAS-model
due to the analytic expression, that can be evaluated using the complex amplitudes. The wavelength
can moreover easily be changed, though this requires the linear system to be solved again. Thus,
what remains to be considered is how to change the polarisation. This can be done by a linear
combination of two choices of incident fields and auxiliary sources, the first of which is analogous
to the theory presented for the nanowires. After considering the fields, we will study the boundary
conditions, that increases in complexity, when we wish to consider an arbitrary curve. Now, as
mentioned, we consider this in relation to real-life measurements in which we are only interested
in the far-field observations of the reflectance. Thus the far field patterns will be studied. Lastly,
we introduce the model simulated in COMSOL in order to verify this forward model and compute
synthetic data for testing of the inverse model.

2.2.5

The forward problem can also be solved with COMSOL. The reason why MAS is more advantageous
in our case, is that we need a both fast and accurate model.
While COMSOL might be quite

the inverse model. Though, for verifying that MAS has been formulated and implemented correctly,

Bayesian Inverse Scattering Solver
The inverse problem utilises measurement data of the reflectance to estimate a curve representing
an unknown nanostructure. In order to consider this, we will introduce Bayesian data analysis,
which in general can be divided into three steps. The first is to formulate a full probability model
considering our knowledge about the problem, perhaps in terms of physical understanding of the
problem as well as specifying the form of the measurements. Secondly, a posterior distribution of
interest needs to be computed. This posterior distribution should be chosen such that it describes
the unobservable quantities M given the measurements U, as these quantities are the objectives
to be estimated. If the full probability model correctly specifies the prior P(M) and likelihood
P(U|M), we can compute the posterior P(M|U) up to some constant by Bayes’ Rule,
P(M|U) = P(U|M)P(M)
P(U)
Lastly, the resulting posterior distribution can be used to assess the estimate of the unobservable
quantities. If the result is satisfactory, we may terminate now, otherwise the steps can be repeated
with new knowledge about the problem. [12, page 3]
This approach allows us to infer some unobservable quantities from observable measurements
using Bayes’ Rule, and the method is thus commonly known as Bayesian inference. The posterior
distribution is usually studied by numerically proposing samples of the distribution, since it generally
is not achievable when considering complex problems [12, page 6-8]. This is also the case of this
project, where we aim to compute the curve representing the nanostructure by given measurements
of the reflectance.
To setup the full probability model, we need to consider the prior and the likelihood first. The
prior can often be formulated by some knowledge of the physical system. The prior can be thought
of as an initial guess of identities before measuring any data. In the case of this project, we will
consider nanostructures represented by curves in an infinite-dimensional space. To address this,
we will introduce Gaussian process priors. A Gaussian process lets us sample from a multivariate
Gaussian distribution to obtain function values in some given grid points. This thus leaves the
choice of parameters of the Gaussian process, that we may estimate based on knowledge of the form
of the biological nanostructures. The likelihood will be computed by solving the forward model for
some given nanostructure and comparing the result with the observed data. This nanostructure is
formulated by a sample from the Gaussian process and the update in the pre-conditioned Crank-
Nicholson Scheme, which is a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method. This method will form
proposed nanostructures and then accept or reject them. The accepted proposals may then lastly
allow us to estimate the curve of the nanostructure, that generated the observed data.

As described above our aim is to compute the posterior density P(f|R). In most cases as with the

In other words, a Markov chain is a sequence of random variables, where the conditional prob-
ability of each random variable given all the previous random variables in the chain depends only
on the random variable just before. The Metropolis-Hastings algorithm is a well known method for
sampling from a Bayesian posterior distribution. It is in fact a general term for a family of MCMC
methods. Overall, the approach is that we first sample from some starting distribution. Next, we
sample a proposal from some jumping or proposal distribution, that is related to the sample that
was previously accepted. Then we compute some acceptance rate between 0 and 1, that is computed
by comparing the proposal to the previously accepted sample. We then accept the proposal by the
probability of the acceptance rate. The main advantage is the fact that every time a new proposal
is sampled, the acceptance of it improves the approximated distribution to converge towards the
target distribution [12, page 275-276].

the Gaussian process. Moreover, we need to determine γ, that will determine how likely we are to
accept a proposal. If γ is large, we will accept fewer proposals, but we will get higher accuracy
on the proposals that are accepted. Both δ and γ have to be picked with consideration as the
choice highly affects the success of the inverse model for the specific choice. Also, they have to be
chosen as a pair, as simply increasing γ without changing δ could simply result in very few accepted

that the proposal is relatively closer, or of course better, to be accepted. Now one could consider
simply lowering δ significantly while increasing γ a lot as well, and then simply wait and then get
the best solution. This will though most likely not be the case, as this could result in reaching a
local maximum in likelihood from which we cannot jump far enough from to be able to find the
true maximum in likelihood.
The simple choice is then to choose some fixed δ and γ, let pCN run and evaluate the result.
Generally, we would like around one fourth of the proposals to be accepted. If this is not the case,
we can change the parameters and run again.
Now, when choosing for example γ too large, we may have relatively small convergence in the
beginning, and thus we may need to run a lot of iterations to simply get close to the solution. In
the same way, if we choose δ too small, we may never get close enough to the true solution. Thus

The theoretical study of the models to be implemented has been presented in Chapter 2 and 3. In
order to compute the results, there has to be a choice of programming language for the implemen-
tation. When we are considering quite complicated models, as we might argue this case is, the most
common choice of programming language would probably be Python or Matlab, due to their
readability and simplicity. Thus, the forward model was first implemented in Matlab. This was
in principle rather satisfactory, and the results computed were within some reasonable timeframe
despite the fact, that we are solving a rather large linear system. Now Matlab is known to be
quite optimized for linear algebra, and therefore quite suitable in this case. It also allows for easy
computations as it includes quite a lot of optimized methods, we might utilise.
The problem of using Matlab mostly arose, when we were to implement the inverse model. An
implementation of the pre-conditioned Crank-Nicholson scheme clearly illustrated how long time
the inverse model could take to converge. At first, a quick solution could be to use the parallel
execution of loops on the CPU in Matlab. Using this, the iterations were quicker, but it still
wasn’t fast in any way. Thus, instead of simply waiting for the results to converge for some hours
or possibly days, it was decided to instead implement both the forward solver, the inverse model
and everything needed for this in C++ and utilise either OpenMP to parallelise on the host (CPU)
or CUDA to parallelise on the device (GPU).
The choice of implementing the models in C++ and moreover using OpenMP and significantly
CUDA is not a trivial task. Where Matlab has good readability, garbage-collection and optimized
methods, that are easily useable with nice documentation, C++ requires us to study the code deeply
and make sure all the allocated data is freed as well as make sure kernels on the device are executed
properly. Now high-performance implementations in C++ could easily be the main part of a project
as it allows for significant performance optimisation, if we were to study it thoroughly and focus
on how computations are done by each thread. For this project, the main objective should be the
forward and the inverse problem presented previously, and thus we will use the implementation in
C++ as a tool to allow us to obtain these results.
In order to limit ourselves in the study of the implementation, we will follow some conventions
for implementing. Consider first a simple loop which is fully parallelizable. Then we would have
a host and device implementation respectively as in Listing 4.1 and 4.2. This is a rather simple
case, and we will consider more complicated computations in the implementation, but they will
somewhat follow this setup if it is a simple loop. We follow the convention of writing _h and _d for
arrays on the host and device respectively.

RealMatrix & ComplexMatrix
The implementations of RealMatrix and ComplexMatrix are fairly similar. The main difference is
that RealMatrix has two arrays, one on the host and one on the device, while ComplexMatrix has
four arrays, two for the real values and two for the imaginary values on the host and on the device.

They both include constructors that allocate either on the host, the device or both according to
what is needed, so we do not allocate arrays that we do not need. This is a reasonable point to
make, due to the fact, that we might be working with relatively large matrices and thus we could
accidentally use a large amount of space if it was not considered. We use cudaMallocHost and
cudaMalloc respectively on the host and on the device. They also contain a method called free,
that frees the allocated arrays respectively using cudaFreeHost and cudaFree on the host and on
the device. If we have a case, where we would like to send the data between the host and the device,
this can be done from the host to the device using toDevice and the other way using toHost. This
is done with cudaMemcpy.
Now for the RealMatrix, we can choose a 1D-, 2D- or 3D-array while for ComplexMatrix it can
be a 1D- or 2D-array. When we are allocating the arrays they are allocated as 1D-arrays though,
but then there are helper-methods that indexes the arrays using row-major storage. These are de-
fined as overloaded methods, and for RealMatrix they are called getHostValue and setHostValue
repectively to get and set the value in host memory, and getDeviceValue and setDeviceValue
repectively to get and set the value in device memory. For ComplexMatrix they are similarly called
getHostRealValue, getHostImagValue, setHostRealValue and setHostImagValue on the host
and getDeviceRealValue, getDeviceImagValue, setDeviceRealValue and setDeviceImagValue
on the device.
There are also the methods setHostZero and setDeviceZero that respectively
uses memset and cudaMemset to overwrite the whole array on respectively the host and on the
device by 0. Now for some situations, we need the pointers to the arrays, which one can get with
getDevicePointer and getHostPointer and set with setDevicePointer and setHostPointer for
RealMatrix. Both classes also includes the method dumpResult that dumps the result in a file from
the host array, which means one need to transfer the array from the device to the host, if we are
computing on the device.
Coordinates, Field & Nanostructure
The three classes Coordinates, Field and Nanostructure all share a common structure in which
their methods are simply inherited from either RealMatrix and ComplexMatrix in the sense that
they include methods that simply calls the method of the same name on some or all its instances
of RealMatrix or ComplexMatrix.
Coordinates includes two instances of RealMatrix, one for both the x- and y-coordinates. It
includes an overloaded constructor that allocates the arrays, either on the host, the device or both.
Moreover it includes free and toHost that simply executes the method in RealMatrix of the same
name for both instances.
Field includes three instances of ComplexMatrix, one for each of the x-, y- and z-components
of the electromagnetic fields. Again it has an overloaded constructor that allocates the arrays in
the same way. It moreover inherits free, setHostZero, setDeviceZero, toDevice and toHost.
Nanostructure includes two instances of RealMatrix, one for the x-values and the function
values f evaluated in the x-values. It simply has a constructor and the method free.

The incident fields do not depend on the nanostructure and can easily be obtained without solving

Solving the Linear Systems
In order to solve the linear system for both β = 0◦and β = 90◦, we now do a loop over these two
polarisations. In each loop we execute forwardSolver that executes everything for the system to
be solved for each segment. Inside the loop we then either execute computeScatteredSubFields,
computeInteriorSubFields and computeIncidentSubFields for the complex field evaluations or
computeFarFieldPattern to compute the far field pattern. These methods store the respective
polarisation in either of the two instances in the variables ending with _pol. The reason this is
done is that it allows us to use the same _pol-variables to compute fields for every polarisation
β ∈[0◦, 90◦], and thus we save quite a lot of runtime instead of solving the problem for each
polarisation.
Obtaining the Solution
In order to obtain the solution, we need to combine the _pol-variables. As explained in Chap-
ter 2, for each polarisation β, we can obtain the solution as a linear combination of the case
where β = 0◦and β = 90◦. To evaluate the result for a specific polarisation, we execute either
computeScatteredFields, computeInteriorFields and computeInteriorFields for the complex
fields or combineFarFieldPattern for the far field pattern. If we were simply interested in the fields
evaluated in the observation points, we can save the results with either dumpFields for the complex
fields or dumpFarFields for far field patterns.

GaussianProcess: Realisations of a Gaussian Process
The computation of the realisations of a Gaussian process was the first to be implemented on the
device as a brief implementation in Python presented just how long time the execution can be
in Python. The implementation was therefore, as with the previous mentioned, implemented in
C++ with CUDA on the device. This allowed us to generate quite a lot of realisations within a very
short amount of time. Fast computation of the Gaussian realisations is essential whenever we need
to run the inverse model, as we need to compute a new realisation in each iteration to compute a
proposed nanostructure.
In a similar way to the previously mentioned classes, we have a constructor in which we allocate

4.5
Reflectance and Log-Likelihood
The last thing, that needs to be done for the the inverse model to be able to run, is the computation of
reflectance and the log-likelihood. We store some dataset in a file, that was computed by COMSOL.
This file stores the information of which wavelengths, polarisations and points of observance to
compute the reflectance for.
This is then stored in a 3D-matrix and computed in the method
computeReflectanceMatrix, that simply iterates over all the different settings. Here we need to
execute the forward solver twice every time we have a new wavelength. We need to execute it
twice to be able to obtain the solution for all possibly polarisations.
If we had more points of
observance we do not need to execute the forward solver several times as we have the liberty of
simply evaluating the solution in any given point. Until now, everything has been done on the
device, but for the computation of the log-likelihood, we transfer the data to the host. The main
reason is that the computation of the log-likelihood is not parallelizable in the same way as most
of the previous implementations, since it introduces a reduction element. Moreover, the size of the
dataset is quite limited, and thus it will not create significant performance-loss to transfer it and
do the computation on the host.

Discussion and Further Work
Let us first consider the models in terms of their mathematical performance. This involves evalu-
ating their outcomes under the given assumptions, without necessarily comparing them to actual
biological nanostructures and possible measurements of their reflectances under incidence of light.
We considered first the simpler geometry of nanowires on a dielectric substrate. It gave us the oppor-
tunity to get insight into the Method of Auxiliary Sources. The presented results were promising,
as the electromagnetic fields displayed patterns consistent with expectations for such a scenario.
The transmission error across the boundary was measured, demonstrating that solving the linear
system successfully enforced the boundary conditions between air and the nanowire. Now, when
considering several nanowires, we considered computing the fields for each of them separately, since
this could decrease runtime as the size of the linear systems were to be decreased. Unfortunately,
this showed loss in accuracy, when the nanowires were close, but could possibly be used if they were
located farther apart. This was also evident, as the boundary conditions were no longer satisfied.
It was not the desired result for this project, as we had hoped to apply a comparable approach to
the nanostructures by partitioning them into segments. The results of the nanowire computations
indicated, that this method is a poor choice for enhancing performance due to its lack of accuracy
and the close proximity of the segmented nanostructures. To make sure, that this tendency was also
apparent for the computations of the nanostructures, the solutions using segmented nanostructures
were compared to using entire nanostructures. It illustrated the expected results, that the accuracy
would decrease significantly, if the nanostructures were parted into smaller segments. This raised
the question of how else the runtime could be reduced.
The large linear systems and the significant amount of computations meant, that the performance
initially was inadequate. That is, when implementing it in Matlab, the runtime was relatively
large. To aid this, the number of grid points could be decreased, which ultimately meant lowering
the accuracy. Considering only the forward problem, this was not necessarily a problem, since large
runtimes of a couple of minutes would not be critical, if the model was simply to be run a few
times. Though, knowing that the forward solver were to be used iteratively in the inverse solver, it
had to be reformulated to improve the performance significantly. Fortunately, quite a large amount
of the computations in the forward solver could be done fully in parallel. This is due to the fact,
that most of the computations are done in specified points, i.e. test points, auxiliary source points
or observation points. The model was then successfully implemented in C++ both in parallel on
the CPU using OpenMP, BLAS and LAPACK and on the GPU using NVIDA CUDA, cuBLAS
and cuSolver. The parallel implementations were done following three conventions, but could in
theory be studied much more in depth to improve the performance further. Nonetheless, the study
of this may require thorough consideration of the thread structure and runtime measurements.
Furthermore, the linear solver utilised the library cuSolver, and was responsible for the majority
of the runtime. Given that the cuSolver library is already optimised, implementing it ourselves
would most likely not result in any performance gains. Also, as the main part of this project has
been to consider the forward and inverse problem, further performance improvements in terms of
implementation was not considered. In addition, it had already shown significant improvements, as
==PAGE:93==
88
the solutions now could be obtained in less than a second, which was significantly faster than in
Matlab.
The forward model using MAS could then be evaluated with very low time cost. Now, this
would all be a waste, if the model itself did not present results with high accuracy. Theoretically,
the method involves several auxiliary sources that represent the field on opposite side of a boundary,
with the boundary condition enforced at test points. MAS have already been presented in numerous
articles and is a well-known and used method. As a result, the theoretical prerequisites necessary for
the success of the method have been established. To validate both the theoretical derivations and
the implementation of the model, the solutions for both complex near fields and far field patterns
were compared with solutions generated by COMSOL. Qualitatively, there was strong alignment
between the two models. The relative error in the near field was beneath 1 % almost everywhere
in the chosen window, in which the largest errors were present closest to the boundary of the
nanostructure. Now due the the absolute far field being used in the inverse model, the most crucial
errors were the ones measured of the far field patterns. The relative error for most angles were low
and almost all of the errors were beneath 2 %. Thus there is a reasonable correspondence between
the two models, thus validating the forward model of this project.
Now that the model was verified, the objective was to decrease the runtime to make sure the the
inverse model could converge within some reasonable time. The solutions, which were compared to
COMSOL, were computed using a fine grid to ensure optimal accuracy. The question was now how
many grid points, that could be removed while still preserving accuracy. Considering these results
scaling from 1000 to 200 grid points would lead to relative error around 10 % or less, while from
1000 to 300 grid points would lead to relative error around 1 % or less. This consideration was
crucial for the inverse model, as it required both fast execution and reasonable accuracy.
Then, before being able to consider the inverse model, the synthetic data computed in COMSOL
was compared to similar results computed by MAS. As we would have expected due to the previous
comparison of the two models, we had good correspondence between the synthetic data and MAS-
results. The relative error between the datasets, both the angle-resolved and the one-observational
data was around 1 %. This correspondence favourable, and if the data was instead obtained through
real-life measurement, we would expect the error to be much larger due to uncertainties.
It may have been a vital decision to implement the forward solver in C++ with CUDA for the
success of the inverse model, at least if it was to be run for enough iterations. The runtime of
the inverse model was first measured for different datasets, as they determine the number of times
the forward solver has to be run in each iteration. As expected, the one-observational data took
significantly longer to run than the angle-resolved data due to the large number of wavelengths for
which the forward solver needs to be run twice for each in every iteration. In real-life measurements,
the observational data could include 500 wavelengths, requiring the forward solver to be run 1000
times per iteration. This would take an incredible amount of time to run, and thus some of the
data points needed to be removed. Both kinds of data may be measured for experiments and thus
to simulate real-life data, they were both considered. Though, with the knowledge of how long time
it would require for the inverse model to run with the one-observational data, it was decided to
mainly test the implementation using the angle-resolved data.
Initially, for the inverse model, one must choose a covariance function and its parameters. There
are a lot of different covariance functions that could be used. A common choice is the squared
exponential which offers quite a simple kernel, while the Matérn kernel offers a bit more complexity
by adding an additional parameter and having a more complex formulation. It was estimated by
comparing realisations to the nanostructures, that the best choice of parameters could be τ = 0.7,
ℓ= 0.07 and p = 2. If we of course didn’t know the structures beforehand, the knowledge of the
parameters could be estimated by a specialist in biological nanostructures. Otherwise one could do
thorough testing with different choices of parameters to try to estimate the best variables. Now,
==PAGE:94==
89
Chapter 7. Discussion and Further Work
even though the forward solver was implemented on the GPU, it did not mean, that it converged
and accepted enough proposals in a couple minutes, and thus it would be a time consuming process.
For this project we considered the estimated parameters and their neighbours, i.e. changing each of
parameters a bit either to be larger or smaller to see how it affects the model. Considering the results,
no significant differences were observed when accounting for the prior, likelihood, and posterior.
Also the errors for the different parameters were quite similar, though considering the shape of
the realisations, and that we wanted to include a Gaussian process that creates realisations, that
are most similar to the nanostructures, the already estimated parameters were kept for additional
testing.
The covariance function and its parameters are not the only identities that had to be chosen.
The choice of step size δ and accuracy-parameter γ also had to be chosen. Out of the box, these
would most likely be set as constants. Though this might have the consequence of the solution not
converging towards the global maximum in posterior but instead towards some local maximum. In
order to avoid this, one may choose a larger δ in order to let pCN explore curves that differ largely
from each other. Now, simply choosing δ very large, say δ = 0.3, either γ had to be chosen low
such that samples would still be accepted or an incredible amount of iterations had to be run. For
the first case the problem would be, that the accuracy of the estimated curve would be far from
the true value, and for the second case the algorithm needs to be run for a very long time. If δ is
too large and γ similarly very large, it might appear that almost no proposals are accepted, thus
leaving a non-converged estimated curve. The solution presented was to let δ be large initially and
then lower it in every iteration, while at the same time increasing γ. This worked quite well, and
the curve error of the reconstructed curves by the mean of three chains were all under 2 %. The
observational error was between 3 to 6 %. Comparing this to the error between the true curve and
COMSOL of around 1 %, it gives a reasonable result.
Real-life measurement data often contains inherent uncertainties, which means it may lack the
precision and consistency of synthetic data generated using COMSOL. Also, the model may not
depict real-life to perfection, thus leading to differences. To consider how well the model could
hypothetically perform in such cases, noise was added to the absolute far fields. As one would
expect, the error increases when adding noise. The reconstructed curves though seemed to capture
some of the structure even with a large amount of noise. With low levels of noise, the model was still
quite accurate, thus relating the model to data more alike real-life measurements. In fact, the noise
added was larger than the observational error, when comparing it with the non-noisy dataset. Now,
the added noise was normally distributed and followed the same exact distribution throughout all the
data points. In real-life measurements it is not uncommon for noise to follow a normal distribution,
but as human errors may lead to slight uncertainties, the distribution could change and might not
necessarily be normal. We also saw that the error when computing the observational error with
respect to the noisy dataset was significantly larger. Most likely, a nanostructure that could perfectly
align with a noisy dataset may not exist, and thus it still somehow fits to the true dataset though
with increasing error when adding noise. The first test using noisy data only had noise added on
the absolute far field, but the polarisation angle and wavelength may also include uncertainties.
An example was provided, demonstrating that the model had difficulty reconstructing a curve that
closely matched the true one. Thus it seemed, that the model is more sensitive to uncertainties
on these parameters. The reconstructed curves still exhibited some of the right features of the
true curves. Remembering, that the absolute far field changes quite significantly when changing
the wavelength a bit, especially for wavelengths of 250-400 nm, it seems reasonable, that the error
increases a lot. It may be noted, that these computations were done without knowledge of how
large the noise could be in real-life. Thus, if the uncertainties exhibit lower variance, we may get
better results, possibly closer to the results obtained without noise.
The data containing only measurements directly above the nanostructure, i.e. the one-observational
==PAGE:95==
90
data, was also used to reconstruct curves. Running the inverse model for this data can be quite
challenging due to the large runtimes, and thus the model was first run on a dataset with 51 wave-
lengths, after which the reconstructed curve was used as initial guess for running the model with
110 wavelengths and lastly 167 wavelengths. The reconstructed curves had a larger curve error, but
they had a smaller relative observational error. This may be a consequence of lost information when
only considering measurements done directly above the nanostructure. This combined with the long
runtimes illustrates, that angle-resolved data might be more ideal for inferring nanostructures from
measured reflectance.
Several assumptions were made in formulating the forward and inverse problems to simulate the
scattered field of a biological nanostructure and estimate the curve of an unknown nanostructure
from observed reflectances. These assumptions include, that the nanostructures may be considered
as finite, that they have a specific height and a constant real refractive index with no frequency
dependency, that they are homogenous and isotropic in the interior, that their surface may be de-
scribed by a function f : R →R and that the incident light can be expressed as a plane wave.
Though these assumptions may not all be aligned completely with the reality of biological nanos-
tructures, they allow for the initial modelling of them, and this may in time allow for less restrictive
assumptions. The proteins forming the biological nanostructures that may be found on the eyes
of insects are for example most likely not completely homogenous. It would be plausible for the
properties to vary throughout the protein and the refractive index may vary in places. It might
though be quite hard to determine this exactly. Another thing that may be easier to establish would
be a refractive index that depends on the frequency of the light. Mathematically, this would also
be quite easy to implement, as it would be a simple computation before running the forward solver
as it is now. The assumption of height of the nanostructure concern mostly the inverse model, as
the forward model may take a nanostructure of any heigh as an input. To address an arbitrary
height in the inverse model, we could run the model a number of iterations after which the mean,
and thus the height, can be computed of the sampled curves, and if it differs significantly, we may
either increase or decrease the height.
The setup for real-life experiments, for which the generated synthetic data is based on, defines
the incident light to propagate along the negative y-axis.
In other words, if we had a smooth
nanostructure or if we were to consider a substrate beneath the nanostructure, the incident light
would be reflected and transmitted under normal incidence on the interfaces. This is a common
way to setup experiments for measurements. Then, as presented in this project, we can have angle-
resolved data or data observed in only on point directly above the nanostructure. The data could in
theory also be observed only at one point at any other angle, and the model could then still be used
as is. There could also be experiments, where the incident light does not only propagate along the
negative y-axis, but also has a x-component. If we consider the case without the dielectric substrate
underneath, we could easily consider this scenario as well by introducing a basis-shift that rotates
the Cartesian coordinate system (x, y, z) 7→(˜x, ˜y, ˜z), such that the incident light propagates along
the negative ˜y-axis. When the complex amplitudes have been obtained, we may then compute the
results in the initial coordinate system by doing the inverse basis-shift. If there were a dielectric
substrate present underneath the nanostructure, a similar approach can be used, but we would have
to keep in mind, that the Fresnel coefficients for the reflection and transmittance of the substrate
need to be dependant on the angle of incidence.
The assumption of the nanostructures being finite would of course be natural due to the impos-
sibility of an infinite nanostructure in real-life. Though, the nanostructures may be much larger,
than the ones simulated in the project. A simple solution could be to simply increase the size
of the nanostructure. This might though introduce computational complications as a significant
larger nanostructure would require more allocated space needed, if we want to achieve the same
accuracy. If we would allow for a less fine grid, it could be possible, but then the small fluctuations

in the nanostructures might be overseen.
This was briefly considered in Section 5.2.6, where a
nanostructure of about 10 times the size of the others was considered. The nanostructure might
though be even larger than this, and that may be hard for the model to achieve solutions for. Also
the runtime increases significantly, when the number of test points and auxiliary source points is
increased, which we would need for a larger nanostructure. Now, the incident light was modelled
by a plane wave for this project, except for a brief computation using a Gaussian beam instead.
At this time, it has not been disclosed which of the options best simulates real-life experiments.
If they are conducted with focused light, a Gaussian beam would be best to simulate the incident
light. Although the width of the focused light is unknown, it could mean that only a subsection of
the nanostructure is illuminated, rather than the entire structure. This could therefore allow us to
model only a smaller part of the nanostructure, that has a width slightly bigger than the width of
the focused light. This means, that even if we need to consider larger nanostructures, it would not
be necessary to include the whole nanostructure in the computations. Now, as we saw, there may be
resonances within the nanostructure. The appearance of them seem to be less prominent for larger
nanostructures, but whether these resonances are present in real-life experiments is also a question
to ask. When considering some finite nanostructure, one would perhaps expect some resonances
to appear. Though the larger the nanostructure is in relation to the wavelength, we would expect
them to diminish.
These considerations could serve as a foundation for further research for mathematical modelling
of biological nanostructures. If we were to consider larger nanostructures and possibly also nanos-
tructures represented by planes, the computational cost would increase significantly. Thus careful
implementations using the GPU may be the best approach. Even so, possibly ways to reduce com-
putational cost while preserving some level of accuracy might be a study of importance. For this
project, the nanostructures were simulated by curves on the top of a box, thus enclosing the media
entirely. The sides and bottom of the nanostructure-box does in fact not disclose any information
about the nanostructure itself, but they contribute largely to size of the linear system. Though,
removing them, leaving an open box, seemed to create a singular linear system, and it did therefore
not provide a solution. One might for future work instead lower the grid on the sides and bottom
to lower their impact on the runtime. Though relating it to nanostructures represented by planes,
there may need to be studied a way that can lower the computational cost more significantly. If the
same approach as presented in this project had to be used for planes, the square of the number of
grid points would need to be used to obtain as fine a grid. For example, a common choice was to
use 300 grid points, and that would mean, that for the nanostructures represented by planes they
would need to be 90.000 grid points. If the dimensions of the nanostructure at the same time had
to be increased, it would both lead to problems with space and time complexity.
The investigation of nanostructures represented by curves also offers potential for further studies.
For this project, we presented the Bayesian inference model, where the realisations of a Gaussian
process were curves that represent nanostructures. This though presented the problem of how to
choose not only the right covariance kernel but also its parameters. Now, we made an educated
guess of the parameters based on the curves of the true nanostructures and considered altering
these parameters a bit to study their influence. In real-life experiments, there might be specialists
in biology that can estimate what the parameters should be, but it might still be hard, if the nanos-
tructure were to differ from the general form of nanostructures. Thus, one could add complexity
to the inverse model by introducing more Gaussian processes, that could generate realisations for
the parameters, thus letting the parameters be updated in the inverse model. To make sure the
parameters stay within some range, one could add conditioning when sampling from the Gaussian
process. On another note, it could be interesting to study how the curves could be generated from
coordinates, where both coordinates follow some Gaussian process. This naturally leads to more
variables to be estimated, but may allow for interesting insights.

Conclusion
It may be concluded, that the theoretical framework presented for the electromagnetic scattering
problem and the inverse model based on Bayesian inference with Gaussian process priors was suc-
cessfully implemented and showed promising results. The forward model employing the Method of
Auxiliary Sources was studied for nanowires on dielectric substrates, for nanostructures represented
by curves and briefly for nanostructures represented by planes. For the nanowires and nanostruc-
tures represented by curves, the auxiliary sources was represented by electric and magnetic line
currents whereas they were represented by dipoles for the nanostructures represented by planes.
The implementation of the forward model were implemented using high performance computing
techniques in C++ with OpenMP and NVIDA CUDA for computations on the CPU and GPU re-
spectively. The model was verified by simulations using COMSOL. The forward model were then
used in the inverse model, that successfully reconstructed nanostructures represented by curves
using Bayesian inference with Gaussian process priors and the pre-conditioned Crank-Nicholson
scheme. These results were obtained with synthetic data generated by COMSOL both with and
without added noise, indicating the model’s capacity to perform robustly under varied conditions.
This study demonstrates promising outcomes in mathematical modelling of biological nanostruc-
tures, with potential future extensions to relax restrictive assumptions, integrate more detailed
knowledge, and adapt to real-life data.
