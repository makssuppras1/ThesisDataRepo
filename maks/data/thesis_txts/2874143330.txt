Abstract
The accurate modeling of associating fluids, such as water and alcohols, represents a significant chal-
lenge in chemical engineering due to the complex hydrogen-bonding networks that govern their thermo-
dynamic behavior. While advanced equations of state like CPA and PC-SAFT offer a framework for
predicting phase equilibria in these systems, their accuracy is contingent upon physically meaningful
parameterization. This work establishes a direct link between first-principles quantum-mechanical sim-
ulations, experimental spectroscopy, and thermodynamic model development to create a more robust
predictive foundation. The investigation begins by experimentally validating a methodology to measure
temperature-dependent monomer fractions in liquid methanol using infrared (IR) spectroscopy, repro-
ducing seminal literature data to establish a reliable benchmark. Concurrently, a high-level quantum-
chemical analysis is performed on a solvated methanol tetramer to construct a two-dimensional potential
energy surface. This theoretical analysis, comparing Density Functional Theory (DFT) and Hartree-Fock
theory, elucidates the critical role of electron correlation, vibrational anharmonicity, and the dynamic
coupling between intramolecular O-H stretching and intermolecular hydrogen-bond bending modes. By
integrating insights from quantum mechanics and experimental spectroscopy, this thesis demonstrates
a multi-scale approach to inform and refine the parameters of next-generation thermodynamic mod-
els, ultimately enhancing the physical realism and predictive accuracy for chemical processes involving
associating fluids.

Introduction
The accurate prediction of thermodynamic properties and phase equilibria is a foundational objective of
chemical engineering, essential for the design and optimization of industrial processes. While classical
equations of state adequately describe the behavior of simple fluids, a class of substances known as associ-
ating fluids—most notably water, alcohols, and their mixtures—presents a persistent modeling challenge.
The unique macroscopic properties of these fluids, such as elevated boiling points and complex mixing
behaviors, are governed by strong, directional, and saturable hydrogen bonds. A hydrogen bond is a
specific dipole-dipole interaction where a hydrogen atom, covalently bonded to a highly electronegative
atom, is attracted to a lone pair of electrons on an adjacent electronegative atom. In alcohols, the hy-
droxyl group facilitates the formation of dynamic molecular networks, including chains and ring clusters.
This self-association at the microscopic level is the origin of their pronounced non-ideal thermodynamic
behavior. Consequently, any theoretical model aiming for physical realism and predictive accuracy must
explicitly account for the energetic and entropic contributions of hydrogen bonding. Classical models that
neglect these interactions are inherently unreliable for systems dominated by association, necessitating
the development of more sophisticated theoretical frameworks.
In response to the limitations of classical models, molecularly-based equations of state have been devel-
oped, with the Cubic-Plus-Association (CPA) and the Statistical Associating Fluid Theory (SAFT), in-
cluding its Perturbed-Chain (PC-SAFT) variant, being prominent examples. These frameworks represent
a significant conceptual advance by incorporating an explicit association term derived from Wertheim’s
first-order thermodynamic perturbation theory. This approach partitions the residual Helmholtz energy
into a physical component, which accounts for isotropic repulsive and attractive forces, and an associ-
ation component that quantifies the contribution of hydrogen bonding. By defining the number and
type of association sites on each molecule (e.g., the ”4C” scheme for water or ”2B” scheme for higher
alcohols), these models can calculate the fraction of non-bonded sites at equilibrium. This provides a
physically sound basis for describing the equilibrium between monomers and associated clusters, leading
to marked improvements in the prediction of vapor-liquid and liquid-liquid equilibria for industrially
relevant mixtures.
The predictive power of association theories is critically dependent on the accurate parameterization
of their molecular and association terms. While physical parameters can be regressed from macroscopic
data, the association energy and volume parameters are best constrained by experimental data that
directly probe the extent of molecular association. Infrared (IR) spectroscopy has emerged as the pre-
eminent technique for this purpose, as it provides a quantitative measure of the monomer fraction—the
proportion of molecules not engaged in hydrogen bonding.
The O-H stretching vibration of a non-
hydrogen-bonded hydroxyl group produces a sharp absorption peak at a high frequency. Upon formation
of a hydrogen bond, this peak shifts to a lower frequency (a red-shift), broadens, and intensifies. By de-
convoluting these spectral features, the relative concentrations of free and hydrogen-bonded species can
be determined. The seminal work of Werner A. P. Luck, who measured temperature-dependent overtone
IR spectra of alcohols, provided the foundational monomer fraction data that has been instrumental in
the development and validation of association theories. This synergy between spectroscopy and thermo-
dynamic modeling is essential, ensuring that model parameters are not merely empirical constants but
are anchored in the physical reality of molecular interactions.
While spectroscopy provides the requisite experimental data, a fundamental understanding of the
observed spectral features necessitates a first-principles, quantum-mechanical approach.
The precise
characteristics of vibrational bands are determined by the potential energy surface (PES) governing
nuclear motion. Modern computational chemistry, particularly Density Functional Theory (DFT), allows
for the accurate mapping of these surfaces, offering insight into the nature of the hydrogen bond and
its effect on molecular vibrations. The O-H stretching motion is highly anharmonic and is dynamically
coupled to low-frequency intermolecular modes, such as the bending of the hydrogen bond itself. A
complete description therefore requires the construction of multi-dimensional potential energy surfaces
that capture the interdependence of these coordinates. By performing quantum-chemical calculations
on representative molecular clusters, such as a methanol tetramer, a detailed PES can be constructed to
analyze the intricate energetic landscape that governs the vibrational spectrum.
The preceding discussion delineates a multi-scale hierarchy of inquiry, from macroscopic thermody-
namic phenomena to the quantum-mechanical details of molecular interactions. The central thesis of this
work is that a robust and predictive understanding of associating fluids requires an integrated approach
that rigorously connects these levels. This investigation aims to forge such a connection by combin-
ing experimental spectroscopy, high-level quantum-chemical simulation, and the principles of advanced
6

thermodynamic modeling.
This thesis is structured to achieve this synthesis. First, the experimental methodology is validated
by reproducing the seminal temperature-dependent monomer fraction data for liquid methanol, thereby
establishing a reliable experimental benchmark. 1 Second, a state-of-the-art quantum-chemical protocol
is applied to a solvated methanol tetramer cluster to construct and analyze a two-dimensional poten-
tial energy surface. This analysis will elucidate the anharmonicity of the O-H stretch and the critical
role of coupling between intramolecular and intermolecular modes. A direct comparison between the
performance of DFT and Hartree-Fock theory will also be presented to demonstrate the importance of
electron correlation in accurately describing the potential energy surface. Ultimately, this work seeks to
demonstrate how first-principles insights into molecular vibrational dynamics can provide the physical
foundation needed to inform and refine the parameterization of next-generation thermodynamic models,
contributing to the development of more accurate and physically grounded predictive tools for chemical
engineering applications involving associating fluids.

2.1
Background and Importance of Hydrogen Bonding
A hydrogen bond is an intermolecular force (IMF) that forms a special type of dipole-dipole attraction
when a hydrogen atom bonded to a strongly electronegative atom exists in the vicinity of another
electronegative atom with a lone pair of electrons. Intermolecular forces (IMFs) occur between molecules.
Other examples include ordinary dipole-dipole interactions and dispersion forces. Hydrogen bonds are
generally stronger than ordinary dipole-dipole and dispersion forces, but weaker than true covalent and
ionic bonds.
Many elements form compounds with hydrogen. By plotting the boiling points of the compounds of
the group 14 elements with hydrogen, the boiling progressively increase as the number of electrons in
each element increases. The increase in boiling point happens because the molecules are getting larger
with more electrons, and so Van der Waals dispersion forces become greater.
The molecules capable of hydrogen bonding include the following:
• The hydrogen is attached directly to a highly electronegative atoms, causing the hydrogen to
acquire a highly positive charge.
• Each of the highly electronegative atoms attains a high negative charge and has at least one
”active” lone pair. Lone pairs at the 2-level have electrons contained in a relatively small volume
of space, resulting in a high negative charge density. Lone pairs at higher levels are more diffuse
and, resulting in a lower charge density and lower affinity for positive charge.[1]
Consider two water molecules coming close together. The δ+ hydrogen is so strongly attracted to
the lone pair that it is almost as if you were beginning to form a co-ordinate (dative covalent) bond. It
doesn’t go that far, but the attraction is significantly stronger than an ordinary dipole-dipole interaction.
Hydrogen bonds have about a tenth of the strength of an average covalent bond, and are constantly broken
and reformed in liquid water.

Water is an ideal example of hydrogen bonding (see Figure 1). Notice that each water molecule can
potentially form four hydrogen bonds with surrounding water molecules: two with the hydrogen atoms
and two with the oxygen atoms. There are exactly the right numbers of δ+ hydrogens and lone pairs for

allow formation of hydrogen bond interactions in a lattice of water molecules. Water is thus considered

2.1.1
Hydrogen bonding in alcohols
Hydrogen bonding is a critical intermolecular force that largely determines the properties and behaviors
of alcohols. It originates primarily from the highly polar nature of the hydroxyl (-OH) group, which con-
tains a hydrogen atom directly bonded to a strongly electronegative oxygen atom. This polarity allows
alcohol molecules to form strong hydrogen bonds through electrostatic attraction between the partially
positive hydrogen of one molecule and the lone pair electrons of oxygen in neighboring molecules. Such
interactions significantly affect physical properties, including elevated boiling points, greater viscosity,
and enhanced solubility in polar solvents when compared to hydrocarbons of similar molecular weight.
Importantly, the extent and strength of hydrogen bonding in alcohols depend on molecular structure:
primary alcohols, which have minimal steric hindrance, typically exhibit stronger and more extensive hy-
drogen bonding networks, while secondary and tertiary alcohols experience diminished hydrogen bonding
due to increased branching.
Beyond physical properties, hydrogen bonding profoundly influences the chemical reactivity and
mechanisms involving alcohols. Alcohols, acting simultaneously as hydrogen bond donors and acceptors,
frequently participate in reactions where hydrogen bonding stabilizes reaction intermediates and tran-
sition states, thereby influencing both reaction rates and product distributions. For instance, during
esterification and dehydration reactions, hydrogen bonding stabilizes specific molecular conformations
and transition states, lowering activation energies and facilitating reaction progress. Additionally, hy-
drogen bonding governs the behavior of alcohols in complex mixtures, such as biological systems, by
affecting molecular recognition processes and enzyme-substrate interactions. Hence, a comprehensive
understanding of hydrogen bonding is essential for accurately predicting the behavior and reactivity of
alcohols in various chemical and biological contexts.
2.1.2
Why does a hydrogen bond occur?
Since the hydrogen donor (N, O, or F) is strongly electronegative, it pulls the covalently bonded electron
pair closer to its nucleus, and away from the hydrogen atom. The hydrogen atom is then left with a
partial positive charge, creating a dipole-dipole attraction between the hydrogen atom bonded to the
donor and the lone electron pair of the acceptor. This results in a hydrogen bond.
2.1.3
Types of hydrogen bonds
Although hydrogen bonds are well-known as a type of intermolecular force, these bonds can also occur
within a single molecule, between two identical molecules, or between two dissimilar molecules.[2]
• Intramolecular hydrogen bonds: These occur within one single molecule. This happens when
two functional groups of a molecule can form hydrogen bonds with each other. In order for this
to happen, both a hydrogen donor and a hydrogen acceptor must be present within one molecule,
and they must be within close proximity of each other.
• Intermolecular hydrogen bonds: These occur between separate molecules in a substance. They
can occur between any number of like or unlike molecules as long as hydrogen donors and acceptors
are present in positions where they can interact with one another.
2.1.4
Properties and effects of hydrogen bonds
• On Boiling Point: When we consider the boiling points of molecules, we usually expect molecules
with larger molar masses to have higher normal boiling points than molecules with smaller molar
masses. This, without taking hydrogen bonds into account, is due to greater dispersion forces.
Larger molecules have more space for electron distribution and thus more possibilities for an in-
stantaneous dipole moment. However, when we consider hydrogen bonding, we see this is not
always the case.
• On Viscosity: The same effect that is seen on boiling point as a result of hydrogen bonding can
also be observed in the viscosity of certain substances. Substances capable of forming hydrogen
bonds tend to have a higher viscosity than those that do not. Generally, substances that have the
possibility for multiple hydrogen bonds exhibit even higher viscosities.[2]

2.1.5
Factors preventing Hydrogen bonding
• Electronegativity: Hydrogen bonding cannot occur without a significant electronegativity dif-
ference between hydrogen and the atom it is bonded to. For example, molecules such as PH3
do not participate in hydrogen bonding. PH3 exhibits a trigonal pyramidal molecular geometry
like that of NH3, but unlike ammonia it cannot hydrogen bond. This is due to the similarity in
the electronegativities of phosphorous and hydrogen (both atoms have an electronegativity of 2.1),
resulting in no significant dipole moment. This prevents the hydrogen from acquiring the partial
positive charge needed to bond with a lone pair in another molecule.[3]
• Atom Size: The size of donors and acceptors can also affect the ability to hydrogen bond. This
can account for the relatively low ability of Cl to form hydrogen bonds. When the radii of two
atoms differ greatly or are large, their nuclei cannot achieve close proximity when they interact,
resulting in a weak interaction.

Factors Affecting Monomer Fractions
• Temperature: Higher temperatures generally increase monomer fractions because thermal energy
disrupts intermolecular forces, preventing dimerization or aggregation.
For example, in water,

• Pressure: Higher pressure can favor molecular association, leading to lower monomer fractions.

interactions (e.g., in acetone, HF) influence clustering but are weaker than hydrogen bonds.
• Concentration: In dilute solutions, monomers dominate because intermolecular interactions are

2.2.4
Monomer Fraction Estimation
Monomer fraction estimation is a fundamental area within chemical engineering and thermodynamics,
significantly impacting the understanding and modeling of associating compounds, especially those with
pronounced intermolecular interactions such as hydrogen bonding. The monomer fraction essentially
quantifies the proportion of individual, unassociated molecules in equilibrium with molecular aggregates,
such as dimers or larger clusters. Accurately determining the monomer fraction is crucial, as it influences
numerous thermodynamic and physicochemical properties, including vapor-liquid equilibrium, viscosity,
solubility, and enthalpy of mixing. These properties, in turn, are pivotal for process design, optimization,
and operation in chemical engineering contexts.
Experimental determination of monomer fractions commonly involves spectroscopic techniques. In-
frared (IR) spectroscopy is particularly valuable, offering direct insight into molecular interactions by
distinguishing between hydrogen-bonded and free hydroxyl groups through characteristic absorption
bands. Recent studies have extensively utilized IR spectroscopy due to its sensitivity to changes in inter-
molecular interactions, allowing researchers to quantitatively assess the extent of association.[3] Nuclear
Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy also plays a vital role by providing detailed molecular-level
information on chemical environments and interaction patterns. Advanced NMR methodologies, such as
diffusion-ordered spectroscopy (DOSY), have improved the accuracy and resolution of monomer fraction
measurements by enabling clear differentiation between monomeric and aggregated species.[4]
On the theoretical side, several thermodynamic models have emerged to accurately predict monomer
fractions. Among these, the Statistical Associating Fluid Theory (SAFT) and the Cubic-Plus-Association
(CPA) models are widely recognized and employed in both academia and industry. SAFT, in particular,
explicitly accounts for molecular association through statistical mechanics, enabling reliable predictions
of monomer fractions and associated equilibria. CPA similarly integrates molecular association terms
into traditional cubic equations of state, enhancing their predictive accuracy for associating compounds.
These models rely heavily on accurate experimental data for parameterization, highlighting the impor-
tance of precise monomer fraction measurements.
However, challenges persist in monomer fraction estimation due to experimental and theoretical com-
plexities. Experimentally, overlapping spectral signals, low sensitivity in dilute solutions, and temperature-
dependent association equilibria complicate data interpretation and accuracy. Theoretically, simplifying
assumptions inherent in association models can limit their applicability, especially in complex multi-
component mixtures or under conditions far from standard ambient temperatures and pressures. Thus,
continued development of experimental techniques, such as two-dimensional spectroscopy or computa-
tional approaches leveraging molecular simulations, is necessary to overcome existing limitations.
Recent studies underscore the importance of integrating molecular-scale insights with macroscopic
thermodynamic models. Efforts have been made to combine quantum chemical calculations and molec-
ular simulations with spectroscopic data, providing a more holistic approach to monomer fraction es-
timation.
Such integrated methodologies not only enhance the precision of the estimations but also
facilitate a deeper understanding of underlying molecular interactions influencing monomeric distribu-
tions. This interdisciplinary approach represents a promising direction for future research, aiming to
bridge microscopic molecular behaviors with macroscopic thermodynamic predictions.

2.2.7
Why Do Overtones Happen?
Overtones happen because molecular vibrations do not behave like simple, perfect springs. Instead, real
molecules experience anharmonic motion, meaning their vibrations are slightly distorted, allowing them
to absorb energy at multiples of their fundamental frequency.
1. The Key Reason: Anharmonicity. Molecules act like tiny springs where atoms are connected
by chemical bonds. But these bonds do not behave like perfectly elastic springs. Instead: • At
low energy, the bond vibrates in a smooth, predictable way (fundamental vibration). • At higher
energy, the bond stretches more and starts behaving differently—it does not return to its original
position exactly the same way. This is called anharmonicity. • Because of anharmonicity, the
molecule absorbs additional energy at higher frequencies, leading to overtones (2×, 3×, etc., of the
fundamental frequency).
2. Quantum Mechanics Behind Overtones. In quantum mechanics, vibrations follow specific
energy levels: The fundamental vibration occurs when the molecule transitions from the ground
state (lowest energy level, v = 0) to the first excited state (v = 1). Overtones occur when the
molecule jumps directly to higher excited states (v = 2, 3, 4, . . . ), which are normally “forbidden”
in a perfectly harmonic system. However, because real molecules are anharmonic, these transitions
do happen, though with lower intensity.
3. Why Overtones Are Weaker. Overtones are less intense than the fundamental vibration be-
cause quantum selection rules make these transitions less likely. Also, energy absorption is not as
efficient at higher levels. That’s why, in infrared (IR) spectroscopy, the first overtone is weaker
than the fundamental vibration, and the second overtone is even weaker.[5]

3.1
Fundamentals of Infrared Spectroscopy
3.1.1
Principles of Molecular Vibrations
Principles of Molecular Vibrations in Infrared (IR) Spectroscopy Infrared spectroscopy is fundamentally
based on the interaction of infrared light with molecular vibrations. When a molecule absorbs IR ra-
diation, the energy of the light promotes specific vibrational transitions within the molecule. These
transitions occur at characteristic frequencies (or wavenumbers), which depend on the strengths of the
chemical bonds and the masses of the atoms involved. The resultant IR spectrum reflects which vi-
brational modes are active (i.e., capable of absorbing IR radiation) and provides a “fingerprint” of the
molecular structure.
3.1.2
Vibrational Modes
In general, a molecule with N atoms has 3N −6 fundamental vibrational modes (or 3N −5 if the molecule
is linear). These modes include:
• Stretching Vibrations: In a stretching vibration, the distance between two bonded atoms in-
creases or decreases rhythmically along the bond axis.
– Symmetric Stretch: All bonds elongate or compress in unison.
– Asymmetric Stretch: One bond stretches while another compresses.
• Bending Vibrations: In bending vibrations, the bond angles change between three or more
atoms. Bending can involve scissoring, rocking, wagging, or twisting motions.
Each mode corresponds to a specific energy (and thus frequency) related to the bond strength and the
masses of the atoms. Stronger bonds and lighter atoms typically vibrate at higher frequencies, while
weaker bonds and heavier atoms have lower vibrational frequencies.
3.1.3
Hooke’s Law and Anharmonicity in Infrared (IR) Spectroscopy
Infrared (IR) spectroscopy is fundamentally based on the vibrations of chemical bonds within molecules.
One of the simplest approximations to describe these vibrational motions is provided by Hooke’s Law.
Hooke’s Law assumes that molecular bonds behave like harmonic oscillators, where the vibrational
frequency (nu) is directly related to the force constant (k) of the bond and inversely related to the
reduced mass (mu) of the atoms involved. Mathematically, Hooke’s Law is expressed as:
ν =
1
2πc
s
k
µ
where c represents the speed of light. Under the harmonic oscillator approximation, bond vibrations
are symmetrical, and energy levels are evenly spaced. This simplified model effectively captures the
general behavior of molecular vibrations, making it a useful initial approach for interpreting IR spectra,
especially for diatomic molecules or simple stretching vibrations.
However, real molecular vibrations deviate from the harmonic oscillator model, exhibiting what is
known as anharmonicity. Anharmonicity arises because chemical bonds do not behave as ideal springs;
they have limitations in stretching and compressing due to the interactions between electrons and nuclei,
resulting in asymmetric potential energy curves. In reality, the vibrational energy levels are not equally
spaced; as the vibrational quantum number increases, energy differences between successive levels become
smaller.
This deviation can significantly affect spectral features, causing overtones and combination
bands to appear at frequencies different from simple multiples of the fundamental vibrational frequency.
Anharmonicity is quantitatively described using the Morse potential or other advanced potentials,
which better approximate the real potential energy surface of molecular vibrations. This phenomenon
explains why higher-energy vibrational transitions observed in IR spectra (overtones and combination
bands) often have lower intensities and shifted frequencies compared to the harmonic prediction. Ac-
counting for anharmonicity is crucial for precise spectroscopic analyses, particularly when quantitative
information on bond strengths, molecular interactions, and structural dynamics is required.

Understanding the balance between harmonic and anharmonic descriptions is essential in advanced
spectroscopic methods, such as quantitative IR spectroscopy and computational spectral modeling. Rec-
ognizing the limitations of Hooke’s Law and incorporating corrections for anharmonicity leads to more
accurate interpretations of molecular vibrations, providing deeper insights into molecular structures and
interactions.[6]
3.1.4
Selection Rules
Not every vibrational mode is IR-active because the absorption of infrared light fundamentally depends
on whether a vibration induces a change in the molecule’s dipole moment. Specifically, only vibrational
modes that generate an oscillating dipole moment, such as the asymmetric stretching modes of polar
bonds like C=O or O–H, are IR-active and thus appear in infrared spectra. Conversely, vibrations that
preserve molecular symmetry and do not produce a net change in dipole moment, such as the symmetric
stretch in linear molecules like CO, remain IR-inactive—though some of these may still be detectable
through Raman spectroscopy. Consequently, molecules containing strong polar bonds typically exhibit
intense IR absorption bands, whereas non-polar bonds or highly symmetrical vibrational modes often
yield weak or undetectable signals in IR spectra. This distinction underscores the complementary nature
of IR and Raman spectroscopy in molecular vibrational analysis.

3.1.5
Influence of Molecular Environment
While bond strength and mass primarily dictate the fundamental vibrational frequency, other
factors can shift vibrational peaks:
• Hydrogen Bonding: Strong hydrogen bonding often causes broadening and red shifts (lower
wavenumber) in the O–H or N–H stretching region.
• Intermolecular Interactions: Solute-solvent interactions can shift peak positions and alter
their intensities.
• Conjugation and Resonance: Conjugated systems can lower the stretching frequency of
double bonds (e.g., C=O in an α, β-unsaturated system).
• Isotope Substitution: Replacing an atom (e.g., H with D) changes the reduced mass and
shifts the vibration frequencies.[7]

3.1.7
Absorption of Infrared Light by Molecular Bonds
Infrared (IR) spectroscopy relies on the fundamental principle that when infrared radiation passes
through a sample, certain wavelengths are absorbed if they match the energy required to excite
specific vibrational motions of the molecule’s bonds. This absorption process transforms photon
energy into vibrational energy, leading to characteristic peaks in an IR spectrum.
a.. Matching IR Energy with Vibrational Frequencies
Each molecular bond can be approximated as a tiny oscillator, akin to a mass connected to
a spring (Hooke’s law analogy). The frequency of vibration depends on:
• The mass of the atoms connected by the bond.
• The strength of the bond (its force constant).
When infrared light of a specific wavelength (or wavenumber) matches the natural vibrational
frequency of a bond, energy is absorbed. This absorption manifests as a dip in the transmitted
light, creating a peak in the IR spectrum at that wavenumber.
b.. Role of Dipole Moments in IR Absorption
For a vibrational mode to be IR-active, it must involve a change in the dipole moment of the
molecule. As a bond stretches or bends, if there is a fluctuating electric dipole, the oscillating
electric field of the IR photon can interact with it and cause absorption.
• Polar Bonds (e.g., O–H, N–H, C=O) show stronger IR absorptions because stretching
or bending these bonds significantly changes their dipole moments.
• Nonpolar Bonds (e.g., symmetrical C–H stretches in nonpolar molecules) can be weak
or sometimes IR-inactive if the net dipole change is negligible.
c.. How Photons Are Absorbed
When IR photons encounter a bond vibrating at the same frequency, the bond can absorb
the photon’s energy. This absorption drives the bond from a lower vibrational energy level
(often the ground state) to a higher vibrational energy level (an excited state). Quantum
mechanically, this transition must obey selection rules, chiefly the dipole moment change
criterion.
d.. Influence of Molecular Environment
The precise wavenumber at which IR absorption occurs also depends on intermolecular in-
teractions, such as hydrogen bonding or solvent effects. For instance:
• Hydrogen Bonding tends to lower (red shift) the stretching frequency of O–H or
N–H groups and broaden the peak because the bond is partially “weakened” by the
interaction with another electronegative group or molecule.

• Solvent-Solute Interactions can alter the local environment of a bond, shifting its
absorption frequency.
Polar solvents, for example, may stabilize certain vibrational
states, causing observable shifts in the IR spectrum.
e.. Applications in Molecular Characterization
By examining which wavelengths are absorbed and how strongly, IR spectroscopy offers rich
information about:
• Functional Groups: Each functional group (e.g., hydroxyl, carbonyl, amine) has a
characteristic absorption pattern (or “fingerprint”).
• Bond Strength and Environment: Shifts in absorption peaks can reveal hydrogen
bonding, conjugation, or molecular strain.
• Quantitative Analysis: Using the Beer-Lambert law, absorption intensities can be
correlated with concentrations, enabling the determination of species in a mixture.

3.2
Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR)
Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy is a technique used to acquire a broad spectral
range of infrared (IR) absorbance or transmittance data quickly and with high sensitivity.
It
contrasts with older “dispersive” IR instruments that used prisms or gratings to separate individual
frequencies before detection. Below are the fundamental elements and steps that define the basic
working principles of FTIR spectroscopy.
3.2.1
IR Source and Optical Path
a.. Broadband IR Source: FTIR instruments typically use a broadband infrared light source
(e.g., a Globar—an electrically heated silicon carbide rod, or Nernst glower). This source
emits IR radiation over a wide range of frequencies (from near-IR to mid-IR or far-IR, de-
pending on the instrument design).
b.. Beam Splitter: The infrared beam from the source passes to a beam splitter, usually made
from a partially reflective material such as a thin film on a substrate that directs roughly half
the light toward a fixed mirror and half toward a moving mirror.
3.2.2
The Michelson Interferometer
At the core of every FTIR spectrometer is a Michelson interferometer. It comprises three key
components:
a.. Fixed Mirror: A stationary mirror that reflects its portion of the split beam back to the
beam splitter.
b.. Moving Mirror: A mirror on a motorized stage that moves back and forth, changing the
optical path length of its reflected beam.
c.. Beam Splitter: Combines the two beams after they reflect from the fixed and moving
mirrors, creating an interference pattern when they recombine. [8]
3.2.3
How Interference Is Generated
As the moving mirror travels, the path difference (also referred to as the optical retardation)
between the two split beams changes. If the path difference is such that the light waves recombine
in-phase, constructive interference enhances the detector signal. If the waves recombine out-of-
phase, destructive interference diminishes the signal. At intermediate path differences, various
degrees of constructive and destructive interference occur, producing a complex signal known as
an interferogram.
3.2.4
The Interferogram
Time-Domain Signal
The detector measures the total light intensity as a function of the moving
mirror’s position, often converted to time or mirror travel distance. This signal is the interfero-
gram, a plot of detector response versus path difference (or mirror position). In FTIR, all infrared
frequencies reach the detector simultaneously, rather than being filtered out one by one.
The
interferogram thus encodes the entire spectral information within a single measurement.[9]
3.2.5
Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)
The recorded interferogram must be converted from the time (or distance) domain into the fre-
quency domain (wavenumber cm−1). This is achieved using a mathematical process called the
Fourier Transform, specifically the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm in modern
instruments. After the Fourier Transform, the instrument outputs a conventional IR spectrum (in-
tensity vs. wavenumber). Each peak corresponds to a specific vibrational transition in the sample
that absorbed at that wavenumber.[10]
18
==PAGE:24==
3.2.6
Sample Handling and Measurement Modes
FTIR measurements can be made in a variety of sample forms—solids, liquids, gases, or thin
films—using different sampling accessories such as transmission cells, ATR (Attenuated Total Re-
flectance) crystals, or diffuse reflectance attachments. Often, multiple interferograms are averaged
to improve the signal-to-noise ratio. The multiplex advantage (Fellgett’s advantage) means FTIR
can accumulate scans rapidly, making real-time or high-throughput analysis feasible.
3.2.7
Advantages of FTIR Spectroscopy
a.. Fellgett’s (Multiplex) Advantage: Since all wavelengths are collected simultaneously, a
complete spectrum can be obtained in a short time. Averaging many scans improves the
signal-to-noise ratio without greatly extending total acquisition time.
b.. Connes’ Advantage (High Wavenumber Accuracy): The instrument uses an internal
laser (usually a He–Ne laser) as a reference for mirror position and interferogram sampling.
This ensures extremely accurate wavelength calibration, an important factor for quantitative
and comparative studies.
c.. Jacquinot’s Advantage (High Throughput): Fewer optical elements (no slits or grat-
ings) increase the light throughput, enhancing sensitivity and allowing for better detection
of weak signals.
d.. Versatility: FTIR can analyze a broad range of materials and is compatible with multiple
sampling techniques (ATR, transmission, reflection). It is widely used in chemistry, pharma-
ceuticals, environmental sciences, and many other fields. [11]
3.2.8
Typical Steps in an FTIR Measurement
A typical FTIR measurement involves the following steps:
a.. Record a Background Scan: First, a background scan is recorded with no sample in
the beam path, or with a reference blank. This is done to account for any instrumental and
environmental contributions to the spectrum, such as signals from atmospheric CO2 and H2O
vapors.
b.. Record the Sample Scan: The sample is introduced, and the instrument records its
interferogram.
c.. Generate and Analyze the Spectrum: The instrument’s software compares the sample
data to the background data. The resulting spectrum is typically the sample’s absorption
spectrum. From there, characteristic IR peaks can be assigned to specific functional groups
or molecular vibrations to identify the compound or assess its purity and concentration.

Interpretation of IR Spectra for Hydrogen-Bonded Systems
3.3.1
Peak Positions, Intensities, and Shapes
Infrared (IR) spectroscopy is a powerful tool for characterizing hydrogen-bonded systems—such as
water, alcohols, carboxylic acids, and other compounds with O–H, N–H, or F–H groups—because
hydrogen bonding significantly influences the vibrational features observed in IR spectra.
The
presence and strength of hydrogen bonds alter peak positions, intensities, and shapes, providing
valuable insights into molecular interactions and structural arrangements.
a. Peak Positions
Red Shift of Stretching Frequencies
• O–H Stretch: Free (non-hydrogen-bonded) hydroxyl groups typically absorb at higher
wavenumbers (around 3,600–3,650 cm−1). When hydrogen bonding is present, the O–
H stretch undergoes a pronounced red shift to lower wavenumbers (e.g., 3,000–3,400
cm−1), depending on the strength of the hydrogen bond.
• N–H and Other Bonds: Similar red shifts are observed in N–H stretching frequencies
when hydrogen bonding is involved, typically shifting from ∼3,500 cm−1 (for free N–H)
to lower values.[12]
Band Shifts with Temperature or Concentration
• Temperature Variations: As temperature increases, hydrogen bonds can weaken or
break, causing the stretching peak to partially shift back to higher wavenumbers and
sharpen due to an increased fraction of “free” or less-associated species.
• Concentration Effects: In dilute solutions (e.g., alcohols in nonpolar solvents), more
free hydroxyl groups exist, leading to a clear “free O–H” peak at higher wavenumbers.
In concentrated or pure states, strongly hydrogen-bonded species dominate, resulting in
broader peaks at lower wavenumbers.
3.3.2
Peak Intensities
Hydrogen bonds often increase the intensity of the associated vibrational modes. For example,
the O–H stretching band in hydrogen-bonded systems can become quite intense compared to free
O–H. This heightened intensity arises from the increased dipole moment change associated with
the more polar environment created by hydrogen bonding.
Because hydrogen-bonded stretches typically have strong absorption, quantitative analysis (e.g.,
using Lambert-Beer’s law) can be performed to estimate the concentration or fraction of hydrogen-
bonded vs. non-bonded species. However, overlapping peaks (e.g., monomer, dimer, or polymeric
bands) can complicate direct intensity-based quantitation, often necessitating deconvolution or
multivariate analysis.
3.3.3
Peak Shapes
a. Broad and Diffuse: Hydrogen bonding results in broadening of the stretching bands, par-
ticularly in O–H stretches.
This broadening occurs because hydrogen bonds can vary in
strength and geometry (e.g., different dimeric, chain-like, or ring-like associations), creating
a distribution of vibrational frequencies rather than a single sharp peak.
b. Band Overlaps: In strongly associating systems (like neat carboxylic acids or water clus-
ters), multiple species (monomers, dimers, oligomers) contribute to the stretching region,
further broadening and sometimes merging peaks into a single enveloping band.

c. Distinct Bands: In some cases, separate peaks can appear for free vs. hydrogen-bonded
stretches. For instance, in solutions where small amounts of monomeric species coexist with
strongly bonded dimers or oligomers, a sharp peak at higher wavenumber (free OH) may be
visible alongside a broad band at lower wavenumbers (H-bonded).
d. Temperature/Concentration Dependence: When temperature is lowered or concentra-
tion is increased, the broad hydrogen-bonded band typically grows more intense and shifts
further, while the free OH peak diminishes.
In some hydrogen-bonded systems (especially with more complex molecules), overtones or combi-

3.3.4
Effects of hydrogen bonding on IR absorption
Hydrogen bonding has a profound influence on the infrared (IR) spectra of molecules containing
O–H, N–H, or other hydrogen bond–forming groups. These effects are most readily observed in
the stretching vibration regions of the involved bonds and offer insights into the strength, extent,
and nature of the hydrogen-bonding interactions. Here are the key ways hydrogen bonding affects
IR absorption:
a. Red Shift of Stretching Frequencies: Hydrogen bonding partially weakens the covalent
bond (e.g., O–H, N–H), causing its stretching frequency to decrease. As a result, the IR ab-
sorption band moves to a lower wavenumber compared to the “free” (non-hydrogen-bonded)
bond. The extent of the red shift correlates with the strength of the hydrogen bond; stronger
hydrogen bonds induce larger shifts.
b. Peak Broadening: Hydrogen-bonded species (dimers, multimers) exhibit a range of bond
strengths.
This broad distribution of vibrational frequencies leads to wider, more diffuse
absorption bands. Different conformations and varying degrees of hydrogen bonding (e.g.,
dimeric rings, linear chains, oligomers) can overlap, contributing to broad shoulders or merged
peaks in the IR spectrum.
c. Increased Absorbance Intensity: The formation of a hydrogen bond often increases the
polar character of the bond, enhancing the overall dipole moment change during vibration.
This leads to stronger, more intense IR absorption peaks.
In concentrated or pure sys-
tems, where hydrogen bonding is extensive, the associated band can dominate the spectrum,
whereas in dilute systems, the “free” bond peak can be more pronounced.
d. Temperature Dependence: As temperature rises, some hydrogen bonds break, increas-
ing the fraction of “free” or weaker-bonded species. This shifts the balance toward higher
wavenumbers (less red-shifted peaks) and sharpens the absorption band. Monitoring the O–
H or N–H stretching region as a function of temperature reveals changes in hydrogen bond
populations and strengths.[13]
e. Solvent and Matrix Effects: In polar solvents, the solute may form solvent-solute hydrogen
bonds, influencing the spectral features of the solute’s own hydrogen-bonding sites. Water
or other hydrogen-bonding solvents can introduce their own broad absorption bands, which
may overlap with the solute’s IR peaks, necessitating careful experimental design and solvent
choice.
f. Quantitative and Qualitative Insights: Resolving free (monomeric) vs. hydrogen-bonded
peaks can help quantify the degree of association in solution.
The shape, position, and
intensity of hydrogen-bonded peaks give clues about molecular conformations, ring structures
(in carboxylic acids), and complex formation.[14]

• Non-ideal Behavior: Deviations can arise when interactions (e.g., hydrogen bonding, dimer
formation) significantly affect the molar absorptivity or lead to changes in the sample’s ef-

3.4.4
Application of Lambert-Beer’s Law to Monomer Fraction Estimation
In systems where hydrogen bonding can lead to the formation of different aggregates (e.g., monomers,
dimers, or higher oligomers), infrared (IR) spectroscopy provides a way to quantify the fraction
of each species. For instance, in an alcohol solution, monomeric O–H groups absorb at a distinct
wavenumber region compared to hydrogen-bonded (dimeric or polymeric) O–H groups. By isolat-
ing the monomer absorption band and applying Lambert-Beer’s law, it is possible to estimate the
monomer fraction relative to the total concentration of the compound.[15]

3.5
Overlapping Peaks and Spectral Deconvolution
In systems where molecules can form both monomeric and dimeric (or higher-order) aggregates,
their respective infrared (IR) absorption bands often partially or substantially overlap. This is
particularly common in hydrogen-bonded fluids (e.g., alcohols, carboxylic acids) because the O–H
(or N–H) stretching region can be broad and complex, with distinct peaks for monomeric and
associated species merging into one another. Below are the main challenges and approaches to
separating these overlapping peaks.
3.5.1
Sources of Overlap
• Broad Hydrogen-Bonded Bands: Dimeric or polymeric O–H stretches are typically red-
shifted and broad.
When the system is partially associated, the monomer peak can be
obscured by the tail of the broad, hydrogen-bonded envelope. The overlapping region often
prevents direct integration or peak height measurements specific to only the monomer or only
the dimer.
• Multiple Equilibria: In many associating fluids, several species (monomers, dimers, trimers,
etc.) coexist. Each species can contribute to the overall absorption band, making it difficult
to assign a single band exclusively to a dimer or monomer.
3.5.2
Spectral Deconvolution Methods
• Curve Fitting (Peak Fitting): Gaussian, Lorentzian, or Voigt profiles are mathematical
functions that can approximate the shape of individual peaks. By assigning starting parame-
ters—e.g., peak positions, widths, and intensities—one can iteratively optimize the fit to the
experimental spectrum.
• Constraints: Physical constraints, such as known frequency shifts for monomer vs. dimer,
or fixed band widths from prior data, can help stabilize the fitting process and yield more
reliable results.
• First or Second Derivative: Taking the derivative of the spectrum can sharpen hidden or
overlapping features, sometimes enabling more precise identification of distinct peaks.
• Reference Spectra: If one can isolate a pure monomeric sample (e.g., at very low con-
centration or in a non-H-bonding solvent) and a predominantly dimeric sample (e.g., neat
compound at low temperature), subtracting these references from the measured spectrum
can help highlight the overlapping bands.
3.5.3
Challenges and Considerations
Deconvolution requires good starting values for peak positions and shapes. Poor estimates can lead
to local minima in curve-fitting algorithms, yielding non-physical solutions. Adequate resolution
is needed to capture subtle differences in peak positions, especially if monomer-dimer shifts are
small. A high Signal-to-Noise Ratio is crucial; noise can obscure minor peaks or make curve fitting
less reliable.
Temperature and Concentration Effects
Equilibria between monomeric and dimeric species
can shift with temperature or concentration, altering the peak intensities and positions mid-
measurement.
Constant or well-controlled experimental conditions are essential for meaningful
deconvolution. Even if a mathematical fit appears good, one must verify that the derived peak
positions and widths match known physical or chemical properties (e.g., expected red shifts for
hydrogen-bonded species).[16]

3.6
Hydrogen Bonding in Infrared Spectroscopy
3.6.1
Influence of Hydrogen Bonding on Spectral Features
Red Shifts and Peak Broadening Due to Hydrogen Bonding
Hydrogen bonding dramat-
ically alters the IR absorption profiles of groups capable of forming such interactions (e.g., O–H,
N–H, and F–H). Two of the most notable spectral effects are red shifts (lowering of the vibrational
frequency) and peak broadening. These effects convey valuable information about the strength and
extent of hydrogen bonding in a sample.
• Red Shifts in Vibrational Frequencies: When a hydrogen bond forms, the donor bond
(e.g., O–H) experiences partial electron density redistribution. This effectively weakens the
O–H bond, causing a shift to lower wavenumbers (lower energy) compared to a free, non-
hydrogen-bonded O–H. For instance, a free O–H stretch might appear at ∼3,600–3,650 cm−1,
while hydrogen-bonded O–H stretches are commonly observed in the 3,200–3,400 cm−1 range
(or even lower, depending on bond strength). The magnitude of the red shift correlates with
hydrogen bond strength—stronger hydrogen bonds induce larger shifts.[17]
• Temperature and Concentration Effects: Increasing temperature can weaken hydrogen
bonds, shifting the peak back toward higher wavenumbers. Similarly, in dilute solutions, a
more prominent free O–H peak may be observed alongside the hydrogen-bonded peak.
• Peak Broadening: Hydrogen bonding is dynamic and can result in varying geometries and
bond strengths (e.g., monomers, dimers, chains, clusters). Each environment slightly alters
the O–H (or N–H) vibrational frequency, creating a continuum of absorption frequencies
rather than a single sharp peak. In systems that partially form hydrogen bonds, both free and
hydrogen-bonded species absorb in the same broad region. Their overlapping contributions
broaden and sometimes merge into a single, wide band. Even within the hydrogen-bonded
peak, stronger or weaker bonding can appear as shoulders or tails, making the overall band
appear diffuse.
3.6.2
Hydrogen Bonding Strength and Temperature Effects
Hydrogen bonding (H-bonding) is a key intermolecular interaction that significantly influences
molecular structure, dynamics, and properties. In infrared (IR) spectroscopy, hydrogen-bonded
species display characteristic red-shifted and broadened absorption bands, especially in the O–H,
N–H, or other hydrogen-bond-forming regions. Temperature variations play a crucial role in mod-
ulating these hydrogen bonds. As temperature changes, the balance between monomeric (free)
and associated (hydrogen-bonded) species shifts, altering both the position and shape of the cor-
responding IR peaks.
Increasing temperature typically imparts more thermal energy to the system. This added energy
can break or weaken hydrogen bonds, reducing the fraction of dimeric or higher-order complexes
in favor of free (monomeric) species. In IR spectra, this shift manifests as a partial move of the
O–H or N–H stretch from a broad, low-wavenumber region (strongly hydrogen-bonded) toward a
sharper, higher-wavenumber peak characteristic of free functional groups.
Conversely, lowering the temperature generally strengthens or stabilizes hydrogen bonds. This can
lead to the formation of larger aggregates or an increased proportion of dimers, trimers, and higher
oligomers. As a result, IR peaks associated with hydrogen-bonded species become more pronounced
and can shift further to lower wavenumbers due to stronger intermolecular interactions.
Broadening at Low Temperature
A larger diversity of hydrogen-bonded environments (e.g.,
varying bond angles and strengths) can lead to a broader absorption band.
Narrowing at High Temperature
As fewer intermolecular hydrogen bonds persist, the IR
band associated with free O–H or N–H becomes more distinct and often appears sharper.[18]

Infrared (IR) Spectroscopy Basics
4.1
Instrumentation Components
Infrared (IR) spectroscopy is a widely used analytical technique for identifying molecular structures
based on the absorption of infrared radiation by molecular vibrations. An IR spectrometer typi-
cally consists of several critical components that collectively facilitate the generation, modulation,
measurement, and interpretation of IR spectra.
a. IR Source: The IR source emits infrared radiation covering a broad spectral range, typically
from 400 to 4000 cm−1.
Common IR sources include silicon carbide (Globar) rods and
nichrome wires, heated electrically to temperatures high enough to produce a continuous
spectrum of IR radiation. The emitted radiation serves as the initial excitation signal for
molecular vibrations.
b. Monochromator or Interferometer: Two primary types of IR spectrometers exist: dis-
persive and Fourier-transform infrared (FTIR). Dispersive instruments utilize monochroma-
tors, typically diffraction gratings or prisms, to separate IR radiation into discrete wave-
lengths before detection. FTIR spectrometers employ interferometers, commonly Michelson
interferometers, which modulate radiation through constructive and destructive interference,
generating an interferogram that is mathematically converted to an absorption spectrum via
Fourier transformation. FTIR is more widely adopted due to its superior speed, resolution,
and sensitivity compared to dispersive systems.
c. Sample Holder and Sampling Accessories: IR spectrometers incorporate sample holders
designed to accommodate different sample states (solids, liquids, gases). Typical sampling
methods include transmission cells for gases and liquids, attenuated total reflectance (ATR)
cells for solids and viscous samples, and diffuse reflectance accessories for powders. These
accessories ensure optimal interaction between the IR beam and the sample, thus facilitating
accurate spectral measurements
d. Detector: The detector is critical in converting IR radiation into electrical signals for data
acquisition. Commonly used IR detectors include thermocouples, pyroelectric detectors, and
photoconductive or photovoltaic detectors, such as mercury cadmium telluride (MCT). Ther-
mocouples and pyroelectric detectors detect thermal changes induced by absorbed IR radia-
tion, whereas MCT detectors directly convert photon energy into an electrical signal, offering
higher sensitivity and faster response times.
e. Data Acquisition and Processing System: Modern IR spectrometers include sophis-
ticated computer systems capable of capturing, storing, processing, and analyzing spectral
data. Data processing involves background subtraction, baseline correction, normalization,
and spectral interpretation through dedicated software. These functionalities significantly en-
hance analytical capabilities and allow comprehensive qualitative and quantitative spectral
analyses.
Collectively, these instrumentation components ensure robust performance and accurate molecular
analysis, making IR spectroscopy an invaluable tool in chemical and material sciences research.[20]

4.2
Sample Preparation Techniques
Effective sample preparation is essential for obtaining accurate and reproducible infrared (IR)
spectra, greatly influencing the quality and reliability of spectral data. The choice of preparation
technique depends significantly on the physical state and chemical characteristics of the sample,
ensuring precise and interpretable results.
a. Solid Samples: For solid samples, the preparation typically involves converting the material
into a thin, uniform film or a finely dispersed powder. A widely used method is the potassium
bromide (KBr) pellet technique, where the finely powdered sample is uniformly mixed with
IR-transparent potassium bromide and pressed into a translucent pellet. KBr pellets offer
high-quality spectra due to minimal interference from the pellet material itself. Alternatively,
the mull technique involves suspending the powdered sample in a mulling agent, usually min-
eral oil (Nujol), then placing the resulting paste between IR-transparent windows. However,
care must be taken as mineral oil introduces characteristic absorption bands, potentially
complicating spectral interpretation.
b. Liquid Samples: Liquid samples require specialized cells constructed from IR-transparent
materials such as sodium chloride (NaCl), calcium fluoride (CaF2), or potassium bromide
(KBr). The liquid is placed between two parallel transparent plates, establishing an opti-
mal path length for accurate analysis. Attenuated Total Reflectance (ATR) spectroscopy
has become increasingly preferred due to its simplicity and minimal sample preparation re-
quirements. ATR involves placing a small amount of liquid directly onto an IR-transparent
crystal (commonly diamond or zinc selenide), enabling fast and straightforward acquisition
of spectra without intricate sample handling procedures.
c. Gaseous Samples: Analyzing gaseous samples typically involves specially designed gas cells
featuring extended optical path lengths to improve sensitivity. These cells frequently incorpo-
rate reflective mirrors to significantly lengthen the optical path, enhancing the detectability
of gases at low concentrations. Window materials such as sodium chloride (NaCl), calcium
fluoride (CaF2), or potassium bromide (KBr) are preferred due to their high IR transparency.
Selecting an appropriate sample preparation method is vital for accurate IR analysis. Each method
has specific advantages and limitations, demanding careful consideration based on sample charac-
teristics and analytical objectives. Proper sample preparation ensures the accuracy and reliability
of spectral data, crucial for both qualitative and quantitative analyses in diverse scientific fields.[21]

4.3
Advantages and Limitations
Infrared (IR) spectroscopy is a widely utilized analytical technique that measures the absorption of
infrared light by molecular vibrations, providing valuable information about molecular structures
and compositions. Its application spans various fields, including chemistry, biology, and materials
science. Understanding the advantages and limitations of IR spectroscopy is essential for its effective
application in scientific research and industrial processes.
a. Advantages of IR Spectroscopy:
• Non-Destructive Analysis: IR spectroscopy allows for the examination of samples with-
out altering or destroying them, making it suitable for analyzing valuable or sensitive
materials. This characteristic is particularly beneficial in fields like art conservation and
forensic science.
• Rapid and Efficient: The technique enables quick data acquisition, facilitating high-
throughput analysis. This efficiency is advantageous in quality control processes and
real-time monitoring of chemical reactions.
• Minimal Sample Preparation: IR spectroscopy often requires little to no sample prepa-
ration, especially when using techniques like Attenuated Total Reflectance (ATR). This
simplicity reduces the potential for sample contamination and loss.
• Versatility: IR spectroscopy is applicable to a wide range of sample types, including
solids, liquids, and gases. This versatility makes it a valuable tool across diverse scientific
disciplines.
• Molecular Fingerprinting: The technique provides unique spectral patterns that serve
as molecular fingerprints, aiding in the identification and characterization of substances.
This feature is crucial in applications like pharmaceutical analysis and environmental
monitoring.
b. Limitations of IR Spectroscopy:
• Limited Sensitivity to Low Concentrations:
IR spectroscopy may struggle to detect
analytes present in very low concentrations, limiting its effectiveness in trace analysis
without appropriate enhancements or sample preparation techniques.
• Water Interference: Water exhibits strong absorption in the IR region, which can inter-
fere with the analysis of aqueous samples. Specialized techniques or sample preparation
methods are often required to mitigate this issue.
• Overlapping Absorption Bands: Complex mixtures can present overlapping absorption
bands, complicating the interpretation of spectra. Advanced data analysis methods,
such as chemometrics, are often necessary to resolve these overlaps.
• Surface Sensitivity: In techniques like ATR-IR spectroscopy, the analysis is confined to
the sample’s surface or a limited penetration depth, which may not represent the bulk
properties of heterogeneous samples.
• Quantitative Limitations: While IR spectroscopy is excellent for qualitative analysis,
quantitative analysis can be challenging due to factors like baseline shifts, overlapping
bands, and matrix effects. Careful calibration and method development are required to
obtain accurate quantitative results.

Thermodynamic Modeling Using SAFT
5.1
Introduction to Statistical Associating Fluid Theory (SAFT)
Statistical Associating Fluid Theory (SAFT) is an advanced thermodynamic framework
developed to model the behavior of complex fluids, particularly those exhibiting association phe-
nomena such as hydrogen bonding. Introduced in the late 1980s and early 1990s by Walter G.
Chapman, Keith E. Gubbins, George Jackson, and Maciej Radosz, SAFT has significantly en-
hanced the predictive accuracy of equations of state for associating fluids.[22][23]
SAFT is grounded in statistical mechanics and employs perturbation theory to account for molec-
ular interactions within fluids; at its core, it represents complex molecules as chains of spherical
segments, explicitly incorporating dispersion, repulsion, and association forces.
This segment-
based, modular approach enables a detailed description of how molecular size, shape, and specific
interactions combine to determine macroscopic thermodynamic properties.
Since its inception, SAFT has undergone numerous refinements to address an ever-broader range
of substances and conditions. Perturbed-Chain SAFT (PC-SAFT), developed by Gross and
Sadowski in 2001, applies a more rigorous perturbation treatment to chain molecules, markedly
improving predictions for polymers and long-chain hydrocarbons. SAFT-VR (Variable Range)
extends the theory by considering intermolecular potentials of variable range, thereby enhancing
accuracy for substances with complex, long-ranged force fields.
Soft-SAFT incorporates soft
repulsive interactions into the formalism, making it particularly well-suited to fluids with softer
intermolecular potentials, such as certain refrigerants and specialty polymers.[24]
These SAFT variants have found broad application across science and industry: in the petrochemi-
cal sector, SAFT models hydrocarbon phase behavior and predicts asphaltene precipitation; in poly-
mer science, it forecasts the thermodynamic properties of polymer solutions and polymer–polymer
blends; and in the pharmaceutical field, it elucidates the solubility and stability of active pharma-
ceutical ingredients in diverse solvents.
SAFT’s principal advantages stem from its molecularly detailed, flexible, and predictive nature.
By explicitly accounting for association and chain formation, it delivers deep insight into fluid
behavior at the segment level; its modular design accommodates substances ranging from simple
gases to complex macromolecules; and its predictive capability often outperforms empirical models,
providing reliable phase equilibrium and property predictions even in systems lacking extensive
experimental data.
Nonetheless, SAFT presents practical challenges: the detailed segment-based modeling demands
substantial computational resources, especially for large or highly heterogeneous systems; its accu-
racy hinges on the availability and quality of molecular parameters, which may not be accessible
for all compounds; and the underlying statistical mechanical derivations and perturbation expan-
sions are mathematically intricate, requiring specialized expertise for correct implementation and
interpretation.[25]

5.2
PC-SAFT Framework and Improvements
The Perturbed-Chain Statistical Associating Fluid Theory (PC-SAFT) is an equation of
state (EoS) developed to model the thermodynamic behavior of complex fluids, particularly those
involving chain-like molecules and associating interactions. Introduced by Gross and Sadowski in
2001, PC-SAFT enhances the original SAFT framework by incorporating perturbation theory to
account for chain-length dependence of segment interactions, thereby improving the accuracy of
thermodynamic property predictions for polymers, hydrocarbons, and associating fluids.
Framework of PC-SAFT
PC-SAFT models molecules as chains of spherical segments, consid-
ering various intermolecular interactions such as dispersion, repulsion, and association. The key
parameters in this model include the segment number (m), segment diameter (σ), and segment
interaction energy (ε). By explicitly accounting for chain formation and specific interactions, PC-
SAFT provides a more detailed and accurate representation of fluid behavior compared to earlier
models.
Limitations of PC-SAFT
a). Computational Intensity: The detailed molecular modeling requires significant computa-
tional resources, especially for large or highly complex systems.
b). Parameterization: Accurate predictions depend on the availability and quality of molecular
parameters, which may not be readily available for all substances.
c). Complexity: The theoretical framework and mathematical formulations can be intricate,
posing challenges for implementation and interpretation without specialized expertise.[23]
In summary, PC-SAFT has significantly advanced the modeling of complex fluids by providing a
robust framework that accounts for molecular interactions and associations. Ongoing improvements
and integrations with modern computational techniques continue to expand its applicability and
accuracy in various scientific and industrial fields.

5.3
Parameter Estimation Using Monomer Fraction Data
In thermodynamic modeling, particularly within association theories like the Statistical Associat-
ing Fluid Theory (SAFT) and its variants, accurate parameter estimation is crucial for reliable
predictions of fluid behavior. Monomer fraction data, representing the proportion of molecules not
engaged in associative interactions such as hydrogen bonding, serve as vital experimental inputs in
this context. Integrating monomer fraction data into parameter estimation enhances the physical
realism of models and improves their predictive accuracy for both pure substances and mixtures.
Significance of Monomer Fraction Data
Monomer fraction data provide direct insights into
the extent of molecular association within a fluid. By incorporating these data into parameter
estimation, models can more accurately reflect the balance between monomeric and associated
species, leading to improved predictions of thermodynamic properties. For instance, studies have
demonstrated that including monomer fraction data in the parameterization of association models
like Cubic-Plus-Association (CPA) and SAFT results in parameters that better represent both pure
compound properties and phase behavior in mixtures.
Methodologies for Parameter Estimation
The process of parameter estimation using monomer
fraction data typically involves the following steps:
a). Data Collection: Experimental determination of monomer fractions using spectroscopic
techniques provides the necessary data for model calibration.
b). Model Calibration: Theoretical models are adjusted to align with experimental monomer
fraction data, ensuring that the model accurately represents the degree of association in the
fluid.
c). Validation: The calibrated model is validated against additional experimental data, such
as phase equilibrium measurements, to confirm its predictive accuracy.
This approach ensures that the estimated parameters are not only mathematically optimized but
also physically meaningful, leading to more reliable predictions across various thermodynamic sce-

c). Parameter Interdependence: Parameters in association models are often interdependent.

Addressing these challenges requires careful experimental design, rigorous data analysis, and the
use of advanced computational methods to ensure that the benefits of incorporating monomer
fraction data are fully realized.

capabilities essential for various industrial and research applications.

Association theories serve as fundamental frameworks for accurately modeling and interpreting the
behavior of fluids that exhibit strong specific interactions, notably hydrogen bonding. Such inter-

macroscopic thermodynamic properties and phase equilibria. To describe these interactions accu-

The CPA equation of state uniquely combines classical cubic equations (e.g., Soave-Redlich-Kwong
or Peng-Robinson) with an explicit association term derived from Wertheim’s statistical mechanical
perturbation theory. This combination allows the CPA EoS to effectively capture both non-specific
intermolecular interactions and site-specific hydrogen-bonding associations. Von Solms et al.[27]
clearly emphasized the critical role that experimental monomer fraction data play in accurately
parameterizing association terms within CPA. Their study illustrated how spectroscopic data sig-
nificantly improve the predictions of phase equilibria, highlighting optimal association schemes for
different fluids. Specifically, they demonstrated the accuracy of the 4-site (4C) scheme for water
and the 2-site (2B) scheme for heavier alcohols.
Further reinforcing these findings, Tsochantaris et al. (2024) [28] provided updated experimental
spectroscopic monomer fraction data, demonstrating the robustness of CPA and SAFT-based mod-
els when parameterized with precise monomer fractions. They confirmed earlier results regarding
the appropriate association schemes and reinforced the necessity of spectroscopic data in parameter
optimization to predict hydrogen bonding structures and phase equilibria more accurately.
In their 2003 study, Von Solms et al [26] introduced significant simplifications to the original
PC-SAFT formulation, reducing computational complexity without sacrificing accuracy.
These
modifications made PC-SAFT more efficient, particularly in modeling asymmetric mixtures and

of cooperative hydrogen bonding effects.

models, particularly in systems exhibiting extensive hydrogen bonding.

the theory’s robustness in capturing complex hydrogen-bonding networks and predicting phase
behavior across diverse thermodynamic conditions.

broader multi-component industrial systems.

simulations highlighted the necessity of explicitly accounting for hydrogen bonding in supercritical
fluids.

alcohols, water, and hydrocarbons, employing CPA and SAFT equations. They confirmed these
equations’ versatility and accuracy in predicting intricate phase equilibria, such as vapor-liquid-

External factors, particularly strong electric fields, have been shown to significantly influence

The sensitivity of

structural characteristic that holds significant implications for accurate interpretation of IR spectra,
particularly when determining monomer fractions and selecting appropriate association schemes.

molecule-specific association models. For example, the disruption of methanol’s hydrogen-bonding
network supports the use of 2-site or 3-site models, while the stability of water’s network justifies
4-site schemes. Additionally, the lack of stable hydrogen-bonded structures oriented perpendicular
to the electric field in methanol—unlike in water—further highlights critical structural differences
that must be considered during spectroscopic analysis.

6.4
Computational Approaches: Molecular Simulations and Continuum
Models
Understanding how an O–H bond vibrates—and how this vibration changes depending on molec-
ular interactions—is critical for interpreting spectroscopic data and modeling fluid behavior. In
hydrogen-bonded systems, hydrogen bonding significantly shifts the vibrational frequency of the
O–H bond, a change clearly observable in infrared (IR) spectra.
These vibrational shifts pro-
vide valuable insight into the strength and nature of intermolecular interactions, thereby playing
a central role in refining models of liquid behavior in both chemistry and chemical engineering.
To simulate these effects computationally, researchers have employed continuum solvation models,
particularly the Polarizable Continuum Model (PCM). Two main variants were explored:
a). Pure Continuum Model:This treats the surrounding liquid as a continuous polarizable
medium, effectively capturing long-range electrostatic effects but ignoring specific local inter-
actions between individual molecules.
b). Semicontinuum Model: This refined approach combines a small explicit cluster of molecules—modeled
using quantum mechanical (QM) methods—with a continuum description of the bulk solvent.
This hybrid captures both local, specific interactions such as hydrogen bonding and broader
solvent effects, offering a more realistic depiction of the liquid environment.
These models were tested against experimental IR observations to evaluate their ability to repro-
duce vibrational shifts accurately.[14] The findings have showing:
a). Local Hydrogen Bonding Effects: Explicitly modeling small hydrogen-bonded clusters
significantly improved agreement with experimental data, accurately reflecting the red-shift
in O–H stretch frequencies.
b). Long-Range Solvent Effects: The continuum treatment remained essential for capturing
the dielectric environment of the solvent.
c). Vibrational Corrections (Anharmonicity): The models incorporated anharmonic cor-
rections, accounting for deviations from the ideal harmonic oscillator approximation, which
led to improved accuracy.
d). Model Parameters (Molecular Cavities): The results were highly sensitive to assump-
tions about the size and shape of the molecular cavity used in continuum models.
In summary, accurate modeling of vibrational spectra in associating fluids requires: a) Inclusion of
explicit hydrogen-bonding interactions, b) A hybrid semicontinuum approach to balance local and
long-range effects, c) Anharmonic corrections to capture real vibrational behavior, and d) Careful
parameterization of molecular cavities. These methods offer a robust foundation for integrating
spectroscopic data with molecular simulation, enhancing the predictive power of thermodynamic
models.
In a complementary study, researchers examined the O–H stretch spectra and vibrational relax-
ation of methanol monomers and dimers dissolved in carbon tetrachloride (CCl4) using adiabatic
quantum molecular dynamics (MD) simulations.[36] This work explored not only the vibrational
characteristics but also how quickly energy dissipates from vibrationally excited states. Key find-
ings included:
a). Open Dimer Configurations: Methanol dimers predominantly form non-cyclic (open)
configurations, exhibiting distinct donor and acceptor O–H vibrations.
b). Rapid Donor–Acceptor Exchange: The roles of donor and acceptor switch rapidly (on
the order of picoseconds), reflecting the dynamic nature of hydrogen bonding.
c). Vibrational Energy Transfer: This fast hydrogen-bond switching facilitates efficient en-
ergy transfer between molecules.
d). Hydrogen Bonding Accelerates Relaxation: The presence of hydrogen bonds enhances
nonadiabatic coupling between vibrational states, accelerating relaxation from excited vibra-
tional modes.

7.1.3
Electron Correlation and the Many-Electron Problem
For systems containing more than one electron (Ne > 1), an additional complication arises: elec-
trons interact with each other through the Coulomb repulsion term 1/|ri −rj|. This term couples
the motion of every electron to all the others, making the Schr¨odinger equation non-separable
into independent one-electron equations. The exact N-electron wavefunction, therefore, describes
correlated motion.
The inability to solve the many-electron problem exactly is directly tied to the concept of electron
correlation. Correlation refers to the effects of electron–electron repulsion on electronic motion
beyond what is captured by an independent-particle model. In the Hartree–Fock (HF) approx-
imation, each electron is treated as moving in the averaged electrostatic field of all the others.[41]
While this mean-field approach simplifies the problem, it neglects the instantaneous adjustment of
electrons to one another’s positions.
The lowering of the energy due to this coordinated motion is the correlation energy, defined as
the difference between the exact energy and the Hartree–Fock energy. Thus, electron correlation
encompasses all many-body effects that cannot be represented by a single Slater determinant.
It is important to distinguish between two types of correlation. Hartree–Fock already includes
Fermi correlation (or exchange correlation), which arises from the antisymmetry of the wave-
function: same-spin electrons avoid each other. However, dynamic correlation, associated with
Coulomb repulsion, remains unaccounted. This occurs even between opposite-spin electrons, as
they also avoid each other more than the averaged HF picture predicts. In summary, a single-
determinant Hartree–Fock wavefunction treats electron–electron repulsion only in an average sense.
This is why the Hartree–Fock energy is always an upper bound to the exact ground-state energy.[42]
Because the exact treatment of correlation scales exponentially with Ne, practical methods rely on
approximations. Typically, one starts from the Hartree–Fock solution and applies post-Hartree–Fock
methods to recover the missing correlation energy. These include Møller–Plesset perturbation the-
ory (MPn), Configuration Interaction (CI), and Coupled Cluster (CC). Density Functional Theory
(DFT), although conceptually different, also accounts for correlation through approximate ex-
change–correlation functionals. The diversity of methods reflects the central challenge of quantum
chemistry: the many-electron problem.[32]

7.2
Born–Oppenheimer Approximation
7.2.1
Validity and Limitations
The Born–Oppenheimer (BO) approximation, introduced by Born and Oppenheimer in 1927,
addresses the complexity of the molecular Schr¨odinger equation by exploiting the large mass dif-
ference between nuclei and electrons.[38] Nuclei are thousands of times heavier than electrons (a
proton is ≈1836 times heavier than an electron), and therefore move much more slowly. On the
timescale of fast electronic motion, the nuclei appear nearly stationary. The physical idea is that
one can first treat the nuclei as fixed while solving the electronic problem, and then account for
the slower nuclear motion afterwards.
Mathematically, the BO approximation assumes a factorized form of the molecular wavefunction:
Ψ(r, R) ≈Φe(r; R)χn(R)
Where:
• Φe(r; R): is the electronic wavefunction (for electrons at positions r, parametrically dependent
on the nuclear coordinates R).
• χn(R): is the nuclear wavefunction.
Inserting this ansatz into the full Schr¨odinger equation ˆHΨ = EΨ and neglecting terms where
nuclear kinetic operators act on Φe, one obtains two separate equations. The BO approximation
relies on two key assumptions:
a). Negligible non-adiabatic coupling. The neglected terms, where the nuclear kinetic oper-
ator acts on Φe, are called non-adiabatic couplings. Their magnitude scales as (me/MA)Ee,
which is typically in the range of 10−3 to 10−5 for most nuclei. For ground electronic states,
these corrections are therefore small.
b). Smooth dependence of electronic states on R. The approximation assumes that elec-
tronic eigenstates vary smoothly and are non-degenerate with respect to nuclear coordinates.
This breaks down near avoided crossings or conical intersections, where different potential
energy surfaces (PESs) come close in energy and non-adiabatic effects become significant.[43]
Under these conditions, the BO approximation is highly accurate.
It simplifies the molecular
Schr¨odinger equation enormously and underpins essentially all modern quantum chemistry.
7.2.2
Chemical Implications of the Born–Oppenheimer Approximation
The Born–Oppenheimer (BO) approximation underpins virtually all of theoretical and computa-
tional chemistry. By separating fast electronic motion from slower nuclear motion, it provides the
foundation for the distinct treatment of electronic structure, vibrational spectra, and rotational
dynamics. Its main implications can be summarized as follows:
• Molecular Geometry and Potential Energy Surfaces (PES). The BO approximation
gives precise meaning to the concept of a molecular geometry.[44] An equilibrium geometry
corresponds to the nuclear configuration R0 that minimizes the electronic energy Ee(R), i.e.,
the minimum of the PES. Molecular conformations, transition states, and reaction pathways
can all be described as features on this surface. For instance, in H2, one first calculates Ee(R)
as a function of the internuclear distance R, obtaining the familiar bonding potential curve,
and then solves for the vibrational levels of the nuclei within that well.
• Electronic Structure Methods.
Nearly all modern ab initio electronic structure ap-
proaches assume the clamped-nuclei (BO) approximation as their starting point. Hartree–Fock,
post-HF correlation methods, and density functional theory (DFT) are all formulated for fixed
nuclear geometries. In practice, this leads to geometry optimizations, where the electronic
Schr¨odinger equation is solved iteratively for different nuclear arrangements until an energy
minimum is found. As emphasized by Levine, the BO approximation is an excellent one for
ground electronic states and is “fundamental and essential” to quantum chemistry.[45]
41
==PAGE:47==
• Vibrational and Rotational Structure. Once the PES of an electronic state is available,
the BO approximation allows nuclear motion to be treated separately, giving rise to the
familiar hierarchy of molecular spectra. Electronic, vibrational, and rotational motions occur
on well-separated energy scales, such that the total energy can be written approximately
as:[46]
Etotal ≈Eelectronic + Evibrational + Erotational
with Eelectronic ≫Evibrational ≫Erotational.
This separation explains the factorization of
spectroscopic term values into Te + G(v) + F(J), with Te the electronic term, G(v) the
vibrational contribution, and F(J) the rotational part. The BO framework also underlies
the Franck–Condon principle: electronic transitions occur so quickly that nuclear positions
remain effectively unchanged, producing vertical transitions on a PES. Concepts such as
normal modes, rotational constants, and vibrational band structures all rely on this layered
separation of motion.[47]

7.3.6
Limitations of Hartree–Fock Theory
Neglect of Electron Correlation
HF includes electron–electron interactions only at the mean-
field level. The instantaneous correlated motion of electrons is absent. The missing contribution
is the correlation energy, defined as Ecorr = E0 −EHF, where E0 is the exact ground-state energy.
This can significantly affect predictions of reaction energies, intermolecular interactions, and bond
dissociation energies.[51]
Single-Determinant Limitation
HF represents the wavefunction as a single Slater determi-
nant, which fails in systems where several electronic configurations are nearly degenerate, such as
in stretched bonds, biradicals, and transition states. In such multi-reference cases, the HF solution
may be qualitatively incorrect.[52]

8.1
Fundamentals of DFT and Kohn-Sham Equations
8.1.1
Motivation: From Wavefunctions to Densities
In quantum chemistry, solving the electronic Schr¨odinger equation exactly is infeasible for systems
with more than a few electrons due to the exponential growth of the many-electron wavefunction’s
complexity. A wavefunction for N electrons depends on 3N spatial coordinates (plus spin), which
makes wavefunction-based methods computationally expensive.
Density Functional Theory
(DFT) offers an alternative approach by using the electron density ρ(r) – a function of only three
spatial variables – as the fundamental quantity. This drastic reduction in dimensionality (from 3N
to 3 variables) leads to a much lower computational cost.
The approach was put on firm theoretical footing by the Hohenberg–Kohn theorems (1964) and
the Kohn–Sham formulation (1965). These laid the foundation for modern DFT, and Walter
Kohn was later awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry (1998) for its development.[55]
8.1.2
Hohenberg–Kohn Theorems: Foundations of DFT
The Hohenberg–Kohn (HK) theorems provide the theoretical foundation for DFT.
a). First Hohenberg–Kohn Theorem (Existence and Uniqueness): The ground-state
electron density ρ0(r) uniquely determines the external potential vext of a system (up to an
additive constant). Because the Hamiltonian ˆH is fixed by vext, it follows that all ground-
state properties of the system are determined by ρ0(r). This establishes the electron density
as an appropriate fundamental variable.
b). Second Hohenberg–Kohn Theorem (Variational Principle): There exists a universal
energy functional E[ρ] such that for a given external potential vext, the value of this functional
is minimized by the true ground-state density ρ0.[56]
Formally, if the energy functional is written as
E[v, ρ] = T[ρ] +

then the variational principle states that

where E0 is the true ground-state energy. The HK theorems guarantee that an exact functional
E[ρ] exists, but they do not provide its explicit form. In particular, the exact forms of the kinetic
(T[ρ]) and electron-electron interaction (Eee[ρ]) functionals in terms of density alone are unknown.
This is where the Kohn–Sham approach is essential.[57]

The KS method, introduced by Walter Kohn and Lu Sham in 1965, provided a practical scheme

kinetic energy functional T[ρ] by introducing an auxiliary system of non-interacting electrons whose
wavefunction can be written as a single Slater determinant of orbitals. This approach ”reduces the
intractable many-body problem... to a tractable problem of noninteracting electrons moving in an
effective potential.”[59]

8.2

Density Functional Theory (DFT) relies on approximations for the exchange–correlation energy
functional. Hybrid functionals improve upon pure DFT by incorporating a fraction of exact

8.2.4

computationally demanding. The predicted bond lengths and angles from B3LYP-D3 are typically

8.3
Gaussian Basis Sets: aug-cc-pVTZ and Beyond
Electronic wavefunctions are represented as linear combinations of atom-centered basis functions.
In the Gaussian approach, each basis function is a Gaussian-type orbital (GTO), for example:
χ(r) ∝xlymzne−αr2
Gaussians are preferred because products of Gaussians are themselves Gaussians, so multi-center
integrals (overlap, kinetic, nuclear attraction, and two-electron Coulomb) have closed-form expres-
sions.
This makes the construction of the Fock and overlap matrices efficient and enables the
solution of the Roothaan–Hall generalized eigenvalue problem, FC = SCε, at a tractable cost.
Enlarging and enriching the basis systematically improves the description of the wavefunction
Ψ.[67]
8.3.1
Dunning’s correlation-consistent sets and augmentation
For correlated calculations and reliable potential energy surfaces, the correlation-consistent polar-
ized valence family, cc-pVXZ (with X=D, T, Q, 5, . . . ), is standard. The hierarchy controls radial
flexibility by providing X independent functions per valence orbital (double-ζ, triple-ζ, etc.) and
adds higher angular momentum ”polarization” functions to capture anisotropy and angular corre-
lation. The sets are constructed to recover correlation energy in a smooth, monotonic fashion as
X increases, giving a well-behaved approach to the complete basis set limit. In practice, triple-ζ
quality (cc-pVTZ) is a common threshold for quantitative results.
Standard cc-pVXZ bases may be insufficient when electron density is diffuse—in anions, highly
polar species, and noncovalent complexes such as hydrogen-bonded dimers. In such cases, adding
low-exponent (radially extended) functions is essential. The augmented sets, aug-cc-pVXZ, add
one diffuse function of each angular momentum already present, providing flexibility in the tail
region of the density. Augmentation improves electron affinities, reduces basis set superposition
error in weak complexes, and yields more physical dissociation curves.
8.3.2
Rationale for aug-cc-pVTZ in this work
This study employs the aug-cc-pVTZ basis set for all potential energy surface scans and vibra-
tional frequency calculations. The choice reflects the physics of the systems considered—oxygen-
containing molecules with O–H groups and hydrogen bonds—where an accurate treatment of both
bonding regions and long-range density is required. The triple-ζ valence space supplies the radial
and angular flexibility needed for reliable geometries and harmonic force constants, while the dif-
fuse augmentation allows electron density to extend into intermolecular regions, which is critical
for hydrogen bonding. As a result, interaction energies, equilibrium separations, and low-frequency
intermolecular modes are described more faithfully.
For vibrational analysis, harmonic frequencies depend on the local curvature of the potential en-
ergy surface. An inadequate basis tends to exaggerate stiffness and shift band positions. A triple-ζ
augmented basis typically yields frequencies within a few percent of experiment for stable molecules
(when paired with a suitable electronic structure method), whereas double-ζ sets often produce
larger systematic deviations. For potential energy scans—torsions, association coordinates, and
especially O–H stretching—aug-cc-pVTZ avoids artificial destabilization and spurious barriers as-
sociated with insufficient radial reach, giving smoother, more physical energy profiles.
In short, aug-cc-pVTZ combines correlation-consistent valence flexibility, polarization, and diffuse
functions, giving reliable potential energy surfaces and vibrational results without the cost of larger
augmented sets.[68]

In the harmonic oscillator approximation, the molecular vibrational potential is modeled as a

bond breaking and the decrease in level spacing as v increases, and it allows transitions to overtone

captures the key anharmonic features of real vibrational spectra – the non-constant level spacings
and the existence of a dissociation limit – while still yielding analytically solvable wavefunctions
and energies.

contrasts with the harmonic oscillator wavefunctions, which extend indefinitely. The Morse wave-
functions, being exact solutions, also adhere to the expected nodal patterns (the v-th state has v
nodes). The associated Laguerre polynomial provides the nodal structure, while the other terms
ensure proper asymptotic behavior. Because of these properties, Morse wavefunctions are often
used as a basis for vibrational calculations.

10.1.4
Utility for OH Vibrations and Hydrogen-Bonded Systems
The Morse potential’s inclusion of anharmonicity and bond dissociation makes it especially useful
for modeling X–H stretching vibrations, such as O–H stretches, which are highly anharmonic –
particularly when hydrogen bonding is involved. In a hydrogen-bonded O–H group, the bond is
weakened and elongated by the interaction, leading to a substantially lower fundamental frequency
and a large anharmonic redshift in the vibrational spectrum. The Morse potential can approximate
this weakened bond by adjusting its parameters, De (often lower) and a (which may be smaller,
giving a broader potential well).
Studies have shown that treating a hydrogen-bonded O–H oscillator with a Morse potential is quite
successful. It has been noted that, “The Morse potential has been widely deployed as a model for the
vibrations of weak or intermediate-strength hydrogen-bonded O–H units,” and Morse-based local
mode models yield accurate vibrational frequencies across various hydrogen-bonding environments.
Essentially, the Morse function captures the anharmonic softening of the O–H bond as the hydrogen
bond strengthens.
For instance, in the water dimer, the H-bonded O–H stretch fundamental is about 1000 cm−1 lower
than a free O–H stretch, and its first overtone intensity is strongly enhanced; Morse oscillator
models with appropriate parameters can mimic these observations. In fact, Morse potentials are
often used in local mode descriptions of polyatomic vibrational spectroscopy: one treats each O–H
bond as an independent Morse oscillator and then introduces coupling between them separately.
This approach has been applied to alcohols and carboxylic acids, where the observed overtone
spectra could be explained by Morse oscillator models. The transferability of Morse parameters is
also noteworthy, as they reflect genuine physical trends (e.g., a stronger H-bond →lower De and
ωe).

10.1.5
Hamiltonian Matrix and Oscillator Strengths Using Morse Functions
Because the Morse model yields a complete set of orthonormal eigenfunctions, Ψv, with known
analytic forms, it is extremely useful for building vibrational Hamiltonians and calculating transi-
tion properties. Morse oscillator wavefunctions serve as a tailored basis for anharmonic vibrations
because they already incorporate the correct anharmonic shape of the potential. Using Morse eigen-
functions as a basis for the O–H stretch, the Hamiltonian matrix elements ⟨Ψv′| ˆH|Ψv⟩can be eval-
uated with high accuracy. The diagonal terms are simply the Morse energies, since ˆHΨv = EvΨv,
and any perturbation (like coupling to another mode) can be expanded in this basis. This is far
more efficient than using harmonic oscillator functions for a highly anharmonic bond, as the Morse
basis already captures much of the essential physics.
Another important application is in computing oscillator strengths and transition intensities. The
intensity of a vibrational transition (v →v′) in infrared or Raman spectroscopy is proportional to
the square of the transition dipole moment integral:
Iv→v′ ∝|⟨Ψv′|µ(r)|Ψv⟩|2
where µ(r) is the dipole moment function.
With Morse wavefunctions, these integrals can be
evaluated with high accuracy. For a harmonic oscillator with a linear dipole moment function,
transitions with |∆v| > 1 are forbidden (the selection rule is ∆v = ±1). However, the asymmetry
of the Morse wavefunctions yields non-zero overlap for ∆v = 2, 3, . . . transitions. This means the
Morse model inherently predicts finite intensities for overtone and combination bands, as observed
experimentally.
By using Morse wavefunctions and an ab initio dipole moment function µ(r),
one can accurately calculate oscillator strengths for both fundamental and overtone transitions,
reproducing experimentally observed trends.[32]
In summary, the Morse potential and its analytical eigenfunctions provide a powerful framework
for theoretical vibrational spectroscopy. They supply realistic zero-order wavefunctions and en-
ergies for an anharmonic bond, serving as a starting point for including additional effects (like
mode–mode coupling or perturbations) via matrix methods. They also allow direct estimation of
transition probabilities (oscillator strengths) for vibrational transitions, which is crucial for com-
paring with infrared absorption intensities. Many quantum chemistry and spectroscopy textbooks
(e.g. Levine 1975, McQuarrie, etc.) and research articles (e.g. Dahl Springborg 1988, and numer-
ous J. Chem. Phys. studies) highlight the Morse oscillator as an archetypal model: it illustrates
how introducing anharmonicity (through the Morse potential) leads to finite overtone intensities
and level convergence at the dissociation limit, aligning theory with the observed spectral features
of real molecules. In the following sections of this thesis, we will leverage the Morse model in con-
structing the vibrational Hamiltonian for the OH stretch, and use its wavefunctions to calculate
matrix elements and predict oscillator strengths for transitions. This will enable a more accu-
rate theoretical simulation of vibrational spectra (including anharmonic shifts and overtone band
intensities) in hydrogen-bonded systems than a harmonic approximation could achieve. The use
of Morse potential thus forms a key part of our theoretical background and toolkit for analyzing
vibrational spectroscopy of O–H bonds in complex environments.

10.2.3
Approximations for Gij in Reduced-Dimensionality Models
In full-dimensional vibrational calculations, the G-matrix can be computed at each point in coor-
dinate space. However, many practical vibrational models use a reduced-dimensionality approach,
focusing on a subset of key coordinates. In such models, one must decide how to treat the G-matrix
elements. Several common approximations are used:
a). Constant Gij (Evaluated at a Reference Geometry): A simple approach is to assume
that the G-matrix elements are roughly constant and use their value at an equilibrium or
average geometry. For example, in one perturbative model for the water dimer, “the kinetic
energy coupling term is determined from the ab initio calculated optimized geometry.” Here
the off-diagonal Gij was taken as a fixed constant from the equilibrium structure. This fixed-
G approximation is reasonable if the coordinate coupling does not change drastically with
displacement and greatly simplifies the Hamiltonian as there is no coordinate dependence in
ˆT.
b). Coordinate-Dependent Gij (Functional Expansion or Grid): For more accurate treat-
ments, especially with large-amplitude motion, Gij can be treated as a function of coordinates.
For example, the torsional G-matrix element Gττ depends on the O–H bond length r and the
torsion angle τ. In one study, the authors “fit the Wilson G-matrix element for the torsion
to an expansion in cos(kτ).” In other words, they computed Gττ(r, τ) at various geometries
and expanded it in a Fourier series. These approaches retain the coordinate dependence of
the kinetic coupling, which can be important for accuracy.
c). Neglect of Minor Off-Diagonal Terms: In some reduced models, very small kinetic
couplings are simply neglected (set to zero) to simplify the Hamiltonian. This is justified
if an off-diagonal Gij is an order of magnitude smaller than the diagonal terms. However,
one must be cautious, as even small Gij values can cause perceptible shifts if the interacting
modes are nearly degenerate.[77]
Thus, depending on the system, one may treat Gij as constant, include it as a parameter fit, or
fully incorporate its coordinate dependence. For instance, in some local mode Hamiltonians for X–
H stretches in hydrogen-bonded complexes, the G-matrix off-diagonals are treated perturbatively.
By contrast, in high-precision variational calculations, the full G-matrix can be used at each point
without approximation.[78]

11.3
System and Methodology
The vibrational analysis was carried out for a solvated methanol tetramer cluster, following the
local mode methodology of Drougkas et al. The model system consists of a known local minimum
conformer of the methanol tetramer embedded in an explicit solvent environment of additional
methanol molecules. The tetramer comprises four methanol molecules arranged in a hydrogen-
bonded network with three O–H groups acting as hydrogen bond donors (bound O–H) and one
terminal O–H remaining non-hydrogen-bonded (free O–H).
The cluster was optimized at the density functional theory (DFT) level using the B3LYP functional
with Grimme’s D3 dispersion correction and the aug-cc-pVTZ basis set. An ONIOM two-layer
scheme was employed: the tetramer was treated at the high-level DFT layer, while ∼20 surrounding
methanol molecules formed a low-level explicit solvent layer described by the UFF force field.
Tight convergence criteria were used to ensure a well-relaxed structure. All quantum chemical
computations were performed with Gaussian 16. The optimized tetramer was verified to correspond
to the reported local minimum of Drougkas et al.,[81] providing a consistent reference geometry for
the subsequent vibrational analysis.
The analysis then focused on one hydrogen-bonded (bound) O–H group. Within the local mode
framework, each O–H bond is treated as an approximately independent anharmonic oscillator. To
capture hydrogen bond effects on this oscillator, it is necessary to consider both the intramolecular
O–H stretch and the low-frequency intermolecular motion that bends the hydrogen bond (often
described as a rocking or twisting of the donor O–H). In strongly hydrogen-bonded systems, the O–
H stretch is coupled to motion that bends or disrupts the O–H· · · O interaction; a multidimensional
representation therefore provides a more complete description of the potential energy landscape.
To this end, a two-dimensional potential energy surface (PES) was constructed by simultaneously
varying the O–H bond length and the O–H· · · O hydrogen bond angle for the chosen bound O–H.
The O–H distance was incrementally compressed and stretched around its equilibrium value, while
the O–H· · · O angle (defined between the O–H bond vector and the line connecting the hydrogen
to the acceptor oxygen) was varied from near-linear to significantly bent configurations. These
variations were centered on the equilibrium geometry, ensuring that the 2D scan mapped the local
potential well in the vicinity of the minimum.

11.5.5
Comparison between the Two Theories
The investigation of the O–H bond stretch was performed using two distinct quantum chemical
methods: Density Functional Theory (DFT) and Hartree-Fock (HF) theory. Crucially, both sets of
calculations employed the same aug-cc-pVTZ basis set, and each potential energy scan was preceded
by a full geometry optimization at its respective level of theory. This rigorous methodology ensures
that any observed discrepancies in the computed molecular properties are a direct consequence of
the theoretical approximations inherent to each method, rather than artifacts of the basis set or
reference geometry.
The results reveal systematic differences in the predicted equilibrium bond length (re) and harmonic
vibrational frequency (ωe), which can be attributed to the fundamental treatment of electron
correlation.
The Impact of the Hartree-Fock Approximation
The Hartree-Fock method is predicated
on the mean-field approximation, wherein each electron is treated as moving within the average
electrostatic potential generated by all other electrons. While this approach incorporates the Fermi
correlation via the exchange term, it fundamentally neglects the instantaneous Coulombic repulsion
and dynamic avoidance between electrons, a phenomenon known as Coulomb correlation. This
neglect leads to an insufficient screening of the nuclear charges, causing the nuclei to be drawn closer
together to reach an energetic minimum. Consequently, HF theory systematically underestimates
equilibrium bond lengths. This theoretical artifact is clearly observed in the present work, where
the HF method predicts an re of 0.947 ˚A, substantially shorter than the 0.980 ˚A obtained with
DFT.
The Role of the Exchange-Correlation Functional in DFT
In contrast, DFT is formulated
to account for electron correlation through the exchange-correlation (XC) functional. This term
serves as a correction to the independent-particle model, approximating the complex effects of elec-
tron exchange and correlation. By modeling the dynamic avoidance of electrons, the XC functional
provides a more physically realistic description of the electron density and a more effective screen-
ing of the nuclear charges. This, in turn, yields molecular geometries that are in closer agreement
with experimental reality. The longer bond length predicted by DFT is a direct manifestation of
this improved theoretical framework.

Consequences for Vibrational Frequencies
The discrepancies in the calculated potential
energy surfaces have direct consequences for the predicted vibrational frequencies. The harmonic
frequency, ωe, is proportional to the square root of the force constant (Fii), which corresponds to
the curvature of the potential energy well at the equilibrium geometry.
• The artificially short bond length predicted by HF theory is associated with a potential energy
well that is too narrow and steep. This results in an overestimation of the force constant and,
therefore, a significantly higher harmonic frequency of 4056 cm−1. The bond is modeled as
being erroneously ”stiff.”
• The more accurate potential energy surface from DFT is broader, yielding a smaller and
more physically sound force constant. This leads to the lower and more realistic predicted
frequency of 3458 cm−1.
In conclusion, the comparative results provide a clear and methodologically sound demonstration
of a foundational concept in computational chemistry. The systematic underestimation of the O–H
bond length and the concomitant overestimation of its vibrational frequency by the Hartree-Fock
method are direct and predictable consequences of its neglect of electron correlation, a critical
deficiency that modern DFT methods are specifically designed to address.

Conclusions and Outlook
The research began by successfully reproducing and validating the classic spectroscopic data of
W.A.P. Luck, confirming the temperature-dependent behavior of the free hydroxyl (monomer)
fraction in liquid methanol. This provided a solid experimental anchor for the theoretical investi-
gations that followed.
The core of the thesis involved a comparative quantum chemical study using both Hartree-Fock
(HF) and Density Functional Theory (DFT). A key conclusion is the critical importance of electron
correlation in accurately describing molecular properties. The HF method, which neglects electron
correlation, was shown to systematically underestimate the O–H bond length and, as a direct con-
sequence, overestimate its vibrational frequency. DFT, by incorporating an exchange-correlation
functional, provides a more physically sound description, yielding geometries and frequencies that
are more aligned with chemical reality. This finding unequivocally demonstrates that correlation-
corrected methods are essential for the reliable modeling of hydrogen-bonded systems.
Furthermore, the analysis of the potential energy surface (PES) for a solvated methanol tetramer
revealed the limitations of simple analytical models for describing complex molecular motions.
While the one-dimensional Morse potential proved to be an excellent and physically robust model
for the strong, covalent O–H bond stretch, it failed dramatically to capture the true anharmonic
nature of the weaker, more flexible O–H· · · O hydrogen bond angle. The 2D PES scan confirmed
that the high-frequency O–H stretch is intrinsically coupled to the low-frequency angular bend-
ing motion. This interdependence is a fundamental feature of hydrogen bonds and is crucial for
understanding vibrational energy relaxation and the characteristic broadening of spectral bands.
Outlook
The insights gained from this work open several promising directions for future research:
a). Advanced Potential Energy Surfaces: The failure of the simple Morse potential for the
angular coordinate highlights the need for more sophisticated, multi-dimensional analytical
potentials for intermolecular vibrations. Future work should focus on developing and param-
eterizing these advanced potentials for various hydrogen-bonded systems, which can then be
used in larger-scale simulations.
b). Refining Thermodynamic Models: The accurate monomer fractions and interaction
energies derived from DFT calculations can serve as direct, physically meaningful inputs for
parameterizing advanced thermodynamic models like PC-SAFT and CPA. This ”bottom-
up” approach could lead to more predictive and transferable equations of state, reducing the
reliance on empirical fitting to bulk experimental data.
c). Bridging the Microscopic and Macroscopic: A significant challenge remains in con-
necting the detailed quantum mechanical picture of small molecular clusters to the macro-
scopic properties of the bulk liquid. Future studies could employ hybrid Quantum Mechan-
ics/Molecular Mechanics (QM/MM) simulations.
By treating the core hydrogen-bonding
network with high-level DFT and the surrounding solvent with an efficient classical force
field, it will be possible to simulate vibrational spectra in the condensed phase.
d). Exploring More Complex Systems: The computational protocol established in this the-
sis can be extended to investigate more complex and industrially relevant systems.
This
includes larger alcohol clusters, alcohol-water mixtures under various conditions (including
supercritical), and the role of hydrogen bonding in solvation and chemical reactions.
