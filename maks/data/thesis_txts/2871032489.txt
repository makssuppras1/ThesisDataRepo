Abstract
This thesis addresses the mismatch between rapidly evolving object­detection algorithms
and the modest compute budget of embedded hardware. We present an end­to­end
pipeline that quantizes and deploys a YOLOv8n detector on an NVIDIA Jetson AGX Xavier.
Guided by a Roofline analysis, we implement INT8 post­training quantization (with op­
tional quantization­aware training) across three toolchains—PyTorch­native, ONNX Runtime,
and TensorRT—and perform layer­wise sensitivity analysis to craft a mixed­precision con­
figuration.
Experiments show that an MSE­calibrated INT8 model achieves 0.362 mAP on COCO,
only one point below the FP32 baseline, while the TensorRT INT8 engine runs at 7 ms per
640×640 image ( 143 FPS), a 4× speed­up over vanilla PyTorch FP32. FP16 inference
reaches 9 ms with no accuracy loss, indicating that half­precision often offers the best
trade­off on modern embedded GPUs.
Comprehensive ablation studies quantify the impact of calibration set size, calibration
method, and Q/DQ fusion on accuracy–latency trade­offs, and the thesis details CPU–
GPU co­optimization for preprocessing and postprocessing. The results demonstrate
that, with careful quantization and system­level tuning, state­of­the­art object detectors
can meet real­time requirements on low­power edge devices, providing a reproducible
blueprint for future embedded AI deployments.

Introduction
1.1
Background
In the past decade, deep learning­driven object detection algorithms have made leaps
in accuracy and complexity, accompanied by a substantial increase in computational re­
quirements. Early deep learning object detection, such as R­CNN (2014), was very slow,
taking about 14 seconds to process an image on a GPU [1]. Later two­stage detectors
(such as Fast/Faster R­CNN) introduced region proposals and shared convolutional fea­
tures, which improved accuracy while still maintaining high computational complexity [1].
To improve detection accuracy, researchers have continued to adopt deeper and more
complex backbone networks (such as from VGG to ResNet to Transformer) and methods
such as multi­scale feature fusion, resulting in a significant increase in model parame­
ters and FLOPs [2]. This is corroborated by more recent studies showing that even in
2020, the frontier AI models’ training FLOPs continue to grow on the order of 4–5× per
year [3]. This means each new generation of state­of­the­art models (for tasks like image
recognition or object detection) tended to require significantly more FLOPs (floating­point
operations) than its predecessor. One­stage detectors (such as the YOLO series [4]) have
greatly improved detection speed since their introduction in 2016. For example, an early
version of YOLO can achieve 155 FPS on the VOC dataset [1]. However, the pursuit of
real­time performance often comes at the cost of accuracy. In recent years, models such
as YOLOv7 have achieved an improved accuracy­speed trade­off in the range of 5–160
FPS through improved architecture [1]. In general, the improvement in the accuracy of
object detection algorithms comes at the cost of larger model sizes and higher compu­
tational costs, showing a trend of geometric growth in computational requirements with
performance.
Despite this rapid advancement in deep learning models, hardware improvements have
failed to keep pace. In recent years, embedded AI hardware (such as the NVIDIA Jetson
series, Raspberry Pi with an acceleration module, and mobile NPU chips) has improved
in terms of computing performance and energy efficiency, but the rate of improvement
is relatively limited. Embedded devices are limited by power consumption and size, and
their computing power has not increased as rapidly as server­grade GPUs. For exam­
ple, mobile devices typically only consume a few watts of power, which cannot match the
computing power provided by data center GPUs, which consume hundreds of watts. Al­
though high­end GPUs can double their performance every one to two years (in line with
Moore’s Law’s doubling cycle of about 24 months) [5], embedded devices have a more
limited performance increase due to power consumption and heat dissipation constraints.
It is generally accepted that the growth rate of computing requirements far exceeds the
rate of hardware improvement: research has shown that the amount of training comput­
ing power has doubled every six months since the beginning of the era of deep learning,
which is much faster than the doubling of hardware transistor density every two years [6].
To this end, chip manufacturers have introduced dedicated AI accelerators to improve
performance per watt, such as the NPU in mobile phone SoCs and Edge TPU, but over­
all, the computing power improvement on the embedded side still cannot keep up with
the growing demand for algorithms. In addition, there is limited improvement in memory
and bandwidth for embedded devices. Unlike cloud GPUs, which have tens of gigabytes
of graphics memory, embedded devices have small memory capacities and limited band­
width, which further restricts the ability to deploy large models. This makes it difficult for
Exploring quantization techniques for object detection on embedded devices

==PAGE:10==
many detection models that perform well on servers to run directly on edge devices in
real time. For example, the original YOLOv4 model can only achieve an inference speed
of about 4 FPS on a smartphone, and after being compressed by model pruning, it is im­
proved to nearly 20 FPS [7]. The annual average increase in the computing power of em­
bedded devices is significantly lower than the increase in the computational requirements
of deep learning models, a trend that has attracted widespread attention in academia and
industry.
In summary, the evolution of deep learning object detection algorithms is far faster than
the improvement in the computing power of embedded hardware, and there is a significant
‘development gap’ between the two. On the one hand, at the algorithm level, the model
size continues to increase in pursuit of higher accuracy, and the amount of computation
doubles every half a year [6]. This has caused the requirements for computing resources
of the latest detection models to rise exponentially. On the other hand, at the hardware
level, the improvement in the computing power of embedded devices is slowing down due
to physical limitations and power consumption constraints (Moore’s Law slowing down).
A review in Proceedings of the IEEE points out that although the complexity and accuracy
of detection algorithms have increased significantly in recent years, this has made their
deployment on resource­constrained devices ‘more difficult’ [8]. Another example is the
research presented at AAAI 2021, which shows that even one­stage detectors (YOLO,
SSD) optimized for speed still require ‘a lot of computing power’ to achieve high accuracy
on mobile devices, which is a major bottleneck for real­time deployment [7]. Therefore, a
large number of studies have focused on model compression and lightweight networks to
bridge the gap between algorithm requirements and embedded computing power. This
phenomenon fully illustrates that the development of deep learning object detection has
been so fast that it has forced hardware to adapt, and the speed is significantly faster
than the improvement of the computing power of embedded devices. In other words, the
current evolution of algorithms is outpacing the ability of embedded hardware to improve
performance, and there is an urgent need in related fields to balance this gap through
collaborative optimization of software and hardware.
To address these challenges, quantization has emerged as a key optimization strategy for
CNN deployment on embedded devices. Quantization reduces both the memory footprint
and computational complexity of deep learning models by representing weights and acti­
vations in lower­precision formats, such as INT8 instead of FP32 [9]. This transformation
significantly improves inference speed while reducing energy consumption. Furthermore,
many embedded AI accelerators, such as TensorRT, are optimized for INT8 computa­
tions, enabling faster inference without excessive hardware modifications [10]. As such,
quantization plays a crucial role in making high­performance object detection feasible on
edge devices.
In this work, we explore various quantization techniques for object detection models on
embedded platforms, using YOLO as a case study. We evaluate different quantization
methods within the ONNX Runtime and TensorRT frameworks, analyzing trade­offs be­
tween model size, inference speed, and detection accuracy. Our goal is to provide insights
into deploying real­time, energy­efficient deep learning solutions on resource­constrained
hardware.
1.2
Problem Statement
The application of quantization techniques in deep learning has demonstrated significant
success in image classification tasks [10, 11]. However, extending these methods to ob­
ject detection models, particularly single­stage detectors like YOLO, introduces unique
2

==PAGE:11==
challenges that remain understudied in existing literature. Moreover, it has been pointed
out in the literature that the amount of floating­point operations in a typical object detec­
tion network may be three orders of magnitude higher than that in a classification net­
work [12], which means that quantization for object detection tasks may be more difficult.
While quantization frameworks such as TensorRT and ONNX Runtime provide general
optimization pipelines, their efficacy in preserving the accuracy of object detection mod­
els under resource­constrained environments is not yet fully understood. First, architec­
tural complexity exacerbates quantization sensitivity. Object detection models inherently
require multi­scale feature extraction and dense prediction heads, which amplify the risk
of precision loss during quantization. For instance, YOLO’s detection heads rely on fine­
grained feature maps to localize objects, a process highly sensitive to quantization cal­
ibration range errors [13]. The standard PTQ workflow can make it difficult to capture
features, leading to a loss of accuracy. [14] observed similar challenges in RetinaNet,
where quantization of bounding box predictors led to significant accuracy drops.
Second, framework­specific optimization disparities complicate deployment. TensorRT
and ONNX Runtime adopt divergent approaches to quantization: TensorRT leverages
hardware­aware calibration, while ONNX Runtime emphasizes cross­platform compati­
bility. These platform characteristics create a fragmented landscape for developers’ de­
ployment tasks. For example, TensorRT’s layer fusion mechanism combines consecutive
operations (e.g., Conv­BatchNorm­ReLU) into a single CUDA kernel to reduce memory
transfers and improve parallelism [10], while ONNX relies on graphics­level optimizations
to maintain framework neutrality [15], but such optimizations are rarely used in bench­
marking for detection tasks.
Third, embedded hardware heterogeneity imposes practical constraints. While TensorRT
excels on NVIDIA edge SoCs, many low­cost devices rely on ARM CPUs with limited
INT8 acceleration support [16].
Additionally, energy consumption(a critical metric for
battery­powered devices) and memory utilization (which has a significant impact on the
deployment efficiency of resource­constrained hardware) are seldom addressed in exist­
ing quantization studies.
In summary, the current research gap lies in the systematic evaluation of quantization
techniques tailored to object detection models on embedded platforms. Key unresolved
questions include:
• Can the object detection model reduce accuracy loss without sacrificing real­time
performance?
• What are the trade­offs between TensorRT’s hardware­specific optimizations and
ONNX Runtime’s flexibility in cross­platform deployments?
Addressing these questions is critical to advancing efficient, deployable object detection
systems for edge applications, from autonomous drones to industrial IoT devices.

2.1
Overview of quantization techniques
2.1.1
Basic concepts of quantification
Model quantization is a technique that reduces the numerical precision of model param­
eters and activations (e.g., from 32­bit floating point FP32 to 8­bit integer INT8) in ex­
change for improved computational and storage efficiency. Reducing the precision can
greatly compress the model size (an INT8 model is only about 1/4 the size of an FP32
model), while using low­bit integer arithmetic to accelerate inference and reduce energy
consumption. Experimental results show that INT8 inference on a general­purpose CPU
can achieve 2 to 3 × the speedup compared to FP32, and the speedup on dedicated
fixed­point hardware can even reach 10× [17]. The mathematical formulation follows:
Q(x) = round
(clip(x, α, β)
s
)
(2.1)
where α/β define the clipping range and s represents the scaling factor.
The cost of quantization is a decrease in numerical representation accuracy, i.e., quanti­
zation error. Weights and activations need to be rounded to discrete levels, and values
that exceed the range will be truncated, which introduces a loss of accuracy. The quan­
tization process therefore involves a trade­off between model accuracy and efficiency: a
higher quantization bit width (e.g., 8 bits) generally significantly improves efficiency while
maintaining accuracy, while more aggressive lower­bit quantization (e.g., 4 bits or less)
may cause a significant loss of accuracy [17]. Therefore, the reasonable selection of the
quantization bit width and the use of appropriate strategies to control quantization errors
are the core considerations of quantization technology.
2.1.2
Mapping and Offsetting
To quantize a real­valued tensor, one typically uses a scale factor and zero­point offset to
map floating­point values into integers. In linear (uniform) quantization, for each tensor
we choose a scaling constant s and an integer zero­point z such that any real value x is
quantized as[16]:
q = round(x/s) + z
(2.2)
and then clamped to the representable integer range. Here s is a positive real number that
determines the step size (resolution) of the quantizer, and z (often called zero­point) is an
integer that typically ensures that a real value of 0 maps exactly to an integer q = 0 (or
mid­point of symmetric signed range). we can describe how the dequantization reverses
this process (in an approximate sense)[16]. Given a quantized value q, the original real
value x is reconstructed as:
x′ = s · (q −z)
(2.3)
Here s is the scale and z is the zero­point offset. The zero­point z is chosen such that a real
value of 0 is exactly representable (i.e. mapped to q = z)[16], ensuring that zero­valued
inputs/outputs incur no error. Because q was obtained by rounding x/s to the nearest
integer, the dequantized x′ will not exactly equal the original x in general. This loss of
information is the fundamental quantization error introduced by the rounding. Fine details
smaller than s are wiped out – many different real values end up mapping to the same

2.1.3
symmetric quantization and asymmetric quantization
According to the selection of dynamic range and the mapping method, we can divide it
into symmetric quantization and asymmetric quantization. In symmetric quantization, the
zero point before and after quantization are aligned, so there is no offset, which makes the
calculation of the quantization process simple. This simplicity enables hardware­friendly
implementations, as quantized operations reduce to a single scaling factor multiplication.
As shown in Figure 1, in contrast, asymmetric quantization introduces an offset term to
accommodate asymmetric data distributions (e.g., ReLU­activated outputs with only non­
negative values). While this improves dynamic range utilization, it complicates computa­
tions by requiring explicit zero­point corrections. For instance, integer matrix multiplication

under asymmetric quantization involves two additional vector additions and multiplicative
scaling steps to compensate for zero­point mismatches, as formalized in [16].
Figure 2.2: Just a normal figure
Despite its computational overhead, asymmetric quantization offers critical benefits in
scenarios with highly skewed data distributions. By decoupling the zero­point from the
range symmetry, it preserves precision for activations or weights with non­uniform ranges
(e.g., strictly non­negative ReLU outputs). This avoids clipping errors caused by symmet­
ric quantization’s forced alignment of positive and negative ranges, which can degrade
model accuracy by up to 2­3% on tasks like image classification[18].
The symmetry of the quantization grid (e.g., −127 to 127 for 8­bit integers) allows hard­
ware accelerators to exploit unified bit­width processing and memory alignment strategies,
minimizing register fragmentation and branch divergence. Empirical studies demonstrate
that symmetric quantization reduces instruction overhead by over 50% compared to asym­
metric methods by avoiding redundant offset­related arithmetic [17]. NVIDIA’s TensorRT
framework adopts symmetric quantization as its default strategy, leveraging the architec­
tural advantages of Tensor Cores in GPUs [19].
2.1.4
Post­training quantification (PTQ) and quantization­aware training
training (QAT)
There are two major paradigms for quantizing neural networks: post­training quantization
and quantization­aware training. PTQ refers to applying quantization after a model has
been fully trained, without updating its weights via gradient descent. In a typical PTQ
workflow, one takes a pre­trained float32 model and determines quantization parameters
(scales/zero­points) for each layer, often using a small calibration dataset (or even none
at all). The weights are then rounded to the nearest quantized value, and activations will
be quantized at inference time. PTQ is appealing because it requires no re­training of
the network – it’s a “push­button” approach that is extremely fast to deploy and does not
require the original training data or costly fine­tuning. However, PTQ struggles for more
aggressive quantization. At lower bit­widths (4­bit or less), simply rounding a pre­trained
model often yields poor accuracy, since the model was never optimized for such coarse
precision [20].
By contrast, QAT integrates quantization into the training process so the model can learn
to compensate for quantization errors. In QAT, one simulates low­precision arithmetic
during network training or fine­tuning. Specifically, fake quantization operations (pseudo­
quantization of weights and activations) are inserted in the graph, and gradients are com­
puted through these quantization nodes [16]. With this approach, the model’s weights
adjust during training to minimize the loss under quantized conditions. For example, the
network might learn to slightly shift weight values to better align with quantization levels, or

adjust batchnorm scaling to reduce activation dynamic range. As a result, QAT generally
achieves higher accuracy at low bit­widths compared to PTQ [20]. It “closes the gap” to
full­precision performance even for 4­bit or lower precision. The downsides are the addi­
tional training time and the need for training data (often the original dataset with labels). In
summary, PTQ is suitable for rapid deployment scenarios that do not require excessively
low precision, while QAT is generally used to restore model accuracy or deployed in very
low precision scenarios.
Calibration
An essential step in post­training quantization is calibration – determining the optimal
quantization ranges (scales and zero­points) for activations and weights based on data.
Several calibration strategies exist:
Figure 2.3: Input activation histogram and calibration range for first conv in Yolov8n
• Min­Max: The simplest method sets the quantization range by the minimum and
maximum values observed in a sample of the data. While straightforward, this ap­
proach is highly susceptible to outliers. A single abnormal activation value can inflate
the range dramatically, forcing a large scale s and thereby reducing precision for the
majority of values [21].
• Percentile: This method clips the distribution at a high percentile of observed val­
ues instead of the absolute min/max. For instance, one might set the lower/upper
bounds such that they include 99.9% (or 99.99%) of the data, and treat any values
beyond that as outliers to clip [18]
• Entropy: A more sophisticated technique involves selecting the range that mini­
mizes the information loss between the original signal distribution and the quan­
tized distribution. This is often implemented by optimizing the Kullback­Leibler (KL)
divergence [18]. In practice, one collects a histogram of activation values and then
tries different clipping thresholds, computing the KL divergence between the original
distribution (with values beyond the threshold lumped at the ends) and the distribu­
tion after quantization. The threshold that yields the smallest divergence is selected
as the optimal range. Entropy calibration tends to preserve more information than
plain min­max, since it allows a slight truncation of extreme tails in order to use more
levels where the bulk of the data lies.
• MSE Calibration: This method determines the optimal quantization range by min­
8

==PAGE:17==
imizing the mean squared error (MSE) between the original and quantized data.
Instead of relying on min­max bounds or percentiles, it evaluates different clipping
thresholds and selects the one that best preserves the signal fidelity after quantiza­
tion. By balancing the trade­off between clipping distortion and quantization error,
MSE calibration often achieves higher accuracy than simpler methods while remain­
ing computationally tractable. It is particularly effective when the data distribution
contains outliers or long tails, as it avoids excessive range inflation while retaining
precision for the majority of values [22].
In practice, calibration exerts a substantial influence on the accuracy of PTQ. A proper
calibration can recover most of the performance, while a poor calibration can lead to
catastrophic loss. For example, in this study, different calibration methods were used
for different models, and the results were quite different. In addition, the experiment also
proved that the data set used for calibration is also extremely critical [18]. Therefore,
calibration is a crucial step to bridge the accuracy gap in PTQ by intelligently selecting
quantization ranges that best represent the data distribution.
2.1.5
Hardware­aware Quantization and Quantization Granularity
The effectiveness of quantization can depend on hardware support. Different acceler­
ators have varying capabilities and constraints for low­precision arithmetic, which mo­
tivates hardware­aware quantization strategies. One consideration is the data format:
some hardware (DSPs, older GPUs) have efficient instructions only for certain quanti­
zation schemes (e.g. 8­bit signed symmetric). For example, hardware like Qualcomm
Hexagon can handle asymmetric quantization, but there is often a slight overhead [20].
However, some CPUs and accelerators (such as NVIDIA’s INT8) natively support affine
quantization with scale and zero­point, allowing efficient asymmetric quantization for acti­
vations. When deploying, one must be mindful: if the target hardware or inference engine
(TensorRT, OpenVINO, etc.) only supports a certain scheme (e.g. symmetric int8), then
the quantization strategy should align with that.
Figure 2.4: Input activation histogram and calibration range for first conv in Yolov8n
Another aspect is quantization granularity – the level at which quantization parameters
(scale/zero­point) are shared in the tensor. The finest granularity is that each element
has a separate quantization parameter. The medium granularity reuses parameters on
each channel of the tensor ­ for a two­dimensional matrix, by row or column; for three
dimensions (similar to images) and four dimensions (such as convolution weights), by
tensor, and so on [18]. The entire quantized tensor is abstracted into a collection of 3D
small squares, as shown in the figure2.4. The most common are per­tensor quantization

and per­channel quantization, where each complete tensor or channel (e.g., all weights
of a layer or all activations of a layer) uses a single scale and zero­point [20].
• Per­tensor quantization
– (Advantage) Low latency: a tensor shares the same quantization parameter
– (Disadvantage) High error rate: one scale can hardly cover all FP32 dynamic
ranges
• Per­channel (layer) quantization
– (Advantage) Low error rate: each channel has its own scale to reflect the dy­
namic range of the data in this channel
– (Disadvantage) High latency: vectors are needed to store the scale of each
channel
This is hardware­friendly: all values in the tensor are converted with the same formula,
so the compute engine can uniformly apply one scale.
In summary, hardware­aware quantization often entails selecting symmetric vs asymmet­
ric schemes, deciding on per­tensor or per­channel scaling, and possibly limiting scale
values or bit­widths, all while trying to maintain model accuracy. Successful deployment
requires co­design: choosing quantization parameters and granularity that both fit the
hardware’s efficient operation modes and meet the model’s accuracy requirements
2.1.6
Existing quantization optimization methods
The current mainstream network quantization optimization methods can be classified into
two dimensions: one is the training paradigm, and the other is the optimization goal.
In terms of training paradigm, different methods differ in training paradigm, including: (a)
post­training quantization (PTQ) methods, which directly perform quantization calibration
and minor adjustments on the pre­trained model, usually without the need for training on
the complete dataset; (b) quantization­aware training (QAT) methods, which introduce
quantization emulation or trainable parameters during training, and retraining the network
to adapt to the quantization. (c) Hybrid paradigms, which combine a small amount of fine­
tuning with post­training calibration, such as layer­wise optimization and a small amount
of unlabeled data­assisted methods.
On the other hand, in terms of optimization objectives, different methods focus on improv­
ing the performance of different aspects of the quantization process. These optimization
objectives are not mutually exclusive, and many methods combine multiple approaches to
improve the accuracy of low­bit quantization. Below, we classify representative methods
according to their optimization objectives.
• Activation range optimization: This method reduces quantization errors by opti­
mizing the interval of activation values. A typical representative is the PACT method,
proposed by Choi et al [23]. PACT introduces a learnable activation clipping up­
per limit parameter in training, truncating the ReLU activation output to a trainable
threshold to find the optimal quantization interval. With this quantization threshold
trainable strategy, PACT achieves 2­bit activation quantization without significantly
losing accuracy.
Another representative is QIL (Quantization Interval Learning),
proposed by Jung et al [24]. QIL directly targets the task loss and reversely learns
the quantization interval of each layer. By iteratively optimizing the upper and lower
10
Exploring quantization techniques for object detection on embedded devices
==PAGE:19==
bounds, the quantized network can almost maintain the original accuracy with 4­
bit, and minimize the degradation of accuracy as the bit width decreases. These
methods usually adopt the QAT paradigm, learning the optimal clipping range dur­
ing training, and are suitable for low­bit (such as 4­bit or even 2­bit) quantization.
Another idea is to choose the clipping value analytically. For example, the ACIQ
method derives the clipping threshold with the minimum loss by assuming an acti­
vation distribution (Laplace/Gaussian)[25, 26]. These methods are mostly used in
the PTQ calibration stage, and although they do not require retraining, they can ef­
fectively improve the accuracy of low­bit quantization. In general, activation range
optimization significantly reduces truncation errors in low­bit quantization by sup­
pressing long­tailed distributions and finding the optimal clipping interval.
• Weight quantization optimization: This method focuses on improving the accu­
racy of weight quantization. The core is to optimize the rounding/rounding strategy
of weights without changing the model structure. The traditional approach is to di­
rectly round to the nearest quantized value, but research has found that this is not
optimal. To this end, Nagel et al. proposed the AdaRound method at ICML 2020,
which adaptively determines whether each weight is rounded up or down by mini­
mizing the task loss on a small batch of unlabeled data[27]. AdaRound relaxes dis­
crete rounding decisions to a continuously differentiable form, and then optimizes
the rounding scheme for each layer’s weights, which is significantly better than direct
rounding and can quantize ResNet­50’s weights to 4­bit with less than 1% accuracy
loss without retraining. AdaRound is representative of the PTQ paradigm, which re­
quires only a small amount of unlabeled calibration data for piece­level optimization,
making it ideal for low­bit deployment.
• Quantization step size optimization: This type of method focuses on optimizing
the quantizer’s step size (scale) parameter to reduce quantization errors. Typical
work LSQ (Learned Step Size Quantization) was proposed by Esser et al. in ICLR
2020[28]. LSQ treats the scaling factor of each layer’s quantization as a trainable
parameter and designs a gradient estimation strategy to learn this step size, thereby
achieving the best ImageNet accuracy at the time with 2–4­bit weights and activa­
tions. By learning the step size during training, LSQ significantly improves the perfor­
mance of low­bit quantization (e.g., successfully training a 3­bit model with accuracy
close to full accuracy). The follow­up LSQ+ method (Bhalgat et al., 2020[29]) further
extends this idea to a wider range of networks and lower bit scenarios by introduc­
ing learnable zero­point offset parameters and an improved initialization strategy
to accommodate asymmetric quantization. Since the quantization step directly de­
termines the scale of the quantization grid, these methods usually adopt the QAT
paradigm, jointly updating the weight and step parameters in supervised training. In
general, trainable steps are especially effective when extremely low bit (4­bit and
below) accuracy is required.
• Global reconstruction optimization: This type of method takes a global approach,
directly minimizing quantization errors by reconstructing network outputs or interme­
diate features. It often optimizes quantization parameters layer by layer or globally
using a small amount of data in the post­training stage. Representative method
BRECQ was proposed by Li et al. at ICLR 2021 with the goal of pushing the limits
of PTQ. BRECQ adopts a block­by­block reconstruction strategy: it freezes most of
the network and only selects a few layers as a block, using a small amount of calibra­
tion data to make the quantized block output approximate the original model output,
thereby correcting the quantization error block by block[30]. Through this greedy

second­order error minimization method, BRECQ is the first to push the quantiza­
tion accuracy to the 2­bit level in a pure PTQ scenario, for example, achieving a 2­bit
ResNet­18/50 with only 1% accuracy loss, and the accuracy of 4­bit MobileNetV2
is comparable to QAT, but the speed of producing quantized models is two orders
of magnitude faster. BRECQ belongs to the PTQ framework, but it achieves re­

demonstrating the power of overall optimization. Also worth mentioning are “zero­

2.2
Fundamentals of model deployment
Model deployment on embedded devices requires a holistic understanding of computa­
tional efficiency, hardware constraints, and algorithmic characteristics. This section intro­
duces key metrics and architectural principles critical to optimizing object detection models
for resource­constrained platforms.
2.2.1
Computational Performance Metrics
FLOPs, TOPS, and MACC: Floating­point operations per second (FLOPS) is a classic
measure of hardware compute throughput, originally denoting the rate of 32­bit floating
point operations a processor can perform. Modern AI accelerators, however, often report
performance in tera­operations per second (TOPS), which generalizes the concept to arbi­
trary precision. For example, one TOPS equals one trillion operations per second, count­
ing each arithmetic operation (e.g. one multiply or one add) regardless of bit­width [32]. In
deep neural network inference, the fundamental computation is the multiply­accumulate
operation (MAC); a single MAC involves one multiplication and one addition, typically
counted as two operations in TOPS metrics [32]. Thus, a chip’s peak TOPS is essen­
tially two times the number of MAC units times their clock frequency [32]. Notably, the
industry­standard TOPS rating is often given for INT8 precision on AI accelerators [33].
This makes TOPS especially relevant for quantized models: for instance, NVIDIA’s Jetson
AGX Xavier is rated at 22 TOPS (INT8) peak performance, meaning it can theoretically
perform more than 20 trillion INT8 operations per second on its accelerated cores [34]. In
practice, TOPS is an upper bound – actual achievable throughput depends on how well
the model can utilize the hardware.
While TOPS (and FLOPS) describe hardware capability, the multiply–accumulate count
(MACC) of a neural network is a model­specific metric that quantifies the total number of
MAC operations required to process one input. MACC (sometimes reported as the total
number of “FLOPs” for a single inference, with the convention that 1 MAC = 2 FLOPs) is a
useful indicator of a model’s computational complexity independent of hardware [32]. For
example, if Model A requires 5 billion MACs per image and Model B requires 10 billion
for a similar task, Model A is computationally more efficient. Reducing MACC is critical
for embedded deployment: a lower MACC generally translates to lower latency and en­
ergy per inference on a given device. In fact, improvements in model architecture often
aim to reduce MACC without sacrificing accuracy. For instance, Li et al. (2023) report
a speech separation network that achieves comparable accuracy to a prior model with
12
Exploring quantization techniques for object detection on embedded devices
==PAGE:21==
only 5% of the MAC operations, resulting in 10% of the inference time on CPU [35]. This
illustrates how MACC directly relates to inference speed and efficiency. In the context of
object detection, YOLO models are known to be MACC­intensive; YOLOv3, for example,
requires on the order of 1010 MACs per image at high resolution, making its deployment
challenging on low­power hardware [32]. Quantization does not reduce the MAC count,
but by using INT8 arithmetic it allows those MACs to execute on units that can handle
more operations per second (thus effectively leveraging the hardware’s TOPS). Moreover,
quantization reduces memory bandwidth and storage requirements (e.g. 8­bit weights are
4× smaller than 32­bit), which can improve effective throughput. In summary, FLOPS/­
TOPS characterize the supply side (hardware capability), while MACC characterizes the
demand side (model complexity). A balanced deployment requires that the hardware’s
TOPS (particularly INT8 TOPS for quantized models) sufficiently exceeds the model’s
required operations per second for the target frame rate, after accounting for real­world
overheads.
2.2.2
CPU/GPU architecture
Modern computer architectures leverage both general­purpose CPUs and highly paral­
lel GPUs, each optimized for different objectives. CPUs are essentially latency­oriented
processors: they dedicate significant chip area to sophisticated control logic and caches,
employing deep out­of­order pipelines, branch prediction, and speculative execution so
that instructions (including branches) execute with as little stall as possible [36]. These
features make CPUs well­suited for serial code and irregular workloads with lots of branch­
ing or dependency, as they aim to avoid latency at all costs. In contrast, GPUs are de­
signed as throughput­oriented processors that trade single­thread latency for raw parallel
throughput [36]. A GPU consists of hundreds or thousands of smaller cores organized to
execute many threads in parallel; it relies on massive hardware multi­threading to tolerate
memory latency (by rapidly context­switching between warps of threads) rather than elim­
inate it [36]. This design expects workloads with abundant data parallelism. In summary,
CPUs are optimized for low­latency processing of complex, branching code, whereas
GPUs favor high­throughput execution of regular, parallelizable workloads – a comple­
mentary heterogeneity often exploited in modern systems [36].
Take Jetson AGX Xavier as an example. It consists of four heterogeneous dual­core
NVIDIA Carmel CPU clusters. The Carmel CPU cores are superscalar out­of­order cores,
efficient at scalar and lightly parallel tasks. They include NEON vector units that can
perform operations on 128­bit registers (for example, four 32­bit floats or sixteen 8­bit
integers at a time). While modern ARM CPUs do support INT8 arithmetic (and even dot­
product instructions for small matrix multiplies), their parallelism is limited to at most a few
tens of operations in flight per core. The CPU is typically memory­centric (large caches,
branch predictors) and excels at tasks like data preparation and control flow. However, for
the dense linear algebra computations in CNNs, an embedded CPU core cannot match
the raw throughput of a GPU core array. In deploying a model like YOLO on Xavier, one
might use the CPU for preprocessing or postprocessing.

Optimization strategies for object detection models on
embedded devices
Embedded edge devices are far less powerful than server­grade GPUs in terms of com­
puting power, storage and energy consumption. They are usually equipped with low­
power CPUs and small GPUs/NPUs. For example, the NVIDIA Jetson Nano module only
provides about 0.5 TFLOPS of floating­point computing power [42], whereas data center
GPUs can deliver tens to hundreds of TFLOPS images.nvidia.com. At the same time,
embedded devices have limited memory capacity (typically only a few gigabytes) and
memory bandwidth (e.g. about 25.6 GB/s for the Jetson Nano) is also much lower than
that of high­end GPUs (up to hundreds of gigabytes/s) [42]. These memory and bandwidth
limitations make it difficult to deploy large models, and the training process can hardly be
completed on the device itself, so it relies on high­end servers [43]. In terms of energy
consumption, embedded devices emphasize low power consumption: Jetson Nano and
the like only consume less than 10 W [42], while desktop­class GPUs such as GeForce
RTX 2080 Ti consume more than 250 W [44]. Overall, the computing power and memory
that edge devices can provide is far inferior to that of cloud servers, so it is necessary to
strictly control resource usage when deploying deep learning models.
2.4.1
Optimization strategy for object detection models
To adapt to these limitations, researchers have proposed various optimization strategies
for embedded end­object detection models. One type is to design lightweight network
structures, that is, to use smaller models to reduce the amount of computation while en­
suring accuracy [45]. Typical representatives include the MobileNet series, EfficientNet,
and Tiny­YOLO. They use deep separable convolutions, width factor pruning, and other
methods to greatly reduce parameters and FLOPs [46]. The MobileNet model was pro­
posed specifically for mobile embedded vision applications, and it achieves competitive
accuracy with less computation on tasks such as ImageNet [46]. Subsequent MobileNet­
SSD or EfficientDet applies this lightweight backbone to detection, achieving real­time
object detection on embedded devices [43]. For example, some studies have shown that
on the same embedded platform, the SSD detection network using MobileNet­V2 as the
backbone takes far less time to reason than a model with a large ResNet backbone [43].

Therefore, the selection of lightweight detection models is the primary measure for edge

Another important strategy is model compression and quantization, including pruning,

2.4.2
Optimization of different inference frameworks
For embedded deployment, it is necessary to select an appropriate inference framework
(runtime) and optimize the software.
Optimizing the inference phase is often consid­
ered even more strategic than training for long­running applications [48]. This section
reviews three mainstream solutions include native frameworks (such as PyTorch), in­
termediate representation runtimes (such as ONNX Runtime), and hardware­optimized
engines (such as TensorRT).
• PyTorch Native Inference. PyTorch is a dynamic computation graph framework
designed with an imperative, Pythonic style to maximize ease of use and flexibility,
while still delivering high performance on modern hardware [49]. Its architecture
executes models eagerly (operation by operation), which simplifies debugging and
model development. To reconcile flexibility with speed, PyTorch’s runtime leverages
highly optimized libraries (e.g., NVIDIA cuDNN for GPUs and MKL/QNNPACK for
CPUs) and offers a just­in­time (JIT) compiler (TorchScript) that can convert mod­
els to a static graph for faster deployment. The design philosophy prioritizes de­
veloper productivity, as evidenced by the seamless integration of PyTorch models
as “regular Python programs” under user control [49]. In terms of quantization, Py­
Torch provides an extensive quantization toolkit as part of its library, supporting post­
training static quantization and quantization­aware training. These techniques allow
converting models to 8­bit integers for inference, leveraging efficient INT8 matrix­
multiplication kernels (FBGEMM on x86, QNNPACK on ARM) to significantly accel­
erate CPU inference , but support for server GPUs (via TensorRT and cuDNN) is still
in development [50]. In summary, PyTorch’s native inference path (with or without
JIT optimization) offers the convenience of using the same framework for training
and deployment, with built­in support for quantized models on supported backends.
•
• ONNX Runtime. ONNX Runtime is an open­source inference engine geared to­
wards portability and cross­framework compatibility. ONNX (Open Neural Network
Exchange) was introduced in 2017 as an open standard intermediate format for
machine learning models, enabling models trained in one framework to be exported
and run in another [48]. ONNX Runtime (ORT) executes these standardized models
with a high­performance engine that supports multiple execution providers for differ­
ent hardware environments (e.g., CPU, GPU, DSP). Its architecture is based on a
static dataflow graph (the ONNX model graph), which allows ORT to perform exten­

sive graph­level optimizations ahead of runtime – such as constant folding, operator
fusion, and elimination of redundant computations. For instance, using the highest
optimization level in ONNX Runtime can improve inference speed by around 8%
compared to default settings, thanks to aggressive graph optimizations [48]. The
design goal of ORT is interoperability and efficiency: developers can train a model
in PyTorch or TensorFlow, convert it to ONNX, and deploy on a variety of embedded
devices using the same runtime. This decoupling eases deployment on resource­
constrained platforms since the lightweight ORT binary (far smaller than a full train­
ing framework) can be installed on the device. ORT also has integrated support
for quantized inference. The ONNX format includes quantized operator definitions
(e.g., QLinearConv for 8­bit convolutions), and ONNX Runtime provides tools to
convert float32 models to INT8 ONNX models [51]. During inference, ORT will ex­

accelerators (for example, via the NVIDIA TensorRT execution provider or Intel’s

compatibility and modular optimization: it may not include hardware­specific tricks
itself, but it seamlessly integrates with libraries like TensorRT to optimize perfor­
mance on a given device.
• NVIDIA TensorRT. TensorRT is a highly optimized inference framework developed
by NVIDIA, tailored specifically for NVIDIA GPUs and system­on­chip accelera­

Unlike general­purpose frameworks, TensorRT functions as a deep learn­
ing compiler: it takes a trained network (via ONNX or directly from frameworks
using provided parsers) and generates an optimized inference engine for the tar­
get GPU[10]. Under the hood, TensorRT performs numerous optimizations such
as layer fusion, kernel auto­tuning, memory layout optimization, and reduced pre­
cision execution. A hallmark feature of TensorRT is its aggressive use of mixed­
precision and INT8 quantization to maximize throughput. Models can be executed
in FP16 (half­precision) or INT8 precision, substantially reducing compute and mem­
ory bandwidth requirements at inference time. TensorRT’s design goal is maximum
efficiency on NVIDIA hardware – it integrates tightly with CUDA and cuDNN, and
even implements custom kernels to squeeze out every drop of performance. The
trade­off is that it is limited to NVIDIA ecosystems (GPUs or Jetson devices with
NVIDIA SoCs). Nevertheless, for embedded deployments that do use NVIDIA Jet­
son boards (which feature GPU and NVDLA cores), TensorRT is often the go­to
solution to deploy heavy models in real­time.

YOLO series and quantization
Since the YOLOv1 algorithm (“You Only Look Once”)[4] was proposed in 2016, the YOLO
series has undergone multiple generations of evolution, continuously improving the speed
and accuracy of target detection. The core idea of the YOLO series is to regard target
detection as a single end­to­end regression problem, using a neural network to directly
predict the bounding box position and category probability, which is simpler and more
efficient than the traditional two­stage detector[13]. Referring to [13], the following is a
brief summary of the key improvements of YOLOv8 and previous versions:
1. YOLOv1 (2016) was the first single­stage detector to predict bounding boxes and
class labels in one pass. It split the image into a grid and made predictions per cell,
offering real­time speed but struggling with small objects and precision.
2. YOLOv2 (2017) improved speed and accuracy by adding batch normalization, using
anchor boxes, and applying multi­scale training and a passthrough layer to better de­
tect small objects. It reached 78.6% mAP on VOC2007, up from 63.4% in YOLOv1.
It also introduced YOLO9000, which could detect over 9000 object classes by train­
ing on both detection and classification datasets.
3. YOLOv3 (2018) made the network deeper with Darknet­53 and used residual con­

logistic regression for class prediction, which allowed overlapping class labels and

4. YOLOv4 (2020) added the CSPDarknet­53 backbone, new activation functions, and

2.5.1
Object Detection Metrics, and NMS
Evaluation metrics for object detection revolve around the notions of precision, recall, and
the Intersection over Union (IoU) overlap. Precision is the fraction of predicted detections
that are correct (i.e., the model’s positive predictive value), whereas recall is the fraction
of true objects that are successfully detected by the model. There is an inherent trade­
off: a detector that fires on more proposals may catch more true objects (higher recall)
but also produce more false alarms (lower precision). To summarize performance across
this precision–recall spectrum, detectors are evaluated by Average Precision (AP). AP is
computed as the area under the precision–recall curve for a given class, often averaged
over multiple IoU thresholds to ensure localization quality (as per the COCO evaluation
protocol). The mean AP (mAP) averages the AP across all object classes, yielding a
single­number metric for overall detection performance. A higher mAP indicates a better
detector, with the COCO dataset setting challenging benchmarks. For example, YOLOv7
achieved an AP50 of 56.8% on COCO test­dev while running at 30 FPS, exemplifying the
balance of accuracy and speed in modern detectors[55].

2.5.2
YOLO Architecture and Quantization Challenges
The YOLO series employs complex CNN architectures to achieve high detection accu­
racy, which in turn poses challenges for model quantization. YOLO models typically con­
sist of dozens of convolutional layers with batch normalization and nonlinear activations
(Leaky ReLU or SiLU) in the backbone, layers with residual connections (e.g. Darknet
residual blocks or CSP modules), and multi­scale feature fusion in the neck (upsampling
and concatenation of feature maps). While these components improve accuracy, they
create difficulties for low­bit quantization[55].
One issue is the high dynamic range in feature maps: activation values can vary widely,
especially after aggressive nonlinearities and merging from different scales. For instance,
researchers have observed that activation distributions in YOLO can be extremely imbal­
anced – a large concentration of near­zero values but a long tail of large positive val­
ues[55]. This imbalance means that a straightforward min–max quantization (which allo­
cates equal range to the tail) wastes many levels on rarely occurring outliers, effectively
reducing precision for the bulk of the data. As a result, naive post­training quantization of
YOLO often incurs severe performance degradation, since important details in the feature
maps get lost or distorted.
Another challenge stems from structural elements like residual addends and feature con­
catenations. In a residual block, a quantized layer’s output must be added to a skip­
connection path output; if their scales are not perfectly aligned, the sum can be inaccurate.
Quantization noise on one branch can propagate and amplify through the skip connection.
Similarly, YOLO’s PANet­like neck concatenates feature maps from different resolutions
(after upsampling), which may have very different value distributions. Quantizing them
with a single scale or with mismatched scales can lead to one feature dominating the
other or to precision loss in one of the concatenated parts.
Moreover, YOLO’s detection head predicts coordinates and class confidences that are
sensitive to even slight shifts. A small quantization error in the box regression output
can significantly drop IoU and thus AP. All these factors – large model depth, residual
merges, multi­scale ops, and sensitive output – mean that applying low­bit quantization
to YOLO is more challenging than to a straightforward sequential network (like plain classi­
fication CNNs). Overcoming these challenges requires advanced quantization strategies
that preserve the critical information flow in YOLO’s architecture despite reduced numeric
precision.
2.5.3
Quantization Approaches for YOLO
Due to the above challenges, specialized methods have been proposed to successfully
quantize YOLO models while maintaining accuracy. One recent PTQ approach is Q­
YOLO (2023 by Wang et al.)[55], which introduces a Unilateral Histogram (UH) based
activation quantization scheme. The idea is to determine the optimal clipping thresholds
for activations by analyzing their distribution. Instead of naively using min/max values
(which, as noted, are skewed by outliers), Q­YOLO computes activation histograms and
chooses the truncation cut­off that minimizes the quantization error (e.g., by minimizing

==PAGE:31==
MSE between original and quantized activation distributions). In essence, it “ignores” the
extreme tail of activations if it rarely occurs, thereby using the quantization dynamic range
more effectively for the bulk of activation values. Q­YOLO provides a fully end­to­end
PTQ pipeline with this histogram­based calibration, and it was shown to outperform other
PTQ methods on COCO, achieving a better balance between accuracy and efficiency. By
mitigating the activation imbalance problem, Q­YOLO can reach higher mAP under INT8
(and even INT4 in some layers) than conventional PTQ approaches, without needing to
modify or retrain the original YOLO weights.
On the other hand, regarding QAT, MPQ­YOLO (2023 by Liu et al.)[56] is a QAT­based
framework that uses mixed­precision quantization tailored to YOLO’s architecture. Specif­
ically, MPQ­YOLO quantizes the backbone layers to an ultra­low 1­bit precision (binary
weights/activations) while keeping the detection head at 4­bit precision. This allocation
recognizes that earlier layers (backbone) can tolerate more quantization error if carefully
trained, whereas later layers (especially the head predicting boxes and classes) require
higher precision for accuracy. To train such a model end­to­end, MPQ­YOLO introduces
a trainable scaling factor between the binary backbone and the 4­bit head, ensuring the
feature magnitudes from the two parts match during forward propagation. It also uses
a Progressive Network Quantization (PNQ) strategy: the model is gradually quantized
during training to avoid sudden shocks that could destabilize learning. This progressive
fine­tuning mitigates oscillations in loss that often occur with mixed precision training, lead­
ing to smoother convergence. Empirically, MPQ­YOLO achieved an impressive trade­off:
on COCO, its fully quantized model (1­bit/4­bit) had <8% mAP drop compared to the full­
precision model, while reducing computation by 78% and model size by 54%. Moreover,
against a recent 4­bit­only method (Q­YOLO), MPQ­YOLO improved detection accuracy
by 5.5% (absolute) and further cut computation by 43%. To our knowledge, MPQ­YOLO
is the first to push YOLO to such low bit­widths with minimal accuracy loss, thanks to its
careful mix of precision and specialized training techniques.
These advances in Q­YOLO and MPQ­YOLO underscore that with appropriate quantiza­
tion schemes—whether smart post­training calibration or custom training regimes—YOLO
models can indeed be compressed significantly without sacrificing their strong object de­
tection performance. The insights from these works inform our quantization strategy for
YOLOv8 in this paper.
2.6
Existing quantization research and its shortcomings
Numerous studies have explored network quantization as a means to compress models
and speed up inference, yielding a variety of approaches. However, a key issue is that
many early quantization studies focused on classification networks and did not directly
generalize to detectors. As mentioned earlier, due to the amount of floating­point opera­
tions, quantization of object detection tasks may be more difficult than classification tasks.
This applies quantization to object detection models, which poses additional challenges
and exposes the shortcomings of previous research.
As for the related quantization research on object detection, most PTQ approaches eval­
uated only the backbone and neck portions of detectors, avoiding the quantization of de­
tection heads (the classification and regression layers) due to their sensitivity [22]. This
is problematic because detection heads contribute a substantial fraction of the compu­
tation and nearly half of the memory footprint in typical one­stage and two­stage detec­
tors [22]. Directly applying standard PTQ on a localization head often causes severe
drops in mean Average Precision (mAP), especially at low bit­widths [22]. To address
this, some works have designed specialized quantization techniques for detection. For
Exploring quantization techniques for object detection on embedded devices
23
==PAGE:32==
example, regression­aware calibration schemes have been introduced to fine­tune quanti­
zation scales for bounding­box regressors, rather than using classification­based criteria
that prove suboptimal for localization tasks [22]. Similarly, researchers have proposed
QAT modifications tailored to detection: Huang et al. observe that the multi­task nature of
object detectors (distinct classification vs. regression objectives) can lead to task inhar­
mony under quantization, where QAT disproportionately hurts one task’s accuracy [57].
Their Harmonious Quantization framework adds task­balanced loss terms during QAT to
align the two objectives, resulting in a 4­bit quantized detector that actually matches or
exceeds the mAP of the full­precision model on COCO [57]. These efforts highlight that
additional algorithmic innovation (e.g. better loss functions, calibrated quantizers) is re­
quired to quantize detectors successfully, beyond what was sufficient for classification
models. At the same time, these studies are still mainly focused on accuracy and com­
pression ratio at the model level, and there is a lack of comprehensive evaluation for real
deployment, especially in different embedded environments. This is the next point to be
discussed.
In general, the evaluation focus in quantization research has skewed toward accuracy
metrics and theoretical efficiency, with less emphasis on real­world deployment impact.
Many state­of­the­art papers report impressively small drops in mAP after quantization
and substantial reductions in model size or arithmetic operations. While these results
demonstrate the potential of quantization, they do not always translate to proportional
gains in deployed systems. In fact, researchers have noted that designing ultra low­
bit networks can yield “unverifiable efficiency on common hardware”[58] – i.e. the hard­
ware or inference engines may not support the novel quantization scheme, or the actual
speedup is limited by other bottlenecks. Most academic works evaluate quantized mod­
els on benchmarks in terms of accuracy, but omit detailed measurements of inference la­
tency, throughput, or memory bandwidth on real devices. As a result, crucial deployment­
centric factors are often under­reported. For instance, 8­bit integer arithmetic can theo­
retically offer an order­of­magnitude energy reduction per operation compared to 32­bit
floating point[58], yet the system­level speedups observed are sometimes far smaller due
to overhead in memory access and partial quantization of the network. Platform compat­
ibility is another concern rarely addressed in depth: a custom quantization algorithm may
require support beyond standard runtime libraries (TensorRT, ONNX Runtime, TFLite),
making it hard to deploy despite academic success. In summary, the prior art on model
quantization, especially for object detection, has largely stressed accuracy recovery and
bit­width reduction, while overlooking many practical deployment aspects. There is a clear
need for research that bridges this gap by examining not only quantization algorithms but
also their end­to­end performance in real embedded settings.

3.2.1
PyTorch native quantization
In this work, we employ PyTorch’s native quantization capabilities (augmented by NVIDIA’s
pytorch_quantization toolkit) to transform the full­precision model into an INT8 quantized
model suitable for efficient inference. The overall workflow consists of a post­training
quantization (PTQ) phase for static calibration and an optional quantization­aware training
(QAT) phase for fine­tuning, followed by exporting the quantized model to ONNX format.
This approach allows us to insert fake­quantization modules into the network and adjust
the model’s parameters to mitigate quantization error, all within the PyTorch environment,
before deploying the model to inference engines.
1. Module Replacement and Initialization: The first step is to replace standard neu­
ral network layers with their quantized counterparts provided by the toolkit. The py­
torch_quantization library supplies drop­in quantized versions of common modules
such as convolutions and linear layers (QuantConv2d and QuantLinear) [59]. We in­
voke the toolkit’s initialization routine to globally enable quantization in the model ar­
chitecture (automatically substituting all eligible torch.nn layers with quant_nn mod­
ules) [59]. This modular replacement augments each layer with associated Ten­
sorQuantizer sub­modules that will handle quantizing inputs and weights. At this
stage, quantization is configured but not yet activated—each quantizer can be tog­
gled between calibration mode, fake­quantization (quantize/dequantize) mode, or
full precision passthrough as needed.
2. PTQ: For PTQ, the quantized model is next subjected to a calibration process using
a representative dataset (without any weight updates). All quantizer modules are
first put into calibration mode, meaning they record activation statistics instead of
altering values [59]. We then execute inference on a stream of calibration data, al­
lowing each TensorQuantizer to collect distribution information (e.g. min/max or his­
togram of tensor values). After this calibration run, the recorded statistics are used
to determine the optimal quantization parameters, and then adopt the selected cali­
bration method to compute the optimal scaling factor (amax) for each quantizer [59].
Once the load_calib_amax() function is called on each quantizer to load these cal­
ibrated scales, the quantizers are fixed to use these parameters and are switched
into quantize/dequantize mode for subsequent operations [59]. This effectively fi­
nalizes the PTQ stage: the model’s weights and activations will now be dynamically
quantized to 8­bit during inference using the calibrated ranges.
3. QAT: Following PTQ, we optionally perform QAT to further improve the model’s ac­
curacy under quantization. In QAT, the model (with fixed quantization parameters
from calibration) is fine­tuned using standard supervised learning, but with quantiza­
tion simulated in the forward pass. But importantly the forward passes now include
fake­quantization: each layer’s weights and activations are quantized to 8­bit dur­
ing the forward propagation and de­quantized for backpropagation. This means the
network learns with quantization in the loop, adjusting its parameters to minimize
the loss under quantized conditions. Specifically, during QAT the Quantized lay­
ers apply fake quantization (via inserted Quantize/Dequantize operations) to activa­
tions and gradients are approximated with the straight­through estimator, making the
training “aware” of quantization effects. According to a best practice recommenda­
28
Exploring quantization techniques for object detection on embedded devices
==PAGE:37==
tion for maintaining training stability, we would be better off fine­tuning for a relatively
short period of time(e.g. on the order of 10% of the original training epochs) and use
a reduced learning rate (starting at about 1% of the initial rate), following a schedule
that decays the learning rate over the fine­tuning period [59]. This slow, modest
retraining allows the network weights to adjust to the quantization­induced pertur­
bations while preserving prior learned knowledge. Through this QAT supervision
process, the model incrementally adapts to the quantized representation, typically
yielding better accuracy than using PTQ calibration alone.
4. ONNX Export: After obtaining a calibrated (and possibly QAT­fine­tuned) quan­
tized PyTorch model, we convert it to an ONNX model for deployment. The export
logic ensures that all quantization operations are correctly preserved in the exported
graph. To this end, we enable the toolkit’s ONNX export mode prior to invocation
of PyTorch’s ONNX exporter [59]. This mode activates the use of standard fake­
quantize flag (use_fb_fake_quant)within the TensorQuantizer modules so that upon
export, each quantizer is translated into a pair of ONNX QuantizeLinear/Dequantize­
Linear nodes with the calibrated scale (and zero­point) parameters embedded [59].
We then call the torch.onnx.export function on the quantized model (with a represen­
tative dummy input tensor) to produce the ONNX graph. The resulting ONNX model
thus contains explicit Q/DQ nodes around quantized layers, faithfully representing
the INT8 quantization as done in PyTorch. This exported quantized ONNX model
can later be run using an ONNX runtime or further converted to a TensorRT engine
for high­performance inference, as illustrated in the workflow figure above. This
PyTorch­native approach thus provides a complete quantization workflow – from
calibration to QAT fine­tuning to export – using a unified framework and codebase
within PyTorch.
3.2.2
ONNX Runtime quantization
ONNX Runtime provides a quantization toolkit to convert trained models into an 8­bit in­
teger format for faster inference. It supports both dynamic and static quantization modes:
in dynamic quantization, the scale and zero­point for activations are computed on­the­fly
during each inference (incurring slight runtime overhead but potentially higher accuracy),
whereas static quantization pre­computes these parameters offline using a representa­
tive calibration dataset [51]. In this work, we focus on static quantization for deploying
the YOLOv8 detector. Static quantization fixes the quantization parameters (scales and
zero­points) for all future inferences by analyzing the model’s activations on sample in­
puts [51].
1. Calibration Procedure: Static quantization begins with a calibration step, in which
the float32 model is run on a set of representative images to gather statistics on
its internal activations. ONNX Runtime offers multiple calibration algorithms to de­
termine the optimal quantization parameters from these statistics, including simple
range­based extremes (MinMax) and more advanced methods like KL­divergence
(Entropy) or percentile calibration [51]. In our implementation, we varied the calibra­
tion dataset and method to observe their effects on the quantized model’s accuracy;
however, detailed comparisons of these methods are deferred to the results chapter.
2. Model Pre­processing: Before quantizing the model, we applied ONNX Runtime’s
preprocessing tool to optimize the YOLOv8 ONNX graph. This pre­processing (in­
voked via the onnxruntime.quantization.preprocess module) performs several graph
transformations and shape inferences to prepare the model for INT8 conversion [51].
For example, a Convolution node followed by a BatchNormalization can be fused
Exploring quantization techniques for object detection on embedded devices
29
==PAGE:38==
into a single op during this optimization; a fused Conv+BN is quantized as one unit,
whereas an unfused BatchNormalization on its own cannot be quantized [51]. Ac­
cording to the official statement, by running these optimizations ahead of time, we
ensure the quantization process can target all applicable layers. It is recommended
to carry out such graph optimizations in a preprocessing stage (rather than dur­
ing the quantization call itself) to maintain a clearer correspondence between the
original and quantized model graphs [51]. In practice, we obtained a streamlined
YOLOv8 model (preprocessed.onnx) with all shapes inferred and key operations
fused, which then served as the input to the quantization routine.
3. Calibration Data Reader: With the optimized model in hand, the next step is sup­
plying calibration inputs. ONNX Runtime’s API expects a CalibrationDataReader
implementation to feed the calibration data to the model [51]. We implemented a
CalibrationDataReader to iterate over our collection of calibration images. For each
image, the reader loads the file and applies the same preprocessing pipeline used
during normal inference (resizing the image to 640×640 pixels, normalizing pixel val­
ues to [0,1], and converting the image to a 1 × 3 × 640 × 640 tensor in CHW format).
The preprocessed tensor is then yielded in a dictionary with the model’s input name
(e.g. {”images”: input_tensor}) as expected by ONNX Runtime. This ensures the
calibration dataset faithfully represents the model’s typical input distribution. During
calibration, ONNX Runtime invokes the data reader to feed each sample through
the float32 model and collects activation value ranges or histograms as required by
the chosen calibration method.
4. Quantization Configuration: After calibration data collection, the core quantization
is performed using the onnxruntime.quantization.quantize_static() function. This is
a very powerful function that is well encapsulated and leaves a large number of
functional option interfaces. In our workflow, we call quantize_static with the pre­
processed YOLOv8 model and CalibrationDataReader, along with specific options
to configure 8­bit conversion. We use the quantization format QDQ (Quantize/De­
quantize), specified by quant_format=QuantFormat.QDQ, which inserts Quantize­
Linear/DequantizeLinear operator pairs around tensors to simulate quantization in
the ONNX graph [51]. The quantization­aware training (QAT) model exported from
PyTorch in the previous section is also in QDQ format. Regarding the choice of
data type, the official documentation says that for low­end ARM64, U8S8 (activa­
tion: uint8, weight: int8), may be faster than U8U8, and there is no difference in
accuracy [51]. For high­end ARM64, there is no difference, and we will test it in
subsequent tests. In the function implementation, all convolution weights are quan­
tized to signed 8­bit integers (weight_type=QuantType.QInt8), and activations use
unsigned 8­bits (activation_type=QuantType.QUInt8). We left per­channel quanti­
zation abled in our runs (per_channel=True), meaning each weight tensor uses a
single scale per output channel(as opposed to one scale for all channels). Addition­
ally, we enabled the reduce_range=True option, which restricts the quantizer to a
slightly narrower range for activations and weights. This reduced range (effectively
7­bit precision for the most significant bit) helps mitigate saturation issues on certain
hardware instructions and can improve accuracy in some cases.

TensorRT quantization
The following focuses on the steps of the TensorRT engine construction phase:
1. Model import and network parsing: The trained model is imported into TensorRT
and a network definition is generated. Model files are usually loaded using a parser
provided by TensorRT (e.g. ONNX Parser), which parses the model structure into
an INetworkDefinition network object. This process creates nodes and tensors for
the computational graph, which represent the structure of the original model in Ten­
sorRT.
2. Create a Builder configuration and set optimization parameters: Use IBuilder
to create a IBuilderConfig configuration object and set optimization options and re­
source limits. For example, configure the maximum batch size, workspace memory
size, etc., to guide the TensorRT optimizer in searching for the optimal combina­
tion of inference kernels. At this stage, set the precision options according to de­
ployment requirements, that is, determine whether to enable FP16 or INT8 mode
through EngineOptions.precision and set the corresponding flag on BuilderConfig
(e.g. config­>setFlag(BuilderFlag::kFP16) or kINT8). If the target precision is FP32
(default), no low­precision flag is set and the model is built in single precision.
3. INT8 calibration data preparation (optional): When you choose to build the INT8
engine and the model itself does not contain quantization information, you need to
specify the dataset for calibration and configure the calibrator. In the C++ implemen­
tation, a custom calibrator is written by deriving from the IInt8Calibrator interface,
and representative input data is provided to TensorRT. When the engine is built,
TensorRT calls the calibrator’s callback function to read the data, perform forward
propagation to collect statistics on the tensor, and generate quantization scaling fac­
tors. Once complete, the calibration results are stored in a cache file and loaded for
subsequent engine builds or inference.
4. Build TensorRT Engine: Call TensorRT Builder to build an optimized inference
engine. This step will synthesize the previously configured network definition, opti­
mization parameters, and precision requirements to graphically optimize the model
and select the kernel. If INT8 precision is used, during the build process TensorRT
will apply the calibrated scaling factor to convert the model weights and some ac­
tivations to INT8 representations to generate a quantized engine. The engine can
be built using builder­>buildEngineWithConfig(network, config) to obtain an ICud­
aEngine object. The built engine contains all the optimized computational graphs of
the model and the corresponding weight data in memory.
5. Serialize and save the engine: The built TensorRT engine is serialized into a bi­
nary stream and saved to a file for direct loading and use during deployment. The
serialized engine is usually saved with the .engine extension. It should be noted
that engine files are not interchangeable between different GPU architectures or
TensorRT versions. The serialized engine can usually only be deserialized and
used under the same environment. Therefore, when saving the engine, you should
specify the applicable hardware and TensorRT version.

Once the optimized TensorRT engine has been built and serialized, the final step is to de­
ploy it on the target system and perform real­time inference. This deployment procedure
typically involves the following steps:
1. Engine Deserialization
• Loading the Engine File: The serialized TensorRT engine (commonly stored
as a *.engine file) is loaded from disk.
• Creating the Runtime: A new instance of the TensorRT IRuntime is instantiated,
which is responsible for deserializing the engine.
• Restoring the ICudaEngine: The engine is deserialized back into an in­memory
ICudaEngine object. This object encapsulates the optimized inference graph,
weights, and other metadata necessary for computation. The ICudaEngine is
device­specific; typically, it must be run on the same type of GPU (and the
same version of TensorRT) for which it was originally built.
2. Execution Context Creation
• Constructing an IExecutionContext: From the deserialized ICudaEngine, the
application creates an IExecutionContext. This context maintains all state needed
to run inference, including layer bindings, workspace buffers, and intermediate
results. If dynamic shapes or batch sizes are used, the context ensures shapes
are properly specified before execution.
• Allocating Host and Device Buffers: The total number of I/O bindings is de­
termined by the engine. For each binding, the application allocates buffers in
CPU (host) memory and GPU (device) memory. These buffers store input data
and output predictions, enabling transfer between host and device.
3. Preprocessing Inputs
• Data Preparation: Input images or other data must be resized, normalized,
or otherwise preprocessed to match the network’s expected input format. For
instance, images might be converted to NCHW layout, cast to float32, or quan­
tized to int8.
• Copying Data to Device: Once preprocessed on the CPU side, the input ten­
sors are copied to the GPU buffers (using cudaMemcpyAsync or a similar API).
Throughout this process, the host must ensure that the memory layout and data
types precisely match those anticipated by the TensorRT engine.
4. Inference Execution
• Launching the Network: With all input buffers in place, the application invokes
executeV2 (or an equivalent method, depending on the API version) on the
IExecutionContext. This call schedules GPU kernels to compute forward prop­
agation through the optimized network, using the designated precision (FP32,
FP16, or INT8).
• Asynchronous Streams: In many cases, inference is executed asynchronously
on a particular CUDA stream. The host can perform other tasks, or launch
additional work on different streams, while the GPU processes the model in
parallel. This design facilitates pipelined execution and high throughput.
5. Collecting and Post­processing Outputs

• Reading Back Results: After the GPU finishes the forward pass, output tensors
are transferred from device memory back to host memory if further CPU­based
post­processing is required. Commonly, host­side code uses cudaMemcpy (or
an asynchronous variant) to copy the results.
• Interpreting Predictions: Depending on the model type, the application then
decodes bounding boxes (for object detection), applies non­maximum sup­

In the event that the model to be deployed has been quantized already (e.g. a Q/DQ

model. Since the quantization step has been completed during the training/offline stage,
the TensorRT build engine does not need to be calibrated again, and its role is purely a

them into an efficient inference engine. This division of responsibilities allows the quanti­
zation results from the training phase to be fully utilized while maintaining model accuracy,
and simplifies the deployment phase.

4.2.1
Calibration Datasets and method
PTQ is highly dependent on the calibration process. The calibration dataset should be
selected to closely match the data distribution of the model’s actual application scenario.
If calibration is performed using overly simple or single data, it may lead to inaccurate esti­
mation of the quantization range, which in turn may lead to poor performance on real data.
At the same time, different calibration algorithms also affect accuracy, their effectiveness
depends significantly on the statistical distribution captured during calibration.
• Calibrator: Different calibration algorithms react differently to the calibration dataset
and its parameters. As discussed in Section 2.1.4, the MinMax calibration method
simply finds the absolute minimum and maximum values of the activation values in
the calibration data and sets them as the quantization range. Although simple and
intuitive, it is highly sensitive to outliers, while entropy calibration suppresses outliers
by sacrificing a small portion of the distribution tail, thereby achieving better accuracy
on most of the data. There is no single calibration indicator that is applicable to all
situations. Only the correct use of calibration methods can better adapt to specific
situations.
• Dataset Size: NVIDIA’s TensorRT toolkit suggests 500 images as a rule of thumb [19].
This helps ensure all feature layers see a representative range of activations. More
data generally yields higher and more stable accuracy, with diminishing returns past

a certain point [61]. It’s often recommended to sample images from across the train­

• Calibration batch size: Calibrating batch sizes may affect the accuracy of activa­
tion statistics. We tested the YOLOv8n model and performed a forward pass on a
subset of 32 images randomly sampled from the COCO 2017 validation set. We
extracted the output activation of the first convolutional layer in the backbone. By
registering a hook, we recorded the maximum activation value for each image in the

poor quantification range.

4.2.2
Partial Quantization
Deep neural network layers do not all respond equally to low­bit quantization – some lay­
ers can be heavily quantized with minimal impact, while others are extremely sensitive.
In a model like YOLOv8, certain critical layers (for example, the initial convolution that
ingests raw input, or the output prediction heads) tend to be more vulnerable to quanti­
zation error. Quantizing these sensitive layers (e.g. converting their weights/activations
to INT8) can cause a disproportionate drop in accuracy, whereas many intermediate lay­
ers tolerate INT8 with negligible effect. Best practices therefore recommend performing
a layer­wise analysis to pinpoint any “problematic” layers if a fully quantized model’s ac­
curacy degrades severely [18]. Research has shown that often a single layer or a small
36
Exploring quantization techniques for object detection on embedded devices
==PAGE:45==
subset of layers can account for most of the accuracy loss under quantization [62]. For
instance, one study found that in a ResNet network, almost all the mAP degradation from
8­bit post­training quantization was attributable to just one layer – and addressing that
layer alone nearly eliminated the drop [62]. This insight motivates a partial quantization
(mixed­precision) strategy: use lower bit­width (INT8) for the majority of the network, but
preserve higher precision (FP16 or FP32) for the few highly sensitive layers.
To determine which layers are most sensitive to quantization, we perform a systematic
layer­wise sensitivity analysis. The core idea is to evaluate the model’s accuracy when
one specific layer is left unquantized (high­precision) and all others remain quantized,
and repeat this for each candidate layer. By observing how the mean Average Precision
(mAP) changes with each layer in float vs. int8, we can gauge that layer’s importance to
overall accuracy. In practice, the procedure is as follows:
1. Baseline measurement: Quantize the entire model (all quantizable layers to INT8)
and evaluate its accuracy on the validation set to get a baseline mAP.
2. Layer­wise tests: For each quantizable layer L in the model (one at a time):
(a) Temporarily disable quantization for layer L (i.e. run layer L in higher precision,
such as FP32 or FP16, while keeping all other layers at INT8).
(b) Evaluate the model on the validation data and record the resulting mAP (call it
mAP_L).
(c) Re­enable quantization for layer L (restore it to INT8) before moving to the next
layer, to ensure each test is isolated.
3. Sensitivity logging: Note the difference in accuracy with and without quantizing
each layer (e.g. ΔmAP = mAP_L – baseline_mAP). Layers that show a large positive
ΔmAP when left in high precision are the most sensitive (their quantization had the
biggest negative impact on accuracy).
4. Ranking: Rank the layers by their sensitivity (for example, by the drop in mAP
incurred when the layer is quantized). This ranking highlights which layers are the
top candidates to keep at higher precision in a mixed­precision scheme.

Deployment optimization
4.3.1
Selection of the quantization granularity
As discussed in Section 2.1.5￿in deployment optimization, the choice of quantization
granularity has a key impact on the balance between model accuracy and inference effi­
ciency. Experiments show that different strategies are required for the quantization of ac­
tivation values and weights: for activation values, per­tensor quantization is usually used,
while for weights, per­channel quantization is preferred. This conclusion is derived from
multiple sets of comparative experiments[19]. For example, different channels of weights
may show significant differences in numerical distribution. Per­channel quantization can
significantly reduce quantization errors by independently calibrating the scale factor and
zero point for each channel[17]. The distribution of activation values is relatively consistent
between different channels. Per­tensor quantization can maintain accuracy, simplify the
computational graph structure, and improve hardware inference efficiency. NVIDIA Ten­
sorRT has adopted this strategy by default: activation values use per­tensor and weights
use per­channel[19]. Its official documentation points out that this design is based on
a large number of end­to­end test results, especially in convolutional neural networks,
showing the best accuracy­latency trade­off. In contrast, other deployment frameworks
(such as some mobile SDKs) may not provide a default quantization strategy, requir­
ing developers to manually adjust parameters, increasing deployment complexity[14]. In
summary, the choice of quantization granularity needs to be combined with data charac­
teristics and hardware constraints, and the default strategy of industrial­grade tool chains
often condenses practical experience and system­level optimization. In our experiment,
we compared the two during our experiment to verify this conclusion.

4.3.3
pre­processing and post­processing
Preprocessing and postprocessing are critical components in the deployment of quantized
object detection models. While the backbone and head of the model are typically accel­
erated through TensorRT or other hardware­specific optimizations, the surrounding data
handling stages often become performance bottlenecks or sources of accuracy degra­
dation if not properly aligned with the model’s training configuration. In our deployment
pipeline, we carefully evaluated and compared different implementations of preprocessing
and postprocessing strategies across multiple methods.
• Preprocessing Strategies: Since the input data source is not fixed, preprocessing
is essential to ensure that the input data meets the model requirements. The pre­
processing step involves resizing input images, performing color space conversion,
normalization, and data format transformation (e.g., HWC to CHW). A crucial as­
pect is how the image is resized to match the model’s input size: this can be done
either via direct resizing (which distorts the aspect ratio) or through letterbox (which
maintains the aspect ratio by padding the image).
A direct cv::resize warp will fill the network’s width and height but distorts the image
if the aspect ratio doesn’t match, potentially degrading detection accuracy (objects
appear stretched or squashed). Letterbox resizing avoids distortion by scaling the
image until it fits and padding the remaining border (often with gray/black pixels).
This preserves object shape – the center image in a comparison retains correct
aspect ratio with padding added, unlike a simple stretch resize. We conducted tests
and found that using the Letterbox method on yolov8n resulted in approximately
10% higher accuracy than direct resizing. However, it should be noted that the letter
box will introduce a fill offset, and the model’s output (bounding box coordinates) is
relative to the filled and scaled image, requiring correct coordinate restoration in
post­processing.
• Postprocessing Considerations: The main focus of post­processing is NMS, which
is divided into two categories based on the processing method: Class­Wise and
Class­agnostic. After the network predicts many candidate boxes, NMS is applied
to filter out redundant detections. Class­wise NMS (the default in most detectors)
means you perform NMS separately per object class – overlapping boxes are only
suppressed if they predict the same class. This way, a person and a bicycle over­
lapping won’t eliminate each other. Class­agnostic NMS ignores class labels dur­
ing suppression, merging any overlapping boxes regardless of class [67].
This
can remove duplicate detections when the model double­detects one object as two
classes, but it risks removing legitimate multi­class overlaps. For example, a per­
son and a bicycle close together may have a high IoU, and class­agnostic NMS will
delete the one with lower confidence, resulting in a false negative.
Class­agnostic NMS is usually optional (e.g. an agnostic_nms=True flag in YOLO)
for specialized cases [67]. Generally, class­wise NMS is safer for accuracy, while
class­agnostic might be beneficial if you expect highly overlapping classes or want
maximum de­duplication [67]. In our tests, we found that class­wise methods per­
40
Exploring quantization techniques for object detection on embedded devices
==PAGE:49==
form better in terms of final accuracy on COCO. Performance­wise, unified NMS
does one pass over all boxes, whereas class­wise does a pass per class. However,
since the total number of comparisons is similar, the runtime difference is minor.
• Deployment Implementation Options: The division of labor between CPU and
GPU for preprocessing and postprocessing can greatly affect performance. The
simplest and common implementation is to do preprocessing (resizing, color con­
version, etc.) on the CPU, feed the image to the GPU for inference, and later the
output tensors sent back to CPU for NMS and drawing results. However, by apply­
ing different methods, we found that there is one aspect that greatly affects overall
performance: the implementation of NMS, with differences of up to several times in
post­processing for each image. There are several ways to implement NMS:
– Implemented in pure Python using NumPy to calculate multiple detection boxes.
However, during post­processing, the pure Python code is mostly executed in a
single thread, unable to utilize multi­core CPUs or GPU parallel computing ca­
pabilities. This results in testing taking several dozen milliseconds per image,
which even exceeds the inference time.
– Use the OpenCV library function cv2.dnn.NMSBoxes, but note that this function
does not distinguish between categories, so we need to call it multiple times
to separate different categories. At the same time, this function is CPU­based,
so its performance may not be as good as GPU­supported methods, but it is
very simple to implement and has few dependencies.
– Customize CUDA kernels or use Torchvision library function torchvision.ops.nms.
torchvision.ops.nms is a pure CUDA implementation on the GPU, with more
frames offering greater advantages, and it can perform class­wise NMS in one
go, avoiding looping Python. In actual testing, single­processing performance
far exceeded pure Python code, but dependencies (Torch + Torchvision) had
to be introduced, and the storage requirements were much greater than those
of OpenCV on devices with limited storage. By writing custom CUDA kernels,
you can perform as much work as possible on the GPU to improve perfor­
mance. For example, if a CUDA operation for NMS is written, after inference
is complete, another CUDA kernel can continue to execute NMS or other filter­
ing operations on the GPU, sending only the final small dataset (bounding box
coordinates and category labels) back to the CPU. This approach minimizes
CPU–GPU transfers – ideally the image data might make a single trip to GPU
and never come back until results are ready – and it keeps the GPU busy with
parallel tasks that it excels at. However, it is clear that the disadvantage is
complexity, which results in high development costs.
This section highlights that even subtle inconsistencies in preprocessing or postprocessing—
such as missing padding correction, improper coordinate scaling, or mismatched NMS
strategies—can result in significant drops in mean Average Precision (mAP) during eval­
uation. The impact of each strategy was quantitatively evaluated in the experiment. In
subsequent comparative experiments, we will try to use more optimized methods and
annotate them.

Deploying deep learning models on embedded, resource­constrained devices (like NVIDIA
Jetson modules in robotics or autonomous systems) demands real­time, low­latency per­
formance and efficient use of limited resources. NVIDIA’s TensorRT library can accelerate

inference on these devices, and it offers both Python and C++ APIs. Both the Python and
C++ TensorRT APIs ultimately execute the same optimized C++ inference engine on the

and GIL (Global Interpreter Lock) constraints. NVIDIA engineers note that the Python API

A C++ application can be compiled into a single executable or library with minimal external
dependencies. This makes it easier to deploy on an embedded device (you don’t need
to ship a Python interpreter on the device). it creates more compact and faster runtime
code, and it’s already the language of choice for 95% of embedded system code[69].
The startup time can also be faster – the program launches and is ready to infer almost
immediately, whereas a Python program might take a moment to load the interpreter and
modules. For devices that boot and need instant AI functionality, this difference matters.
In contrast, the advantage of the Python API is the ease of integration with Python’s rich
ecosystem (NumPy data loaders, SciPy, etc.) and faster development/prototyping. This
can effectively reduce development costs. In the experiment, we tried two APIs to test
the performance of TensorRT, and the comparison results are discussed in the following

Conclusion
In this thesis, we presented a full pipeline for deploying a deep learning object detec­
tion model on resource­constrained embedded hardware by leveraging model quanti­
zation and optimization techniques. We selected a state­of­the­art one­stage detector
(YOLOv8n) as our case study due to its favorable accuracy­speed trade­off, and sys­
tematically applied quantization to reduce its computational and memory demands. The
workflow spanned from initial model analysis and quantization strategy design, through
implementation using multiple toolchains (PyTorch, ONNX Runtime, and TensorRT), to
final deployment on an NVIDIA Jetson AGX Xavier embedded device. Throughout this
process, we addressed key challenges in preserving detection accuracy while achieving
real­time performance, and we evaluated trade­offs between model efficiency and accu­
racy at each stage.
Summary of the Workflow: We began by analyzing the YOLOv8n model’s characteris­
tics and the target hardware’s capabilities, using a Roofline model analysis to under­
stand theoretical performance bounds. This informed our optimization focus by high­
lighting whether the model was compute­bound or memory­bound on the Jetson plat­
form.
Next, we explored three quantization and deployment workflows: (1) PyTorch­
native quantization, where INT8 quantization (post­training static calibration and optional
quantization­aware training) was performed within PyTorch (augmented by NVIDIA’s py­
torch_quantization toolkit) and the model was then exported; (2) ONNX Runtime quantiza­
tion, where the model was converted to ONNX format and quantized using ONNX’s toolkit,
with deployment via the ONNX Runtime on different execution providers; and (3) NVIDIA
TensorRT, where the model was converted directly into a highly optimized TensorRT en­
gine for INT8 inference. All three approaches start from the same trained model and aim
to produce an efficient INT8 deployment, but they involve different conversion steps and
tool­specific optimizations. We carefully calibrated the model using representative data
(COCO images) to determine proper quantization scales, experimenting with multiple cal­
ibration algorithms (e.g., min­max, percentile, MSE, entropy/KL) and varying the amount
of calibration data to study their impact on accuracy. For the TensorRT pipeline, we also
applied graph optimizations and builder settings (such as per­channel quantization for
weights) to maximize throughput. Once quantized models were obtained, we profiled
them and optimized the inference pipeline on the embedded device – this included con­
siderations like moving pre­processing and post­processing (image resizing, non­maxima
suppression) onto the GPU, using C++ for inference execution to avoid Python overhead,
and tweaking runtime settings (CUDA streams, batch sizes, etc.) to fully utilize the hard­
ware. By the end of the workflow, the quantized YOLOv8n model was successfully de­
ployed on the Jetson Xavier, and we conducted extensive experiments to measure its
accuracy and performance under various configurations.
Key Achievements and Findings: This work resulted in several notable technical contri­
butions:
• End­to­End Quantization Pipeline: We implemented an end­to­end pipeline that
compares and integrates multiple quantization frameworks. By evaluating PyTorch
vs. ONNX Runtime vs. TensorRT­based quantization, we demonstrated how each
toolchain affects the ease of deployment, achievable performance, and model accu­
racy. For instance, we showed that exporting a PyTorch model to ONNX and running
it with the ONNX Runtime (FP32) alone yielded about a 25% inference speedup over
Exploring quantization techniques for object detection on embedded devices
53
==PAGE:62==
the raw PyTorch model due to graph optimizations, illustrating the benefit of format
conversion and runtime choice. However, we also uncovered that ONNX’s built­in
INT8 quantization, without further graph clean­up, introduced extra memory copy
operations that negated its performance gains on GPU and even caused compati­
bility issues with TensorRT. Identifying and addressing these pitfalls (through graph
surgery tools to remove incompatible quantization patterns) was an important step
in our deployment process.
• Effective Post­Training Quantization (PTQ) for Object Detection: We demonstrated
that careful calibration is critical for successful PTQ on object detection models. Our
experiments quantified how calibration dataset size and algorithm choice influence
the final mAP. Using only a handful of images for calibration can lead to severe
accuracy degradation (e.g., with an entropy KL calibrator, a single calibration batch
yielded mAP below 0.20, far worse than other methods), whereas providing a more
representative dataset (e.g. 256 images) and a robust calibrator (MSE­based or
percentile) narrowed the accuracy gap. We achieved an INT8 PTQ accuracy of up
to 0.362 mAP (COCO 50:95) on YOLOv8n – which is within about 1 percentage point
of the FP32 baseline (0.373) – using a per­channel weight quantization and MSE
calibration strategy. This indicates that, even for complex detection networks, PTQ
can preserve accuracy to a high degree if done properly. We also found that simpler
calibration methods (like min­max or percentile) perform adequately when sufficient
calibration data is available, with differences under 0.5% mAP in our tests, whereas
more data­sensitive methods (like entropy) require larger sample sizes to converge
to similar accuracy. These findings provide practical guidance for applying PTQ to
detection models.
• Quantization­Aware Training (QAT) for Recovery of Accuracy: To further close the
gap between the quantized model and the full­precision model, we incorporated a
short QAT fine­tuning stage. By treating the original FP32 model as a teacher and
minimizing the layer­wise mean squared error between FP32 and INT8 feature maps
(a form of knowledge distillation), we were able to improve the quantized model’s
accuracy to 0.365 mAP, nearly reaching the original model’s performance. This 0.3
point gain over the best PTQ model demonstrates that QAT can successfully recover
some lost accuracy. However, we also highlight the cost: each epoch of QAT on the
COCO dataset took several hours on a GPU, whereas PTQ calibration completed
in minutes on a CPU. Thus, while QAT yielded a minor accuracy improvement (on
the order of 0.8% relative increase, achieving 98% of the baseline accuracy), it
comes at a significant computation and development time cost. In scenarios where
rapid deployment is needed or resources are limited, PTQ alone may be the prefer­
able trade­off. Our results underscore the classic accuracy­efficiency trade­off: QAT
provides the best accuracy for INT8 models at the expense of time and complexity,
whereas PTQ is much quicker and simpler with only a slight accuracy penalty.
• Layer­wise Sensitivity Analysis and Mixed­Precision Strategy: We conducted a de­
tailed sensitivity analysis to identify which parts of the YOLOv8 model are most
prone to quantization error. By enabling FP32 for one layer at a time in an otherwise
INT8 model, we measured the change in mAP for each layer. Most layers proved
relatively robust (<0.3% drop when quantized), but a few critical layers – notably
the detection head (output layer) and some early feature­extraction layers – caused
disproportionate accuracy drops when quantized. Guided by this analysis, we imple­
mented a mixed­precision quantization scheme: the top three most sensitive layers
were left in higher precision (FP16 or FP32) while all other layers remained INT8.
54

==PAGE:63==
This strategy (denoted as INT8 skip­3) recovered about 0.8 percentage points of
mAP (raising INT8 accuracy from 0.304 to 0.312 in our worst­case scenario with
native TensorRT PTQ) at the cost of a small increase in latency ( 1 ms per image).
While the partial INT8 model (0.312 mAP) was still significantly below the full ac­
curacy of FP32, it showed an improvement over the fully quantized model. More
importantly, the mixed configuration provided a useful compromise: it gained back
some accuracy without forfeiting all the performance benefits of INT8. This result
highlights that selectively not quantizing certain layers can mitigate accuracy loss,
and it suggests a direction for future automated mixed­precision methods that allo­
cate precision dynamically based on sensitivity (e.g., using FP16 for critical layers
and INT8 elsewhere).
• Optimized Embedded Deployment and Real­Time Performance: A major achieve­
ment of this work is that we successfully deployed the optimized YOLOv8n detec­
tor on the Jetson AGX Xavier and achieved real­time inference speeds. By using
TensorRT and a C++ inference pipeline, our INT8 model ran in as low as 7 ms per
640×640 image (approximately 143 FPS), and our FP16 model in about 9 ms per im­
age ( 111 FPS). For comparison, the original unquantized model running in PyTorch
(FP32) took around 28 ms per image ( 36 FPS) on the same device with Python,
and about 13 ms (77 FPS) using TensorRT FP32 in C++. Therefore, through quan­
tization and optimization, we obtained a 4× speedup over the naive FP32/Python
implementation and about 1.4–1.8× speedup over an optimized FP32 engine. No­
tably, the FP16 inference emerged as the sweet spot on this device – it delivered a
2× reduction in latency from FP32 with no loss in accuracy, fully utilizing the Jetson’s
NVIDIA Tensor Cores for acceleration. In contrast, moving to INT8 provided a further
22% reduction in latency (9 ms →7 ms) but incurred a substantial accuracy trade­
off (about 18% relative drop in mAP from 0.373 to 0.304). These results illustrate the
core trade­off: INT8 quantization offers diminishing returns on GPU when FP16 is
available, unless accuracy can be recovered or the INT8 execution overheads can
be reduced. Nonetheless, even the fully quantized INT8 model maintained reason­
able detection performance and would be acceptable for certain applications where
speed or resource usage is absolutely paramount and some accuracy loss is tolera­
ble. Additionally, we found that choosing the right deployment strategy (runtime and
language) was crucial: executing the TensorRT engine in C++ (native) avoided the
15 ms/frame overhead of Python bindings, and ensuring that pre­processing (image
resize/normalize) and post­processing (NMS) were done on the GPU (or with highly
optimized libraries) prevented the CPU from becoming a bottleneck. In our tests, a
naive pipeline that offloaded those tasks to the CPU roughly doubled the end­to­end
inference time. By contrast, our optimized pipeline kept the CPU utilization modest
(around 35–45% on the 8­core CPU during INT8 inference) and GPU utilization at
a healthy level, thus meeting the strict real­time latency budget (<10 ms per frame)
required for high­FPS applications such as autonomous systems and video analyt­
ics on the edge. Overall, this work demonstrates that with careful quantization and
system­level optimizations, modern object detectors can indeed be deployed on em­
bedded GPUs in real time, bridging some of the gap between advanced algorithms
and edge hardware capabilities.
Reflection on Results: The experimental results provide valuable insights into the inter­
play between quantization, accuracy, and performance on embedded hardware. First,
we confirmed that quantization dramatically reduces model size and compute – the INT8
model is 4× smaller than the FP32 model and uses integer arithmetic, which is advan­

tageous for memory­limited scenarios and can enable deployment of models that other­
wise might not fit on device. On the Jetson platform, we observed that pure computation
throughput was not the limiting factor for INT8; instead, the overhead of frequent quantize­
dequantize operations and memory traffic became the bottleneck. This was evidenced

on average, indicating that the GPU was often waiting on memory transfers or synchro­

embedded GPUs, while supporting INT8, benefit greatly from fused operations and effi­
cient memory management when using low precision. We also demonstrated real­world
implications of software choices: using ONNX Runtime or TensorRT’s graph optimizations
yielded noticeable speedups, and writing performance­critical code in C++ (or using C++
TensorRT APIs) is necessary to unlock the full potential of the hardware. The accuracy
vs. speed trade­offs we quantified (e.g., an INT8 model running 20% faster than FP16
but with 7 mAP points lower accuracy) provide a concrete basis for decision­making in
deployments – depending on an application’s tolerance for error versus need for speed, a
practitioner can choose an appropriate precision or even a mixed approach. Importantly,
our results validate that for object detection, maintaining some higher precision in key
layers or resorting to half­precision might often be the optimal choice on GPUs like the
Xavier, whereas full INT8 quantization might shine more on CPU­only or DSP/NPU­based
systems where floating­point throughput is limited. In summary, the project achieved its
goal of delivering a quantized object detection model on an embedded device and offers
a nuanced understanding of how different quantization techniques perform in practice on
such hardware.
Future Work
• Improve INT8 efficiency: Continue to integrate Q/DQ operators, reduce memory
copies and Host–Device synchronization, or customize TensorRT plug­ins to achieve
high utilization of ”one quantization, one way INT8”.
• Lower bit width and mixed precision: Explore 4­bit or 4/8­bit mixed quantization,
and combine sensitivity analysis to automatically allocate FP16/FP32 to key layers,
taking into account both accuracy and speed.
• System­level verification and energy consumption optimization: Embed the model
into a complete application (such as a drone or edge camera), measure end­to­end
latency and power consumption; study adaptive inference strategies under hard­
ware features such as DVFS, NPU/DLA coprocessors, etc.
