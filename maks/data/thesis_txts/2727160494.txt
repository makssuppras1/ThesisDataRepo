Abstract 
Microbial interactions are essential to the dynamics and the functioning of microbial communities. 
However, despite technological advances, microbial interactions remain poorly understood. As we 
aspire to control and engineer microbiomes, we must increase our understanding of the complex 
interactions that happen within these microbial communities. Central to microbial interactions are the 
chemical signals secreted and sensed by community members. These chemical metabolites mediate 
the communication between microorganisms and shape microbial communities. Therefore, it is vital 
that we are able to detect and measure such metabolites.  
In this project the potential of whole-cell biosensors and genetic memory devices was explored. These 
living cells provide unique methods of detecting and recording chemical metabolites. We aimed to 
expand the catalog of available genetic biosensors and this was achieved through the successful 
cloning of three different types of whole-cell biosensors. These biosensors were capable of sensing 
and responding to the agriculturally relevant secondary metabolites tetracycline, naringenin and 
salicylate. The response performance of the biosensors was evaluated by investigating a series of 
performance metrics. The tetracycline biosensor named JJ001 displayed low leakiness, high dynamic 
range and high sensitivity to its inducer, and was thus considered highly successful. However, it was 
found that performance of the naringenin and salicylate biosensors was subpar. Consequently, genetic 
tuning strategies were devised and employed to improve their performance. This resulted in the 
construction of a new set of modified naringenin and salicylate biosensors. While initial 
characteristics of the new naringenin and salicylate biosensors looked promising, final conclusive 
evidence of their performance was not obtained due to time constraints. In conclusion, this study 
illustrates a strategy for the genetic construction, testing and optimization of novel whole-cell 
biosensors.

Resumé 
Mikrobielle interaktioner er afgørende for dynamikken og funktionaliteten af mikrobielle samfund. 
På trods af teknologiske fremskridt er vores forståelse af mikrobielle interaktioner stadig begrænset. 
Eftersom vi bestræber os på at kunne kontrollere og udvikle mikrobiomer, er vi nødt til at øge vores 
forståelse af de komplekse interaktioner, som foregår i disse mikrobielle samfund. En afgørende 
faktor for mikrobielle interaktioner er de kemiske signaler, der bliver udsendt og opfanget af det 
mikrobielle samfunds medlemmer. Disse kemiske metabolitter medierer kommunikationen mellem 
mikroorganismer og er med til at forme de mikrobielle samfund. Af den grund er det også essentielt, 
at vi har metoder til at detektere og måle disse metabolitter. 
I dette projekt blev potentialet af hel-celle biosensorer og genetiske hukommelses-apparater 
undersøgt. Disse er levende celler, der kan bruges til at måle og registrere kemiske metabolitter. 
Igennem projektet er kataloget af genetiske biosensorer blevet udvidet via kloning af tre typer af hel-
celle biosensorer. Disse biosensorer kunne opfange og respondere på de tre sekundære metabolitter 
tetracyklin, naringenin og salicylat, som alle er relevante indenfor agrikultur. Biosensorernes 
responsydeevne blev evalueret ved at teste en række parametre. Tetracyklin-biosensoren ved navn 
JJ001 udviste god ydeevne i kræft af dens lave utæthed, høje sensitivitet og høje dynamiske 
rækkevidde. Ydeevnen af naringenin- og salicylat-biosensorerne viste sig imidlertid at være 
utilstrækkelig. Derfor blev der udarbejdet og anvendt strategier til finjustering og forbedring af disse 
biosensorer. Det førte til konstruktionen af et nyt sæt af modificerede naringenin- og salicylat-
biosensorer. Selvom umiddelbare observationer af disse nye biosensorer var lovende, så blev der 
grundet tidsbegrænsninger ikke skaffet endelig evidens for forbedringer i deres ydeevne. Samlet set 
eksemplificerer dette projekt en strategi og fremgangsmetode, som kan bruges til at konstruere, teste 
og optimere nye hel-celle biosensorer.

1 Introduction 
Biological life is one of the most complex and dynamic systems found on Earth. The sheer number 
of microorganisms present within and around us is staggering: A single gram of soil may contain up 
to 10 billion microbes [1]. Billions of years before the advent of humankind, bacteria existed and 
evolved to survive and adapt [2]. In this context, it is not surprising that bacteria have developed 
intricate communities amongst themselves and other microbes. Bacteria do not simply coexist. They 
interact with and around each other in a myriad of ways [3]. Microbial interactions shape the 
dynamics and the functioning of microbial communities. Understanding of microbial interactions is 
essential if we, as bioengineers, hope to monitor, manipulate and develop microbial communities for 
novel use cases. However, despite significant advances in recent years, our knowledge on microbial 
interactions remains limited. 
Central to microbial interactions are the chemical signals which are secreted and sensed by 
community members. These chemical metabolites drive the interactions between microbes – they act 
as the language through which microbes communicate. Chemical metabolites play vital roles in 
microbial defense mechanisms, symbiosis, proliferation and much more [4]. Therefore, we must be 
able to detect and decipher these chemical signals. Otherwise, we remain illiterate in the chemical 
language of microorganisms. 
Whole-cell biosensors provide a powerful detection tool for chemical metabolites. In short, a whole-
cell biosensor is a cell that can sense a specific signal and then respond with a measurable output [5]. 
Additionally, whole-cell biosensors provide an opportunity to tap into the genetic machinery of living 
cells. This allows for a large range of modification possibilities by using genetic engineering and 
synthetic biology [6]. It is even possible to further develop whole-cell biosensors into so-called 
genetic memory devices [7]. Genetic memory devices can leverage the fact that the genetic code of 
the cell functions as information storage. They can sense specific chemical signals and then record 
this information directly into their DNA. This allows for tracking of biological and environmental 
events, providing the cell with memory.  
Throughout this project, the exciting prospects of whole-cell biosensors and genetic memory devices 
will be explored. Several unique possibilities through use of these genetic sensors will be outlined. 
Additionally, we will construct, test and optimize multiple biosensors in an effort to unravel the 
potential within microbial interactions.

1.1 Microbial interactions and chemical signaling 
In a general context, the subject of interactions is very broad. Many different types of interactions 
exist. However, here we will focus primarily on microbial and biological interactions and the 
chemical signals that drive them.  
Some chemical metabolites function as antimicrobial compounds. These are viewed as being 
microbial weapons, capable of causing harm to other microbes [8]. This is achieved through a process 
called interference competition, in which a microorganism secretes an antimicrobial product with the 
aim of harming competitor microbes [8]. A good example of an antimicrobial compound capable of 
mediating this effect is the polyketide antibiotic tetracycline [9]. The chemical structure of 
tetracycline is shown below in Figure 1. This broad-spectrum antibiotic is naturally produced by 
species found within the Streptomyces genus, such as S. rimosus and S. aureofaciens [9]. The 
antibiotic thus provides its producer with a competitive advantage by harming or killing competitors. 
Naturally, as a response to antimicrobial metabolites, target microbes have evolved methods of danger 
sensing and subsequent initiation of defense mechanisms to survive [10], [11]. These defense 
mechanisms can lead to antibiotic resistance, which – despite being a global threat – is not within the 
scope of this project. 
Interactions are not only antagonistic in their outcome, such as in the example of competition above. 
Sometimes biological interactions can be mutualistic, such as allowing cross-feeding between 
microbes [12]. Another example is the mutualistic symbioses found between plants and microbes 
[13]. The rhizosphere is the soil surrounding plant roots, and this is a microbiome inhabited by a 
plethora of microorganisms, many of which display biocontrol or biofertilization properties [13], [14]. 
Nitrogen-fixation is one of the common methods in which plant growth promoting rhizobacteria can 
provide benefits to plants [13]. Nitrogen-fixation symbiosis is also an interaction that depends heavily 
on chemical signaling between the plant and the associated bacteria. For instance, induction of the 
nodulation symbiosis between legumes and nitrogen-fixing rhizobia can be mediated by secondary 
metabolites [15]. This has been shown to be the case for flavonoid compounds such as naringenin 
[15]. When legumes are deficient in nitrogen, they can secrete naringenin and other flavonoids in 
their root exudates to promote nodulation. This is a great example of how chemical metabolites can 
be secreted during stressful conditions as a cry for help, which can then be accommodated by other 
community members.

Many other chemical metabolites are secreted by plants through root exudates. These can have a large 
impact on the microbes colonizing the rhizosphere. One example is the phytohormone salicylic acid 
and its derivatives called salicylates [16]. Salicylic acid functions as a key immune regulator in the 
plant immune system. However, when released through root exudates it can also change the microbial 
community composition within the rhizosphere [16]. It was found that removal of salicylic acid and 
related defense phytohormones leads to abnormal microbial compositions of the root microbiome, 
which may also be linked to lowered plant survival [16]. This exemplifies how plant-microbe 
chemical interactions can have a large impact on microbial communities. 
The examples above highlight scenarios where chemical metabolites are vital to microbial community 
composition and functioning. Therefore, it is essential that we are able to investigate and detect these 
metabolites if we aim to engineer microbial communities.  
 
1.2 Current detection methods of chemical metabolites 
In recent years, sequencing technologies have undergone rapid development [2]. Sequencing based 
technologies such as metagenomics, transcriptomics and proteomics have allowed us to investigate 
microbiomes and microbial communities to a previously unfathomable level of detail [2]. There is no 
denying that these technologies have increased our understanding of microbial diversity and potential. 
We have gained a deep understanding of the species that are present within microbial communities 
and the products and metabolites they are producing. However, interactions between microbes – 
mediated by the secretion and sensing of chemical metabolites – cannot be fully explained based on 
sequencing alone. Thus, detection and measurement of these molecules are vital to understanding 
microbial interactions.

A number of efficient detection methods have been developed and employed for direct measurement 
of chemical metabolites. The current gold standard of metabolite measurement typically relies on 
analytical chemistry detection methods [17]. These are most commonly high-performance liquid 
chromatography (HPLC), mass spectrometry (MS) or variants and combinations of these (UPLC, 
LC-MS, GC-MS etc.) [17]. In short, HPLC functions by separating metabolites within a liquid sample 
based on their physical and chemical properties [17]. This is done via a separation column, also called 
the stationary phase. Depending on the affinity between the sample chemical metabolites and the 
separation column, the metabolites will be eluted at different speeds. A detector can then detect the 
eluted compounds and convert the data into electrical signals that can be processed into 
chromatograms. Combining liquid chromatography with mass spectrometry (LC-MS) allows for 
further analysis of the separated compounds within the sample. The mass spectrometer will ionize 
the separated compounds and provide information on their molecular weight [18]. Consequently, LC-
MS is a potent separation and identification technique for chemical metabolites.  
Another powerful technique for investigation of the spatial distribution of chemical molecules within 
a biological system is matrix assisted laser desorption/ionization (MALDI) – imaging mass 
spectrometry (IMS) [19]. MALDI-IMS is a successor to mass spectrometry that connects MS spectra 
to the locations where ions originated. It works by mounting the sample on a conductive support, 
followed by applying a thin matrix layer to the sample surface. Then a laser is used to irradiate the 
sample surface, resulting in molecular ionization and desorption. The sample is then moved according 
to a 2D-grid such that every spot of the sample is hit by the laser. A mass spectrum for each spot is 
recorded, along with information on the coordinates from which each mass spectrum originated. The 
result is a reconstructed 2D-image, complete with mass spectrometry data for every coordinate of the 
sample. Thus, the presence of different chemical metabolites as well as their locations are recorded. 
This allows for investigation of microbial interactions as the spatial distribution of the chemical 
metabolites mediating the interactions are visualized and analyzed [19]. 
LC-MS, MALDI-IMS and other related detection methods provide great opportunities for detection 
and screening of chemical metabolites. However, these analytic chemistry methods also have 
limitations. One limitation is that they require the removal of a sample from its natural environment, 
before any chemical metabolites within the sample can be detected. This effectively eliminates the 
opportunity for in situ use of these methods. Related to this limitation are the equipment requirements 
and associated costs of these methods. The equipment is expensive and specialized, which further 
requires personnel trained in carrying out the assays. Additionally, in analytical chemistry methods

1.3 Whole-cell biosensors and their fascinating use cases 
While powerful, analytical chemistry detection methods still have certain limitations when it comes 
to investigation of microbial interactions within biological communities. There is still a need for 
alternative detection methods, which may open new opportunities to understand the chemical 
language of microorganisms. With recent developments within synthetic biology, one attractive 
alternative lies in the construction of whole-cell biosensors. 
The term biosensor is short for biological sensor. A biosensor can be defined as a measurement system 
that is composed of two base components: A sensor component that can recognize and detect an 
analyte, and a transducer that can produce a measurable signal in response to the sensing of the analyte 
[20]. Biosensors are then further classified into different types based on their specific sensor 
component. These are typically divided as being of molecular, tissue or cellular origin [5]. In 
molecular-based biosensor systems, sensing elements are subcellular components or macromolecules 
such as antibodies, enzymes and nucleic acids [20]. Tissue-based biosensors utilize sensing elements 
derived from intact tissue from plants or animals [20]. Cell-based biosensors, more recently also 
termed whole-cell biosensors, are genetically engineered cells, which can detect analytes and respond 
via natural transcriptional responses [21]. Thus, whole-cell biosensors are living cells, which can 
harness the inherent abilities of a living organism. 
Examples of simple whole-cell biosensors are shown in Figure 2. A whole-cell biosensor depends on 
two heterologously expressed genetic elements: A transcriptional regulator, which provides the cell 
with the sensory component, and an output promoter which can direct expression of a reporter gene, 
providing the cell with the ability to produce a measurable response signal [22]. The transcriptional 
regulator controls expression from the output promoter, thus coupling the sensory and transducer 
components.

Figure 2: Concept of two simple whole-cell biosensors. Whole-cell biosensor A uses a transcriptional repressor as its regulator. In the 
presence of the inducer, the repressor will alleviate repression of the PReg promoter and allow expression of the reporter gene. Whole-

The transcriptional regulator is essential to the function of the whole-cell biosensor. These regulators 
can be split into two groups: transcriptional activators and transcriptional repressors [23]. Examples 
of both are shown in Figure 2. Both can sense the analyte of interest, which is also commonly referred 
to as the inducer molecule. However, they respond differently to the presence of the inducer. The 
transcriptional activator depends on the inducer for its activating function – only in the complex with 
inducer can the transcriptional activator bind to the output promoter and activate expression of the 
reporter gene. The opposite is true for the transcriptional repressor. In the absence of inducer, the 
repressor will block transcription from the output promoter. Only when the inducer is sensed and 
bound by the repressor will repression of the output promoter be alleviated, allowing expression of 
the reporter gene [6]. 
Whole-cell biosensors have proved themselves capable as application tools for sensing and 
responding to inducers of interest within a range of scientific fields. Several examples will be 
provided shortly. First though, it is important to ask the questions: Why use whole-cell biosensors? 
What advantages do they exhibit to warrant their use over conventional detection methods?

While the answer is multifaceted it can be summed up by: using biology to sense biology provides 
many benefits. One of the major benefits is the ability of whole-cell biosensors to consider 
bioavailability of a molecule [5]. The definition of bioavailability varies slightly depending on the 
scientific field in which the term is used. However, the definition used here refers to the bioavailability 
of chemical compounds to bacterial cells. Thus, the bioavailability of a chemical compound describes 
to what extent the compound can be assimilated by living cells [24]. Additionally, the bioavailability 
depends on transport of the chemical compound into the vicinity of the living cell, as well as transport 
of the compound over the cell wall and cell membrane(s) [24]. In their natural environments, 
microorganisms are spatially and temporally distributed. For instance, this is the case within the soil 
microbiome. Not all chemicals present in the soil matrix will necessarily have an impact on living 
organisms [25]. A fraction of the chemicals will be trapped elsewhere in the soil and will never be 
transported into the vicinity of the living cell. Additionally, even when the chemical metabolites are 
near a cell, only a fraction will be transported into the cell. A whole-cell biosensor, being a living 
cell, will thus provide a more accurate detection of the bioavailable chemical metabolites [5]. This is 
in contrast to the chemical analysis methods such as HPLC and LC-MS discussed earlier, which will 
detect a larger fraction of a chemical metabolite within e.g. a soil sample, typically overestimating 
the impact of the compound on cells [5]. 
Whole-cell biosensors rely on genetic circuits. This allows for utilization of the entire genetic toolbox 
which has been developed and expanded considerably during the last decades. Through synthetic 
biology it is now possible to introduce, change and optimize genetic elements in modified cells [6]. 
Rational engineering and directed evolution allow for the optimization of whole-cell biosensor 
performance [21]. The sensitivity and specificity of biosensors are gradually being improved, 
rivalling the detection limits of analytical chemistry methods [22], [3]. With future efforts, sensitivity 
and selectivity of whole-cell biosensors have the potential to surpass current chemical detection 
methods. Additionally, conventional analytical chemistry requires expensive, specialized equipment 
and facilities, which is a challenge that can largely be overcome through use of whole-cell biosensors 
[5]. 
Another important quality of whole-cell biosensors is their non-invasiveness and related potential for 
unique in situ use [21]. Because of the micron scale of whole-cell biosensors, they can be deployed 
in environments that are difficult to access and survey with other detection methods. This is a quality 
that has already proven invaluable in a multitude of different scientific fields, such as in 
environmental engineering, immunology, human health, agricultural biotechnology and many more.

Within environmental diagnostics and pollution detection whole-cell biosensors have been employed 
for a variety of uses [5]. Tan et al. (2015) developed an E. coli whole-cell biosensor capable of sensing 
the highly explosive 2,4,6-Trinitrotoluene, better known as TNT [26]. TNT is not only explosive but 
is also a toxic and carcinogenic pollutant. The TNT-sensing whole-cell biosensor provided an 
efficient and simple detection method, circumventing the need for expensive and complex equipment 
and sample preparation [26]. In another example, Wang et al. (2020) constructed an E. coli signal-
amplified mercury whole-cell biosensor, capable of sensing the toxic metal pollutant and responding 
through GFP fluorescence [27]. They managed to optimize both sensitivity and selectivity, obtaining 
a whole-cell biosensor with high specificity for Hg2+ but not for other metal ions [27]. 
In immunology, human health and medical diagnostics, whole-cell biosensors allow for unique in situ 
applications. Mimee et al. (2015) programmed the human gut commensal, Bacteroides 
thetaiotaomicron, to function as a whole-cell biosensor for use in the mammalian gut microbiome 
[28]. They developed several whole-cell biosensor strains, among which was a rhamnose inducible 
B. thetaiotaomicron biosensor. This rhamnose biosensor could colonize the gut microbiota of mice. 
It successfully sensed and responded to the addition of rhamnose when this inducer was supplemented 
in the mouse drinking water [28]. This study highlights the robustness and the possibilities for 
therapeutic use cases of whole-cell biosensors. Another great therapeutic potential of whole-cell 
biosensors lies within development of sense-and-respond genetic circuits [21]. Here, the sensing of 
one or more biomarker inducers can be coupled to a therapeutic output response. Xie et al. (2011) 
demonstrated an example of this when they designed a synthetic regulatory circuit capable of 
determining whether a cell matches the profile characteristic for the cervical cancer cell line, HeLa 
[29]. Several biomarkers were sensed in their whole-cell biosensor, allowing it to differentiate other 
cells as being HeLa cancer cells or not. Moreover, once a series of conditions were met and a match 
was identified, the genetic circuit would trigger apoptosis of the HeLa cell through regulation of the 
human Bcl-2-associated X protein hBax [29]. In this manner, the cancer-sensing whole-cell biosensor 
not only recognized specific cancer cells – its output also resulted in a therapeutic function by 
selectively clearing these cancer cells. 
The above examples illustrate the vast potential and unique use cases provided by whole-cell 
biosensors. However, this does not mean that they come without limitations. Whole-cell biosensors 
require optimization and genetic tuning if they are to be relevant detectors for real-life applications. 
Furthermore, the output response of whole-cell biosensors can present an additional challenge.

bacterial luciferases, are used to provide easily measurable responses [5]. Such reporter genes do have 
their own advantages and drawbacks. For instance, GFP fluorescence can suffer from signal 
interference by background fluorescence of some host cells, while the luciferases can be impaired by 
thermal lability and dimeric protein interference [5]. To overcome these limitations while 
simultaneously providing whole-cell biosensors with entirely new abilities, some have turned their 
attention to the design and construction of genetic memory devices. 
 
1.4 DNA-based memory and genetic memory devices  
In some cases, a direct readout from the reporter gene of a whole-cell biosensor is not optimal or even 
possible [28]. This can be the case if the environment where the whole-cell biosensor must be 
deployed does not allow for easy direct measurement or detection of e.g. a fluorescence or light 
output. Examples of such environments could include the gut microbiota or the soil microbiome [28]. 
For non-disruptive in situ use of biosensors in these scenarios, another option is to change the type of 
output response entirely. This can be accomplished through the development of whole-cell biosensors 
into genetic memory devices. Genetic memory devices can track and record the presence of chemical 
signals directly in their DNA [7]. Thus, these DNA-based cellular recording devices use DNA as the 
storage medium for responding to metabolites. This provides several advantages beyond being less 
disruptive than reporter outputs. DNA can be stably preserved for decades [7]. With next-generation 
sequencing, it is possible to analyze the DNA data later in a cost- and time-efficient manner. Genetic 
memory also allows for continuous data acquisition, and DNA recording can be multiplexed without 
disruption of the biological state of cells or microbiomes [7]. Moreover, because genetic memory 
devices do not need to express reporter genes, they experience a lower metabolic resource burden 
compared to conventional whole-cell biosensors [30].  
To develop and construct genetic memory devices, it is necessary to incorporate the correct synthetic 
parts into a genetic circuit. The core required component is a DNA-targeting and modifying enzyme 
that is capable of “writing” onto DNA in some capacity [7]. This can be through addition, excision, 
recombination or other alterations of the target DNA sequence. Many enzymes exist which are 
capable of this, and common examples include nucleases, polymerases and clustered regularly 
interspaced short palindromic repeats - associated proteins (CRISPR-Cas) [7]. However, this project 
will focus on serine integrases.

Serine integrases are a sub-family of site-specific recombinases that can mediate recombination 
between two recognition sites [31]. In nature, serine integrases are used by bacteriophages to allow 
for integration of their viral genome into bacteria. This is required for temperate phages that choose 
the lysogenic life cycle. The recombination happens between two recognition sites, the phage 
attachment site (attP) and the bacterial attachment site (attB) [30]. Serine integrases achieve this 
through cleavage of DNA to form double-stranded breaks, a process mediated by their catalytic 
serine. This allows for covalent bond formation between the serine integrase and the DNA backbone, 
which results in integration after re-ligation of DNA. After recombination, the attachment sites 
become hybrid attachment sites called attL and attR. This process is illustrated in Figure 3 below. 
 
Figure 3: Step-by-step mechanism of serine integrase mediated site-specific recombination. (i) First, the serine integrase binds its attP 
and attB sites, in this case located on two different DNA sequences (e.g. a phage and its target bacterium). (ii) The serine integrase 
mediates cleavage of the attachment sites. (iii) Two serine subunits rotate to swap the positions of the now half-sites of attP and attB. 
(iv) If 3’ overhangs of the half-sites are complementary, then re-ligation of the DNA can occur, resulting in insertion of phage DNA 
and formation of attL and attR sites. Figures taken from [30]. 
Serine integrases vary from the related tyrosine integrases in that they do not require any additional 
cofactors to bind to or bend DNA [30]. This makes their function simpler – only the integrase protein 
and the short attachment sites attP and attB (typically 40-50 bp in size) are necessary for 
recombination [32]. Furthermore, the recombination reaction of serine integrases is unidirectional, 
meaning that it is not reversible merely by the serine integrase alone [31]. The reaction can only be 
reversed in the presence of a protein called the recombination directionality factor (RDF). 
Consequently, if RDF is never added, then integrase recombination will result in a permanent change 
to the DNA sequence – a permanent genetic memory [32]. 
The above qualities of serine integrases make them ideal for use in genetic engineering and in 
construction of genetic memory circuits. Their recombination function can be used for rearrangement 
of DNA through clever positioning of the attachment sites, see Figure 4 below. If the attP and attB 
sites are placed in opposite directions on the same DNA sequence, then integrase recombination will 
result in DNA inversion of the sequence between the attachment sites [33], [30].

Figure 4: Two examples of DNA rearrangements mediated by serine integrases and the recombination directionality factor RDF. In 
figure a, a phage containing the attP site can integrate its DNA into a bacterium containing DNA with the attB site. This results in the 
formation of attL and attR sites. Excision is possible if RDF is present. In figure b, the attP and attB sites are placed in opposite 
directions on the same DNA sequence. The serine integrase will then invert the DNA sequence present between the attachment sites, 
simultaneously forming attL and attR sites. The inversion is reversible only if RDF is added. Figures taken from [30]. 
In recent years, considerable efforts have been made to expand the repertoire of functional integrases. 
Optimal integrases display several characteristics that make them ideal for use in the construction of 
genetic memory devices. First, they need to function efficiently and quickly – a fast 
recombination/inversion event is desirable to increase responsiveness and accuracy of the genetic 
memory device [32]. However, this must be achieved without leaky expression of integrase, as that 
will lead to an irreversible recombination event in the absence of inducer. Leakiness is a parameter 
that can be genetically tuned in several ways, and something that will be discussed later. Furthermore, 
the integrase must not be toxic to the whole-cell memory device that produces it. This depends both 
on the inherent properties of the integrase but also on the chassis used to carry the memory circuit 
[32]. Lastly, it is advantageous to use integrases that are orthogonal, meaning they do not bind to non-
cognate attachment sites [32]. This allows for multiplexing of genetic circuits, where several 
integrases can be used simultaneously to respond to separate inducer molecules without interference 
between them. With the above parameters in mind, Yang et al. (2014) managed to identify and test 
11 different orthogonal integrases [32]. These were successfully incorporated into the same genetic 
memory array, verifying the possibility of multiplexing a genetic circuit to respond to several different 
inducer molecules simultaneously. One of their integrases, Integrase12, was used to design functional 
whole-cell biosensors during this project.

Serine integrases represent a major synthetic part in the development of genetic memory devices. 
However, for the construction of functional genetic circuits, a range of other genetic parts need to be 
considered. These parts must be investigated before a design strategy for whole-cell biosensors and 
genetic memory devices can be envisioned.  
 
1.5 Tuning of genetic parts within whole-cell biosensors 
Both whole-cell biosensors and their successors, the genetic memory devices, have huge potential in 
the detection of chemical signals and thus our understanding of microbial interactions. However, 
before they can meet practical application requirements, many synthetic biology strategies must be 
applied. Engineering and tuning of genetic circuits, their regulators, promoters and other elements are 
required before well-functioning biosensors can be obtained [23]. Therefore, understanding of both 
the genetic elements and the tuning strategies is vital. This section highlights some of the genetic 
parts and tuning strategies that we have used during experimental construction of whole-cell 
biosensors.  
As mentioned previously, the principal genetic element needed for a whole-cell biosensor is a 
transcriptional regulator [5]. The resulting regulatory protein may be either an activator or a repressor. 
Transcriptional repressors have been employed to a large extent in the construction of genetic circuits, 
whole-cell biosensors and genetic memory devices [34], [6]. By placing the operator site of the 
repressor within a target promoter, expression of the target gene is turned off, effectively creating a 
“NOT” logic gate within the circuit [34]. The TetR family of regulators contains several repressors 
which have been used extensively in prior studies [22]. These TetR-like repressors have a simple 
mode of repression in which dimers bind the target promoter and results in physical blockage of RNA 
polymerase [35].  
In contrast, transcriptional activators can mediate ligand-inducible activation of expression from their 
target promoter. While not as commonly used as repressors, activators such as the arabinose-inducible 
activator AraC have been used in genetic circuit design [32]. Additionally, activators within the 
prokaryotic superfamily of LysR-type transcriptional regulators have proven to be useful in 
biosensors [36]. In contrast to transcriptional repressors, the transcriptional activators allow for 
creation of “YES” logic gates, which can similarly be beneficial when creating complex genetic 
circuits [36]. 
==PAGE:20==
Master Thesis │ Jens-Jakob Krogh Nørgaard                                         
June 2023 
                                                                                    
 
 
Page | 13  
 
Both transcriptional repressors and transcriptional activators bind operator sites in proximity to the 
output promoter to mediate their regulatory effect. However, for transcriptional repressors it is 
possible to change the position and/or the number of operator sites to alter repression [37]. This will 
change binding efficiency of the repressor and ultimately leakiness of the output promoter [38]. Cox 
et al. (2007) demonstrated that placing an operator between the -10 and -35 sequences (core region) 
of the target promoter provides maximum repression efficiency [39]. Operator placement downstream 
from the -10 sequence (proximal region) provided lower relative repression efficiency, while 
placement of the operator upstream from the -35 sequence (distal region) provided the lowest relative 
repression efficiency [39]. Additionally, repression efficiency can be further increased by adding 
multiple operator sites within the same promoter, or by letting the operator sequence overlap with the 
-10 or -35 sequences [37], [40]. This alteration of repression efficiency dependent on operator sites 
provides many options for the tuning of leakiness and biosensor function. Notably, these options are 
only available for transcriptional repressors – not for transcriptional activators. Activators can only 
function by binding to an activator binding site/operator located in the distal region upstream from 
the -35 element [39]. This is a relevant limitation to keep in mind when choosing between 
transcriptional regulators for genetic circuit design. 
An issue commonly encountered when constructing and testing novel whole-cell biosensors is 
lackluster performance. Leakiness of the output promoter, as mentioned above, is one of the critical 
performance parameters, as it is essential that a biosensor or genetic memory device does not respond 
when not induced. Performance of genetic parts will typically need to be tuned before circuits can 
meet the requirements needed for their application [6]. Generally, tuning of the response function of 
a biosensor can be achieved via alterations made on the transcriptional, the translational or the post-
translational level [37]. The changing of operator site location is one example of tuning at the 
transcriptional level. Commonly, tuning at these three gene expression levels are achieved by 
changing one or more of the following: 1) promoter strength, 2) ribosomal binding site strength or 3) 
degradation rate/stability of proteins [6], [41]. The promoter strength is typically altered by changing 
the -10 and/or -35 sequences within the promoter [42]. This will impact gene expression at the 
transcriptional level by altering the binding of RNA polymerase to the promoter, thus changing 
transcription initiation [42]. Similarly, the ribosomal binding site assists in recruitment of the 
ribosome to mRNA during translation. Consequently, changing the RBS sequence will alter 
translation initiation [43].  
 
==PAGE:21==
Master Thesis │ Jens-Jakob Krogh Nørgaard                                         
June 2023 
                                                                                    
 
 
Page | 14  
 
Lastly, response function can be altered on a post-translational level [44]. Protein stability can be 
tuned by the addition of specific peptide tags that are recognized by intracellular tail-specific 
proteases [45]. Andersen et al. (1998) found that fusion of a peptide tag with the amino acid sequence 
“RPAANDENYALAA” to the C-terminal end of GFP could lead to increased protein degradation of 
the GFP mutant in E. coli [45]. Moreover, they illustrated that varying the last three residues in the 
peptide degradation tag consensus sequence resulted in GFP mutant proteins with varying stability. 
Figure 5 below shows how the stability of GFP mutants changes through fusion to different peptide 
degradation tag variants. 
 
Figure 5: Decrease in GFP fluorescence over time as a result of fusing degradation tag variants onto gfp. The half-lives of the GFP 
mutants GFP(ASV), GFP(AAV) and GFP(LVA) depend on the last three residues within their peptide degradation tag. Figure taken 
from [45]. 
The protein stability of the GFP protein GFPmut3 is high, with an in-vivo half-life of more than a day 
[45]. However, the fusion of peptide degradation tags to GFP results in significantly lower half-lives. 
The degradation tag ending in the three residues “ASV” provides a half-life of 110 min, while “AAV” 
and “LVA” variants have even lower half-lives of 60 and 40 min, respectively [45]. Therefore, these 
degradation tags can be rated based on the degree of protein instability they confer: “LVA” is the 
strongest degradation tag, resulting in the fastest protease degradation of the fused protein. “AAV” 
provides medium speed protein degradation, while “ASV” confers the slowest protease degradation. 
Alteration of protein stability is a viable strategy of tuning performance of whole-cell biosensors, and 
it will be employed during the experimental work of this project to counteract leakiness of output 
promoters.

1.6 Assembling the parts - Design of our whole-cell biosensors 
Different genetic elements, tuning strategies and considerations have been described in the previous 
sections. Now this knowledge must be combined to design and construct the genetic machinery 
required for our whole-cell biosensors. The design of the biosensor system is inspired from previous 
efforts made by several talented people. Meyer et al. (2019) constructed a range of whole-cell 
biosensors and provided essential regulatory genes and genetic elements for this work [22]. Likewise, 
Yang et al. (2014) identified and provided the serine integrase called Integrase12, which allows for 
our development of whole-cell biosensors into genetic memory devices [32]. Notably though, Postdoc 
and supervisor M. L. Hansen managed to combine many of the above genetic elements to construct 
a prototype whole-cell biosensor (ongoing work). This biosensor can sense and respond to the 
secondary metabolite 2,4-Diacetylphloroglucinol (DAPG). The work presented in this thesis is a 
continuation of the ongoing project to develop biosensors and thus the design is based upon the initial 
work by M. L. Hansen. 
The biosensor design relies on a two-plasmid system. These plasmids are low-copy plasmids that we 
have named reporter plasmid (pReporter12) and integrase plasmid (pSEVA231::Integrase12). 
Plasmid maps of these can be seen in Appendix 7.9 “SnapGene maps of reporter and integrase 
plasmids” . The reporter plasmid is responsible for the output function of the biosensor, as it contains 
two marker genes: dsRed, which encodes a red fluorescent protein, and gfp, which encodes a green 
fluorescent protein. The output of the reporter plasmid is thus easily measurable via fluorescence of 
the biosensor cells. Figure 6 below illustrates the genetic arrangement of elements within the reporter 
plasmid.

The integrase plasmid contains a transcriptional regulator gene, in this case a repressor, under the 
control of a constitutive promoter. In the absence of ligand, this repressor binds to the operator site(s) 
located in the promoter upstream of the integrase gene, thereby sterically blocking transcription 
initiation. In contrast, once the inducer is added, it can complex with the repressor, causing a 
conformational change such that the repressor can no longer bind to its operator site. This alleviates 
repression of the integrase promoter, resulting in integrase expression.

1.7 Aims of the project 
With our whole-cell biosensor system introduced, the goals of this project will now be defined. The 
first and major aim lies in the expansion of the repertoire of genetic whole-cell biosensors. Therefore, 
three different types of biosensors will be cloned during this project: 
1. Expand the catalog of whole-cell biosensors to include: 
- 
TetR (tetracycline) biosensors 
- 
TtgR (naringenin) biosensors 
- 
NahR (salicylate) biosensors 
These biosensors depend on the transcriptional regulators TetR, TtgR and NahR. TetR and TtgR both 
function as TetR-like repressors, alleviating repression of the integrase promoter in the presence of 
their respective inducers tetracycline and naringenin [35]. Meanwhile, NahR is a LysR-like 
transcriptional activator, capable of activating expression of the integrase promoter in the presence 
of its inducer salicylate [46]. As established earlier, the target molecules of these three biosensors are 
secondary metabolites, which are all relevant within the soil microbiome. Therefore, these biosensors 
will represent important and useful tools in the effort to understand microbial interactions, and 
ultimately become valuable assets in future engineering prospects. Figure 9 below provides an 
overview of the whole-cell biosensors that will be constructed.

of the biosensors and the genetic elements that allow the biosensors to sense their respective inducers. Previously, a 2,4-
Diacetylphloroglucinol (DAPG) biosensor was constructed by M. L. Hansen. In this project, we aim to expand the catalog of 
biosensors. Figure adapted from M. L. Hansen.

However, before the potential of the biosensors can be further explored, it is crucial to verify that the 
biosensors function as expected. Thus, the second aim revolves around proving that the concept of 
the biosensors does in fact function in vitro:  
2. Verify the function and evaluate performance of the whole-cell biosensors using flow 
cytometry and data analysis 
Due to implementation of the two fluorescence genes in the genetic design of the biosensors, flow 
cytometry is a suitable method to verify their function. Flow cytometry can be used to count the 
number of cells that fluoresce red, fluoresce green and red, or do not fluoresce at all [47]. This will 
allow for analysis of how the biosensors respond to their respective inducer molecules. Leakiness, 
sensitivity and dynamic range are all biosensor performance parameters that will be investigated 
through this data analysis. 
Once proof of concept has been shown, the next aim will be to genetically tune the biosensors. Ideally, 
we want to construct biosensors that not only function, but function well: 
3. Improve biosensor functionality and performance through tuning and implementation of 
modified genetic elements 
This aim will be split into several steps. First, it is necessary to investigate which genetic elements 
within the biosensors can be tuned or changed, and then try to predict how such changes may affect 
the performance of the biosensors. Second, these new genetic elements must be tested experimentally, 
to show the impact of their modifications. Lastly, the modified genetic elements must be implemented 
into the biosensors to ultimately obtain biosensor variants with improved performance.

2.4 Growth media and growth conditions 
The growth medium used during experiments was Luria-Bertani (LB) medium. LB-medium powder 
was obtained from Carl Roth Gmbh & Co. KG and had Art.-Nr. X964.3. The powder contains a 
mixture of tryptone (10 g/L), yeast extract (5 g/L) sodium chloride (5 g/L) and is adjusted to pH 7.

20 g of the powder is added to 1 L of H2O to produce LB liquid medium. Additionally, agar can be 
added (20 g/L) to make solid LB agar. 
During this project, Escherichia coli was the cloning organism of choice. In general, all E. coli strains 
were incubated in LB medium over night (O/N) at 37℃. Strains were grown either in liquid LB or 
on solid LB agar plates. If grown in liquid LB, cultures were grown with vigorous shaking (200 rpm). 
Strains containing any variant of the pSEVA231::Integrase12 plasmid have the resistance marker neo 
(KanR). Thus, kanamycin antibiotic selection at a concentration of 50 µg/mL was used in both solid 
and liquid LB for these strains. Strains containing the pReporter12 plasmid have the resistance marker 
CmR (CmR), and chloramphenicol antibiotic selection at a concentration of 10 µg/mL was used for 
these strains. Double selection at the same concentrations was used for strains containing both 
plasmids. Strains containing any variant of the pBG42 plasmid have the resistance marker aacC1 
(GmR), and gentamicin antibiotic selection at a concentration of 10µg/mL was used for these strains.

2.6 Construction of biosensors through cloning in E. coli 
Construction of genetic whole-cell biosensors was achieved through cloning in Escherichia coli. A 
flow diagram showing the general cloning procedure can be seen in Appendix 7.1 “Flow diagram of

2.6.1 DNA purification of plasmids 
E. coli DH5α strains each containing the plasmids pAJM.011_TetR, pAJM.661_TtgR and

in liquid LB with proper selection and grown O/N at 37℃. Following completed incubation, plasmids 
were purified using the Macherey-Nagel Nucleospin Plasmid kit and following the protocol seen in 
Appendix 7.2 “Macherey-Nagel Nucleospin plasmid purification”. Plasmids were purified from 4 mL

of each culture to provide sufficiently high plasmid concentrations. All subsequently obtained

2.6.2 PCR amplification of fragments required for construction of biosensors 
PCR amplification was used to obtain the PCR fragments needed for construction of the integrase 
plasmid of the different biosensors. PCR was carried out in accordance with the program listed in 
Appendix 7.4 “Phusion PCR amplification program for obtaining PCR fragments”. The primers used 
in this project are listed in Appendix 7.11 “Primer overview”.  
Following the completion of PCR, PCR fragments were incubated with FastDigest DpnI enzyme for 
1 hour at 37℃. DpnI cleaves methylated and hemimethylated DNA. E. coli strain DNA is typically 
dam methylated, and DpnI can be used to effectively digest any methylated parental DNA template 
that may still be present amongst the PCR fragments. Thus, only the newly amplified PCR-generated 
DNA will remain after DpnI treatment [50]. 
After DpnI treatment, PCR fragments were cleaned using the Macherey-Nagel Nucleospin Gel and 
PCR Clean-up kit. The relevant protocol is listed in Appendix 7.3 “Macherey-Nagel Nucleospin PCR 
clean-up”.  
 
2.6.3 Gel electrophoresis for verification  
Gel electrophoresis was used to screen PCR products to verify sizes of integrase plasmid fragments. 
0.8% agarose gels were used. Ethidium bromide solution was used as visualization stain. 
Approximately 5 µL of PCR product was loaded into the wells of the gel. For PCR products obtained 
through Phusion PCR, a 6X DNA Gel Loading Dye was used to dye the PCR product. For PCR 
products obtained through DreamTaq PCR, no loading dye was required as the buffer used here had 
dye added in advance. The GeneRuler Express DNA ladder was used to compare and interpret the 
results of gel electrophoresis.

2.6.5 Heat shock transformation of biosensor constructs into E. coli cells 
Heat shock transformation was used to allow E. coli cells to take up the Golden Gate assembled 
ligation mixes and subsequently the integrase plasmids required for the acquisition of functional 
whole-cell biosensors. Before carrying out transformation, heat shock competent E. coli cells had to 
be prepared. This was done in accordance with the protocol seen in Appendix 7.7 “Making heat shock 
competent Escherichia coli cells”. During the cloning work, it was found that high competency of 
cells was very important to achieve successful whole-cell biosensors. Cells lose competency over 
longer periods of storage time, and thus it was important to regularly test cell competency through 
the inclusion of transformation (positive) controls. 
Following acquisition of competent E. coli cells, heat shock transformation could proceed. The 
protocol found in Appendix 7.8 “Heat shock transformation” was followed. Notably, two rounds of 
transformation were required to obtain a finished biosensor. In the first transformation, 5 µL of 
Golden Gate assembled ligation mix was transformed into 50 µL competent TOP10 E. coli cells. 
However, this only composes half of the biosensor function (the integrase plasmid), so after integrase 
transformants had been verified, another transformation was carried out. Here, 1 µL of purified 
transformant integrase plasmid was transformed into competent DH10B+pReporter12 E. coli cells. 
Thus, biosensor cells containing both the integrase and pReporter12 plasmids were obtained. 
During the promoter and RBS optimization experiment, 1 µL of Golden Gate assembled pBG42 
variant ligation mix was transformed into competent CC118λpir E. coli cells. Descriptions of different 
E. coli strains can be found in Table 1, while plasmids are listed in Table 2.

contained correct plasmids. 
 
2.7 Microtiter growth experiment of biosensors 
Prior to flow cytometry, whole-cell biosensors were tested in a growth experiment to evaluate any 
potential growth impact from carrying the integrase plasmids. Prior to starting the experiment, stock 
solutions of the inducer chemicals were prepared. Anhydrotetracycline (aTc) hydrochloride (Merck 
CAS No. 13803-65-1) was diluted in 1:1 MQ water and EtOH to a stock concentration of 200 µM. 
Naringenin (Merck CAS No. 67604-48-2) was diluted in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) to a stock 
concentration of 100 mM. Sodium salicylate (Merck CAS No. 54-21-7) was diluted in MQ water to 
a stock concentration of 100 mM. All inducer stocks were kept at -20℃ when not in use.  
Biosensor cells were then inoculated to a starting OD600 of 0.01 in selective LB Kan25 Cm10 in 96-
well microtiter plates. Biological triplicates were included for all biosensor variants. Different 
concentrations of inducer chemicals were added to their respective biosensor wells. The maximum 
inductions used were 200 nM aTc for TetR biosensors, 1000 µM naringenin for TtgR biosensors and 
200 µM salicylate for NahR biosensors, respectively.

June 2023 
                                                                                    
 
 
Page | 33  
 
Lastly, biosensor cells were grown for 20 hours at 37℃ with shaking in the 96-well microtiter plates. 
The OD600 of cells was measured and recorded every 10 minutes using the Agilent BioTek Synergy 
H1 Multimode Microplate Reader. Growth data could then be exported and analyzed. 
 
2.8 Flow cytometry assays of biosensors 
Flow cytometry experiments were carried out in extension to microtiter growth experiments. 
Therefore, biosensor cells had been growing in selective LB for 20 hours at 37℃ under different 
induction concentrations prior to flow cytometry. Grown biosensor cells were transferred to a new 
96-well microtiter plate and were diluted 8x in 0.9% NaCl. Flow cytometry was then carried out using 
the MACSQuant® VYB Flow Cytometer from Miltenyi Biotec. In the MACSQuant software, the 
settings used were the 96-rack program with medium flow rate, medium sample mix and an uptake 
volume of 25 µL. This uptake volume typically led to cell counts between 10.000-15.000 cells. Flow 
cytometry data was later analyzed using the licensed FlowJoTM analysis tool, allowing for gating of 
cells, investigation of biosensor flip rates and acquisition of response curves. 
 
2.9 Prediction of improved biosensor genetic elements 
After evaluation of the results obtained from flow cytometry of the degradation tag variants of the 
TetR, TtgR and NahR biosensors, it was clear that TtgR and NahR biosensors did not perform at a 
desired level. In an effort to improve the dynamic range of the TtgR_LVA (JJ002) and NahR_LVA 
(JJ003) biosensors, genetic elements within these biosensors were investigated. Specifically, two 
genetic processes were examined: 1) Transcription initiation rates of the PTtg and PSalTTC promoter 
regions, and 2) Translation rates of ribosomal binding sites downstream from the PTtg and PSalTTC 
promoter regions. Software supplied by Salis Lab - De Novo DNA was used [52]. The promoter 
calculator version 1.0 (predict mode) was used to predict transcription initiation rates across all 
transcription start sites located within the promoter regions of the PTtg and PSalTTC promoters. The

3 Results 
The following sections present the results obtained throughout this project. Results are ordered in a 
mostly chronological fashion to how experiments were conducted. Generally, the findings represent 
an approach to complete the aims described in Section 1.7, beginning with the design and cloning of 
biosensors, testing and evaluation of biosensor performance and lastly optimization efforts to improve 
biosensor performance parameters through the modification of genetic elements. 
 
3.1 Biosensor design and construction 
The first goal was to construct three types of different genetic whole-cell biosensors: TetR 
(tetracycline), TtgR (naringenin) and NahR (salicylate) biosensors. Additionally, we implemented the 
use of peptide degradation tags in the biosensors. Each of the three types of biosensors were thus 
constructed in a set containing four variations: One variant with the strong LVA degradation tag fused 
to Integrase12, one with the medium AAV degradation tag, one with the weak ASV degradation tag 
and a non-tagged variant. The effects of these degradation tags on protein stability were described 
previously in Section 1.5. 
All three of these biosensor types in each of their four variations were successfully cloned, as verified 
by Sanger sequencing. This was achieved through the molecular cloning steps outlined in Section 
2.6, following a cloning procedure visualized in Appendix 7.1 “Flow diagram of the experimental 
cloning procedure”. Thus, twelve whole-cell biosensors were obtained. As described previously in 
Section 1.6, the biosensors’ functions rely on a two-plasmid system, depending on the pReporter12 
plasmid and the pSEVA231::Integrase12 plasmid. Genetic plasmid maps of these can be found in 
Appendix 7.9 “SnapGene maps of reporter and integrase plasmids”.

3.2.1 Investigation of potential growth impact of the biosensor system 
The first experiment aimed to investigate the growth of biosensors. Growth is an important natural 
quality to test, as we desire whole-cell biosensors that are not severely impacted by the carriage of 
their genetic machinery. A previous study conducted by Yang et al. (2014) found that high levels of 
integrase expression can become toxic to E. coli cells [32]. The integrase plasmid has since been 
changed from a high-copy version (pUC-derivative) to a low-copy (pBBR1-derivative) by our group. 
Thus, we decided to test whether growth was affected for our whole-cell biosensors. Growth 
experiments were used to investigate potential integrase toxicity in two ways. First, biosensors were 
grown in the presence of their inducer molecules. This leads to gradually higher expression of the 
integrase protein within the biosensors, as a function of increased inducer concentration. Thus, the 
hypothesis is that if the integrase indeed hampers growth of cells, then a higher inducer concentration 
will lead to reduced growth parameters, such as reduced specific growth rates of the induced cells. 
Second, we compared growth between biosensors containing the LVA degradation tag versus 
biosensors without any degradation tag. As stated earlier, by fusing the LVA degradation tag to the 
integrase, it will lead to a more unstable integrase protein. Thus, the hypothesis is that induced non-
tagged biosensors (NoTag variants) will accumulate more integrase protein over time compared to 
the LVA tagged biosensors. Consequently, we would then expect growth to be worse for induced 
non-tagged biosensors compared to the induced LVA tagged biosensors. Additionally, these growth 
assays also enabled the investigation of any potential toxic activity of the inducing molecules – as 
secondary metabolites often possess antimicrobial properties. 
As described in Section 2.7, whole-cell biosensors JJ001-JJ012 were inoculated in selective LB liquid 
in 96-well microtiter plates. Additionally, different concentrations of their respective inducers were 
added. For the TetR (tetracycline) biosensors, the tetracycline derivative anhydrotetracycline (aTc) 
was used. Anhydrotetracycline is still able to complex with the TetR repressor similarly to 
tetracycline, but it is less toxic and has lower antibiotic activity against E. coli [53]. Biological 
triplicates were included for all biosensors to account for any individual differences between single 
colonies. While growth of all biosensors was tested, two representative growth curves of TetR_LVA 
(JJ001) biosensors and TetR_NoTag (JJ010) biosensors are shown below in Figure 10 and Figure 11.

3.2.2 Proof of concept of biosensor function with flow cytometry 
To examine the functionality of our whole-cell biosensors and verify non-leaky expression in the 
absence of inducer, we used flow cytometry to distinguish between cell populations that experienced 
an active integrase or not. Flow cytometry allows for investigation of multiple properties of cells and 
particles [56]. Fundamentally, flow cytometry works by directing a cell suspension into a focused 
stream in a fluidics system. This allows for single cells to pass through a laser beam [47]. Passing 
through the laser light, the cells will scatter the light at different wavelengths depending on the 
properties of the cells. For instance, forward scatter (FSC) is measured by a detector in front of the 
light beam. FSC of laser light will depend on the ratio between the size of cells and the wavelength 
of the laser used [47]. Thus, FSC will allow for differentiation of cells by size, and FSC intensity is 
proportional to the diameter of the cell. Similarly, side scatter (SSC) of a cell can be measured by a 
detector angled 90º to the laser beam. The side scattered light depends on the internal complexity of 
the cell, also called the cell granularity. This side refraction happens due to intracellular organelles 
==PAGE:48==
Master Thesis │ Jens-Jakob Krogh Nørgaard                                         
June 2023 
                                                                                    
 
 
Page | 41  
 
and other structures within the cell [57]. Combining the information obtained from the FSC and SSC 
of a cell enables distinguishing between different types of cells or particles. 
Furthermore, flow cytometers contain fluorescence detectors that can measure fluorescence emitted 
by fluorophores within cells [47]. This allows for identification of cells that express specific 
fluorophores. This feature is ideal for analysis of our biosensor cells, as it permits the identification 
and counting of cells which only fluoresce red, fluoresce red and green or do not fluoresce at all. This 
enables the grouping of cells into the categories of non-flipped biosensor cells (red fluorescent), 
flipped biosensor cells (red and green fluorescent) and other particles/noise (non-fluorescent). 
We induced biosensor cells by cultivation for 20 hours at different inducer concentrations as 
described in Section 2.8. Biosensor cells were then subjected to flow cytometry. The resulting data 
then had to be analyzed. The FlowJo software was useful for this purpose and allowed for gating of 
cells. An example of the gating process used on the biosensor TetR_LVA (JJ001) is shown in Figure

The first gating step was to define which of the measured particles were E. coli cells. This could be 
done through gating of the FSC and SSC properties of cells. E. coli cells are rod-shaped and are 
approximately 2 µm long and 0.5 µm in diameter [58]. We thus chose a gating strategy that captured 
most events but eliminated events with very low or very high FSC and SSC. Some events are expected 
to be particles, cell fragments or debris, and these should be mostly eliminated using this strategy. 
Next, we had to verify that the counted cells were in fact biosensor cells. This was achieved by gating 
based on the red fluorescent properties of the cells, as all living biosensor cells should fluoresce red 
because of their constitutive expression of dsRed. Therefore, a gate that captured the cells that 
fluoresce red at a value above 1 was added. It should be noted that the far majority of cells (> ~97%) 
across all different biosensor types did display red fluorescence (data not shown). This further 
indicated that cloning and transformation of the biosensor system into cells was generally successful.  
Lastly, the biosensor cells that had sensed their inducer and correctly inverted the orientation of the 
gfp gene due to integrase expression were counted. These cells are called the “flipped” population. 
To quantify these cells, two gates were added to a histogram displaying green fluorescence. The gates 
then captured either non-flipped or flipped cells. 
In Figure 12b above the TetR_LVA biosensor cells had been induced by 200 nM aTc. It is clear that 
the far majority of these TetR_LVA biosensor cells had flipped to fluoresce green. However, the 
opposite is true for the un-induced TetR_LVA biosensor cells seen in Figure 12a – these only 
displayed red fluorescence. 
This increase in flip-rate to green fluorescence of biosensor cells was proof that the concept of our 
two-plasmid biosensor system functioned as intended. A similar approach was carried out for the 
remaining eleven whole-cell biosensors. The next step was to investigate biosensor performance and 
evaluate how the difference in degradation tags influence the functionality of these biosensor variants.

3.2.3 Evaluation of biosensor performance through response curves 
The function and performance of twelve whole-cell biosensors had to be evaluated and quantified 
after flow cytometry proved the concept of the biosensor system. Several performance parameters 
can be used to evaluate the efficiency of a whole-cell biosensor. The performance metrics that were 
tested here included the false positive rate of the biosensors (leakiness), the dynamic range of the 
biosensors and the sensitivity of the biosensors. How these performance metrics related to our 
biosensors will be summed up here. 
The first important parameter to assess was the false positive rate of the biosensors [17]. A biosensor 
may respond with an incorrect false positive output signal despite not sensing its target inducer. In 
our biosensor system, a false positive response will typically arise due to erroneous expression of the 
integrase protein, as the integrase is key to the flip of the gfp gene. Erroneous expression can happen 
if the promoter controlling the integrase expression is leaky. Leakiness of the promoter allows for 
low expression of the integrase even when repression has not been alleviated or activation has not 
been induced by addition of the inducer molecule. It is therefore vital to counteract the leakiness of 
the integrase promoter. This is exacerbated by the fact that the flip of the gfp gene is irreversible in 
our biosensors, as the RDF is not present. If leakiness is not prevented, then flipped false positive 
biosensor cells will accumulate over time, rendering the biosensor mostly useless.  
Another vital performance metric is the dynamic range [17]. Dynamic range can be interpreted as the 
intensity of the biosensor response. For our biosensors this is the difference in flip-rate between the 
un-induced biosensor cells and the flip-rate of the maximally induced biosensor cells. 
The sensitivity of a biosensor is defined as the concentration of inducer required to activate and turn 
on 50% of biosensor cells [22]. Therefore, high sensitivity of biosensors is an advantage, as the 
biosensors will be able to sense and respond to low inducer concentrations. 
To visualize the performance of the twelve biosensors, response curves were created. These response 
curves show the percentage of biosensor cells that have flipped to green fluorescence as a function of 
the concentration of the biosensor’s respective inducer. Response curves for all biosensors are

in previous TetR biosensor studies, such as those obtained by Meyer et al. (2019) [22]. Based on these 
performance metrics, the TetR_LVA (JJ001) is deemed the best functioning TetR biosensor variant

3.3 Improving biosensor performance through modification of genetic elements 
The performance of the two biosensors TtgR_LVA (JJ002) and NahR_LVA (JJ003) was not deemed 
adequate. Especially their dynamic ranges were lackluster and had to be improved. Several genetic 
tuning strategies exist to alter the response function and performance of genetic circuits. Common 
strategies involve the alteration of one of the following: promoter strength, ribosomal binding site 
(RBS) strength or protein degradation rates [6]. The alteration of protein degradation rate was a 
strategy that we had already employed by introducing the degradation tag variants LVA, AAV and 
ASV, which decreased the stability of the integrase protein to various degrees. Therefore, we looked 
to apply one of the other strategies.  
The goal was to increase the dynamic range of TtgR_LVA (JJ002) and NahR_LVA (JJ003) without 
making the biosensors leaky. Our hypothesis was that this could be achieved by increasing either the 
strength of the integrase promoter or the strength of the RBS located upstream of the integrase gene. 
These two strategies each work by tuning one of two fundamental processes involved in the 
expression of the integrase protein. Tuning of the promoter strength will alter the initiation of 
transcription, while tuning of the RBS strength will alter initiation of translation. Ideally, both 
strategies should be tested. However, for the sake of cloning and time efficiency, we wanted to choose 
a strategy that had potential to be implemented easily in all biosensor variants. To do this, it was 
important to take a closer look on the integrase promoter regions and genetic context found within 
the three different types of biosensors.

An additional element of note within the 5’ untranslated regions (5’ UTRs) of the biosensors is the 
RiboJ ribozyme insulator. This genetic element was designed by Lou et al. (2012) and was shown to 
be successful in insulating against genetic effects on reporter gene expression [60], [32]. However, it 
was found that the presence of the RiboJ ribozyme insulator impacts the transcriptional function of 
the promoter. Therefore, we decided to also investigate the genetic effects of this element.  
 
3.3.1 Predicted effects of changes to genetic elements 
Changes to two genetic elements, the RBS and the RiboJ ribozyme insulator, needed to be explored. 
However, to rationally engineer these elements in a controlled manner, it was important to first obtain 
information about how changes to these elements would affect the outcome of integrase expression. 
Fortunately, several bioinformatic software options exist, which can predict such genetic effects. One 
option is the software provided by Salis Lab called De Novo DNA [52]. This website contains several 
tools that can aid in genetic design, optimization, control and prediction. Specifically, the tools that 
were useful to the efforts of this project were the Promoter Calculator and the RBS Library Calculator 
tools [61], [59]. These tools are based on thermodynamic models and were used to investigate 
transcription initiation and translation initiation properties within the integrase promoter regions and 
5’ UTRs of the biosensors.  
The Promoter Calculator version 1.0 (predict mode) was used to predict transcription initiation rates 
across all transcription start sites (TSSs) located within the promoter regions of the PTtg and PSalTTC 
promoters. As of version 1.0, the algorithm only allows for prediction in host organism Escherichia 
coli strain K-12 substrain MG1655 (NCBI accession: NC_ 000913), and therefore this was used.  
To include as many off-target effects as possible, we uploaded a region starting 85 nt upstream from 
PTtg/PSalTTC and ending at the start codon of the integrase gene, which thus included the 75 nt long 
RiboJ ribozyme insulator and a 26 nt RBS-containing DNA sequence downstream from 
PTtg/PSalTTC. Submitting this region to the tool allowed for investigation of the transcription 
initiation profiles of the PTtg and PSalTTC promoters. We observed predicted transcription in the 
correct orientation. However, we also observed a -35 and -10 element in the opposite orientation. The 
presence of these reverse -35 and -10 elements allowing for reverse transcription was due to the

Page | 50  
 
location of the TtgR operator. Since this genetic element could not be moved as described above, we 
sought to investigate alternative options to how potential reverse transcription could be avoided. See

been lowered compared to that of the 75 nt RiboJ PTtg variant. However, Figure 17d shows that the 
change makes a large difference for the predicted reverse transcription rate: The highest transcription

have any effect on RiboJ’s insulating effect, as these nucleotides are not involved in formation of any 
hairpins or other secondary structures. To sum up, removing the first two nucleotides from the RiboJ 
ribozyme insulator improves the predicted transcription initiation profile for the PTtg promoter region 
by minimizing reverse transcription initiation. Therefore, it was decided that a 73 nt RiboJ variant of 
PTtg should be cloned and investigated experimentally. 
The PSalTTC promoter region of the NahR_LVA (JJ003) biosensor was also investigated with the 
Promoter Calculator. However, introducing the shortened 73 nt RiboJ variant into the PSalTTC did 
not improve its predicted transcription profile. It is likely that the sequence of the PSalTTC promoter 
does not result in formation of reverse -10 and -35 elements as was the case for PTtg. Consequently, 
cloning of a shortened 73 nt RiboJ PSalTTC variant is not a viable strategy for the optimization of 
the NahR_LVA (JJ003) biosensor. 
 
We saw previously how it was necessary to add the LVA degradation tag to eliminate leakiness of 
the integrase promoter. However, as was illustrated by Andersen et al. (1998) and presented in Section 
1.5, the LVA tag is much stronger compared to the AAV tag. This hampers the dynamic range of the 
TtgR_LVA (JJ002) and NahR_LVA (JJ003) biosensors. Thus, our hypothesis was that by increasing 
the strength of the integrase RBS slightly, we should be able to hit an integrase expression sweet spot 
at which these biosensors obtain improved dynamic ranges while remaining non-leaky.

4 Discussion and Future Perspectives 
The results obtained during this study have several important implications and related considerations, 
which will be discussed during this section. Additionally, this project represents a design and 
engineering approach for the genetic construction of whole-cell biosensors. However, it should be 
acknowledged that alternative strategies exist, which may yield novel information or improved 
results. Therefore, limitations, alternative approaches, future applications and perspectives of our 
whole-cell biosensors will be explored.  
 
4.1 Growth of biosensor cells and long-term impact of the integrase protein 
In Section 3.2.1 the growth of the biosensors was investigated. The main findings were that carriage 
and expression of the integrase plasmid did not adversely affect growth of the TetR, TtgR and NahR 
biosensors. The addition of degradation tags also did not seem to significantly change their growth 
dynamics. For the TtgR biosensors, the maximal OD600 of cells was impacted by the presence of 
high naringenin concentrations (500 µM and 1000 µM induction concentrations, respectively). 
However, this growth impact was explained by the fact that naringenin is shown to possess 
antimicrobial activity against Gram-positives and to a lesser extent Gram-negatives like E. coli [55]. 
Consequently, the conclusion of the growth experiment was that presence of neither integrase nor 
degradation tags matter much in relation to growth dynamics of biosensors. However, it should be 
noted that the growth experiments were carried out for a relatively short period of time of 20 hours. 
This is comparatively short if we aim to use our biosensors in more complex environments such as 
in soil-like scenarios. Ideally, biosensors should be capable of in situ use, where prolonged monitoring 
of microbial interactions within e.g. the soil microbiome is of great interest. It is plausible that 
continued integrase production over longer time periods could impact growth or metabolism of the 
biosensor cells. This is something that should be tested experimentally. Nonetheless, for extended 
application of biosensors, there is a good argument for the addition of degradation tags. The peptide 
degradation tags lead to more unstable integrase protein via increased protease degradation. Thus, 
integrase will not remain in cells or potentially accumulate to high concentrations over time. This 
should limit the growth and metabolic burdens that may be associated with large amounts of 
intracellular integrase. However, it should be recognized that the dynamics of degradation tags and 
the function of the cell proteases that confer the protein degradation must be confirmed under new 
environmental conditions of novel use scenarios. There can be marked differences in protease activity 
==PAGE:68==
Master Thesis │ Jens-Jakob Krogh Nørgaard                                         
June 2023 
                                                                                    
 
 
Page | 61  
 
based on environmental parameters such as changes to pH and temperature, and thus protease activity 
will often vary widely between lab conditions and field-relevant conditions [62]. The issue of 
conditional biosensor function and optimization will be discussed further in Section 4.4. 
 
4.2 Biosensor specificity and cross-reactivity 
When testing the function of whole-cell biosensors, there are many performance parameters that are 
relevant. In Section 3.2.3, we evaluated several of these performance metrics, including the false 
positive rate (leakiness), the dynamic range and the sensitivity of our biosensors JJ001-JJ012. 
However, we did not investigate one of the most crucial biosensor characteristics – specificity. A 
biosensor should be specific for its cognate inducer, whilst simultaneously not cross-reacting with 
non-cognate inducers [17], [22]. However, non-target small molecules may still be able to bind to 
biosensor regulators, resulting in cross-reactivity and ultimately an unreliable biosensor output.  
Therefore, the investigation of cross-reactivity is an important point for future work. One simple 
initial experiment could test the orthogonality of our TetR, TtgR and NahR biosensors by adding the 
non-cognate inducers to the three biosensors. Then it would be possible to observe whether e.g. the 
TetR (tetracycline) biosensors respond to induction by naringenin or salicylate. However, more 
extensive experiments testing specificity will have to be conducted if we aim to develop our 
biosensors for use in more complex environments. As an example, the TtgR (naringenin) biosensors 
could be subjected to non-cognate flavonoids with similar chemical properties to naringenin. These 
related compounds would be more likely to induce a non-specific response in comparison to less 
similar compounds such as tetracycline and salicylate. 
On the other hand, the term cross-reactivity could also refer to molecules interacting with the 
regulators used in our biosensors. Thus, our whole-cell biosensors could also serve the purpose of 
investigating potential unknown interactions affecting the regulation posed by TetR, TtgR, and NahR. 
This in turn reflects the increased or decreased transcription of biosynthetic gene clusters often 
controlled by such regulators, and hence the biosynthesis of their associated metabolites.

4.3 Alternative strategies for optimization of biosensor performance 
During this study we attempted to optimize biosensor performance through the genetic tuning of 
integrase gene expression. An RBS calculator tool was used to predict alternative RBSs with 
increased translation rates. We saw indications that this strategy could produce biosensor candidates 
with improved dynamic range, although it must be verified that this is not achieved at the expense of 
increased leakiness. However, a large range of different expression control “knobs” exist for fine-
tuning of transcription and translation, many of which were introduced in Section 1.5. For tuning of 
transcription, changes to promoter -10 and -35 sequences alter the binding affinity of RNA 
polymerase. Likewise, changes to operator sequences may modify the binding of transcriptional 
regulators. Additionally, variation in copy numbers of the transcriptional regulator gene as well as 
the output gene can alter expression dynamics [42], [63]. Tuning of translation can be achieved by 
changing the RBS sequence, and post-translational degradation also affects the final expressional 
output. Several of these strategies have been utilized to tune biosensor function prior to and during 
this study. However, it is likely that applying tuning strategies in different combinations may lead to 
further improvements in biosensor performance. 
Here, we chose to tune integrase expression by alteration of integrase RBS strength. One alternative 
strategy proposal could be to instead tune promoter or RBS strength of the transcriptional regulator 
gene. The TtgR transcriptional repressor and the NahR transcriptional activator are expressed by their 
respective genes located downstream from the integrase gene on the integrase plasmids. Refer to 
Appendix 7.9 “SnapGene maps of reporter and integrase plasmids” for a visual overview. If we 
hypothesize tuning of the regulators’ RBS in a manner that increases the translation of the respective 
transcriptional regulator, then that will lead to a different regulation dynamic. More TtgR 
transcriptional repressor would result in increased repression of the integrase promoter, thus leading 
to a less leaky biosensor. However, this would likely come at the cost of decreased sensitivity, as 
more inducer would be required to fully alleviate repression. The opposite scenario is true in the case 
of increased translation of the transcriptional activator, NahR. Here, more activator would likely lead 
to increased sensitivity of the biosensor, although probably at the cost of increased leakiness. It is 
likely that an optimal combination of the different control knobs exist, and this is something that will 
be highly relevant for guiding future development on the biosensors.

4.4 Limitations of the chosen development strategy of biosensors 
During this project we constructed and obtained a biosensor TetR_LVA (JJ001) with high sensitivity, 
low leakiness and a large dynamic range. Additionally, efforts were made to improve the performance 
of the biosensors TtgR_LVA (JJ002) and NahR_LVA (JJ003), which resulted in the construction of 
eleven additional biosensor strains JJ013-JJ023. However, it is important to note that these biosensors 
were all constructed and improved under a particular set of narrow conditions. These were the 
conditions typical for laboratory experiments: Growth was carried out in monocultures, in rich LB 
medium, at 37℃, with generally short incubation times of ~20 hours. Most importantly these 
biosensor systems were kept within their chassis organism, Escherichia coli, which typically is not 
well-adapted for use outside the lab, although it has been used in situ for detection of secondary 
metabolites in soil [64]. This illustrates that development and optimization of our biosensors were 
carried out within the context of a very narrow use case scenario. While all great things begin 
development with an initial proof of concept, it is recognized that the conditions found in real-life 
applications are all but simple. It must be emphasized that microorganisms do not live in solitude. In 
contrast, they live amongst billions of other microbes in intricate, complex communities. In this light, 
it is possible that the biosensors constructed in this study cannot be expected to function optimally 
for real-life applications. We will need to conduct a series of engineering, optimization and 
development steps before our biosensors can be applied to new scenarios. However, the potential and 
groundwork are there. During Section 1.3, several great examples were presented, in which scientists 
have used whole-cell biosensors and genetic memory devices for a large range of fascinating 
applications. The toolbox of synthetic biology is rapidly expanding, allowing increased control over 
biosensors, and this will likely lead to further application possibilities. In this study, we have 
developed and demonstrated an initial strategy for construction of functional whole-cell biosensors. 
Our optimization work continues with the end goal of applying our biosensors to gradually more 
complex use cases.

4.5 Future perspectives and development of biosensors 
The whole-cell biosensors constructed during this project provide a great framework for further 
developments. With the potential of whole-cell biosensors in mind, some relevant future perspectives 
and developmental strategies should be discussed.  
During this study, we designed our biosensors to respond with easily measurable fluorescent proteins. 
Green and red fluorescent proteins allowed for simple investigation of the function of the biosensors 
and subsequent quantification through flow cytometry. Therefore, this was an advantageous design 
during this preliminary chapter of development. However, it has been highlighted in Section 1.4 that 
this type of reporter output often is not practical to use in real-life settings. Thus, for the future 
development of our biosensors a principal goal is to focus on their use as genetic memory devices. 
The two-plasmid design and the incorporation of the serine integrase as the mediator between them 
opens a range of possibilities in this regard. For instance, it would be rational to exchange the inverted 
gfp gene for a unique “barcode” sequence, which could easily be recognized as flipped or non-flipped 
using PCR or sequencing for evaluation. This approach could draw inspiration from Yang et al. 
(2014), who designed a memory array consisting of unique spacers placed between attachment sites 
cognate to different integrases [32]. They then designed primers that bind to the interface between 
the attachment sites and the inverted spacer sequence, resulting in amplification only if the spacer 
had been correctly inverted by the integrase. In our genetic memory biosensor system, this approach 
would link the sensing of an inducer directly to genetic memory in the form of an inverted sequence 
barcode, which could subsequently be PCR amplified for easy verification. Furthermore, this 
approach would alleviate some of the cellular metabolic stress associated with production of 
fluorescence proteins. This exact strategy was applied successfully for in vivo use by Mimee et al. 
(2015) [28]. They were able to use qPCR to quantify flip rate of a genetic memory biosensor 
containing a rhamnose inducible memory array that had been colonizing the murine gut microbiota. 
Development of our biosensors into genetic memory devices allows for many additional future use 
cases. One such use case could be monitoring and control of interactions within hard-to-reach 
environments. A prime example is the potential application of our biosensors within the soil 
microbiome. In Section 1.1 it was explored how the flavonoid naringenin and the plant phytohormone 
salicylic acid were important to the modulation and composition of the rhizosphere microbiome. 
Applying genetic memory biosensors capable of measuring and recording such chemical signals is 
potentially of great value within agriculture. We saw how naringenin was important to the promotion 
==PAGE:72==
Master Thesis │ Jens-Jakob Krogh Nørgaard                                         
June 2023 
                                                                                    
 
 
Page | 65  
 
of nodulation and subsequent nitrogen-fixation in legumes. Liu et al. (2019) reported a significant 
linear relationship between naringenin content and the number and dry weight of nodules [15]. 
Furthermore, legumes were found to increase naringenin secretion when deficient in nitrogen [15]. A 
genetic memory biosensor recording the presence of naringenin is thus useful to continually evaluate 
the degree to which these plants are nitrogen starved. This would theoretically allow for a more 
efficient fertilization effort, in which nitrogen fertilizer could be provided only when plants require 
this nutrient. Taking this a step further, we could imagine changing the output of the genetic memory 
biosensors to one that provides a “plant therapeutic” function of some kind, e.g. by coupling the 
sensing of naringenin to a nitrogen-fixation response. In a study conducted by Ryu et al. (2020) they 
managed to move the nitrogen fixation gene cluster nif to heterologous organisms such as 
Pseudomonas stutzeri and Pseudomonas protegens, providing them with nitrogenase function [65]. 
Furthermore, they managed to make the nitrogen-fixation inducible using biosensor genetic circuits. 
This illustrates the legality of such a perspective and highlights the potential extensive agricultural 
perspectives.  
Taken together, it is undeniable that whole-cell biosensors and genetic memory devices hold vast 
potential in many different scenarios across several scientific fields. However, the perspectives 
presented for the development of our biosensors require genetic engineering and tuning. Additionally, 
it is recognized that GMO legislation and regulatory issues exist once it comes to application of 
genetically modified biosensors in real-life scenarios. While these bottlenecks must be overcome, it 
should be remembered that whole-cell biosensors and genetic memory devices represent a unique and 
highly adaptable set of detection and recording tools. In the future, it is all but certain that they will 
continue to contribute to increased understanding, engineering and control of microbial interactions 
and communities.

5 Conclusion 
In this study, we aimed to design and construct a series of whole-cell biosensors capable of sensing 
and responding to three agriculturally relevant chemical metabolites: tetracycline, naringenin and 
salicylate. Different degradation tag variants of TetR (tetracycline), TtgR (naringenin) and NahR 
(salicylate) biosensors were successfully cloned, and their growth and response functions were 
verified. The performance of the biosensors was evaluated by investigating performance metrics such 
as leakiness, dynamic range and sensitivity. It was found that the strong LVA degradation tag was 
vital to obtain non-leaky biosensors. Further performance testing found that the biosensor TetR_LVA 
(JJ001) performed well on all tested parameters. It was non-leaky, had a large dynamic range and 
high sensitivity. However, performance of the biosensors TtgR_LVA (JJ002) and NahR_LVA 
(JJ003) was subpar, and thus optimization strategies were devised and carried out in an effort to 
improve these biosensors. This led to the cloning of eleven modified biosensors, JJ013-JJ023. While 
preliminary observations of these new biosensors indicate that candidates with improved performance 
may be present, the final conclusive evidence of their performance was not obtained.  
In conclusion, this study has contributed to broadening the catalogue of available whole-cell 
biosensors for detection and recording of important secondary metabolites. Furthermore, it 
exemplifies a strategy for genetic cloning, verification and optimization of novel whole-cell 
biosensors, which will guide future efforts to further expand the biosensor repertoire.

7.9 SnapGene maps of reporter and integrase plasmids 
The pReporter12 plasmid shown below provides the whole-cell biosensors with fluorescence outputs 
by allowing for expression of red fluorescent protein (mRFP1) and green fluorescent protein (gfp). 
The promoters for both reporter genes are constitutive, however, gfp orientation is reversed and thus 
this gene will only be expressed in the presence of integrase protein. pReporter12 is common to all 
biosensors and must be present inside the cells to obtain measurable outputs from the biosensor 
system.

The pSEVA231::Integrase12 plasmids shown below provide whole-cell biosensors with the ability 
to sense their inducer (anhydrotetracycline, naringenin or salicylate, respectively). Most elements 
within the integrase plasmids are common to all biosensors. However, varying genetic elements 
between the three different types of biosensors include: The promoter region upstream from 
Integrase12 (PTet*, PTtg or PSalTTC), the degradation tag fused to Integrase12 (either LVA, AAV, 
ASV or NoTag), the transcriptional regulator gene (tetR, ttgR or nahR), its RBS (tet1, ttg2 or nah2)

7.11 Primer overview 
All primers used in this project are described in Table 7 below. 
Table 7: Descriptions and sequences of the primers used in this project. Most primers used in this project were designed to be 
compatible with Golden Gate assembly. For Golden Gate assembly primers to function optimally, they must include the following: 
Firstly, starting in the 5’ end, three to six arbitrary nucleotides NNNNNN (marked in uppercase yellow) are added. These increase the 
efficiency of the assembly reaction. Then follows GGTCTC and a spacer nucleotide N (marked in bold uppercase letters), which is the 
recognition site of the BsaI type IIS restriction enzyme. Downstream from the recognition site, four bases (marked in red) denominate 
the Golden Gate overhang, which directs the assembly and ligation. Following this, an optional overhang sequence of bases (marked 
in italics) can be introduced into the target sequence via the primers. These optional bases were included e.g. to introduce alternative 
ribosomal binding sites (marked in cyan) or promoter sequences into the target sequence. Lastly, the binding part of the primer (marked 
in small letters) allows the primer to bind to the target sequence.
