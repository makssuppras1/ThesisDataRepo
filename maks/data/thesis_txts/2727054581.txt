INTRODUCTION 
 
Throughout the last decades there has been an increase in the number of entrepreneurship 
courses and programs offered in higher education institutions. (Piperopoulos et al., 2015) All 
academic activities that are intended to promote an entrepreneurial mindset and aptitudes 
fall under the category of "entrepreneurship education", which encompasses a wide variety 
of topics including idea development, analysis of business situations, growth, and innovation. 
(Arasti et al., 2012) Innovation is seen as an internal driver in entrepreneurial education, as it 
relates to the concept of “entrepreneurial mindset” and represents a crucial element in the 
development of new ventures. (Wei et al., 2019) Therefore, throughout this paper, the aspect 
of innovation will be contained in the term “entrepreneurial education”. Moreover, the high 
importance that entrepreneurial education has in society is highlighted by various studies 
concluding that this is one of the fastest-growing fields of education in the world.  (Solomon, 
2007) It is theorized that the growth of this discipline is fueled by the belief that these 
programs can improve learners’ attitudes, abilities, competencies, and thus increase the 
number of individuals that start new ventures. (Barron and Ruiz, 2021) As it will be later 
detailed, education in entrepreneurship encompasses a wide range of audiences and 
instructional techniques. (Borchers, 2011) 
 
Furthermore, the rapid development of Information and Communication Technologies 
initiated an ongoing fundamental transformation of the societies we live in, modifying 
amongst others, the way we teach and learn. (Coiro et al., 2013) Additionally, the COVID-19 
pandemic and increased accessibility to affordable Internet connectivity had a substantial 
impact on the field of education. (Bai et al., 2021) More specifically, the shift from traditional 
in-person instruction to digital teaching and learning has accelerated drastically. (ibid.) In fact, 
e-learning quickly became one of the fastest-growing industries worldwide and is projected 
to observe a 20% CAGR by 2028 conforming to Global Market Insights (2022).  
 
In entrepreneurial education, the transition from analog to digital education presents 
numerous new opportunities for teachers and students and has the potential to facilitate the 
advancement of innovative pedagogical practices. (Nanjundaswamy et al., 2021) For instance, 
technology enables learners to access state-of-the-art resources from around the globe, 
augmenting their perception of different entrepreneurial topics, as well as allowing them to 
connect with communities of practice across geographical boundaries. (Greenhow et al., 
2022) For educators, some of the benefits of using novel technologies in teaching reside in 
enabling them to design engaging and motivating virtual learning environments using rich 
media sources, serious games, simulations, augmented and virtual reality, amongst many 
others. (Bai et al., 2021) Similarly, teachers can now deliver synchronous and asynchronous 
learning units, and create interactive sessions that often include a vast array of individual and 
group activities mediated by different educational software. (ibid.) As a result, it is theorized 
that through the meaningful integration of technologies into entrepreneurial teaching, 
educators can potentially increase students’ interest in learning about entrepreneurship, 
whilst preparing them to solve complex societal problems in the context of a rapidly changing 
world. (Ramana et al., 2019; Rodrigues et al., 2019) 
 
However, integrating technology into teaching practices also brought about diverse 
intricacies and challenges. (Dillenbourg, 2016) Moreover, owing to the accelerated

development of technology in education, teaching, and learning have changed in ways that 
are still not completely understood despite decades of research. (Cowling et al., 2022) The 
present study aims at contributing to this field by investigating the effects of two prevalent, 
but distinct pedagogies applied to the discipline of entrepreneurship in the context of e-
learning in higher education.  
 
LITERATURE REVIEW 
 
The advent of e-learning provides numerous new opportunities for developing 
entrepreneurial knowledge, skills, and mindset. (Chen et al., 2022) This had a profound impact 
on teachers and students alike by creating a paradigm shift in how courses are delivered. 
(Oliver, 2022) As education transitioned from the classroom into virtual learning 
environments, increased pressure was placed on educators to deliver high-level online 
experiences for learners. (Dillenbourg, 2016) The complexity of creating learning units 
increased due to the flexibility and affordances of technology-driven solutions. (ibid.) 
 
As educators are striving to respond to the demands of the digital age, a common pitfall is to 
rely on technology to improve the learning process in and of itself. (Cowling et al., 2022) A 
prevalent issue in digitalized teaching is that oftentimes course materials are repackaged from 
the classroom setting and implemented directly into the online environment. (Bangert, 2008) 
These instances of “forcing content to fit into available technology”, a phenomenon also 
known as “shovelware”, are likely to generate an inferior result to that of the physical model 
which they imitate. (Kehrwald and Parker, 2019) This is because merely digitalizing curricula 
via technology does not take into consideration the new dynamics of teaching and learning in 
virtual settings. (Crawford, 2021) Thus, it is common in online courses that the affordances of 
novel technologies are underutilized, and that the quality of the instruction is compromised. 
(Cowling et al., 2022) Furthermore, the improper integration of technology into the e-learning 
course design can lead to distractions. (Rasheed et al., 2020; Greenhow et al., 2022) In some 
cases, students spend more time learning how to utilize the technology, taking the focus away 
from the content of the educational materials presented. (ibid.) This emphasizes the fact that 
educators must maintain a balance between learners’ “technological needs and sufficiency”. 
(Rasheed et al., 2020) Another discrepancy between the use of technology and educational 
theory in e-learning is argued for by scholars such as Bates (2015). He emphasizes the fact 
that literature on e-learning does not present practicable theories for selecting technology 
suitable for different disciplines, topics, learning tasks, and student populations. Therefore, 
he argues that in most cases the choice of technology is dictated by economic and human-
political factors, rather than pedagogical considerations. (ibid.) Ertmer and Newby (2016) 
highlight the fact too often online education is developed through an act of adapting 
instructional materials to match available technology, thus illustrating the technology push 
scenario in education. It is claimed that e-learning development should be done through a 
process of collaboration between educational theorists and technology providers. (ibid.)  
 
While scholars are debating different perspectives on the implications of the digital era on 
learning and teaching in entrepreneurial education, it must be retained that effective, 
efficient acquisition of knowledge cannot happen if the fundamental conditions for learning 
are not met. (Merrill, 1996) In this context, it is sustained in literature that teachers must be 
==PAGE:5==
 
 
3
knowledgeable of a variety of pedagogical approaches and instructional practices, as well as 
have a deep comprehension of their appropriate use. (Ertmer and Newby, 2013) Therefore, 
it is often argued that the success of online education depends on educators’ abilities to 
create online learning experiences that are well-designed from both pedagogical and 
instructional standpoints. (Orellana et al., 2009) 
 
The term "pedagogy" refers to the theory and practice of teaching. (Watkins, 1999) Educators 
possess different pedagogical orientations based on their perceptions regarding the design of 
the learning process and their views on the roles of students and teachers alike. (Law, 2009) 
Over the past century, pedagogical approaches have been based on the major modern 
learning theories of behaviorism, cognitivism, and constructivism. (Ertmer and Newby, 2013) 
These learning theories are underpinned by epistemological beliefs and conceptualizations of 
knowledge acquisition. (Paniagua, 2018) Accordingly, these have been developed through a 
combination of education philosophy and psychology, with additional influences from 
adjacent disciplines such as sociology, and neurobiology. (Hammond et al., 2001) However, 
the learning theories of behaviorism, cognitivism, and constructivism do not reflect the 
dynamics of virtual learning environments as they were developed before learning was 
impacted by technology. (Hendricks, 2019) Thus, to create optimal e-learning experiences 
that lead to achieving desired outcomes, an educator must not only rely on pedagogy but also 
utilize knowledge from the field of instructional design (ID). (Keengwe et al., 2014) The field 
of ID has been developed through scientific inquiry and empirical testing to enable the 
systematic development of instruction. (Wagner, 2011) Unlike pedagogy, ID does not stem 
from philosophy and psychology, but it rather represents a collection of strategies and 
techniques for creating learning materials and experiences. (ibid.) The role of ID is to connect 
technology and pedagogy and to keep the process of learning at the forefront. (Siemens, 
2002) Furthermore, ID principles and models can help students achieve academic 
performance within virtual environments, by bridging the gap between theory and practice 
whilst supporting the authentic use of technology in education. (Keengwe et al., 2014)  
 
Throughout time, numerous models and theories have been developed within the field of ID. 
(Reiser, 2001) However, it must be emphasized that not all ID theories apply to every situation 
or task. (Siemens,2002) Numerous studies show that high academic achievement in online 
environments is correlated with distinct learner characteristics such as well-developed self-
regulatory skills, metacognitive learning skills, critical thinking, self-efficacy capacities, and 
intrinsic goal orientations. (Broadbent et al., 2015; Kebritchi et al., 2017; Keskin, 2019; Martin 
et al., 2020; O’neill et al., 2004; Sharin, 2021; Tawafak et al., 2020) These findings led various 
scholars to conclude that online education is more suitable for adult learners, or those with 
an advanced level of education. (Singh et al., 2009; Kahu et al., 2013; Arghode et al., 2017). 
This further affirms the fact that teaching entrepreneurial topics in online formats is a suitable 
and promising approach, especially for students enrolled in higher education.  
 
As e-learning was found to be more suitable for mature students that can deploy deeper 
learning strategies, an important perspective in ID that addresses these specific learner 
characteristics is the theory of andragogy popularized by Malcolm Knowles. (Singh et al., 
2009; Kahu et al., 2013; Ferreira, 2017) Andragogy is a theory that outlines the principles that 
educators must consider to successfully teach adult learners. (Holton et al., 2009) 
Incorporating adult learning principles into the design of online courses is imperative for 
==PAGE:6==
 
 
4
enhancing learning outcomes through adequate instructional design and delivery of content. 
(Ferreira, 2017) Knowles theorized a set of characteristics for adult learners. (ibid.) Firstly, he 
argued that adults are autonomous and self-directed. (Johnson, 2014) Therefore, teachers 
should guide the students in their learning, whilst allowing them to take ownership of the 
process. (Holton et al., 2009) The next principle is that adults have a variety of life experiences 
and knowledge from their past. (Johnson, 2014) As a result, educators should treat students 
as equal partners in the learning process and recognize that their experience is a good 
repository for learning. (Keyon and Hase, 2001) Therefore, teachers should draw connections 
between the current learning topics and the relevant knowledge and experience background 
of learners. (Holton et al., 2009) Furthermore, the theory of andragogy claims that adults 
are goal-oriented, and relevancy-oriented. (Keyon and Hase, 2001) More specifically, adults 
are more intrinsically motivated and strive to acquire knowledge on subjects that matter to 
them. (ibid.) Accordingly, adult learning tasks should be life-focused, and problem-centric. 
(ibid.) Thus, educators must clearly describe the course objectives and correlate those with 
their relative uses, emphasizing the value of what the students are learning in connection to 
their professional or personal life. (Holton et al., 2009)  
 
As the field of entrepreneurial education is expanding and maturing, increasing emphasis is 
placed on rendering more effective teaching methods. (Hägg et al., 2016) Additionally, efforts 
are 
directed 
toward 
establishing 
a

instruction. (ibid.) However, current research conclusions on practicable theories for 
selecting appropriate technologies, pedagogical approaches, and instructional strategies are 
deemed inconsistent. (Bates, 2015; Yang et al., 2021) Similar observations were made by 
Sirelkhatim and Gangi (2015) who explored the issue in depth. They found a lack of consensus 
on how entrepreneurship education is taught and what the curricula should 
contain. (ibid.)  Furthermore, they found a wide variety of courses and an absence of 
uniformity. (ibid.) However, they devised one of the most prominent classifications in the 
field of entrepreneurial education. (ibid.) This classification is grounded in pedagogical 
differences, respectively grouped around three themes named “about”, “for” and 
“through”. (ibid.) The “about” approach is a theory-oriented method, intending to expand 
students’ knowledge and increase their awareness of entrepreneurship as a career 
choice. (ibid.) The other two approaches are practice-oriented, aimed at building 
entrepreneurial skills. (ibid.)  The “for” approach adopts simulation and role-play methods, 
whereas the “through” approach emphasizes learners acquiring real experiences in 
entrepreneurial processes. (ibid.)  A similar classification has been made by Piperopoulos and 
Dimov (2015) that simply differentiated between theory-oriented courses and practice-
oriented courses in entrepreneurial education.  
 
In recent years, numerous sources argued that a practice-oriented approach, or the “for” and 
“through” pedagogies, are more appropriate in entrepreneurial education. (Kakouris, 2009; 
McFarland, 2017; Olusegun, 2015) It must be highlighted that due to a lack of data, it was not 
possible to determine if the inclination towards practice-oriented approaches is only 
recommended in literature or if its prevalence can be observed in actual teaching practices 
worldwide. (Ismail et al., 2018) However, it has been observed that in cultures where student-
centered education is the norm, such as in many Western counties, that the practice-oriented 
approach is highly favored. (ibid.) On this account, it is sustained that the development of 
entrepreneurship education should be influenced by educational theories that have action 
==PAGE:7==
 
 
5
and change in focus, predominantly the constructivism and experiential learning theory. 
(Oliver et al., 2022) Constructivism is a student-centered learning theory based on the 
perspective that reality is internal, and knowledge is individually constructed. (Siemens, 2005) 
In this context, the teacher’s role is one of a facilitator, that guides the learners to achieve a 
deeper understanding of the subject. (ibid.) Based on an indirect instruction method, the 
students are allowed to investigate, and draw inferences from the data, thus defining their 
learning. (Ruutmann, and Kipper, 2011) Experiential learning is a branch of constructivism 
that describes the individual as an active participant in the learning process. The central belief 
is that people learn more when they are actively involved. (Ertmer and Newby, 2013; 
Olusegun, 2015) This is perspective is based on Piaget’s cognitive theory. (Arghode, 2017) He 
sustained that all knowledge is recorded in the mind in the form of cognitive 
schemas. (ibid.) We can add to these mental models through assimilation, as well as change 
them through accommodation. (Greitzer, 2002) The aim is to repeat the process of 
assimilation and accommodation through numerous experiences until cognitive equilibrium 
is reached. (ibid.) Cognitive equilibrium is the stage that we naturally and biologically strive to 
achieve, where the external world and the internal mental structures are 
aligned. (ibid.) Similarly, in the experiential learning theory, it is asserted that knowledge is 
acquired through inquiry, analysis, synthesis, and reflection. (Lackéus, 2020) The interplay 
between action and reflection as a way to boost knowledge development has been vastly 
discussed in entrepreneurial education. (Hägg et al., 2016) The idea that experience and 
action are the most effective approaches to entrepreneurial education stems from the 
observation that subsequent business ventures are generally more fruitful than the first 
ones. (ibid.) Reflection is a term that describes intellectual and affective activities in which 
people explore their experiences to come to new understandings. (McFarland, 2017) 
Therefore, in the context of entrepreneurial education, the constructivist theory and action-
based learning pedagogies focus on transforming experience into knowledge whilst using 
reflective practices as a tool to distill meaning from action. (Ertmer and Newby, 2013) 
 
Furthermore, another central concept to this current is the notion of sociocultural cognition 
developed by Lev Vygotsky, which describes that all learning takes place in a cultural context, 
and is mediated by social interactions. (Arghode and Wang, 2016; Swan, 2005) In this 
perspective, meaning is constructed through communication and interactions with others, 
and only later individualized. (ibid.) Therefore, instructional approaches such as problem-
based learning are encouraged and promoted. (Hammond et al., 2001) Problem-based 
learning is a constructivist method where students work in small groups to solve a real or 
realistic open-ended problem. (Murray, 2012) This puts the learners in a “memorable and 
engaging context for learning” and stimulates them to obtain new knowledge in a self-
directed manner. (Reis and Formosinho, 2014) Particularly, it is argued that problem-based 
learning may promote the development of an entrepreneurial mindset, as well as 
entrepreneurial competencies such as creativity and critical thinking. (ibid.) Similarly, it is 
sustained that practicing collaboration is benefic for entrepreneurial education, as students 
learn how to work in a team and engage in an open process of exchanging ideas. (Murray, 
2012) Collaboration is also seen as a way for learners to challenge their beliefs and negotiate 
new understandings. (Zhou et al., 2019) Furthermore, peer-review activities, in which learners 
assess their colleagues’ work, are promoted as methods that help students develop 
interpersonal communication skills and reflexivity. (ibid.) Consequently, these instructional 
==PAGE:8==
 
 
6
methods emphasize the constructivist stance that learning is inherently social and reflective. 
(Richardson, 2003) 
 
The “for” and “through” approaches to EE are consistent with other theories in ID such as the 
minimalist theory developed by J.M. Carroll. (Johnson, 2014) The minimalist theory 
complements the theory of andragogy as it argues for the importance of building upon 
students’ prior experience through learner-directed activities that have immediate 
applications. (Carroll and Rosson, 2006) Furthermore, Carroll’s theory has multiple 
implications for designing e-learning. (Carroll, 2014) Firstly, it recommends that teachers 
allow the students to start working on meaningful and realistic tasks as soon as 
possible. (ibid.) Furthermore, educators should minimize the amount of reading or other 
passive forms of instruction. (ibid.) In this context, teachers should encourage students to 
explore and improvise. (ibid.) For instance, summaries are preferred over full-length texts and 
incomplete instructional materials can be offered to determine the learners to fill in the gaps 
themselves. (ibid.) This principle allows students to learn in a self-directed and goal-oriented 
manner. (ibid.)  
 
Although not as supported in recent years by scholars, the theory-oriented, or “about” 
approach, is also widely used in entrepreneurial education. (Piperopoulos et al., 2015; Yang 
et al., 2021) This approach is often referred to as “traditional” or “content-oriented 
knowledge transmission approach”. (Otting et al., 2010) Frequently, the primary focus of this 
pedagogy is put on more passive instructional methods. (Piperopoulos et al., 2015) Theory is 
used to present entrepreneurship as a “science of the probable”. (ibid.) In entrepreneurial 
education, the most frequently discussed themes within this pedagogical approach are 
business planning, financial management, and entrepreneurial traits. (Sirelkhatim and Gangi, 
2015) The roots of the theory-oriented approach are found in Skinner’s learning theory of 
behaviorism. (Illeris, 2018) The underlying principle is that learning represents a behavior 
change. (Hammond et al., 2001) A stimulus is presented by the instructor, thus eliciting a 
response from the student. (ibid.) It is asserted that learning is generated through rewards or 
reinforcements, causing the desired responses to repeat. (ibid.) In this context, the role of the 
educator is to manipulate the learning environment and to provide clear instruction through 
a direct teaching approach. (Otting et al., 2010) Learning activities are sequenced in gradual 
steps, allowing for the design of a focused and achievable structure that the student can 
follow. (Woolard, 2010) This approach is focused predominantly on individual learning, as 
each student impacts the pace of their learning process within the bounds set by the 
educator. (ibid.) 
 
A prevalent instructional method used in theory-oriented teaching is “mastery learning”. 
(Ertmer and Newby, 2013) Mastery learning is a principle that dictates that a student must 
demonstrate a full understanding of a topic before being allowed to move on to the 
next. (ibid.)  This ensures that concepts are acquired within one’s mental schema before 
graduating to more complex tasks. (ibid.) Furthermore, “drill and practice” is a technique 
aligned with mastery learning and it is often used in combination. (Woolard, 2010) This 
strategy relies on the use of various activities that allow for information to be conveyed in 
different formats until mastery is achieved. (ibid.)  For example, students can be given 
flashcards, and matching and sorting exercises to improve knowledge retention. (Arghode et 
al., 2017) Another important characteristic is that immediate feedback must be provided at 
==PAGE:9==
 
 
7
every stage, thus strengthening the stimulus-response association. (Ertmer and Newby, 2013) 
In an e-learning format, this is readily achieved as educational software offers the 
functionality of automatic marking. (ibid.) Furthermore, in the “about” approach to 
entrepreneurial education, common forms of teaching practice reside in lecturing, study 
cases, and the use of templates and examples. (Solomon et al., 2007; Yang et al., 2021) The 
use of study cases in teaching is regarded as an active form of instruction, as it requires 
students to confront complex problems through analysis and decision-making. (Bonney, 
2015) In entrepreneurial education, case studies are used to tell compelling and relevant 
stories that help the learners understand certain theoretical and conceptual issues in a real-
world context. (ibid.) Furthermore, example-based learning is a principle that states that 
presenting examples in teaching helps students acquire knowledge more readily and 
efficiently. (Ashman et al., 2020)  
 
Furthermore, despite the growing number of entrepreneurial programs in higher education, 
scholars are striving to comprehend the causes and influences of entrepreneurial behavior as 
well as the critical function of education. (Barron and Ruiz, 2021) As entrepreneurship is a 
complex social activity, the effectiveness of this discipline continues to be a prevalent 
research focus that draws academics. (Liu et al., 2020) One of the factors that raise difficulties 
in judging the programs’ outcomes and effectiveness is the fact that entrepreneurial 
education lacks uniformity in terms of curricula contents and teaching approaches. 
(Sirelkhatim and Gangi, 2015) Furthermore, evaluating the impact of entrepreneurial 
education is often delayed as the number of businesses and jobs produced can only be 
assessed after a few years. (ibid.) On this account, numerous scholars devised tools and 
theories to measure its effects within a short-term period. (Arasti et al., 2012) However, 
current research lacks a unified framework for evaluating the effectiveness of different types 
of entrepreneurial programs. (Borchers, 2011)  
 
In numerous studies, the success of entrepreneurial education is measured through the 
evaluation of the changes in participants’ entrepreneurial intentions. (Arasti, 2012; Borchers, 
2011) The prevalence of this factor in literature is due to the assumption that intention is the 
most reliable predictor of behavior. (Piperopoulos et al., 2015) As a result, numerous 
instruments have been developed to evaluate intent, resulting in often overlapping 
scales. (ibid.) Despite the strong belief that formal education can influence students’ desires 
of starting a venture, experimental comparisons have not been conclusive. (ibid.) Certain 
studies showed that participating in entrepreneurial programs leads to stronger 
entrepreneurial aspirations, whereas other studies revealed an insignificant or even negative 
impact of entrepreneurial education on intentions to start a business. (ibid.) This issue points 
towards the fact that the measurement of efficiency in entrepreneurial education should not 
be limited to evaluating the dimension of entrepreneurial intentions, but it ought to be 
realized through a comprehensive and multi-dimensional approach. (Liu et al., 2020) 
 
A qualitative study done by Barron and Ruiz (2021) identified several prevalent factors related 
to evaluating the effects of entrepreneurial education found in existing scales used across 
different countries and educational programs. One of the key concepts identified refers to 
the knowledge acquired by the students as an outcome of participating in entrepreneurial 
education. In virtual environments, the learning outcome dimension was found to be closely 
tied to attributes such as “learning efficacy” and “the quality of online instruction”. (Lo, 2022) 
==PAGE:10==
 
 
8
Learning efficacy is defined as one’s beliefs and attitudes regarding their capabilities to be 
successful in an academic setting, as well as confidence in one’s capacity to attain the learning 
objectives. (Hayat et al., 2020) Furthermore, the quality of online instruction is closely tied to 
the performance of the educator. (Lo, 2010) In entrepreneurial education, it is often 
emphasized that the educator plays a crucial part in shaping students’ attitudes toward 
venture creation by encouraging motivation and proactivity. (Arghode et al., 2017) 
Additionally, in the context of e-learning it has been observed that the teacher’s role changes 
from a guide and facilitator to a designer and manager of the learning environment. (Higgings 
and Elliott, 2011) Educators must focus on information architecture, navigation, as well as the 
inclusion of clear instruction and innovative learning activities in visual, auditive, and 
interactive formats. (Snjezanna, 2011) In close connection to the previous, another relevant 
factor for measuring the effect of entrepreneurial education is the “learning satisfaction”. 
(Barron and Ruiz, 2021) This construct represents a reflection of students’ attitudes 
concerning their desires and needs being fulfilled through the learning activities and process. 
(Lo, 2022) Online learning satisfaction is a psychological response to the contents and design 
of the learning environments, formed through a comparison between the perceived effects 
and the participants’ expectations. (ibid.) This construct is influenced by the other factors 
previously mentioned, namely the quality of online instruction and one’s learning efficacy, 
while it has also been observed to have an impact on entrepreneurial intentions. (ibid.) 
 
In summary, the rapid transition from in-person education to e-learning generated both 
opportunities and challenges in entrepreneurial education. To design successful online 
learning units, literature indicates that educators must maintain a strong connection between 
pedagogical approaches and instructional strategies, whilst ensuring a meaningful integration 
of digital technologies into teaching. (Ertmer and Newby, 2013; Orellana et al., 2009) Despite 
decades of research, a consensus regarding a clear and uniform standard for online teaching 
in the field of entrepreneurship has not been reached. (Bates, 2005; Yang et al., 2021) 
Scholars acknowledge a lack of consistency regarding how entrepreneurial education is 
taught and the contents of the curricula. (Sirelkhatim and Gangi, 2015) However, the 
academic community seems to largely agree that online education is more suitable for 
learners that can deploy deep learning strategies. (Singh et al., 2009; Kahu et al., 2013; 
Arghode et al., 2017) In this context, educators must consider the theory of andragogy when 
designing e-learning. (Holton et al., 2009) Particularly in higher education, teachers must 
incorporate adult learning principles such as autonomy, self-direction, and goal and relevancy 
orientations. (Hase and Kenyon, 2001; Johnson, 2014) Furthermore, in recent times, scholars 
seem to move away from theory-oriented approaches and promote practice-oriented 
methods, especially in the case of entrepreneurial education. (Kakouris, 2009; McFarland, 
2017) The theory-oriented approach is often deemed outdated, whilst constructivist 
pedagogies rise in popularity and are often referred to as being more effective in teaching 
entrepreneurship. (Olusegun, 2015) Nonetheless, an in-depth investigation of the current 
literature revealed that both the practical and the theoretical approaches are widely used. 
The absence of uniformity in how entrepreneurial education is taught creates challenges in 
determining best practices, as well as difficulties in evaluating its effectiveness. (Sirelkhatim 
and Gangi, 2015) On this account, research indicated that measuring the effects of 
entrepreneurial education must be conducted in a comprehensive manner. (Liu et al., 2020) 
A multi-dimensional perspective must be adopted, considering outcome factors such as 
entrepreneurial intentions and knowledge acquisition, but also the students perspectives in 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
9
terms of learning efficacy, the quality of instruction, and learning satisfaction. (Barron and 
Ruiz, 2021) Consequently, researchers argue that there is a need for “explicit comparisons 
between different teaching methods” in entrepreneurial education, and especially those that 
“rigorously isolate” the effects of different pedagogical interventions in e-learning. (Lackeus, 
2020)  
 
RESEARCH AIM AND HYPOTHESES 
 
The motivation behind this study is to contribute to the current research into entrepreneurial 
education by offering suggestions for suitable and effective practices that may improve the 
quality of e-learning. The focus of this study is to further explore the relationship between 
theory and practice in e-learning in entrepreneurial education. More specifically, the effects 
of two prevalent pedagogies, namely the theory-oriented and practical-oriented approaches, 
will be compared through an experiment. For each pedagogical approach, a distinct learning 
experience will be designed considering the appropriate instructional strategies and methods, 
whilst ensuring the meaningful integration of technology into teaching.  
 
To investigate this topic in-depth, the impact of each pedagogical approach will be measured 
through a multi-dimensional perspective. Accordingly, a series of hypotheses were 
formulated. The hypotheses are predictive statements made about the expected 
relationships between the variables of the experiment. (Creswell, 2009) These predictions are 
based on the information gathered through the review of literature that included journal 
articles, conference proceedings, reports, and other published texts. According to the insights 
found in literature, the research questions and associated hypothesis were categorized into 
two groups, respectively entrepreneurial education outcomes and measures of the 
participants’ perceptions of the learning experience. Furthermore, the review of current 
literature indicated that the practical-oriented approach, based on constructivist principles is 
predominantly considered as being more effective in entrepreneurial education. (Kakouris, 
2009; McFarland, 2017; Olusegun, 2015) Consequently, each hypothesis will illustrate a 
preference for the practical-oriented approach.  
 
In terms of entrepreneurial education outcomes, two hypotheses were formulated as follows: 
 
Hypothesis 1: “The practical-oriented teaching approach leads to higher knowledge 
acquisition than the theory-oriented approach.” This prediction is inspired by the work of 
academics such as Ertmer and Newby (2013), Keengwe et al. (2014), and Olusegun (2015) 
who argued that constructivist teaching principles lead to higher learning outcomes when 
compared to traditional strategies. More specifically, they sustain that knowledge acquisition 
is strengthened by practical applications, as well as the fact that individuals retain information 
more readily when they are highly involved in the learning process. 
 
Hypothesis 2: “The practical-oriented approach leads to higher entrepreneurial intentions in 
comparison to the theory-oriented approach.” This prediction is based on studies conducted 
by scholars such as Aidin (2013), Purwatiningsih et al. (2018), and Karp (2006) that 
emphasized that constructivist learning models have a significant impact on entrepreneurial 
intentions. This belief stems from the perspective that engaging in practical and task-oriented 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
10
activities encourages the development of entrepreneurial behaviors and mindsets by 
cultivating creativity, exploration, and risk-taking. Furthermore, it is considered that these 
changes in behavior and cognition may determine learners to choose an entrepreneurial path. 
  
In terms of the participants’ perceptions regarding the learning experience, three hypotheses 
were formulated, as follows: 
 
Hypothesis 3: “The practical-oriented approach leads to higher learning efficacy in 
comparison to the theory-oriented approach.” The prediction made for this hypothesis stems 
from research conducted by academics such as Dzakkiyuddin et al. (2020), Hammond et al. 
(2001), and Simatupang et al. (2021) that indicated that practical experience is the leading 
factor in influencing one’s perceived learning efficacy. They argue that practical activities, or 
the “learn by doing” approach, leads to increased beliefs in one’s ability to understand and 
successfully carry out academic tasks in entrepreneurial education. Similarly, it is sustained 
that engaging in practical learning activities helps the student be more confident in engaging 
in entrepreneurship and venture creation.   
 
Hypothesis 4: “The practical-oriented approach leads to a higher quality of online instruction 
in comparison to the theory-oriented approach.” This prediction is motivated by numerous 
studies that sustain that the constructivist, practical-oriented instruction is especially suitable 
for technology-based education. In this context, it is argued that teachers can use technology 
to encourage students to collaborate and engage in social negotiation with their peers, as 
well as to connect to remote communities of practice. (Siemens, 2005) Furthermore, 
educators can create minimalistic instruction and motivate students to explore, curate, and 
extract value from various sources, thus encouraging active engagement in the learning 
process. (Carroll, 2014) Similarly, teachers can efficiently design authentic problem-based 
activities by linking to state-of-the-art resources and presenting learners with materials in 
various multimedia formats. (Reis and Formosinho, 2014)  
 
Hypothesis 5: “The practical-oriented approach leads to higher online learning satisfaction in 
comparison to the theory-oriented approach.” Various studies showed that the construct of 
“learning satisfaction” is not only a predictor of entrepreneurial intentions, but it is also 
closely related and influenced by other characteristics of entrepreneurial education such as 
the quality of instruction, learning efficacy, and choices of pedagogy. (Gao et al., 2021; Lo, 
2022) It is argued that e-learning units that emphasize constructivist principles focused on 
interaction, collaboration, and discussion are more suitable for e-learning, generating higher 
positive effects on online learning satisfaction. (Carroll and Rosson, 2006; Woraphiphat et al., 
2023) Similarly, it is claimed that e-learning experiences built around practical activities that 
have immediate uses in one’s professional or personal life generate higher levels of 
satisfaction than theory-oriented approaches. (Bollinger et al., 2004; Lo, 2010)

METHODOLOGY 
 
In this chapter, the philosophical underpinnings of the research will be presented. 
Furthermore, other design choices will be described including the research strategy, the 
sampling method, instruments used for data collection, data analysis methods, and the 
limitations of the study. 
 
Research philosophy  
 
The choice of research philosophy was based on the “research onion” model developed by 
Saunders et al. (2019). Committing to a specific philosophical position allows for the design 
of a coherent project, in which elements of research strategy, time horizon, data collection 
methods, and analysis procedures are aligned. (Saunders et al., 2019) Furthermore, following 
a philosophical position can guide one toward making better judgments while conducting the 
study, assessing the validity and reliability of the data, coming to conclusions, and interpreting 
the results. (ibid.) Therefore, before choosing a research philosophy it was paramount to first 
understand the main assumptions made by scholars and to distinguish between these 
stances. These are called structure and agency assumptions and refer to different aspects of 
how individuals understand knowledge. (ibid.) Firstly, ontological assumptions are made 
about the nature of reality. Secondly, epistemological assumptions refer to what is 
acceptable, legitimate knowledge and how can this be communicated to others. (ibid.) Lastly, 
the role of values, ethics, and worldviews consist of axiological assumptions. (ibid.) 
 
Based on these philosophical assumptions, out of the five major philosophies that Saunders 
et al. (2019) included in their model, the present study follows the positivism philosophy. 
Ontologically, the research project assumes that the nature of reality is external to all social 
actors. A single tangible reality exists and can be understood, identified, and measured. (Park 
et al., 2020) Furthermore, from an epistemological standpoint, the study is focused on the 
scientific empiricist method. The emphasis is on discovering observable and measurable facts 
and producing credible and meaningful data. (ibid.) The goal is to obtain quantifiable 
observations that can be analyzed statistically. These findings are used to predict or explain 
similar events in other contexts. Axiologically, positivism is based on objectivity, therefore, 
the study must be carried out without the values of the researcher or participants influencing 
its development.  (ibid.) A positivist researcher must remain detached from the data and not 
alter or influence the results. (ibid.) This degree of objectivity is difficult to achieve since the 
researcher chose the topic of study, the research objectives, and the data collection methods. 
Therefore, it can be argued that adopting a neutral perspective indicates the existence of a 
certain value position.  
 
Research type  
 
According to the positivist method, the current study followed a deductive reasoning process. 
This type of research starts with general rules or existing theories and aims to verify these 
through data collection. (Melnikovas, 2018) In this context, the deductive approach is used to 
test the study’s hypotheses. Generally, deductive research is quantitative in nature. (Du 
Plessis, 2004) Accordingly, the present study will focus on quantitative data collection.  
==PAGE:14==
 
 
12
 
Research strategy  
 
The positivist methodology favors research settings where variables can be controlled and 
manipulated. (Saunders et al., 2019) Therefore, the process through which the research was 
conducted consisted of an experiment. The experiment was designed as an online split test, 
also known as an A/B test. (Kohavi and Longbotham, 2017) More specifically, the experiment 
included two treatments, “A” and “B”. This format was chosen as it was suitable for 
comparing two different approaches to teaching in an online environment. Internet 
connectivity, websites, and online services provide numerous opportunities to evaluate ideas 
and designs in controlled experiments. (Kohavi and Longbotham, 2017) Split testing allows 
the researcher to observe the effects of specific changes on key metrics with real 
users. (ibid.)  Furthermore, this format is especially powerful because it allows for fast 
iterations. (ibid.) Moreover, online experimentation is easily scalable, thus the experiment 
design was not bound by a limit on the number of participants. For this experiment, two 
learning experiences were designed. After creating the experimental treatments, these were 
immediately tested on a small scale with a few subjects. This was done to uncover potential 
issues with the software used, problems in terms of user experience, as well as to evaluate 
how the organization of knowledge was perceived, and the time necessary to complete the 
learning modules and post-evaluation components. The issues that were signaled in the initial 
testing were resolved before running the experiment in full scale. 
 
The experiment was integrated into an online course at DTU, named “Developing an 
entrepreneurial mindset through serious games” that ran in the spring semester of 2023. It is 
important to note that this was an introductory course on the topic of Innovation and 
Entrepreneurship. Thus, the premise was that the participants did not have prior knowledge 
or experience regarding the subject matter. More specifically, the experiment was held in one 
of the course sessions. The topic of the session regarded business models, strategy, and 
innovation. The experiment was administered in the first half of the session, lasting for 
approximately two hours. The experimental treatments introduced the participants to the 
subject matter, respectively Business Models, and the Business Model Canvas. Detailed 
information about the structure and content of the two experimental treatments, as well as 
the technologies used to support these can be read in the “Experimental Design” chapter. 
 
Variables 
 
Furthermore, one of the first design choices for the experiment was selecting the 
independent and dependent variables. According to the scientific method, variables can be 
defined as factors that can be observed, measured, and recorded. (Leatham, 2012) The 
independent variable is the factor changed and controlled by the researcher. (ibid.) The 
dependent variable is the one that responds to the independent variable and represents what 
is being tested and measured. (ibid.)  Therefore, in this research project, the independent 
variable was the teaching approach, respectively theoretical-oriented versus practical-
oriented. The dependent variable was represented by the test scores of the students obtained 
in a quiz, as well as the results of a survey containing Likert scale questions. More precisely, 
the quiz aimed to measure subjects’ knowledge acquisition as a result of the two treatments. 
Furthermore, the survey was administered to measure subjects’s perceptions regarding the 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
13
learning experiences. The controlled experimental setting resided in the fact that the quiz and 
survey questions were identical for both treatment groups, as well as the fact that the 
treatments were administered at the same time of day, for the same duration of time, within 
the same online learning environment.  
 
Time horizon 
 
The research was conducted as a cross-sectional study. Therefore, the data was collected at 
one point in time. The motivation behind this design choice was given by practical reasons. A 
cross-sectional approach allows for data to be gathered in a timely manner.  
 
Sampling strategy  
 
Due to practicalities and resource constraints, the sampling method used in this experiment 
was non-probability sampling, precisely convenience sampling. (Wharrad and Silcocks, 2009) 
This choice has been made due to ease of access and existing contact with the population. To 
achieve greater statistical power and higher confidence in the results, the goal was to include 
as many subjects in the study as possible. According to a report, in 2022 there was a 
population of approximately 5300 master’s students enrolled at the university. (DTU Facts 
and Figures 2022, n.d.) A sample size calculator was used to compute the minimum number 
of samples that would have been necessary to meet a statistical confidence level of 95% with 
a 5% margin of error. The result obtained was that 359 measurements would have been 
needed to achieve the statistical constraints. (Sample Size Calculator, n.d.) However, 
achieving this number of participants has not been possible, and thus, only 162 subjects 
participated in the study.  
 
Assigning subjects to treatment groups  
 
The subjects were randomly assigned to treatment groups. This means that each participant 
had an equal chance of being assigned to each treatment group. (Bell, 2017) Furthermore, 
each participant was assigned to a treatment group independently of other 
participants. (ibid.) The method of randomly assigning subjects took place as further 
described. First, the 162 subjects joined a Microsoft Teams meeting. Then, the Microsoft 
Teams software was used to assign the participants to breakout rooms in a completely 
random manner. The only requirement given to the system was to assign three to four 
subjects to each breakout room. Consequently, 45 breakout rooms containing three to four 
participants were obtained. Furthermore, the 45 breakout rooms were divided equally and 
assigned to the two treatment groups. More specifically, breakout rooms number 1 to 22 
were assigned to treatment “A” and breakout rooms number 23 to 45 were assigned to 
treatment “B”.  
 
Furthermore, a between-subject design was applied. This means that every subject 
experienced only one condition (i.e., treatment “A” or treatment “B”). (Bell, 2017) The goal 
was to compare the dependent variable outcomes and observe if the manipulation of the 
independent variable produced any significant effect. If a significant difference could be 
noticed between the two treatment groups, this would likely indicate that the independent 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
14
variable manipulation is what caused the variation. This approach is suitable, especially in this 
instance, where the two treatments differed on one key characteristic, namely the teaching 
approach. One of the benefits of using a between-subject design was that carryover effects 
were prevented. (Bell, 2017) These are the lingering effects of being presented with one 
condition and subsequently with another condition, as would be the case in a within-subjects 
design. (ibid.) More importantly, the participants were less likely to experience fatigue effects 
because they did not experience multiple treatments in a row, fact that could lead to 
boredom or tiredness. (ibid.) Similarly, the participants did not experience practice effects, 
where repeated exposure would improve their performance in subsequent treatments. (ibid.) 
 
Primary data collection  
 
Primary data was gathered to investigate the hypotheses formulated on the background of 
theoretical inputs. (Murtada, 2019) According to the positivist philosophy and the deductive 
research method, the study was focused on quantitative data collection. In this context, two 
data collection methods were used to measure the differences between the treatment groups 
and to assess the relationships between the independent and dependent variables. Precisely, 
a survey and a quiz were administered. To compare the effects of the two treatments, the 
content and structure of the survey and quiz were identical for both experimental groups.  
 
Two distinct data collection methods were included because each methodology has its 
drawbacks and biases. Therefore, no methodology can be counted on to be completely error-
free. (Muhammad and Kabir, 2016) That is why the likelihood that the results offer an 
accurate overview increases when various data sources are considered. (Breslow, 2007) On 
this account, best practices in educational research dictate that effective evaluation 
procedures include both direct and indirect measurements. (ibid.) Therefore, using both 
procedures provided a richer understanding of the effects of the two distinct learning 
experiences. For this study, the direct measurement practice resided in administering a quiz 
that was testing participants’ acquisition of knowledge after the learning modules. The 
indirect practice resided in administering the survey that captured subjects’ perceptions, 
attitudes, and opinions regarding the learning experience.  
 
The quiz was developed as a form of summative assessment, thus it was administered after 
the learning units. The quiz contained ten questions and can be viewed in full in Appendix 
1. The questions varied in type, respectively “true/false”, “fill-in-the-blank”, “multiple 
choice”, and “multiple answers”. The results of the quiz were calculated based only on the 
participants’ scores on their first attempt at taking the test. This was done to ensure that the 
quiz scores measured their actual knowledge and not their ability to memorize or master the 
content by retaking the quiz multiple times. Each question was valued at one point, thus the 
highest score result possible was ten points. Furthermore, to oblige the participants to 
respond to the questions to the best of their knowledge, the testing timeframe was restricted 
to only seven minutes. The time limit was set so that it provided enough time for a participant 
that knows the information to be successful, but not enough time to search for answers in 
the learning materials provided or other external resources. 
 
The questions included in the quiz were formulated in a manner that provided equal chances 
of success for the participants of both treatment groups. Even though the two experimental 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
15
treatments were based on different teaching approaches, both worked towards 
understanding the same topic. Consequently, the quiz aimed to investigate whether there 
would be statistically significant differences in the effect of the two pedagogies on the 
knowledge acquisition of the subjects.  
 
Furthermore, the survey administered contained twenty questions and can be viewed in full 
in Appendix 2. The first two questions regarded demographic information respectively the 
participants’ age and gender. The rest of the eighteen questions included in the survey were 
close-ended questions, based on a 5-point Likert scale. For all eighteen questions, the 
response categories were presented in ranking order from “1 = strongly disagree” to “5 = 
strongly agree”. The Likert scale questions included in this survey were taken from existing 
instruments that measure the effects of entrepreneurial programs in university settings. The 
scales developed for these instruments have been tested and validated with large samples. 
(Bangert, 2008; Baron and Ruiz, 2021; Linan and Chen, 2009; Thompson, 2009) Therefore, 
these instruments present few limitations and are generally viewed as reliable and applicable 
to different populations. Moreover, since the instruments were extensively tested at the time 
of use and developed by experts in their respective fields, the confidence that the items 
included are good indicators of the concepts of interest is increased. (Hyman et al., 2006)  
 
However, it is important to note that the existing scales found in literature were not used in 
the survey in their original form. More precisely, only several questions from each instrument 
were selected for the survey administered in this study, whereas some of the questions were 
slightly modified. This is because not all the items included in the original instruments are 
suitable for the circumstances of this experiment. Thus, a degree of customization was 
necessary to fit this research project. Furthermore, if the original instruments were 
administered in full, this would have resulted in a very extensive survey containing upwards 
of one hundred questions. As a result, the risk that the participants would complete the 
survey due to its length would have greatly increased. (Hyman et al., 2006)  
                                             
Teaching is a complex activity, thus when evaluating its effects multiple dimensions must be 
considered. (Bangert, 2008) Consequently, the eighteen Likert scale questions included in the 
survey were divided into three sections, based on the topic that was investigated. The first 
section regarded subjects’ opinions on their learning efficacy. This section contained 
questions developed by Barron and Ruiz (2021) in their instrument measuring the efficacy of 
entrepreneurial teaching in university programs. The original instrument contained 39 Likert 
scale questions aimed at capturing students’ perceptions of the value that entrepreneurship 
education programs offer in terms of learning, resources, and other factors. (Barron and Ruiz, 
2021) However, based on relevancy, only 5 questions were chosen and included in the survey 
for this study.  
 
The second section of the survey regarded subjects’ levels of entrepreneurial intent. In 
general, entrepreneurial intent is defined as a person’s conviction that they want to start a 
venture at a point in the future. (Thompson, 2009) That point in the future is not strictly 
defined and may never be reached due to numerous factors. (ibid.) The questions for this 
section were taken from two different scales, respectively Linan and Chen’s (2009) instrument 
and Thompson’s (2009) instrument, both regarding the measurement of entrepreneurial 
intentions. From these instruments, a total of 5 questions were selected for the survey. 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
16
Furthermore, to increase relevancy, these questions were slightly modified. The questions 
included in the survey explored attitudes towards starting a new venture in general, but also 
as an effect of participating in the experimental treatment “A” or “B”.  
 
The next section of the survey regarded participants’ opinions on the quality of online 
instruction associated with the two experimental treatments. These questions were taken 
from Bangert’s (2008) instrument on evaluating the effectiveness of online teaching practices, 
formally known as “Student Evaluation of Online Teaching Effectiveness”. The original version 
of the questionnaire contained 26 items. In this study, only the most relevant 8 questions 
were included. Furthermore, to evaluate the subject’s learning satisfaction with the e-
learning modules presented, an additional item was extracted from the same instrument. 
 
Data analysis methods 
 
To extract meaningful insights about the effects of the experimental treatments from the data 
collected and to test the hypotheses, several statistical analyses were conducted. 
(Komorowski et al., 2016) Firstly, descriptive statistics were used to summarize the 
characteristics of the data sets. (Seltman, 2011) Specifically, measures of central tendency 
such as the mean, median, and mode were calculated to identify the performance level of 
different score groups. (ibid.) Next, measures of variability such as variance and standard 
deviation were calculated to describe the spread of scores among different groups of 
participants. (ibid.) Additionally, frequency distribution analyses were conducted to measure 
the number of times specific data points occurred, for instance when analyzing the quiz scores 
of participants. Furthermore, inferential statics were used to verify the hypotheses and assess 
whether the data can be generalized to the broader population. (Peters, 2001) On this 
account, t-tests were conducted to determine if there was a significant difference between 
the means of different data sets. (ibid.) Thus, verifying if the treatments had a significant 
effect on the sample. Moreover, exploratory factor analyses were conducted to investigate 
how certain variables are interrelated. For example, the factor analysis was used to 
investigate relationships between the items included in the survey. The objective of the factor 
analysis was to divide the variables into groups that are highly correlated with each other. 
(Peters, 2001) As a powerful data reduction technique, this statistical procedure revealed the 
underlying dimensions, that explained relationships between variables. (ibid.) Therefore, 
certain themes and trends could be observed throughout data sets. As a result, it was possible 
to determine which survey questions were associated with a common theme or factor, 
enabling the author to provide an interpretation.  
 
Limitations of the study 
 
Generally, “true” experiments contain a series of key features, namely independent and 
dependent variables, pre-testing and post-testing, as well as experimental and control groups. 
(Wharrad and Silcocks, 2009) On this account, a first observation is that the experiment did 
not include a pre-test. Several reasons led to this design choice. On one hand, it was assumed 
that the group of subjects was homogenous. The participants were all master’s students 
enrolled in a Technical University. Therefore, it can be noted that the individuals in the sample 
have self-selected. More specifically, they made a conscious choice to enroll in a specific 
educational program at the university and also to enroll in the course within which the 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
17
experiment was held. Thus, this increases the likelihood that the subjects are similar in terms 
of personality and focus of knowledge. As a result, it was assumed that the differences in their 
entrepreneurial levels of knowledge were equally distributed among the students that were 
part of each treatment group. A second observation is that the experiment did not include a 
control group. The experiment featured two treatment groups, respectively group “A” and 
group “B”. The reason for this choice was that there is strong evidence in literature that both 
pedagogical interventions are associated with positive outcomes. The experiment aimed to 
compare the two approaches in an online environment and investigate the hypotheses. 
 
Another limitation regards the sampling approach. As this study was based on non-probability 
sampling, the risk for research bias, and more particularly sampling bias is increased. (Peters, 
2001) Sampling bias occurs when some members of a population are more likely to be 
selected in the sample than others. (ibid.) In this study, the sample was entirely composed of 
master's students that are enrolled in a course at a technical university. This limits the 
generalizability of the results and is a threat to the external validity of the study. Therefore, 
the results of the experiment can only be generalized to populations that are similar to the 
sample. Additionally, it must be noted that the sample size has not been sufficiently large. 
Apart from resource constraints, the between-subjects design weakened the statistical power 
even further as the participants only experienced one condition. Thus, the study results may 
not have had sufficient statistical power to detect meaningful effects.  
 
Lastly, another limitation regards the method of evaluation of the knowledge acquired by the 
participants. The main challenge resided in the fact that measuring learning outcomes in soft 
applied disciplines such as Innovation and Entrepreneurship is inherently ambiguous. (Smith 
et al., 2008) While the quiz is a quantifiable and objective method of assessing the learning 
achieved by both groups, it may not provide a comprehensive view of the actual knowledge 
acquired by all participants. On one hand, those in treatment “A” engaged in a collaborative 
problem-solving activity. In that case, each approach was unique, and their performance 
should have been assessed through constructive feedback. However, providing individualized 
feedback to these participants was not possible due to resource constraints. On the other 
hand, the exercises that treatment “B” engaged in were objective and quantitative in nature, 
thus the computer system provided automatic feedback that allowed them to self-correct. 
However, evaluating participants’ learning through different methods would have posed 
difficulties in correlating and comparing the results.

EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN 
 
In this chapter, the detailed structure and content of the experimental treatments will be 
described, while the differences between them will be explained. Furthermore, the 
technologies used to create the two learning experiences will be presented. On this account, 
both affordances and limitations of each software will be discussed.  
 
Comparison of the experimental treatments 
 
This subchapter describes the similarities and differences between the two experimental 
treatments in terms of pedagogy and instructional design. Learning experiences on the topic 
of business models were developed for both treatments “A” and “B”. The choice of topic was 
informed by studies that identified that teaching business models and business model 
innovation is not only common practice but also “the most important feature of 
entrepreneurship courses”. (Arasti, 2012) An extensive research study showed that 95% of 
educators of diverse disciplines such as entrepreneurship, strategy, and marketing, used the 
business model concept in their teaching. (Holm et al., 2019) The prevalence of this topic has 
been recognized in the academic community, as business model thinking is considered to 
unite all the fundamental choices regarding a venture, respectively creating, delivering, and 
capturing value. (Bidmon and Holm, 2021) Despite the extensive resources dedicated to 
developing teaching materials for this subject, teachers have diverse experiences and 
perceptions on how to impart this knowledge most effectively to learners. (ibid.) Therefore, 
this topic sparks a great deal of debate within the educational theorists’ community, and thus 
it is argued that pedagogies and instructional methods pertaining to it should be further 
investigated. (Hutasuhut et al., 2020) 
 
The overall structure of the two learning experiences was identical, containing four modules 
that had to be completed by the participants in order. (See Figure 1)

Treatment A  
 
Treatment “A” was based on the “for” approach to entrepreneurial education, also known as 
a practice-oriented approach. First, those in treatment group “A” were presented with a brief 
introduction to the topic of study. According to the minimalist theory, the learning materials 
were minimized, thus the participants were only asked to read short definitions of terms such 
as “Business Models”, “Business Strategy” and the “Business Model Canvas”. Furthermore, 
the subjects were presented with links to external resources where they could independently 
research more information on the previously mentioned topics, thus allowing the participants 
to be self-directed in the learning process. The materials presented in the external resources 
were meant to support them in the group assignment that followed thereafter.  
 
Based on the principles of constructivism and experiential learning theory, the goal was to 
allow group “A” to gain knowledge through practical experiences embedded in a social 
context and reflective practices. Consistent with the minimalist theory, the learning 
experience was designed to allow learners to engage in active and realistic tasks as soon as 
possible. Therefore, upon completing the short introduction, the participants participated in 
a synchronous group activity that was problem-based. The stages of the collaborative activity 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
20
and the associated completion deadlines were detailed through written instruction. The 
structure can be observed in Figure 3. 
 
Stage 1 
Create a Business Model Canvas  
Stage 2 
Peer-review  
Stage 3 
Reflect & Implement Feedback  
 
Figure 3– Structure of the group activity (treatment “A”) 
 
As described in the “Methodology” chapter, the participants were randomly split into 
different teams. Each team was instructed to work together on creating a Business Model 
Canvas (BMC) for a fictional startup. Additionally, they had to devise a business strategy that 
would differentiate the company in the market. Upon finalizing the first stage, the teams were 
asked to engage in a peer-review activity. More specifically, each team was instructed to give 
meaningful feedback on the BMC constructed by another team. Furthermore, they had to 
propose a way of innovating the other team’s business model whilst taking into consideration 
the hypothesis that should be tested. As a result, in the second stage, all teams provided 
feedback as well as received feedback. After the peer-review task was completed, the teams 
were instructed to critically review the feedback received. They had to discuss and decide 
which elements of feedback they would like to implement and improve their original canvas 
accordingly. Lastly, the participants were asked to reflect on how the iteration of their original 
BMC would affect the initial business strategy that was devised. This problem-based activity 
emphasized the constructivist perspective on learning, putting the focus on the interplay 
between action, reflection, and collaboration. 
 
Treatment B 
 
Treatment “B” was based on the “about” approach to entrepreneurial education, also known 
as the theory-oriented approach. Thus, the participants were given an in-depth presentation 
on the topic of study in an asynchronous format. The participants were instructed to 
individually go through the learning materials at their own pace, however specific timeframes 
for completion were recommended. Furthermore, the learning materials were substantially 
more detailed than those in treatment “A”. Additionally, the students were presented with 
interactive materials that allowed them to explore the material and engage with it in an 
immersive manner. This design choice was motivated by the fact that learner-content 
interaction has been shown to increase the understanding of a topic (Domagk, 2010), as well 
as the fact that it increases learning satisfaction (Li et al., 2018).  
 
The learning experience was enriched with multimedia content such as videos and graphic 
elements. This design choice was motivated by the fact that the use of visual elements has 
been shown to reduce the intrinsic cognitive load experienced by the participants, thus 
increasing learning motivation and outcomes. (Carney & Levin, 2002; Sweller et al., 2019) For 
example, images were used to depict the Business Model Canvas.  Interactive markers were 
overlayed on top of the images. When clicked, the markers would open pop-up windows with 
detailed explanations of the building blocks of the BMC. Other images were used to show the 
interconnections between the BMC blocks and elements of feasibility, viability, and reliability. 
According to the categorization devised by Levin et al. (2007), the imagery used was 
representational, illustrating the textual information with the goal of reinforcement.

Furthermore, the videos presented had either an explanatory character, such as a short

passages of text that highlighted key information.  
 
According to the principles of mastery learning, the content was separated into manageable 
units and delivered at an increased complexity level. To help the students master the content,

Upon completing the learning materials previously mentioned, group “B” was asked to work 
individually on a comprehensive study case on the topic of business models. The study case 
included learnings from the early stages of venture creation of three well-known companies 
that have managed to achieve success. Thus, the contents of the study case were focused on 
example-based learning principles. Firstly, the study case emphasized the fact that new 
ventures need to be aware of the underlying assumptions associated with their business 
model and test different hypotheses in the market. This emphasized the importance of 
iterating on the business model, as often successful companies change numerous key aspects 
of the business model one by one before becoming successful. Next, the study case depicted 
instances of business model innovation. The case highlighted that new ventures benefit from 
challenging the status quo of an industry and finding better ways to serve their customers 
than existing alternatives. Lastly, the paper emphasized that successful companies need to 
devise a clear business strategy, respectively a vision towards a future state of affairs of the 
business whilst setting goals on outperforming the competition. Upon reading the case, the 
participants were asked to complete a matching exercise between the companies discussed 
in the paper and the key takeaways that could be deducted from the material.  
 
Although the learning experience and teaching method differed between the two treatment 
groups, both worked towards understanding the same topic. More precisely, both groups 
learned about business models, the Business Model Canvas, iteration, innovation, and 
strategy. On one hand, group “A” approached learning through a practice-oriented method, 
whereas group “B” followed a theory-oriented method.

Analysis of quiz data 
 
As described in the “Methodology” and “Experimental Design” chapters, each learning 
experience included a quiz. The quiz was administered after the learning units. To compare 
the effects of the treatments on the learning outcomes of the participants, both experimental 
groups were given the same test, containing ten questions. Therefore, the role of the quiz 
was to investigate the first hypothesis of the research project, which stated that the practical-
oriented approach leads to higher knowledge acquisition than the theory-oriented approach.  
 
The data gathered was analyzed. Firstly, the average score for each question in both groups 
“A” and “B” was calculated. The average scores were calculated by adding each participant’s 
result and dividing the sum by the total number of examination marks. Then, the values 
obtained were expressed as percentages. (See Figure 5)

Based on this data, descriptive statistics were computed on the compound scores of both 
groups. (See Figure 6) The participants of treatment “A” achieved an average score or mean 
value of 68%, whereas those in group “B” achieved a mean value of 72%. The standard 
deviation values are low, indicating the scores were normally distributed.  The count value 
indicates that this analysis was based on the average scores of the ten questions included in 
the quiz.

To further investigate the distribution of scores between the two groups, a frequency 
distribution analysis of the scores obtained in both groups “A” and “B” was computed. (See 
Figure 7) For this, a histogram was used. For the frequency distribution analysis, each 
participant’s final score in their quiz attempt was considered. The x-axis represents the scores 
achieved by the participants expressed as percentages. The y-axis shows the number of 
participants that achieved a specific score. It can be observed that the scores registered by 
those in treatment group “A” had a flatter and broader distribution. The distribution of scores 
obtained by those in treatment “B” was more focused, and slightly skewed to the right-hand 
area, indicating more observations for higher results.

Furthermore, a t-test of the two samples assuming equal variances was conducted. The test 
revealed that there are no significant differences between the mean values of the two 
treatment groups when comparing the average scores for the 10 questions of the quiz. This 
is due to the “p” value for the one-tailed and two-tailed analyses. The “p” value indicates how 
probable the results are due to chance. (Verhagen et al., 2004) In scientific research, it is 
considered that a “p” value of less than 0.5 is acceptable for rejecting the null 
hypothesis. (ibid.) However, in entrepreneurial research it is often accepted for the “p” value 
to be less than 0.1, indicating weak evidence against the null hypothesis. (ibid.) Additionally, 
a “p” value of less than 0.1 should be accepted due to the small sample of the study. However, 
for this analysis the “p” value was above 0.1, thus hypothesis cannot be confirmed. (See 
Appendix 3) This means that in terms of learning outcomes, there is no difference between 
the effect of the traditional and the combined approach to teaching.  
 
The data was further investigated by performing t-test analyses between groups. The 
objective was to investigate whether there is a significant difference in the individual scores 
between the two groups. The reason behind performing this analysis was to investigate 
whether there were a few questions with non-significant t-test results that impacted the 
entire sample. The STATA software package was used to compare all the individual scores 
between the two treatment groups for each question. A binary value of 0 was given to 
treatment “A” and a value of 1 was given to treatment “B”. The full output can be seen in 
Appendix 4.  
 
For each of the ten questions, the results for both one-tailed and two-tailed analyses were 
examined. All the questions showed no statistical difference, except for one, respectively 
question 6 of the quiz. This was a multiple-choice question, that regarded the definition of 
the value proposition. The statistical analysis revealed a significant difference in the one-
tailed test, with a “p” value of 0,03. The one-tailed test indicates that the distribution of scores 
is one-sided so that it is greater or lesser than a certain value. (Verhagen et al., 2004) 
Therefore, the results for this item contradict the first hypothesis of the study since the 
participants of the theoretical-oriented treatment, on average, obtained significantly higher 
scores as compared to their counterparts.

Summary of the quiz data analysis 
 
Overall, the statistical analysis of the quiz data showed that nine out of the ten items 
registered non-significant differences between the effects of two pedagogical approaches on 
the learning acquisition of the subjects. Therefore, the hypothesis cannot be confirmed. 
Through the review of literature, a preponderance of claims supporting that the practical-
oriented approach leads to higher knowledge acquisition than the theory-oriented approach 
was identified. Numerous scholars including Arghode and Wang (2016), Ertmer and Newby 
(2013), and Olusegun (2015) sustained that learning is achieved more readily and efficiently 
in entrepreneurial education through pedagogies based on constructivist principles. This 
belief is based on the perspective that meaning is distilled from practical activities and 
collaboration as well as the fact that knowledge is strengthened through experience and 
reflection.  However, the results of the data collection in this study indicated that the two 
learning experiences in treatment “A” and “B” were very similar in terms of the information 
that they communicated, and the learning provided. Moreover, the average scores obtained 
by the two groups, respectively 68% and 72%, were satisfactory. This indicates strong 
evidence of the fact that although different, the two pedagogical approaches were equivalent 
in terms of their quality and potential.  
 
Analysis of survey data  
 
As described in the “Methodology” and “Experimental Design” chapters, a survey was 
administered to participants of both treatments, “A” and “B”. The survey aimed to investigate 
the last four hypotheses formulated for this study. The survey contained 20 items. The first 
two items investigated demographic data, respectively the subjects’ age, and gender. The 
next 18 questions aimed to capture the subject’s opinions and satisfaction level with the 
learning experience based on a 5-point Likert scale.  
 
The first two questions of the survey regarding the subjects’ age and gender were included in 
the survey to verify the homogeneity of the two experimental groups. The ambition was to 
minimize the sampling bias through effective random distribution of the subjects between 
the two treatment groups. (Seltman, 2011) As can be observed in Figure 8, the two groups 
were very similar in terms of age distribution. Precisely, 30% in group “A” and 31% in group 
“B” identified as female; 68% identified as male in both groups; and only 2% in group “A” and 
1% in group “B” preferred not to say. Similar results can be observed in Figure 9 which depicts 
the gender distribution. More specifically, 46% in group “A” and 48% in group “B” reported 
being aged between 18-24; 51% in group “A “and 48% in group “B” identified as being aged 
between 25-34; and 3% in group “A” and 4% in group “B” identified as being aged between 
35-44. Therefore, no significant difference between the demographics of the two groups was 
registered. Registering no significant difference in gender or age between the groups, 
combined with the fact that the participants were studying in the same university and had 
similar educational backgrounds, indicates that any effect found in difference is likely to be 
caused by the treatment that they were given rather than by any inherent differences.

theoretical perspectives on the topic. This result is confirmed through literature, particularly 
through the constructivist lens. Scholars such as Hammond et al. (2001) and Swan (2005)

knowledge and helps the learner gain more confidence with the method studied.

entrepreneurship than the theoretical-oriented approach. This would be a confirmatory 
finding in connection to literature review. Scholars such as Aidin (2013) and Gao et al. (2021) 
sustained that engaging in collaborative, task-oriented activities encourages the development 
of entrepreneurial behaviors, whilst also having a positive impact on the learning satisfaction 
of the participants.

module included interactive exercises that directly involved me in the learning process”. This 
item relates to the construct of “quality of instruction”. The results indicate that participants 
of group “A” regarded the learning experience as more actively engaging. This result is 
confirmatory, as the design of the practical-oriented approach was predominantly focused on 
elements of active participation. The review of literature revealed that the constructivist 
pedagogy and instructional methods including project work in small groups, reflection, and 
peer-review elicit interaction between the participants, thus they are regarded as highly 
active learning strategies. (Yordanova et al., 2015)

Exploratory Factor Analysis  
 
Furthermore, an exploratory factor analysis was conducted to explore the relationships 
between the items of the survey across the entire dataset. The objective was to obtain groups 
of variables that were highly correlated and to reveal the underlying dimensions, or factors, 
that explain the relationships between them. More specifically, each factor identified shows 
a pattern of similarity concerning how the participants answered the questions of the survey. 
An orthogonal varimax rotation method was utilized to minimize the number of variables with 
high loadings for each factor, thus simplifying the interpretation of each factor. (Peters, 2001) 
To classify a survey item into a factor class, an eigenvalue of 0.5 or higher was accepted. The 
full output of the analysis can be found in Appendix 7. Consequently, four factors were 
identified, expressing several themes observed throughout the dataset. A summary of each 
factor and its component variables can be found in Appendix 8.  
 
Upon analysis, it was observed that the three questions (Q3, Q4, Q6) comprised the first factor 
expressing a common theme, respectively “learning efficacy”. Precisely, the questions regard 
an increase in one’s confidence toward their entrepreneurial competencies, as well as an 
increase in understanding of the processes and challenges associated with venture creation. 
Furthermore, this finding was compared to the results obtained in the one-tailed t-test 
analyses. It was revealed that for all items comprised in this factor, group “B” recorded 
significantly higher mean scores than group “A”, thus contradicting the third hypothesis of 
the study. The factor analysis indicated that the theory-oriented approach led to overall more 
positive attitudes regarding one’s ability to understand and attain academic success in 
entrepreneurial education.   
 
The second factor obtained comprises three questions (Q13, Q14, Q15) that regard an 
underlying theme, respectively the “quality of online instruction”. More specifically, the 
questions explore participants’ opinions regarding the clarity of the materials, the relevancy 
of the examples used for instruction, as well as the novelty of the content and its 
presentation. Comparing the factor analysis with the one-tailed t-test results, it was observed 
that for all the items of this factor, those in group “B” recorded significantly higher mean 
scores than in group “A”, thus contradicting the fourth hypothesis of the study. This further 
highlighted that overall, the quality of online instruction designed through the theory-
oriented pedagogy was regarded as being superior to that of treatment “A”.  
 
The third factor contains three questions (Q9, Q10, Q12) that express a common theme, 
respectively “entrepreneurial intentions”. The factor analysis was compared with the one-
tailed t-rest results, however, no consistency has been found. Item 10 registered a

significantly higher mean score for those in group “A”, indicating that these participants may 
have enjoyed the learning experience more. Items 9 and 12 registered no significant 
differences between “A” and “B”. The average means for these constructs were between 3.2

a positive attitude towards becoming an entrepreneur and founding a startup in the future. 
These findings are promising, considering that the sample was composed of students that

a venture as compared to the other group. Contrasting to the insights gathered through the 
literature review, no categorical evidence was found to indicate that the practical-oriented 
pedagogy yields a higher increase in entrepreneurial intentions as compared to the theory-
oriented approach.  
 
For the third hypothesis of the study, which concerned the evaluation of the “learning 
efficacy” construct, the results indicated that generally, the theory-oriented approach led to 
higher confidence in learners’ abilities to comprehend the learning materials and attain 
success in formal entrepreneurial education. Five items of the survey were used to measure 
this construct. For four of these items, the data analysis showed that the theoretical approach 
led to significantly more positive results than the opposite treatment. These findings were 
further confirmed by the exploratory factor analysis, respectively through the first factor 
obtained. Furthermore, the only item where the results were contradictory, was question 7 
which revealed that the practical approach led to a better command of creating a business 
model than the theoretical approach. These insights emphasize the different benefits 
attained through the “about” and “for” pedagogies in entrepreneurial education. 
Respectively the fact that the theoretical approach is conducive to helping learners 
understand the characteristics, challenges, and processes of entrepreneurial activities, 
whereas the practical approach is more suitable for learning about the application of specific 
methods and tools associated with venture creation. As a result, it must be emphasized that 
the findings of this study contradict the literature review, as the data analysis revealed that 
largely, the theoretical approach led to higher learning efficacy than the practical-oriented 
pedagogy. 
 
For the fourth hypothesis of the study, which regarded the quality of online instruction, the 
data analysis revealed that generally, the theory-oriented approach produced a more positive 
effect as compared to the other pedagogy. This perspective is not only sustained by the 
results of the t-test analysis but also illustrated in the factor analysis concerning the second 
factor identified. Furthermore, it must be noted that seven items of the survey regarded the 
evaluation of this construct. Out of these, five items recorded statistically significantly higher 
results for the theoretical approach as compared to the practical one. Furthermore, one item, 
respectively question 17 registered no significant differences in the effects of the two 
approaches, however, the mean value obtained by both groups was 3.5. This indicated that 
the participants had the same opinion, slightly agreeing with the fact that the design of the 
learning units motivated them to perform well. However, a single item of the survey 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
34
registered significantly higher scores for the practical-oriented treatment, respectively item 
16 that regarded active learning, specifically the fact that the interactive exercises directly 
involved the subjects in the learning process. Overall, the insights gathered through the 
analysis of data contradict the literature review and initial hypothesis, as no concrete 
evidence has been found to indicate that the practical-oriented approach leads to a higher 
quality of online instruction than the theoretical pedagogy.  
 
Lastly, for the fifth hypothesis of the study, which regarded online learning satisfaction, no 
significant differences were found in the effects of the two treatments. This theme was 
directly addressed in question 20 which read “I feel satisfied with the learning of this 
module”. The mean value recorded for the practical group was 3.4 and 3.6 for the theoretical 
group. Even though the latter group registered slightly more agreement on average, these 
differences are not statistically significant. Thus, this finding indicates that both learning 
experiences generated similar learning satisfaction between the participants of the two 
groups. These results are not consistent with the hypothesis, as the data analysis did not 
reveal any concrete evidence towards the fact that the practical pedagogy yields higher online 
learning satisfaction as compared to the theoretical approach.  
 
DISCUSSION 
 
In this chapter, the significance of the findings obtained through the data analysis will be 
presented in the context of the overall research. The results revealed by the data analysis 
process previously described, largely showed that the practical-oriented approach is not 
necessarily more efficient in teaching entrepreneurship and innovation as compared to the 
theory-oriented pedagogy. Even though the literature review revealed a preponderance of 
publications that support the use of constructivist methods, especially in Western society, the 
insights collected emphasized that both pedagogies have strengths as well as drawbacks 
when analyzed in different contexts.  
 
When it comes to the knowledge acquired by the participants, it was revealed that the two 
approaches yielded largely similar outcomes, with no significant differences being recorded 
between their effects. However, one item of the quiz, respective question 6, showed that 
those in the theoretical group, on average, obtained significantly higher scores than their 
counterparts. Interpreting the significant difference obtained in the one-tailed t-test for this 
item of the quiz required an investigation of how each group explored the definition of the 
Value Proposition as a building block of the Business Model Canvas. On one hand, the learning 
experience in treatment “A” was focused on a collaborative problem-based activity, whereas 
the instructional and learning materials were minimized. The participants were encouraged 
to explore additional resources on the topic, but this was not enforced in the design of the 
learning unit. The exploratory nature of this pedagogical design encouraged the subjects to 
learn about the Value Proposition through an inductive reasoning process. On the other hand, 
the learning experience of group “B” was designed with an emphasis on theory, thus it 
contained extensive information about the building blocks of the Business Model Canvas. The 
participants were repeatedly exposed to the definition of the Value Proposition in multiple 
formats and were not allowed to progress within the learning unit until they had 
demonstrated a full command of the concepts presented. Therefore, it must be highlighted 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
35
for this group the core theories were presented straightforwardly, thus the subjects learned 
about the Value Proposition through a deductive reasoning process. Furthermore, it is 
paramount to consider the premise that all participants had a low proficiency level within the 
field of Innovation and Entrepreneurship. In this context, sources in literature argue that 
learners should first be presented with directly expressed facts and concepts through a more 
direct and didactic pedagogical approach. (Duke et al., 2013) This position is also consistent 
with claims of academics such as Ertmer and Newby (2013) and Arghode et al. (2017) who 
argued that theory-oriented approaches are more suitable for novice learners, helping them 
understand and identify the standards concepts within a topic. The result of the t-test for 
question 6 of the quiz confirms this position.  
 
This finding illustrates that when designing a learning experience, the educator must be aware 
of how the learning process is impacted by individuals’ proficiency levels within a specific 
topic. (Higgings and Elliott, 2011) The result obtained revealed that a theoretical approach 
inspired by behaviorist principles should be used first to help the learners master the basic 
contents within a specific course or program. (Duke et al., 2013) Next, the constructivist 
approach should be utilized when the learners became more familiar with the subject, thus 
being able to infer the general to the particular and apply the already acquired knowledge to 
more complex cases. (Ertmer and Newby, 2013) This perspective is also highlighted in the 
prevalent discussion found in literature on whether the students should be first presented 
with theories and examples or problems to solve. (Ashman et al., 2020) According to the 
cognitive load theory and later research, it has been found that studying an example or theory 
first helps learners focus on assimilating new information in their schema, rather than using 
working memory resources to deal with guessing possible solutions to a problem. (Paas et al., 
2020) Thus, providing a straightforward presentation of the materials first can aid the 
knowledge 
retention 
of 
students, 
especially 
when 
dealing 
with 
complex 
topics. (ibid.) Consequently, even though each pedagogy has its avid supporters in the 
academic community, it must be noted that neither one is categorically more suitable for 
teaching the discipline of innovation and entrepreneurship. Instead, the teachers should use 
the appropriate pedagogical and instructional methods based on a strong understanding of 
the student's characteristics, prior knowledge, and experience. (Higgins and Elliot, 2011) 
 
Furthermore, a similar perspective is also highlighted by the insights gathered through the 
survey, specifically those related to the “learning efficacy” construct. The analysis of data 
showed that the theoretical-oriented treatment was, on average, more effective in educating 
learners about the responsibilities, duties, and potential consequences of an entrepreneurial 
career. By comparison, it was revealed that the practical-oriented group felt, on average, 
more confident in designing a business model compared to those in the theoretical group. 
Therefore, it can be concluded that when designing online learning experiences, educators 
must not only consider students’ proficiency levels within a topic but also take into account 
what are the skills, competencies, and overall learning objectives that the participants should 
attain through a specific educational program or course. (Piperopoulos et al., 2015) If the 
learning unit aims to educate participants about entrepreneurship as a potential career and 
introduce them generally to what venture creation entails, then a theoretical-oriented 
pedagogy should be adopted. However, if the goal is to develop aptitudes regarding the 
application of specific methods and tools used in entrepreneurship, then a “learn by doing”, 
active approach should be favored.

An interesting insight gathered from the analysis of the “learning efficacy” construct was that 
the group presented with the “about” pedagogy felt, on average, that the learning experience 
was more conducive to enhancing their entrepreneurial competencies as compared to the 
other treatment group. It is often argued in literature for the opposite effect, namely that the 
practical-oriented approach is more efficient in fostering entrepreneurial aptitudes. 
(Hammond et al., 2001; Swan, 2005) The reason why the theoretical group felt more 
positively toward the development of their competencies may relate to the principles of 
andragogy. This theory states that adult learners want to acquire knowledge quickly and 
immediately understand how the learnings can be applied to their personal and professional 
life. (Johnson, 2014) As it has been previously described, the direct instructional strategy 
focused on knowledge transmission may enable learners with a low proficiency level within a 
topic to acquire a solid foundation of knowledge more readily. (Duke et al., 2013; Ruutmann 
and Kipper, 2011) Furthermore, the “about” pedagogy may be more conducive to developing 
one’s understanding of what the venture creation process entail in general. This highlights 
the perspective that although the “for” pedagogy may enable learners to train 
entrepreneurial behaviors and associated tasks and procedures, the theoretical approach also 
contributes to increasing learners’ confidence toward the understanding of the topic, and 
thus towards their ability to be successful within entrepreneurial practices. Therefore, this is 
another example that indicates the fact that both teaching approaches should be utilized in 
combination, as positive effects on learning efficacy can be acquired both through practical 
experiences, and relevant didactic materials such as readings, lectures, and case studies.  
 
Next, the quality of online instruction was, on average, more highly regarded in the 
theoretical-oriented group. This illustrates the fact that in a virtual learning environment, 
clear, detailed, and straightforward instructional materials are preferred. These results 
contradict the minimalist theory that recommends the use of incomplete materials in e-
learning. (Carroll, 2014) While it is generally agreed that adult learners must be provided with 
the ability to be autonomous, self-directed, and offered the opportunity to explore and 
improvise, educators must not overlook the importance of devising explicit and coherent 
instruction. (O’neill et al., 2004) This is because the dynamics of the teacher-student 
interaction are different in a virtual learning environment as opposed to a classroom setting. 
(Arghode et al., 2017) More specifically, in e-learning, the teacher presence is not continuous, 
especially in asynchronous settings or in activities that do not involve the presence of a live 
facilitator. (ibid.) This situation is described in literature through the concept of transactional 
distance, which explains that in online environments learners can feel disconnected from 
their instructors and peers, thus oftentimes being at risk to become more anxious in e-
learning as compared to in-person settings. (Smith et al., 2008) Therefore, teachers should 
ensure that the learners can progress smoothly through the learning units with minimal 
support, whilst trying to reduce the number of ambiguities that may appear. (Siemens, 2002) 
For this reason, educators must be consistent and systematic in the design of the instructional 
materials, fostering students’ capacity to self-instruct and exercise self-regulation strategies. 
(Kebritchi et al., 2017; Rasheed et al., 2020) The learning units should create challenging 
academic scenarios that are motivating and encouraging to the personal and professional 
development of the participants. (Hartnett et al., 2011) However, educators must ensure that 
the design of the instruction is not confusing or conducive to negative emotional states 
because this could distract or demotivate the learners. (Ekmekci, 2013) Therefore, the 
==PAGE:39==

instructional materials must support the participants in the knowledge acquisition process, 
ensuring enough support to successfully replace the traditional continuous teacher presence 
encountered in classrooms. (ibid.) 
 
Another theme of interest comprised in the “quality of online learning” construct regarded 
elements of active participation. Confirmatory to the insights gathered from literature, the 
data analysis process revealed that the practical-oriented approach elicited more active 
engagement and interactivity. Generally, academic publications within the field of 
entrepreneurship education encourage the use of collaborative activities. (Swan, 2005) This 
is due to the belief that knowledge is acquired more easily through active participation, as 
well as the fact that learners can engage in more authentic learning situations, thus becoming 
more confident in their command of the topic and more satisfied with the learning process 
overall. (Bollinger et al., 2004; Olusegun, 2015) However, the findings of this study revealed 
an often overlooked pitfall associated with online collaboration, namely the fact that working 
on joint tasks can pose constraints to one’s ability to engage in self-regulated learning. 
(Hadwin et al., 2017) This is because the participants engaged in group work must negotiate 
control with their peers and enter a state of co-regulation. (ibid.) Thus, when designing online 
learning experiences in entrepreneurial education, the educator must consider the dynamics 
of collaboration in e-learning. (Smith et al., 2008; Greenhow et al., 2022) Oftentimes, virtual 
collaboration poses additional challenges to group work as compared to in-person 
interactions. (Muuro et al., 2014) Technology can pose difficulties in the communication

(Wong, 2007; Keengwe, 2017) In turn, these hindrances can lead to a lack of individual 
accountability, poor motivation, and negative effects on group interdependence. (ibid.) By 
contrast, the participants of the experiment that engaged in e-learning individually, reported 
feeling more control over the learning process. This is because they were able to personalize 
their learning approach and had full ownership over elements such as pace and sequence. 
(Broadbent et al., 2015; Kebritchi et al., 2017) Furthermore, an online environment designed 
for individual learning can enable the participants to experience the conveniences associated 
with online education, specifically the ability to choose the time and space for learning. (Singh 
et al., 2009) Additionally, various studies indicated that learners perceive the convenience 
and flexibility of online courses as more important than interaction with instructors and peers. 
(Bollinger, 2014) It is also claimed that offering opportunities for individual learning may 
foster lifelong learning attitudes in adult students. (Amarneh, 2021; Kant et al., 2021) 
 
Furthermore, no significant differences were recorded in the effects of the two approaches 
in terms of participants’ online learning satisfaction, when considering the results obtained 
for the survey question that directly investigated this matter. However, it must be highlighted 
that the concept of learning satisfaction regards a complex and multifaceted issue, and its 
overall effects can be further explored in-depth through a series of factors. More specifically, 
student learning satisfaction in online environments is heavily influenced by perceptions 
regarding the quality of instruction, interactivity, and learning efficacy. (Lo, 2022) Based on 
the findings gathered through data analysis, it was revealed that on average, in terms of 
quality of instruction and learning efficacy, the theoretical group expressed more positive 
feedback. The results regarding interactivity showed that the practical-oriented pedagogy

by leveraging the respective strengths of both pedagogies, the overall learning satisfaction 
could be further increased. In addition to this, another factor that plays a crucial role in one’s

publications that motivation enhances learners’ engagement, satisfaction with the learning 
environment, and intrinsic achievement goals. (Woraphiphat et al., 2023) This was also

they felt motivated to learn about this topic. This factor could explain, in part, why the 
participants of the two treatments reported almost equal opinions on their learning

Lastly, when it came to evaluating the effects of the contrasting pedagogies on the 
entrepreneurial intentions of the participants, no significant differences were recorded. 
However, it must be considered that sources in literature argued that the learning satisfaction 
of the participants may influence the effects on entrepreneurial intentions. (Karp, 2006) This 
position is also confirmed through the exploratory factor analysis that indicated that 
entrepreneurial education may have the potential to positively impact learners’ aspirations 
of starting a new venture, especially in instances where the participants enjoyed the learning 
process. On this account, it can be assumed that if educators design e-learning units that meet 
the learning needs of the students, whilst being engaging and motivating, then they can also 
foster positive attitudes towards entrepreneurship. This position is also supported by 
academics such as Barba-Sanchez et al. (2023) that sustain that learners who report high 
levels of satisfaction in entrepreneurial education are also more likely to have more positive 
attitudes regarding their desire to engage in entrepreneurship.  
 
IMPLICATIONS 
 
In this chapter, the findings of the study will be explained in connection to their impact and 
possible effects in real-world contexts. This is especially relevant as the objective of this study 
is to contribute to the field of entrepreneurial education by offering suggestions for suitable 
and effective practices that could improve the quality of e-learning. Firstly, the implications 
of the insights gathered in relation to higher education will be presented. Secondly, the 
implications of this study will also be described in a broader context, as the findings can be 
relevant in connection to industry practices, respectively to the case of entrepreneurial 
learning in organizations. Lastly, implications for the field of research will be presented. 
 
Implications for higher education  
 
As presented in the “Discussion” chapter, the findings of this study pose numerous 
implications regarding entrepreneurial education for adult learners, in universities. 
Contributing to best practices in this field, one of the main suggestions for teachers is to 
provide a balance of both pedagogies to meet the diverse learning needs of students. The 
findings indicate that educators should choose their pedagogical and instructional strategies 
==PAGE:41==
 
 
39
based on a strong understanding of the learners’ proficiency level within the topic. Therefore, 
it is recommended that teachers should analyze the target audience first. For students with 
low levels of knowledge and experience, a direct, theoretical-oriented approach should be 
used to help the participants acquire and master the basic concepts within a particular 
subject. Next, educators should adopt a practical-oriented approach based on constructivist 
principles to encourage the students to apply the already acquired knowledge to more 
complex, problem-based cases. Similarly, the designers of learning experiences must also 
consider what are the intended outcomes of the entrepreneurial course or program before 
they start choosing pedagogies and instructional strategies. A theory-oriented pedagogy is 
useful in teaching general facts about entrepreneurship and venture creation, whereas the 
practical approach is more suitable for developing specific competencies and skills related to 
the application of tools and methods used in the venture creation process. 
 
Next, the findings of the study suggest that in online learning environments, the instructional 
materials must be clear, consistent, and systematic to alleviate the lack of continuous teacher 
presence. Therefore, good quality of online instruction is achieved when confusion and 
ambiguities are consciously eliminated by the educator, allowing the participants to focus on 
the learning materials without the need for additional support. In doing so, the teacher must 
keep a balance between the guidance and instruction offered and the creation of thought-
provoking and motivating learning scenarios. Furthermore, integrating interactivity into the 
design of the learning experience was found to be crucial in e-learning. It must be highlighted 
that interactivity can be obtained both through student-content interaction and peer 
collaboration. The use of authoring tools is recommended as these allow for novel learning 
environment designs, incorporating visual and auditory materials, as well as a diverse range 
of engaging exercises that provide automatic feedback. Presenting the materials in various 
formats caters to different learning styles possessed by different individuals. (Liu, 2016) 
Furthermore, according to behaviorist principles, the technological affordance of system 
feedback may represent an effective strategy for improving learners’ knowledge acquisition 
and their satisfaction with the learning experience. (Ertmer and Newby, 2013) In terms of 
peer collaboration, educators must consider the additional challenges that come with online 
interactivity and their associated risks. The potential drawbacks can be mitigated by 
integrating reliable technologies into teaching, as well as devising easy-to-follow instruction, 
and protocols regarding online communication and responses to technical difficulties. 
However, it must be emphasized that individual learning may allow the students to better 
engage in a self-regulatory process and to benefit from the conveniences and flexibilities of 
online learning. Therefore, a balance between individual and group tasks should be achieved. 
Ultimately, the decision of integrating collaborative tasks should be made based on careful 
consideration of the learning unit objectives, the type of learning content, and the needs and 
abilities of the students.  
 
Furthermore, to increase the learning satisfaction of the participants in e-learning, educators 
must ensure that the learners’ expectations are met in terms of the learning content, the 
quality of online instruction, the efficient use of educational technologies, as well as offering 
opportunities for interaction with the instructor and peers. Additionally, creating an 
enjoyable and motivating learning experience by considering all the previously mentioned 
strategies has been found to potentially have a positive impact on participants’ intentions of 
starting a new venture. Therefore, by striving to create an enjoyable and motivating learning 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
40
experience, educators may be able to encourage students’ aspirations of becoming 
entrepreneurs. The insights obtained also suggested that to increase entrepreneurial 
intentions, educators must stimulate students learning efficacy, by offering learning 
opportunities aimed at fostering confidence in one’s general understanding of facts and 
concepts, as well as confidence in one’s abilities to engage in processes specific to 
entrepreneurship. Therefore, as previously stated, both the theoretical and practical 
pedagogical approaches must be utilized based on a thorough understanding of the 
appropriate context of prescribing each strategy. 
 
Implications for the industry  
 
 
In businesses and organizations, the need for employees that have entrepreneurial 
orientations has been highlighted in literature. (Baggen et al., 2016) This is because 
entrepreneurship is increasingly viewed as a way of thinking and behaving that can positively 
impact society and the economy. (ibid.) More specifically, corporate entrepreneurship 
training can enable employees to generate business ideas that lead to the creation of new 
subsidiaries or spinoff companies, as well as instigate strategic innovation within the existing 
scope of the firm. (Cooney, 2012) Furthermore, entrepreneurial training may equip staff with 
the ability to make better decisions in their job roles, by becoming more able to shift between 
strategies of action based on specific goals, needs, and circumstances. (ibid.) Additionally, 
entrepreneurial training may also help the employees to adopt a more customer-centered 
mindset, and gain useful knowledge related to financial and human resource 
management. (ibid.) Confirmatory to this belief are studies that showed that entrepreneurial 
training can help small businesses transform into growth-oriented firms. (Baggen et al., 2016) 
 
As the field of corporate entrepreneurship is not widely investigated and could benefit from 
further research, it has been emphasized that various learnings can be taken and adapted 
from how entrepreneurship is approached in the higher education context. (ibid.) Various 
studies characterized the link between theory-oriented, formal training participation and 
increased firm performance as being weak. (Cooney, 2012) One of the main disadvantages to 
traditional training in companies found in literature was the fact that the delivery of the 
content was not appropriate for the audience, as employees perceived the formal programs 
as being too academic and theoretical. (ibid.)  Moreover, analysis of such programs revealed 
that formal entrepreneurial training is usually not tailored to the individual needs of the 
participants, being perceived as too “generic”. (ibid.)  Therefore, similar to how teachers in 
universities must consider the student characteristics and learning objectives when designing 
the online learning environment, those responsible for training employees within 
entrepreneurship must also take into account what are the backgrounds, roles, goals, and 
motivations of their audience. On this account, it can be assumed that to generate positive 
effects in the modern business environment, a theoretical-oriented approach should not be 
favored. Instead, an alternative model characterized by a “learn by doing” approach should 
be recommended. Through this method, the employees can dynamically develop behaviors 
and competencies associated with entrepreneurship. Rather than focusing on traditional 
business management training, entrepreneurial education in organizations should foster 
abilities to innovate, adapt to changing environments, and creativity in decision-making.   
 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
41
Therefore, entrepreneurial learning in the industry should be primarily done through practical 
and experiential strategies conducted through a more informal, but continuous approach. 
This means that rather than solely relying on employee attendance in sporadic training 
classes, staff members should always be involved or encouraged to invest part of their time 
in innovation-related activities. Furthermore, as in higher education, employees must be 
offered opportunities to collaborate within teams and departments. As opposed to working 
in silos, open collaboration would allow organic structures and strategies to emerge that 
could benefit the overall business efficiency and innovation within a firm. (Cooney, 2012). 
Conforming to the constructivist principles, staff members should be able to construct 
individualized meanings of the industry environment and the threats and opportunities within 
it, by being given the freedom to observe and investigate. Therefore, employees should be 
allowed to collaborate externally with stakeholders and clients. They should be encouraged 
to attend conferences, business events, and engage with professional networks outside of 
the firm. Features such as collaboration and external exploration could help staff members to 
receive diverse perspectives useful in identifying and validating perceived business 
opportunities. (Edmonds, 2016) Furthermore, as students must have control over their 
academic learning process, employees must be allowed to have a degree of control within 
their job. This means that staff members should not only be praised for completing tasks 
efficiently but also encouraged to make independent choices and think critically and 
creatively to find novel ways to solve problems.  
 
However, as in the case of higher education, not all entrepreneurial learning can be obtained 
through a “learn by doing” approach. (Baggen et al., 2016) This is why brief introductions to 
key aspects of entrepreneurial matters could be presented in a more direct and theoretical 
manner. Investing in such learning opportunities for employees would be beneficial 
predominantly for developing shared understandings of the processes and ways of thinking 
specific to entrepreneurship. Moreover, especially in the case of larger organizations that 
possess the necessary resources, a designated team of professionals could be devised, or 
experts could be hired periodically to consult managers and team leaders on how to foster 
entrepreneurial behaviors from their employees. Similarly, the designated training personnel 
could organize workshops for various departments, facilitating and guiding employee teams 
in how to deal with specific innovation issues that they currently face. Furthermore, 
behaviorist principles could also be utilized for corporate entrepreneurial learning, especially 
in terms of offering incentives. These incentives can be monetary but could also be linked to 
the professional and personal development of the employee. According to the theory of 
andragogy, adult learners are goal-oriented and must understand the importance of learning 
in connection to their professional or personal life. (Carroll, 2014) Therefore, the benefits of 
adopting and cultivating an entrepreneurial mindset as an employee should also be linked to 
improvements in performance. For instance, the benefits of acting in entrepreneurial ways 
on one’s job performance can be displayed and publicized on virtual employee portals.  
 
Overall, as in the case of higher education, both pedagogies can be leveraged in 
entrepreneurial learning in organizations when applied in suitable contexts. However, in the 
case of corporate training, more emphasis should be put on informal learning, where 
knowledge and competencies are distilled from action within the workplace. The leaders of 
the organization should deliberately focus on encouraging entrepreneurial behaviors and 
mindsets amongst their employees. For larger organizations, resources could be also invested 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
42
in theoretical training sessions, as well as specialized workshops aimed at addressing specific 
problems faced by different departments. Adopting a targeted approach to training could 
mitigate the risks of these efforts being perceived as too generic or too academic. 
 
Implication in research and future studies 
 
This was an exploratory study aimed at contributing to the field of entrepreneurial education 
research. However, it must be recognized that this study was marked by various limitations 
and had a fairly narrow scope. Additionally, it is often emphasized that, despite vast efforts, 
digital education in entrepreneurship remains an under-theorized field. (Cowling et al., 2022) 
Therefore, more research that integrates theory and evidence-based practices should be 
conducted to unveil the impacts of different pedagogical and instructional practices and their 
efficiency in virtual learning environments within this discipline. Various other research 
directions could be pursued to provide a pathway toward the identification of stronger effects 
and broader relevance in entrepreneurial education.  
 
For instance, in this study, the importance of learner motivation was briefly touched upon. In 
this sense, it would be relevant to conduct a more thorough investigation into the different 
types of motivation and the connection between these and specific learning materials, their 
perceived importance and utility, and the design of online instruction. Another pertinent 
exploration in this sense would regard the degree to which motivation is an intrinsic learner 
characteristic or an effect of the learning environment. This type of research could generate 
a deeper understanding of the strategies that teachers could use to foster positive attitudes 
toward entrepreneurship. Furthermore, a perspective that was not given prominence 
throughout this study but could have expanded the scope and relevance of the findings, is the 
impact of learners’ backgrounds and cultures on online entrepreneurial education. Precisely, 
an investigation into how culture affects the way people process information, how they 
perceive and react to feedback, as well as their expectations towards the design of the 
learning environment, could be devised. This research could provide evidence into how 
cultural backgrounds affect one’s learning satisfaction in entrepreneurial education, as well 
as providing useful insights for educators towards the creation of culturally responsive and 
inclusive e-learning units.  
 
Furthermore, another aspect that should be considered in evaluating the effects of online 
entrepreneurial education regards the fact that more longitudinal studies should be 
conducted. For instance, in terms of the measurement of long-term knowledge acquisition, 
Baker and Robinson (2018) claim in their research that the results measured by a test shortly 
after the end of instruction are not necessarily definitive proof of the actual knowledge 
acquired by the learners. Their results showed that learners, generally achieve increased 
scores in assessment when the evaluation is conducted immediately after instruction. 
However, it has been observed that six weeks later the same individuals register a significant 
decrease in their overall performance on the same assessment method. (ibid.) Therefore, 
measuring the long-term retention of the knowledge acquired by the participants would have 
enriched the observations on learning outcomes for this study, as well as being a relevant 
consideration for similar research that may be conducted in the future. Furthermore, another 
element worth to be further investigation refers to the fact that the present study only 
addressed the evaluation of entrepreneurial intentions, but not actual behavior. Although 
==PAGE:45==
 
 
43
intention is often considered the “best predictor” of one’s behavior (Piperopoulos et al., 
2015), it is in fact, only a forecast. Therefore, it would be significant to follow up on this aspect. 
One way of realizing this would be to evaluate the entrepreneurial intentions after the 
learners have been exposed to either practical-oriented or theoretical-oriented teaching, and 
then document their choice of career after graduation. This type of investigation would 
enable the researcher to further validate the intention-action relationship and the real effects 
of each pedagogy on one’s desire to become an entrepreneur.  
 
Lastly, it is important to highlight that this does not represent an exhaustive research agenda 
for digital education within the field of innovation and entrepreneurship. As previously stated, 
there is a need for more contributions to research aimed at closing the gaps in the current 
literature. For example, studies that include larger samples of participants, and more types of 
programs from various parts of the world should be conducted. Similarly, more research into 
best practices in e-learning within this discipline should be realized. Examples of important 
themes for further research in entrepreneurial education include studies on the effects of 
specific technologies in e-learning; studies on the development of online education quality 
standards and frameworks for assessment; and studies on the analysis of online course design 
and content development.  
 
CONCLUSION 
 
This research project aimed at evaluating and comparing the effects of two prevalent, but 
distinct pedagogies within the discipline of entrepreneurship and innovation in the context of 
e-learning in higher education. The overall goal was to contribute to the field of research 
within this field and offer suggestions for suitable and effective practices that may improve 
the quality of online education. The findings revealed that neither model should dominate in 
teaching and provided evidence for the strength of combining both the practical-oriented and 
theoretical-oriented approaches in creating a well-rounded learning experience. Teachers 
should prescribe the “about” and “for” pedagogies upon a thorough understanding of learner 
characteristics and the desired outcomes of the instruction, whilst also taking into 
consideration the dynamics of online learning and collaboration. Instructional materials and 
strategies must be devised accordingly, maintaining a balance between the creation of 
challenging learning scenarios and sufficient learner support. Additionally, the insights 
gathered throughout the paper indicate that learning experiences that are perceived as high 
in quality and that lead to increased student learning efficacy may generate higher learning 
satisfaction, and thus, may lead to better attitudes towards entrepreneurship as a career 
path. Overall, in the context online education, it must be highlighted that the role of the 
teacher has shifted from a facilitator and guide to a designer of learning environments. 
Educators should develop e-learning experiences using reliable and appropriate technologies, 
whilst continuously testing and exploring avenues toward the development of new practices 
in online education. On this account, teaching entrepreneurship and innovation in e-learning 
formats can be seen both as an art and a science, as it requires the use of technical and 
creative perspectives. However, digital pedagogy and its effects on learners are not entirely 
understood yet, thus educators and researchers must continue to investigate this field and 
document the newly found best practices, contributing to a culture of professional support.
