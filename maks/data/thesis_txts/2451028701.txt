Abstract
One of the projects on the Denmark Hospital Plan 2020 is to construct the North
Wing treatment facility.
The North Wing is a step towards a more modern and
specialized Rigshospitalet and a step toward a value-based care structured. The chal-
lenge that remains at the North Wing is to assess the patient flow at the central hall
where it is estimated around 600 patients will arrive daily along with their relatives.
The managers’ main concern is if the seating capacity at the main waiting area will
be insufficient and how it will affect the patient flow within the North Wing. Also,
there is a need for investigate the coordination of the patient flow and the space uti-
lization in the central hall and in the Centre of Diagnostic Investigation departments
located at the ground floor. This project aims to analyze and investigate the pa-
tient flow patterns to improve decision making on the planning and thereby improve
the quality of service within the North Wing.
A discrete-event simulation (DES)
model was designed and developed to analyze the patient flow and identify whether
poor patient flow, queues or overcrowding could occur.
DES has been proven to
be the most suitable simulation application for analyzing healthcare systems and it
allows to study the behavior of the system under various circumstances. The Any-
logic simulation software was selected to construct the model, where the pedestrian
library and the process modeling library were combined together to address the North
Wing patient flow. Additionally, a simulation-based Six-Sigma analytic framework
was applied to facilitate the process of embedding the DES model and to carry out
the project in a systematic way. The findings from the simulation model indicates
that an overcrowding could occur at the central hall after the patient peak arrival
hours. Thereby, different experimental scenarios were tested to investigate further
the impact of changes on the system. The experimental scenarios reveal that the per-
formance of the system can be improved and it is recommended to combine the main
waiting area in the central hall with the waiting area in the Department of Diagnostic
Radiology. However, as there were many assumptions and limitations, that has to be
taken into consideration.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
Denmark has a long tradition of public welfare provision and has had a relatively
stable healthcare structure for more than 30 years [1]. The Danish public healthcare
sector has been characterized by being a decentralized system since 1970, where the
municipalities and the county had the primary role of providing a satisfactory health-
care service and the state more specialized service. This decentralized administrative
structure was questioned and debated whether the three administrative levels, mu-
nicipalities, county and state, were necessary since each of them had the authority to
levy taxes [2]. Additionally, was the structure considered to be composed of too many
small units at both the municipal and the county levels, where the main concern was
that levels were too small to provide an efficient hospital service of a satisfactory
quality [3].
Few political parties recognized this as a structural problem and it was brought to
the attention of the media in 2002 where the debate increased and possible future so-
lutions suggested. Subsequently, in 2004, a report, commissioned by the government,
was published where the report listed the advantages and the disadvantages of the
structure and defined the suggested alternative structure for the public healthcare sec-
tor. A political agreement on a major structural reform was reached shortly after the
three levels were maintained, but with a reduction of a number of municipalities from
271 to 98 and a replacement of the 13 previous counties by five regions. The main
objective with the reform was to create administrative units that were large enough
to support a hospital structure with few acute hospitals in each region that serves as
a university hospital and to centralize high specialized care in fewer hospitals. The
reform took effect in 2007 and from that time to 2016 the Danish hospital structure
was redesigned and reorganized which resulted in decreasing the public hospitals from
40 to 21 hospitals. Some hospitals were closed down or merged, other remodeled to
healthcare centres [3]. The construction of new acute hospitals is still taking place to-
day in the five region where hospitals are being extended and moderated [4]. The plan
is to decrease the number of beds compared to the ones they substitute and thereby
admit far fewer patients to the specialized wards by hopefully improving the munic-
ipal service and the effectiveness of the treatments. This will enable the patients to
be discharged earlier and prevent an unessential hospital admission. The government
expert panel expect to reduce the beds by 20% and increase the outpatient visits by
50% by the moderation process before 2020, according to the Hospital Plan 2020 [3].

Additionally, the renewal hospitals will improve and ensure higher quality, increase
the efficiency of resources, be able to meet the future demand, while at the same time
create an attractive and dynamic working environment for healthcare employees.
One of these renewal and modernization hospitals is the Rigshospitalet. Rigshospi-
talet is the largest university hospital in Denmark and has been Denmark’s leading
hospital in highly specialized treatment for decades. In the structural reform, the
Rigshospitalet plays a key role at the region level since the plan is to remain as the
leading hospital in providing a large part of the highly specialized treatments. Addi-
tionally, will Rigshospitalet play the role of being responsible for more than half of the
health science research taking place in Denmark and thereby providing state-of-the-
art research in health science and treatments to be able to treat future patients. The
plan is to expand and renovate Rigshospitalet to become Denmark´s international
hospital and most modernized. The four main projects on the plan was to build a
patient hotel and administration, parking house, sterilization center and a new pa-
tient treatment facility, the North Wing. The patient hotel & administration building
and the parking house building have already been established and was brought into
service in 2015, but the sterilized center and the North Wing building are still being
constructed and are planed to be completed before the end of the year 2020.
1.1
The North Wing
The North Wing building is a step towards a more modern and specialized Rigshos-
pitalet, which must be established to remain the high quality of care and treatments.
In addition, it is a step toward more value-based care, where the North Wing build-
ing aims to offer better quality service, to improve patient healthcare experiences,
and deliver services at lower costs. The building will expand Rigshospitalet size by
approximately 54.000 m2 and will comprise 238 private rooms, 33 modern operating
theatres, 29 intensive beds, and outpatient departments. The departments which will
operate in the North Wing building will be a collection of highly specialized depart-
ments within the Neuroscience Centre, the Centre of Head and Orthopaedics, the
Centre of Diagnostic Investigation and the Service Center. The main planning, de-
signing, and construction of the North Wing have already been established, but the
inside construction is still taking place.
Setting up, designing and building the North Wing treatment facility is a complex
task. The wide range of medical services and functional units which will be provided
at the North Wing can be challenging to structure as they serve and support a di-
verse group of users and stakeholders. It is crucial to managing this diverse group of
users, which includes both health care employees, patients, and relatives, to provide
a safe and productive environment. When a patient arrives at the hospital in need
of medical attention, a productive and effective patient flow that can facilitate the
timely care that the patient needs. It has been proven that an effective patient flow

delivers a beneficial medical outcome for patients, satisfied patients, and reduction of
healthcare cost [5]. Therefore, it is essential to ensure an effective patient flow at the
North Wing to provide treatment to patients in a timely and effective manner.
One of the challenges that remain at the North Wing is to assess the patient flow at
the central hall on the ground floor, where it is estimated around 600 patients will

at the North Wing and prevent the quality of service. The management team at the
Rigshospitalet is mainly concerned about if the seating capacity at the main waiting
area will be insufficient, meaning that it will not meet the patient demand, which
could result in unsatisfied patients towards the overall healthcare experience at the
North Wing. Furthermore, there is a need for coordination of the patient flow in
the central hall, and in the reception and waiting areas at the Centre of Diagnostic
Investigation(CDI) departments located at the ground floor, which are the Blood
Bank and the Department of Diagnostic Radiology. The management team at the
CDI have a concern that the space at their waiting areas will also be insufficient.
1.2
Statement of Purpose
The purpose of this project is to identify and investigate whether overcrowding will
occur at the central hall due to insufficient seating capacity at the main waiting area
in the North Wing, and how it will affect the overall patient flow and the quality of
service. Moreover, investigate the coordination the patient flow and the use of the
waiting areas within the Blood Bank and the Department of Diagnostic Radiology,
and the central hall. These concerns lead to the formulation of the key research ques-
tion of the master project. The question is formulated here below:
Research Question:
RQ: How will the seating capacity at the main waiting area affect the patient flow?
The research question will investigate the patient flow at the ground floor within the
North Wing and predict of whether an overcrowded situation will occur, which is
the presence of more people in a space than it is comfortable, safe, or permissible
[6]. Additionally, the results from the question will help investigate if there is a need
to coordinate the patient flow and the waiting area space at the Blood Bank and
at the Department of Diagnostic Radiology. The ability to predict the flow of the
patients will help the North Wing management team to support their decision of how
to manage the flow on the ground floor, investigate where the constraints arise, who
they are, and what alternative improvements can be suggested based on the results.

CHAPTER2
Literature Review
The healthcare market has been shifting toward value-based care over the past years.
Previously, volume-based care was the standard, where hospitals operated mostly on a
fee-for-service approach, meaning that their goal was to deliver the greatest number
of services to the highest number of people, but in response to changing markets
and customer preference, hospitals are transitioning to value-based care structure,
meaning fee-for-value. The value-based healthcare structure is driven by providing
the best care and customer experiences at the lowest cost [7]. The providers’ mindset
should focus on quality over quantity, which entails that providers get paid based
on the quality of care they give patients, rather than the quantity of care they give
[8]. In the fee-for-service approach, each department independently addressed the
patient symptoms that fall under their concern of care. On the other hand, in the
value-based care approach, the focus is on connecting departments to improve overall
health long-term [9]. This change, from focusing on quantity to concentrate on quality,
has had a positive effect by increasing the patient’s quality of care and satisfaction,
provided overall health improvements for the population and lower the costs of care.
However, the change has not been an easy adjustment since many challenges need
to be addressed to meet a quality healthcare system successfully. Providing a high-
quality care service within a healthcare organization is critical to delivering excellent
patient care experience and appropriated treatment. Effective work and a variety of
factors influence the provision of high-quality service within a complex facility such as
a healthcare organization and every part in each process need to be carefully managed
to meet the expectations. However, to provide and ensure the quality care service
within the North Wing, and meet the value-based care structure, it is important to
understand what involves in quality-based healthcare service. This entails defining
what quality healthcare is, what are the desired expectations, and who desires to
meet and receive these expectations.
This chapter focus on studies and methods which have been carried out to support a
value-based care structure and improve healthcare services. Section 2.1 investigates
studies about healthcare quality and their definition of quality in healthcare. In the
following Section 2.2, the simulation technique used in this project is described and
its different usage in healthcare. The last section, Section 2.3, covers recent studies
of simulation frameworks which have been applied to systematic guide the simulation
model construction.

2.1
Healthcare Quality
Quality is a term widely used not only within healthcare but throughout society and
in all industry fields. Managers have recognized the strategic importance of quality
in current competitive markets, but no conclusive definition of quality has been ac-
cepted [10]. However, the various definition of quality exists which all share common
elements. The common elements of the definitions are that quality involves meeting
or exceeding customer expectations, which are the values that serve a useful purpose
when purchasing a product or service, quality is dynamic, since it can change over
time, and thereby quality must be continuously improved. Quality can also be viewed
from various perspectives, and like beauty, quality exists in the eye of the beholder.
What one person perceives to be quality, another person might not consider it a qual-
ity.
Thereby, when defining healthcare quality, various healthcare stakeholders must be
considered. These stakeholders can be grouped into three categorize, consumers, pur-
chasers and providers. Health consumers are the people who use the health service,
patients and their relatives, and they all expect quality in the delivery of healthcare
service. Patients want to receive the right treatments and experience good outcomes.
All of the consumers want to receive a satisfaction service where their healthcare facil-
ity provides clean and pleasant care and their doctors to operate on the best available
technology [11]. Also, consumers desire to have minimal to no wait times in waiting
rooms [12]. Purchasers can view quality differently or in terms of cost-effectiveness.
They are usually individuals and organizations that pay for healthcare services, but
can also be consumers if they are financially responsible for some or all of their health-
care costs. Viewing quality in terms of cost-effectiveness means that they want value
in return for their healthcare expenditures. This applies both to individuals and or-
ganizations. Providers can also be individuals and organizations. They provide the
healthcare service and care, which include the doctors, nurses, radiographers, techni-
cians, clinical support, clerical staff, managers, hospitals, outpatient clinics, and all
other institutions that provide care. The providers can view quality in the same way
as the consumers and the purchases, but in addition to the consumers’ and the pur-
chases’ values, providers are concerned about the service delivery and legal liability.
These concerns can both influence how providers define quality, and therefore it is
crucial for them to provide and to manage them properly.
The quality priorities which each healthcare stakeholder group views as an important
factor of a quality healthcare service experience are necessary to take into consider-
ation when defining what healthcare quality is. The definition of healthcare quality
has been evolving since 1966, when Dr. Avedis Donabedian, professor and renowned
health services researcher, noted first that healthcare quality is a “remarkably diffi-
cult notion to define.” [13]. Since then a couple of definition have been released, but
the definition that has stood the test of time was published in 1990 by the Institute
of Medicine (IOM). The IOM definition states that ”Quality of care is the degree to
==PAGE:21==
2.1 Healthcare Quality
7
which health services for individuals and populations increase the likelihood of desired
health outcomes and are consistent with current professional knowledge.” [14, p. 21].
However, in 2001, the IOM Committee on Quality of Health Care in America further
clarified the concept of healthcare quality in the report, Crossing the Quality Chasm:
A New Health System for the 21st Century [15]. The report states six dimensions of
quality which are studied to be essential to take into consideration when designing
and improving the high-quality healthcare system. The six dimensions highlight the
complex nature of a healthcare quality system and also influences all of the quality
priorities of the three stakeholder groups. The six dimensions are safety, effectiveness,
patient centeredness, timeliness, efficiency and equity. Each of the six dimensions is
distinguished, and none of them is more important than the others. Safety refers
to reduce the likelihood of harming patients by given medical treatments that are
intended to help them and the healthcare system itself. Effectiveness focuses on how
well treatments are carried out compared to how they are intended to treat and how
well resources and services are used. Patient centeredness relates both to customer
service and to considering and accommodating individual patient needs, preferences,
and values when making care decisions. Timeliness emphasizes access and system
responsiveness, patients care should be provided promptly when the patient needs it.
Efficiency refers to eliminate waste, including the waste of equipment, supplies, ideas,
and energy, and, as a result, the total cost of care. Equity is all about fairness, to
provide the best care to everyone, regardless of age, sex, race, financial status, or any
other demographic variable. These IOM healthcare six dimensions, together with the
1990 IOM report quality of care definition, encompass what most people consider as
attributes of healthcare quality and are recommended to be taken into account to
provide, improve and achieve a high-quality healthcare service. A systematic eval-
uation and systems thinking process is essential to be implemented to develop and
deliver a quality healthcare performance and desired level of excellence. This pro-
cess is known as quality management, and it entails reviewing and revising internal
processes to ensure they yield expected results. Quality management also helps to
manage, minimize, and mitigate risks. The risks can be the areas of weakness within
the quality dimensions gaps which present great opportunities to improve the quality
of care. The quality management activities which involve to seek out medical errors
and deficiencies that can be remedied through process improvement can be viewed
as proactive risk management [16]. Risk management is an integral part of keeping
patients safe by preventing risks to occur and ensuring that the care they receive does
not threaten their health or safety [17]. Healthcare managers need to focus on being
proactive, rather than being merely reactive, to achieve effective enterprise quality
and risk management. Being proactive can both drive competitive advantage and
sustain future profitability and growth. The proactive healthcare approach focuses
on what you can do to prevent potential health issues before they occur and using
simulation has been proven to be an effective method to identify and prevent risks,
and to guide improvement decisions.

2.2
Discrete Event Simulation in HealthCare
Simulation is the imitation of an operation of a real-world process or system over
time [18]. By emulating the logic and randomness of a system, such as the flow of
patients through a healthcare facility and the random duration of each treatment, a
simulation can be valuable for evaluating and comparing proposed system changes to
predict their impacts. Simulation allows the search for ”What if” questions which can
lead to optimal decisions by evaluating them through performance measures of the
system. Wide range of applications of simulation in healthcare have been published
and the number of publications have significantly increased over the years [19]. Var-
ious simulation techniques exist, but the majority of healthcare modern simulation
models are performed with either discrete-event simulation (DES), system dynamics
(SD), or agent-based (AB) modeling methodology. DES models are most suitable
for analyzing patient and process flows and the capacity and utilization of healthcare
resources. SD models are useful for analyzing healthcare policy decisions, and AB
models are most appropriated for a large variety of decisions, but especially those
requiring individual or population level analysis [20]. In this thesis project, a DES
modeling will be applied since it is the most suitable chose to evaluate the patient
flow patterns on the ground floor in the North Wing building. DES focuses on pro-
cesses within systems which can be described as a sequence of separated, discrete,
events.
Modeling an operation system with DES allows studying the behavior of
the system under various circumstances through the implementation of the suggested
model modifications instead of a rather costly realization of such modifications in
real systems. Moreover, it also allows the construction of modeling to any opera-
tional level of details as needed [4]. Healthcare manager and researchers who favor
the application of simulation in their field, desire to view methodologies and results
of different projects [21]. Thorwarth et al. (2009) [21] provided a structured review
of process simulation applications in healthcare areas of published DES papers from
1995 to 2008. The review showed a significant increase in the number of publica-
tions of DES applied in healthcare, where the publication rate doubled within the
last decade [21]. Another more recent review carried out by Zhang et al. (2018)
[22], also showed a significantly increased in number of publications of DES studies,
especially after 2010. He classifies the applications into four categories, health and
care systems operation, disease progression modeling, screening modeling, and health
behavior modeling. The study illustrated that the health and care systems operation,
which enable health care managers to understand better the underlying mechanisms
of how a system operates, comprehensively investigate complex relationships among
different sections within a system to make optimal operational and administrative
decisions, accounts for the most considerable proportion, or 65%, of all modeling pub-
lications. However, his study was limited in its applications as his categorizes studies
could overlap each other [22]. Recent simulation studies have shown that DES can
be applied to predict, improve, and optimize healthcare systems. Hoot et al. (2008)
[23] developed a DES of emergency department patient flow for the purpose of fore-
casting near-future operating conditions, which revealed to be a valuable tool for the

hospital operation. Baesler et al. (2003) [24] illustrated that simulation models can
be effectively used as a predictive tool for estimating the maximum possible demand
level that an emergency department staff could handle. Furthermore, DeRienzo et al.
(2017) [25] demonstrated how to develop a DES to help healthcare managers predict
and plan for staffing needs in a hospital by translating the process flow into a model.
The results indicated that the model could provide a valid method of modeling re-
source needs and also a forecast of how changes in a unit’s staffing, referral patterns,
or patient mix would affect a unit in a future state. However, the model had not
yet been implemented in a real-world planning environment when the research was
published to obtain an understanding of its utility, but the model was able to pro-
vide managers with better and more informed information for staffing decisions. The
recent demands for improved performance have grown significantly. Therefore, have
the DES applications increased vastly to guide improvement decisions, both in im-
proving leveling resources and improving system processes and performances [26]. For
example, Cao et al. (2013) [27] research was conducted to redesign the process flow of
a multi-clinic outpatient healthcare facility to improve patient satisfaction and maxi-
mize the healthcare staff productivity by using DES model. The DES model helped
to determine an optimal balance of staffing levels and service procedures within the
facility and also showed that by centralizing the reception the patients’ waiting time
could be reduced and the productivity of reception staff optimized. Another study
researched by Rahman et al. (2013) [28] demonstrated how a complex healthcare sys-
tem, an outpatient cancer clinic, could be translated into a DES model. The study
also revealed how different components and the interaction between them could be
addressed in the simulation model and how the model could be used to analyze and
improve the performance of the overall system.
Moreover, the simulation process
within the study showed how important it is to start with a simple model and add
the complexity incrementally to address such a complex system. Furthermore, in the
last few years, have DES healthcare studies been published that addresses crowding
situations, but they have mainly been researched within the emergency departments
to analyze the overcrowding problems and their causes. A study carried out by Bern-
stein et al. (2009) [29] addressing the effects of crowding on the six quality domains
on clinically oriented outcomes, illustrated that crowd is linked with waiting times,
and the consequences of crowding can become severe. Yet, the crowding’s effect on
patient satisfaction, which is an integral part of high-quality service and can be used
to measure patient‐centeredness, was not concluded due to insufficient data.

are no specific technical requirements that indicate that one simulation software over
another is more suitable for the DES application. For this study, the Anylogic soft-
ware was selected as it provides a simple interface that facilitates model building
and is capable of accurately modeling complicated events. Also, it offers powerful
libraries which enable the user to apply a unique set of specific tools. These libraries

Integrated Simulation-Based Framework
A structured technique is needed to address complex systems, such as healthcare
systems. Simulation-based analytic frameworks to predict and improve patient expe-
rience, quality of care, and hospital process flows, have been used in several studies
for healthcare projects. After the political element of healthcare emphasized the need
for objective methods and tools to inform the debate and provide a better foundation
for decision-making, Harper (2002) [37], proposed a generic framework for modeling
of hospital resources in the light of perceived user-needs and real-life hospital pro-
cesses. A detailed simulation model of concerned patient flow was developed within
the framework, which resulted in capturing resources consumption over time. Hamad
et al. (2011) [38] applied a similar approach and developed an interactive simulation-

based decision support framework to improve planning and efficiency of healthcare
processes. Recent studies have applied frameworks linking together Lean Six-Sigma
methodology and DES as a decision-supporting tool. Both Lean Six-Sigma and DES
modeling have been widely used in systems applications, but seldom implemented to-
gether in literature. Six Sigma is a systematic and statistic-based quality management
improvement methodology which can be adopted into projects focusing on improving
and providing a high-quality service within the healthcare field, and most often is the
Six Sigma tool define-measure-analyze-improve-control (DMAIC), a five step proce-
dure, implemented as the baseline in the framework. The DMAIC methodology is
adopted whenever process improvement should be investigated by comparing an exist-
ing configuration to alternative scenarios, and when process has measurable variables.
Hussein et al. (2017) recently applied this approach in his study to guide improvement
decisions regarding mitigating overcrowding within a emergency department (ED) in
a private hospital in Egypt. The study demonstrated the effectiveness of using the
integrated Six Sigma-DES approach on reducing the ED crowdeness. The use of DES
in the Improve step, in the DMAIC procedure, provided inexpensive assessments of
the improvement alternatives and eliminated the troubles associated with real sys-
tem modifications. Additionally, have W.D. Lin among other researchers applied this
approach to two of his studies. In his first study [39], he used this approach to im-
prove the operations in an outpatient eye clinic in Singapore, which showed that by
redesigning the appointment system the clinic was able to reduce patient waiting time
by 23.7% in average. In his second study [40], he used the Six Sigma-DES framework
as guidance through his case study to establish requisite bed capacity when cohorting
pediatric patients, which illustrated that hospital could better assess the feasibility
of different cohorts scenarios based on the resulting impact before implementing a
change. All of these studies applied the Six Sigma-DES approach to existing pro-
cesses or systems to improve their operation. However, when the healthcare objective
is to develop something new, process, service or even a whole system, the Six Sigma
define-measure-analyse-design-verify (DMADV) methodology is more suitable to be
followed, as the outcome of it enables to forecast the process capabilities [41]. Celano
et al. (2010) [42] proposed this innovative approach to bridge the gap between the
Six Sigma methodology and DES by integrating the DES, as a decision-supporting
tool, within both the DMAIC and DMADV methodologies. A structured roadmap
of the approach was proposed in the study to manage quality improvement projects.

methodology is drawn as the outer frame of the roadmap, and the DMADV is drawn
as the inner frame. The DES model design and development steps are placed within
the design and verify phases belonging to the inner DMADV method, but within the
improve phase of the DMAIC method. Given that the two methodologies consist

is also part of the outer DMAIC method, see Figure 2.1. Celano presented a case
study, with the aim of reorganize the flow of emergency patients affected by vertigo

High-Level Process Map
After the interviews conducted with the North Wing management team and the
healthcare professionals at the Centre of Diagnostic Investigation, a high-level pro-

bring clarity to the defined process flow and to visually describe the primary out-
patient and day surgery patients pathways at the ground floor. The map identifies
the inputs of the process, the key activities which add value to the process flow, and
the outcomes of the process. The inputs are the patients who arrive at the North
Wing, and their journey begins with a registration process, where they check-in at
a self-service kiosk or a reception desk. Next, they wait at the main waiting area
for their appointments, which have been booked in advance, where they remain un-

Subsequently, they
move to their treatment locations and get examined and treated by the North Wing

4.2.4
IDEF0 Diagram
IDEF0 stands for Integration Definition for Process Modelling and is a methodology
designed to model the decisions, processes, and activities of an organization or sys-
tem [47]. The IDEF0 method is a decomposition hierarchy modeling approach which
illustrates the relationship between parent and child systems, thus, providing a di-
agram for understanding a organizational processes. It is a detailed flowchart that
maps the processes in a more detail and includes the resources. A parent system is
created first when building the IDEF0 model, as it helps to indicate the purpose and
the main viewpoint on the diagram to support setting the overall context. There-
after, the parent system is broken down into multiple child systems which indicate
the sub-activities within the system and it can be broken further down at any level.
The IDEF0 model consist of two primary modeling components, activity building
blocks and four factors that interrelate those activities. The main unit is the activity
building block which describes the prime function of a process or a sub-function and
is represented on the diagram by a box. The four factors are the objects that influ-
ence the function, inputs, outputs, controls and mechanisms, and are represented by
horizontal and vertical arrows. As shown by Figure 4.4, the position of the arrows
attaches to the box represent the four different factors of the interface. The inputs
enter the box from the left and are transformed into the outputs which leaves the
right side of the box. The controls enter the top of the box, which are a form of
inputs that are used to direct the activities in the process, whereas the mechanisms
enter the bottom of the box, which provides supporting means for performing and to
complete the process [48].

4.4
Analyze Phase
The analysis of the data extracted during the measure phase is essential in develop-
ing a robust simulation that considers the time features of the intended system in
terms of capacity and patterns. During the analyze phase a data mining techniques
were applied to the collected data. The patients’ rate within the North Wing was esti-
mated based on the patients’ arrival rate within the Center of Head and Orthopaedics,
the percentage of patients accompanied by relatives were calculated, the information
about each department was further classified into the defined process flows and then
summarized within each process. Furthermore, probability distributions were fitted
to the collected patients’ treatment times and a process sequence diagram established
as a helping implementation tool for the simulation model.
4.4.1
Patient Arrival Rate
The outpatient and day surgery patient arrival rate can have a significant impact on
the patient flow within the North Wing. All the arrivals were estimated as scheduled
arrivals as outpatients and day surgery patients arrive at a specific time where they
have booked appointments.
However, some of the outpatient departments accept
urgent and random walk-in patients, but as they are considered a small percentage of
the overall patient arrival rate and not considered have that high impact on the patient
flow on the ground floor within the North Wing they were not taken into account.
The patient arrival rate was determined by calculating the proportion of the patients
arriving at each hour of the given data, and then the ratios were computed for each
hour multiplied by the total number of outpatients and day surgery patients arriving
at the North Wing, which was 684 patients. The calculated values gave information
about the patients’ arrival rate per hour at the North Wing over twelve hours, from
7:00 in the morning to 18:00. The determination of the outpatient arrival schedule
can be found in Figure 4.18.

4.4.2
Relatives Percentage
Relatives need to be taken into consideration when process flow is being predicted and
improved within a healthcare facility. An adult most often accommodates patients
who are under eighteen years old, and relatives generally accommodate patients who
are old or disabled. Some patients bring their whole family with them to a hospital
visit, and others come by themselves. Thereby, it is essential to include the relatives
when developing a future simulation model as they can have a significant influence
on ensuring a sufficient space within a healthcare facility. From the collected data
about the age distribution of Rigshospitalet outpatients and day surgery patients in
the measure phase, we were able to estimate how many patients will arrive with their
relatives to the North Wing. An analysis was performed of the data and based on
information from the Rigshospitalet managers, an assumption was made about that
at least one relative will accompany every patient under twenty-two years old and
patients older than seventy-eight years old. A calculation in an excel spreadsheet
was performed and the percentage of patients who will arrive with a relative was
computed. The proportion of the number of patients at every age, from newborns
to twenty-two years old and from seventy-eight years old to ninety, was calculated
from the given data and then divided by the total number of outpatients. These
calculations concluded that relatives will accompany 26% of patients.

4.4.5
Probability Distributions
After most of the data had been provided and analyzed in the define, measure and
analyze phases to construct the simulation model, the most adequate probability dis-
tribution functions were assigned to the generated stochastic data. Most of the data
collected were considered stochastic variables due to random behaviours of people
and service times, such as the registration service time, the waiting time for an eleva-
tor, the preparation time and the treatment times within each patient pathway. The
distributions for the patients activities were determined according to similar health-
care simulation studies [52, p. 230], [53], [54]. The registration time for both the
self-service kiosks and registration at the reception desk was fitted to uniform dis-
tribution function. The uniform distribution, UNIF(a,b), assumes that values are
equally distributed between a lower and upper boundary, where the a and b are re-
spectively the minimum value of the uniform interval and the maximum value of the
uniform interval. The waiting time for an elevator at stairway six and seven was fit-
ted to triangular distribution, TRIA(α, β, γ), as well as all the patients’ preparation
times and treatment times. The triangular distribution has proven to be useful when
little information about the values have been provided, but can provide lower and
upper bounds, α and γ, along with the most likely value or the mode value, β [55].

4.5
Improve Phase
The improve phase in the analytic framework is considered the core phase as it in-
volves the simulation model design and validation. It includes the design and verify
phases which belong to the inner frame of the DMAIC-DMADV roadmap. During
the design phase, the DES model was constructed within the Anylogic software based
on the conceptional maps designed in the defined phase. Each building block of the
DES model was specified by the quantitative data modified in the analyze phase and
parameters were defined and added, to measure the performance indicators and ex-
plore different scenarios. Once the DES model was developed, the model was tested
and validated during the verify phase. The validation was performed through a set
of pilot simulation runs to demonstrate its consistency with the real process data.
Following the verify phase, a series of scenarios were developed and tested to investi-
gating the impact of potential changes in the outpatient process flow regarding the
quality of service within the North Wing. These scenarios were defined according to
the findings and in cooperation with the North Wing management team.

4.5.3
Model Construction
The DES model was constructed based on the understanding gained through the
two process maps, the IDEF0 diagram, analyzed data and the fitted distributions
discussed in the define phase and analyze phase. The eight processes defined in the
IDEF0 diagram were simulated in Anylogic, where the activities within each process
were defined by the different building blocks from the pedestrian library and the pro-
cess modeling library. The pedestrian blocks were used to construct the main flow,
where as the process modeling blocks were used enhance the detail level of the main
flow. Each block was customized by setting the properties within it and then they
were connected together to create the simulation flowchart.
In the DES model, the model time unit was set to minutes and the model time frame
from 7:00 to 19:00. Two patients’ arrival rate schedules were created as it is assumed
that 50% more patients will arrive at the main entrances, see Tables 4.11 and 4.12.
Schedule one is for the main entrance and schedule two is for entrances two and three.
The schedules describe the patients arrival time pattern per hour which was defined
in the analyze phase. The average walking speed for all the patients and staff mem-
bers was set to 1 meter per second. Although, in reality, individual walking speed
varies, and patients arriving at the hospital can be patients with reduced mobility,
in a wheelchair or even in a hurry, this value was selected to reduce the complexity
of the modeling. The model starts with three sourcing blocks, which indicate the
three entrances at the North Wing. Theses sourcing blocks generate the pedestrians,
representing the patients, and the target lines defined in the graphical layout, specify
where the patients will appear in the model or enter the North Wing building. The
patients’ arrival rate schedules were defined in the Ped Source blocks, where the arrival
rate stream was expressed with a Poisson distribution. Once the patients appeared
in the model, they either arrived with a relative or by them self into the North Wing
building. The probability of outpatients or day surgery patients being accompanied
by a relative was defined with Select Output blocks, where they defined 26% patients
arriving with one relative, the probability was made as a parameter which can be ad-
justed in the model. Following the arrivals of the patients, a the registration process
began, which will be briefly described how was built in the model here below, as well
as the other following eight processes, child systems, defined in the IDEF0 diagram.

duration at the kiosks was found to be uniformly distributed between 1 and 2 min-
utes. Two receptionist were available at the reception desk, where the registration

were defined in a Ped Wait block. The probability value for the decision that need to
be made after every blood test was set to 50% as it is assumed that many patients
attend other treatments at the North Wing after their blood test has been performed.
A.8 - Radiology
The last pathway constructed was the radiology pathway which involved the opera-
tion within the Department of Diagnostic Radiology. As the patients need to check in
at the department a Ped Service block was defined, where the patients wait in line to
be checked in by a receptionist at a reception desk. The capacity of the receptionists
working at the reception desk was set to one in a Seize block, but the capacity was
made as a modifying parameter for further investigation. The check in duration was
found to be uniformly distributed between 1 and 2 minutes, the same as at the occu-
pational therapy reception desk. After the check in activity, the patients move to a
waiting area with a Ped Go To block where the seat capacity was set to 15. The wait-
ing area was constructed as resource pool, and seized and released by the Seize block
and the Release block in order to measure the utilization of the area. The patients
waited in a queue at the waiting area until their initials appeared on a screen within
the area or when there was available space in the next activity block, defined by a
Queue block. Before the patients moved into the imaging rooms, their relatives were
released as it is assumed they are not allowed to go with the patients into the imaging
rooms. A Select Output 5 block was constructed to divide the patients attending to
different imaging techniques and to define the decision if the patients needed a prepa-
ration. Three output ports were used of the block, where the first exit indicates the
patients attending X-ray imaging, the second exit the patients attending CT scanning
and the third exit the patients attending MRI. The probability of patients going to
a X-ray imaging was set to 35%, to a CT scan it was set to 35% and to a MRI it
was set to 30%. These probabilities were calculated from the number of patients ar-
riving to each imaging technique per day. The patients arriving to an X-ray imaging
moved straight to an imaging room, where the x-ray imaging was performed. The
X-ray room capacity was set to two, but was made as a modifying parameter, and
the imaging duration was triangular distributed with the minimum time of 10 minute,
average time of 12 minutes and maximum time of 15 minutes. The patient arriving
to a CT scanning first moved to a preparation room, defined by the Ped Go To block
and the Ped Wait block. The capacity of CT scan preparation rooms was set to 6, but

4.5.4
Density Map
In order to evaluate the density of the patients in a particular area at the ground floor,
a Pedestrian Density Map was built in the model. The density map displayed the
layout gradually painted in different colors as the patients moved in the simulation
space while the model was running [56]. It was used to detect the areas with critical
densities. Pedestrian density is the average number of pedestrians per unit of an area
within a pathway or queuing area, expressed as pedestrians per square meter (p/m2).
In the DES model, the critical density value was set to 1.5 pedestrians/m2 and red
color was used to express the critical condition in the model. The blue color was
used for low densities, and when the density was measured zero in a model run, the
density map was not drawn at all. All the colors and their corresponding density
values can be seen in Figure 4.21. At the model runtime, the density measurement
scale was shown, which helped to evaluate the density when the patient flow was
being observed, and in each run, the density map was shown, but it was possible to
take it off while the model was running if preferred.

4.5.5
Verify Phase
To increase the confidence in the DES model construction, verification and validation
were carried out all the way through the design phase by confirming all the inputs and
the model creation with the management team at the North Wing. After each model
development, the model operation was verified by verification techniques which were
essential to ensure that the logic of the model was correct [57]. One of the techniques
applied during the verify phase was visually tracing the patients through the processes
by using animation. This technique helped significantly when the model was verified
with the Rigshospitalet managers. Another technique used was extreme condition
tests, where the input parameters were changed to confirm that the model performed
as intended by checking the output values. Interviews with the professionals were also
conducted to access the accuracy of the model. Although some of the techniques were
time-consuming, they helped to establish credibility with the managers. Following
the verification, the model was validated by presenting the DES model results, the
average utilization and the waiting times, for the managers and some of the staff
involved in the upcoming operation at the North Wing. The results were discussed
with the managers, compared to the managers’ predictions and thoughts, and lastly
confirmed.
4.5.6
Scenarios Design
Once the base DES model results had been validated, experimental tests were de-
signed. They were designed by changing the patient arrival rate schedule and the dif-
ferent parameters that influenced the quality of services on the ground floor. Several
experimental runs were carried out, but three scenarios were selected in consultation
with the North Wing management team to investigate further. The scenarios were
selected based on the findings from the base DES model and on the possible changes
that may occur in the patient flow. According to the North Wing managers will all
patients under eighteen years old move to another treatment facility in 2024, a new
children hospital which is being constructed. Thereby, the first scenario selected was
to extract all the patients under eighteen years old from the model. The percent-
age of number of patients under eighteen years old was determined to be 15% of all
the outpatients and the day surgery patients arriving at the North Wing. Therefore,
the patient arrival schedules were only changed where the total number of patients
decreased from 684 patients to 581 patients. The selection of the second scenario
aimed to investigate when the central hall would collapse or when the it would not be
able to support the patient flow. After several experimental test, a 20% increment of
patients was chosen to be explored further. The patients arrival schedules were again
only changed where the total number of patients were increased from 684 patients to
821 patients. The third and the last scenario selected involved increasing the percent-
age of patients arriving to the Department of Diagnostic Radiology, or the radiology
pathway, and increasing the receptionists working at the reception desk within that
department. Around 10% of the patients percentages arriving to the other pathways

Results
This chapter demonstrates and explains all the obtained results from the developed
DES model and the different scenarios selected. The measured outputs of the per-
formance indicators identified in the Define phase are presented for each scenario,
which are the utilization and the waiting times. Moreover, the patient flow behavior
in the model is discussed and compared to the base model. As the system had a cer-
tain degree of stochastic behavior, the average utilization and average waiting time
values across ten replications of model runs were calculated for all the significant out-
put measures. The number of replications was first determined based on the rule of
thumb, that recommends running at least 3 to 5 replications [58], but ten replications
were determined based on a literature review of previous simulation model studies
using the pedestrian library [59]. All the replications were executed with a length of
720 minutes, from 7:00 to 19:00. The critical areas were detected by the use of the
density map and the bottlenecks identified for a particular scenario. The chapter is
divided into five sections were the first four present the results for each scenario, and
the last section summarizes the results together.

Discussion
The DES model designed and developed in this project provided valuable insight into
the performance of the patient flow behavior and the impact of the seating capacity
at the main waiting area within the North Wing facility. The results of the model
and the experimental scenarios tested in the previous chapter will be discussed and
compared in this chapter. Also, the research question will be answered by reflecting
on the findings. Moreover, the limitations of the model construction will be addressed,
as well the drawbacks of the assumptions. Furthermore, recommendations for future
work will be suggested and the lesson learned from this project discussed.
6.1
Base Scenario Findings
Based on the results obtained from the base model is the main waiting area utilized
on average 52.27% over the model time, see in Figure 5.1. The result indicated that
the main waiting area would have more than enough space to accommodate all the
outpatients and day surgery patients arriving at the North Wing. However, as the
density map and the simulation flowchart in the model replications showed, the main
waiting area reached a maximum seating capacity right after the patient arrival peak
hours in most of the replications. The maximum seating capacity in those replications
were reached from the time 10:00 or 11:00 and the waiting area continued to be fully
occupied until around 13:00 to 15:00. Thereby we can not estimate that the waiting
area would accommodate all the patients during the overall model time. Moreover, as
the time period with all the seats occupied at the main waiting area lasted sometimes
for 3 hours in the model, the patients began to line up which in some of the replica-
tions went maximum up to 50 patients waiting to get a seat at the main waiting area.
This queue of patients which was observed by the seize block seizing the main waiting
area in the process flowchart and displayed by the density map as orange to red areas
at the self-service kiosk service areas or at the reception desk area, indicated that an
overcrowding could occur at the central hall. However, in real life, the patients and
their relatives would unlikely wait at these areas gathered together, they would spread
out more, but overcrowding nevertheless would occur. The overcrowding would lead
to poor access to the check-in service, both to the self-service kiosks and the recep-
tion desk, as the space at the central hall would get crowded. Also, if the patients
and their relatives would wait at the special care service area, it could hindrance the

important to meet to provide a quality healthcare system. Furthermore, the patient

Long waiting time at the main waiting area would decrease the level of quality in

Recommendations
Based on the findings from the base scenario and all the experimental scenarios it
is in all likelihood that the seating capacity at the main waiting area will reach its
maximum around the time from 10:00 to 15:00. As this could affect the patient flow by
resulting in overcrowding and since the waiting area at the Department of Diagnostic
Radiology was not utilized almost at all, it is recommended to combine the radiology
waiting area with the main waiting area and coordinate the patient flow. However,
to support an efficient patient flow within the North Wing and the coordination of
the patient flow, it is recommended to design an effective way finding system. The
way findings for patients could be implemented in the self-service kiosks and colorful
signs should be provided for directions. Effective way findings have proven to improve
patient flow within healthcare facilities and make the consumers feel more comfortable
with their navigation experience [64].
Also, it is recommended to investigate further improving the patient arrival schedules
since the overcrowding appeared only when the peak hours had taken place and
because the average utilization of the waiting areas and treatment rooms indicated
that there are still room for the patients, as the average utilization never reached
100%.
Two test runs were performed by having equal patients arriving rate over
the model time, even though that is unlikely to occur in the real-world, see in the
appendix in Figure B.5 and B.4. The outcomes from these simulation runs indicated
the waiting time would become much better for the patients and the utilization of the
treatment rooms within the Blood Bank and the Department of Diagnostic Radiology.
However, both these recommendations has to be explored further as there were many
assumptions in the model and limitations.

6.4
Limitations
One of the limitations of this project was the lack of data required to develop the
DES model.
The robustness of the data provided by the Rigshospitalet was not
considered good as many assumptions had to be made. As simulation outputs rely
on the accuracy of the model inputs, it can be argued that the model gives limited
information about the patient flow within the North Wing. The primary limitations
that are a concern to the model outcomes are the fixed treatment times, the number
of relatives, the percentage of how many patients wait first at the main waiting area,
and the rates of how many patients need to have another treatment in each path-
way. These limitations can have a significant effect on the seating capacity usage and
the density condition within the central hall. One of the restrictions regarding the
model construction and reliability of the system was that only the outpatients and
day surgery patients were taken into consideration. As inpatients are not assumed to
use the main waiting area, they may go to the café located within the main waiting
area and have a seat. Also, the inpatients could have to have a blood test or radiology
diagnose, which would impact on the performance indicators outcomes of the waiting
time and the utilization of the room within these departments. Another limitation
regarding the model construction and reliability of the system was that the patients
were only built as pedestrian agents and the relatives as resource agents in the model.
Therefore, the density map was not able to measure the real density conditions at the
model time, which can be argued as a drawback of the results regarding the density
of the patient flow. The third limitation can be that all the patients and all the
resources were customized with the same speed, but in the real world, the disabled
patients may walk slower. Additionally, the number of replications can be argued
as a limitation to this project as some variance was determined for the performance
indicator. Moreover, the performance measurement of the waiting time at the De-
partment of Diagnostic Radiology can be seen as a shortfall. Since the time from
when a patient wait to be served for a check in at the reception desk and until he is
alerted to move to a preparation room or an imaging room within the department,
it can be seen as a limitation as the reception service time was taken into account.
Furthermore, due to time restrictions, only a limited number of scenarios were tested
with ten replications to investigate the patient flow and the impact of changes on the
system.
Lastly, the main limitation regarding the Anylogic software was that the pedestrian
library had to be used with the University Research License to run the model with
the pedestrian blocks for more than one minute in model time. This was a shortfall as
it was only possible to get the University Research License for two months. However,
the model was first constructed with the Personal Learning Edition, where seconds
were denoted as minutes, but when there was a need to change the speed of the
patients and the resources it was only possible to have meter per seconds which did
not add up for the outcomes of the model. Another limitation regarding combining
the Anylogic libraries was that it was not possible to send resources build with the

6.5
Future Work
The DES model constructed and the results have been presented for the management
team and the healthcare professionals at the Rigshospitalet. Although the model re-
sults did not provide accurate outcomes, it still provided valuable information about
the patients flow at the ground floor and within the North Wing. For future work,
the DES model could be extended by investigating the stairway seven pathway in
more depth. Although the total capacity for the other floors was taken into account
in the model building, the different floor layouts could be built into the model to
investigate the space and room utilization of the departments within the stairway
seven pathway with more precise treatment times. Also, to examine if a critical bot-
tleneck occurs at any of the departments that could be the cause of the overcrowding
and the long waiting times at the main waiting area. Moreover, as previous studies
which have used DES model to improve patient flow in a complex healthcare system,
the constructed model of this project can be extended by building all the resources
involved in the pathways and maximize their utilization to decrease the waiting time
at the main waiting area [27], [28].
Different set of scenarios could be tested for the assumptions made in the project by
changing the modifying parameter built in the DES model. For example, decreasing
or increasing the blood sampling stations or imaging rooms, or the percentages of
patients needing another treatment at the North Wing. The seating capacity in each
waiting area can also be increased if the manager expects that it would be possibles to
add more chairs or sofas and thereby investigate the patient flow further. According
to a study carried out by Harper et al. (2003) [65] for an Ear, Nose and Throat
outpatient department, the waiting times can be improved by changing the appoint-
ment scheduling without increasing the resources. Therefore, a better scheduling of
the patient arrivals could provide the overcrowding to occur after the peak hours at
the main waiting area and at peak hours at the waiting area within the Blood Bank.
Also, it is recommended to measure the waiting time to be served at the reception
desk to evaluate the quality of the patient service further. However, what should be
the most crucial future work for the manager at the Rigshospitalet in order to get the

6.6
Lesson Learned
This project has been a very good exercise in learning the concepts of simulation. It
has helped me realize the potential of simulation as a very useful tool with many areas
of applications, both in the healthcare sector and other industries. It also taught me
that simulation building and verification takes much time, and longer than I expected
in my project plan. The extensive discussion on the limitations of the model also
provided me a deeper understanding of simulation and provided a foundation for
improvements on the DES model constructed. The project has also helped me gain a
working knowledge of the simulation software AnyLogic. Another important lesson I
have learned from this project is that a healthcare facilities are very complex systems
and that it is important to decide the scope of the project very carefully. Investigating
the patient flow at the North Wing also opened my mind how many opportunities
are within the healthcare sector to explore and optimize further. Furthermore, the
analytic framework used as guidance through the model development provided me
with a valuable lesson to systematic structure the formulation and the execution of
the DES model. In addition, the DMAIC-DMADV framework helped me structure
my thesis outline which provided me with a good overview of my thesis project.

CHAPTER7
Conclusion
The main objective of this project was to investigate the patient flow and the space
utilization at the ground floor within the North Wing facility in order to guide im-
provement decisions for the managers. The main concern was that an insufficient
seating capacity within the main waiting area could result in overcrowding and pre-
vent the quality of the service and the patient experience. Other concerns was the
patient flow within the Blood Bank and the Department of Diagnostic Radiology to
investigate the coordination of the patient flow and the waiting areas between these
departments and the central hall. A discrete-event simulation model has been de-
signed and developed to analyze the patient flow at the ground floor by combining
the pedestrian library with the process modeling library.
The base scenario model findings concluded that the seating capacity at the main
waiting area could affect the patient flow by resulting in overcrowding from 10:00 to
15:00 and long waiting time. This would affect the access to the registration service,
the special care service and the quality of the patient experience. The three experi-
mental scenarios tested conclude that the main waiting area can manage the patient
flow if the total number of patients arriving to the North Wing would decrease by
15%, but the waiting area and the overall ground floor could result in high level over-
crowding by increasing the patient flow by 20%. However, the last scenario tested
concluded that the performance of the patient flow at the main waiting area can be
improved by adjusting the percentage of patient going to each pathway within the
model. Although, the managers can not decide the percentage of patients arriving to
each pathway, it can be seen that the main waiting area can manage the estimated
outpatient and day surgery patient flow. Moreover, the Department of Diagnostic
Radiology can manage more patients and thereby it is recommended to further inves-
tigate combining the main waiting area with the waiting area within the Department
of Diagnostic Radiology. However, as there were many assumption in the model con-
struction that has to be taken into consideration.
Overall, a DES model was constructed and provided the managers at the Rigshos-
pitalet with more informed understanding about the patient flow at the central hall
and the CDI departments. By observations of the model logic, it was detected that
the leading cause of the overcrowding occurring at the main waiting area was induced
by the stairway seven pathway. This gives the managers better information on what

with the constructed DES model which could help the manager at the Rigshospitalet
