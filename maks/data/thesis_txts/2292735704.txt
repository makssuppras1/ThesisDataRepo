Abstract
Two photon microscopy is a 3D biological imaging technique, that can potentially be used for in
vivo diagnosis of diseases, such as cancer. The two photon microscope depends on an expensive
and bulky femtosecond laser source. Today these lasers are typically Titanium Sapphire lasers,
which require a powerful green pump laser with a good beam quality. Currently the only
commercially available option is a frequency doubled diode pumped solid state laser (DPSS).
This thesis examines the viability of exchanging the DPSS with a tapered diode system which
is both cheaper and more compact than the DPSS. To this end a two photon microscope has
been assembled with a Femtosource Compact Titanium Sapphire laser, modiﬁed to allow for
exchanging of the green laser source. It is shown that under identical conditions the new light
source can match the performance of the DPSS.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Microscopy has been around for over 400 years and it has been under continuous develop-
ment since its invention. Today microscopy has become an invaluable tool in the diagnostics
of several important diseases, such as cancer. In medicine the microscopical examination of
tissue to diagnose diseases is know as histopathology, and to do the diagnosis the pathologist
depend on the ﬂuorescence microscope and highly speciﬁc ﬂuorescent dyes. The success of this
technique is partly because the modern ﬂuorescence microscope has become extremely reliable,
user friendly and aﬀordable.
Since its invention by Denk et al. in 1990[1], two photon microscopy (TPM) has been an
increasingly important tool for medical research due to several beneﬁts over conventional ﬂuo-
rescence microscopy. Two photon ﬂuorescence microscopy utilize a non-linear process in which
ﬂuorophores can be excited with near infrared (NIR) light.
The technique is ideal for 3D
imaging in biological tissue because the non-linear process conﬁnes the ﬂuorescence to a small
volume and the longer wavelength allows for imaging up to a depth of 1 mm[2]. These advan-
tages has lead to the usage of TPM in vivo for examining mice liver tissue[3] and it has shown
promise for imaging skin cancer[4].
One obstacle for a wider use of TPM is the high price, which in part is due to the need for a
high performance mode-locked laser producing pulses in the 10 fs regime. These lasers need a
high quality green pump laser, and so far the only laser capable of living up to the required
speciﬁcations have been frequency doubled diode pumped solid state lasers (DPSS). Recently
it has been proven that frequency doubling of high powered tapered diodes can reach 3.7 W
of diﬀraction limited green light[5]. It has also been shown that this type of laser can pump a
Titanium Sapphire laser and used for biomedical imaging and that it could lead to an increase
in image quality[6]. The use of a tapered diode system would also signiﬁcantly reduce the cost
of the Titanium Sapphire laser, while decreasing the overall size of the system.
This thesis will examine the impact of a frequency doubled tapered diode system on images
acquired using two photon microscopy and compare how it performs against a commercial
DPSS laser. To perform a user independent comparison of the images an algorithm for es-
timating the perceived contrast will be tested. The algorithm was developed by Dr. E. Peli
from Harvard Medical School in Boston[7; 8]. To do a fair comparison it is important that the
images are taken under identical conditions and to do that an understanding of the process
involved is needed. Chapter 2 gives an overview of the development of microscopy and how
these techniques came together in two photon microscopy. The chapter also gives an intro-
duction to ﬂuorescent dyes and ultra short pulses, but the reader is expected to have basic

and calibration of the system can be found in Chapter 4, together with the images used for

2.1
Early Development
As with many other inventions there is some dispute over who originally invented the micro-
scope, but the earliest known microscope is believed to have been made in the Netherlands
around 1590 by Johannes Jansen and his son Zacharias[9]. This microscope was a compound
microscope and consisted of two convex lenses in a tube with the means to adjust the separa-
tion of the lenses, see Figure 2.1. Galileo Galilei also invented a simple compound microscope
in 1609 which was used for the ﬁrst known microscopy publication, contained in the book Api-
arium (Rome, 1625)[10]. These early compound microscopes were plagued by blurred images
due to aberrations. This could be why Antony van Leeuwenhoek used a single lens microscope
when he observed, among other, bacteria and red blood cells in the 1670s. Leeuwenhoeks micro-
scope, which can be seen in Figure 2.2, consisted of a double convex lens with a magniﬁcation
of around 270×. Despite these issues it is believed that it was a compound microscope Robert
Hooke used for the work contained in his 1665 book "Micrographia" in which he showed draw-
ings of, among other, ﬂeas, lice and nettles[11]. This was also the ﬁrst time the word "cell" was
seen in a publication. All through the 18th century the microscopes were plagued by mainly
two problems: Spherical and chromatic aberrations. Chromatic aberration arises because the
glass of the lens has a diﬀerent refractive index for diﬀerent wavelengths of light, and therefore
focus them to diﬀerent focus positions. The longer the wavelength of the light the longer the

at speciﬁc positions. By 1830 the achromatic lens was also further developed and all the basic
components for a microscope objective was therefore available[9]. There has of course been
numerous improvements since then but the basic principle of minimising aberrations using a
series of lenses of varying materials and shape, is still the same and even today the heart of
any microscope is the objective.
In 1870 Karl Ernst Abbe formulated the diﬀraction theory for images in a microscope. This re-

the UV light came from a spark source[12]. A very important factor for a wide-ﬁeld microscope

2.2.1
The Nature of Fluorescence
Fluorescence occurs when a specimen absorbs light by exciting an electron into a state of higher
energy. After somewhere between 1 and 100 ns it returns to its original state, known as the
ground state, by emitting a photon with less energy than the original excitation photon. The
exited state of the electron, in the case of ﬂuorescence, is also known as a singlet state. A
singlet state refers to a state where the exited electron has the opposite spin of the electron
it left behind in the ground state. Quantum mechanical laws state that two electrons in the
same orbital must have opposite spin, and therefore it is easy for the electron to return to the
ground state. It can, however, also happen that the electron is exited to a state were it has
the same spin as the ground state electron, known as a triplet state. Decay from this state
back to the ground state is much less likely because the electron needs to change its spin in
order to decay. The decay from a triplet state will emit light with a lower energy and it is
known as phosphorescence. Phosphorescence typically has lifetimes ranging from milliseconds
to seconds, but examples of much longer lifetimes exist. What is commonly known as "glow-
in-the-dark" materials, such as used for toys and some emergency exit signs, is an example of
very long lived phosphorescence. The transition between states is usually illustrated using a
Jablonski diagram, named after the polish physicist Aleksander Jablonski. An example of such
a diagram with ﬂuorescence and phosphorescence is shown in Figure 2.3. The diagram show
3 singlet states, denoted S0, S1 and S2, and one triplet state denoted T1. Each of these states
also contain sub-states due to low energy vibrations in the molecule. First the electron is exited
to either S2 or S1, from which it can decay to the lowest possible sub-state of S1, without the
emission of light. Typically the energy lost here is transformed into heat. The electron can
also make what is know as an intersystem crossing to move over to the triplet state. Any loss
of energy without the emission of light is also referred to as a non-radiative decay. When the

Fluorophores are characterized by their excitation and emission spectrum which shows some
general characteristics that hold for most ﬂuorophores. In 1852 Sir George Gabriel Stokes
published his observations of one such characteristic.
Stokes observed that the otherwise
colorless solution of quinine in some cases showed a beautiful blue color. He also noted that
the color only came from the top most part of the solution. He said in his 1852 publication:
"After passing through this stratum, the incident light, though not sensibly enfeebled nor
coloured, has lost the power of producing the same eﬀect, and therefore may be considered as
in some way or other qualitatively diﬀerent from the original light."[13] The reason for Stokes’
6
==PAGE:17==
observation was that the quinine absorbed UV light, which was undetectable to Stokes, and
emitted visible blue light. Because he was the ﬁrst to publish this observation, the change
in wavelength has become known as the Stokes Shift. The shift occurs because the electron
is generally exited from the lowest vibrational level of S0 to a higher level of S1. The decay
on the other hand is generally from the lowest vibrational level of S1 to a higher vibrational
level of S0, resulting in a loss of energy in Figure 2.4 and an increase in wavelength in Figure
2.5. Sometimes the spectrum of a ﬂuorophore reveals some interesting information about the
physics of the ﬂuorescence. Figure 2.4, for example, shows how distinct vibrational levels can
sometimes be seen directly from the spectrum. From Figure 2.5 it can be seen that quinine
contains a higher singlet state S2 which can be used for excitation, but not for emission, leading
to an extra peak in the excitation spectrum. The emission spectrum is in general independent of
the excitation wavelength. This is because the non-radiative relaxation processes in which the
electron decays to the lowest vibrational state of S1 has lifetimes in the order of 10−14 −10−11
seconds, while the ﬂuorescence lifetime is 10−9 −10−7 seconds. Therefore the electrons will
have decayed to the lowest level of S1 before any ﬂuorescence starts.
A rule of thumb is that the emission is the mirror image of the excitation. This is often the
case because the vibrational sub-states have a similar energy distribution as the vibrational
sub-states in the excited state, and the probability of decay between two sub-states is closely
correlated to the probability of excitation between the same two sub-states. One interesting
example of a ﬂuorophore that does not completely abide the mirror "rule" is quinine. This is
because there is almost no decay directly from S2 to S0 because the decay to S1 is much more
likely. It is generally the case that the emission is a mirror of the S0 to S1 excitation and not
the complete excitation spectrum. It is also worth noting that in Figure 2.5 the spectrum is
plotted on a wavelength axis, therefore the emission spectrum is wider than the excitation.
Since the mirror eﬀect originates from the energy distribution of the vibrational levels it is
more clearly seen if the spectrum is shown on an energy (or wavenumber) axis[14].
One of the major problems with ﬂuorescence is that all ﬂuorophores experience photobleaching,
meaning the emission will decrease over time as the ﬂuorophore is exposed to light. There
are two overall types of photobleacing: temporary bleaching and permanent bleaching. The
temporary case is often caused by the electron crossing over to a long lived state from which it
takes time to decay back to the ground state. Permanent bleaching on the other hand is often
a photo-induced chemical reaction in which the ﬂuorophore is changed so that it can no longer
ﬂuoresce. Today very stable ﬂuorophores exist, though they are often toxic to cells, but even
with the most stable ﬂuorophores, bleaching is easily observed and remains a problem.
Biological tissue can emit ﬂuorescence without the addition of dyes. Any intrinsic ﬂuorescence
from a sample is known as autoﬂuorescence. When excited around 400 nm tissue can give
autoﬂuorescence from 400 nm and up to 750 nm[15].

component. Antibodies are a common intermediate medium because the binding sites of these
can be very speciﬁcally controlled. I will here give a few examples of typical ﬂuorophores and
their binding cites in cells.

dye for staining the cell nucleus. It can pass through a living cell membrane and can
therefore be used for live cell imaging, however, the penetration is not as eﬃcient as for
dead cells and the ﬂuorescence will therefore be less eﬀective.
• Hoechst AG has made a range of blue dyes which are commonly used alternatives to
DAPI. The Hoechst stains stops DNA replication and can therefore introduce mutations.
For dead cells the stain Hoechst 33342 is very similar to DAPI with excitation max at
350 nm and emission max at 460 when bound to DNA[14].
• BODIPY is a series of ﬂuorophores based on a molecule containing boron. The BODIPY
ﬂuorophores are characterised by having a very small stokes shift, but they have a high
quantum eﬃciency, meaning almost all excited ﬂuorophores will emit light, and a high
extinction coeﬃcient[14]. The original version (BODIPY FL) has an excitation peak
of 503 nm and an emission peak of 512 nm[17]. Both excitation and emission can be
moved towards higher wavelength by changing the molecular structure. BODIPY has no
intrinsic binding cite but can be bound to e.g. microtubules, which are a part of the cell
skeleton, using antibodies.
• Texas Red is a red ﬂuorescing dye with a maximum excitation at 596 nm and emission
at 615 nm [18]. This dye has no intrinsic binding cites in a cell but it can be coupled
to either antibodies or Phalloidin to stain a variety of cell components. Phalloidin is a
toxin that binds very well to the ﬁlamentous actin, often refered to as F-actin. F-actin
is, like microtubules, a part of the cell skeleton. Texas Red is widely used because of its
high photo stability.
For more information on ﬂuorescence and ﬂuorophores I recommend the book Principles of
Fluorescence Spectroscopy by J. R. Lakowicz[14].
2.3
The Fluorescence Microscope
The working principle of a modern wide-ﬁeld reﬂectance ﬂuorescence microscope can be seen
in Figure 2.6. It is named reﬂectance because a dichroic mirror is used to reﬂect the excitation
light down unto the sample through the same objective which gathers the emitted ﬂuorescence.
This type of illumination is also called episcopic illumination. The term wide-ﬁeld means that
the entire image is illuminated at the same time and imaged with a camera. The excitation
light ﬁrst passes through an excitation ﬁlter to make sure the excitation has no, or very little,
overlap with the emitted ﬂuorescence. Next is a dichroic mirror reﬂects the excitation light
down onto the sample, where it excites the ﬂuorescence. The part of the ﬂuorescence which
is emitted back within the light cone of the objective is picked up and transmitted through
the dichroic mirror. The emission ﬁlter then ﬁlters out any unwanted wavelengths before the
light is passed to a camera. The three ﬁlters, excitation, emission and dichroic, are usually
mounted in a box which is placed in a wheel so ﬁlter sets can be easily swapped depending
on the ﬂuorophore imaged. Alternatively a wide excitation spectrum can be used to excite all
ﬂuorophores at the same time, and an array of ﬁlters and cameras image all the ﬂuorophores
simultaneously. The maximum resolution of the system is determined by the objective and the
excitation wavelength, see equation (2.1).

2.4
Confocal Microscopy
Wide-ﬁeld ﬂuorescence microscopy does not produce good images in thick samples because
the ﬂuorescence is not limited to the focal plane, but is exited in all depths were UV light is
present. This blurs the image if ﬂuorophores outside the focus plane is exists. A solution to
this problem is confocal microscopy, which was patented by Marvin Minsky in 1961. Minskys
idea was to insert a pinhole in the focal plane before the detector. At this position only light
that is in focus on the sample will be focused back down to a spot and thereby pass through the
pinhole, while most out of focus light will be blocked, see Figure 2.7. Confocal microscopy has
two major drawbacks. The ﬁrst is that it can only image one point at a time. If, for instance,
the focus point would be moved down on the sample in Figure 2.6 then the resulting point
on the pinhole would also move and therefore be blocked. Therefore scanning of the sample
is needed, which greatly increases the time needed to acquire an image. In Minskys original
design the sample was also illuminated at a point by placing a pinhole in front of his light
source and then focus the resulting light with a second objective. If wide-ﬁeld illumination
had been used then scattered light from elsewhere in the sample could still be detected. Since
only one point is imaged at any given time confocal microscopy usually use very sensitive point
detectors called photo multiplying tubes (PMTs). The second drawback of confocal microscopy
is that while the use of point illumination reduces the area where photobleaching occurs, the
pinhole reduces the overall signal, meaning more light is needed to image. Therefore bleaching
is still a big issue within the light cone of the illuminating objective. The term "confocal"
originates from the fact that the two pinholes are "focused together" or cofocused[9].
For a system like this the Abbe formula is no longer suﬃcient to describe the resolution. Here
one needs to examine, the point spread function (PSF), which is the systems response to a point
source. In ﬂuorescence microscopy such a point source would be an inﬁnitely small ﬂuorophore
that always gave a ﬂuorescence directly proportional to the intensity of the excitation light. If
such a ﬂuorophore was scanned in 3 dimensions and there was no detection pinhole then one
9
==PAGE:20==
Figure 2.7: Sketch showing how confocal microscopy uses a pinholes to block out of focus light.
would get an image of the focus of the objective, also known as the illumination PSF. If a pinhole
is then added then the resulting 3D scan image would be the product of the illumination PSF
and the PSF of the pinhole, thereby increasing the resolution in all 3 dimensions. However, the
improvement of the lateral resolution is of little to no practical importance[19]. The evaluation
of the point spread functions is quite complicated, and of little signiﬁcance for this thesis, but
it has been shown that for an NA of 0.94 (Water immersion) the theoretical FWHM of the PSF
for a confocal system is 190 nm in the lateral directions and 790 nm in the axial direction[19].
2.4.1
Laser Scanning
With the invention of the laser came a much higher degree of control over the illumination.
This enabled a radical new way of scanning the sample known as laser scanning, also referred
to as raster scanning since the typical pattern of scanning is a raster pattern. Laser scanning
utilize the fact that the focus point of a objective can be moved by having a collimated light
source input with an angle, as illustrated in Figure 2.8. The sample can be scanned by changing
the angle of the laser using two mirrors, one for each axis. The mirrors used are usually fast
scanning mirrors, known as galvo mirrors because the driving mechanism used is similar to
a galvanometer.
With this technique the sample can be scanned up to a few Hz, though
anywhere from a few seconds to a few minutes is usually needed to produce a good image,
depending on the resolution and signal level. One important note here is that the mirrors need
to be placed in focus of the scan lens, otherwise the input to the objective will not only have
its angle scanned but the center of the input beam will also move. If both axis needs to be
scanned perfectly then an optical relay system, consisting of two lenses, is needed between the
mirrors to be sure both are in focus. In most systems it is suﬃcient to place the mirrors as
close as possible to minimize the error, though this will limit the ﬁeld of view on the sample.
As pointed out in the previous section, moving the point on the sample will also move the point
at the pinhole. Therefore the signal needs to be descanned, meaning the signal is propagated
back through the scanning system to make sure the signal focus always matches the pinhole.
When using laser illumination it becomes important to make sure that the laser beam can be
focused down to a single spot. The shape of the focus is determined by the transverse modes
of the laser. The mode that focuses to a single round spot is known as the fundamental mode,
while other modes are known as higher order modes. Higher order modes can be suppresed

2.5
Two Photon Microscopy
One major drawback still remains with confocal laser scanning ﬂuorescence microscopy: the
use of UV light. Most ﬂuorophores need to be exited by UV light, but UV light only penetrates
around 200 µm into human tissue[21]. The penetration depth is determined by the scatter-
ing and absorption of the tissue. The scattering from small particles, known as the Rayleigh
scattering, is proportional to λ−4 so it drops oﬀvery quickly with longer wavelengths. The
absorption, on the other hand, is a more complicated matter. The body contains a lot of
water, which is highly absorbent below 200 nm and above 1100 nm. Between 200 and 600 nm
protein, hemoglobin and melanin have a high absorption[22]. Therefore the region 600 to 1100
nm is often called the biological window within tissue optics.
Two photon microscopy is based on the principle that a ﬂuorophore can be exited using two
photons of half the energy normally needed for excitation. Thereby exchanging the UV light
for near-infrered light around 800 nm which is right in the biological window.

chanics. This would be beyond the scope of this thesis, but I will try to give an overview of the
concept here. It is well known in quantum mechanics that Heisenbergs uncertainty principle
allows for so called virtual states to exist[23]. These states violate conservation of energy and
can only exist for a shorter time then the time needed to measure them directly according the
the uncertainty principle:

3.1.1
Light source
The light source for the two photon microscope is a mode-locked Femtosource Compact Tita-
nium Sapphire (Ti:Sa) laser, capable of producing pulses in the 12 to 20 fs seconds regime with
a spectral FWHM above 90 nm centred at 800 nm. The Ti:Sa laser originally came with a
Verdi V5 green laser from Coherent, but it has been modiﬁed to allow the V5 to be exchanged
for a tapered diode based sum frequency generation system (SFG).
The V5 is a frequency doubled diode pumped solid state laser (DPSSL), and it contains two
crystals inside the laser cavity. The ﬁrst crystal is a neodymium-doped yttrium aluminium
garnet (Nd:YAG) crystal which emits 1064 nm light when pumped by the diodes. The second
crystal is a non-linear crystal in which two 1064 nm photons are combined to make a 532 nm
photon in a process known as second harmonic generation (SHG)[27]. Because the non-linear
crystal is inside the laser cavity, the process is known as intra cavity frequency doubling, and
the non-linear process is very eﬃcient because the infrared light passes through the non-linear
crystal many times.
The SFG system was developed by the Diode Lasers and LED Systems group here at DTU
Photonics. The system takes advantage of the high power and good beam quality of state of
the art tapered diode lasers[28]. The high power of the diodes makes it possible to obtain a
high conversion eﬃciency with only a single pass of the non-linear crystals[29]. A sketch of the
system can be seen in ﬁgure 3.2. To achieve optimum mode locking the Compact needs to be
pumped with between 3.4 and 3.6 W of 532 nm green light. If the compact gets below this
the spectrum narrows signiﬁcantly and the pulse broadens. If the power gets above 3.6 W the
Compact will emit both the mode-locked pulses and CW light. In this case a narrow peak will
can be seen rising out of the spectrum. To avoid this a portion of the infrared light from the
diodes in the SFG system is dumped before focusing the rest into the ﬁrst crystal. The output
of the Compact is up to 400 mW, but to avoid cell damage the power at the sample needs to
be kept below 10 mW, using the attenuable ﬁlter.
When the pump laser was swapped, the spectrum and autocorrelation was checked to insure
good mode-locking was achieved. If the mode-locking was unsatisfactory the stability of the
Compact was adjusted. The output beam of the Compact could also move slightly, and the
transverse position of the pinhole in the spatial ﬁlter was therefore checked. A change in the
pinhole placement is imaged down onto the sample, but the adjustments were to small to be
noticeable.

3.1.4
Spatial ﬁltering
A spatial ﬁlter using a pinhole is inserted in the system to suppress higher order modes as

3.1.5
Scanner
The X/Y beam scanner is a Thorlabs GVS012 galvano scanner. The scanner system has a
small angle settling time of 400 µs for ±0.2◦, corresponding to a pixel scan rate of 2.5 kHz.
A scan rate above this can potentially degrade the image quality depending on the step size.
To correctly map the angle of the scanner into a position on the sample the scanning mirror
needs to be in focus of the scan lens, see section 2.4.1. The GVS012 is a dual axis scan system
with no relay lenses between the mirrors, meaning both mirrors cannot be in focus of the scan
lens at the same time. This introduces an error in the focus proportional to the scan angle,
eﬀectively limiting the size of the area that can be scanned, known as the ﬁeld of view (FOV).
In this work single or few cells will be imaged and the loss of ﬁeld of view is therefore not an
issue. The scan lens, L3 is an achromatic lens with a focal length of 35 mm. It is placed with
the focal plane between the two scanning mirrors to make the ﬁeld of view equal in both axis.
L4 has a focal length of 150 mm and together with L3 it expands the beam to ﬁll the back of
the objective. The two lenses are referred to as the beam expander.

Objective
The objective is a water dipping XLUMPLFLN objective from Olympus, with a NA of 1.00,
20x magniﬁcation and a working distance of 2 mm. A water dipping objective is not corrected
for cover slides, so the focus will be slightly worse if a sample with cover glass is used. A
water dipping objective is used because thick biological samples in general cannot be enclosed
by a cover glass. While this thesis will only examine thin samples the microscope is meant
to examine thick samples at a later time. The objective has a transmission above 70 % from
450 to 750 nm and roughly a 70 % transmission in the 800 nm region. The transmission does
drop in the near infrared but at 900 nm it is still roughly 65 % so the slope of the transmission
is not high enough to signiﬁcantly distort the spectrum of the pulse. To achieve the optimal
resolution the incoming beam needs a certain size. Normally it’s said that the objective needs
to be overﬁlled, but overﬁlling also clips the beam, reducing the transmission of the objective.
It has been shown that there is an optimum beam width which yields almost the full resolution
while still keeping transmission losses to a minimum[31]. This is given by:

medium, in this case water. The authors call this a rule of thumb so a radius of at least 3
mm should be achieved because loss of transmission for the near infrared can be compensated
for, while a loss in resolution cannot. The 1/e radius of the compact is roughly 1 mm, so the

3.1.8
Detection
The ﬂuorescence signal is focused softly onto a H5784-01 PMT from Hamamatsu using a 50
mm lens (Thorlabs LA1131-A). The detection area of the PMT has a diameter of 8 mm. The
detected signal is not descanned, therefore a point on the sample will be imaged onto the PMT
chip. Because of this the PMT needs to be close to the objective, otherwise the output beam
of the objective will not be collected by the lens L5. The setup only contains one PMT and
a short pass ﬁlter so the total ﬂuorescence intensity is measured, ergo there is no distinction
between diﬀerent ﬂuorophores. The PMT was not calibrated so only relative measures are
performed. To avoid scattering of stray light inﬂuencing the measurements, the microscope is
covered in a fabric with a light blocking rubberised coating (Thorlabs BK5). Information on
the wiring of the PMT follows in the next section on the electrical setup.

3.2.1
Data Acquisition
The data acquisition (DAQ) is handled by a National Instruments DAQ 6353-USB board with
a maximum sampling rate of 1.25 MHz for single channel sampling. The digital to analog
converter maps voltages between -0.5 and 0.5V to a 16 bit signed integer (int16).
Other
dynamic ranges can be chosen, but this range was the relevant one for our ﬂuorescent signals.
The board controls all the necessary electronics apart from the XYZ stage (Thorlabs MLS203
and MZS500 for XY and Z respectively). The XYZ stages are connected to the PC using USB.
A complete diagram of the wiring of all components controlled by the DAQ board can be seen
in ﬁgure 3.4.
The scanning mirrors are controlled by a separate board for each axis, both of which are
conﬁgured to 0.5 Volt pr. degree of mechanical tilt of the mirror (V/◦). The mirror control
boards are connected to two analog output channels and a common ground reference. Control
boards and power supply for the mirrors are from Thorlabs and made for the galvo mirror
system.
The 0 V output of the PMT power supply is not grounded, making the PMT a ﬂoating signal
source. The PMT signal is delivered by a 50 Ωcoaxial cable to shield it from radiation noise
and is measured using referenced single-ended (RSE). The shield of a coax cable needs to be
grounded and this is done on the DAQ board, while the signal is sent to an analog input
channel. The PMT signal is terminated by a 50 Ωresistance to impedance match the cable
with the DAQ, avoiding reﬂections that could inﬂuence the signal. The shield on the coax cable
is internally connected with the ground on the PMT, and the analog input, output and digital
grounds are all connected internally on the DAQ board making sure the drive and control
voltage of the PMT use the same reference.
If the timing between the mirror positioning and the signal readouts are not controlled perfectly
the image will be distorted. The communication between the board and the PC is to slow to
control the timing, therefore the board listens for a trigger signal at which point it will start

3.2.2
Labview Software
The Labview program controls the scanning parameters, the XYZ stage, the shutter and does
the data processing. To acquire an image the scanning position, PMT control voltage, ﬁeld of
view and resolution is ﬁrst set. Once a scan is started, the program converts the ﬁeld of view
and resolution to voltage stair cases which is loaded onto the DAQ board together with the
sample rate of the PMT. The PMT is always set to the maximum sampling rate of 1.25 MHz,
and the program limits the allowed pixel scan rates to values which gives an integer number
of samples for each pixel. After loading the parameters the shutter is opened and the DAQ
is given the trigger to start acquiring the image. The Labview program then initiates two
parallel loops, one which reads the data from the DAQ board and moves it to a circular buﬀer
in Labview. The other loop loads a number of samples from the circular buﬀer corresponding
to one pixel, calculates the mean and saves it in a 2D array. When the program has ﬁnished
processing a line of data, the image is display on the screen so the user can follow the process,
though not in real time as the DAQ is always ahead of the Labview program. The continious
readout of the PMT has the disadvantage that the signal is also read out while the mirrors
are moving. Therefore the program has the option of discarding a number of samples away
before the mean is calculated to counter this if necessary. The image is displayed with µm axes,
but to do this it needs a system speciﬁc scaling factor which needs to be found by examining
a sample of a known size. Once the image is done the program saves three ﬁles: a .dat ﬁle
containing the raw digital data of the image, a text ﬁle containing the information of all the
21
==PAGE:32==
parameters used for the image and a 16 bit tiﬀimage. The data from the analog to digital
converter are signed integers, but as a tiﬀimage can only contain unsigned integer, the data
is therefore converted by adding 32768. When the program is done the shutter is closed and
the program is ready for another image.
3.2.3
Post Processing of the Images
One of the goals of this thesis is to compare the images produced using the two diﬀerent pump
lasers. This raises the question of how the quality images can be compared? What makes an
image good is very subjective and depends highly on what the image is to be used for, therefore
there are no rigid deﬁnitions which apply to all types of images. Instead I will present some
cases here and explain how they will be handled in this thesis.
The ﬁrst issue is how to display the images so they accurately represent the quality of the
information they contain. In principle one could argue that the most "true" way to represent
the images was to display the full range. This is impossible because the raw data from the
DAQ is 16 bit, and any standard display today will only have 256 (8 bit) levels for grey. Any
displaying of the image will therefore constitute a loss of information. The image also doesn’t
use the entire 16 bit range, so mapping it directly to 8 bit would leave the viewer with an image
containing roughly 40 slight variations of gray. Therefore I will instead try to display as much
of the available information as possible. To do this the 16 bit image data will be mapped to 8
bit by making the lowest 16 bit value equal to 0 (black) in the 8 bit image, while the highest
16 bit value is mapped to 255 (white). The implication of this is that all images shown in this
thesis will have the same dynamic range when displayed, unless the dynamic range of the 16
bit data is below 256, but in general the 16 bit data has a dynamic range between 1 and 10
thousand. The dynamic range can contain some interesting information which is lost when
the image is mapped to 8 bit and displayed. If, for example, one laser produces shorter pulses
than the other, then the result should be a higher signal and a higher dynamic range for the
same average power. Because of this it can be relevant to give the dynamic range of the 16 bit
data. The dynamic range will from this point refer to the range from the minimum value of
the 16 bit data to the maximum value, given as a unitless number.
The signal to noise ratio (SNR) is a very common way to describe the quality of a signal, and
it is very well deﬁned if one has a noiseless image for comparison. When no noiseless image
exists the the problem arises of how to deﬁne what is signal and what is noise. If for example
the SNR were to be deﬁned as the average signal over the average background level, then the
SNR would worsen if a constant was added to every pixel in the image. However the adding
of such a constant would in reality not worsen the image since it would be displayed with its
full dynamic range, which remains unchanged. If instead of using the average background, the
standard deviation of the background was used, then adding a constant would improve the
SNR without improving the image quality. One way of making a simple expresion for the SNR
that is unchanged under the addition of a constant signal is to use the dynamic range as a
measure of the signal and the standard deviation of the background as a measure of the noise.
This leads to the following expression for the SNR in dB:
SNR = 10 log10
 
DR

where DR is the dynamic range and σbg is the standard deviation of the background. An
obvious problem with this deﬁnition is that the dynamic range depends only on two pixels,
meaning 1 faulty pixel can ruin the measure. Another downside is that the background area

3.2.4
Perceived Contrast
The SNR deﬁned above is likely to simple to give correct information about the quality of
an image, therefore a more complex measure is used to describe contrast, which is one of the
most important measures of image quality. There exist several deﬁnitions of contrast in simple
patterns, such as the Michelson formula, which basically just examines the dynamic range,
but these have been found insuﬃcient to judge the perceived contrast[7]. Therefore Dr. E.
Peli from Harvard Medical School in Boston, has, together with his collegue Phd A. M. Haun,
worked on a way to computationally determine the perceived contrast[7; 8]. They have very
kindly provided me with a copy of the software used in their experiments to test if it could
give useful results for the types of images presented in this thesis. I will here try to give a brief
description of the basic principles of their contrast measure, how they validated it and what
implications this has on the conclusions that can be drawn from the numbers.
The basic assumption made by Dr. Peli is that human contrast perception is highly dependent
on the spacial frequencies of the image[7]. The image is split into a series of spatial frequency
bands and each of them is divided by the sum of all the frequency bands below.
This is
comparable to taking each pixel in each of the frequency bands and dividing it by the local
mean intensity in the area.
This makes some intuitive sense because high frequencies are
needed to describe edges, so this measure will give a measure of how much and how fast
the intensity changes, and a high change in a short distance is the basics of contrast. The
problem is now how to combine all the values from each pixel in each band limited image
into one meaningful number. For this they apply a highly nonlinear function known as the
contrast sensitivity function[8]. One major issue with this function is that even though all the
pixel contrast values are summed up in a highly non-linear way, it is still a summation. The
implication of this is that an image with more areas of high contrast will ultimately yield a
higher contrast value. Therefore it is only really useful in comparing two images of the same
are. For cell imaging this has the problem that for contrast comparison two images of the same
position on the slide is needed, however, this could give problems with bleaching.
In their later article they made a series of experiments in which test subject were presented
with two versions of the same image. The images were 480 by 480 pixels, which corresponded
to approximately 18 cm on the screen. A spatial frequency ﬁlter which randomly re-weighted
the diﬀerent spatial frequencies of the image was applied to both images. The subject was
placed 1 meter from the screen in a dark room and told to pick the image with the highest
contrast. From this they deduced which spatial frequency bands were the most important
when determining contrast. Then they could ﬁt the parameters for the contrast sensitivity
function to their subjects. The model is primarily validated by examining what the highest
weighted frequency band was for the images, grouped by the edge density and the slope of the
amplitude spectrum. The amplitude spectrum slope is interesting because it has been shown
that natural images generally have a linear negative amplitude slope[32]. The contrast measure
was in both cases able to mirror the highest weighted frequency band of the test subjects. The
test was done with 4 subjects, and it was not tested whether the contrast measure could
accurately predict which image would be chosen by a user. It is also untested how factors such
as the display, the resolution of the image, the viewing distance and the room light factors
into the reliability of this contrast measure. On one hand one should therefore be very careful
in drawing conclusions based on it. On the other hand it could be very interesting to have a
23
==PAGE:34==
measure of contrast to evaluate microscopes, which is why it is interesting to see if the contrast
measure agrees with my, and the readers, assessment of the images. The test images used in
reference [8] were all the same size, since this is not the case here it was necessary to alter the
code slightly to make it usable for all the images generated with our microscope. The original
algorithm was already scaling the ﬁnal contrast value with the total number of pixels, and no
alterations to the actual algorithm was therefore needed. All contrast values are generated
using an image size of 150 mm, and a viewing distance of 400 mm, to simulate typical values
for a user sitting at a computer.

4.3.3
Eﬀect of Stripes on Contrast
The stripes present in Figures 4.9a and 4.10a makes contrast comparison diﬃcult because
they might boost the measured contrast of the image. To examine how the stripes aﬀect the
contrast, artiﬁcial random noise stripes have been added to the V5 image in Figure 4.10b to
allow a relative comparison with the same position imaged with the SFG. To generate stripes
which correspond to the stripes seen with the SFG, the distribution of the output power of the
Compact when pumped with the SFG is examined. The histogram of the power relative to the
mean power can be seen in Figure 4.12a, and it is clearly a skewed distribution. To make the
artiﬁcial stripes it is assumed that the level of the noise does not vary vertically in the image,
which isn’t strictly true but the variations in the vertical direction is very slow. To make sure
the artiﬁcial noise keeps the distribution 512 random values are taken from the data in Figure
4.12a, and the histogram of these values can be seen in Figure 4.12b. From this it can be
seen that the new artiﬁcial data does follow the same distribution, though not very closely due
to the low number of values. If it is then assumed that this noise is transferred directly to
the images, an artiﬁcial noisy image can be generated by multiplying each vertical line of the
original image with one of the 512 new random numbers. The resulting image is readjusted
so that it is displayed with its minimum value at 0 and maximum at 1 as with all the other
images. The image can be seen in Figure 4.13 together with the original image taken with
the SFG for easy comparison. The image with the artiﬁcial stripes looks very similar to the
SFG image except for the overall higher intensity. Interestingly the contrast of the image with
the artiﬁcial stripes only increased by 0.6 % indicating that the stripes does not contribute as
much as expected.
The next question is then how bad would the stripes have to be signiﬁcantly increase the
contrast? To give an estimate of that the random number distribution has been broadened to
giver higher changes in the image intensity while still preserving the shape of the distribution.

Comparison Without Bleaching
A way to combat the bleaching issue is to only image every position once. This was done for
the image shown in Figure 4.15. Here the focus was found on a cell outside the area seen
in the image and then the sample was moved to a new position nearby. First images 2, 4, 6
and 8 was done with the SFG, and then the other images were taken with the V5, all images
overlap by roughly 4 microns. All the images were imported in Matlab and scaled so that the
black value in the total image is the global minimum value of all the images, and the white
value is the global maximum, after which they were converted to 8 bit pngs. All the images
are therefore in the same value range and can be compared directly. The images were visually
overlapped using Inkscape and arranged so that the image taken ﬁrst is on top, the second is
below and so forth. This way every pixel seen in the total image was only imaged once. There
seems to be a kind of haze over all the V5 images compared to the SFG images, which is also
evident if one examines the lowest pixel values in the images. If one assumes that the average
background of the image can be represented by averaging the bottom 1 % of the pixel intensity
values, then it can be seen that this mean background pixel value is on average 112 higher for
the V5 images than for the SFG images. Contrary to what was seen with the images in Figure
4.10 the V5 images here are not increased in both minimum and maximum values. In this test
case the maximum intensity and the maximum dynamic range is in image 8, which is taken
with the SFG. This could just be because this cell has a higher concentration of ﬂuorophores
but if one examines the lower part of the border between image 7 and 8 closely, it can be seen
that the background is lower in image 8 while the intensity of the F-action is roughly the same
on both sides. It is also clear in the diﬀerence between images 6 and 9. Here the intensity of
the cell nuclei is roughly the same on both sides while the cell skeleton is clearer in image 6.
Interestingly the background of image 6 also seems higher than the background of the other
SFG images.

Chapter 5
Summary
This thesis has investigated the feasibility of exchanging the pumping laser for a Titanium
Sapphire oscillator from a frequency doubled diode pumped solid state laser (DPSS) with a
more compact and cheaper tapered diode based sum frequency generation system (SFG). To
that end a two photon microscope has been constructed and characterized. Great care was
taken to insure both the optical and electrical parts of the system did not inﬂuence the data
in a way that could inﬂuence the comparison of images done with the two pump sources. To
estimate image quality an algorithm for estimating the percieved contrast of a complex image
is employed.
A Femtosource Compact Titanium Sapphire laser was used as the light source for the micro-
scope, and the output of this laser was characterised with each of the two pump lasers. Both
lasers yielded very similar spectral distributions, and a pulse width of 17.1 and 17.2 fs for
the SFG and DPSS respectively. The pulse duration was measured using an autocorrelator
and assuming a Gaussian pulse form. The maximum output when the laser was mode-locked
dropped signiﬁcantly when pumping with the SFG, from 349 mW with the DPSS to 239 mW
with the SFG. The output power also became unstable, with the DPSS the Compact power
had a standard deviation of just 0.6 mW over 30 minutes, while pumping with the SFG gave
a standard deviation of 6.4 mW. This instability came directly from a power instability on the
SFG due to a lack of active stabilisation. The focus of the system was examined using ﬂuores-
cence spheres with a diameter of approximately 100 µm. By imaging these the excitation focus
was found to have a FWHM of 0.5 µm which is roughly a factor of 2 above the theoretical limit
but small enough to be used for cell imaging. The focus images also identiﬁed a ring pattern in
the focus approximately 4 µm below the focus plane of the objective. It is currently unknown
where the ring pattern originates, but it not expected to have any practical inﬂuence on the
results presented in this thesis, because the cells are thin samples.
Two types of cell samples have been imaged and compared. A sample of Hela cells with DAPI
stained DNA, and a sample of bovine pulmonary artery endothelial (BPAE) cells, again with
DAPI stained DNA and Texas-Red stained F-actin. In the case of the Hela cells, two images
of the same location were nearly identical with respect to the dynamic range and standard
deviation of the background. For the two BPAE images of the same location, there was a clear
problem with bleaching leading to the DPSS image having 50 % higher dynamic range. For
both Hela and BPAE cells it became clear that the instability of the Compact when pumped
with the SFG lead to vertical noise stripes in the image, due to the power ﬂuctuations having
a frequency in the same order of magnitude as the line scanrate. The contrast algorithm found
the contrast of the SFG image of the Hela cells to be 21 % higher than its DPSS counter part,
39
==PAGE:50==
while for the BPAE cells the DPSS was 4.5 % better. This was a surprising result given that
in case of the Hela cells there were no clear visible diﬀerence apart from the noise strips, while
in the BPAE case the DPSS image was signiﬁcantly better, due to the higher dynamic range.
Therefore a test was done, where artiﬁcial noise similar to the power noise on the SFG pumped
Compact was created and added to the DPSS BPAE image. The artiﬁcial noise succeeded in
visibly representing the noise stripes from the SFG image, but the addition of the noise only
increased the contrast by 0.6 % indicating another intrinsic diﬀerence in the images which
raises the perceived contrast when pumping with the SFG.
To counter the problem with bleaching, a large area image was taken by stitching together
alternating images taken with the two pump sources. This yielded a complete image contain-
ing clearly comparable images without bleaching. Here it was found that the images done
with SFG generally had a lower background level and a higher dynamic range yielding clearer
cellular structures. There is not yet grounds for a clear verdict on whether the SFG can in
general improve the image quality. The improvement seen could be coursed by a higher exci-
tation of autoﬂuorescence by the DPSS pumped system, but no evidence have been found so
far. Further study into the reason for the image diﬀerence is needed before a conclusion can
be made on this subject.
5.1
Conclusion
A two photon microscope capable of direct comparison between images taken with the same
Titanium Sapphire oscillator (Femtosource Compact), but pumped with two diﬀerent types of
green lasers: a commercial frequency doubled diode pumped solid state laser (Coherent V5)
and a sum frequency generation system based on tapered diode lasers (SFG). It has been proved
that, even though the SFG currently experiences signiﬁcant power instability, the system can
be used to take two photon images of cell samples and that these images are comparable in
quality to those done by the commercial system. However, for the SFG system to be truly
competitive with the commercial system, active stabilization of the power is necessary. The
algorithm for estimating the perceived contrast of images shows potential for comparing two
versions of the same image, and even when comparing similar but not identical images it
performed well.
5.2
Outlook
First of all the new light source needs to be stabilized to allow for a better comparison be-
tween the pump sources. The microscope system is also ready for several improvements and
expansions. Spectral resolution can be implemented giving separation of ﬂuorophores and an
optical coherence microscope can be implemented directly by the addition beam splitter giving
a reference arm and an arm for measuring the interference between the back scattered near
infrared light overlapped with the reference. This would allow for simultaneous imaging of
the physical structure of the sample and the ﬂuorescence, greatly increasing the information
gained without increasing the scan time. Together with the new pump source this can lead to
a cheaper and more versatile system for medical research then what is currently available on
the market.
