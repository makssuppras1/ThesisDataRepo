SUMMARY 
Blue whiting (Micromesistius poutassou) is a migratory, mesopelagic gadoid that is widely distributed 
in the northeastern Atlantic and of great commercial importance. Studies have shown that the 
spatial distribution of spawning of blue whiting is correlated with the dynamics of the North Atlantic 
sub-polar gyre (SPG) that triggers changes in the water mass properties in blue whiting’s spawning 
region (Hátún et al., 2009).  
 
Key environmental factors influencing blue whiting’s spawning distribution are identified, in order to 
examine the observed shifts of the spawning distribution and the effect of the physical environment 
in greater detail. Subsequently, the main environmental factor are applied in a species distribution 
model (SDM) based on presence/absence data of blue whiting larvae from 1951 to 2005, obtained 
from the Continuous Plankton Recorder (CPR) survey, that enables the delineation of the potential 
niche of blue whiting spawning. The SDM indicates that the photoperiod experienced by blue whiting 
during spawning, is the most important variable in explaining the presence probability of blue 
whiting larvae and suggests that spawning commences from the vernal equinox onwards. Moreover, 
light has an effect on the sampling efficiency of the CPR, seeing that the probability to sample larvae 
in the surface waters is predicted to be highest during the period shortly before and during twilight, 
suggesting that blue whiting larvae perform diel vertical migration. Spawning is suggested to occur 
mainly in regions with water depths of at least 300 meters. Furthermore the model indicates a 
seasonal northward progression of potential spawning habitat from March until June.  
Additionally, the temperature and salinity experienced by the spawning adults is an important 
variable in delineating the potential niche of blue whiting’s spawning distribution. The strength of the 
SPG influences the extent and latitudinal distribution of potential spawning regions. Under warm and 
saline conditions (i.e. under a weak SPG) the distribution is spatially more constricted and is located 
further north, while under cold and less saline conditions (strong SPG) the distribution has a greater 
extent and is located further south. 
 
On the basis of these results, potential model improvements are discussed, and an extension of the 
International Blue Whiting Spawning Stock Survey (IBWSS) is proposed, in order to encompass 
Rockall Plateau region in more detail and thereby provide a more comprehensive picture of the 
spawning stock of blue whiting.

RESUME 
Blåhvilling (Micromesistius poutassou) er en migrerende, pelagisk torskefisk. Blåhvilling er vidt 
udbredt i den nordøstlige del af Atlanten og er af stor kommerciel betydning. Tidligere undersøgelser 
har vist at udbredelsen af gydende blåhvilling er koreleret med ændringer i salinitet og temperatur 
forårsaget af skift i den Nordatlantiske Subarktiske Gyre (NSG; Hátún et al., 2009).  
 
For at undersøge observerede ændringer i gydende blåhvilling udbredelse samt undersøge effekten 
af det fysiske miljø pa disse identificeres vigtige miljø faktorer afgørende for deres udbredelse. 
Efterfølgende bliver de identificerede miljø faktorer anvendt i en artsudbredelsesmodel baseret på 
tilstedeværelse/fravær data af blåhvilling fiskelarver fra 1951 til 2005. Data er indsamlet fra 
Continuous Plankton Recorder (CPR) - undersøgelsen, der muliggører en afgrænsning af den 
potentielle niche for gydende blåhvilling.  
 
Resultaterne fra modellen indikerer at den oplevede lysperiode for blåhvilling under gydningen er 
den vigtigste variabel i forklaringen af tilstedeværelsessansynligheden af blåhvilling fiskelarver og 
antyder at gydningen indledes fra forårsjævndøgn og fremefter. Endvidere ses en effekt af lys på 
indsamlingseffektiviteten af CPR. Sandsynligheden for at indsamle fiskelarver ved overfladen er 
højest kort inden og under tusmørke, hvilket antyder, at blåhvilling udfører daglig vertikal migration. 
Gydning ser ud til at foregå primært i regioner med en vanddybde på mindst 300 m. Modellen viser 
et sæsonmæssigt, geografisk nordligt skift i potentielle gyde habitater i månederne marts til juni. 
Derudover er den temperatur og salinitet de gydende adulte blåhvilling oplever vigtige variabler i 
afgrænsning af den potentielle niche for blåhvillingens gydeudbredelse. Udbredelsen er mere 
indsnævret og beliggende længere mod nord under varme og saltholdige forhold, dvs. under en svag 
NSG, modsat under koldere og mindre saltholdige forhold (stærk NSG), hvor udbredelsen er større og 
strækker sig længst sydpå. 
 
På baggrund af disse resultater, bliver potentielle forbedringer af modellen diskuteret, og en 
udvidelse af International Blue Whiting Spawning Stock Survey (IBWSS) foreslået til at omfatte 
Rockall Plateau - regionen i større detaljeringsgrad og derved give et mere fyldestgørende billede af 
gydebestanden af blåhvilling.

INTRODUCTION  
Fish are considered to choose the time and location to deliver their eggs in a way that maximises the 
survival of their offspring (Neill, 1984). Especially migratory species are believed to rely on cues from 
the physical environment to guide them to their optimum spawning ground (Neill, 1984). Since the 
marine environment is generally characterised by great variations in physical processes, the reliance 
of physical cues for spawning can create considerable shifts in the spawning distribution of fish and 
thereby create problems for the monitoring and management of fish stocks. The population of blue 
whiting (Micromesistius poutassou, 1827 Risso) in the North Atlantic provides an excellent example 
of this close coupling between their spawning distribution and the physical environment (Hátún et 
al., 2009). 
 
Blue whiting is a mesopelagic gadoid that is widely distributed in the northeastern Atlantic, ranging 
from the Bay of Biscay into the Norwegian Sea and is primarily found in depths between 300 and 600 
meters (Bailey, 1982; ICES, 2014a). Spawning commences in January in its southern distribution 
range, i.e. Bay of Biscay, and advances northwards into Scottish and Norwegian waters where it 
occurs around May and June (Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973; Bailey, 1982). The majority of spawning, 
however, takes place from March to April along the European continental shelf edge and above 
banks to the west of the British Isles (Bailey, 1982; ICES, 2014a). Most of the adult fish are thought to 
migrate annually between their main spawning area west of the British Isles and their feeding 
grounds in the Norwegian Sea (Bailey, 1982). 
 
 Before 1993, blue whiting were managed as two stocks, with a larger northern and a smaller 
southern stock divided by Porcupine Bank (ICES, 2014a) as proposed by Bainbridge and Cooper 
(1973) and Bailey (1982). According to this hypothesis, the northern stock migrates from its main 
spawning site west of the British Isles to feeding grounds in the Norwegian Sea during summer, while 
the southern stock spawns mainly around and southward of Porcupine Bank and likely feeds in the 
Bay of Biscay during summer (Bailey, 1982; ICES, 2014a). However, from 1993 onwards until today, 
blue whiting is managed as a single stock even though its stock structure is still subject to controversy 
owing to recent physiological (Brophy and King, 2007), genetic (Was et al., 2008) and oceanographic 
research (Bartsch and Coombs, 1997; Skogen et al., 1999) that suggests the presence of two separate 
Atlantic populations. In recent decades the recruitment of North Atlantic blue whiting and its 
associated fishery have experienced great fluctuations (Figure 1). From 1991 onwards catches 
increased steadily, reaching a peak in 2004 when landings of blue whiting amounted to 2.4 mio tons 
making it the third largest fishery in the world (Payne et al., 2012; FAO, 2014). This was followed by a 
drastic decline with 0.1 mio tons caught in 2011, while more recently blue whiting landings increased 
again (ICES, 2014b).

Hátún et al. (2009) noted a close temporal correlation between these remarkable fluctuations blue 
whiting’s spawning stock biomass (SSB) and changes in the marine climate in the northeastern 
Atlantic. Moreover, Hátún et al. (2009) found a correlation between the spatial distribution of blue 
whiting spawning and the dynamics of the North Atlantic sub-polar gyre (SPG) which controls the 
flow path of the North Atlantic Current (NAC) in the northeastern Atlantic. The variations in the sub-
polar gyre cause large water-mass exchanges which alter the chemical (i.e. salinity) and physical 
features (i.e. currents and temperature) within the spawning region of blue whiting (Hátún et al., 
2009). When the gyre is strong it extends far eastward allowing the NAC to flow through the 
submarine canyon, named Rockall Trough (RT), east of Rockall Plateau (RP), and thereby spreading 
subarctic water masses that are characterised by low temperatures and low-salinity east over RP 
(Figure 2 a; Hátún et al., 2009). In years when the gyre is weak on the other hand, it shifts westwards, 
allowing warm and saline subtropical water masses to flow through RT and over RP (Figure 2 b; 
Hátún et al., 2009). Accordingly, the marine climate within the spawning region of blue whiting is 
either characterised by a warm and saline climate, as associated with a weak SPG, or cold and fresh 
conditions that occur under a strong SPG.  
 
Hátún et al.'s (2009) study indicated that when the gyre is strong, blue whiting  mainly spawn along 
the European continental shelf edge west of Ireland, in particular on Porcupine Bank (PB), while 
during a weak SPG when the marine climate within the spawning region is characterised by warm 
and saline conditions, spawning extends onto RP and shifts northward along the continental shelf 
(Hátún et al., 2009). When comparing the catches of adult blue whiting in spring, which mainly 
consist of spawning adults, similar shifts in spawning distribution were observed. During the early 
1990s when the gyre was strong, the majority of catches were located along the continental shelf

APPROACH TO THE PROBLEM 
Seeing as blue whiting is a migratory species and sensitive to the ambient salinities and temperatures 
they experience during spawning (Schmidt, 1909), it is likely that adults choose an optimum marine 
environment, comprising of chemical, physical and biotic conditions, to spawn, in order to maximize 
the survival of their offspring (Neill, 1984). Since a mechanistic understanding of the processes 
influencing the spawning distribution of blue whiting is currently lacking, this study will examine the 
shifts in blue whiting spawning distribution in greater detail. The basis for this study is a unique data 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
set containing more than 100 000 Continuous Plankton Recorder (CPR) observations spanning over a 
time period of 54 years and including over 1000 observations of blue whiting larvae. 
For this purpose, the study is divided into two sections. In the first part of this study the main 
spawning locations of blue whiting along the continental shelf and within the area of Rockall Plateau 
over time were illustrated. This approach enabled the comparison with recent findings on the 
regulation of the spawning distribution with the North Atlantic SPG as hypothesised by Hátún et al. 
(2009). More specifically, the aim of the “Data Visualisation” part, is to 
i. 
visualize the main spawning distribution of blue whiting from 1951 to 2005 with respect to 
latitude along the continental shelf and with respect to longitude within the area of Rockall 
Plateau and to compare this  
a. to the temperature at the spawning depth of blue whiting and 
b. to the results of Hátún et al. (2009), 
ii. 
and to analyse changes in the latitudinal and longitudinal distribution of blue whiting 
spawning with respect to the temperature at spawning depth in order to perform a 
statistically robust analysis of the results of Hátún et al. (2009).  
In view of the fact that the sub-polar gyre is a basin-scale feature spanning thousands of kilometres, 
while individual fish respond to their local environment, the approach of Hátún et al. (2009) to 
correlate the spawning location of blue whiting by means of a gyre index, indicating the strength of 
the SPG, might be too broad-scale. For that reason, the overall aim is to examine the effect of the 
local marine climate on the spawning distribution of blue whiting in more detail. Accordingly, the 
second part of the study is intended to generate more knowledge on the processes that regulate 
spawning in blue whiting and identifying the key factors that regulate their spawning distribution.  
In detail, the second part of this study aims at: 
i. 
identifying the key environmental factors that influence blue whiting (Micromesistius 
poutassou) spawning distributions, 
ii. 
creating a statistical model of these mechanisms, i.e. develop a statistical model of the 
spawning distribution of blue whiting based on presence-absence data of blue whiting larvae, 
iii. 
analysing the model results and its predictive qualities and compare these with data from 
independent observations from fisheries surveys, 
iv. 
evaluating the model assumptions and providing insights on possible model improvements,  
v. 
discussing how knowledge gained from the model can applied in blue whiting monitoring and 
management

The modelling technique applied in this study in order to model the potential spawning distribution 
of blue whiting is a so-called species distribution model (SDM). The underlying theoretical 
background of SDMs and their diverse applications will be presented in the following chapter. 
 
SPECIES DISTRIBUTION MODELS AND THEIR APPLICATION  
BACKGROUND 
Species distribution models (SDMs) are statistical models that correlate the occurrence of plant and 
animal species with environmental variables in a geographical area, for example to discover 
underlying causes associated with ecological patterns. Their theoretical foundation is closely tied to 
the duality between niche and biotope as proposed by Hutchinson (Colwell et al., 2009). A biotope 
comprises of the physical world in which a species lives and can be understood as a map where each 
location is characterized by specific geographical coordinates and prevailing environmental features 
at a certain point in time (Colwell et al., 2009). A niche, as conceived by Hutchinson, is a 
“multidimensional volume, defined in a hyperspace” that provides accommodating conditions and 
essential resources for a certain species or population (Colwell et al., 2009). Consequently, a niche 
can be understood as a species or population characteristic regarding their biotic and abiotic 
environment (Colwell et al., 2009). Accordingly, in species distribution modelling the environmental 
conditions at the site where a species occurs at a certain period in time (Hutchinson’s biotope), can 
be used to model its niche and thereby predict its potential (future) distribution in environments 
where its niche requirements are or will be satisfied (Colwell et al., 2009; Wiens et al., 2009). 
Consequently, SDMs enable the prediction of changes in species distributions under different 
climatic regimes and allow for the exploration of links between a species and its environment. This 
represents a direct application of Hutchinson’s duality between niche and biotope (Colwell et al., 
2009). As a result, SDMs are also called ecological niche models (ENM; Wiens et al., 2009) and have 
become central in (predictive) geographical modelling in ecology (Guisan and Zimmermann, 2000). 
SDMs are opposed to mechanistic ecological models, which parameterize the (fundamental) niche of 
a species based on physiological and behavioural knowledge, and then predict the response (Guisan 
and Zimmermann, 2000). 
In this paper, the prevailing environmental conditions, at which blue whiting larvae were found over 
a period of 54 years, will be used to delineate the potential niche of blue whiting spawning 
distributions. Before presenting an SDM for potential blue whiting spawning distributions, the 
following section highlights how SDMs can be applied as a tool in various contexts.

SDMS IN A MANAGEMENT CONTEXT 
SDMS IN MARINE CONSERVATION MANAGEMENT AND SPATIAL PLANNING 
Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) are distinct geographical regions that are selected, regulated and/or 
managed to accomplish specific conservation targets (OSPAR Commission, 2013). SDMs can benefit 
species conservation and planning of MPAs by identifying vulnerable ecosystems, which are areas 
that recover slowly when disturbed by human activities. Examples of marine ecosystems that are 
easily disturbed, comprise of habitats of fragile species which are negatively affected when exposed 
to mechanical disturbance by fishing gear, areas where suitable habitat for a species is rare and 
spatially fragmented, or areas that commonly experience low levels of natural disturbance (Cochrane 
and Garcia, 2009).   
 
SDMs can also be used to identify areas of conservation priorities, such as important feeding habitats 
of animals, and their temporal and spatial dynamics. Druon et al. (2012) for example, used a SDM to 
map the potential foraging habitat of fin whales (Balaenoptera physalus) in the Mediterranean Sea in 
order to produce daily distribution maps of fin whales that can be used to create alerting systems to 
minimize the occurrence of ship strike and thereby protect the species. By this means, SDMs can 
directly contribute to spatially and temporally dynamic and adaptive marine spatial planning 
measures (Druon et al., 2012). One might even envision a future where the geographic area of MPAs 
changes over time, i.e. seasonally, in accordance with modelled distributional changes of the species 
of interest.  
In the past, the creation of MPAs has principally been determined by opportunity, mainly due to 
limited knowledge on species and habitat distributions (Sundblad et al., 2011). SDMs can be 
particularly useful under such data-limited situations, especially to improve the ecological coherence 
of a network of MPAs (see OSPAR Commission (2013) and Sundblad et al. (2011) for an explanation 
on ecological coherence), for example by creating a better connectivity between them (Johnson et 
al., 2014). As a result SDMs can also be applied to assess different conservation strategies at suitable 
spatial scales (Lurgi et al., 2014) and to create new MPAs or improve existing networks to meet the 
requirements of environmental legislations such as the Marine Strategy Framework Directive (Druon 
et al., 2012) and to improve Natura 2000 networks (Sundblad et al., 2011). Additionally, SDMs can 
project regions where species richness might increase (Wiens et al., 2009) and thus highlight regions 
where conservation effort could be adjusted.  
Hence, SDMs can be used as a scientific tool for spatial planners, conservation managers and policy 
makers to accurately consider different management scenarios and policy options in a defined spatial 
context (Bergström et al., 2013; Lurgi et al., 2014). Consequently, SDMs have been identified as 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
important tools to include ecological knowledge into decision-making (Bergström et al., 2013) and to 
strengthen the decision-making process itself (Wiens et al., 2009). 
SDMS TO ENHANCE MONITORING AND FISHERIES MANAGEMENT  
Modelling can also support fisheries management, especially under data-limited circumstances 
(Hoggarth et al., 2006). The generation of more knowledge can benefit fisheries directly, since the 
less is known about a species, the smaller commonly is its maximum sustainable yield (MSY), when 
the precautionary approach is applied (Cochrane and Garcia, 2009). Vice versa, the more is known 
about a species, the higher generally is the permitted level of catch up to a certain maximum from 
which the stock would decline (the MSY typically assumes 50% of the carrying capacity of a species; 
Cochrane and Garcia, 2009). 
 
 
SDMS TO PREDICT AND MANAGE CHANGES 
Climate change has already altered the physical and chemical characteristics within the oceans and 
caused changes in the abundance and distribution of marine organisms (Hoegh-Guldberg et al., 
2014). Fish and zooplankton are moving to higher latitudes in response to warming at the fastest 
rates among marine organisms and thus already demonstrate changes in migration patterns and key 
life-history events (i.e. phenology) such as the onset spawning (Hoegh-Guldberg et al., 2014). This in 
turn can create large uncertainties and risks to fisheries or aquaculture (Hoegh-Guldberg et al., 
2014). In order for (fisheries) resource and conservation management to be successful in the long 
term, future environmental and distributional changes need to be anticipated (Wiens et al., 2009) 
and incorporated into stock assessment and management.  
 
Hilborn and Ekerhovd (2010) for example, assessed the resilience of management agreements under 
different climate scenarios of blue whiting. In one of their scenarios the stock is assumed to migrate 
into the exclusive economic zone of Russia in response of warming (Hilborn and Ekerhovd, 2010). The 
authors therefore conclude, that the early inclusion of Russia in state coalitions and trade 
agreements (such as on total allowable catch) would improve future fisheries management (Hilborn 
and Ekerhovd, 2010). In view of that, SDMs have the potential to positively contribute to politics and 
the development of international agreements.   
Since models are only approximations of the complex reality (Anderson, 2008; Zuur et al., 2009), 
there is an inherent uncertainty in modelling. Accordingly, a sound analysis of the projections 
uncertainty is required and the possibility of taking actions that result in adverse results have to be 
carefully balanced with the costs of taking no action at all (Wiens et al., 2009).

THE BEAUTY OF SDMS 
A common proverb states, that one picture can say more than a thousand words. The same proverb 
holds true in science communication. The result of SDMs can be visualized by a variety of maps 
delineating predicted species distributions or habitats of particular interest or concern. Maxwell et al. 
(2009) stated that the need for distribution maps for marine benthic fauna and fish will continue to 
increase in future. One reason is, that good quality maps based on SDMs can add value to data, in 
particular when used in combination with independent sources of information (Maxwell et al., 2009). 
Good species distribution maps are not only essential to marine spatial planning, for example to 
reduce disturbance of vulnerable habitats (Johnson et al., 2014) or to analyse the coherence of MPAs 
(Sundblad et al., 2011), but also to visualize and communicate results to policy makers, conservation 
managers and other stakeholders (Sundblad et al., 2011). Especially in collaborative adaptive 
management, which involves a range of experts and stakeholders from diverse backgrounds, map 
based analysis can be a superior tool to visualize and communicate scientific results effectively.

BATHYMETRY 
It has been hypothesised, that there is a connection between the main spawning location of blue 
whiting, where the highest concentration of larvae are found, and the ocean’s local bottom 
topography, i.e. bathymetry (Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973; Bailey, 1982).  Blue whiting mainly spawn 
in the vicinity of RP, Porcupine Bank (PB) and along the European Continental Shelf of the south-
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
western Celtic Sea (Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973). Especially PB has been identified as an important 
spawning area of blue whiting (Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973; Bartsch and Coombs, 1997; Kloppmann 
et al., 2001; Dransfeld et al., 2004). Boehlert and Genin (1987) pointed out that seamounts change 
the physical and biogeochemical properties of the overlying water column, for instance by internal 
wave and eddy generation, or the creation of Taylor columns which in turn affect the pelagic 
ecosystem over the seamount.   
Taylor Columns are closed anti-cyclonic, geostrophic circulation systems that occur over oceanic 
banks or seamounts, where they create local upwelling of nutrient rich water into the photic zone 
and trap the water above (Boehlert and Genin, 1987; Bakun, 1996). They act as “ocean triads” by 
providing areas where enrichment (i.e. upwelling of nutrients from the depth into the euphotic 
zone), concentration and retention processes act at the same time and thereby fuel biological 
production (Boehlert and Genin, 1987; Bakun, 1996) and potentially create improved feeding 
conditions for blue whiting larvae (Kloppmann et al., 2001). Their effect on biological processes is 
determined by the column’s vertical extend and residence time (Boehlert and Genin, 1987). Boehlert 
and Genin (1987) hypothesised, that if Taylor Columns are a recurrent phenomenon and persist for 
longer periods of time, nektonic (free-swimming) organisms may be attracted to or concentrated 
within these regions. Accordingly, adult blue whiting might choose regions to spawn, where eggs and 
larvae are retained by physical processes and food conditions are good, so recruitment success is 
increased, as proposed for herring (Clupea harengus) by Iles and Sinclair's (1982) Retention Area 
Hypothesis. According to Hillgruber and Kloppmann (1999), this may have occurred during the 
spawning season of blue whiting in 1994, when a Taylor Column above PB retained blue whiting 
larvae above the bank, where they benefitted from superior feeding conditions. 
The main distribution depth of adults ranges from 300 to 600 m (Bailey, 1982; ICES, 2014a) and also 
the majority of spawning takes place in depth beyond 180 to 200 m, according to Bainbridge and 
Cooper (1973). Other studies found that eggs are mostly spawned at even greater depths, 
encompassing 250 - 450 m (Bailey, 1982) or 300 - 400 m depth (Hillgruber and Kloppmann, 1999; 
Munk and Nielsen, 2005). From the spawning depth, the hatched larvae gradually rise towards the 
surface within about two to three weeks (Bailey, 1982; Ådlandsvik et al., 2001). These results were 
confirmed by the finding that larger larvae are more frequently distributed in shallower water than 
smaller larvae, indicating that larvae grow as they ascend (Kloppmann et al., 2001). Accordingly, 
depth was included as an explanatory variable in the SDM.

TEMPERATURE AND SALINITY 
Already in the beginning of the last century, Schmidt (1909) proposed that blue whiting are sensitive 
to the ambient salinities and temperatures they experience during spawning. Spawning fish and 
juvenile Gadidae are particularly sensitive to ambient environmental conditions since their tolerable 
ranges are more restrictive than those of non-spawning adults (Bigg, 2014). Blue whiting are thought 
to spawn within a narrow range of 9 - 10 °C SST and within a salinity of 35.35 to 35.45, according to 
Hátún et al. (2009) and in line with Bailey (1982). As a result, the abundance of blue whiting has been 
associated with water masses of a certain temperature and salinity which in turn possess distinct 
biogeochemical properties (see e.g. Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973; Bailey and Heath, 2001; Hátún et 
al., 2009).  
 
As described before, the SPG controls the path of the NAC in the northeastern Atlantic and thereby 
alters the water mass characteristics in blue whiting’s spawning region (Hátún et al., 2009). 
Accordingly, it has also been hypothesised that the SPG intervenes with blue whiting spawning 
aggregations with the proposed mechanism being, that cold and fresh water masses, that spread 
over the spawning region during a strong SPG, constrict the spawning distribution of blue whiting 
(Hátún et al., 2009). The combination of temperature and salinity in one explanatory variable (i.e. as 
two-dimensional smoother) in the model represents these processes. However, temperature and 
salinity can also potentially affect the spawning distribution of blue whiting independently from each 
other.  
TEMPERATURE 
Temperature, by itself, is the climate-influenced variable that is most influential on marine 
ecosystem processes (Barange, 2010). On the one hand, temperature influences fish indirectly for 
instance by reducing the solubility of oxygen in the water, causing a decrease in the water’s oxygen 
concentration with warming temperatures, or by changing the phenology of lower trophic levels such 
as phyto- and zooplankton which in turn also affects higher trophic levels like fish (Barange, 2010). 
On the other hand, temperature also has a direct effect on an organisms’ physiology, since metabolic 
process are faster under elevated temperatures (Miller and Wheeler, 2012). For many fish species, 
including blue whiting, temperature affects the rate of gonadal maturation, causing the onset of 
spawning to be earlier under elevated temperatures (Bailey and Heath, 2001). Moreover, embryonic 
development and growth of blue whiting proceed faster under elevated temperatures and successful 
hatching has been hypothesised to be unlikely below temperatures of about 5 °C (Bailey, 1982; Bailey 
and Heath, 2001).  
 
Accordingly, the temperature adults experience during spawning, as well as the temperature 
experienced by the eggs at their spawning depth and of the hatched larvae in the deep waters, are 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
considered to be important explanatory variables in delineating the potential niche of blue whiting 
spawning. 
However, eggs and especially larvae are found throughout the water column (Hillgruber and 
Kloppmann, 1999), because larvae are buoyant and gradually rise towards the surface from the 
spawning depth within around 3 weeks from spawning (Bailey, 1982; Ådlandsvik et al., 2001). 
Accordingly, the sea surface temperature experienced by the larvae, as they reach the sea surface, 
has also been identified as an important explanatory variable. Larvae grow faster under elevated 
temperatures, with growth rates increasing by around 0.01 per °C according to Houde (2002). Since 
the CPR’s opening is fixed, the probability of sampling larvae with the CPR is hypothesised to 
decrease with increasing temperature, reaching zero as the larvae outgrow the opening of the CPR. 
Moreover, larger larvae are able to swim faster than smaller ones and generally have further 
developed sensory organs and a greater area of view (Hunter, 1981) which might enable them to 
migrate out of the CPRs sampling depth or actively avoid the CPR. Thereby temperature can 
indirectly affect the sampling efficiency of the CPR, which is the only source of blue whiting larvae 
data employed in this study. 
SALINITY 
Eggs are spawned by blue whiting at depths beyond 200 meters (Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973). The 
density of blue whiting eggs increases during development, making them increasingly less buoyant 
when exposed to the same water density and thereby enabling them to maintain a bathypelagic 
distribution (Ådlandsvik et al., 2001). Blue whiting larvae, in turn, are more buoyant with a neutral 
buoyancy at salinities of 36.6 and gradually rise through the water column (Ådlandsvik et al., 2001). 
Their ascent in the water column is dependent on their buoyancy, which is in turn affected by the 
ambient salinity (Ådlandsvik et al., 2001). Models have shown that small variations in larval buoyancy 
can cause great changes in the larvae’s vertical distribution (Ådlandsvik et al., 2001).   
Furthermore, the larvae’s ascent is critical for their survival. Upon hatching blue whiting larvae 
passively rise through the water column while feeding on a yolk sac that is attached to their body 
(Ådlandsvik et al., 2001). In the mean time they grow and develop functional eyes and jaws, which 
enable them to feed exogenously, once they reach the surface waters (i.e. the photic zone), where 
feeding conditions are superior to those in the depth (Ådlandsvik et al., 2001). When the salinity 
decreases during larval-ascent their buoyancy decreases which may slow their ascent. Because the 
larvae’s yolk sac is a finite resource and food conditions are thought to be scarce in deep waters, an 
increased period of ascent may increase their risk of starvation. Nutritional conditions in turn affect 
the larvae’s growth rate and stage duration which can affect their susceptibility to predation (Houde, 
2002).  
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
On the other hand, if larvae rise too quickly, and reach the surface waters at earlier developmental 
stages, they might be more susceptible to predation. Since larval development is mediated by the 
ambient temperature as well, the combined effect of salinity and temperature can be seen as crucial 
for blue whiting larval survival and the CPR’s sampling efficiency which both affect the larval-
presence probability in the surface waters. 
 
LIGHT CONDITION AND DAY LENGTH 
PHOTOPERIOD 
In many fish species, while the rate of gonadal maturation is related to temperature, the onset of 
gonadal maturation is driven by the environment. The main cue triggering the beginning of gonadal 
maturation in temperate species is thought to be the photoperiod (Neuheimer and MacKenzie, 
2014). For different species of the Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) the annual change in day length, i.e. 
the time after the autumnal equinox or summer solstice, is considered to be the primary cue for 
gonadal development (Neuheimer and MacKenzie, 2014). Adult blue whiting perform diel vertical 
migrations, and are thought to move into shallower waters  of around 100 - 200 m depth at the onset 
of dusk and descend again during dawn (Bailey, 1982). Under very clear water conditions around 5 % 
of 465 nm light can be found at depths of around 100 m (Stewart, 2008). Accordingly, the 
photoperiod might affect the timing of spawning in blue whiting and will be included in the SDM. 
DAY – NIGHT EFFECTS 
The majority of blue whiting larvae found in the surface waters have already developed functional 
eyes and mouth so that they can feed exogenously, for example on copepod nauplii (Kloppmann et 
al., 2001). The majority of marine fish larvae depend on their vision for feeding (Hunter, 1981). Blue 
whiting larvae are visual predators as well and depend on a suitable combination of prey 
concentration, light and turbulence to feed successfully (Hillgruber and Kloppmann, 2000). In 
general, feeding rates of visual predators increase asymptotically up to a certain light intensity, since 
capture success increases with light, while also the risk of being preyed upon by other predators 
increases (Haraldsson et al., 2012). Therefore, as a behavioural response, larvae may alter their 
vertical position in the water column as a response to changes in the light conditions during the day. 
Hillgruber and Kloppmann (2000) pointed out, that blue whiting larvae demonstrate a distinct diel 
feeding pattern by avoiding water layers near surface, except for dusk and dawn. Evaluations of the 
larvae’s gut content, showed that blue whiting larvae feed significantly more during light, with two 
hours before sunset including dusk being the main feeding period (Hillgruber and Kloppmann, 2000). 
Bailey (1982), on the other hand, stated that small larvae show peak feeding rates during the day and 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
only larger ones exhibit peak feeding intensities at dusk. While a more recent study did not find a 
significant difference between size classes (Hillgruber and Kloppmann, 1999).   
As a result the CPR capture efficiency may change with the ambient light conditions, either as a result 
of active avoidance of the gear or diel vertical migrations (DVM) of the larvae, as pointed out by 
Pointin and Payne (2014). Since larvae in the surface waters are thought to be in a developmental 
stage where eyes are functional (Kloppmann et al., 2001), increases in the underwater luminance 
might increase the larvae’s perception distance which in turn increases the chance of a larvae to 
actively move away from the CPR. If this holds true, the CPR’s sampling efficiency would be expected 
to be greater during the night compared to during the day. On the other hand, it has been 
hypothesised that larvae perform DVM, for example by ascending to the surface waters during dusk 
and dawn (Hillgruber and Kloppmann, 2000), accordingly, one would assume a greater sampling 
efficiency of the CPR during twilight. For this reason different coefficients indicating when a sample 
was taken, i.e. during day or night, dusk or dawn, were included in the SDM.  
SOLAR ELEVATION 
The different hypothesis concerning the DVM patterns of blue whiting larvae and the effect of light 
on the sampling efficiency, have lead to the conclusion to study the effect of light in more detail. 
Besides including different day-night coefficients, the solar elevation angle, which is the angle of the 
sun measured from the horizon upwards, was considered as an alternative explanatory variable. At 
sunrise and sunset the elevation is 0°, while nautical dusk and dawn commence when the sun is 12° 
below the horizon. Since irradiance, which is the radiant energy that passes from the atmosphere 
into the ocean within a certain time period, is a function of solar elevation and water depth, the light 
conditions in the upper water column are largely influenced by the solar elevation, in particular when 
light scattering is low under clear sky and clear water condition (Soloviev and Lukas, 2014). Irradiance 
and solar elevation co-vary, with irradiance levels peaking when the sun is directly above the horizon 
and enabling the light to penetrate deepest into the water column. Accordingly, compared to the 
day-night coefficient, the solar elevation can be seen as a more nuanced indicator of the light 
conditions experienced by the larvae within the upper water column. 
 
MATERIALS AND METHODS 
CONTINUOUS PLANKTON RECORDER (CPR) DATA 
Data of Blue whiting larvae were obtained from the Continuous Plankton Recorder (CPR) survey 
(Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973; Reid et al., 2003) and have been kindly reworked and supplied by the 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
Sir Alister Hardy Foundation for Ocean Science (SAHFOS), Plymouth, UK. The CPR is a plankton 
sampling machine that is towed behind vessels of opportunity at 7 to 10 m depth, allowing for 
continuous sampling of the of upper mixed water column across major commercial shipping routes 
(Reid et al., 2003; Richardson et al., 2006). As it is towed, water enters through a small (1.27 cm 
width, 1.61 cm2) opening in front of the recorder, and is subsequently filtered onto a 270 µm silk 
screen which is continuously replaced and preserved in formalin (Richardson et al., 2006). After the 
silk is divided into samples corresponding to 10 nautical miles of tow, it is analysed under a 
microscope by a taxonomist (Richardson et al., 2006). The CPR was designed in 1925 by Sir Alister 
Hardy and has remained relatively unaltered after a modification in 1931 (Richardson et al., 2006). As 
a result the CPR survey has become one of the most wide-ranging long term surveys of marine 
organisms in the world (Batten et al., 2003). Blue whiting larvae are the most commonly occurring 
fish species found in CPR samples, with 10% of the fish larvae identified in total and accounting for 
75% of all larvae found west off Scotland (Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973). Accordingly the CPR 
provides good data on blue whiting larvae abundance in the upper mixed water column.  
For this study the blue whiting larvae abundance data was transformed to presence/absence data. 
Moreover only CPR data within 19 °W to 1 °W and 45 °N to 63 °N, referred to as Entire Sampling 
Region (ER) ranging from 1951 to 2005, was used, since this region and time period has been 
reworked in the modern reanalysis program and provides a good data coverage of CPR observations 
(Figure 4).

TEMPERATURE AT SPAWNING DEPTH  
Since no temperature measurements at the spawning depth were available for the time span of the 
CPR data, modelled temperatures were obtained from the Max Planck Institute Ocean Model that 
was forced with the NCEP–NCAR atmospheric reanalysis (MPI-OM; Matei et al., 2012).   
 
In order to analyse changes in the spawning distribution of blue whiting spawning with respect to the 
temperature eggs and larvae experienced after being spawned, the average temperature between 
183 and 560 m depth was selected. This depth range was chosen based on literature research and 
data availability and encompasses the spawning depth of blue whiting beyond 200 m (Bailey, 1982; 
Hillgruber and Kloppmann, 1999; Munk and Nielsen, 2005; ICES, 2014b) and the main distribution 
depth of adults from 300 to 600 m (Bailey, 1982; ICES, 2014a).   
For the Data Visualisation part, first of all a regularly spaced sampling grid was set up with 2537 
sampling points (with 52 latitudinal and 52 longitudinal sampling points on average, amounting to ca. 
2.9 sampling points per degree) within the Entire Sampling Region (red polygon, Figure 5) using the 
sp package’s spsample() function (Pebesma and Bivand, 2005). All points in the sampling grid were 
transformed from geographic coordinates (longitude, latitude) into the MPI-OM grid coordinates 
(x,y) by means of the Akima interpp() function (Akima et al., 2013) and subsequently transformed 
into a spatial points data frame (Pebesma and Bivand, 2005). The modelled, monthly temperature 
data from MPI-OM brick containing all temperature information from March to May (i.e the months 
of highest larval occurrence in the CPR) of each year was created (from here on referred to as year-
month combination). Afterwards, the temperature data for each point within the sampling grid was 
extracted by means of bilinear interpolation with the Raster package’s extract() function and stored 
in a data frame (Hijmans, 2015). Since only the temperature in the spawning depth of blue whiting 
was of interest, the temperatures between 183 and 560 m [i.e. 183, 220, 263, 310, 363, 420, 485, 
560 m] were extracted from the data frame. Then the arithmetic mean of the temperatures was 
calculated for each sampling point. If the water depth at the location of a CPR observation was 
shallower than 183 m, the temperature closest to the sea floor was extracted. The resulting 
temperature data was then combined with the geographical coordinates of the sampling points. 
Thereby each CPR observation was matched-up with the average temperature at spawning depth. 
This method was performed for each year-month combination. Moreover, the average temperatures 
at the spawning depths in the Entire Study Region, Rockall Region and the Continental Shelf Region 
(Figure 5) of each year were calculated.

DATA ORIGIN AND PREPARATION FOR THE SPECIES DISTRIBUTION MODEL 
CPR DATA 
For the species distribution model of blue whiting spawning, a subset of the CPR data ranging from 
January to June was chosen. Additionally only data within the region of highest data coverage west of 
the British Isles,was chosen ( i.e. the red polygon in Figure 4).  
TEMPERATURE AND SALINITY AT SURFACE (OM.SST AND SSS) 
 AND SPAWNING DEPTH (T.DEEP, AND S. DEEP) 
In order to analyse changes in blue whiting’s spawning distribution with respect to the temperature 
and salinity eggs experienced after being spawned and the larvae experienced during their ascent, 
the average temperature and salinity between 183 and 560 m depth was selected, for the same 
reasons as described above. Modelled temperatures and salinities were obtained from the Max 
Planck Institute Ocean Model (MPI-OM; Matei et al., 2012). 
 
Since the CPR samples the upper water column, the sea surface temperature (SST) and salinity (SSS) 
were of interest to depict the conditions experienced by the larvae when sampled and to account for 
effects on the CPR’s sampling efficiency.  
For the Species Distribution Model, the geographical location (longitude, latitude) of all CPR data 
points were transformed into MPI-OM grid coordinates (x,y) by means of the Akima interpp() 
function and subsequently transformed into a spatial points data frame (Pebesma and Bivand, 2005). 
The MPI-OM monthly modelled temperature and salinity data were then extracted for each year-
month combination at each CPR data point using bilinear interpolation and the Raster package’s 
extract() function (Hijmans, 2015) and stored in a data frame. The resulting data frame contained 
temperature/salinity data for the whole data frame at each year-month combination. The first layer 
within the data frame corresponded to the first 6 m, measured from the sea surface. Hence these 
values were assigned to SST and SSS. 
  
The temperature and salinity at the spawning depth of blue whiting were extracted for each CPR 
data point from the data frame, by calculating the arithmetic mean between 183 and 560 m depth 
[i.e. for 183, 220, 263, 310, 363, 420, 485, 560 m]. If the water depth at the location of a CPR 
observation was shallower than 183 m, the temperature/salinity closest to the sea floor was 
extracted.  
TEMPERATURE AND SALINITY DURING SPAWNING (T.SPAWN AND S.SPAWN) 
As outlined before, the spawning depth of blue whiting encompasses 300 to 600 m (Bailey, 1982; 
ICES, 2014a) with spawning taking place in more shallow waters but beyond 200 m, from which the 
eggs and larvae rise towards the surface within about two to three weeks (Bailey, 1982; Ådlandsvik et 
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
al., 2001). This is based on model simulations have shown that at a neutral salinity of 35, larvae rise 
to the surface within 20 - 25 days which approximated the observed vertical distributions of larvae in 
the field (Ådlandsvik et al., 2001). Upon reaching the surface blue whiting larvae are around 2.6 to 
7.5 mm in length (Ådlandsvik et al., 2001). Seeing that the majority of blue whiting larvae found 
within the CPR samples are smaller than 6 mm in length (Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973) and blue 
whiting larvae have an average growth rate of > 0.3 mm per day (Bailey and Heath, 1996), it is very 
likely that that larvae captured by the CPR were spawned not more than three weeks before.   
This, coupled with the fact that the MPI-OM had a monthly resolution, led to the decision to take the 
temperature and salinity in the spawning depth one month before each CPR observations, as a proxy 
for the conditions experienced by the spawning adults.  
 
Accordingly, the average temperature and salinity at spawning depth one month before each CPR 
data point was recorded (T.spawn and S.spawn, respectively), were used as a proxy for the 
conditions experienced by the spawning adult blue whiting in the SDM. T.spawn and S.spawn were 
extracted from the MPI-OM data in the same manner as T.deep and S.deep, except that the data was 
extracted for the month prior to a CPR observation. For example, given a CPR observation in January 
1956, it was matched-up with the temperature and salinity at the location of the CPR observation, 
averaged over blue whiting’s spawning depth in December 1955. 
DENSITY 
Since temperature and salinity both influence the density of the water, the density at each CPR 
observation was derived. The density was calculated from the extracted temperature and salinity at 
spawning depth data for each CPR data point by the “oce” packages swRho() function. As input for 
pressure the average pressure between 183 and 560 m was taken, i.e. 383.55 dbar. 
HADLEY SEA SURFACE TEMPERATURE (HAD.SST) 
In cases were SST was modelled as a one-dimensional smoother, data obtained from the Met Office 
Hadley Centre's sea ice and sea surface temperature data set HadISST1 (Rayner et al., 2003), were 
used and will be referred to as had.SST. had.SST is based on in-situ SST observations until 1981 and 
from 1982 onwards on a combination of in situ/satellite SST observations is used (Rayner et al., 
2003). It has a monthly resolution and is based on a 1° latitude-longitude grid (Rayner et al., 2003). 
had.SST was then matched-up with the point in time and space of each CPR observation by means of 
bilinear interpolation.  
BATHYMETRY, I.E. WATER DEPTH 
Water depth was incorporated into the model by matching each CPR observation with the 
corresponding water depth, found at the specific location of a CPR observation, based on NOAAs 
==PAGE:25==
24 
 
ETOPO1 product (Amante and Eakins, 2009). The depth was extracted for each CPR data point by 
means of bilinear interpolation. Since the CPR observations covered a great depth range of up to 
5063 m depth, depth was log-10-transformed, to increase the resolution at lower depths, in 
particular to increase the resolution at the spawning depth of blue whiting. 
LIGHT CONDITION AND DAY LENGTH 
PHOTOPERIOD  
As outlined before, it is likely that that larvae captured by the CPR were spawned three weeks 
before. Accordingly, the photoperiod (PP) was defined as the amount of daylight hours, i.e. the hours 
(h) between sunrise and sunset, during spawning, i.e. 21 days prior to the capture of a larva in the 
CPR. For each CPR data point the time of sunrise and sunset was calculated by means of the 
sunriset() function in the maptools package in R (Bivand and Lewin-Koh, 2015) based on the 
geographic location and time of each CPR data point.  
DAY-NIGHT COEFFICIENT 
The ability of the CPR to capture fish larvae may change with the ambient light, either as a result of 
active avoidance of the gear or diel vertical migrations of the larvae (Pointin and Payne, 2014). 
Accordingly a categorical factor indicative of whether the sample was taken during the day or night 
was included into the SDM and referred to as DN. The DN coefficient was derived for each CPR 
observation by calculating the time of sunrise and sunset by means of the sunriset() function in the 
maptools package in R (Bivand and Lewin-Koh, 2015) based on the geographic location and time (in 
GMT, as POSIXct object) of each CPR data point. For DN the time from sunrise to sunset was defined 
as day (Figure 6). Care was taken with calculating each day-night coefficient, so that eternal days and 
eternal nights were correctly represented.  
 
Since blue whiting larvae have frequently been encountered at the sea surface during dusk and dawn 
(Hillgruber and Kloppmann, 1999), a set of different day-night coefficients were created to take this 
behaviour into account (Figure 6), and analysed in the chapter: Investigating the Effect of Light 
Parameterization. 
 
A second categorical day-night coefficient was created, where the definition of a day was extended 
to the hours between dawn to dusk (DNDD). The factor DNDD was derived for each CPR data point 
by calculating the time of dusk and dawn by means of the crepuscule() function in the maptools 
package in R (Bivand and Lewin-Koh, 2015) based on the geographic location and time (in GMT, as 
POSIXct object) of each CPR data point. Dawn and dusk were defined in line with the “nautical dawn” 
convention, when the sun is 12° below the horizon, i.e. the solar elevation angle is - 12°.  
 
In order to increase the information content of the coefficient, a day-night coefficient with three 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
levels was created, named DNTL, where day was defined from sunrise to sunset, night from dusk to 
dawn and twilight (TL) during the remaining hours. From April onwards when there was no defined 
dusk or dawn (i.e. the sun never went below 12° under the horizon), the time after sunset and before 
sunrise were defined as twilight as well. 
To study the effect of twilight exclusively, on the CPR’s capture efficiency, a fourth day-night 
coefficient was created, referred to as TL. The first level was twilight which was defined as described 
above, and the remaining hours were assigned to the second level (referred to as N in Figure 6).  
 
 
SOLAR ELEVATION ANGLE (THETA)  
In order to account for the nuanced changes in the underwater light regime during the course of a 
day, the solar elevation angle was included as an explanatory variable. The solar elevation angle was 
calculated using the solarpos() function in the maptools package in R (Bivand and Lewin-Koh, 2015) 
based on the geographic location and sampling time of each CPR data point. The solar elevation 
angle is referred to as theta in the model equations.

that the fisheries and acoustic survey data exceeds the SDM data set by three years, since three 
years is the minimum time period required for blue whiting to contribute to the spawning stock 
biomass (ICES, 2014b). The fisheries and acoustic survey data were reported in ICES rectangles and 
transformed into geographical coordinates by using the function ices.rect() within R’s mapplots

MODELLING APPROACH 
DATA VISUALISATION 
The primary aim of the data visualisation part is to characterize the state of the system during the 
main spawning period of blue whiting, i.e. the months with highest larval-presence frequency from 
March to May over the entire sampling period from 1951 to 2005 (Figure 8 d) and to compare these 
results with the study of Hátún et al. (2009). Hátún et al. (2009) analysed latitudinal shifts in the 
spawning distribution of blue whiting along the continental shelf and longitudinal shifts along a 
section across the continental shelf and Rockall Plateau. Accordingly, to facilitate a comparison with 
Hátún et al. (2009), changes in the latitudinal and longitudinal distribution of blue whiting larvae 
were analysed for two regions within the ER, namely Rockall region (RR) and the Continental Shelf 
Region (CS, Figure 5), resembling Hátún et al.'s (2009) study region.  
More specifically, a Generalized Additive Model (GAM) with a binomial distribution and logit link 
structure was employed, since the presence/absence aspect of the CPR data was used:  
P(Xi = TRUE|πi) ~ Binomial(πi) ; with  logit(πi) = f() 
 
 
where Xi is the presence/absence of observation i , and πi  is probability of Xi being true (i.e. the 
presence of blue whiting larvae). 
 
In order to analyse changes in the latitudinal distribution of blue whiting along the CS, the 
presence/absence was modelled with latitude as explanatory variables, i.e. logit(πi) = 
f(latitude|year), and only data within the CS was considered (Figure 5). In RR the presence/absence 
of blue whiting larvae modelled with longitude as explanatory variable with, logit(πi) = 
f(longitude|year).  
Changes in the latitudinal and longitudinal distribution over the time period were represented as 
smooth variations over the years. Accordingly, the year in which a CPR observation was made, was 
included as a factor in a “by” variable in the one-dimensional smoothers with “id” set to 1 to obtain

the same smoothing parameter for each year and to reduce the variability of smoothness between 
the different years. Moreover, cubic regression splines with shrinkage were used in the one-
dimensional smoothers (s), to include smoothness penalties that will set smooth parameters to zero 
“for large enough smoothing parameters” and to make the computation more efficient (Wood, 
2006a, 2006b). Each model was fitted with a “gamma” parameter of 1.4 to avoid overfitting as 
recommended by Wood (2006a).  
The overall objective of the second part of the Data Visualisation was to analyse changes in the 
spawning distribution of blue whiting with respect to temperature in a statistically robust approach. 
For this analysis, the presence-probability of blue whiting larvae was modelled by two-dimensional 
smoothers including latitude or longitude and the average temperature at spawning depth, 
represented by the average temperature between 183 and 560 m depth during the months of peak 
spawning (March - May). More specifically, the models contained a tensor product (te) smooth, 
which allowed for computationally inexpensive smooth interactions of variables measured in 
different units (Wood, 2006a) and included following covariates : 
a) latitude and the mean temperature at spawning depth during the peak spawning months 
averaged over the CS 
b) longitude and the mean temperature at spawning depth during the peak spawning months 
averaged over RR 
c) latitude and the mean temperature at spawning depth during the peak spawning months 
averaged over the ER 
d) longitude and the mean temperature at spawning depth during the peak spawning months 
averaged over the ER. 
Thereby Model a) and b) relate to the specific sub region, i.e. CS and RR, respectively, while 
temperatures in Model c) and d) can be directly compared since the temperature is averaged over 
the same region (ER).

MODEL VALIDATION AND ASSESSMENT 
The validity of model fit, was evaluated by means of the mgcv packages gam.check() function 
following the guidelines set out by Wood (2006a). Additionally the qq.gam() function with 50 
replicate datasets was applied to plot response residuals versus theoretical quantiles (Wood, 2006a). 
Models were classified as valid, when there was no outlier of great magnitude (Annex, Figure 23).  
In this study the Information Theoretic (IT) approach to data analysis was applied. The most essential 
step in the IT approach is the development of a set of plausible alternative hypothesis a priori, i.e. 
before data analysis (Anderson, 2008; Burnham et al., 2011). Accordingly, several hypothesis to 
describe blue whiting spawning distributions were made a priori, based on literature research and 
understanding of the system, as described in the chapter: Hypothesis Development. Subsequently, 
models were derived to represent each of the science hypotheses.  
 
Since reality is complex, models merely represent approximations of full reality (Anderson, 2008; 
Zuur et al., 2010). The “information” that is lost, in the process is termed Kullback–Leibler (K-L) 
information and is the basis of the IT approach (Anderson, 2008; Burnham et al., 2011). The model 
with the smallest information loss in the set is “closest” to full reality and thus can  be interpreted as 
the “best” model (Anderson, 2008).

maximum likelihood corrected for bias (Anderson, 2008). Akaike’s Information Criterion (AIC) makes 
it possible to rank models and to quantify their evidence (Anderson, 2008; Burnham et al., 2011). The

The accuracy of a model can be evaluated in terms of its ability to correctly predict presences and 
absences and is often measured in terms of sensitivity and specificity. The sensitivity of a model is 
the probability that the model correctly predicts an observation of a species at a site (i.e. sum of all 
correctly predicted (i.e. true) presences divided by the sum of all observed presences), and specificity 
is the probability that a known absence site is correctly predicted (sum of all true absences divided by 
the sum of all observed absences) (Liu et al., 2011).  
Evaluating models based on the same data sets that were used for their calibration, has been 
proposed to be a poor approach (Guisan and Zimmermann, 2000). Therefore, for the following 
prediction-based measures, the models were trained with 80% of the data and subsequently 
predictions were made for the remaining 20% of the data. To somewhat randomize the prediction 
data set, data from every 5th year within the CPR data set (i.e. 1951, 1956, 1961, 1966, 1971, 1976, 
1981, 1986, 1991, 1996, 2001) was used for making predictions and subsequently the model quality 
was assessed.    
A good threshold-independent measure to assess model quality in terms of their predictive 
performance is the application of receiver operator curves (ROC) and their associated area under the 
curve (AUC) (Freeman and Moisen, 2008). In an ROC the true positive rate (sensitivity) of a model is 
plotted against its false positive rate (1 - specificity) with a threshold varying between 0 and 1 
(Freeman and Moisen, 2008). The AUC is a measure of prediction accuracy and corresponds to the 
probability of a model to rank a randomly chosen presence site higher than a randomly chosen 
absence site (Sing et al., 2005). An AUC value of 1 indicates an ideal discrimination between 
presences and absences while models with poor discrimination, like a random guess, have an AUC 
value of 0.5 (Freeman and Moisen, 2008). Models with an AUC above 0.75 are considered be “useful” 
in discriminating between presences and absences (Ferrier et al., 2006). The AUC was calculated 
using the R package ROCR (Sing et al., 2005). 
 
Moreover a threshold-dependent measure to assess the model quality was applied. The threshold 
used for translating the predicted probability of blue whiting larval occurrence back into presences 
and absences was chosen for each model, so that the predicted prevalence was equal to the 
observed prevalence. Accordingly, the total number of presences in the prediction data set was equal 
to the sum of presences in the observed dataset. This is in accordance with Freeman and Moisen 
(2008), who stated this to be the best threshold selection method when aiming at depicting unbiased 
estimates of species occurrence. The translated presence/absence data of the prediction was then 
used to calculate the true skill statistic (TSS), which is the average of the net prediction success rate 
for presences and absences (Freeman and Moisen, 2008; Liu et al., 2011). The TSS is calculated by 
subtracting 1 from the sum of sensitivity and specificity (Liu et al., 2011). Accordingly, when the TSS is 
==PAGE:33==
32 
 
1 it indicates that there are no false positives or false negatives, i.e. the model accuracy is very good. 
While a TSS of zero indicated that the model is useless since it is not accurate at all.  
Additionally, the permutation importance of each independent variable within the best ranked 
model was evaluated to assess each varibale’s relative importance or sensitivity. To apply this 
method, data for each variable (n) was replicated and then randomly assigned to a CPR observation 
by means of R’s sample() function. After the random permutation of variables, the best ranked 
model was run again n times; each time with one randomly assigned variable while the other 
variables remained fixed, i.e. as in the original best model. For each of the n models the AUC was 
calculated, as described before. Subsequently the difference between the original model’s AUC 
(containing no random variable) and each of the n model’s AUC was calculated and normalized. The 
higher the difference in AUC compared to the original model, the higher is its permutation 
importance and the greater is the sensitivity of the variable that has been randomized, accordingly 
the more important is the variable in explaining the response.

INITIAL INVESTIGATION 
From the preliminary SDM set containing 18 distinct models (see Annex, Table IV) a set of three 
different models was chosen. The two best models according to the model selection criteria (AIC, 
AUC, TSS) were also the most complex models, with the greatest number of explanatory variables, 
which included SST, log-transformed depth as well as the interaction between T.deep and S.deep in 
Model 3 and S.spawn and T.spawn in Model 5 (Table II). Unlike, TSS and AUC which are measures of 
model quality and will always select the most complex model (unless used in a cross-validation 
setup), the AIC has a penalty for the model complexity, so it can be seen as a superior selection 
criterion when selecting models with different complexity. Another model was chosen, since it was 
the best performing, least complex model in the set, comprising of an interaction between SSS and 
SST and depth as explanatory variables (Table II, Model 1). In all models, the smoothers and day-
night factor were significant (p-value < 0.001). Overall, the model including S.spawn and T.spawn as 
explanatory variables, Model 5, performed best (Table II). 
 
In the next step the photoperiod (PP) experienced by the spawning adult (i.e. 3 weeks prior to a CPR 
observation) was added as an explanatory variable to each of the models, which considerably 
improved their performance (Table II). The AIC was considerably reduced with the addition in PP and 
the AUC increased by about 0.1 peaking at 0.907 in Model 6, which reached an Akaike weight of one. 
Also the accuracy, as measured by the TSS increased more than two-fold for the two complex models 
(Model 4 and 6). Moreover the explained deviance increased with the addition of PP in all models.  
Model 4 achieved the highest TSS with 0.378, compared to 0.365 in Model 6. Since the ∆AIC for 
Model 6 versus Model 4 was 25, the evidence ratio was around 270 000, which indicates that the 
evidence for considering Model 6 as the best model is strong.   
The explanatory variables in Model 6 were all significant (p-value < 0.001) and included S.spawn and 
T.spawn (X2 = 109.6, edf = 14.84), SST (X2 = 609.2, edf = 7.38), log-transformed depth (X2 = 245.9, edf = 
4.52), and PP (X2 = 561.4, edf = 7.10).

INVESTIGATING THE EFFECT OF LIGHT PARAMETERIZATION 
In a further model set, the effect of different parameterizations of the day-night coefficient (Figure 6) 
and the addition of solar elevation angle (theta) as an explanatory variable was investigated for the 
Best Model (Model 6, Table II). Including dusk and dawn in the definition of a day (Model B) resulted 
in a DN coefficient comparable to the model where PP was absent (i.e. Table II, Model 5), with. a 
greater probability of encountering larvae during the day (incl. dusk and dawn) than during night. An 
improvement in model skill was observed compared to Model A (while the AIC decreased and the 
AUC increased slightly compared to Model A, the TSS remained at the same level).  
Including a more elaborate day-night coefficient with day defined from sunrise to sunset and night 
between the end of dusk and the onset of dawn with the remaining hours being classified as twilight 
(Model C) yielded a very similar model performance as Model B which included dusk and dawn in the 
definition of a day. The probability of encountering larvae was greater during the day and also 
slightly greater during twilight compared to night. 
Excluding the day-night coefficient from the model (Model D), resulted in a similar model 
performance with a minor decrease in AIC and moderate increase in TSS compared to Model A. 
Notably is that this model yielded one of the highest TSS within the set.

EVALUATION OF THE BEST MODEL  
ONE-DIMENSIONAL, ADDITIVE TERMS 
The function of the smoother for PP was very similar in all models mentioned in Table III with a broad 
peak in larval-presence probability when the photoperiod experienced by the spawning adult 
amounted to 11 to 14 hours (see Figure 13 a).   
The smoother of the solar elevation angle (theta) in the Best Model took positive values from a solar 
elevation angle of -10° onwards and assigned the greatest probability of encountering larvae 
between a solar elevation angle of  0° and 20°, i.e. the period shortly after sunrise and/or before 
sunset (Figure 13 b). 
 
The smoother of SST reached positive values between temperatures of 9 °C to 12 °C with a peak 
around 10 °C (Figure 13 c). Increasing depth resulted in an increase in larval-presence probability up 
to around 300 m, i.e. 102.5 m (Figure 13 d). 
 
 From depths of around 300 m onwards, the probability of encountering larvae remained at a 
constantly high level.   
Analysing the permutation importance of each variable for each of the Best Model’s explanatory 
variables as the relative loss in AUC, indicated that PP was the most sensitive variable, followed by 
SST and depth while theta was least sensitive (Figure 14). Accordingly, the Best Model heavily relies 
on PP and SST in terms of prediction accuracy. 
INTERACTION BETWEEN TEMPERATURE AND SALINITY 
The 2D smooth of T.spawn and S.spawn in the best performing model showed the greatest 
probability of encountering larvae around a salinity of 35.5 and a temperature between 9 °C - 10 °C 
within the spawning depth of blue whiting (Figure 13 e). Since GAM’s tend to perform best in the 
centre and not so well on the edges (Wood, 2006a), the high probabilities found at the margins of 
the temperature and salinity range will be disregarded. A linear effect was observed, with a 
probability greater than 0.1 spanning from water of low salinity and low temperature to saline and 
warm water. This suggested an effect of density on presence-probability, as the probability > 0.1 
aligned to some extend to the contour lines of equal density (Figure 13 f). 
 
In order to find out whether density was a better explanatory variable for explaining the spawning 
distribution of blue whiting, the 2D smooth of T.spawn and S.spawn was exchanged by density as a 
one dimensional smooth term (Model E2). In Model E2, the smooth term for density (X2 = 28.5, edf = 
5.0), SST (X2 = 690.9, edf =7.32), depth (X2 = 431.92, edf =5.78), PP (X2 = 561.85, edf =7.15), and theta 
(X2 = 26.85, edf = 3.01), which were all significant (p-value < 0.001).

performance, with an ∆AIC of 79 compared to the Best Model and AUC and TSS of 0.905 and 0.382, 
respectively. Moreover the smooth function of density did not show a clear pattern (Figure 13 f) 
indicating that density was of minor importance and also that information is lost when using density 
instead of the interaction between temperature and salinity. Accordingly, the Best Model and will be

DISTRIBUTIONAL CHANGES IN TWO REGIMES DURING THE PEAK SPAWNING MONTHS 
Within the study region in the northeastern Atlantic (ER, Figure 5), large changes in the oceans 
temperature and salinity characteristics have occurred over the study period (Figure 19). During most 
of the study period, the marine climate within the ER can be characterized by two distinct regimes, as 
can be seen from the Temperature-Salinity (T-S) Diagram (Figure 19 c): the deep water was typically 
either saline and warm or fresh (i.e. of low-salinity) and cold. Accordingly, years with a lower salinity 
and temperature than the average, were classified as fresh and cold, while years where temperature 
and salinity exceeded the average, were classified as saline and warm.  
 
To depict these changes in oceanographic conditions within the spawning region of blue whiting and 
their effect on blue whiting’s spawning distribution, predictions were made for the two regimes for 
the months with highest predicted larval-presence, namely April and May (Figure 9 d),.  
The seasonal progression of larval-presence and the associated northward shift and spatial 
contraction of the potential spawning niche from April to May (Figure 17) is also apparent under the 
different oceanographic regimes (Figure 20). However marked changes in the spawning distribution 
between the two different regimes were observed. 
 
 
During fresh and cold conditions, the spawning distribution is predicted to have the greatest extent 
(Figure 20). In April during fresh and cold years, probabilities of larval-presence are high on RP and to 
its south and south-west and in particular east of RP, where probabilities peak at 0.6 (Figure 20, top 
row). Moreover high probabilities are found at latitudes north of 55 °N along the CS. Moderate 
probability of spawning distributions are found around RP and at latitudes above 50 °N, especially 
north of PB spanning across the shallow regions of RT (< 3000 m). During warm and saline years in 
April, however, the distribution is spatially more contracted while the largest spawning region where 
probabilities of larval-presence is high, remains at the eastern edge of RP. 
 
During May, under fresh and cold conditions, the peak spawning distributions (p > 0.5) are located at 
higher latitudes, mainly covering the northern parts of RP and especially its western, northern and 
eastern edge as well as along the CS at 58 °N (Figure 20, bottom row). Again the region of moderate 
larval-presence probability is notably more contracted in May compared to April. When the water is 
warmer and more saline during May, regions of high and moderate larval-presence probability 
decrease considerably, with the main spawning regions located on RP and along the CS between 
latitudes of 58 °N and 61 °N. The difference in the potential spawning niche during the two regimes, 
becomes, however, most apparent, when mapping the probability differences between the two 
regimes. The map of probability differences clearly illustrates the more constricted potential 
spawning region under warm and saline conditions and the greater probability of spawning further 
south, along the CS and on PB, during cold and fresh conditions (Figure 20, right column).

DISCUSSION 
REPRESENTATIVENESS OF CPR DATA 
In this study, the presence and absence of blue whiting larvae in CPR samples was used as the basis 
for a species distribution model of blue whiting spawning distribution. Blue whiting spawn at depths 
between 200 to 600 m, from which the eggs and larvae rise towards the surface within about two to 
three weeks (Bailey, 1982; Ådlandsvik et al., 2001). Modelling and field studies have shown that blue 
whiting larvae grow as they ascend and reach body lengths of around 2.6 to 7.5 mm (Ådlandsvik et 
al., 2001) while being on average 3 mm upon reaching the surface (Kloppmann et al., 2001). As the 
majority of blue whiting larvae found within the CPR samples are smaller than 6 mm in length 
(Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973), it is very likely that that these are recently hatched larvae, that have 
not been subject to horizontal drift in the surface waters and thereby represent the distribution of 
spawning adult blue whiting, as hypothesised by Pointin and Payne (2014). Accordingly, the 
predicted probability of larval-presence can be used as a proxy for the spawning distribution of blue 
whiting.  
Several limitations have been put forward with regard to the use of CPR data, such as the fixed 
sampling depth of 7 to 10 m (Batten et al., 2003), the restriction of sampling to commercial shipping 
routes causing inconsistent timing and location of CPR sampling transects (Bailey, 1982; Richardson 
et al., 2006) and the decrease in sampling frequency, especially in Rockall Region during the recent 
years (Figure 9 a; Pointin and Payne 2014). The fixed sampling depth is not problematic, since it has 
been shown that blue whiting larvae are present throughout the upper water column during the 
spawning period (Hillgruber and Kloppmann, 1999) and only the presence-absence characteristic of 
the data was used. Moreover, the non-random sampling design is not of concern, since it only limits 
the comparisons of single years or short time series (Bailey, 1982; Batten et al., 2003), while in this 
study CPR data from 54 years was analysed, thereby providing a valid indications of long-term trends 
and inter-annual variability. While, the reduction in sampling coverage over Rockall Region however, 
might be seen as the biggest limitation for the data visualisation part, since it limits the inter-annual 
comparison in this region, the SDM is however rather unaffected by this, as data from the entire time 
period is used for calibration.  
Other limitations put forward are the relatively low sampling volume (ca. 3m3 for each 10 nautical 
mile sample (Richardson et al., 2006)) and the low yields of abundances from CPR data compared to 
net hauls (Batten et al., 2003), since the likelihood of encountering small patches of larvae increases, 
with an increasing sampling volume (Pepin, 2002). Moreover, fish larvae captured by the CPR are 
grouped in abundance categories from four individuals onwards (Richardson et al., 2006), which 
==PAGE:55==
54 
 
reduces the data’s information content and would require more complex statistical models to 
analyse (Pointin and Payne, 2014). All these issues, coupled with the finding that the majority of CPR 
samples with larval-presence contained less than four individuals, re-confirm the decision to use the 
presence/absence characteristic of the data, in agreement with Pointin and Payne (2014).  
 
 
LOCATIONS OF PEAK SPAWNING  
Comparing the probability of blue whiting larval-presence from 1951 to 2005 with the average water 
temperatures at spawning depth within the Continental Shelf Region (CS, Figure 5) only showed a 
modest trend for this region (Figure 11 and 12 a) while no trend was observed for Rockall Region 
(Figure 10), possibly due to the low data availability in this region (Figure 9 a). During warm periods, 
i.e. 1953 - 1959 and 1996 - 2005, the greatest probability of blue whiting larval occurrence was 
located north of Porcupine Bank (PB), i.e. north of 54 °N. During cold periods, on the other hand, 
spawning took place more frequently at PB and towards its south in Porcupine Seabright. This is in 
accordance with reported shifts in spawning distribution by Bainbridge and Cooper (1973), who 
found Rockall Plateau (RP) to be the major spawning ground during the rather warm years of 1950 to 
1960, while during colder years that followed until the late 60s the spawning distribution shifted 
southward ranging from RP to PB.  
In order to analyse these changes in the main location of spawning distribution of blue whiting with 
respect to temperature in more detail, a two-dimensional model incorporating latitude/longitude 
and the water temperature at spawning depth during the main spawning period (March - May) was 
applied (Table I). The analysis of blue whiting larval-presence probability, as a proxy of their spawning 
distribution, showed that there are distinct changes in the latitudinal and longitudinal extent with 
respect to the water temperature at spawning depth. In general, the spawning distribution showed 
the greatest latitudinal and longitudinal extent and the highest probability under medium water 
temperatures at spawning depth (11.0 - 11.2 °C averaged over ER; Figure 12 c and d), suggesting that 
spawning takes place around this temperature. It has to be noted, that it is inherent to theoretical 
models, that optimal environmental conditions are found at the centre of  a species niche (Colwell et 
al., 2009). This also holds true for the simple latitudinal and longitudinal model, seeing that the 
temperatures at which the highest probabilities are found, correspond to the mean temperature 
encountered in the ER (11.14 ± 0.25 °C, Figure 19 a). The model indicates that blue whiting spawn 
around 11 °C, whether this temperature is optimal for larval survival can, however, not be addressed 
here. 
==PAGE:56==
55 
 
The latitudinal contraction and westward expansion of the spawning distribution under low 
temperatures seen in this work is neither supported by Hátún et al. (2009) nor by Bainbridge and 
Cooper (1973). A possible reason is that the time series applied in these studies are based on more 
narrow temperature ranges. The longitudinal analysis by Hátún et al. (2009) only included CPR data 
from the years 1951 to 1970 to analyse the longitudinal shift of spawning distribution, in respect to 
annually averaged SST, and also Bainbridge and Cooper (1973) used a shorter time series from 1950 
to 1969 of the mean SST from April to March. The greatest fluctuations in SST and temperature at 
blue whiting’s spawning depth were, however recorded from the end of the 1970’s onwards (Figure 
19). For the time period in this study (1951 - 2005) the minimum and maximum temperatures at 
spawning depth in the ER averaged over March - May occurred in 1979 (10.66 °C) and in 2004 (11.68 
°C), respectively, with an average temperature of 11.14 ± 0.25 °C over the entire time period within  
the ER (Figure 19). Considering that the minimum and maximum temperatures included in this 
analysis, were not covered in the previously mentioned studies, this analysis could provide a more 
comprehensive picture of the effect of temperature on blue whiting spawning distributions.  
Modelling the presence-probability of larvae by means of a two-dimensional smoother including the 
mean temperature within ER at the spawning depth of blue whiting and latitude or longitude (Table I, 
Model c and d), yielded a considerably lower explained deviance compared to the best model within 
the SDM set (Table III, Model E). This suggests that temperature alone is not sufficient to model the 
presence probability of blue whiting, and that there is a finer scale structure. Accordingly, 
interactions between variables, such as the effect of depth or the photoperiod, which were not 
included the two-dimensional models, lead to their poor performance. Moreover, this confirms the 
approach of this analysis, to examine the key environmental parameters that may influence the 
spawning distribution of blue whiting spawning and to subsequently apply these in a SDM to 
delineate blue whiting’s potential niche of spawning, which will be discussed below. 
 
 
THE INFLUENCE OF LIGHT ON THE CPR CAPTURE EFFICIENCY AND BIOLOGICAL IMPLICATIONS  
PARAMETERIZATION OF THE DAY-NIGHT COEFFICIENT  
The capture efficiency of the CPR was hypothesised, to change with the ambient light conditions, 
either as a result of diel vertical migrations of the larvae or their active avoidance of the gear during 
daylight, as pointed out by Pointin and Payne (2014). In the initial SDM set the day-night coefficient 
(DN) was positive for all models (Table II) indicating that the CPR was more efficient at capturing 
larvae during the day than during the night and demonstrating that active avoidance of the sampling 
gear by larvae during was not an issue, in accordance with the results of Pointin and Payne (2014). 
==PAGE:57==
56 
 
When including the photoperiod experienced by the adult at the day of spawning (PP) into the 
models, the difference in day and night was not significant anymore, thereby diminishing the 
influence of the day-night coefficient on the larval-presence probability (Table II). Further 
investigations with different parameterizations of the day-night coefficient (Figure 6) suggested the 
greatest probability of larval-presence during the day and twilight in most cases, but lead to no 
notable increases in model skill (Table III). Excluding the day-night coefficients from the model while 
including the PP yielded the best model, indicating the low importance of the day-night coefficient in 
explaining the presence of blue whiting larvae within the CPR. Accordingly, a more sophisticated 
variable was included to reflect the nuance in the changes of light in the upper water column during 
the course of a day, namely the solar elevation angle. 
THE INFLUENCE OF THE SOLAR ELEVATION ANGLE 
Including the solar elevation angle (theta), while omitting the day-night coefficient all together, 
showed a major increase in model skill and very strong evidence as judged by the model selection 
criteria towards this model (Table III, Model E) as being the best model within the set, henceforth 
referred to as Best Model.  
 
The Best Model indicated that blue whiting larvae start to be present in the upper water column 
from the onset of twilight (i.e. from dawn or sunset onwards) with the greatest probability during the 
hours following sunrise and/or preceding sunset (Figure 13 b). The greatest probability of larval-
presence was indicated during a couple of hours following sunrise and/or preceding sunset (Figure 13 
b). Note that and/or is used, since the solar elevation angle does not contain information of the exact 
time of the day with sunrise and sunset both being defined at a solar elevation angle of 0°. 
Accordingly, the solar elevation angle is merely an indicator of the light regime underwater that is 
influenced by changes in the solar elevation during the course of a day.  
Since the greatest larval-presence probability is suggested during twilight and the period shortly 
after, the hypothesis that larvae avoid the sampling gear can be rejected. Accordingly, it is more 
plausible that larvae perform a diel vertical migration (DVM), which confirms the results of Hillgruber 
and Kloppmann (2000) that blue whiting larvae exhibit a distinct DVM by ascending into the upper 
water column at the onset of twilight.  
Although, the day-night coefficient also pointed towards greater capture efficiency during the day 
and twilight, it was too crude to reflect the effect of the subtle changes in light regime around 
twilight, while the use of theta increased the information content of the light regime in the model 
and thereby increased the model’s performance and explanatory power.  
 
The DVM of blue whiting larvae commencing at the onset of twilight, can be hypothesised to be a 
feeding migration. The majority of larvae found in the surface waters during spring are larger than 3 
==PAGE:58==
57 
 
mm in length which corresponds to a developmental stage where eyes and mouth are functional and 
exogenous feeding occurs (Kloppmann et al., 2001), while being small enough to be captured by the 
CPR (Richardson et al., 2006). Hillgruber and Kloppmann (2000), found the greatest feeding incidence 
of blue whiting larvae from two hours before sunset until one hour after sunset (i.e. from 18:00 to 
21:00 in the beginning of April 1995). Though, this does not explain the possible ascent of larvae in 
the period after sunrise, Bailey (1982) on the other hand affirmed that some larvae (esp. more 
developed ones) exhibit peak feeding intensities at dusk. Considering that the most common type of 
DVM in zooplankton, is the ascent from the depth of the ocean to the surface waters at the onset of 
night and their subsequent descent around sunrise (Cohen and Forward, 2002), while the peak in 
larval-presence probability around a solar elevation of 0° and 20° corresponds to the time shortly 
after twilight, the DVM of blue whiting larvae might represent a trade-off between the optimum 
amount of light for larvae to successfully prey on zooplankton and the period of highest zooplankton 
abundance during night. 
 
Moreover, the vertical migration of larvae towards the surface during twilight, as suggested by the 
Best Model, might mimic the DVM performed by adult blue whiting, which are believed to move into 
shallower waters of around 100 - 200 m at the onset of dusk and down again during dawn (Bailey, 
1982). The vertical extent of the larvae is most likely more constrained, however, due to inferior 
swimming abilities. Moreover, these results suggest a rather short planktonic stage of blue whiting 
larvae and it would be favourable to gather more knowledge, at which stage in their development or 
within which period of time larvae are in command of their own spatial position, as also put forward 
by Payne et al. (2012). Accordingly, more research on blue whiting’s behaviour and ontogeny could 
shed further light on these processes.  
PHOTOPERIOD 
Including the photoperiod (i.e. the hours from sunrise to sunset) experienced by the adult at the day 
of spawning (PP), improved all models within the initial set (Table II). Analysis carried out with the 
Best Model (Table III, Model E) showed that PP was in fact the most sensitive parameter, as indicated 
by its great permutation importance (Figure 14). As a result, when PP was randomly permuted 
among the recorded presences and absences of larvae within the CPR data set, the relative loss in 
AUC was greatest for PP compared to the other explanatory variables within the Best Model, 
indicating a heavy reliance of the model on PP. Therefore PP can be considered as the most sensitive 
and thus most important variable for explaining the presence-probability of blue whiting larvae 
within the CPR.  
==PAGE:59==
58 
 
Since the main cue triggering the onset of gonadal maturation in temperate fish species is thought to 
be the photoperiod (Neuheimer and MacKenzie, 2014), the beginning of spawning and subsequently 
the presence-probability of larvae captured by the CPR are likely to be mediated by the photoperiod 
experienced by the spawning adult, to some extend as well.  
 
 
All models that included PP, showed a broad peak in larval-presence probability, when the PP at the 
day of spawning was between 11 and 14 hours of daylight (Figure 13 a). When the photoperiod is 12 
hours, day and night are of equal length, which is termed equinox. For blue whiting, the model 
indicates the greatest probability spawning to be around the vernal equinox that is commonly 
between the 20 - 21 March. Since day length changes occur consistently in time over the latitudinal 
extent of the study region, from 45 °N to 63 °N  (Neuheimer and MacKenzie, 2014), a similar PP 
might trigger the onset of gonadal maturation within individuals throughout the study region. Also in 
Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) the change in day length, i.e. after the autumnal equinox, is considered 
to be the primary cue for gonadal development (Neuheimer and MacKenzie, 2014). 
 
Seeing that a PP between 11 and 14 hours represents the months between the end of February or 
beginning of March until mid April and peak larval-presences observed within the CPR occur one 
month later (Figure 9 d), the PP can thus be seen as a timing metric of spawning in blue whiting. 
Thereby it functions in a similar way, as the day of the year term that was included in models of blue 
whiting larvae presence-probabilities presented by Pointin and Payne (2014) to indicate the main 
spawning period.  
 
MAIN POTENTIAL SPAWNING REGIONS 
INFLUENCE OF WATER DEPTH  
The depth of the water had a clear effect on blue whiting larval-presence probability. In regions 
shallower than 300 m, increasing water depths increased the probability of encountering larvae 
(Figure 13 d). On the other hand, at water depths exceeding 300 m, the probability of encountering 
larvae remained at a constantly high level, indicating that the primary spawning niche of blue whiting 
encompasses regions with a depth greater that 300 m and suggesting that spawning primarily takes 
place in regions where the water is at least 300 m deep. This also corresponds to the depth range, 
where the majority of adult blue whiting are found, i.e. between 300 to 600 m, with spawning taking 
place beyond 200 m (Bailey, 1982; Hillgruber and Kloppmann, 1999; Munk and Nielsen, 2005; ICES, 
2014b). It has been hypothesised that blue whiting spawn at such great depths, since the predation 
risk of planktonic larvae during their early developmental stages might decrease with increasing 
water depth (Kloppmann et al., 2001).

SPAWNING ALONG BATHYMETRIC FEATURES 
Already during the initial investigation, when larval-presence was modelled simply as a function of 
longitude and mean temperature at spawning depth (in Data Exploration and Visualisation), a 
topographic effect was apparent, seeing that the longitudinal extend of the spawning distribution in 
Rockall Region was clearly delimited towards the east by the CS as indicated by early vertical isolines 
around 8 °W (Figure 12 b, d). Additionally, all predictions by the SDM showed a clear delineation 
along the CS north of 54 °N, with moderate to high probabilities of larval-presence west of the CS and 
generally low probabilities on the shallow CS (p < 0.3). Accordingly, the CS at latitudes above PB can 
be seen as an eastern boundary of blue whiting spawning. 
Averaging the larval-presence probability over the entire study period (i.e. from January to June 1951 
to 2005) depicts the principal spawning niches of blue whiting within the study region (Figure 16 a). 
The main centres of potential spawning activity can be found along the CS north of 55 °N and the 
region on and around RP, with the greatest probability east of RP. The CS region has been reported 
by many authors to be an important spawning ground of blue whiting (e.g. Bainbridge and Cooper 
1973; Bartsch and Coombs 1997; Kloppmann et al. 2001; Dransfeld et al. 2004). And also the RP has 
been identified as an important spawning ground, with a particular reference to the eastern border 
of RT from 1948 to 1956 (Henderson (1961), referenced within Hátún et al. 2009). Moreover, 
Bainbridge and Cooper (1973) reported the greatest intensities of spawning from 1948 to 1967 (as 
measured by the average number of larvae per sample within a defined rectangle) on RP and 
towards is eastern margin.  
 
Another important spawning ground of blue whiting, that was frequently cited, is the region over PB, 
west off Ireland (Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973; Bartsch and Coombs, 1997; Kloppmann et al., 2001; 
Dransfeld et al., 2004). Before 1993, blue whiting in this region, were managed as a separate (i.e. 
southern) stock, that was divided from the larger northern stock by PB (ICES, 2014a), as proposed by 
Bainbridge and Cooper (1973) and Bailey (1982). Though PB was reported as an important spawning 
ground by Bainbridge and Cooper (1973), their study also showed that the spawning intensity in this 
region was considerably lower compared to RP (around 4 times less, when comparing the two 
rectangles of highest spawning intensity).  
 
Oceanographic modelling (Bartsch and Coombs, 1997; Skogen et al., 1999) as well as observational 
studies (Hillgruber and Kloppmann, 2000), genetic investigations (Was et al., 2008) and a statistical 
model based on CPR presence/absence data (Pointin and Payne, 2014) suggest the separation of blue 
whiting populations over Porcupine Bank [i.e. at around 53 - 54 °N], that might be caused i.a. by 
opposing surface flow, separating larval drift patterns into northerly and southerly directions. 
Moreover the frequently occurring anti-cyclonic circulation, i.e. Taylor column, above PB might 
==PAGE:61==
60 
 
facilitate the existence of a self-sustaining spawning population due to its high retention potential 
(Hillgruber and Kloppmann, 1999; Kloppmann et al., 2001). This separation can, however, not be 
confirmed by this study, since the probability of spawning activity was of the same magnitude (0.4 < 
p ≤ 0.5) and continuously distributed, ranging from the northern extent of Rockall Trough (RT) onto 
PB when averaging over the whole time series (Figure 16 a), as well as during April (Figure 17 a).   
It has to be put forward, that inter-annual variability in ocean circulation can contribute to large 
changes in the latitude at which currents, that possibly cause the stock separation, diverge, 
potentially resulting in mixing of the two populations (Skogen et al., 1999). Moreover wind stress 
above PB, can cause the local retention system (Taylor column) to break down due to mixing 
processes (Kloppmann et al., 2001). These processes might be an explanation for the seemingly 
continuous distribution of moderate spawning activity, seeing that local processes that vary monthly 
or inter-annually are smoothened out by averaging over the entire time series.   
 
Accordingly, the stock hypothesis can neither be confirmed by the model nor denied and more 
investigations into that matter would be important to resolve this issue in order to facilitate the 
sustainable management of blue whiting. Better ways to resolve this issue by means of an SDM could 
include i) averaging predictions of shorter time periods (e.g. by a weekly, or bi-monthly resolution) 
with similar oceanographic conditions, in particular with respect to the prevailing current systems or 
ii) the coupling of numerical dispersion models of blue whiting larvae (i.e. along the lines of Bartsch 
and Coombs (1997) analysis) into the SDM.  
COMPARING POTENTIAL SPAWNING NICHES WITH FISHERIES SURVEYS 
The fisheries survey data of the North East Atlantic Fisheries Commission (NEAFC) showed the 
greatest amount of adult blue whiting at the southern tip of RP, stretching over RT onto the northern 
parts of PB (Figure 16 b). Additionally, large amounts were found along the CS above latitudes of 56 
°N. Since no catch data was available for the eastern part of RP, more recent acoustic survey data 
(IBWSS, IESNS) was added. These also imply spawning aggregations north of RP, with a greater 
biomass of blue whiting found above the northern parts of the CS. A great proportion of spawning 
adults was found at the southern tip of RP in the fisheries survey, which is in line with the model 
predictions, which indicated high larval-presence probability in this region. Overall, the predicted 
probabilities of blue whiting spawning aggregations reflect the survey data quite well. An exception is 
the high predicted probability eastern part of RP that is not reflected by the survey data, while the 
high amount of blue whiting caught along the northern slope of PB is not predicted as a region of 
high probability by this analysis. 
There are, however, several issues one has to be aware of, when comparing the predicted 
probabilities of larval occurrence as a proxy for their spawning niche or distribution with fisheries 
==PAGE:62==
61 
 
survey data. Since monthly fisheries survey data was only available from 1977 onwards and acoustic 
survey data from 1998 during the peak spawning period of blue whiting (IBWSS) and from onwards 
2000 during late spring (IESNS), while predictions were made from 1951 to 2005, the comparisons 
have to be viewed with caution and are not directly comparable.  
 
 Another issue when comparing the data is the fact, that blue whiting are highly migratory species, 
causing a rather large uncertainty in the correct geographic representation of the spawning adults by 
the survey data. Moreover, the fisheries survey data only show the survivors, while the SDM predicts 
the potential niche of blue whiting spawning based on presence-absence data of larvae. Since Larvae 
are more susceptible to predation pressure, changes in temperature and physical processes than 
adults (Houde, 2008), processes affecting larval survival will subsequently affect recruitment which is 
then captured by the fisheries surveys.   
Concluding, even though the fisheries survey data can be argued to depict the main regions of adult 
(spawning) blue whiting to some extent, comparison of fisheries survey data with the potential 
spawning niche of blue whiting larvae is limited and therefore comparisons might be inconclusive. 
However, there is no other recorded data on blue whiting spawning distributions available, so the 
comparisons of the model predictions with the survey data will have to suffice.  
 
TEMPORAL PROGRESSION OF SPAWNING  
In a further analysis, predicted probabilities of blue whiting larval occurrence were averaged for each 
month over the entire time series, thereby enabling a monthly comparison of the main potential 
spawning niches of blue whiting (Figure 17). Overall, a northward progression of larval-presence 
probability from March to June was observed, suggesting a northward progression of spawning. This 
is in line with the findings that spawning of blue whiting commences in January its southern 
distribution range, namely the Bay of Biscay and advances northwards into Scottish and Norwegian 
waters where it occurs around May and early June (Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973; Bailey, 1982).   
Since the accuracy of model predictions for February was rather low and not useful in discriminating 
between presences and absences (Figure 15) as judged by the AUC < 0.75 (Ferrier et al., 2006), the 
small probability of larval-presence around 51 °N within Rockall Trough in February might be 
misleading. Moreover, Bainbridge and Cooper (1973) reported the first occurrence of blue whiting 
larvae in February further south along the CS off the English Channel. 
Larval-presence in March is generally low with probabilities of larval occurrence (0.04 < p ≤ 0.07) 
within RT south of RP and south of PB within Porcupine Seabright (PS, Figure 17). Since spawning was 
==PAGE:63==
62 
 
assumed to take place three weeks before a CPR observation was made, the niche requirements for 
spawning will be met three weeks prior, indicating low spawning activity during February in this 
region. Considering that blue whiting spawning likely commences in mid March within the study 
region (Bailey, 1982) while the probability was averaged over the entire month, this might cause the 
low probability. Another statistical model based on the same data, but with a substantially different 
structure, also showed a peak in larval-presence at latitudes of around 50 °N from mid March 
extending northwards towards 53 °N by the beginning of April (Pointin and Payne, 2014) which 
corresponds to the regions of highest larval-presence probability (0.04 < p ≤ 0.07) in March this 
study. Moreover, the model predictions, are supported by Bainbridge and Cooper's (1973) CPR data 
analysis that ranged from 1948 to 1967. In this study, two centres of spawning activity were 
reported: one in the Celtic Sea at latitudes of around 48 °N, and another one to the south of RP 
commencing from 53 °N. In consequence, despite of the low probabilities in March, the region within 
RT south of RP and south of PB are noteworthy, since they might represent the regions where 
spawning commences towards mid or end March. 
 
The single location of high larval-presence probability in March within the English Channel is probably 
a misidentifications or error in data entry seeing that the presence of blue whiting has not been 
recorded here previously as proposed by Pointin and Payne (2014). 
The greatest larval-presence probability is predicted during April and May (Figure 17), which 
corresponds to the months of highest larval-presence recorded by the CPR (Figure 9 b) and suggests 
the main potential niche of blue whiting spawning between March and April. This is in line with other 
studies who identified the peak spawning months of blue whiting from the end of March/early April 
(Kloppmann et al., 2001) or mid March to early May (Bailey, 1982).  
During April the main larval-presence probability is predicted on RP and to its west, south and 
especially eastern edge. Moreover a centre of high probability is found at latitudes north of 55 °N 
along the CS. One month later, during May, the centres have moved northwards by around 2 °. These 
regions have also been identified previously as the main spawning regions of blue whiting (Bailey, 
1982; ICES, 2014a) and also the northward progression of potential spawning regions is in 
accordance with past research (Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973; Bailey, 1982). Moreover, this result is 
confirmed by the modelling study performed by Pointin and Payne (2014; Figure 8), that suggested 
that the main spawning activity (i.e. larval-presence) is located northward of 55 °N with a peak in 
spawning around latitudes of 57 °N during April while peaking further north during the beginning of 
May. Pointin and Payne's (2014) model predictions moreover indicate that the region of moderate 
spawning probability is more contracted in May compared to April. The study region in this analysis, 
might however be too small to depict the entire extent of the regions of moderate spawning 
==PAGE:64==
63 
 
probability, thereby not depicting potential spawning habitats further north along the CS and west of 
RP.  
 
During June the highest larval-presence probability is found above latitudes of 56 °N. Blue whiting 
have possibly migrated out of the study region by May, because spawning is thought to occur in 
Norwegian waters from early June onwards (Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973; Bailey, 1982) since niche 
suitability is potentially low within the region during June.   
Pointin and Payne (2014) proposed the presence of two populations based on a clear temporal and 
spatial separation of the two main centres of spawning within RP and PB in March and April. The 
temporal resolution of this analysis, i.e. the use of monthly averaged data for prediction, might have 
been too coarse to reflect these patterns. Therefore the use of a data set of finer temporal 
resolution, e.g. weekly, of the environmental parameters used in the SDM, could largely improve the 
analysis of potential spawning habitats particularly during the onset of spawning in blue whiting in 
March. 
 
SST AND THE CPR’S SAMPLING EFFICIENCY 
The second most sensitive variable, as judged by the loss in AUC when each variable was randomly 
permuted, was sea surface temperature (SST, Figure 14). SST was included in the SDM to account for 
the presumed decreasing in sampling efficiency as the growth rate of larvae increases in response to 
increasing SST. Accordingly, it was expected to see a monotonically decreasing term with lower larval 
observation probability at higher SST. Nevertheless, SST in the SDM functioned in a different way, 
seeing that there was a peak in blue whiting presence probability at SST around 10 °C, with an 
increasing probability from around 8 to 10 °C (Figure 13 c). This corresponds to the same 
temperature range during spawning time and at spawning depth where the greatest probability of 
larval-presence is found (Figure 13 e). In winter when the water column is well mixed, SST and deep 
water temperature will be similar. This suggests that SST functions in a similar way to the deep water 
temperature. In another analysis, not shown in the results section, the interaction of S.spawn x 
T.spawn, was replaced by S.deep x T.deep. This yielded very similar smooth functions as observed for 
the Best Model (see Annex, Figure 22) accordingly the average temperature at spawning depth at the 
time of a CPR observation, one month prior to an observation as well as the SST function in a similar 
way.  
In the initial model set the larval-presence probability was solely modelled as a function of SST 
(Annex, Table IV). Since two data sets of SST were available, one model contained had.SST derived 
==PAGE:65==
64 
 
from SST measurements (in-situ and satellite observations) compiled in HadISST1 (had.SST, Rayner et 
al. 2003), while in another model SST was derived from the Max Planck Institute Ocean Model (MPI-
OM; Matei et al., 2012). It was assumed that had.SST and SST were interchangeable, nevertheless the 
model containing had.SST as explanatory variable performed considerably better than the one 
containing MPI-OM SST data, with an ΔAIC of 310 when comparing these two models (Annex, Table 
IV). For this reason had.SST was chosen for one-dimensional smoothers in the SDM set (Table II and 
III). Moreover, Richardson et al. (2006) suggested the use of had.SST in combination with CPR data. 
One possible reason for the difference in the two SST data sets is that the SST data derived from the 
MPI-OM, actually corresponds to the temperature at 6 m depth (since no data from shallower water 
depths was available), while had.SST originates from observations at the sea surface. It is more likely 
however, that the MPI-OM has limitations when modelling temperatures in the upper water layer, 
and is not adequately capturing the variability in this region. Therefore, a comparison of MPI-OM 
data with alternative data sources or and its improvement is recommended. Accordingly, further 
investigations are needed to improve the temperature and salinity data used in the SDM of blue 
whiting spawning distributions.  
 
Seeing that had.SST was not related to the CPR’s sampling efficiency at low temperatures, while the 
addition of had.SST as a one dimensional smoother increased each model’s performance 
considerably (see e.g. Annex Table IV) and was rated as the second most sensitive variable in the 
SDM (Figure 14), including had.SST might correct for the bias of the MPI-OM in the model.  
 
The high sensitivity of had.SST coupled with the finding that the hypothesized decreases in larval-
presence probability with increasing SST was not observed and had.SST functioned similar to the 
temperature in the deep water, suggest dropping the SST term altogether.  
 
EFFECT OF TEMPERATURE AND SALINITY ON BLUE WHITING SPAWNING DISTRIBUTIONS 
PREFERRED SPAWNING TEMPERATURE AND SALINITY  
The analysis of the different models revealed that the interaction between temperature and salinity 
at the spawning depth of blue whiting is important for explaining the presence-probability of blue 
whiting larvae (Table II, Annex Table IV). In particular the interaction between temperature and 
salinity experienced by the spawning adult (te(T.spawn, S.spawn)) was identified as an important 
explanatory variable, suggesting that adults choose certain temperature and salinity conditions for 
spawning.  
 
The SDM indicates that blue whiting prefer to spawn at temperatures between 9 and 10 °C and a 
salinity of around 35.5 (Figure 13 e). This in agreement with Hátún et al. (2009), who found the 
==PAGE:66==
65 
 
preferred spawning temperature and salinity (at 300 m depth northward of 50.5 °N along the CS) to 
be between, 9 - 10 °C and 35.35 - 35.45, respectively. The potential spawning niche was however not 
constricted to this narrow temperatures and salinity range, seeing that larval-presence probabilities > 
0.1 were found fairly linearly distributed between the maximum and minimum temperatures and 
salinities reported over the time period, i.e. from waters of rather high salinity and temperature to 
waters with lower salinity and temperature (Figure 13 e).  
POTENTIAL SPAWNING REGIONS UNDER A DIFFERENT MARINE CLIMATE 
 
CHARACTERISING THE TWO MARINE REGIMES 
Large changes in the oceans temperature and salinity characteristics have occurred in the study 
region over the study period (Figure 19). These changes in the marine climate, have been attributed 
to changes in the ocean circulation, in particular to changes in the North Atlantic sub-polar gyre (SPG) 
which in turn controls the path of the North Atlantic Current (NAC) in the northeastern Atlantic 
(Hátún et al., 2009). When the gyre is strong, the NAC flows through RT east of RP, spreading cold 
and low-saline subarctic water masses east over RP , whereas in years when the gyre is weak and 
shifts westwards, warm and saline subtropical water flows through RT and over RP (Figure 2). For 
that reason, besides characterising the region by the strength of the SPG, the marine climate within 
the ER can also be characterized by the regions prevalent temperature and salinity characteristics. 
During most of the study period the region was characterised by one of the two distinct regimes, 
with the water at blue whiting’s spawning depth being typically either warmer and more saline, or 
fresher and colder than on average (Figure 19 c). These two regimes correspond to the prevalence of 
water masses of distinct origin within the region: water masses of subtropical origin create saline and 
warm conditions while water masses of Atlantic origin are colder and less saline (Tomczak and 
Godfrey, 2005). 
When comparing predictions of the potential spawning niches for the years of low and weak SPG as 
defined by Hátún et al. (2009) however, the regions of high spawning probability were located solely 
on RP during weak SPG years and a northward shift and latitudinal contraction was solely observed 
over PB, where moderate probability was predicted under a strong SPG (Figure 18 a) while low 
probability was observed under a weak SPG (Figure 18 c).  
 
The slight difference between the regions of high predicted probabilities when comparing the two 
regimes (Figure 20) with the conditions under a weak or strong SPG (Figure 18) might be the 
dissimilarity in the definition of the cold and fresh regime compared to the strong SPG years. Half of 
the years classified as strong SPG years by Hátún et al. (2009) depicted in Figure 18 a), did not 
correspond to the years classified as fresh and cold (Figure 19 c). Additionally the short time period 
==PAGE:67==
66 
 
of 10 years used for prediction during the weak SPG years (in Figure 18 c), comprised of years that 
were exceptionally warm and saline compared to the whole time series of 54 years (See Figure 19 a, 
c).  
The most prominent difference between the two regimes is that under warm and saline conditions, 
the potential spawning region is smaller with a notably more contracted latitudinal extend compared 
the distribution under a colder and less saline regime (Figure 20). This also became apparent when 
comparing strong and weak SPG years (Figure 18). Interestingly, also the simple latitudinal analysis of 
the CS region (Table I, Model a, c) pointed towards a latitudinal contraction of the spawning region at 
temperatures higher than the average (11.14 ± 0.25 °C) within the ER (Figure 12 a, c). Moreover 
when the water is warmer and more saline than average during April, the regions of high spawning 
probability are predicted to be spatially more scattered (Figure 20). Studies to confirm these results 
however, are lacking. 
POTENTIAL SPAWNING REGIONS AROUND RP 
It has been hypothesised that under warm and saline conditions, as associated with a weak SPG, blue 
whiting’s spawning distribution spreads westwards over RP (Hátún et al., 2009). This can however 
not be confirmed by this analysis, in fact the regions of highest spawning probability had a slightly 
further westward extend during cold and fresh conditions (Figure 18, 20). Also the longitudinal 
analysis of RP region (Table I, Model b, c) showed a greater westward extent of the spawning 
distribution as mean temperatures at spawning depth during the main spawning period within ER 
were lower than average (11.14 ± 0.25 °C, Figure 12 c).  
A possible reason for the inconsistency with Hátún et al.'s (2009) analysis, is the handling of the CPR 
data. While this analysis is based on presence and absence of larvae within the entire study region, 
Hátún et al. (2009) chose a cross-section from the CS to RP in which the abundances of larval 
observations were transformed onto a common linear axis and weighted according to their distance 
from the axis. Subsequently the larvae counts were normalized by the annual mean abundance 
found along the axis (Hátún et al., 2009). Using abundance data obtained from a CPR can be 
problematic due to several reasons, which were described in the section on the “Representativeness 
of CPR Data”, such as the CPR’s low sampling volume, the patchiness of larval distributions as well as 
the use of abundance categories, which can cause biases toward exceptional occurrences of large 
larval abundances. Moreover the abundance of larvae in the surface waters do not truly reflect the 
abundance of larvae in the water column below, while the presence of larvae in the surface is in 
greater accordance with the observed presence of larvae in the water column beneath, as suggested 
by sampling data in Kloppmann et al. (2001, Figure 12). Accordingly, the use of larval counts from 
CPR data as a basis for modelling will emphasise regions with higher abundance, while understating 
==PAGE:68==
67 
 
regions of low larval abundance on the surface. Using presence-absence data however reduces bias 
caused by the limitations of the sampling gear. 
POTENTIAL SPAWNING REGIONS UNDER THE TWO REGIMES 
The potential spawning habitat of blue whiting is predicted to shift northwards along the continental 
slope, during warm and saline conditions in the main spawning period compared to colder and 
fresher conditions (Figure 20). This is supported by the results of teh simple latitudinal analysis of the 
CS region (Table I, Model a, b), which indicated northward shift to latitudes of around 57 °N as 
temperature increased above the mean (Figure 12 c). This result is also in line with Bainbridge and 
Cooper (1973), who reported a southerly shift in spawning distribution with decreasing (mean 
March-April sea surface) temperatures from the rather warm 1950s to the colder 1960s, that was 
also suggested by the latitudinal analysis of the CS region in this study (Figure 11). Moreover these 
results are in accordance with Hátún et al. (2009), who found a northward shift in blue whiting 
spawning aggregations along the CS to around latitudes of 58 °N west of Hebrides under a weak SPG, 
when the water is generally warm and saline.  
 
In detail, Hátún et al. (2009) hypothesised that the main spawning regions during strong SPG years 
are located along the CS and around PB and Porcupine Seabright (PS), while during a weak SPG 
spawning is located over RP and further north along the CS to the west of the outer Hebrides (at ca. 
58 °N). This can be confirmed by the results of this analysis in particular, when looking at the map of 
probability difference in April, which shows that the probability of encountering blue whiting larvae 
around PB and PS is much greater under cold and fresh conditions, while the probability is higher for 
under warm and saline conditions (Figure 20).   
Seeing that Hátún et al. (2009) used the estimated biomass of the spawning stock biomass of blue 
whiting obtained from acoustic surveys for predictions of the potential spawning niche along the CS 
and a completely different model structure compared to this SDM, which modelled the presence 
probability of blue whiting larvae based on CPR presence-absence data as a proxy for their spawning 
distribution, indicates that the SPG indeed affects the spawning distribution of blue whiting. 
In spite of the northward shift in spawning and spatial contraction of the potential spawning 
distribution during warmer and more saline conditions, the geographic location of the regions of 
highest probability at the eastern edge of RP in April, and on RP during May as well as along the CS 
west of the Outer Hebrides, remained fairly constant (Figure 20). These might represent regions 
where the niche requirements of blue whiting spawning are met during both regimes. A possible 
reason might be that the effect of the different regimes is rather subtle in these regions, as indicated 
by salinity changes of around 0.05 when comparing the two regimes (Annex, Figure 24).

BLUE WHITING ABUNDANCE AND RECRUITMENT UNDER THE TWO REGIMES AND LIMITATIONS OF THE SDM   
Comparisons of the predicted probabilities and the fisheries catch data are limited during strong SPG 
years due to low data coverage, in particular around RP (Figure 18 b). During weak SPG years, greater 
catches were obtained in the region south of RP and along RT towards PB (by NEAFC) while a 
substantial biomass was also found along the CS north of 56 °N (by IBWSS and IESNS, Figure 18 d) 
while predictions for this time period attribute the greatest probability of spawning on and east of RP 
with a small region of high probability towards the southern tip of RP (Figure 18 c). However as 
explained previously, there are several limitations to comparing the mapped potential niche of 
spawning, i.e. the presence-probability of larvae, to the observed or modelled distribution of 
spawning adults. Even though, the predicted niche of suitable blue whiting spawning habitat is 
predicted to be smaller under warm and saline conditions, these conditions have been hypothesised 
to improve recruitment success in blue whiting (Payne et al., 2012) and are accordingly associated 
with a greater SSB when comparing the past 25 years (Hátún et al., 2009), which highlights that a 
great potential spawning habitat does not necessarily correspond to greater recruitment success.  
It has to be noted, that the relationship between the larval-presence that is used as a basis for 
modelling the potential niche of blue whiting spawning and the actual spawning distribution might 
not always hold. The potential niche is possibly larger than the niche where blue whiting larvae are 
observed, i.e. their realized niche, since not all factors that determine the niche of blue whiting 
spawning have been included in the SDM, as one can never find all factors that determine a species 
niche (Wiens et al., 2009) due to the inherent complexity of nature.  
 
For instance, water-mass exchanges do not merely change the ocean’s chemical and physical 
features such as salinity, temperature or the current systems, but also affect the region’s biological 
properties, by changing the regions phyto- and zooplankton composition and concentration (Hátún 
et al., 2009) and thereby the food conditions for blue whiting. While the copepod Calanus 
finmarchicus is commonly associated with cold and fresh Atlantic water masses (Nielsen et al., 2014) 
and is abundant under a strong SPG, the warm-water species Pseudocalanus spp., Acartia spp. and 
Oithona spp. are more dominant warmer and more saline conditions, i.e. under a weak gyre (Payne 
et al., 2012). Moreover, it has been hypothesised that the weak circulation in the area under a weak 
SPG, creates retention areas above RP which might create more favourable food and growth 
conditions for blue whiting larvae, seeing that larger calanoid nauplii create a higher energetic gain 
for blue whiting larvae (Hillgruber and Kloppmann, 1999; Hátún et al., 2009). Consequently it has 
been suggested, that blue whiting concentrate where food is abundant (Bailey, 1982; Hillgruber and 
Kloppmann, 1999), seeing that the location of the peak in blue whiting larvae has corresponded to 
the location of the highest copepod density in the past (Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973). Since 
==PAGE:70==
69 
 
nutrition affects  the fish’s physiological condition, growth rate and thereby stage duration of larvae, 
it also affects the juveniles susceptibility to predation and thereby affects recruitment success 
(Houde, 2008). These processes might thus also be important for the survival of early life stages of 
blue whiting, as hypothesised by Payne et al. (2012), too. 
 
 
Another effect of the warm and saline water masses on blue whiting larvae, is that it increases their 
growth rate (Bailey and Heath, 2001). Larval otolith growth histories indicated, that blue whiting 
distributed in warm and saline water masses along the southern CS edge had significantly greater 
growth rates than larvae originating from cold and fresh water masses at lower latitudes (Bailey and 
Heath, 2001), which might reflect their difference in food conditions as well as temperature (Payne 
et al., 2012). Since colder water implies a longer stage duration, it increase the risk of predation 
(Houde, 2008) possibly contributing to the lower recruitment found under cooler and fresher 
conditions. Consequently there are a multitude of different environmental factors and many of them 
interconnected, so that one cannot directly infer population dynamics from the distribution of the 
potential spawning niche of blue whiting and comparisons between potentially spawning niches and 
observed distributions of spawning adults are have to be viewed with care. 
On the other hand, the geographical range of blue whiting larvae could for example be constrained 
by species interactions that were not included in the model, which would also result in an 
overestimation of the potential spawning niche. Most notably is the predation by mackerel on blue 
whiting, since mackerel may feed on blue whiting eggs, larvae as well as juvenile blue whiting (Payne 
et al., 2012). It has been hypothesised that biotic interactions can even have a greater effect on 
species distributions than climate (Wiens et al., 2009). Accordingly, one might compare SDMs for 
mackerel with those of blue whiting to explore potential interactions with respect to their 
distribution. 
 
FURTHER MODEL IMPROVEMENTS 
Besides the previously mentioned ideas for improvements of the SDM, such as the improvement of 
the temperature and salinity data (or the ocean model), the use of environmental data with a finer 
temporal resolution or dropping the one-dimensional smoother of SST, several measures could 
possibly enhance the SDM of blue whiting spawning distribution that was presented here. 
 
The temperature and salinity at the spawning depth during the spawning time of blue whiting 
(T.spawn and S.spawn) were defined as the temperature and salinity one month prior to a CPR 
observation. This was mainly based on the hypothesis that larvae captured by the CPR were spawned 
three weeks before, due to the average size of larvae found in the surface waters indicating their age 
==PAGE:71==
70 
 
and their average time needed for ascending to the surface (Bailey and Heath, 1996; Ådlandsvik et 
al., 2001). Using a monthly resolution was based on available data of the MPI-OM’s that was 
provided on a monthly resolution. Accordingly, a finer temporal resolution, would improve model by 
defining T.spawn and S.spawn, as the temperature and salinity at the spawning depth during the 
actual spawning time of blue whiting, i.e. three weeks before a CPR observation. 
The time period of ascent for a larvae from the spawning depth to the surface waters was, based on  
findings from model simulations that larvae rise to the surface within 20 to 25 days at a neutral 
salinity of 35, which approximated the observed vertical distributions of larvae in the field 
(Ådlandsvik et al., 2001). Accordingly, the time needed for a larva to arrive in the surface waters is a 
function of its buoyancy which is largely determined by the ambient salinity. This buoyancy effect 
could be included in the SDM, by including a one dimensional smoother of the average salinity of the 
water column from the surface to the maximum spawning depth integrated over three weeks 
preceding a CPR observation, iff this leads to an increase in model skill. Another way would be to let 
the spawning period, i.e. the number of days prior to a CPR observations representing the days from 
the day an egg is spawned to the ascent of the hatched larvae on the surface, vary in accordance 
with the average salinity during a defined time period prior to a CPR observation. For example as 
salinities increase above 35, a larvae is assumed to ascent faster, thereby the day at which T.spawn 
and S.spawn are defined will be earlier, compared to lower salinities.   
Seeing that the model indicates that spawning primarily takes place in regions with water depths of 
at least 300 m, this suggests that the majority of eggs are spawned below 300 m. Accordingly, the 
temperature and salinity at spawning depth might be adjusted by averaging over 300 m to 600 m 
depth. 
The northern North Atlantic is an extremely windy region (Holt et al., 2014) and larvae can be 
influenced by this wind-induced turbulence in a variety of ways, for example by mediating encounter 
rates and capture success of larvae which peak at intermediate turbulence levels (Hillgruber and 
Kloppmann, 1999; Houde, 2002). The mean wind stress could for example be used as an indicator for 
the mixed layer depth (Holt et al., 2014) and included into the SDM. When the wind stress is high, 
the mixed layer depth will deepen over time, so that larvae are hypothesised to be carried from the 
surface waters into the euphotic zone, where food conditions are less favourable. Turbulent 
conditions could moreover limit the larvae’s ability to reach the illuminated surface waters, which in 
turn could increase the risk of starvation. Additionally, early planktonic larval-stages from deeper 
water layers may be transported upwards by means of deep mixing processes, which might increase 
their risk of being preyed upon.  
Additionally, it has been shown that the retention system, i.e. Taylor column, above PB that provides 
==PAGE:72==
71 
 
larvae with improved food conditions, breaks down wind stress above PB is strong (Kloppmann et al., 
2001). Thereby, wind stress can influence larvae directly via survival-related processes and indirectly 
via changes in turbulence and retention systems. The addition of wind stress as an explanatory 
variable in the SDM, would thereby reflect the potential decrease in larval-presence probability 
under windy conditions in the spawning region. Possible sources of wind stress are high-resolution 
ocean models such as the physical ocean model (Nucleus of a European Model for the Ocean, NEMO) 
with a 1/12° latitudinal and longitudinal resolution (Holt et al., 2014).  
Another means, by which the model could be improved, involves the addition of the population size 
of blue whiting as a one-dimensional smoother. A recent study by Falcy (2015) highlighted the 
importance to study density-dependent effects on habitat selection in fish. A proposed mechanism is 
that early-arriving or dominant individuals could exclude others from superior spawning grounds, so 
that increases in the population size could lead to shifts in the peak locations of spawning (Falcy, 
2015). Moreover, larger population sizes could decrease the survival of blue whiting larvae due to 
competition (Houde, 2008). Accordingly, density-dependent processes could alter the larval-presence 
probability and the spatial distribution of the potential spawning niche of blue whiting. Thereforen, 
the inclusion of blue whiting’s population size as an explanatory variable in the SDM should be 
investigated. 
 
IMPLICATIONS FOR BLUE WHITING MONITORING AND MANAGEMENT 
The SDM has shown that the distribution of potential regions of blue whiting spawning change 
seasonally and in response to changes in the ambient temperature and salinity. This knowledge could 
be used in a forecasting sense to design survey routes of blue whiting spawning regions that 
encompass the entire range of potential spawning habitats. 
 
The International Blue Whiting Spawning Stock Survey (IBWSS) usually takes place during two weeks 
from the end of March to the beginning of April (WGIPS and WGWDS, 2014). It typically initiates 
sampling in the southern distribution range of blue whiting and then progresses northward (WGIPS 
and WGWDS, 2014), reflecting the northwards progression of spawning in blue whiting to some 
extent. Nevertheless, two weeks of sampling might be too short, seeing that blue whiting have a 
protracted spawning period between 6 weeks to two months (Loots et al., 2011).  
 
Moreover, when comparing the main regions of potential blue whiting spawning with the sampling 
region of the IBWSS, several limitations of the survey design become apparent.  While the IBWSS 
generally has a good coverage of the CS region covering the region from PB towards latitudes of 
around 62 °N, the region around RP is neglected in the acoustic survey in 2012 and 2013 (Figure 21). 
==PAGE:73==
72 
 
That these years are no exception, becomes apparent in Hátún et al.'s (2009; Figure 4 a) study 
depicting a high coverage of the acoustic survey from 1981 to 2007 along the CS, but a comparably 
low coverage around RP. 
 
The model predictions, however, show a rather large potential spawning habitat of blue whiting with 
high probability (p > 0.5) to the east of RP (between latitudes of 55 °N and 60 °N) reaching into RT 
until around 11 °W (Figure 21). This is especially pronounced in periods where the SPG is strong 
(Figure 18 a), i.e. when the water is fresher and colder than average (Figure 20). The acoustic surveys 
carried out in 2012 and 2013, did not encompass this potentially important spawning region (Figure 
21). Moreover in 2013, the survey commenced at the west of RP and then moved southward to PB 
before continuing sampling northward along the CS (WGIPS and WGWDS, 2013; Figure 3). Since most 
studies (Bainbridge and Cooper, 1973; Bailey, 1982; Pointin and Payne, 2014) point towards the 
onset of spawning commencing further south around PB, the survey might have sampled RP region 
too early.   
 
In 2014 the survey design was improved by encompassing RP region as well as the CS region from PB 
at 53° N and progressed northward, reaching latitudes of 61 °N within 10 to 13 days (WGIPS and 
WGWDS, 2014; Figure 3). Nevertheless, the cruise track was much narrower along the CS compared 
to RP and these two survey regions were apart by 1 ° longitude. I therefore suggest including the 
entire RP region in the IBWSS survey with the same sampling coverage as applied in the CS region in 
order to gain a more comprehensive picture of the spawning distribution of blue whiting.

CONCLUSION 
The species distribution model of blue whiting spawning indicated that the photoperiod experienced 
by blue whiting during spawning, is the most sensitive variable in explaining the presence probability 
of blue whiting larvae. The model also indicated that gonadal maturation commences from the 
vernal equinox onwards in blue whiting and that the potential spawning habitat progresses 
northward from March to June. Moreover, the model suggested that spawning primarily takes place 
in regions with water depths of at least 300 m.  Seeing that the probability to sample larvae in the 
surface waters was predicted to be highest around twilight, in particular during the period shortly 
after sunrise and before sunset, it is hypothesised that blue whiting larvae perform diel vertical 
migrations. 
 
Additionally, the temperature and salinity experienced by the spawning adults is an important 
variable in explaining the presence-probability of blue whiting larvae and thus in delineating the 
potential niche of blue whiting’s spawning distribution. Overall, the spawning distribution is 
predicted to be smaller and located further north under warm and saline conditions, while it has a 
larger distribution and extends furthest southward when the conditions are fresh and cold, which is 
the case under a strong North Atlantic sub-polar gyre (SPG). During the principal spawning month 
April, Rockall Plateau was identified as the main potential spawning region under warm and saline 
conditions while Porcupine Bank and the Continental Shelf were dominant potential spawning 
regions during cold and fresh conditions. This result is in accordance with previous studies and 
indicates that the SPG has an effect on the spawning distribution of blue whiting, as put forward by 
Hátún et al. (2009). 
 
Two regions, were however characterised by a constantly high probability of providing suitable 
conditions for blue whiting spawning, and were not greatly affected by changes in the marine 
climate: Rockall Plateau and the region along the CS to the west of the Outer Hebrides.  
On the basis of these results, an extension of the International Blue Whiting Spawning Stock Survey 
(IBWSS) is proposed, in order to encompass Rockall Plateau region in more detail and thereby 
provide a more comprehensive picture of the spawning stock of blue whiting.  
Overall, the model results are in agreement with previous studies on blue whiting spawning 
distributions and larval behaviour; however the model might be enhanced by means of applying 
improved, modelled temperature and salinity data for model calibration and predictions that better 
reflect the actual temperatures experienced by blue whiting and provide a finer temporal resolution.

Note that when the SPG is weak, there is a considerably higher salinity around RP, in particular north-west of 
RP and within RT at the southern tip of RP, while under a strong SPG regions of high salinity are found below 
latitudes of 52 °N within RT. This corresponds to the described changes in the NAC in response to changes in 
the SPG. When SPG is strong, the NAC spreads cold and low-saline subarctic water masses east over the Rockall 
Plateau (RP) region, whereas in years when the gyre is weak, it shifts westwards causing warm and saline 
subtropical water to flow through Rockall Trough (RT) and over RP (Hátún et al., 2009). Accordingly, it has also 
been hypothesised that the SPG intervenes with blue whiting spawning aggregations (Hátún et al., 2009).
