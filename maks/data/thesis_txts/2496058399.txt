Abstract
In this work, we demonstrate a new single-step method based on co-extrusion process
to produce multilayered optical fiber preforms of 7-10 mm diameter. We report the
development of an all-polymer based preform consisting of two different grades of
ZEONEX material (grade 480R and E48R). These engineered thermoplastics have
excellent optical as well as mechanical properties and low moisture absorption be-
low 0.01% which make these suitable candidates towards low-loss polymer optical
fibers. In addition these two thermoplastics have matching thermal properties such
as the glass transition temperatures of ZEONEX grade 480R at 142.9 oC, and grade
E48R at 142.7 oC, and their viscosity-temperature curves which are measured using
a rheometer. In addition high-temperature thermoplastics such as PSU and PES are
demonstrated to have better mechanical- and thermal properties, which can be incor-
porated in a multimaterial fiber. The work also aims to optimize and determine the
extrusion parameters necessary for a stable material flow through a given die geom-
etry to reduce distortions like die swell and tapering. At the last step, the extruded
preform are fiber drawn into a robust multimaterial step-index fiber constituted high
temperature outer cladding such as PSU. Finally, the performance of the multima-
terial fiber (ZEONEX-PSU) in high-temperature environments are investigated in
comparison with an all-ZEONEX fiber by thermal stability measurements.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Optical fibers are made of multiple materials or a single material consisting of a
core and a cladding. Essentially the refractive index of the core is slightly higher
than the refractive index of the cladding, so that light can propagate through the
optical fiber by total internal reflection [1]. To achieve such an optical fiber, different
methods are needed to produce a fiber preform containing the core and cladding at a
macroscopic larger-scale, typically millimeters in diameters. The preform can then be
thermally drawn to achieve the final optical fiber geometry typically with diameters
of 100-200 µm. Usually, the most common material used to fabricate single-mode or
multi-mode fibers is silica glass. Low transmission losses are achieved with a single-
mode silica glass fiber of 0.2 dB/km at the telecommunication wavelengths 1550 nm
[2]. Apart from silica glass, other materials such as various polymers or soft-glasses
can be proposed to form an optical fiber with multiple functionalities.
The advantages of polymers are their flexibility since they are less brittle than
glass, easier to handle and cheaper to produce than glasses.
Such polymer opti-
cal fibers (POFs) based on conventional polymers such as polymethyl methacrylate
(PMMA) [3] and polycarbonate (PC) [4] have been demonstrated as both core and
cladding material. Although the transmission losses of these fibers are much higher
than conventional silica glass fibers, they can be used in short distance applications
requiring less space. Other polymer materials with excellent mechanical, thermal and
optical properties can be incorporated to form a POF. These include high-performance
thermoplastics such as cyclo-olefin polymers TOPAS and ZEONEX. This class of
polymers have better chemical resistance to bases and acids and many other solvents
compared to conventional PMMA [5]. ZEONEX has great mechanical- as well as
optical properties, very low moisture absorption below 0.01% compared to PMMA
and better heat resistance than conventional PMMA [5], due to its higher glass transi-
tion temperature. The low moisture absorption enables an optical fiber consisting of
ZEONEX to withstand high humidity environments. A microstructured POF based
on ZEONEX has been demonstrated before for high temperature and humidity envi-
ronments [6].
Soft-glasses such as chalcogenide glasses have many unique properties which can
be incorporated into an optical fiber. Chalcogenide glasses transmit to much longer
wavelengths than silica and have much lower glass transition temperatures than con-
ventional silica fibers [7], which means they can be fiber drawn at much lower temper-
atures. These glasses have various potential applications in medical areas, military

areas, non-linear optics, sensing and more. Examples of potential applications are
in the short distance area such as remote temperature monitoring, thermal imaging,
chemical sensing and power delivery of intense IR laser emission [8].
Recently, a
popular field of research have been on supercontinuum (SC) generation in the mid-
infrared range based on chalcogenide glass fibers for applications in optical coherence
tomography (OCT) [9], optical sensing and absorption spectroscopy [10], [11].
The distinct properties of each different material type can be incorporated into
a single fiber, called multimaterial fibers.
Multimaterial fibers are a new class of
fibers, which aims to develop new structures with multiple materials with different
unique functionalities. For instance materials with different optical, mechanical, elec-
tronic and thermal properties can be incorporated into a single fiber. One example
is combining soft-glasses and polymers to form a robust mid-infrared multimaterial
fiber with increased mechanical and optical properties [12]. Multimaterial fibers com-
bining polymers, metals and semiconductors as electronic and optoelectronic devices
have been presented as well [13].
The most traditional technique used to fabricate silica-glass preforms is Modified
Chemical Vapor Deposition (MCVD). This method allows the deposition of the core
material and cladding material layer by layer e.g. silica doped with germanium to
increase the refractive index of the core [14]. However for polymers and soft-glasses
this method is not suitable and efficient.
Here we present a fabrication method, a so called extrusion technique which is
considered one of the most well-known and versatile methods to fabricate optical
fiber preforms consisting of polymers and soft-glasses. In particular, a co-extrusion
process based on multiple stacked bulk materials heated to their softening tempera-
tures and pushed through a die that dictates the shape, provides a single-step process
to fabricate preform structures with multiple materials, minimizing post-processing
requirements. For instance co-extrusion of robust chalcogenide glasses fiber preforms
have been demonstrated throughout the years [15], [16], [17], [18], [19]. Understanding
the extrusion process parameters and die geometry has been investigated in different
works due to distortion effects such as die swell and tapering that can potentially
be a problem during extrusion [20], [21], [22]. Die swell is observed in visco-elastic
fluids that expand when exiting the die and causes the preform diameter to be larger
than the die exit diameter. Tapering is observed at decreased viscosities where the
material is pulled by its own weight leading to decreased preform diameters.
This thesis aims at fabricating high-quality core-clad preforms consisting of vari-
ous high-performance thermoplastics and chalcogenide glasses using the co-extrusion
technique. These mulimaterial preforms will then be fiber drawn to a robust multi-
material optical fiber in applications requiring high temperatures and and mechanical
strength.

CHAPTER 2
Optical Fibers
This chapter contains a general theoretical overview and basic principles of optical
fibers. The key properties of polymer optical fibers (POFs) compared to conventional
silica fibers are discussed.
2.1
Guidance mechanism
An optical fiber consists of a transparent core having a refractive index ncore and
a surrounding cladding with refractive index nclad. To confine light in the core, the
refractive index of the core must be greater than of the cladding, so that ncore > nclad.
Light which is incident on the core-cladding boundary at angles greater than the
critical angle undergoes total internal reflection [2]. The properties of the wave guided
through a fiber are dependent on the refractive index profile of the core and cladding.
Conventional fibers with constant refractive index across the entire cross-section of
the core and cladding are called step-index fibers. Graded-index fibers are made by
decreasing the refractive index of the core from a maximum value at its center to a
minimum value at the core-cladding boundary. This can be seen in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1:
A single mode step-index fiber, a multimode step-index fiber and graded
index fiber, where the light path through the core and refractive index profile are shown
respectively. Adapted from [1]
The fiber geometry hence the core size determines the set of fiber modes that can
propagate in the optical fiber. A step-index fiber with large enough core, can support

2.1.2
Modes
An optical mode corresponds to a particular intensity profile where the intensity pro-
file does not change as it propagates through the fiber. When light is guided through
the fiber, the intensity profile will be set into one mode or a combination of modes
[23]. The guided fiber modes are simplified into linearly polarized modes, expressed
as LPlm which are described by the indices l and m that govern the azimuthal and
radial distribution respectively [24]. A single mode-fiber will support the lowest or-
der mode called the fundamental mode. The fundamental mode LP01 has the highest
intensity with an intensity profile approximately similar to a Gaussian distribution
[23].

frequency, also called V-parameter is a dimensionless number that gives the number

If the V-parameter is less than 2.405, the fiber can support only one mode. When

wavelengths used in silica fibers [2].
2.1.3
Attenuation
The most important property of an optical fiber when light is guided through is
attenuation.
Attenuation describes the loss in power as light propagates through
an optical fiber with a given length. Attenuation is given in the units of dB/km
or dB/m, which is expressed in a logarithmic scale [2].
In order to measure the
attenuation or losses of an optical fiber often a cut-back technique is used. It involves
comparing the output power transmitted through a longer piece of a fiber with a
shorter piece of fiber [25]. A single-mode silica fiber has a low loss of 0.2 dB/km at
the telecommunication wavelength of 1550 nm [2]. The loss in polymer optical fibers
is usually higher and caused by intrinsic and extrinsic losses. The intrinsic losses
consist of electronic absorption bands dominated at visible wavelengths, molecular
vibrations at longer wavelengths (e.g. C-H molecules) and Rayleigh scattering mainly
dominated at visible wavelengths (goes as 1/λ). Extrinsic losses are due to bending
of the fiber, scattering at impurities in the core and fluctuations of the core diameter
[2]. Since polymers consist of long polymer chains primarily by C-H bonds, these
absorption bands are difficult to avoid. Nevertheless, polymers usually have their
low loss region located at the visible region such as PMMA as shown in Figure 2.2,
since the losses increase as we move to longer wavelengths (infrared region). Usually,
PMMA is the most used polymer material for fabrication of polymer fibers, due to
its low cost and easy manufacturing. However, PMMA has high optical losses and
lower glass transition temperature of around Tg = 105 oC [26]. Another POF based
on fluoropolymers such as CYTOP has been developed throughout the years. For
example, commercially available optical fibers based on CYTOP has shown to have
a low loss of 15 dB/km at 1300 nm [27].

3.1
Chalcogenide glasses
In this thesis we worked with chalcogenide glasses specifically the two glass compo-
sitions As2S3 and As2Se3 due to their various unique properties that can be incor-
porated as multimaterial fibers. Chalcogenide glasses can transmit to much longer
wavelengths compared to conventional silica glasses and therefore are suitable for
applications requiring light propagation in the mid-infrared range for medical appli-
cations, optical sensing or thermal imaging. However, most commercially available
chalcogenide glasses are expensive than polymers and contain a fraction of arsenic
which is a toxic material. Nevertheless, the toxicity can be minimized as long as glass
surface is not oxidized. The most important properties of these type of glasses will
be presented here in this chapter.
3.1.1
Properties of chalcogenide glasses
Chalcogenide glasses are based on the chalcogen elements S, Se and Te. They are
formed by the addition of other elements such as Ge, As, Sb, Ga and others [7].
The most common compositions include As2S3, As2Se3, Ge-As-Se, Ge-As-Se-Te and
Ge-As-S-Se [29]. These glasses are oxygen free and are generally transparent from
visible to mid-infrared wavelength range. They transmit to much longer wavelengths
compared to conventional silica glasses. Moreover, the bonding between the atoms in
chalcogenide glasses are weakly bonded due to weak van der Waal’s bonding which
makes changes in the structure relatively easy incorporated. Due to the weak bonding
strength of the atoms, chalcogenide glasses are considered to be low-phonon-energy
materials compared to silica glasses [29]. In addition chalcogenide glasses have lower
glass transition temperature Tg and are less robust compared to silica glasses due
to their higher atomic mass and weaker bonding strength. These unique glasses also
behave as a semiconductor, with optical bandgaps of 1-3 eV [29]. One of the main
unique features of chalcogenide glasses is their transmission window, which can be
tuned based on the composition of the glass. This allows a wide range of atomic
ratios to be incorporated into the glass structure. A comparison of the transmission
window of the various chalcogenide glasses and silica glass can be seen in Figure 3.1.
It can be observed that chalcogen elements sulfides, selenium and tellurides transmit
from near-infrared (NIR) to mid-IR region covering up to 26 µm compared to silica

The refractive index of chalcogenide glasses is generally larger than silica glasses

adding a lighter element such as sulfur (S) lowers the refractive index [31].
Chalcogenide glasses also exhibit high non-linear properties which makes them
useful in applications such as optical switching, Raman amplification and supercon-
tinuum generation. For example the non-linear index, n2 of As2S3 glass composition
range from 15-2000 times higher than for silica glass [32].
3.1.2
Synthesis of chalcogenide glasses
There are various techniques for the synthesis of chalcogenide glasses. The quenching
technique is the most common method for preparing chalcogenide bulk glasses. This
technique requires first weighing the individual elements in the desired atomic per-
centages and then purification of the elements using heat treatment to remove any
contamination. Afterwards the materials are sealed in silica ampoules under vacuum
which is heated by a furnace so that the components melt. The quenching process
is done by applying extremely rapid cooling to the sealed silica ampoules to prevent
formation of crystal structure. The quenching rate is crucial in order to avoid break-
ing the glass. The glass is then subjected to an annealing procedure at a temperature
slightly lower than the glass transition temperature to reduce internal stress. Finally
the glass can be extracted, cut and polished [33]. Most glass samples such as the
As2Se3 and As2S3 composition used in this thesis are commercially available from dif-
ferent manufacturers such as Amorphous Materials Inc. (USA) [34] and IRradiance
Inc. (IRG USA) [35].

Viscosity is an important physical parameter which determines the material flow.
Viscosity describes a fluids resistance to flow and is in units of pascal seconds (Pa
s) [40]. For glasses, the viscosity has an influence on the structural relaxation and
crystallization. Structural relaxation is a very slow structural rearrangement of glass,
and the process can be realized through viscous flow [41]. At low viscosity, crystal-
lization can become an issue which could compromise its shaping ability and increase
scattering losses in an optical fiber. Most amorphous glasses are considered to be
Newtonian fluids where the viscosity is only dependent on the pressure and temper-
ature [42]. Due to the small influence of pressure on the glass material, the pressure
dependence of the viscosity is usually neglected. The viscosity of chalcogenide glasses
from most works are focused on the temperature dependence since temperature in-
fluences the viscosity most significantly [42]. Generally the viscosity decreases with
increasing temperature until it becomes a viscous fluid.

in [17]. The glass composition As2Se3 is measured using a thermomechanical ana-

3.2
Polymer materials
The search for alternative materials for multimaterial optical fibers led to an increased
focus on polymers due to several factors. Firstly, polymers are more flexible and less
brittle than glass. Because of polymers low mass density and high degree of flexibility,
optical fibers made of polymers have an advantage over glass fibers in applications
that require limited space and lightweight materials. Since polymers have both larger
thermal expansion coefficient and flexibility than glass, is possible to fabricate optical
fibers based on inscribed Bragg gratings in the core for sensing temperature, strain
and bend with a large tuning range [3].
3.2.1
Properties of polymers
It is beyond the scope of this thesis to make a detailed description of polymers and
their chemistry. However a few important key concepts relevant for our work are
presented here.
Generally polymers are composed of hydrocarbons i.e. carbon and hydrogen, made
by repeating units of structural groups. Polymers can be divided into three categories

14

depending on to their molecular structure, such as thermoplastics, elastomers and
thermosets [47]. In this work we will only focus on thermoplastics.
Thermoplastics such as polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA), ZEONEX, polyether-
sulfone (PES) and polysulfone (PSU) are polymers that are deformable. They soften
and flow when heated and become hard again when cooled. This process can be re-
peated several times which makes it suitable for processes such as extrusion and fiber
drawing [47]. Most thermoplastics exhibit high molecular weight and their chains
associated by intermolecular forces. As the temperature the polymer chains start
to slide between each other in a so called rubbery-state. The temperature at which
this change happens is the, glass transition temperature Tg, where the polymer chain
structure instigate molecular motion [48]. Thermoplastics can be either amorphous,
crystalline or both. Amorphous polymers do not exhibit crystalline structures and are
more elastic and transparent. They do not have a specific melting point, but soften
in a broad range of temperatures [47], which are proper candidates for extrusion and
fiber drawing processes since they exhibit a continuous change of viscosity with re-
spect to temperature. Typical amorphous polymers include PMMA, ZEONEX, PSU
and PES.
Some engineering thermoplastics including ZEONEX, PSU and PES have better
mechanical properties and heat resistance than conventional thermoplastics. Until
now the most used POF based on PMMA material can reach in the best case 90
oC, because of its lower glass transition temperature [49]. Among these engineering
thermoplastics, the polyethersulfone (PES) and polysulfone (PSU) have even better
thermal properties, with high glass transition temperatures and excellent mechani-
cal properties. All the mentioned high performance thermoplastic polymers will be
presented in the next following sections.

3.2.2.2
Glass transition temperature and viscosity
Polymers exhibit both viscous and elastic behavior and are therefore called viscoelas-
tic materials. Which behavior is dominating depends on the molecular structure itself
and processing conditions. Too much elasticity can lead to unwanted effects during
extrusion [57]. One example is swelling of a polymer flow when exiting the narrow die
of an extruder. Therefore knowing the viscoelastic properties of a polymer material
is crucial in order to optimize a given process.
A Dynamic Mechanical Thermal Analysis (DMTA) is an excellent technique to
analyse the mechanical and thermal properties of polymers. The material is exposed
to an oscillatory stress or strain while the temperature is changed and thus the re-
sulting stress or strain response is measured. The obtained data are used to identify
==PAGE:34==
18
3 Material properties
characteristic phase transitions such as the glass transition temperature Tg and mea-
surements of viscosity [58].
The measurements of ZEONEX grade 480R and grade E48R samples are done
using a tension mode in a rotational rheometer (TA Discovery Hybrid HR-2). Thin
rectangular samples of dimensions 2 mm thickness, 10 mm width and 25 mm length
are loaded into the clamps and ensured to be locked with the screws. A constant
upward force is applied in the axial direction to analyze the solid samples. An axial
force of 1 N with a sensitivity of 0.1 N is chosen. The sample is scanned during a
temperature sweep from 25 oC to 200 oC at a heating rate of 10 oC/min across its
glass transition temperature to measure the elastic modulus (E′), the loss modulus
(E′′) and the (tan(δ)) of the polymer material.
The experiment is set to run at
frequency of 1 Hz, in order to record a modulus value every second. This means
that the frequency is held constant while the temperature is changed. The elastic
modulus gives information about the energy stored in the elastic structure of the
sample. The loss modulus gives information about the material’s ability to dissipate
stress through heat. The ratio of the loss and storage modulus is defined as tan(δ)
which describes the degree of energy dissipation or damping of the material [ref]. The
experimental setup of the rotational rheometer after one measurement can be seen in
Figure 3.7a where the sample got stretched during the measurement. A schematic of
the experimental procedure can be seen in Figure 3.7b.

plateau region [62].

3.2.5
Final material choice
The choice of core and cladding materials plays an important role in the extrusion pro-
cess and hence optical fiber drawing of multimaterial fibers. First of all the core must
have a greater refractive index than the refractive index of the cladding. Second of all,
the materials must be thermally compatible in terms of their viscosities and glass tran-
sition temperatures, so that they have close or overlapping softening temperatures. In
addition, the materials should have relatively similar thermal expansion coefficients
==PAGE:42==
26
3 Material properties
in the temperature range of fiber drawing or extrusion to avoid fracture or cracks and
reduce residual stress in the drawn fibers due to thermomechanical mismatches. For
chalcogenide glasses, this becomes even more difficult since the viscosity range for
extrusion and fiber drawing is narrow due to the steep viscosity-temperature curves.
There is also a possibility for crystallization of the glasses at high temperatures which
should be avoided at any time to reduce optical losses. Moreover, care must be taken
to avoid fluid instabilities that can occur in multimaterial fibers due to the decreased
viscosity and the transverse dimension reduced [69]. This is explained through the
Rayleigh-Plateau instability where the core material breaks up into spherical droplets
[70]. This effect is more significant in multimaterial fibers since high surface energy
exists at the interfaces and at smaller dimensions the high surface energy increases
[69].
Based on the viscosity curves of the different materials, glass transition tempera-
tures and thermal expansion coefficients suitable material combinations can be deter-
mined. The two different grades of ZEONEX (grade 480R and E48R) with Tg = 142.9
oC and Tg = 142.7 oC respectively and viscosity curve as seen in Figure 3.13a are ther-
mally compatible. The thermal expansion coefficients of the two materials are also
similar as seen from Table 3.2. Therefore for the core material we selected ZEONEX
grade E48R which has a slightly higher refractive index than ZEONEX grade 480R
acting as the cladding material. Furthermore, a protective outer cladding material
can be incorporated to improve the performance of the optical fiber in various con-
ditions. As mentioned before, PES and PSU are high- temperature thermoplastics
with enhanced mechanical properties which can further strengthen an optical fiber
consisting of materials with lower glass transition temperatures. However, the dif-
ferent materials need to be thermally compatible specifically during a fiber drawing
process. Since PSU has a lower glass transition temperature than PES and therefore
closer to the glass transition temperatures of the two different grades of ZEONEX, it
is best suited for an outer cladding layer. The thermal expansion coefficient of PSU is
also relatively similar to the two different grades of ZEONEX. As mentioned before,
the molecular weight of PSU is larger compared to ZEONEX as depicted from the
viscosity-temperature curve, which is beneficial for thermal drawing. This is because
the flowability of thermoplastic material in general decreases with increasing molec-
ular weight hence flows slower than ZEONEX which in turn enables to get a wide
range of drawing temperatures with better control of the fiber drawing. Therefore
we selected PSU as the protective outer cladding material. The two glass composi-
tions As2Se3 and As2S3 are thermally compatible in terms of their thermal expansion
coefficient as listed in Table 3.1 and their glass transition temperatures, although
there is a 13 oC difference between the glass transition temperatures. This is however
based on the fact that the claimed glass transition temperatures of the two glass com-
positions according to [37],[38] agree well with our materials. Otherwise, there is a
possibility that the glass transition temperatures of the two glass compositions might
differ. According to [36] the glass transition temperature of As2S3 is measured to
be Tg = 210 oC using a differential scanning calorimetry (DSC). Since chalcogenide
glasses are fragile, a cladding material based on a tough and elastic thermoplastic

4.1.4
Hot embossing of samples
Another important factor before extrusion is that the facets of the samples need
to be flat as possible, specifically during co-extrusion at the interface of two sam-
ples, to reduce any possible loss factors that can appear in the final optical fiber.
Throughout the years several methods based on polishing have been presented, such
as a rotational diamond cutter [71], sandpaper cutter, diamond paste for polishing
chalcogenide glasses [16] and chemical-mechanical polishing [72]. Nevertheless, these
polishing methods are costly, time consuming and provides ineffective results. Here
we demonstrate a so called thermal imprint method or hot embossing by applying
heat and pressure. This method allows low cost, time-efficient, and high quality hot
embossed surfaces. A schematic figure for the hot embossing method can be observed
in Figure 4.2 and the experimental setup during the imprint can be seen in Figure
4.2b. The polymer sample is placed on a cleaned flat silicon wafer between two cylin-
drical metal plates. One of the metal plates can be controlled from the computer to
move downward and upwards. The other metal plate is heated and cannot be moved.
The silicon wafer after being cleaned thoroughly with isopropanol or acetone is then
placed on top of the heated steel plate. The temperature of the heated steel plate
can be controlled from the computer. When the temperature has reached slightly
above the glass transition temperature, the metal plate is moved downwards slowly
and applies moderate pressure to the sample for a short time. The ZEONEX samples
are heated to a temperature of 165 oC with an applied force of around 50 N/cm2
for approximately 1 min. The temperature is afterward decreased, and the pressure
will start to decrease slowly since the polymer starts to shrink, and it is made sure
that the pressure is maintained until the temperature is below the glass transition
temperature. Care has to be taken in order not to apply too much pressure since the
material will otherwise deform.

imprint. However, in this work, a temperature of 220 oC was chosen for hot embossing
the As2Se3 glass with an applied force below 7 N/cm2. This is because the sample

been demonstrated to be a non-wetting material [18]. For extrusion of the polymer
materials, a stainless steel die without graphite has been used. One reason is, that
the polymer material after extrusion and cooling becomes so hard that it is difficult
to remove the polymer properly without introducing cracks on the graphite. Second
of all a small region of the preform end shows imprint from the die graphite after
extrusion during cooling. The different extrusion dies can be observed in Figure 4.5.
The movement and the speed of the punch/piston can be controlled using a set
of controllers from the pump system. The punch force is automatically measured by
the load cell and the drive system of the extrusion machine. Furthermore, a bronze
coated bobbin is inserted between the piston and the top of the sample in order
to reduce adhesion between the piston and sample that would likely occur during
extrusion. The stainless steel die is placed on top of a guide tube where the preform
is guided through the tube during extrusion in order to maintain a constant vertical
direction. The arrangement of the mentioned extrusion components can be seen in
Figure 4.4. The measurements of the individual components are listed in Table 4.4.
The guide tubes are fabricated from stainless steel as well. In addition, some of the
guide tubes are lined with graphite in order to reduce the friction between the die
and the sample surfaces. The threaded support is lowered after extrusion to release
the surrounding tube from the barrel and the die and safely remove the equipment.
Furthermore, a outer tube from the bottom of the threaded support surrounding
the die exit is purged with nitrogen (N2) during the whole extrusion process of the
chalcogenide glasses. The temperature is measured via two thermocouples, where one
is placed in contact with the outside of the barrel and the other near the die exit.
This is because it is not possible to measure the actual temperature of the sample
during extrusion without interrupting the polymer or glass flow. Nevertheless, when
the extrusion temperature has reached and stabilized, the extrusion die body will
follow the same temperature of the sample within the die. Therefore when heating
the furnace it is important to reach a stabilized temperature before extrusion. The
uncertainty of the thermocouples is ±1oC. The temperature of the furnace can be
chosen from the extrusion machine. This temperature is chosen approximately 100
oC above the measured die temperature. The reason is due to the heating loss of
the furnace which ultimately causes a temperature difference between the die -and
furnace temperature. The measured data of the die- and barrel temperature, punch
force and position as a function of time are recorded using the DAQami Software.

Single extrusion of polymer samples
Before starting with co-extrusion of the polymer samples, it is necessary to optimize
the different extrusion parameters such as the temperature for extrusion, punch force
and punch speed, in order to produce the desired extruded preforms with high optical
quality. In addition, distortion effects such as die swell and tapering need to be consid-
ered as well since these can affect the final dimensions of the preform. Consequently,
we will start with single extrusions of the polymer material ZEONEX of grade 480R
and grade E48R before we begin co-extruding two materials. Furthermore next a
single extrusion of the polymer material polyethersulfone (PES) will be presented.
The different extrusion equipment, as shown before in Figure 4.4 are cleaned and
prepared to be inserted into the extrusion machine. One of the sample rod before
extrusion based on a ZEONEX grade 480R was measured to have a diameter of 29.5
mm and height of 30 mm which can be seen in Figure 4.6a. Later, one sample rod of
PES material was cut into a diameter of 23.5 mm and height of 20 mm to try match
the inner ring of the outer die, so that less material is sliding out at the top surface
of the die during extrusion. The final prepared samples were loaded into the barrel
together with the bobbin on top, as shown in the schematic Figure 4.6b. The furnace
is aligned to be surrounding the barrel and die within. The furnace temperature was
raised to a temperature of approximately 100oC above the desired die temperature,
which in our case was (Tg+65oC) in order to make sure that the material was soft
enough and exhibited viscous flow. The furnace was left for a few hours for the die
temperature to reach a stabilized temperature. Depending on the temperature set
for the furnace this usually took 2-3 hours. When the die temperature is stabilized at
a temperature above its softening point, the punch is lowered in a constant vertical
direction. The extrusion process for most of the samples took approximately 1-2 to
hour to complete. However this depends on the punch speed, temperature and initial
total height of the sample. At the end of extrusion, the furnace was switched off
and the preform was left to cool down slowly at room temperature. Several single
extrusion trials of the polymer samples are demonstrated and can be seen in Table 4.5.

the die. This is due to the compression of the sample to fill the die. As the force
reaches a steady-state region where the polymer starts to emerge from the die exit,
the punch force does not change considerably with time and position. This is because
the contact area between the sample and the die decreases hence frictional effects are
lowered. A major part of the preform is extruded in this region. This process occurs
when the viscosity of the polymer is lowered and the sample is soft enough to flow out
of the die. The punch force increases significantly at the end of extrusion since the
remaining material is being forced through the die and the bobbin reaches the top of
the die. The swelling and tapering of the preforms can also be demonstrated through
the measurements of outer diameter (OD) at intervals along the preform length using
a caliper with an uncertainty of ±0.2 mm. The OD is plotted as a function of position
of preform with 0 corresponding to the first polymer to exit die, see Figure 4.10b.

(cladding), which could be due to its higher refractive index and lower transmission

band [5]. Light was shined through preform 1 and 2 to observe the core-cladding
interface throughout the whole preform length, as depicted in Figure 4.15a and Figure
4.15b. The core material was forced through the cladding material and exhibited
a parabolic shape (due to the parabolic velocity profile), where the flow diverged
afterwards throughout a large section of the preform.
In this region the core to

surrounds the cladding material of the preform. It was not possible to measure the

proportional to the initial total height of the sample stack before co-extrusion, due
to the mass-conservation law.
For co-extrusion trial 1, the initial total height of
sample stack was 58 mm which resulted in a total preform length of 410 mm and
for co-extrusion trial 2, a 37 mm initial total sample stack resulted in a 220.3 mm±1
mm. However, the length of the preform is also proportional to other parameters
such as the inner diameter of the die, speed, punch force and temperature. A die
with a smaller inner diameter will increase the total length of preform. This can be
observed for the extruded preform trial 5, where the total length was measured to be
325±1 mm. An increased speed and force can result in large swelling of the preform
which in turn will decrease the total length of the preform. Too high temperatures
can lead to tapering of the extruded preform due to decrease in viscosity, which can
increase the total length of preform. The different combinations of heights of the
individual samples were initially chosen to observe the effect on the core-cladding
interface. However, the flow behaviour of the core material (ZEONEX E48R) for all
co-extruded preforms showed similar trend, with a large core through most of the
preform length.

Summary
We have now demonstrated both single extrusion and co-extrusion of thermoplastic

Fibre drawing &

In this chapter, we present the optical fiber drawing process of the extruded preforms
and characterize the drawn multimaterial optical fibers in terms of their geometrical
properties and transmission properties. In addition a thermal stability measurement
of the fabricated multimaterial optical fiber is demonstrated in comparison with an
all-ZEONEX step-index fiber to investigate the performance of the drawn fibers in
high temperature environments.
5.1
Fibre drawing process
After an optical fiber preform has been fabricated, it can be further drawn to an
optical fiber, by a fiber drawing tower based on a heat and draw process. The preform
is inserted into a preform feeder which is gripped at one end and hanging vertically.
The lower end of the preform is heated by a 3-zone furnace in order to heat the
preform material up to its softening temperature in a controlled temperature. Due
to the decreased viscosity of the material and gravity, the diameter of the preform
will be reduced and the preform can be further drawn by a capstan which pulls the
optical fiber. When the viscosity of the material is decreased and pulled down, a
characteristic neck-down region will form where the diameter is decreased rapidly,
as depicted in Figure 5.1b.
The length of the neck-down region depends on the
temperature profile, amount of heat applied and thermal properties of the material.
However it is important that the furnace exhibits a radial temperature profile that
is uniform as possible. The diameter of the fiber is monitored using a laser diameter
gauge during the fiber drawing process. The preform is continuously pulled slowly at
a given draw speed. The drawing speed and the feeding speed is proportional to the
fiber diameter. Using mass-conservation, the following relationship between the draw
speed vdraw, feed speed vfeed, preform diameter dpreform can be used to calculate the
fiber diameter dfiber [75],

5.2
Characterization of drawn optical fibers
After fiber drawing it is important to characterize the multimaterial polymer fiber in
terms of their geometrical- and transmission properties. We started out with fiber
preparation by cleaving the polymer optical fiber end-facets. A high quality cleaving
with flat end-facets perpendicular to the fiber axis is necessary in order to reduce any
significant losses that can arise due to poor coupling efficiency. However one major
issue with multimaterial polymer fibers is cleaving.
Since our multimaterial fiber
consists of three materials, PSU and two different grades of ZEONEX having differ-
ent glass transition temperatures and mechanical properties, it is not straightforward
to find the optimum cleaving technique and conditions. Nevertheless, a hot-blade
cleaving technique can be used to cleave the multimaterial fiber. According to [76]
cleaving of polymer materials such as PMMA and TOPAS is possible. The temper-
ature of the hot-blade and speed can be optimized for the best cleave. Furthermore
a mechanical cleave using a razor blade can also be used followed by polishing of the
end facet of the optical fiber. However, this method requires alignment of the blade
such that it is perpendicular to the fiber axis and to avoid stretching of the core and
cladding interface. For the multimaterial ZEONEX-PSU fiber both end-facets were
mechanically cleaved and polished. Optical microscope images were captured of the
cross-sectional facet of the optical fiber, as depicted in Figure 5.3a and 5.3b. Each
individual material of core, cladding and outer cladding of the multimaterial fiber can
be seen clearly.

The reason the ZEONEX-PSU fiber manages to maintain a stable power up to
180 oC can be due to the following reasons. The thermal conductivity is defined as
the rate of heat transmitted through a unit thickness in a direction normal to the
area with unit temperature difference going from a high temperature to low tem-
perature, in units [W/m·K]. Generally, amorphous thermoplastic have low thermal
conductivity compared to conductive metals, such as steel with thermal conductivity
of 450 W/m·k [78]. This is because polymers are non-metallic with no free electrons
==PAGE:88==
72

and the heat transfer occurs mainly through lattice vibrations [78]. Additionally, due
the complex morphology of polymers when amorphous polymer chains are randomly
entangled, this results in low thermal conductivity. Moreover, the thermal resistance
(R-value) which describes the ability of a material to resist a heat flow is proportional
to thickness of the material and inversely proportional to the thermal conductivity
[79],
R = d
kA
(5.2)
where k is thermal conductivity, d is the thickness of material where heat flows and
A is the area normal to the direction of heat flow.
As observed, a low thermal
conductivity results in a high temperature resistance (high R-value) which means that
it is insulating effective. For PSU the thermal conductivity is low as 0.21 W/m·K [55].
For instance, the thickness at which the heat flows in the multimaterial ZEONEX-PSU
fiber, starting from the surface of the outer cladding to the surface of the core, is much
larger which increases the R-value. Therefore, the heat transfer in the multimaterial
fiber is much slower compared to all-ZEONEX optical fiber. ZEONEX also has a lower
glass transition temperature than PSU, hence the heat resistance becomes lower.
Furthermore, another parameter which describes the amount of heat required
to change its temperature by one degree, is the heat capacity. The heat capacity
of thermoplastics are generally much higher than ceramics and metals [80]. This is
due to the linear thermal expansion of thermoplastic which means that the amount of
heat absorbed by the material from a given heat source is primarily used as increasing
inter-atomic distances by the polymer chains, hence expand, rather than increasing
the temperature of the polymer [81]. The specific heat capacity of PSU is 1200 J/kg·K
according to [55].

In this chapter we are going to summarize and discuss the key achievements of the
results. Furthermore future possibilities and work will be discussed.
6.1
Conclusion
This thesis has presented for the first time a new single-step co-extrusion technique
for producing high-quality multimaterial fiber preforms based on high-performance
thermoplastics and chalcogenide glasses. During the first part we presented the key
properties of optical fibers specifically polymer optical fibers (POFs).
In the sec-
ond part, it was important to gain knowledge on the properties of the materials
used for further extrusion and fiber drawing process. For thermoplastics knowing
the viscoelastic properties is crucial in order to optimize the extrusion parameters
and reduce possible distortion effects. In this work a rotational rheometer was used
to measure the glass transition temperatures and viscosities of the thermoplastics
ZEONEX grade 480R and E48R, PSU and PES. The two glass compositions As2Se3
and As2S3, their glass transition temperatures and viscosities were obtained as well
but using a different method. Based on the obtained properties, it was possible to find
suitable material combinations for extrusion and thermal fiber drawing. The more
narrow viscosity-temperature region suitable for extrusion introduced restrictions on
the material combinations compatible for a co-extrusion process.
Fiber preforms based on two different grades of ZEONEX (grade 480R and E48R)
with diameters of 10 mm and down to approximately 7-8 mm could be achieved. The
co-extruded preforms did not show any trapped air or bubbles, since the samples were
annealed for at least a day. Therefore it was important that all samples were annealed
before extrusion. Furthermore, single extrusions of the different thermoplastics made
it possible to optimize and find suitable parameters such as extrusion temperature,
punch force, speed and die type, for reducing distortion effects such as swelling and ta-
pering. Although swelling was difficult to avoid specifically in thermoplastics since all
preforms showed swelling at start of the preform length, due to their elastic part. In
addition, tapering was minimized for the co-extruded preforms of ZEONEX, however
==PAGE:90==
74
6 Conclusions and Outlook
slight tapering along the length of the preform could still be seen. The important
factor was that the core material exhibited a stable core region in a given length,
which was not an issue for our extruded preforms. A single extrusion of the As2Se3
glass composition was successfully demonstrated, where the preform did not show
swelling, but small tapering along the length. This was due to the lack of elasticity in
chalcogenide glasses, in which swelling is reduced significantly. The single extruded
preform can be incorporated into a thermoplastic rod to protect the core glass, apply-
ing a rod-in-tube technique for thermal drawing. However due to the time scope of
the thesis, it was not possible to demonstrate this. Furthermore, co-extrusion of the
high-temperature thermoplastic PSU and As2Se3 showed extrusion issues where the
preform exhibited a screw thread flow which could be caused by various parameters.
Either the extrusion parameters were not optimal, or the thermoplastic material and
the glass composition are not compatible in a extrusion process but instead may be
compatible in a thermal fiber drawing process. Nevertheless, the PSU/As2Se3 pre-
form exhibited a small region of stable core region which was useful for further fiber
drawing. Co-extrusion of the two glass compositions As2Se3 and As2S3 resulted in a
fracture of the preform, which was caused by the large glass transition temperature
difference of the two glasses. It was more likely that the glass transition temperature
of As2S3 was around 210 oC as stated in [36] and therefore a co-extrusion of these
two glass compositions was not be compatible.
The co-extruded preform of two different grades of ZEONEX with diameters in
range of 7-8 mm was thermally drawn with a high-temperature thermoplastic PSU.
The drawn multimaterial fiber exhibited a fiber diameter of approximately 600 µm
with core size of 137 µm and cladding size 13 µm. High-quality cleaving of the mul-
timaterial fiber was difficult to achieve, however a mechanical cleave or hot-blade
cleave technique of the multimaterial fiber could still be applied. A thermal stability
measurement was demonstrated to investigate the performance of the drawn multi-
material in high-temperature environments by applying heat to the fiber and measure
its transmission in the visible region and output power as a function time. The mul-
timaterial fiber showed stable output power up to 180 oC for 1 hour and started to
fail at 195 oC where the output power no longer was stable and decreased after 10
min. The corresponding transmission spectra showed a significant decrease at 195 oC.
In comparison the performance of an all-ZEONEX based optical fiber with a fiber
diameter of approximately 100 µm and core size of 4 µm in high-temperature environ-
ment was demonstrated as well. The fiber already started to fail at 140 oC where the
output power was fluctuating and decreasing. At 145 oC the power decreased even
more and was fluctuating. The corresponding near-field images of both the multima-
terial fiber and all-ZEONEX based optical fiber showed that the light intensity from
the core decreased at high-temperatures. The multimaterial fiber survived at higher
temperatures compared to the ZEONEX-based fiber, indicating that it can be used
in applications where high temperatures are required.

6.2
Outlook
The multimaterial fiber consisting of two different grades of ZEONEX 480R and E48R
and an outer cladding PSU that was developed in this work has the potential in ap-
plications requiring high-temperature, high- humidity and space limitations. During
the thermal stability measurement, thermal imaging could be used in the future to ob-
serve the heat flow or the temperature difference between the outer cladding and the
core material. Furthermore, the losses of the multimaterial fiber can be measured in
the future using a cut-back technique. In addition, the large core size can be reduced
if a smaller preform size is fabricated, e.g. co-extrusion using a die exit diameter of
5-6 mm. However due to fluid instability that can occur when transverse dimension
is reduced significantly and decreased viscosity introduces restrictions on the die exit
diameter. Furthermore, a multimaterial fiber based on the glass composition As2Se3
as core material and PSU as cladding can be fabricated in the future.
Multimaterial co-extrusion of other high-performance materials can used as well
and even three or four different sample materials can be implemented in a single pre-
form. Fluorinated ethylene propylene (FEP) has excellent mechanical properties and
is biocompatible which makes it an excellent material choice for medical applications,
with glass transition temperature of Tg = 98oC but viscous flow around 250 oC which
can act as a great cladding material combined with PES as core material or other
materials as well.
