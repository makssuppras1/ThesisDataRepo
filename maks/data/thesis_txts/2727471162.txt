Abstract
The present project investigated the optimal conditions in the chemically enhanced treatment of Combined
Sewer Overflows (CSOs) through jar tests with PIX-118 as the coagulant and BoFloc C 6378 M as the
flocculant. CSOs occur when the sewer system’s capacity is exceeded during heavy rainfalls, which leads to
the discharge of untreated wastewater carrying nutrients such as phosphorus into nearby bodies of water.
Climate change and increased precipitation exacerbate the issue of CSOs, necessitating effective treatment
approaches.
This project explores the potential of coagulation-flocculation as a treatment method for CSOs, focusing
on evaluating the removal efficiency of TP and heavy metals under different conditions such as mixing,
sedimentation time and coagulant/flocculant dosage. The results indicate that coagulant dosage significantly
removes turbidity, and high removal of TP and heavy metals was also observed. The different mixing conditions
and flocculant dosage has shown limited influences on the removal of TP and heavy metals.
From an economic perspective, the research contributes to estimating pollutant discharge in CSOs, which
is essential for determining appropriate wastewater taxes. The study suggests that treating CSOs before
discharge is more cost-effective than having to restore the bodies of water when the damages are irreversible.
The optimal dosage of coagulant is evaluated to be 6 mg Fe(III)/L and removing the flocculation process can
reduce operational costs without compromising removal efficiency.
In light of the research conducted, the project highlights the significance of innovative approaches in the
treatment of CSOs. In-situ chemically enhanced treatments have demonstrated their potential in this regard,
with the project providing valuable insights into the optimal coagulant dosage for the removal of total phos-
phorus and heavy metals. While acknowledging certain limitations such as replicating wastewater dilution by
stormwater in a laboratory setting. Overall, this research enhances our understanding of CSO treatment and
its environmental impact.

Preface
This thesis is conducted as the final project of the Master of Science in Engineering within the field of
Environmental Engineering, at The Technical University of Denmark (DTU), by students Christian Palm
Clausager Madsen and Ning Li in equal collaboration. The thesis were supervised by Luca Vezzaro, Associate
Professor at DTU Sustain and Project Engineer at Krüger A/S, and Ravi Kumar Chhetri, Senior Researcher

Sewer Overflows by performing jar tests utilizing the coagulation-floccculation method. This thesis is relevant
due to the fact that literature and data within the field of chemically enhanced treatment of Combined
Sewer Overflows is limited. This thesis is furthermore conducted to collect data for a project, which freely
translates to OVERFLOWS - Data-driven solutions for reducing environmental effects from overflows, under
The Environmental Technology Development and Demonstration Program (MUDP) and in collaboration with
Krüger A/S and Novafos A/S.

Introduction
The first combined sewer system as we know them today was constructed in the late 19th century and was an
extensive improvement of the system at the time. One flaw for which an ideal solution is still to be found today
is the overflow of water from the sewer system to the receiving bodies of water. The daily flow of wastewater
in the sewer system is well-known nowadays, and the sewers’ size is estimated from this, with an added
capacity in the event of rain and other run-offs, known as stormwater run-offs. When the wastewater and
stormwater exceed the sewer system’s capacity, we experience Combined Sewer Overflows (CSOs), which can
be detrimental to public health and the environment (Tibbetts, 2005). Discharged untreated water carrying
a mixture of organic matter, chemicals, debris, and nutrients is discharged from the CSO, which can cause
blackening and odorization in the receiving bodies of water. The nutrients such as nitrogen and phosphorus
can cause eutrophication and lead to immediate and long-term damage. Furthermore, it can lower the level
of dissolved oxygen in the water and thereby threaten the survival of aquatic organisms. The discharged
water from the CSOs also carries pathogens that can lead to diseases ranging from mild gastroenteritis to
life-threatening illnesses such as cholera, hepatitis, etc. (El Samrani et al., 2008; US EPA, 2022b).
Climate change has been acknowledged to be a serious global concern that has led to many environmental
problems affecting the world in various ways. One of these is more frequent and intensified precipitation,
which leads to erosion, flooding, and environmental and health issues related to CSOs (FRI, 2020; Tabari,
2020). The average annual precipitation in Denmark in 2021 was 741 mm, which is higher than the 2014-2021
typical year average precipitation of 712 mm (Miljøstyrelsen, 2023). The frequency of heavy rain events has
also increased and is set to increase in the future (Rubek, Scharling Mikael, & Cappelen John, 2020). A
solution for the intensified precipitation is to move away from combined sewers and separate the sewers into
two separate systems - one for wastewater and one for stormwater run-offs. The sewer system in Denmark
consists of a combination of separate and combined sewers. In 2021, the total area taken up by separate
sewers and combined sewers was 169.712 hectares and 86.921 hectares, respectively, which corresponds to
66% separate sewers and 34% combined sewers (Miljøstyrelsen, 2023). Denmark’s share of separate sewers is
expected to increase in the coming years. In contrast, the share of combined sewers is expected to decrease
due to a strong joint effort towards reducing Combined Sewer Overflows (CSO). However, transforming the
sewer systems requires time and investments, while the remaining urban areas with combined sewer systems
are still discharging untreated wastewater to the nearby bodies of water in the event of CSOs.
Apart from separate sewer systems, the construction or expansion of already existing retention basins is
another solution to accommodate CSOs. The primary function of the retention basins is to capture and store
the excessive volumes of wastewater from the CSO, which otherwise would be bypassed into the receiving
bodies of water. However, the size of the basins is fixed, and expanding the capacity requires comprehensive

Instead of minimizing the extent of overflows, others have looked
into locally treating the wastewater overflow utilizing the well-known chemical treatment of coagulation-

One such solution is the Actiflo®, developed by Veolia, which is an easy-to-implement and

there is a growing need to discover alternative means of treating CSO and reducing its impact on the aquatic
environment.
1.1
Case study: Sjælsø
The case-specific water body studied in this master thesis is Sjælsø, a lake with an area of 293 hectares
and a maximum depth of 5.5 meters, located in the northern part of Zealand bordering three municipalities;
Rudersdal, Allerød, and Hørsholm (Kuhlman & Serritslev, 2013). Sjælsø is being used for fishing and other
recreational purposes, and in 2021 it was evaluated to have excellent bathing quality by the European En-
vironmental Agency (European Environment Agency, 2022). However, examining the data provided by the
Danish Point Discharge System (PULS), Sjælsø is receiving a high amount of phosphorus from CSO discharge
points, as the nearby urban sewage network mainly consists of combined sewers (Danmarks Miljøportal, 2023).
The overall catchment, CSO structure, and basins are visualized in Figure 1.

to retention basin B06 with a volume of 582 m3 and pipe basin B07 with a volume of 940 m3. B06 is a

B06 is expected to remove a significant share of particulate pollution.
However, the dissolved
contaminants and non-settling particles in the mix of the storm- and wastewater will be discharged to Sjælsø
through B07. In both basins, the particulate pollution is significantly removed, but the dissolved compounds
are not reduced. Typically 30-40 % of the total phosphorus in the storm wastewater is in the dissolved form
(Rudersdal kommune, 2022). Thus, there is great potential in reducing the dissolved phosphorus (P) with
the coagulation-flocculation method in the sewage pipes and using the existing basins for the subsequent
sedimentation process. UB07 had 38 CSOs in the reporting year, where 2,282 kg of Total-Nitrogen (TN) and
380 kg of Total-Phosphorous (TP) were discharged into Sjælsø with 190,127 m3 of water over the course of a
year (Danmarks Miljøportal, 2023).
This master thesis aims to investigate the potential of in situ coagulation-flocculation treatment of the over-
flowing wastewater in the sewage pipes and using the existing retention basins as sedimentation tanks to
reduce the polluting nutrients and heavy metals entering Sjælsø. The use of coagulation-flocculation as an
in situ treatment method has already been investigated in a study from China, which showed great results
concerning the removal of TP (Chen et al., 2021). This project is carried out in collaboration with Krüger
A/S and Novafos A/S as a part of the initial work for the project called OVERFLOWS - Data-driven so-
lutions for reducing environmental effects from overflows under the Environmental Technology Development
and Demonstration Program (MUDP).
The main objective of this paper is to assess the performance of chemically enhanced treatment of CSOs by
investigating the most efficient dosage of coagulant and flocculant through lab-scale experiments, known as
jar tests. Furthermore, several parameters, such as mixing time and speed, will be varied throughout the
jar tests to explore their effect on the removal. It is hypothesized that a higher dosage of coagulant and
flocculant will lead to higher removal of turbidity, phosphorus and heavy metals, based on the results from
other papers (El Samrani et al., 2008; Chhetri et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2021). However, the dosage of coagulant
and flocculant should be as low as possible to minimize cost and negative side effects from excessive use of
chemicals, while still attaining sufficient removals. The cost of operations will be considered and compared to
other scenarios.

Literature review
This comprehensive literature review delves into the crucial aspects of CSOs, providing valuable insights into
its origin and associated pollution. It sheds light on the variabilities in both CSOs and the receiving waters
and elaborates on the specific contaminants present in CSOs. The review also delves into the coagulation-
flocculation method and its effectiveness in removing nutrients and heavy metals. Overall, this literature
review is an essential foundation for the experimental settings of this project, providing crucial information
and analysis on the critical aspects of CSOs.
2.1
Pollution from CSO
Assessing the impact of CSO pollution on water bodies is complex due to the numerous variables that can
differ from one body of water to another. These factors are essential in determining the magnitude of pollution
from CSOs to the waters. The total overflow volume, flow speed, the stormwater to wastewater ratio, concen-
trations of nutrients, heavy metals, etc., can all incommode the evaluation of the effect of CSO events on the
receiving waters. Furthermore, the bodies of water have varying sizes, depths, ecosystems, nutrient retention
capacity, and sensitivity to eutrophication. On the other hand, the CSO water also has high variabilities. For
instance, with increased precipitation, there will consequently be a higher dilution of the wastewater during
the CSO event. Furthermore, it is also essential to contemplate whether the stormwater is diffused from
strongly urbanized areas, as the stormwater itself can also be contaminated from the different diffuse sources.
The stormwater could be potentially contaminated during its formation in the atmosphere and when the
stormwater is washed off on the catchment that contains chemical pollution (Sakson et al., 2018). Up to 650
organic substances, 30 metals, and other inorganic compounds could be present in the stormwater (Hannouche
et al., 2011; Eriksson et al., 2007). Additionally, the amount of sanitary wastewater inside the combined sewers
is also dependent on the time of the day, where in the morning and afternoon, there is often higher usage of
water for routine activities such as bathing, toilet flush, cooking, and other housekeeping activities. The share
of wastewater in the CSO that occurred during the morning and afternoon will most likely be more significant
compared to CSO that occurred at other times of the day. It is evident from studies that the first flush of
the CSO is often the most contaminated flow, as the wastewater in the sewers is being pushed forward by
the high amount of stormwater, corresponding to a peak of contaminants that gradually decreases with time
due to dilution. Thereby, the physicochemical composition of the untreated water can vary dramatically as a
function of time (El Samrani et al., 2008; Gruber et al., 2005). There might also be a difference between CSO
caused by short and intense storm events during spring and summer compared to long-lasting low-intensity
rain events in fall and winter. CSOs resulting from storm events usually have high turbidity and suspended
solids (SS) similar to dry weather wastewater flow. At the same time, the pH, conductivity, and alkalinity are
relatively lower than the dry-weather wastewater flow. In contrast, CSOs caused by weak rain events tend

2.1.1
Nutrients pollution from CSO
P is one of the primary nutrients that are essential for plant growth. P is also one of the major pollutants in
aquatic systems that cause eutrophication and water quality degradation. Thereby, P removal is often desired
in many cases, as well as the CSO setting (US EPA, 2023). The source and share of P in wastewater can be
domestic and industrial. The dissolved P from domestic wastewater is generally higher than the industrial
wastewater, as it contains P from human excreta, household detergents, etc. But depending on the type
of industry effluents, the P can be released in both particulate form or dissolved form (Kroiss et al., 2011).
Stormwater runoff contributes only a small fraction to the P content in the wastewater. Therefore, the CSO
is expected to have a lower concentration of P due to rainwater dilution (Kroiss et al., 2011).
In Denmark, the most recent report made by NOVANA in 2021 states that a total of 3,873 CSO events occurred
in 2021, where 79 tons of total phosphorus (TP) were discharged into the Danish aquatic environment in a
volume of 34 million m3 of untreated rain-diluted wastewater (Miljøstyrelsen, 2023). It is crucial to notice
that the TP mass is the accumulated mass from 3,873 CSO events, where the data about where and when the
P measurements were taken are lacking from the report. There are currently no limit values for the discharge
of nutrients due to the variability aspect mentioned above. However, to put it into perspective, the Danish
wastewater permit made by Miljøstyrelsen states the national limit wastewater effluent concentrations must
be below 1.5 mg TP/L (Miljøstyrelsen, 1999). After intense precipitation, the first flush of CSO is expected
to have the most untreated wastewater due to the stormwater pushing and gathering more wastewater as
it flows into the sewer.
Untreated wastewater from CSO, which is comparable to the first flush, has a
concentration of 11 mg TP/L, whereas The Danish national average concentration of TP in CSOs is 2 mg/L
(SEGES Innovation, 2022), indicating nutrient pollution’s significance in the Danish aquatic environment.
The average TP concentration in CSOs in Korea showed average TP concentration in CSOs to be 3 mg/L
(Lee et al., 2013), whereas the typical TP in the USA was found to be between 1.2-2.8 mg/L (Botturi et
al., 2020). These values suggest similar TP concentrations in the CSOs from very different countries and
locations. These values will be used to compare with the TP concentration found in this project.

2.1.2
Heavy metals pollution from CSO
Heavy metals are a group of high-density metals and metalloids that, in elevated concentrations, can cause
negative impacts on the natural environment.
The most noteworthy heavy metals are arsenic (As), lead
(Pb), cadmium (Cd), chromium (Cr), copper (Cu), mercury (Hg), nickel (Ni), zinc (Zn), and strontium (Sr)
(Ida & Eva, 2021).
Heavy metals are non-biodegradable, stable, persistent in the environment, and can
also be bioaccumulated in aquatic organisms, affecting the whole food chain. Therefore, heavy metals have
been intensively investigated in many environmental fields, such as soils, sediments, biotas, and wastewater
overflows worldwide (Suami et al., 2020). Pb and Cd are generally not abundant in wastewater, but they
can be very toxic at low concentrations (Sakson et al., 2018). Heavy metals in the CSO can originate from
stormwater runoff, primarily from industries, buildings, vehicles with metal roofs and parts, and leaks of oils
and fuels, among other sources (Sakson et al., 2018).
In Denmark, the Danish Environmental Protection Agency (in Danish: Miljøstyrelsen) has set stringent
regulations to limit the discharge of heavy metals into the environment through wastewater effluent since
2006 (Miljøstyrelsen, 2006).
It has been extremely difficult to regulate heavy metals in CSOs due to the high variability between CSOs
and the characteristics of receiving bodies of water also vary significantly. A high amount of Heavy metals in
CSOs can be extremely problematic in the long run, as they are not regulated by any legislation due to being
discharged through overflows. The abundance of different heavy metals in untreated wastewater, wastewater
sludge, and CSOs have been investigated in many studies (Suami et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2018; El Samrani et
al., 2008). The most recent Danish report made by NOVANA published in 2022 has documented the heavy
metal concentrations from CSO events that occurred in Denmark, and the results are presented in Table 2
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2022). The table also contains average heavy metal concentrations from 7 CSOs in the French
city Nancy (El Samrani et al., 2008) and humus effluent from UK (Hargreaves et al., 2018). Another study
suggests that Cr and Cu will likely exist for extended periods in the CSOs, and according to the risk index
analysis and the geo-accumulation index analysis, the CSOs pose a considerable risk to receiving waters due
to the higher bioavailability of Cd (Xu et al., 2018).

2.2
Coagulation-flocculation principles
Coagulation-flocculation is a widely employed method for water treatment, especially when it comes to purify-
ing wastewater. The method is divided into two distinct stages that involve first adding a coagulant to desta-
bilize the particles and subsequently adding a flocculant to aggregate the destabilized particles (Pivokonský et
al., 2022). These aggregates settle in the following sedimentation process, forming even larger aggregates due
to vertical orthokinetic aggregation (Teh, Budiman, Shak, & Wu, 2016). Both stages are typically used in tan-
dem to achieve optimal removal outcomes. In the CSO setting, it is important to understand which particles
and pollutants can be targeted and removed via coagulation-flocculation. The content of CSO wastewater can
vary depending on excessive precipitation levels. It typically contains contaminated particles of varying sizes,
including settleable particles (>100 µm), supra colloidal particles (1-100 µm), colloidal particles (0.08-1 µm),
and soluble particles (>0.08 µm) (Okamoto, 2016). The particles <100 µm are the target of the coagulation-
flocculation process. In the following sections, a thorough explanation of the coagulation and flocculation will
be provided. Afterwards, parameters such as turbidity and absorbance will be explained as they can be used
to assess the removal of pollutants, namely P and heavy metals. The coagulation-flocculation principles and
their influences on the pH and conductivity are also presented.

2.2.1
Coagulation
The target particles of the coagulation process are mainly the colloidal particles whose movements are in-
fluenced by their electrokinetic charges. The colloidal impurities in the wastewater are mostly negatively
charged. Figure 2 shows how an electronegative colloid interacts with the ions in the wastewater. The colloid
attracts the positively charged counter-ions in the suspension and forms a compact layer around the negative
charge known as the Stern layer. The counter-ions in the Stern layer also attract other ions with the opposite
charge called the co-ions, forming a thicker layer called the diffuse layer. The attracting force of ions in the
diffuse layer is not as strong as in the Stern layer. When the colloids move, the co-ions can diffuse in and
out of the diffuse layer, and together they become a stable electric double layer. The four mechanisms that
can destabilize the colloids are double-layer compression, charge neutralization, colloid entrapment, and in-
traparticle bridging, where the most promising method used in industries is the charge neutralization method
(Sincero & Sincero, 2003).
Figure 2: The double layer consisting of Stern layer and diffused layer around the electronegative colloid (Teh et al., 2016).
In the wastewater, the attractive body forces between the colloids are often less than the electrokinetic repelling
force, which means they repel each other, making it difficult to form larger clusters of particles known as flocs
that can be removed through gravitational settling. Therefore, the coagulant is used to charge opposite to the
colloidal particles to neutralize the charges of the colloids. The hydrolyzing coagulants effectively neutralise
the negative surface charges of the particles, bacteria, and clays. The most commonly used coagulants are
aluminium coagulants (alum, aluminium chloride, and sodium aluminate) and iron coagulants (Iron chloride,
8
==PAGE:19==

Iron sulfate, Iron(II) sulfate, Iron(III) sulfate, and Iron chloride sulfate).
Other effective prepolymerized
coagulants such as Poly Aluminum Chloride (PAC), Poly Aluminum Sulfate (PAS), and Polyalumnium Chloro-
Sulfate (PACS) can be used in different ranges of colloid concentrations (Teh et al., 2016). Once the charges
have been neutralized, settleable particles can aggregate with each other and form slightly larger particles
called microflocs, which are not visible to the eye. One way to identify the forming of microflocs is to check
whether the surrounding water is clear. If the water is unclear, it can indicate insufficient neutralization of
the particles, and further addition of coagulant might be needed (Pivokonský et al., 2022). The coagulation
process is commonly induced by high energy, rapid mixing conditions that can properly disperse the coagulant
to neutralize as many particles as possible. Furthermore, research has shown that over-mixing does not affect
the coagulation process, but insufficient mixing will reduce the efficiency of the coagulation process. The
mixing time of the coagulation process usually takes about 1-3 minutes (Pivokonský et al., 2022). The addition
of acidic coagulants will likely cause the pH to decrease, and it is also likely to change the conductivity as
the coagulant cations are introduced to the samples, but the relationship can vary due to the variability
of many factors (Villa, Fölster, & Kyllmar, 2019). Notably, it was discovered that in chemical P removal
via precipitation, the phosphate can be substituted with sulfate, and no reduction in conductivity will be
observed (Levlin, 2007). Thus, the pH is expected to decrease after the addition of acidic coagulants, and
the conductivity can vary or remain the same depending on whether the coagulant has compounds such as
sulfate or not.
2.2.2
Flocculation
Flocculation is the process that uses flocculants, such as synthetic polymers, to induce the neutralized mi-
croflocs to become larger aggregates that can settle faster (Hargreaves et al., 2018). The larger particles
formed are called pinflocs, which are visible. The pinflocs can become even larger particles from the reaction
with polymers, which serve as the flocculant and helps the bridging, binding, and strengthening of flocs. The
ultimate aggregates formed are called macroflocs, which have a much faster settling rate than microflocs. The
typical mixing time of the flocculation process varies from 15 minutes up to an hour or more. The microflocs
must collide and stick to each other while preventing them from tearing apart. The torn flocs will be unable
to reform their size and strength again (Pivokonský et al., 2022). In terms of the polymer’s effect on pH
and turbidity, it was found that high flocculant dosage will lead to high turbidity (Basaran & Tasdemir,
2014). The pH can also be affected as the cationic polymer has a pH range of 5-7 (Shandong IRO Polymer
Chemicals Co., 2023).
2.2.3
Relationships between turbidity, absorbance, conductivity, TP, and heavy metals
Turbidity is defined as the cloudiness in the wastewater, and cloudiness is caused by tiny particles that are
invisible to the naked eye. Turbidity is one of the physical characteristics of the wastewater and can be used
to determine the magnitude of pollution in the wastewater. The turbidity in wastewater is typically formed

by particles of SS, fine organic matter, microorganisms, and different forms of colloidal particles mentioned
above (Bote & Desta, 2022). The turbidity is measured by a turbidity meter or nephelometer, which measures
the turbidity in Nephelometric Turbidity Units (NTU). The turbidity utilizes light to determine the clarity
of the water by measuring the intensity of light scattered by suspended particles at 90 degrees when a light
beam passes through the water. The typical turbidity level in the CSOs is around 20-250 NTU, where the
CSO turbidity is generally expected to be lower than the untreated wastewater (van Daal-Rombouts et al.,
2013).
Coagulation-flocculation treatment of wastewater to remove turbidity has been proven to be effective, as
turbidity has been proven to have linearity to the Total Suspended solids (TSS) and TP (Hannouche et al.,
2011; Villa et al., 2019), where the TSS furthermore has proven to have linearity with absorbance (Piro et al.,
2012). The conductivity has a low correlation to turbidity as well as TP (Villa et al., 2019). The heavy metals,
namely Ni, Pb, Cd, Cu, Zn, Co, As, and Sr, have shown linearity to the TSS from a study by Nasrabadi et
al. (2016). Based on the studies, it is expected that the turbidity will be linearly correlated to TSS, which
is linearly correlated to absorbance, TP, and heavy metals. The conductivity is expected to have limited
correlations to other parameters.

2.2.5
Heavy metals removal
Heavy metals are very difficult to remove from wastewater, as they are only present in low concentrations and
susceptible to biodegradation. The main mechanisms for removing heavy metals through the coagulation-
flocculation method are adsorption and polymer bridging, where the coagulants can remove heavy metals
from wastewater (Alazaiza et al., 2022). Many studies have demonstrated that it is possible to reduce the
concentration of heavy metals such as Cd, Cu, Zn, Ni, and Pb via the coagulation-flocculation method
(Johnson et al., 2008; Ida & Eva, 2021). It was also found that the Fe-based coagulant PIX-118 and FeSO4
themselves contain high concentrations of heavy metals, namely Cr (71 mg/kg Fe), Ni (250 mg/kg Fe), and Zn
(406 mg/kg Fe) (Vollertsen et al., 2012). In contrast, when added to the water during coagulation, PIX-118
could increase the total heavy metal concentrations for Cr, Ni, and Zn, but the dissolved concentrations of
these metals can be reduced. Adding PIX-118 led to an increase of Ni, which could not be removed at all
(Vollertsen et al., 2012).
Several factors can affect the efficiency of P and heavy metals removal, including pH, coagulant dosage, mixing
conditions, and settling time (Hargreaves et al., 2018; Qasem et al., 2021; Pivokonský et al., 2022). However,
implementing this method directly in sewage pipes can pose a significant challenge, given the unpredictable
nature of CSOs and the specific requirements of these various factors.
It is believed that the coagulation-flocculation technique is effective in eliminating P and heavy metals from
wastewater, as demonstrated by several studies. However, the successful application of this method in the CSO
setting requires specific conditions to achieve the desired results. It is crucial to determine the appropriate
dosage and type of coagulant and flocculant, as well as the correct pH levels and mixing rates, for each
case. Failure to comply with these requirements can result in inadequate impurity removal, chemical residual
coagulants in the environment that are harmful, and a waste of resources. Therefore, it is essential to conduct
experimental jar tests to establish the optimal conditions for coagulation-flocculation before implementing the
method.

Materials and methods
As aforementioned this thesis is conducted as part of the initial work for a project under MUPD, in which
in situ coagulation-flocculation is investigated as a possible way of treating wastewater discharged in the
case of CSO. In this case a specific CSO in Sjælsø in used for the experiments, but to be able to conduct
the in situ experiments a discharge permit had to be obtained from the Municipality of Rudersdal, which
was done by Herle M. Madsen from Novafos A/S (Rudersdal kommune, 2022). In the discharge permit, the
wastewater had to be tested for certain parameters, from which this project has looked into TP, TN and TSS.
Furthermore, limits for the addition of coagulant and flocculant had to be complied with, hence this thesis
based the addition of coagulant and flocculant on these limits.
Initially, the project had set out to investigate wastewater sampled by an autosampler during a CSO event, as
part of the MUDP project in Sjælsø. However, due to low precipitation during the experimental phase of this
thesis, it was not possible. Consequently, untreated wastewater was simulated using a combination of 40%
wastewater and 60% De-Ionized (DI) water to replicate the "first flush" of CSO, referred to as ’Simulated
CSO I’. The standard CSO was simulated using a combination of 15% wastewater and 85% DI water diluted
with untreated wastewater, referred to as ’Simulated CSO II’ (Chhetri, 2017).
The wastewater used for the experiments was collected on six different days between March and May of
2023 from Sjælsø WRRF. These samples were obtained during the morning hours between 8:30 and 10:30
(see table 11), post-mechanical treatment aimed at reducing larger constituents present in the water.
In
total, approximately 260 litres of wastewater were collected for the project, which involved 43 experiments,
including 17 preliminary jar tests and 26 additional jar tests that were conducted using a variety of parameters
to explore their potential impact on the treatment of CSOs. Through the use of rigorous testing and analysis,
the project aimed to develop a better understanding of how to effectively treat CSOs and minimize their
impact on the environment.
The undertaken project involved executing lab-scale experiments, known as jar tests, to evaluate the efficacy
of chemically enhanced treatment for CSOs using coagulation-flocculation. The experimental setup, as shown
in Figure 3, was designed to test a variety of parameters, including coagulant and flocculant dosage, mixing
time, and speed, to derive the most suitable recommendations for the implementation of chemically enhanced
treatment for CSOs. The succeeding sections will provide an overview of the materials and methods employed
for the jar tests. Firstly, a general account of the jar tests’ methodology and design, along with the measuring
techniques used, will be presented. Subsequently, the methodology will be refined and tailored to accommodate
the Sjælsø case study. Lastly, a detailed account of the tools and methods utilized to analyze the collected
samples will be furnished.

Preliminary Jar Tests
There are various ways to conduct jar tests, but the most common approach is identifying the ideal coagulant
dosage (Pivokonský et al., 2022). This approach was the focal point of the initial jar tests carried out in this
project. The preliminary tests consisted of 15 jar tests, where only turbidity, absorbance, pH, and conductivity
were measured. The used water type is only untreated wastewater.
Six Kavalier 1 L tall form beakers (referred to as "jars") made by SIMAX®, each with a spout, were used to
conduct the initial test. A mixing machine with six propellers by A/S trillingerne with S series 220 V variable
speed controller by Shin Myung Serco CO., Ltd. was also utilized. Before the jar tests, the stirring speed of
the propellers was measured to determine the RPM and ensure that each stirrer had the same RPM for the
same setting on the mixing machine. This was accomplished by adjusting the mixing machine to different
levels and manually counting the RPM for each stirrer. No significant differences were found between the
individual mixers. Based on the results shown in Figure 26, the mixing speeds were set to 134 RPM for fast

The samples were analyzed for turbidity, absorbance, conductivity and pH during the jar tests. The 20 mL

the intensity of scattered light, the higher the turbidity, which can somewhat reflect the water’s purity level.
Turbidity is a useful preliminary indicator of water quality, providing an instantaneous result for relative
removal during the jar tests. However, it has limitations as it cannot identify the types of particles and
constituents in the samples. Therefore, further experiments also analyzed the samples for TP and heavy

larger flocs, each vial was thoroughly wiped using Delicate Task Wipes, Kimberly-Clark Worldwide, Inc., and

3.4.1
Inductively Coupled Plasma-Mass Spectrometry
Inductively Coupled Plasma-Mass Spectrometry (ICP-MS) is a widely used analytical technique for liquid
sample analysis, enabling the identification of elemental composition, concentration, and isotopic abundance
in a range of matrices. This project utilizes ICP-MS to detect and quantify the presence of heavy metals in
the samples under examination.
The ICP-MS instrument typically consists of 6 components, (i) a sample introduction system, (ii) an ion
source which is called the Inductively Coupled Plasma (ICP), (iii) the electrostatic lenses, (iv) an interface,
(v) a mass spectrometer and finally (vi) a detector (Mazarakioti et al., 2022). The ICP-MS instrument used
in this project is the Model 7850X ICP-MS produced by the company Agilent Technologies. This model
has Ultra-High Matrix Induction (UHMI), which can handle samples with up to 25% solids, avoiding the
dilution time trap. It is equipped with a helium mode collision cell and half mass correction that removes the
18
==PAGE:29==
3.4
Sample analysis
2nd of July 2023
polyatomic and double charged ion interferences with Kinetic Energy Discrimination (KED) and the M2+
correction feature in MassHunter respectively (Nelson & Jones, 2021). It has a MicroMist glass concentric
nebulizer, a double pass Scott spray chamber that operates at 2 °C, and a quartz torch with a 2.5 mm id
injector. The interface is equipped with a Platinum tipped skimmer and sample cone (Nelson & Jones, 2021).
It is capable of measuring Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Zn, As, Sr, Mo, Cd, Hg, and Pb. The internal standard
solution used is Ytterium.
The ICP-MS samples were acidified to 2 % HNO3 and 1% HCl(v/v) to help
stabilize multiple chemically unstable elements such as Hg and Sb (Nelson & Jones, 2021). The UHMI added
an argon gas flow which dilutes the sample aerosols before reaching the torch, and the samples were analyzed
in the helium collision mode in the ORS4 collision cell. As CSO contains solids of different sizes, there could
be unpredictable matrix-based polyatomic interferences that need to be removed. The Helium flow in the
Helium collision mode was controlled to be 5 mL/min to measure Cr, Fe, Ni, Zn, and As, and no gas mode
was used to measure Pb. The chloride-based interferences are automatically removed while using the Helium
collision mode. Data were processed on the MassHunter 5.2 Workstation Software (v. D.01.02).
To get more consistent results and lighten the technician’s workload, the samples were grouped together in
two larger batches consisting of 9 and 14 experiments, with 7 samples from each experiment. Unfortunately,
three experiments were overlooked by the technician, so they were analyzed in a separate batch. Before each
batch, the ICP-MS was calibrated with a standard, which was measured three times to obtain a reference
value (Ref. value) and Relative Standard Deviation (%RSD). It was further investigated how much of the
reference value, also known as the expected value, was retrieved as a quality control of the data, which will
be referred to as %Recovery. From the literature, it was found that the acceptance criteria for the use of
ICP-MS were the %Recovery in the standard of 80∼120% and a relative standard deviation (%RSD) of <20%
(Zhang et al., 2016; Sun & Zhang, 2020). As mentioned above, there could be unpredicted interferences in
the matrix, but to minimize these interferences, the samples were diluted before analysis. Unfortunately, the
dilution meant that the only heavy metal which could still be detected was Fe. Hence diluting the samples
before analysis was only done once. Fortunately, there was enough water left in the samples to be able to
reanalyze them undiluted. Figure 6 depicts the schematic diagram of the Agilent 7850X ICP-MS instrument

3.4.2
Inductively Coupled Plasma Optical Emission Spectroscopy
The concentration of TP was measured using a technique called Inductively Coupled Plasma Optical Emission
Spectrometry (ICP-OES) in this project. The principle of ICP-OES is based on the fact that ions and atoms
can absorb energy and excite electrons to higher energy levels. The instrument uses Argon plasma heat as
an energy source, operating at a temperature of 10,000 Kelvin (9,727 °C). When electrons return from high
energy levels to lower ones, they emit light at a specific wavelength that corresponds to the energy required
for the transition. The number of atoms or ions transitioning between energy levels is measured based on the
Beer-Lambert law by measuring the amount of light from this specific wavelength. The Avio 200 ICP Optical
Emission Spectrometer from Perkin Elmer, which can measure elements between 200 nm and 500 nm, was
used for ICP-OES. Yttrium was used as an internal standard, and the detector was operated in Radial mode
with an optical wavelength of 213.617 for P detection. A total of 26 samples were analyzed, after acidification
with 2% HNO3 and 1% HCl (v/v). The instrument is equipped with a baffled, cyclonic Twister spray chamber,
a central transfer tube, and a MicroMist nebulizer. The Charge-Coupled Device (CCD) detector measures
the wavelength range around the emission line and performs background correction measurements. The data
was processed using Syngistix™for ICP software v. 2.0 from Perkin Elmer. Figure 7 provides a schematic
overview of the Avio 200 ICP-OES instrument.

4.4
Economic perspective
The wastewater tax law (in Danish: Spildevandsafgiftsloven), is a Danish law about the tax for emitting
wastewater to lakes, streams and the ocean (Skatteministeriet, 2016). From this, it might seem that the
emission of wastewater from CSOs should be taxable, but by July 2023, it is not yet being taxed. This is
mainly due to the lack of precise data that could be used to estimate the discharges more precisely, which is
one of the main reasons for the MUDP project.
The political attention regarding CSOs, and the reduction hereof, has become increasingly more discussed
in recent years, and in 2019 it was brought up to consultation in the environment committee of the Danish
parliament.
The minister of Environment at the time, Lea Wermelin, said that the Danish water-utility
companies should have more incentives to reduce CSOs, but the lack of data regarding the amount and
the constituents of the CSO water was the biggest obstacle to implementing this at the time (Rothenborg,
2019). In the Danish government foundation from 2022, it is mentioned that one of their goals is to reduce
the number of CSOs. Furthermore, they would like to change the wastewater fee to make the emissions of
untreated wastewater from CSOs more expensive than treated wastewater (Statsministeriet, 2022).
This goes to show that significant economic incentives in the treatment of discharged water from CSOs lie
ahead, and possibly new and more economic solutions have to be investigated.
Constructed wetlands as
receivers of discharge from CSO have been investigated with regard to efficiency and costs, and compared
to the prevention of CSOs by retention basins. When comparing the potential reduction of CSO volumes
and duration, it has been shown that treating the CSOs before discharge to the receiving waters is more
cost-effective compared to preventing CSOs (Quaranta et al., 2022). As this considers constructed wetlands
it cannot directly be compared to the chemical treatment investigated in this project, but it goes to show
that the way of thinking about CSOs might need new and innovative solutions. One of these is the chemically
enhanced treatment of CSOs.
The coagulant and flocculant used for this thesis were chosen mostly due to their availability at the beginning of
the experimental phase, hence they were not the exact same as was used in the MUDP project. As mentioned
in Section 3.1, for the sake of this thesis they are deemed to be comparable based on their properties. Based
on this it is assumed that they would be in the same price range, which for the MUDP project is 10 DKK/kg
for the coagulant and 30 DKK/kg for the flocculant (L. Vezzaro, personal communication, 29 June 2023). To
check whether the investigated treatment could be an economically viable solution, a simple cost analysis from
the results will be conducted. In this, it is assumed that the coagulant is capable of achieving approximately
the same removals without the addition of flocculant, and furthermore looking solely at the treatment of
simulated CSO I, as it is the worst kind of simulated CSO water. The reason for leaving out the flocculant
is based on what is mentioned in Section 4.2.5. It was found that it might be advantageous to remove the
42
==PAGE:53==

flocculation process entirely from the treatment of the CSOs, as it did not show any significant TP removal,
within an acceptable range from the allowed amount in the discharge permit. As the flocculant has also shown
to be the more expensive part of the treatment compared to the coagulant, it would be an easy choice to
remove this to save money. If assuming that the optimal dosing of coagulant in the case of simulated CSO I
is 6 mg Fe(III)/L in which an absolute removal of approximately 2 mg TP/L is achieved, the total removed
amount in a year is calculated to be approximately 380 kg TP. This comes from multiplying the removed
amount with the total discharged volume to Sjælsø in a year (Section 1.1). This can be seen to be the same
as the amount that is being discharged each year from the CSO. From the concentration of the coagulant,
it is found that approximately 1,140 kg of Fe(III) has to be used, and as the used coagulant had a 12%
concentration of Fe(III), the total amount for the use of coagulant evaluates to around 95.000 DKK a year.
This is then compared with the worst-case scenario of needing to restore the lake after irreversible damage
by the discharged water from CSOs. It is found that a lake restoration lasting five years utilizing the method
of Phoslock, which is a method for settling phosphorus, costs 139,200 SEK/hectare (Sellergren et al., 2023).
This gives a total cost of approximately 25.5 million DKK for Sjælsø over a period of five years. The cost
of coagulant used in five years is 475.000 DKK, which still is way lower than the price of lake restoration.
It also needs to be taken into account that the cost of coagulant is far from the only cost connected with
the chemical treatment of CSOs, but it still leaves a significant amount of money to set up and operate the
system over the five-year period. It should be noted that these calculations rely on several assumptions, but
they can be used as an estimate for the potential costs for the removal of TP.
If the sediment can be collected from the basins, and the P content is high enough, it could be relevant to
utilize it, even though the amount of heavy metals might be increased compared to pure sedimentation (Ida
& Eva, 2021). This kind of sediment can then be utilized as fertilizer, hence it is also an economic gain from
treatment of CSOs.

Improvements for further work
If further work were to be conducted on this topic it is recommended to work with real-life wastewater from
the CSO in Sjælsø, instead of collecting the raw wastewater from the Sjælsø WRRF and afterwards diluting it
with DI-water. As aforementioned, this was unfortunately not possible for this project. Wastewater collected
from the CSO could then be used for similar experiments to try and replicate the results. From these results,
the dosing of coagulant and flocculant could be adjusted for further work with in situ chemical treatment of
CSOs, to investigate whether the results from the laboratory can be replicated in real-life scenarios.
If further work were to be conducted in the laboratory it is recommended that the samples to be analysed
are pooled together to get them analysed on the same day, to avoid variations due to different calibrations of
instruments, as seen in Table 10. Even though it was seen that both %RSD and recovery rate were within

between samples due to calibration.
Another factor that could affect the variation between the experiments is the way that the coagulant and
flocculant were measured and added to the jars. This was done by syringes with which it was somewhat
challenging to know if the exact amount of chemicals were added. The reason for utilizing these kinds of
syringes was due to the access to a lot of them, and due to the need for varied amounts to be added to the
jars. This could have caused some minor variations to the results, but as it was conducted in the same way
throughout all the experiments it is expected to not have any significant impact on the results and hereby
conclusion of this project. It is furthermore expected that some uncertainty is linked with the dosing of
coagulant and flocculant at the dosing points to the sewer systems, especially as significantly larger amounts
are to be added to the sewer. Due to this, it is concluded that the uncertainties experienced in the lab are
not significant compared to the uncertainties that could potentially be experienced in the real-life example.
Lastly, it is important to mention that every sewer system is different with regards to flow, levels and compo-
sition of pollutants, amount of water, the receiving body of water etc., making this kind of solution difficult
to implement. When considering how to treat the potentially growing amount of CSOs happening each year,
and hereby also the amount and extent of the pollution, the specific CSO and surrounding waters should be
considered (Vezzaro et al., 2022).

Conclusion
The findings from the preliminary and subsequent jar tests have provided valuable insights into using the
coagulation-flocculation method in the chemically enhanced treatment of CSOs. A proposal on the optimal
conditions for treating CSOs with coagulation-flocculation was found. The preliminary jar tests have provided
valuable insights into how different coagulant dosages affect turbidity, absorbance, conductivity and pH.
Turbidity and absorbance were effectively ruled out as measures, while the pH only decreased marginally
with increasing dosage of acidic coagulant. The conductivity fluctuated and provided limited insights into the
removal of turbidity.
The TN and TOC were also investigated to see whether they could be removed, but no clear removal was
observed from adding coagulant and flocculant. Further jar tests provided insights into the effect of mixing
conditions, coagulant, and flocculant dosage on TP and heavy metals removal. It was found that varying
mixing conditions had a limited influence on removing TP and heavy metals.
It was observed that the
coagulant dosage of 20 mg Fe(III)/L leads to the highest TP removal rate of around 66 % and an absolute
TP removal of 5.43 mg P/L, whereas for comparable experiments a dosage of 6 mg Fe(III)/L leads to a TP
removal rate of 40 % and an absolute TP removal of 3.05 mg P/L. In terms of high polymer dosages, even
1000 times higher than the maximum allowed polymer dosage in the discharge permit, it has not significantly
improved TP removal, which indicated the used polymer has limited influence on TP removal. Furthermore,
the high polymer dosage also led to between 9-21 % increase in turbidity. From this, it is suggested that
removing the flocculation process entirely can be advantageous in terms of reducing the overall operational
cost of the chemical treatment of CSOs without affecting the magnitude of removal.
In contrast, the heavy metal As had a maximum removal of around 40 % and maximum absolute removal of
around 0.5 µg As/L at a coagulant dosage of around 16 mg Fe(III)/L, Pb had the maximum removal rate of
90 % and maximum absolute removal of 0.5 µg Pb/L at coagulant dosage of 6 mg Fe(III)/L, and Cu had the
maximum removal around 80 % and maximum absolute removal of 35 µg Cu/L with coagulant dosage of 16
mg Fe(III)/L. In contrast, Ni and Zn increased after the addition of coagulant and flocculant, which have also
been observed to originate from the used coagulant (PIX-118).
The dilution of wastewater by stormwater is hard to replicate in the laboratory, but the potential P and
heavy metals pollution from the stormwater run-off has been taken into account as one of the experimental
limitations.
However, the attempt of simulating the first flush and standard CSO was done by artificial
dilution of untreated wastewater, the dilution of wastewater within the CSO is observed to have reduced the
initial turbidity, TP and some heavy metals compared to untreated wastewater.
Taking all results into consideration an optimal dosage of coagulant would be recommended to be in the range
45
==PAGE:56==
2nd of July 2023
of 6-20 mg Fr(III)/L, as it was within this range the overall best removals were found. When looking at the
difference between the removals from 6 to 20 mg Fe(III)/L, which is approximately a three-times increase, we
do not see a corresponding removal, hence we recommend using coagulant around 6 mg Fe(III)/L. The dose
of coagulant would depend on which kind of wastewater needs to be treated and which pollutants one would
like to remove. From an economic point and taking the residual constituents of the coagulant into account,
the less amount of coagulant used is considered to be better.

Calculations and exact values for stock solutions used for jar tests
To accommodate consistency between the performed jar tests, stock solutions of the coagualant (PIX-118)
and the flocculant (BoFloc C 6378 M) were prepared in advance. The following shows the reasoning and
calculations behind.
For the coagulant a stock solution of 1 g active iron liter was prepared. This was done as the active iron
concentration of the original coagulant from Kemira was 12%, which means that per liter of original solution,
0.12 L of active iron was in the solution. Then to get to the 1 g/L stock solution, 1 g/L was divided with
0.12 L of active iron, to get that 8.33 g of the original solution had to be mixed with 1 L of DI-water, which
is 2.0833 g per 250 mL of DI water.
The polymer was diluted to a 12% polymer concentration by Novafos A/S, which gave an original solution of
132 g/L, and to prepare the 1.32 g/L stock solution it was simply diluted 100 times with DI-water.
