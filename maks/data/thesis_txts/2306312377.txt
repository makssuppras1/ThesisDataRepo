Abstract 
 
 
 
Abstract 
The Concerted Action EPBD (CA EPBD) addresses the Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD). It 
aims to contribute to the reduction of energy use in European buildings, through the exchange of 
knowledge and best practices in the field of energy efficiency and energy savings between all 28 EU 
Member States plus Norway [1]. Following this, the heat demand is decreasing and a low temperature 
district heating system is ready to be applied. Several studies have been carried out in order to examine 
how this low temperature district heating system will affect the consumers and the district heating 
network. However, not so many studies have been taken place in order to examine the consequences of 
the low temperature district heating system according to the supply side. This master thesis project tries 
to investigate this subject. By using a modelling and simulation tool called DNA (Dynamic Network 
Analysis) which is developed by the department of Mechanical Engineering of DTU (Denmark’s Technical 
University), the current thesis analyses the effects of low temperature district heating system to a small 
scale biomass Combined Heat and Power (CHP) plant. Additionally, it examines the integration of other 
renewable energy sources such as solar thermal and large heat pumps in the system. 
More specifically, in chapter one, a short introduction where the background, the aim of the thesis project, 
the methodology that was used and the limitations that were faced are presented. In chapter two, a short 
presentation of the history of district heating system takes place and in continuation a description of the 
4th Generation District Heating System and the benefits that this system has to offer both to the consumer 
and the supply side. At the next chapter, chapter three, a comprehensive description of the biomass CHP 
plants and technologies used is aimed. The basic technologies, such as direct combustion and gasification, 
is tried to be presented in the most simple but also coherent way. Then, a detailed presentation of the 
investigated biomass based CHP plant and the processes that are taking place is carried out. Methods, 
such as the flue gas condensation and the top up of the steam cycle with a gas turbine cycle in order to 
increase the efficiency of the plant are also objectives of the study. At the following chapter, chapter four, 
after a short description of DNA, starts the main goal of the master thesis which is the modelling and the 
simulation of a small scale biomass CHP plant and the influence of several supply and return temperatures 
to it. The influence of a low temperature district heating network to the plant’s efficiency and fuel 
consumption are the basic subjects of the investigation but also the effect of reduced return temperature 
to the flue gas condensation method is analysed. The results are presented thoroughly and discussed. As 
mentioned, another important issue that rises with the implementation of low temperature district 
heating system is the better utilisation of renewable energy sources for heating and potentially cooling 
purposes. This is presented in chapter five. Based on the concept of SUNSTORE 4 in Marstal, solar thermal 
and large heat pumps from the master thesis’ point of view are modelled and simulated. Once again, 
results are presented, compared and discussed. Final conclusions and recommendations for expansion of 
the study for future projects are presented accordingly at the chapters six and seven.

Introduction 
 
 
 
Introduction 
1.1. 
Background 
1.1.1. District heating networks 
Renewable energy sources, CHP production and energy conservation and efficiency, are the fundamental 
factors in the climate change response. The feasibility of a district heating system in terms of 
implementing a sustainable energy system based on renewable energy and including substantial 
reductions in the space heating demand is an important subject for several studies. These studies 
conclude that the role of district heating is significant as well as that district heating technologies must be 
further developed to decrease grid losses, exploit synergies, and thereby increase the efficiencies of low-
temperature production units in the system. Several conditions contribute in order district heating to be 
more preferable for heat supply and distribution. One important condition is that the heat demands must 
be concentrated in order to minimise distribution costs and heat losses. It is obvious, that areas that are 
underpopulated need higher investments for distribution and present higher losses [2]. 
1.1.2. Combined heat and power biomass based plants 
According to the ambitious target of weaning off fossil fuels by 2050, more and more convenient CHP 
plants are replaced by biomass CHP plants. In the content of this thesis the advantages and disadvantages 
of biomass CHP plants will be presented and a thorough study of the several technologies will be carried 
to develop the important background in order to create the most precise model of a CHP biomass plant 
for the simulations.  
1.1.3. Motivation for this thesis topic 
A number of demonstration projects have proven that the district heating supply temperature at slightly 
above 50°C can meet the end-user’s Space Heating (SH) and Domestic Hot Water (DHW) demands in 
central-northern European climates, in properly designed and operated district heating networks and in-
house installations.  
Several studies have been carried out to examine how the network should be transformed in order to 
work under low temperature district heating and satisfy the demand especially of existing buildings. Many 
case studies are presented in order to demonstrate the benefit of low temperature district heating both 
for the network efficiency and for the consumer. 
On the other hand, according to a thorough research in literature not so many studies have be done in 
order to examine the effect of low temperature district heating to the supply side, meaning the CHP plants 
and also the renewable sources of heat such as solar thermal energy or large scale  heat pumps . 
1.1.4. Literature study 
In order this thesis to be written, several hundred of papers, articles, master thesis, PhD thesis, books and 
internet sites and sources were studied. The above mentioned literature was originated from several 
fields about the production, distribution and consumption of power and heat. It was mandatory to gain 
thorough knowledge at the field of district heating network, at the field of CHP plants and the several 
technologies used, at the field of renewable energy sources such as solar energy and at the field of large

1.2. 
Aim of the thesis 
The scope of this master thesis is to investigate the effects of low temperature district heating from the

temperature renewable sources like solar thermal and large heat pumps in the low temperature district 
heating network.  
1.3. 
Methodology 
The above investigation will be carried out by the thermal plant process simulation tool called DNA 
(Dynamic Network Analysis).  DNA is the present result of an ongoing development at the Department of 
Mechanical Engineering of Technical University of Denmark. The component library and the existing 
models of the tool will be used in order to evaluate plant performance under different design and 
operational conditions and in the case that is completely crucial there will be an attempt to form new 
models. 
1.4. 
Assumptions and limitations 
In general, DNA gives the ability to create and integrate new components and mediums. This has to be 
done in FORTRAN. Unfortunately, the insufficient knowledge of FORTRAN and the limited time of the 
thesis had as result to use only the existent components. This had as a consequence to not simulate an 
Organic Rankine Cycle due to the working fluid in it which is not included in the database of DNA. 
Furthermore, the simulation of a large heat storage on DNA didn’t succeed to be done. Even if it didn’t 
have a vital role in the present project it is proposed as further work. The whole simulation is based in a 
steady state. In order, a dynamic simulation to be carried out the combination between DNA and Matlab 
is considered necessary. Due to the limited time of the master thesis this combination didn’t carry out. 
In order a fair comparison between high and low district heating temperatures to take place, a constant 
temperature difference between supply and demand is assumed in every case and also a constant mass 
flow of water for district heating. This lead to the same heating load for every case. However this has also 
a practical meaning [3].  
In the modelling and simulation process, an assumption that could be mentioned is that the heat losses 
and the pressure drops in the several components of the model were neglected. This could probably 
resulted to a bit higher plant efficiency than in reality. However, as this assumption applied in every case 
the comparison between the several simulations remains valid and fair.

Low temperature district heating network – design and benefits 
 
 
 
2. Low temperature district heating network – design and benefits 
2.1. 
History and development of district heating network 
District heating systems have been developed since 1880 in USA. At the beginning, the first generation of 
district heating systems were using steam as the heat carrier. Typical components were steam pipes in 
concrete ducts, steam traps, and compensators. The basic reason for that was that pumps had not been 
invented yet so the power of steam was used in order to circulate the heat.  Today, such systems using 
steam can be considered an outdated technology, since high steam temperatures generate substantial 
heat losses and severe accidents from steam explosions have even killed pedestrians. The condensate 
return pipes have often corroded, giving less condensate returns and lower energy efficiency. The primary 
motivation in society for the introduction of these systems was to replace individual boilers in apartment 
buildings to reduce the risk of boiler explosions and to raise comfort. The main part of heat was delivered 
by steam condensation in radiators at the consumers [2, 5].  
In the 1930’s, when the ability to use pressurised hot water as the heat carrier was developed, the second 
generation of systems was introduced, with supply temperatures mostly over 100oC. The basic reason for 
the development of this generation was to achieve fuel savings and better comfort by utilising CHP plants 
in urban areas. Typical components were water pipes in concrete ducts, large tube-and-shell heat 
exchangers, and material-intensive, large, and heavy valves [2, 5].  
In the 1970’s, mostly due to the oil crisis a focus on energy efficiency took place. For this reason, a third 
generation of systems was developed. Pressurised water is still the heat carrier, but the supply 
temperatures are often below 100oC. This third generation is sometimes referred to as “Scandinavian 
district heating technology”, since many district heating component manufacturers are Scandinavian. 
Typical components are prefabricated, pre-insulated pipes directly buried into the ground, compact 
substations using plate stainless steel heat exchangers, and material lean components. At the same 
period, fuels like biomass and waste started to replace fossil fuels. Additionally, the integration of solar 
and geothermal as sources for heat started to be considered [2, 5]. 
As it can be seen from this short description of the history of the district heating system the efforts were 
focused towards lower distribution temperatures, material lean components, and prefabrication leading 
to reduced manpower requirements at construction sites. Following the evolution in the past three 
generations, a future fourth generation of district heating technology should comprise lower distribution 
temperatures, assembly-oriented components, and more flexible pipe materials.  
2.2. 
Low temperature district heating network 
2.2.1. Design temperatures – supply and return 
Several studies and projects that carried out came to the conclusion that the new and renovated buildings 
will be able to satisfy their thermal needs with a supply temperature around 50°C.  
Low Temperature District Heating (LTDH) system is defined as a system of district heat supply network 
and its elements, consumer connections and in-house installations, which can operate in the range 
between 50-55°C to 60-70°C supply and 25-30°C to 40°C return temperatures and meet consumer 
demands for thermal indoor comfort and domestic hot water [2].

2.3. 
Effects of the low temperature district heating 
In the future two are the main targets for district heating but also for the entire energy production and 
consumption and these two are the most high energy efficiency as possible and the integration of 
renewable energy sources in order to replace the fossil fuels.  Low temperature district heating system 
has the ability to contribute in both of these goals. 
As mentioned above the new and renovated buildings have managed to low their energy consumption 
for heating. By that the energy efficiency on the demand side presented a high increase. On the other 
hand, the heat losses in the existent district heating network increased. For this reason, a low temperature 
district heating supply has several advantages. First of all, the losses of the low temperature district 
heating supply will be reduced significantly as the network temperatures will be reduced.

Furthermore the integration of renewable energy and low temperature sources in a higher share will be 
more feasible. For example: 
 
Low temperature RE resources: Low supply temperature helps geothermal plants, solar thermal 
plants, both roof applications and largescale solar thermal fields to become more efficient in 
order to contribute to the base load heat demand. 
 
Heat pumps: As the required district heating supply temperature gets lower the efficiency of heat 
pumps is becoming higher.  
 
Surplus heat: The excess heat from industrial processes or by heat recovery from cooling 
processes could be used in a higher share.  
 
Flue gas condensation: The lower return temperature will act beneficial for flue gas 
condensation. Especially for biomass and waste plants where the fuel has a high moisture 
content. 
Finally, according to the network the lower temperatures reduce pipeline thermal stress. The unevenly 
distributed temperature gradient along the pipeline is decreased.  As a consequence, the risk for pipe 
leakage due to thermal stress and related maintenance costs are therefore reduced. Furthermore, 
reduced thermal stress also prolongs lifetime of DH network [4, 5].

3. Design description of a biomass based CHP plant  
3.1. 
General presentation of biomass based power plants 
Biomass as fuel could be recovered from several sources. These are by-products from the timber industry, 
agricultural crops, raw material from the forest, major part of household waste, and demolition wood. 
This recovery of biomass is clearly a renewable energy resource but the amount that is recovered is not 
enough. For this reason, biomass is also cultivated. In case that biomass is cultivated for used as fuel also 
could be considered as a renewable resource. In a general point of view, the amount of carbon dioxide 
that is emitted to the atmosphere when biomass is burned as fuel could be considered equal to the 
amount of carbon dioxide that is absorbed from the plants during the process of growing. Another 
important advantage of biomass in comparison to coal and other fossil fuels, which has to do with the 
power plants, is the low sulphur content of biomass resulting to less acidifying flue gases and by that 
reducing the corrosion of the several parts of the plant [6]. 
A schematic representation of the principal options for the biomass conversion into energetic products is 
reported in the figure below. 
 
Figure 2: Main conversion options for biomass to secondary energy carriers [6] 
In comparison to the fossil fuel plants, biomass plants are significant smaller scale. Biomass plants could 
reach up to a size of 50 MW in size when there are coal plants up to 1000 MW. In their majority biomass 
plants are direct-fired plants. That means that biomass is burned in a boiler to produce high-pressure 
steam and directed to a steam turbine-driven power generator in order to produce electricity.  As it will 
be described more analytically in the following chapters in many applications, steam is extracted from the 
turbine at medium pressures and temperatures and is used for process heat, space heating, or space 
cooling [7].

Another interesting way to treat biomass is biomass gasification. By heating biomass at high 
temperatures, but without having combustion and with controlled amount of oxygen and steam, biomass 
breaks down to form a flammable gas.  In continuation, the produced gas also called synthesis gas, or 
syngas, can be cleaned, filtered, and then burned in a gas turbine in simple or combined-cycle mode.  
Syngas is comparable to landfill gas (LFG) or biogas produced from an anaerobic digester. In very small 
systems, the syngas can be fired in reciprocating engines, microturbines, Stirling engines, or used in fuel 
cells. Gasification technologies using biomass by-products are popular in the pulp and paper industry 
where they improve chemical recovery and generate process steam and electricity at higher efficiencies 
and with lower capital costs than conventional technologies. Pulp and paper industry by-products that 
can be gasified include hogged wood, bark, and spent black liquor [7]. 
3.1.1. Direct combustion CHP systems 
The direct combustion CHP plants are the main subject of the thesis and will be described thoroughly in 
the following chapters. However, in the following paragraphs a very short description of the alternatives 
of this method is considered as appropriate to be done. By this the differences, the advantages and 
disadvantages of each method will come in surface but mostly and more important the proper occasion 
to use each method and furthermore the combination between them will be demonstrated.  
3.1.2. Organic Rankine Cycle (ORC) engine 
As mentioned from the beginning, in order to have a good efficiency from steam turbines, high 
temperatures and pressures are required. If instead of water another working fluid with a lower boiling 
point such as freon, organic or silicone oil is used, the system works more efficiently at lower 
temperatures, pressures and at smaller scale. This cycle is called Organic Rankine cycle. 
Many ORC plants have been installed in geothermal power stations, solar thermal plants and a few ORC 
generators are in operation with industrial waste heat and also for small scale biomass CHP applications. 
The benefits of using an organic working medium in place of water are: 
 
It is less corrosive 
 
The necessity for superheating to ensure there is no droplet formation during the pressure drop 
in the turbine is removed 
 
The turbine can operate at a lower speed, reducing stress on components and allowing direct 
drive of the generator without reduction gearing [9]. 
The ORC technology is based on a long term development with the aim to efficiently use solar energy, 
geothermal energy as well as energy from biomass in decentralised units. 
The right choice of the organic working medium used is very important for an optimised operation of the 
ORC process. Considering the framework conditions given for biomass Combined Heat and Power 
applications (CHP plants), silicon oil is the most appropriate working fluid [10]. In the following figure a 
schematic and the thermodynamic cycle of an ORC cycle is presented.

3.1.3. Gasification 
A different way to treat biomass, instead of directly burning it, is biomass gasification. Gasification is 
applied in order to convert biomass to producer gas. The resulting gas mixture is called syngas or producer 
gas and consists of hydrogen, carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide and methane [11]. Producer gas is in most 
cases currently used as a fuel for heat and power generation in highly efficient CHP systems.

The main advantage of biomass gasification is the high power-to-heat ratio which enables a considerable 
reduction of pollutant emissions from power generation. Other advantages of biomass gasification are 
the multiple possibilities for the use of producer gas which can be applied for the production of heat, 
power and biofuels. The disadvantages of biomass gasification are the relatively complex plant operation, 
problems with gas cleaning and the lack of long term practical experience from biomass gasification 
plants. Biomass gasification along with the water-steam power process and the ORC process one of the 
few proven technologies for heat and power generation from biomass fuels [13]. 
The producer gas can be used in more efficient power generation systems called combined-cycles, which 
combine gas turbines and steam turbines to produce electricity. The efficiency of these systems can reach 
60 percent [14]. 
3.2. Basic description of the investigated biomass based CHP plant 
Based on the existence literature and the most well proven technologies for which there is a long term 
practical experience it was decided that for a small scale biomass CHP plant between 5-15 MW the most 
suitable technology is the steam Rankine cycle with steam superheating.  
Furthermore the potential of large biomass-fired power plants is limited due to the transportation 
distances as also to the cultivation area for biomass. 
For practical applications, three design types and operational modes of steam plants can be distinguished:  
1. Back-pressure plants: In these plants the total waste heat from the condensation of the steam is 
used. The back-pressure of the turbine corresponds to the temperature needed for the heat 
utilization. 
2. Condensing plants: These plants are used only for power production without heat utilization. For 
this reason, the back-pressure and the corresponding temperature are as low as possible to 
achieve maximum electrical efficiency 
3. Extraction plants: These plants are used for power production and also for variable heat output. 
Such plants have the ability for a variable extraction of steam at an intermediate pressure and 
temperature level for heat utilization with the remaining steam being utilized to drive an 
additional low-pressure section of the turbine in condensing mode. It combines the advantages 
of back-pressure and condensing plants but exhibits higher complexity. 
CHP production, or co-generation, is performed by back-pressure plants and by extraction plants. 
Condensing plants exclude heat utilization and are thus for dedicated power production. Reasonable 
efficiencies of condensing plants demand medium or, better, large scale power plants ranging from 
at least 25MWe to more than 500MWe, since smaller applications achieve only poor electrical 
efficiencies. Back-pressure steam turbines with utilization of the total waste heat from the 
condensation of the steam are typically in the range 0.5–5MWe, while extraction turbines are typical 
for applications of greater than 5MWe [15]. 
Back-pressure plants are mainly used for applications with an almost constant heat demand for 
several 1000 hours per year and are of interest, for example, in wood industries with a continuous 
heat demand. Within a limited range of operation, the plant load can be controlled as a function of 
==PAGE:20==

the heat demand. In addition, an emergency cooler is needed for safety reasons; this enables a heat 
transfer to the ambient air to avoid overheating of the plant in cases of interrupted heat demand. 
For this reason back-pressure plants are not suitable for district heating applications where the 
heating load is variable during the day and also from season to season. According to the above the 
steam cycle technology that is related to our interest is the extraction plant. 
3.3. Detailed presentation of the components and processes of the investigated 
biomass based CHP plant  
The biomass plant that is decided to be simulated according to the above is a small-medium scale biomass 
extraction plant of a power production of around 10 MWe. A comprehensible presentation of the 
components and the processes that are taking place is presented below.  
At the beginning, the fuel which is dry wood with an LHV of 19 MJ/kg is inserted in the burner with 
atmospheric air at a temperature of 250C and an excess air ratio λ=1.5. The flue gas that is produced is 
directed to the Heat Recovery Steam Generator, HRSG unit. This unit consists of three components which 
are the economizer, the evaporator and the superheater. These three components are three heat 
exchangers. From the other side of these three heat exchangers pressurized water is coming. This water 
at low pressure of 1-2 bar was led through a feed water pump, which increased the pressure to the high 
pressure level of 80 bar (point 3 to point 5 in Figure 6). The pressurized water is heated up to the 
evaporation temperature in the economiser (point 4), then evaporated to saturated steam (point 5), and 
finally superheated in the superheater section to dry steam (point 1). The live steam produced of this 
process has a temperature of 5000C and a pressure of 80 bar. The steam is expanded at an extraction 
turbine, which provides the kinetic energy to the generator. The extraction turbine gives the opportunity 
to use steam at an intermediate pressure level for heat production. This enables operation of the plant at 
maximum overall efficiency in periods with high heat demand and at maximum electrical efficiency in 
periods with low heat demand. The steam at the end of the turbine is led to the condenser, where the 
remaining condensation enthalpy needs to be transferred to the heating medium. The two streams are 
mixed in the deaerator from where water at low pressure leaves and is led to the feed pump to start the 
cycle again.

3.4. Flue gas condensation 
The most effective way to recover energy from the flue gas – and in many cases also an economically 
interesting technology – is flue gas condensation. In addition to the high energy recovery potential of this 
process (up to 20 per cent of the energy input by the biomass fuel related to the Net Calorific Value (NCV)), 
dust precipitation efficiencies of 40–75 per cent can be achieved. Furthermore, there is the possibility of 
preventing condensation of the flue gas in the chimney down to ambient temperatures of about –10°C 
[15]. 
==PAGE:22==

Figure 7: Flue gas condensation unit for biomass combustion plants [15] 
The whole plant normally consists of three parts: the economizer (recovery of sensible heat from the flue 
gas), the condenser (recovery of sensible and latent heat from the flue gas) and the air preheater 
(preheating the combustion air and the air used to dilute the saturated flue gas before entering the 
chimney). 
The amount of energy that can be recovered from the flue gas depends on the moisture content of the 
biomass fuel, the amount of excess oxygen in the flue gas and the temperature of the cooling water, in 
this case the return water of the DH network. The lower the temperature of the return water, the higher 
the amount of latent heat that can be recovered when the flue gas is cooled below the dew point.

Figure 8: Efficiency of biomass combustion plants with flue gas condensation units as a function of flue gas temperature [15] 
 
With regard to economic aspects, flue gas condensation is generally recommendable for biomass 
combustion plants. Flue gas condensation units are of interest if wet biomass fuels are utilized (average 
moisture content 40–55wt% (w.b.)) and if the return temperature of DH network is below 60°C [15]. 
In case of a low temperature district heating network, a great part of the heat obtained from the flue gas 
condensation unit can be recuperated. The process could be described as follows: In the economizer the 
flue gas is cooled down from 180 °C to about 70 °C. At the next stage, the condenser, reduces the 
temperature of the flue gas to approximately 45 - 50°C. As the dew point of the flue gas is in the range of 
600C, condensation and consequently recovery of latent heat starts at this stage. With the energy 
obtained from the economizer and the condenser, the district heating network could be heated. At the 
third stage of the condensation unit, the air pre- heater, the flue gas is cooled down to about 35 - 45°C by 
warming up ambient air. The pre-heated air could be used as combustion air and also for drying the fuel 
[16]. 
In addition to the mentioned energetical advantages of the flue gas condensation unit, dust emissions are 
also reduced significantly. This is due to the fact that, as soon as the dew point of the flue gas is reached, 
dust particles serve as condensation nuclei for water droplet formation. About 60% - 70% of the dust load 
at the inlet of the flue gas condensation unit are precipitated with the condensate [16]. 
To sum up, the advantages of the flue gas condensation unit lie in the significant increase in plant 
efficiency from about 85% to even more than 100% (related to the fuel NCV) and efficient dust separation, 
which reduces the particle emissions of the plant to a level < 50 mg/Nm³ [16].

4. Modelling the process on DNA 
4.1. General description of DNA 
For the simulations of the biomass CHP plant and the integration of the renewable sources that will be 
proposed, DNA was used. DNA is a modelling tool and a simulation program for simulation of thermal 
systems such as power plant processes, both in steady state and dynamic operation. 
 The program is written in FORTRAN and the components are implemented in a template which makes it 
easy to implement new.  
DNA has been demonstrated in a large number of projects at DTU. The projects have mainly used steady 
state simulations but the dynamic solver is well documented and has been tested in several projects. 
Especially when DNA is combined with Matlab the dynamic opportunities are more easily accessible [18]. 
The continued development of the program and the use of it from many students and academic staff has 
resulted to improvement of user friendliness, efficiency and flexibility. Moreover, the component library 
has been extended and includes models of: 
• Heat exchangers 
• Burners 
• Gasifiers 
• Turbo machinery 
• Fuel cells 
• Fuel pre-treatment, e.g., dryers and decanters 
• Energy stores 
• Engines 
• Valves 
• Controllers 
As well as more specialized components and utility components. Users may implement additional 
components, which are usually written in FORTRAN77 [19]. 
For the user, DNA is a modelling language and a command line program parsing and compiling this 
language. DNA has been integrated with the GNU Emacs editor. 
DNA is portable as it is written in FORTRAN77 and is distributed both in binary form (for Microsoft 
Windows) and as source code. This makes it an open source alternative to Aspen+, Gate Cycle, GT Pro, 
Ipsepro, MMS/ACSL, Modelica and similar component-based tools. 
The work with DNA for simulation of a given process, usually starts with a sketch of the flowsheet of the 
process. Each device of the system is enclosed by a control volume, which in DNA terms is a component.

Each component is named. Components are described by a number of equations and a number of 
branches connecting the component to the surroundings [18, 19]. 
The branches of components may be connected by nodes in order to build the system. The nodes are 
numbered. To write a model for use in DNA, each component has to be identified as well as the nodes it 
is connected to and values of all parameters of the component. 
 
Figure 10: The basic elements of a network as modelled by DNA [18] 
The variables in DNA are divided into three types as indicated in the above figure: 
Component variables: These are all dynamic variables (Mass and internal energy of a fluid inside the 
component control volume and internal energy of the solid mass of the component). In addition 
components may define additional algebraic and/or dynamic variables. 
Branch variables: These are variables connected to the streams flowing in and out of a component. 
They are mass flow, specific enthalpy and possibly mixture composition for a fluid flow. Other media like 
these are heat flow, mechanical or electrical power. 
Node variables: The node variable of a fluid flow is pressure. Other node variables are control signals and 
rotational speed of a shaft [19]. 
Each line is started by a keyword. A short description of these keywords is given below: 
System Structure: STRUC inserts a component in the model. 
Media in nodes: Having specified a node connected to a component it may be necessary to tell which 
medium flows in it. This is the case if the component may be used for many different fluids. The keyword 
MEDIA should be used in this case. 
Composition of ideal gas media: To introduce new media in a system two keywords are available. One 
specifies ideal gas compositions, such as oxygen-enriched air. In order to specify such the keyword FLUID 
should be used. Many mass flows will not be ideal gases, e.g., coal or biomass. In order to specify such the 
keyword SOLID should be used. 
Operating Conditions: The operating conditions have to be specified. DNA does not require any particular 
variables to be input or output as long as the system of equations resulting from the input makes sense 
mathematically. The keyword that is used is ADDCO. One effect of this is that the number of variables in 
the system created by the components in the model minus the number of equations created by the 
components must equal the number of additional operating conditions.

Start guesses: DNA contains a numerical solver and for this reason requires all variables to have a guess 
value to iterate from until the solution to the system of equations is found. The keyword that is used is 
START.  
Parameter variation: In order to run simulations with varied parameters DNA includes the keyword SIMUL 
which will end the current model setup and allow for another simulation of the same model with different 
conditions.  
Title of the model: TITLE gives the model a title. 
Comments: The keyword C is used in front of a sentence in order this sentence to be recognized from 
DNA as a comment. Another way to put comments is delimited by braces {}. They are also used for 
removing parts of a model code [18].

air from flue gases is not a net energy supply as it originates from the flue gases. In opposite case, that 
there was a supplementary firing for example, in the formula the extra fuel consumption should be added 
at the denominator of the formula.  
4.2.3. Modelling of a BIGCC power plant 
In order to simulate a BIGCC plant some new components should be introduced. First of all, there is a 
wood dryer simulated by the component DRYER_01 in order to dry the wet biomass fuel. The dryer uses 
the exhaust from the HRSG to dry the raw biomass. It is followed by the gasifier which will produce the 
syngas. This is simulated by the component GASIFI_3. 
The produced syngas will be directed to a gas compressor simulated in DNA by the component 
COMPRE_1. 
 In continuation there is the Gas Turbine Cycle also known as Brayton Cycle. It consists of a gas combustor 
where the combustion of the syngas will take place, another compressor will be used to compress the air 
for the combustion, a gas turbine and a generator for producing electricity. The combustor is simulated 
by the component GASBUR_2. The compressor will be simulated by the component COMPRE_1. The gas 
turbine by the component TURBIN_1 and the generator by the component SIM_GENE. 
The other part of the simulation, the steam cycle, remain the same as in the above simulation of the 
biomass extraction plant. 
It should be mentioned here that a basic study of a BIGCC was made so a simple gas turbine cycle was 
simulated. In case that a more assiduous study should take place, the ability of recuperating and/or 
reheating of the GT cycle should also be taken into account.

4.4. Cultivation Area 
A critical factor that should taken into account when we are using biomass as a fuel is the cultivation area, 
namely the necessary area to cultivate the chosen fuel (wood). This parameter can be calculated by the 
following equation: 
𝐴𝑐𝑢𝑙𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛= 𝜅

 [21] 
 
 
Where: 
𝐴𝑐𝑢𝑙𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛: Cultivation area in km2 
𝑃𝑒𝑙: Electrical power in MW 
𝐻 : Plant’s operating hours during one year 
𝑛𝑝𝑙𝑎𝑛𝑡: Efficiency of the plant

According to the above formula for a biomass CHP plant of 10 MWe which is in operation for 5000 
hours/year and with an electrical efficiency of 0.3 or 0.5 the cultivation area that is calculated equals to 
60 km2 or 36 km2 accordingly. 
As it can be seen this is a significant large area for a plant of just 10 MW. Even if some amount of this 
biomass is acquired from residues or imports still remains a large occupied area. Furthermore, importing 
biomass from long distances loses its advantage as renewable source and also could add an extra cost to 
the operation of the plant.

5.2.1. Solar thermal

efficiency of the plants as very thoroughly studied. Although, as also mentioned biomass availability in

areas could be probably substituted by solar energy. 
One disadvantage of solar power plants is their variability and seasonality. This could be faced by the 
combination with a biomass CHP plant and the use of a large heat storage on the ground.

Systems with light structures and low cost technology for process heat applications up to 4000C could be 
obtained with parabolic through collectors (PTCs). PTCs can effectively produce heat at temperatures 
between 50 and 4000C [24].

5.2.2. Large scale heat pump and heat storage 
An interesting integration in a district heating network are a heat pump and a heat storage. When these 
technologies are in operation further RES, such as solar thermal can be efficiently integrated. Such 
measures will allow for the integration of up to 40 percent of fluctuating RES into the electricity supply 
without affecting the overall system efficiency.  
The principle of operation of a heat pump can be presented shortly as following: a refrigerant is used as 
an intermediate fluid to absorb heat where it vaporizes, in the evaporator, and then to release heat where 
==PAGE:50==
HEATING SUPPLY FROM RENEWABLE SOURCES IN A LOW-TEMPERATURE DISTRICT HEATING SYSTEM                                                
                              
 
40

the refrigerant condenses, in the condenser. The refrigerant flows through insulated pipes between the 
evaporator and the condenser, allowing for efficient thermal energy transfer at relatively long distances. 
In case that the heat pump is used also for cooling, it employs a reversing valve to reverse the flow of 
refrigerant from the compressor through the condenser and evaporation coils. In the following figure a 
reversible heat pump is presented. 
 
 
Figure 26: Schematic of a heat pump [29] 
 
The opportunity of large heat pumps to use various different heat sources to drive the process like waste-
heat from industrial processes, heat in waste water, ambient air, ground, lakes or the sea is significant. 
Large heat pumps have a relatively high efficiency, but on the other hand also relatively high investment 
costs. In order to invest in large heat pumps, a relatively high number of full load hours should be 
guaranteed. The suggestion is to operate large heat pumps in all hours where the ratio between the 
electricity price (in the given power region) and the district heating price (in the given area) is below the 
COP of the heat pump. For at heat pump with a reasonable COP, i.e. around 3, this can occur many hours 
of the year.  
As mentioned, large heat pumps can be connected to thermal storages in the district heating system and 
thereby represent a flexible electricity demand. Furthermore, large heat pumps will have the ability to 
relieve CHP plants from heat bound power production and thereby increase system flexibility and support 
wind power integration.  
More specific, it could be said, that when wind power is high and the power production from CHP plants 
should be decreased and accordingly the heat produced from the CHP, the heat demand could be satisfied 
from the heat storage, and when wind power is low, feasibility of increasing power production at CHP 
plants is improved, since surplus heat production can be stored. The obvious benefit from this set-up is

that the heat pumps could be turned off in longer periods with high electricity prices and in peak load 
hours, determining for the need for peak/reserve capacities. 
Overall, large heat pumps are therefore suited for flexibly activating demand in periods of up to several 
days. As such, they can provide intra-week balancing, in addition to intra-day and intra-hour balancing. 
Conventional heat storages due to storage capacity limitations and storage losses are not suited for 
storing heat across months. The solution could be to up-scale to very large heat storages. In Denmark 
there are a few large seasonal thermal storage demonstration plants. The use of seasonal thermal 
storages have traditionally been focusing on improved utilisation of solar thermal, but can also be relevant 
for large heat pumps [30].

Conclusions 
 
 
 
6. Conclusions 
The main goal of the project has been fulfilled. The results occurred from the simulations of a small scale 
biomass extraction CHP plant point out the beneficial effect of a lower temperature district heating 
network to the electrical efficiency of the plant. More specific, going from an existent network of 900C 
supply and 600C return temperature to a low temperature district heating network of 500C supply and 
200C return temperature an improvement of 3.9% in electrical efficiency can be noticed which equals to 
a 13.9% improvement in power production for this small scale biomass plant of 10MWe. 
From the examination of how the low temperature district heating network will affect the process of flue 
gas condensation, the results are also really positive. Due to the ability to reduce much further the 
temperature of flue gases that are used to heat the water for district heating and by that to condensate 
more water the heat that obtained is higher. By quantifying this observation and according to the results, 
the improvement in total efficiency of the low temperature district heating case compared to the case of 
high temperature is 31.9% due to the extra heat gained from the flue gases. 
In the case that the exergy of the flue gases is also used for preheating the air for combustion by an air 
preheater used before the flus gas condensation unit, a small reduction will be noticed in the fuel 
consumption which will lead to less cost for fuel and less emissions. In terms of numbers, the calculated 
electrical efficiency of the plant for low temperature district heating increased by 1.7% compared to the 
case of low temperature district heating but without air preheating. 
A more effective way to treat biomass is by a Biomass Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle. The 
advantages of these plants are as already mentioned the high electrical efficiency leading to better 
utilisation of the fuel and lower emissions. One disadvantage is that this type of plants are more suitable 
for bigger scale power plants. Although, just for the investigation of how the lower district heating 
temperature will affect these plants, it was decided to simulate a BIGCC plant of the same magnitude as 
the above biomass extraction plant. According to the results the lower temperature district heating 
network will have a positive effect to the power production of the plant reaching up to 7.1% between the 
high temperature district heating network and the low temperature district heating network. 
In continuation of the investigation of the benefits of low temperature district heating network the higher 
contribution of solar thermal and heat pumps came to foreground. This is important for another reason. 
When the biomass that is used as fuel is cultivated and not taken as residue from several activities needs 
large occupied areas. For the small scale biomass plant of 10 MWe that used for the simulations this area 
calculated to be 60 km2.  Furthermore, there are places over the world that the cultivation of biomass is 
almost impossible. Instead of that solar thermal energy or heat pumps could be used in order replace part 
or even whole of the biomass used. 
An economic and efficient way to use solar energy is the PTSC technology. According to the simulation of 
a PTSC plant which had the ability to produce superheated steam and drive a steam turbine and by that 
replacing completely the biomass boiler the influence of a low temperature district heating network was 
positive. The improvement in power production from a high temperature district heating network to a 
low temperature district heating network reach up to 14.15%. Furthermore, in the simulation where the 
PTSC plant can produce steam up to 3000C and a biomass boiler is used to superheat this steam in order 
to be useful from the above steam cycle plant, a reduction of 75% of the biomass used is calculated due

for the case of the PTSC plant, is the fact that the area occupied from this system is significant smaller

Finally another way to reduce the biomass usage is the large heat pumps. There are periods of excess 
electricity in several countries due to the renewable energy sources, such as wind energy in Denmark or 
hydro power generation in Norway or photovoltaic installations in other countries. If there is no way to 
export this electricity problems will occur in the electricity network. A solution to this problem is to use 
this electricity in order to operate large scale heat pumps in order to transfer heat from a source to the 
district heating network. These sources could be excess heat from several industrial processes, 
geothermal fields or large heat storages which constructed in order to store seasonably the solar thermal 
energy as in the case of Denmark. The simulation of a large heat pump under low temperature district 
heating network show a reduction of power consumption of 43% and accordingly an increase of the COP 
value of the heat pump of 74% in comparison with a high temperature district heating network. These 
results manifest the great energy savings that low temperature district heating network has to offer to 
the whole spectrum of the district heating and power generation industry.

Discussion and recommendations 
 
 
 
7. Discussion and recommendations 
The current project was initially documented in order to investigate the influence of low temperature 
district heating to the supply side. More specifically to investigate the influence of the low temperature 
district heating to a decentralized biomass power plant though a simulation tool. Through this 
investigation a more ambitious idea came up. The simulation of a state of the art and 100% RES plant 
similar to that of Marstal. During the development of the model several limitations had as a consequence 
some parts of the Marstal plant to be replaced by other and some assumptions to be made. The basic idea 
although remained the same and the results came to prove the beneficial influence of lower temperature 
district heating to the several renewable energy sources that contribute to a district heating network and 
the power production. 
This project could be used as a guideline or better say as a base for further studies. The models created 
for simulations are fully customizable giving the opportunity to be used in several case studies. They can 
become more detailed by the addition of other components and more important they can be combined 
with dynamic models if a software like Matlab is used. 
More specific, in case that a dynamic heat storage is considered as necessary it should be developed in 
Matlab and then integrated in DNA. It is not an easy task and could be considered as another project. 
Furthermore, due to the lack of some organic fluids in the database of DNA an Organic Rankine Cycle 
didn’t managed to be simulated. This could be also a task that involves knowledge of programming in 
FORTRAN. 
Looking the project from the perspective of the district heating load, a dynamic simulation following the 
heat load profile and the load duration curve could have high interest. Although it should be investigated 
first if this could be done in DNA or another simulation tool would be more suitable. 
In order this project to become more valuable and applied should be adapted in special case studies. By 
that, it could be specified and become more detailed. Furthermore, it will gain more value if a techno-
economic analysis and comparison between the above mentioned technologies will take place. Obviously, 
this requires specific cases to be examined. By this way, the optimization of these cases will take place 
and the best available technology will rise up.
