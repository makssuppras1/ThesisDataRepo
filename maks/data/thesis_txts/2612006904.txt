Abstract (English) 
The topic of finding usable transporters for export of substrate or drug delivery is highly relevant, since 
transporters are important for import and export of molecules across biological membranes. However, 
while there are tools to predict transporter substrate specificity or whether or not a protein sequence is 
a transporter, there are no tool that predicts possible transporters for a specific substrate. This has limited 
drug- and cell factory development, since it would demand more time researching any possible 
transporters that could be used, before even initiating the research into the development of the drug or 
cell factory.  
The focus of this project has been on developing a program that can predict possible transporters based 
on the search of a substrate and an organism in the protein interaction database called STRING. 
STRING provides possible proteins based on both experimental evidence on research and primary 
evidence that relies on articles found by ontological searches. The program then filters the hits based 
on three ontological factors: 1) initial annotation, 2) localisation in the cell and 3) function and process 
retrieved from UniProt. This results in suggested transporters of varying similarity, identity, function 
and domains. In order to analyse the results, two types of searches were performed and analysed on 
general sequence similarity and functional phylogenetic inference to known transporters and presence 
of Pfam-domains. This showed that the program could get transporters of varying sequences, domains 
and functionalities, and that it could retrieve the wanted transporters in 78% of the cases, where the 
organisms were specified and where hits could be retrieved from STRING. Though hits could only be 
retrieved in 45% of these specified searches, meaning that the desired transporter could only be retrieved 
in 35% of searches with specified organisms.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Motivation and goal 
Several tools have been developed for predicting transporter substrate specificity or prediction of 
transporters on a sequence-scale compared to specific databases. But no transporter prediction tool 
incorporates a broad spectrum of evidence beyond that of experimental evidence and gives a list of 
possible transporters. Typically, the tools or models are either highly focused on a simple type of 
transporter1, reactions of existing transporters2 or against specific databases3,4. They all try to predict 
something specific about the transporters, instead of giving a comprehensive list of possible transporters 
for given substrates. This have not made it easy for researchers, who are trying to find possible 
transporters for substrates, for instance when researching possible targets for drug transporter for drug 
delivering or carriers for cell-factory development. Due to this issue, this project has been focusing on 
making a transporter prediction program that gives the user a list of possible transporters for a substrate 
or for a substrate in a specific organism. 
Python is a programming language for, which is frequently used in the field of bioinformatics. The basis 
for the Python language is ideal for text parsing and analysis, which is great for sequence analysis on a 
text basis. Python also has access to a broad number of additional packages that makes it easy for the 
user to implement more complex functions in their programs. It is also possible to make functions for 
broader functions in Python, which makes Python an extremely flexible programming language. This 
makes Python the ideal programming language for making the program presented in this thesis.  
The focus will then be to develop a program based on the STRING database that can give the user a 
comprehensive list of possible transporters for a substrate or for a substrate in a specific organism. This 
will use ontological sorting on multiple levels to ensure that both the functional and localization criteria 
are fulfilled.  
1.2 Transporters 
1.2.1 Background 
Transporter proteins serve a major role in cellular metabolism, since they enable active transport of 
substrates in and out of cells5–7, especially for transport of secondary metabolites out of the cell8. This 
uptake and efflux of substrates are of particular importance in both pharmacokinetics for import of 
drugs to specific, cellular compartments7,9,10. In metabolic engineering, and development of cell 
factories5,11. Particularly in the latter case, transporters are relevant, since successful secretion of 
biosynthetic compounds prevents inhibition, cellular toxicity and degradation of the compounds in 
question. In order to counteract this, novel transporters could be introduced as well, when implementing 
novel biosynthetic pathways into the organism. But introduction of too many novel genes can disrupt 
the cells normal functions hence the optimal solution would be to use existing transporters in the cell12. 
Though transporter-less permeability through passive transport is possible and do occur in the absence

In nature, transporters serve both in secretion of secondary metabolites as either signalling molecules, 
adaptation or cellular competition, or in uptake of substrates of metabolite importance into the cell8. 
These transporters belong to many large protein families with a wide distribution of hosts ranging from 
microbes, plants and animals to humans. All of which are involved in the uptake and efflux of different 
metabolites across the cell membranes. The most well-studied transporter groups are the so-called 
primary, which includes H+/H+-ATPases as well as efflux-pumps, and secondary active transporters, 
which includes the MFS(Major facilitator superfamilies)-13 and the SLC(Solute Carrier)-superfamily 
solute transporters14. These transporters achieve movement of compounds down the electrochemical 
gradient in one of two ways. One way is by ATP-hydrolysis for the upward movement (in case of 
primary active transporters14). The other way is by coupled upward movement of substrates to the 
downward movement of other ions like Na+ (in case of secondary active transporters13), thus achieving 
a continuous momentum of exchanging compounds. Proteins of each of these classes work together 
with members of other classes, like the ion channels, to achieve ion homeostasis, ion secretion and re-
establishing the ion balance after action potentials. These account for a lot of the proteins that either are 
imbedded in or interact with the cell membrane, in order to fulfil their role. Along with ATP-dependent 
and independent transporters, another type of transporter also includes channels13. Channels are open 
to both sides of the membranes and catalyse the highly specific movement of ions, molecules or water 
across the membrane down their concentration gradients, also termed “downhill transport”. These 
channels facilitate transport out of the cell. The secondary transporters can also be sorted into uniporters, 
symporters and antiporters, where the latter two have been termed to be among the secondary active 
transporters13. Uniporters generally transport one substrate type and their transport is driven by the 
energy in the concentration gradient of the substrate across the membrane. They move the substrate 
down the concentration gradient from higher to lower concentrations across the membrane. Symporters 
and antiporters on the other hand catalyse the transfer of one substrate against its concentration gradient 
and is driven by the movement of co-transported substrate(s) down their electrochemical gradients. 
These two differ in the relative direction of movement of the transported and co-transported substrates. 
Where symporters transport substrates in a cyclical matter with delivering substrates to low 
concentration gradients and a new substrate in the opposite direction, antiporters only facilitate a one-
way transport of multiple substrates. Like other secondary active transporters, both of them utilise the 
free energy from the concentration gradient of the substrates across the membrane to facilitate the uphill 
transport. This transport of other substrates with the directionality of the transport is given by the 
combined electrochemical potentials of the involved substrates. A visual representation of the 
transporter types can be seen in figure 1 adapted from Bosshart el al. 201913:

1.2.2 Transporter Structures - Transmembrane α-helices and transmembrane β-barrels 
As part of either temporary or complete embedded stage of the transporter process, it is only natural for 
these proteins to have protein structures that support binding to the cell membrane. A structural fold of 
these membrane proteins have been identified to be the TM(transmembrane)-helix13, which are special 
α-helices that are specific for certain transporters. These helices are either shown on the surface of 
transporters that are always embedded in the membrane or are hidden in the protein core and are only 
shown to the environment, when they undergo conformational changes upon interacting with the 
membrane. In some cases, TM-helices have been proposed to be bundled together to make a gating 
mechanism and hence act as a channel32. The TM-helices are typically located close together interlinked 
by loops in the C-terminals of the helices to form bundles, which in some cases like the MFS-
transporters have been identified with a specific sequence. In 1/4 of the cases, these C-terminal motifs 
are similar to Gly-caps of the globular proteins32. There has also been done sequence analysis on the 
TM-helices themselves, though no concrete sequence motif has been found. The TM-helices themselves 
have an average sequence length of 17.3 residues and consists of ~78.5% hydrophobic residues with 
only 4.5-6.6% polar residues32. But despite these helices having been investigated in such details, it is 
difficult to characterise TM-proteins and -domains experimentally. This is because they only exist in 
the lipid bilayer of the cell membrane and are therefore less accessible33. An example of this structure 
is seen in figure 2. 
Certain transporters, like ion transporters34 and porins35, are anchored in the membranes and act by 
letting substrates pass through the membrane36. These passages are enabled by protein structures called 
transmembrane β-barrels, or TMBB. TMBB consists of between 8 and 2237,38 (sometimes 2435, though 
always an equal number) β-strands arranged as antiparallel sheets connected by simple loops38. These 
sheets are connected to the ends of each other, making a cylinder shaped structure with a hollow middle. 
Each strand in a TMBB are about a dozen amino acids long with the residues facing the centrum of the 
cylinder being both polar and non-polar38. These structures are seen exclusively in proteins in the 
bacterial outer membrane, mitochondrial and chloroplast membranes35,39,40. Transporters with TMBB 
perform various functions such as allowing passive diffusion of ions and hydrophilic molecules, specific 
import of substrates, and energy-dependent toxin-export. TMBB’s are involved in substrate transport 
across the cell membrane, as they are the main component of translocation complexes41, along with 
playing critical roles in multiple cell functions, signalling, membrane biogenesis42, and in bacterial

learning methods46–48. Some examples of these are TMpro, which utilises a combination of singular

proteins also will have a bigger core of hydrophobic residues and hence give false positives. There are 
however recently developed methods for predicting TMBB’s that are primarily based on machine 
learning methods like artificial neural networks34,43, and HMM41.

1.2.3 Transporter prediction 
General characterisation of transporters are crucial for application, but experimental characterisation in 
the laboratory are expensive and time-consuming. Therefore, bioinformatic-approaches are strongly 
preferred for both functional, structural and metabolic characterisation3,4,50. Most of the experimental 
issues is due to them being membrane proteins, which make them difficult to isolate, purify and test 
experimentally. Experiments in general on transporters in natural membranes are difficult, since the 
lipid bilayer is a unique environment with a high protein-to-lipid ratio (between 1:1 and 3:1) 6.  Their 
function can be defined on both a molecular, metabolic and physiological level just like any other 
protein and therefore the prediction can become subjective50. There is also the issue on large scale, 
automatic function prediction concerning defined function.  Simply because of the many ways to define 
protein function, a protein can end up having more functions and with no priori straightforward measure 
that can be used to quantify the similarity between the functions of two proteins. A method used for 
functional prediction for unannotated proteins has been function annotation transfer50, where the 
experimentally verified function of an annotated homolog is used to infer the function of the 
unannotated protein. This procedure should be used cautiously, since homology (simply that two 
proteins have a common evolutionary origin) often is confused with similarity of function, like in the 
cases of orthologues against paralogues. Orthologues, which are genes achieved through a speciation 
event in a common ancestor, have more conserved functions than paralogues, which arise from 
duplication events in the same genome. Punta and Ofran, 200850 also claim that functional similarities 
between proteins that are not reflected in homology can exist. This means that transferring functions 
from homologous proteins might not be all that easy or straight forward, hence other methods are 
needed.  
The most common method, used in practice, for predicting homology is through sequence similarity. 
Here, the sequence of the given protein is aligned against a database of annotated proteins and compared 
to the hits from the database with the highest similarities. It will, however, generally leave out remote 
relations, since they would have lower similarity and there are no sequence similarity threshold value 
that guarantees conservation of function. With this being said, a higher sequence similarity increases 
the confidence in a justified functional annotation transfer between the two hits. This also leads to 
another problem, when it comes to the domains in the protein. When searching based on entire 
sequences, sequence alignment can be made between any of the functional domains within the protein 
aligned to the queried sequence. The functional annotation in the database can refer to any of the protein 
domains and if that domain in the queried protein does not align to the hit protein, the functional transfer 
is unjust and it will give rise to misinformation. Some of these problems can be mitigated by using 
phylogenetic inference that frame sequences’ evolutionary relations into a phylogenetic context18. But 
the classic 25-30% sequence identity would not suffice for functional annotation transfer here due to

Similarly to VolSurf+, several models have been developed on congeneric series of compounds,

1.3 Ontology 
Ontology is the concept of identification using words and terms and the practise of extracting knowledge 
of these53. It has therefore been proposed that it is difficult to construct any type of logic without the 
use of ontology. This is why ontology have been used for a long time to describe all units in  various 
fields, especially in informational scientific fields53–56. It has been especially relevant when translating 
scientific and technical texts through concept structures and hence is often used as text-analysis54,55,57.

Ontology-based methods have also been proven to be necessary in constructing both databases and 
knowledge bases, since the definition of ontological categories are the defining, sorting property of the

The richest source for ontological categories are the vocabularies of the natural languages, since that is

ontology-based methods. Finally, the chosen name or term can still diverge from naming conventions 
of other platforms. This can be a problem, when searching multiple databases, since platforms can have 
different definitions of the same entry.  
1.4 TCDB 
TCDB(transporter classification database) is a database for transporter proteins and the research for 
transporter proteins11,61,62. The database contains >10000 unique proteins that represent all of the 
currently known transmembrane molecular transport system families61 and also functions as a genome 
transporter-annotation tool11. Proteins in this database are organised in a 5-level hierarchical system 
with the first two levels being the class and subclass, the next two are family and subfamily, and finally 
the transport system61. TCDB is manually curated, connected to other databases and is recognized by 
the international union for biochemistry and molecular biology. It classifies transporters by use of a 
functional/phylogenetic coding system given as D1.L1.D2.D3.D4, where D is a single digit and L is a 
letter. For protein classes, it classifies transporters as either channels, secondary carriers/transporters, 
primary active transporters, group translocators or transmembrane electron carrier/transporters. 
Remaining proteins are either classified as accessory proteins, empty or insufficiently characterised 
proteins, if they could not be classified as any other transporter class. The number for proteins in the 
classes (D) provide information on11: 
• 
The type of transporter (D1) 
• 
The subtype of transporter like the type of energy source (D2) 
• 
The transporter subfamily (D3) 
• 
The specific transporter with a given polarity, specificity and mechanism (D4) 
The superfamily status is based on the superfamily principle, which is homology on logical equation 
that if A=B and B=C, then A=C independent on the degree of sequence similarity. Even though there 
are a few examples of transporters within families that can use more than a single mode of action or can

1.5 STRING 
The STRING-database is a protein database that includes protein interactions, both direct physical and 
indirect functional associations, which consist of substrate chaining and information relay interactions 
among others57,59,60. It evaluates said associations based on experimental data from sequence analysis 
and on direct evidence from word searches of PubMed articles. The associations between proteins are 
derived from statistical co-occurrence in documents and from natural language analysis60. The 
functional associations are a useful operational term for specific and functional productive interactions 
of any type59,60. The natural language processing combines part-of-speech tagging, semantic tagging 
and a chunking grammar to achieve rule-based extraction of both physical and regulatory interactions60. 
When all the known and predicted associations for a given organism is found, it will make a protein 
network of genome-wide functional connectivity. But unlike other protein network resources, STRING 
differs by: 
• 
Comprehensiveness, since it covers the largest number of organisms and uses the widest input-
sources, including automatic text-mining and computational predictions59 
• 
Usability due to an intuitive interface, which also give programmable access59 
• 
Quality control and traceability with each interaction being annotated with benchmarked 
confidence scores separately for each evidence type and the underlying evidence can be traced 
to their sources59 
The confidence score mentioned in the third point is given to each protein-protein interaction as an edge 
weight (weight on an interaction between protein nodes) scaled between 0 and 1. This confidence score 
indicate the estimated likelihood of whether the interaction is biologically meaningful, specific and 
reproducible given the supporting evidence. The confidence scoring scheme takes co-occurrences 
within sentences, paragraphs and whole documents into account and combines them through an 
optimised weighting scheme that favours co-occurrences within a paragraph60. A named entity 
recognition engine also allows it to read through the abstracts and full texts, making the scoring scheme 
more precise. The supporting evidence for each association is divided in one or more of 7 evidence 
channels dependent on the origin and type of the evidence. The 7 evidence channels are: 
1. The experimental channel, where the evidence comes from actual experiments from 
laboratories (both biochemical, biophysical and genetic) 
2. The database channel, where evidence that are assumed by expert curators from pathway 
databases, are gathered

3. The text-mining channel, where mentions of protein names in all PubMed-abstracts in a

4. The co-expression channel, where gene expression data is normalised from multiple different

STRING also computes a functional association scoring for interaction transfer. This score is founded 
on the idea that most protein-protein interaction interfaces (at least for stably bound protein partners 
located next to each other in the protein complexes) remain well-conserved over time60,68,69. It is based 
on the taxonomic level of the hosts, the prior probability of two proteins being linked and the number 
of associations between their member proteins. The functional association score is penalised by the 
number of paralogs and based on the similarity of the species. This score is repeatedly calculated for all 
pairs of orthologous groups at every taxonomic level. Afterwards, the scores between orthologous group 
pairs are transferred back to protein pairs, which then results in the evidence transfer between 
organisms60.

different API’s have been developed for the usage of and programming with STRING both for mapping 
identifiers of proteins hits from STRING and for retrieving protein-protein interaction networks70.

1.7 Pfam 
Pfam is a database of protein families defined by smaller protein sequence, i.e. protein domains, which 
are the structural, functional and evolutionary units for proteins72–76. Each Pfam entry consists of an 
initial (or seed) alignment that makes the basis for building a profile-HMM, which are the probability 
models for the evolutionary changes in a set of related sequences77, through HMMER72. This is done in 
order to recognise the protein domains in a similar manner to how modern speech recognition 
applications use probability models to recognise words73. The only difference is that Pfam focuses on 
protein sequences instead of written or spoken sentences. 
A Pfam-entry is a representative subset of a whole set of matching sequences aligned against each other 
in order to make the seed alignment74. The typical seed alignment for an entry being 80 % non-
redundant. Relations between entries are identified through sequence information, which relates entries 
grouping into clans. Each entry is marked with one of six different type: family and domain, which 
constitute 97.2% of all Pfam-entries; motif, repeat, coiled-coil and disordered72. Family and domain is 
distinguished by a known structure, which indicates the entry to be a single globular domain, with 
protein families being based on sequence similarity to these domains. Protein similarities are often 
characteristic for shared protein domains that can be seen as being common for protein structure and 
function75. 
It is far from uncommon that UniProt entries also contain Pfam-domains that helps describe transporter 
functions (PF00005; ATP-binding domain for ABC-transporters) and families, like MFS (PF07690).  
1.8 Phylogeny and phylogenetic inference 
Phylogeny is a high-level description of evolutionary relations between groups of species and their 
ancestors. It is generally depicted as either a rooted/unrooted tree (though clustering) or network 
(through ordination), with the most well-known phylogenetic tree being Charles Darwin’s tree of 
life78,79. Phylogeny have been used historically to classify organisms in natural evolutionary groups 
based on sequence identity. This have been used in multiple biological fields ranging from genetics, 
virology, ecology and comparative biology. In fact, the entire field of comparative biology is focused 
on phylogeny, since it gives the statistical framework necessary to compare features to related

organisms or proteins. This is especially interesting for transporters, since according to Saier et al. 
201662 functional classes and phylogenetic clustering correlate almost perfectly without exception. This 
means that phylogeny can help check if there is a correlation between substrate specificity and function 
and check if the hits are transporters.  
Many methods for constructing phylogenetic trees exist that each have their strengths and weaknesses. 
For instance maximum parsimony tries to minimise the amount of changes needed along a tree in order 
to take present sequence character values into consideration80. This enables it to incorporate insertions-
deletion events and asymmetrical changes, but makes it unable to account for evolutionary rates and 
multiple character changes at the same position. On the other hand, the maximum likelihood uses 
probability models that describe how characters in a sequence can change over time and tries to 
maximise the probability that the model can give the observed data. Unlike maximum parsimony, 
maximum likelihood can account for differences in evolutionary rates and multiple character changes 
at the same position80. Although maximum likelihood cannot account for insertion-deletion events or 
asymmetrical changes in a sequence. Yet another method that can be used to construct phylogenetic 
trees are the neighbour-joining method that uses evolutionary distance data in order to calculate the 
distance and branch length of each operational taxonomic unit81. The neighbour-joining algorithm finds 
the pairs of operational taxonomic units that minimise the total branch length by each clustering event 
that is part of the tree construction process. A pair of neighbours are a pair of operational taxonomic 
units that are connected through a single interior node in an unrooted, bifurcating tree. The number of 
pairs of operational taxonomic units (N) for the smallest tree (N≥4) is between 2 and N/2. The sum of 
the branch length (or evolutionary distance) is calculated for each pair of operational taxonomic unit. 
The tree nodes (or leaves) with the smallest evolutionary distance are considered a true neighbour and 
are clustered together in the tree. This process is done for all pairs until all N-3 interior branches are 
found. This is a fast method that gives both the topology and the branch length of the given data, and 
can be used for sequential character data like nucleotide and amino acid sequences. 
In order to investigate the functions of the transporter search hits, partial phylogenetic inference will be 
conducted only for investigating results and not for transferring annotations. Phylogenetic inference 
tries to investigate protein function in an evolutionary manner by investigating clustering proteins and 
infer function based on closely clustering proteins18. For annotation transfer, it is a multi-step that is 
based on multiple sequence alignment, phylogenetic tree construction and discriminating between 
orthologues and paralogues, before inference of function for a protein based on orthologues. This 
process is so frequently used that the majority of new sequences are assigned a putative function through 
annotation transfer from the best hits through this method. It is done to such a degree that only 3% of 
informative UniProt proteins have experimental support, but this does also mean that systemic error can 
occur in some cases. These errors can be mitigated, if phylogenetic inference is performed correctly. In 
order to transfer a function from one protein to another, the classic 25-30% sequence identity is 
insufficient and the minimum for SwissProt proteins are 40% sequence identity50. For simple functional

1.9 Possible pitfalls 
It is important to be completely sure of the terms used for ontological analysis, when programming 
based on ontology. If only part of words are being used, then it can yield false positives, if the terms 
used to indicate a hit is too non-specific. For instance, if a program is searching for indications of women 
in a text and one of the check-terms is the word “lady”, then the program could get a hit like “ladybug”. 
This would indicate that ladybugs are all females, which is not true. Similarly, the Danish word for 
ladybug is “mariehøne” directly translated into meaning “mary hen”, which could mistakenly be 
ontologically categorised as being a type of poultry. This can be mitigated by checking for function and 
process to ensure that whatever is found to be possible transporters also can function as transporters.  
Broadly-defined terms can also be an issue, as this would yield too many false positives. For instance, 
in the section about ontology, one of the mentioned categories were “thing”. What is defined under that 
term? Anything that is tangible, can be defined under this term, which makes specific categorisation 
with this term impossible. Even for less ambiguous terms, this issue can arise, though on a smaller scale:  
The English word “pound” can both refer to the weight metric and a British coin of currency. But if a 
program was going to check for one of the two definitions, it would report hits for both definitions, 
unless specified to the contrary. In the same way, when trying to define transporters ontologically, it is 
necessary to choose the check-terms with care as to avoid ambiguous terms. This is the case, since 
proteins with receptor- or enzymatic activity sometimes lack transport activity, though not 
exclusively62. This can also be mitigated by ensuring transporter function to be present for the possible 
transporter candidates.  
The proteins that have receptor- or enzymatic activity and lack transport activity can still at times be 
present in transport protein families62. This means that it will not be possible to filter based on protein 
family, since it can end up including candidates that have no transporter activity (false positives) and 
exclude candidates with transporter activity (false negatives). Analysis can be made for protein families 
later, but filtering on this basis could lead to many false positives and negatives. This also means that 
proteins that does not belong to a non-transporter family, say if a protein does not have a domain as 
either an ABC-transporter or a part of MFS, still can have transporter activity. For the analysis part, it 
can only be compared to the transporters from the data set in appendix 1, but they should not be 
unambiguously categorised as transporters on that account. There are also some correlation between 
enzymes and transporters on the transcriptional level in most cases8, so it might not be possible to rule 
out transporter candidates with enzymatic Pfam-domains. This is especially the case, since certain 
permeases have shown functions that are associated with other enzymatic functions, but still play crucial 
transporter roles82.

Most annotations in databases are done through annotation transfer from existing proteins, which can 
lead to systemic errors18. The causes and results of such errors have been summarised to be:  
1. gene duplication, which make protein superfamilies able to innovate novel functions on the 
same structural template, so the function of the best hit is different from the query  
2. domain shuffling with domain fusion/fission events that results in queries only sharing local 
regions of homology with different molecular functions and structures  
3. propagation of existing errors in database annotations that is rarely detected and not necessarily 
corrected  
4. then two proteins can share both a common ancestor and domain structure, while still having 
very different functions due to them originating in very divergently related species 
If a novel function is introduced into a protein superfamily, it is no guarantee that that function is similar 
in nature to the rest of the super family. Therefore, errors caused by gene duplication and domain 
shuffling would mean that protein family domains is not correlated to function, so Pfam-domains would 
not necessarily extend to function. Therefore, similarity of Pfam-domains would only be some evidence 
to functional similarity and not a guarantee for functional identity. The fourth problem would be visible 
in the phylogenetic trees, with some hits clustering well with others, but not sharing a functional 
annotation. This means that possible “cluster-breakers”, which are hits that does not share the function 
with the rest of the cluster, could be seen as outliers, since they share structure, but not function. The 
propagation of existing annotation errors is especially difficult, since the use of annotation ontology is 
being used. Annotations from multiple sources can mitigate the issue, but it cannot completely eliminate 
the issue, since there can be errors in the annotations of multiple databases, especially if they borrow 
from each other.

2.5 Transporter analysis 
Searches in the program only with substrate input were performed for all of the substrates from the data 
set in appendix 1 with a hit limit of 200 STRING hits in order to ensure that there were hits on all of 
the searches. 
In order to compare transporter similarity between hits from a search, sequence similarity are computed 
using MatGAT2.0186 with default settings and heatmaps were made in custom scripts afterwards. 
MatGAT2.01 (or Matrix Global Alignment Tool) calculate the similarities and identities between 
unaligned sequences in a data set through a series of pairwise alignments using a global alignment 
algorithm.   
Sequence domain analysis are made by comparing Pfam-domains among the hits. Transporter function 
analysis is made through phylogenetic inference, where the phylogenetic trees are made, by processing 
the sequences of the substrate-only searches through MAFFT87. MAFFT is a multiple sequence 
alignment tool that can construct phylogenetic trees through the neighbour-joining method. MAFFT 
will be run on the standard settings of BLOSUM62, 1.53 gap open penalty, and 0.123 gap extension 
aligned order, 2 tree rebuilding’s and no fast fourier transformation-performance reports. BLOSUM62 
(Block Substitution Matrix 62) is a method for sequence alignment scoring that gives scores to 
alignments based on substitutions with a basic similarity of <62%, which is normal for protein 
searches88. For visualisation of the trees and for performing phylogenetic inference, iTol89 was used to 
show and indicate functional clusters. In order to infer functions among the hits, each cluster in the 
phylogenetic trees will be grouped based on two criteria:  
1. Their evolutionary distance  are lower or equal to 0.6, referring to a maximum of 60% sequence 
substitution or at minimum 40% sequence identity. The evolutionary distance is here 
understood as the sum of branch lengths between two leaves (hits) in the phylogenetic tree, or 
the fraction of mutations per position per sequence length. For instance, an alignment between 
two sequences of length 10 with a single amino acid substitution would have an evolutionary 
distance of 1/10=0.1. 
2. The hits that are within the accepted range of evolutionary distance share a functional 
annotation 
3. Results and Analysis 
A program has been written that can find possible transporters based on a STRING search. This program 
filters the search results based on ontological factors from the STRING annotation, localisation and 
GO-annotation, where the latter two are obtained from UniProt. Aside from these, UniProt were used 
to extract information on Pfam-domains, sequence and presence of TMBB’s. After the UniProt search 
has been performed, the hit will be sent through the second and third filter to ensure localisation and 
functionality, respectively. The functionality is checked through the GO-annotated function and 
process, since certain transporters does not have transporter function, but is part of a transport process.

The sequence is then used as input for TMHMM, which then predicts the number of TMHMM’s present

default, it will retrieve 100 hits, since that is the standard of the STRING mapping-identifiers API.

3.2 Sequence similarity 
The hits from the original broad searches were gathered and, to ensure consistency, sequences were 
extracted from UniProt, which ensured 219 out of the total 288 hits (76.04%) to be analysed. Even 
though this was a significant number of lost sequences, it still ensured a sufficient number of sequences 
to be analysed. The loss of candidates for sequence analysis in this case is caused by the entries not 
being linked to their respective STRING-IDs, which can change with further development of the 
STRING- and UniProt databases. Sequence similarity were made from the protein sequences of the hits 
taken from UniProt and computed through MatGat on default settings with an initial gap penalty of 12 
and a gap extension penalty of 2 and using BLOSUM62. Sequence similarity was used over sequence 
identity, because of the uncertainty among sequence identity and functional conservation among 
databases50. The similarity percentages were then processed through a Python script to make a similarity 
matrix. This was for the percentages to be used in heatmaps that were computed in another python script 
with the seaborn-package. The numbers were manually inserted into the script and each heatmap were 
made separately and arranged afterwards to give an overall image of the similarity outputs.  The 
heatmaps for the broad searches can be seen in appendix 3.  
The heatmaps show many cases of varying similarity across the hits. There are a few hits that show 
100% similarity (dark blue) to the original transporter from the data set, meaning that the program have 
been able to find the wanted transporters in a number of cases. In similar cases, there are also a number 
of hits that show high, though not 100%, similarity to the original transporters from the data set. So 
even though they are not the same, they could in theory share similar functions to the data set 
transporters, unless their deviations are in functional domains. At present, there is still uncertainty, 
whether they are functionally similar, although there are at least a bit of evidence for it. For quite a 
number of hits, there are also high sequence similarity between each other, but not to the data set 
transporters, so they should have a transporter function that is different from the data set transporter. 
This is seen in the broad searches of aerobactin, anguibactin, enterobactin, lincomycin, novobiocin and 
vanillate. This is not necessarily a bad thing: both LmrB and YegB are defined as playing a role in 
resistance against lincomycin and novobiocin, respectively. While LmrB is an antiporter, YegB is an 
efflux pump, but that does not guarantee that there are not lincomycin efflux pumps and novobiocin 
antiporters (or other types of transporters). It could also be that there are differences in substrate 
specificity, so perhaps those, who show clustered similarity, are transporters for more than one 
substrate. The only thing that can be stated with certainty at present is that there should be quite a few 
clusters on phylogenetic trees, and then perhaps a lot of evolutionary heredity between groups of 
transporters.  
Few of the hits in the heatmaps show hardly any similarity (yellow) to any of the other hits, which is a 
bit odd since they should have some similarity to other hits. They are most definitely outliers, but it is 
difficult to say why that is the case.

all of the written results and registered all of the Pfam-domains that were present by all of the substrate 
searches. This subscript first registered the Pfam-domains that were present and the number of hits that 
were present in each broad search. Then the subscript when through all of the broad searches again to 
calculate the number of Pfam-domains in each of the broad searches for it to calculate the percentage 
of mentions of each Pfam-domain in each broad search. It should be noted here that it was percentage 
of mentions of each Pfam-domain (rounded to the nearest integer) and each protein can have more than 
one domain. Therefore, it should not be expected that either the sum of the columns or the rows will 
equate to 100%. On a similar note, the Pfam-domains are extracted from the UniProt entries and not all 
hits have had their STRING-ID’s linked to their respective UniProt entries nor have all UniProt entries 
registered any Pfam-domains. So there are also hits that do not have any Pfam-domains. These results

were then manually categorised based on the annotations of the Pfam-domains from the Pfam website. 
Since the largest and most studied transporter superfamilies are the MFS and ABC-transporter 
superfamilies, Pfam-domains in those superfamilies would have more evidence for them to be 
transporters than others. Pfam-domains were therefore sorted firstly based on membership in these 
superfamilies. Pfam-domains that were linked to TCDB and showed them to be members of those 
superfamilies were gathered together and the rest were sorted based on functional annotations. Members 
that initially were too unique to be categorised in one common category were placed in the “other” 
category. Finally, for visual aid, the cells with percentages had a conditional formatting rule placed that 
coloured the cells in a similar manner to the colour scheme and rule as the heatmaps. This rule indicated

3.4 Phylogenetic analysis 
In order to investigate functions and diversity among the hits of the broad searches, partial phylogenetic 
inference was performed to see the quality of the program through protein function and conservation. 
After all of the hits were found through broad searches, another Python subscript was made to extract 
the protein sequences from their UniProt entries in order to have sequences for phylogeny. The 
sequences, plus the sequences for the data set transporters, were then aligned in MAFFT, which also 
made the phylogenetic trees. The phylogenetic trees were then all edited in iTol to include their 
annotated UniProt names and group them based on the criteria of an evolutionary distance of 0.6 and 
similar annotation. The evolutionary distance were chosen in accordance to the minimum of 40% 
sequence identity that Punta and Ofran 200850 suggested for conservation of function for Swiss-Prot 
sequences. Evolutionary distance is understood here as the fraction of different amino acids per position 
in a sequence, when aligned to another. For instance, if two sequences of 10 amino acids are aligned 
and are identical except at a specific position, the evolutionary distance between the sequences would 
be 1/10=0.1. It should be noted that the sequences used here are all hits that had STRING-IDs linked to 
their UniProt entries and which entry were not obsolete. One such obsolete entry that was left out of the 
phylogeny was Q7DAA8 from the broad chloramphenicol search. All of the phylogenetic trees can be 
seen in appendix 3-18. It is assumed that the transporters from the data set in appendix 1 are correctly 
annotated in terms of function, so clustering with them would provide better evidence for a shared 
function.

also have too high an evolutionary distance to the wanted MdtM-protein from the data set for them to 
share a function, so they lack evidence for them being transporters outside their annotation. Most of the 
remaining hits in the tree are all putative, meaning that their functions have only been predicted based 
on similarity of functions between identical proteins. The only other possible cluster would be the one 
between Q9KYU0 and Q9FBQ5 based on the evolutionary distance, but their annotated function is 
different from each other, making clustering of the two inaccurate at best. There is therefore insufficient 
evidence for any of the hits in this search to have a shared function or be transporters based on the 
search results.  
The phylogenetic tree for the pyochelin search yielded a smaller tree that can be seen in appendix 17. 
It had two data set transporters in FptX/AmpP and FptA, but there was no clustering to any of these two 
based on evolutionary distance. Even though all three are some sort of transporter receptor, their 
evolutionary distances are just too high for them to share functions with each other. In addition, there 
is more uncertainty for K8VYP2 being a transporter, since receptors are misleading, when it comes to 
transporters.  
Finally, the vanillate search also yielded a small phylogenetic tree (appendix 18), where there was only 
a single cluster to VanK of the data set, which also originate from different organisms. Both O24832 
and A4FDH6 originate from a vanK-gene, though while only O24832 has been submitted as VanK, 
A4FDH6 has been submitted as Benzoate transport with origin from the vanK-gene. Since vanillate is 
a benzoate, it might suggest that A4FDH6 is a transporter with a particularly broad substrate specificity 
that can transport various types of benzoate molecules. Similarly, the program also got Q43975, which

3.4.2 Summary of the phylogenetic analysis 
Overall, it seems that there are a lot of hits that are not only different from each other and the data set 
transporters, they also seem to be too far away evolutionarily to have their functions inferred from them. 
Quite a number of the searches were able to both find the data set transporter and/or make clusters with 
them, but there were still additional small clusters and non-clustered hits. There were even hits that 
functionally could be clustered together, but they either had a too high evolutionary distance or were 
separated by other functional hits for them to make clusters. It is therefore difficult to functionally 
classify many of the hits found by the program as transporters. 
4. Discussion 
The actinorhodin analysis had high similarities and identities, but with different functional annotations 
and evolutionary distances beyond the threshold. All of the hits that had sequences had the same Pfam-
domain, which also indicated them to be part of the MFS, which the data set transporter also was, so 
there is some evidence for them being transporters. However, it was only 62% of the hits found by the 
program that were linked to UniProt entries and hence had sequences to be analysed. That still leaves 
38% of the hits to be completely different in both function and domains. The aerobactin search also 
yielded high sequence similarity, with 60% similarity to the data set transporter in terms of Pfam-
domains. Almost all of the hits that had sequences also shared the same functional annotation and were 
within the evolutionary distance threshold for functional inference. The only exception was a clear 
outlier in all of the analysis and the second aerobactin had an evolutionary distance beyond the 
threshold. There is generally the same issue among all of the searches that many of them have a high 
similarity in sequence, domains and functions, but leave out sequences from the analysis, since they 
have not been linked or are expired. There are also a lot of hits that cannot be clustered, since they are 
beyond the evolutionary distance threshold, differently annotated or both, which makes it uncertain 
whether they are transporters or the results of erroneous annotation. On the other hand, there were also 
a couple of the searches that did yield completely different hits in both domains, functions and 
sequences, like the hits from the alcaligin and erythromycin searches. The lincomycin search did also 
yield overall high sequence similarity with all of them being part of the MFS, but with only few 
functional clusters. It is still interesting that almost all of the lincomycin hits were related to lincomycin 
resistance, so they should be part of the same or a similar process at least. The novobiocin search also 
had an overall high sequence similarity and most of the hits were part of the same resistance 
superfamily. Unlike the lincomycin search, most of the hits in the novobiocin search could be clustered 
together based on them being multidrug transporters. Though this classification is a broad definition, 
since it has not been specified what drugs they transport other than the proposed novobiocin. However,

some of the hits in the searches, like the hits of lincomycin and novobiocin, but they are generally of a

it excluded the majority of the known transporters that the program otherwise found and even left fewer 
hits after each search, hence it would lead to a high amount of false negatives. A finer adjustment of 
this filter could be used to check for domains that were known to be part of known transporter 
superfamilies, but the accuracy of such a filter is uncertain. It could also still leave out known

The use of the filters are based on a few parameters that had to be fulfilled:  
• 
Is it called a transporter?  
• 
Can it be found in membranes?  
• 
Does it have transporter functions or are part of a transport process?  
The first ontological filter in this program was made, so both plurals and conjoined words, such as 
“transporters” and “ABC-transporter”, are acceptable. These check-terms have also been made as part 
of the output to better analyse what type of transporter the hits are. Though a number of ontological 
check-terms have been chosen, these terms have still been chosen in order to avoid broad 
categorisation53. The original first filter even included more specific search terms for inclusion of more 
specific transporter types, though they were not registered by any of the hits from STRING. That can 
make it debatable whether they were relevant, since they hardly were used, but are still registered as 
being transporter categories by TCDB. Though it can easily be the difference of annotations between 
databases, so they might not have been the best things to include, hence why they were removed. One 
of the words that were considered early in the process and later removed, was the word “receptor”, 
which would have been a broad ontological category, since receptors functions differently. Where 
transporters allow for transport of substrate over the membrane, receptors bind to a substrate and then 
sends a signal on the other side of the membrane, normally inside the cell91. Previous versions of the 
program did however include the term “receptor” in the first filter, since TCDB also had registered 
receptors as transporters11,61,85. This term also guaranteed IutA from Y.pestis to be present in the search 
for aerobactin, but it was a broad term that also could include non-transporter hits, which would be false 
positives for this program. Before removal, it was estimated that 19.1% of the hits found in the searches 
with the receptor term (89 hits in total) in STRING were either found in or homologous to transporter 
proteins found in TCDB. So some possible transporters could have been lost by this, though it is not 
certain. It has also been proposed that a possible future implementation into the program was to let the 
user decide if they want to include receptors as their search results. This can even be a possibility, since 
a lot of the test data set transporters had “signalling receptor activity” as their GO annotation for 
molecular functions and still be part of transporter processes. Earlier versions of the program, before 
the implementation of the second filter, had a few hits that were located in the cytoplasm or periplasm, 
which is contrary to the fact that transporters are found in membranes13. This just showed that the first 
filter was not enough on its own and hence needed more checks in order to filter the hits even further. 
The third filter also used an ontological category that ensured that either transport is part of the process 
or the function of the hit, which should ensure more accurate transporter hits. The check on the process 
was made to include transporters that did not have a registered transporter activity, but still were part 
of a transport process. Transport process was checked as well, because a decent amount of the data set

transporters were registered to be part of a transport process, but not to have transport function. Simply

of the data set would have been predicted. It should be an acceptable strategy, since the transporters 
from the data set are registered transporters.  
It has been concerning that less than half of the wanted transporter were predicted by the program, but 
when the first filter was removed, it was only one of the missing transporters that was predicted. This 
could suggest that either the remaining filters removes too many possible transporters or that the issue 
lies in the STRING-database. Both the second and third filter are somewhat liberal with the filtering, 
so they only filter away hits that are proven not to be in a membrane and not to have transporter activity 
or being part of a transport process. This means that it is more likely that the fault lies with the STRING-
database with one of two possibilities. Either the hits have too low STRING-scores among many 
possible hits, so they would not appear among 200 hits, or that the remaining wanted transporters cannot 
be found in the STRING-database. More search-hits could therefore have been used, however the 
program had a tendency to exit with a lack of internet connection, which would result in lost search 
data, hence a lower number of search hits were chosen. Smaller runtime increment increases (how many 
percent increments were increased per STRING hit) were also only encountered on searches that 
naturally had a lot of STRING results, like enterobactin. It is therefore unlikely that a lot more of the 
searches would include more hits. It is therefore most likely that the remaining data set transporters, 
that were not found, is not a part of the STRING-database. 
Many TM-helices were registered during the searches, which should be expected as the TM-helices are 
widespread among transporters. They are particularly widespread among ABC-transporters8, MFS20, 
and other cytoplasmic proteins in both prokaryotes and eukaryotes33. With 80.645% of the hits in the 
specific searches and 87.319% of hits in the broad searches containing TM-helices, there can be a lot 
of transporter among them, since they can act as both channel and anchors in the membranes32. 
However, in gram-negative bacteria, TM-helices are only found in proteins in the inner membrane and 
rarely in the outer membrane33,40. That leaves out proteins in the outer membrane and similar cellular 
membranes. Regardless, that still leaves at maximum 12.681%, which must have TMBB’s, but without 
a good TMBB-predictor, it is difficult to determine.  
The phylogenetic inference was made in order to check and infer functions from the hits as though they 
were novel transporters, since it have been hypothesised that transporters cluster together by 
function50,52. Though it is not desirable in this project to infer functions to novel transporters, but just to 
investigate for functions and hence only a partial phylogenetic inference was done. A text-based 
analysis of the hits to infer functions and compare them to the functions of the data set transporter was 
a possibility. However, that would leave out the evolutionary aspect and could leave the analysis 
erroneous due to gene duplications and erroneous annotation18. This would not give a detailed 
perspective on how diverse the hits of the different searches are.

In order to perform the searches in STRING, it could only be done on through the mapping identifier 
API70. The remaining STRING API’s can only extract images for the networks that STRING makes for 
each search. The mapping identifier API did provide a limited amount of usable information, which 
made the initial sorting difficult aside from ontologically sorting based on the protein annotations. 
Searches in alternative databases were possible through the use of the STRING ID. It was more taxing 
on the internet connection, since this step was performed on each hit, before the analysis step was 
initiated. It is definitely possible to perform the UniProt search (or any database search) before filtering 
to achieve the optimal amount of information before filtering. However, the connection from the 
working server had a tendency to opt out of the program due to not being able to connect properly to 
the internet at the database search. This was why the first filter was made, so there was a sorting process 
before any additional information was downloaded and why the number of hits for the analysis were 
set to 200 so it would still guarantee a significant number of hits. Searches in alternative databases could 
be possible, as long as precise searches are possible in those alternative databases as they were in 
UniProt. Otherwise, other measures must be incorporated in those versions in order to make the searches 
more precise. In UniProt, precise searches were possible by searching based on STRING IDs, so if 
something similar is available in other databases, such inclusions might be more easily implemented. 
In the same manner, it was suggested searches in NCBI or RefSeq could be possible, but there was no 
similar method to perform specific searches based on the individual STRING IDs in those databases. 
For such methods to be possible, it would be a demanding task for creators of both databases. It would 
be an effort from the NCBI creator’s side in order to make an API or similar search engine that can 
perform the searches. It would also take a collaborative effort between NCBI and STRING in order to 
integrate the STRING IDs with the correct entries. Which these efforts in mind, it is unlikely that such 
an effort would be plausible within the next couple of years if they ever will be. Searches could also be 
made on just protein and organism name, which would yield a number of hits of varying quality and 
relevance. Previous versions of the program performed this type of UniProt searches, but this yielded a 
number of hits, where not every one of them were accurate to the hit found in the STRING search. So 
it is more accurate to search based on the STRING IDs for at precise hit, except that it left out a number 
of hits in UniProt that were not linked to the STRING ID. The same issue of no-linked IDs could also 
be an issue in those alternate databases as well. Information from UniProt could also be used for the 
searches in alternate databases, but information in those databases would then be unavailable, if no 
UniProt entry was linked. Therefore, it would be preferable to search in other databases based solely on 
the results from STRING. 
The STRING-API did also provide a NCBI taxon identifier that could be used, however searching for 
those on NCBI revealed that they were IDs for the organism that the protein originates from and not the 
protein itself.

5. Future perspective 
The topic of developing this program into a webtool have been debated ever since the initiation of the 
project. This had to be postponed due to fine-tuning of the program, analysis of the results and repetition 
of said analysis. This is still an option and can be developed in the future for the benefit of other 
researchers.  
The information gathering could also be expanded in order to better guarantee that each hit have data 
to analyse outside of the options from STRING-linked UniProt. This would only demand that other 
databases either have a way to do precise searches based on STRING IDs or in other ways guarantee 
the same hit to be found in those alternate databases. With increased information gathering, the program 
could also be changed to be showing results based on supporting evidence from other databases. The 
option of using RefSeq was also a suggestion during the project. It would however be difficult to 
implement within the timeframe, since there is no existing API for accessing the RefSeq-information 
based on the individual hit.  
With the update that STRING had, it might be necessary to rework the program from the ground up. It 
could still be possible to use the same principles on another database to achieve a multi-hit analysis.  
Outside of the program itself, with such a high number of hits in the lincomycin and actinorhodin 
searches with the PF07690-domain, it could be interesting to investigate, if it is possible to predict 
certain types of transporters based on Pfam-domains. It was already not possible in the general case, 
since a lot of the searches resulted in the majority of the hits having Pfam-domains different from the 
data set ones and since the hits had a high distribution among several domains.  
6. Conclusion 
The program can only find 35% of the wanted transporters of a selected data set. 
The aim of this project was to develop a program that could predict possible transporters for a substrate 
and a possible organism based on a STRING search. This aim have been fulfilled to the point, where 
the results are highly diverse in both functions, domains and sequence similarity and identity. Based on 
the Pfam-domains and the phylogenetic analysis, there are some uncertainty of some of the hits being 
actual transporters, but there is strong evidence for most of them to be transporters. Possibilities for 
improvements and alterations for this program have also been proposed and discussed, including a 
possibility for accuracy testing, though it is limited based on a recent STRING update.
