Preface 
 
The work reported in this Master Thesis, entitled “Effect of biochar on the biogas production – 
Unveiling how biochar characteristics are influencing the biogas process”, was conducted at 
the Department of Chemical and Biochemical Engineering at the Technical University of 
Denmark from January 2024 to July 2024. PhD and Professor Irini Angelidaki was the main 
supervisor and post-doc Parisa Ghofrani-Isfahani was the co-supervisor. 
The thesis consists of two main parts. The first one is represented by the theoretical 
information regarding the anaerobic digestion process, pyrolysis and biochar’s properties, 
while the second part of the thesis presents the experimental work performed in the laboratory.

Abstract 
 
This study aims to investigate the effect of biochar on the biogas production via anaerobic 
digestion (AD). Biochar is a carbonaceous material derived from the pyrolysis process and it 
acts as an additive in the process. AD is a well-known biotechnological process, which suffers 
from low gas production, inhibition, process instability, etc. With the advantages offered by 
biochar, AD can be stabilized, and the biogas production increased. Depending on biochar’s 
raw materials and operation condition of pyrolysis process (temperature, residence time, etc.), 
biochars can have different physico-chemical properties, such as ash and carbon content, 
volatile matter, surface functional groups, cation exchange capacity, electrical conductivity and 
pH. 
Experimental trials were conducted and monitored for the entire length of the thesis. The effect 
of biochar on AD was investigated by adding different biochar samples to batch reactors with 
inoculum and 2 types of substrates, a model one known under the name of Avicel 
(microcrystalline cellulose) and cattle manure. 18 different samples of biochar, coming from 
green waste (GW) and composting residues (CR) have been tested, from which 10 were 
pyrolyzed at 500, 600 and 700°C for residence times of 15 minutes and 30 minutes and the 
other 8 included post-treatment at increased temperatures of 800, 900 and 950°C for 30 and 
60 minutes, respectively.  
The biogas composition and production was monitored during all batch experiments in this 
study. The best biochar is coming from CR at 500°C for 15 minutes residence time, with an 
increase in the methane yield of 43.8%. For the same biomass, but in different experiments, 
the results show that tests 600-15 CR and 900-60 CR have the second and third highest 
production of methane. In the alternative of GW, the most effective sample was 600-15, which 
registered the highest change in two different experiments. Further research and experiments 
are required in order to explore the full potential of biochar as an additive in AD.

1. Introduction and background 
1.1 
 Anaerobic digestion 
Anaerobic digestion (AD) has become a hot topic in recent decades due to the need to 
transition away from the fossil fuel sector and towards renewable and green energy. The shift 
towards sustainable energy is crucial nowadays due to climate change and environmental 
reasons. Since population growth and urbanisation is developing fast, the demand for energy 
is directly linked to this issue. This means that the world needs to change and embrace 
emerging technologies that are able to reduce greenhouse gas emissions (GHGs) and reduce 
the carbon footprint [1]. 
AD can be referred to by a variety of terms, but most commonly it will be named a 
biotechnological process or a combination of biological and chemical processes. Its most 
famous application is to utilize organic waste, also known as biomass, to produce biogas (50-
70% methane, 25-50% carbon dioxide) [2]. Biomass is lignocellulosic waste, that can be 
collected from different industries such as animal manure and slurry, sewage sludge, 
municipal solid waste and food waste [3]. 
Biomass shows a diverse composition, which can be observed in Figure 1 [4]. The 
lignocellulosic waste is mostly made from carbohydrates, proteins, fats, cellulose, 
hemicellulose and lignin. According to the feedstock type utilized in the manufacturing and the 
pretreatment methods, the composition and yield of biogas may vary enormously [3].  
 
Figure 1. The components of lignocellulosic biomass [4] 
It is believed that lignocellulose is the most abundant source of organic matter on Earth, and 
it is found in the secondary cell wall of plant cells. Although the main components of the 
lignocellulosic biomass are cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin, minor components are present 
as well such as: proteins, lipids, tannis, pectin, non-structural polysaccharides, waxes, and 
inorganic materials [4]. 
Cellulose, also known as the linear polymer of D-glucose, is responsible for the mechanical 
strength of the plant’s cell wall [3]. Its polymers, due to the linkage via hydrogen and Van der 
Waals bonds, promote the formation of microfibrils, that are covered by the other main 
components of the lignocellulosic material (based on the master course “Biorefinery” directed 
by Manuel Pinelo at DTU-KT). Compared to cellulose, hemicellulose possesses a branched 
structure, formed out of monosaccharides such as pentoses, hexoses and uronic acids. Lastly, 
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
lignin is a complex aromatic polymer, formed out of units of phenolic monomers: p-coumaryl 
alcohol, coniferyl alcohol and synapil alcohol. It plays a major role in the primary cell wall of 
the plant, offering structural support and resistance against microbial attacks and oxidative 
stress [3].  
AD is quite a complex process which involves four main stages known as hydrolysis, 
acidogenesis, acetogenesis and lastly methanogenesis, from which biogas (a mixture of CH4 
and CO2) is formed [2]. For a better understanding of the matter, Figure 2 is the visual 
representation of the general steps involved in the process. 
 
Figure 2. General steps involved in anaerobic digestion process 
In the context of AD, the breakdown of lignocellulosic biomass into simpler compounds such 
as sugars and organic acids is essential to produce biogas. This breakdown involves the 
action of various microorganisms, including bacteria and archaea, which hydrolyses complex 
polymers into fermentable substrates that can be further metabolized to produce biomethane 
[2]. 
The first stage of AD is represented by hydrolysis, from which complex organic matter such 
as polymers are converted into soluble monomers. This entire stage is possible due to the 
hydrolytic bacteria, which hydrolyses the substrate with external enzymes (e.g. cellulases, 
hemicellulases, proteases, etc.). The choice of enzyme is crucial to the hydrolysis step and if 
chosen wrong, it can have a rate-limiting effect for the lignocellulosic substrates [2]. 
Once the hydrolysis step is complete, the acidogenic bacteria convert the soluble organic 
molecules into volatile fatty acids (VFAs), which are characterized by low values of pH (approx. 
4 units). The methanogens cannot directly obtain the products in the second step, thus the 
acetogenesis stage comes into play. The metabolizing bacteria, known as obligate hydrogen-
producing bacteria (OHPB), produces mainly acetate, H2 and CO2, as well as other organic 
acids present in small quantities. The second and third stages (acidogenesis and 
acetogenesis, respectively) play a crucial role in the performance of the AD process [2]. 
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
The last step of AD is known under the name of methanogenesis, and it counts on 
hydrogenotrophic and acetoclastic microorganisms, which transform the products of the 
previous stage into methane through hydrogenotrophic and acetoclastic pathways. The 
amount of these microorganisms is dependent on environmental factors, operational 
conditions and solid contents [2].  
The tremendous amounts of organic waste have raised a critical concern worldwide, including 
in countries like Denmark. Thus, there is a huge demand for AD technologies since they are 
able to comply with all of the 17 United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) as 
seen in Figure 3 [5]. 
 
Figure 3. SDGs in relationship with Anaerobic Digestion process [6] 
AD can offer the implementation of new businesses that will diminish the poverty in the 
surrounding areas, checking the first SDG off the list. SDG 2 can be accomplished by 
enhancing agricultural productivity through the production of organic fertilizers. Another goal 
that can be achieved is the good health and well-being one (SDG 3), due to the fact that AD 
reduces the indoor air pollution by providing alternative cooking solutions like biomethane 
production. This comes in direct contact with SDG 7, which is responsible for affordable and 
clean energy offered by the AD process [5]. 
In addition, the prevention of waste discharge into the water via AD is consistent with SDG 6, 
as well as SDG 14, which aims to protect the marine environment. SDGs 4 and 5 are both 
contributing to the energy supplement in poor areas, which will diminish the firewood collection 
done by mostly women or children and will offer quality education and gender equality. SDGs 
8 and 9 support each other, since economic growth relies on the innovative ideas in waste 
management offered by AD and its infrastructure development. SDG 9 also collaborates at 
the sustainable cities and communities (SDG 11) by promoting clean energy solutions, but it 
is also in a strong connection with SDG 10 which will create more job opportunities for the low-
income population [5]. 
It can be said that SDGs 13, 14 and 15 are related since their main goal is to reduce the 
pollution via greenhouse gas emissions for the life on land and under water, which in the end 
will promote peace worldwide (SDG 16). Another major goal is number 12, which is promoting 
sustainability and through it, SDG 16 as the ultimate goal, which proposes reduction of the 
==PAGE:10==

urban waste, can be achieved. Without a doubt, all of these objectives would be incredibly 
impossible to attain if not for the final one. SDG 17 is all about partnerships, and it plays a 
significant part in the plan since stakeholders use this opportunity to contribute to more 
sustainable environmental progress [5]. 
Challenges and inhibitors in AD 
Although it has many benefits, AD has also some drawbacks that keep companies doubt its 
overall effectiveness. Its low gas production, difficult substrate degradation and imbalance 
between acidogenic and methanogenic microorganisms are some of the few issues that need 
to be addressed [7]. In recent studies, it has been proven that three factors constrain this type 
of process: the bioconversion phase is slowed by the hydrolysis of substrates, the volatile fatty 
acids (VFAs) cannot be used more efficiently, and methanogens have a slow development [2].  
Since AD is an engineered ecosystem which relies on a wide range of Bacteria and Archaea, 
the process is subjected to multiple inhibitors. Accumulation of long-chain fatty acids (LCFA), 
volatile fatty acid (VFA), free ammonia and parameters such as temperature and pH are only 
a few barriers that can cause inhibition. It has been established that the microbial population 
impacts the process’s inhibitory levels. The inhibition of the metabolic pathways in AD occurs 
during hydrolysis and methanogenesis. The first step of AD degrades complex insoluble 
polymers to oligomers and monomers using hydrolytic fermentative bacteria. In most of the 
feedstocks used, the complex polymers can represent a restriction. The enzymes that help 
with the degradation of these polymers can also be inhibited by high levels of VFAs and 
LCFAs. The inhibition is manifested mostly via activity loss and reversible reduction in 
hydrolases. Multiple studies have shown that VFAs concentrations should not exceed 1.8-2 
g/L since can inhibit cellulose hydrolysis. Syntrophic bacteria plays a crucial role in 
methanogenesis, degrading fatty acids into acetate, hydrogen and carbon dioxide. High VFAs 
and ammonia can inhibit the hydrogenotrophic methanogens, while acetoclastic methanogens 
are more affected by ammonia [8].  
Since AD is the most promising technology to degrade complex organic substrates into 
biomethane, researchers have come up with a multitude of solutions to the inhibitory issues. 
Some of these solutions consist in adjusting the waste composition and nutrient content via 
trace elements as well as the usage of direct interspecies electron transfer (DIET) promoting 
additives. Activated carbon, biochar and carbon fibres are the most popular choice for 
improving biogas generation because they act as a bridge, allowing electrons to be transferred 
easier and faster between a variety of microorganisms. Activated carbon has a high surface 
area, as well as high electrical conductivity, which allows the direct electron transfer between 
syntrophic bacteria and methanogenic archaea. Although it has many positive effects on AD 
such as reduction of the lag phase and rapid VFA degradation, this carbon material lacks a 
deeper understanding of its properties. Carbon fibers can also be used since they act as a 
supporting media for bacterial growth in AD, but the high cost restricts its application on a large 
scale. As comparison to the other two carbon-based materials, biochar proves to be the most 
suitable option for many reasons. Biochar is capable of adsorbing inhibitors such as heavy 
metals and pesticides, promotes microbial immobilization, it has the potential to shorten the 
lag phase, just like activated carbon, and its low cost allows its usage in multiple applications 
[9]. 
Based on a review from Tech Science Press [10], biochar has the capability to accelerate the 
entire AD process, as well as improving the production of methane. Biochar enriches the 
hydrolysis bacteria and increases the activity of the external enzymes used in the first stage 
such as protease and lipase. The impact on acidogenesis has as a result faster production 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
rate of VFAs, as well as an increase in the methane yield. Lastly, biochar enriches the 
methanogens in the last stage, achieving a higher production rate of methane and thus, a 
better methane yield. All these details, regarding the effect of biochar on the AD process, will 
be further discussed in the thesis, with plenty of details. 
1.2 
 Pyrolysis 
Typical strategies currently used to achieve a more sustainable future are categorised into two 
classes of treatments: thermochemical and thermophysical. The thermochemical processes 
are of main interest in this project, and they are known to use high temperatures to convert 
materials with high amounts of carbon [11]. There are three major processes for obtaining end-
products such as bio-gas, bio-oil and biochar at high temperatures: pyrolysis, gasification and 
hydrothermal carbonization, also known as combustion [12].  
 
Figure 4. Thermochemical processes overview (based on the master course “Biorefinery” 
directed by Manuel Pinelo at DTU-KT) 
The first step in the mechanism of pyrolysis is the depolymerization of hemicelluloses, 
producing oligosaccharides that subsequently break down into furfural and other minor 
molecules. In a similar manner, cellulose depolymerizes into oligosaccharides and then forms 
levoglucosan by the cleavage of glucosidic bonds into D-glucopyranose. Lignin is the last 
component of the lignocellulosic biomass that decomposes and forms phenolic compounds 
via the pyrolysis process [13]. 
Regarding the effect of pyrolysis condition on biochar’s different parameters, two main aspects 
are studied – biochar properties and biochar yield. From the journal article [11], amongst the 
parameters that influence the most the pyrolysis process in respect with biochar are 
temperature, heating rate, particle size of the raw material and residence time, as well as gas 
type and flow rate, pressure, type of reactor and catalyst used. 
Biomass usually undergoes treatment by composting or combustion, both of which emit air 
pollutants such as H2S, SO2, and NH3 and have poor resource use efficiency. Thus, new 
technology is required for a more sustainable future and reaching the SDGs by 2030 [11]. 
Types of pyrolysis 
Among the three processes for the decomposition of organic materials (pyrolysis, gasification 
and combustion), pyrolysis is the only one that operates in anaerobic conditions, more exactly 
without oxygen in the system or extremely low levels [11]. 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
According to [11], there are six main technologies that involve the pyrolysis concept: 
 
Fast pyrolysis 
 
Flash pyrolysis 
 
Slow pyrolysis 
 
Vacuum pyrolysis 
 
Hydro-pyrolysis 


Out of all of these different technologies, the most promising ones for the production of biochar 
are fast pyrolysis, slow pyrolysis and microwave pyrolysis. 
Fast pyrolysis 
Biochar is one of the products of fast pyrolysis, but the main final product is bio-oil, which is 
the liquid component. The heating rate is extremely high as compared to other types of 
pyrolysis, in a short residence time. In general, fast or flash pyrolysis (which has similar 
parameters) is not favourable for biochar production, but it is believed that the biochar formed 
by this type has a better specific surface area (SSA) [11]. 
Slow pyrolysis 
Slow pyrolysis is the most common method of biomass treatment, which is why it is also known 
as conventional pyrolysis (CP). It is known for operating in the continuous system, in the 
absence of oxygen or limited oxygen [14]. In this case, the biomass materials are pyrolyzed 
at temperatures above 300°C and up to 700°C [11].  
Microwave pyrolysis 
In comparison with slow pyrolysis, the microwave pyrolysis (MWP) process is a more 
innovative technique with multiple advantages, including more controlled condition, higher 
energy efficiency and lower costs, making it more desirable than the traditional way [15]. In 
this case, the microwaves are responsible for the heating energy and the parameters of this 
technique that have a great impact on the product yields are residence time, microwave power, 
concentration of the microwave absorber, etc [11].

1.3 
 Biochar 
1.3.1 What is biochar? 
As previously mentioned, biochar is the solid end-product of the thermochemical process 
known as pyrolysis and it is mainly formed out of carbon [16]. Another definition of biochar 
was found in the article [17], which states that biochar can be characterized as the solid 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
material remained from the carbonization of biomass. Its production and application to diverse 
fields have been known for a long period of time. The versatility of biochar extends to a wide 
range of uses, including agricultural improvement, maintenance of farm animals, heat and 
energy production, and even medical purposes. In recent years, the carbonaceous material 
gained fame due to its capability to reduce the GHGs emissions by acting like a replacement 
for fossil carbon applications [16]. 
As mentioned before, biochar is manufactured from the decomposition of biomass, but in the 
crucial step of carbonization, the properties of the biomass are modified while retaining a 
significant amount of the carbon content. Thus, the desired outcome of biochar must be 
carefully chosen based on the biomass and the parameters of the process [16]. 
 
Figure 5. Visual representation of biochar 
Multiple feedstocks can be used via pyrolysis to obtain the desired end-product as it can be 
observed in Figure 5, but obviously some of its characteristics may vary depending on the 
respective biomass processed. Some of the most popular biowastes are from industries such 
as agriculture, farming, food processing, forestry and they can be wood pellets, crop residues, 
as well as sewage sludge, animal manure which come from lignocelluloses matter [18]. 
Another interesting option to produce biochar is to utilize the digestate after AD as feedstock. 
Multiple studies suggest that the biochar derived from digestate can improve biogas 
production in AD systems [19]. 
The importance of biochar goes beyond its technological applications such as biogas 
production for heat and energy, including its main role in mitigation of the climate change. This 
is possible due to its soil improvement and reduction of carbon dioxide and ammonia 
emissions. The upgrade of the soil is possible thanks to the char’s properties such as high 
specific surface area, great porosity, and high pH, which enables certain microorganisms to 
flourish and a better water flow underground for plant growth [18].  
1.3.2 Biochar composition  
Understanding the composition and characteristics of biochar is essential for harnessing its 
potential as a sustainable additive in AD systems, paving the way for enhanced biogas 
production and efficient organic waste management [20]. 
Although biochar is mainly made of carbon, from its elemental analysis, other elements can 
be noticed such as hydrogen, oxygen and micro- and macro-elements like potassium, sodium, 
zinc, iron, etc. From Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) spectra, it has been 
discovered that biochar’s composition mimics the carbohydrate structure of cellulose and 
hemicellulose [18]. 
The composition of biomass feedstock, which is formed mainly out of cellulose, hemicellulose 
and lignin, shows significant effect on the biochar production, influencing its characterization 
[21]. 
As mentioned in a study from 2020 [22], biochar’s atomic composition, when temperature is 
applied, undergoes specific steps. This phenomenon can be better understood by looking at 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
Figure 6 at the same time. At temperatures above 700°C, the biochar tends to be more 
organized into the C layers, known under the name of amorphous. The next main step begins 
with temperatures above 1400°C, from where the biochar gets the name non-graphitic or 
turbostatic. From the last step, the graphene arrangement of biochar, with less surface 
functional groups, but more well-arranged layers of carbon can be observed [18]. 
 
Figure 6. The pyrolysis temperature effect on biochar's composition [22] 
1.3.3 Physical and chemical properties of biochar 
It has been proven that the quality of biochar depends on multiple characteristics such as 
feedstock, process parameters and physico-chemical properties. Consequently, finding the 
proper conditions to manufacture a specific type of biochar, necessitates a deep 
understanding of affecting variables, from a qualitative and quantitative point of view [18]. 
Thus, some of the most common physico-chemical properties of biochar are specific surface 
area (SSA), ash and carbon content, volatile matter, surface functional groups, cation 
exchange capacity, electrical conductivity (EC), pH, etc. 
Specific surface area 
SSA is a crucial property of biochar, which refers to the total surface area of its pores and 
external surface area per unit mass of biochar. The method known for measuring SSA is called 
Brauner-Emmett-Teller (BET) analysis, and it is performed through gas adsorption [23]. It has 
been proven, according to [18], that an increase in the process temperature not only modifies 
the biochar’s surface area and porosity, but also increases it. The explanation behind this is 
that the substances that would usually block the pores, when exposed to higher temperatures 
will be thermally cracked, thus making the surface area achievable for other purposes. In other 
words, the decomposition of the cellulose and hemicellulose of the feedstock will form new 
channels in the biochars, increasing the SSA [21]. 
Another aspect that can influence the SSA is the presence of non-ignitable components which 
are derived from the biomass feedstock utilized. By non-ignitable components it is understood 
that ash and moisture contents are used as a reference. In a recent study, it has been 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
suggested that the ash content in biomass has a negative impact on the SSA, since it occupies 
the pores of the biochar, making the microorganism not stick [21]. 
Ash and carbon content  
The by-product of the pyrolysis process is mostly made of two components – carbon (60-80%) 
and ash, which represents the inorganic part. As it was found in the article [18], the biochar’s 
components are believed to increase as the temperature of the process increases. 
Both [21] and [18] agree on the fact that cellulose and lignin are responsible for the higher C 
content, which can reach 93% via the increased polymerization degree. A consequence of this 
increase leads to a better resistance of biochars against microbial degradation. 
As a rule of thumb, for biochars derived from wood, the increase in temperature above 500°C 
leads to values greater than 80% (w/w) carbon content. However, in the case of animal 
residues used as a feedstock, such as manures, there seems to be the opposite effect. The 
explanation behind this phenomenon is that biomasses from animal residues tend to have 
larger ash contents [13]. 
Regarding the ash content, an increase up to 19.1% can be observed as the temperature of 
the pyrolysis rises [21]. The minerals present in the ash such as Ca, Mg, P and K are also 
increasing, but compared to the C content, the mineral matter undergoes the carbonization 
reaction [18]. Biochar, depending on the ash percentage, it can also possess traces of metals 
and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH). The greater the ash content, the more 
atmospheric pollutants the biochars can have, making it a risk to be added to the soil [18]. 
Volatile matter 
The volatile matter represents the combustible substances (gases) that are being released 
when the biochar is heated up. Compared to the other two properties, the volatile matter is 
decreasing as long as the temperature is being increased. This behaviour was noted because 
instead of producing the product of interest (biochar), the volatile matter is subjected to 
breakdown into lighter liquids and gases [18]. 
These gases are able to affect the stability of the biochars as well as the plant growth capacity 
in the following ways: phenol, which is one of the toxic compounds released at high 
temperatures damage the root growth of the plant, while oligosaccharides serve the purpose 
of carbon source for the microbial activity [18]. 
As the temperature of the pyrolysis process is being increased, the volatiles are released from 
the feedstock, leading to a greater amount of volatile organic compounds (VOCs). This results 
in the production of smaller solid end-product, since the volume decreases [13]. This effect is 
not necessarily a negative one since it represents one of the advantages of biorefineries, by 
creating more space, while using the biomass (based on the master course “Biorefinery” 
directed by Manuel Pinelo at DTU-KT). 
Surface Functional Groups 
Besides the changes of SSA, volatile matter, ash and carbon content, the surface components 
may also vary with temperature and type of feedstock. These functional groups are in direct 
contact with the adsorption properties of biochar, since high amounts of VOC lead to a 
reduction in the surface functional groups. Thus, biochars obtained at lower temperatures 
(below 400°C) tend to have more polar groups than high-temperature biochars, such as 
hydroxyl, carboxyl, and carbonyl. As a consequence, the biochars obtained at temperatures 
above 500°C have more difficulties in trapping the heavy metals [13]. The surface functional 
groups of biochar can be analyzed qualitatively by FTIR [7]. 
It has been observed that as the temperature increases during pyrolysis, the pore formation

Cation exchange capacity 
Another property of the biochar that is extremely influenced by the temperature and feedstock 
material is the cation exchange capacity (CEC). It is believed that due to the alkali metals 
present in the lignocellulosic biomass that form surface groups containing O2, CEC is higher 
for feedstocks with high content of ash. According to the same research paper, CEC is greater 
for non-wood derived biomass such as manure. Generally, biochars with high surface area 
and high aromaticity tend to have higher CEC values [18]. Regarding the temperature impact, 
CEC decreases when the pyrolysis temperature is increasing, due to the removal of surface 
functional groups [21]. 
[24] discusses the relationship between alkaline content and CEC and how their adsorption 
performance has been neglected in most studies. CEC has an impact on the cationic 
contaminant retention and plays a major role in the ammonium adsorption. Alkaline metals, 
such as potassium and calcium, act as exchangeable cations, directly affecting the CEC. 
Electrical conductivity  
Another important chemical property of biochar that contributes to diverse applications, is the 
electrical conductivity (EC). According to [25], EC depends on multiple factors such as 
feedstock composition, pyrolysis conditions, the presence of surface functional groups and 
CEC. 
The feedstock composition can significantly affect the EC and it is believed that biochar from 
wood biomass tends to have a higher EC. The same effect has been observed on temperature 
of the pyrolysis process; EC increases with increasing temperature [25].  
Another extremely important aspect of this property is that it depends on the amount of soluble 
salts within the biochar’s composition. The porous nature of biochar allows to sequester 
various ions and molecules, including the soluble salts [25]. 
pH 
pH variations in biochar can be influenced by both temperature and type of feedstock used in 
its production. In general, biochar is highly alkaline with pH values in the range of 7.2-10.3. 
These values occur due to the different types of feedstock which are often categorized as 
wood-based or non-wood-based. The difference in pH could be attributed to variations in the 
ash content and organic matter decomposition between the two feedstocks [21]. 
It has been suggested that oxygen-rich functional groups such as carboxylic and aromatic 
structures mostly contribute to the alkalinity of biochar. 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
Higher pyrolysis temperatures have been found to result in biochar with higher pH values due 
to the destruction of acidic functional groups and an increase in alkaline functional groups [21]. 
[26] states that pH is the main parameter for an efficient AD process. When the pH decreases, 
so does the microbial activation and C/N ratio, acidifying the system. The undesired 
consequence of acidification is the reduction of the biogas yield; thus, the biochar is used for 
its alkaline nature. 
1.3.4 Effects of biochar on the biogas production process  
Biochar has gained attention in the past few years for its potential benefits in the AD process. 
Studies have determined that biochar possesses various effects, such as enhancing the 
process stability, improving the biogas production and methane content, buffering capacity 
and the mitigation of certain inhibitors (e.g. ammonia and VFAs). Since biochar has also 
conductive properties, it can stimulate the DIET mechanism between the syntropic acetogenic 
bacteria and methanogenic microorganisms involved in AD [27]. 
[27] reported the addition of biochar to the AD system showed positive results. Biochar formed 
from rice husks at 900-1000°C, together with cattle manure as a substrate, obtained a biogas 
yield increase of 28.9% [27]. An experiment performed at mesophilic conditions with biochar 
formed at 550°C from waste wood chips of apple trees, showed an increase of 45.24% biogas 
yield. Although experiments were successful also for thermophilic conditions (55°C), the 
results were significantly lower than the ones registered with mesophilic AD (p < 0.001). The 
microorganisms require a longer time to adjust to the temperature changes in thermophilic 
AD, which is why mesophilic AD is a better choice, being able to reduce the methanogenic lag 
phase [27]. 
In a recent study [28], it was investigated the impact of biochar addition on the methane 
production with enriched and non-enriched swine manure inoculant at different total solids 
concentrations. The results show not only that biochar can have an inert impact on AD, but a 
negative one. The cumulative methane yield in both the inoculants used, showed a decrease 
of 5.3% and 23.1%, respectively. While biochar in general has several advantages, if it does 
not possess enough redox-active characteristics, then the AD process can be inhibited in the 
methanogenesis [28]. 
Besides the pyrolysis temperature and feedstock type, the biochar dosage plays a crucial role 
in AD systems. Although in general the higher the biochar dosage, the higher the methane 
production is, but there is an optimal level for biochar addition. For instance, [29] mentions 
that extremely high doses of biochar can have inhibitory effects on the overall AD. The article 
also states that the biochar dosage of 10 g/L helped the process by even doubling the methane 
yield, but higher values suggested a decrease in the production. In another study by [30], the 
optimal biochar dose with positive effects was below 10 g/L (the largest increased was 
registered <5 g/L). 
Methanogenesis, which is the last step in the AD process, can be inhibited at the smallest 
variations in conditions. The inhibition of methanogenesis depends on the substrate utilized 
as well, because of the organic materials found inside. This can ultimately destabilize the AD 
process by excessive accumulation of ammonia and VFAs. Luckily, solutions include adjusting 
the pH, temperature and reducing the loading rate. Another option for overcoming the 
ammonia and VFAs accumulation is the addition of biochar into the AD system [27].

1.3.5 Other biochar applications 
Biochar has increased in popularity in the last decade due to its advantageous and multiple 
applications. Its main application is on environmental management and sustainability, possible 
through GHGs mitigation, wastewater treatment, agriculture, heavy metal immobilization, toxic 
pollutant remediation, etc [31]. Some of its uses are described below. 
GHGs emission reduction 
Due to biochar’s ability to sequester carbon and reduce the emissions from the soil, such as 
methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O) and carbon dioxide (CO2), it can reduce the GHGs 
emissions. The interaction between biochar and the abiotic factors mentioned before it is still 
inadequately comprehended, but four different mechanisms that are helping this cause are 
known [31]. 
The first mechanism is represented by the reduction of emissions during the production. In 
other words, instead of burning directly the biomass, when biochar is produced from biomass, 
bioenergy is being generated and reduces the GHGs emissions. A secondary effect of this 
phenomenon is that the biochar converted from biomass, the carbon in the feedstock becomes 
more stable and the chances of re-entering the atmosphere are far lower. The second 
mechanism is related to the soil fertility, which can be greatly improved by addition of biochar 
and diminishes the economic pressure. Lower soil emissions are the third one, but it is quite 
unreliable since it depends on different soil properties like moisture, type and how biochar is 
aging in the soil. The last mechanism is characterized by the production of biochar, which 
prevents the carbon from feedstock to be released back into the environment. Research finds 
that addition of biochar to the soil in large-scale contributes to the climate change mitigation 
[31]. 
Wastewater treatment 
Biochar can be used also as a cheaper granular activated carbon (GAC) so that the water 
could be free of organic contaminants. It is recommended that the biochar used is subjected 
to post-treatment since secondary pollution can be produced if done otherwise. Thus, the 
biochar must be checked prior to its usage for possible pollutants, such as PAH and heavy 
metals [31]. Because of its adsorptive qualities, biochar may be utilized in water filtering 
systems to produce clean water. Given its porous nature, which allows it to trap contaminants, 
biochar is effective in eliminating the impurities and enhancing the overall potability of the 
water [31]. 
Agriculture 
Multiple research papers state the ability of biochar to improve the agricultural domain with its 
characteristics. The sustainable management in agriculture can have an increase in the crop 
yield  due to biochar, which can improve the soil fertility, soil structure, water holding capacity 
(WHC), CEC and the microbial activity in the soil. The application of biochar in diverse 
experimental setups and soil types, strengthens the affirmation of biochar’s positive effect on 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
crop yields. In the article [32], multiple studies have been collected and tested on the effect of 
biochar on crops yield. The studies divided their discoveries according to the following 
characteristics: biochar type, application rate of biochar, experimental crop, soil type, duration 
of experiments, country, and type of experiment.  
Most of the experiments were performed on the field, but there were also some interests in 
the pot setup in countries like Germany and Thailand. Depending on the type of biochar, 
Acacia Mangium showed a significant improvement in the crop yield, but it was used together 
with a fertilizer on acidic soil. In a long-term experiment, biochar combined with chicken 
manure proved to have the highest cumulative yield for rice and sorghum. Regarding the 
application rate and its effectiveness, in general higher application rates have a more 
significant yield improvement. For example, biochar from Eucalyptus deglupta at 90 g/kg 
resulted in a 46% increase in bean yield. From the overall trends presented in [32], biochar 
demonstrates once again its versatility, being able to significantly improve the agricultural 
sector in different ways.  
1.4 
 Research gap and motivation 
The motivation for this project is to improve the bio-methane production from the AD process 
by adding biochar obtained from the pyrolysis process. By utilizing biochar in the AD process, 
the biomethane production can be improved and overcome certain constraints. The effect of 
different biochars produced from 2 feedstocks, green waste (GW) and composting residues 
(CR) in different pyrolysis conditions (temperature and residence time) is evaluated as well. 
According to earlier research, biochar can play a major role in the increase of AD process’s 
capacity to produce bio-methane. However, there is still a research gap in understanding the 
potential of biochar in improving AD. The main question of the thesis is represented by: 
“How different biochars produced from different biomass and under different pyrolysis 
conditions can affect the biogas production?” 
The aim of this study is to fill this research gap and provide a better understanding of how 
biochar affects the process. The findings of this study will hopefully contribute to the 
development of more efficient and sustainable anaerobic digestion, while also promoting 
sustainability and environmental progress [5].

 Objectives and hypothesis

to analyse the effect of different biochar on bio-methane production during AD process;

 
to analyse the effects of different biochars with different properties on methane yield 
during AD process; 
 
to investigate the possible correlations between the characteristics of biochar and its 
effect on biogas production.

BMP experiments 
In recent years, the biochemical methane potential (BMP) tests have become a popular 
method to determine the methane potential and biodegradability of waste biomass. The BMP 
test method consists in samples known under the name of blank, control and substrate. For 
the sake of consistency and statistical analysis, the experiment should be performed in 
triplicates, but due to limited time and high quantity of batches, this project was done in 
duplicates [34] [35]. 
The three types of groups mentioned above can be differentiated easily. The blank bottles are 
the first to be assembled since they contain the least number of compounds – inoculum with 
a medium, which in general is water. The control bottles consist of inoculum with water, just 
like blank, but also the substrate (Avicel as model substrate or cattle manure as real substrate). 
They play a major role in the BMP experiments since it leads to the calculation of the 
theoretical BMP and determines the accuracy of the experiment. Lastly, in this case, the 
substrate bottles are the test bottles, and they are made from inoculum, the medium, substrate 
and biochar [34]. 
Multiple research papers state that for a successful outcome of the BMP method, consistent 
conditions must be maintained in order to increase the performance. Thus, the experiments 
performed in this project were ensured to have an environment suitable for the consortia to 
prosper under certain temperature value, a thorough mixing given by the incubator and a long 
enough period of time (at least 29 days) so that the biodegradable material could be 
disintegrated [34].

At least three injections for each gas bag were inserted into the GC machine until the 
calibration coefficient reached the value of 0.999. Once this part was completed, all 4 
experiments were assembled at different times, ready for injection. 
The individual VFA (acetate, propionate, isobutyrate, butyrate, isovalerate, valerate, hexanoic 
acid) composition was measured using Agilent 7890A gas chromatograph (Agilent 
Technologies), equipped with a flame ionization detector (FID) and SGE capillary column with 
helium as the gas carrier. The concentration (mg/L) of each detected VFA was summed up to 
obtain the total VFA (TVFA) concentration. The pH trend was monitored using FiveEasy Plus 
Benchtop FP20 [38]. 
 
2.6 
Statistical analysis 
For a comprehended result, statistical analysis was applied to all the data collected in this 
project. Each experiment was conducted twice, and the average values and standard 
deviations were determined. The analysis indicated that the measurements taken had no big 
differences between the replicates for each of the experiments. In order to compare 
quantitative variables among different experiments, one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) 
was applied, following Tukey post hoc test (p < 0.05) [34].

3.5 
 Biochar characterization 
CIRAD performed the following examinations for the characteristics of the 18 biochar samples 
provided to DTU: specific area, organic contaminants (PAH and PCDD analysis), 
granulometry for WHC and CEC, pH, EC, salt content, total moisture, elemental analysis, and 
atomic ratios. 
For the BET surface area, the highest value was registered for biochar 600-15 GW with a 
value of 382 m2/g and the lowest 181 m2/g for 500-15 GW. It can be concluded that the short 
duration biochars produce less BET surface area which is in accordance with other research 
studies. See Figure 6.3 from Appendix. The organic contaminants analysis was performed for 
PAH and PCDD, only for the biochars treated at 500°C and 700°C. Both raw materials showed 
that the PAH and PCDD content in biochars are below the threshold values from EBC at 
laboratory scale facilities, proving that the samples possess no risk in usage. 
All of the biochars used in this project own the same values of granulometry for WHC and 
CEC (Figure 6.4  from Appendix). The most important findings are related to the pH, EC, C/N 
and WHC, which were done only for the first 10 biochar samples, without the post-treatment. 
The pH increases significantly with increasing temperature, with the highest pH registered at 
700-30. From Figure 6.5, it can also be noticed that the pH increases with 30 minutes 
treatment compared to 15 minutes. The feedstock was an important parameter as well, 
proving that CR gives a higher pH. The lowest EC value was registered for biochar treated at 
500-15, with 0.352 mS/cm. As well as pH, EC tends to increase with temperature and 
residence time. CR presents better EC than GW feedstock. Regarding the atomic ratio of C/N, 
it is increased by temperature and time of pyrolysis, but this time, the GW proves to be higher 
than CR. Lastly, the WHC takes into account an extra parameter compared to the other 
important analysis -  granulometry. The data shows that WHC is decreasing with lower 
temperatures and higher residence time. CR is the favourable raw material once again. In 
respects to the last parameter, WHC is increasing with finer granulometry. See Figures 6.6-
6.8.

characterization, and they require more time to finalize the laboratory and data analysis for all 
of the biochar treatments. 
Further characteristics of biochar should be unveiled such as functional groups and elemental 
analysis in order to find the main mechanism behind the positive effect of the biochars used 
in this study. These properties could provide a deeper understanding of the experiments, and 
the association between main biochar specifications and CH4 yield in AD process.

4. Conclusion 
 
The pyrolysis temperature, retention time and the type of biomass used are the most important 
parameters when it comes to the biochar’s properties. Through the experiments performed in 
this thesis, it was showed how these parameters can affect the AD process and the 
biomethane production. In general, for the biochar treatment without gasification, the best 
results were registered for lower temperatures and residence time. From EXP-1 and EXP-2, 
the most advantageous biochars are test 500-15 CR, 600-30 CR and 600-15 GW, 500-15 GW, 
respectively. The trend shows that the smallest change in methane yield comes from the 
biochars treated at 700°C, no matter the type of raw material used. EXP-3 tends to be different 
since only biochars at 500°C and the ones with post-treatment were tested. Although in 
general the base biochars (500-15), which were tested in every experiment, showed the best 
results, the extra step in treatment proved to be better. The best results come from CR 
biochars treated at 900°C for 60 minutes and 950°C, which were the highest pyrolysis 
temperatures. The full characterization of biochar should be completed in order to understand 
the main role of the additive in the increase of methane yield. 
The characterization of biochar received from CIRAD, can conclude that properties such as 
moisture content, EC, pH, C/N and WHC possess a great effect on the biogas production. For 
the 10 biochars without post-treatment, lower pH, EC and C/N ratios are linked to higher 
methane yield production.

5. Recommendations and future perspectives 
 
The characteristics of biochar provided by CIRAD are not complete. In order to investigate the 
real effect of biochar on the biogas production process, full knowledge of the characterization 
is required. According to the statistical analysis performed for the experimental data, there is 
no significant difference between the types of biochar, therefore a more economical and 
sustainable solution would be to use the biochar produced at different conditions together in 
the AD process.  
The data obtained and collected at the laboratory scale could be validated through a technical-
economic-environmental 
assessment. 
A 
comprehensive 
technico-economic 
and 
environmental analysis can be done, following the 3 criteria: technical feasibility, economic 
viability and environmental sustainability. From the technical feasibility perspective, the 
experiment performed in lab scale can be validated by scaling up from 118 mL to 500 mL, 1L, 
10L and 100L, and so on, which will ultimately make the industrial scale possible. Even though 
a reduction of methane production might be detected, scaling-up must be validated via an 
economical point of view. The economic assessment takes into consideration 3 main aspects: 
capital expenditure (CAPEX), operational expenditure (OPEX) and revenues, coming from the 
selling of biogas and digestate produced in AD. If the profit and the net present value (NPV) 
are higher than zero in the chosen life plant cycle, then the study is successful, and it can be 
confirmed with the environmental analysis via Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). ISO-14040-
14044 is the standardized method used for LCA. The goal and scope of the environmental 
analysis in this project is to reduce the kg CO2 equivalent of AD. Once these factors are 
defined, the inventory, which is the collection of the operative parameters can be fulfilled and 
the impact can be predicted. The implementation of this project at full scale can be done if the 
interpretation of results supports it [39]. 
In the future, for a better understanding of the main role of biochar on different steps of AD 
process, further experiments should be conducted with model substrates such as glucose, 
butyrate, propionate, acetate and H2/CO2 to observe which step in the process is affected by 
biochar and what is the main mechanism responsible for improvement in process yield by 
biochar addition. As a future perspective, the next plan is to select the best 4 samples of 
biochar and test them at different concentrations to be sure if the used concentration was 
optimum for all of the selected biochars or not. 
Ongoing studies are essential to explore biochar’s full potential, especially in various soil types 
and conditions, to maximize the benefits and minimize the risks. By using model substrates, 
the Gompertz model could be applied for a thorough kinetic study. The mathematical model is 
able to predict the theoretical maximum production of methane, as well as the theoretical lag-
phase of the process, simplifying future experiments [40]. Additionally, microbial analysis 
should be conducted for the biochar containing bottles with the highest methane yield. Based 
on this investigation, any potential changes that may occur between different groups of 
bacteria and archaea, as a results of biochar addition, will be studied.
