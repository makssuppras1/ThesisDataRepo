Abstract 
This project deals with the modelling of flow and heat transport in the VADER 1000 machine, Figure 1 using 
Comsol Multiphysics. The modelling is carried out for two parts of the machine the filament chamber and 
the distribution chamber.  
The filament chamber contains a filament which in this project has a temperature higher than the ambient. 
The purpose of this modelling is to map the flow and calculate the heat transfer coefficient. In the 2D 
simulations it was found that by setting the inlet as small as possible (governed by parameters R3 and R4, 
Table 4) the highest heat transfer coefficient was achieved with a value of 60.05 [W/m2K]. The inlet in this 
case had the following configuration: R3=9[mm] and R4=9.5[mm]. 
A 3D simulation was also carried out for the filament chamber, however only one twentieth of the geometry 
was simulated. These results also showed that the best heat transfer coefficient would be achieved by setting 
R3=9[mm] and R4=9.5[mm] reaching a value of 88.1 [W/m2K] for the heat transfer coefficient. This result was 
obtained after carrying out a mesh refinement, where the final velocity profile is seen in Figure 46. The jet 
Reynolds number was estimated to 1168, showing that the jet must be laminar.    
An analysis was carried out in which the geometry was slightly altered. The amount of inlets where changed 
from 20 to 15 and 25. One fifteenth and one twenty-fifth of the geometry was analyzed in the same manner 
as the original twentieth piece. However neither case proved better than original case as the heat transfer 
coefficient for the one 15th piece yielded a lower value and for the 25th piece a value which was almost the 
same as the original case.  
The distribution chamber was also analyzed, however due to the geometry only in 3D. This part required 
turbulent models, and three different models were tested: The K-Epsilon, K-Omega and the Algebraic Yplus 
model. After the first simulations had converged it was clear that the K-Epsilon outperformed the K-Omega 
model and the Algebraic Yplus model, therefore all remaining simulations were carried out using only K-
Epsilon. The Reynolds number was also calculated, and yielded a value of 5425 which is considered turbulent. 
Using a paired t-test it was found that the flow could only be assumed uniformly distributed among along 
the outlets when the difference between R3 and R4 was maximum 1.3[mm], Table 11. The amount of outlets 
was altered also to 15 and 25 to see if a better distribution could be achieved however this was not the case. 
There was found no evidence that using 15 or 25 outlet/inlets would in any way improve either the 
distribution of the flow or the heat transfer coefficient. It was however found that by changing the location 
of the inlet, Figure 65 a much better distribution could be obtained.  
The final part of the project combines the distribution values found in the distribution chamber with the full 
figure of the filament chamber. This simulation was carried out for R3=9[mm] and R4=9.5[mm] as this setting 
throughout the project yielded the highest heat transfer coefficient. In the final flow figure, Figure 82, it was 
seen that the flow profile was not equal to that found in twentieth piece simulation, as the flow profile in the 
full figure was much more affected by the Coandâ effect. The Reynolds number was slightly lower, 1039, 
however the heat transfer coefficient was found at 146[W/m2K], which is considered to be the final result for 
the heat transfer coefficient.  
The full part simulation of the filament chamber were also carried out for R3=9[mm] and R4=11[mm] to test 
the increase in the heat transfer coefficient for larger tubes. This simulation yielded a heat transfer coefficient 
of 61.56[W/m2K] which is better than the corresponded 2D and 20th part simulation but still much lower than 
the result obtained for the smallest tubes.

Background: 
Rheometers come in many different styles and variants. A filament stretching rheometer (FSR) is used for 
measuring the rheological properties of viscous and viscoelastic fluids. When this became of interest to 
implement the early rheometers had no temperature control and the rheological properties were measured 
at room temperature only [1].  
However later design implementations allowed for testing at higher temperatures by installing ovens into 
the FSR. One FSR which uses an oven to heat the viscous samples is the Versatile Accurate Deformation 
Elongation Rheometer (VADER 1000). This apparatus is capable of reaching up to 250 ᵒC however the 
temperature is not clear [2].  
Purpose and project description 
This project seeks to investigate this FSR apparatus described above through thorough Computational Fluid 
Dynamics (CFD) analysis. The CFD application used is the computer software program Comsol Multiphysics, 
in which both 2D and 3D simulations of the heat transfer and flow will be performed. The purpose of the 2D 
simulations is to acquire preliminary results for the system before performing the rigorous 3D simulations. 
Using these simulations, estimations of the heat transfer coefficient will be made, and system optimization 
will be proposed. Comparison of the results from the 2D and 3D simulations will be made and the deviation 
between the two must be analyzed. Furthermore the geometry of the system will gradually be expanded to 
include more elements of the apparatus. The expansion will initially include the distribution chamber 
beneath the actual stretching of the filament.   
The structure of the filament will be assumed to be uniform and investigations of different deformations of 
the filament are not included in this project.

Introduction 
In chemical industry the physical properties of a medium are crucial when designing process equipment. 
Equally important are the studies of fluid dynamics which has a wide range of applications which among 
other include calculation of forces, momentum and mass flow in pipelines. Fluid dynamics is a part of the 
fluid mechanics which deals with fluid flow.  
Using today’s modern technology it has become possible to simulate the fluid dynamics of simple and 
complex systems using computational fluid dynamics (CFD) tools. Using CFD tools it is possible to make a 
numerical analysis of a fluid flow and simulate the velocity profile for a fluid or gas throughout a system. In 
order to do so it is required that the system is well specified, meaning that all boundaries in the system must 
be known.  
Being able to simulate a flow correctly has proven valuable as this knowledge serve as a basis which allows 
for better decisions to be made. Performing a simulation of a system can provide information on whether 
the system behaves as intended or not. This can save money and time as the system would not have to be 
physically built until it is certain it operates satisfactory.  
In this project a CFD model is made for an elongation filament rheometer, more specific the Versatile 
Accurate Deformation Elongation Rheometer (VADER 1000), Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1: Picture of the VADER 1000, [3].  
The primary use of the VADER 1000 is to measure the viscoelastic properties of polymers. In the VADER 1000 
a transport of heat also occurs as the filament might be warmer than the ambient gas. The CFD model is not 
made for the complete geometry of the VADER 1000 but only for parts of it. In such case the heat transport 
from the filament to gas must also be included in the CFD model. This means that beside a flow model to 
solve the fluid/gas flow a heat model must also be included to solve the heat transport. 
Before the simulations of the system are conducted a literature study which investigates the theory is 
performed.

Literature Study  
The purpose of this section is to examine literature which is relevant for the project. In this project flow and 
heat transfer is modelled for a specific rheometer, the VADER 1000.  
However lots of different kinds of rheometers exist and the literature in this area is therefore investigated, 
to understand how rheometers generally operate. The most commonly used rheometers are the Münstedt 
tensile rheometer, the Meissner elongation rheometer and the filament stretching rheometer [4].  
After a short review of these rheometers, flow types are investigated. Modelling flow transport is a crucial 
element of this project and choosing the right model for the right system is essential in order to obtain a 
correct result. A few flow models will thus be analyzed and the parameters which are used in the models are 
examined. It is expected that jet flow will be an important part of the project and it is therefore also reviewed.  
The other part of this project is heat transport. In the same manner as flow transport heat transport is 
examined, and the governing equations are presented. As this is all modelled in a computational fluid 
dynamics program, it is also reviewed how these programs operates in general and how the basics of the 
program is setup.

The Münstedt Tensile Rheometer 
The Münstedt tensile rheometer (MTR) was developed by H. Münstedt [5]. The MTR can be used as a tensile 
testing machine by stretching a polymer sample with exponentially increasing speed. The main feature of the 
MTR compared to other rheometers is the easy access to the sample during the time of testing [5].  
 
Figure 2: Münstedt tensile rheometer, a) Heating liquid; b) Load-measuring cell; c) Specimen; d) Oil bath; e) Temperature-controlled 
vessel; f) Belt; g) D.c. motor; h) Strain gauge; i) Takeup spool; k) Monitoring and control unit. [6] 
On Figure 2 a sketch of the MTR can be seen. The sample is as shown suspended in an oil bath which must 
be adapted to that of the polymer melt. The oil compensates the weight of the sample caused by buoyancy 
and also operates as a medium transferring heat from the vessel to the sample. One end is fixed to the force 
transducer and the other to the pulling device. [7] 
The MTR has typically been used to characterize the elongation properties of polymer materials, specifically 
in dependence of the molecular structure.

Meissner Elongation Rheometer 
In 1994 J. Meissner published an article about a new elongation rheometer, later to be known as the Meissner 
Elongation Rheometer (MER), [8]. The MER is formed by six metal plates which have electrical heater wires 
imbedded. These metal plates which are blackened on the inner side generate a homogeneous temperature 
inside the rheometer. The apparatus supports temperatures of well over 300 ᵒC [8]. 
In the MER the sample is placed horizontal between two metal conveyor belts, which can be seen on Figure 
3.  
 
Figure 3: Schematic of the Meissner Elongation Rheometer. The sample is clamped by a metal conveyor belt (MB), supporting the 
sample by a gas cushion(SSS) and video recording (VC),[8].  
For detailed information about the abbreviations in Figure 3 the reader is referred to [8]. In a MER it is 
possible to measure the polymer melt elongation which is important for polymer characterization.

Filament Stretching Rheometer 
A third rheometer is the filament stretching rheometer (FSR). The FSR consist of two end-plates where the 
sample is placed between the two. An electronic control system imposes a predetermined velocity profile on 
either one or both of the plates [1]. The most important measurements to obtain are the force applied over 
time 𝐹(𝑡) and the diameter of the filament at the midplane. A basic schematic of the FSR can be seen on 
Figure 4. 
 
Figure 4: Schematics of a basic filament stretching rheometer, showing the essential components required for the apparatus [9].  
The filament diameter is measured using devices such as lasers micrometers, wire gauges or by analyzing  
video images of the filament [1]. In this setup the geometric dimensions and the motor capacity of the system 
which operates the motion of the endplates determine the range of the accessible experimental parameters.

Versatile Accurate Deformation Extensional Rheometer 
The Versatile Accurate Deformation Extensional Rheometer (VADER 1000) is like the FSR a stretching 
rheometer as the primary objective is to obtain properties for viscoelastic materials. However the VADER 
1000 is more advanced than the FSR and it is possible to have a temperature control which allows testing at 
a wider variety of temperatures [3]. Heating the polymer sample allows melting all the crystals and is thus 
similar to the Münstedt tensile rheometer, which however used a heating jacket. A sketch of the VADER 1000 
can be seen in Figure 5. 
 
Figure 5: VADER 1000 as seen from the side, edited picture from [10] 
In the VADER 1000 the sample, the upper and lower pulling element is heated like an oven using a 
surrounding chamber which means it is possible to keep a constant high (<250ᵒC) temperature inside the 
rheometer [2]. As it was for the FSR the smallest diameter of the stretched filament is measured. In the 
VADER 1000 a jet stream of nitrogen is used to keep the filament isothermal.

Laminar Flow 
Whereas the previous sections dealt with different rheometers, this section will deal with understanding 
laminar flow. This includes an analysis of the Reynolds number, description of characteristic of laminar flow, 
and an analysis of the governing equations.  
It is possible to predict if a flow in a pipe (and other geometries) will be either laminar or turbulent. Whether 
a flow will be laminar or turbulent depends on the Reynolds number (Re). The Reynolds number is defined 
as the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces and thus quantifies the relative importance of these forces in 
a given flow [11].

When the Reynolds number is below 2000 the flow is considered laminar, and the viscous forces dominate 
the flow. When the Reynolds number is above 4000 it can surely be assumed that the flow is turbulent [12],

met in order for the Navier-Stokes equation to be applicable:

Turbulent Flow 
Having analyzed the laminar flow, the same must now be done for turbulent flows. As described in the last 
section the Reynolds number determine the turbulence of the flow. For laminar flow the Navier-Stokes 
equation is used to model the flow, however this equation is not sufficient alone to model turbulent flow. As

Using the RANS formulation for the flow field additional unknowns are added to the system, and 
approximations for the flow field at the walls must be introduced. How these unknowns and approximations 
are dealt with depended on which turbulent model is used. 
Usually a turbulent viscosity is introduced, which has nothing to do with the physical viscosity [17]. The 
purpose of the turbulent viscosity is to model the transport and dissipation of energy which has otherwise 
been neglected when making the average assumption as shown in Figure 8. The turbulent viscosity is 
incorporated into the momentum balance by adding it to the physical viscosity introducing the effective 
viscosity as shown in (6), [17].

Algebraic yPlus Model 
The Algebraic yPlus model is a zero-equation turbulent model which means it does not require the solution 
of any additional equations, as they are calculated directly from the flow variables [18]. This results in the 
fact that the model may not be able to correctly account for history effects on the turbulence, which for 
example might be convection and diffusion of turbulent energy. The algebraic yPlus model is based on 
Prandtl’s mixing length theory and is suitable for internal flows and is less mesh sensitive than most other 
turbulent models [19].  
The Algebraic yPlus model computes the turbulent viscosity based only on the local fluid velocity and the 
distance to the closest wall [16]. Using this turbulent model the equation of motion becomes (7).

The Coandâ Effect 
The Coandâ effect was first discovered by Henri Coandâ in 1930 [25]. The Coandâ effect describes the 
tendency in which a moving fluid or gas attach itself to a surface and flow along it.  As the medium flows 
across a surface friction between the two is formed and slows the moving medium. This resistance draws the 
fluid towards the surface to the point where it sticks to the surface. This means that a jet discharging from a 
nozzle tends to follow a nearby curved surface, in extreme cases this extends to point where the jet is bending 
around corners. An example of the Coandâ effect can be seen on Figure 10.  
 
Figure 10: Example of the Coandâ effect, [26]. Notice on the right figure the stippled lines which indicate an asymmetry.  
When a liquid or gas is injected into a wide container the jet will entrain liquid or gas from both sides and 
become broader. The jet will pick up mass along the sides which will cause a pressure drop in the zones 
between the stream and the wall. This creates an unstable situation where the higher pressure of the 
ambient surroundings will push some of the medium back into the low-pressure zone on both sides to 
equalize the pressure [26].  
However in asymmetric geometries it is possible that this equalizing return flow pushes the jet towards one 
of the walls. As the jet keeps flowing the zone between the jet and wall narrows, and there is less room to 
access for the counter flow to replace the medium which is picked up by the jet. This causes the pressure to

Pressure drop 
Considering the jet stream theory above it is likely that a pressure drop would occur in the system. If this 
pressure drop becomes too large it might have critical impact on the system. Pressure drop can be defined 
as the quantity of pressure which is lost when a gas flows through a device/pipe etc. This drop in pressure 
can be due to frictional resistance, height differences even walls of a system will cause a certain amount of 
pressure drop. Pressure drop should be taken seriously as cavitation takes place when the operating pressure 
becomes lower than the vapor pressure, [27]. This cavitation causes bubbles and gas pockets which 
eventually will lead to a collapse of the system. In large systems (piping and distillation columns) these 
cavitation can result in explosion and collapse of the system.

Computational Fluid Dynamics 
Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) is a tool in which it is possible to simulate fluid dynamics in simple and 
complex systems. Lots of different CFD programs exist, e.g. Blend, OpenFoam and Ansys, however only one 
CFD program will be used in this project, Comsol Multiphysics.  
CFD programs all have that in common that they use numerical methods to solve complex mathematical 
models which are required to describe the relevant interactions in the system. It is important to keep in mind 
that when using CFD it is unlikely if not impossible to include all aspects of the system. Therefore results from 
a CFD model should be treated as estimations only and not conclusive results.  
As with all investigation methods the formulation of the system, boundaries and parameters must be exact 
for the CFD procedure to work. The crucial part of CFD is to balance momentum, heat and mass. This is done 
using the continuity theory approach in which all balance quantities and physical parameters changes 
continuously within each phase. This becomes especially relevant when considering two phase systems 
which might require different approaches [36].  
==PAGE:28==

In order to solve the CFD model the initial and boundary conditions must specified. These conditions are used 
to solve the partial differential equations which must be solved for the CFD model. Typically for CFD models 
boundary conditions include an [36]: 
 
Inlet, which is where the fluid/gas enters the system 
 
Outlet, which is where the fluid/gas exits the system 
 
Wall, which constitutes the system 
 
Symmetry, in some systems it is an advantage to symmetries to decrease computational effort 
Having setup op the boundary conditions for the CFD model, a mesh must be applied to the system. When 
analyzing a fluid or heat flow the geometry must be divided into small nodes. It is then possible discretize 
and solve the govern partial differential equations (PDE) inside each node. In order to obtain a proper picture 
of the flow proper continuity among the nodes must be ensured. This is normally achieved by dividing the 
geometry into smaller and smaller nodes. This does however require more computational power as more 
PDE’s most be solved and can yield long computational times.  [37] 
Once the CFD model is well described (boundary conditions set, along with parameters and an acceptable 
mesh is constructed) it can be solved. There exist multiple ways of numerical methods of solving the 
problems, however it is not covered in this project.  
When setting up the CFD model it is also to be considered whether the system is stationary or transient. In 
stationary cases it is assumed that variables does not change with time, however in transient cases the 
variables does change with time. For transient cases it is thus necessary to discretize the time, which 
introduces a new “error” to the mathematical modelling. The discrete solution which is computed by the 
software and the “real” solutions might thus differ due to the difference made by the continuous 
mathematical models and the discretized equations.  
Industrially CFD is a tool which is of increasing importance. The application of CFD tools is becoming more 
and more required as technology require more advanced systems to be efficient. The CFD tool gives good 
estimations and allows for both simple and fast calculations of the functionality of systems. This is an 
economical benefit as the simulations are faster to run and analyze than building the specific system and 
then do the testing.

2D Mesh Analysis 
Depending on how much computer memory is available a very coarse mesh can be chosen or a very fine 
mesh can be used. It has earlier been shown [37] that the heat transfer coefficient is mesh sensitive. It is 
therefore important to generate a mesh in which the heat transfer coefficient is completely or almost 
independent of mesh.  
Two different kinds of mesh has been investigated, an unstructured mesh or a mapped mesh. The 
unstructured mesh splits the geometry into tetrahedrals and is built in Comsol simply by using the “build all” 
bottom in Comsol after setting the mesh to “user-controlled” and choosing the wanted quality.  
Applying a mapped mesh requires a bit more modification to Comsol code in order to work. In order to apply 
the mapped mesh the geometry is accessed and a “Partition Domains” tap is added under “Booleans and 
Partitions”. In this tap the whole geometry is the box “Domains to partition”, afterwards the drop-down box 
“partition with” is changed to “extended edges” and the edges chosen is as shown on Figure 15 where the

uniform distributed. In order to do so a paired t-test has been used to conduct this analysis. In a paired to t-
test the average value in two paired test samples are tested.  
The procedure is relative simple as the difference between each pair of numbers is calculated. After all 
samples have been calculated it becomes possible to test if the average between the two is significantly 
different from zero. [38] 
The achieved results from the distribution chamber are thus compared to the ideal value that each outlet 
should have; 1∙10-5[m3/s].

is NO difference between measured and expected values”. The alternative hypothesis thus being “There is a 
difference between measured and expected values”.  The hypothesis may also be presented in the typical 
statistical way shown in (42) where µ present the true average value between the two samples

In such cases it is possible to state that with 95 % certainty that the null hypothesis is true.

obtained and the theoretical results are written in separate column vectors, naming the first vector e.g. R8

Discussion 
In this section the obtained results are discussed and put into perspective. The purpose is to explain how and 
why the results obtained behave as they do. 
The most interesting thing to analyze is why the heat transfer coefficients obtained in 2D and 3D differs 
significantly. It was seen in Table 15 the heat transfer coefficient increase both when going from 2D to 3D 
piece simulation and from piece simulation to full figure simulation. In 2D axial symmetric the simulation is 
based on an infinitely thin slice of the geometry which is duplicated to yield a 3D view. This neglects the fact 
that the inlet is based on tubes and instead yields a slit inlet. This thus provides a solution which does not 
reflect the actual geometry which is investigated. Comparing the flow profiles it could also be seen that 
velocity in the 2D simulation was significantly lower compared to the 3D simulations.  
Looking at the Reynolds number found for 2D and the 20th piece a large increase was seen for especially for 
the smaller tubes, Table 16.

In Table 16 it can be seen that the 3D 20th piece simulation yield a Reynolds number which is 554.77 higher 
for the small tubes compared to the 2D simulation. In a laminar flow scale this is a very large increase and 
the effect is directly seen in the heat transfer coefficient which also increases.  
Comparing the 20th piece simulation to the full figure simulation an even larger increase in the heat transfer 
coefficient is observed. This increase does not seem to be coupled with the difference in the Reynolds 
number as the full figure actually provides as lower Reynolds number than the 20th piece simulation.  
The increase in heat transfer coefficient can instead be found by analyzing the velocity profiles. The figures 
in question are Figure 46 and Figure 82. In Figure 46 the final velocity profile for the 20th piece simulation is 
seen. The flow is not very affected by the Coandâ effect and the flow only comes into contact with the upper 
part of the filament. Compared to the full figure, Figure 82 the velocity profile is immediately affected by the 
Coandâ effect and the flow is in contact with the filament from bottom to top. This explains why the heat 
transfer coefficient becomes so much higher compared to the 20th piece simulation.  
This raises the question as to why do the 20th piece simulation yield this flow profile. The final simulation 
were carried out with over 230,000 mesh elements which compared to the full figure (425,000) is much more 
dense. If the full figure should have been simulated using the same mesh density as the 20th piece the amount 
of nodes used should have been approximately 4,600,000. Such simulation would not be possible to carry 
out on the current available computer equipment. Pushing the amount of nodes to 425,000 proved hard to 
converge and took most of the computer memory, thus going for an even finer mesh would not be possible. 
In the mesh tab in Comsol the quality of the mesh can be found under “statistics” which will show the average 
quality of the mesh elements. Using this tab it was found that the average quality of the mesh elements for 
the 20th piece were 0.70 and for the full figure 0.65 (max is 1). The difference in average mesh element quality 
is not very large and the difference between the flows is not due to a significantly better mesh in one of the 
simulations.

A reason for the difference in flow might be found in the boundary conditions. In the 20th piece simulations 
the inner boundaries are set as “symmetry”, cf. Figure 31. It is possible that another boundary condition 
would be more appropriate. Seen from a mathematical point of view the equation is given as 𝒖̅ ∙𝒏̅ = 0, 
meaning that the velocity times the normal vector must be equal to zero in this boundary. In such case it is 
not possible to incorporate the interaction between the jets. It is unlikely that each jet will only move inside 
a twentieth part of the geometry as they will expand moving into each other and merge into one flow inside 
the chamber. Even though the geometry is symmetric in the filament chamber it would seem that the 
interactions between the jets require a full figure simulation in order to properly view the flow.  
If the full figure is compared to the 2D simulation the flow looks much alike, as both flows are immediately 
affected by the Coandâ effect. However here the difference is found in the fact that the 2D simulation under 
predicts the velocity magnitude by a factor 10 which is why the Reynolds number is also lower for the 2D 
results.   
For the larger tubes the flow profiles showed a clear Coandâ effect for both the 20th piece simulation and for 
the full figure. In this case the heat transfer coefficient also increases from the 20th piece simulation to the 
full figure, however due to the distribution found the flow ended being very different from what the 20th 
piece simulation predicted.  
In the distribution chamber section it was found that by using a tangential inlet a much better distribution 
were obtainable. In all but one case it was possible to accept the null-hypothesis, which stated that with a 95 
% certainty it can be assumed that the flow was uniformly distributed. If the distribution found for the 
tangential inlet were used instead of the original a smaller difference between the flows would have been 
seen for the large tube simulation. In such case the flow predicted by the 20th piece simulation would be 
closer to the full figure simulation. 
In the simulations for the distribution chamber approximately 100,000 mesh nodes were used. It was not 
possible to achieve convergence on finer mesh. This could have contributed to a nicer flow profile especially 
for the smaller tubes. However as the system in this part is turbulent and turbulent models thus had to be 
used it is likely that this influenced the convergence for better meshes.  
It was also investigated how additional and fewer tubes might influence the distribution and heat transfer 
coefficient. When using fewer tubes (15) than the original 20 it was quickly found that it was even harder to 
achieve convergence, especially for the filament chamber. In the cases where the amount of tubes were 
decreased the flow must be of a higher magnitude as 15 tubes has to have the same volumetric flow as 20 
tubes. These simulations where generally converged with fewer mesh nodes. As stated in the section which 
investigated the heat transfer coefficient for 15 and 25 tubes it was not possible to achieve convergence 
using more than 15,000 nodes for 15 tubes. However using 25 tubes it was the opposite case as the 
simulations where easier to converge and had more mesh nodes. This is also clearly reflected in the figures 
made showing the heat transfer coefficient to the different inlet tube sizes Figure 47 and Figure 48. In Figure 
47 it was seen that the data points does not follow a clear tendency while the data points in Figure 48 follows 
a smoother tendency.  
This might affect the conclusion made for the 15 tube simulation as it was also seen in Table 9 that a mesh 
refinement actually also resulted in better heat transfer coefficients. For the 25th tube simulation a 
comparison where made of the heat transfer coefficients obtained to the heat transfer coefficient obtained 
for the 20th tube simulation, Figure 49. Here it was seen that there was not much difference between the 
heat transfer coefficients whilst having approximately the same amount of mesh nodes.

It is possible that the filament chamber have multiple steady state solutions. In this case it means that all of 
the heat transfer coefficients found are correct for the given steady state which the solver has found. The 
multiple steady states could occur as the jet flow moves a bit from side to side (like a flame). This means that 
the jet is only in one steady state for a short period of time and will seek a new steady state. This could also 
explain why there is such difference between the flow and heat transfer coefficient found for the 20th piece 
simulation and the full figure.  
The best way of solving the system would thus be to use a transient simulation in which it could be observed 
how the flow behaves over time. This could be done for a short period of time or longer in order to see if a 
final steady state solution might occurs. In doing so it would be possible to see how the heat transfer 
coefficient develops as a function of time.

Conclusion 
The flow and heat transfer for the VADER 1000 has been investigated in 2D and 3D simulations using Comsol 
Multiphysics. In conclusion it was found that 2D simulations yield a maximum heat transfer coefficient of 
60.05[W/m2K], when R3=9[mm] and R4=9.5[mm], Figure 25. The Reynolds number was estimated to 613 
using the width of the jet as characteristic length, and using the average velocity based upon (38) when 
dealing with a parabolic flow.  
A 3D simulation using only one twentieth of the figure was carried out. These results also showed that the 
best heat transfer coefficient would be achieved by setting R3=9[mm] and R4=9.5[mm]. The heat transfer 
coefficient was found to 87.9[W/m2K]. This result was obtained after carrying out a mesh refinement, where 
the final velocity profile is seen in Figure 46. The Reynolds number was estimated to 1168, showing that the 
jet must be laminar.    
An analysis was carried out in which the geometry was slightly altered. The amount of inlets where changed 
from 20 to 15 and 25. One fifteenth and one twenty-fifth of the geometry was analyzed in the same manner 
as the original twentieth piece. The results showed clearly that it was hard to achieve convergence for the 
fifteenth piece and the heat transfer coefficients obtained were lower than the corresponding twentieth and 
twenty-fifth simulations. The results for the one twenty-fifth geometry achieved convergence much easier, 
yet the heat transfer coefficients found were similar to the ones found for one twentieth.  
A 3D simulation of the distribution chamber was carried out using turbulent models. It was found that the K-
Epsilon model was the most robust in terms of solving the system. Using a paired t-test it was found that the 
flow could only be assumed uniformly distributed when the difference between R3 and R4 was maximum 
1.3[mm], Table 11. This distribution analysis was also carried out using 15 and 25 outlets instead of 20. These 
results showed that using less tubes yielded a better distribution however it was found earlier that in these 
cases the heat transfer coefficient becomes lower. It was also found that using more tubes than originally 
decreased the distribution quality. Conclusively it was found that it was not an improvement to increase or 
decrease the amount of tubes. 
A final geometric alternation was investigated (using 20 outlets) in which the inlet was moved 10[mm] in the 
positive y-direction, Figure 65. In this case it was found that the flow could be assumed uniformly distributed 
in all but one case, Table 14. In conclusion using a tangential inlet is advised due to its improvement on the 
distribution.  
Finally the filament chamber was simulated using the full figure, and specifying each inlet based on the 
distribution found from the distribution section. This simulation was carried out for R3=9[mm] and 
R4=9.5[mm] as this setting throughout the project yielded the highest heat transfer coefficient. In the final 
flow figure, Figure 82, it was seen that the flow profile was not equal to that found in twentieth piece 
simulation, as the flow profile in the full figure was much more affected by the Coandâ effect. The Reynolds 
number was slightly lower, 1039, however the heat transfer coefficient was found at 146[W/m2K].  
This investigation was also carried out setting R3=9[mm] and R4=11[mm] to examine if the heat transfer 
coefficient would also increase in this case. The heat transfer coefficient did in this case also increase to 
61.56[W/m2K] however this increase is not as high as for the smaller tube simulation. In conclusion it has 
been found that the best heat transfer coefficient can be found by decreasing the diameter of the tubes, 
setting R3=9[mm] and R4=9.5[mm].
