Abstract 
Agent-Based and Activity-based Modeling have gained traction due to their ability to adapt in 
navigating complex situations, like the rise of telecommuting technology and automation or 
assessing management strategies. However, their innate complexity and their computational 
requirements have hindered the full-scale deployment of these advanced models. For example, 
activity-based models, which originated from behavioral theory, entail the calibration of 
hundreds of behavioral parameters in order to precisely reflect the socio-economic characteristics 
of a given population in transportation studies. To tackle this challenge, this study incorporates a 
cutting- edge Bayesian optimization approach that combines a custom Random Forest Regressor 
as surrogate model, Expected Improvement as acquisition function, and L-BFGS-U as utility 
maximization method.  
This approach aims to automate the calibration of these behavioral parameters, saving precious 
time and resources for the researchers. It is evaluated using SimMobility Mid-Term software 
integrated with the data originated from 2021 Danish National Travel Survey for Copenhagen 
area. The model under calibration encompasses 282 behavioral parameters and witnessed a 
notable 17.93% enhancement in the model's weighted error against the baseline during the initial 
three calibrations.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
The evolution of technology, computational power, data quantity, and quality as well as the 
Internet access and speed have been responsible for real time accurate land-use data, allowing us 
to research large-scale complex models more efficiently. Urban planners and policymakers can 
improve their decision making using significant insights that transportation modeling and 
especially Agent Based Models can offer. Transportation simulation models, that Agent Based 
Models are using, represent complex traffic networks consisting of multiple elements such as 
drivers, vehicles, road, traffic lights and other offline and online control systems that are 
interconnected and functionally related to one another. Individuals , households, or cars are used 
as autonomous agents and access their interactions with the system. Agent Based Models 
combined with activity-based approaches can detect behavioral patterns of agents through 
simulation. 
In this paper, SimMobility, a simulation platform that integrates different mobility-sensitive 
behavioral models within a multiscale simulation platform that considers land use, transport, and 
communication interactions, is used to encompass the modelling of agents and their 
environment. This platform follows the activity schedule paradigm where the agents’ choices are 
tied to their goal of performing different activities based on their time schedule. We will dive 
deeper into detail in chapter 2.  
To be able to analyze in depth these unpredictable interactions we need to use a set of parameters 
that can represent reality accurately (D. Sha, 2020). Most simulation models, especially large-
scale models, are computationally expensive in terms of both time and resources. Thus, it is 
necessary to conduct fewer simulations to find the best parameters. The calibration procedure to 
adjust these numerous parameters is a challenging task. A stochastic traffic simulation model 
often lacks an algebraic description, therefore falls within the category of “Black-Box” problems. 
Essentially a “Black-Box” problem has an unknown function, and the calibration focuses on the 
search of the optimal setting considering only the input and the output data. To avoid the 
numerous expensive, regarding time and resources, simulation runs the calibration algorithms 
must be precise, robust, and efficient. 
Bayesian optimization has emerged as a promising approach to optimize objective functions 
without derivates. The key concept of this technique focuses on drawing information based on 
prior information by optimizing a probabilistic model of the objective function to choose the best 
performing value for future evaluations. This model, known as acquisition function, is a lot 
easier to optimize. The approach deploys this function within an iterative loop, allowing 
continuous update in each step. This iterative process progressively refines the original objective 
function to be aligned with the true objective function. When applying Bayesian optimization to 
simulation calibrations, the leverage of prior knowledge will help achieve faster convergence and 
save significant evaluation time. 
This study is devoted to the testing of a high-dimensional Bayesian optimization model in 
Python, demonstrating swift convergence and versatility for the calibration of numerous

1.2 
Prior Work 
Due to their potential to provide accurate and extensive insights on human mobility patterns and 
their effects on transportation systems, activity-based models have grown in popularity in the 
field of transport and mobility research. These models depict individuals and household everyday 
activities and travel decisions based on their socioeconomic characteristics and environmental 
factors.  
By 
considering 
intra-household 
interactions 
and 
recognizing 
the

and 
interconnectedness of travel decisions, activity-based models also get around some of the 
drawbacks of the conventional four-step travel demand models. However, they require creative 
methods for calibration and application because they are extremely complicated and 
computationally demanding. To address these issues, several approaches of research have been 
developed, and the authors of (Rasouli, 2014) have distinguished three categories of activity-
based demand models: 
• Constraints-based models 
• Computational process 
• Utility maximizing models 
Constraint-based models are the first activity-based models developed. They examine the 
feasibility of a given activity program. These models focus on the impact of external conditions 
such as road capacity and physical infrastructure on the agents’ behavior. (Ha¨ gerstraand, 
1970).The constraints in these models are classified in capability constraints, coupling 
constraints, and authority constraints. However, they do not perform well when there is 
variability of special and temporal data and they do not predict travel patterns. These limitations 
are significant because they overlook aspects of individual decision-making processes and 
preferences impacting on person travel behavior. 
 
Computational process models combine both utility maximization and constraint models’ 
aspects. They consider the sequence that the individuals make choices. Applications of these 
models include SCHEDULER (Ga¨ rling, 1989),AMOS (Pendyala R. M., 1997), (Pendyala R. 
M., 1998), ,ALBATROSS (Arentze T. A., 2000) (Arentze T. A., 2004) (Arentze T. A., 2005), 
ADAPTS (Auld, 2012), and MATSIM (Balmer, 2008). Although computational process models 
represent a powerful tool for transportation studies, they decipher to capture the intuitive logic of 
the human decision-making process.

Utility maximizing models are rooted in economic theory. They use econometric models and 
assume that each agent in the system seeks to maximize his/her personal utility. These models 
offer high resolution of individual behavior and system dynamics. Earlier applications proposed 
multinomial logit model for the representation of individuals choice (Adler, 1979) (Recker, 
1986a) (Recker, 1986b) that further developed in a nested logit model for the different 
multidimensional choice  with shared unobserved elements (Ben-Akiva, 1996) (Bowman J. L., 
1995) (Bowman J. L., 1998) (Bowman J. L.-A., 2000) (Bowman J. L.-A., 1998). Other

maximization is used by the framework to produce a complicated variety of DAS. It is 
constructed using a series of discrete choice models that are functions of various individual traits 
and network parameters.  
 
1.3 Calibration  
The calibration of Activity-Based Models (ABMs) is a challenging task due to their non-linear, 
non-stationary, and stochastic nature. Traditional calibration methods such as maximum 
likelihood estimation or least-squares optimization can be computationally expensive and time-
consuming, especially for high-dimensional ABMs. As a result of the models’ complexity, the 
calibration procedures are also complicated. To guarantee that the outputs of activity-based 
models match actual observations as closely as possible, calibration includes adjusting several 
factors. Because of the high dimensionality and potentially complex relationships between 
parameters,  this work can be difficult. Since the effectiveness of these models' calibration is 
critically dependent on it, numerous researchers have investigated various methods to calibrate 
these parameters.  
The simulation calibration can be formulated as an optimization problem. The authors of (D. 
Sha, 2020) present a summarized table that shows a variety of techniques that tackle the 
optimization problem in the literature.

These methods can be classified based on  the variables of the problem they face and whether 
they focus on finding local or global optimums. From trial and error  to more sophisticated ones 
like genetic algorithms(GA), gradient based optimization techniques, and Response Surface 
Methodologies (RSM) .These methods present interesting possibilities to improve and automate 
the calibration procedure, resulting in activity-based models that are more trustworthy and 
accurate. 
The oldest approach for the calibration is the trial-and-error method. As the name suggests, this 
method includes the manual adjustment of the parameters to align with the observed data. Of 
course, this method is labor intensive and does not guarantee the identification of the optimal 
solution. 
The GA algorithm is a common random search technique that operates  by imitating the 
principles of natural selection and genetics  to test a variety of solutions over time.  It is usually 
utilized because the implementation is easy to apply (B. Shahriari, 2015) (J.Corander, 2016) 
(M.U. Michael, 2018). GA is a more methodological and effective approach than trial and error  
but can be computationally intensive  and experience convergence problems when it faces large 
scale stochastic simulation networks.

Gradient-based approaches are also known as stochastic approximation methods because they 
estimate the gradient by means of difference(FD). These methods modify the parameters 
iteratively according to the gradient of the objective function. Nonetheless they underperform 
with high dimensional problems (Y. Zhang, 2020). A modified method called Simultaneous 
perturbation stochastic approximation (SPSA) demonstrates better results with problems with 
increasing numbers of parameters. SPSA has been proved to be more effective and can reach 
convergence with only two measurements in each recursion (Leclercq, 2018) (A. Aushev, 2020). 
Moreover, the black box nature and the outputs of simulations always include noisy information. 
Thus, the computational effectiveness of these algorithms is debatable given the resource 
demanding nature of transportation simulation models. Therefore, effective strategies for 
calibrating transportation simulation models are still required. 
Response surface methodology (RSM) utilizes a metamodel to approximate the relationship 
between inputs and outputs of the simulation. In this way, the functional form can be defined  so 
that  the majority of the derivative based optimization techniques can be used.    
Bayesian optimization is a type of RMS that frequently employs methods like Kriging or 
Gaussian regression process (Siyu, 2015). In recent years, Bayesian optimization (BO) has 
emerged as a promising approach for calibrating ABMs, as it can efficiently search the parameter 
space and handle noisy and expensive-to-evaluate simulators. It also possesses the traits of direct 
search techniques.  
Direct search methods, as defined in (Sokolov, 2018), refer to the sequential examination of trial 
solutions, involving the comparison of each trial solution with the ''best'' obtained up to that point 
and a method for figuring out (based on earlier results) what the next trial solution will be 
(Sokolov, 2018). Extensions of derivative free optimization make up most direct search 
techniques created for simulation optimization problems. Such characteristics increase the appeal 
of Bayesian optimization for simulation calibration. We may still use Bayesian optimization to 
look for the global optimum even if the gradient does not exist anywhere for our simulation 
model because it does not rely on gradient information. Several studies have explored the use of 
BO for ABM calibration, with varying degrees of success. In this part of literature review, we 
examine the different approaches used for BO-based ABM calibration, including the choice of 
acquisition functions, prior information, and the use of surrogate models to improve 
computational efficiency. 
In computer simulation, active learning is a useful tool. In (Scott Koermer, 2023) Kennedy and 
O'Hagan (KOH) calibration combine a Gaussian Process  surrogate with Gaussian Process bias 
correction, involves active learning via Integrated Mean Squared Prediction Error(IMSPE) in this 
setting to progressively obtaining new simulator data. In that calibration framework the Gaussian 
processes (GPs) are used to meta-model an expensive simulator (first GP), adjust its "knobs" 
(calibration inputs) to match observations most closely, and correct for any modeling bias 
(second GP) when forecasting under novel field conditions (design inputs). This method 
encountered some challenges when trying to evaluate the gradient of KOH-IMSPE. Authors 
managed to overcome these obstacles with “careful engineering”, which may affect how 
applicable their solution is. It is further mentioned that due to increased uncertainty

quantification of the calibration parameters, Bayesian inference coupled with an effective 
evaluation of KOH-IMSPE criterion may bring improvements. Moreover, when probing the 
simulator input space, Bayesian optimization maximizes the likelihood estimation, potentially

Another approach focuses on calibrating aggregate statistics such activity shares, mode shares, 
time-dependent & mode-specific OD fluxes, and time-dependent and mode-specific sensor 
counts in utility-maximizing nested logit activity-based model systems. The authors of (Siyu 
Chena, 2020)tackled the calibration problem by describing it as an optimization problem based 
on simulation and provided a stochastic gradient-based approach. Their solution avoids 
evaluating complex analytical expressions of the entire model-system or calculating gradient, 
based solely on simulation by using approximative analytical expressions for the gradient of the 
objective function that are assessed by microsimulation. It greatly outperformed gradient-free 
optimization methods in terms of computing efficiency, steady performance, and quick 
convergence while reducing all components of the objective function. Nevertheless, the use of 
preset aggregated statistics, the lack of supply simulator calibration, and the use of (only) count-
based observations analytical expressions were some of the restrictions. 
To solve the calibration problem, the authors of (Laura Schultz, 2022) proposed the use of a 
Bayesian surrogate-based approach to automatically construct a low-fidelity surrogate. As a 
surrogate, they employed a nonparametric Gaussian Process (GP) and extended the calibration 
framework. They incorporated a deep learning pre-processing step that reduced dimensionality 
and altered the nature of the simulator function. A surrogate model combining the non-linear 
deterministic dimensionality reduction technique with the probabilistic GP model is used, and an 
inference scheme that estimates the parameters of those two components jointly. The sequential 
design experiment techniques, together with the proposed surrogate model, were then used to 
implement Bayesian optimization for an activity-based transportation simulator (POLARIS). The 
only potential drawback of this method is spotted in auto-encoders, the deep-learning technique 
used for dimensionality reduction, which in the context of transportation activity-based models 
entails more model runs. 
The latest approach is the one that finds the best application to the problem we want to solve. 
Authors of (Serio Agriesti, 2023) focused on a method for calibration via effective global 
optimization (Bayesian optimization) to promote greater use of activity-based models (BO). BO 
seeks the global optimum by a sequential sampling strategy and approximation of probability 
function utilizing a surrogate model and surrogate (response) surface. Using random forest as 
surrogate model the authors were able to model the relationship between the input parameters 
and the objective function without assuming any specific functional form, allowing for more 
flexibility in modeling the complex relationships in activity-based models. This approach can be 
more robust to high-dimensional problems, as it can capture complex interactions between the 
input parameters and the objective function.

1.4 Problem Definition 
Activity-Based Transport Models (ABMs) are a class of transportation models that simulate 
travel demand at a fine-grained level, considering individual decision-making processes of 
travelers. Unlike traditional aggregate models that predict travel demand based on average 
behavior, ABMs model travel demand at the individual level, considering personal 
characteristics, preferences, and constraints of each traveler. The ability to handle complex 
applications like tackling disruptive trends or designing and evaluating disaggregated 
management strategies has led to an increase in the usage of ABMs in transportation. 
Previously, automated mobility-related policy assessments, accessibility studies , forecasting 
experiments have all been carried out with success using activity-based and ABM models. 
Moreover, the continuous development of technology has led to the rapid increase and use of 
autonomous vehicles. Thus, deems necessary the use of disaggregate approach, where factors 
like fleet size and routing algorithm need to be evaluated based on a realistic demand, grounded 
in behavioral models rather than in past patterns.  
However, they typically involve hundreds of parameters that need to be calibrated to fit the 
population's specific socioeconomic characteristics. Consequently, ABMs are increasingly being 
applied in practice, but still very complex to estimate and update, when new travel survey data is 
available. 
 
1.5 Motivation  
Bayesian Optimization is the cutting-edge method for hyperparameter inference. This method is 
based on probabilistic models and performs inference using a Bayesian framework. The 
fundamental principle of Bayesian optimization is to describe the unknown function that 
converts hyperparameters into the desired performance metric using a probabilistic model. After 
each iteration, the model is updated using the fresh data, and the optimization process uses the 
revised model to choose which hyperparameters to sample next. 
In this project we explore recent Bayesian Optimization (BO) techniques for the calibration of 
large agent- and activity-based travel demand models (ABM). The high dimensionality of the 
parameter set and the complexity of sub-models’ interactions in ABMs make this problem hard, 
and recent BO techniques have been proposed in the literature. We test these  methods for the 
Danish ABM recently built in SimMobility and we explore alternative frameworks for its 
calibration.

2 Methodology 
 
2.1 SimMobility  
The mobility simulation platform called SimMoblity seeks to act as the hub for Future Mobility 
assessments. It combines a variety of behavioral models that are sensitive to mobility with 
cutting-edge scalable simulators to forecast how the demand for mobility will affect 
transportation networks, intelligent transportation services, and vehicle emissions. The platform 
makes it possible to simulate the outcomes of a variety of technological, governmental, and 
investment options under various future scenarios. In particular, SimMobility includes the 
modeling of millions of agents, including drivers, pedestrians, phones, traffic lights, GPS, 
automobiles, buses, and trains, in simulations that range from second-by-second to year-by-year 
(Muhammad Adnan, 2015). 
The SimMobility simulation platform integrates with an activity-based modelling framework 
first introduced by (Siyu, 2015) . It uses utility maximization to create various mobility sensitive 
behavioral models which are functions of individuals’ characteristics and network parameters. 
SimMobility is a versatile simulation platform that is built around an activity-based, multi-
modal, multi-scale, entirely modular framework. It is activity-based because it represents the 
individuals as agents allowing the simulation of people’s reaction in the uncertain future. It ties 
together multiple trips and activities into schedules that shape the demand of transport networks. 
As a multi-modal platform, it tackles mobility transportation problems such as congestion  and 
high demand on peak times or certain locations it simulates private traffic, public transit, 
pedestrian traffic, and freight transportation  and allows agents to switch between these models  
whenever it is required. Moreover, it provides flexibility for the agents in the essence that it can 
simulate future modes of transport such as autonomous vehicles. 
SimMobility is also mutli-scale factoring in the behavior of the systems through three distinct 
modules that operate on different timeframes (Adnan, 2015):

Mid Term Level Parameters 
If we dive deeper into SimMobility’s Mid-Term level, we can distinguish three interconnected 
parts. The Pre-day simulator, that provides the initial demand of the simulation , the Supply 
simulator that contains information about the network and its capacity and Within-day simulator 
whose models interact with Supply allowing users to modify their decisions based on real-time 
network conditions (Lu, 2015).   
To develop its models, SimMobility utilized various datasets from Singapore. The Pre-day 
simulator generates the daily activity schedules(DAS) using twenty-two models to simulate the 
individuals’ travel patterns. The Within-Day simulator simulates departure times and route

The framework is structured as a sequence of discrete choice models linked via inclusive values 
and which can be divided  in three levels:  
Day Pattern Level models determine whether an individual will travel, the purposes of those 
tours and what type of stop he/she is going  to make during those tours.  
Tour Level models intervene after the Day Pattern Level models . These models ascertain the

Finally, the Intermediate stop Level models come into play and produce stops on the way to 
and from the main activity.

2.2 Virtual City 
The continuously changing urban landscape creates the need for flexible tools for urban planning 
and transportation modelling. Traditional Land Use Transport Interaction(LUTI) models are 
commonly used for these problems but usually they face limitations in adaptability of the models 
across different scenarios and settings, because they depend on specific software and localized 
context.  
The authors of (Rounaq Basu, 2021) developed a strategy to convert any prototypical 
transportation infrastructure network into a realistic land use zoning plan and synthetic 
population that are appropriate for spatially detailed LUTI microsimulation of a virtual city.  
Essentially, virtual cities address the aforementioned limitations by providing a platform that 
serves as a testing ground for different parameters of LUTI models. With virtual cities, urban 
planners and researchers can formulate robust urban policies by simulating multiple hypothetical 
scenarios, providing useful insights about potential outcomes, highlighting benefits and 
drawbacks.   
After presenting the fundamental principles, framework, and parameters of Simmobility, it is 
pivotal to display another crucial component, the population synthesis. The synthetic population  
emulates the socio-economic characteristics, demographic structure, and spatial distribution  
providing a  comprehensive representation of real-world city’s population that is also important 
for the accuracy of  Traditional Land Use Transport Interaction(LUTI) model,  while  playing a 
critical role in validity and variability of model predictions. 
Simmobility  generates disaggregate units like parcels and postal codes  derived by the 
transportation network, by utilizing geospatial techniques, to serve as block elements of the 
virtual city. These blocks are the origin(home) and destination(work, school, etc.) locations for 
the population’s activities. The residences and jobs are sampled from an actual city and 
distributed within the virtual city after the spatial framework has been established. The 
distribution of homes and employment in the virtual city is made to resemble the distribution 
patterns seen in actual cities by matching density gradients that direct the allocation.  For 
example, the city’s center is more populated than the outskirts. It is worth noting that a realistic 
vacancy rate is essential for housing market models to function properly. In addition, to allocate 
the educational institutions,  the same sampling technique was used but only the density of 
households including children was considered. The completed version of the virtual city of 
Singapore can be seen in the image below. The virtual city of Copenhagen was designed the in

over a surrogate surface.

2.6 
Acquisition function 
At this point, the unknown function  f(x) has assumed to be represented using a Random Forest 
Regressor surrogate model. To answer the second question, we need to choose the acquisition 
function or in simple terms the exploration strategy which provides the point we should evaluate 
next.  
The acquisition function is a heuristic that guides the optimization process. Essentially, it 
balances both the exploitation meaning the process of choosing of parameters where the model 
predicts a good outcome, and the exploration meaning the process of choosing parameters that 
will provide the most information about the target function. 
Commonly used acquisition functions include Expected Improvement (EI), Probability of 
Improvement (PI), and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) which  have similar end-goal, but 
they can exhibit different short-term behavior, assumptions of the model and performance 
bounds. 
Probability of Improvement is the first introduced acquisition function.  It follows an  intuitive 
strategy aiming to maximize the probability of improving the current best value we have at this 
point (D. Sha, Applying Bayesian Optimization for Calibration of Transportation Simulation 
Models, 2020)The utility function is defined by evaluating a new point every time. This results 
in strange behavior of the algorithm which gets stuck at local optimal narrowing the area once it 
finds a point better than the others and only focuses on the improvement of this point without 
paying attention to the magnitude of the improvement. 
Upper Confidence Bound adds  confidence intervals to the predictions in order to balance the 
exploration and exploitation tradeoff. It derives from the notion of constructing acquisition 
functions that minimize regret over the course of optimization by utilizing lower confidence 
bounds (Candelieri, 2019). Despite its effectiveness this method is very sensitive to the scaling 
factor of the uncertainty term. This factor controls the emphasis given on exploration or 
exploitation direction, and it can lead  to potentially missing opportunities if it is not set 
appropriately.

improvement by computing the amount by which a sample would improve over the current best 
observation. It provides several benefits regarding exploration-exploitation balance, by resulting 
in high values where the prediction μ(θ) is high(exploitation) and also where the uncertainty σ(θ) 
is high(exploration), efficiency, by requiring only one parameter(location of maximum) to be 
optimized,  and noisy optimization by considering the variance that represents the noise/errors in 
the observations.

In the context of our study, the L- BFGS-B  algorithm was used. It belongs to the quasi Newton

The strength of Preday can be spotted in the series of nested logit functions; its behavioral 
models. Even in situations where ground data are not available  behavioral models can simulate

Nested logit models make predictions about people's decision-making behavior by taking into 
account a range of options and assuming that people will select their most desired option. These 
options are organized into "nests," enabling more flexible substitution patterns between options. 
This means that alternatives within the same nest are more likely to be able to replace each other 
than those in other nests. Moreover, Logsums is a key idea in these models. Logsums are used to 
determine the expected maximum utility, or the amount of preference or satisfaction that is the 
highest among a group of options. The natural logarithm of the exponentiated utilities for each 
choice is known as the logsum. In the logit model, this variable aids in calculating the 
probabilities of the various choices.

4.1 Objective Function 
In simple terms, calibrating a simulation model is like fine-tuning it. The goal is to reduce the 
inadequacy between the simulation outputs and the observed values of the real world. This 
difference is found using a custom objective function. In our study, we used the weighted error of 
two key elements given the importance weights of w1and w2.

4.2 Initialization Stage 
To commence with the process of calibration the two distribution tables of the real-world 
observations were imported as primary data inputs. Alongside these tables, a matrix that contains 
the behavioral parameters with their initial values as showcased in table 12 was also incorporated 
in the process. The lower bounds and the upper bounds of each parameter were established by 
adding a tunnel width of 2 for the upper bound and extracting 2 for the lower bound. For the 
logsum parameters, the tunnel width adjustment was slightly different, with an addition or 
subtraction of 0.05 rather than 2. 
The subsequent step entailed the creation of the initial parameter space which constituted of the 
different sets of values of the behavioral parameters’ weights. To proficiently achieve this, we 
utilized the Latin Hypercube Sampling method derived  from pyDOE package. LHS is a 
statistical method for generating a sample of plausible collections of parameter values from a 
multidimensional distribution This method is typically used as part of a Monte Carlo method for 
exploring the behavior of a complex system over a large parameter space. It generates a Latin 
Hypercube Sample for each input dimension independently and then it combines these individual 
one-dimensional samples to create a sample for the multi-dimensional distribution. This method 
ensures that the entire parameter space is searched efficiently, as the generated samples are well 
distributed, and they span the whole parameter space.  
The function returned a 10 (sample_size) x 282 (sample_dim) NumPy array with the generated 
samples. The output values were uniformly distributed in the interval [0,1], and for each 
dimension, all the intervals between the samples were non-overlapping, which is the defining 
characteristic of LHS.  
The provided dataset was scaled up based on the given starting parameter values. In this way the 
normally distributed data from the LHS matches the ranges defined by each parameter's lower 
and upper limits. This operation involves multiplying the dataset column with the range of the 
parameter (difference between the upper and lower limits) and adding the lower limit.

4.3 Iteration Process 
Utilizing the smallest inadequacy value as benchmark we can identify the optimal response 
surface in our dataset, signifying the most favorable parameter combination. To advance further, 
the Random Forest Regressor was trained on the parameter space. Here, inadequacy represents 
the target value while the all the coefficient values represent the independent variables of our 
unknown function.

The hyperparameter values were set as follows: 
• Minimum number of samples to be at leaf node – 1 . A smaller leaf can capture the 
noise in our data. With value equal to 1 each leaf can accommodate a minimum of one 
sample. 
• Number of trees in the forest – 500. This value strikes a balance between computational 
efficiency and performance. 
• Number of features considered when looking for the best split – 16. This is calculated 
as the squared root of the number of features(rounded to the smaller integer) as proposed 
in the literature(see Methodology section). It offers a good tradeoff between performance 
and overfitting. 
• The out-of-bag score – True. Provides the idea of models’ performance without needing 
a separate validation set. 
The model was trained in the first iteration and every 3 iterations based on the parameter space. 
Retraining the model periodically ensured it remained up to date with the latest samples from the 
objective function. As the optimization progresses, the nature of the objective function in the 
parameter space could reveal different dynamics (local minima). Periodic updates ensured that 
the surrogate model accurately captures these dynamics. 
Before diving into the optimization, an initial grid of parameter sets was synthesized again using 
the LHS algorithm of pyDOE package, echoing the method implemented in the initialization 
stage. This time, a 30 (sample_size) x 282 (sample_dim) NumPy array is formulated with the 
generated samples which represent the parameter search space.  
The notable surge in the sample size came into play for practical reasons. It was geared towards 
ensuring that the sample size aligned with the available optimization cores that DTU’ s hyper-
computer. This was pivotal for facilitating a technique known as parallel processing. This 
technique speeded up the optimization, by leveraging all the available CPU cores minus two 
(cpu_count() – 2 = 30).  For this purpose, we used the cpu_count and Pool tools from 
multiprocessing package. 
At this juncture, multiple quasi newton optimizations were initialized in parallel  for each 
starting point in the parameter search space. This time the inadequacy values were not calculated 
by running the simulation and  comparing each time with the observed values, but they were 
predicted utilizing the trained the custom Random Forest Regressor model (5).  
Our model leverages an ensemble of 500 decision trees to produce predictions. We prioritize two 
specific outputs from this ensemble: 
• The mean μ (7) serves as the aggregate output of all the individual trees and  
• the standard deviation σ (8) which captures the spread of these individual predictions, 
measuring the uncertainty of our predictions.  
In this way our approach becomes more robust  and less susceptible to overfitting.

To forge ahead the challenge of measuring the utility of each candidate point, we calculated the 
difference between optimal response surface parameters and the mean prediction values. This 
difference was then normalized with σ to calculate the το z (10) value. Furthermore, integrating 
the z value in the Expected Improvement function resulted in the utility value of each candidate. 
Moving forward, the final algorithm that emerged as a tool of choice is L-BFGS-B. Its primary 
role was  to identify the minimum of the utilities provided by the Expected Improvement 
algorithm for each starting point of the parameter search space. To achieve this, we resorted to 
the optimize module from scipy library. The 'minimize' function therein was particularly 
beneficial for our needs, as it orchestrates the minimization of our scalar function. As previously 
highlighted the L-BFGS-B method was employed, supplemented by the following parameters: 
• x0 – Each starting point of the parameter search space. It is the initial guess for the 
variables.  
• Fun – Expected Improvement . This value represents the function to minimize.  
• Args – Additional arguments passed to the function (surrogate function name, surrogate 
utility function name, optimal response value etc.) 
• Method – L-BFGS-B 
• Bounds – Bounds on the variables that we created in the initialization stage. These are 
required for L-BFGS-B, as it is a bounded optimization method(see Methodology). 
• Maximum iterations – 1000 If the algorithm does not converge (i.e., reach a solution 
where the gradient is sufficiently close to zero) within the specified number of iterations, 
it will stop and return the best solution found up to that point. Based on the literature 
1000 iterations gives the algorithm the opportunity to find a good solution but without 
running indefinitely on potentially unsolvable combinations. 
• Step size – 0.5 (default) The gradient points in the direction of steepest ascent, but the 
step size determines how big of a step to take in that direction. In that light a step of 1 
would result in one step along to the gradient direction which might skip the optimal 
solution, while 0.1 would take long time to reach it. The 0.5 value is a middle-ground 
choice, allowing the algorithm to make significant progress towards the solution without 
risking excessive overshooting, while together with the 1000 max iterations value have 
been tested in the literature (Serio Agriesti, 2023). 
Given the parameters above the algorithm computed the gradient of the EI function to be 
minimized each point, it used the gradient and the limited memory approximation of the inverse 
hessian to determine the search direction, and along with the direction it  used line search 
techniques to find the step size (0.5 default initial) that minimized the function. Each time the 
current estimate of parameters was updated until the gradient is sufficiently small, or a maximum 
number of iterations (1000) had been reached. 
And each run provided  an optimal set  output which includes: 
• X - the optimal parameters found 
• Fun - the function(Expected Improvement) value (utility)  at the optimal parameters 
• Success - a boolean variable which indicates if  the optimization was successful

6 Limitations  
The purpose of this chapter was to underline  limitations  of the proposed methodology in the 
context of activity-based modeling to make better decisions and interpretation of the results.  
Firstly, as much  as LHS  ensures the uniformity of our initial parameter space, it may sometimes  
fail to capture the complexities  of the real-world parameter distribution. This characteristic can 
significantly influence the outcome of the process.  
Secondly,  while Random Forest Regressor can capture the non-linear relationships of human 
behavior effectively, it might fit too closely to the data when the sample size is small. In 
particular, when they deal with sparse datasets they might lead to results where the training 
dataset has high accuracy, while the generalization sets perform poorly. 
Thirdly, our approach, even though efficient, relies heavily on computational power. The DTU’s 
hyper computer utilized by parallel processing provides a solution to some extent, but as the 
complexity of the model keeps rising the need for computational resources grows exponentially.  
Finally, the dynamic nature of our tools caused some unpredicted challenges. The SimMobility  
platform, which is a cornerstone of our work, is constantly updated to retain and enhance its 
cutting-edge capabilities. One of these updates occurred close to the deadline of our project 
narrowing down the time window for multiple iterations possibly affecting the depth of the 
calibrations results. 
Acknowledging those limitations, we do not identify them as insurmountable obstacles but  as 
directions to guide future research to overcome these challenges.

7 Future Research  
Font of Bayesian optimization technique for calibration of activity-based models’ parameters 
unrevealed many complexities, nuances, and implications. Our customized approach surely 
enhanced the speed and precision of the model calibration, while the choice of the Python as 
programming language of our script made it easier to read and use by most developers, but just 
like all the evolving areas of research, it presents many opportunities  for future exploration. The 
purpose of this chapter is to highlight potential research direction that could extend and maybe 
even benefit the current knowledge and application of the presented methodology.  
To begin with, the LHS method derived from pyDOE was crucial in creating the initial parameter 
space values. Future research could test different techniques that could capture a more 
representative set of parameter combinations resulting in faster calibration. Alternative 
techniques that could become handy are adaptive sampling techniques such as Sequential Monte 
Carlo sampling that adjust the predicted values according to regions of interest in the parameter 
space. Available Sequental  Monte Carlo packages for python are ‘pymc3’ or ‘particles’. 
To continue, while the Random Forest regressor offers robustness and tackles overfitting 
concerns for our case, future endeavors in other areas could explore more advanced tree- based 
algorithms utilizing Neural Network approaches to better capture nonlinear patterns of their 
focus groups. Commonly used Neural Network packages are ‘tensorflow’, ‘keras’, and 
‘torch’(pytorch). 
Moreover, while the Expected Improvement algorithm is employed to quantify in the most 
efficient way the utility of each candidate point, other researchers could try alternative heuristics 
measures that may perform better in problems of other domains.  For example, compared to 
other bandit algorithms, Thompson Sampling usually converges faster. Bayesian Optimization 
approaches with Thompson sampling could utilize the package ‘GPyOpt’. 
Additionally, the parallel processing part could be refined using more efficient techniques or 
tools to further optimize computational speed without compromising accuracy. Alternative 
techniques that allow scalable data processing across clusters are the distributed computing 
platformns like Apache Spark or Hadoop and ‘pyspark’ and ‘Pydoop’  are their respective 
packages available in Python. 
Finally, as the scalability of the urban datasets grows in size and complexity future research 
could assess the methodology against Big Data challenges and evaluate its performance in real-
time calibration scenarios. Leveraging Cloud-based infrastructure solutions using packages such 
as ‘google-cloud-python’ (Google Cloud for Python developers)might provide dynamic 
allocation of resources  based on the volume of data. 
The application of Bayesian optimization to calibrate activity-based models represents a 
promising new direction with enormous potential. While all of these alternative approaches bring 
various advantages, they also introduce unique challenges. Future researchers might consider 
blending the proposed solution with different alternatives depending on their specific goals and 
applications. Also, it should be noted that the suggested libraries aim to provide a starting point  
for the research considering that the more recent packages or updates might emerge over time.

8 Conclusions 
In this study we employed a state-of-the-art methodology to automate the calibration process of 
numerous hyperparameters by exploiting a Random Forest Regressor as a surrogate model 
coupled with Bayesian Optimization techniques. The method was applied to a case study of 
Copenhagen to validate and prove the effectiveness of the proposed method in calibration of 
hundreds of behavioral parameters for the SimMobility Mid-Term Preday.  
To put the importance of this procedure in perspective, it is paramount to contrast it with 
conventional methods. The traditional calibration of the approximate 1800 Preday parameters is 
carried out manually, by tuning the various parameters to refine results. All the results presented 
in previous chapters  emerged from a completely automated procedure that tests, corrects, 
updates, and runs the simulations without any human interference. To illustrate our point, 
imagine that  each Preday run requires around 20 minutes for DTU’s hypercomputer. During 
these 3 calibrations runs summing up in a total of 27 iterations of the algorithm we saved more 
than 270 hours while improving the performance of the model by almost 28% (percentage of 
reduction in the weighted error). 
This result shows a satisfactory match between the modeled outputs and the baseline that built 
from the Danish National Travel Survey for the Copenhagen Area (Christiansen, 2022) aggregate 
data. It is worth mentioning that the algorithm presented is able to tune a wider set of behavioral 
parameters than manual or heuristics methods. Additionally, the algorithm searches the best 
solution by perturbing 282 parameters, a very challenging task that is nearly impossible to 
achieve by hand given the precision we attained.   
Our study also drew inspiration from related academic pursuits. More specifically, the study that 
preceded ours concluded with a suggestion for calibrations against more disaggregated data sets, 
rather than just aggregate benchmarks (Serio Agriesti, 2023). Following this counsel, our 
approach leverages a more nuanced data landscape, factoring in modal shares for varied tour 
types. In this light this approach significantly reduces the local discrepancies in the modal shares, 
enhancing  the overall robustness of our findings. 
Over the course of this project several methodologies and techniques have come to the forefront. 
We explored the activity-based models’ intricacies through hands-on work with the SimMobility 
Preday model. This hands-on approach was extended with the use of advanced Bayesian 
optimization techniques such as a Random Forest Regressor ensemble method. The  different 
techniques regarding the acquisition function and search optimization were studied and led to a 
more in-depth experience with Expected Improvement and L-BFGS-U. We hope that together 
these elements  will provide a useful toolkit of modern computational methodologies.
