Abstract
World wide many people suﬀer from the mental disease schizophrenia, in Denmark alone 25,000
people are diagnosed. Symptoms of the disease include hearing voices that are not there and
feeling one is being followed. Existing medical treatment can reduce some of the symptoms, but
in many cases, there is severe side eﬀects from the treatment. Despite pronounced side eﬀects
the pharmacological treatment of schizophrenia has not changed signiﬁcantly during the past 60
years and a new treatment is therefore highly desirable.
A newly established cellular model of schizophrenia forms the basis of two image data sets, which
are generated and supplied by H. Lundbeck A/S for this thesis. Both data sets are based on
calcium imaging of neuronal activity in vitro, but diﬀer in spatial and temporal resolution.
The two supplied data sets are used to gain knowledge about schizophrenia and classifying neurons
as wild type or transgenic based on their calcium oscillation patterns. New knowledge about
schizophrenia will, hopefully, in the long term lead to an improved treatment of schizophrenia.
A network approach is applied to detect structural connectivity diﬀerences between the two
neuron types and the classiﬁcation is based on a statistical approach with classiﬁers such as lasso
regularized logistic regression.
An improved discrimination between wild type and transgenic network oscillations has been
achieved in this thesis using image data. Several important features, extracted from the image
data, that diﬀer between wild type and transgenic neurons have been identiﬁed. Further, structural
connectivity diﬀerences between the two neuron types have been discovered by using a network
approach. Based on this work, it can be concluded that Lundbeck can gain more knowledge
about schizophrenia by using images of calcium signaling in transgenic neurons instead of the
current one dimensional time traces.

Resumé
På verdensplan lider mange mennesker under den psykiske sygdom skizofreni; alene i Danmark
er 25.000 mennesker diagnostiseret. Sygdommens symptomer kan f.eks. give sig til udtryk i at
man hører stemmer eller føler sig forfulgt. Den eksisterende medicinske behandling kan reducere
nogle af symptomerne, men i mange tilfælde er der alvorlige bivirkninger. På trods af markante
bivirkninger har den medicinske behandling af skizofreni ikke udviklet sig nævneværdigt over de
sidste 60 år og derfor er en ny behandling meget ønskværdig.
En ny cellulær model for skizofreni ligger til grund for to billeddatasæt, hvilke er genereret
og leveret til denne afhandling af H. Lundbeck A/S. Begge datasæt består af in vitro kalcium
billeddannelse af neuron aktivitet, men adskiller sig fra hinanden ved at have forskellig spatiel og
temporal opløsning
De to medfølgende datasæt er brugt til at opnå viden om skizofreni, samt klassiﬁcerer neuroner
som vildtype eller transgene baseret på deres kalcium oscillationer. Forhåbentligt vil ny viden i
det lange løb lede til en forbedret behandling af skizofreni. Der er anvendt en netværksbaseret
tilgang til at detektere forskelle i de strukturelle forbindelser mellem de to forskellige neuron
typer. Envidere er klassiﬁkationen baseret på en statistisk tilgang med klassiﬁkations metoder
som lasso regulariseret logistisk regression.
Ud fra denne afhandling er der opnået en forbedret diskrimination mellem vildtype og transgene
kalcium netværks oscillationer ved at anvende billeddata. Adskillige vigtige features, ekstraheret
fra billeddata, som er forskellige mellem vildtype og transgene er blevet identiﬁceret. Ydermere
er der opdaget forskelle i de strukturelle forbindelser mellem de to neuron typer ved at anvende
en netværkstilgang. Baseret på dette arbejde kan det konkluderes at Lundbeck kan opnå større
viden omkring skizofreni ved at anvende billeder af kalcium signalering i transgene neuroner, i
stedet for de nuværende endimensionelle tidsserier.

Introduction
In Denmark 25,000 people suﬀer from the mental disease schizophrenia and many more is aﬀected
by the disease by having a friend, a relative, a partner or a parent diagnosed with the disease.
A cure for schizophrenia has not yet been found but there exists medical treatments that can
reduce some of the symptoms. However, medical treatment, in many cases, results in severe
side eﬀects including, but not limited to, restlessness, weight gain and depression [1]. Despite
the pronounced side eﬀects the pharmacological treatment of schizophrenia has not changed
signiﬁcantly during the past 60 years [2]. A new treatment is therefore highly desirable.
The thesis has been written in collaboration with H. Lundbeck A/S. Lundbeck is a pharmaceutical
company primarily working with brain diseases, including both psychiatric and neurological
disorders. One of these diseases is schizophrenia. Lundbeck is interested in clarifying the utility
of genetic models of schizophrenia for discovering new drug candidates as well as advancing the
current understanding of schizophrenia.
The overall goal of this thesis is to improve the utility of a newly established cellular model of
schizophrenia at Lundbeck. The cellular model is based on genetically modiﬁed neurons and
monitoring of their activity over time. Such neuronal activity data forms the data foundation
of the thesis, and will be described in detail later. The thesis work is focused on establishing
methods to discriminate between normal neuronal activity and activity derived from genetically
modiﬁed neurons based on the two diﬀerent data types.
It is the intention that a reader with an engineering background in mathematical modelling and
computer science should fully comprehend the thesis. Furthermore because the work has been
done in collaboration with Lundbeck, the mathematical modelling theory has been elaborated to
make it accessible for a reader with a biological background.
1.1
Etiology and modelling possibilities for schizophrenia
In the following schizophrenia will be brieﬂy described leading on to a description of a genetic
model for the disease. This is followed by an explanation of how the genetic model has further
been simpliﬁed to a cellular model. As a completion it will be argued why the neurons was chosen
as the cellular model.
1.1.1
Schizophrenia
Schizophrenia is a mental disorder and symptoms of the disease can be hallucinations, e.g. hearing
voices that are not there, and delusions, e.g. feeling that one is being followed [3]. Symptoms

1.1.2
A genetic model for schizophrenia
A study with 3,285 patients and further 7,951 controls from six diﬀerent countries established
an association of DNA copy number variations with a number of mental disorders including
schizophrenia [6]. When a region of the DNA has been deleted or duplicated on the chromosome
the region is termed a copy number variation. A gene deletion is illustrated in Figure 1.1. The
copy number variations that were investigate in Stefansson et al. ([6]) were gene deletions that
despite having a relatively high mutation rate was not highly represented in the population [6].
This indicated that the copy number variations are likely to be under negative selection pressure
[6], which corresponds well to reduced fecundity for people suﬀering from mental disorders [7]
[6]. The authors of the study concluded that there is a high risk of schizophrenia for all three
deletions studied. The odds ratio is equal to 14, 2 and 11 for individuals having the 1q21.1,
15q13.3 or 15q13.3 gene deletion [8] [6].
Figure 1.1: An illustration of a copy number variation. The 15q13.3 gene deletion is a copy number
variation. Figure from [9].
Using this knowledge about the relationship between schizophrenia and the DNA structure an
animal model can be generated. When wanting to gain more knowledge about a disease it is
common to use an animal model, which is when an animal is used as a model for a human disease.
When having an animal model diﬀerent kinds of test can be conducted which is not possible to
do on humans. In this case mice is used as the animals.
Mice have a similar genomic region around the 15q13.3 gene as humans [8]. Mice which have been
genetically engineered with a 15q13.3 deletion exhibit, when tested, several features that resemble
the clinical syndrome [8]. These include a higher body weight, a more aggressive behaviour
when exposed to a stressful environment and a change in neuronal excitability (shown by using
EEG) [8]. The presence of these features qualiﬁes a mouse with this particular deletion to be a
schizophrenia model with face validity and due to the gene deletion also a model with construct

1.1.3
A cellular model of schizophrenia
From the mouse model a cellular model is generated. A cellular model is generated by ﬁrst pairing
a transgenic female mouse with a wild type male. The oﬀspring can be both transgenic and wild
type mice at an equal ratio. To determine the genotype, a small part of the oﬀspring’s tail is cut
oﬀshortly after their birth. The pups are then euthanised and their brains are collected, because
the neurons from their brains constitute the cellular model that is the subject of study. The
neurons are placed as separate cells in small containers, called wells, and arranged on a plate.
A plate holds 32 wells and each well contains neurons from either transgenic mice or wild type
mice.
Studying neurons in a well is called an in vitro study. A study is said to be in vitro when cells
are studied outside of their natural environment, as is the case for this data [12]. An in vivo
study is on the other hand when neurons are studied directly in the brain [13]. The advantage of
studying the neurons in vitro, is that the system is more simple than in vivo with less biological
variation, and it is possible to easier aﬀect the neurons with for example diﬀerent chemicals and
measure the response [14].
A neuron is a specialised cell. It carries signals from the sense organs to the central nervous
system, the brain and the spinal cord, and the other way; from the central nervous system to
glandular cells and muscle cells [15]. The cell membrane is very unique in the way it makes
the neuron able to communicate with other cells. Signals are send out from the cell with the
axons and received by the dendrites[16], see Figure 1.2a. The axons can be compared to long
arms with a lot of ﬁngers at the end delivering the signal, and the dendrites acts like small
antennas receiving the signal. No matter which type of cell the neuron is communicating with,
the communication is the same: a change in the electrical potential across the neuron’s cell
membrane called the action potential [16]. What is special about the neurons action potential
is that it is very fast [15]. Figure 1.2 gives an overview of what neurons are and how they get
brighter when communicating, i.e., are active.
It has been demonstrated that the intracellular concentration of calcium ions in the neuron is
tightly coupled to the generation of the action potential [17]. This makes it possible to observe
the activity of the neurons by monitoring the calcium ion level. The calcium ion level can in
turn be measured indirectly with a ﬂourescent probe. When calcium ions ﬂow into the neuron
the probe binds the ions, which increase the ﬂuorescence intensity of the probe more than a
100 times, see Figure 1.2b. The increased ﬂuorescence intensity causes active neurons to look
brighter when illuminated with UV-light.
The neurons studied in this thesis are visualised using a microscope called UltraVIEW VoX [18].
A laser sends out light at a given wave length, which excite the ﬂuorescence probe. The probe
subsequently emits light with a longer wave length. This emission light is recorded using the
microscope and a bandpass ﬁlter to separate the excitation and emission light, see Figure 1.2c.

1.2 Short data presentation
1.1.4
EEG in a dish
In the experiments, that form the basis of this thesis, neurons from both wild type and transgenic
mice are studied in wells. Neurons at a high density are placed in wells, and due to the high
density the neurons spontaneously start to connect into a neuronal network that will display
electrical activity [14]. The activity can be monitored by making use of the calcium sensitive
ﬂuorescent probe, as described beforehand. The activity of the neuronal network is characterized
by sudden bursts of activity recruiting the majority of the neuronal network, leading to the whole
well lighting up. It appears that the activity among neurons are highly synchronised throughout
the well. In between these bursts there are cases of individual neurons ﬁring/lighting up. In
general, the activity for each individual neuron depends on its connectivity to other neurons, as
well as its own ability to ﬁre [14].
It has been observed in clinical experiments that patients with schizophrenia display abnormal
brain activity compared to healthy controls. The change in brain activity has been inferred from
from electroencephalogram (EEG) recordings, which captures the integrated activity of neuronal
networks close to the surface of the skull [20]. Studying neurons in vitro is by no means the same
as studying a brain, but is does allow for monitoring of the basic cellular activity underlying
brain activity. In this simple sense in vitro neuronal activity resembles an EEG measure, i.e.,
EEG in a dish. Thereby knowledge about schizophrenia can be gained by studying neurons from
both wild type and transgenic mice in wells.
The way the neurons are studied will be presented in the next section.
1.2
Short data presentation
The thesis builds on data acquired from two diﬀerent types of experiments. In both experiments
the activity of neurons from transgenic and wild type mice have been monitored by calcium
imaging, but the neurons have been observed with two diﬀerent techniques. Both of the techniques
are based on calcium imaging of neuronal activity in wells, but the resolution of the images, the
amount of the wells that are pictured and the sampling rates diﬀer substantially. The two data
sets are named plate data and micro data, see Figure 1.3.
Plate data depict a plate over time. An image of the whole plate with 32 wells is saved for each
time step. On each plate there are 16 wells containing neurons from transgenic mice and 16 wells
containing neurons from wild type mice. The sampling rate is 1 Hz.
Micro data are generated by recording part of a single well over time in a microscope, making it
possible to follow the activity of individual neurons. 16 wells have been recorded where half of
these are with neurons from transgenic mice and the other half are neurons from wild type mice.
The sampling rate is 10 times higher than plate data namely 10 Hz.
Lundbeck has already established an analysis of these types of experiments, but the analysis
does not employ the spatial information. The analysis relies solely on integrated time series for
the overall neuron activity in a well. One such time series is exempliﬁed in Figure 1.3.

There has been conducted a huge number of experiments with ﬂuorescent measurement of
neuronal calcium oscillations [17] [14] [21]. In 1991 Rafael Yuste and Lawrence C. Katz [22]
suggested, for the ﬁrst time, that visualizing calcium changes in brain slices could provide a more
accurate picture of the neuronal activity and networks in developing brains. However, presently
there is a lack of published material where in vitro experiments, with ﬂuorescent measurement of
neuronal calcium oscillations, have been analysed as images. This thesis takes a new approach to
the analysis by using the generated images instead of time traces.
Lundbecks current technique is at the moment unpublished, but a short overview will be given
in the following. The experiments are analysed by looking at the total intensity for each well
at each time step and thereby generating as many time traces as there are wells, recall Figure
1.3. Hence all spatial information of the signals within a well is lost. From a time trace peaks
are detected and features relating to both peaks and the trace as such are extracted. These
include mean and standard deviations of peaks and amplitude distributions, percentiles for the
variance, autocorrelation functions and more. From very large datasets it has become evident
that there is a diﬀerence between transgenic and wild type neurons in a number of these features,
speciﬁcally for features relating to the timing of peaks such as the coeﬃcient of variation and
autocorrelation function. However, no single feature can on its own predict the genotype. When
multiple features are used in classiﬁcation methods like logistic regression, random forest and
support vector machine it becomes possible to discriminate between transgenic and wild type.
The accuracy for the classiﬁcation, using this method, vary, depending on whether the tested
wells are from the same week or two independent weeks. The accuracy for classifying wells from
the same week is 80%, whereas the accuracy is a bit lower for two independent weeks, namely
around 70%.
An improvement of classiﬁcation accuracy is highly desirable as it would improve the utility and
value of the in vitro model.
1.4
Problem statement
The main goal of the thesis is:
• To determine if Lundbeck could gain more knowledge about schizophrenia by using images
of calcium signaling in transgenic neurons instead of the current one dimensional time
traces.
The thesis is concerned with the problem of classifying neurons as a wild type or transgenic
based on their oscillation patterns. The main goal can further be divided into sub goals. The
three sub goals are:
• To improve the discrimination between wild type and transgenic network oscillation using
image data.
• Identify features extracted from the plate images that diﬀer between the two types.
• Determine structural connectivity diﬀerences between the two neuronal types by using a
network approach.

Before going into details about the data it is important to understand the data structure. The
data set-up is shown in Figure 2.1. On each plate all of the mice, that the neurons originate
from, have the same mother. The wild type/transgenic ratio is 50% meaning that a mother

observed diﬀerence between two wells was due to a diﬀerence between wild type and transgenic
or a diﬀerence between two individuals. On the other hand, it should be emphasised that since
there is only neurons from one mother on each plate, it is not possible to determine if a diﬀerence
between two plates is due to the treatment of the plate or due to a diﬀerence between mothers.

2.3.2
Detect the centers for each well
To detect the centroids on the plate it is utilized that all the wells are aligned on the plate. That
means that wells on the same row have the same y-coordinate for the centroid and wells on the
same column have the same x-coordinate for the centroid. When summarising the image row

When a well has more activity in one area than another, that aﬀects the summation, especially
if the area of activity is close or on the edge of the well. Instead of using the original plate image,
the image is converted into a binary image. A cut-oﬀvalue of 0.085 is used. Values less than
0.085 are set to zero and values above are set to one, utilizing that the actual plate diﬀer in
intensity from the wells. In this binary image, pixels inside wells take the value one, and the rest
of the plate is set to zero. All pixels in the wells now have the same weight when making the
summations, see Figure 2.7.
Figure 2.7: The well image turned into a binary image.

value. To ensure that each maximum is well-deﬁned, a Gaussian ﬁlter with a standard deviation
of two pixels is applied. Figure 2.9b shows three maxima, with and without using a Gaussian
ﬁlter. It can be seen that the maxima using the Gaussian ﬁlter are well-deﬁned. Finally the eight
and four coordinates for the maxima is joined into the 32 centroids’ coordinates, see Figure 2.8,

2.5.1
Temporal average for robust cell detection
For a neuron to be detected as a neuron it should be active, meaning its intensity should oscillate
over time. At a given time point an active neuron looks like a bright circle on the dark background
in the grey scale image. If the neuron is not active it just looks like a part of the dark background
which makes it very hard to see. To make the cell detection more robust, it is decided to detect the
cells in an image S which is a sum over all the images, equal to a recording time of approximately
12 minutes. If only one image was used, there is a risk that some of the cells are not active at
that speciﬁc time, and will therefore not be detected. When using a sum over all the time steps,
all of the cells should have been active in the period and it will be possible to detect them. The
well is stationary during the experiment and therefore it is not necessary to adjust for movements
like it was done in the plate data.

2.5.2
Identify potential cells
To identify potential cells from the image S the well known image analysis technique blob
detection will be applied [25].
A blob is a small, compact image primitive [26], very often a bright region on a dark background
or vice versa [27]. This is exactly how the neurons look in the grey scale image. Blob detection
emphasizes the curvature in the image, making it more like a landscape with mountains and
valleys with the cells as the tops of the mountains.
Blob detection consists of two steps: applying the Laplacian of Gaussian ﬁlter and ﬁnding local
maxima. The method for applying the Laplacian of Gaussian ﬁlter is described in detail in a
scale-space formulation in [25]. Since it is already well described, the following section will deal
with the method in general terms. The method for ﬁnding local maxima will be described in
more detail.

2.5 Pre-processing of micro data
Local maximum
To ﬁnd the coordinates for the centers of the cells, the local maxima from the LoG ﬁltering of
the image S shall be found. When ﬁnding the local maxima it is assumed that two cells can not
lie on top of each other, meaning that the minimum distance between two cell centres is the
diameter of the cell. It is given in Table 2.1 that the diameter of a neuron is 4-5 pixels, therefore
the minimum distance between to cells is 4-5 pixels. In the previous section it was chosen to set
the radius to 2.5 pixels making the minimum distance between two cells 5 pixels.
It is chosen to use a two step process for ﬁnding the local maximum, which will be described in
detail here. The ﬁrst step is to perform a grayscale dilation of the LoG image, which is creating
areas with the same value as the local maxima. The second step is to compare this dilated image
with the image S, pixel by pixel. Pixels with highest value in the image S is a local maximum.
When performing a dilation a structuring element, (SE), is used, see Figure 2.19 for an example
of a SE. The SE determines how the dilation is made. It is chosen to use a circle with a radius of
5 pixels as the SE. The radius is chosen as the minimum distance between two neurons.

3
Theory
The following chapter will present the theory behind the analysis, but ﬁrst a motivation will
be given for the choice of image and network features respectively. This will be followed by a
motivation of the choice of methodology.
Image features will be extracted for the analysis of the low resolution plate data. The features are
constructed to describe diﬀerent aspects of the image. The reason for using features instead of
the raw image is that the goal is to learn about the diﬀerences between wild type and transgenic
neurons. When using features to classify wells as containing wild type or transgenic neurons, some
features will be more important than others. The important features can add to the knowledge
about the diﬀerences between the two neuron types. Twenty-six image features will be extracted
and will each be given names. The mathematical foundation for ten of the features will be
described in Section 3.7 since extraction of these is non-trivial. In Section 4.1, the information
captured by all 26 features will be visualised.
Network measures are extracted to describe the neuronal activity in the high resolution micro
data. The network measures each capture diﬀerent information about the network and may add
to the knowledge about diﬀerences between the two neuron types. The measures are selected
with inspiration from [30] [31] and [32] and will be described in Section 3.9.
3.1
Small N, large p
In neither of the two data sets can the observations be claimed to be independent. For the high
resolution micro data there is not independence due to the observations being over time. For the
low resolution plate data the 16 wells (transgenic or wild) on each plate are replicates of the
same neuronal culture. Eﬀectively, the data have more variables p than observations N. This is
called a p > N problem, and models selected to analyse the data should be able to handle this.
One of the risks when having p > N is over-ﬁtting [31]. One of the ways to handle this, is to
employ regularisation [33].
Regularisation is a kind of continuous subset selection. In subset selection variables are either
retained in the model or discarded. When using regularisation, a penalty is imposed on the
coeﬃcient size. This means that some of the coeﬃcients will be shrunk towards zero, but not
necessarily set to exactly zero. This continuous subset selection will lead to the models being
more stable, compared to normal subset selection, meaning that a small change in the data will
not lead to very diﬀerent models [34]. Two diﬀerent types of regularisation will be used: lasso
regularisation and Schäfer regularisation.
The methods used in this thesis include logistic regression with lasso regularisation, random

the prediction error, since N is low. These methods will be described in Sections 3.3 to 3.5 and

Finally, a mixed eﬀect model will be used for the statistical analysis. The mixed eﬀect is used

3.2
Bonferroni correction
The Bonferroni correction is used when testing multiple hypotheses. It is applied because the
probability of observing one signiﬁcant result by chance increases when the number of hypotheses
increases. So even though all the tests are not signiﬁcant the sheer volume of test might result
in one or several tests being signiﬁcant. When testing on an α signiﬁcance level the Bonferroni
correction adjust it by dividing with the number of hypotheses tests. The new signiﬁcance level
for n hypotheses would therefore be α
n [35]. It should be mentioned that the Bonferroni correction
is quite conservative, and one could also have used false discovery rate [33].

3.3 Cross-validation
3.3
Cross-validation
Cross-validating (CV) is a method for estimating the prediction error. The method is especially
useful when having a limited amount of data compared to the amount of predictors.
Ideally, when a large amount of data is given, the data are split into a test and a training set.
On the training set one would train the model, and on the test set determine how well the model
performs - the prediction error. When data are limited, as is the case with data in this thesis, it
is not possible to ﬁnd the prediction error this way.
The reason being that the test and training set will be too small to represent the structure of
the data.
To overcome the problems with limited data k fold CV is used. Figure 3.1 illustrates how CV
works with 4 fold CV, k = 4. First the data are divided into four equally sized parts. The model
is then trained on the ﬁrst three parts and the fourth part is used for testing. The prediction
error is saved. Then the ﬁrst, second and fourth part is used for training and the third part for
testing. Again the prediction error is saved. This is repeated until all the parts have been used to
test the model on. The prediction error for the model is then a mean of, in this case, all four
prediction errors [33].

Diﬀerent versions of CV can be used. In this report leave-one-out CV will primarily be used. As
the name indicates one observation is left out to test on and the other observations are used to
train the model. This is a good approach when data is particularly sparse. One thing to be aware
of when using leave-one-out CV is that there is a low bias but there could be a high variation
[33].
For both the plate data and the micro data it is important to be aware of how the data is divided
into test and training in the CV. The division is done according to whole plates. When using
leave-one-out CV the test set is therefore one whole plate and the training set is the remaining
plates. Most often the division of the data set is done by randomly selecting the test and training.
In the plate data case that would mean randomly selecting well observations on the plates, but
that is not a correct way to do it on this data set. If the test and training set is taken at random
from the data set, it would mean that wells from the same plate could be in both the training
and the test set. Here it should be remembered that the 16 wells with neurons from wild type
mice on one plate is just repetitions of the same set-up. Therefore if wells from the same plate

3.4 Logistic regression
3.4
Logistic regression
Logistic regression is a probabilistic classiﬁcation and a linear function of the data, x, that models
the posterior probabilities of the classes. Logistic regression can be used to model several classes
but in the following only the two-class case will be described [33].
The logistic regression has the form:
log P(G = wild type|X = x)
P(G = transgenic|X = x) = β0 + βT x
where β is the coeﬃcients and β0 is the intercept. The two classes are marked by wild type and
transgenic. The probabilities sum to one and remain in the interval [0, 1]. The logistic regression
is ﬁtted by maximum likelihood estimation.
It is an advantage to use logistic regression when it is wanted to analyse how the input variables
explain the outcome. When the coeﬃcients are found they can be interpreted the following way:
If a coeﬃcient is equal to 0.7 then an increase in one unit means an increase in the odds of the
outcome of e0.7 = 2.014, when everything else is kept the same [33].
When a lot of variables are used one is very often interested in ﬁnding a subset of variables
that describes the strongest eﬀect. This makes the interpretation easier and will describe the
overall tendency in the data [33]. Another advantage by using a subset of the variables is that
the prediction accuracy can sometimes be improved. The improvement will be due to a reduced
variance, but at the cost of introducing bias [33].
To improve the prediction accuracy and the interpretability of the logistic regression, lasso
regularisation is therefore imposed. Lasso regularisation is an acronym of Least Absolute Shrinkage
and Selection Operator 1. It shrinks some of the coeﬃcients, β, towards zero and sets other to
exactly zero by imposing a one-norm penalty on the size of the coeﬃcient [34]. The lasso estimate
is deﬁned by Eq. (3.1):

3.5 Random forest
3.5
Random forest
Random forest is a method for non-linear regression and classiﬁcation 2. In the following only
the classiﬁcation part will be described. Random forest is a modiﬁcation of bagging combined
with classiﬁcation trees.
Bagging is an ensemble method that is made to improve predictions by averaging over bootstrap
samples. A bootstrap sample is a sample from the data, the sample is drawn at random with
replacement such that the bootstrap sample size is the same as the size of the data. To each
bootstrap sample a model is ﬁtted and bagging then averages over a collection of bootstrap
samples to ﬁnd the predicted value. Bagging reduces variance and it is therefore an advantage
when the models have low bias and higher variance.
Classiﬁcation trees have low bias and high variance [33]. A classiﬁcation tree splits the feature
space into rectangles and then ﬁt a simple model in each, see Figure 3.2a. Thinking of it as a
tree each split can be represented as a branch splitting into two twigs. The splits are binary
splits, i.e., the feature is either lower or higher than a given constant see Figure 3.2b. At each
split the quality of the split is judged by using a purity measure.
A random forest consists of many classiﬁcation trees. The trees are de-correlated, which is done
by only using a part of the features, p, for each split. Typically √p is used [33]. When the
trees are de-correlated the variance reduction made by bagging is improved. The reason for this
improvement is that the variance for the bagging over T trees is given by Eq. (3.2). Here it
is assumed that the trees are identically distributed but not necessarily independent, and the
correlation between the trees are ρ.
ρσ2 + 1 −ρ

over all the trees with this permutation [33]. The larger the decrease in accuracy, the more

simple version of the mixed eﬀect model is the random eﬀect model, which is what will be

population that the observation is a part of [37]. The covariance illustrates that not all the
observations are assumed to be independent in the mixed eﬀect, whereas in the ﬁxed eﬀect all
observations are independent.

The Schäfer regularized partial correlation coeﬃcient
Using the high resolution micro data the Schäfer regularized partial correlation coeﬃcient will
be calculated for each well. The high resolution micro data consist of intensity observations for
each individual neuron in the well. In the following, the Schäfer regularized partial correlation
coeﬃcient will be explained and lead on to the weight connectivity matrix which represents the
connection strength between two given neurons [30].
The reason for considering a partial correlation is that correlation values between two neurons far
apart are likely to be aﬀected by neighbour regions and therefore estimating network connectivity
can be imprecise [30]. To get around this, partial correlation can be used instead. When using
partial correlation, the relationship between two neurons can be found without including the
remaining neurons [30]. This measure of correlation is only applied at a given time, meaning
that it is assumed the network has no time delay.
Recalling that this is eﬀectively a small N, large p problem, Schäfer’s regularisation is ap-
plied to avoid getting inaccurate regression coeﬃcients [31].

Results
The results from the analysis are presented in the following chapter. The results include identifying
features extracted from the low resolution plate images that diﬀer between the wild type and
transgenic neurons. Further, the structural connectivity diﬀerences between the two neuronal
types using the high resolution micro data will be identiﬁed and ﬁnally a classiﬁcation between
wild type and transgenic will be conducted.
First the results from analysing the low resolution plate data are described followed by the
results from analysing the high resolution micro data. The results from plate data begin with
a description of the image features and a mixed eﬀect analysis of these. Next, the features are
used to classify a well as wild type or transgenic. The method ordinarily used by Lundbeck for
classiﬁcation is used as a reference.
The results from analysing the micro data start with the results from applying the Schäfer
regularised partial correlation coeﬃcient on the data. This is done to see if there is a diﬀerence
in the measure between wild type neurons and transgenic neurons. Then follows ﬁve network
measures, each of which are used in a mixed eﬀect model to separate wild type neurons from
transgenic. The network measures are then ﬁnally combined and used to classify the wells as
wild type or transgenic.

4.1.7
Test for signiﬁcance of features
The mixed eﬀect model is used for testing whether one feature is better than the others, to
discriminate between wild type and transgenic. The null hypothesis is that the ﬁxed eﬀect
coeﬃcient for type is equal to zero, meaning the feature can not be used to detect a diﬀerence
between wild type and transgenic (see Section 3.6). Table 4.1 gives the results for all the features
using all eleven plates.
The assumption that the residuals are independent, identically distributed and follows a Gaussian
distribution with mean zero is tested for each model. The residual analysis is done by plotting
the residuals for each model, see Figure 4.7. For the majority of the models, the feature needs to
undergo a logarithmic transformation. Figure 4.7a and Figure 4.7b is an example of a feature
before and after the log transformation. Figure 4.7a shows that the residuals do not have equal
variance, but after the log transform, in Figure 4.7b, the variance is similar for all the residuals.
These to ﬁgures also conﬁrms that the residuals are independent. In Figure 4.7c a QQ plot shows
that the residuals follows a Gaussian distribution.
In Table 4.1 it is marked for each feature whether it is log transformed. If it is necessary with a
log transformation the feature stay log transformed in the rest of the analysis, meaning that it is
also log transformed in the logistic regression and the random forest.
When testing on a 5% level and using the Bonferroni correction, the P-value for the features
should be smaller than 0.0019. It can be seen that for the majority of the features the H0
hypothesis can be rejected, since the P-value is much smaller than 0.0019. When a coeﬃcient
is signiﬁcant, it means that it can be used to describe the diﬀerence between wild type and
transgenic. There is in total 23 coeﬃcients that are signiﬁcant and three that are not. The
three non-signiﬁcant coeﬃcient are not close to being signiﬁcant, which justiﬁes the use of the
conservative Bonferroni correction. Even though the coeﬃcient for the feature is not signiﬁcant
in this test, the feature will still be used in the logistic regression and random forest. A feature
that is non signiﬁcant in the univariate test might still together with another feature describe

4.4.5
Betweeness centrality
The assortativity coeﬃcient, local and global eﬃciency are measures for the whole network,
meaning they give a single value for each well. The betweenness centrality and the average
weighted degree are measures for each individual neuron. For each well there is therefore between
700 and 1900 values for the measures.
The betweeness centrality is the fraction of all shortest paths in the network that pass through a
given note (see Section 3.9). Cells that connect diﬀerent parts of the network as a bridge note
will have a high betweenness centrality value. Figure 4.15a shows the betweenes measure for
two diﬀerent wells. It is hard to conclude anything from the plots and therefore the estimated
cumulative distribution function (CDF) for each well is plotted in Figure 4.15b with a color
diﬀerence between the two types. There is no diﬀerence between the estimated CDFs for the
two types, meaning it can not be used to distinguish between wild type and transgenic. This
is investigated further in Appendix E.2 where the estimated CDFs for two wells at a time are
plotted; one well contains transgenic neurons and one well contains wild type neurons and both
wells are from the same plate. Again no diﬀerence can be detected. Finally a possible spatial
diﬀerence between the two types using the betweeness centrality measure has been investigated

4.4.6
Degree
The degree measure has been reformulated into a new measure for each well (see Section 3.9),
because there are no neurons that are not connected to another neuron. The new measure is
the number of zero connections in percent, meaning the number of zero connections related to
the total number of connections in a well. The result is shown in Figure 4.16. Here it can be
seen that half the wells have no zero connections, meaning that all the neurons are connected to
each other and the other half of the wells have very few zero connections. For the eight wells
with wild type neurons there are three wells that have non-zero connections. For the wells with
transgenic neurons there are ﬁve wells with non-zero connections.
A null hypothesis is stated and tested for the new degree measure. The null hypothesis is that
the proportions (probabilities of zero connections) in the two groups are the same. To test this
hypothesis a Chi-squared test is performed [43]. The P-value is 0.3 meaning that there is not
a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the two types using this measure. Please not that, testing this
measure it has not been taken into account that some of the wells are on the same plate, having
the same mother (like it was in the mixed eﬀect model).

5
Discussion and future work
The discussion is structured the same way as the report: First the results from the low resolution
plate data are discussed and then a discussion of the results from the high resolution micro data.
This is followed by a comparison of the time series data with the image data. Finally a discussion
of the sampling rate and the data structure before the section about future work.
Plate data
Random forest (RF) gave a better classiﬁcation than logistic regression (with lasso regularisation)
for the low resolution plate data. This is not surprising since the signal in the data is not
necessarily linear, and the non-linear trend can be captured by RF.
From the logistic regression analysis of the plate data it is indicated that the variance distribution
is diﬀerent between wild type and transgenic neurons (the 5th and 75th percentile for the variance
are important features). The hypothesis of a diﬀerent variance distribution is supported by the
fact that the 75th percentile for the variance also was the feature that, when tested individually,
was best at discriminating between wild type and transgenic. This means that the most active wild
type neurons are more active than the most active transgenic neurons. In projects conducted by
Lundbeck where large time series data sets were analysed, a similar diﬀerence in the distribution
of activity was found [44]. It is speculated that the reason for this diﬀerence is due to a diﬀerence
in the way the action potential works.
Micro data
When analysing the high resolution micro data it was found that the global eﬃciency can be
used to discriminate between wild type and transgenic. The global eﬃciency is based on the
coordinates for the neurons and is the average inverse shortest path length. The measure is higher
for transgenic than wild type which means that the wild neurons are more spread out than the
transgenic. In Section 4.4.2 an exploratory analysis investigated whether the global eﬃciency
measure is driven by certain cells. A certain percentage of the neurons was used to calculate the
global eﬃciency. The P-value for the type coeﬃcient in the mixed eﬀect model (see Eq. (4.2))
was used as a cut-oﬀto evaluate whether the global eﬃciency could discriminate between wild
type and transgenic. It was found that when cells were selected at random, a lower percentage
was needed to achieve the cut-oﬀvalue, than when the cells were selected according to high/low
variance. This indicates, that it is not certain cells that drive this measure and that there must
be a system in the cells’ spatial distribution that disappear, when the most or the least active
cells are used.
Something else to consider regarding the global eﬃciency is that it is not the same spot in each
well that is pictured. The place that is pictured is decided by the technician. But since it is
the same technician taking images of both wild type and transgenic neurons, a possible bias
would be the same for both types and can therefore be disregarded. The fact that it is not the

The betweenness centrality and the degree measures do not diﬀer signiﬁcantly between the two
types. This can be attributed to the fact that the networks are highly inter connected, nearly all
neurons are connected to each other. Therefore these two kinds of measures are perhaps not as
interesting as they could be for other kinds of networks.
The local eﬃciency can not, like the global eﬃciency, be used to discriminate between wild type
and transgenic neurons when tested on a 5% level and using the Bonferroni correction. The
P-value, however, is 0.018 which is close to being signiﬁcant, meaning that if no correction or
another correction than the Bonferroni correction had been used the local eﬃciency would have
had a signiﬁcant P-value.
Using the weight connectivity matrix to calculate the betweenness centrality, it is assumed that
a strong connection means a low distance. Whether this relationship is in fact true, has been
investigated by looking at the weight connectivity for a diﬀerenced signal and the Euclidean
distance for neuron pairs. The result is that as the distance between the neurons increase the
weight connectivity decrease, conﬁrming the basis of the betweenness centrality.

better than RF which may be a bit surprising, since the opposite was true for the low resolution
plate data. Combining the two results leads to the conclusion that the signal in the data is
neither completely linear nor non-linear. For the logistic regression the two most important
features are the global and local eﬃciency. The network implication of the global eﬃciency being
important has been discussed previously and next follows the implications of an important local
eﬃciency. A diﬀerence in local eﬃciency between wild type and transgenic means that there
is a diﬀerence in functional segregation, i.e. a diﬀerence in how much cells within groups and
clusters are synchronised. There is a better (or higher) synchronisation within cluster for the
wild type neurons than for the transgenic. When the global and local eﬃciency diﬀer between
the two neuron types, there is a diﬀerence in both how the active neurons physically construct
the network and also how the synchronisation is within the network. When wanting to aﬀect the
transgenic neurons with a drug/compound then both areas can be targeted. It should, however,
be noted that the detected diﬀerence is a diﬀerence between the active cells extracted in the
pre-processing step. This means that a change in the pre-processing could aﬀect the measures.
The high resolution micro data is a small data set and it would therefore not be strange to see a
high CV error. Since this is not the case (the standard deviation of 0.23 is a bit large, though)
it would be interesting to use the same analysis on a bigger data set to see if an even better
classiﬁcation can be achieved.
Sampling rate and data structure
The plate data have been sampled at a low sampling rate and the micro data at a higher sampling
rate. It should be discussed if an even higher sampling rate is necessary. A higher sampling rate
for the high resolution micro data could make it easier to see the ﬂuorescent signal propagate
throughout the well making the network a bit less homogeneous. Thereby a higher sampling rate
could lead to ﬁnding new network properties. Eytan and Marom [45] together with Bonifazi et
al. [46] have discovered so called leader neurons. These neurons start their oscillating pattern
before the others and trigger the rest of the network to start oscillating. How high a sampling
rate that is necessary to detect this is not certain. Eytan and Marom showed that the leader
neurons start their oscillating pattern tens of milliseconds before the other neurons. To replicate
these ﬁndings the sampling rate would therefore need to be a lot higher. Bonifazi et al. used
an experiment with brain slices to show that when triggering certain neurons to oscillate, the
rest of the network starts oscillating one second later. These ﬁndings indicate that a sampling
rate of 10 Hz should be enough, but the experimental set-up diﬀer signiﬁcantly from the one in
this thesis. Finding leader neurons could be interesting for two reasons. First, the amount of
leader neurons could diﬀer between wild type and transgenic and thereby the classiﬁcation could
be improved. Secondly, further knowledge about the network structure could help identifying
targets for drugs/compounds.
In Section 2.1 the data structure for plate data was described. It was mentioned that there is a
potential risk that the eﬀect from the neurons are combined with a side eﬀect from the plate. If
there is an eﬀect from the right/left side of the plate, that is further mixed with the eﬀect from
the neuron type (wild type/transgenic) making it very diﬃcult to separate what results are due
to plate side and what is due to neuron type. The way of arranging the neurons in the wells have
been changed in subsequent generation of data sets. Further, since there is only neurons from
one mother on each plate, and only one plate for each mother, it is not possible to distinguish
between mother and plate eﬀect. If there is a plate eﬀect it is not possible to separate it from a
mother eﬀect.

5.1
Future work
There are several interesting areas that could be investigated further. In the following, examples
will be given for new methods and also how to improve the data sets.
It could be interesting to further develop the features for the low resolution plate data. The
variance for each well has been the basis of all the features. Using features that describe the
development over time could improve the classiﬁcation. Among others the correlation between
each image over time could be an interesting feature to generate, similar to the analysis of the
time series. Including the time in the modelling, as in tensor analysis [47], could lead to detection
of temporal and spatial signals in the data.
It has been chosen to apply a linear and a non-linear model to the low resolution plate data.
Since the non-linear RF gives the lowest CV error trying another non-linear method, like the
support vector machine, could perhaps improve the classiﬁcation even more. Further a more
thorough interpretation of the important features for the RF could be interesting with recently
published software [48].
It would have been a great advantage to make the pre-processing of the micro data supervised
by having a reference set. A reference set could be a couple of images where a technician had
marked all or some of the active neurons. Having such a set would make it possible to calibrate
the model. First the radius for the neurons could be adjusted to a size where as many of the
neurons as possible would be detected in the pre-processing. It could also be possible to evaluate
whether the Gaussian Mixture modelling was including too many or too few in the group of
active neurons.
It has been shown throughout the thesis that the week eﬀect, i.e. a batch eﬀect, is inﬂuencing
the data to a high degree. Lundbeck is aware of this phenomenon, but it should still be pointed
out as an area of improvement when generating the data. Furthermore the arrangement of the
wells in these data sets is not optimal, as mentioned in Section 1. With the current arrangement
As the arrangement there is a risk that the type eﬀect is mixed with an eﬀect from the left/right
side of the plate. The well set-up has been changed in newer versions of the data sets and it
would be interesting to try the models on these data sets.
When the statistical models include both week, plate and type many degrees of freedom are
used and it is necessary with a large data set. The two data sets used for this report are not
particularly large and it would be interesting to have more data to do the analysis on.
In the pre-processing of the plate data it was chosen only to analyse a part of the data where no
drugs/compounds were added to the wells. This was done due to the speciﬁed goal of the thesis.
Given the opportunity to analyse data where drugs have been applied, means it could be tested
how the two neuron types react diﬀerently to diﬀerent drugs, improving the understanding of
the neuronal networks.
Finally as mentioned previously further understanding of the network properties could be gained
by analysing similar data with a higher sampling rate.

6
Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis has been to distinguish between transgenic and wild type neurons
from in vitro experiments. Two diﬀerent data capture techniques employed by H. Lundbeck A/S
have been assessed by statistical analysis of the resulting data. The two diﬀerent techniques
result in low resolution image data, named plate data, and high resolution image data, named
micro data. Automated methods for pre-processing the two data sets have also been developed
as part of this thesis.
An arsenal of image features were implemented to capture the variability in the low resolution plate
data. The purpose has been to gain knowledge about which of these features, and thereby which
aspects of the data, are relevant for classifying between the two neuron types. A classiﬁcation
error of 21% was achieved using the linear classiﬁer logistic regression with lasso regularisation.
Three diﬀerent features were identiﬁed to be the most important for this task, namely the 5th and
75th percentile of the temporal variance and the median of the spatial gradient. A combination
of these features indicates that the variance distributions diﬀer between the transgenic and wild
type neurons.
The low resolution plate date were further analysed with the goal of discriminating between wild
type and transgenic network oscillations. Using the non-linear classiﬁer random forest, the lowest
cross-validating error of 19% was achieved. This is a better classiﬁcation than the current method
employed by Lundbeck, where one dimensional time traces of the same data are used to achieve a
cross-validation error of 26%. Thereby this work has shown, that using the more expensive image
data, which carry information about the spatial properties of the neuronal networks, actually
allows us to gain substantial knowledge about the diﬀerences between the two types.
The high resolution micro data have been analysed by looking at the structural connectivity of
the neuronal networks. It was found that the two network types could be identiﬁed with a cross-
validation error of 13% using the the linear classiﬁer logistic regression with lasso regularisation.
The two network measures that proved most signiﬁcant for the task were global and local
eﬃciency. Thus, it can be concluded that there is a diﬀerence in 1) how the neurons physically
construct the network, and 2) how synchronised the network is.
Prior to this work, an untested hypothesis existed that the weight connectivity, representing the
connection strength between neurons, decrease as the spatial distance increase. This hypothesis
has now been conﬁrmed. The classiﬁcation of the high resolution micro data outperformed the
classiﬁcation using the low resolution plate data, which further underlines that additional spatial
information about the neurons can be useful in discriminating between wild type and transgenic
neurons.
In summary, an improved discrimination between wild type and transgenic network oscillations

C
Permutation test
The theory for the weighted connectivity matrix is described in Section 3.8. The weight connec-
tivity matrix is applied to the high resolution micro data.
To calculate the weighted connectivity matrix a minimum correlation value that reaches statistical
signiﬁcance, at a 95 % conﬁdence level is used [30]. For a 95 % conﬁdence level the α values
in the two sample test is αlow = 2.5% and αhigh = 97.5%. To ﬁnd the cut-oﬀvalues for the
correlation equal to the two alpha values a permutation test is performed.
The data matrix X for one plate from the micro data have the dimensions: X ∈R6988×1087.
The rows represent the time steps and the columns the neurons, thereby each column is the
neurons intensity oscillations over time. For each neuron the time steps are permuted and thereby
randomising the time structure.
The data matrix for each of the eight wells are permuted one thousand times. For each permuted
data matrix the partial correlation matrix is calculated. The upper triangular part of the partial
correlation matrix is saved, since the matrix is symmetric around the diagonal. Figure C.1a is a
histogram for the saved correlation values. To determine the two cut-oﬀvalues in Eq. (C.1) and
Eq. (C.2) a cumulative sum over the correlation values are made, see Figure C.1b.
rα=2.5% = −4.8656 · 10−5
(C.1)
rα=97.5% = 4.8065 · 10−5
(C.2)
The correlation values are, as expected, close to each other and the rα=97.5% is chosen as the
cut-oﬀvalue.
Eight out of sixteen wells are used to calculate the permutation value. The eight wells used
are from week 18, and the remaining wells are from week 19. Due to this choice a week eﬀect
could be emphasised even more for the weight connectivity matrix. However when looking at the
weight connectivity matrices in Appendix E it does not seem to be a problem.
