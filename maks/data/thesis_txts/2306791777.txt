Abstract 
The project has assumed the investigation of the modern single family house in relation 
to the sustainability. Space solutions has been a result of reconsidered functions that 
home should provide to its tenants. The purpose of the project was to check, whether 
it is possible to design and build a house with the low energy use thanks to the passive 
solutions in the Scandinavian climate and to keep its architectural values at the same 
time. The architecture and energy performance have been equally important in order 
to create well thought house in terms of energy usage and unmeasurable – visual 
aspects. Should people totally give up on the form in order to design sustainable 
houses? Can actually architecture and sustainability be connected? What is the 
extent of the connection? – those questions constitute the essence of the issues 
analyzed in the project, which contains two major parts. First part has focused on the 
research of the existing solutions and human needs in relation to the topic. The second 
part includes the detailed investigation of the solutions applied in the design process 
for the low energy house, which has not resigned from providing visual and tenant-
friendly values to the people. The investigation has shown that adequate solutions 
applied in the early phase of designing process, when form of the building is decided 
about, can bring benefits in terms of energy consumption and thermal indoor climate. 
Implementation of overhangs resulted in number of hours with operative temperatures 
above 27°C in average zone within acceptable limit - stated by Danish Building 
Regulations 2015. After the investigation of other passive solutions, overheating hours 
have been reduced by 40.6% while window areas have been increased by 24% 
without increasing heating demand.

1 Introduction 
1.1 
Background 
Studies about buildings are very wide. It is a big sector and combines many 
interrelated topics. Everyone strives for better and better solutions and surprisingly more 
and more conflicts appear between different actors. The key to success is to upskill 
designers giving them a wider spectrum, a holistic view on how the building should be 
designed. The ability and understanding of how all branches complement rather than 
interfere, seems to be crucial. This would allow agreements between highly skilled 
professionals and result in balanced design. 
Studying buildings can be held differently. I have a Bachelor background within 
architectural engineering gained at the technical university. However, a special 4 
years education program combined architecture and civil engineering - two different 
studies about the same topic. Studying history of architecture, ergonomic, art 
compositions, aesthetics, taking part in many architectural, urban, and drawing 
studios resulted in my architectural background. On the other hand, at the same time, 
the studies were more technical. Civil engineering structures, mechanics, statistics, 
foundations and many more concurred to understanding of the structural design. 
During my student exchange semester at DTU, I realized, that something very 
important is missing. There were no sustainability and environmental aspects taken into 
consideration. In order to get a knowledge about sustainable buildings techniques I 
decided to continue my education at DTU. During my whole master studies at DTU, I 
had a great focus on green energy and sustainable architecture. On the other hand, 
while working with many fellow students at DTU, I realized many people lost themselves 
in numbers and totally forgot about or never gained a sensitive sense for buildings. 
Thus the question aroused. If actually perceptive aspects, that are the main concern 
for architects, can be connected with technical studies of sustainable design? This 
must be taken care of at the very early stage in the designing process while passive 
elements are being designed. The passive solutions have been investigated in this 
project in order to satisfy human needs and senses on different platforms, while 
bringing benefits in terms of energy use and indoor climate. The passive form designed 
and developed in this project provides a good base for the design of the active 
solutions, later on. 
1.2 Objective 
The main objective of this Thesis is to investigate how and if right architectural - passive 
solutions can bring benefits to energy performance of the single family house, while, 
at the same time, bringing maverick architectural features to it.

2 Problem description 
Research have been done in order to identify the issue based on the contemporary 
accomplished buildings. The investigation is based on several topics that are 
interrelated. The outline of the most important principles has been obtained from both 
scientific documents and real projects analysis. Nowadays, developed countries 
depart from natural environment. Thus, versatile studies refer to the term ‘built 
environment’. It is a reasonable practice because most of the people of the well-
developed society spend around 90% of time inside the buildings, either at work or at 
home. At the same time, awareness concerning importance of natural environment 
at those countries is rising. Thus, whole cities are becoming ‘smarter’ by implementing 
more and more sustainable solutions. What is more, as presented in Figure 1, 20%-40 % 
of the energy use in the society is consumed by building sector (Wall, 2005) (Hosein , 
et al., 2013) and this distribution is similar in many developed countries (Hosein , et al., 
2013). Environmental impact of buildings in US has been presented in Figure 1.  
 
Figure 1. Environmental impact of buildings in US (Hosein , et al., 2013). 
Buildings’ sustainability is a wide topic, where not only environmental but also 
interrelated studies about people and society in general must be performed (Hosein , 
et al., 2013). To contribute broadly defined architectural evolution, building’s design 
should be based on the holistic thinking, connecting financial, environmental and also 
social aspects. The only substantiation for rising a building is when it serves to people, 
and what does the people need? People need to feel comfortable and perform well. 
Proper temperature, humidity, daylight, lack of partitions and drafts - all this factors, 
and many related, have an influence on living quality. Big pursuit to reduce CO2 
emissions unfortunately pull away attention from perceptual, sensual aspects, 
atmosphere and sometimes also deep aesthetic. The great conceptual platform, 
innovative solutions, must be rethought during each design process to reach also 
perceptual aspects. Important elements are how it is to live here, what is, what I am 
feeling, how do I sense proportions, scale, the light, the warmth. For each human also 
human aspects are important, it can be for example traditions and culture. The fact 
that it is not a global scale affair does not mean it can be forgotten by designers, 
because it can be very important for human individually. Together with the functional 
and technical aspects, perceptual and humanistic expressions should create unity

2.1 Architecture of sustainable housing 
Thinking a bit back, Aristoteles’ philosophy seems to be reflected in the sustainable 
housing sector. Buildings are being recognized by what they do, and also more by 
how they interact with environment instead of how they look like. Some studies show 
even, that the concept of balanced development, sustainability, is not well led 
(Kaltenbrunner, 2008). It is very oriented on optimizing and upgrading in fact those 
isolated aspects. This practices concern mainly global environmental issues, and 
nowadays, also indoor climate. Robert Kaltenbrunner (Kaltenbrunner, 2008) describes 
that, so far, cultural and social aspects are taken out from how sustainability is 
perceived. Air tightness, solar cells, heat recovery and super good energy 
performance is not enough. Social aspects have not been balanced with economic 
considerations. While strong focus on environmental issues is put, the deep 
consideration of socio-cultural sustainability is missing. Because of this, some studies 
question even the quality of living (GhaffarianHoseini, et al., 2011), whereas, the main 
goal of implementing sustainable solutions is actually to improve this quality. Also 
(Vanhoutteghem, et al., 2002) raises the issue of human oriented house, in terms of his 
culture, form and way of life.  
 “The way civil engineers think is predominantly technical and rational and not geared 
enough to the complexity of human behavior. The civil engineer lacks socio-political 
starting points and strategies to achieve his goals” (Kaltenbrunner, 2008). 
Harmonization of different values should be included in spatial solutions of a house 
(Korsgaard & Esbensen, 1974). The study (GhaffarianHoseini, et al., 2011) describes 
how relevant is the analysis of spatial and cultural issues in order to determine feasible 
sustainable solutions. The need of implementing vernacular architectural features to 
low energy buildings should be inevitable because built environment affect current 
and future communities (GhaffarianHoseini, et al., 2011). Up to now, global scale has 
been mostly considered. Smaller scale considerations show however problems of 
incorporating sustainable approaches with people. Thanks to including traditional and 
cultural values, a better built in environment could be achieved.  
Many governing bodies have the concept of sustainable environment as a goal 
however, this is a very wide topic and main trends do not seem to consider any user 
interactions. That is why, sustainability has been subdivided by [ (GhaffarianHoseini, et 
al., 2011) as presented in Figure 2.

2.2 Low energy houses in Denmark 
As one of the leading countries in sustainable housing development, Denmark, has 
been selected as a focus place of this research. Sophisticated houses have been built 
by architects and engineers. For many years not energy but comfort and trends have 
been a main focus points. In many different countries this practices are still being used 
and all local energy is devoted for housing. However, the awareness of environment 
is rising and more and more of them promote sustainability and good indoor climate. 
One of those countries with strict sustainable economy is aforementioned Denmark.

common views in their development. The environmental impact of buildings is an

2.3 Low energy and passive houses 
Once thinking about passive solutions, Smads and Wall in their work (Smeds & Wall, 
2006) say that the cheapest and, in fact, most ecofriendly energy is the energy that 
has not been actually used.  
 
Figure 14. Comparison between different types of energy buildings (Hosein , et al., 2013). 
There is no doubt, energy used by buildings needs to be reduced. It should be done 
so that environmental impact of buildings will be limited as much as possible. There 
are several ways to do so. On the other hand, energy used cannot be the only factor 
we are looking at. While designing a building, the holistic approach needs to be taken 
into account. Additionally, studies indicates more and more attention is being put to 
the indoor conditions within the building (Mahdavi, et al., 2010). The answer on how to 
satisfy expectation is the topic of a big open discussion in the current years. Should all 
of them or just the most relevant needs be achieved and what would be the best way 
to do so.  
There are passive houses, low-energy houses, green houses, sustainable houses and 
high performance houses. All of them tend to perform better than just a house. All of 
them are somewhat environment friendly. A performance comparison of passive and 
low-energy buildings has been done in Vienna, Austria (Mahdavi, et al., 2010). It has 
been described, that the low-energy building consists of systems that consume 
energy. Passive house is better insulated, meaning, in this particular case, that U-value 
of external walls is 3,08 and U-value of windows is 1.67 times higher than those used in 
the other building. In this case, it resulted with energy demands lower more than 50% 
than in case of low-energy building. From the presented calculations, which takes into 
==PAGE:23==

Joanna Wojciechowska

Department of Civil Engineering 
 
account embodied energy loses, 8-18 years of payback time is predicted for the 
additional investment in solutions used in passive houses.  
The focus areas, while designing high performance houses differ also according to 
their location. Improving energy efficiency in cold climates focuses mostly on reducing 
space heating demands. Through integrated design that combines geometry, 
building envelope, shading devices and balanced systems and mechanical solutions, 
space heating demand might be reduces in significant way. According to research 
presented in (Smeds & Wall, 2006), savings even up to 83% might be achieved in 
Nordic countries.    
When sustainable house is being considered, excellent thermal insulation, heat 
recovery and passive solar heat gains are marked as crucial (Schniedersa & 
Hermelinkb, 2004). Low energy buildings are usually well insulated and air tight, thus 
the heating season is most likely shorter than for other buildings (Wall, 2005). Knowing 
the principles of passive solutions, every house design should start with considerations 
of the passive forms. Nowadays, when companies are developing well performing 
materials and systems, designers can choose among great variety of solutions and 
technologies. Thanks to this, new opportunities appear from the early design stage. In 
case passive practices are not sufficient, active systems have to be implemented in 
order to obtain well performing building.

4.1 Inspiration 
4.1.1 
18.36.54 by Daniel Libeskind 
18.36.54 is the name of the house project done in 2010 in Connecticut, USA. The house 
has been designed for the couple and has an area of 185 m2. Its form and its territorial 
spaces are defined by folded planes. The walls are supported by 4 steel frame arches 
and exterior is covered by stainless steel cladding that reflects surrounding and sky.  
The sharpness is the result of 18 planes, 36 points and 54 lines, hence its name. It has a 
concept of the shelter that surrounds the space and provides roof. Windows are used 
just to enclose interiors and allow beautiful view out. Thanks to this bended shape, 
glazing areas are created, which are provided in different locations. This very artistic 
form that is very sharp outside, is actually very warm inside thanks to the wooden 
finishing. The house can be described as an uncompromising architecture. In Figure 
25 one can see the structure after realisation. Figure 26 and Figure 27 present house’s 
3D model.

5.4.1 Building envelope 
5.4.1.1 
External walls 
Concrete sandwich panels with polyisocyanate insulation have been used. By using 
double concrete layer, a good heat capacity is ensured and heat loses through the 
outer wall are decreased. The damping mass effect is allowed due to the big 
selfweight of the structure with the density of 2400 kg/m3. The mentioned effect helps 
maintaining the indoor environment despite of day/night changing conditions and 
indoor temperature changings. The insulation material the polyisocanate has been 
used with the thermal conductivity of the material equal to λ =  0,02  W/K*m. The 
material with such a good thermal parameters allowed to reduce the thickness of the 
concrete wall.  
In order to establish tenant – friendly interior design, the gypsum insulating plaster 
boards are used with the thermal conductivity equal to λ  =  0,18  W/m*K and density 
of 600 kg/m3.  
A significant decrease of the durability and thermal performance are secured by 
vapour barrier underneath the internal cladding layer, which prevents the moisture 
from penetrating through the external walls. Moreover, protection against the different 
atmospheric conditions e.g. rain, wind, etc.  is provided. Mentioned protection has 
their contribution in the longer life time of the materials by preventing them from

6.1.1 
Natural Ventilation 
The natural ventilation is commonly used method of exchanging air inside dwellings. 
Because of no energy needed for operation it seems to be a reasonable solution. 
Especially in places such as house where there is no high people density and light 
density self-standing natural ventilation could be sufficient.  
The natural ventilation can be obtained even by simple window openings, however 
this would demand people operations. Because of the fact that people will not be 
always at home to control openings, it needs to be self-operating system. Automated 
openable windows could be solution, however this is the expensive way. What is more, 
while opening windows air can move in both directions. Such a system is hard to 
control and not always efficient.  
Supply and exhaust system is chosen for investigation. Supply inlets are placed at the 
external walls 20 cm above the ground. Outlets are placed at ceilings. This may result 
in stack effect. Cold air enters the building at lower levels and while going through the

6.2 Windows optimization 
A special attention has been put to the glazing units. Windows are crucial elements of 
the building envelope because of their properties. What is more, thinking about 
building envelope in terms of energy, windows seem to be its weakest point. However, 
nowadays more and more companies pay attention to windows development and 
the current available windows have incomparable properties to those that have been 
used previously. When it comes to the window design, all of their parameters matter. 
Windows affect not only heat losses, but also are the cause of extremely high solar 
heat gains during summer, even in Northern countries. Balanced design that allows a 
proper amount of light entering the building and providing nice views though 
windows, at the same time avoiding worst scenarios should be strived for. 
For this investigation where direct and diffuse solar heat gains are looked into, windows 
have been placed always exactly in the middle of the interior’s external wall.

area defined by regulations (Building regulations, 2015). According to the Building 
Regulations (6.5.2(1)) (Building regulations, 2015) side lights in habitable rooms should 
correspond to 10% of floor area. In case of sky lights, this percentage equals 7%. 
However, they are valid only when glazing light transmittance of not less than 0,75 is 
used. 
The area of sky light should be smaller because of the constant exposal on sun 
operation. More light enters through the roof lights, however the risk of overheating is 
increased.

6.3 Shading 
Shading is a crucial feature while speaking about solar heat gains. Windows itself have 
solar heat gain coefficient (g-value) which defines the amount of heat gained. The 
higher value the more heat can be gained through the window to heat the interior. 
This is desirable during the heating period in order to reduce heating power demands. 
On the other hand, solar radiation seems to be enemy during the summer where air 
temperatures are high even in Northern Europe and additional solar radiation should 
be avoided. However, the g-value is constant for each glazing unit. Reduction of the 
summer heat gains can be done by properly designed shading.  
6.3.1 Overhangs  
Overhangs are a type of shading that is placed above glazing. Those horizontal planes 
are really powerful because of simple principle. The sun path differs throughout the 
year. During the winter solar altitude is low, whereas during summer it is high (Figure

6.5 Vertical screening 
Vertical obstructions are investigated as a next step together with applied overhangs 
and after changes of wall facing directions. Their impact on temperatures inside rooms 
is checked because similarly to the overhangs, vertical screens can shade windows. 
Because of low sun altitude, vertical obstructions can create long unwanted shades 
during the winter.

or evening sun. When sun is rising, the morning sun hits Eastern façade, until it rises and 
before overhangs start to create shadows. It is similarly but inversely on Western 
façade during sun set. Meaning, during the summer, the time when there is direct 
operation of sun could be reduced. What is more, thanks to the shorter sun path during 
winter, vertical screening placed on Eastern or Western façade, closely to north side, 
would not have even a chance to create shadows on windows during winter, see

6.6 Ceiling heights  
Investigation of different ceiling heights have been done as a last step because of the 
fact it has a big impact on thermal comfort inside the building all over the year. High 
ceiling can lead to lowering the temperatures both in summer and winter. The volume 
of the room is increased and the heat spreads in bigger volume. During winter when 
saving heat is crucial, high interiors are not favourable solutions. What is more, heated 
air is always cumulated higher, right below the ceiling – further from the human level.  
That is why, increasing of rooms heights have been investigated only in interiors where 
hot hours occur.

6.8 Final optimized building   
After the investigation of above mentioned solutions, the best case has been 
obtained. This final solution includes implemented solutions from all of the optimization 
lines. Its performance in terms of solar energy gains, heating demand, thermal comfort 
and appearance has been compared with the models at different stages of the 
designing process. 
When the shape and form of building had been investigated, so that solar heat gains 
are optimized, heating system have been implemented. This have been done in order 
to provide satisfactory indoor temperatures also during colder days. Knowing that the 
form of the building provides proper temperatures during summer, focus on 
implementing low energy heating system must be put.  
For the needs of this project however, exemplary heating system has been applied. 
This has been done in order to investigate changes in energy demands, while different 
passive elements are being checked. In this chapter also the heat gains through 
windows are looked into and their impact on heating demand. The more energy 
gained from sun through windows, the lower should be heating demand. On the other 
hand, solar heat gains causing overheating should be avoided. Thus, the solutions 
should be the result of heating demand and indoor climate considerations. 
Floor heating has been implemented in the house. This kind of the water-based 
radiative system is solution which is affordable for residential purposes. Current 
technologies allow to implement the floor heating also beneath wooden floors, 
however special type of panels should be chosen. In order to obtain best heat flow 
through the layers, the system construction should perform its best in terms of thermal 
conductivity and heat transfer coefficient. Additionally, thermal insulation must be 
used beneath the system to improve its performance and avoid heat down flows.  
Big and equally distributed heating area allows obtaining good indoor climate. Also 
the benefits are taken from the fact, that heated air will move from lower levels up to 
the ceiling and distribute heat. This, however, can cause comfort dissatisfaction due 
to the vertical thermal stratification. Minimum air temperature indoors has been set to 
21°C while floor temperature shouldn’t exceed 29°C.

9 Further investigation 
As far as further investigation of this design is considered, more passive solutions might 
be investigated. Development of form should be followed by consideration of leaning 
faces. Currently all walls are perpendicular and roof faces are parallel to the ground. 
Sloped faces would result in more dynamic form of the building, thus their influence on 
building performance should be also considered.  
What is more, different cladding materials should be under investigation. Their thermal 
characteristics, as well as colour should be checked in order to find the best 
performing solution.  
Big focus has been put on solar heat gains and windows seem to be crucial element 
of the whole design. Detailed analysis of windows with different parameters should be 
performed. Windows with very good characteristics have been used in the design, 
however, it should be checked how far with the design one can get while using 
‘superwindows’. Lowering U-value of windows is bringing new solutions on market. 
Reduction on heat loss through glass units is possible by usage of multilayer glass panes 
where inner panes are made out of ultra-thin glass, resembling foil. Ultra-thin glass 
features excellent optical flatness and is very light, thus the glazing units are not getting 
heavier than the standard glazing units. 
Glazing presented by Vis Inventis Company (Vis Inventis, 2016) has certified U-value 
equal to 0.3 W/m2K. As stated by executives, its optical parameters are close to that 
of standard triple glazing unit. However, Visible transmittance and exact G-value are 
yet to be certified. This innovative solution creates new possibilities for designers and 
could be implemented in the house, together with wide frame which eliminates linear 
loses. Investigation could include also untypical windows shapes, which could not be 
evaluated in IDA ICE Software. 
Further considerations should concern active forms. Fore mostly, mechanical 
ventilation of variable air volume should be investigated. Usage of heat recovery unit 
with efficiency 85%, or higher, would allow big energy savings on heating. What is 
more, decentralized system should be considered. Focus should be also put on 
detailed design low temperature heating system.

10 Conclusion 
The comprehensive literature study has shown, that there are two different 
mainstreams in the modern architecture, heading different directions. One is focusing 
on abstract forms and visual aspects of buildings, while the other one has very strong 
focus on sustainability. Both of the mainstreams sometimes recourse to extremes. The 
review showed, that it has been already noticed by some, that the low energy housing 
is lacking an architectural input. The strict law regulations in Denmark require designing 
houses with low energy demands and, for example, allow designs with very small 
windows areas. The reduction of energy consumption concurs to globally considered 
aspects, however, shape of the space, as well as indoor climate has more direct 
influence on particular human being and balanced design should be strived for. 
Additionally, the overheating problem has been identified as one of the main issues 
occurring in low energy houses. 
The investigation has shown that passive form of the building has influence on its 
performance. The detailed investigation of passive form of the building can result in 
interesting shape and better indoor climate while providing actually the same amount 
of energy. It has been proven that some passive solutions can be integrated in the 
design and be consistent with provided form other than cuboid. 
Providing overhangs is solution that brings most of the benefits. While reducing the net 
heat gains by 26,3%, the energy demands for heating is increased by 4.9%. When only 
overhangs are applied, indoor temperatures already meet requirements and 
overheating is reduced by 79,3%. The overhangs should be provided on the South 
facing façade as well on East and West. The overhangs cannot be omitted while 
designing the low energy house. 
The direction which walls are facing also has a big influence on temperatures. 
Windows facing south get the most provision from the overhangs. Walls facing directly 
East or West should be avoided. The fan shaped building, when placed in such a way 
with most of its facades facing south, allows to reduce the overheating hours by 84.2%. 
The benefits are noticed also in the reduction of the heating demand by 2.2%. This 
could not be achieved with building having a rectangular floor plan. 
As far as East and West sides are considered, vertical screens should be applied. The 
vertical screens should be however placed only on the side closer to North. The vertical 
screens on side close to South should be in general avoided. Again, this is possible 
thanks to the fan shaped floor plan and vertical screens, provided on the East or West 
façade close to North side, would not bring benefits as they did in this case. As far as 
South facades are considered, not very long screens could be used because of the 
fact, that they are neutral in terms of the heat gains. When applied to the design, it 
allowed to reduce the overheating hours by 8.3% not influencing heating demand.

The increase height of the building is resulting in higher energy demands and should 
be avoided. On the other hand, it might be used in purpose of reducing the 
overheating hours when needed. Increasing Living Room by 1m, reduced overheating 
by 36.7% but raised the heating demand by 8.5%.  
Thanks to above mentioned practices of reducing solar heat gains, and therefore hot 
temperatures, comfort of living might be also improved by providing a bigger glazing 
without causing the overheating. This has been done in the considered building. 
Increase of glazing area by 24%, resulted in the same heating demand as in the case, 
when only the overhangs have been used. What is more, thermal indoor climate has 
been improved by reduction of the overheating hours by 40.5%. In this case, 
according to the Danish Building Regulations 2015 no unacceptable thermal 
conditions occur in the building.  
The reduction of the overheating might be done also by placing rooms, where 
activities are performed during evening on East, and inversely when morning 
occupancy is considered. However, it is decided that sun observation while 
performing activities is very important, especially in Norther country when its 
appearance is rather rare.  
Attention should be also put on providing appropriate rooms areas. Investigation has 
shown, when thinking about overheating and high temperatures, it is important to 
avoid too small rooms. Too small interiors are also at risk of getting bad indoor climate 
in terms of CO2 concentration. Thus, special attentions should be put, especially while 
designing bedrooms intended for two people. Proposed maverick floor plan provides 
possibility of easy modifications which, in this case, would be its special advantage.   
The architectural idea of providing fully glazed facades, so favourable in the current 
designing world, cannot be however implemented. Lowering window sill exposes 
windows to the direct sun operation also during summer days, therefore too big 
overhangs would be needed to avoid this energy gains. What is more, to allow gains 
during winter through the whole window area, the overhangs should not be used 
directly above windows. On the other hand, placing them to high is resulting in more 
summer solar heat gains. This brings also restrictions to the design of the building where 
the overhangs are to be integrated.  
All in all, the detailed analysis of the passive solutions can bring out solutions that will 
reduce need for active systems and eliminate need for cooling during summer. At the 
same time, those considerations help to shape form of the building. The ‘beauty’ of 
the design is not evaluated as this is always an individual case. However, the designed 
building diverges from the most of the Danish low energy house designs and, what is 
more, takes advantages from its shape - as far as the energy and the indoor climate 
are considered.
