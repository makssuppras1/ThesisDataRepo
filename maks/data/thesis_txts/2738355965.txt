Abstract
This article explores the utilization of a Dynamic Vision Sensor (DVS) in conjunc-
tion with a Star Tracking Camera for ground-based satellite tracking. The study
investigates the noise characteristics inherent to low-light observations with a DVS
sensor, identifying conditions under which the noise resembles an excitable medium.
A novel filtering approach is introduced that uses the apparent velocity of satel-
lite events to effectively differentiate them from noise. This filter is integrated into
a detection algorithm that matches or exceeds the performance of a Star Tracker
systems, as demonstrated by the Root Mean Square Error in arc minutes from a
second-order least squares fit.
This research demonstrates the potential of DVS sensors for enhancing satellite
tracking and proposes methodologies that contribute to advancements in space sit-
uational awareness. The proposed filtering and tracking techniques offer promising
improvements in satellite detection and tracking accuracy, laying the groundwork
for future exploration and development in this field.
The combination of a conventional Star Tracking Camera for accurate pointing
and a DVS sensor for satellite tracking is proposed as a compact, low-power solution
suitable for real-time operations on small embedded systems.

Introdution
With advancements in rocket launch technology, space, the last uncharted territory,
has become increasingly reachable. This has led to a significant rise in the number
of artificial satellites entering Earth’s orbit over recent years (Murtaza et al. 2020).
Low Earth Orbit (LEO), in particular, has experienced a notable surge in satel-
lite presence. This region is highly active among both private and governmental
entities due to its suitability for numerous applications, including Earth observa-
tion, communication, and surveillance. The swift rise in the number of satellites
and the congestion of LEO also bring a heightened risk of collisions between space
objects. The consequences of such collisions could generate thousands of pieces of
debris, further increasing the likelihood of additional collisions. This phenomenon,
known as the Kessler syndrome, could make LEO completely inaccessible to space-
craft (Kessler 1991). Space situational awareness (SSA) aims to develop methods for
determining and updating the orbits of space objects to predict and avoid collisions.
SSA encompasses the understanding of both natural and man-made objects that
traverse and orbit near-Earth space.(Kennewelll and Vo 2013)
The surge in satellites compounds the challenge of tracking all objects. Fur-
thermore, some existing tracking systems do not publish the orbital parameters of
military satellites.
Astrometric measurements play a vital role in SSA, which is
essential for tracking and managing objects in Earth’s orbit. Precise astrometric
data allow for the accurate determination of orbital paths, helping to prevent colli-
sions and manage the growing issue of space debris. By continuously updating the
positions of these objects, SSA systems can provide timely warnings of potential col-
lisions, enabling spacecraft operators to perform evasive maneuvers when necessary.
This is crucial to protect valuable satellites, the International Space Station, and
future space missions(Kennewelll and Vo 2013).
Despite advances in SSA, significant challenges remain. Current tracking systems
are often limited by their inability to detect small debris or to provide real-time
updates on rapidly changing conditions. Additionally, the increase in the number of
active satellites and space debris has outpaced the capabilities of existing monitoring
systems, especially space-based SSA has seen development, yet it is constrained by
conventional image processing algorithms.(Wang et al. 2022)
Event-based cameras represent a shift from traditional frame-based imaging sys-
tems. Unlike conventional cameras that capture images at fixed intervals, event-
based cameras operate asynchronously, detecting changes in the scene at the pixel
level(Lichtsteiner, Posch, and Delbruck 2008).
This approach offers several no-
table advantages that are particularly beneficial for astrometric measurements and
SSA(Afshar et al. 2020).
Cameras based on event detection are capable of recording scene changes with

2.1
Orbit determination
Orbit determination is a cornerstone of space situational awareness (SSA), which
involves the precise calculation of the orbits of celestial objects such as artificial
satellites and space debris.
The process begins with collecting observational data, including astrometric ob-
servations, which involve precise measurements of an object’s position relative to
a known reference frame. These measurements are typically obtained using tele-
scopes, radar, or other tracking instruments. Additional data includes range and
range rate measurements, the distances from the observer to the object and the rate
at which these distances change, acquired via radar or lidar systems. Doppler shifts,
or changes in the frequency of signals received from the object due to its relative
motion, are also used to determine its velocity along the line of sight.
2.1.1
Optical Observations
Optical observations involve capturing images of celestial objects using telescopes
equipped with sensitive detectors. These observations provide angular measurements
(right ascension and declination) of the object’s position relative to the background
stars. The process begins with the careful acquisition of images using ground-based
or space-based telescopes. The observational strategy must ensure that the object of
interest is within the field of view at specific times. The precise timing of observations
is crucial, as it allows for the accurate determination of the object’s position at given
epochs (Pastor, Sanjurjo-Rivo, and Escobar 2021). This Observation geometry is
show in Fig. 2.1
Calibration of the images is essential to correct for various distortions and ensure
accurate positional measurements.
This calibration involves accounting for lens
aberrations, atmospheric distortions, and any other instrumental effects that could
affect the accuracy of the measurements. The next step, centroiding, determines the
exact position of the object within the image by calculating the centroid of its light
distribution. This requires sophisticated image-processing techniques to enhance the
precision of the positional data.
For Initial Orbit Determination (IOD) commonly used methods are the Gauss’s

2.1.2
Refinement of the Orbit
After obtaining an initial estimate of the orbit, refinement is necessary to achieve
higher accuracy.
This involves differential correction techniques, where iterative
methods such as the least squares approach are used to minimize the discrepan-
cies between the observed and predicted positions of the object. The least squares
method adjusts the orbital parameters to fit the observational data better, thereby
reducing the residuals.
The precision of orbit propagation is highly dependent on the accuracy of the
initial orbit determination and the quality of the observational data.
Advanced
numerical integration techniques are employed to solve the equations of motion over
time, predicting the object’s future positions and velocities. These predictions are
crucial for SSA applications, such as collision avoidance, space traﬀic management,
and reentry predictions. (Pastor, Sanjurjo-Rivo, and Escobar 2021)

2.2
Dynamic vision sensors
Dynamic vision sensors (DVS) belong to the category of biologically inspired neu-
romorphic sensors, Neuromorphic engineering is a field of technology that seeks to
design and create systems inspired by the structure and function of the human
brain and nervous system. It involves building hardware and software that mimic
neural processes, enabling eﬀicient, adaptive, and intelligent computing. These sys-
tems leverage principles such as parallel processing, event-driven communication,
and learning from experience, aiming to achieve high performance with low power
consumption, similar to biological brains (Etienne-Cummings and Van Der Spiegel
1996). DVS, also known as event cameras, operate asynchronously, inspired by the
human retina and its neural processing mechanisms. In the retina, photoreceptors
detect light and send signals to ganglion cells, which fire action potentials in response
to changes in light intensity and motion. This asynchronous, event-based signaling
reduces data transmission and enhances eﬀiciency. DVS fundamentally differs from
CMOS sensors, which utilize global illumination of the sensor and sample photons
during the exposure period. DVS continuously collects photons across the entire
sensor, with each pixel asynchronously triggering an event when the logarithmic
intensity exceeds a threshold. The detailed mechanism will be discussed further in
Sec. 2.2.1. The logarithmic response to light intensity in (DVS) is a critical as-
pect of the design that allows them to handle a wide range of lighting conditions
with high dynamic range and sensitivity. This response mimics the behavior of the
human retina, where light sensitivity changes logarithmically rather than linearly.
(Lichtsteiner, Posch, and Delbruck 2008)
This event-based approach gives the DVS distinct characteristics compared to a
conventional camera. The primary difference lies in the DVS that generates data
only when there is a change in light intensity within its FOV. When objects move
within the FOV, the output resembles that of a high-pass filter in standard image
analysis. This logarithmic transformation offers multiple key benefits. Firstly, it
reduces the range of output signals, allowing the sensor to handle a wide spectrum
of light intensities without becoming saturated. For example, in extremely bright
environments, minor changes in light intensity result in relatively smaller variations
in the output signal, thus avoiding sensor overload. However, in dim lighting, slight
changes in intensity cause more significant variations in the output signal, increasing
the sensor’s sensitivity to small changes in light (Graça, McReynolds, and Delbruck
2023).
Other benefits of DVS over traditional frame-based cameras include low power
consumption, reduced bandwidth requirements (in certain scenarios), high temporal
resolution, and high dynamic range. These advantages, combined with the high-pass
characteristics of the data, make DVS highly suitable for tracking applications in
computer vision (Yang, Liu, and Delbruck 2015). However, the technology is still
quite new and its application potential is largely unexplored.

Pixel Operation
As previously stated, pixels operate independently and asynchronously in reaction
to changes in light intensity. Fig. 2.2 illustrates a simplified circuit overview. Each
pixel contains an active logarithmic photoreceptor that feeds into an amplifier and
comparator, producing a discrete event. An event is triggered when light hits the
photodiode, generating a photocurrent Ip.
This current is logarithmically trans-
formed into an output voltage Vp. Using a switched capacitor, Vp is inverted and
amplified. The comparator stage receives the amplified voltage Vdiff. This com-
parator constantly evaluates the amplified voltage against two preset thresholds:
the ON threshold hetaON and the OFF threshold hetaOFF . After an event is gener-
ated, Vdiff is maintained at the reset level for a refractory period trefr (McReynolds,
Graça, and Delbruck 2022).

2.2.2
Noise Characteristics
In general, noise, i.e., events that are not correlated with any actual change in the
FOV, are considered noise events. The noise event rate, or background activity,
depends on intensity. noise event rate varies with photocurrent levels. At low pho-
tocurrents, noise events are primarily due to shot noise, which increases significantly.
As the photocurrent increases, the parasitic photocurrent contributes more to the
overall noise, but the filtering effect of the photoreceptor output stage reduces the
noise event rate at high photocurrents (Graca and Delbruck 2021).

Thermal dependent noise
Thermal noise, also known as Johnson-Nyquist noise, is a type of electronic noise
generated by the random motion of charge carriers (electrons and holes) within a
conductor or semiconductor material due to thermal agitation. This noise is inherent
in all electronic circuits and components and is particularly relevant in sensors and
amplifiers, including Dynamic Vision Sensors. Consider a DVS operating in a low-
light environment. The photodiodes in the sensor generate a small photocurrent in
response to the limited light. However, thermal noise in the photodiodes and the
associated circuitry can cause random fluctuations in the signal. These fluctuations
might be suﬀicient to cross the event detection threshold, leading to false events. By
cooling the sensor and optimizing the circuit design, the level of thermal noise can
be reduced, thereby improving the accuracy and reliability of the DVS (Nozaki and
Delbruck 2017).
Dark current is mainly caused by the thermal creation of electron-hole pairs
within the semiconductor material of photodiodes. This type of electrical current
flows through the photodiodes of the sensor even when no light is present. The
impact of dark current is twofold: it reduces contrast, thereby lowering event de-
tection in low light conditions due to an increased detection threshold, and it raises
shot noise levels, which degrades event timing and causes random event generation.
thermal noise can also stem from junction leakage in the reset transistor.
With
rising temperatures, the thermal generation of electron-hole pairs within the semi-
conductor material increases. This results in higher leakage current across the p-n
junctions (Nozaki and Delbruck 2017).
An other source of thermal dependent noise comes from the parasitic photocur-
rent which is caused by unintended light-induced current in the transistor junctions
that are not part of the primary photoreceptor, Any p-n junction in the sensor,

Temporal Noise
Temporal noise emerges due to variations in the sensor’s response over time, even
when lighting conditions do not change. The thresholds θON/OFF are uneven across
the sensor but typically follow a normal distribution around the threshold (McReynolds,
Graça, and Delbruck 2022). This can lead to imprecise event timing. Additionally,
the thermal noise outlined in Sec. 2.2.2 impacts temporal accuracy, as variations
affect the instant the photocurrent surpasses the threshold.
If multiple events occur in quick succession, the readout circuit may not be able
to process them all simultaneously. This can result in temporal clustering, where
events that should have distinct timestamps appear to have occurred at the same
time or very close to each other. This effect can distort the temporal resolution
of the sensor, affecting the accuracy of motion detection and other time-sensitive
applications. The bandwidth of the read-out circuit is a limiting factor. If the event
rate exceeds the readout bandwidth, some events might be dropped or delayed,
leading to loss of information and potential inaccuracies in the event stream.
Hot Pixels
Hot pixels are caused by defects in the sensor material and manifest as a constant
event stream in the data from specific pixels, with event rates close to or at 1/treft
(Xu et al. 2014).

its simplicity, and frames from this surface are often useful for examining raw data.

Abstract—This article explores the utilization of a Dynamic
Vision Sensor (DVS) in conjunction with a Star Tracking Camera
for ground-based satellite tracking. The study investigates the
noise characteristics inherent to low-light observations with
a DVS sensor, identifying conditions under which the noise
resembles an excitable medium. A novel filtering approach is
introduced that uses the apparent velocity of satellite events to
effectively differentiate them from noise. This filter is integrated
into a detection algorithm that matches or exceeds the perfor-
mance of a Star Tracker systems, as demonstrated by the Root
Mean Square Error in arc minutes from a second-order least
squares fit.
This research demonstrates the potential of DVS sensors
for enhancing satellite tracking and proposes methodologies
that contribute to advancements in space situational awareness.
The proposed filtering and tracking techniques offer promising
improvements in satellite detection and tracking accuracy, laying
the groundwork for future exploration and development in this
field.
The combination of a conventional Star Tracking Camera
for accurate pointing and a DVS sensor for satellite tracking is
proposed as a compact, low-power solution suitable for real-time
operations on small embedded systems.
I. INTRODUCTION
With the near-Earth environment becoming more crowded
due to years of space launches and an anticipated increase
in future launches [1], the need for robust Space Situational
Awareness (SSA), which involves monitoring orbiting space
objects including active and decommissioned satellites, rocket
parts from launch vehicles, and debris from collisions, has
become increasingly vital [2]. This congestion poses a sig-
nificant risk of collisions that could potentially result in loss
of critical space infrastructure and create more space debris,
further exacerbating the problem [3]. Accurate and timely
Orbit Determination (OD) ensures that satellite operators can
make informed decisions about maneuvering their assets to
avoid potential collisions, and helps in assessing the impact
of space weather on satellite operations.
Currently, OD from the ground is done primarily utilizing
active sensors such as radar and or passive sensors with optical
telescopes [1]. Recent studies have investigated the application
of Dynamic Vision Sensors (DVS) for tracking, demonstrating
their effectiveness in monitoring satellites under both daytime
and nighttime conditions [4][5][5]. DVS or Event cameras
operate on a fundamentally different principle compared to
traditional cameras. Rather than sampling static images at
predetermined intervals, DVS cameras asynchronously capture
changes in log-light intensity at the pixel level, greatly mini-
mizing data redundancy and enhancing the temporal resolution
of the collected data, resulting in less power consumption
compared to traditional CMOS systems with comparable tem-
poral resolution of 400 fps [6][7]. Additionally, DVS cameras
possess a high dynamic range inherent to their technology and
remain unaffected by the global exposure problems that affect
conventional optical sensors[8][9]. These characteristics render
them ideal for tracking applications[10][11][12][13]. Given
that DVS capture changes in log-light intensity, the pixels
exhibit a large dynamic range, enabling daytime tracking[4],
where standard CMOS sensors would be saturated. Prelimi-
nary research indicated that tracking satellites during daytime
conditions is feasible with the use of smaller optics [14].
A. State of the Art
Recent research has expanded the application of DVS
technology in satellite tracking, focusing on the development
of novel observation methodologies and the evaluation of DVS
performance against traditional imaging sensors.
The use of DVS for space situational awareness using the
feature-based detection and tracking method proposed in [15].
By implementing a cascade of increasingly selective event
filters, this approach rapidly isolates events associated with
space objects, while maintaining the high temporal resolution
of the sensors. The initial filters reduce the volume of events by
eliminating noise, whereas the more complex filters accurately
identify and track relevant events. An event surface stencil is
applied to filter events pertinent to satellite tracks for a tracking
algorithm. This method has proven to be effective in filtering,
detecting, and tracking in environments with high noise[15].
This cascading method ensures efficient processing and robust
performance even at high noise levels, demonstrating its utility
in real-world SSA applications.
Methods employing spiking neural networks[16] have
shown promise in processing asynchronous data from DVS
cameras for satellite detection. These networks operate simi-
larly to biological neurons, emitting spikes when inputs exceed
a certain threshold, making them well-suited for the sparse
data generated by DVS, reducing the event stream density
while preserving temporal information. This encoding enables
the training of deeper spiking neural network architectures,
enhancing their classification and object detection capabili-
ties on event-based datasets. This approach has demonstrated
competitive performance, achieving high accuracy on event
data benchmarks for satellite observation like N-MNIST and
DVS-CIFAR10, and showing potential in real-world satellite

Other research has investigated the OD capabilities of the

B. Article outline
This paper seeks to further explore the use of DVS cam-
eras for satellite tracking, employing a wider field of view
(FOV) with optics that are comparably small to other research
endeavors. This is done as the accuracy of initial OD relies
on the angular separation between observational points [17].
We aim to investigates the application of DVS cameras in
combination with star tracking cameras, with a similar FOV
for satellite tracking during nighttime operations, aiming to
highlight performance differences. Considering the significant
noise associated with DVS technology during nighttime obser-
vations, and the observational features associated with a wide
FOV, this article introduces a novel filter designed for event-
based data. This filter operates on an event-based principle
within the spatio-temporal domain, using the apparent velocity
of events. Other methods for tracking moving objects use
a surface-based approach for feature detection [15] [18] or
utilize spatiotemporal clustering [10]. The proposed filter,
however, does not construct an event-surface but is applied
directly to the data, preserving the high sparsity of the data.
The filter’s design involves assessing a spatio-temporal cone
around each event, assigning high values to events that are
causally connected. This filter functions similarly to the Y-
noise filter, which uses a spatio-temporal Gaussian to identify
areas of high spatio-temporal density[19]. This filter collab-
orates with clustering techniques and a Least Squares fit to
monitor the satellite’s trajectory.

a Star Camera, followed by an investigation into the noise
characteristics of DVS data in low-light night observations.
Section III outlines the satellite tracking method, which is
divided into three sections. The first section details the filters
used and introduces the Novel Event Velocity filter. The sec-
ond section explains the technique for detecting the presence
of a satellite in the data. The third section demonstrates the
tracking method.

1) Scope
The scope of this article is restricted to tracking only and
does not attempt orbit determination. It suggests potential
methods to combine the pointing accuracy of the star camera
and the DVS. Orbit determination would require precise lens
calibration between the two sensors, which is beyond the
scope of this article. Furthermore, the article solely highlights
the performance of the proposed method without comparing
it with other state-of-the-art satellite tracking methods. This
article also focuses exclusively on nighttime observations, as
comparisons with the star camera are only feasible during
nighttime, and the equipment used could not reliably detect
light from satellites in a daylight setting.

1) Noise
There are various types of noise that can cause false events
to occur, but under low light conditions, such as nighttime
observations, the predominant noise is Front end noise. This
noise originates from various sources like dark current and
shot noise. The event rate of noise depends on the background
illumination and the temperature of the DVS [21][22][9].
Another different prevalent form of noise observed in DVS
is hotpixel noise. This type of noise originates from specific
pixels that exhibit abnormally high levels of activity compared
to their neighbors.
The noise observed, especially during nighttime observa-
tions at relatively high threshold settings θON/OF F = 9% and

III. METHOD
In this section, an in-depth explanation of the proposed
method is provided. The suggested approach for detecting and
tracking satellites is structured as a cascading algorithm. As
the event rate of each step decreases, each step also reduces
the data density. At the highest level, the algorithm is based on
a series of filters followed by a detection loop and a tracking
loop. Filters are utilized to reduce event noise. As the tracking
loop is computationally intensive, the detection loop is used to
decide whether the tracking loop should be applied to a given
batch of events. A high-level representation of the filters and
the detection method is depicted in Fig. 4 and the tracking
algorithm in Fig. 7.
A. Filters
The filters utilized comprise a Hotpixel rejector CITE[] and
a background activity filter [20]. For the final filtering, the
article introduces a novel surface-free event-based velocity
filter, which is similar to the Y-noise event density filter[19].

B. Event Velocity filter (EVF)
The BA filter allows some noise to pass through when event
rates are exceedingly high, and given that the noise patterns
during night-time observations become spatio-temporal cor-
related, this article introduces a novel Event Velocity Filter
(EVF). This filter is made to enhance events with a specific
apparent velocity. This filter is constructed upon the principle
of spatio-temporal correlation that is inherent to the BA filter.
However, rather than simply checking for neighboring events,
this filter performs a sparse spatio-temporal convolution with
every event within the spatio-temporal region of each event
akin to the Y-noise filter [19]. The EVF operates in an event-
based principle within the spatio-temporal domain. This filter,
however, does not construct an event-time surface, but is
applied directly to the data, preserving the high sparsity of the
data. The filter function EVF(t,r) depends on the pairwise time
and radial distance between each event. The filter operates like
a convolution of all events relative to the event being evaluated.

D. Tracking
The flow sequence of the tracking algorithm is illustrated
in Fig. 7. The tracking framework employs three main parts:
the Density-Based clustering algorithm the Density-Based
clustering algorithm (DBSCAN) [27][28] for initializing the
track, and least squares fitting for event assertion, in addition
to using potential for identifying stale tracks.
1) Inital track
If the detection algorithm identifies a satellite and there
are no existing satellites being tracked, DBSCAN Clustering
algorithm run on the events from the EVF filter using the
distance matrix calculated as per Eq. (8) that is outputted by
the EVF. The Clustering is done solely on spatial data, both to
reduce computational demands and to avoid the complexities
that arise when adjusting temporal separation for effective
clustering. DBSCAN includes two principal parameters: eps,
representing the maximum distance for events to be linked,
and min samples, which specifies the minimum number of
events within the eps distance necessary to form a core point.
The algorithm is used to find clusters that belong a satellite, as
these events are spatially dense compared to noise that might
pass the filters. If a cluster is found by DBSCAN, events within
that cluster are added to a list of events with a track id like
e(t, x, y, p, id) of the track to which the events belong.

F. Output Signal and comparison with Star Camera
In this section, we present the signal obtained by ap-
plying the complete cascading algorithms to the acquired
observations. When examining the isolated event stream from
the satellite, several emerging properties are observed. By
analyzing event polarities accumulated within a 100-ms tem-
poral window, we notice that the satellite has a core point
where approximately 5 to 10 events are triggered within this
timeframe. Surrounding this core point, a blooming effect can
be observed, causing adjacent pixels to trigger events as well.
By accumulating the event polarities onto a frame that
moves with the satellite over the entire satellite track, the over-
all event signal structure for a point-like source is established.
This signal is depicted in Fig. 15. It can be seen that the
satellite produces a shockwave-like pattern of ON events, with
the bow shock likely created by the blooming from the core
pixel at a speed exceeding that of the satellite. OFF events
trail has its core point a few pixels behind.
The blooming effect might result from electrical interactions
between nearby pixels, which can further exacerbate blooming.
A high charge in one pixel can induce a voltage in adjacent
pixels, leading to the creation of false events.
Examining the distributions of distances from the core pixel
to satellite events, which are generated by the spatial distance
error from the second-order LS fit to events, we observe the
distribution of three different tracks in Fig. 16. It is evident
that the spread of the ON events follows a normal distribution,
which aligns well with the intensity distribution of standard
CMOS sensors [29]. For OFF events, the spread is generally
similar to that of ON events; however, due to the lower event
count for OFF events, the peak is significantly smaller, making
it unsuitable for tracking purposes.
At low event rates, the distribution of OFF errors is almost
uniform and biased from zero, which further degrades the
accuracy of tracking using LS fit. This issue does not occur
for ON events.

A. Performance
1) Filter Performance
The Event Velocity Filter proposed in this article demon-
strates effective performance in enhancing events with a spe-
cific velocity, even when the signal strength is low relative to
the noise. The filter excels in filtering out noise that exhibits
spatio-temporal structures similar to cellular automata, where
waves propagate with event velocities exceeding those of the
satellite. In contrast, filters such as Ynoise [7] and BA filters
struggle to filter out these waves, as they only consider spatio-
temporal density, allowing any spatiotemporal structure in the
data to pass through.
It remains unclear whether cellular automata noise is unique
to the DVS camera used or if this noise behavior has been
observed in other DVS systems. It is believed that a combi-
nation of cross-talk between pixels and thermal noise at low
DC illumination might sustain and propagate these waves.
The filter operates in real-time, leveraging parallelization to
significantly enhance throughput at high event rates. Given the
overhead of executing the algorithm for each new event, which
leads to unmanageable latency and a notable reduction in arith-
metic intensity, this method is justified. However, employing
batched computing for the filter negates the asynchronous
advantage of event data when the data stream is batched
using a temporal slice. This batching introduces latency for
the oldest event in the slice. Many of these trade-offs could
be mitigated by dynamically switching between sequential
asynchronous computation on the CPU at low event rates
and batched parallelized computation at high event rates to
maintain real-time performance. Considering the trend towards
heterogeneous processor designs and the use of dark silicon
to enhance performance in embedded systems, dynamically
switching between operational modes based on event rates
could be advantageous for embedded systems.
The EVF filter output introduces an additional dimension to
the event data, proportional to the number of events supporting
a specific velocity within the sensor plane. This can be applied
to other tasks, as demonstrated in the article in which the out-
put value is utilized for detection. The filter has the potential
to enhance performance in machine learning applications, as
the output values could assist neural networks in recognizing
spatio-temporal patterns more effectively.
Comparing the two proposed filter shapes, the conical filter
and the bottle-shaped filter, reveals a complex tuning space.
The conical filter requires tuning of the temporal window,
temporal decay, and maximum velocity, while the bottle-
shaped filter adds parameters for spatial decay and bandwidth
of velocities. This complexity can be managed by noting
that the conical filter is simpler to design and requires only
the tuning of velocity and temporal influence. Introducing
a minimum velocity might further refine the conical filter,
though it was deemed unnecessary due to the slow movement
of satellites within a field of view (FOV) of approximately
16 degrees. The bottle-shaped filter, while more complex, can
better enhance the satellite tracks when tuned correctly.
2) Detection
The detection method, while fairly effective, lacks robust-
ness against non-satellite objects such as airplanes, birds,
insects, or other particles in the air. Detection occurs if
the velocity is sufficiently low to be enhanced. However,
the wavelet-based detection method was found to effectively
reduce most random noise, with the Daubechies 2 wavelet
proving to be optimal for signal detection, particularly in
cases of weak signals. Detection methods using Spiking Neural
Networks have shown promising results and are likely to be
more robust.
At present, a fixed threshold is used for detection, designed
to reach a particular false alarm rate in relation to the true
detection rate. This is largely influenced by the anticipated
signal strength and noise level. Notable enhancements in the
true detection rate could be realized by applying a fixed
threshold to the wavelet coefficients. Currently, the coefficients
are thresholded according to their relative value to the highest
wavelet coefficient, which permits noise to create signals
even when a true signal is absent, though it diminishes the
magnitude.
3) Tracking
The tracking algorithm described in the article performs ex-
cellently in identifying events associated with satellite passes.
Utilizing second-order polynomials has proven to be a straight-
forward yet effective method for satellite tracking. Although
Kalman filter-based tracking methods were considered, they
were deemed unnecessary since the satellite’s trajectory is not
expected to change abruptly, and tracking does not require de-
termining the satellite’s state. Additionally, the Kalman filter,

4) Full Stack Performance
When examining the complete algorithm, encompassing all
filters, detection, and tracking, the results indicate that the
method is suitable for real-time tracking purposes on the
hardware used. When tracking is inactive, typically when
filtering and performing detections on a 100ms temporal slice
with a 200ms temporal window on real data, the overhead
percentage (the percentage of slice time used for computation)
is approximately 5%. When one to two additional tracks are
active, about 10% of the time is spent on computation on
the system used. Only the initial clustering is computationally
intensive, and if the event rate is too high after filtering,
causing DBSCAN to process too many events, it can result in
significant lag. This can be mitigated by randomly sampling
the events passed by the filter if the event rate is too high, or by
not running DBSCAN in situations where the event temporal
density is too high.
The DBSCAN algorithm is recognized as a significant
bottleneck in the system due to its computational intensity. In
scenarios where the signal originates from non-point sources,
such as birds or airplanes, the clustering process can con-
siderably slow down. Generally, the full stack demonstrates
effective tracking capabilities. With advancements in optics
and potential improvements in DVS technology, which would
provide better signal-to-noise ratios, tracking performance is
expected to improve further. However, with current technology,
the system has proven effective in isolating and tracking point-
like sources in high-noise data.
B. DVS-based Tracking
Event-based tracking at night, compared to star trackers,
shows great potential. Although nighttime tracking introduces
a significant amount of noise in the DVS compared to the Star
Camera, the improved temporal accuracy and resolution could
enable substantial improvements in Orbit Determination (OD)
capabilities. The analyzes conducted in this article indicate
that the RMSE in arcminutes for the DVS sensor was lower
when applying a second-order polynomial fit, suggesting that
it might be more appropriate for tracking subpixel satellites.
However, since this is a comparison of the output of tracking

algorithms, it cannot be definitively concluded that DVS tech-

However, comparing the output of the tracking algorithm for
both the star tracker and the DVS shows that the tracking

can utilize three or more observations. As the blooming of
events is well-defined by a Gaussian distribution, the DVS
track, when fitted to a second-order polynomial, can achieve
subpixel accuracy with high temporal resolution. Combining a
conventional Star Tracking Camera for accurate determination
of the pointing vector with a DVS sensor for satellite tracking
leverages the strengths of each sensor. Given their small size
and low power consumption, the combination of these sensors
offers an efficient solution for satellite detection and tracking.
This integration is sufficiently compact and could be deployed
on space-based platforms for SSA and can be executed in real-
time on small embedded systems, addressing one of the main
challenges of space-based SSA [31].
VI. CONCLUSION
This study further investigated the application of a Dynamic
Vision Sensor (DVS) for ground-based satellite tracking to
estimate orbital parameters and comperes it with a Star Track-
ing Camera. We examined the noise characteristics typical of
low-light observations using a DVS sensor and found that,
under certain configurations, the noise could be described as
an excitable medium. While the underlying mechanisms of
this behavior require further investigation, there appears to be
a correlation between wavelength and threshold settings.
Given these noise characteristics, we proposed a novel filter
that effectively distinguishes satellite events from noise based
on their apparent velocity. This filter was shown to be effective
in detection algorithms, with the tracking algorithm achieving
equal or better performance compared to the Star Tracker, as
evidenced by RMSE in arcminutes from a second-order least
squares fit.
The study faced limitations due to the lack of calibration
between the star camera and DVS sensor, preventing accurate
determination of the DVS pointing vector and subsequent orbit
determination comparisons.
In summary, this study has shown the potential of DVS
sensors in satellite tracking and identified areas for further
research and enhancement. The proposed filtering and tracking
methods present a promising approach to improving satellite
detection and tracking precision in a small embedded real-
time system. Additionally, the filter could be valuable in
other DVS applications where velocity-based filtering would
be advantageous.
Future work should focus on addressing these calibration
issues and exploring the underlying noise mechanisms in
greater detail.
Additionally, combining a conventional Star Tracking Cam-
era for accurate pointing vector determination with a DVS
sensor for satellite tracking leverages the strengths of each
sensor. This combination could provide a compact, low-power
solution for satellite detection and tracking, suitable for real-
time operation on small embedded systems.

Detection is performed using a threshold on the projected EVF output values along
the x and y axes. Given that projecting the filter values can introduce minor spikes
that are purely noise, and that genuine signals create localized peaks in space, var-
ious noise reduction methods could be applied. However, considering the signal’s
spatial localization, employing an FFT and bandpassing the signal would be inap-
propriate. Therefore, a wavelet transform is advantageous for balancing time/space
and frequency localization. We observe that when applying a DWT and examining
the coeﬀicients at different levels, the signal exhibits significant values at Level 1
(low pass filtering) and Level 4 (high pass filtering), while the noise predominantly
resides at the intermediate levels, as illustrated in Fig. A.2

B.1
Shape of the filter
As discussed, EVFs exhibit different shapes based on velocity. Figure B.1 illustrates
the three possible configurations for the B-EVF. Here, we display a 100ms slice
under evaluation within a 200ms window, resulting in a 100ms buffer. From this,
it is evident that very low velocities perform optimally, as the window in use is not
longer than the buffer. Higher velocities, on the other hand, lead to a more uniform
distribution of output values.
However, this is highly dependent on the field of view (FOV), since a smaller
FOV will make the satellite’s apparent velocity seem higher. The velocity used can
theoretically be set based on predicted pixel velocities derived from scene analysis,
but the other parameters need to be tuned. This is also the benefit of the C-EVF, as
this filter only requires tuning of the decay parameter, given the maximum velocity
can be derived from scene analysis.

C.1
Initial Clustering with DBSCAN
For adjusting the parameters of the DBSCAN algorithm, an accumulation surface
is constructed from the output of the EVF filter over a 100ms temporal slice. This
accumulation surface is depicted in Fig. C.1. The core point has approximately
10 ON events in that duration, while the surrounding pixels have about 8 to 6.
Consequently, setting the minimum sample to form a core point to at least 10 is
suitable, and setting an epsilon distance of 2 ensures that only nearby points are
included. This configuration also helps to exclude surrounding noise as shown in
Fig. C.2.
Figure C.1: Shows the accumulated ON events after the EVF filtering of
satellite within the time window of 100ms
Ensuring a minimum sample size of 14 guarantees the reliability of the initial
cluster trajectory estimate. If the initial estimate includes too few events, the subse-
quent tracking will be inaccurate. This, however, presents a trade-off, since setting
the minimum sample size too high results in the initial track not being formed at
all.
