PROBLEM FORMULATION
Problem Formulation
Silicone elastomers are gaining more and more importance in industry for a wide range of
applications. Addition curing elastomers are by far the easiest to formulate to speciﬁc uses
if species inhibiting the Pt catalyst is not present. However, for many industrial applica-
tions, the Pt catalyst is too expensive and a catalyst less sensitive to poisoning is required.
Therefore, condensation curing chemistry with tin as catalyst is preferred. However, the
condensation curing scheme requires a certain level of humidity, and the reactions are
multiple and more complex than for the addition curing system. This project deals with
clariﬁcation of various parameters’ inﬂuence on the curing of condensation-curing silicone
elastomers, namely crosslinker to polymer ratio, type of crosslinker, catalyst concentra-
tion, and/or water concentration, depending on what is found to have the largest inﬂuence.
The gelation of the elastomer will be followed by viscosity measurements, and the data will
be coupled to stochastic models describing the network formation. Furthermore, various
spectroscopic methods will be investigated with respect to providing useful information
on extent of curing to give predictions less sensitive to modelling.
After the gelation threshold, the structural development can no longer be followed by
viscosity measurements, and therefore linear viscoelastic analysis will be utilized. The
models and spectroscopic methods utilized above will be used as well.

ABSTRACT
Abstract
This project aimed to experimentally determine and model the reaction kinetics for a con-
densation curing silicone coating. The understanding of the reaction kinetics is of great
interest as it enables the possibility to understand and control the reactions involved in
the condensation curing process. Silicone elastomers are used in a wide range of diﬀerent
applications, in various industries. Therefore it is of high importance, to understand and
be able to control the curing reactions.
Prior to the experimental work, PDMS networks and a wide range of curing mecha-
nisms were investigated through a literature study. The literature study also includes an
overview of methods to experimentally determine and model reaction kinetics for silicone
elastomers.
The possibility to follow the curing behavior of condensation curing PDMS networks with
the use of rheology was proven. Two crosslinkers with diﬀerent molecular weight were in-
vestigated, and for both of them, the stoichiometric ratio between polymer and crosslinker
is varied. By rheology, it was possible to determine the gelation point for all of the sys-
tems investigated. It was found that the time to reach the gelation point decreases with
the increase in stoichiometric ratio for both of the crosslinkers. Besides, it was found that
increasing the stoichiometric ratio leads to faster reaction kinetics. Furthermore, it was
discovered that an intramolecular reaction is occurring.
The intramolecular reaction could stem from the hydrolysis of the crosslinker functional
groups. FT-IR was used to show that a signiﬁcant amount of the functional groups are
present past the gel point. As a result of this, it was proven that not all functional groups
are hydrolyzed initially as suggested in the literature. However, the two competing reac-
tions increase the complexity of the condensation curing mechanism.
By the use of the experimental results from rheology, the possibility to determine the
curing degree and model reaction kinetics for the condensation curing system was proven.
It was shown that the curing rate increases as the stoichiometric ratio are increased. Fur-
thermore, the curing behavior changes signiﬁcantly as the stoichiometric ratio is increased.
This leads to signiﬁcantly diﬀerent kinetic parameters for the higher ratios, showing the
increased inﬂuence of the intramolecular reaction.

PREFACE
Preface
This thesis is prepared by Kristoﬀer Rønsbro as the assignment in connection with the
Master thesis. The thesis deals with experimental determination and modelling of reac-
tion kinetics for condensation curing silicone coatings. The thesis is therefore initiated

determine reaction kinetics for silicone elastomers. This is followed by experimental work
in the testing of reaction kinetics with the use of some of the methods from the literature

model the reaction kinetics.

INTRODUCTION
Introduction
The ﬁrst use of silicones is reported in the early 1940s and even though the material did
not gain much focus and potential at that time, silicones have been on a long journey
to the material we know today.[1] The use of silicones ﬁnds a wide range of applications
in our daily life, which include usage in a wide variety of ﬁelds such as, transportation,
construction, electronics, energy, health care, industrial processes, and personal care and
consumer products. The large range of applications resulted in a sale of more than 2 mil-
lion metric tons of formulated silicones in 2013. This corresponds to the manufacturing
of silicones being an EUR 10 billion industry on a global scale.[2]
Despite the great development, silicones are sill on its long journey, both in improving the
usage in already existing applications but also ﬁnding new applications. In 2013, more
than EUR 10 million were spent on research and development globally, which corresponds
to 4% of the turnover.[2] This taken into account along with a large number of scientiﬁc
papers and journals covering the development of silicones clearly shows the large and
growing interest for optimizing and expanding the use of silicones, but also the need to
continue the research within this area.
The preparation of silicone elastomers can be divided into several categories in which one
of them being condensation curing, also known as room temperature vulcanization (RTV).
Over the years, the chemistry of the condensation curing processes and the properties of
the formed silicone elastomers have been extensively studied. However, publications de-
scribing methods to follow the curing process and the results hereof are lacking. This
does not only account for condensation curing, but for curing processes in general.
The performance of silicone elastomers is to a large extent determined by the structure of
the crosslinked network being formed during the curing process and the residual chemical
groups in the bulk material and interfaces. Therefore, it is important and of great interest
to understand and as a result of this be able to control the reactions involved in the cur-
ing process. For this purpose, especially following the rheological and thermal behavior
for the vulcanization process have been stated as interesting phenomena to investigate
further.[3–5]
The scope of this project is to identify and utilize various methods to follow the curing
process of a condensation curing system and utilize the results hereof in the determination
of reaction kinetics. This will lead to a greater understanding of the reactions involved
in the curing process, allowing for better control. Lastly, it will be investigated if it is
possible to utilize a model in describing the results obtained from the experiments, and
thus expanding the understanding of the processes to a larger extent.

CHAPTER 1. LITERATURE STUDY
Chapter 1
Literature study
In this chapter, the literature study for this thesis is presented. This includes a presen-
tation of polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) networks in terms of properties and applications.
Furthermore, this chapter includes a description of diﬀerent curing techniques, their mech-
anisms, and their pros and cons. This is followed by an investigation of diﬀerent techniques
for experimental determination of reaction kinetics for PDMS networks. Lastly, diﬀerent
models that can be used for modelling of reaction kinetics for PDMS networks will be
presented.
1.1
PDMS networks
Polymer networks are three-dimensional structures that are composed of polymer chains
linked to each other by covalent bonds. The covalent bonds are formed in the crosslinking
reaction between polymer and crosslinker. The reaction is also called curing and is most
often an end-linking process, where the crosslinker has a certain functionality f and the
linear polymer has functional groups in both ends. The network that is formed, consists
of three diﬀerent structures, namely, (1) the elastically active network chains, (2) the dan-
gling chains and substructures, and (3) the sol fraction which is not chemically attached
to the network, but is acting as a solvent. The three diﬀerent structures are illustrated in
Figure 1.1. In the network, the dangling chains and the sol fractions are contributing to
the softness which gives better extensibility, but at the same time lower the mechanical
break down strength.[6] Polymer networks are possible to be created with an extensive
amount of diﬀerent polymers and crosslinkers. In this thesis polymer networks formed
with the use of silicone as the polymer is of main interest. Speciﬁcally, networks formed
with the use of PDMS is in focus. Therefore primarily these networks will be investigated
in this literature study.

In the formation of polymer networks, diﬀerent kinds of ﬁllers and particles can be used in
addition to the basic components; polymer and crosslinker. Even though unﬁlled PDMS
networks possess good ﬂexibility they often suﬀer from weak mechanical properties such
as low tear strength. This shortage can aﬀect that especially thin ﬁlms as coatings are
not easily handled and the lack of mechanical properties causes the elastomer being un-
able to meet the practical requirements of commercial applications. Therefore ﬁllers and
particles are used to enhance the mechanical properties.[7,8] However, the ﬁnal mechanical
properties are not the scope of this project and thus it will not gain much focus in the
experimental part of this thesis.
Polymer networks formed from crosslinked PDMS are known as silicone elastomers or
silicone rubber. PDMS elastomers have obtained a major interest both from researchers
and companies because of its unique properties.
This includes elastic behavior, low-
temperature ﬂexibility, mechanical-, chemical-, long term- and temperature stability which
usually ranges from -100°C to 200°, just to name some of them. Because of these useful
properties, PDMS elastomers ﬁnd a broad range of applications that have been widely
investigated over the years.[4,5,9–12]
The wide variety of applications includes gasket seals and adhesives, electrical and optical
devices, medical implant, dielectric elastomers (DEs), electronics encapsulation and pro-
tective coatings. PDMS elastomers are therefore used in industries such as construction,
aerospace, energy, health care, and transportation. The application of silicone elastomers
in the diﬀerent areas is further described in Figure 1.2. Recently especially the application
of silicones as DEs has been investigated extensively as silicones are one of the most used
materials for DEs.[11] Another area that has gained much focus is the use of silicones in
the form of coatings and thin ﬁlms. In this area, silicones ﬁnd numerous applications
that include both coatings for spacecrafts, stents, and catheters. One of the reasons why
silicones are applied in this ﬁeld is the mentioned unique physical and chemical properties
that are present both in the bulk form and at interfaces. Especially the inertness against
chemical attack is the reason to silicone networks potential as protective coatings.[13,14]
1.1.1
Protective silicone coatings
Silicone protective coatings are normally applied as multi-layered ﬁlms on large surface
area metal substrates. The general purpose of using protective coatings are mostly to
protect the surface from environmental inﬂuences, covering areas such as anticorrosion,
anti-biofouling, anti-icing, ﬂame-resistance, self-cleaning, and anti-reﬂection. In general,
a coating is developed to be used for one of the purposes, but a need for dual-functional
materials have yielded the development of a few examples of anticorrosive coatings which
also possess anti-biofouling properties.[14]
Some of the properties that makes PDMS the most prominent material for coatings,
include some of the earlier stated but is in general, the low surface energy, mechanical and
thermal stability, excellent lubricating eﬀect and dielectric properties, biocompatibility
and environmentally friendly characteristics, high ﬂexibility and reliability over a wide
range of temperatures and humidity, and high resistance to chemical and UV attack.[14]

1.2
Curing of PDMS networks
In this section, the basic concepts of diﬀerent curing possibilities will be presented and
some of the recent work is brieﬂy described. In the end, a more detailed description of
the curing mechanism for this study is presented.
To prepare a PDMS network, typically three diﬀerent curing routes are possible to utilize:
addition curing, condensation curing, or radical curing. All three methods of curing suﬀer
from some deﬁciencies but also has some advantages compared to the others. Deﬁciencies
include that both addition and radical curing needs energy to initiate the reaction in terms
of heat or UV light, respectively. Therefore, these curing routes are often performed at
higher temperatures. Furthermore, addition curing uses an expensive metal, platinum, as
the catalyst. In contrast condensation curing needs air humidity to initiate the reaction,
thus it is possible to perform the curing at room temperature, but the method suﬀers
from slow reaction kinetics and insuﬃcient mechanical properties. Common for all three
curing routes is that two liquid components are needed, A and B, the polymer and the
crosslinker, respectively. The reaction between A and B gives a solid product, the polymer
network, as is illustrated in Scheme 1.1.[9,15,16]

curing routes. That includes the use of additional components such as ﬁllers and addi-
tives, but also the use of other curing routes.[17] This will be elaborated later in this section.

Using the addition reaction as the curing mechanism, gives several advantages, including
a more stable material at elevated temperatures and fast curing. But this mechanism
also has disadvantages as presented earlier, including the use of the expensive platinum
catalyst and the need for energy, most often in terms of elevated temperature, to initiate
the reaction. Anyhow, the addition curing mechanism has been commonly used both
commercially and in research.[5,9]
It has been reported that the addition curing proceeds in three stages[18]: 1) Induction pe-
riod; a slow reaction regime in which the active species of the catalyst is formed, 2) a rapid
exothermic regime in which most of the products are formed, and 3) the post-curing stage;
a regime with very slow or no reaction. The three diﬀerent reaction periods are illustrated
in Figure 1.3. Furthermore, it has been reported that the reactions in all stages and the
products formed are very sensitive to the reaction conditions. This includes both internal
parameters such as concentration and nature of the polymer, crosslinker, and catalyst,
but also external parameters such as temperature and ﬁlm thickness.[4,5,13] Esteves et.

the temperature is increased. This was conﬁrmed by Andersson et. al.[4] who showed that
the time to complete the curing is lowered when the temperature is increased. Thus, the
reaction kinetics are strongly dependent on the temperature for addition curing. Simpson
et. al.[13] investigated the inﬂuence of ﬁlm thickness on the reaction kinetics. They found
that the ﬁrst-order reaction constant was decreasing with increasing ﬁlm thickness. Thus
the reaction kinetics is directly dependent on the ﬁlm thickness. The studies introduced
above, which investigated the reaction kinetics are further described in section 1.3.

For addition curing, a range of studies has been made to investigate the mechanical prop-
erties. One study was performed by Zakaria et. al.[11] who showed that PDMS elastomers
require post-curing to obtain mechanical and electrical stability. This post-curing process
is performed after the normal curing process and most often by increasing the tempera-
ture signiﬁcantly. This will lead to the evaporation of volatiles and other residues, thus
leading to a stable network.
The possibility to obtain softer PDMS elastomers have also been investigated. This was
performed as it remains a challenge to develop soft PDMS elastomers, without the use of
solvent and with controllable viscoelastic properties through a simple approach. The soft
PDMS elastomers were successfully prepared by the use of bottlebrush rather than lin-
ear polymers. The use of bottlebrush polymers prevents the formation of entanglements
during crosslinking, thus giving a soft polymer.[19] The elastic moduli obtained by this
procedure was in the range of ca. 1 to 100 kPa, which is much softer than typical PDMS
elastomers. Furthermore, it was shown that it is possible to tune the elastic moduli in
the range from 1 to 100 kPa by varying the density of crosslinks. The density was varied
by adjusting the number of crosslinking chains, thus the ratio between crosslinker and
polymer.[19]
In addition to that, Madsen et al.[7] investigated the inﬂuence of bimodal networks on
mechanical properties. The bimodal networks are prepared by the use of very short and
relatively long chemically identical polymer chains that are crosslinked into a network.

This gives networks which have mechanical properties that are superior to traditional

1.2.2
Condensation curing
Condensation curing is named from the fact that humidity is needed for the curing to
proceed. This is because water acts as an active part in the reaction mechanism. The
crosslinking reactions in condensation curing are catalyzed by organotin or organotitanic
compounds but it has been shown that the actual catalyst is an organohydroxide which
is formed in the presence of a water molecule. Therefore humidity is essential for the
crosslinking reactions, and thus also for the network formation.[20,21]
In condensation curing of PDMS networks, the polymer is always of the art OH termi-
nated PDMS. One of the polymers that are used often is α, ω-dihydroxy PDMS. However,
a lot of other PDMS polymers can also be used. For the multifunctional crosslinker, it
is possible to use a range of diﬀerent components. But the crosslinker that is used for
condensation curing must be an alkoxysilane. This means that the functional group is
methoxy or ethoxy etc. The reaction between OH terminated PDMS and alkoxy termi-
nated crosslinker will form the three-dimensional network as illustrated in the general re-
action Scheme 1.3. This scheme is a simpliﬁed overview of the actual curing, as it proceeds
through complicated multistage reactions, which include both the polymer, crosslinker,
catalyst, and humidity.[3,4,21]

In short, some of the research performed over the years include; Weij[20] who investi-

of the tin catalyst above 1 wt% which promotes signiﬁcant chain extension in the ab-

was very little change in the viscosity, thus no signiﬁcant chain extension. Robinson et
al.[10] continued the investigation of crosslinker and catalyst concentration on compression
set. An optimum ratio between ethoxy groups from TEOS and OH groups from PDMS
(OEt/OH) was found to be between 3 and 4. In general, increasing the crosslinker con-
centration above stoichiometry increased the compression set resistance due to increasing
network crosslink density and generation of silica in-situ. Subsequently it was found that
increasing catalyst concentration results in enhanced compression set due to the phenom-
ena as the PDMS chain extension described by He et al. Simultaneously it was shown
that lower catalyst loadings (i.e. < 1.5%) gave networks with lower soluble fractions and
indicates that an improved network formation is obtained with the reduced catalyst con-
centration.

1.2.3
Other curing possibilities
Aside from the commonly used curing mechanisms described above, several studies have
been performed in the investigation of additional curing methods due to various reasons,
such as obtaining combined properties and avoiding expensive and/or poisoning catalysts.
The possibility to obtain combined properties was investigated by Ramli et al.[9] by the
incorporation of both addition and condensation curing into one mechanism. This was
done to yield an elastomeric network with synergistic thermal and mechanical properties.
PDMS elastomers were successfully prepared at room temperature and showed signiﬁcant
improvement in both hardness and thermal properties compared to a control sample.
This contribution comes primarily from the increased crosslink density and the combined
thermal properties from the addition and condensation system.
The use of a simple and eﬃcient catalyst-free system was investigated by Gonzaga et
al.[15] A PDMS elastomer were prepared via uncatalyzed 1,3-dipolar cycloadditions, also
known as metal-free click chemistry. The curing process is possible to perform at various
conditions, for instance in an organic solvent, both at room temperature or at elevated
temperatures depending on what is wanted. This simple process obviates all the prob-
lems associated with the use of expensive and/or potentially toxic catalysts and high-
temperature requirements. Thus resulting in a simple, eﬀective and cheap curing process.
Another possibility to obtain a catalyst-free system was investigated by Yin et al.[21]
Aminomethyl triethoxysilanes were used as the crosslinker, which simultaneously can act
as a catalyst to self-catalyze the system. In this way, a PDMS elastomer was obtained
without the use of platinum or tin catalyst. Furthermore, they investigated the inﬂuence
of various parameters and found that moderate temperature (25-50°C) and room humidity
were the best ﬁt for this curing mechanism. It was also found that with the increase in

1.2.4
Condensation curing mechanism
From the above sections, it is clear that it is possible to create PDMS elastomers from a
wide range of curing mechanisms. However, this thesis focus on the use of condensation
curing due to the possibility of performing the curing at room temperature. This is a big
advantage for silicone curings as they are often applied to big surface areas as described in
section 1.1.1. This section presents a description of the curing mechanism of condensation
curing which was proposed by Weij[20] and later has been widely accepted.[10,21,27]
In the study by Weij, α, ω-diol PDMS was used as polymer and TEOS was used as
polyfunctional alkoxysilane crosslinker.
The mechanism proposed by Weij is initiated
with the hydrolysis of the catalyst as it is found that the actual catalyst of the reaction is
the organotin hydroxide which is formed from the hydrolysis of the initial tin compound.
Thus it is possible to use a range of diﬀerent tin compounds, in the form of dialkyl
tin dicarboxylates, as catalysts, as they will form the organotin hydroxide when being
exposed to humidity. The formed organotin hydroxide is consumed in a reaction with the
crosslinking compound containing an alkoxy group, forming a compound that contains
the Sn-O-Si linkage. In this reaction, a byproduct is formed. The form of the byproduct
depends on the alkoxy group on the crosslinking compound. For instance, if the alkoxy
group is an ethoxy group, ethanol is formed. The active component which is formed can
react with the hydroxy-functional PDMS to create the Si-O-Si linkage and the organotin
hydroxide. After this reaction, the formed compound is prone to further reaction with
the organotin hydroxide, whenever accessible alkoxy groups are present.
In this way,
the crosslinked network is being formed.
The above described reactions are the ones
representing the condensation curing. In general, it can be seen as the use of an alkoxy
terminated crosslinker, OH terminated polymer and tin catalyst results in a compilation of

1.3
Experimental determination of reaction kinetics
In this section, the experimental methods which can be used to determine reaction ki-
netics of the curing process will be presented. For the investigation and monitoring of
the crosslinking reaction, various methods exist such as Fourier-Transform Infrared Spec-
troscopy (FT-IR), Diﬀerential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC), Rheology, Size Exclusion
Chromatography (SEC), and Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR). The actual principle
of the methods will not be presented in detail, as the focus is how the results from the ex-
periments can be used to follow the curing reaction and determine reaction kinetics. This
will include a presentation of earlier studies in which these methods have been utilized to
determine reaction kinetics.
The curing process, which includes reaction kinetics, the extent of reaction, etc. is im-
portant to understand as it will have a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the chemical, physical, and
mechanical properties of the PDMS elastomer. Thus it is of high importance to know
the optimum temperatures, curing times, ratios, etc. to achieve the desired properties.
Furthermore, it is of high interest to understand the curing kinetics and the mechanisms
of the curing to control the curing process. More speciﬁcally for silicone coatings, espe-
cially the extent of reaction has a strong eﬀect on the release and adhesion properties.
But in general, the rate of a crosslinking reaction is a complex function of the catalyst,
inhibitor, polymer and crosslinker nature and concentration. This displays the need for
investigating the curing process for each polymer network individually.[13,16]
Even though a wide range of methods exists and the importance of understanding the
crosslinking kinetics are obvious, very few publications describe the kinetics in silicone
coatings, in the open literature. Furthermore, most often the methods that have been
tested are primarily restricted to measuring bulk samples rather than thin coatings. One
explanation for this could be the diﬃculty of monitoring a physical or chemical property of
the coating during the curing reaction. Therefore most often a bulk sample has been used
in the identiﬁcation of curing kinetics. However, the kinetics of the crosslinking reaction
can vary from the bulk behavior, primarily due to four physicochemical properties: (1)
surface segregation, (2) altered molecular mobility, (3) high surface area to volume ratio,
and (4) chemical reactions with a substrate.[13] Despite these factors, the most prominent
methods for investigating the curing kinetics exist for bulk samples.
Therefore these
methods will be described and used in this thesis taking the variations into consideration.
1.3.1
Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy
One method that can be used to experimentally follow a curing reaction is FT-IR spec-
troscopy. Ramli et al.[9] reported the use of FT-IR for a condensation curing system. The
method was based on the following two speciﬁc peaks during the cross-linking reaction.
As described in section 1.2.4, the condensation curing includes the conversion of methoxy
groups. This phenomenon was utilized by following the disappearance of the methoxy
peaks at 2840 and 1190 cm−1, respectively. In this study, the disappearance of the peaks
was successfully observed which proved the formation of the crosslinked network.
The curing process of a condensation curing system was also studied by Yin et al.[21].
However, the peaks used to study the reaction in this case were diﬀerent compared to
Page 11 of 87
==PAGE:29==
CHAPTER 1. LITERATURE STUDY
Ramli et al. In this case, the Si-OH band at 3690 cm−1 from the OH terminated PDMS
was used to follow the reaction. It was observed that this peak disappears, conﬁrming the
crosslinking reaction. Furthermore, it was observed that the Si-O-Si bands at 1130-1100
cm−1 increased signiﬁcantly. These observations suggest that the Si-OH groups on the
PDMS take part in the crosslinking reaction to form Si-O-Si bonds. In addition, they ob-
served that the time it takes for the Si-OH band to disappear corresponds to the results
obtained from an extraction experiment.
The use of both attenuated total reﬂectance FT-IR (ATR-FTIR) and FT-IR has also
been investigated for an addition curing system. The use of FT-IR for an addition cur-
ing system has some similarities and diﬀerences to the use for condensation curing. The
diﬀerences stem from the fact that the functional groups of the crosslinker are diﬀerent
for the two curing methods. Therefore diﬀerent IR bands are used to monitor the curing
reaction for addition curing compared to condensation curing. Esteves et al.[5] followed
the stretching band of the silane group at 2136 cm−1. However, it was observed that
this band overlaps with another band at 2160 cm−1. Therefore another band has to be
used. Esteves et al. reported the use of the band at 902 cm−1 for the ﬁrst time. This
band corresponds to the bending vibration of the Si-H groups in the crosslinker. It was
observed that this band was reasonably sharper, well-deﬁned and could be normalized
with the use of a band at 864 cm−1. It was successfully observed that the band decreased
during the crosslinking reaction, and thus it was possible to monitor the curing process
with the use of the bending vibration band, using FT-IR.
ATR-FTIR was used to correlate the integrated area of the band at 902 cm−1 to the
actual concentration of the Si-H groups. This was attempted for diﬀerent ratios between
the Si–H groups of the crosslinker and vinyl groups of PDMS in the starting formulations.
However, this quantitative analysis was inconclusive for the formulations tested. This was
proposed to be due to the occurrence of mutual reactions after the induction period, which
includes both the hydrosilylation and secondary reactions of Si-H. The problem was that
the secondary reactions are much slower and proceed after the ATR-FTIR measurement
has ended.[5]
Another approach to follow an addition curing system with the use of FT-IR was reported
by Madsen et al.[7]. In this case, it was chosen to follow the C=C band at 1610 cm−1 from
the vinyl crosslinker. As expected the intensity of the vinyl band was decreasing through
the reaction conﬁrming the addition curing taking place. Though it should be noted that
the data from FT-IR spectroscopy cannot stand on its own, and thus it was compared to
data from other methods conﬁrming the observations.
From the studies performed, it is clear that FT-IR spectroscopy can be used to conﬁrm
the reaction(s) taking place. The method is, therefore, possible to use in addition to
other methods, supporting the data obtained, rather than clarifying reaction kinetics or
the extent of reaction.
1.3.2
Diﬀerential Scanning Calorimetry
DSC measurements can also be used to study the proceeding of a curing reaction. An-
dersson et al.[4] studied a range of commercial silicone formulations, for which some of
them utilizes the addition curing mechanism and some the condensation curing. To study
Page 12 of 87
==PAGE:30==

the proceeding of the curing several temperature scans were performed at diﬀerent times
of the curing. Each temperature scan revealed the additional heat of curing until the
complete curing. As expected, the additional heat decreased as a function of curing time,
at the end reaching a value of zero, thus completed curing. In addition to this experiment,
an isothermal experiment was performed at which the sample was allowed to cure in the
DSC instrument. Showing the heat ﬂow as a function of temperature, three signiﬁcant
points in the curing were possible to detect; (1) the time to start (tstart), (2) the time to
maximum cure rate (tmax), and (3) the time to complete curing (tend). For all three points
of the curing, an Arrhenius dependence as a function of temperature was found. Further-
more, it was possible to show that tend decreased as a function of the curing temperature
for all three systems investigated. However, the analysis performed in DSC was restricted
to the addition of curing systems. As discussed, humidity is needed for the condensation
reaction to occur, which was impossible to add in a controlled manner for the isothermal
experiments.
Hong et al.[16] also used results from DSC experiments to evaluate curing reaction for
an addition curing system. Again experiments were performed both at various heating
rates and as isothermal experiments. It was shown that independent of the heating rate
the total heat of reaction was almost constant. From isothermal experiments, both heat
release rates and the running integral were obtained. These were divided by the total heat
of reaction to obtain the rate of the curing and the degree of curing as a function of curing
temperature. Furthermore, it was possible to determine kinetic parameters as the pre-
exponential factor, activation energy, and reaction order with the use of diﬀerent methods.
The investigated studies show that DSC has only been used to determine reaction ki-
netics for addition curing systems. This could be due to various reasons. As mentioned
it is complicated to obtain the necessary humidity during curing in the closed environ-
ment. Furthermore, in contrast to addition curing, condensation curing produces volatile
byproducts, eg. methanol, which cannot escape in the instrument. Moreover, the DSC
method is conducted over a range of temperatures, whereas condensation curing most
often is performed at room temperature. Therefore, it seems that the utilization of DSC
for analyzing curing kinetics is restricted to addition curing.
1.3.3
Rheology
Another method that can be utilized to experimentally monitor the progress of a curing
reaction is rheology. Tang et al.[3] reported the use of rheology for a condensation curing
system. A dynamic viscoelasticity test was performed at 25°C resulting in data yielding
the storage modulus (G′), loss modulus (G′′), phase angle, and viscosity (η) as a function
of time. From this data, it was possible to determine the gelation point, as the crossover
between G′ and G′′. Furthermore, the data showed that the phase angle increased until
the gel point was reached, at which the phase angle decreased drastically. This is seen
because the elasticity dominates the viscosity of the elastomer, which is also clear from
the fact that the storage modulus is higher than the loss modulus. Moreover, it was
observed that the gel point was reached sooner when the ratio of crosslinker to polymer
was increased. This means that the curing reaction is accelerated when the ratio is in-
creased. This observation was just as expected, as the functional groups of the crosslinker
facilitate the crosslinking reaction, whereas an increase in polymer functional groups will

1.4
Modeling of reaction kinetics
In this section, some of the possibilities to model the reaction kinetics are presented. Two
types of models have been investigated in the literature, kinetic models and phenomeno-
logical or empirical models. However, in this thesis, it is the kinetic models that are in
the focus, and therefore the phenomenological models will not gain more attention. In
the literature, the kinetic models have been used to relate the cure degree and rate of
reaction to describe the chemical crosslinking reactions taking place in the curing process.
With the use of kinetic models, it is possible to determine kinetic parameters, which can
be used to control and assure the quality of the silicone elastomer components during
manufacture. Many kinetic models exist and can describe the curing behavior. Especially
the Deng Isayev and Kamal models have shown to predict experimental results in a good
manner. Therefore, the Deng Isayev and Kamal models are described in the following
sections.[12,16]

of all, an appropriate geometry is necessary to choose, because a too small geometry will

compared to open-air curing. The problem is, that the surface area which is exposed to

Experimental results from weight loss
Simultaneously with the measurements performed in the rheometer, the weight loss of the
sample was measured, which made it possible to measure the weight loss in triplicates as
well. Though it should be noted, that the weight loss measurements were performed with
the use of Di-50 as crosslinker only. The weight loss experiment was performed to support
the results of the other experiments and with the idea that it could be used for determi-
nation of the extent of reaction. This idea stems from the fact that when the crosslinking
reaction is occurring methanol is formed. The methanol is formed due to the elimination
of OCH3 groups. Therefore the amount of methanol that is formed should correspond to
the number of OCH3 groups that have reacted. This methanol will evaporate from the
sample, and thus giving the possibility to measure the amount of reacted OCH3 groups.
The experiments were carried out as described in section 2.3.4, thus giving the possibility
to follow the development of the weight continuously for the ﬁrst 24 hours, except for a

3.3.1
Inﬂuence of stoichiometric ratio
From the evaluation of the weight loss at the ﬁrst 12 hours in Figure 3.19a it can be seen
that the change in weight loss is largest within the ﬁrst three hours for all ratios. From
this point, the weight loss ﬂattens out. However, it is not possible to clarify whether the
reason why the rate of weight loss decreases after three hours is due to the decrease of
curing rate or because the network is more cured, thus making it more diﬃcult for the
methanol to diﬀuse in the sample. Another reason could also be that for all samples both
water and inhibitor were added. It may be the case that these components are evaporating
within the ﬁrst period thus contributing to the weight loss within the ﬁrst hours. In this
relation, it should be noted that the amount of added water, inhibitor and catalyst are
the same for all samples. The diﬀerence between the weight loss for the diﬀerent ratios is
therefore due to the evaporation of methanol as this is the only component which should
contribute to a diﬀerence.
From the evaluation of the weight loss in a period of 49 days in Figure 3.19b it is clear
that the tendency seen in Figure 3.19a continues. The most signiﬁcant weight loss is seen
within the ﬁrst 7 days, and from that point, the weight loss ﬂattens out. However, it is
also clear that the weight loss does not completely ﬂatten out before about 35 to 42 days
depending on the ratio. The weight loss indicates that methanol keeps evaporating from
the sample, which as expected indicates that the condensation curing is a slow process.
Though it should be mentioned that the reason for the continues weight loss could also
be due to diﬀusion limitations in the sample. This leads to, that methanol has diﬃculties
to evaporate from the sample. Therefore the continues weight loss could be due to a
delay of the evaporation of methanol rather than actual new methanol formed due to the
condensation reactions taking place.

3.6
Modelling of reaction kinetics
In this section, the results obtained for the modelling of reaction kinetics will be presented.
The modelling of reaction kinetics can be performed with the use of the Deng-Isayev model
or the Kamal model as described in section 1.4. Both of the models utilize the curing
degree and the reaction time to determine the reaction kinetics. Each of the models has
advantages and disadvantages. The biggest diﬀerence is that the Deng-Isayev model uti-
lizes two parameters, k, and n, while the Kamal model utilizes four parameters K1, K2, m,
and n. For the two models, the kinetic parameters, k, K1, and K2 can be determined from
an Arrhenius dependence of the temperature. However, in this thesis, the temperature
has not been varied, why it is not a possibility. This increases the complexity for both of
the models, as these parameters must be determined also from the minimization problem
using the least squared method. Especially for the Kamal model, it is complex, as all four
parameters must be determined from the minimization problem. Therefore it is chosen to
perform the modelling with the use of the Deng-Isayev model, where only two parameters
must be determined from the minimization problem.
However, another disadvantage for the Deng-Isayev model is, that it cannot model the
curing degree during the induction period, hence until the gelation point is reached. There-
fore it is chosen to determine the experimental curing degree, from the same method as
in section 3.5, but assuming that the curing starts at the gelation point. Thus α = 0, at
the gelation point. To model the curing degree, the mathematical tool for numerical and
symbolic modelling, Matlab is utilized. In Matlab, the experimentally determined G′ is
implemented and thus giving the possibility to determine the experimental curing degree.
Furthermore, the Deng-Isayev model for curing degree in equation (1.4) and initial values
of the two parameters k and n are introduced. By the use of the Matlab model, ’lsqcurve-
ﬁt’, it is possible to perform the least square minimization. This function determines the
parameters k and n, by minimizing the diﬀerence between the experimentally determined
curing degree and the curing degree determined from the Deng-Isayev model. Therefore
it utilizes the least square method as was done in literature.[12] The Matlab script which
was developed to perform the modelling can be seen in Appendix C.3. From these results,
it is possible to compare the experimentally and modelled curing degree and furthermore
the kinetic parameters for the diﬀerent stoichiometric ratios. This is done in the following
sections for the two crosslinkers.

3.7
Overal discussion
In this section, an overall discussion that is based on the experimental results is performed.
In the discussion, a speciﬁc focus will be put on three key elements of the results. The
ﬁrst thing is how curing in the rheology instrument inﬂuences the results compared to
the curing in an open environment. Secondly, a comparison of the results from the two
crosslinkers, which are diﬀerent in terms of molecular weight, will be performed. Lastly,
the inﬂuence of the two competing reactions during condensation curing will be discussed.
3.7.1
Inﬂuence of Rheology
The inﬂuence of curing in the rheometer instrument is discussed because it was assumed
that the results obtained from curing in the rheometer will represent the results that would
be obtained if the curing was performed in open-air conditions. Therefore, it was chosen
to prepare a reference sample, for all the samples where Di-50 is used as the crosslinker.
The reference sample was prepared in a square metal mold as described in section 2.2.2.
For all reference samples, approximately 0.9 mL was added to obtain a sample of approx-
imately 1 mm in thickness. This ensured a sample which is similar to the sample cured
in the rheometer.
From the mold, a square sample was obtained. This sample was cut out, to a round
sample which could be analyzed in the rheometer, with the use of a frequency sweep.
As the rheology experiments performed over time were carried out at 1 Hz, the value of
G′ was obtained at 1 Hz for the reference samples. This made it possible to compare
the value of G′ for the two diﬀerent curing methods, cured in rheology and the reference
sample cured in open-air, as can be seen in Figure 3.35. It is clear that for all samples
with r > 1, G′ is signiﬁcantly larger for the sample cured in the rheometer. Only for r ∼1,
the value of G′ is approximately the same. All numerical values can be seen in Appendix
D.1.
One reason for the diﬀerence in G′ could be that the curing in the rheometer will inﬂuence
the network formation. This could be in terms of the oscillation of the geometry, giving
a diﬀerent dynamic to the sample during curing. It could also be due to heat formation,
from the friction between geometry and sample, thus accelerating the curing of the sample.
The accelerated curing would cause the sample in the rheometer to be closer to full curing,
and thus a higher value of G′. If this was the case it is expected that the reference sample
would have reached the rheology cured sample, and thus equalized the diﬀerence between
the two samples after one month. Therefore it was chosen to measure the reference samples
again after one month. These measurements are denoted as ’Reference - 2’ and can be
seen from the red dots in Figure 3.35. From the comparison between the two reference
measurements, it is clear that the value of G′ is approximately the same. Longer reaction
time has therefore not allowed the reference samples to obtain the same value of G′, and
thus the higher value of the rheology cured samples is not due to an accelerated curing
reaction.

3.7.2
Crosslinker comparison
In this thesis, two diﬀerent crosslinkers, in terms of their molecular weight, have been
used. The two crosslinkers, Di-10 and Di-50, have been investigated with the use of rheol-
ogy, which made it possible to investigate the curing behavior over time and calculate the
reaction rate. With the use of the obtained results from rheology, it was also possible to
determine the experimental and modelled curing degree as a function of time. For all the
results, it has been possible to compare and see the direct inﬂuence of the stoichiometric
ratio. This section will focus on a comparison between the use of the two crosslinkers.
For the properties at the gelation point, it was observed that the time to reach the gelation
point decreased signiﬁcantly when using Di-50 compared to Di-10. This could indicate
that the network was formed faster when using Di-50. At the same time, it was observed
that the experimental reaction rate was signiﬁcantly lower for Di-50. The experimental
reaction rate describes how fast the plateau of G′ is reached. This shows, that even though
the gelation point is reached at an earlier point for Di-50, the reaction is slower, and hence
the fully cured network is most probably obtained at a later point. This indicates that
from the point where the gel point is reached it takes signiﬁcantly longer to reach the
plateau for G′. Besides, it was observed that the tendency for G′ as a function of ratio
was diﬀerent for Di-50 compared to Di-10. This could be explained by the dilution for
Di-50 due to the higher molecular weight crosslinker, and thus fewer functional groups
per gram. This could also be the reason for the lower reaction rates as the reaction rate
is a combination of the competing reactions taking place. For Di-50 it might be that the
OCH3 groups have more diﬃculties in reacting with each other, due to the big distance
between the functional groups. This would lead to a lower reaction rate.
For the curing degree as a function of reaction time, which was evaluated in section 3.5
the same tendencies were observed for the two crosslinkers. For the curing degree, it is
diﬃcult to compare the results as the plateau was only reached in a few cases. In general,
it would have been an advantage to run the samples for a longer time. However, this
was not possible as the rheometer was alarming in some cases. Despite these problems,
it is possible to compare the curing rates for the two crosslinkers and it is clear that
signiﬁcantly higher curing rates are obtained for Di-10. This is consistent with the higher

==PAGE:78==

reaction rate for Di-10. This conﬁrms the indication that the curing reaction is faster
when using the lower molecular weight crosslinker.
From the modeling of the curing degree, the kinetic parameters were obtained for both
of the crosslinkers. For Di-10 the rate constant was higher than that of Di-50 in all cases
except for r ∼5. Especially for r ∼8 and 12, the rate constant is signiﬁcantly higher for

However, it should also be noted that k is highly dependent on the value of n, which was
seen from the modiﬁed values of k for r ∼8 and 12. The variations in n between the two
crosslinkers could therefore also be the reason for the diﬀerence in k.
3.7.3
Inﬂuence of competing reactions
From the rheology results, it was observed for the higher ratios, that the Flory-Stockmayer
theory was not obeyed. This indicated that not only the primary reaction between poly-
mer and crosslinker is taking place. Therefore it was suggested that simultaneously with
the primary reaction, an intramolecular reaction between OCH3 groups is taking place.
This reaction was conﬁrmed from the investigation of the inﬂuence of the diﬀerent com-
ponents. This investigation clariﬁed that OCH3 groups can react with each other, and
thus forming the network, while OH groups are not performing an intramolecular reaction.
In literature[27,36], it was shown that an alkoxy crosslinker is hydrolyzed in the presence
of water and catalyst. This could be the reason why the intramolecular reaction is seen.
And thus it is not an actual intramolecular reaction between OCH3 groups, but rather
hydrolysis of some of the OCH3 groups. Then some of the other OCH3 groups are capable
of reacting with the formed OH groups. In this way, the intramolecular reaction between
OCH3 inﬂuence the curing mechanism.
However, it was also stated in literature[27], that the alkoxy groups are hydrolyzed imme-
diately. This is in contrast with the observed curing of the pure crosslinker for sample
TC.3. Here it was observed that an induction period of more than twenty minutes was
necessary before the reaction was initiated. If this is the time needed to hydrolyze some
of the OCH3 groups and thus start the reaction, it seems like a long period compared to
literature. Furthermore, it was observed for sample TC.4, that when the sample consists
of purely OH groups, the network cannot be formed. This indicates, that if all the OCH3
groups were hydrolyzed immediately, no network would be formed. Therefore it was cho-
sen to investigate the curing behavior with the use of FT-IR.
The curing was possible to monitor with the use of FT-IR by investigating the band of
the Si-OCH3 bond at 2840 cm−1. For this band, it was clear that it decreased over time.
However, after 120 min of reaction time, the band was still present. This proves that
not all of the OCH3 groups are hydrolyzed initially. However, it could still be the case
that some of them are hydrolyzed initially. This hydrolysis of OCH3 groups, yield the
second reaction in addition to the condensation reaction. Therefore the system contains
two competing reactions during the curing.

CONCLUSION
Conclusion
In this thesis, it has been possible to investigate and experimentally determine and model
the reaction kinetics for a condensation curing PDMS network. The reaction kinetics
has been investigated with the use of a wide range of diﬀerent methods, which include
rheology, weight loss determination, SEC, and FT-IR. The condensation curing network
involves the reaction of an OH terminated polymer and one of the two OCH3 terminated
crosslinkers with the functionality of six, but with diﬀerent molecular weight.
In the
condensation curing, dibutyltin diacetate was used as the catalyst for all of the prepared
samples.
By rheology, it was possible to follow the curing behaviour of the sample. The develop-
ment of the storage modulus and loss modulus was used to follow the curing behaviour
over time and thus being able to detect the gelation point and the point at which the
material is fully cured. Rheology was used to investigate the curing behaviour for both
of the crosslinkers, Di-10 and Di-50, respectively. For both of the crosslinkers the stoi-
chiometric ratio between the polymer and crosslinker functional group was varied, in the
following ratios; 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, and 12.
For the use of Di-10 as crosslinker it was found that the time to reach a plateau for
G′ was decreased when the stoichiometric ratio was increased. Therefore increasing the
stoichiometric ratio leads to faster reaction kinetics. In addition to that, it was found,
that when the stoichiometric ratio is increased above a value of three, the end value of G′
changes only slightly. This leads to the surprising ﬁnding that a polymeric network was
obtained even for the highest ratio. This is in contrast with what is expected from the
Flory-Stockmayer theory, which predicts that a ratio of ﬁve is the upper limit for gelation
when the functionality is six. This indicates that one or more of the assumptions from
the Flory-Stockmayer theory is not obeyed. One explanation is that an intramolecular
reaction for the crosslinker takes place.
In addition to that, it was found that the time to reach the gelation point is decreasing
with the increase in the stoichiometric ratio, while the value of the G′ and G′′ is increas-
ing at the gelation point. This increase indicates that more crosslinks are formed at the
gelation point when the stoichiometric ratio is increased. Furthermore, it was possible
to determine the reaction rate from the obtained storage modulus. From the determined
reaction rates, it was possible to conﬁrm that an increase in the stoichiometric ratio, leads
to an increase in the reaction rate.
For the use of Di-50 as the crosslinker, similar results in terms of the reaction kinetics
were found. The increase in the stoichiometric ratio leads to faster reaction kinetics. In
addition to that, it was also found that the time to reach the gelation point decreased as
expected. However, in contrast to the results form Di-10 it was found that the value of
G′ at the gelation point only increases to a ratio of 3. When the ratio is increased above

3, the value of G′ is decreasing with the increase in ratio. This was expected to originate
from the higher molecular weight crosslinker. The results indicate, that the increase in
molecular weight leads to a dilution, which means that the distance between crosslinks
is increased. However, it was observed that despite the diﬀerences at the gelation point,
the end value of G′ reached a plateau at the approximate same value for the ratios 3, 5,
8 and 12.
Rheology was also used to investigate the inﬂuence of the diﬀerent components in the
sample. It was found that the use of the inhibitor increases the time to reach the gelation
point with almost 10%. In addition to that, it was found that no network is formed in
the absence of either catalyst or crosslinker, which conﬁrms the mechanism suggested in
literature. Furthermore, it was found that for a sample which only contains crosslinker,
catalyst, and water a polymeric network is formed. This conﬁrms that an intramolecular
reaction is taking place for the crosslinker.
From the weight loss determination, it was possible to continuously follow the weight loss
within the ﬁrst 24 hours of the reaction. From this point, the weight loss was investigated
for seven weeks, by one weekly measurement. This was done to correlate the weight loss
to the extent of reaction. However, it was found that the weight loss continued for more
than 40 days, and even at that point, the experimental weight loss was lower than the
expected theoretical weight loss for the ratios 3, 5, 8 and 12. However, it could be seen
that, as expected an increase in stoichiometric ratio leads to an increase in weight loss.
The increase in stoichiometric ratio leads to an increase in the amount of methanol that
is formed as a byproduct.
By the use of FT-IR and SEC, the curing behaviour for Di-10 as the crosslinker and with
a stoichiometric ratio of three only was investigated. The curing behaviour was inves-
tigated by obtaining samples at diﬀerent times of the curing. For the FT-IR analysis,
it was chosen to follow the change in the 2840 cm−1 band from the Si-OCH3 bond in
the crosslinker. From the obtained spectra it was clear that the peak decreased, as time
progress, showing that the condensation reaction occurs. However, it was also observed
that even past the gelation point, a signiﬁcant OCH3 peak was observed, proving that a
signiﬁcant amount of groups is still prone to further reaction.
From the use of SEC, it was possible to determine the number average molecular weight
and polydispersity index at diﬀerent times of the curing. The SEC analysis was performed
with the use of two diﬀerent columns, toluene and THF, respectively. The increase in
the number average molecular weight as a function of reaction time was seen as a clear
tendency, as expected. However, the tendency was not strict since ﬂuctuations were seen
in the measurements. Therefore, the z average molecular weight was determined. In this
case, a clear tendency of increasing z average molecular weight was seen as a function of
reaction time.
By the use of the results from rheology, it was possible to determine the curing degree as a
function of reaction time. From the curing degree graphs, it was clear that the behaviour
of the curing for a stoichiometric ratio of 8 and 12 was signiﬁcantly diﬀerent for both of
the crosslinkers. This indicates that for these ratios, the large excess of OCH3 groups
leads to the intramolecular reaction becoming more dominant. This was conﬁrmed from
the calculations of the curing rate as a function of the curing degree. Here it was observed

that the behaviour for these two ratios was signiﬁcantly diﬀerent. However, as expected
it was observed, that when the ratio is increased, the maximum curing rate is increased.
The results from rheology were used to model the reaction kinetics with the use of the
Deng-Isayev model. This model utilizes the experimental results to model the curing
degree as a function of reaction time. For both of the crosslinkers, and for all of the
investigated stoichiometric ratios, it was possible to model the experimental behaviour in
a good manner. Furthermore, it was possible to determine the kinetic parameters, rate
constant and order of the reaction. Also, in this case, signiﬁcantly diﬀerent results were
obtained for the stoichiometric ratios of 8 and 12.
The results obtained from the diﬀerent analysis methods showed that in addition to the
condensation reaction between polymer and crosslinker, an intramolecular reaction oc-
curs for the crosslinker.
Therefore it can be concluded that two competing reactions
are proceeding simultaneously. This makes it more complicated to determine the reac-
tion kinetics, and especially for higher stoichiometric ratios, it has been shown that the
intramolecular reaction has a large inﬂuence on the curing behaviour.

OUTLOOK
Outlook
For the condensation curing elastomer, it would be advantageous to understand the inﬂu-
ence of the competing reactions in more detail, enabling the possibility to determine the
experimental reaction kinetics with a bigger precision. It is suggested that the competing
reactions is due to hydrolysis of the crosslinker functional groups. Therefore it would
be interesting to investigate the inﬂuence on reaction kinetics from the variation of the
added amount of water. The reaction kinetics was determined with the use of rheology in
a relatively easy manner, why this method could be applied to investigate the inﬂuence
of the intramolecular reaction in more detail.
For the modelling of reaction kinetics, it would be interesting to investigate the rheo-
logical curing behaviour at diﬀerent temperatures. This would enable the possibility to
determine the kinetic parameters with a better precision and enable to possibility to use
the Kamal model for the determination of kinetic parameters. This investigation could
be used to compare the kinetic parameters obtained from diﬀerent models.
The further investigation of the curing behaviour and reaction kinetics would provide
knowledge, applicable in the control of the reactions. This would give the possibility to
determine reaction conditions for the design of an elastomer network which would posses
the properties that are desired in the application as a silicone coating.
