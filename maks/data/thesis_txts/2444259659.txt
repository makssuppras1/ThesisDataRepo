ABSTRACT
Over the last couple of decades studies have proven that the well-being and performance of
students are directly related to the Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ), containing the pa-
rameters thermal comfort, visual comfort, acoustic comfort, and air quality. For this reason,
it is generally desirable that educational institutions offer their students the most beneﬁcial
and optimal indoor climate, in order to support that the students achieve the best academic
outcome of their studies, and that IEQ is continuously subject to focus and has positive de-
velopment.
In this master thesis the DTU Smart Library was used as a case study. Currently the in-
door environment at the library is controlled by a traditional BMS, dividing the three upper
ﬂoors into 4 ventilation zones each. However, in 2017 the library underwent a larger ren-
ovation, which, among other things, resulted in the implementation of CAS Sensor Boxes
based on the IoT concept. Several Sensor Boxes were placed on each ﬂoor monitoring vari-
ous IEQ parameters.
Thus, the aim of this project has been to evaluate the overall IEQ at the DTU Smart Li-
brary, taking a basis in physical as well as subjective measurements, and subsequently to
evaluate the results, and to address if/how possible improvements might be made through
the implementation of IoT.
The physical measurements showed that the overall IEQ was in general at a satisfactory
level, and in accordance with DS/CEN 16798 (2017) and WEA guide lines, however, still
leaving room for improvement. Temperatures showed to be slightly too high on the 2nd
ﬂoor, which matched the students responses, stating that the majority preferred a lower
temperature. Relative humidity (RH) measurements showed to be rather uniform across all
ﬂoor levels, and within the recommended guidelines. Illuminance levels were mainly mea-
sured to be around 300 to 400 lux; except for the north zone on the 2nd ﬂoor that had sig-
niﬁcantly higher illuminance levels, which more often resulted in students being bothered
Page III
==PAGE:6==
by glare. Noise levels showed, in 30% of the period measured, to be above the WEA rec-
ommendation of 50 dB in 3 out of 4 of the measured zones. Of all noise sources students
felt the most bothered by nearby conversation. CO2 concentrations were highest on the 3rd
ﬂoor, which did not match with the students perception of the air quality. It was concluded
that the implementation of IoT within the BMS could allow for an improved control of the
indoor environment. Further, through an assessment of the already existing IoT CAS Sen-
sor Boxes, it was concluded that such implementation requires proper management and lots
of effort in order to succeed.

1. INTRODUCTION
1. INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
During the last few decades several studies have shown that the well-being and perfor-
mance of students are directly related to the indoor environmental quality (IEQ) [13]. Stan-
dards concerning IEQ have been developed in order to deﬁne the acceptable ranges of these
parameters. Yet, studies also suggest that even though the requirements are met, not all stu-
dents will be satisﬁed with the indoor environment. In fact, the same indoor environmental
conditions may often lead to different subjective responses [37].
At DTU the students spend much of their time inside open-plan study environments such as
the DTU Smart Library. The Smart Library initiative was initially started as a subdivision
of the Smart Campus concept, which was established to create a better and smarter campus,
whilst also creating a testing ground for students, researchers, and companies, to develop
and help build tomorrow’s smart city technologies [1]. The library environment was de-
signed for individual studies as well as group work and to encourage interaction between
students. However, the indoor environment at the library is like many other open-plan envi-
ronments controlled by a traditional Building Management System (BMS), which, through
few expensive hard-wired sensors, maintains a uniform IEQ [24]. This method contradicts,
however, with the need of adjusting the indoor environment to comply with individual stu-
dent satisfaction and comfort. The solution to this issue may be found in the ever-growing
market of inexpensive IoT sensors, promising easy implementation and installation. Dur-
ing the 2017 renovation of the library several of these new smart technologies, utilizing the
IoT concept, were implemented as part of the DTU Smart Library initiative. However, the
capabilities and limitations of the IoT sensors are yet to be investigated and evaluated.

1. INTRODUCTION
1.2
Hypothesis
The objective of this project was to assess the overall IEQ at the DTU Smart Library as
well as the potential of utilizing IoT to improve the IEQ, based on the desire to create the
best indoor environment for students.
The project will through physical measurements and subjective measurements (question-
naire), of the thermal comfort, visual comfort, acoustic comfort and air quality, evaluate the
overall IEQ. By comparing the physical measurements to BMS and CAS Sensor Box data,
it will be determined if IoT could be a favorable way and a feasible method to control the
indoor environment of a large open space, such as DTU Smart Library.
In this respect the following questions will be investigated and answered:
• What is the current status of the Indoor Environmental Quality at DTU Smart Li-
brary?
• How do the students subjectively experience the IEQ at DTU Smart Library?
• How accurately does the BMS sensor measure?
• Is traditional BMS the most optimal way of controlling the IEQ?
• How accurately does the CAS Sensor Box measure?
• Is IoT a better way of controlling the IEQ compared to the traditional BMS?
• What does it take to utilize IoT as a way of monitoring and controlling the IEQ at
DTU Smart Library?

2. LITERATURE STUDY
2.1
Indoor environmental factors affecting the study environment
Numerous studies on how the indoor environment affects adult ofﬁce workers or students
in elementary and secondary schools have been conducted through the years, yet there still
seem to be only a bare minimum of studies investigating the effects speciﬁcally on univer-
sity students [13]. Related studies suggest, however, that the IEQ affects university students
similarly to how it affects adult ofﬁce workers [10, 67]. Thus, this literature study will fo-
cus on studies related to university environments, yet the ﬁndings concerning ofﬁce envi-
ronments will be adapted and included when ﬁt.
Important factors which inﬂuence the IEQ are: thermal comfort, visual comfort, acoustic
comfort and indoor air quality [27]. Figure 2 presents these IEQ factors in a more illustra-
tive manner.
Figure 2: Illustration of main factors inﬂuencing the IEQ [4].
Hence these are all important IEQ conditions that need to be controlled properly in order
to secure a productive study environment. However, occupants do not attach equal impor-
tance to each IEQ factor. In fact, thermal comfort is regarded by building occupants to be
of greater importance than visual comfort, acoustic comfort or good air quality [18].
2.1.1
How thermal comfort impacts the study environment
When thermally comfortable, an individual will wish to feel neither cooler nor warmer, if
asked about thermal state and preference. This sensation of thermal comfort varies within

each individual and thus a group of e.g. students will have different opinions on the matter.
In these more general cases the criteria for an acceptable thermal environment are speci-
ﬁed as requirements for general thermal comfort, which is determined (most commonly)
through the use of the PMV/PPD model [37]. This model makes it possible to predict the
average thermal sensation for a group of people [18]. The indoor thermal environment is
characterized by several parameters including air temperature, mean radiant temperature,
air velocity, relative humidity and the two personal variables which are clothing insulation
and metabolic rate [50].
Studies suggest that thermal conditions can affect the students’ performance by at least
six different mechanisms which have been summarized as a diagram in ﬁgure 3. Start-
ing from the top: Thermal discomfort distracts attention; Warmth lowers arousal and in-
creases the probability of sick building syndrome (SBS), causing further negative effects
on performance, and has a negative effect on cognitive thinking; The combined effects
of the aforementioned mechanisms are assumed to have a negative impact on the moti-
vation to exert effort; Cold conditions decrease ﬁnger temperatures, which causes a neg-
ative effect on manual dexterity; Vertical thermal gradients creates a false feeling of cold
at ﬂoor level, which however is in fact due to vasoconstriction; and ﬁnally raised temper-
atures can result in elevated CO2 concentration in the blood, which may cause headaches
[26, 33, 36, 61, 63, 65] cited by [67].

2. LITERATURE STUDY
A comprehensive experiment examined the effects of classroom temperatures on the perfor-
mance of 18 university students over a period of 36 days [50]. During this period the tem-
peratures were intentionally manipulated in the range of 22-29 ◦C. The students repeatedly
performed two different attention tests: The Kraepelin test which focuses on the subject’s
ability to concentrate under stress [19, 28] and the Prague test which is intended to test the
subject’s ability to handle multiple tasks and creative thinking [56, 54] cited by [50]. The
results showed that the ideal temperature for optimal performance were different in each
test. The ideal temperature for the Kraepelin test ranged from 25-26 ◦C and a little higher,
27-28 ◦C, for the Prague test. This is exactly in accordance with psychological theory of
arousal, where moderately higher temperatures reduce the arousal and are thus beneﬁcial
for tests requiring memory and creative thinking, whereas lower temperatures raise arousal
and thus improves concentration. However, in both cases this only applied if the temper-
ature was only a few degrees above or beneath thermal neutrality [67]. Figure 4 shows an
experimental relationship between thermal discomfort and performance. The study was
based on a testing of a wide range of tasks including arithmetical calculation, typing, logi-
cal reasoning and tasks examining memory [25].
Figure 4: The relationship between thermal discomfort due to heat and performance in ac-
cordance to [67, 25].
The results suggest that students performing assorted tasks would perform optimally, while
feeling close to thermally neutral. Other studies have shown that individual control of the

2.1.2
How visual comfort impacts the study environment
Visual comfort is deﬁned as a “subjective condition of visual well-being induced by the
luminous environment” [44]. Although the deﬁnition seems rather subjective, several phys-
ical and measurable parameters exist in which the visual environment can be objectively
evaluated. The main parameters determining the luminous environment with respect to ar-
tiﬁcial light and daylight are: luminance distribution, illuminance, directionality of light,
correlated colour temperature, colour rendering, glare, ﬂicker and amount of daylight [43].
Flicker causes distraction and can in severe cases lead to physiological effects such as seizure
[68], which is why any new lighting system should be designed to avoid this. Low CCT is
perceived as warm and creates a relaxed atmosphere, while high CCT is perceived as cool
and associated with a more frisk atmosphere[6]. This behavior is similar to that of thermal
comfort where temperatures slightly higher than thermally neutral decreases arousal. The
thermal sensation is, however, affected only around a thermally neutral condition, but not
when people feel slightly cool or slightly warm [57]. Overall, a 300 lux illuminance thresh-
old has by expert and occupant assessment been found to be the most suited for building
types such as libraries, open plan ofﬁces and classrooms [21].
2.1.3
How acoustic comfort impacts the study environment
Providing good acoustics, to create a healthy open plan study environment, is mainly as-
sociated with preventing distraction or annoyance. The quality of a healthy sound environ-
ment is mainly determined by the following two factors: physical properties of sound itself
and room properties. Sound is characterized by the sound pressure level in a short-term and
long-term period and by sound frequency. Important room properties include sound insula-
tion, absorption and reverberation time [18]. The inﬂuence of acoustic design of open plan
spaces has been studied mostly in relation to speech intelligibility, where a relation between

==PAGE:24==

the speech transmission index (STI) and work performance has been found [22]. Another
study, investigated the inﬂuence of noise on human performance which resulted in the fol-
lowing conclusions[55]:
• In particular, cognitive and communication tasks are inﬂuenced by noise.
• Intermittent noise is more disruptive than continuous noise.
• Speech-like noise is the most disruptive sound for cognitive tasks.
Further, a Dutch ﬁeld study carried out across multiple open plan study environments at
different universities showed that students are mostly disturbed by noise when perform-

writing. In addition, the study showed that simultaneous use of the same open plan environ-
ment for both communication in group work and concentration on individual tasks creates
an acoustic dilemma, where the only current way out is the use of headphones or earplugs
[10].

Acceptable air quality is deﬁned as “air in which there are no known contaminants at harm-
ful concentrations as determined by cognizant authorities and with which a substantial ma-
jority (80% or more) of the people exposed do not express dissatisfaction” [38]. Poor in-
door air quality is found to affect the performance of students through similar mechanisms

2.2
Building standards and guidelines
The Danish Building Regulation along with the Danish Working Environment Authority
(WEA) provide standards and guidelines concerning the indoor environment which have
been developed to provide general procedures for contractors, consultants etc. on how to
achieve speciﬁc indoor environment comfort goals.
DS/EN 15251 (2007) is the most up to date standard, that considers the overall indoor cli-
mate. However, a proposal for a new standard with the purpose of replacing the current one
has been developed. This standard is called DS/CEN 16798 (2017), and is essentially an
adaptation of DS/EN 15251 (2007), with a few tightenings of requirements. Both standards
rely on the 4 most commonly used parameters for evaluating the indoor environment: i.e.
thermal conditions, lighting, acoustic conditions and air quality. In addition the DS 3033
(2011) has been developed with more ambitious, yet voluntarily guidelines for the indoor
environment. Guidelines and forth mentioned standards will be presented in the following.
2.2.1
Indoor environment guidelines
DS/EN 15251 (2007) and DS/CEN 16798 (2017) are standards for energy calculations of
the indoor environment. The two standards describe the classiﬁcation categories: Category
I, II, III and IV. Category I being of best quality, while category IV being worst. Category

only be accepted for a limited time of the year [45, 42].

2.3
Sensor technology in smart buildings
The implementation of smart sensor technology in buildings to control the indoor environ-
ment is not exactly a newly invented concept. In fact, millions of buildings already have
their indoor environment controlled by sensor-based regulation systems, such as the Build-
ing Management System (BMS). However, this should only be considered as the beginning
of the smart building development going forward. The objective of smart buildings is to
know every detailed information within every zone in the building, and to make the build-
ing perform, so it is able to adjust to the indoor changing environment, e.g. by use of IoT
technologies [49, 29].
2.3.1
Building Management Systems
A BMS is a system installed in buildings, which controls and monitors building services,
such as heating/cooling systems, ventilation systems etc. [15, 47]. It is a distributed system
designed to computerize control and manage building services [15]. The BMS ecosystem
can be distributed into three layers; The lowest layer, Field Layer, The middle layer, Au-
tomation Layer and the top layer, Management Layer [15]. Figure 7 shows an illustration
of the BMS layers and their connections.
Figure 7: Illustration of the BMS ecosystem [2].
Page 15
==PAGE:32==
2. LITERATURE STUDY
2.3.1.1
Field layer
The ﬁeld layer is where interaction with sensors and actuators occurs [15]. The BMS col-
lects information and data regarding the different states and demands in the building, which
is executed through the sensors and actuators. The sensors could be room sensors mea-
suring the room temperature or PIR sensors registering if any individuals are in the room.
Whilst the actuators could be motors opening valves on radiators, adjusting lighting, open-
ing dampers in the ventilation system etc. [47]. The sensors and actuators are connected
through hard-wired data cables, via I/O ports(input/output) in the BMS substation [47, 15].
This could, however, bring along inconvenience of rerouting and reprogramming when
wanting to e.g. change the layout of the sensors or simply add more sensors [14]. The sen-
sors can either be placed in zones or in HVAC equipment. The zone installations are cate-
gorized as intelligent building installations, IBI, and a zone controlled by IBI is called an
IBI-zone [47].
2.3.1.2
Automation layer
The automation layer is where measurements are managed, alarms activated, control loops
executed and the building’s HVAC systems controlled [15]. The automation layer reacts
on data measurements received from sensors which, at the automation layer, then send sig-
nals to the actuator that reacts on these signals by e.g. opening valves, fan speeds in the air
handling unit, supply temperatures etc. [47]. Additionally, in the automation layer the sub-
station can react and be controlled by set points. This communication type is identiﬁed as a
control loop [5]. Figure 8 illustrates a control loop.
Figure 8: Illustration of a control loop [5]
Page 16
==PAGE:33==
2. LITERATURE STUDY
2.3.1.3
Management layer
The management layer is where activities such as system data presentation and data archival
occurs. This can be operated through a work station, typically being a computer, where in-
terface such as HMI and SCADA are integrated [15]. Human machine interface (HMI) is
an interface, which makes it possible for users to utilize the building automation system in
an efﬁcient way. The HMI is typically placed at the main station. Through the interface it
is possible to operate the technical plant, create log-on data in the system, monitor energy-
use, changing set points and create alarm lists from incidents within the system. The sys-
tem control and data acquisition system(SCADA) is a platform integrated into the main sta-
tion, which allows for interaction with third part systems. From this, old and new building
automation systems can be gathered and administrated in one platform [47].
2.3.2
Internet of Things
IoT is universally integrated and has a large impact on our daily life. IoT is a technology,
based on things or devices, which are connected and thereby able to communicate. A sen-
sor (tiny sized mobile transceiver) is installed in these devices and objects, and are able to
sense, collect data, share information through the internet, by a wired or a wireless connec-
tion [52]. A great number of sensors are available on the market today, which are able to
measure in real time, indoor climate parameters such as: indoor air quality, thermal condi-
tions, acoustics, lighting, etc.[52, 49]. Additionally, IoT makes it possible to track people’s
movement, their gender and age, fully anonymously [3, 49, 29]. IoT basically lets people
and things be connected anyplace, anytime, with anything that can be connected through
any network [52]. All these sensors show potential in strengthening the today’s quality of
life, such as a healthy indoor environment [52, 29].
The IoT concept has both a great amount of advantages and limitations, due to it still being
considered as a rather new technology. The tiny size of the sensors makes it possible to
place it in any troublesome area of an object, or in any object having a fairly small size.
This advantage could make it easier to gain more information of more speciﬁc situations
within building installations.
Use of IoT systems may create less privacy for users, and less data security in general, due
to the risk of hackers being able to brute force the Cloud where the system captures and re-
ports all information obtained. Moreover, a sensor has an internal complexity to it, which
Page 17
==PAGE:34==
2. LITERATURE STUDY
could lead to more failures than wanted, and make it harder to manage the system if tech-
nical insight and knowledge is limited [52]. Another relevant perspective to have in mind
within the IoT concept, is the quality of the available systems. Today many cost-friendly
IoT systems are available on the market, and there is a risk of the quality of the sensors
being poor, which then could lead to poor measurements with deterioration of the indoor
climate. This raises a concern if the common inexpensive IoT sensors are reliable enough
to maintain a healthy indoor environment or if cheap technology will have the reverse effect
[32].
2.3.3
IoT in BMS
The IoT concept begins to challenge the more traditional way of thinking BMS, where few
expensive hard-wired sensors are controlling the indoor environment of set points. This of-
ten leads to a large variation in the indoor environment, especially in larger rooms where
e.g. centralized sensors control the temperature. Thus, by implementing a large number of
sensors by IoT in a BMS the indoor environment would be much easier to control and ad-
just, regardless of the aim being a uniform environment or a more local one[29, 20, 14, 49].
Moreover, due to IoT often being wireless, compared to wired traditional BMS compo-
nents, it would make it easier to add or renew IoT measuring devices.
In conclusion IoT shows great promises in the optimization of the indoor environment
along with the creation of more sustainable and less energy consuming buildings [20].
However, the cost efﬁcient IoT technology has yet to prove it’s worth as the ordinary pick
of choice in operating the indoor environment of buildings [52, 7, 29].
2.3.3.1
IoT in study environments
The ﬁndings in section 2.3.2 and 2.3.3 suggest that implementation of IoT in study envi-
ronments could have its beneﬁts. The sensor being tiny and wireless, may make it more
feasible to place it in a study environment, or onto hassle location on the building systems
connected to the study areas. Further, implementing many versus few sensors may bring a
more adjusted and satisfying indoor environment for the students. So overall, with use and
support of IoT when evaluating the indoor climate could bring newer and more advanced
methods for when aiming towards the best indoor environment for students.

Calibration of Measuring Stations
To ensure the quality of the measured data, a calibration of each Measuring Station was
carried out. The Vaisala transmitters were calibrated with the associated software using a
2 point calibration. The ﬁrst calibration was made with clean nitrogen and the second cali-
bration was made with a mixture of nitrogen and 5000 ppm carbon-dioxide. Calibration of
the HOBO loggers’ temperature sensors happened within a climatic chamber. The temper-
ature of the chamber was regulated by a HVAC unit, and the Br¨uel & Kjaer air temperature
transducer was used to check the actual room temperature. Three reference temperatures
were measured; 15 ◦C, 20 ◦C and 25 ◦C, with a 10 minute interval within every measure-
ment. In doing so 3 data points could be plotted in a graph, with reference temperatures out
of one axis and measured temperatures out of the other axis. Finally, linear regression of
the data points provided a function for each trend line. Figure 32 shows one of the tempera-
ture calibration graphs for Measuring Station #88. All Measuring Stations measured within
an average deviation of ± 0.54 ◦C from the reference temperatures. Calibration graphs for
each Measuring Station can be found in appendix I.

Subjective measurements
In order to make an adequate, full, assessment of the indoor environment at the DTU library
it was not enough to look at the physical assessment only. A subjective assessment was
just as crucial, in order to discover the actual experiences of students and other users of
the library. Thus, two kinds of questionnaires were created, a short simple survey and a
longer more detailed one. Participation was non mandatory and no prizes were offered as
incitement to answer the questionnaires.
4.2.1
Detailed questionnaire
The detailed questionnaire was created to investigate, in depth, the four indoor environ-
mental conditions; thermal comfort, visual comfort, acoustic comfort and air quality. The
questionnaire was made available on-line, in the period from November 7 till December 10,
with a format compatible with both computers and mobile devices. To make participants
aware of the survey, pamphlets were designed and distributed evenly all over the library,
with weekly inspections if new pamphlets should be distributed. The design of the pam-
phlets can be seen in ﬁgure 45. At the end of the data gathering period the hunt on ﬁlled in
surveys intensiﬁed, as the questionnaires would be handed out directly to students occupy-
ing the library. The duration of the survey took approximately 3 min, depending on answers
chosen. The participants answered the survey by ticking off boxes, writing short replies as
well as setting marks on a 1 to 100 scale. The questionnaire in its entirety can be found in
appendix J. All sensitive personal data, like gender and age, which were gathered during
the measuring period were handled in accordance with the the EU General Data Protection
Regulation (GDPR).

4.3.1.1
BMS data
BMSnet is a browser containing BMS data from all building services at DTU campus, in-
cluding DTU library. The data base includes data from all BMS sensors, which are strictly
named in accordance with the DTU Standard Bygningsinstallationer, Lyngby. The sensor
names consists of an identiﬁcation string containing numbers and abbreviations. A string
code could for instance be, B102 IB038 01 TT203 SP001 LD. The string code refers to
building 102 (B102), room 38 on 1st ﬂoor (IB038), plant number one (01), temperature
transmitter (TT), day set point (SP001) and data logging (LD). In this manner a unique
name is given to each sensor which contains information about exact location and type of
sensor.
When a desired unit is found in BMSnet, the trendlogs can be seen directly in the browser
or be extracted to a CSV ﬁle. The data extracted from BMSnet, for this master thesis, were;
ventilation system set points as well as temperature and CO2 concentration measurements.
The measurements were logged every 5 minutes and updated in BMSnet. Downloaded data
were in the period from October 17 till December 10, same as the measuring period, pro-
cessed in excel and RStudio. Figure 47 illustrates the BMS data chain.

4.4
Data processing
In order to consider in the analysis only the time span when the library was actually in use,
the data which were measured outside of the deﬁned occupancy hours (8 am til 10 pm)
were removed from all collected data sets. An illustration of the data cut out process is
shown in ﬁgure 51.
Figure 51: Illustration of the data processing.
This meant that values measured during nighttime were neglected which would otherwise
have lowered the median of each data set, giving an unrealistic picture of the IEQ during
occupied hours. Furthermore, all results related to a zone were named in accordance with
CAS and not true north, as the building is rotated slightly towards east as pictured in the
case introduction (ﬁgure 1).
4.4.1
Measuring equipment
Data were extracted twice from the Measuring Stations during the measuring period. The
data extraction required a mini USB cable and the HOBO-software to export data directly
from the HOBO-logger’s integrated memory. Each data ﬁle contained CO2 concentration
measurements, air temperature measurements and relative humidity measurements logged
every 5th minute. The data were shortened down to only consider occupancy hours follow-
ing the same procedure as described earlier, additionally an hour were removed from the
Page 55
==PAGE:72==
4. METHOD
data set when changing from summer to winter time in order to not have measurements
with identical time stamps. In addition false values like -1250 ppm were deleted from the
data set, since these would be recorded whenever the power cable was disconnected from
the Measuring Station or the power outlet. Furthermore, the CO2 transmitter on the Mea-
suring Station no. 118 located on the 2nd ﬂoor within the northern zone was malfunction-
ing, and thus had to be removed from the results.
The illuminance measurements were extracted through a SD card from each light meter
in an Excel format. The extraction of data took place at the end of the measuring period,
December 3rd. Each ﬁle contained measurements in 30 second intervals given in the SI
unit lux. The light meter shot down a couple of times during the measuring period due to
dying batteries which had to be changed. Furthermore, unrealistically low values below 50
lux during day time were removed, in order to account for the sensors being covered up by
unaware students.
The noise measurements were extracted from each sound level meter in a similar fashion to
the light meter, through a SD card. However, a data sampling rate of 1 second meant that
the data had to be separated in to two different Excel ﬁles for each sound level meter. The
data were processed in accordance with the deﬁned occupancy hours. However, when com-
paring the noise measurements with the questionnaire responses it was decided to calculate
an average SPL 30 min prior to the hand in of the questionnaire, instead of comparing with
a single value.
4.4.2
Questionnaire
The data from the questionnaires were accessed through two websites, one for the detailed
questionnaire and one for the short questionnaire. Data were exported directly into an Ex-
cel spreadsheet by copy pasting, creating two tables showing every user response. After-
wards all unanswered or otherwise unusable answers were sorted out and removed. Hence,
it was possible to create statistics by multiple diagrams, showing age, gender, user satisfac-
tion, reasons for discomfort etc. Moreover, for the detailed questionnaire, it was possible to

attached Excel ﬁle containing the created tables and charts for both the detailed and short

4.4.3
BMS
For each ventilation zone at the library a CSV trend log ﬁle was exported. Each ﬁle con-
tained data regarding temperature or CO2 concentration. The measurements were logged
continuously every 5th minute during the measuring period. However, data were missing
in a short time period spanning from October 18 until October 21, presumably due to the
change from summer to winter time. Only occupancy hours from 08:00 am until 10:00 pm
were considered.
4.4.4
CAS Sensor Box
It was only possible to obtain useful data from December 7 until December 10 since the
Azure network only allows access to data through time series dating one month back in
time. Due to this issue it was not possible to compare light and noise measurements with
the light meter and sound level meter measurements. Additionally, the output of the noise
measurements conducted by the CAS Sensor Box was given in ADC (Analog to Digital
Conversion), which, without the proper technical sensor speciﬁcations, could not be trans-
lated in to dB. Furthermore, half of the CAS Sensor Boxes were malfunctioning during the
measuring period, and did not provide any data. In terms of the data which were available,
the data were extracted in CSV format with a data sample rate of 12 minutes. The data set
contained measurement of illuminance, air temperature and relative humidity.

6. DISCUSSION

the overall indoor climate at the DTU Smart Library. Then, an assessment of the BMS and
CAS data, followed by a review of the advantages and disadvantages of the two technolo-
gies. Next, IoT will be set into perspective, considering other environments to apply the
feature. Last, possible future work within the area of this master thesis will be listed.

==PAGE:106==
6. DISCUSSION
6.1
Assessment of the overall indoor environment at the DTU Smart Li-
brary
In this section an assessment of the overall indoor environment will be introduced. Subse-
quently the results will be compared and discussed in relation to standards and studies.
6.1.1
Thermal comfort
The room temperatures at the 1st ﬂoor were more or less kept within DS/CEN 16798 (2017)
category I or II during the entire measuring period. However, 14% of the students still in-
dicated that they were dissatisﬁed with the temperature. The 2nd ﬂoor had the most tem-
perature measurements within category III and IV, which is reﬂected in the questionnaire
responses, showing that 27% were dissatisﬁed with the temperature. Most of those dissat-
isﬁed preferred a lower temperature, but still some also preferred an even higher temper-
ature. The metabolism and personal preferences vary from person to person and thus this
had to be expected. The high temperatures at the 2nd ﬂoor might be caused by a mix of the
high number of students occupying the space and the many desktop computers. The 3rd
ﬂoor thermal environment looked similar to that of the 1st ﬂoor, however the spread of data
was larger and more high/low temperatures were measured. This again was reﬂected in the
questionnaire responses where 25% of the students felt dissatisﬁed with the room temper-
ature. A quick ﬁx would be to adjust the supply temperature of the radiators and lower the
temperature set point for the ventilation system on both ﬂoors.
The PMV and PPD calculations were mostly in category III and IV of DS/CEN 16798
(2017) predicting 6 to 20% of the students to feel slightly warm and thus dissatisﬁed with
the temperature. Compared with the questionnaire responses, this was not too far off since
19% of those surveyed preferred a lower temperature. However, the calculated PPD had a
median at 12%, which was slightly lower than the questionnaire responses. The explanation
might lie with how spot measurements were carried out. Several of the measurements were
done close to hallways, where the measured air velocities might not have been representa-
tive. In the PPD calculations the slightly elevated air velocities make higher temperatures
more tolerable.
Page 90
==PAGE:107==
6. DISCUSSION
6.1.2
Visual comfort
In terms of lighting, the measurements showed illuminance medians around 300 to 450
lux for all zones except for the 2nd ﬂoor’s north zone, which had a median at 585 lux. As
explained in the case introduction, each zone is controlled by a daylight sensor which reg-
ulates the artiﬁcial lighting in order to deliver 300 lux within the occupied zone. However,
the high illuminance levels measured during the evening hours at the 2nd and 3rd ﬂoor’s
north zones suggest that the artiﬁcial lighting control was either malfunctioning or that it
was not set up properly.
6.1.3
Acoustic comfort
In general, the SPL was too high at the library when comparing with WEA guidelines. 30%
of the time the SPL was measured to be above the recommended 50 dB [40]. DTU is well
aware of the acoustical challenges but might not be aware of their exact extent. The acous-
tic improvements made since the 2017 renovation, may have helped, but they are still not
sufﬁcient. The acoustical issue is a delicate one, since the surface area where absorbers are
applicable, is very limited. An acoustic suspended ceiling has already been implemented,
facades are mostly covered with windows, and the ﬂoor cannot be covered by carpets due
to the large inconvenience of cleaning and maintaining such a large surface. However, one
large improvement might be to replace the hardwood radiator covers with a better sound
absorbing material. Another improvement could be to coat the hard-surfaced structural
columns with a material better suited for absorbing sound.
6.1.4
Air quality
At the 3rd ﬂoor’s north and south zones concentrations were in more than 20% of the time
above 900 ppm corresponding to DS/CEN 16798 (2017) category III. The reason for the
higher CO2 concentrations might be a result of the newly installed glass facade facing the
atrium. The ﬂoor is hence no more part of the large open area and thus it is feasible to be-
lieve that the concentrations have increased because of that. However, what is peculiar is
that students seemed less dissatisﬁed with the air quality at the 3rd ﬂoor compared to the 1st
and 2nd ﬂoor. This could, however, be explained by students getting accustomed to the air
quality before reaching the top ﬂoor, contrary to students who just entered the library.
Page 91
==PAGE:108==
6. DISCUSSION
6.1.5
Sick building symptoms
According to the literature, studies suggest that warmth lowers arousal and has a negative
effect on cognitive thinking as well as a negative impact on the motivation to exert effort
[67]. The ﬁndings of the subjective and physical measurements seem to prove this theory.
As it was seen from the measurements, room temperatures were during the measuring pe-
riod often far beyond the recommended limit value stated in DS/CEN 16798 (2017), which
might explain why 56.3% of the students indicated that they felt tired.
38.4% of the students indicated that they felt it was hard to concentrate. In some cases, this
could also be caused by elevated temperatures as described in the literature [67], however,
it is more likely that noise was the main contributor, since a total of 45% of the those sur-
veyed indicated noise as bothering. Out of those dissatisﬁed, 79% pointed out that nearby
conversation was the most distracting factor. In large open group work environments, this
is unavoidable. However, the issue should be considered and addressed in areas where stu-
dents work individually, since students who perform individual complex cognitive tasks,
such as studying for an exam, reading, or writing are particularly disturbed by noise [10].
The questionnaire did also show that students located in the silent zone would be more sen-
sitive to noise. A solution to this problem might be to physically separate silent zones or
zones for individual studies, with walls of glass or partition walls, since a simultaneous use
of an open plan environment for both group work and individual studies creates an acous-
tic dilemma [10]. The effects of implementing such a physical barrier would most likely
immediately lower the background noise and annoyance of nearby conversation within the
silent zone.
25% of those surveyed indicated that they had irritated eyes. This is hard to explain since
such adverse perceptions are often associated with the intensity and CCT of the lighting
[58]. However, the lighting was indicated as the least dissatisfying IEQ parameter with only
12% dissatisﬁed. In terms of the illuminance, measurements showed medians around 300
to 400 lux, which complies with DS/CEN 16798 (2017) and expert recommendations for
open plan environments such as the library [21]. Further, the CCT was adjusted during the
day according to the natural daylight [24].
Page 92
==PAGE:109==
6. DISCUSSION
6.1.6
A world class study environment
As seen from the results the DTU Smart Library complied with category I or II of the DS/-
CEN 16798 (2017) during most of the measuring period. However, as seen from the lit-
erature study even more ambitious standards exist. Thus if the scope is to create the best
possible study environment, then complying with DS 3033 (2015) category A++ should
be recommended. The distinction from category I to Class A++, is mainly that individual
control should be obtainable. As mentioned in section 3, CAS is already testing individ-
ual control of lighting which is a big step of the way. However, further implementation of
individual control of the different indoor environmental parameters still have to be made
available.
6.1.7
The short questionnaire as a tool for the DTU Smart Library
The short questionnaire responses showed to be quite similar to the more detailed ones,
with only minor deviations. This indicates that the short questionnaire gives a quite reli-
able representation of student satisfaction with the IEQ at the library. However, in terms of
the number of answers, the short questionnaire did not provide an overﬂow of responses,
even though the stand with the iPad was located right at the library’s main entrance, where
students pass several times within a day. The explanation may be that students might have
overlooked the stand, students did not have time when leaving the library, or students were
well satisﬁed with the indoor environment, and therefore did not care to answer the ques-
tionnaire. Furthermore, a complication with the iPad did not help either. The complication
was, if no response was received within eight hours, the questionnaire would shut down,
which meant the system had to be reset before a new response could be recorded.
6.2
IoT vs. traditional BMS monitoring at the indoor environment at DTU
Smart Library
The results of the BMS and CAS Sensor Boxes data, which were compared to measure-
ments, will in this section be assessed and considered. Moreover, pros and cons will be dis-
cussed.
Page 93
==PAGE:110==
6. DISCUSSION
6.2.1
Traditional BMS
A comparison between the BMS and the measurements in section 5, showed that the data
did not agree well. The best matching temperatures deviated approximately ±1◦C, and
the best matching CO2 concentrations deviated approximately ±200 ppm. However, in
the worst cases if a Measuring Station measured 23◦C, then the corresponding BMS sen-
sor would indicate the temperature to be anything between 20 and 24◦C. An explanation of
the discrepancy is that the BMS sensors were located within the main extraction ventilation
ducts of each zone, providing average measurements for the entire zone in opposition to the
Measuring Stations, which measured the IEQ at a smaller better deﬁned space and there-
fore was affected by more local conditions. As seen from the results, the temperature varied
considerably across the open ﬂoor plans - even within the same zone. The reason might be
that some Measuring Stations were placed close to the outer facade, in opposition to others
being placed closer to the middle; some were placed in areas with lots of desktop comput-
ers emitting heat, in opposition to areas without desktop computers, and so forth. In regards
to CO2 concentrations the difference between measurements and BMS should not be as
evident, considering that CO2 concentrations might be more or less uniformly distributed
within an open area if full mixing of air is assumed. However, this was not the case in some
parts of the library e.g. the 1st ﬂoor’s west zone where a Measuring Station would measure
anything between 400 and 1300 ppm, while the BMS measured 450 ppm. However, BMS
sensors are expensive and thus only a bare minimum is often used for controlling the IEQ.
Taking this into consideration, the utilized measuring method might be the most efﬁcient
way of creating a uniform indoor environment, since randomly placing one single BMS
temperature sensor within a large occupied space may mean even greater non-uniform IEQ
throughout the zone.
6.2.2
IoT
In general, the Sensor Box data, which were compared to our own measurements, showed
to be very unreliable. Around half of the Sensor Boxes were out of order, and of those
recording data only few measured values remotely close to our own measurements. How-
ever, of those CAS sensors which actually worked properly, the best complying data still
showed a reasonable correlation between the measured values of the Sensor Box and the
Page 94
==PAGE:111==
6. DISCUSSION
values of the Measuring Station. This showed that the Sensor Boxes -including all internal
components- if actually working and being calibrated properly, might have some potential
after all. However, theory is one thing - and practice is another. The Sensor Boxes did in
fact not work properly, indicating that the sensor quality might be too poor or too fragile.
Furthermore, the Sensor Boxes also mainly measured rounded integers, which might seem
undesirable when especially working with temperature control. However, the context is im-
portant: If a correlation exists in the data, if decimal numbers are redundant, and if sensors
at least measure close to what they are supposed to, then such sensors might be sufﬁcient.
The CAS Sensor Boxes were attached to the bottom side of the lighting tracks, close to
ceiling height. The Sensor Boxes might better have been placed within the occupied zone,
since this would have had created a more representative picture of the actual working envi-
ronment of the students. However, this is a matter of aesthetics and where the Sensor Boxes
are not dominating or inﬂuencing the overall impression of the environment.
In terms of collecting and analyzing the Sensor Box data, it turned out to be more chal-
lenging than ﬁrst anticipated. Because of the various angles, several internal stakeholders
from various departments at DTU, were involved in the Sensor Box project. From an out-
sider perspective, the downside of this was little overview and understanding at a local level
meaning gaining slower access, despite the positive attitude of all people involved. E.g. this
meant for an outsider with no broader programming experience, that data were not easily
accessible through the Azure network, due to the convoluted interface. Further, time series
data were only accessible one month back, which might be ﬁne for here and now analyses
but makes it difﬁcult for tracking and working with data over longer periods of time.
As discussed in section 6.2.1, the traditional BMS may be the best option when control-
ling large open plan environments with few expensive sensors, but it is still very inﬂexible.
The great beneﬁts of the IoT technology is that it is inexpensive and that it is wirelessly
connected to the internet [52, 29]. Thus, by implementing IoT into the BMS, numerous of
sensors could be deployed within the occupied zone splitting the large zones into several
smaller and more local zones. With this ”micro” climate control the sensors would provide
more representative measurements within each zone, and thus would create an indoor en-
Page 95
==PAGE:112==
6. DISCUSSION
vironment which can be easier adapted to different IEQ preferences in opposition to the
current more uniform indoor environment. In addition, the IoT might provide energy sav-
ings, due to the HVAC system being optimized and thus only addressing the loads within
each speciﬁc smaller zone.
Nevertheless, there still seems to be a huge ﬂip side to these cheap IoT sensors, and thus
this still might not be the most optimal solution. Working with the CAS Sensor Boxes
showed that sometimes ”cheap” might be too cheap. The whole Sensor Box process indi-
cated that cheap IoT is not a ”plug and play” technology, as often advertised. It requires
a huge amount of effort in order to implement, and further it needs to be well managed,
thought through, and made sure that the chosen IoT sensors operate properly and measures
accurately enough for the desired purpose.
6.3
Other environments which could beneﬁt of IoT implementation
In this master thesis, the main focus has been on the use of IoT as a way of improving the
indoor environment at the DTU Smart Library. Yet, the technology could probably be im-
plemented within other similar indoor environments. In the following a couple of examples
is presented.
6.3.1
Other study environments
Universities, study halls and other similar study environments, most often contain several
open plan environments. The zones in such areas, usually have different and individual
optimal requirements considering the indoor climate parameters, due to the various sur-
roundings of occupants, ofﬁce equipment, windows etc., just as it is at DTU Smart Library.
For this reason, IoT would be an evident feature to implement when controlling the indoor
environment.
6.3.2
Ofﬁce buildings
Several of ofﬁce buildings nowadays consist of open plan ofﬁces. These open plans contain
hallways, ofﬁce desks close to or far from windows, meeting areas, lounge areas etc. The
zones within such an open plan ofﬁce, will experience very different loads, which provide a
need for local adaptation. Thus, IoT would be an evident technology to apply.

6.4
Future work
Future work for developing at better indoor environment for students at DTU Smart Library
through IoT, could be some of the following points listed below.
• From ﬁeld measurements to implementation; Now that the potential and limita-
tions of the IoT concept have been uncovered the next natural step is to implement it
in a BMS and test its ability to micro manage multiple zones at once.
• Automation of student IEQ preferences; This could for instance involve having
students download an IEQ preference app, which would change the indoor climate of
a zone in accordance with the average student preferences of that particular zone.
• Optimization of energy consumption from building installations with IoT.

7. CONCLUSION
It can be concluded that with IoT it is possible to improve the indoor environment. How-
ever, it is found to be a very complex process, which requires proper management and
lots of effort. By comparing BMS measurements with own measurements a deviation was
found, due to the BMS sensors measuring average values, whereas the Measuring Stations
would measure more local values within the occupied zone. This is where the IoT has its
potential, since it would be possible to place several IoT sensors within the occupied space
dividing the traditional larger BMS zones into smaller ones, where the IEQ is controlled
more locally. The implementation of IoT, however, has proved not to be as straightforward
as the technology often is presented as. First and foremost, it must be ensured that the in-
expensive IoT sensors measure accurately enough to fulﬁll its desired purpose. Lastly, it is
important to focus on how data is accessed and stored. If the data is to be used for anything
other than set point control, then it is important that the way in which the data is accessed is
easily transparent for non programmers like students, researchers etc.
Overall the indoor environment at the DTU Smart Library seemed to be satisfactory in
terms of the DS/CEN 16798 (2017) and WEA, and according to the student responses.
Temperatures were mostly kept within DS/CEN 16798 (2017) category I and II at the 1st
and 3rd ﬂoor. The temperatures at the 2nd ﬂoor, were during longer periods of time elevated
above 24◦C. The calculated draught rates were consistent with the ﬁndings of the question-
naire where only 5% of the students indicated that they felt draught. The relative humidity
was found to ﬂuctuate between 20 and 60%, which complies with WEA guidelines, and
was to be as expected during the winter period. PMV and PPD calculations were more or
less consistent with the questionnaire replies, where 23% indicated that they were dissatis-
ﬁed with the temperature, of those dissatisﬁed most preferred a lower temperature. In order
to address this issue, it was suggested that set points for ventilation and supply temperature
of radiators were adjusted accordingly.
The artiﬁcial lighting was the one IEQ parameter which students were the least bothered
with. The measured illumination levels complied most of the time with the 300 lux require-
ment set for adult education in DS/CEN 16798 (2017). However, it was suggested that fur-
ther investigations into the daylight control had to be done in order to optimize the regula-
tion of the artiﬁcial lighting.
Page 100
==PAGE:117==
7. CONCLUSION
The sound pressure levels at the library’s 2nd and 3rd ﬂoor were in general too high with
medians close to 50 dB which is the upper recommended limit by WEA. Students described
nearby conversation as the main reason for dissatisfaction. Furthermore, a comparison of
questionnaire responses and SPL measurements showed that students sitting in the silent
zone were more sensitive to noise. Thus, it was suggested that a physical barrier should be
made to separate silent and group environments from each other.
The CO2 concentrations on the 1st and 2nd ﬂoor were within DS/CEN 16798 (2017) cate-
gory II during most of the measuring period. The 3rd ﬂoor, on the other hand, had concen-
trations above 900 ppm in around 25% of the measuring period corresponding to DS/CEN
16798 (2017) category III. However, most students were dissatisﬁed with the air quality at
the 1st and 2nd ﬂoor, due to students having gotten accustomed to the air quality on the 3rd
ﬂoor, contrary to students who just entered the library.

First interview with Lene Kuszon
Can you tell us a little about the library?
The artiﬁcial lighting is divided in to 10 zones at each ﬂoor except of the top ﬂoor where it
is only 7. The colour temperature and intensity of the lighting varies over the day depend-
ing on the natural daylight. The natural daylight is measured by 3 sensors at the 2nd ﬂoor
facing north, south and west.
Can you describe the library conditions before the renovation, was it a good indoor envi-
ronment?
We had a lot of complains from students about the 3rd ﬂoor being to warm and noisy. In
addition the 3rd ﬂoor ventilation system did not seem to work properly at the time.
When did DTU start to focus on creating a better indoor climate at the library?
In the beginning the renovation was planned to only focus on creating sufﬁcient ﬁre escape
routes. However, if we were going to make a huge renovation we might as well ﬁx some of
the other issues. This included looking at the indoor environment.
What was the design criteria for the renovation?
Non that i know off.
Has the indoor environment improved after the renovation?
yes, a lot have improved. In terms of acoustics a coating of stucco plaster have been ap-
plied to the underside of the walking bridge in the atrium and all ceiling sheets have been
replaced with thicker sheets. Further a glass facade has been implemented all around the
atrium at the top ﬂoor. The glass is intended to shield off noise and lower the temperature
at the 3rd ﬂoor. Other than that we intend to cover the walls outside of the group rooms at
the top ﬂoor with acoustic absorbers disguised as large images. All this was planned ahead
of the renovation, however as the building installations were exposed during the renova-

test showed that the main ducts had developed so many leaks throughout the systems ser-

Do you experience any issues after the renovation?
yes, as a result of the new LED lighting, the atrium isn’t as well lit as before the renova-

Can you explain a little about how the BMS works?

The ventilation system is divided in to 4 different zones at each ﬂoor controlled by set points

I don’t really know much about them to be honest. You will have to talk to those that put
