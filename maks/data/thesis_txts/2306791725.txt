0. Abstract

Abstract 
TMS is a widely used technique for stimulating the brain non-invasively. It is possible to 
stimulate the cortical neurons down to ~1 cm in focality without damaging the skin or the 
brain. It has been applied to both in research and in therapy. The big issue however is 
that the results are highly variable. One case where this is visible is the stimulation of the 
primary motor area (M1), which results in a MEP. The size of the MEP is highly variably, 
even when stimulating the same area with the same intensity. It is therefore believed 
that other factors must play a role in how the resulting MEP takes shape. One hypothesis 
is that the phase of EEG carries enough information about the brains state to be able to 
somewhat predict the resulting size of the MEP, and previous work has shown significant 
correlation between the two. If this is true it would be possible to control the 
input/output of the TMS setup, not only increasing the reliability, but maybe also lead to 
more optimal stimulation. The aim of this project therefore two-fold: First to find a 
suitable and reliable technique for estimation of phase in EEG data, and acquire 
necessary insight of merits and inherent limitations of the techniques. Lastly, the 
technique will be used to establish the connection between EEG phase and MEP size. Out 
of the 3 different methods investigated: the Hilbert transform, an online maximum 
likelihood based pitchtracking algorithm and the Morlet wavelet. Investigations indicated 
that the Morlet wavelet was superior in terms of reliability and also making it easier to 
perform comparisons to earlier work. Based on these estimations the connection 
between the phase and the MEP size was investigated. Under specific circumstances of 
high alpha power and with certain subjects, the correlation was significant; we did not 
find that EEG phase estimation was sufficient for prediction of MEP size on its own. To 
summarize, the phase estimation technique developed appears to be a good tool for 
performing phase estimation on EEG or other noisy data, but information on the EEG 
phase does not provide sufficient information to provide accurate predictions of MEP 
amplitude alone.

1. Introduction 
Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) has recently seen a big growth in interest and 
use both in research and therapy since it can modulate cortical excitability. However, one 
of the problems with this method is that the effects are highly variable (Ziemann, 2008) 
and that TMS effects can often only be measured indirectly through behavioural changes. 
One setup that allows measuring TMS effects more directly is stimulating the M1 and 
then measuring the resulting muscle twitch called the Muscle Evoked Potential (MEP). 
However, also MEPs have high variability between subjects and even within the same 
subject using the same stimulation intensity, maintaining the same relative location of 
the coil to the head and same rotation. There are many known sources of TMS variability 
(Ridding, et al., 2010) but one of the hypothesis that has been gaining support lately is 
that brain state fluctuations influence MEP variability (Bergmann, et al., 2012). Neural 
oscillations, measured with EEG might be able to give an indication of the Brain State with 
oscillation frequency bands such as Alpha or Beta waves might be reflecting changes in 
the underlying state. There is evidence suggesting that the power of Alpha and Beta 
waves respectively is correlated to the resulting MEP size (Takemi, et al., 2013;  Schulz, et 
al., 2013; and Karabanov, et al., 2016) and to a lesser extent the same is true for Alpha 
and Beta phase (Berger, et al., 2014; Mäki, et al., 2010). The aim of this study is therefore 
first of all to investigate different methods for estimating the phase of Alpha or Beta 
waves in EEG data. When the performance of these methods has been established and 
tested thoroughly, the next goal is to try and implement one or more of the methods in 
an experiment using post-hoc sorting of MEPs based on the calculated phase to try and 
investigate if any correlation between the two parameters can be established, as has 
been suggested in previous work. The end-goal of the project is to get a step closer to a 
brain state triggered system, where the output is controllable to a larger extent such that 
the variability is minimized (Bergmann, et al., 2012).

2. Background 
2.1 
Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) 
TMS is a technique that can stimulate neurones in a relatively focal area directly beneath 
the scalp non-invasively via electromagnetic induction (Bailey, et al., 2001). TMS is 
performed by placing a coil on head, above the intended target area. A strong, time-
varying electro-magnetic field is generated by the coil and translates to induced current in 
the order of 10-100 mV/mm in the tissue and often the depolarization of neurons (Bailey, 
et al., 2001). Depending on the chosen intensity TMS can induce action potentials in the 
stimulated neurons. Different coil shapes are available. Different coil shapes are available 
for different purposes. The oldest is the round coil, which induces a mirror imaged circle 
of stimulation in the tissue. For this project we used the figure-of-eight coil, which has a 
much more focalized generated field as seen in Figure 1 (Bailey, et al., 2001). 
One of the most common TMS measures is the Motor Evoked Potential (MEP), recorded 
from the muscle following TMS stimulation of the primary motor cortex (M1). With a high 
enough stimulation intensity, neurons in M1 can be depolarized beyond firing threshold 
and thereby produce an action potential that travels along the corticospinal tract and 
initiates muscle contractions. The amplitude of the resulting MEP is taken to reflect 
cortical excitability, but displays both a large inter- and intra-individual variability.  
MEPs reflect corticospinal excitability at the moment of stimulation. However, the path 
from the activated neurons goes from the M1 primary motor area through a 
monosynaptic relay down to the anterior horn in the spinal cord. Here it synapses with an 
interneuron, which then again synapses with lower motor neurons going to the muscle 
and ending in the motor unit where it finally activates the muscle fibers. At all these

and a figure-of-eight coil. We see the focality is greater in the 2nd coil.

stages the individual excitability plays a role in changing the size of the resulting MEP 
(Seeley, et al., 2011).  
There are a number of additional factors that influence the MEP to some extent. Most 
importantly (and quite logically) is the position and orientation of the coil, where even a 
small movement away from the hotspot can greatly reduce the MEP size. Another very 
important factor is facilitation, which is about background contraction of the muscle. It 
has been shown that background contraction of the same muscle stimulated with TMS, 
greatly increases the MEP response to the TMS. It is therefore important that you keep 
the contraction constant (either present or absent) during the experiment. In this project 
we did not want facilitation, and therefore discarded MEPs where background activity 
was present. A number of other factors have been shown to have a sizeable effect (up to 
±40 % change) on the MEP size like: Regular exercise, age, attention, sex, time of day, 
synaptic history and genetics (Ridding, et al., 2010). Other factors that have been shown 
to also influence the MEP size, although to a lesser extent are: Imaginary movement, 
Speech and the amount of muscle stretch (Ziemann, 2008). These are however easily 
controllable in most cases, which makes them less of a problem. 
 
 
2.2 
EEG 
The neurons in the brain communicate with two mechanisms (Seeley, et al., 2011). The 
first is using action potentials that are electrical in nature and propagates along the axis 
of the neuron. The second is purely chemical and happens between neurons at the 
synapses. Electroencephalography aims at registering the electrical part of the 
communication of neurons. 
The electrical potential generated by a single neuron is far too weak to be picked up by 
the scalp electrodes used for EEG. What you can measure however, is the summation of 
thousands or even millions of neurons, when they synchronize enough for the 
constructive interference to add up to a measurable signal. For this to happen the 
neurons need to be somewhat spatially oriented in a similar fashion and also be located 
close to the scalp since the electrical field generated drops by the square of the distance 
to the source.  
Traditionally you can classify the EEG activity based on the frequency of the detectable 
signal. The exact span of each class is not always agreed upon, but roughly can be 
separated into: Delta (0.2-4 Hz), Theta (4-7 Hz), Alpha and Mu (8-13 Hz), Beta (13-30 Hz) 
and Gamma (30+ Hz). These classes of frequencies are defined purely on observation 
without any direct physiological explanation. However, research of EEG spectral 
parameters (often changes in power in a specific band) have shown that these specific 
==PAGE:10==
2. Background 
                                                                            Page 10 of 89 
frequency bands have importance for specific motor and cognitive tasks and it is believed 
that oscillations organize information flow (Silva, 2013) . For motor control, the changes 
in power have been most noticeably seen in Alpha and Beta frequency bands and are 
believed to be caused by event related synchronization and desynchronization (Silva, 
2013). For this study particularly Beta, Alpha and Mu rhythms are therefore of particular 
interest. 
 
2.3 
TMS-EEG 
The potential benefits of combining the two techniques are manifold: With TMS we have 
the unique possibility to noninvasively activate neurons, and with EEG it is possible to see 
how almost the entire brain responds to this focal activation with a great temporal 
resolution both up to and shortly after the TMS pulse has been given. There is evidence 
that phase and amplitude play a role in determining the variability of the MEP response 
(Karabanov, et al., 2016;Mäki, et al., 2010;Berger, et al., 2014;Bergmann, et al., 2012). 
Using a phase-triggered TMS paradigm Bergmann could convincingly show that MEPs are 
larger when given in the up-phase when compared with the down-phase of slow sleep 
oscillations, but in alpha and beta the relationships shown using offline phase-sorting 
approaches are not that simple or strong. 
However, the use of TMS and EEG simultaneously also has some problems. The EEG 
electrodes record electrical activity and TMS induces an E-field on the scalp orders of 
magnitudes larger than what is biologically created, there are of course some large 
artefacts picked up by the EEG electrodes. The artefact is usually two-fold: there is the 
initial spike from the stimulation, which has a huge amplitude compared to the 
background EEG and then there is the following baseline offset which can last up to 
several hundred milliseconds. Different approaches have traditionally been applied to try 
and handle these artefacts. Some EEG recording systems are using the TMS trigger to 
switch off the amplifier for a few ms just before the pulse and during the stimulation. This 
effectively removes the artefact, but of course makes it analyse the EEG signal during this 
period. Other systems try and sample the artefact with a high sampling frequency and a 
high dynamic range, such that it does not saturate the amplifiers and the EEG can be 
recorded continuously. This of course leaves us with the trouble of trying to remove the 
artefact afterwards, which is not a trivial task.  
Beyond these artefacts, two additional artefacts can typically also be present, namely the 
muscle artefact and the auditory induced artefact induced by the click-sound coming 
from the pulse of the TMS coil. The muscle artefact is greatly dependent on where you 
stimulate and is often more expressed if the stimulation occurs lateral rather than 
central. Masking of the click-sound for the auditory artefact has been attempted with 
varying success by using headphones and playing white noise with a similar spectrum to

3. Methods 
3.1 
Simulations 
The first aim of this thesis was to find a phase estimation technique that allows for 
reliable EEG phase extraction. This is investigated by evaluating 3 different phase 
estimation techniques on simulated data.  
3.1.1 Phase estimators 
The real goal of any phase estimator is to calculate the phase of a signal for all time 
points. This is impossible due to Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle, which, for time-
frequency analysis, means that one cannot know the frequency (or phase) of a single data 
point. Therefore you have to assume that the frequency is somewhat stable over multiple 
data points, and base you estimations on all these. This means that there is always a 
trade-off between temporal resolution and frequency resolution when performing this 
kind of analysis.  
To investigate and establish a good method for performing phase estimation for our 
specific purpose of post-hoc MEP sorting, three different approaches were investigated. 
These were 1: the Morlet wavelet approach also used in previous work (Berger, et al., 
2014), 2: the Pitchtracker, an in-house developed algorithm for online phase estimation 
based on previous work (Christensen, 2012) and 3: an approach based on the Hilbert 
transform, which is often a go-to method for phase estimation in many situations. 
 
3.1.1.1

based on the Fourier spectrum and therefore does not have a resolution that scales with 
different frequencies i.e. it will always have the same resolution for all frequencies 
because they will all be based on the same window.

3.2 
Experimental work 
The second aim of the thesis is to establish whether there is a connection between EEG 
phase and MEP amplitude. To investigate this connection, an experimental setup was 
devised to investigate this connection. 
TMS-EEG data was recorded from 7 subjects total in the age of 25-35 with 5 male and 2 
female subjects. Each participant received 300 monophasic TMS pulse at the individual 
motor threshold intensity delivered by a standard figure-of-eight coil (MagVenture B70) 
connected to a MagVenture X100 stimulator (MagVenture, Farum, Denmark)  while EEG 
was recorded simultaneously. Pulses were delivered to the hot spot of the left M1 with a 
posterior-anterior current direction in the brain. To minimize any carry-over effect from 
one trial to the next, the time between each pulse was jittered uniformly distributed to 
intervals between 4 and 6 seconds. It was the hope that this would also uniformly cover 
all parts of the phase cycle randomly.

3.2.1 TMS protocol

was employed (Localite, Sankt Augustin, Germany). At the beginning of a navigation

of the First Dorsal Interosseous (FDI) on the M1 handknob by giving TMS pulses with the 
coil over different positions over and close to the hand knob and comparing the resulting 
MEP amplitude. The location and orientation yielding the largest MEP amplitude was

3.2.3 Source projection 
Source projection is a method where you calculate a specific set of weights for all you EEG 
channels, such that when you multiply them onto you channels, and add them all up, you 
get a single signal, which (hopefully) contains primarily the signal from that (or those) 
sources (Bigdely-Shamlo, et al., 2013).  
In this project we have a very strong hypothesis where the source is located, since we are 
targeting a specific area on the cortex with the TMS, and we expect the output to come 
from the same area. For this reason, and because it will make it easier to do comparisons 
to the Berger paper, we therefore deem it very sensible to use source projection 
software. For this project a source projection algorithm developed by Leo Tomasevic was 
used to estimate the source activity.  
 
3.2.4 Resting motor threshold hunting 
During the experiment the resting motor threshold was estimated before each MEP 
block, in order to compensate for biological drifts and ensure that the stimulation 
intensity results in 50 % supra-threshold MEPs. For this purpose an algorithm developed 
by Friedemann Awiszus (Awiszus, 2003), which estimates the probability of invoking a 
MEP with a given intensity based on Parameter Estimation by Sequential Testing (PEST) 
and Maximum likelihood regression. This method is considered highly efficient way of 
estimating the correct intensity and reduces the number of required pulses compared to 
other methods (Groppa, et al., 2012).

When detecting the size of the MEP it is important that there is no prior muscle activity

3.2.5.2 
The MEP GUI 
The following section describes the GUI developed during the project to calculate the 
MEP amplitudes from the data collected.  
The GUI implements the MEPAnalyser function, which was also written for the project 
for detecting the MEP size. The function has 3 different methods for detecting the peaks 
and troughs, but the one that was used in the end was a very simple method. The 
algorithm simply picks the largest and the smallest value in a certain time window, which 
ended up yielding the closest results to what the doctors would pick. It also had the 
lowest amount of false negatives, which was very useful since the results needed human 
inspection anyway.   
The GUI starts by running the MEPAnalyser peak detection algorithm, and if the is a 
significant MEP (peak-to-peak value above 50 μV AND no activity in the 100 ms prior to 
stimulation bigger than the allowed threshold), it is presented to the user for final human 
approval. Since the algorithm gets it right in most of the cases, the default highlighted

downsampled to 500 Hz enough to give detailed information about alpha and beta

by simply taking every 10th datapoint and storing only those and then the data is ready for 
phase estimation.

3.2.9 Phase estimation 
For the real EEG it was decided that the Morlet wavelet should be used as the phase 
estimator. After pre-processing the EEG data is passed to the MorletPhaseAnalysis 
function (see appendix). This makes a segment based on the TMS trigger locations and 
the 500 ms prior to stimulus up to 1 ms prior to stimulus. This is a total of 250 points with 
a Fs of 500 Hz. The reason to only go to 1 ms prior to stimulus and not 0 ms, is that there 
might be some slight inaccuracies in the synchronization between the stimulator and the 
data recording system and indeed if the EEG is inspected you see in some cases that the 
TMS artefact starts rising already at 1 ms prior to where it should be (at 0 ms). Since the 
TMS artefact to can disturb the phase estimation, it is essential to only use datapoint 
prior to where the artefact is located.

4.1.1.3 
The Morlet Wavelet 
When using the cwtft function for performing the morlet wavelet transformation, it is 
important to realize that you need to scale the frequencies that you want to investigate, 
before performing the transformation. The frequencies that you give as input, are not the 
exact same frequencies that will be calculated. Therefore you have to scale them with 
what they call the Morlet Fourier Factor. In the documentation for the cwtft function 
they give the scaling factor when using the default morl setting, which gives the analytic 
signal. In this case it is 4*pi/(6+sqrt(2+6^2) (Torrence, et al., 1998), but when using the 
morlex setting, it is in fact equal to 6/(2*pi). You simply have to take the inverse of your 
frequencies and multiply them with this factor, and use this as the input for the function. 
This can also be confirmed by inspecting the struct that is returned, where it states, which 
frequencies were investigated. 
In order to verify that it scales correctly a signal was generated with a 10 Hz sinusoid of 
length 10 seconds, and then the Morlet Wavelet transform was calculated at 30 different 
frequencies evenly spaced between 7 and 13 Hz (both included). In Figure 19 below you 
can see the absolute value of the Morlet wavelets generated from the 10 Hz signal.

4.1.2 Intermediate discussion – Simulated Data 
The goal of the simulated data was twofold: First of all to make an informed decision on 
which method to use for the real EEG data and secondly, to investigate the chosen 
method and its strengths and weaknesses.  
The Hilbert transform does not give reliable results on raw data and requires heavy 
filtering, in such a way, that only one frequency remains in the given window of 
investigation. This has drawbacks for our purposes, since filtering of the data would take 
time, which is always a problem when trying to make real-time systems. Secondly there 
are edge effects from any filtering, and this will inevitably tweak the results of any later 
analysis. Also the filtering itself will impose strong assumptions upon the signal, which 
might affect the outcome in an unpredictable way. Little is actually known about the basic 
mechanisms of the EEG waves we record and interpret as stable frequencies (Silva, 2013). 
They might be possessing properties that are unfamiliar to normal signal processing 
paradigms like sudden phase resetting or reduction/increase in power. And if this was to 
be the case, filtering the signal might have unwanted consequences and it would 
therefore be preferable if it could be avoided. Even when applied to the filtered version 
of the simulated data the std. error of the estimate was still large compared to the other 
two methods being in the order of 0.4 compared to 0.1 for the Morlet wavelet and 0.2 or 
higher for the Pitchtracker depending on the frequency of the signal and other 
parameters. On the other hand the estimate was very stable across frequencies and 
across the window of investigation.  
For the Pitchtracking algorithm it is important to state that a direct comparison of 
performance is not fair. It is a tool developed for online purposes and as such it has 
greater limitations in many aspects compared to the other methods. That being said, the 
greatest drawback was that it was unstable. In some cases it would not just give a bad 
estimate, but simply stop working. In some iterations it never got hold of the frequency 
and therefore the phase estimates would never converge. In these cases it simply gave 
back random numbers.  
For the Morlet wavelet there was a clear performance dependence difference dependent 
on where in the window the estimation was performed. When you estimation was 
performed in the middle of the window, where sufficient data was available to both sides, 
the estimation was very stable and close to the truth, with a slight but constant bias to 
one side. The closer to the edge of the window the estimate was performed the worse 
was the estimate. The bias would not be a problem as long as it is stable enough to simply 
be subtracted. The bias varied some across different frequencies, but seemed to be very 
predictable based on the frequency. As such it does not seem to pose big problems for 
the phase estimation. The edge effect is also to be expected since the Morlet wavelet is 
shaped as it is, with the highest peak in the middle and Gaussian attenuation to both 
==PAGE:42==
4. Results 
                                                                            Page 42 of 89 
sides. From the closer investigation of the phenomenon it was also apparent that it point 
where the power of the estimate broke off corresponded to half the length of the 5-cycle 
Morlet. This means that the deterioration of the phase estimate is happening in a 
predictable way, which makes it less of a problem, because it enables us to only use the 
estimate when we know it is reliable.  
All in all for the purpose of investigating the off-line post-hoc sorting of MEP’s based on 
phase, the Morlet wavelet seems to be the best choice for two reasons: First of all it will 
make it much easier to compare the results to those of previous studies (Berger, et al., 
2014), and second of all it seems to perform well and be easily implemented and 
optimized. Therefore this is the phase estimation technique that will be used for the 
following analysis.

4.2.2 Phase analysis 
Since the Morlet wavelet proved to be the most reliable method for performing phase 
estimation, it was also this technique that was chosen for the further analysis. The first 
analysis was an attempt to recreate the results found in the Berger paper (Berger, et al., 
2014). This is where they calculated the circular-linear correlation coefficient between the 
phases and the amplitude of the corresponding MEP at each timepoint individually. Then 
they plot the mean of this value across subjects together with the standard error of the 
mean. This they compared with surrogate data generated by permuting the labels of 
MEP’s to the phases and then calculating the circular-linear correlation for this dataset as 
well. Then they perform t-tests to see if the two means are statistically different. In the 
end they conclude that it is significant if 10 consecutive t-tests are below 0.05 in p-value. 
Since they found significance in Alpha and Fast beta, and since those would be the 
frequencies that would be interesting for our project as well, those were the ones that 
were investigated.  
Using the same statistical approach as in the Berger paper, significance was only found in 
a single point, namely in the middle part of the Beta which was also the band where they 
found the biggest significance. This can also be seen in the t-test for the Alpha and Beta

4.2.3 Classification – SVR 
Even though the correlation analysis did not show any immediate connection between 
the Alpha or Beta phase and the MEP amplitude, we decided to try test whether a 
classifier might be able to still predict the resulting MEP size due to some hidden factors 
that we might have overlooked. For this purpose we decided to implement the support 
vector regression method. Using the phase estimates as prediction parameters and the 
MEP amplitudes as “labels”, we trained SVR models to predict the size of the MEP based 
on the phase information.  
We chose to do this for each subject individually, since it might increase the chance that it 
may work and at this stage we do not have a high anticipation of it working well. The way 
the test was performed was separating the trials into a testing session containing 10

different tests. For each of these tests a ROC curve was calculated for both groups

group better than guessing randomly. Subject 5, which can be seen in the figure yielded

5. Discussion 
5.1 
Simulated data 
5.1.1 Phase estimators 
The simulations served their purpose of providing information on the limitations and 
strengths of the 3 different approaches.  
Based on the results from these simulations we saw that if we expect to have a low SNR 
or even just multiple frequencies present simultaneously, the Hilbert transform would be 
a poor choice for performing the phase estimation. 
The implementation of the Pitchtracking algorithm did not yield stable enough results and 
the within the scope of this project it did not seem feasible to get it to a stage, where it 
would be the better choice.  
The Morlet wavelet performed fairly well with 90% of the estimates being within ±0.1 rad 
(or ~6°) in the middle zone, even in when faced with a low SNR. Additionally it is the same 
method used in the earlier publication (Berger, et al., 2014), and therefore there was a 
strong argument for using this particular method. Especially since we did not see any 
immediate strong correlation between phase and MEP size, so it seemed wise to stay as 
close as possible to their methods, to see it was possible to even replicate their results or 
not.  
There are of course also some problems to using the Morlet wavelet approach. First of all, 
it is only possible to get a reliable estimate approx. 200-250 ms away from the actual 
stimulation time, and if there was a phase reset or significant change in frequency during 
this period, the phase estimate projected into the future would be much worse than 
presented here. It would however be hard to account for these types errors. 
There is also the fact that it is not the exact same method as in (Berger, et al., 2014), since 
it has been shown that there is a larger error to using the same wavelet for all 
frequencies rather than using one closer to the actual frequency. But since using this 
method should yield phase estimates that are strictly better in almost all cases, it still 
seems a good idea to use this method.

5.2 
Experimental work 
5.2.1 MEP 
It seemed that for at least the first 6 subjects, MEPs were throughout the session at 
resting motor threshold resulting in approximately 50 % of the MEP’s being above 
threshold. The last subject seems to be an outlier and should be excluded from the 
conclusions 
It is interesting that the percentage of supra threshold MEP’s is somewhat constant 
across sessions, since the stimulation intensity is not always constant but readjusted 
using the PEST-algorithm (Awiszus, 2003) after 50 stimuli. This indicates that it is in fact a 
good operating procedure to re-estimate the MRT once in a while, as it seems to be 
vulnerable to slow drifts and subject attention.  
The MEP size detection algorithm and the corresponding GUI also seems to do its job as 
well as can be expected, with only a minimal of MEP’s, where a correction was needed. At 
least for me, the designer, it proved to be an easy and very helpful tool for performing 
the MEP size estimation.  
 
5.2.2 Phase effects 
When applying the same methods as previous publications (Berger, et al., 2014), we see 
somewhat similar results. When using the phase from the Beta activity, we see a circular 
correlation of ~0.1 in a short window 50 ms. and when using the same statistical method 
as in the previous publication, this is also concluded as significant. However, sufficient 
proof that 10 consecutive t-test is actually correcting for the multiple comparisons 
problem is not supplied. The no. 10 seems arbitrarily chosen, and as such there is no 
guarantee that it corrects properly. It is therefore not a strong argument for concluding 
that there is a significant correlation between the two. When we apply a more stringent 
multiple comparisons correction method, that is theoretically validated, we no longer see 
any significance. 
In any case it is also debatable whether a correlation of 0.1 has any relevant potential. If it 
is true that there is causality between the EEG phase and MEP size, but it is so weak as 
the results suggest, it is questionable whether it would be possible to utilize it for any 
closed-loop implementations.  
There does however seem to be a slightly stronger correlation between EEG phase and 
MEP size when only using data points where the relevant power is also above some 
threshold. In general this lead to a 3 fold increase in correlation, which is quite

6. Conclusion 
The first aim of the thesis was to find a phase estimation technique that would give 
reliable phase estimate for performing a post-hoc phase dependent MEP size sorting.  
Concerning the first aim we found the Morlet wavelet to be best suited for our later 
experimental aim since it gave the most stable estimate with the lowest error out of the 3 
approaches that we investigated (the Hilbert transform, the Pitchtracker and the Morlet 
wavelet). It also allowed direct comparison of our experiment with earlier work (Berger, 
et al., 2014). 
The second aim was to to establish a clear relation between MEP amplitude and 
oscillation phase. We wanted to test if stimulating targeted in specific oscillation phases 
would change MEP amplitudes.  Here the conclusion is not as straight forward. On one 
hand we managed to some extent to replicate the results from previous publications 
(Berger, et al., 2014): applying the same statistical methods we were able to replicate 
their finding that there is a weak correlation between high beta frequency and MEP size. 
On the other hand we also conclude that a more stringent data analysis approach is 
needed: 
Our simulations have showed that: 1) the edges cannot be trusted (and there Berger finds 
a lot of correlations), 2) that individual Morlet frequencies should be applied. 
Additionally we believe that more stringent multiple comparison correction should be 
applied. When such a method is applied namely the max/min permutation testing, the 
significance disappears.  
Generally, it remains questionable if 0.1 correlations are useful for future 
implementations for closed-loop systems for phase-dependent TMS triggers. 
It was also not possible to use the phase information to build a SVR classifier that could 
successfully predict the size of the MEP given the time of this project. This also points to 
the conclusion that the connection between EEG phase and MEP size is small, if it exists 
at all. 
However, our investigations showed that phase-MEP correlations are the corresponding 
power of the oscillation frequency. When only selecting the top 50 % of power trials the 
correlation increased by a factor 2-3.  
With the experimental setup that was investigated in this thesis it does not seem like 
there is enough of an effect to be able to control the MEP size based on EEG phase. It 
does however seem that the effect is largely affected by other factors, and these were to 
be mapped properly as well, the effect might be large enough to allow for a truly closed-
loop EEG phase dependent TMS triggered system.

a. Circ_corrcl 
This function is from the CircStat toolbox, and calculates the circular-linear correlation. 
It has minor adaptations to handle multi-dimensional input at once, to increase 
computation speed. 
function [rho pval] = circ_corrcl(alpha, x) 
% 
% [rho pval ts] = circ_corrcc(alpha, x) 
%   Correlation coefficient between one circular and one linear 
random 
%   variable. 
% 
%   Input: 
%     alpha   sample of angles in radians 
%     x       sample of linear random variable 
% 
%   Output: 
%     rho     correlation coefficient 
%     pval    p-value 
% 
% References: 
%     Biostatistical Analysis, J. H. Zar, p. 651 
% 
% PHB 6/7/2008 
% 
% Circular Statistics Toolbox for Matlab 
  
% By Philipp Berens, 2009 
% berens@tuebingen.mpg.de - www.kyb.mpg.de/~berens/circStat.html 
  
  
if size(alpha,1)~=size(x,1) 
  error('Input dimensions do not match.') 
end 
  
n = size(alpha,1); 
  
% compute correlation coefficent for sin and cos independently 
rxs = corr(x,sin(alpha)); 
rxc = corr(x,cos(alpha)); 
rcs=zeros(1,size(alpha,2)); 
for i=1:size(alpha,2) 
rcs(i) = corr(sin(alpha(:,i)),cos(alpha(:,i))); 
end 
  
% compute angular-linear correlation (equ. 27.47) 
rho = sqrt((rxc.^2 + rxs.^2 - 2.*rxc.*rxs.*rcs(end,:))./(1-
rcs(end,:).^2));

pval = 1 - chi2cdf(n.*rho.^2,2);
