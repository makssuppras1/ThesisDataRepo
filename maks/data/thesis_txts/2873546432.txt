ABSTRACT
July 11, 2025
Abstract
As a function of irradiation time, the reactivity in a nuclear reactor is decreasing due to the
combined effect of the consumption of fissile materials and the build-up of neutron poisons.
This master thesis aimed at investigating the impact of actinides and fission products on the
reactivity of the Generation IV Molten Salt Fast Reactor (MSFR) and comparing the results
with the case of current operating commercial Pressurized Water Reactor (PWR). The MSFR
studied in this project operated with a thorium fuel cycle and is started with 233U as initial
fissile load. The Monte-Carlo transport code TRIPOLI-4® was the main simulation tool used
for the study for performing steady-state and depletion simulations on the two corresponding
models. Thanks to the calculation of the reactivity and fuel inventory as a function of bur-
nup, it is possible to assess the individual contribution of isotopes of interest on keff. The
comparison between the PWR and MSFR results clearly highlights the impact of the neutron
energy spectrum on the main reactivity contributor repartition, especially regarding the impact
of 135Xe as a neutron poison. Validation calculations were also performed with Serpent2 for
the MSFR model showing a global good agreement between the results regarding the main
reactivity contributors, which are Nd, Sm, Zr and Cs. The observed discrepancies between the
reactivity-worth values of these contributors can be attributed to the different depletion chains
used by the two codes. The results obtained are also consistent with the available literature.

Introduction
The planet is currently facing a phenomenon of global warming due to human activities, which
needs to be taken into account as a matter of urgency. It therefore seems essential to change
people’s consumption habits. Moreover, global demand for energy is rising steadily, with the
majority coming from fossil fuels such as oil, coal and natural gas, which will account for more
than 80% of global energy production in 2021 [1]. That is why humans need to move away
from fossil fuels and increase the proportion of decarbonised, green energy that is produced.
Nuclear power seems ideally suited to this objective, as it allows the safe production of low
carbon, competitive electricity on a continuous basis as seen in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Safest and cleanest sources of energy [2]
Nuclear power’s share of the energy mix has been multiplied by 1.6 between 1980 and 2021,
reaching 5% of global production [1].
The majority of reactors currently in operation around the world are Generation II reactors,
mainly Pressurised Water Reactors (PWRs) or Boiling Water Reactors (BWRs). Generations
III and III+ comprise large-power reactors of the order of 1,000 MWth. The emphasis is on
enhanced safety, particularly in accident situations.
Most of the nuclear reactors currently
under construction around the world are of Generation III or III+.
Generation IV, which is the latest generation of nuclear reactors, includes reactors currently
under design, which belong to six main technologies selected by the Generation IV International
Forum [3]. The original list of reactor concepts has been reduced to the six most promising
concepts, which have been retained for the research and development phase. The goals of Gen IV
are: improving sustainability, economics, safety and reliability and resistance to proliferation.

One of these Gen-IV concepts is the Molten Salt Reactor (MSR), which includes the Molten
Salt Fast Reactor (MSFR) design [5].
This design was studied in the context of different
international collaborative research projects such as SAMOFAR (Safety Assessment of the

and dEployment) project started in October 2024 [8]. The MSFR can be started either with
enriched uranium and/or transuranic elements as initial fissile load, and has a fast neutron
spectrum. This concept is considered a viable candidate for future nuclear systems, providing
safe and sustainable energy.
As in any nuclear reactor, the MSFR fuel composition will change under irradiation due to
the combination of fissile materials consumption and to actinides and fission products build-
up. Those new isotopes are produced and consumed by nuclear reactions, such as fission and
radiative capture and radioactive decay. These changes in the inventory directly impact the
reactor reactivity and thus the power production. Figure 1.3 shows the impact of the fuel
depletion on the infinite multiplication factor in a PWR.

Breeder and fast reactors such as the MSFR produce more fissile material than it consumes.
Being able to assess the inventory evolution as a function of burnup or irradiation time helps to
monitor how much new fissile material is being produced from fertile materials. The calculation
of the fuel inventory is a starting point to quantify the individual impact on the reactor operation
and then identify the main contributors to neutron poisoning or increase of reactivity due to
production of new fissile materials.
In this context, the objective of this master thesis is to assess the impact on reactivity of the
main actinides and fission products of interest in the Molten Salt Fast Reactor, identifying
the major contributors to reactivity changes as a function of burnup. The results will then be
compared with the case of a thermal system such as the PWR to understand the differences
observed.
Performing Monte-Carlo in depletion simulations is one possible approach to calculate the
effective multiplication factor and the spent fuel inventory as a function of burnup. In this
approach, a Monte-Carlo transport calculation is providing the input data to a depletion solver
which uses a deterministic approach to solve the Bateman equations and calculate the new fuel
inventory that is then used for the following transport step. A succession of coupled transport-
depletion calculations is then performed until reaching the end of a given irradiation period.
For code qualification, those inventory results are the ones that can be compared to available
experimental data through Post Irradiation Experiments (PIE).
The first part of this report is describing the Molten Salt Fast Reactor principles and design,
as well as the PWR concept. The third section is consisting in a presentation of the neutronics
concepts used and studied in this project.
Section 4 presents the methodological aspects,
including a description of the Monte-Carlo method and a description of the two neutronics
simulation codes used for the project: TRIPOLI-4® and Serpent2. Section 5 is detailing the
modelling of MSFR and the PWR using the two Monte-Carlo radiation transport codes. First,
a validation of the two models was performed in steady-state. The models were then used
to perform depletion simulations model and assess the impact on the reactivity of the main
actinides and fission products.
The results are presented and analyzed in Section 6 before
concluding and discussing future work perspectives.

Description of reactors of interest
This section provides a general description of the design and operation of the two reactors
studied during this project : the Molten Salt Fast Reactor and the Pressurized Water Reactor.
2.1
The Molten Salt Fast Reactor (MSFR)
The Molten Salt Reactor (MSR) is one of the six reference systems selected during the Generation-
IV International Forum (GIF) as future nuclear reactors systems. These reference systems are
the following:
• The Gas-cooled Fast Reactor (GFR)
• The Lead-cooled Fast Reactor (LFR)
• The Molten Salt Reactor (MSR)
• The Sodium-cooled Fast Reactor (SFR)
• The SuperCritical-Water-cooled Reactor (SCWR)
• The Very High-Temperature Reactor (VHTR)
A schematics of these six concepts is presented in Figure 2.1 :
Figure 2.1: Gen IV concepts [3]
Molten Salt Reactors (MSRs) are a class of nuclear fission reactors where molten salts serve as
the reactor fuel, coolant, and/or moderator. Research on MSRs began early in the development
of nuclear energy. It started with the Molten-Salt Reactor Experiment (MSRE), which was
an experimental molten-salt reactor research reactor at the Oak Ridge National Laboratory
(ORNL) in Oak Ridge, Tennessee. This technology was researched through the 1960s. This
experiment reactor achieved its first self-sustaining nuclear reaction on June 1, 1965. Three
years later, on Oct.
8, 1968, it became the first reactor ever to run on 233U [11].
These
MSRs reactors presents the advantage that they can operate at lower pressures (ambient) and
Page 9 of 64
==PAGE:13==

higher temperatures compared to conventional water-cooled reactors.
Operating at low or
atmospheric pressure reduce the risk of pressure-related accidents and explosions compared
to high-pressure water reactors.
Operating at higher temperatures, typically above 600°C,
allow for a higher thermal efficiency electricity generation and a more efficient coupling to non-
electrical applications such as production of hydrogen [12] or desalination [13]. Molten Salt
Reactors also enable higher fuel utilization and reducing the frequency of refueling [3]. The
fact that the fuel is liquid also allows for core draining.
On the other hand, MSRs also present some challenges for their deployment. These reactors
operate at high temperatures and with corrosive molten salts. It is crucial to find materials that
can support this chemical environment, resist corrosion, endure high temperatures, and tolerate
neutron fluxes over long periods, and that still remains a significant challenge [3]. Moreover,
the possibility of online reprocessing of liquid fuel salts to extract fission products is one of the
most promising aspects of MSRs, allowing for a better neutron economy. However, this area
needs further investigation and demonstration to ensure reliability and efficiency. Fuel cycle
need to be investigated and new experimental facilities are needed for validation going towards
future licensing.
As part of the research on the MSR, the National Centre for Scientific Research (CNRS) has
focused studies on a MSR concept called the Molten Salt Fast Reactor (MSFR), which has been
officially selected by the GIF since 2008 for further studies. The Euratom FP7 project called
EVOL (Evaluation and Viability of Liquid Fuel Fast Reactor Systems) had been carried out
since 2011 together with Russian research organizations cooperating in the ROSATOM project
called MARS (Minor Actinides Recycling in Molten Salt) in order to propose a design for the
MSFR. This system has been chosen because of a number of interesting features, including
negative temperature and void coefficients, lower fissile inventory, no initial criticality reserve,
simplified fuel cycle and waste reduction. Some other projects studying the MSFR can be cited,
such as the SAMOFAR project, carried out between 2015 and 2019, whose objective was to
prove the innovative safety concepts of the MSFR by advanced experimental and numerical
techniques and to deliver a breakthrough in nuclear safety and optimal waste management
[6]. One of its specific goals was to provide a roadmap for further exploitation of the project
results by creating a momentum among key stakeholders that can validate and demonstrate
the MSFR beyond SAMOFAR. This project served then as a precursor to follow-up projects
like SAMOSAFER, whose objective was to develop and demonstrate new safety barriers for
more controlled behaviour of Molten Salt Reactors in case of severe accidents [7]. This project
was followed by the ENDURANCE project (2024-2028), which acts as a key enabling platform
for developing robust, long-lived, and safe MSRs, including the MSFR design [8].
2.1.1
MSFR design
The first innovative feature of the MSFR compared to other MSR concepts like the MSRE
is the absence of graphite moderator in the core. This allows for a breeding reactor with a
fast neutron spectrum based of the thorium fuel cycle.
The reactor design includes a fuel
processing system. A small part of the fuel salt (around 40 L each day) can extracted from the
core in order to remove the fission products acting as neutron poisons before being reinjected.
It is interesting to note that this process can be carried out simultaneously with the operation
of the reactor. This reactor also has the advantage of being able to operate with fuel salts
of various compositions. The initial load of fissile material can comprise 233U, 235U-enriched
natural uranium, but also the transuranic elements (TRU) produced by Light Water Reactors
(LWRs) or other future Gen IV reactors [14].

- Core :

external cooling system is required, since this heat can not be removed through the walls by

can be replaced by some inert reflector.

There are 16 of them, each associated with a pump (which will be
described in the next paragraph). An heat exchanger and a pump form a recirculation loop.
Each heat exchanger has to extract 187 MW during normal operation. Regarding the heat
exchanger structure, a compromise has to be found between a small exchanger that would limit
the volume of salt outside the reactor core and a structure that can accomodate the constraints
in terms of pressure drop and maximum authorised fuel salt speed. The chosen design is then
a good balance between compactness for the exchange surface and pressure drop.
- Pump :
The circulation of the fuel salt is allowed by 16 pumps located in the recirculation

power impacts the circulation time of the salt in the core and thus directly the salt heating.

The core and the other systems described are contained inside a reactor
vessel filled with argon. This gas has two main purposes : first it allows to cool the reactor
components by maintaining a gas temperature of 400°C, and it also permits to detect a possible
fuel salt leak. The structure of this reactor vessel has no influence on the core performance but
is important for safety.
This fuel circuit is connected to a salt draining system, which can be used in case of either a
planned shutdown or any incident leading to an excessive temperature reached in the core. In
this case, the fuel salt undergoes a process of gravity-driven draining which leads it into a large
water pool located under the reactor and where a passive cooling phenomenon takes place. At
the bottom of this pool, there is a layer of dilution salt than can be mixed with the fuel salt in
order to provide neutron poisons in the fuel leading to passive cooling of the fuel. This whole
system is connected to heat pipes used to transfer this decay heat into the atmosphere.

Nuclear Reactor Physics
This section describes the elements of theoritical nuclear reactor physics involved in this project.
A nuclear power plant is a thermal plant where the thermal energy is derived from the fission
process. A nuclear fission is a reaction in which the nucleus of a heavy atom splits into two or
smaller nuclei—also known as fission products. When the atom splits, some energy is released.
Around 90% of the energy released comes promptly from fission, which represents around 180
MeV, and 10 % comes from radioactive decay, with a timescale being minutes, hours or even
years. A schematics of a fission reaction is visible in Figure 3.1. The fission of the 235U atom
produces two fission products and three new neutrons that can induce new fission events.
Figure 3.1: Schematics of a fission chain reaction [20]
3.1
Cross sections
Neutron interactions can be represented as follows:
n + a →c + d
(3.1)
n being the incident neutron. Neutron interactions with matter can be of different natures :
scattering interactions ( elastic and inelastic scattering interactions), absorption interactions
(radiative capture and fission) or transfer interactions. The transfer interactions include charged
particles interactions in which the neutron is captured by the nucleus, which will then decay
by emitting a proton or electron, but also neutron emission reactions where the neutron is
absorbed by the nucleus, which will then decay by emitting two neutrons. The probability of
each of those interactions to happen can be quantified by microscopic cross sections σ, which
are a measurement of the cross sectional area of the “sphere of interaction”, i.e., the apparent
size of the nucleus that the neutron “sees” given the interaction. The cross section is measured
in barn, with 1 barn= 10−28m2. Its value depends on the interaction, the neutron energy and
the particles involved in the interaction. The total microscopic cross section for an isotope can

3.4
The effective multiplication factor
The effective multiplication factor, keff, is a measure for quantifying the balance between neu-
tron production and neutron loss in a nuclear system. In order for the chain reaction to be
balanced, on average, exactly one neutron must give rise to one new fission.
Considering a first generation of neutrons, the neutrons will proceed following a chain reaction
as depicted in Figure 3.4:
Figure 3.4: Chain reaction [24]
with ν the average number of neutrons released per fission. The effective multiplication factor is
then defined as the ratio between the neutron production from fission in one neutron generation
and the neutron absorption and leakage from the preceding generation. With this situation,
three situations can be described :
• keff = 1: The reactor is critical, and time independent.
• keff < 1: The reactor is sub-critical and the power goes down.
• keff > 1: The reactor is super-critical and the power goes up.
3.5

This section will describe the lifecycle of a neutron in a thermal reactor core. Neutrons are
released by fission reactions with a high energy (around 2 MeV). They are called fast neutrons
Since the reactor considered is a thermal reactor, most of the fission reactions happen at thermal
energies.
Fast fission factor :
Fast neutrons can induce fission, quantified by the fast fission factor
ϵ defined as the ratio between the number of neutrons produced by fast and thermal fission
and the number of neutrons produced by thermal fission. Typically, its value is between 1.02
and 1.08. It can be assumed that the reactor is of infinite size so that neutron leakage can be
neglected. In this case, all neutrons end up absorbed in the reactor.

Fast neutron will not begin to slow down through scatterings.

having intermediate (resonance) energies, it has a high probability of being absorbed. In order
to quantify this, the resonance escape probability p is defined, which is the probability that a
fast neutron survives down to thermal energies. Most of the contributions to the absorption of

4.2.1
TRIPOLI-4®
TRIPOLI-4® is a Monte-Carlo radiation transport code dedicated to shielding, reactor physics,
criticality-safety and nuclear instrumentation applications. TRIPOLI-4® is the fourth gener-
ation of the 3D continuous-energy Monte-Carlo code developed by the Service d’´Etudes des
R´eacteurs et de Math´ematiques Appliqu´ees (SERMA) at CEA Saclay. This code has been
developed in C++, with a few parts in C and Fortran. The version used for this project is
TRIPOLI-4® v11, allowing to perform depletion calculations [32].
This code can simulate neutral particles such as neutrons in the energy range from 10−5 eV to 20
MeV, and photons in the energy range from 1 keV to 20 MeV. It is possible to run the code both
in fixed-source and criticality simulation modes. In the fixed-source mode, the Monte-Carlo code
is used to solve the stationnary Boltzmann equation with a given source, which is usually given
by the user. Each batch begins with the same imposed source, and the particles are tracked
from appearance to disappearance. This mode is typically used for radiation protection and
shielding analysis. In the criticality mode, the Monte-Carlo code is used to solve the effective
multiplication factor keff eigenvalue equation, by finding the fundamental neutron flux mode
and the associated eigenvalue keff . To do so, an initial neutron source is set for the first batch,
and the equilibrium neutron distribution is then calculated. At the end of each batch, the
secondary neutrons coming from fission are used as a source for the next batch. After several
steps, the neutron distribution reaches convergence. The convergence criterion can be given by
the user, but also automatically set by the code.
TRIPOLI-4® can also perform depletion calculations, thanks to a coupling to the MENDEL
depletion solver developed at CEA. This coupling solves the Boltzmann-Bateman nonlinear set
of equations which characterizes the burnable materials evolution under neutron irradiation.
A schematics of the TRIPOLI-4® transport-depletion coupling can be found in Figure 4.2.
Figure 4.2: TRIPOLI-4® depletion calculation principle [33]

In the first transport step, TRIPOLI-4® computes the 1 or 2 energy-groups reaction rates in
each depleted medium, which are then provided to the MENDEL evolution solver. These rates
are then used as an input to calculate the end-of-step concentrations for each depleted region,
thanks to the knowledge of the initial concentration, the irradiation history and the depletion
data. TRIPOLI-4® can then calculate both global quantities such as keff, and local quantities
such as the local burnup, reaction rates, nuclides concentrations and masses, for every depleted
region.
To solve the Bateman equations, MENDEL uses a numerical method based on the fourth-order
Runge-Kutta method. This method, which is independent of the relations between isotopes, is
efficient for depletion problems [33].
To solve the transport-depletion coupling, TRIPOLI-4® proposes three different temporal
schemes. The first scheme is a first order scheme based on the explicit Euler method while
the two other schemes are second order methods.
Considering a fixed time t, the Euler method consists in calculating the flux and the reaction
rates at the considered time step. The depletion solver then uses these data to calculate the
new isotopic vector at the end of the time step (see Figure 4.3).

different schemes. One of them is the mid-point method, assuming that the flux calculated
in the middle of the timestep is the most representative of the flux behavior during the whole
timestep (see Figure 4.4).
Figure 4.4: Temporal scheme : Midpoint [34]
The second one is the mean method, which calculates the new isotopic vector as the mean
value between the results obtained at the beginning and the end of the considered time step as

The predictor-corrector time scheme is the one providing the most accurate results and allows to
get rid of the numerical unstability arising with the Euler method, but is also computationnally
more heavy since two transport calculations are realized for each timestep.
There are two
ways of parallelizing the calculations in TRIPOLI-4® [33]. The classical option consists of
parallelizing only the Monte-Carlo transport calculation while the sequential option consists of
simulating several independent transport-depletion coupled calculations and then process the
results with the post-treatment tools. The method using independent replicas is recommended
since it allows to estimate the uncertainty propagated throughout the calculations. Indeed, the
transport calculation gives statistical uncertainties, but since the MENDEL depletion solver
uses a deterministic method to compute the isotopes concentrations, the uncertainties cannot
be propagated to the next depletion step. This is the reason why the sequential method with
independent replicas is the one used for this study.
TRIPOLI-4® has three estimators of keff available: KSTEP, KTRACK and KCOLL. The
chosen estimator for this study is KCOLL which is an estimator based on collisions.
4.2.2
Serpent2
Serpent2 is a continuous-energy Monte Carlo multipurpose transport code, whose development
started at the VTT Technical Research Centre of Finland in 2004 [35]. The Serpent code is
written in standard ANSI-C language and is mainly developed in the Linux operating system.
It was originally developed as a reactor physics code. Similarly to TRIPOLI-4®, this code
covers neutron, photon and coupled neutron-photon simulations. It allows to perform both
steady-state and burnup calculations. The version used in this project is Serpent 2.1.32.
To perform depletion calculations, the code uses an internal calculation routine for solving the
system of Bateman equations describing the changes in the material compositions caused by
neutron-induced reactions and radioactive decay. Serpent2 has three options to solve these
Bateman equations. The first method is Transmutation Trajectory Analysis (TTA), which is
a method treating the depletion equations by decomposing the depletion system into different
linear chains and then solving each one analytically [36]. The second method, which is used
by default, is an advanced matrix exponential solution based on the Chebyshev Rational Ap-
proximation Method (CRAM) [37]. The third option is the variation TTA method, in which
cyclic transmutation chains are handled by inducing small variations in the coefficients instead
of solving the extended TTA equations. Different time schemes are available (Euler method
and predictor-corrector) to solve the transport-depletion coupling.
In this study, the parameters used are the default parameters: Chebyshev Rational Approxima-
tion Method and the use of a predictor-corrector time scheme to solve the transport-depletion

4.3
Principle of analysis
The objective of the thesis is to assess the individual impact on reactivity of fission products
and actinides in the MSFR, and then compare it with the literature and with the case of a
PWR fuel pin lattice.
In order to achieve this goal, the first step is to implement TRIPOLI-4® models of the MSFR
and PWR designs.
Depletion calculations are then performed allowing to calculate the fuel inventory as a function
of burnup. The depleted compositions given in the output files of these calculations are used
to study the individual impact of the actinides and fission products of interest on the core
reactivity by removing the specific isotope studied from the depleted composition obtained
for a given burnup value and use this new composition as input for performing a steady-sate
criticality calculation. This steady-state calculation is providing a new value of keff for the
modified system. Thanks to this new keff value it is then possible to estimate the reactivity
effect of the specified isotope. The results obtained for each isotope of interest are then analyzed
using the neutron spectrum of the system, cross section data and were compared with existing
literature.
All simulations were performed on the DTU Sophia HPC cluster.

of keff. A first rapid decrease of the reactivity is then observed due to the build-up of fission
products and actinides. This rapid drop is explained by the production of 135Xe. On the longer
time scale, 235U and 238U are consumed, fission products accumulate and act as neutron poisons
and even though some plutonium isotopes such as 239Pu are bred and contribute to fission, keff
decreases, until most of the most fissile material is consumed.
This calculation appeared to give some accurate results since no apparent fluctuations are seen,
but the uncertainty of the keff values were high. However, before studying the impact on
reactivity of individual isotopes, some checks were done to assess if it was the case for the
reactivity worth of 135Xe and 149Sm, the two main contributors to neutron poisoning in PWRs.

6.3
Counteracting the neutron poisoning in MSFR: salt cleaning and reprocessing
The study of the impact of on reactivity of the fission products in the MSFR presented has
been conducted without considering any fuel reprocessing during operation.
However, a liquid fuel enable the possibility to remove the fission products continuously pro-
duced in the core and this process can be done without stopping the reactor. The fuel salt can
be treated in two ways : on-line neutral gas bubbling and remote mini-batch reprocessing on-
site, as represented in Figure 6.22. On the left of the figure is represented the on-line treatment
with gas bubbling and on the right one can observe the on-site salt reprocessing.
The first reprocessing circuit is a continuous gas on-line bubbling in order to remove all the
gaseous fission products and metallic elements present in the salt.
This extracted gaseous
stream is sent to a provisional storage area where most of the Xe and Kr decay into Rb and
Cs, preventing their accumulation in the fuel salt.
The second process is a semi-continuous salt reprocessing with a rate of around 40 L a day.
This is done to prevent the accumulation of lanthanides and Zn in the fuel salt. Before the
sampled salt is returned back into the core, some adjustments to the composition are realized,
by adding Th and 233U if needed. The fission products are also removed from the core, with a
rate of around 0.4 L of salt cleaned per day.
Figure 6.22: Representation of the fuel salt treatment [27]
These processes are not essential for the operation of the reactor, but they contribute to an
improved breeding ratio and reactor efficiency.

Conclusion and perspectives
During the operation of a nuclear reactor, the isotopic composition of the fuel is modified due
to the different nuclear reactions occuring such as fission and capture and to radioactive decay.
This change of composition is affecting the reactivity of the system through the competition
of positive effects of new fissile materials created and negative effects from neutron poisons
production. Being able to predict the fuel composition and and its impact on reactivity is
essential for reactor operation but also for safety purpose.
The objective of this master thesis was to assess the reactivity effects of actinides and fission
products in the Gen IV Molten Salt Fast Reactor due to fuel irradiation a reactor belonging to
the Gen IV reactors. The study was carried-out mainly using the Monte-Carlo radiation trans-
port code TRIPOLI-4®. The obatined results were compared to the ones obatined studying
a PWR fuel pin lattice using the same methodology to analyze the impact of one being a fast
system and the other a thermal system on the reactivity-worth contributions from the main
fission products and actinides of interest.
The methodology used wa the following: depletion calculations were performed allowing to
calculate the fuel inventory as a function of burnup. At a given burnup value, the fuel depleted
compositions obtained were then used as input to assess the individual impact of the actinides
and fission products of interest on the core reactivity by removing the specific isotope studied
from the composition for performing a steady-sate criticality calculation. This steady-state
calculation was providing a new value of keff for the modified system. Thanks to this new keff
value it is then possible to estimate the reactivity effect of the specified isotope.
The results obtained confirm the main contribution of Nd, Zr, Cs and Sm in the MSFR neutron
poisoning. In the PWR case, the main contributor to antireactivity is clearly 135Xe, followed
by 143Nd and 103Rh.
The impact of 135Xe is less visible, highlighting the spectrum effect.
The different behavior of the 149Sm inventory between the case of a thermal and a fast rector
was also shown, as in the thermal spectrum, the competition between buildup production
and capture consumption is higher.
The comparison with literature shows a general good
agreement regarding the list of main contributors with reactivity-worth. However, the values
obtained are slightly different. Cross-check calculations were also performed using Serpent2
as a previous student project highlighted discrepancies in the depletion chains used by the
codes when simulating a thorium fuel cycle system. Especially, discrepancies were observed
on 233U inventory. As an example of discrepancies observed on the individual isotope impact
on reactivity, the Serpent2 calculation provided a reactivity-worth of -994 pcm for Nd and the
TRIPOLI-4® simulation a value of -669 pcm. Further work on investigating the discrepancies
between the depletion chains used by the two codes is needed.
An area of improvement for this study concerns the accuracy of the results. Indeed, due to
the limited duration of this project and the time required for each simulation, it has not been
possible to carry out simulations with a significant number of batches and neutrons simulated
per batch, which would have greatly increased the calculation time, but which would also
have allowed obtaining more accurate results, with lower statistical uncertainty. Thus, some
reactivity-worth results obtained during the simulations were not fully consistent, and gave
positive reactivity impacts where the expected result was a negative value. These inconsistent
results can be explained simply by the statistical uncertainty linked to the results which was
too large in comparison to a small reactivity effect to be observed.
The MSFR reactivity study was also mainly carried out without simulating the effect of fuel
cleaning and reprocessing which aims at the online extraction of fission products.
