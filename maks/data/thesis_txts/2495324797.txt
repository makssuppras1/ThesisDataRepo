Abstract 
 
The common use of antibiotics as treatment for infections has caused a global antibiotic resistance 
crisis. The case of MRSA is well known, with Staphylococcus aureus displaying resistance to a wide 
range of antibiotics and as such alternative ways of treating pathogenic infection is needed. Non-
antibiotic treatment of S. aureus has shown promise through peptidomimetic inhibition of the Agr 
system. This report attempts antibody inhibition of the AgrC receptor of the S. aureus quorum sensing 
system. Phage display was used as a means of delivery and binding. As this was the first attempt at 
Agr antibody inhibition, assumptions were made regarding receptor access, neglecting the effects of 
the peptidoglycan layer. Three rounds of in vitro panning were performed using the 2.63 x 1013 pfu / 
mL IONTAS K antibody presenting phage library over S. aureus cells. Direct infection of E. coli 
with produced phage stock resulted in a CFU of 5.75 x 1012 / mL, showing ten times decrease in 
phage numbers compared to IONTAS K stock. From S. aureus phage treatment prior to E. coli 
infection, CFU levels of 2.48 x 1011 / mL for stock #1 and 1.48 x 1011 / mL for stock #2 were achieved. 
Initial hypothesis was that phage count through multiple rounds of panning would be reduced 
substantially further than 1011. Results showed that the bound fraction of phages increased from 0% 
to 51.4% for stock #1 and to 70.5% for stock #2, displaying increased antibody affinity as phage count 
was lowered. Further research should be explored, incorporating panning procedures with 
peptidoglycan depolymerisation or unknown alternatives.

1. Introduction 
 
Antibiotic resistance is a growing global problem. The world health organization (WHO) reports, that 
more than 85% of deaths worldwide are caused by acute respiratory infections, diarrhoea, diseases, 
measles, AIDS, malaria and tuberculosis. Antibiotic resistance plays a large role, as pathogens show 
resistance to the primarily used drugs, with resistance ranging from 0 to 100% [WHO, 2001]. This 
could potentially lead to a medical crisis, as treatment with antibiotics become more and more 
ineffective. One of the most publicised cases of antibiotic resistance is that of Staphylococcus aureus 
(S. aureus), specifically methicillin resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) [Jiang, et.al., 2018]. 
Years of treatment of pathogens such as S. aureus using only antibiotics has led to many pathogens 
developing resistance. In the case of S. aureus, methicillin works by inhibition of penicillin-binding 
proteins (PBP). These binding proteins play a central role in S. aureus as they are a key component 
for synthesis of the peptidoglycan layer. S. aureus has however evolved to express a variation of PBP 
which is resistant to methicillin, known as PBP2a. This resistance is critical as S. aureus PBP is no 
longer affected by methicillin. Problematically MRSA also shows resistance to other antibiotics 
[Stapleton and Taylor, 2002]. For treatment of MRSA the last line of usable antibiotics includes; 
vancomycin, linezolid and daptomycin. With daptomycin being suggested in 2006 as an alternative 
to the standard treatment of bacteraemia and endocarditis caused by S. aureus [Fowler, et. al., 2006]. 
Daptomycin was however already in 2002 found susceptible to resistance [Stapleton and Taylor, 
2002]. They found that single cls2 point mutations were enough to make S. aureus resistant to 
daptomycin, resulting in continuous infection of the host [Stapleton and Taylor, 2002]. Even though 
resistance has been shown to other drugs, vancomycin still shows promise against S. aureus after 50 
years of use. It is however unknown how long this will last [Holmes et.al., 2015]. Because of the 
widespread antibiotic resistance, research is being mounted at alternative ways of dealing with 
pathogens such as S. aureus. This includes the use of lipoglycopeptides and anti-MRSA 
cephalosporins [Holmes et.al., 2015]. Common ways of antibiotic action against a pathogen is 
through either inhibition or degradation, as described with methicillin inhibition of PBP [Stapleton 
and Taylor, 2002]. There does however exist other ways of inhibition, namely using antibodies as 
inhibitors of specific receptors. In 1997 Mendelsohn discovered that monoclonal antibodies could be 
targeted directly at receptors, inhibiting activation of other systems [Mendelsohn, 1997]. This has led 
to extensive research on antibody use for anti-cancer therapy [Gasser and Waaga-Gasser, 2016]. 
Recent developments in the use of antibodies in anti-cancer therapy shows novel potency [Adler and 
Dimitrov, 2012] and as such the effects of antibody treatment should be explored further.

The research performed in this thesis is aimed at inhibiting one of the systems in S. aureus which lead 
to virulence expression. This is approached in vitro and is based on inhibiting the Agr quorum sensing 
(QS) system in S. aureus using antibodies as the inhibitor, with the specific inhibition target being 
the surface receptor AgrC. Why and how this QS system works is explored in section 2. Phage display 
is used to select antibodies with binding affinity for AgrC. Using phage display, a panning is to be 
conducted, mixing a human antibody M13 library with S. aureus cells. This is described in section 3, 
and the performed experiments are described in section 4. Upon successful selection of antibodies 
with affinity for AgrC inhibition, anti-virulence was to be analysed. As phage results were not 
conclusive this was not performed. Results and discussion of these are seen in sections 5 and 6. 
 
 
 
2. Quorum sensing in Staphylococcus aureus 
 
2.1 Quorum sensing 
 
 
The term quorum sensing covers bacteria utilizing a system where the regulation of gene expression 
is a result of differentiation in cell-population density. The cell will detect cell-population numbers 
and respond accordingly through gene regulation [Miller, and Bassler, 2001].  Bacteria with a 
functioning QS system will produce and release signal molecules, termed autoinducers [Novick, and 
Geisinger, 2008]. At a minimal concentration needed for detection, the autoinducer will bind to the 
bacterium through a surface receptor. Through binding the surface receptor is activated, which then 
sends a signal causing upregulation of the autoinducing ligands [Waters, and Bassler, 2005]. This 
self-regulation continues until cell-population density reaches a critical value for gene alteration and 
expression. Bacteria with functioning QS will use this to regulate many factors such as: virulence, 
biofilm formation, competence, conjugation and so on [Miller, and Bassler, 2001]. Therefore, 
inhibition of QS should in theory stop regulation of these factors, rendering the bacterium less 
effective in infection. 
 
2.2 The accessory gene regulator quorum sensing system 
 
 
The QS system in S. aureus is known as the accessory gene regulator system. It functions as a standard 
QS system, releasing an autoinducing signal molecule. In S. aureus, the signal molecule is an

The Agr QS system works in a self-activating circuit. AgrD is expressed and functions coherently 
with AgrB for secretion of AIP. Once minimal stimulatory concentrations of AIP are detected, AIP 
binds to the transmembrane surface receptor AgrC, which forms a two-component signalling module 
with AgrA. Binding of AIP to AgrC induces phosphorylation of the AgrC histidine protein kinase 
domain, resulting in activation of AgrA through phosphate being transferred. AgrA will then 
upregulate its own promotor P2, resulting in increased production of AgrD [Le and Otto, 2015]. When 
cell-population density reaches a minimal stimulatory concentration, AgrA will also upregulate the 
P3 promoter, which in hand upregulates expression of RNAIII [Novick and Geisinger, 2008]. The 
self-activating Agr system is visualized on Figure 2. 
 
2.2.1 RNAIII 
 
Ribonucleic acid III or RNAIII is the intracellular effector of the Agr QS system. It is a 514-nt large 
regulatory RNA which is responsible for regulating synthesis of toxic exoproteins, expression of 
surface proteins and translation of pleiotropic regulators [Novick and Geisinger, 2008]. Besides 
regulation of synthesis and expression, it also is responsible for encoding d-hemolysin, also known 
as Hld [Bronesky, et. al. 2016]. RNAIII is a complex molecule, which is characterized by having 14 
stem-loop motifs [Benito, et al. 2000]. Some of these stem-loops are highly conserved and three of 
the hairpin stem-loops have a very C abundant sequence. The high C sequence could possibly bind 
to a Shine-Dalgarno sequence on an mRNA target [Benito, et al. 2000]. Among mRNA targets, two 
have been identified so far [Boisset, al. 2006]. The RNAIII - mRNA binding takes place without 
helper proteins, as it happens with a very fast association rate constant. This formation of RNAIII -

2.2.2 Consequences of Agr inhibition 
 
When analysing the Agr QS system found in S. aureus it becomes apparent that inhibition of the 
AgrC surface receptor should essentially shut down the QS system. This is because the Agr system 
is dependent on binding of AIP to AgrC to continue increasing cell-population density. Studies have 
been made using AIP peptidomimetics acting as peptoid inhibitors with good results, showing lower 
RNAIII expression when AgrC is inhibited [Karathanasi, et. al. 2018]. This confirms that Agr 
inhibition will lead to lower RNAIII expression. This is thought to be positive as this would 
essentially downplay the effects that RNAIII has on S. aureus virulence. This being true, does not 
mean that S. aureus infection would no longer take place, as low Agr activity strains have been 
isolated as the cause of bacteremia in at least three cases [Painter, et. al. 2014]. In 2012 it was reported 
that patients suffering from bacteremia responded in a worse way to Agr defective strains of S. aureus, 
it was however unknown at that point if the Agr dysfunctionality would cause bacteremia or if it was 
a direct consequence thereof [Smyth, et. al. 2012]. Further studies did however present that Agr 
dysfunction in most cases was seen prior to bacteremia. This suggests that Agr defective S. aureus 
strains would be able to initiate bacteremia [Painter, et. al. 2014]. This does however not imply that 
inactivation of the Agr system would not necessarily result in bacteremia [Painter, et. al. 2014]. Most 
cases of Staphylococcal bacteremia, come as a consequence of cuts, wounds or insertion of 
intravenous catheters [Fowler, 2003]. This suggests that most cases of bacteremia would happen 
through infections suffered at hospitals. This is supported by a hospital case from South Korea, where 
Agr defective MRSA spread in patients [Chong, et. al. 2013]. The study reported that in a hospital 
setting the specific Agr– MRSA strain might have had an advantage over strains with functioning Agr 
systems. In 2004 Fowler et. al. presented a study linking persistent bacteremia due to MRSA with 
Agr dysfunctionality. This study suggested that there might be a correlation between heightened 
antibiotic resistance and lower Agr functionality [Fowler, et. al. 2004]. These correlations should 
however not mean that inhibiting a strain with a functional Agr system would cause it to assimilate 
the functions of a strain with a non-functioning Agr system. As Agr inhibition has already proven 
successful using AIP peptidomimetics, the aim of this thesis is to explore possibilities of inhibition 
using antibodies. For this Agr inhibition, selection of antibodies and presentation of these to S. aureus 
is however needed. This is where the technique known as phage display is useful.

3. Phage display 
 
3.1 Introduction 
 
 
Phage display utilizes the ability to link the phenotype and genotype of a protein through a 
bacteriophage [Ledsgaard, et. al. 2018]. The technology was originally created by Smith in 1985, 
where foreign DNA was inserted into an M13 bacteriophage, allowing it to present foreign peptides 
on its coat proteins known as G3P [Smith, 1985]. In 1990 McCafferty et. al. discovered that they 
could display antibody variable domains on the surface of bacteriophages. This would pave the way 
for antibody phage display, making this a viable way of presenting antibodies to receptors. They also 
discovered that the phages would bind specifically to antigen and that unique phages could be isolated 
from a large pool of different phages [McCafferty, et. al. 1990]. This provides the means of selection 
of antibodies with binding affinity for S. aureus. Different types of bacteriophages have been used in 
phage display, namely: T4, T7, l, M13 and fd. However, filamentous M13 and fd are used under 
normal circumstances as they have shown to not lyse the bacteria they infect [Huang et. al. 2012]. As 
the M13 phage is commonly used for phage display it is the chosen phage of the experimental 
procedure. 
  
 
3.2 The filamentous M13 bacteriophage 
 
 
The M13 phage was originally discovered in the sewers of Munich, Germany in 1963 by 
Hofschneider [Hofschneider and Preuss, 1963]. The M13 bacteriophage is a filamentous phage, also 
known as a Ff phage. Other common Ff phages are fd and f1 [Branson, 2009]. Ff phages are long     
(~ 880 nm) and thin (~6 nm) [Berkowitz and Day. 1976]. Inside the M13 phage lies the genome, a 
single stranded DNA (ssDNA) consisting of 6407 base pairs. These base pairs make up nine genes 
encoding eleven different proteins. Approximately half of these are the five coat proteins: G3P, G6P, 
G7P, G8P and G9P [Ledsgaard, et. al. 2018]. The remaining six proteins are responsible for phage 
assembly and DNA replication [Branson, 2009]. The proteins and their function are outlined in Table 
1. The phage coat largely consists of the a-helical protein G8P (~ 7.6 kDa), as it forms most of the 
coat with its 2700 base pairs [Overman, et. al. 1996]. The remaining coat proteins are found at the 
ends, with G6P (~ 12.3 kDa) and G3P (~ 45 kDa) forming the proximal tip, and G7P (~ 3.4 kDa) and

3.3 M13 Infection and replication 
 
3.3.1 Infection 
 
“Male” E. coli cells (F+) move around the environment, trying to catch “female” E. coli cells (F-) with 
their flexible F-pili. The F-pili undergo constant differentiation in length by elongation and 
shortening, which allows them to “scan” the environment around the cell [Clarke, et. al. 2008]. The 
initial Ff phage infection takes advantage of this by binding to the F-pilus of the “male” E. coli cells. 
Specifically, the N2 domain of the G3P protein binds to the tip of the F-pilus, as the specific function 
of the N2 domain is to recognize the pilus tip [Click and Webster, 1997]. Once G3P is bound to the 
F-pilus, the F-pilus undergoes disassembly resulting in the phage being brought to the surface of the 
bacteria [Jacobson, 1972]. Near the surface of the bacteria, the G3P-N1 domain of the M13 phage, 
will bind to TolA, which acts as a co-receptor. This is one of three Tol receptors in E. coli, the others 
being TolR and TolQ. The Tol proteins are essential for infection as they are used in depolymerization 
and translocation of ssDNA [Ledsgaard, et. al. 2018].  
 
3.3.2 Replication 
 
As the TolA protein induces depolymerization of the phage coat, the ssDNA of the phage will enter 
the cytoplasm of the E. coli cell [Huang et. al. 2012]. Here it undergoes transformation from ssDNA 
to dsDNA, also known as the replicative form (RF). This happens via synthesis of a complimentary 
DNA strand, which in hand will form a supercoiled dsDNA strand. This sort of DNA replication is 
known as rolling circle replication (RCR) [Weigel and Seitz 2006]. As the name suggests replication 
of the injected phage chromosome will always happen at the RF strand [Ledsgaard, et. al. 2018]. In 
RCR, as is the case for the M13 bacteriophage, an initiator protein will nick the + end of the strand 
of the dsDNA. The G2P initiator protein will bind to the 5’-end of the nicked strand, through a 
tyrosine residue [Weigel and Seitz, 2006]. The strand is then replicated, starting at the 3’-end, using 
the strand as a template [Nomura, and Ray, 1980]. When strand replication is complete, the formation 
of dsDNA will be reversed, resulting in release of ssDNA from the RF [Weigel, and Seitz, 2006]. 
This newly formed ssDNA will then be ready to either be converted into a new RF, or to undergo 
packaging into a new M13 phage [Ledsgaard, et. al. 2018]. In the early stages of infection, the ssDNA 
strand will again enter the replication phase and be converted into a RF. However, in later stages of 
infection, conversion from ssDNA to RF will become impossible, as G5P will bind specifically to 
ssDNA in a dimeric confirmation, actively preventing conversion into RF. This will only happen

The G4P channel will open upon receiving a morphogenetic signal. This signal stems from G1P

3.4 Displaying antibody on M13 bacteriophages 
 
To be able to use phage display technology for delivery of antibodies to the AgrC surface receptor in 
S. aureus, a monovalent display of antibodies is critical. Multiplication of M13 phages is done as 
described in section 3.3, but to ensure a monovalent display of antibody, using helper phages is 
necessary. When the first trials of cloning antibody genes into phages were conducted, they would 
use the whole genome [Ledsgaard, et. al. 2018]. This means that all the necessary genes for infection, 
replication, extrusion and so forth are included. This is problematic as the genome also carries the 
genes for fusion of the G3P coat protein to antibodies. As described in section 3.2, each Ff phage will 
have 5 copies of G3P. If the phage is constructed with the gene coding for antibody fusion to G3P, it 
will result in a multivalent display of antibody onto the G3P surface [Ledsgaard, et. al. 2018]. To 
ensure a monovalent display of antibody, using phagemid vectors is necessary. The phagemid vector 
does not include the whole Ff genome, but only three important parts: The origin of replication, the 
gene encoding the G3P-antibody fusion and antibiotic markers for selection and propagation. The 
origin of replication is needed for production of the + strand, which is required for phage assembly 
[Ledsgaard, et. al. 2018]. A phagemid vector is not enough to produce a complete phage, but if 
combined with a helper phage such as R408 or M13KO7 phage formation would be possible [Qi, et. 
al. 2012]. If helper phage is present without any phagemid vector, Ff phages will be produced, as the 
helper phage genome includes everything necessary for production of a Ff phage. However, if it is

vector, resulting in creation of a phage carrying the DNA from the phagemid vector. On Table 2 the

will incorporate G3P from the M13KO7 helper phage, as well as the G3P-antibody fusion.

3.5. Using antibody presenting phages as a panning tool against AgrC 
 
It has been established that phages are capable of presenting antibodies on G3P at their proximal tip. 
Ideally each phage would only carry one scFv and include only the antibody’s specific genotype in 
the phage ssDNA. This is the optimized situation for selection of specific antibodies against AgrC. 
To search for the ideal antibodies, a panning over S. aureus cells, using a phage library is to be 
performed.

3.6 Bacterial strains 
 
Two bacterial strains are to be used in this research, namely: Staphylococcus aureus 8325-4 and 
Escherichia coli TG1. Additional information and reasoning for choosing the strains can be found in 
section 3.6.1 and 3.6.2 
 
3.6.1 Staphylococcus aureus 8325-4 
 
Research using S. aureus has been focused on a select number of strains which are believed to be 
good markers for general species behaviour. The commonly used strains are known as 8325-4 and 
SH1000 [O’Neill, 2010]. Strain 8325-4 is a derivative of the NCTC 8325 strain, with 8325-4 having 
the three prophages found in NCTC 8325; F11, F12 and F13 removed by an excision in the genome 
[Novick, 1967]. Strain SH1000 is created by reinstating a rsbU gene which is responsible for the 
upregulation of sigma factor B. Both 8325-4 and SH1000 are used extensively in research, however 
8325-4 is selected as SH1000 suffers from a 63 bp genome deletion upstream of the gene coding for 
spa [O’Neill, 2010]. Strain 8325-4 was also selected because its phenotype is Agr+, which is essential 
for research on the Agr QS system. 8325-4 is also a methicillin sensitive strain, which is an additional 
benefit, as the general aim of research in this field is aimed towards antibiotic resistance in S. aureus 
strains [Herbert, et. al. 2010]. Strain 8325-4 has also been used heavily in research for the past 
decades, being the basis for models on S. aureus virulence regulation [Bæk, et. al. 2013]. This further 
strengthens the base of reasoning towards using 8325-4 as the S. aureus strain in this project. It should 
however be noted that there are significant sequence differences between NCTC 8325 and 8325-4, 
and that alterations in the coding sequence is thought to influence virulence regulation in 8325-4. 
This is due to 8325-4 lacking phenol soluble modulin a3 (PSMa3) which is responsible for biofilm 
structure, surface spreading and neutrophil chemotaxis [Bæk, et. al. 2013]. This is however not 
believed to be essential, as the project only aims at inhibition of Agr QS.

3.6.2 Escherichia coli TG1 
 
An E. coli strain is also needed for the project, as phage multiplication is to be done through infection 
of E. coli. It is essential for this infection that the selected E. coli strain is F+ so that the bacteria have 
F-pili which surveys the cytoplasm as described in section 3.3.1 The Escherichia coli strain TG1 is

labs [ABDesignLabs 2019] and more. 
 
 
3.7 Considerations and assumptions 
 
The experimental procedure was created in simple fashion with a simplistic approach. As this is the 
first project on AgrC inhibition using antibody presenting phages, it was decided that it would be run 
as straight forward as possible. Because of this the created panning procedure assumes some factors 
as negligible. It was assumed that all non-bound phages would be removed through PBS washing, 
that 1 Hr would be sufficient for binding phages to the AgrC receptor and so forth. The most crucial 
assumption made being that the phages can penetrate the peptidoglycan layer surrounding S. aureus 
cells, enabling contact with AgrC. It is also assumed that inhibiting effects on other surface receptors 
besides AgrC have insignificant effects on bacterial growth and phage selection.

4.2 Initial panning of antibody presenting phages to S. aureus 
 
4.2.1 S. aureus and E. coli cultivation 
 
Using a pipette tip, a small chunk of frozen S. aureus strain 8325-4 was picked from a cryotube and 
streaked onto a TSB plate. In the same way, a chunk of frozen E. coli TG1 was picked from a cryotube 
and streaked onto a 2TY plate. Both plates were left over the weekend at RT. After the weekend, S. 
aureus was picked from the TSB plate and transferred to a test tube containing 5 mL TSB media. In 
the same fashion, E. coli was picked from the 2TY plate and transferred to a test tube containing 5 
mL 2TY media. The test tubes were placed in a 37°C shaking cabinet and left O/N. The next morning 
the test tubes were removed from the shaking cabinet and placed in a rack at the water bath. Two 250 
mL Erlenmeyer flasks were prepared, each containing either 50 mL TSB media for S. aureus growth, 
or 50 mL 2TY media for E. coli growth. The flasks were preheated in a 37°C water bath. 500 µL S. 
aureus O/N growth was transferred to the TSB flask, and 500 µL E. coli O/N growth was transferred 
to the 2TY flask. Two minutes after addition of media OD600 was measured. The flasks were in the 
water bath until OD600 reached a value of 0.5. It was important that the cells were not allowed to grow 
beyond OD600 = 0.5, as growing past this threshold and then diluting could result in a loss of 
infectivity. The OD600 values can be seen on Table A1 in the appendix and the growth curves can be 
seen on Figure 6 in section 5.1. Once the OD600 of the E. coli growth in 2TY had reached 0.5 the 
entirety of the media was transferred to a 50 mL falcon tube and stored on ice. From the falcon tube, 
100 µL was transferred and spread onto a 2TYGK (A) plate and a 2TYGA (B) plate. These plates 
functioned as negative controls, as E. coli should not be able to grow due to the added antibiotics. 
The plates were incubated O/N at 37°C. The falcon tube was stored on ice until needed for phage 
amplification.

4.2.2 Binding of M13 phages to S. aureus 
 
Once OD600 of the S. aureus growth reached 0.5, 1 mL was transferred to an eppendorf tube and 
centrifuged for 5 minutes at 7000 rotations per minute (RPM). Following centrifugation, the 
supernatant was discarded, and the pellet was resuspended in 1 mL PBS buffer. Following 
resuspension, the eppendorf tube was centrifuged for 5 minutes at 7000 RPM. The supernatant was 
discarded, and the pellet was resuspended in 1 mL PBS buffer. Following resuspension, 1 µL 
IONTAS K phage library was added to the eppendorf tube. To ensure binding of phages to surface 
receptors, the mixture was incubated at 37°C for 1 Hr in a heated cabinet. After incubation, the 
eppendorf tube was centrifuged for 5 minutes at 7000 RPM. After centrifugation, the supernatant was 
discarded. This supernatant included a lot of M13 phages, as all the non-receptor bound phages should 
be present in the supernatant, as bound phages should be in the pellet. The pellet was resuspended in 
500 µL PBS and centrifuged for 5 minutes at 7000 RPM. The supernatant was discarded, and the 
pellet was resuspended in 250 µL PBS. The first round of panning for antibody presenting phages 
was now complete. Section 4.2.3 describes elution of bound phages, here using trypsin to cleave at 
the phosphate linking group in the G3P-scFV protein. 
 
 
4.2.3 Replication of M13 / phagemid through TG1 infection 
 
For elution of phages a 10 mg/mL trypsin in 50 mM Tris buffer with a pH of 8.0 was prepared and 
stored in the freezer. The buffer was diluted to 100 µg/mL trypsin concentration, and 250 µL ice cold

from section 4.2.2. The eppendorf tube was set a RT for 15 minutes to ensure proper cleavage of 
phages from bound antibodies. Following trypsin cleavage, the eppendorf tube was centrifuged for 
10 minutes at 17000 G. All eluted phages should then be present in the supernatant. The 500 µL 
supernatant was then transferred to a 15 mL falcon tube. 5 mL of the TG1 E. coli culture stored on 
ice from section 4.2.1. was also added to the falcon tube. The falcon tube was placed in a heated 
cabinet at 37°C with shaking for 1 Hr. This was to ensure proper replication of phages. After 1 Hr the 
falcon tube was removed from the cabinet and placed in a holder. 20 µL culture was spread onto 
plates 2TYGK (C) and 2TYGA (D). These plates were incubated O/N at 37°C. The falcon tube was 
then centrifuged for 5 minutes at 4000 RPM. Most of the supernatant was discarded, leaving 100 µL 
behind. The 100 µL was used to resuspend the pellet, and the resuspension was plated onto a 2TYGA

6. Discussion 
 
The case of MRSA is well known to the public and increasing antibiotic resistance of bacteria such 
as S. aureus is a massive global health problem. If the growing resistance is to be controlled, 
development of new treatment technologies is necessary. It is a subject of utmost importance, as 
unchecked antibiotic resistance has a chance of spiralling out of control in the very close future. Some 
drugs such as vancomycin still show efficacy in infection treatment, however it is unknown how long 
these will last. Non-antibiotic treatment of S. aureus has shown promise, as current research aims at 
inhibiting the quorum sensing Agr system. This has been done successfully by other studies, using 
==PAGE:41==
Kristoffer Thrane Lundstedt 
 
19/1/2020 
 
 
40
AIP peptidomimetics resulting in reduced RNAIII expression. For strains containing a functioning 
Agr system, this is a step in the right direction as RNAIII upregulates virulence factors and represses 
ROT. Research shows that S. aureus infection should be treated differently based on the strain being 
Agr+ or Agr-. This thesis focused on Agr inhibition in Agr+ strains, namely S. aureus 8325-4 
stemming from the NCTC 8325 strain. The experimental procedure aimed at using antibody 
presenting M13 bacteriophages as a way of antibody delivery to the Agr surface receptor AgrC. The 
2.63 x 1013 pfu / mL IONTAS K antibody presenting phage library was used in a simple in vitro 
panning procedure over 8225-4 S. aureus cells. The panning procedure was performed to create new 
antibody presenting phage stock, containing fewer but more specific phages. As the use of phage 
display in such a capacity is a relatively new field, some assumptions were made prior to designing 
the experimental protocol. It was assumed that the antibodies would show inhibitory effects against 
not only AgrC but multiple S. aureus surface receptors, but that this would have no significant effects 
on bacterial growth or antibody selection. It was also assumed that the phages would be able to 
penetrate the peptidoglycan layer surrounding S. aureus cells. These assumptions entail a simplistic 
approach to the effects of the panning and phage display. The importance of this decision is displayed 
on the in vitro plate results from Figure 14. The different phage stock were created through a selection 
in S. aureus cells, however the addition of phage stock to E. coli influenced growth, as the negative 
test with water shows no bacterial growth. The plate results clearly dictate that addition of phage 
stock has an effect, but to what extend is unknown. On most plates, the dilution of phages stock has 
little effect on CFU, even down to dilutions of 10-9. IONTAS K plates A, B, C from Figure 11 and 
stock #2 plates g, h, i from Figure 14 being the only cases of lower CFU caused by dilution. This 
could be accredited as human error, however throughout plate results, stock #2 show significantly 
less CFU than other stocks regardless of dilution. From examining plates on Figure 14 and 16, CFU 
dropped through the rounds of panning from IONTAS K to stock #2. Plate i stems from stock #2 and 
displays a CFU of 115 / 20 µL x 10-9 or 5.75 / mL x 1012. This is lower than the 2.63 x 1013 pfu / mL 
of the IONTAS K library, however not as much as expected, as multiple rounds of panning should 
have caused a significant drop in CFU. Some uncertainty is present, as plate i has the only countable 
CFU from the procedure. As it is less than 10% of the total plates, the conclusions made on behalf of 
plate i are made with reservations, as there exists a significantly higher chance that plate i is a faulty 
outlier from the rest of the plates. Phage stock binding affinity was tested in S. aureus cells at different 
growth stages; lag-phase, early exponential phase, mid exponential phase and late exponential phase, 
between OD600 values ranging from 0.05, 0.1 and 0.5 to 1.0. The cells were however diluted to the 
==PAGE:42==
Kristoffer Thrane Lundstedt 
 
19/1/2020 
 
 
41
values, and thus only artificially in the specific growth phase. From Figure 16, the results reaffirm 
the conclusion from E. coli phage infection that CFU is lowered through multiple rounds of panning. 
This is also seen from CFU calculations as stock #1 has an average undiluted CFU of 2.48 / mL x 
1011, and stock #2 has an average undiluted CFU of 1.48 / mL x 1011. This is a significant difference, 
albeit still a higher CFU than expected. Compared with the 5.75 / mL x1012 from direct E. coli 
infection, the values obtained through S. aureus panning prior to E. coli infection are 10 times lower. 
This decrease in phage count can be explained by non-bound phages being removed through washing 
and centrifugation, as non-bound phages should be present in the supernatant. The effects of the S. 
aureus OD on CFU varies as seen on data from Table 4. Stock #1 data varies by 33% with lowest 
CFU at 423 and highest at 633, however the variance is 12% if the outlier value is removed. The same 
is observed for stock #2 with a difference of 27%, but only 10% if the outlier value is removed. This 
hints that if the phage stock is mixed with S. aureus cells prior to phage E. coli infection, the CFU 
changes a little, but not by significant amounts. Calculations made on bound versus non-bound 
fractions of phages, suggest that while CFU decreases, binding affinity increases. The bound fraction 
increased from 0 % for the IONTAS K library, to 51.4 % for stock #1 and to 70.5 % for stock #2. 
This proposes that the antibody binding affinity towards S. aureus increases with each panning. 
However, the changes in antibody affinity do not follow the expected results as seen on Figure 17. It 
was expected that through rounds of panning antibody binding affinity would increase exponentially, 
as non-bound antibody presenting phages would be removed via washing. While this is the case, in 
vitro results do not follow the expected exponential increase in bound phage percentage. This does 
not necessarily lead to results being discarded as this may be caused by insufficient rounds of panning. 
Through conducting additional rounds of panning, the bound fraction might increase. These findings 
do not take Stock #3 into consideration due to the bound fraction being 0. The bound fraction of 
phages should have been higher for stock #3 as it underwent an additional round of selection through 
panning. This can be explained by either faults in the panning procedure, or faults in the dilution of 
phage stock. As described, it was assumed that antibodies would bind to other surface receptors than 
AgrC, but that this would have negligible effects. This might be the cause of the high CFU counts, 
seen even when S. aureus is treated with phage stock #2. If this receptor binding is significant, it 
could lead to many phages being bound to other receptors and thus few phages would be removed 
via washing. This could potentially explain why CFU is high after two rounds of panning. Using a 
phage pool with the size of the IONTAS K library can lead to finding the needle in the haystack, and 
==PAGE:43==
Kristoffer Thrane Lundstedt 
 
19/1/2020 
 
 
42
antibodies with high binding affinity for AgrC might have been selected. Nonetheless when many 
phages are present, antibodies with affinity for other receptors than AgrC could also be selected.  
 
 
6.1 Further work 
 
 
Before antibody Agr inhibition is put aside as non-functioning, an acquisition of conclusive data is 
required. This entails some future work, as different strategies would be applied. First and foremost, 
steps should be taken to examine the effects of the peptidoglycan layer in regards to phage display. I 
suggest that the conducted panning procedure is repeated on S. aureus cells which have had their 
peptidoglycan layer stripped. This can be done by dissolving by using peptidoglycan degrading 
enzymes [Oliveira, et. al. 2018]. The enzymes are known as polysaccharide depolymerases and they 
are designed to degrade the structural integrity of the polysaccharide layers and can be used prior to 
phage infection [Drulis-Kawa, et. al. 2015]. These enzymes have successfully been used in attempts 
of infecting encapsulated E. coli and could be an option for peptidoglycan depolymerisation [Drulis-
Kawa, et. al. 2015]. When considering this approach, it is important that the selected method of 
peptidoglycan degradation does not destroy the cell membrane as structural integrity should be 
somewhat upheld, but phages should be able to reach the target receptor. Further research should be 
made to uncover alternatives to enzymatic degradation of peptidoglycan, such as cutting open a 
channel through the layer. Nonetheless degrading the peptidoglycan layer by using enzymes would 
be a less delicate option and should thus be explored initially. Besides membrane disruption, running 
the panning process again would also be of interest. This should be done to determine the optimal 
number of rounds needed to achieve high titers of bound phages. While the stated experiments are of 
importance, it is also crucial that selection is done only on behalf of AgrC binding affinity, as to not 
include large amounts of phages with affinity for other receptors. This is important, as if neglected 
experiments might yield higher phage count than expected. It would also be beneficial to have a closer 
look at the crystal structure of the AgrC binding site. This crystal structure has however not been 
mapped yet, and thus cannot be analysed at this point in time.  Should future attempts at panning 
procure phages with binding affinity for AgrC, anti-virulence activity should be screened upon 
interference with Agr quorum sensing. This was originally a goal of the thesis, however due to the 
nature of the results this was never performed.

6.2 Closing remarks 
 
 
The stated observations are based exclusively on counted bacterial colonies grown on plates. These 
results do not alone give conclusive evidence that only AgrC antibody inhibition took place during 
the phage display procedure, nor the efficacy of the resulting different phage stock. It is however 
clear that growth is dependent on addition of phage stock, as water control plates exhibited no growth. 
It is unknown what the results would have been if assumptions regarding receptor binding and 
passage through the peptidoglycan layer had not been made. As this was the initial attempt at Agr 
inhibition using phage display, the considerations were done in an attempt of making the process as 
straightforward as possible. In conducting a novel approach of Agr inhibition, the simplistic way had 
to be attempted, if not only to be verified as a non-solution.
