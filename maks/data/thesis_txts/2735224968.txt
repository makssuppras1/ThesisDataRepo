Release of per- and polyfluorinated substances from solar panels
by Simon A. Özkan
This thesis investigates the environmental contamination risk from per- and polyfluorinated substances (PFAS)
in solar panels, specifically within the front sheet and photovoltaic cells (PVC). Addressing the notable gap in
literature regarding PFAS use and quantification in solar technology, this study proposes and applies methods
for PFAS detection, including a surface wipe test and PVC disassembly. Despite recognized benefits of PFAS
in electronics, their potential health and environmental impacts remain a concern. This thesis findings reveal
PFHxA levels of 13, 35, and 83 ng/m2 in JA Solar 570W, Jinko Solar 575W, and Jinko Solar 580W panels, re-
spectively, through the wipe tests. Similarly, PFPeA was detected at levels <0.1, 7, and 17 ng/m2 for the same
panels, respectively. In assessing potential contamination at a solar park in Kolding, the thesis estimates soil
and groundwater contamination levels to be significantly lower than Danish Environmental Quality Standards
(EQS) by a factor of 4 · 107 and 67 times, respectively, with runoff concentrations into a nearby stream being
100 times lower than guideline levels. The disassembly revealed PFAS presence within the PVC, with PFHxA,
PFDA, and PFDdA detected in samples which were not detected in control samples. Scenario analysis for land-
fill disposal indicates minimal leaching risks to surface waters, except in a scenario where 100% leaching could
exceed guideline levels by 25 times. Given the extent of solar panels included in this thesis and sample size, it
calls for further research into PFAS distribution mechanisms and their long-term environmental impacts. This
research underlines the critical need for continued investigation into PFAS use in solar technologies, aiming to
balance their benefits with environmental safety.

1
Background
Providing renewable and sustainable energy have for the last decades been highly desired as to mitigate the
emission of greenhouse gases from fossil fuel burning (Dincer and Ezzat, 2018). In 2021 the total electricity
production from renewable sources accounted for 27% of the global production (IRENA, 2023). This trend is
similar to what is seen in Europe where 22% of the energy consumed in 2021 came from renewable sources.
The three main renewable energy sources are hydro, wind and solar, which accounts for 55%, 23% and 13%,
respectively, of the global green energy production. The EU goal for 2030 is that 32% of energy production
comes from renewable sources (EEA, 2023b). If we are to meet the increased future demands for renewable en-
ergy, then the production and installation of renewable energy technologies must increase accordingly. Among
renewable technologies, solar energy has experienced remarkable growth, with a nearly 23% increase in elec-
tricity generation from 2020 to 2021 worldwide (Arvizu et al., 2011). As this growth continues, understanding
the materials and chemicals used in solar panels becomes essential to assess and mitigate potential environ-
mental impacts, both during their use and disposal phases.
Conventional solar panels are primarily composed of materials such as silicon, gallium arsenide, and various
metals, alongside plastic polymers and glass(Younas et al., 2022). However, concerns have arisen regarding
the use of per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) in manufacturing and in components, as they are per-
sistent and potentially harmful to ecosystems and humans (VMR, 2022). As Denmark strives to expand its
solar energy production significantly by 2030 (Regeringen, 2022), it is crucial to examine the potential for the
presence of PFAS in solar panels, especially if projects are placed near groundwater protected areas.
To address concerns regarding the use of PFAS in solar panels, the scopes of this thesis are:
• A comprehensive literature review, covering PFAS in solar panels to identify potential areas of concern
and strategies to mitigate PFAS contamination
• Testing and assessing the wipe test method, as well as the presence of PFAS in solar panels and potential
groundwater contamination pathways.
• Quantifying potential PFAS masses across three different solar panels and differences in used and unused
panels
Furthermore, this thesis recognizes the extensive array of per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances and their diverse
applications. However, in order to establish a clear scope for the objectives of this thesis, specific boundaries
have been defined. Firstly, the examination of a selected set of 18 PFAS is included. Secondly, only nonpoly-
mers are discussed, a choice informed by the availability of literature and data indicating their prevalence in
the environment and associated harmful effects. Thirdly, the classification for PFAS provided by (OECD, 2021)
is adopted to categorize the types of PFAS under examination. The rationale behind these boundaries will
be elaborated upon in subsequent sections of the thesis. Furthermore, the thesis will introduce methods for
determining PFAS mass in and on solar panels and deploy worst case scenarios for a risk assessment of PFAS
contamination to groundwater reservoirs.

Introduction
2
Understanding per- and polyfluorinated substances
Per- and polyfluorinated substances (PFAS) are recognized for their diverse range of physical and chemi-
cal properties. Possessing exceptional chemical and thermal stability, while also exhibiting hydrophobic and
lipophobic characteristics, PFAS have been excellent as surfactants and in polymer applications utilized in a
variety of industries since the 1940s (Banks et al., 1994; 3M, 1999). However, in the early 2000s concerns re-
garding environmental persistence, bioaccumulation and toxicity were raised, in particular the potential for
accumulation in human food sources, with subsequent adverse effects on health. (Giesy and Kannan, 2001;
Hansen et al., 2001). Following this, experts and working groups have been trying to classify the scope of
industrial PFAS applications including the types of compounds with the aim of regulating the use and iden-
tifying the potential for substitution with other compounds with similar properties (Giesy and Kannan, 2001;
Buck et al., 2011; VMR, 2022; Baun et al., 2023). Some areas of greater concern have been identified in scien-
tific literature, which includes common consumer products like textiles, paper, and carpets treated with PFAS
coatings, their use as processing aids in fluoropolymer production, and their presence in aqueous film-forming
foam (AFFF) (Glüge et al., 2020; Buck et al., 2011). However, given the versatility of PFAS, research has shown
that it is highly plausible that PFAS have been directly integrated into components or used in upstream pro-
cesses across most industries (Glüge et al., 2020; Tsitonaki and Nicolajsen, 2016). Recent studies have revealed
a concerning trend in the presence of PFAS in the energy sector, including in photovoltaic energy (Glüge et al.,
2020; OECD, 2022). Understanding the intricate use of PFAS in solar panels and their potential environmental
implications is crucial as the world transitions to cleaner and more sustainable sources of energy.
This study seeks to contribute to the growing body of knowledge on PFAS, their environmental fate, and the
imperative need to address their presence in technologies like solar panels. Moreover, the specific challenges
posed by PFAS in the context of solar panels will be explored, aiming to assess their presence and the potential
consequences of their use in the expanding renewable energy sector.
2.1
Terminology and classification of PFAS
PFAS are compounds that contains fluorine atoms attached to a carbon chain, also called the ’tail’ with a func-
tional group at the end called the ’head’. However, there is no universal definition encompassing the entirety
of PFAS. Currently, two definitions are most widely recognized. The first is the definition by (Buck et al., 2011)
from 2011, which was the first time a scientific labeling was provided for PFAS. (Buck et al., 2011) defined PFAS
as "highly fluorinated aliphatic substances containing one or more carbon atoms where all hydrogen substituents (found
in their nonfluorinated analogues) had been replaced by fluorine atoms, forming the perfluoroalkyl moiety CnF2n+1 (with
a minimum requirement of at least one CF3 group)" (Buck et al., 2011). However, this definition excludes some
compounds that are structurally similar but do not meet the exact CnF2n+1 moiety, such as perfluoroalkyl di-
carboxylic acids. Therefore the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) suggested
a revised edition of the definition in 2021 (OECD, 2021). This updated definition classifies PFAS as "fluori-
nated substances containing at least one fully fluorinated methyl or methylene carbon atom, devoid of hydrogen, chlorine,
bromine, or iodine atoms attached to it". In essence, any chemical with a perfluorinated methyl group (–CF3) or
a perfluorinated methylene group (CF2–) qualifies as a PFAS, with a few exceptions (OECD, 2021). This defi-
nition includes compounds with the moiety CnF2n −(n ≥1. The exact extent of how many PFAS are available
varies greatly and depends on the definition. For example in the OECD report 4,730 PFAS were identified,
whereas the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) includes almost 15,000 compounds according to

2.2
Exploring the PFAS family tree
The PFAS family can be categorized into two principal classes: polymers and nonpolymers. Nonpolymers are
the most frequently identified compounds within the PFAS family in environmental contexts, underscoring
their ecological relevance (ITRC, 2022). Moreover, data on potential human health and environmental effects
resulting from exposure to these nonpolymer PFAS compounds are readily available (ITRC, 2022; EFSA, 2020).
Figure 1 outlines the groups and subgroups of PFAS. A further expansion of this figure can be found in (ITRC,
2022). Furthermore, the groups and subgroups encapsulated in blue squares encompass the 18 PFAS this thesis
examines (see annex 11.1 for the molecular structure, name and CAS-nr of the 18 investigated PFAS). The
choice of focusing on nonpolymers is primarily dictated by the laboratory’s analytical limitations. However,
the emphasis on nonpolymers in this study is supported by several key factors, which are also highlighted in
the ITRC report from 2022 "Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances Technical and Regulatory Guidance" (ITRC,
2022).

Introduction
2.3
Types of PFAS
The general chemical structure of perfluoroalkyls and polyfluoroalkyls consists of a carbon chain, known as the
tail, with varying degrees of fluorination. Perfluoroalkyls exhibit a fully fluorinated carbon chain, replacing all
hydrogen atoms with fluorine, while polyfluoroalkyls retain some hydrogen atoms (Buck et al., 2011; OECD,
2021). Attached at one end of the chain is a charged functional group, known as the head, imparting unique
properties to the PFAS, contributing to their diverse applications in industrial and consumer products (Buck
et al., 2011; Glüge et al., 2020). More than 42 different groups and subgroups of per- and polyfluoroalkyls have
been identified, all with different homologues members and isomers (linear and branched). Perfluoroalkyl
acids (PFAAs) and perfluoroalkane sulfonamides (PASAFs) are two important types of PFAS which are often
discussed in literature (Buck et al., 2011; VMR, 2022; ITRC, 2022). PFAAs, including carboxylic acids (PFCAs)
and sulfonic acids (PFSAs), have historically undergone extensive research and regulations, driven in part by
the early detection of compounds like perfluorooctanesulfonic acid (PFOS) and perfluorooctanoic acid (PFOA)
in the environment (Giesy and Kannan, 2001; Houde et al., 2006). Figure 2 displays the linear isomer of PFOS
and PFOA. Typically, PFAAs are categorized as being long-chained or short-chained. While different variations
of this categorization exist, this thesis will adhere to the OECD definition, which defines long-chain PFSAs
as having six or more fully fluorinated carbon atoms in the alkyl chain (CnF2n+1SO3H,n ≥6) and PFCAs as
having seven or more fully fluorinated carbon atoms in the alkyl chain (CnF2n+1COOH,n ≥7) (OECD, 2023).
PFAAs detected in the environment have either been produced and used for industrial purposes or as the final
step in biotic or abiotic degradation of polyfluorinated substances known as ’precursors’ (Buck et al., 2011;
ITRC, 2022). This have led to PFAAs being termed ’terminal’ PFAS, as further degradation in nature does not
occur (Buck et al., 2011; VMR, 2022). Historically, linear PFAS with an even number of carbon atoms in the
chain have mainly been manufactured by telomerization (TM). Branched PFAS isomers with both even and
uneven numbers of carbon atoms in the chain have been manufactured up until 2002 with Electrochemical
fluorination (ECF) (Buck et al., 2011).

Environmental fate and transport of PFAS
The fate and transport mechanisms of PFAS in the environment are complex and depend on various analyt-
ical parameters, encompassing both their physical-chemical properties and site-specific characteristics (Baun
et al., 2017; VMR, 2022). The chemical and thermal stability of PFAS, is attributed to the strong covalent
C-F bond, which requires a high oxidation potential to break. Furthermore, the F atoms attached to the C
atoms are shielded from potential displacement due to their size (Banks et al., 1994; VMR, 2022; ITRC, 2022).
Biodegradation processes of PFAS vary among different groups and subgroups. In literature, polyfluorinated
PFAS have been observed to undergo degradation in the environment, categorizing them as precursors. These
precursors frequently convert into terminal PFAAs, which do not undergo further degradation once they are
formed (Wang et al., 2009; Rhoads et al., 2008; Kwon et al., 2014). The 20 PFCAs and PFSAs encompassed
in the Environmental Quality Standard (EQS) framework are all regarded as persistent and does not degrade
under environmental conditions in soil or groundwater (VMR, 2022). The intricacy on the fate and transport
is further complicated by the dual nature of PFAS surfactants, characterized by the hydro- and lipophobic tail
contra the hydrophilic head. Moreover, diversity among different types of PFAS manifests notable variations
in behavior within environmental media (VMR, 2022). This is all exacerbated by the scarcity in available scien-
tific literature and the information regarding the majority of PFAS is limited. Consequently, researchers often
resort to generalizations, extrapolating from known physical and chemical properties of categorized short and
long-chained PFAS to deduce the behaviors of other PFAS with analogous chain lengths and similar head
properties (ITRC, 2022).
The Interstate Technology and Regulatory Council (ITRC) continuously update a database with physical-
chemical properties for different PFAS found in literature, which can be found at their website (ITRC, 2023).
It includes, but is not limited to, solubility (S), Henry’s constant (KH or Kaw), adsorption coefficient (Koc),
Acid dissociation constant (pKa) and vapour pressure (Vp). The values are determined based on Experimental
(E)/laboratory (L) work, Extrapolated (Ex) or mathematical modeled methods (M), see Appendix 11.2 for de-
scription of the different model definitions. From the data extracted from the database, the physical-chemical
properties are plotted against the chain length of a selected 22 PFAS, following the compounds encompassed
by the EQS framework, which are discussed in Section 4. The entirety of the plotted figures for all the physical-
chemical properties can be found in Appendix 11.2. The data were extracted in November 2023, and it is
important to note that subsequent changes in the dataset may have occurred.
3.1
Behavior in water
The hydrophilic nature of the PFAS moiety, as signified by its water-attracting head, indicates a readiness for
dissolution in aqueous environments. Figure 3a shows solubility plots generated through two distinct meth-
ods, revealing the potential water solubility of short-chained PFAS. Notably, the solubility values exhibit sig-
nificant variability across different methodologies, often spanning orders of magnitude (103), as illustrated in
the solubility plots provided in Appendix 11.2. Despite these methodological variations, a consensus emerges
within the scientific literature, indicating that the solubility of PFAS generally decreases with an increase in
the length of the carbon chain (ITRC, 2022; VMR, 2022). Utilizing the most conservative data from the ITRC
database to estimate maximum and minimum solubility for PFCAs, PFSAs and their precursors, the following
trends are observed:

• Perfluorinated carboxylic acids (PFCAs) exhibit a solubility range decreasing from approximately 50,000

Noticeably, PFOSA also exhibits higher pKa values compared to the other investigated PFAS, at approximately

typical environmental conditions, especially in groundwater where the pH typically ranges from 5 to 9 (VMR,
2022; Ågren and Andersson, 2012). In contrast, PFCAs and PFSAs display a higher degree of dissociation,
primarily as anions due to their lower pKa values. It is worth noting that while PFAAs, including PFCAs
and PFSAs, are considered strong acids due to their low pKa values, their presence in groundwater does
not significantly impact the pH levels. This is attributed to the low concentration levels of PFAS found in
groundwater aquifers, typically measured in the range of nanograms to micrograms per liter (Johnson et al.,
2022; VMR, 2022). In more contaminated sites, such as old fire training areas and manufacturing plants, higher
concentrations in the milligrams per liter range can be found in the soil (Brusseau et al., 2020; ITRC, 2022).
3.2
Distribution in solid phases
The mobility of PFAS in solid phases, including soil, is often characterized by the adsorption coefficient be-
tween organic carbon and water (Koc) (Mccall et al., 1980; Baun et al., 2017). According to (Mccall et al., 1980),
chemicals with Koc values between 0 and 50 are classified as very mobile in soils, while those with Koc > 5,000
are classified as immobile. Koc is typically derived from the octanol-water partion coefficient Kow (Baun et al.,
2017). However, due to the surfactant properties inherent to most PFAS, measuring Kow infeasible. Conse-
quently, Koc is estimiated in most literature, introducing considerable uncertainties (VMR, 2022). Nonetheless,
some experimental values for Koc are available in the ITRC sheet. Figure 3b depicts the Koc for PFAS with
chain length from 3 to 14, with FE representing the field-derived experimental data and LE extrapolated data.
Although there are differences in the order of magnitude between the two methods, a consistent trend emerges:
• Short-chained PFAAs (C3 to C6) and PFBS exhibit higher mobility in soil, with lower Koc values, aligning
with established scientific literature (Higgins and Luthy, 2006; Gellrich et al., 2012).
• Conversely, longer-chained PFCAs (C7 to C8) and PFHxS (C6) display reduced mobility in soil, indicated
by higher Koc values as the perfluorocarbon chain length increases.
It is important to note that Koc introduces challenges in capturing the complexity of PFAS behavior. This
is due to the amphiphilic structure of PFAS and potential electrostatic interactions with soil organic matter
(SOM) and minerals under relevant environmental conditions (Higgins and Luthy, 2006; Nguyen et al., 2020).
While hydrophobicity and electrostatic interactions are generally considered as primary sorption mechanisms
to solid phases (Nguyen et al., 2020; Pereira et al., 2018), the dominant mechanism remains unclear (Higgins
and Luthy, 2006; ITRC, 2022). In (ITRC, 2023) it is suggested to use the retardation (R) coefficient to evaluate
contaminant-specific velocity. The retardation coefficient describes how much slower a compound moves in

Other distribution mechanisms
The distribution coefficient between air and water (Kaw, Henry’s constants) serves as a valuable metric for
assessing the potential evaporation of PFAS in the soil (VMR, 2022). Categorically, Kaw values > 0.1 indicate

high evaporation, values from 0.00005 - 0.1 suggest medium evaporation, and values < 0.00005 indicate low
evaporation (VMR, 2022). Applying these criteria reveals that most PFAS demonstrate a medium evaporation
potential, with only the long-chained PFCAs PFUnDA, PFDoDA, and PFTrDA classified as highly volatile
(refer to Appendix 11.2 for the plotted Kaw values). This assessment suggests that, if diluted in water, the
majority of the investigated PFAS are less likely to transition into the air in the pore spaces. However, the
surfactant properties inherent to PFAS may contribute to their propensity to migrate towards air-water inter-
faces, as discussed earlier. Additionally, it is important to note that the degree of dissociation at different pH
for PFAAs dictates that Kaw is pH-dependent, and therefore, the values can vary significantly under different
environmental conditions (ITRC, 2022).
In VMR (2022) an analysis of the phase distribution between pore water, pore air, and soil was performed for
selected PFAS in different soil types: sand (Koc = 0.1%), clay (Koc = 0.1%), and sandy loam (Koc = 2%). This
analysis utilized the JAGG 2.1 tool provided by the Danish Environmental Agency. The findings revealed
that PFAS are primarily distributed between the soil and pore water, with minimal presence in the pore air.
Additionally, the study highlighted a decrease in the amount of PFAS in pore water with increasing chain
length. Notably, in both sand and clay, characterized by lower carbon content, the concentration in pore water
is anticipated to be higher compared to soil types with higher carbon content (VMR, 2022).
The vapor pressure (Vp), illustrated in Appendix 11.2, describes the tendency of compound to partition into
the gas phase (i.e. the volatility). The higher the Vp the more volatile the compounds is and it’s tendency
to undergo long-range transport increases. The given values are, as for the other parameters, for the none-
dissociated PFAS, and it is stated in the ITRC that these values are related to great uncertainties when as-
sessing PFAS in normal environmental condition, where they mostly are present in their dissociated form.
However, a general trend for the Vp can also be seen for the categorized PFAS, where the volatility decreases
as chain length increases. Generally all PFAS are less volatile than typical pollutants such as benzene and
trichlorethylen (TCE) (VMR, 2022).

Environmental and drinking water quality standards
Due to the comprehensive nature of PFAS, studies on adverse effects in humans and animals have been con-
fined to a few specific substances. The primary focus has been on PFCAs and PFSAs, where PFOS and PFOA
serve as proxies for general assessments of adverse effects resulting from exposure to elevated concentra-
tions of PFAS (ITRC, 2022). The main concern revolves around PFAS bioaccumulative nature, as although
they generally exhibit low acute toxicity, prolonged exposure to elevated levels can lead to various health is-
sues including reproductive problems, carcinogenicity, and endocrine disruption. The adverse effects depend
on parameters such as exposure routes, substance composition, and individual susceptibility factors, includ-
ing health, age, and gender (VMR, 2022). The European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) establishes Tolerable
Weekly Intake (TWI) levels, with the latest revision from 2020 setting a unified threshold of 4.4 ng/kg of body
weight per week for PFOS, PFOA, PFNA, and PFHxS (EFSA, 2020). The Danish Environmental Protection
Agency (EPA) aligns with these standards, establishing Environmental Quality Standards (EQS) to prevent
adverse effects on human health and ecosystems across various environmental media. The EQS framework,
as outlined in (VMR, 2022) and (Miljøministeriet, 2023), encompasses PFAS in drinking water, groundwater
and soil. Additionally, the EQS extends to PFOS in surface water and biota, as Denmark follows EU’s environ-
mental quality standard (VMR, 2022). In Table 1 the EQS for the different environmental matrices is presented.
The EQS framework involves 22 specific PFAS compounds, with a heightened focus on PFOA, PFOS, PFNA,
and PFHxS, based upon the TWI determined by EFSA. Stricter requirements apply to these four compounds
(Miljøministeriet, 2023).

Regulatory overview of PFAS
The adverse health effects, coupled with the diverse physical and chemical properties of PFAS, are apparent
reasons for the stringent regulations that have been imposed on the different types of PFAS. Global regulatory
efforts have primarily targeted long-chained PFAS due to early discoveries of PFOS in human blood. PFOS,
PFOA, and, as of 2022, PFHxS are categorized as Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs), and long-chained PF-
CAs (C9-21) are all included in the Stockholm Convention for global elimination (EEA, 2023a; Stockholm Con-
vention, 2019). In the EU, restrictions on PFCAs (C9-14), their salts, and precursors have been in effect since
February 2023. Additionally, various PFAS groups, including certain short-chained PFAS used as substitutes
for PFOA, have been identified as Substances of Very High Concern (SVHC) under the REACH regulation
(EEA, 2023a).
Anticipating future regulations, the proposal in January 2023 by the Danish, German, Dutch, Swedish, and
Norwegian governments seeks to restrict the use of 10,000 PFAS, indicating a continued commitment to strin-
gent regulatory measures (EEA, 2023a). Identifying areas where insufficient data is available regarding the
application of PFAS becomes increasingly apparent, particularly in technologies essential for our future, such
as solar panels in energy production. This awareness is crucial as the EU strives towards achieving a society
with net zero pollution, encompassing air, soil, and water (EU, 2023).

Literature review of PFAS in solar panels
A literature review of PFAS utilization in solar panels currently reveals a scarcity of information. Despite em-
ploying keywords such as "photovoltaic" or "solar panels" alongside "per- and polyfluorinated substances" or
"PFAS" in literature searches, there is an absence of scientific documentation detailing the types and quantities
of PFAS utilized in solar panels. Despite the limited availability of explicit documentation on the application
of PFAS in solar panels, recent attention has emphasized their presence and significance in the renewable en-
ergy sector (Glüge et al., 2020; Gastel, 2021; OECD, 2022; ACC, 2023). Moreover, the historical use of PFAS as
insulators in electronics, driven by their thermal and chemical stability, suggests a potential role for PFAS in
photovoltaic cells (Glüge et al., 2020; Tsitonaki and Nicolajsen, 2016; OECD, 2022). A key consideration is the
global manufacture of approximately 90% of all solar panel units in China (Statista, 2021), leading to limited
transparency in communication between Western consumers and Chinese suppliers, thereby complicating the
understanding of the entire production chain (Wilczewski et al., 2018).
The construction of monocrystalline and polycrystalline silicon solar panels typically involves embedding
photovoltaic cells in encapsulants, sandwiched between a glass front sheet and a back sheet. The structure is
reinforced by an aluminum frame, and a junction box at the back facilitates the transfer of electricity out of the
unit (Pastuszak and We¸gierek, 2022). Figure 5 illustrates a standard solar panels construction, emphasizing
potential PFAS use in different components determined from literature sources.

6.2
Case Study: Kolding Municipality Solar Park Project
In 2022, Kolding municipality entered into a collaborative agreement with Better Energy A/S to undertake
an expansive solar park project. This initiative is situated within a 347-hectare area in Viuf, with the solar
park encompassing 218 hectares. Notably, the remaining 129 hectares have been earmarked for conversion
into recreational zones and a dedicated groundwater protection area, as outlined in the project details (Orien,
2022).
The solar park’s strategic location in proximity to Viuf water plants, which draw water from a nearby ground-
water reservoir, adds a layer of complexity and significance to the project. Given the dual objectives of pro-
moting recreational activities and safeguarding the groundwater reservoir, an environmental assessment was
incorporated into the project agreement (Andersen, S., 2022). Addressing contemporary concerns about the
presence of PFAS in solar panels, Better Energy took a proactive approach by entering into a collaboration
with Technical University of Denmark (DTU) to conduct an in-depth investigation of the three types of solar
panels proposed for installation in the park.
The initiative aligns with Denmark’s goal to quadruple sustainable energy production by 2030 compared to
2022, indicative of the nation’s commitment to advancing solar panel technologies (Regeringen, 2022). No-
tably, the Viuf project is emblematic of a broader trend, with numerous solar park proposals emerging across
Denmark (Orien, 2022).
Moreover, considering the collective stance of Denmark, Sweden, Norway, Netherlands, and Germany in
proposing a comprehensive ban on all PFAS substances to REACH the European Union’s (EU) chemicals
regulation (EEA, 2023a), it becomes evident that validating the absence of PFAS in solar panels is pivotal for
future installations. Consequently, the research conducted by DTU and this thesis assumes a crucial role in
shaping the trajectory of solar panel deployment, not just in Kolding municipality but potentially influencing
regulatory perspectives at a broader regional scale.
6.2.1
Site Characteristics and Geological Overview

Despite the slow transport, several studies have highlighted that accumulated PFAS can permeate into un-
derlying aquifers, facilitated by extreme precipitation or disturbance of the soil structures (Nguyen et al.,
2020; Shao et al., 2016; Guelfo et al., 2020; Borthakur et al., 2021). Considering that most PFAS, especially
long-chained perfluorinated PFAS, do not degrade under normal environmental conditions, the potential for
elevated concentrations in groundwater reservoirs resulting from years of accumulation raises concerns. Fur-
thermore, PFOA, PFNA, PFHxS and PFOS are all long-chained PFAS and conversely shorter-chained PFAS
are, as described earlier, not expected to behave similarly in the environment. Furthermore, besides soil and
groundwater infiltration potential soil surface runoff into nearby lakes or stream is also a potential contam-
ination source. This is especially relevant given the addition of guidelines for surface waters at 4.4 ng/L.
Considering surface runoff into a stream, Equation 3 can be used to calculate the maximum concentration of a
pollutant at the entry of a stream from a nearby pollution source.
Cmax = J/Q
(3)
Where J is the discharge given in kg/yr, Q is the waterflow in the stream, given in l/s and (Cmax is the max-
imum concentration at the inlet, given in kg/l. The equation does not consider adsorption, dispersion or
degradation and can thus be applied to illustrate a worst case emission scenario. Furthermore, due to mixing
the concentration will be diluted as mixing increases the further it travels from the shoreline. Consequently,
the specific point chosen to extract measurements along the stream will provide different concentrations of the

7.1.1
Screening process
Figure 8a illustrates the step-by-step process employed in the wipe sampling technique in the screening phase.
Single sterilize gauze pads (10x10cm) were soaked in 5 ml isopropyl alcohol(99.9% HPLC grade, Sigma-
Aldrich) prior to wiping and stored in 50 ml polypropylene blue cap tube. Using a single pad, squares 7,
8, and 9 at each solar panel module, see Figure 9, were wiped twice with a short period in between following
an S-shaped pattern, substituting the pads between each newly initiated wipe. In the result section the two
wipes are denoted as "Wipe 1" and "Wipe 2". After wiping the pads were stored in the same 50 ml polypropy-
lene vial used prior to wiping until extraction. Stringent measures were taken to minimize cross-contamination
during wiping, including the use of protective coats and gloves, as well as using stainless steel crucible tongs
rinsed in methanol before and during wiping.
For extraction of the samples 5 ml of methanol was added to each sample tube, followed by placement in
an ultrasonication bath for 30 minutes. Subsequently, the solutions was decanted into 10 ml vials without
transferring the pad. This procedure was repeated twice. The decanted solutions were subsequently sent to
LC/MS-MS analysis.
7.1.2
Total mass estimation
The process employed for estimating the total mass estimation is illustrated at Figure 8b. Single sterilze pads
were soaked in 5 ml of methanol and stored in 50 ml polypropylene blue cap tubes, prior to wiping. The
S-shaped pattern was deployed to collect 15 (n=15) individual wipes from a single square area with pauses
between wipes to allow excess methanol to evaporate. After wiping, pads were stored in the same 50 ml
polypropylene vial used prior to wiping until extraction.
Extraction of samples involved the addition of 10 ml of methanol to each tube, followed by 30 minutes of
ultrasonication. The resulting solution was decanted into 10 ml vials, once again without transferring the
pads, and sent to LC/MS-MS analysis. This extraction procedure was performed only once.

the retrieval of 20 ml samples. In the discussion section, a detailed breakdown of the samples collected from

each PFAS analyte can be quantified in a maximum of nine samples in both the solar panel and control samples.
The extracted samples were stored in polypropylene blue cap bottles for both sets (solar panels and control).
Additional methanol was added whenever necessary due to evaporation, as the containers were not entirely
airtight. This extraction process spanned a duration of three weeks, with the collected samples being stored at
room temperature throughout this period.
Moreover, during the solar panel cutting procedure, a total of 34 grams of dust particles was collected. These
particles were gathered and placed inside a single polypropylene blue cap bottle, followed by the addition
of 50 ml of methanol. This mixture was left undisturbed at room temperature until the end of the three-week
period, when it was subjected to extraction. At the end of the three weeks, the collected samples were decanted
into 10 ml vials and sent to LC/MS-MS analysis.

7.3
Sample preparation and analysis
This section outlines the procedure followed to prepare the samples collected from solar panels for analy-
sis, and then to analyze these samples to determine the presence of the 18 specific PFAS. The approach uti-
lizes High Performance Liquid Chromatography coupled with Triple Quadrupole Mass Spectrometry (HPLC-
MS/MS).
Initially, the liquid extracts from the solar panels were placed in 10 ml vials. These vials were then subjected to
a gentle stream of nitrogen gas. This step evaporates all the solvent, leaving behind the extracted substances
for further analysis.
After drying, the residues were reconstituted in a solvent mixture of methanol and a 10 mM ammonium acetate
solution (50:50). Methanol serves as a versatile solvent that dissolves a wide range of organic compounds,
while the ammonium acetate buffer, maintained at a pH of 4, helps stabilize the PFAS.
7.3.1
Analytical Procedure Using HPLC-MS/MS
For the analysis, the reconstituted samples are introduced into an HPLC system equipped with a special-
ized chromatography column (Zorbax EclipsePlus C18 RR). The separation is achieved by employing a gradi-
ent elution method, which gradually changes the solvent composition from primarily water-based (aqueous
phase, containing 10 mM ammonium acetate at pH 4) to mostly methanol-based (organic phase, with 95%
methanol and 10 mM ammonium acetate at pH 4). This technique separates the mixture of PFAS present in
the samples.
Following separation, the PFAS are detected using Triple Quadrupole Mass Spectrometry in electrospray ion-
ization (ESI) mode. This detection method uses Dynamic Multiple Reaction Monitoring (dMRM) to specifically
target and quantify the PFAS.
To guarantee the integrity of the results, blank samples, consisting solely of the solvent, are analyzed alongside
the actual environmental samples.
The efficiency of the sample preparation and analytical process is evaluated by adding a known quantity (10
ng) of each PFAS analyte to blank samples. These samples undergo the same preparation and analysis process
as the actual samples. Comparing the measured amounts to the known amounts added provides insight into
the recovery efficiency of the method, ensuring the reliability of the results.
The method’s sensitivity is reflected in the quantification limit, set at 0.1 ng/l for both the wipe tests and the
disassembly analyses. This indicates the minimum concentration of PFAS that can be accurately detected and
quantified.

9
Discussion
The PFAS extraction during the wiping phase was carried out using either an isopropanol or methanol so-
lution prior to wiping. Isopropanol resulted in an average recovery rate of 83%, while methanol yielded an
average recovery rate of 68% for all 18 PFAS tested. In this study, a recovery rate between 80% and 120% was
considered acceptable for the proposed method. Consequently, while isopropanol met this criterion, methanol
did not, falling below the acceptable range. However, to ensure subsequent emission scenarios were assessed
without introducing further uncertainties, the quantified values obtained using the methanol solution were
not adjusted.
9.1
Quantified PFAS from solar panel surface employing the wipe test method
In Section 5 a literature review raises the possibility that PFAS can have been utilized in the front sheet of the
panels. The quantification of PFHxA and PFPeA in the screening phase suggests, that for these two PFAS, this
concern is valid. Subsequent investigation into the solar panel types, revealed that JiSo575W and JiSo580W
consistently exhibited greater levels of PFHxA and PFPeA during both the first and second wipe cycle, regard-
less of the weathered condition, as illustrated on Figure 10. Notably, JiSo580W exhibited the highest quantified
mass of PFHxA. However, from the JiSo575W fewer total wipes were collected for both the new and used
condition. Consequently, less data points are available for this module type. Nevertheless, the intention of
the screening test was to identify the presence of the analysed PFAS, which was effectively achieved. Con-
versely, it could be hypothesized that PFHxA and PFPeA might originate from dry atmospheric deposition.
In the study by Paragot et al. (2020), an average of 0.52 pg/m3 for the sum of 10 PFCAs (including PFHxA
and PFPeA) were detected over a two-year sampling period. Considering this atmospheric concentration and
the flow rate of 31.4 m3/h from a high-volume air sampler, calculations indicate that to achieve the observed
concentration of 83 ng/m2 PFHxA on the surface of a solar panel, approximately 160 m3 of air would need to
pass over the panel. This corresponds to a duration of approximately 212 days, assuming a constant air flow
and complete deposition of PFAS onto the panel’s surface. While these findings could suggest the influence
of atmospheric deposition, the scenario is complicated by the storage conditions of the panels. Notably, the
quantified PFHxA from the weathered JiSo575W is less than from the JiSo580W, which had been stored inside.
Moreover, considering that in (Paragot et al., 2020), short-chained PFAS such as PFHxA contributed less to
the particle fraction from the collected volume compared to long-chained PFAS, underscores the potential for
alternative sources or mechanisms contributing to the observed PFAS on solar panels. (Skjolding et al., 2023)
analysed atmospheric deposition of 15 PFAS from the surface of weathered old glass facade (>20 years old) and

9.2
Release scenario from the surface of the panels
Following the proposition that the total mass of PFAS can be quantified from the surface of the panels, the sub-
sequent section revolves around assessing the potential release scenarios of PFAS to each distinct pathways as
illustrated on Figure 7. It is assumed that any surface volatilization into the atmosphere is negligible, primarily
due to the low evaporation potential of short-chained PFAS like PFHxA. This assumption is further supported
by the fact that their vapor pressure is considerably lower compared to typical volatile compounds such as
benzene and TCE, as discussed in Section 3. Additionally, it is assumed that the entire mass of PFHxA is dis-
charged into each specific environmental medium. These scenarios, deliberately conservative, aim to delineate
the scope of PFAS emission from the PVC, serving as a preliminary measures of environmental impacts.
Given the minimal quantified mass of PFPeA at 7 ng/m2 compared to the 83 ng/m2 of PFHxA from the
JiSo580W, Table 7, the release scenarios will be based upon the quantified mass of PFHxA. The EQS levels
specified by the Danish EPA, see Table 1, are used as reference values for the scenarios. For soil and ground-
water the EQS for the sum of 22 PFAS is 0.4 mg/kg dw and 100 ng/l, respectively. Moreover, for surface water
contamination assessments, the guideline level for the sum of 24 PFAS at 4.4 ng/l is used for reference.
Firstly, with respect to soil contamination, assuming 100% sorption to solid phases and a volume of 1 m3 soil,
which is directly below 1 m2 of solar panels, would amount to a 83 ng PFHxA/m3 soil. Using a standard dry
bulk density for soils at 1400 kg/m3 (Yu et al., 1993), the resulting PFHxA content in the soil directly below
the panels will be approximately 0.1 ng/kg dw. This is a factor of 4 · 107 less than the EQS level. Additionally,
referring to Table 3, soil PFAS levels in forests and urban areas ranged from <0 to 0.7 µg/kg dw for 16 different
PFAS. Specifically for PFHxA, (Olsen and Sukstorf, 2022) reports a mass of approximately 0.1 µg/kg dw for
urban areas. Thus, potential PFHxA contamination from the solar panels would be about 9000 times lower than
observed in Danish and Norwegian cities. Therefore, soil contamination at the site is of minimal concern given
these assumptions. Furthermore, PFHxA is a short-chained PFCA containing five fully fluorinated carbon
atoms. Consequently, the Koc value is small compared to other PFAS as seen at Figure 3b. Consequently, it
would be assumed that PFHxA is transported through the soil potentially contaminating groundwater.
Following this, the potential groundwater concentration was calculated for the three different solar panels for
PFHxA and PFPeA, as seen in Table 7, assuming the entire mass of PFAS from the project area is emitted to
the groundwater aquifer, as outlined in Section 9.2. The greatest resulting concentration for PFHxA was found
to be 1.5 ng/l for the JiSo580W. It should be emphasised that the assumptions illustrate worst-case scenarios.
Consequently, it is challenging to envision a scenario where EQS levels are exceeded under environmental con-
ditions, with these assumptions. Especially given that the concentration at 1.5 ng/l is 67 times lower than the
EQS level. Additionally, incorporating the dilution factor, at 700, calculated based on an annual precipitation
of 600 mm over the entire project area, indicates that the likelihood of surpassing groundwater level thresholds
remains minimal, with the given assumptions. For JaSo570W and JiSo575W, even less concern is associated
with their use, due to the quantified masses. This figure, although not exceeding the EQS, surpasses the typical
PFAS levels (<0.1-1 ng/l) identified in Danish groundwater, as shown in Table 3. Such a concentration could
suggest a risk of increased PFHxA levels near solar park projects, yet confirming this risk necessitates evidence
of continuous PFHxA discharge, which is currently undetermined. Additionally, it should be acknowledged

conceptual model shown in Figure 7. Given the high solubility of PFHxA at approximately 1,000 mg/l, as

on guidelines outlined in (SCHEER, 2022). With this RPF in mind, even with the highest groundwater con-

and adsorption. Nevertheless, this analysis confirms that under conservative assumptions, the established
guideline level will not be exceeded.
In summary, the wipe test method was proven effective in identifying PFAS on solar panels, leading to a choice

the need for collaboration among manufacturers, regulators, and consumers, balancing energy efficiency with
environmental protection. Engaging stakeholders in transparent discussions about PFAS risks and sustainable

9.3
End of life scenarios
Section 5 highlighted the increasing deployment of solar panels concurrently with the growing adoption of
green energy sources. However, inadequate regulations and infrastructure for proper disposal of solar panels
persist in many countries, potentially leading to thousands of units ending up in landfills (CBS, 2023). Similar
to the disposal of electronic hardware, pollutants such as PFAS can leach into the environment over decades
as solar panel components degrade or are fragmented before disposal (Tansel, 2022). To address this concern,
the disassembled module test was incorporated into this thesis to investigate the presence of PFAS in deeper
layers of the PVC, comprising the encapsulants and the photovoltaic cell (Figure 5).
Regarding the declining trend observed towards week three for each PFAS (Figure 12), two potential mech-
anisms are proposed. Firstly, PFAS may undergo degradation in the methanol solution used for sampling,
resulting in a decrease in concentration over time. However, a study by (Zhang et al., 2021) concluded that
degradation of various fluorinated substances in a methanol solution is negligible, challenging this explana-
tion. Alternatively, early displacement of PFAS from the PVC is suggested, where PFAS present in the inner
layers may be displaced and released during module disassembly, particularly upon contact with an adsorp-
tive medium. However, this proposition relies on the assumption that the quantified PFAS originates from the
PVCs.
A Welch t-test, based upon the values provided in Table 8, assessed the statistical significance of differences
in quantified mass between PVC and control samples, with a confidence interval of 95%. Notably, while
statistically significant differences were observed only for PFOA in week two, the higher mass of PFOA in
control samples makes it inconclusive to determine its origin. Despite the lack of statistical significance for
most PFAS, including PFHxA, several considerations regarding result validation should be emphasized. These
include a small sample pool, extraction of PFAS from the same sampling pools, quantification uncertainties,
and limitations in the statistical test’s premise. Additionally, a qualitative assessment, assuming that in cases
of quantification in two or three of the PVC samples and none of the control samples, offers insights into PFAS
utilization.
Throughout the three weeks of sampling, PFHxA was the most frequently quantified PFAS with a total of eight
occurrences in the PVC samples. While the Welch t-test indicates no significant difference in quantified masses,
it’s noteworthy that PFHxA was identified in the screening phase. Additionally, its mass was approximately 8,
3, and 2 times higher in the PVCs than in the control samples for weeks one, two, and three, respectively. This
could suggest that the additional mass originates from the PVC. Furthermore, comparing the quantified mass
from the surface of JaSo570W, at 13 ng/m2, with that of quantified mass from week one reveals a 6.5 times
greater mass in the PVCs than on the front sheet. This substantial difference could imply that the additional
mass of PFHxA originates from the interior of the PVC rather than just being present on the surface.
The quantified masses of PFNA, PFUdA, and 6:2 FTS emerged as the top three highest quantified PFAS,
as shown in Figure 12. Notably, these compounds exhibited less disparity in quantification between PVCs
and control compared to other PFAS, suggesting alternative sources for their presence. For instance, 6:2 FTS
likely originated from metal containers, as evidenced by minimal traces found in the dust sample stored in
polypropylene blue cap bottles for three weeks, as illustrated in Figure 13a. Moreover, the decreasing trend
observed for 6:2 FTS over the three-week period implies a fast release from the containers. Conversely, PFNA
and PFUdA showed higher concentrations in the dust sample, indicating a potential origin from polypropy-
lene blue cap bottles. This aligns with the decreasing trend observed in PVCs and control samples, where
minimal traces of PFNA were quantified in samples analyzed on the same day of extraction. Additionally,

9.4
Disposal and leaching scenarios
This section proposes analytical scenarios for the environmental impacts of PFAS following the disposal of
solar panels into a landfill. The analysis follow the assumption that PFAS concentrations exceeding those in
control samples originate from the PVC. The methodology incorporates an aggregation of all PFAS concentra-
tions identified above control levels to simulate worst-case environmental leaching scenarios.
Data on PFAS leaching from landfills within the Danish context are scarce. Nevertheless, research conducted
in the United States, specifically by (Tolaymat et al., 2023), indicates that around 11% of PFAS present in mu-
nicipality waste materials may leach from landfill sites. Recognizing the variance in composition between
municipal waste and e-waste, this analysis adopts the 11% leaching proposition, alongside 100% leaching sce-
narios, as a foundational assumption due to the limitations in literature.
The scenarios established for the disposal of the solar panels mirrors the scenarios outlined in Section 9.2,
projecting both soil, groundwater and stream concentrations under hypothetical conditions reflective of the
Viuf site. The cumulative mass of PFAS from one square meter of PVC was derived from the difference between
the quantified mass from the PVC samples and the control samples for each PFAS compound over the three-
week period. This process involved subtracting the quantified mass of each PFAS compound in the control
samples from that in the PVC samples. Subsequently, the sum of these differences was calculated to obtain
the cumulative mass of PFAS. It is thus assumed that any difference in quantified PFAS above the control
samples originates from the PVC. PFAS with a higher quantified mass in the control samples compared to the
PVCs, namely 6:2 FTS, PFPeA, PFOS, and PFOSA, were excluded from the calculations. The resulting mass
amounted to 4,000 ng PFAS/m2 PVC (for PFHxA, PFHpA, PFHxS, PFOA, PFNA, PFDA, PFUdA and PFDdA).
Since PFOA, PFNA and PFHxS were greater in the PVC samples the EQS for 4 PFAS will be included in the
scenarios as well.
Employing the assumption of 100% sorption to solid phases, the resulting mass in 1 m3 of soil with a dry
bulk density of 1400 kg/m3 below 1 m2 of PVC amount to 2 and 3 ng/kg dw for the sum of 4 and 22 PFAS,
respectively. These values are approximately 2 · 104 and 6 · 106 lower than the EQS levels at 0.01 and 0.4
mg/kg dw for the sum of 4 and 22 PFAS, respectively. Consequently, in order to exceed the EQS for the sum of
4 PFAS, contamination from 20,000 units would be required to pollute the same area. Moreover, compared to
soil contamination levels in forest and urban areas, which range from <0 to 0.9 µg/kg dw (as shown in Table 3),
the contamination at the landfill would be significantly lower. Consequently, minimal concerns of exceeding
EQS levels for the PFAS is suggested, with the given the assumptions.
In the scenario involving groundwater extraction near the landfill, from an aquifer identical to the Viuf site,
the resulting groundwater concentration amounts to 50 ng/l for the sum of 4 PFAS and 70 ng/l for the sum of
22 PFAS. Consequently, the resulting sum for 22 PFAS will not exceeded the EQS levels. Conversely, for the
sum of 4 PFAS, the resulting concentration would be approximately 25 times higher than the EQS level at 2
ng/l, necessitating further considerations. As described in (VMR, 2022; ITRC, 2023), the leaching behavior of
PFAS is multifaceted, with not all constituents exhibiting equivalent tendency for migration. Particularly, long-
chained PFCAs and PFAAs (CnF2n+1SO3H (where n ≥6) and CnF2n+1COOH (where n ≥7)), are assumed to
undergo adsorption onto solid phases, leading to restricted leaching. Considering these adsorption dynamics
alongside the estimated 11% leaching potential for PFAS compounds leads to a reduction in potential ground-
water concentrations by a factor of 13 · 103 for the sum of 4 PFAS and by 175 times for the sum of 22 PFAS,
yielding projected concentrations of 0.004 ng/l and 0.4 ng/l, respectively. Within this analysis it is assumed
that long-chained PFAS are sequestered within the landfill soil, while 11% of the short-chained PFAS mass

When examining the impact of PFAS leaching to surface waters, the RPF is considered for each PFAS. Similar

served range of <1-20 ng/l for the sum of 4 PFOS in Danish streams is likely underestimated due to detection
limits, suggesting the possibility of actual concentrations being lower than those projected in the worst-case
scenarios. Moreover, the established scenarios are contextual, depending on localized environmental factors
and the specific characteristics of the water bodies in question. Thus, while the analysis offers reassurance
regarding the low likelihood of surpassing established PFAS guidelines in surface waters, it also underscores
the critical need for further research.
The analytical approaches in this thesis are based on worst-case scenarios. While this provides a foundation
for determining the extent of contamination from solar panels, it does not encompass the broader perspective
where additional mechanisms such as solubility, pH, and retardation are incorporated. These mechanisms,
highlighted as the primary drivers for PFAS transport in Section 3, play a crucial role. Incorporating them more
comprehensively could contribute to a more nuanced understanding of PFAS contamination from solar panels.
However, as this lies outside the scope of this thesis, it is recommended that future research specifically explore
these mechanisms and their relationship with the use of PFAS in solar panels in more detail. Furthermore, the
conclusion are tempered by the current limitations in conclusively tracing PFAS directly to the interior of the
PVC. This gap underscores the need for more detailed research to fully understand the sources and pathways
of PFAS contamination linked to solar panels. Such investigations are crucial for developing comprehensive
strategies to mitigate environmental risks while advancing the sustainable use of solar energy technologies.
Finally, with the prospect of recycling solar panels, as opposed to landfill disposal, introduces additional con-
siderations, particularly regarding dust contamination in e-waste recycling facilities (Tansel, 2022). Excluding
PFNA, PFUdA, and 6:2 FTS, which likely originated from other sources, the cumulative PFAS content in the
dust amounts to 0.4 ng/g dust. This mass falls below levels observed from dust collected at e-waste recycling
sites and indoor environments in American homes, as reported in prior studies (Tansel, 2022; Hall et al., 2020).
However, it is important to note that drawing a direct correlation between the amounts found in this thesis
and those found at e-waste facilities is not straightforward. This challenge arises from the module disassembly
process, which did not encompass the entire module. Furthermore, quantification of the PFAS mass from dis-
assembly without knowledge of the PVC separation methods used at recycling facilities presents difficulties.
Therefore, further investigation into dust emissions from recycled PVC is warranted.

Conclusion and future perspectives
The literature review revealed a significant gap in scientific studies on PFAS levels in solar panels. Most studies
acknowledge the use of PFAS in the front sheet of solar panels for its surfactant properties but fall short of
quantifying amounts or potential emissions. This gap underscored the necessity for developing methodologies
like the wipe test method for PFAS estimation. The success of the wipe test method in quantifying PFAS,
specifically PFHxA and PFPeA, on the solar panel surfaces marks a critical step towards understanding their
environmental implications.
Analysis of emission scenarios to soil, groundwater, and surface waters demonstrated that even with 100%
emission of the quantified levels, the concentrations did not exceed EQS or guideline levels. This finding
suggests a minimal risk under the studied conditions.
Further investigations into the interior of a single module revealed the presence of 12 distinct PFAS com-
pounds. This analysis, albeit without statistical significance against control samples, indicated the presence of
PFHxA, PFDA, and PFDdA. These findings suggest that these PFAS originate from within the PVC. Proposed
leaching scenarios from landfill disposals raised concerns only under a conservative 100% emission model.
However, accounting for sorption processes and limited leaching significantly mitigated these risks.
Focusing on only three solar panel models is a significant limitation that highlights the necessity for expanded
research to corroborate these findings across a diverse range of panel types. Additionally, the analysis was
restricted to sorption based on chain length, not accounting for other potential distribution mechanisms. This
restriction may lead to inaccuracies in estimating contamination levels in soil, groundwater, and surface wa-
ters. Thus, future studies should explore a variety of distribution mechanisms and sorption models to provide
a clearer picture of contamination scenarios. Furthermore, the limited data collection, particularly evident in
the disassembly test, introduces uncertainties that could be mitigated by increasing the sample size.
This thesis stands as the introduction of scientific literature’s aim to quantify PFAS masses in solar panels and
assess contamination levels. Although the preliminary scenarios suggest no immediate concern, the scarcity
of literature on PFAS quantities in solar panels calls for further investigation. The growing integration of
green and sustainable technologies in energy production amplifies the importance of screening for hazardous
chemicals like PFAS. Such efforts are crucial for safeguarding public health and the environment both locally
and globally. Denmark’s establishment of a scientific PFAS task force in 2023, aimed at bridging the knowledge
gap on PFAS, highlights the urgency of this issue (Baun et al., 2023). This initiative underscores sustainable
technology’s vulnerability to PFAS contamination and emphasizes the need for comprehensive regulatory
frameworks.
While this study did not examine the aluminum frame, backsheet, and junction box, it is hypothesized that the
primary components studied are likely the major PFAS sources. Despite this, the potential impact of weath-
ering on PFAS emissions and the presence of PFAS in newer PVC generations warrant further research. Such
investigations are crucial, especially as the industry evolves towards the use of third and fourth-generation
PVCs known for their chemical complexity.
In conclusion, the findings advocate for proactive PFAS screening to guide policy decisions on solar panel
deployment and ensure public support for solar energy. This approach is vital for mitigating potential en-
vironmental impacts in strategic locations, like the Viuf project. As we pivot towards a sustainable future,
vigilance against the environmental footprints of green technologies is imperative to ensure their true benefit
to society and the planet.
