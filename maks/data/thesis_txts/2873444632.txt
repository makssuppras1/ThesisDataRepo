Abstract 
Next Generation [ ] Action (NG[ ]A) is a global initiative and academic course at the Technical 
University of Denmark (DTU) designed to empower youth-led actions toward sustainable 
futures under the principle of “leaving no one behind.” This study uses the NGDA 2024 
course as a case to explore cross-cultural virtual collaboration in global education. 
Employing qualitative methods including interviews and participant observation, the research 
examines how digital collaboration tools shape communication dynamics and cultural 
exchange within multicultural virtual teams. Focusing on NG[ ]A’s blended online-offline 
co-creation model, the study identifies key cultural barriers in virtual teamwork and proposes 
design strategies—such as structured activities and digital facilitation—to foster inclusive 
participation. The findings inform a design approach for NGDA 2025 aimed at supporting 
equitable and culturally sensitive collaboration, contributing to inclusive innovation models for 
global virtual learning environments. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Keywords: cross-cultural collaboration, digital co-creation, virtual teams, inclusive design, 
global education, sustainability

1. Introduction 
This introduction presents the background of the Next Generation [ ] Action (NG[ ]A) program 
and the purpose of the study, using the Next Generation Digital Action 2024 (NGDA 2024) 
course as a case study to explore multicultural virtual collaboration within the context of 
global sustainability education. The NG[ ]A program comprises multiple participation tracks; 
this research specifically focuses on the Young Academics Track, which engages university 
students in intercultural, challenge-based learning. The study draws on insights from course 
design, student experiences, and the author's direct involvement. It is motivated by 
communication challenges frequently observed in virtual multicultural teams—challenges 
that are increasingly relevant in today’s globalized and hybrid educational environments. 
Accordingly, the research investigates how digital collaboration tools can help overcome 
cultural communication barriers and foster more inclusive student participation. Employing a 
qualitative case study approach, the study incorporates interview data and observational 
insights from NGDA student participants. This section outlines the research background, 
questions, objectives, and significance, laying the foundation for the subsequent literature 
review. 
1.1 Background of the NG[ ]A Program 
The Next Generation [ ] Action (NG[ ]A) is a global innovation and education initiative aimed 
at empowering youth-led ideas and fostering sustainable futures, guided by the principle of 
“leaving no one behind.” The flexible bracketed naming format in "NG[ ]A" reflects the 
evolving thematic focus of the program each year. For example, NGDA 2024 stands for Next 
Generation Digital Action, and the 2025 edition continues with the same digital theme. 
According to the official NGDA 2024 website, the program is structured into two key tracks: 
the Young Academics Track, where university students from partner countries collaborate to 
address real-world sustainability challenges, and the Young Entrepreneurs Track, which 
supports early-stage tech startups in bringing their ideas to global sustainability discussions.  
In NGDA 2024, the program brought together students, academics, and entrepreneurs from 
eight different countries, forming 26 interdisciplinary student teams and collaborating with 17 
startups. These teams worked on six sustainability challenges proposed by international 
partners, supported by mentorship, digital tools, and a hybrid learning environment that 
combined online collaboration with in-person bootcamps and events. NG[ ]A is organized by 
DTU Skylab, the innovation hub of the Technical University of Denmark, in collaboration with 
DTU Sustain and DTU Compute, with support from the Danish Ministry of Foreign Affairs and 
other global stakeholders. 
 
The NG[ ]A emerged from both external and internal drivers. Externally, it was initiated by the 
Danish Ministry of Foreign Affairs to foster youth engagement in global sustainability 
dialogues. Internally, the course evolved from a short-term hackathon-style format into a 
more 
structured, 
academic 
program 
that promotes interdisciplinary collaboration, 
cross-cultural learning, and design-based innovation. (Next Generation Digital Action, n.d.)

In NGDA 2025, student teams from Denmark, Korea, India, Kenya, Tanzania, South Africa, 
Ghana, Colombia, Mexico, and—newly added—the USA will take part in this global 
innovation journey. Compared to NGDA 2024, the core structure, course content, and 
thematic focus on digital technologies transforming societies remain largely consistent. This 
continuity ensures that insights gained from the study of NGDA 2024—particularly regarding 
communication and collaboration dynamics within multicultural virtual teams—are directly 
relevant and transferable.  
 
1.1.1 Establishment Drivers 
The establishment of the NG[ ]A was shaped by both external and internal factors. 
Externally, the initiative was launched by the Danish Ministry of Foreign Affairs with the 
strategic objective of engaging young people in global sustainability dialogues and 
empowering them to contribute innovative ideas on international platforms. Internally, the 
program originated as a hackathon-style event or case competition, which the project team 
at DTU identified as inadequate for addressing the complexity of global challenges. The 
short-term format limited students’ opportunities for deep engagement with issues and critical 
reflection on their proposed solutions. As a result, the program was restructured into a 
longer-term academic course that awards credits and prioritizes interdisciplinary teamwork, 
cross-cultural collaboration, and real-world problem-solving. 
 
1.1.2 Course Evolution 
To gain a deeper understanding of the course's origins and objectives, a semi-structured 
interview was conducted with key members of the NG[ ]A project team. Details of the 
interview methodology and content can be found in Sections 3.2 (Research Methods) and 
3.3.5 (Semi-Structured Interviews). 
According to interviews with Marie and Björgvin from the project team, the NG[ ]A has 
undergone substantial evolution since its inception. Originally introduced as a supplementary 
module within the DTU Entrepreneurship program, it has since developed into a standalone, 
internationally recognized educational initiative. This transformation was primarily driven by 
the desire to create a more cohesive and structured learning experience that could support 
deeper interdisciplinary collaboration among students from multiple universities across the 
globe. 
A central goal of this evolution has been to bridge academic theory and practical application 
by actively involving external partners—such as government ministries, private companies, 
and NGOs—in both the design and delivery of the course. This shift has allowed students to 
move beyond traditional academic settings, engaging directly in international conferences 
and real-world sustainability challenges. As a result, the NG[ ]A now functions not only as an 
educational platform, but also as a driver of global perspective-building and collaborative 
problem-solving.

1.3 Research Question 
The NG[ ]A, as a multicultural and partially virtual learning environment with participants from 
a wide range of countries, entails considerable cultural complexity. This diversity 
necessitates careful consideration of course design from multiple perspectives to ensure 
effective coordination and engagement. Consequently, the program presents unique 
challenges in communication across cultural boundaries. In particular, cultural differences 
among participants have been observed to reduce engagement in online communication, 
thus impeding effective cultural exchange and collaboration in virtual settings. Despite the 
growing importance of multicultural virtual collaboration in global sustainability education, 
there remains limited research on how digital tools can be effectively designed and utilized to 
overcome these communication barriers and promote inclusive participation. The NG[ ]A 
program, with its diverse international student teams and hybrid learning model, offers a 
valuable context to explore these challenges and opportunities. This identified gap motivates 
the central research question of this thesis. 
Main Research Question 
How can digital collaboration tools be designed to address communication-related cultural 
barriers in virtual collaboration, to foster active participation and cultural exchange in 
multicultural teams within global innovation programs? 
Sub-questions 
1. What are the main cultural communication barriers encountered in virtual 
collaboration within multicultural innovation teams? 
 
2. How do digital collaboration tools influence communication dynamics and cultural 
exchange among team members from diverse cultural backgrounds? 
 
3. What practical exercises or strategies can digital collaboration tools incorporate to 
address cultural barriers and enhance participation in multicultural teams? 
 
These questions frame the study from a design perspective, aiming to inform how digital 
tools can be intentionally shaped to support inclusive communication and intercultural 
collaboration in virtual, multicultural teams. They arise not only from the observed challenges 
within NG[ ]A but also from the author’s personal engagement with the program, which will 
be further discussed in the following section on personal motivation.

1.5.1. Research Aim 
The aim of this research is to develop a comprehensive understanding of the challenges and 
effective practices in virtual multicultural collaboration within the NGDA course. Specifically, it 
seeks to identify key cultural and communicative barriers, examine successful strategies for 
digital teamwork, and propose a framework to improve inclusivity and collaboration in virtual 
learning environments. Grounded in a case study of NGDA 2024, the study further aims to 
inform and test design interventions for NGDA 2025, with the ultimate goal of enhancing 
intercultural exchange and fostering more inclusive participation in future iterations of the 
program and similar educational contexts. 
 
1.5.2. Research Objectives 
The primary objectives of this study are as follows: 
1. Identify and analyze key challenges in online collaboration within multicultural, virtual 
environments: This objective focuses on exploring common obstacles to effective 
virtual collaboration in multicultural teams, such as limited engagement, reluctance to 
use cameras, and cultural communication barriers. It aims to understand the factors 
that hinder collaboration and identify points of intervention. 
 
2. Explore the successful elements of virtual collaboration in the NG[ ]A course: This 
objective aims to examine the aspects of virtual collaboration that have been most 
effective in fostering engagement and teamwork within the NG[ ]A course. It includes

deep-dives, and the use of collaborative tools like Miro board. 
 
3. Examine the role of structured activities and clear guidelines in enhancing 
engagement and collaboration: This objective investigates how well-defined activities 
and guidelines contribute to overcoming challenges and fostering inclusive and 
effective collaboration in virtual environments. It aims to understand the importance of 
these elements in promoting better communication and teamwork. 
 
4. Develop a framework for reducing cultural barriers in virtual collaboration: Based on 
the findings from the previous objectives, this study will propose a practical design 
approach to minimize cultural barriers and improve collaboration in online, 
multicultural co-creation settings. The framework will focus on enhancing inclusivity 
and bridging communication gaps in virtual teams. 
 
5. Assess the potential for implementing the proposed framework in the NGDA course 
and similar environments: This objective aims to evaluate the feasibility and 
practicality of applying the developed framework in real-world educational settings, 
such as the NG[ ]A course and other global collaboration initiatives. It will assess how 
the framework can be adapted and tested to enhance the collaboration experience in

2. Literature Review 
This literature review examines research on cross-cultural collaboration in digital learning 
environments, with a focus on Global Virtual Teams (GVTs) and higher education contexts 
such as the NGDA course. It draws on peer-reviewed studies, cultural theory, and recent 
work on virtual co-creation and inclusive design. The purpose is to understand how cultural 
diversity shapes teamwork in digital spaces and to identify strategies that support effective, 
inclusive collaboration. A systematic search was conducted following the PRISMA framework 
to ensure transparency and relevance. The review is organized into five themes: the rise of 
GVTs, benefits and challenges of cross-cultural teamwork, co-creation issues in virtual 
contexts, strategies for inclusive innovation, and gaps in current research. Key findings 
highlight both the potential and complexity of multicultural virtual teams. While diversity can 
drive innovation, it also requires thoughtful design and facilitation. These insights lay the 
groundwork for the thesis’s exploration of inclusive, intercultural collaboration in digitally 
mediated learning environments. 
 
2.1 Methods 
This literature review adopts the PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews 
and Meta-Analyses) framework to ensure a systematic, transparent, and replicable process 
for identifying relevant academic studies. The goal is to explore literature on cross-cultural 
collaboration in digital learning environments, particularly within higher education contexts 
similar to the NG[ ]A. 
 
2.1.1 Search Strategy 
A comprehensive search was conducted using Google Scholar, DTU Findit, and Connected 
Papers, guided by combinations of the following keywords: multicultural, cross-cultural, 
virtual collaboration, online communication, digital environment, online co-creation, 
multicultural learning, and cultural identity. Boolean operators (AND, OR) were applied to 
broaden and refine the results. 
Only English-language sources were considered, including journal articles, conference 
proceedings, and book chapters. While no strict publication year limit was set, priority was 
given to literature from the past five years to reflect recent developments in hybrid and virtual 
learning, particularly after the COVID-19 pandemic. Foundational works, such as Hofstede’s 
cultural dimensions theory, were also included when relevant. 
Search results were organized using Zotero. Duplicates were removed, and sources were 
screened based on titles and abstracts. Full-text reviews were conducted to confirm 
relevance, focusing on studies addressing cultural communication, digital collaboration, and 
educational co-creation in multicultural contexts. 
 
18 
==PAGE:19==
 
2.1.2 Screening and Selection 
A total of 36 articles were initially identified through the search process. After removing 
duplicates and conducting preliminary screening of titles and abstracts, 26 articles were 
selected for full-text review. These studies spanned multiple disciplines—including higher 
education, 
interaction 
design, 
virtual 
team 
management, 
and 
intercultural 
communication—offering interdisciplinary insights relevant to the NG[ ]A course context. 
Inclusion criteria focused on studies that addressed digital collaboration within multicultural 
or educational settings. Publications were excluded if they focused solely on technical tool 
functionalities without addressing team dynamics or cultural elements, or if they described 
virtual teams in purely corporate or industrial environments unrelated to learning contexts. 
The final corpus of 26 studies was organized into three thematic categories to support a 
structured analysis: 
● Research on the development of global virtual teams and online learning 
environments 
 
● Studies examining cross-cultural barriers and competencies in digital collaboration 
 
● Literature on inclusive strategies and future directions for hybrid and virtual education 
models 
 
This process adheres to the PRISMA framework, with clear steps for identification, 
screening, eligibility assessment, and inclusion documented throughout the review. 
The thematic categorization of the reviewed literature highlights ongoing challenges at the 
intersection of digital collaboration and multicultural teamwork. To better understand these 
dynamics, the following section explores the rise of global virtual teams and the broader 
context of digitalized collaboration in education and innovation. 
 
2.2 The Rise of Global Virtual Teams in a Digitalized World 
In recent years, globalization, digital transformation, and increasing cultural diversity have 
changed how people collaborate across borders. These developments have led to the 
growth of Global Virtual Teams (GVTs), which rely on technology to support teamwork 
among individuals from different time zones and cultural backgrounds. As Jimenez et al., ( 
2017, p. 341) stated, "global expansion and mobility, along with technological developments, 
sparked the need and created the possibility of expanding the scope of teams beyond the 
traditional collocated arrangements". The COVID-19 pandemic further accelerated this trend, 
making virtual collaboration a standard way of working and learning. 
This shift is also reflected in education. Programs like NG[ ]A, which combine online and 
offline collaboration, show how digital spaces can connect people from different cultures to 
work on shared challenges. According to Shonfeld et al., (2021), digital learning 
19 
==PAGE:20==
 
environments can do more than just provide tools—they can help build bridges between 
cultures and support smoother collaboration. However, even though many students are 
comfortable with technology, research shows they are often not ready for online 
collaboration. Difficulties in communication, leadership, project management, and cultural 
understanding are common (Flammia et al., 2010; Robey et al. 2000) 
Universities are increasingly trying to help students develop global skills. Kolm et al. (2022) 
pointed out that preparing students for intercultural teamwork has become a priority in higher 
education. Still, many studies on GVTs focus on cultural theory frameworks such as 
Hofstede’s dimensions (Tian et al., 2018), rather than exploring how virtual spaces can be 
designed to reduce cultural barriers during actual teamwork. This creates an opportunity to 
study digital co-creation in more practical and inclusive ways, especially in global projects 
like NG[ ]A. 
 
2.3 The Value and Challenges of Cross-cultural Collaboration 
“Non-spatial proximity has been found to be more likely to influence organizational 
collaboration, interactive learning, and innovation performance than geographical proximity. 
Non-spatial proximity, which includes organizational proximity, cognitive proximity, 
institutional proximity and social proximity (Boschma, 2005), can enhance interaction and 
imitation and in turn result in knowledge spillover. It also fosters the formation of knowledge 
clusters (Lazzeretti & Capone, 2016).” (Hung et al., 2021, p.600) 
In projects like NGDA, where people from different cultures work together on global issues, 
diversity becomes essential. Cross-cultural collaboration brings many benefits to innovation, 
especially in virtual teamwork. Diverse teams can offer more ideas, viewpoints, and ways of 
thinking, which supports creativity and inclusive problem-solving (B. Christensen et al., 
2017).  
Intercultural competences are especially important in these environments. Shonfeld et al. 
(2021) explained that to support inclusive learning, it's important to understand learners’ 
cultural backgrounds and connect digital learning with their lived experiences. When 
facilitators design culturally responsive learning environments, participants are more likely to 
engage actively and feel included. This is also true in virtual co-creation, where team 
members need to respect and adapt to each other’s communication styles. 
Some studies also show that diverse teams tend to be more aware of possible challenges 
and prepare more carefully. For example, Magnusson et al. (2014, as cited in Jimenez et al., 
2017, p. 343) found that multicultural teams often work harder to avoid misunderstandings. 
Similarly, Jimenez et al.(2017,p. 343) noted that working in GVTs can increase motivation 
and satisfaction, partly because of the freedom and learning opportunities these teams offer . 
Collectively, these findings indicate that when managed thoughtfully, team diversity can be a 
significant strength rather than a barrier. 
Yet, despite its acknowledged importance, the role of culture in shaping collaboration 
remains under-explored, particularly across different stages of the co-creation process. Tian 
et al. (2018) noted that most studies continue to rely on broad cultural dimensions like 
20 
==PAGE:21==
 
Hofstede's framework, without sufficiently addressing how cultural dynamics unfold in 
practical, task-based interactions. This reveals a critical gap in the literature: understanding 
how cultural diversity influences collaborative innovation in virtual contexts, and what 
strategies can foster both inclusion and effectiveness. 
Ultimately, advancing intercultural competence and acknowledging both the opportunities 
and complexities of cultural diversity are essential to building high-functioning, globally 
minded teams—an imperative that lies at the heart of initiatives like NG[ ]A. 
 
2.4 Co-creation Challenges in Virtual, Culturally Diverse 
Environments 
Effective communication in virtual multicultural teams is widely recognized as a critical 
challenge, particularly when foreign language use is involved. Presbitero (2020, p. 12) 
highlights that virtual global teams (GVTs) rely heavily on verbal communication and 
language proficiency to ensure individual tasks are completed efficiently . Similarly, Titus and 
Ng'ambi ( 2023, p. 12) emphasize language as a vital medium through which team members 
collaboratively address social issues, underscoring the importance of clear and inclusive 
communication in diverse virtual settings. This is especially relevant in NG[ ]A environments 
where teams from different countries jointly analyze social problem cases. 
Beyond language, fostering inclusivity in virtual teams involves respecting and embracing 
diverse identities, including personality, values, nationality, gender, religion, and physical 
appearance. Hung et al. (2021, p. 600)argue that such inclusiveness creates a contributive 
environment where members feel valued and empowered, which is essential for effective 
collaboration. Moreover, they assert that diversity contributes to the team’s absorptive 
capacity—the ability to recognize, assimilate, and apply diverse knowledge—thus driving 
innovation (Hung et al., 2021, p. 602). Without this capacity, teams struggle to transform 
varied perspectives into creative solutions. 
However, overcoming cultural barriers in virtual co-creation is complex, as cultural 
differences can hinder trust, communication, and coordination (Presbitero, 2020). These 
challenges necessitate deliberate design approaches and facilitation strategies that support 
intercultural competence and create psychologically safe spaces for open dialogue. Only by 
addressing these challenges can virtual multicultural teams like those in NG[ ]A unlock their 
full potential for inclusive innovation. 
 
2.5 Inclusive Innovation in Digital Spaces 
Fostering global collaboration in virtual, multicultural teams requires more than just 
technological infrastructure—it demands intentional strategies for inclusion, communication, 
and engagement. Prior studies have shown that cultural similarity often leads to more 
effective knowledge production within homogeneous groups, while limiting the exchange of 
21 
==PAGE:22==
 
ideas across cultural boundaries (Titus & Ng’ambi, 2023, p. 2). In co-creation settings such 
as NG[ ]A, this dynamic can hinder innovation by restricting cultural cross-pollination. This 
highlights the need for digital environments that do not merely connect participants but 
actively support interaction across differences. 
Designing culturally adaptive digital platforms is one key strategy. Tian et al. (2018, p. 12) 
emphasized that both organizational and national cultural dimensions significantly shape 
innovation outcomes, suggesting that collaborative systems should account for diverse 
expectations and norms. Similarly, Jimenez et al. (2017, p. 347) pointed to the evolving role 
of online tools in shaping team dynamics, underscoring the need to investigate how such 
platforms can better support global virtual teams (GVTs), especially through mechanisms like 
identity brokerage and adaptive interfaces. 
Effective communication practices are also central to inclusive virtual collaboration. In 
contexts where participants carry different assumptions about hierarchy, expression, and 
timing, structured communication tools and clearly defined interaction norms can help reduce 
misunderstandings. This is closely tied to student engagement, which, as Titus & Ng’ambi, 
(2023) noted, is a strong indicator of student success in higher education. When students 
feel heard and understood, they are more likely to participate actively and contribute 
meaningfully. 
Engagement strategies such as co-created digital rituals, rotating leadership roles, or 
reflection sessions can further enhance participation and belonging. These approaches help 
level the playing field, particularly for those from underrepresented cultural groups. As 
Jimenez et al. (2017, p. 347) noted, the right kind of facilitation and platform design can 
either amplify or diminish the benefits of diversity. Therefore, cultivating global collaboration 
awareness must include not only tool development but also thoughtful integration of cultural 
insight, empathy, and inclusive design into the virtual co-creation experience. 
 
2.6 Gaps in Existing Research 
Despite growing recognition of cultural diversity’s impact on virtual collaboration, significant 
gaps remain in understanding how inclusion directly influences knowledge sharing and 
innovation within global virtual teams. Hung et al. (2021, p. 599) explicitly note the absence 
of research investigating the role of inclusion in facilitating these critical processes, 
highlighting a key opportunity for further study. This gap is especially relevant at the 
intersection of intercultural virtual team-building and inclusive design, areas that have been 
insufficiently integrated in existing literature. 
Titus & Ng’ambi (2023, p. 12) argue that failing to harness rich interactions in culturally 
diverse digital environments represents a missed chance to capitalize on diversity’s potential 
to enhance engagement and learning. They further suggest that emerging technologies, 
such as digital games, can create conditions conducive to cross-cultural interaction and 
should be thoughtfully employed in multicultural classrooms to maximize their benefits(Titus 
& Ng’ambi, 2023, p. 16). This points to the need for deliberate, inclusive design strategies 
that leverage technological affordances to support diverse participants. 
22 
==PAGE:23==
 
Moreover, Hung et al. ( 2021, p. 608) caution that factors like geographical and social 
proximity interact in complex ways, and that current research—often based on 
individual-level data—must evolve to more fully consider intercultural encounters at the team 
level, especially in international R&D contexts. Fermín González (2019, p. 152) also 
highlights the multifaceted nature of inclusive virtual environments, encompassing human 
differences, disabilities, and higher education challenges, further underscoring the 
complexity and breadth of this research domain. 
Addressing these gaps is essential for advancing both theory and practice, enabling virtual 
multicultural teams such as those in NG[ ]A to fully leverage diversity for inclusive innovation.

3. Methodology and Framing 
This section presents the methodology and framing of this thesis study. It begins by outlining 
the research problem, main research question, and sub-questions that guide the 
investigation. The following subsection defines the scope of the study, focusing on 
participants from the NGDA 2024 who engaged in virtual, cross-cultural collaboration. Next, 
the research framing is introduced through the Double Diamond design model, which 
structures the inquiry and development process. This is followed by a description of the 
qualitative research methods used in the study. Finally, the section explains the data 
collection and analysis procedures applied to interpret participant experiences and generate 
insights.This methodological approach reflects the study’s design-oriented nature and the 
importance of centering participant experiences in cross-cultural contexts. However, due to 
the voluntary nature of participation, the cultural diversity of the data collected remains 
somewhat limited, which may influence the generalizability of the findings. 
 
3.1 Research Framing: The Double Diamond Model 
This study utilizes the Double Diamond methodology to develop a framework for enhancing 
inclusive and effective virtual collaboration in multicultural co-creation settings. Originally 
introduced by the UK Design Council, the Double Diamond design process provides a clear, 
structured model for navigating complex design challenges through four distinct phases: 
Discover, Define, Develop, and Deliver. It emphasizes both divergent and convergent 
thinking to first explore the problem space and then arrive at user-centered, tested solutions 
(Design Council, 2019). 
In the context of this thesis, the Double Diamond model offers a practical and iterative 
structure that supports both theoretical exploration and empirical work. As illustrated in the 
visual project framework shown in Figure 1, each milestone in the research process 
corresponds to a phase of the Double Diamond: 
● The Discover and Define phases focus on research clarification and design scope, 
including literature review, stakeholder mapping, and gathering participant insights 
through surveys and interviews. 
 
● The Develop and Test phase involves the synthesis of findings into case studies and 
initial framework development, followed by small-scale implementation and 
observation. 
 
● Finally, the Delivery phase is dedicated to reflecting on the implementation, gathering 
feedback, and finalizing a design framework aimed at reducing cultural barriers in 
virtual collaboration. 
 
This model aligns well with the iterative, participatory nature of the NG[ ]A context, where 
innovation is co-created across cultures and continuously refined through reflection and 
interaction.

3.2 Research methods 
This study adopts a qualitative research approach, which is particularly suitable for 
understanding participants’ experiences, perspectives, and the social dynamics of 
cross-cultural collaboration. As Creswell (2013) defines, “Qualitative research is an approach 
for exploring and understanding the meaning individuals or groups ascribe to a social or 
human problem” (p. 4). Given the exploratory nature of this research—focused on 
intercultural interaction, group dynamics, and virtual teamwork—qualitative methods offered 
the necessary flexibility to capture complex, context-rich insights.  
To enhance the credibility and depth of the study, method triangulation was employed. This 
approach involved the use of multiple qualitative methods—namely semi-structured 
interviews, survey responses, and observational data—to examine the research topic from 
different angles. Rather than seeking convergence alone, the triangulation process was 
designed to enable critical reflection on the strengths and limitations of each method. For 
example, interviews provided detailed personal insights, while surveys offered broader 
trends, and observations captured behavior in real-time team interactions. Each method 
contributed unique value, but also came with constraints: interviews may be influenced by 
self-reporting bias, surveys can lack contextual depth, and observational data require 
interpretive judgment. Through triangulation, these methods were used not only to validate 
25 
==PAGE:26==
 
recurring themes, but also to enrich and contextualize one another, allowing for a more 
comprehensive understanding of intercultural dynamics in virtual collaboration. This reflective 
approach to triangulation served as both a validation strategy and an opportunity to explore 
how different forms of data interact and complement each other in the design research 
process. 
In alignment with the Double Diamond model, a range of qualitative methods was applied 
across different stages of the design process. These methods were selected to support both 
discovery and validation, and to inform the development of an inclusive collaboration 
framework. Data sources and methods will be described in the following subsections. 
 
3.3 Data Sources and Methods 
To comprehensively investigate the challenges and dynamics of cross-cultural virtual 
collaboration within the NGDA 2024 program, this study employed a multi-method qualitative 
approach grounded in a single-case study design. The selected methods—desk research, 
observation, surveys, and semi-structured interviews—were strategically aligned with the 
four phases of the Double Diamond design framework (Discover, Define, Develop, and 
Deliver) to enable iterative exploration, synthesis, and evaluation. This pluralistic approach 
facilitated methodological triangulation by cross-validating insights from multiple data 
sources, thereby enhancing the validity and reliability of the findings. It also allowed for a 
holistic understanding of both individual and collective experiences within the NGDA context, 
ultimately informing the identification of key problem areas and the development of targeted 
design recommendations. The analysis of the research findings will be presented in the 
following sections.

intercultural teamwork.  
NGDA 2024 serves as a representative context to examine challenges and opportunities in 
virtual, cross-cultural collaboration, allowing an in-depth look into: 
● The evolution of the course structure and curriculum, 
 
● The experiences and perspectives of students and organizers, 
 
● The collaborative dynamics manifested during team project work.

3.3.4 Survey  
To broaden the research perspective and complement the qualitative insights obtained

3.4 Data Analysis Methods   
To ensure the robustness and credibility of the findings, this study adopted a mixed-methods

semi-structured interviews, survey responses, and observational notes. Each data source

the NGDA program, and their combined use enabled triangulation—where insights from one 
method were supported or challenged by evidence from another.

4. Empirical Findings  
This section presents the empirical findings of the study, based on data collected through five 
in-depth interviews, a questionnaire survey, and feedback forms from participants of the 
NGDA 2024 course. These materials offer a descriptive account of participant experiences in 
virtual, cross-cultural collaboration. The purpose of this section is to outline the key factors 
that influenced collaboration and engagement in the course's digital setting. The structure of 
this chapter is organized around the key themes that emerged from the thematic analysis, 
including participation and structural clarity, onboarding processes, trust and cultural

4.1 Current Challenges in NGDA - Interview results from the 
NGDA project team 
This section presents findings from a semi-structured interview conducted with two key 
members of the NGDA project team, Björgvin and Marie, aimed at uncovering their 
perspectives on the current challenges, motivations, and developmental goals of the course. 
The interview was conducted in person at DTU SKYLAB, lasted approximately 45 minutes, 
and was audio-recorded and later transcribed for analysis. Prior to the interview, a 
comprehensive desk research phase had been conducted to understand the course 
background, enabling a more informed and focused conversation. This section is included to 
complement participant-centered findings with internal perspectives from those responsible 
for course design and facilitation. By doing so, it provides a more balanced understanding of 
the structural and pedagogical considerations behind the NGDA program, and to identify 
recurring tensions and aspirations from the project team’s point of view. These insights serve 
as both a contextual foundation and a critical contrast to the participant experiences 
described later in the empirical findings chapter. The project team emphasized the following 
areas as current challenges in the course. 
 
Sustaining Global Collaboration Across Formats 
One of the key challenges is balancing global collaboration with the logistical constraints of 
virtual and hybrid learning environments. Initially, the course adopted a hybrid format during 
the COVID-19 pandemic, but it has since shifted to an in-person model. While this has 
improved networking and face-to-face interactions, it raises questions about how to sustain 
global collaboration in virtual settings. This challenge is particularly relevant for students who 
may be in different time zones or have varying levels of technological access. 
34 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
Fostering Meaningful Cross-Cultural Engagement 
A key challenge lies in fostering cross-cultural collaboration that goes beyond surface-level 
interaction to support genuine, goal-oriented engagement. While the NGDA course promotes 
intercultural communication and teamwork, students often struggle to translate cultural 
diversity into productive collaboration. Misunderstandings and communication barriers 
persist, particularly in virtual settings, where informal dialogue and shared context are 
limited. Meaningful engagement in this context refers to the ability of diverse teams to 
collaboratively address global challenges—bringing together varied perspectives to co-create 
solutions for sustainable development. However, this form of engagement requires not only 
openness and curiosity, but also structured support to ensure inclusive and equitable 
participation. 
 
Attracting and Supporting Motivated, Prepared Participants 
A key challenge identified by the project team is attracting and supporting students who are 
genuinely motivated and adequately prepared for the course’s interdisciplinary and 
cross-cultural nature. While the selection process has improved, some participants still

4.2.1 Improving Participation Through Structure in Virtual Collaboration 
A central theme that emerged from participant interviews was the need for greater structure 
to support participation in virtual cross-cultural collaboration. Many interviewees described 
the online environment of the NG[ ]A as disorganized and difficult to navigate, particularly 
during the initial phases. This lack of structure—both in terms of onboarding and session 
facilitation—contributed to confusion, reduced motivation, and limited engagement 
throughout the program. 
 
Tool Unfamiliarity and Timing Sensitivity 
One of the most frequently mentioned challenges was unfamiliarity with digital collaboration 
tools, especially Miro. Although Miro was a core platform used throughout the course, its 
interface and navigation were not intuitive for many participants, particularly those with 
limited prior experience. The inconsistent application of the tool across sessions—often 
without sufficient instruction—caused additional confusion. As one participant put it, “Each 
session felt like starting from scratch again.” (Interviewee #1, semi-structured interview) 
Time pressure further exacerbated the issue, as participants often had little opportunity to 
familiarize themselves with tools or clarify their responsibilities before exercises began. This 
combination of cognitive overload and lack of guidance negatively impacted participants' 
ability to engage fully with the tasks and with one another. 
 
Limited Engagement and Communication Barriers in Virtual Settings 
Participants also expressed frustration with the low level of interaction and communication 
during virtual sessions. Many reported prolonged silences, a lack of spontaneous 
conversation, and difficulties in understanding peers due to diverse accents and unfamiliar 
communication styles. These barriers not only disrupted the flow of conversation but also 
discouraged participation. Some participants felt hesitant to ask for clarification repeatedly, 
fearing they would hinder group progress. Others described the virtual sessions as overly 
task-oriented, with little room for informal or meaningful dialogue. As a result, participants 
tended to focus on their own assignments rather than engage with others’ contributions, 
undermining the collaborative intent of the course. 
 
Need for Warm-Up and Social Introduction Tasks 
A widely supported suggestion among participants was the inclusion of warm-up activities or 
informal social interactions before formal collaboration began. These early moments of 
connection—such as introductions, icebreakers, or casual check-ins—were seen as 
essential for building trust, reducing anxiety, and establishing comfort within culturally diverse 
teams. Without such social grounding, collaboration often felt transactional and impersonal. 
One participant noted, “Before diving into exercises, we should have had space to get to 
know each other a bit—it builds trust.” (Interviewee #2, semi-structured interview) 
37 
==PAGE:38==
 
 
Centralized and Visual Onboarding 
Another key recommendation was the development of a centralized and visual onboarding 
system. Participants suggested a shared digital space—such as a dashboard or visual 
guide—that clearly presented tools, schedules, roles, and course goals. This would allow 
team members to orient themselves more effectively and reduce reliance on facilitators for 
basic information. The compressed nature of the existing bootcamp sessions was criticized 
for overwhelming participants without fostering true understanding or connection. A more 
gradual, visually scaffolded onboarding process was proposed to support learning across 
different cultural and cognitive styles. 
 
In summary, participants emphasized that virtual collaboration requires thoughtful and 
sustained structuring—not only through clearer task guidance and tool instruction, but also 
by fostering early-stage social interaction and providing accessible, centralized information. 
These strategies are essential for building confidence, engagement, and inclusivity in 
multicultural virtual environments. 
 
4.2.2 Developing Trust and Cultural Curiosity in Virtual Teams 
Trust and cultural curiosity emerged as essential components of successful virtual 
co-creation in multicultural teams. Participants repeatedly emphasized that without a 
foundation of interpersonal connection and openness, virtual collaboration often became 
mechanical, fragmented, or overly task-driven. The interviews revealed that building trust 
was not simply a matter of time, but of intentional design—requiring informal interactions, 
shared experiences, and opportunities for spontaneous cultural exchange. 
The Role of Social Warmth and Informal Interaction 
Several participants described the need for more organic, informal moments within the virtual 
environment to help foster a sense of psychological safety and social warmth. They 
suggested that introducing oneself before exercises or sessions should be a standard part of 
the workflow. This small but significant gesture was viewed as a simple way to reduce 
formality and humanize digital interactions. One participant shared that they often felt 
disconnected from other teams and missed opportunities to engage with individuals who left 
a strong impression, such as someone with an interesting voice or idea, but who remained 
inaccessible due to the rigid session structure. These observations indicate that fostering 
trust in virtual spaces requires more than functional communication—it requires deliberate 
space for authentic, informal connection. 
From Competition to Mutual Support 
A few interviewees noted that the competitive framing of certain challenges may have 
inadvertently undermined collaborative spirit. Participants occasionally felt pressure to 
“perform” rather than to connect, which reduced their willingness to share ideas openly. This 
dynamic was especially problematic in multicultural contexts, where participants were still 
navigating unfamiliar communication norms. As one participant explained, the emphasis on 
competition made them feel like they were “competing rather than collaborating,” limiting 
opportunities for cross-team learning and emotional support. These findings suggest that 
38 
==PAGE:39==
 
reducing competitive pressure and reframing tasks as shared challenges could encourage 
more open dialogue and peer support. 
Cultural Curiosity and Everyday Sharing 
When asked about what cultural elements they were most curious about, participants often 
expressed a broad and spontaneous interest in others’ lifestyles, values, and daily habits. 
Rather than seeking structured presentations about culture, they preferred casual 
conversations and storytelling that revealed everyday realities—what people eat, how they 
spend weekends, or what music they listen to. This type of informal sharing was seen as 
more accessible and engaging than formalized cultural exchanges. One participant proposed 
a “Virtual Cultural Day” as part of the program (Interviewee #2, semi-structured interview), 
where team members could share aspects of their own culture through storytelling, visual 
media, or food-related themes. Such initiatives could create a relaxed environment for 
curiosity to flourish and for participants to feel seen and valued. 
Lighthearted Activities and “Funny Moments” 
Another recurring theme was the value of incorporating playful, low-stakes activities to foster 
social bonding and reduce formal barriers. Participants suggested ideas such as short 
team-based puzzles, lighthearted competitions, or even casual, gossip-style conversations. 
These “fun moments” were seen as essential for breaking the ice and encouraging natural 
engagement across cultures. Several interviewees specifically recalled moments during 
NGDA 2024 when visual elements—such as images, memes, or playful digital 
stickers—were used to spark cross-cultural conversations and lighten the atmosphere 
(Interviewee 4 and Interviewee 5; see Appendix 3.2 for interview excerpts). These 
interactions not only created space for cultural exchange but also added a sense of joy and 
spontaneity to the collaboration process. 
In summary, participants strongly advocated for a design approach that intentionally 
incorporates informal cultural exchange, relaxed entry points for social interaction, and 
opportunities for personal storytelling. Encouraging spontaneous curiosity and trust-building 
through low-pressure activities was seen as a key factor in improving the inclusivity and 
overall effectiveness of multicultural virtual teams. 
 
4.2.3 Goal-Oriented Learning Model 
Findings show that participants experienced a tension between completing project tasks and 
engaging in deeper, reflective learning within the NGDA 2024. While the course emphasized 
global collaboration and problem-solving, many participants felt that the focus on 
deliverables limited opportunities for reflection, cultural exchange, and informal interaction. 
The data highlight how goal-oriented structures influenced participant engagement, with 
certain design features either supporting or restricting meaningful collaboration in the virtual 
multicultural environment. 
Tension Between Output and Reflective Learning 
Participants consistently observed that the virtual nature of NGDA 2024 often prioritized 
deliverables over learning. Time zone gaps and asynchronous collaboration led teams to

==PAGE:40==
 
focus on their own output, with limited opportunities for broader exchange. Sharing sessions 
were commonly experienced as performance tasks rather than reflective discussions. Many 
participants expressed the need for structured moments to reflect not only on outcomes but 
also on team processes and working styles. 
Need for Informal, Cross-Cultural Interaction 
A key insight from the interviews was the importance of non-task-driven interactions. Informal 
moments—such as storytelling, cultural sharing, or casual check-ins—were seen as 
essential for building trust and group cohesion in multicultural teams. When such elements 
were included, particularly through smaller group sessions or creative exercises on tools like 
Miro, participants felt more connected and engaged. 
What Worked: Purposeful Engagement 
Both participants and project team members pointed to successful course components that 
balanced structure and creativity. Smaller group formats, interactive tools, and exercises with 
real-world relevance were identified as particularly effective in fostering motivation and 
deeper collaboration. These elements allowed learning to feel purposeful without being 
overly rigid. 
Synthesis of Learning and Collaboration 
There was a shared recognition that effective virtual co-creation requires more than clear 
tasks—it depends on integrating diverse perspectives through informal exchange and 
reflective learning. NG[ ]A’s core aim of cultivating a globally-minded, problem-solving 
generation was best supported when interpersonal connection, creativity, and cultural 
dialogue were deliberately embedded into the learning model.

5. Framework development 
This chapter introduces the design solution developed to address the challenges uncovered 
in the previous empirical findings. Drawing on input from participant interviews, feedback 
forms, and relevant case studies, this section focuses on creating a practical framework to 
enhance trust, cultural connection, and inclusivity in virtual collaboration within the NGDA 
2025. The need for this framework arises from recurring issues such as lack of structure, 
weak interpersonal bonding, and cultural misunderstandings in digital spaces. To address 
these challenges, the study adopts a human-centered design approach grounded in 
social-constructivist principles and cross-cultural learning theory. The chapter begins with a 
case study that highlights effective practices—such as randomized grouping and 
game-based learning—that support psychological safety and teamwork. It then presents a 
two-part cultural engagement exercise ("Who’s Here" and "Culture Wall") designed to 
strengthen interpersonal connections and cultural curiosity early in the course. These 
activities are supported by flexible participation guidelines that aim to lower barriers to 
engagement and promote inclusive communication. The output of this chapter is a prototype 
for structured cultural onboarding activities that can be integrated into the NGDA 2025 or 
adapted for similar global educational contexts. Finally, reflections on design considerations 
and limitations are discussed to inform future improvements and scalability of the proposed 
approach. 
 
5.1 Case Study 
This case study explores how randomized group formation, game-based learning, and high 
team proximity can enhance collaboration and psychological safety in intercultural virtual 
teams. It draws on findings from Titus & Ng’ambi (2023) and uses Hall’s Culture Iceberg 
Model as a conceptual lens. These insights are relevant to the NG[ ]A context, where virtual, 
cross-cultural teamwork presents communication and engagement challenges. Through a 
literature-based qualitative analysis, the case highlights how intentional group design and 
inclusive learning strategies can reduce cultural barriers and promote equitable participation. 
The findings inform later design proposals for improving digital collaboration in NG[ ]A and 
similar global programs. 
 
5.1.1 Enhancing Collaboration and Psychological Safety through 
Randomized Grouping and Game-Based Exercise 
A study by Titus & Ng’ambi (2023) demonstrated that the randomization of groups in a digital 
game-based learning environment fostered improved social interaction and collaboration 
among participants. The shift away from familiar cliques enabled individuals to engage more 
openly with diverse peers, promoting a stronger sense of social presence and psychological 
safety. Over time, this unfamiliar yet inclusive group setting enhanced learners' confidence, 
which in turn positively influenced their performance and motivation. 
Participants also reported that being assigned to random groups encouraged a more 
professional work ethic compared to participating within their usual social circles. This

and instead enabled more adaptive, productive interactions within the digital environment. As 
a result, productivity levels increased, suggesting that randomization can serve as a powerful 
design strategy for equitable collaboration. 
The study further recommended the use of a social-constructivist, game-based learning 
environment as an innovative pedagogical approach. This method not only improved 
engagement but also contributed to positive behavioral changes toward learning. 
Additionally, the concept of high team proximity was identified as crucial for fostering trust 
and minimizing self-interested behaviors. Close-knit teams enabled complementary learning 
across diverse skill sets and supported efficient knowledge flow, ultimately enhancing 
collaborative innovation.

5.1.2 Theoretical Framework: Culture Iceberg Model 
This study adopts Edward T. Hall’s (1976) Culture Iceberg Model as its central theoretical 
framework to inform the design of intercultural collaboration exercises. The model 
conceptualizes culture as an iceberg with two interrelated layers: surface culture and deep 
culture. Surface culture refers to visible expressions such as language, food, clothing, and 
rituals, while deep culture encompasses underlying values, thought patterns, and social 
norms that profoundly shape communication (British Columbia Council for International 
Education [BCCIE], 2020). 
In multicultural team settings, especially those operating remotely, many interpersonal 
challenges—such as miscommunication, lack of engagement, or misunderstanding—can be 
traced to differences rooted in deep cultural assumptions. Hall’s metaphor highlights the 
notion that, much like the submerged portion of an iceberg, these deep elements remain 
largely unseen but carry the most weight in shaping intercultural interactions. 
To support a shared understanding of cultural complexity, Figure 4 presents the Culture 
Iceberg Model, which visually communicates the distinction between surface and deep 
cultural elements. This visual was introduced to participants at the beginning of the Culture 
Session to provide a common conceptual foundation. By offering a clear metaphor, the 
model helped participants recognize that much of what shapes intercultural interaction lies 
42 
==PAGE:43==
 
beneath the surface—encouraging deeper reflection and more respectful engagement 
throughout the exercises. 
The Culture Iceberg Model was selected for its dual utility as both a diagnostic tool and a 
design scaffold. First, it aids in identifying and interpreting the sources of cultural friction by 
encouraging practitioners to look beyond surface-level diversity. Second, it provides a 
conceptual roadmap for designing learning interventions that foster both initial engagement 
and deeper reflection.(CultureMonkey, n.d.) 
In this project, the model informed both the sequencing and structure of the Culture Session 
exercises. The design aimed to: 
● Begin with surface-level cultural expressions to facilitate initial connection and reduce 
interactional friction; 
 
● Gradually guide participants toward deeper cultural reflection through storytelling, 
inquiry, and group dialogue; 
 
● Support both verbal and silent forms of participation to promote inclusivity and 
psychological safety. 
 
The Culture Iceberg Model thus serves as both a diagnostic tool (to understand cultural 
friction) and a design framework (to guide interventions that go beyond superficial diversity. 
The Culture Iceberg Model serves as both a tool to identify cultural misunderstandings and a 
guide to create solutions that go beyond surface-level diversity. To make the session more 
effective, the Culture Iceberg Model was introduced to participants at the beginning of the 
Culture Session. This helped participants understand that culture has many layers, including 
visible and hidden parts. 
Using this model at the start of the session had two main goals. First, it gave everyone a 
common language to talk about culture, which helped participants from different 
backgrounds work together more respectfully. Second, it encouraged participants to think 
more deeply during the activities, moving past obvious cultural differences to understand the 
reasons behind them. This clear explanation helped participants engage better and created a 
strong foundation for meaningful cultural exchange throughout the session.

5.2.1 Design Strategies and Considerations 
Based on insights from user research and analysis of key challenges, the following design 
principles were developed to guide the creation of collaborative activities. Each principle 
addresses a core issue identified in the study and aligns with established user experience 
(UX) design standards. Together, they serve as the foundation for structuring visually intuitive 
and pedagogically meaningful virtual exercises: 
● Feasibility: Activities must be logistically practical within the existing program 
timeline and technical environment. This addresses the challenge of time constraints 
and tool unfamiliarity frequently reported by participants. 
 
● Alignment: The exercises should directly support the learning objectives of the 
intercultural session, ensuring coherence between activity content and pedagogical 
intent. This responds to the need for meaningful cultural engagement within limited 
interaction windows. 
 
● Effectiveness: Activities must foster informal communication, trust-building, and 
active participation among culturally diverse team members. This directly addresses 
46 
==PAGE:47==
 
the reported lack of familiarity and psychological safety in virtual environments. 
 
Exercise Design and Implementation 
To meet these standards, each activity incorporates clearly defined learning objectives, a 
structured format, and detailed participant guidelines. These elements provide a framework 
for engagement, clarify expectations, and promote inclusive and equitable collaboration 
within virtual intercultural environments. 
A central aspect of the design strategy is ensuring accessibility and inclusion. Recognizing 
participants’ diverse communication preferences, digital literacy levels, and linguistic 
backgrounds, the exercises are intentionally designed to support multiple modes of 
expression—including speaking, typing, using visual materials, and interacting with 
whiteboard tools. Rather than emphasizing polished or performative outputs, the activities 
prioritize authenticity, fostering a psychologically safe space where participants can express 
themselves comfortably and meaningfully. 
To reduce cognitive load and encourage participation, each activity also includes an open 
reflection space that accommodates silent or asynchronous engagement. This allows 
learners to process and respond in their own time and manner. Together, these strategies 
aim to lower participation barriers, support intercultural exchange, and cultivate an open, 
welcoming atmosphere in digital learning environments. 
Discussion Design Considerations 
Each exercise includes a dedicated discussion component aimed at achieving cultural 
exchange goals within the virtual environment. Participants are grouped by challenge topics 
into breakout rooms. Each challenge consists of five small groups, which helps control the 
number of participants sharing simultaneously, balancing time constraints and team size. 
This challenge-based grouping fosters intercultural familiarity and understanding by exposing 
participants to diverse cultural perspectives throughout the co-creation of solutions.  
Additionally, recognizing that participants may be geographically dispersed within the virtual 
learning environment, extra breakout rooms beyond the challenge groups are provided. 
These additional rooms offer opportunities for more open and flexible discussions, 
accommodating different time zones and encouraging informal interactions. 
Discussions initially take place within the designated challenge breakout rooms. When time 
permits, an additional open discussion session (approximately 5 minutes) is held in the main 
room between exercises. This larger group discussion allows participants to share any 
reflections or insights related to the exercises. It also serves the purpose of gathering 
feedback on the activities and better understanding participants’ experiences during the 
exercises.

This section presents the design rationale and pedagogical intentions behind the “Who’s

cultural awareness in virtual, cross-cultural teams. Informed by insights from participant

Activity Structure and Content Design 
The exercise consists of three main components—Cultural Card, Map It Out, and 
Sharing—and is designed to be completed within approximately 15 minutes: five minutes for 
individual preparation and ten minutes for group interaction. The activity takes place on Miro, 
a collaborative digital whiteboard platform that remains accessible throughout the program, 
enabling both real-time engagement and ongoing asynchronous reference. By facilitating the 
sharing of cultural and personal narratives, the exercise aims to build early familiarity and 
trust among participants. 
 
Culture card 
In the first part of the exercise, participants create a Personal Culture Card, a visual 
self-introduction board designed to be shared before team collaboration begins, fostering 
mutual understanding and cultural identification. The template guides participants to provide 
key information that reflects multiple aspects of their personal and cultural identity. 
Specifically, participants are encouraged to include their name to facilitate identification 
within the team; share personal interests or hobbies to highlight individuality; and upload 
representative photos to visually express their personality. They also indicate the languages 
they speak, which can showcase language interests and open opportunities for language 
exchange. Participants specify their city and country of origin, providing geographical and 
cultural context. Additionally, they are invited to share a greeting in their native language or a 
personalized expression, adding cultural richness and warmth to the card. 
The template allows for creative personalization through emojis, colors, images, or doodles, 
enhancing expressiveness and making the card both an informative and visually engaging 
representation of individual identity and culture.

initiate informal dialogue. 
The environment is intentionally low-pressure, aiming to foster openness and psychological

5.2.3 Culture Wall Exercise 
The design of this exercise was directly inspired by insights gathered from the interview data. 
When asked about ideas for enhancing cultural exchange in virtual collaboration, one 
participant suggested organizing a “culture day” or a dedicated cultural sharing session to 
provide more space for meaningful intercultural engagement. Based on this suggestion, the 
Culture Wall Exercise was developed. This section outlines the design and structure of the 
exercise, which served as the second activity in the Culture Session. It was introduced after 
the completion of the initial Who’s Here exercise and a five-minute open discussion. The 
Culture Wall Exercise was implemented to facilitate cultural representation and peer 
exchange within international and intercultural teams. The activity consisted of two phases: a 
team-based creation of a Culture Wall, followed by a group presentation session. 
Following this introduction, the subsequent sections detail the Activity Structure and Content 
Design, Platform and Interaction Design, and Activity Objectives of the Culture Wall Exercise. 
Figure 8 provides a visual overview of the Culture Wall Exercise design, illustrating the flow, 
components, and intended interactions within the Miro platform. During the design process, 
particular attention was given to exploring various forms of visual representation, with the 
aim of testing how Miro could be used to create a shared visual space. This included 
considerations for how each team could clearly present diverse cultural elements while also 
ensuring the availability of open and interactive spaces to encourage dialogue and mutual 
exploration. 
 
Activity Structure and Content Design 
The Culture Wall Exercise is structured into two phases and is designed to be completed 
within approximately 25 minutes. In the first 10-minute segment, each team is assigned a 
country or cultural group and tasked with collaboratively constructing a digital Culture Wall 
using the Miro platform. The structure of the wall emphasizes surface-level cultural elements 
based on the Cultural Iceberg Model. A designated space for text, images, emojis, and video 
links is provided to assist with the organization and visual presentation of cultural content. 
Each team is also required to select a “Name Code”—a single word that symbolically 
represents the assigned culture. This word is displayed prominently on the wall and serves 
as a thematic anchor for the team’s contributions. To support the design process, teams are 
provided with a sample template that includes visual examples (e.g., food pictures, videos, 
traditional symbols) and guiding prompts. For instance, a sample wall titled “Jing’s Culture 
Wall” is included in the template, featuring a collection of images, embedded links, and brief 
descriptions in both English and Chinese. This sample contains examples such as traditional 
dishes, holiday symbols, and a link to a Chinese cultural music video.In cases where 
participants do not have direct cultural affiliation with the assigned country, they are 
encouraged to contribute based on prior knowledge, observation, or cross-cultural 
comparison. Inputs may be collaboratively sourced or individually selected. 
The second phase of the activity, lasting approximately 15 minutes, is a group sharing 
session, during which each team presents its Culture Wall to the full group. Each 
presentation typically lasts around three minutes. Team members introduce selected features 
52 
==PAGE:53==
 
of their wall and describe the significance of individual cultural elements. This sharing 
process allows teams to showcase both commonalities and distinctive features of their 
assigned cultures. Presentations can be delivered using multiple communication modes, 
including verbal explanation, written annotations in the chat, or on-screen visual cues. 
 
Platform and Interaction Design 
The Culture Wall is hosted on Miro, allowing both synchronous and asynchronous 
interaction. Participants can upload media files, add text boxes or post-it notes, and modify 
the wall collaboratively in real time. In addition to the main workspace for each team, shared 
reflection areas are integrated into the digital space to support interaction between groups. 
A set of prompts is displayed on the board to guide contributions. These prompts focus on 
recognizable cultural expressions and are phrased as sentence starters (see Figure 7). A 
sample Culture Wall, based on Chinese cultural materials, is included in the template to

5.5 Summary of implementation Results 
 
This section summarizes the outcomes of implementing the Culture Session within the 
NGDA 2025 program, based on observations and participant feedback in a live virtual 
classroom setting. It evaluates the feasibility, clarity, and overall user experience of the 
exercises, highlighting both the session’s strengths and areas for improvement in fostering 
intercultural communication, peer interaction, and collaborative engagement. 
 
59 
==PAGE:60==
 
The findings reveal cultural barriers encountered during the exercises and the potential for 
intercultural exchange in this context. A key insight is that, while the session design 
effectively encouraged individual engagement and cultural reflection, it fell short in facilitating 
sustained intercultural dialogue and dynamic group participation. Limited interaction points to 
structural and contextual challenges—such as attendance variability, platform familiarity, and 
social dynamics—that extend beyond the design itself. These factors not only affected the 
session experience but also shaped the scope and validity of the findings. 
 
High Individual Engagement, But Limited Peer Interaction 
Participants showed strong individual engagement, especially in exercises centered on 
personal storytelling and cultural self-representation. The Who’s Here activity had a relatively 
high completion rate, with over half of the participants fully completing their culture cards. 
Based on the outputs observed on the Miro board, participants approached the task with 
creativity and flexibility, fulfilling the intended objectives of the exercise. The open-ended 
structure, supported by encouraging prompts and sample responses, effectively guided 
participants while allowing for diverse and personal modes of expression. The Culture Wall 
outputs also demonstrated creativity and thoughtful visual expression. This indicates that the 
session design successfully activated personal reflection and supported identity exploration. 
 
However, this high task focus did not consistently foster team-level dialogue. Participants 
primarily worked in parallel rather than collaboratively, with limited spontaneous conversation 
or group synthesis. In teams with lower attendance, unclear role division and weak group 
dynamics further reduced participation during sharing phases. These patterns reflect an 
imbalance between individual insight and collective meaning-making. 
 
Minimal Visual and Verbal Interaction Impeded Group Dynamics 
Despite efforts to create an interactive environment, only 2–3 out of approximately 35–40 
participants turned on their cameras, limiting social presence. Verbal participation during 
transitions and plenary phases was sparse, even with direct facilitator prompts. This silence 
restricted real-time adaptation, emotional reading, and group momentum. 
The lack of visible and verbal engagement also impacted psychological safety, making 
participants 
hesitant 
to speak publicly. This was a critical barrier to cultural 
dialogue—particularly in an intercultural setting where trust and openness are essential. 
While participants showed task focus, the social context necessary for deeper exchange 
remained underdeveloped. 
 
Instructional Complexity and Uneven Task Execution 
While most participants were familiar with the Miro platform, the session’s multilayered 
instructions, embedded prompts, and non-linear structure contributed to increased cognitive 
load. Participants often spent significant time trying to decode task sequences before 
contributing content. Tasks such as “Map It Out” in Exercise 1 and the “Open Whiteboard: 
60 
==PAGE:61==
 
What Do We Have in Common” in Exercise 2 were noticeably underused, likely due to 
unclear relevance or sequencing within the flow of activities. 
An exception was observed in Challenge 3, where the team actively used the Open 
Whiteboard space to engage with the prompt, writing reflections such as “We are all very 
proud of our cultures,” “good food,” and “many traditions.” This example demonstrates the 
potential of such shared spaces as meaningful prompts for intercultural reflection—especially 
when participants are exposed to others' input, which can trigger further personal insights 
and a sense of connection across differences. 
Compounding these design challenges were technical issues—such as disappearing Miro 
content during Challenge 1—and attendance gaps. Only about half of the expected 
participants were present, resulting in uneven group sizes and inconsistent task completion. 
While some teams managed to complete all components, others submitted partial or minimal 
responses. These issues underscore the importance of clearer task logic, modular 
structuring, and contingency planning to ensure session resilience and equitable 
participation. 
 
Isolated Cases of Peer Facilitation Highlight Design Potential 
Despite structural limitations, moments of spontaneous peer facilitation emerged. For 
instance, one student voluntarily screen-shared during Challenge 3, enabling informal 
cross-team visibility. The interviewee from the Mexico team described their team’s open, 
conversational atmosphere and eagerness to engage other groups during plenary sessions. 
Similarly, during the Culture Wall exercise, participants showed strong enthusiasm for 
sharing their cultural backgrounds. Many teams collected a rich variety of information to 
represent their cultures, demonstrating a high level of engagement and pride in cultural 
expression. 
However, the absence of clear speaking order or structured interaction guidelines led 
participants to focus more on gathering content than transitioning into oral sharing or 
cross-team exchange. As a result, peer-initiated collaboration remained isolated and did not 
scale across the session.  
 
These observations were further supported by insights shared during a post-course 
interview. The interviewee reflected that while the Culture Session activities were inspiring, 
challenges emerged during real-time participation. She described instances where only a few 
people actively contributed while most remained silent, leading to awkward conversations 
and a lack of interaction. Although her team made efforts to initiate communication with other 
groups, they encountered low reciprocal engagement. She attributed these challenges to 
factors such as age differences, competing priorities, and time zone issues, which 
collectively hindered collaboration. 
 
Despite the limited interaction during the session itself, she later revisited the content on Miro 
to reflect on her team's work and explore how other teams addressed similar challenges. 
She also noted that WhatsApp, a primary communication platform for the program, was 
61 
==PAGE:62==
 
underutilized. Its limited use reduced opportunities for both intra-group and cross-team 
dialogue, suggesting that even simple, familiar tools require better integration to support 
engagement and continuity in virtual collaboration. 
 
5.6 Challenges 
During implementation, several challenges arose that impacted the effectiveness of the 
prototype. One major issue was the discrepancy between expected and actual participant 
numbers, with significantly fewer attendees than anticipated. This limited group cohesion and 
reduced opportunities for meaningful intercultural dialogue. 
The diverse cultural and technical backgrounds of participants presented additional 
challenges in ensuring equitable engagement. Task design was often too ambitious for the 
allotted time, causing participants to rush through activities and prioritize task completion 
over deeper interaction and reflection. Furthermore, facilitators struggled to align session 
delivery with learning objectives, resulting in participants focusing more on finishing tasks 
than on fostering communication and cultural sharing. 
The fast pace of the session, combined with limited onboarding and group formation time, 
further hindered participation, particularly for introverted or less digitally fluent individuals. 
Finally, key interactive features designed to promote trust and social presence—such as 
emoji check-ins and enforced camera use—were not implemented during testing, which may 
have affected the development of psychological safety and spontaneous collaboration. 
 
5.7 Limitations 
In addition to the practical challenges described above, this study faced several limitations 
primarily related to its research design and scope. 
Firstly, during the initial testing phase, the sample size was extremely limited—only one 
participant took part, who also had prior experience with the NG[ ]A and a background in 
design. This familiarity likely introduced bias into the feedback and reduced the 
generalizability of the results to a broader and more diverse user base. Moreover, testing 
with a single individual prevented the assessment of critical collaborative dynamics, such as 
group communication, intercultural exchange, and psychological safety. As a result, many of 
the issues that later emerged during implementation could not be anticipated during initial 
testing. 
Secondly, during the implementation in the NGDA 2025 Culture Session, attendance was 
significantly lower than expected, with only about half of the anticipated participants joining 
and some leaving early. This limited the representativeness of observed group interactions 
and engagement. Additionally, the initial participant’s prior familiarity with the session tools 
may have positively influenced her experience, offering limited insight into usability 
challenges faced by less experienced users. 
62 
==PAGE:63==
 
Lastly, time constraints impacted the overall depth of feedback and reflection. In particular, 
the time allocated for open discussion was frequently compressed, reducing opportunities to 
gather real-time participant insights and experiential feedback during the session. 
These limitations underscore the complexity of designing and implementing virtual 
multicultural collaboration activities and highlight the need for broader testing, longer session 
durations, and iterative refinement in future research.

6.1 Insights from the Culture Session in NGDA 2025 
As the designer and facilitator of the culture session within the NGDA 2025 program, I 
structured the session around two core components: a theoretical introduction and an 
experiential exercise. The session began with an overview of Edward T. Hall’s Culture 
Iceberg Model and the NGDA Cultural Mindset Framework, aiming to help participants 
recognize both visible and invisible cultural layers and prepare their mindset for intercultural 
collaboration. This was followed by two interactive exercises—Who’s Here and Culture 
Wall—designed to promote self-reflection, cultural storytelling, and team-based exchange. 
While the session successfully introduced cultural frameworks and fostered individual 
reflection, it revealed persistent limitations in stimulating interpersonal connection, group 
dialogue, and psychological safety within a virtual, multicultural context. Participants primarily 
engaged with tasks on an individual basis, with limited verbal or social interaction, despite 
the activities being designed to promote collaborative exchange. The intermittent presence of 
facilitators in breakout rooms did not sufficiently compensate for the lack of continuous 
team-level support, further constraining interaction. These dynamics point to broader 
structural challenges in virtual environments, particularly regarding the cultivation of trust and 
relational engagement across diverse cultural groups. Observations from the NGDA 2025 
implementation further highlighted a disproportionate emphasis on task completion over 
communication and culture exchange, suggesting that relational goals were insufficiently 
integrated into the session design. To address these issues, future iterations should adopt a 
more holistic approach—prioritizing relational outcomes alongside content delivery—through 
clearer task delineation, simplified structures, and embedded facilitation checkpoints. 
Cultural activities should be reframed as collective meaning-making processes rather than 
isolated assignments, ensuring alignment between pedagogical intent and experiential 
outcomes.

6.2 Theoretical Implications 
This study contributes to existing theories on cross-cultural collaboration, virtual 
communication, psychological safety, and co-creation by offering practical insights into how 
inclusive innovation can be supported in global virtual teams (GVTs) through intentional 
design. 
 
First, the findings reinforce the relevance of non-spatial proximity theory (Boschma 2025 
Hung et al., 2021) in virtual environments. Activities such as “Who’s Here” and “Culture 
Wall,” embedded in the NGDA co-creation framework, demonstrate how organizational and 
social proximity can be fostered digitally, establishing psychological safety through visual 
guidance. These design interventions helped participants feel more connected and 
recognized across geographical and cultural boundaries, thus supporting prior research 
suggesting that non-spatial proximity is more influential than physical closeness in driving 
collaboration and knowledge exchange. 
Second, this study expands the understanding of psychological safety in multicultural virtual 
teams. While much of the foundational work on psychological safety has been conducted in 
relatively homogeneous, co-located teams, our findings highlight how carefully designed 
digital activities—such as randomized grouping, informal icebreakers, and co-creation 
rituals—can reduce anxiety and foster open dialogue in diverse teams. These practices 
directly respond to the communication challenges identified in GVTs (Presbitero, 2020; Titus 
& Ng’ambi, 2023) and offer new ways to cultivate psychological safety in digitally mediated, 
intercultural spaces. 
 
Third, the study responds to calls from scholars to move beyond static cultural typologies 
(e.g., Hofstede’s dimensions) and focus on the dynamic, situated nature of cultural 
interaction in real-time collaboration. The inclusion of narrative-based cultural reflection tasks 
within co-creative exercises allowed participants to explore and articulate their identities, 
values, and social contexts in a way that enriched team understanding. This suggests that 
cultural identity can serve not only as a source of potential conflict, but also as a valuable 
resource for creative innovation—if activated through thoughtful facilitation. 
 
Furthermore, this study contributes to inclusive innovation and design education literature by 
illustrating how culturally responsive design methods can promote belonging, agency, and 
engagement in global learning environments. The results show that digital co-creation 
activities can support students in developing intercultural competence and collaborative 
confidence, even in temporary or asynchronous virtual settings. 
 
Finally, this study suggests that digital tools are not neutral infrastructures, but rather 
culturally situated mediating artifacts that can shape emotional, social, and epistemological 
dynamics in global teamwork. Understanding how these tools influence participation, 
recognition, and inclusion opens new directions for research at the intersection of design, 
culture, and education. Specifically, the role of facilitation, interaction rituals, and adaptive 
interfaces merits further exploration as key variables in fostering meaningful collaboration 
across cultural boundaries. 
 
In sum, this research not only supports and extends theoretical models in cross-cultural 
collaboration and psychological safety, but also introduces a design-oriented perspective on 
65 
==PAGE:66==
 
how virtual environments can be intentionally structured to enhance inclusive innovation in 
global education and practice. 
 
 
6.3 Practical Implications 
The findings of this study provide practical insights for members of global virtual collaboration 
teams from multiple perspectives, particularly relevant to project organizers, educators, 
facilitators, and designers working in multicultural educational and professional settings. 
Although the study focused on a specific pedagogical prototype within NGDA 2025, the 
developed practices and structures are scalable and adaptable to other international 
projects, corporate training programs, and global innovation initiatives, serving as 
frameworks for inclusive collaboration. 
From the perspective of project organizers, it is recommended to include a dedicated 
onboarding phase or cultural Boot Camp session that focuses on cultural sharing, 
expectation alignment, and team-building activities. Such sessions should be led by trained 
facilitators and aim to reduce participant uncertainty by fostering exploration of values, 
communication styles, and work habits. Facilitators need to accommodate diverse writing 
abilities, technological proficiency, and cultural norms to ensure full participation and 
psychological safety from the start. Additionally, providing flexible content preparation 
options—such as asynchronous pre-work involving background readings or reflection 
exercises—can alleviate time pressure during live sessions and prepare participants to 
engage more effectively. 
For educators and curriculum designers, it is crucial to allocate sufficient time not only for 
task completion but also for cultural learning and social interaction. Employing random and 
rotating group assignments can disrupt cultural silos and promote cross-cultural 
engagement. Matching the complexity of tasks with appropriate support resources is 
essential, especially in diverse teams. Educators should also prioritize training facilitators in 
both digital facilitation skills and intercultural competence. Designing activities that treat 
communication and interaction as learning objectives—rather than mere means to complete 
tasks—can foster deeper intercultural understanding and peer connection. 
Facilitators and digital co-creation leads play a key role in maintaining participant 
engagement and interaction. Virtual collaboration benefits from clear, time-boxed tasks, 
step-by-step instructions, and structured digital rituals such as mood check-ins, mandatory 
camera-on policies during key moments, and random participant prompts to enhance focus 
and accountability. Breaking down complex activities into smaller, manageable phases with 
defined outcomes can reduce cognitive overload and improve attention. Importantly, 
continuous team-level facilitation is necessary to overcome challenges related to limited 
interpersonal exchange and to build psychological safety, which remains difficult in virtual, 
multicultural settings. These observations suggest that successful intercultural collaboration 
cannot rely solely on task design or participant initiative. It requires more intentional 
facilitation—such as clear rules for intergroup exchange, rotating presentation roles, or 
guided opportunities for interaction—to help build trust, increase visibility, and strengthen 
group cohesion. 
66 
==PAGE:67==
 
From the perspective of designers, the integration of cognitive and relational dimensions is 
critical. Task structures should be simplified with clear hierarchies, and cultural activities 
should be framed as collective meaning-making experiences rather than isolated 
deliverables. Providing tool orientation resources, such as onboarding videos and guided 
walkthroughs, reduces participant anxiety and promotes equitable access—especially for 
those less familiar with digital platforms. Complex or reflective components, such as visual 
storytelling or identity mapping exercises, benefit from asynchronous pre-assignment, freeing 
synchronous session time for rich peer dialogue, cultural comparison, and active 
engagement. 
In summary, while the session effectively introduced cultural frameworks and encouraged 
individual reflection, it revealed challenges in fostering sustained intercultural dialogue, social 
bonding, and group-level engagement. Although the tools provided in the Culture Session 
hold some lasting value—such as being repurposed as icebreaker activities in academic 
programs like NGDA that involve many cross-cultural teams—their direct applicability to 
more conventional team-building contexts or projects unrelated to multicultural themes may 
require further adaptation. These findings highlight the importance of intentional design that 
balances cognitive tasks with relational processes to create psychologically safe and 
inclusive virtual environments. Applying the recommendations presented here can help 
institutions and organizations move beyond mere connectivity toward truly intercultural and 
co-creative collaborations.

7. Conclusion 
This final chapter synthesizes the key insights of the study and reflects on the core research 
question in the broader context of virtual cross-cultural collaboration. It is organized into 
three sections. Section 7.1 directly addresses the main research question by summarizing 
how digital collaboration tools can be designed to support inclusive participation and cultural 
exchange in multicultural teams. Section 7.2 provides critical reflections on the nature of 
cultural barriers in virtual collaboration and their implications in an increasingly digital and 
globalized work environment. Section 7.3 outlines directions for future research, suggesting

7.1 Answering the Research Question 
This research explored how digital collaboration tools can be designed to address 
communication-related cultural barriers in virtual collaboration, with a focus on enhancing 
active participation and cultural exchange in multicultural teams within global innovation 
programs such as NG[ ]A. Using a mixed-methods approach—including interviews, 
observations, and surveys—the study examined both the opportunities and constraints of 
current digital collaboration practices in cross-cultural virtual settings. The findings are 
synthesized below in response to the research question and its sub-questions. 
 
Addressing Sub-question 1: What are the main cultural communication barriers 
encountered in virtual collaboration within multicultural innovation teams? 
The research identified several core cultural communication barriers: differences in 
communication styles, degrees of formality, attitudes toward hierarchy, and expectations 
regarding participation. These issues often become more pronounced when team members 
are unfamiliar with one another, leading to hesitation in initiating conversations or 
contributing actively in group settings. In such contexts, individuals may prioritize task 
completion over relationship-building, thereby limiting the potential for meaningful cultural 
exchange. The absence of shared norms or psychological safety can further intensify 
misunderstandings and reduce engagement. 
 
Addressing 
Sub-question 
2: 
How 
do 
digital 
collaboration 
tools 
influence 
communication dynamics and cultural exchange among team members from diverse 
cultural backgrounds? 
Digital collaboration tools play a significant role in shaping team communication. While they 
facilitate asynchronous coordination and enable visual co-creation, they often limit 
spontaneous interaction and informal social bonding—unless deliberately designed to 
support those goals. Participants frequently perceive these platforms as task-focused 
69 
==PAGE:70==
 
environments, using them primarily for efficiency rather than interpersonal engagement. This 
means that tools in themselves are neutral mediums; what shapes their impact is the 
intentional design of how they are used. Without clear structures and guidance, their 
potential to foster cultural exchange remains underutilized. 
 
Addressing Sub-question 3: What practical exercises or strategies can digital 
collaboration tools incorporate to address cultural barriers and enhance participation 
in multicultural teams? 
To overcome these communication barriers, the study identified several practical and 
design-based strategies. Early-stage onboarding activities are essential: these include 
exercises where participants introduce themselves, share cultural work habits, and 
co-develop team norms. Such practices help foster familiarity, mutual understanding, and 
psychological safety. In addition, clearly framing the purpose of the tool—as not only a task 
management space but also a site for exploration, reflection, and co-learning—can expand 
how participants interact with it. 
Design elements such as embedded cultural prompts, maps, and emoji-based expressions 
can stimulate informal sharing and cultural reflection. Structured but flexible exercises, when 
well-timed, help maintain engagement and prevent fatigue, enabling sustained and 
meaningful participation. These strategies, when combined with inclusive facilitation, 
contribute to building collaborative team cultures that are both productive and culturally 
aware. 
 
Synthesizing the Findings: Main Research Question 
In conclusion, the research shows that digital collaboration tools can effectively support 
inclusive participation and cultural exchange in multicultural virtual teams—but only when 
they are intentionally designed and facilitated with those goals in mind. Tools that integrate 
onboarding routines, clear purpose framing, and space for informal interaction go beyond 
task coordination; they become enablers of intercultural understanding and shared 
meaning-making. 
Ultimately, successful cross-cultural virtual collaboration depends on a synergistic approach 
that combines tool design, facilitation strategy, and team cultural awareness. While the 
technology can be helpful and also can cause barriers for communication, it is the emphasis 
on sharing and communication as the primary goal of the exercises, along with allocating 
more time or making it the main activity, built around it that determines whether participation 
is inclusive and culturally enriching.

7.2 
Final 
Thoughts 
on 
Cultural 
Barriers 
and 
Virtual 
Collaboration 
Cultural barriers in virtual collaboration remain a highly relevant and important topic, 
especially given the rise of Global Virtual Teams (GVTs) and the ongoing trend of digital 
transformation. As international collaboration increasingly takes place online, a number of 
variables must be carefully considered and addressed. Technical challenges, inconsistent 
participation, and varying levels of engagement can all impact the success of virtual 
teamwork. Therefore, flexible and adaptive methodologies are essential. 
Understanding cultural backgrounds across different countries and regions plays a crucial 
role in interpreting behavior patterns. However, it is equally important to pay attention to how 
individuals from multicultural teams adapt their working habits and communication styles in 
virtual settings. This shift highlights the need to not only focus on national or regional culture, 
but also on the evolving practices within digital multicultural environments. As remote 
collaboration becomes more normalized, further research should explore these behavioral 
dynamics to better support effective and inclusive virtual teamwork. 
 
7.3 Future Research Directions 
Building on the findings of this study, several directions for future research can be proposed 
to further explore how digital collaboration tools and facilitation strategies can enhance 
cross-cultural collaboration in virtual, multicultural teams. 
First, in multicultural virtual programs like NG[ ]A, where diverse teams collaborate online, 
exercises should be designed with clearly defined project goals to guide participant 
engagement. Future research could specifically explore facilitation methods tailored to such 
multicultural virtual settings, including various moderation techniques, turn-taking protocols, 
conflict resolution strategies, and the use of inclusive language. Investigating how these 
facilitation approaches influence team participation, trust-building, and the quality of 
cross-cultural communication would provide valuable insights for enhancing collaboration in 
virtual, culturally diverse educational and innovation projects. 
Second, further research could focus on the design and evaluation of online onboarding or 
warm-up exercises in global innovation and education programs. Early-stage structured 
activities—such as storytelling prompts, shared value mapping, or intercultural games—may 
play a critical role in reducing communication anxiety and fostering familiarity. Future work 
could test various formats, lengths, and levels of guidance in these exercises to identify 
effective design patterns that support inclusive collaboration from the outset. 
Third, there is growing potential to explore adaptive digital collaboration tools that respond 
dynamically to team composition and cultural profiles. For example, AI-assisted platforms or 
personalization features could help detect participation imbalances, offer culturally relevant 
communication prompts, or adapt workflows based on team needs. Research in this area 
could contribute to the development of more inclusive, responsive virtual environments.

Finally, longitudinal studies are needed to investigate how cultural exchange and 
participation patterns evolve over time within virtual teams. Most current studies, including 
this one, are based on short-term observations. Long-term research could shed light on the 
sustainability of early interventions, changes in team dynamics, and the long-term impact of 
tool design choices on collaboration outcomes.
