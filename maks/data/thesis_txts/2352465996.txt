Abstract 
This thesis investigates how Be15 performs in terms of estimation of boilers-, cooling- and ventilation plant 
load, compared to a dynamic simulation program (IES-VE). A total of three specific HVAC systems are chosen 
for the comparison. Possible optimization of the Be15 energy frame calculation, by utilization of IES-VE results 
is found suitable for one system type, namely when having a system cooling purely by mechanical ventilation.  
Prior to the comparison, the Be15 software calculation core and HVAC related inputs are explained in detail. 
Especially the cooling part of Be15 is described extensively. Also, commonly used HVAC systems in Denmark 
are described, where the mechanical ventilation part is described in detail.  
The three specific system types that are the subject of comparison are:  
System type 1. Cooling & fresh air: mechanical ventilation (VAV), heating: radiator 
System type 2. Cooling: Chilled ceiling & partly mechanical ventilation, fresh air: mechanical ventilation (VAV), 
heating: radiator 
System type 3. Cooling: Termo Active Building System, fresh air: mechanical ventilation (VAV), heating: Termo 
Active Building Systems 
A single-zone case building is developed, with a total gross area of 96.0 m². The analysis is conducted on the 
case building by doing a Be15 calculation and a corresponding IES-VE dynamic hourly simulation with the 
three system types. An additional study, using system type 1, is done with a multi-zone IES-VE model, created 
by dividing the initial model into four equally sized zones.  
The conclusion of the analysis is that Be15 estimates a higher boilers- and cooling load. Furthermore, the 
study show that Be15 estimates a higher ventilation plant load when the cooling is only done through the 
mechanical ventilation system. In contrast to this, the results show that when having cooling done partly by 
other systems, in this case chilled ceiling or a TABS system (System 2 & System 3), the annual difference in 
ventilation plant load between Be15 and IES-VE is small. By doing a correlated Be15 calculation, using IES-VE 
monthly averages for the volume flow as new volume flow input in Be15, the annual difference between 
Be15 and IES-VE ventilation plant load estimation, comes close to zero. Using the correlated Be15 input for 
the volume flow, the energy frame can be reduced.

Resumé 
Dette kandidatspeciale undersøger hvordan Be15 beregningsprogrammet regner på opvarmnings-, kølings 
og ventilations laster, til sammenligning med et dynamisk simulerings værktøj (IES-VE). Tre specifikke HVAC 
systemer er valgt for undersøgelsen. Eventuel optimering af energirammen, ved at udnytte IES-VE 
simulerings resultater, er fundet muligt, for et system hvor køling udelukkende haves via den mekaniske 
ventilation.  
Før sammenligningen af de to værktøjer finder sted, bliver Be15 beregningsmetoden samt de inputs der er 
relevante ang. HVAC systemer beskrevet. Især kølingsdelen af Be15 er beskrevet i detaljer. Derudover 
beskrives de typiske HVAC systemer der bliver brugt i den danske bygningsmasse, hvor der lægges vægt på 
at uddybe mekaniske ventilations systemer.  
De tre systemer, der bliver undersøgt, er opbygget som følger: 
System type 1. Køling og frisk luft: mekanisk ventilation (VAV), opvarmning: radiator 
System type 2. Køling: kølelofter og delvis mekanisk ventilation (VAV), frisk luft: mekanisk ventilation (VAV), 
opvarmning: radiator 
System type 3. Køling: termo aktive bygnings konstruktioner, frisk luft: mekanisk ventilation (VAV), 
opvarmning: termo aktive bygnings konstruktioner 
En en-zone model bruges i sammenligningen. Bygningen har et bruttoareal på 96.0 m². Sammenligningen 
udføres ved at lave en Be15 beregning og en tilsvarende simulering på time basis i IES-VE for de tre 
systemtyper. Der foretages en yderligere sammenligning på en multi-zone model, med samme geometri som 
en-zone modellen, hvor system type 1 benyttes.  
Analyserne viser, at Be15 generelt estimerer en højere opvarmnings- og kølings last end hvad de dynamiske 
simuleringer viser. Hvis køling udelukkende haves via den mekaniske ventilation, estimerer Be15 også en 
højere ventilations last. Hvis der derimod kun haves delvis køling via ventilation, f.eks. ved system type 2 og 
system type 3, viser analyserne, at ventilations lasten estimeret af Be15 og IES-VE, på årlig basis, ligger tæt 
på hinanden. På baggrund af analyserne, foretages en korrigeret Be15 beregning af system type 1, hvor IES-
VE resultater for den mekaniske ventilation bruges som ny input til Be15. Ved den korrigerede Be15 
beregning, bliver den årlige ventilations last estimeret tæt på ens i de to programmer. Derudover kan 
energirammen reduceres ved at bruge denne udviklede metode.

1. Introduction 
The Danish building legislation in its current form has existed since 1961, when the first national building 
legislation was presented. Ever since the presentation of the first building legislation, the provisions in the 
legislation have been adjusted to fit the continuous development in the building sector, which has been 
driven by research and innovation. A main focus in the period has been the rising concern about building 
energy use as this takes up a large portion of the total national energy use. The first provisions concerning 
energy consumption came in 1977. In recent years, the focus on thermal comfort has gained strength. The 
thermal comfort and thermal satisfaction of the occupants in a given building are strongly connected to a 
building’s heating, ventilation and air conditioning system, which furthermore has a large influence on the 
building’s energy consumption.  
In order to get a building permission in Denmark, compliance with the Danish Building Legislation regarding 
energy consumption has to be proven through an energy frame calculation. It is commonly known in the 
building industry that the Be15 software (Version 8.16.2.4, SBI, 2015), used to show compliance with the 
Danish Building Legislation, does not give a realistic estimation of the buildings’ energy consumption.  
Nevertheless, many building owners use the energy-frame calculation conducted prior to the construction of 
a building, as a guideline for estimation of the actual building performance. According to the developers of 
the legislation compliance software, Statens Byggeforskningsinstitut, the purpose of the Be15 calculation is 
to compare the performance of a building without taking the specific use of the building into consideration, 
and consequently it does not give a realistic estimation of the energy consumption.  
Numerous methods to estimate a building’s actual energy consumption are used in the building industry. 
One method is the use of dynamic simulation tools, where detailed simulations can be conducted. Another 
piece of software, which is in beta stage, is the BeREAL software, made by Steffen Petersen and others from 
Aalborg University. BeREAL uses Be15 as input, and calculates with best and worst guesses on e.g. volume 
flow, U-values etc., and gives the energy consumption in an interval rather than an exact value. 
Motivation 
If the intention of the compliance calculation in Be15 is not to give a realistic estimation of the energy 
consumption of a given building, then why is this thesis even relevant? One could also ask why the Be15 
software is actually trying to calculate a somewhat realistic energy consumption based on (for heating and 
cooling) the international standard DS/EN 13790 (Dansk Standard, 2008)? Looking into the 'core' of the 
software, the estimation/calculation of the heating and especially cooling need is rather complex and 
==PAGE:12==
 
Page | 11 
 
comprehensive, and small changes in input values can have a large relative influence on the final estimation 
on energy consumption.  
The fact that small changes can have a large influence on the calculation, combined with the fact that Be15 
according to many consultants is used as a design tool in the building phases, makes it crucial that the users 
are aware of the inputs; how they work, and what influence they might have.  
This is where this thesis comes into play, since this thesis describes in details the Be15 calculation 'core' 
engine, related to the heating and cooling estimation, and furthermore develops a method to utilize dynamic 
simulation results in the energy frame calculation. 
Problem Statement 
By understanding the calculation method of the Be15 software and by utilizing IES-VE results in the Be15 
calculation in a clever way, the Be15 calculation not only becomes more detailed, but the energy frame for a 
given building can be lowered, too. 
Method & Approach 
How to analyze this hypothesis? In order to do so, one must first of all understand how commonly used HVAC 
systems operate in buildings in practice, represented by dynamic IES-VE simulations (Version: 2016.0.0.0, IES, 
2016), and how Be15 represents and assumes the operation strategy of the same HVAC systems. This 
procedure implicitly results in a description of different commonly applied HVAC strategies in non-residential 
buildings, along with a detailed investigation and description of the Be15 calculation 'core' engine and the 
inputs to Be15.  
Different approaches are suitable for the comparison of Be15 and IES-VE. In this thesis, it has been decided 
to compare single-zone to single-zone, and to look solely at the energy delivered to the zones, named load 
within the report, without efficiencies, COP values et cetera in the heating/cooling systems. An additional 
study is conducted by comparing a multi-zone IES-VE model with the single-zone Be15 software.  
By using the knowledge obtained in this thesis, consultants will become more aware of the data they input 
into the Be15 software. Furthermore, a method to utilize HVAC performance results is developed, which is 
usable in building projects where dynamic simulations are conducted. By utilizing detailed simulation results 
for e.g. mechanical ventilation in the right way, the Be15 software can give results closer to what is found in 
the dynamic simulation, which studies have shown to give a more realistic estimation of buildings’ actual 
energy consumption.

2. Background: HVAC Systems & Software 
This section serves as the foundation and background for the analysis conducted in this thesis. Sub-section 
2.1 includes a description of typical HVAC systems for office buildings in Denmark. The purpose of the section 
is to outline what systems will be applied for the analysis within the thesis.  
Sub-section 2.2 includes a description of the Be15 calculation core, a description of the HVAC related Be15 
inputs and a description of the dynamic simulation program used in the analysis, IES-VE.  
2.1 HVAC Systems in Denmark in Office Buildings 
This sub-section briefly describes the most common HVAC systems in Denmark, in order to outline the 
systems that will be used within the work of this thesis. It is important to emphasize that only a small fraction 
of the possible HVAC systems is described, since the possible combinations and system variations are 
numerous.  
2.1.1 Ventilation Systems & Strategy 
Most office buildings in Denmark have a balanced mechanical ventilation system to supply fresh air and to 
exhaust warm polluted air. According to Haghighat et al. (1997) the main purpose of the mechanical 
ventilation system is to provide a healthy and comfortable working environment for the occupants, while 
maintaining minimum energy consumption. The mechanical ventilation system is most commonly a CAV 
system (Constant Air Volume) or a VAV system (Variable Air Volume). The ventilation supply strategy can be 
either displacement ventilation or total volume ventilation/mixing ventilation. Total volume ventilation 
secures an uniform indoor environment, whereas displacement ventilation supplies fresh conditioned air 
which spreads uniformly in the zone and acts as a piston where the polluted air is exhausted at the ceiling. 
Displacement ventilation relies on proper interior building design since occupant placement of e.g. bookcases 
in front of the inlet diffuser reduces the ventilation’s effectiveness to a great extent. Draft can occur at the 
ankles if the displacement ventilation supply system is not properly dimensioned regarding air velocities.  
Draft can occur with any ventilation principle/system/setup if not dimensioned properly.  
Other ventilation principles in new office buildings, which are not used that commonly, could be natural 
ventilation systems or hybrid ventilation systems. Natural ventilation systems could e.g. be: single sided, 
cross ventilation or buoyancy driven natural ventilation. Hybrid ventilation systems could be when a 
mechanical exhaust is operating in combination with a natural ventilation system, or other similar 
combination of natural ventilation and some sort of mechanical control/ventilation.  
In the following sub-section, the principles of the systems mentioned above will be described briefly.  
==PAGE:16==
 
Page | 15 
 
2.1.1.1 Mechanical Ventilation Systems 
The CAV ventilation principle is a ventilation strategy where the AHU delivers a constant volume flow to the 
zone/zones in a given building. In some systems, the inlet temperatures can be adjusted in the Air Handling 
Unit (AHU). One of the advantages of a CAV system is that it is easy to balance - system wise - and that the 
fan load is constant. Furthermore, the ventilation system is very simple in terms of system components since 
no damper control in the ducts is needed. Some of the disadvantages of the CAV system are related to the 
dimension principle – dimensioned for maximum load – the ducts and units tend to be large and over-
dimensioned for the actual operation situations. The fact that the CAV system in most cases has a centrally 
controlled inlet temperature can cause problems with thermal comfort in different zones e.g. a south 
oriented room vs. a north oriented room. An inlet temperature of 18 ⁰C might be suitable on a warm summer 
day for a south oriented zone but be unsuitable for the north oriented zone of the building which could 
ultimately cause thermal discomfort. The nature of the CAV strategy makes it suitable for buildings/zones 
with little or no change in indoor conditions e.g. in bathrooms or similar.  
The VAV system, sometimes referred to as Demand Controlled Ventilation, can adjust the amount of air 
volume depending on a set of predetermined conditions e.g. by the operative temperature in the zone, the 
humidity or the CO₂ concentration. The system control is in most buildings a fully automatic Building 
Management System (BMS) with the possibility for occupants to overwrite the automatic control via a locally 
placed thermostat securing the occupants control over the indoor environment.  
Studies have shown that there is an correlation between occupant dissatisfaction and the level of personal 
control over the working environment (Haghighat et al., 1997). It is therefore preferable in new buildings to 
allow some sort of occupant control of their indoor environment which is also the dominating tendency in 
new building projects.  
Compared to a CAV system the VAV system has a series of advantages (Melikov, 2013): 
 
The flow rate can be adjusted to provide sufficient conditioned air to a given zone to obtain an 
acceptable indoor environment 
 
The ducts and units in the ventilation system may be sized smaller can corresponding CAV system 
due to the diversity factor 
 
The operating cost is lower than for a CAV system since the volume flow can be lowered in periods 
with no occupants 
The systems can be either a central system, with an AHU operating in one or more zones, or a decentralized 
solution where operation is placed in the different zones of the building.

2.1.1.2 Natural Ventilation Systems 
Natural ventilation systems rely on buoyancy effects and pressure difference. Depending on the building’s 
shape and design, various natural ventilation principles can be used: single sided, cross ventilation or thermal 
plumes due to buoyancy effects in the air. The principle of the cross ventilation implies that positive and 
negative wind pressure differences on the building’s façades drive the volume flow. The single sided natural 
ventilation occurs when a window/opening is opened and due to pressure differences fresh air is entering 
from the lowest part of the window/opening area (taking the shape coefficient into consideration) while 
indoor/polluted air is leaving from the top part of the window area. It is difficult to implement heat recovery 
in natural ventilation system and it is therefore preferable to put to use as an additional ventilation source in 
hot summer periods where heat recovery is not necessary.  
2.1.1.3 Hybrid Ventilation Systems  
Hybrid ventilation systems are natural ventilation systems that are driven by e.g. a mechanical exhaust placed 
in the center/polluted areas of the building/zones. This is a strategy that is used in many dwellings where 
there is a constant exhaust in the bathroom and kitchen and fresh air enters through windows/cracks in the 
building envelope. 
Hybrid ventilation is most commonly applied in large atriums in non-residential buildings where the warm air 
is ventilated out through an exhaust in the top of the atrium, and the exhaust enhances the naturally driven 
buoyancy flow.   
2.1.2 Heating & Cooling System Strategies 
This sub-section briefly describes airborne and fluidborne heating and cooling systems.  
2.1.2.1 Airborne Heating & Cooling 
The heating/cooling demand can be covered by the ventilation system. In the new building legislation, it is 
not allowed to have a heating strategy relying completely on mechanical ventilation. Cooling through the 
ventilation system is a commonly used solution since good mixing of air is achieved due to the temperature 
differences of the supply air and room air. 
2.1.2.2 Fluidborne Heating & Cooling 
The conventional strategy to cover the heating demand is by using either radiators, convectors or floor 
heating. In most cases, the response time for convectors/radiators is relatively quick which is suitable for 
environments that are affected by exterior conditions. This is suitable for buildings with poor insulation and 
where the zone heating demand can change from day to day quite quickly.   
==PAGE:18==
 
Page | 17 
 
In recent years, the heating demand in new buildings has declined rapidly compared to the existing building 
mass, ultimately reducing the heating demand to a minimum. The fact that the heating demand is relatively 
low enhances the use of Thermo Active Building Systems (TABS). TABS uses the thermal mass of a given 
building through fluid flow in pipes embodied in the construction/slabs to cool or heat the building depending 
on the season. The system solution relies strongly on the amount of building mass that is present and it is 
therefore only applicable in buildings with some degree of thermal mass e.g. buildings with concrete floors 
et cetera. The disadvantage of TABS is that the system response time is relatively long compared to other 
conventional heating systems. 
Another fluidborne cooling strategy is the use of chilled ceilings, which is a commonly applied solution in 
places where there is a limitation on the possible height of the suspended ceiling.  
2.1.3 Preferred HVAC Systems 
A study has been conducted among recent building projects which has shown that there is a tendency to 
prefer a VAV system for fresh air and cooling, combined with either TABS or radiators/convectors for heating 
and TABS/Chilled Ceiling for additional cooling. This section describes three systems representing three 
commonly used HVAC strategies in new building projects. By choosing three commonly used HVAC strategies 
the analysis and investigations conducted in this thesis will be highly relevant for consultants.  
The above mentioned preferred HVAC systems are described in the following sub-sections, along with system 
principles, illustrated in corresponding figures.

2.2 Software & Legislation 
Firstly, the energy and indoor requirements and legislation will be described in this sub-section. Next comes 
a description of the Be15 software, including a description of the inputs and calculation core. The final topic 
in the sub-section includes a description of the IES-VE software.  
2.2.1 Legislation & Thermal Indoor Environment Requirements 
According to the Danish Building Legislation (Building class 2020) the energy consumption for non-residential 
buildings with primary energy factors should not exceed 25 kWh/m² annually. Regarding the thermal indoor 
environment, the Danish Building Legislation has no specific requirements for non-residential buildings, but 
prescribes that building owners can set requirements themselves. However, the legislation states: 
 
''Buildings must be constructed such that, during the intended use of the buildings, a 
comfortable, healthy thermal indoor climate can be maintained in the rooms occupied by any number of 
people for an extended period considering the human activity in the rooms.'' (Danish Building Regulations, 
2015) 
In many projects, building owners uses the DS 474 (Dansk Standard, 1993) guidance on thermal indoor 
environment. DS 474 prescribes that there should be no more than 100 hours above 26⁰C and no more than 
25 hours above 27⁰C annually in order to achieve an acceptable thermal indoor environment. Regarding 
minimum ventilation amounts BR15 (Danish Building Legislation, 2015) prescribes that the volume flow pr.

2.2.2 Be15: Energy Frame Compliance Software 
In this sub-section the energy frame compliance software Be15 will be described in terms of inputs and 
calculation engine. Only the part of the software related to the building HVAC system is described in detail.  
The purpose of the description is to get an overview of the software inputs and how the inputs are used in 
the calculation of the heating and cooling need. 
2.2.2.1 Be15: Inputs Ventilation 
Be15 is the program used to document compliance with the provisions in the Danish Building legislation 
regarding energy consumption and transmission loss through the building envelope. The program consists of 
a main menu and a set of submodules corresponding to the different building systems: building envelope 
systems, windows, ventilation systems, heating systems and so forth. The ventilation sub-module is built in 
such a way, that it assumes a normal mixing ventilation system with radiators as heat source (Aggerholm, 
2016). SBi has published a guide, SBi 213 (Statens Byggeforskningsinstitut, 2014), which is the manual to the 
Be15 software.  
When operating a mechanical ventilation (VAV) system in practice, the volume flows and inlet air 
temperatures to the different zones vary throughout the hour, day, week and year, depending on the control 
method. In Be15, under the ventilation submodule, the volume flows and temperature are treated as 
constant values during the heating season and outside the heating season.

2.2.2.2 Be15: Other Energy-balance Relevant Inputs 
There is a series of other inputs in the Be15 software that influence the energy-balance calculation. 
The building’s thermal capacity is specified in the main menu in the software, along with the daily and weekly 
usage time. The usage time has a large influence on the internal gain and possible ventilation. The reason for 
this is that the internal gain for people, equipment and mechanical ventilation are directly calculated as 
monthly average based on the usage time. For dwellings, the relative usage time is equal to 1.0, but varies in 
non-residential buildings based on building type. However, the usage time in most office buildings is equal 
to 45 hours per week or from 8.00 to 17.00 in weekdays.  
The internal gain for occupants/people and equipment is specified in the internal heat supply sub-module. 
SBi 213 states that for non-residential houses default values of 4.0 W/m² and 6.0 W/m² of people and 
equipment gain should be used respectively, adding that other values might be used if these quantities differ 
remarkably from the default values. This might be the case in open offices, where the people density exceeds 
the default estimation.  
The artificial lighting is specified in the lighting sub-module. The artificial lighting has a large influence on the 
energy balance of the building, as the lighting is a casual or passive interior gain, which influences the cooling 
and heating load.  
Thus far, most of the inputs to Be15 have been described. The next subject to look into is how the inputs are 
used in the calculation of the heating and cooling need. The next sub-section includes a detailed description 
of the calculation of the energy balances, and also discusses the Be15 outputs and challenges regarding the 
Be15 calculation method.  
2.2.2.3 Be15: Program Engine, Heating/Cooling Calculation 
This sub-section describes the program engine in terms of the calculation of heating, cooling and ventilation 
need.  
Overall Description of Method 
The calculation of the heating/cooling demand and consumption is based on the international standard 
DS/EN ISO 13790 (Dansk Standard, 2008) using the Danish Design Reference Year (DRY) weather data (Danish 
Design Reference Year, 2010). The DRY weather file uses monthly totals and averages for quantities such as 
temperature, global radiance et cetera. There are no further specifications prescribed in SBi 213 regarding 
cooling need aside from the fact that the program takes into account possible solar shading and cooling, due 
to additional ventilation in hot summer periods in and outside the working hours.  
==PAGE:28==
 
Page | 27 
 
The Be15 software operates with single-zone models. This means in practice that the software calculates a 
total/combined building energy balance, even though the building is divided into several sub-zones in 
practice. 
DS/EN ISO 13790 suggests three basic types of methods to calculate the annual energy-consumption for 
heating and cooling: 
1. quasi-steady-state method 
- 
a monthly quasi-steady-state calculation method 
- 
a seasonal quasi-steady-state calculation method 
2. a fully prescribed simply hourly dynamic calculation method 
3. dynamic method 
The Be15 software uses the monthly quasi-steady-state calculation method, and this is the method that will 
be described (briefly) in this section. This will be coupled with a description of how the Be15 software 
interprets the method and procedure. The method and procedure are described in this thesis, as it has a 
large influence on the estimation of energy consumption. The analysis of Be15 results is strongly connected 
to a certain understanding of the calculation method.  
The following description of calculation of heating and cooling need is based on the Be05 Excel sheet, 
developed by SBi (attached as electronic material).  
Prior to the calculation of the energy consumption for cooling and heating there is a series of calculations 
that has to be made.  
The first step is to estimate the duration of the heating/cooling season. The duration of the heating/cooling 
season, or the fraction of the month that lies within respectively the heating or the cooling season, has a 
large influence on the energy calculation, as the fraction is/can be decisive for operation of the HVAC system.   
The second step is to calculate the utilization factors for gains and losses. This factor describe how big a part 
of the gains and losses that can be utilized to reduce the heating and cooling demand respectively.  
After the calculation of the heating/cooling season and the utilization factors for gains and losses, the 
monthly energy consumption for heating and cooling can be calculated.

Usage Factors for Cooling/Mechanical Ventilation Strategy 
The final usage factor of the different ventilation/cooling steps (forcing, night ventilation and mechanical 
cooling) is determined as the minimum value of (all dimensionless values): 
1. part/fraction of the month with temperatures exceeding the temperature setpoint of the different 
ventilation strategies calculated according to DS/EN 13790 7.4.1.2 Method b, in practice being the 
part/fraction of the month within the cooling season (calculation method described in the beginning 
of this section) 
 
2. part/fraction of the month with temperatures above the threshold (e.g. 23 ⁰C for forcing), calculated 
from the rise in degree hours above threshold with a temperature difference of 1 ⁰C divided by the 
total number of hours within the specific month 
 
3. if cooling need is higher than possible cooling capacity from ventilation/cooling strategy, this has a 
value of 1, if not, this factor is calculated from: cooling need/cooling capacity 
In most cases, the second usage factor described above is decisive for the final usage factor in the warmest 
months, as it in most cases, yields a lower value than the two other possible usage factors. The usage factor 
is calculated from the difference in degree hours above threshold for a rise in 0.1⁰C indoor temperature, 
summarized over 1⁰C divided by the total number of hours in the given month. The factor can be seen as the 
effective reduction in degree hours by having 1⁰C increased indoor temperature (expressed by a reduction in 
cooling need as ventilation loss increases with 1⁰C higher indoor temperature), divided by the total number 
of hours in the month.  
The first possible usage factor, the part/fraction of the month that lies within the cooling season, is calculated 
as described in Heating/Cooling Season Calculation. The third possible usage factor is of a logical nature, as 
it guarantees, that the volume flows are not unnecessarily high.  
The calculation of the usage factors has a very large influence on the annual energy consumption in the 
mechanical ventilation system. The usage factors combined with the related ventilation principle, 
minimum/maximum/night ventilation, and the SFP, give the monthly energy use in the ventilation plant.  
After the calculation steps described above has been conducted in the software, the final cooling calculation 
is conducted, calculating the mechanical cooling need (eq. 13). This means in practice that the buildings 
specific heat loss has increased notably in the months with the largest cooling need, since the volume flow is 
increased based on the usage factors calculation.

Now the heating- and cooling calculation in Be15 has been described. The next subject to look into is the 
criteria for method use prescribed by the DS/EN 13790 standard.  
Criteria for method use  
DS/EN 13790 prescribes the following criteria for single-zone calculation compared to multi-zone calculation 
of energy need for cooling/heating:  
 
a) Set point-temperatures for heating of the spaces (zones) differ by no more than 4 K. 
 
b) All zones are either mechanical cooled or has no mechanical cooling, and the cooling differ 
 
by no more than 4 K. 
 
c) All zones are serviced by the same heating/cooling system. 
 
d) If there is a ventilation system or systems operating in at least 80% of the floor areas. 
 
e) The amount of ventilation in the spaces differs by no more than a factor of 4 within 80% of 
 
the floor area, or the doors are likely to be frequently open. 
All the criteria listed above must be fulfilled, in order to simplify a multi-zone building into a single-zone 
building, when calculating the annual energy need for heating and cooling using the method presented in 
DS/EN 13790. The rules mean in practice that it is only meaningful to simplify the building into a single-zone 
model, when the conditions in the different zones in a given building are close to equal. In the Be15 software 
there is no limitations in ventilation inputs regarding the magnitude of the volume flows, which in many 
projects means that there is a larger difference in volume flows than a factor of 4 between the lowest/highest 
volume flow in a given building.   
Furthermore, the conditions for different zones in a building in Denmark can vary a lot; where south-oriented 
zones might have a cooling need in certain periods, this might not be the case for a north-oriented zone in 
the same building, which might instead have a need for heating. In a single-zone model, the relative need for 
heating and for cooling could therefore, in some situations/periods, be equal to zero, since the zones 
equalizes each other with positive and negative corresponding values. However, this problem could be solved 
by conduction several Be15 calculations where the building is divided into sub-zones with similar conditions; 
e.g. a zone consisting of the northern part of a building and a zone consisting of the southern part of a 
building. This specific example would result in two Be15 calculations.

Be15 software versus the Danish Building Legislation 
One remarkable discrepancy between the Be15 software cooling/ventilation calculation and the Danish 
Building Legislation is related to the amount of hours above a certain temperature threshold. 
The Be15 software calculates the total cooling need in order to keep the temperatures below 25⁰C. The 
Danish Building Legislation, on the other hand, has no specific requirements for temperatures in non-
residential buildings but states that the building owners can specify their own requirements for the thermal 
environment. In many projects, DS 474 is used as a guideline for the thermal indoor environments. DS 474 
requires a maximum of 100 hours above 26 ⁰C and 25 hours above 27⁰C annually. The fact that the Be15 
software always calculates with a maximum indoor temperature of 25⁰C results in higher energy 
consumption for ventilation and cooling compared to the actual situation.  
This 'locked' parameter regarding maximum indoor temperature is, like e.g. the prioritized ventilation 
strategy, an example of how the Be15 software has fixed conditions regarding the building’s use, which the 
engineers conducting the energy-frame calculation cannot change or adjust.  
As mentioned in the beginning of this section, the Be15 energy consumption calculation serves as a tool to 
describe a building independent of the building’s actual use. This should, in my opinion, result in much more 
simple calculations, and, additionally, much more simple calculation results. The Be15 result file has more 
than 180 results available for the building performance. The fact that there are so many detailed results, and 
that the calculation is rather comprehensive using the DS/EN 13790 standard, does not leave the impression 
of it being a simple tool.

2.2.3 IES-VE: Dynamic Simulation Program  
Virtual Environment, developed by IES, is a dynamic simulation program that is used widely in the building 
industry to calculate energy consumption, conduct detailed thermal indoor studies and much more. IES-VE 
is used for authority building approval in the United Kingdom.  
IES-VE is built up by a set of core modules, like the ModelIT where the building/zone geometry can be 
made/exported to (by e.g. most often a SketchUp model) and a series of sub-modules with different 
functions. Perhaps the most important sub-module in relation to the work of this thesis, is the ApacheHVAC 
module, where a detailed HVAC system can be developed and connected to one or more zones. This sub-
module is intended to be used by professionals, who have a high level of skills within the program. The 
controls for e.g. the volume flow or inlet temperature can be set according to IAQ or thermal indoor air 
conditions (and other values such as relative humidity etc.). This would in most projects result in two control 
strategies controlling the volume flow simultaneously.   
Example: if the dry-bulb temperature in the zone exceeds 24 ⁰C, then the inlet temperature 
should be 18 ⁰C and maximum volume flow rate AND/OR if the CO2 concentration exceeds 800 ppm but the 
dry-bulb temperature is below the threshold then the volume flow should also be at the maximum level.  
The example above is only one way of handling the control of the volume flows. Thus there can be many 
other strategies depending on the purpose and conditions in a given space or building e.g. if the most critical 
condition is the relative humidity or something similar. The controls for the volume flow can be proportional 
control or ON/OFF control. Figure 8 and Figure 9 show an example of proportional control and ON/OFF 
control respectively.

It is important to note that IES-VE does not give the actual energy performance, however, studies have shown

IES-VE calculates energy balances for each timestep, specified by the user, and operates with thermal mass

Regarding the convection from the room surfaces, several models can be used. The CIBSE Fixed Convection 
Coefficient is the default convection model type in the Apache simulation option, which operates with a 
constant convective coefficient.  
Other options are the CIBSE Variable Convection Coefficient, Alamdari & Hammond Convection Coefficients 
and the BS EN 15265 convective coefficient models. A common feature for them all is the adjustment of the 
convective coefficient depending on the surface orientation, air speed et cetera: all to a larger or lesser 
degree and with a varying detail level.

while the relative influence on which model to use when having a simple building geometry is less important. 
Within the work of this thesis, the CIBSE Fixed Convection Coefficient is used.  
There is a very large amount of output options from IES-VE. If a dynamic hourly simulation has been 
conducted, it is e.g. a possible to export results for the mechanical ventilation volume flows in the zone for 
every hour throughout the year. 
2.2.4 Be15 versus IES-VE: Differences 
Perhaps the most remarkable difference between the two software is the calculation method. The Be15 
software calculates with monthly average values for all factors, while IES-VE calculates with absolute values 
in the given timestep, specified by the user. Furthermore, IES-VE is operating with dynamic effects from e.g. 
the solar gain, where energy can be 'stored' in the constructions, with lesser or higher influence on the energy 
balance depending on construction materials. The fact that solar energy radiated at the exterior surfaces can 
be absorbed by the exterior construction, also has an influence on the energy balance, as the heat flow 
through conduction is influenced.  
The fact that monthly averages are used in the Be15 calculation makes the impact of certain days with 
extreme weather conditions less remarkable.

Regarding the controls in IES-VE for the mechanical ventilation system, the sensed value by the sensors is the 
dry-bulb temperature. In many non-residential buildings, it is the air/dry-bulb temperature that is sensed and 
for which the control is set up by. Operating based on the operative temperature would be more suitable 
from an occupant perspective, but it is very difficult to measure in practice and therefore the dry-bulb 
temperature is used in many cases. In IES-VE the space heating (radiators) and chilled ceilings (System 2 and 
System 3) are however controlled by the operative temperature.  
Description of which parameters to compare: boilers load, cooling load and ventilation plant load 
The boilers load, the cooling load and the ventilation plant load in the two programs will be compared. It is 
important to notice that the term load is used, and not the term energy. The term energy is used when 
looking at the energy-frame calculation, using primary energy factors and COP values in the cooling coil in 
relation to the individual loads. The three loads are explained further in the following.   
First, the term boilers load represents the heat demand in the HVAC system: the heat or energy delivered by 
the convector/radiator to the room to keep an indoor operative temperature of 20⁰C, and the energy 
delivered by the heating coil in the AHU to have an inlet temperature in the mechanical ventilation system 
of 18⁰C.  
Second, the term cooling load represents the energy needed to keep the indoor temperature below 25⁰C. 
The value represents two different things in the two programs: in Be15 the cooling load is the additional 
cooling energy that is needed to cool the internal temperature to 25⁰C after cooling with the mechanical 
ventilation system, whereas the cooling load in IES-VE is the cooling needed in the zone to cool the inlet 
temperature down to 18⁰C.  
Third, the ventilation plant load is the energy used by the fan in the AHU to deliver the required volume 
flow. The ventilation system 'effectiveness' is represented by the SFP value that is kept constant in the two 
software.  
In addition, the volume flow within working hours is presented.

estimation, one strategy could be to lower the maximum ventilation input in Be15. Using this strategy, the 
prediction is that the cooling load is increased, as there is no change in internal gain, yielding a constant total 
need for cooling. This way the energy needed to keep the temperature below 25⁰C is 'pushed' or 'transferred' 
into the cooling load part of the energy consumption. The advantage of the strategy is that when the COP 
value is taken into consideration in the cooling system, the total energy consumption is lowered using 
primary energy factors.  
How much should the maximum ventilation rate be lowered? One possibility could be to look into the 
average ventilation rate during the warmest months, e.g. July and August using the IES-VE results. July and 
August are the months suggested by SBi to be the months to use when estimating average volume flow in 
the summer period.

the higher estimation, of which the most important has to do with to the difference in calculation method. 
The calculation method used in Be15, using monthly averages for e.g. exterior temperatures, results in a 
higher transmission loss. Other noticeable calculation method differences are: solar absorption in IES-VE, 
radiant quantities in IES-VE (influencing the heat-flow in constructions), thermal mass in IES-VE, and not to 
forget the calculation in Be15 of the utilization factors for gains and losses.

TABS zone modeling 
There is no direct way to model TABS in IES-VE, so an alternative method to model TABS is used. The 
procedure to model TABS in IES-VE is by creating conditioned zones adjacent to the model/zone with the 
same thermal properties corresponding to a TABS system. Idealized radiator and chilled ceiling systems is 
connected to the TABS ‘zone’ with a radiant fraction of 99.0%. In practice that means that all energy is 
transferred to the conditioned zone through conduction in the internal floor construction of the TABS zone. 
Only the TABS zone adjacent to the ceiling of the zone is modeled. The method for this approach is a radiant 
slab modeling method suggested by IES-VE (IES, 2015).

and Cp=900 m²K/W. The internal convection model is set according to Alamdari & Hammond, which is 
available in the IES-VE software.

3.4.5 Tendencies in the Studies: System 1, System 2 & System 3

estimating a higher boilers and cooling load, and when having cooling only through the mechanical

What happens when we increase the number of zones in IES-VE and conduct the same comparison? Will the 
boilers and cooling load difference be larger? What about the ventilation plant load? Most likely, a higher 
ventilation rate is required to maintain indoor operative temperatures below 25⁰C when having sub-zones 
within the building. Having higher volume flow rates in Be15, enhances the use of the mechanical ventilation 
system to 'cool' instead of the mechanical cooling in the software. Therefore when having a larger volume 
flow, compared to the same model with the same building properties, with lesser volume flow available, it 
would be expected that the mechanical cooling load would be reduced.

3.6 Additional utilization of IES-VE results: average SFP 
One parameter, which is simplified within the investigations conducted, is the SFP value. The SFP value has a 
large influence on the energy consumption, especially in buildings with large volume flows.  
Usually, a value of around 1500 J/m³ is used in the energy frame calculation. The maximum SFP value for VAV 
is 1800 J/m³. In the following two strategies to determine the average annual SFP using IES-VE output are 
presented and (briefly) described.  
Strategy 1 
If specific SFP values are available for the volume flow, e.g. having at optimum at 80.0% capacity, this can be 
included in the IES-VE model, in the ApacheHVAC setup of the fans. By integrating the actual performance 
curve of the fan in the IES-VE model, post-simulation calculation of the average SFP can be conducted. By 
having the average annual SFP, it can be included in the Be15 calculation instead of the maximum possible 
SFP. 
Strategy 2 
The second proposed strategy to determine an average SFP value based on IES-VE results, is by volume flow 
level time averaging, assuming SFP values for percentage of maximum flow. By having hourly results for the 
volume flow, the following methodology can be used: 
 
Example: Total mechanical ventilation operation time = 200 hours. Mechanical ventilation is 
running; 100 hours with 100 l/s (50% of the maximum flow) (assuming SFP=1000 J/m³) and 100 hours with 
200 l/s (100% of the maximum flow) (assuming SFP=1500 J/m³) Then the  average SFP is 1250 J/m³.  
Using either strategy 1 or strategy 2, the SFP input to Be15 is determined based on calculations rather than 
assumptions.

4. Discussion  
The purpose of the thesis was to analyze, compare, and, if possible, investigate how to utilize dynamic 
simulation results in the energy frame calculation. The approach was to analyze a set of commonly used 
HVAC systems, and by understanding the Be15 software 'core' discuss the (possible) reasons for the 
differences between Be15 and IES-VE loads estimation.  
The initial description and discussion of the Be15 software core, addressed a set of inputs and program 
calculation method parameters, that had a large influence on the final calculation of boilers and cooling load. 
This was primarily the fact that Be15 uses monthly averages and calculates monthly balances, making specific 
days with extreme exterior weather less remarkable. Regarding the inputs, the building inertia is an input, 
which has a large influence on the calculation of the utilization factors, and through this ultimately also the 
boilers and cooling load.  
Regarding the Be15 calculation core, the default values e.g. for the reference time constant and the 
dimensionless reference numerical parameter have values differing much from the DS/EN 13790 default 
values. The program default values are rather conservative, yielding utilization factors for losses and gains, 
lower than what would be estimated using the DS/EN 13790 default values. However, the Be15 default values 
are of empirical origin and it is therefore difficult to know exactly how suitable these values are for the 
building mass in Denmark.  
Based on the analysis of the calculation results from the two programs, it seem that Be15 estimates a higher 
boilers- and cooling load with all system types, except the multi-zone comparison, where IES-VE calculated a 
higher cooling load. Regarding the ventilation plant load, the studies have shown that Be15 calculates a 
higher load in all months outside the heating season, when having cooling only through the mechanical 
ventilation system. However, when having additional cooling through either a TABS system or a chilled 
ceiling, the annual ventilation plant load is close to equal in the two programs.  
The studies have shown that when having a cooling system relying only on mechanical ventilation, IES-VE 
volume flow results can be used and utilized in the Be15 calculation. By utilizing IES-VE results for average 
volume flow in the energy frame calculation, the gap between the ventilation plant load estimation of the 
two programs, on an annual basis, almost disappears.  
The analysis suggests that the method for utilization of IES-VE results in the Be15 calculation is only applicable 
when having cooling with a VAV mechanical ventilation system.  
Furthermore, when taking COP value of the cooling coil and primary energy factors for electricity into 
consideration, the energy frame can be lowered by 1.7-4.7 kWh/m² by reducing the ventilation plant load in 
==PAGE:102==
 
Page | 101 
 
Be15. These results are based on a set of assumptions, e.g. the COP value in the cooling coil, which in the 
analysis, was assumed to have a value of 5.0.  In order to quantify the potential 'saving' in the energy frame 
calculation using IES-VE results, it would be necessary to make additional studies with more complex model 
geometry.  
How valid are these results? It is obvious that the results and findings within this thesis, are to be seen in 
relation to the assumptions on for which the analysis is based. What if a much more complex, multi-zone, 
model geometry, was analyzed? How would Be15 calculate with own shadows from a complex building 
geometry; would it result in more or less annual shading estimated by Be15 compared IES-VE? Currently, 
these considerations have not been analyzed, and it is therefore difficult to assess whether or not the findings 
in the thesis is of general nature or only partly applicable in more complex system setups and geometries.  
Tendency in the building industry 
The rising need for the building owners to estimate the buildings actual energy consumption has led to the 
development of BeREAL. The purpose of BeREAL is to give a more realistic estimation of the energy 
consumption, by giving an interval of the estimation of the energy consumption. The interval is calculated 
using statistics and working with best and worst guesses on e.g. the building envelope U-value or mechanical 
ventilation rates. The core of the calculation is still the Be15 program engine, since Be15 is an input to the 
tool.  
Bearing in mind that the software still uses the Be15 calculation core, the intention still is to give a more 
realistic estimation of the buildings energy consumption. The advantages of BeREAL is that it operates with 
the best and worst guesses, whereas a concern is that it still gives an unrealistic estimation due to the fact 
that it uses the Be15 calculation core. 
In more and more projects, the building owners require consultants to give an estimation of the buildings 
actual energy consumption along with the mandatory Be15 calculation. This is where dynamic simulation 
tools, e.g. IES-VE, come into play. In the future, the key to success is using the dynamic simulation programs 
intelligently in the different building project phases.  
Future work 
Additional studies could be conducted in order to address the exact influence of several building parameters 
in the energy frame calculation in Be15, compared to the corresponding influence of the parameters in IES-
VE.  
==PAGE:103==
 
Page | 102 
 
The additional analysis and studies could be conducted with step-by-step increments in parameters, e.g. 
starting with analyzing a model with only the building envelope without windows. The next parameter to 
compare could be the windows, and after this e.g. internal load in the form of people, then adding artificial 
lighting and so on. One very interesting parameter to study in relation to this methodology, is the influence 
of the magnitude of the building's inertia on the load estimation. If this study was conducted, the influence 
of the different parameters could be studied more in detail.  
In many building projects, compliance with the energy frame is achieved by adding solar cells in the late 
project phases. A future research topic could therefore be to investigate, if the amount of solar cells could 
be reduced using the methodology presented within the thesis, regarding utilization of IES-VE results. A case 
study could be conducted on an office building with cooling purely by mechanical ventilation.

5. Conclusion 
The analysis of System 1, with cooling purely through the mechanical ventilation system, showed that Be15 
estimates a higher boilers-, cooling- and especially ventilation plant load compared to IES-VE. The study 
showed that IES-VE results for the average volume flow in the warmest months can be utilized in the Be15 
calculation, conducting a correlated Be15 calculation, lowering the difference in ventilation plant load 
estimation by the two programs. With the correlated Be15 inputs and calculation, the cooling load increases, 
increasing the difference between Be15 and IES-VE cooling load estimation. However, when taking COP 
values and primary energy factors into consideration, the study showed that the energy frame could be 
lowered by 1.6 kWh/m² annually.  
The study of System 2 and System 3, having cooling partly through the mechanical ventilation system, and 
partly through a chilled ceiling and a TABS system respectively, showed, once again, that Be15 estimates a 
higher boilers and cooling load than IES-VE. However, the estimation of ventilation plant load were fairly 
equal annually for the mentioned systems. This finding suggests that when having additional cooling though 
other systems than the mechanical ventilation system, the annual ventilation plant load is estimated fairly 
equally in the two programs. 
Finally, the study of the multi-zone model, using System 1 HVAC strategy, showed that Be15 estimates a 
higher boilers-, cooling- and ventilation plant load compared to IES-VE. Having a multi-zone model results in 
the same tendencies for the ventilation plant load, as was the case for the single-zone comparison and study 
of System 1. As for System 1, IES-VE results for the average volume flow in the warmest months can be 
utilized in the Be15 calculation in order to lower the difference of the ventilation plant load estimation.  
The principal tendency was in all four studies that Be15 estimates a higher boilers-, cooling- and ventilation 
plant load if cooling is purely by mechanical ventilation. If having additional cooling, in this case chilled ceilings 
or a TABS system, the ventilation plant load estimation is fairly equal. The differences for the boilers and 
cooling load estimation is due to several factors, most importantly being the difference in calculation method.
