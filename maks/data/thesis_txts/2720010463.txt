A B S T R A C T
Ideal daylighting should meet both visual and non-visual lighting needs. Balancing these demands is challenging
as high levels of corneal illuminance are needed for circadian entrainments. At the same time, a desirable
view-out often requires more oversized windows, which can result in uncomfortable glare. A holistic evaluation
requires a multi-criteria assessment, and therefore this thesis aims to combine visual and non-visual potentials in
a simulation workflow.
The project uses a human-centered approach to evaluate daylight performance in buildings, predicting human
responses to light in indoor environments. A simulation workflow was created based on a literature review of
daylight’s impact on visual and non-visual aspects, including metrics for circadian-effective daylight, view-
out, and glare protection. The simulation featured a dynamic photometric view-out model developed through
linear mixed-effects modeling with experimental data from the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The
simulation’s accuracy was tested through a validation pilot study at DTU SMART Library by comparing its
predictions to responses collected in different locations.
The study found that the established photometric models did not accurately predict the view-out responses made
by subjects, as shown by a low correlation of 0.12 in a scatterplot. However, analysis of variance (ANOVA)
showed that photometric variables background luminance, maximum luminance of the brightest spot, average
luminance, and complexity were significant for the assessment of view-out. However, the study required larger
sample size and more experiments in controlled settings to be able to draw definitive conclusions.
The simulation’s parallel structure was synthesized in a visual potential, showing an optimized daylight assess-
ment for a point in space considering occupant well-being and productivity. The visual potential offered a more
personalized evaluation and insight into performance trade-offs, which enables informed decision-making.

P R O B L E M S TAT E M E N T & O B J E C T I V E S
The subject of daylight in buildings encompasses a broad range of research that links architectural design and
construction engineering, considering human behavior and physiology. In the past, energy issues were the main
driving force behind research in this area, as the focus was on reducing reliance on electric lighting through
increased use of natural light. However, it is crucial to remember that buildings serve as a place of residence for
people, and therefore, the priority should be creating an environment that promotes comfort and health.
“This is where daylight can have most impact. Good daylighting might save a few percent in terms of energy
costs, but what proportion of the overall building cost does this represent? Almost nothing. In terms of wellbeing,
productivity and health, however, daylight can make a huge difference.”
Marilyne Andersen
The discovery of a new photoreceptor in the human retina in 2002 (Berson et al., 2002) has led to growing
concern about the non-visual impact of daylight on human health, especially the circadian rhythm regulating
daily hormones and functions. Research has revealed the connection between daylight exposure and increased
alertness in humans (Cajochen, 2007).
Windows are a crucial means of connecting to the outdoors, especially since people spend most of their time
indoors. Views from windows have been found to have a positive impact on individuals’ physiology and
psychology (Beute & de Kort, 2014). Given that people spend nearly 90% of their time indoors, creating a
healthy indoor environment is crucial. As such, there is a need for a human-centered approach to daylight design
that prioritizes health and comfort by considering the impact of light on physiology and psychology.
However, it is crucial to maintain visual comfort while exploiting the numerous benefits of daylight - large
windows can lead to discomfort from glare, so a balanced approach is necessary. A holistic design should
consider multiple evaluation criteria to make the most of daylight’s advantages without causing inconvenience
for its occupants.

V I S UA L E N V I RO N M E N T
The concept of comfort is directly associated with spaces that create physical and mental well-being. In
architecture, it usually refers to ambient qualities such as temperature (thermal) and noise levels (acoustics).
However, it is equally important to maintain visual comfort when designing buildings that promote occupant
wellness (see Figure 1).
Figure 1: Visual Comfort, made with inspiration from (Wienold, 2020)
Visual comfort is, in its definition, ambiguous as it is not directly observable. It is best defined as the absence
of visual discomfort (Boyce, 2014). Several coexisting variables influence visual comfort. These interrelated
variables can affect us both psychologically and physiologically (Edwards & Torcellini, 2002); for instance,
proper lighting can improve productivity, focus, and alertness (Cajochen, 2007), visual acuity (Berman et
al., 2006), and mood (K¨uller et al., 2006). Conversely, poor lighting conditions interfere with vision and
influence performance (visual system), health (circadian system), and personal well-being (perceptual system)
5
==PAGE:17==
2.2 C I R C A D I A N- E F F E C T I V E DAY L I G H T P ROV I S I O N
6
as well. Symptoms can include eye irritation, fatigue, and headaches due to unnecessary eye strain (Velux, n.d.).
Eventually, this can lead to mood declines and depression (Abbott et al., 2020) (Germain & Kupfer, 2008).
Defining visual comfort and how it can be achieved is a complex task. Visual comfort’s primary goal is
to optimize daylight availability in a building without causing visual discomfort and to use artificial light
appropriately. It not only improves human performance but also saves energy by reducing the need for electrical
lighting, enhances human health, and improves the comfort of a room. In this thesis, visual comfort is defined as
providing circadian-effective daylight, access to quality views from within the building, and protection from
glare. These factors are considered essential in enhancing well-being and comfort within the scope of this study.
Additionally, this study adopts a human-centered approach, considering the needs and preferences of building
occupants. By balancing visual comfort with a human-centered approach, the goal is to create a visually pleasing
and comfortable environment that promotes well-being and productivity.
2.2
C I R C A D I A N- E F F E C T I V E DAY L I G H T P ROV I S I O N
Developed to meet our visual needs, standards and practices within daylight design are ahead of our scientific
understanding of how light regulates various bodily functions. The human eye is not just for seeing - it contains
a third type of photoreceptor, referred to as Intrinsically Photosensitive Retinal Ganglion Cells (ipRGCs)
(Hattar et al., 2002), that serves as an essential input in synchronizing the human circadian rhythms to the daily
24-hour cycle of light and dark. ipRGCs are unique because they can send signals through a photopigment
called melanopsin. Melanopsin is light-sensitive to short wavelengths peaking in the blue region of the visible
spectrum (490 nm). In contrast, the visual system, mediated by photoreceptors rod and cone cells, is most
sensitive to wavelengths peaking in the green region of the visible spectrum (555 nm). Thus, the description of
circadian-effective daylight needs to be more adequate in terms of quantities and units dependent on the visual
system (Thapan et al., 2001).
Figure 2 shows three different spectral curves; the CIE photopic luminous efficiency function (CIE 018.2-1983,
1983), the melanopic luminous efficiency function as proposed by (Lucas et al., 2014), and CIE standard
illuminant D65 (CIE, 1986). The photopic curve (green) represents the average spectral sensitivity of human
visual perception, whereas the melanopic curve (blue) represents the average spectral sensitivity of ipRGCs.
In addition to the differences between the two curves, the benefits of daylight can also be observed. The D65
(yellow) is intended to represent average daylight and has a correlated color temperature of 6500 K. The D65
has high intensity and a broad spectrum. Moreover, the high intensities peak near the melanopic curve.

2.2 C I R C A D I A N- E F F E C T I V E DAY L I G H T P ROV I S I O N

The daily cycle of day and night is influential in maintaining 24-hour rhythms in many aspects of our physiology,
metabolism, and behavior. These rhythms are controlled by a timing system inside the body, which makes it
possible to anticipate and adapt to recurring phases and changes in environmental conditions. The internal timing
system follows a rhythm that is not precisely 24 hours long and is also called the ’circadian system’, after the
Latin words circa (about) and dies (day). Consequently, a human in constant darkness (the free-running rhythm)
will have a deviation from the 24-hour cycle in his or her behavior and physiology (Fahrenkrug, 2018).
Hormone secretion, sleep/wake cycle, heart rate, body temperature, and many more are among the body functions
controlled by the circadian system. Essentially, the circadian system instructs the body to perform the right
actions at the appropriate times, and daylight is the most powerful cue to synchronize these rhythms with the
external environment. Desynchronization of the circadian system can cause jet lag, with prolonged insufficient
and mistimed light it can have consequences for our performance and health (Lockley, 2009).
Figure 3: Overview: Daylight’s influence on the human circadian system
The most critical component of the circadian system is the ’circadian pacemaker’ which is also called the ’master
clock’ and is located in the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus. As established now, the eye is
a dual light detector - when light hits the retina, rod and cone cells respond to it to convey the sense of sight.
This is referred to as a visual effect. Parallel to this, ipRGCs is activated, which transmits signals directly to
the SCN and other brain regions and mediates the non-visual effects. The SCN then plays a role in signaling to
other brain areas, such as the pineal gland and ventrolateral preoptic nucleus (VLPO). VLPO is essential in the
control of the sleep–wake rhythm (Saper et al., 2005), whereas the pineal gland plays a role in the timing of the
circadian system. Since melatonin is a sleep hormone, it is secreted by the pineal gland during sleep. Darkness
prevents signaling between the SCN and the pineal gland, which is responsible for melatonin secretion and
makes us sleepy. However, if there is light, the activity between the SCN and the pineal gland will increase and
dampen the release of melatonin (Te Kulve et al., 2016). Figure 3 shows a schematic overview of the circadian
system and the light factors that affect it.
2.2.1
Key Indicators
According to (Lockley, 2009), five light factors affect our circadian system: timing, intensity, duration, spectrum,
and previous exposure to light, as depicted in Figure 3.
==PAGE:19==
2.2 C I R C A D I A N- E F F E C T I V E DAY L I G H T P ROV I S I O N
8
Timing
The internal clock has a period-limited sensitivity to light. It is known as phase delay and phase advance. Light
exposure in the morning is more effective at resetting the circadian system than light exposure in the evening
(Lockley, 2009).
Light Intensity & Duration
The intensity also affects the circadian system. The circadian rhythm is better regulated by bright light than by
dim light. In addition, the duration of light exposure also influences the circadian system. Extended periods
of light exposure are more effective at resetting the circadian rhythm than shorter periods. However, there is a
non-linear relationship between intensity, duration, and the magnitude of circadian rhythm resetting, as shown in
(Lockley, 2009).
Spectrum
It is also essential to consider wavelength, as ipRGCs signal through melanopsin, whose spectral sensitivity
is primarily bluish. It is why short-wavelength light is most effective in stimulating the circadian system.
Long-wavelength light gives, on the other hand, minimal circadian stimulus and therefore suppresses only some
melatonin. Long-wavelength light is, therefore, helpful in promoting alertness in the late afternoon and evening
without disrupting the circadian system (Lighting Research Center, 2020b).
History
The circadian system shows adaptation to previous light exposure in determining response to a current stimulus.
Researchers (Lockley, 2009) found that subjects exposed to 200 lux for 6.5 h prior to a study showed significantly
greater suppression of melatonin than those exposed to 200 lux for 15 h preceding the study.

developed by (Lucas et al., 2014). mel-EDI is based on standard daylight D65 as a reference source and
expresses the quantity of daylight that provides the same melanopic irradiance as the test source. The CIE
recommends high levels of mel-EDI during the day and low levels of mel-EDI in the evening and night; however,
they do not specify any thresholds. In (Brown et al., 2020), 250 mel-EDI is proposed throughout the day. A
maximum mel-EDI of 10 at least 3 hours before bedtime while only 1 mel-EDI during sleep. The assessment
of circadian-effective daylight is a complex task involving measuring different light attributes, such as timing,
intensity, duration, spectrum, and history. One limitation of this assessment is the need for more standardization,
as there is currently no widely accepted method for measuring and evaluating circadian-effective daylight,
making comparing results across different studies and research challenging. Despite these limitations, the field is
constantly evolving, with ongoing research and advancements in technology aimed at improving the assessment
of circadian-effective daylight.
2.2.3
Multi-Spectral Simulations
Simulating daylight can be a time-consuming process if calculations have to be done for every wavelength in
the visible spectrum. In order to simplify this, a tristimulus color space is commonly used, which defines and
calculates spectral information about light and materials using RGB data. This simplification is possible because
humans have trichromatic vision, meaning we perceive color through three channels. However, to accurately
simulate non-visual effects, calculations should be done through multiple channels to get an accurate result.
Several tools are available for simulating light’s non-visual effects, such as Lark Spectral Lighting (Inanici &
ZGF Architects LLP, 2015) and Adaptive Lighting for Alertness (ALFA) (Solemma & Alertness, 2018). Lark
Spectral Lighting is an open-source tool, while ALFA is a commercialized version. Both tools use an N-step
method (Ruppertsberg & Bloj, 2006), where the spectrum is divided into N consecutive wavebands, and a
simulation is performed in each of these channels rather than the standard single RGB simulation (see Figure 4.
This approach increases the accuracy of the simulation and allows for a better representation of the non-visual
effects of light.

2.3
A S S E S S M E N T F O R V I E W- O U T
Research has shown that view-out has several positive effects on people, such as health, well-being (Beute
& de Kort, 2014), emotion (Ko et al., 2020), cognitive performance (Boubekri et al., 2020), environmental
satisfaction (Yildirim et al., 2007), reduced discomfort (Aries et al., 2010), and stress recovery (Ulrich, 1984).
However, there need to be more design guidelines for assessing a view-out and a better agreement on a unified
definition of the quality of a view-out. Thus, (Ko et al., 2022) defines the whole quality of a view out a window
as a combination of content, access, and clarity, as seen in Figure 5. A combination of building standards,
green certification systems, and scientific literature has been used to conduct the research. The categories and
assessment tools will be elaborated on in the following sections.
Figure 5: Definition of a quality view-out based on three key indicators
2.3.1
View Content
View content refers to the collection of visual features visible in a window view, for instance, natural or urban
features or the sky. The following describes the subcategories that define the view content.
Nature and urban features Views of nature as green elements are generally favorable and are often desired by
its occupants (Matusiak & Kl¨ockner, 2016). In addition to nature, water is also a favorable element (Kfir et al.,
2002). Both nature and water elements are also features that can increase the value of a property. Natural views
are generally more favorable than urban views, but urban views with landmarks are a desired feature (Baranzini
& Schaerer, 2011). However, an urban view increases desirability when natural green or water is visible. Age,
maintenance, and complexity generally influence how the urban view is perceived.
Horizontal stratification Horizontal stratification divides the view into different layers of information. It usually
contains ground, landscape, and sky. The ground layer gives cues of the floor, such as roads, whereas the
landscape layer gives cues of the surrounding land, and the sky gives cues of time of day and weather. The more
layers visible in a view, the more information is provided, which is preferred by occupants (Markus, 1967).
==PAGE:22==
2.3 A S S E S S M E N T F O R V I E W- O U T
11
Content distance The distance between the window and the viewed objects can also affect the quality of the
view. (Ne’Eman & Hopkinson, 1970) showed, e.g., that content far from the observer can relieve eye strain -
which can relax the eyes in offices where people are focused on computer screens for long periods. (M. Kent &
Schiavon, 2020) showed that this only primarily applies to urban features, not nature.
Dynamic features Dynamic features capture human attention, as shown in (Orquin & Mueller Loose, 2013).
This also includes changes that occur naturally at the time of year or day, such as daylight patterns and seasonal
changes in greenery. These features make occupants aware of their surroundings and connect to the outside
world. However, excessive dynamism defined by scale, frequency, or periodicity may negatively impact the
occupants (Ko et al., 2022). Thus, the presence of traffic near windows and nearby buildings reduces the quality
of the view outside (Hellinga, 2013).
2.3.2
View Access
View access refers to the amount of view an occupant can see from the viewing position. It primarily depends on
the geometric relationships between the occupant and the window. The following describes the subcategories
that define the view access.
View angle of a window The view angle refers to the angle from the view position to the vertical and horizontal
perimeters of the window. Preferred view angles depend on a number of factors, including the distance to
visual features in the view. For instance, the minimum acceptable view angle tends to be smaller in distant
views as large features appear smaller from a far distance (Ne’Eman & Hopkinson, 1970). (Keighley, 1973a)
demonstrated that the ideal window size was affected by the horizontal stratification of the content, where the
location of the skyline in the view impacted the desired height of the window and its frame.
Distance from the window The occupant’s position in the room and the distance to the window significantly
affect the angle of the window view. Thus, the distance from the window also influences the view satisfaction
(Keighley, 1973a).
Spatial assessment of view The spatial assessment of view-out is defined by the percentage of relevant floor
area related to the view-out of a specific window. A spatial assessment method is necessary to estimate a space’s
aggregated view access since the available view depends on occupant position. However, these methods should
be used in conjunction with a specification of a view-angle that specifies how much is enough visual contact (Ko
et al., 2022).
2.3.3
View Clarity
View clarity refers to how clearly the viewer can perceive the content in the window. It depends on the design
and properties of the glazing and shading systems. The following describes the subcategories that define view
clarity.
Window design Window frames and mullions can also affect the perception of a view-out. A window frame
can, for instance, block the view and shift the boundary of visible elements. It has been shown that a window
frame’s horizontal and vertical proportions vary based on the content of the view (Ne’Eman & Hopkinson,
1970). A horizontal mullion distorts the clarity of visual content from certain positions, whereas a vertical
mullion somewhat retains the clarity of visual content due to the visual parallax effect. There is a preference
for a minimum amount and width of mullions since content and access can influence the clarity of perception
(Keighley, 1973b).
Glazing and shading materials Optical properties can affect the clarity of the view content in the window.
Glazing and shading materials are often designed to reduce the risk of glare and overheating or obtain privacy.
==PAGE:23==
2.3 A S S E S S M E N T F O R V I E W- O U T
12
However, they can also affect the perception of a view-out. Tinted glass can, e.g., affect the colors in the view
and thereby also the contrast sensitivity and color perception (Ko et al., 2022).
Temporal attributes of view clarity Glare control can sometimes conflict with view clarity, as shades that allow
view clarity may not prevent glare. Therefore, it is essential to consider the temporal (duration and frequency)
and compositional (level of obstruction caused by the shading) aspects of changes in view clarity. Therefore, the
shading type should be chosen based on how many hours it is used to achieve the best possible view quality (Ko

Luminous and Visual Stimuli
Understanding the view-out and how the human visual system perceives it is equally essential to understanding
its characteristics. The assessment of human perception of a scene has evolved over the years, leading to the
development of several methods using photometric values and luminous images. Globally or locally, these
methods use the luminous contrast in the field of view as an indicator - the assessment methods described in the
following cover contrast and visual complexity.
Contrast
Contrast is a crucial aspect of visual perception, as it helps the human visual system to detect and process
details in the visual field, such as edges, shapes, and textures. The human visual system is highly sensitive to
luminance contrast, which is the difference in brightness between two or more elements in an image or scene.
High luminance contrast is more easily detected and distinguished than low luminance contrast. Therefore
contrast has been used as an essential indicator in assessing digital image quality and implemented in indoor
lighting conditions evaluations.
Contrast methods can be divided into two categories: global contrast and local contrast. Global contrast refers to
the overall contrast of an image or scene. It is calculated by taking the ratio of the maximum luminance to the
minimum luminance in the image (Simone et al., 2012). Root Mean Square (RMS) contrast measures the global
contrast in an image or scene. It is defined as the standard deviation of the luminance values of the pixels in the
image, divided by the mean luminance value (Pavel et al., 1987). The equation can be seen below:

2.4.1
What is glare?
Glare is defined by the European Standard 17037 (EN17037, 2021) as ”A negative sensation and the cause is
bright areas with sufficiently greater luminance than the luminance to which the eyes are adapted to, producing
annoyance, discomfort or loss in visual performance and visibility”. Glare generally falls into three categories:
disability glare, discomfort glare, and glare reflections.
Disability glare
Disability glare reduces the visibility of the visual task or visual acuity. This means the observer can no longer
distinguish details of objects or people in his or her field of vision. Physiologically, this type of glare results from
a loss of contrast in retinal images caused by intraocular light scattering (Putnam., 2017). It can occur in office
spaces when the sun or specular reflections of the sun can be seen. When disability glare occurs, discomfort
glare will already be reported under less bright situations. On the other hand, this means that when the light level
is comfortable, disability glare will not be reported (Wienold, 2009a).
Discomfort glare
Discomfort glare can distract or bother the observer without necessarily impairing the vision of objects or people.
Nevertheless, it can lead to side effects like visual fatigue and migraines in the long term (Osterhaus, 2009). It is
difficult to fully understand the effects of discomfort glare as it is a subjective phenomenon, and the physiological
and psychological mechanisms involved need to be better understood (M. G. Kent et al., 2017). Therefore,
discomfort glare is the subject of many studies in contrast to disability glare which is more quantifiable.
There are two causes of discomfort glare, namely, ’absolute’ glare, or the saturation effect, and ’relative’ glare, or
the contrast effect. Absolute glare occurs when the amount of light reaching the observer’s eyes is too high and
causes discomfort because of saturation. This means that even if the object were viewed under proper contrast to
the background, the observer would feel discomfort as the person’s eyes could not adapt to the light. On the
other hand, relative glare occurs when there is a high contrast between the direction the observer is looking and a
bright spot in the field of view. This can cause discomfort because the eyes may adjust to the low luminance of
the visual task, resulting in excessive glare, or they may adjust to a bright environment, making the visual task
appear dim (Pierson et al., 2018).
Glare reflections
Glare reflections are unwanted reflections of incident light on surfaces, such as screens, that obscure the details
to be seen on the surface by reducing contrast.
2.4.2
Characteristics of Glare
A key parameter involved in determining glare is luminance. Luminance is a physical quantity that closely
resembles the visual perception of light. It refers to the luminous intensity from a point on a given surface in a
given direction and is measured in candela per m2. The light can either come from a light source (e.g., the sun
or a light fixture) or be reflected directly by a surface. In the latter case, it depends on the illuminance on the
surface and its reflective properties. In contrast, illuminance is a quantity of how much light falls on a given area
(measured in lux) (Boyce, 2003) as exemplified in Figure 8.

The feeling of discomfort arises when the luminance in a part of the visual field is greater than the adaptation
luminance, which is the luminance the visual system has adapted to at a given time. A significant or rapid change
in lighting conditions requires the eye to readjust. The same applies to large variations in luminance within the
scene. This constant readjustment is a source of discomfort and fatigue for the eyes (Osterhaus, 2009).
A source of glare’s position to the observer is also essential for the perception of glare. Generally, the closer the
glare source is to the line of vision, the more intense it is. Another significant factor that affects glare perception
is the apparent size of the source. The position of the observer in a space directly influences these parameters
(Paupardin, 2021). As a result, the design of interior spaces plays a significant role in preventing glare.

2.4.4
Types of Assessments
The British Glare Index (BGI) is developed from studies of small, uniform light sources by (Petherbridge &
Hopkinson, 1950). However, it is not suitable for evaluating large non-uniform sources of light, such as daylight.
It does not consider the adaptation effect (Chauvel et al., 1982). Furthermore, it needs to be improved in its
assessment of multiple glare sources. The Visual Comfort Probability (VCP) method was also developed by
(IES, 1993) from experimental studies of uniform light sources. The metric provides a unit-less index of visual
comfort in terms of the percentage of people who will consider a given lighting system acceptable (Wienold,
2009a). However, it does not apply to very small, very large, or non-uniform glare sources (IES, 1993). The
CIE aimed to combine the best aspects of available discomfort metrics and create a simple system that could be
accepted internationally. The CIE Glare Index (CGI) was developed to address the mathematical inconsistencies
in the BGI metric (Einhorn, 1979). It considers the observer’s visual adaptation and includes vertical and direct
illuminance (Ed and Ev). (Iwata et al., 1990), however, showed that the metric overestimates glare under large
artificial sources. The CIE then combined the BGI, VCP, and CGI methods to create the Unified Glare Rating
(UGR) (CIE, 1995), which includes background luminance (Lb) as adaption instead of illuminance. Common to
all the assessment forms in Table 4 is that they are only applicable under artificial lighting sources.
Table 5 presents applicable assessments under daylight. . The Daylight Glare Index (DGI) is a metric developed
for predicting daylight glare based on experiments using artificial light sources (Hopkinson, 1972). However,
validation studies have shown that the correlation between actual glare from windows and the predicted glare
using this index is not as strong as with artificial lighting (Chauvel et al., 1982), (Boubekri & Boyer, 1992).
Additionally, DGI is not applicable when the window is parallel to the view direction, and it has been shown to
predict higher values than are perceived under daylight conditions (Boubekri & Boyer, 1992). The Predicted
Glare Sensation Vote (PGSV) is another metric designed to predict daylight glare, which was developed using
experimental testing with simulated windows (Tokura et al., 1996). The index was created to address some of the
Daylight Glare Index’s (DGI) limitations. However, PGSV does not consider a position index and only applies
when evaluating glare from windows in the viewer’s line of sight.
The Daylight Glare Probability (DGP) index, developed by (Wienold & Christoffersen, 2005), and validated
by (Wienold & Christoffersen, 2006), is based on a statistical analysis of full-scaled office mock-ups involving
more than 70 participants. It considers the vertical illuminance reaching the eyes, which sets it apart from the
Daylight Glare Index (DGI). Its been considered the most accurate metric by several studies (Suk et al., 2017)
and (Van Den Wymelenberg et al., 2010); however, it is time-consuming to use, leading to the creation of two
simplified versions (DGPs and eDGPs (see Table 5)) to reduce simulation time. Additionally, predictions made
using DGP are less accurate under direct sunlight, as noted by (Van Den Wymelenberg et al., 2010). Despite
these limitations, the metric is adaptable to various scenarios. It accounts for both saturation (overall illuminance
on the eye) and contrast (luminance ratio in the field of view) effects. It is also adopted by the European Standard
(EN17037, 2021) and therefore used in this thesis. (Wienold & Christoffersen, 2005) has proposed the following
comfort ranges of DGP, as shown in Table 3.

A human-centric approach to assess daylight in buildings for non-visual health potential, visual interest
and gaze behavior
A 3D environment is used to exemplify and demonstrate the impact of space, time, and sky on three mathematical
daylight performance models. The non-visual direct-response model (nvRD) can predict the non-visual effects of
light and is based on data obtained from experimental studies of melatonin suppression. Modified spatial contrast
(mSC) is an image-based method that can predict subjective impressions of daylight composition obtained by
studying subjective ratings of daylight renderings. Finally, the gaze-responsive light-driven model (GRL) can
predict angular shift and glare-free zones and is based on an experimental study using eye-tracking data.
The simulations are based on a point in space and a view direction. Spherical renderings are then generated for
the selected timestep, skies, and dates, which are used as input in the predictive models. The models’ output
is combined to get a nuanced picture of gaze direction, visual interest, and health potential in time, place, and
space.
Due to the parallel workflow, achieving the desired goals for all three performance indicators was not possible.
However, the models gave a more accurate assessment of behavior and performance at the occupant level,
together with a nuanced understanding of the relationship between conflicting performance goals.
Characterization of parameters affecting the quality of view out for appropriate selection of solar shading
systems
The main goal of the thesis was to advance knowledge in view-out and solar shadings. Its purpose was to
investigate if solar shading systems influenced the view-out quality and if photometric parameters can be used
as an indicator. For this purpose, a user-assessment experiment was conducted. The experiment was designed
as a field study in an office environment, and 64 subjects attended. The subjective responses were recorded
for several view conditions, which consisted of a view-out and six different solar shading systems. In addition,
the slat angle of one of the shading systems was also investigated. The different view-out were rated by an
assessment method developed by (Hellinga & Hordijk, 2014). The method is called View Quality Rating (VQR),
and it was used to ensure both high and low-quality views during the experiments. Five different rooms were
investigated, and the view-out quality was rated 2, 4, 5, and 8 on a VQR scale.

In addition to collecting subjective data, the authors measured photometric parameters. The measurements were
measured within a 180-degree field of view with a photometer. The data were collected for each subject’s view
condition, yielding 682 observations. In order to determine the relationship between subjective responses and
the different view-out conditions, three hypotheses were established and tested through the development of
linear mixed-effects modeling and ANOVA. The subjects were asked to ’rate’ the view-out using three different
questions, but assessment, acceptance, and satisfaction were differentiated. These questions are called Q1-Q3
and are used as response variables in the linear mixed-effects models - it is these answers that we would like to
be able to predict. Q1 and Q3 were investigated in this study, and linear mixed-effects models were developed
for data with and without solar shading. Both consisted of photometric variables and a control variable that
could become a demographic question.
It was discovered that VQR is an appropriate measure for view-out quality when solar shading is excluded. It
also showed that solar shading types and solar shading characteristics have a significant impact on view-out
quality. Local achromatic contrast was a good indicator of view-out quality regarding solar shadings. Overall,
it could be concluded that solar shading characteristics influence the view-out quality and that photometric
parameters could be used as an indicator.

5.1.3
Workflow: Non-Visual Effects
The second part of the simulation generates non-visual effects through the Lark Spectral Lighting plug-in. The
plug-in can be used to analyze circadian effects in building environments by choosing the color of the sky,
glazing, and building materials based on spectral data (Inanici et al., 2015). Thus, Lark Spectral Lighting uses
an N-step method to divide the visible part of the electromagnetic spectrum into corresponding wavelengths
and performs the calculation in N channels rather than the standard RGB method. The simulation of multiple
channels will contribute to more precise results regarding non-visual effects, such as the effect of the circadian
rhythm, which does not depend on photopic spectral sensitivity.
Figure 12 shows a schematic overview of the workflow. Material and light values are defined in nine channels,
and calculations are performed at nine intervals spanning the visible spectrum. The division of the bins means
there will be three materials for the sky and the 3D model surface materials, separated into red, green, and blue
bins. In this part of the workflow, the sky is assigned a color based on the CIE D65 illuminant. The grid-based
simulation is performed with the three material bins, resulting in a 3x3 simulation. The channels are then
post-processed with Lucas’s circadian weighting function (Lucas et al., 2014) to derive circadian illuminance
according to the adopted metrics of EML and mel-EDI. The outputs of the workflow are written and saved to
Excel automatically.

D E V E L O P M E N T O F A P H O T O M E T R I C V I E W- O U T M O D E L
One method to examine the relationship between explanatory and dependent variables is linear mixed-effect
modeling. Unlike simple linear models, a linear mixed-effect model can incorporate fixed and random effects.
They are beneficial when there is non-independence in the data, such as arising from hierarchical structures and
repeated measurements (Bruin, 2006). Since the subjects in the study by (Nielsen et al., 2022) participated in
repeated measurements, they provided several responses that cannot be said to be independent. Therefore, it was
a clear choice to derive linear mixed-effects models from this data.
It was generally the goal to develop data-driven models to implement them in a simulation workflow. It is,
therefore, necessary that the input variables can be derived from the same simulation process. As the mixed-
effects models developed in the study mentioned above also examined the effect of shading devices on view-out,
these could not be used in this particular study. The models rely on only parts of the entire data set, divided into
responses with and without shading. In this study, models were established based on the entire data set to obtain
a more general description of the perception of the view-out.
6.1
L I N E A R M I X E D E F F E C T S M O D E L L I N G
Linear mixed-effects models can be expressed in the following way:
Response ∼fixed effects + (1 | Random effect ) + ε
The model consists of three variables: dependent (response), fixed, and random effects. A fixed effect refers
to a relationship between an explanatory variable and a response variable that remains the same across all
observations (Kumar, 2023). A random effect refers to the variations in a population that the predictor variables
cannot explain. In many cases, the variation can be attributed to characteristics such as those specific to an
individual. Random effects can be added to a model to account for variation in an intercept or slope parameter.
The models developed in this study can incorporate random intercepts to account for variation among individuals
in their baseline levels of view-out assessment.
6.1.1
Handling of Data
As mentioned before, the photometric models derived in this study come from data collected by (Nielsen et al.,
2022) through a user-assessment experiment at The Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The experiment
was designed as a field study in an office-like setting. A total of 64 subjects participated, and their subjective
responses were recorded for different view-out conditions. A view-out condition was defined by a selected
view-out (according to its VQR) and a solar shading condition. The authors used VQR to select different test
rooms where the view quality varied. Overall, five different view-out and six solar shading systems were tested.
Data were collected from questionnaires and photometric measurements during the experiments. A photometer

6.1.2
Forward Selection and Akaike Information Criterion (AIC)
In statistics, stepwise selection is a method used to build regression models. A form of stepwise selection is
by so-called forward selection. Forward selection adds one variable at a time based on its ability to improve
the model’s performance. Starting with an empty model, the variable that improves the model’s performance
the most is added after each step. This process is repeated until it is no longer possible to improve the model
(Glen, 2019). Thus, the final input variables are those that provide the best performance. The starting point of
the models developed in this thesis was a random intercept to account for subject variability. Forward selection
is useful when there are many potential variables to choose from, and given the time frame of the thesis, it was
assessed as better suited than, for example, a backward selection that can be more time-consuming.
The forward selection was used in conjunction with a model selection criterion, such as the Akaike information
criterion (AIC), to select the appropriate subset of variables in the models. The Akaike information criterion
(AIC) is a mathematical method used to estimate a model’s relative quality. According to AIC, the best-fit model
is the one that explains the greatest amount of variation with the fewest independent variables (Bevans, 2022).
Using this method, you can quickly determine which photometric variables should be included in the model. It
can be done with R scripting by looping through all explanatory variables one by one, adding them to the model,
and calculating its AIC. The data is then collected in ascending order in a data frame. The process is continued
until the difference between two models’ AICs is less than two units. After developing each basic model, it was
==PAGE:43==
6.2 T H E M AT H E M AT I C A L R E P R E S E N TAT I O N O F T H E F I NA L M O D E L S
32
then investigated whether adding a random slope would further improve the model’s AIC. Nevertheless, AIC is
only a relative measure, so it cannot provide an absolute measure of goodness of fit but can be used to compare
models.

6.1.3
Collinearity and Variance Inflation Factor (VIF)
The presence of collinearity in a multiple regression analysis refers to the existence of a high level of correlation
between two or more explanatory variables. It occurs because one or more explanatory variables are correlated
with each other, meaning that they cannot independently predict the value of the response variable as they
measure the exact effects (Felicity, 2013). It can lead to several problems, but essentially unstable or unreliable
coefficient estimates.
Collinearity can be detected using a correlation matrix or calculating the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF). VIF
is a measure of how much the variance of the estimated regression coefficients is increased due to collinearity
(Zuur et al., 2010). A VIF of 1 indicates no collinearity, while a VIF ¿ 10 is generally considered an expression
of multi-collinearity. However, it is an arbitrary value, and different studies report different thresholds. In this
study, a threshold of VIF 2.5 (Johnston et al., 2018) was used to examine whether adding a variable would result
in collinearity.
The correlation matrix (Appendix 12 Figure 47) indicated whether the addition of a variable would cause
problems if the correlation coefficient between the newly introduced variable and already existing variables
were as high as 0.8. The VIF was calculated after each addition of a new variable. If it was higher than 2.5,
the variable was removed, and the second-best variable was investigated (second-best according to AIC). The
process was finished when the model’s overall VIF was below 2.5, and the lowest possible AIC combination was
achieved.

The linear mixed-effects models must meet several assumptions in order to be valid (Winter, 2013):
• Linearity: The relationship between the response variable and the predictor variables is linear.
• Homoscedasticity: The variance of the residuals are equal for all predicted values.
• Normality of residuals: The residuals are assumed to be normally distributed.
• Absence of Collinearity: The predictor variables should be independent of each other.
• Independence: Observations are assumed to be independent of each other.
Violations of these assumptions may lead to inaccurate parameter estimates and invalid conclusions. Hence, it is
important to validate the assumptions of the models with diagnostic plots before interpreting the results.

measured on the library’s ground floor. The measured materials are assumed to be usable on the entire building
model. However, it is to be expected that the wear and tear of the materials will be different depending on where

The reflectances are measured with spectrometer BYK-Spectro-Guide, as seen in Figure 25. The BYK-Spectro-
Guide has a spectral range of 400-700 nm, with a 10 nm resolution. Measurements were conducted with a D65
illuminant and a 2-degree observer. The BYK-Spectro-Guide has a sample port with a diameter of 11 mm that
is placed directly on the surface of the material to be tested (BYK, n.d.). The surface material is measured
three times to ensure no deviation between measurements. An average of the three measurements is used in the
simulations.

somewhere between these two extremes. As shown in figure 26, the ceiling, interior walls, and columns have

reflectance close to zero. As the materials could only be measured between 400 and 700 nm, both limit values

8.2.2
ANOVA TEST
An ANOVA test was used to test whether any of the fixed and/or random effects of the established models
were statistically significant at a 5% level. An ANOVA test is a statistical method used to determine whether
there is a significant difference between two or more categorical groups by testing for differences in means
using a variance. This is done by establishing a reduced model where the variable of interest has been removed
and comparing it to the full model. The null hypothesis then reads ”X has no significance for Y” and if the
significance level is lower than 0.05, the null hypothesis can be rejected, and it can be concluded that the variable
has significance for the response variable - in this case, Q1 - How will you assess the view from your position?.

8.4
V I S UA L P OT E N T I A L
Three daylight assessment methods have been used to predict the effects of light exposure using a human-centric
approach. The results of the models have been presented in parallel through section 8.1 and, in the following,
synthesized into a so-called visual potential. The visual potential will illustrate a multi-criteria assessment of
daylight. It is a combination of circadian-effective daylight, assessment of view-out, and protection against glare.
Based on the suggested threshold values, the predicted result in each area has been scaled and expressed as a
percentage. The visualization enables a holistic assessment of a point in space for a given view direction.
The results of predicted and self-assessed values are synthesized for subjects at the exact location and view
direction to be comparable. It should also be mentioned that only results have been included where there have
been at least two subjects to combine data from. However, due to various events on the ground floor during the
study period, most data came from the library’s second floor. Figure 45 shows the results of the visual potential
of five points at DTU SMART Library. The relevant location and direction of view are marked on the figure.
Figure 45: Visual Potentials: For location and view direction (VD) please refer to Figure 23 and 48
==PAGE:69==
8.4 V I S UA L P OT E N T I A L
58
The graphs are sorted from minimum to maximum access to view-out depending on the represented points in
space. They are assessed based on their VQR. In addition, both predicted values and the subjects’ responses of
the view-out, alertness/performance, and glare are shown. The responses come from the questionnaire in section
7.2 Figure 24 and have been assessed on a scale from 0-100.
At the time of the study, the recommended thresholds could only be met on some points. It was due to the
winter season, which generally has low levels of daylight availability. In general, view-out contact ensures a
higher exposure at eye level; particularly, the availability of mel-EDI underlines this. On average, the point with
maximum view-out contact also has the highest prediction and responses. The second best point deviates slightly
from this trend. It may be because it is the only point from the ground floor with lower daylight levels, which
may affect the view-out prediction. However, the test subjects also responded to it as having a poorer view-out
despite the high VQR. In other words, the view-out assessment in VQR might be limited.
Glare has not been a problem at this time of year, so predictions show plenty of potentials to be comfortable, i.e.,
to avoid glare. However, as can be seen, the subjects’ responses differ slightly from this tendency - in general,
not many subjects give extreme responses. A lack of extreme responses could be due to central tendency bias,
which means that subjects choose middle or neutral options rather than providing honest and accurate responses.
This results in a lack of variability in the results. The differences could also be because electric lighting needs to
be taken into account, which may be what bothered the subjects when assessing the experience of glare. In the
point of minimum view-out contact, some subjects could have been confused by the scale for assessing glare and
got the opposite answer.
On the graph, it can also be seen that higher view-out contact yields higher alertness/performance responses.
The same trend can also be seen for predictions of mel-EDI; however, the magnitude of predictions differs from
responses. It may be because mel-EDI is not a direct expression of performance/alertness, but research has
shown that it can enhance it. Instead of a response from the subjects, they could be made to do a performance
test so that everyone has the exact prerequisites for an assessment.
The graphs provide a nuanced understanding of view-out, glare protection, and health potentials in time, space,
and place. Due to the parallel workflow, achieving the desired goals for all three performance indicators is
only sometimes possible, as humans have conflicting needs and want regarding daylight. However, the models
gave a more accurate assessment of behavior and performance at the occupant level, together with a nuanced
understanding of the relationship between conflicting performance goals. Designers could use this to make
informed decisions based on trade-offs, e.g., glare would probably be prioritized in an office because of long
hours sitting in the same task orientation. On the other hand, stores with short durations of occupancy may
prioritize an interesting view-out. In addition, other indoor climate parameters can be added to the visualization
and scaled accordingly. It provides a holistic indoor environmental assessment, as visual comfort cannot stand
alone, and factors such as acoustics, air quality, and thermal comfort could be added.

9
D I S C U S S I O N
In this study, three steps have been taken to combine visual and non-visual potentials. A simulation workflow has
been developed, data-driven modeling has been carried out with implementation in the simulation workflow, and
a validation pilot study has been framed. The findings will be discussed based on the success of the objectives
and the quality of their implementation.
9.1
S I M U L AT I O N W O R K F L OW
The developed simulation workflow was demonstrated in a validation pilot at DTU SMART Library. Its main
limitation is that the workflow does not support annual calculations. However, as demonstrated in Section 8.4, a
multi-criteria assessment allows for a nuanced understanding of a point. The same assessment technique could
be applied to annual results, but the calculation time would be drastically increased. Thus, the information this
workflow provides in its current state can be valuable to individuals regarding how they position themselves in
the building. This information can aid in making informed decisions towards creating personalized, healthier
and more comfortable indoor environments.
The workflow does not calculate complexity and local achromatic contrast as they were processed with MATLAB
scripts. These scripts could be translated into the Python coding language and used directly in the workflow as a
component. It would make the workflow more smooth and easy to use. In addition, more time can also be spent
generating automatic visualizations and processing more data automatically.
In addition, the reliability of the simulations should be further investigated by physical measurements. DGP is
a robust metric, and Evalglare, which the workflow relies on, has also been validated. However, mel-EDI is a
newer metric on which there is less research, and it could be interesting to test this deviation.
9.2
P H OTO M E T R I C V I E W- O U T M O D E L
The established photometric models could not describe the assessment of view-out when compared with the test
subjects’ responses. A scatterplot showed a low correlation of just 0.12. However, upon closer examination of
the photometric model that describes the assessment of view-out at medium VQR, it was found that the model
in isolation had a correlation of 0.50 with the test subjects’ responses. Therefore, this model had a moderately
positive linear relationship with the test subjects’ responses. It was also shown that the demographic questions
influenced the model’s precision. Physical well-being and mood, as well as working hours, were the factors
that improved the scatterplot and correlation the most. In addition, an ANOVA test showed that background
luminance and maximum luminance of the brightest spot are significant for the assessment of view-out when
VQR is low, average luminance and complexity are significant for the assessment of view-out when VQR is
medium, and only average luminance is significant for the assessment of view-out when VQR is high.
59
==PAGE:71==
9.3 D T U S M A RT L I B R A RY ( A VA L I DAT I O N P I L OT S T U DY)
60
It was expected that complexity would be a suitable parameter in the assessment of view-out due to findings
in previous studies. However, this was different in the established models. One of the most critical factors to
consider is how the experimental data has been processed. Instead of calculating complexity for just a window
area, complexity was estimated for the entire image. It would be easier to differentiate between views with
multiple objects if complexity was calculated only for the view-out area through the window. However, it could
be seen from the ANOVA test that complexity had a significant effect on the model established for medium
VQR. Although the parameter was not included as a fixed effect but as a random slope, it significantly impacted
the effect of the explanatory variables on the response variable.
The fact that the experiments were conducted during the fall gives a limited valid range of photometrics. This
could have affected the results, as a comparsion with subjects during the winter period is a more extreme situation
that may not be supported by the models’ valid range. The experiments could be recreated and performed at
each season to capture important trends that can be used for more robust model development. The models might
also be improved by incorporating a factor or time dependence. Since photometric data is dependent on daylight
availability, there is naturally a difference in assessing the same view-out at 12.00 and 15.00. However, it does
not necessarily mean that the view-out has already deteriorated at 15.00; a time dependency could correct this.
Moreover, medium VQR was observed to be a superior model to low and high VQR. The skewed data size on
which the models are based, and the limited variation in VQR may contribute to this. The model developed
for ’low’ VQR values is valid in the interval ≤4VQR. In the experimental data, there is, e.g., only performed
experiments for view-out rated at 2 and 4 VQR, with a clear predominance of 4 VQR. When compared with
view-out, which is rated at 1 and 2 VQR, some important trends may be overlooked in this more extreme
situation. This could be one possible explanation as to why the VQR low model turned out to be imprecise. A
possible explanation as to why the VQR high model was not as accurate may lie in the fact that it only contains
101 observations, whereas the VQR medium model is based on 246 observations. In general, more data is almost
always better for building more nuanced models. Experiments carried out over a more extended period covering
several seasons and with more variations in VQR can improve the quality of the photometric models.
9.3
D T U S M A RT L I B R A RY ( A VA L I DAT I O N P I L OT S T U DY)
The developed Validation Pilot Study allowed for further assessment and development of the project concept.
However, due to the small sample size and limited experimentation period, the relation between predictions
and responses cannot be conclusive. It was also shown that the demographic questions influenced the model’s
precision. Physical well-being and mood, as well as working hours, were the factors that improved the
relationship between predictions and the subject’s responses the most. It could indicate that more studies in
controlled conditions are necessary to achieve this goal. However, when the visual potential was explored, there
was generally higher eye-level exposure for points with view-out contact. Among other things, higher responses
to performance/alertness coincided with contact with view-out.
It was expected that there would be a correlation between the test subjects’ responses and the predictions from
the simulation. This was generally not the case for view-out assessment; however, it was clearly seen that the
point with the highest visual potential also had the most favorable responses on average. There were also some
clear patterns for circadian-effective daylight and glare. According to the simulations, there should not be enough
daylight for the subjects to be bothered by glare - this was also a trend that could be seen in the responses, but
they were generally just below 100%. This could be because electrical lighting was not taken into account, and
this was the cause of discomfort. Otherwise, it could also be about central tendency bias. Central tendency bias
can be particularly relevant when analyzing data from questionnaires, as individuals may tend to choose middle
or neutral options rather than providing honest and accurate responses. In order to overcome central tendency
bias in questionnaire data, more consideration can be given to using different types of questions, such as open or
forced-choice questions. The order of the questions can also make people lose interest. In the questionnaire,
==PAGE:72==
9.3 D T U S M A RT L I B R A RY ( A VA L I DAT I O N P I L OT S T U DY)
61
e.g., three similar questions about the view-out are asked right after each other. Since the questions are not
very different, people answer almost the same to these questions. It could be improved by randomization or
mixing them all. An idea could also be to conduct a pilot test of the questionnaire in a small group to identify
any potential issues or biases that may be present in the questions.
Higher ratings of performance/alertness coincided with view-out contact. However, it did not wholly agree with
the predictions of mel-EDI. It was likely that the subjects had a different starting point when they responded to
their performance. Some may be very positive, while others may be critical. Therefore, including a performance
test such as the D2 test would provide a more nuanced result. This way, all subjects will have the same starting
point in the assessment. However, care must be taken to ensure that the questionnaire is brief and precise.
It is a limitation that the sample size is not larger, as few people can quickly twist the results. It could, for
example, be seen by simply removing four individuals from the overall scatterplot as mentioned in Section 8.3.
Their assessments of the view-out were influenced by fatigue and their inability to concentrate after long hours
of work. The difference in correlation without these four individuals increased from 0.12 to 0.40 - more than
three times as much. More studies in controlled settings may improve this. In addition, the validation pilot study
should also be expanded to include studies over all seasons of the year to capture important trends and improve
the robustness and variability of the results. Finally, it is also possible to compare the predicted results with the
view-out assessment based on the European Standard, EN17037, to see if there is a better correspondence. This
could show whether the subjects had misunderstood the questions or lost interest if the predictions aligned better
with EN17037.

C O N C L U S I O N
A simulation workflow was designed, considering the results of a literature review on the effects of daylight on
visual and non-visual aspects. The simulation included metrics for circadian-effective daylight, view-out, and
glare protection. The simulation also incorporated a photometric view-out model that considers the dynamic
changes of daylight throughout the day. The model was developed using linear mixed-effects modeling on
experimental data gathered at The Technical University of Denmark. Finally, the reliability of the simulation
was tested at the DTU SMART Library, where predictions could be compared to subjective responses made at
various points in space.
The study found that established photometric models did not accurately predict the view-out responses made by
subjects, as shown by a low correlation of 0.12 in a scatterplot. However, when looking at a specific photometric
model for assessing view-out at MVQR, it was found that the model had a moderately positive correlation of 0.50
with the subjects’ responses. It was also determined that demographic factors, such as, physical well-being and
mood, as well as working hours affects the model’s precision. Therefore, the study requires a larger sample size
to be able to draw definitive conclusions. Nevertheless, analysis of variance (ANOVA) showed that photometric
variables background luminance, maximum luminance of the brightest spot, average luminance, and complexity
was significant for the assessment of view-out.
The validation pilot study allowed for further assessment and development of the project concept. However, the
comparison of predicted and response values were centered around the photometric view-out models. More
effort can be put into the development of the questionnaire to avoid central tendency biases, and possibly use
a D2 performance test to make valuable comparisons between performance and spectral exposure. Studies in
controlled settings can also lead to essential findings. For example, the electric lighting in the library needs to
be taken into account, which in reality, should either be included in the simulation workflow or switched off
entirely for the respective locations where investigations are carried out in the library. In general, this experiment
should be investigated several times a year to assess the models’ effect under higher daylight exposure. The low
availability of daylight during this season makes it more challenging to come up with an overall conclusion.
The simulation workflow provided a detailed understanding of view-out, glare protection, and circadian-effective
daylight in terms of time and space. However, balancing human preferences and daylight needs is sometimes
difficult, leading to trade-offs in meeting all three performance targets. The visual potential allows for more
personalized evaluations of behavior and performance and a deeper understanding of performance trade-offs.
This simulation’s value is in informed decision-making by weighing trade-offs. For example, glare might be
given priority in an office setting where people spend long hours sitting in the same task orientation. In contrast,
view-out might be prioritized in a retail setting with shorter occupancy times. The method of visualizing the
multi-criteria assessment can be expanded to include additional factors such as acoustics, air quality, and thermal
comfort, as visual comfort alone is not sufficient for a comprehensive evaluation of the overall comfort in a
room.
In bullet points, potential future research topics and suggestions for enhancing current study procedures are
presented based on the analysis and limitations outlined in the discussion.
