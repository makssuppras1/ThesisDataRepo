Abstract  
  
The aim of this project was to use several different methods of additive manufacturing to 
check its feasibility and efficiency in terms of dimensional tolerances and surface finish 
for extrusion of the polymers-ABS and Polypropylene, by experimentally testing 
additively manufactured PEEK dies and calibration slides. Conventionally manufactured 
calibration slides were set as benchmarks to make a comparison with other slides. Three 
profiles were chosen with the most commonly found features in extrusion such that 
results from these runs could be extrapolated to any other commonly used profile used 
for extrusion. 
Three additively manufactured techniques were used to make the calibration slides, 
namely-Masked Stereo Lithography (MSLA), Fused Deposition Modelling (FDM)/Fused 
Filament Fabrication(FFF) and Binder Jetting(BJ). The calibration sides were made using 
steel infiltrated bronze, a high temperature resistant resin, TR300, by Phrozen technology 
using MSLA and Carbon Fiber Nylon and PETG using FDM/FFF. The Extrusion Dies 
were also made using Carbon Fiber(10%) filled PEEK, using FFF. 
Due to the limited time available to do this Thesis, only one of the profiles, with the best 
flow, were used for testing at different line speeds using the polymers ABS and PP. The 
extrudates produced were then characterized in terms of surface roughness and 
dimensional conformities.

1 Introduction 
To understand the use of additive manufacturing in Extrusion an attempt will be made to 
first try to understand the basic principles of Additive manufacturing, challenges, 
advantages and disadvantages, its usage in modern day technologies followed by the basic 
principles of extrusion and challenges faced in extrusion. Subsequently, an attempt will be 
made to combine the two, to paint a complete picture of additive manufacturing in 
extrusion within the context of this Thesis. 
1.1 Project Objective and Investigations 
The previous work has made a study on the use of soft tooling for a simple 20mm x 1mm 
rectangular profile using the two commonly used polymers for extrusion in commercial 
applications-ABS and PP[1][2]. This Thesis will attempt to build upon that further by using 
additively manufactured calibration slides with different technologies along with carbon 
fiber(10%) PEEK extrusion dies. Three profiles were chosen such that in totality they 
encompass features such as flat faces, circular faces, sharp corners, and fine protrusions 
that can generally be found in extrusion profiles.[3]  Thus, if these three profiles can be 
successfully extruded in theory these results can be extrapolated to any other extrusion 
profile. 
The objectives of this project can be summarized as follows: 
1. Choosing the additive manufacturing technologies as well as the materials for the 
calibration slides. 
2. Developing the optimized design of calibration slides based on the die profile 
3. Developing CAD design using inserts for the existing calibration die 
4. Performing the dimensional characterization of the die using confocal microscopes 
5. Experimental testing of Calibration dies along with PEEK dies using PP and ABS 
as the extrudates 
1.2 Project Structure 
The Thesis starts by giving a brief introduction to Additive manufacturing, in Section 2, 
with focus on the technologies used in this Thesis, that is Vat Photopolymerization, Fused 
Deposition Modelling, Binder Jetting followed by a small section (Section CNC 
Machining) on CNC Machining to highlight the differences between Additive and 
Reductive (Conventional) Manufacturing. Section 4 describes the polymer extrusion 
process in detail to understand the intricacies of the process, difficulties in optimizing and 
designing parts and the co-dependencies of various parameters on the final extrudate. 
Section 5 describes the profiles chosen for extrusion and graphically shows its dimensions. 
Section 6 justifies the choice of material for the extrusion die and outlines the build 
parameters used. Section 7 describes the criteria for material selection and the designing 
strategy used for various slides manufactured with different techniques. Section 0 describes 
the Measurement strategy used to characterise the dimension and the surface roughness of 
the slides and calibration. Section 9 outlines the Experimental setup. Section 10 gives a 
preliminary analysis of the extrudates and experimentation. Section 11 analyses results 
obtained from confocal analysis of the extrudates. Section Error! Reference source not 
found. gives the cost analysis. Section 0 provides a conclusion to the Thesis.

2 Additive Manufacturing 
2.1 Introduction 
Additive manufacturing, more commonly known as 3D printing is a versatile technology 
that has found its way into a large spectrum of domains in the recent past. Its principles 
date back from the times of pyramids of Giza and today also finds applications on the 
present-day war in Ukraine. 
 
Figure 2-1 Pyramids of Giza, Source[4] 
 
Figure 2-2 3D printed Tourniquet used in Ukraine 
war[5]
2.1.1 Concept 
Additive manufacturing can be defined as the manufacture of a 3-dimensional object from 
a CAD file, in a file format that can be understood by the machine manufacturing the 
object. It generally consists of a layer-by-layer manufacture of the part.  
 
Figure 2-3 Typical additive manufacturing processing steps[6] 
A typical algorithm for manufacturing a part is shown in Figure 2-3. The part is first 
designed keeping the principles of additive manufacturing in mind. The part is then 
converted to an STL file format or a similar format that can be understood by the machine 
==PAGE:18==

and sliced. The machine is then prepared for printing. Preparations could involve pre-
heating a bed and filament, loading the filament, levelling the bed, and filling the resin tank. 
The machine is then typically brought to its home position or start position and the 
building process begins. After the part is manufactured, it is either removed using a scraper 
or if the part is resilient enough it is simply pulled or twisted apart from the build plate. 
Often the part may require post-processing to remove unwanted parts like rafts and 
supports and may sometimes even require sanding and curing depending on the geometry, 
technology and material used and the quality of the product desired.

2.1.3 Advantages 
Additive manufacturing is not a one solution to all problems and cannot replace some 
time-tested manufacturing processes like injection moulding. But they can act as 
==PAGE:21==
 
  
  
  
5  
  
complementary processes to support manufacturing, e.g., in soft and hard tooling. 
However, there are certain industries where additive manufacturing finds strong 
application such as aerospace, automotive, medical devices, energy, infrastructure. 
Some of the advantages of additive manufacturing over traditional manufacturing are as 
stated below[9]: 
• Topology optimization, use of FEM generated complex structures for a given 
design space within a given set of loads, boundary conditions and constraints allow 
for material reduction and overall weight. [10] 
• Accelerated prototyping, the creation of digital prototypes allows for easy 
changes in the final tooling before commitment 
• Customisation, easy changes in the design from the specification based on the 
product demand and customer needs 
• Energy savings, fewer resources and no requirement of auxiliary tools reduces

2.2 Vat photopolymerization 
2.2.1 Introduction 
Vat Photopolymerization is an AM technique that makes use of a power source like UV 
lamps, laser radiation, gamma rays, LED screen, X-rays, or electron beams to cure a 
photocurable resin or ceramic mixture in a resin tank or vat to print parts on a build plate. 
There are many variations to this technique depending on the technology used- line-wise 
scanning SL(SLA), masked projection stereolithography MSLA, microstereolithography 
(µSL), two-photon polymerization (TPP) and Continuous Liquid Interface Production 
(CLIP).[11]

2. Bottom-up configuration

In the top-down configuration, the curing light source is positioned above the resin vat 
and the build plate is immersed into the vat. The top-down configuration has the 
advantage of no separation required from the vat bottom as it is a free surface curing but 
has several disadvantages like resin level required in the z-direction must be higher than 
the height of the part being printed, complex mechanisms required for recoating viscous 
resin, inability to cure very thin layers because of the oxygen in the air inhibiting the 
curing process and susceptibility to curling of cured layer.[11] 
In the bottom-up configuration a very thin layer of resin, with thickness equal to desired 
layer thickness, is trapped between the build plate and the vat bottom and is cured 
through the light source used in the printer. The part then must be separated by pulling 
apart the cured part from the Vat bottom in the z-direction by lifting-up the build plate. 
This also allows for fresh resin to flow between the newly printed layer and vat bottom. 
Thus, in theory the volume of resin required in the vat is equivalent to the resin required 
to build the part. The disadvantage with this technique is that a longer time is required to 
print the part as separation and recoating take a considerable amount of time.[11]

2.4.1 Binder Jetting

Binder jetting technology was initially developed by the Massachusetts Institute of

4 Polymer Extrusion 
4.1 Introduction 
 
Figure 4-1 Schematic of an extrusion machine [30] 
Extrusion is a type of manufacturing process in which a material flows through an orifice 
or die, thereby undergoing plastic deformation under the application of a force. The 
material that flows through the orifice takes up the shape of the die with possibilities of 
shrinking and warpage depending on the material and processing parameters. [31] 
Extrusion constitutes about 36wt% of all plastics consumed worldwide as opposed to 
injection moulding at 32wt%, as the second major process consuming plastics.[31]. Some 
of the most commonly used materials in polymer extrusion are Polyethylene (PE), 
Polypropylene (PP), Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS), Acetal, Polystyrene (PS),  
Acrylic, Polyvinyl chloride (PVC), Nylon (polyamides), and Polycarbonate (PC). [32] 
Depending on the temperature of the material used Polymer extrusion can be classified 
into hot extrusion and cold extrusion. When the temperature of the material used is above 
its recrystallisation temperature, it is known as hot extrusion. When the temperature of the 
material used is at room temperatures or close to room temperature and below its 
recrystallisation temperature, it is known as cold extrusion. Some of the materials 
commonly used for extrusion are polymers, metals, concrete, ceramics, and food-
material.[33] The force for extrusion can be provide by either a piston (discontinuous 
extrusion) or screw (continuous extrusion) of which the latter usually requires a device to 
pull the extrudate through the die. The manufactured parts are often referred to as 
extrudates.

4.2 Single Screw Extrusion-Working Principle 
The plastic material is fed to the machine through the hopper in the form of powders, 
flakes, pellets, or combinations of these forms. The plastic is melted with the aid of a screw 
and a cylindrical heated barrel, as shown in Figure 4-1 Schematic of an extrusion machine 
[30].  The screw also helps to push the plastic through the die. There are four ways to feed 
an extruder- flood feed, starve feed, crammer and melt feed. 
 
Figure 4-3 (a) flood feed extruder (b starve feed extruder (c) crammer feed extruder (d) melt feed. Extruder [34] 
Flood feed makes use of gravity and the movement of the screw to feed the extruder. In a 
starve feed, the material is fed directly onto the extruder screw. There is no material build-
up in the extruder and the throughput is determined by the feed rate rather than the 
extrusion screw speed as in flood fed extruders. In a crammer feed extruder, a screw 
conveys the material to the extruder screw and helps to increase the throughput. In a melt 
feed extruder, another extruder produces the melt used as input material for the main 
extruder and thus essentially works as a pump to extrude the polymer at a constant 
temperature and pressure to the die. [34]

The screw can be divided into three sections-the feed section, compression section and 
metering section.  
In the feed section the material enters through the hopper through gravity and comes 
directly into contact with the screw. In this zone the plastic is pre-heated. The hopper may 
be mechanically vibrated to allow for the plastic to enter the barrel. To produce parts with 
desired properties, the plastic may have also been blended beforehand with colour 
pigments or concentrators, flame retardants, fillers, lubricants, reinforcements, and 
additives such as stabilizers for heat, oxidation, and ultraviolet stability. Some polymers, 
such as ABS, must be dried beforehand to prevent degradation due to moisture. [36] 
In the compression section the plastic pellets are melted by the compressive action of 
the screw and the heated barrel. The heaters provide the heat required to melt the plastic. 
Heat is also produced because of the intense pressure and friction inside the barrel. In 
some cases, if the extrusion line is running fast enough the heating sections may be turned 
off as the heat produced due to the pressure and friction would be enough to melt the 
plastic. In some extruders cooling fans may be provided to maintain the temperature of 
the melt.[32] 
The metering section is made to allow for a uniform pressure and temperature generation 
to obtain a constant flow. The polymer is then forced through a screen pack, reinforced 
by a breaker plate. The screen pack removes any contaminants in the melt. The breaker 
plate is essentially a thick metal puck with holes drilled through it. A pressure of up to 
34MPa can be generated at this point which serves to create the back pressure in the barrel. 
The back pressure is essential to obtain a uniform melt and homogenised mixture of 
polymer. The back pressure can be changed by varying the screen pack. [32] 
Eventually the melt passes through the die and takes the shape of the profile it comes out 
through. As the plastic flows from a cylindrical profile to the shape of the final profile, care 
must be taken to design a die that gives a uniform flow to the melt as it exits the die. An 
uneven flow can result in a profile with thickness greater than required in sections with 
relatively high flow rate and lower thickness in sections with relatively low flow rate. [32] 
The extrudate can then be passed through a calibration slide followed by a cooling water 
bath, cold surface or an air-cooling system while being pulled at a constant velocity, by a 
caterpillar roller, to obtain the desired cross-section. One or more calibration slides can be 
used to allow for better control over the final dimensions of the profile. The continuous 
==PAGE:39==
 
  
  
  
23  
  
extrudate can then be chopped into discrete parts or rolled into one long piece as per 
requirements. 
It is important to note that different polymers may require different kinds of screw design. 
Although most polymers have only the above three stated sections some polymers also 
make use of a decompression zone, to allow for escape of trapped gasses and pressure 
drop, and a second metering zone, to repressurize the melt to pass through the screens 
and the die. [32]

Small changes in parameters can greatly affect the quality of the final extrudate. Polymer

temperature at various points on the screw and die, throughput, screw speed, die head 
pressure, die design, screw design, cooling rate, pulling speed and environmental factors.

Increase in a throughput rate results in a higher percent screw fill and requires more torque 
and generates higher head pressure generally decreasing the melt temperature. Increase in

leads to a decrease in torque and head pressure. Increase in barrel temperature decreases 
the polymer melt viscosity resulting in lower torque, head pressure and higher melt

torque and melt temperature and decrease in throughput and head pressure.[38] 
4.4 Trouble Shooting Extrusion Process 
To gain process control, optimize the parameters and produce optimum quality extrudates 
the following steps can be followed to eliminate any problem.[39] 
Define the problem. Before defining the problem, it is important to take note of all the 
factors such as the dimensions of the part produced, blend quality, moisture content of 
resin, the throughput rate, the operating conditions (temperature, pressure, and torque), 
the cooling method, pulling speed and the dimensions of the screw used. It is important 
to ensure that the parts produced were within the recommended conditions. If parts with 
better quality available a comparison can be made on the time during which the parts 
started failing. Thus, the first step involves a clear definition of the problem statement. 
Identifying the root cause. The root cause can be attributed to one of the three 
categories. The material must be properly dried and blended, with additives in the right 
ratio, if any. The running parameters such as screw speed, barrel temperature, cooling 
tank temperatures, throughput rates, distance between die and cooling tank and pulling 
speeds should be set at recommended values. Environmental conditions such as room 
temperature and moisture can affect the extrudate quality and must hence be monitored. 
Taking corrective actions. There may be several corrective actions that can be taken to 
solve a problem but from an industrial standpoint, the one that is most cost-effective must 
be chosen. The process must then be monitored for a long duration to ensure an 
equilibrium for the process has been achieved and the solution is a long-term solution.

must relax before leaving. The profile in the die must also account for the drawn-down

in the profile dimension. 
The back pressure generated in the die must be within the stipulated range and the 
frictional heat must also be controlled. [40]

• Avoid dead spots in the flow channel 
• There should be a steady increase in flow velocity along the die channel 
• abrupt changes in flow channel geometry should be avoided 
• smaller approach angles should be used 
• the die should allow for easy assemble and disassembly

Bubbles in extrudate may not be visibly seen in opaque 
plastics but may lead to generation of parts with weak 
mechanical properties. They may also show up as surface

• moisture absorption 
• trapped air

Moisture absorption: some polymers like ABS, PET, 
Polycarbonates and Nylons have a large affinity for 
moisture absorption. When pellets of such polymers are 
heated the moisture trapped in these polymers also heats 
and turns to steam creating bubbles and pitting defects in 
the extrudate as shown in Figure 4-15 and Figure 4-16.  
The solution to this problem is to pre-dry the pellets 
before use, using an oven or a dehumidifying drier. 
Venting the barrel can also be used to remove any trapped 
steam. 
Trapped air is commonly found with powder feed and is 
less common with pellets. Running at slower speeds can 
prevent trapping of air in the melt. Venting and vacuums 
hoppers can be used to rectify this issue. [44] 
Degradation of the polymer melt occurs when the barrel is over-heated. Polymers with 
processing temperatures close to it decomposition temperature are more susceptible to 
this defect. The degradation causes the polymer to produce gases which can get trapped 
inside the melt. A possible remedy to this problem is to use lower temperatures to heat the 
polymer to avoid degradation and more accurate temperature control of the barrel. [44] 
Depolymerization of polymer is the change of a polymer into a monomer or a mixture 
of monomers at high temperatures. The products formed due to depolymerization could 
be volatile and remain trapped inside the material. To avoid such defects lower 
temperatures should be used in the barrel to avoid overheating. [44] 
Blistering can occur due to trapped air or water in 
the granules or on the surface of the extrudate. It 
depends on the amount of water or air present and 
extrusion temperature. It can be caused due to 
excessive screw speed that can draw air into the 
barrel. Lower back pressure can also lead to a less 
dense melt that is unable to push out excessive gases.

In wet calibrators the heat from the calibrators is transferred completely or partially by 
direct convection to the coolant. In case of partially cooled calibrators the remainder of

In dry calibrators the heat from the melt is directly conducted away by a metal calibrator

Thus, the dimensions of the calibrator play a significant role in the intensity of cooling 
provided to the melt. There are also frictional forces that act on the melt as it is pulled 
through the calibrator. This is required to create normal forces for obtaining good 
calibration. This force is provided by the pulling device and is known as take-up force. 
There is a limit to the force that can be applied at this stage as the melt which comes out 
of the die is still solidifying and may be over stretched if the force is too large. The 
stretching causes a decrease in dimensions perpendicular to the flow and increase in the 
direction of flow. This leads to a change in the normal forces and hence the frictional 
forces and thermal contact with the calibrators. It is for this reason the calibrators used in 
extrusion are over-sized to compensate for these effects. It is also possible to have a 
tapered calibrator or use a series of calibrators with gradually decreasing dimensions to 
obtain the desired profile with strict tolerances with progressive cooling. But the maximum 
length of such a calibration line will be limited by the loading capacity of the extrudate or 
maximum stretch of the extrudate as the take-off force increases with increase in length of 
the calibration line. [50] 
The maximum line speed attainable can be defined by determining the minimum time 
required to keep the extrudate in the calibrator so that it may resist changes in its 
dimensions due to external stresses such as gravity, take-up force and internal stresses 
formed due to cooling for a given dimensional tolerance. Thus, to achieve the highest line 
speed the calibration section should cool with a minimum frictional force and as intensively 
as possible. [50]

between a white and a black pixel it will cure in such a way that it is closer to the white 
pixel. A partially cured pixel tends to cure close to the white pixel than the black pixel as 
shown in figures that follow.[53]

fiber nylon as the standard brass nozzles can easily wear out. The print reolution is also 
dependent on the step size of the stepper motor used for printing. Ender 3 Printer uses a 
8mm/200 steps stepper motor. Thus, the minimum possible print resolution is 0.04mm 
for one step. A step can also be a half step or a fractional step, but such settings are unstable 
and thus undesirable. Thus, a good resolution is above a multiple of 0.04mm(eh 0.08mm, 
0.12mm, 0.16mm) and the next preferable setting is full steps followed by a movement 
with an additional half step. Eg.0.1mm= 0.08mm (2 full steps)+0.02mm(1 half step).

12 Conclusion 
The Thesis was investigating the application of additive manufacturing in extrusion using 
Additively manufactured PEEK Die and Calibration Slides.  
Although Carbon fiber provides excellent mechanical properties to FDM materials in the 
form of short fibre embedded in a matrix-composite, it also tends to increase the 
brittleness of the material and as such any small finishing operation can result in a rough 
surface. 
During the design phase it was discovered that although conventional manufacturing can 
produce very smooth surfaces, there are certain geometries which are difficult to produce 
with Conventional manufacturing. Like the small circular features on profile MEK02. 
During the design face it was also understood that unlike CNC machining where tight 
tolerances can be obtained, it is difficult to do so with FDM/FFF as slight change in 
parameters can easily affect surface quality and dimension. 
In case of MSLA, the final quality of the surface produced is dependent on the surface 
roughness of the resin tank on which the part is printed. Also, the quality of prints can 
vary significantly based on the processing parameters used and curing methods employed 
after the part is printed. Also the use of anti-aliasing allows for obtaining very smooth 
surface on MSLA parts. 
Another problem that was found with using AM is moisture absorption. This is not a 
problem with metal slides as they don’t absorb water. Swelling of the die and slides can 
occur leading to a change in dimension. 
Another drawback with using AM technology is the low thermal conductivity of 
materials. This tends to increase the heat in the calibration slide and thus makes it 
unsuitable for performing extrusions at high speeds. 
In order to make AM more suitable for extrusion it is mandatory to use internal cooling 
channels for the slides.  
Thus it can be conclusively said that with more research through simulation for resolving 
heating issues in slides and proper curing, it is possible to use MSLA based TR300 to 
produce extrudates with surface quality comparable with standard machined parts for low 
speeds and short durations.
