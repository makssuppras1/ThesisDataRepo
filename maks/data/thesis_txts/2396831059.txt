Summary (English)
In this thesis we focus on image restoration problems with a directional prior. In
order to incorporate directional information into the prior information we pro-
pose a spatially varying image prior, spatially varying total variation (SV-DTV)
functional, which is inspired by directional total variation (DTV) [13], which suc-
cessfully has been used to restore images with textures that mainly follow one
direction. In this thesis we extend the idea of DTV to include more directions
such that the direction may vary within the image domain.
The thesis is divided into four parts, where we in the ’¼ürst two parts de’¼üne SV-
DTV in respectively a continuous as well as in a discrete setting. In the third
part we describe a primal-dual algorithm for solving a L2-SV-DTV regularisation
problem. Finally in the fourth part we propose three ways of approximating direc-
tional information from a low-resolution (LR) image for a super-resolution (SR)
reconstruction problem, which is solved by L2-SV-DTV regularisation. The results
from the SR reconstructions shows that by applying directional information we are
able to improve the quality of the LR images under certain circumstances.
All numerical computations have been carried out in Matlab.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In the ’¼üeld of inverse problems, regularisation techniques have been introduced to
overcome ill-posedness in order to obtain reasonable as well as stable solutions. It
is common to incorporate prior information through regularisation. In the ’¼üeld
of image reconstruction one widely used regularisation technique is total variation
regularisation (TV), which was introduced in [18] and has shown to be a good
and noise-robust regularisation term for reconstructing images that contains sharp
edges.
In [13] the directional total variation has been de’¼üned in a continuous
setting an has shown to be a good reguliser for restoring images that contains tex-
tures, which follow a certain direction. In the following sections we brie’¼éy describe
the idea behind inverse problems and regularisation and we will motivate the usage
of another regularisation term which we will call spatially varying directional total
variation (SV-DTV) regularisation.
1.1
Inverse Problems
An inverse problem is the opposite of a forward problem. In a forward problem one
’¼ünds e’¼Ćect (data) from a cause (model parameters), while in an inverse problem
one recovers the cause from the e’¼Ćect. The goal of solving an inverse problem, is to
approximate the model parameters that best matches the data. The mathematical

6.1
Conclusion
The objective for this thesis has been to utilize directional information as reg-
ulation for image reconstruction. We have used the directional information for
solving a super-resolution reconstruction problem an have formulated two ways of
degrading an HR image to an LR image and formulated three methods for ob-
taining directional data from an LR image. We ’¼ürst tested a reconstruction based
on directional data from the true image, which yielded very good results almost
indistinguishable from the true image. This showed that directional data can be a
very important factor for image reconstruction. Next we tested the performance of
the three methods and based on the PSNR values from reconstructions we found
that either Method 1 or Method 3 gave the best reconstructions. Lastly we tested
another model, which assumes that the original image has been convoluted be-
fore down-sampled. This yielded better reconstruction results however, the results
required a regularisation parameter signi’¼ücantly lower than the model without
convolution.
==PAGE:76==
56
Conclusion Discussion and Future work
6.2
Discussion
In this thesis we have con’¼ürmed that directional data plays a crucial role for
SR reconstruction when it is solved through SV-DTV regularisation. For all the
numerical tests we have quite na├»vely based the directional data directly upon the
LR image in order to reconstruct the HR image. In general this way of obtaining
the directional data relies highly on the fact that the LR image has structures
reminiscent of the HR image. The big question is now; how can we improve the
way of obtaining the directional data? One way could be to incorporate adaptive
regularisation i.e., after obtaining a regularisation solution based on some initial
directional data, and then adapt new directional data from the solution and in
this way obtain a new solution in this directional data, this procedure will then
continue until satisfactory result is obtained under some stopping criteria.
6.3
Future Work
Several interesting studies presented themselves while working on this thesis, some
of which could not be included, because of time limitations, including the following.
First of all directional data could be introduced for many other purposes than just
SR reconstruction. Other purposes includes image denoising, image deblurring,
CT reconstruction and MRI reconstruction [8] etc. Furthermore as mentioned in
the discussion section, adapted regularisation for SV-DTV it would be interesting
to investigate further into this ’¼üeld of study. In [5] the concept of generalized
total variation (GTV) has been introduced which includes higher order derivatives
than the standard TV and has also yield better reconstruction. [13] the concept
of GTV has been extended to generalized directional total variation (GDTV) and
has similarly yielded better reconstructions for directional images. It would be
interesting to also try to include a generalized spatially varying directional total
variation for image reconstruction.
