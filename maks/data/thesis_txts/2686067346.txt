ABSTRACT
Tropical forests, such as the Amazon, are among the most species rich areas on
Earth and provide several beneﬁts for humans. Biodiversity monitoring is crucial
to determine where conservation efforts should be focused. However, the tradi-
tional method, ﬁeld work, is time consuming, expensive and difﬁcult in a densely
vegetated tropical forest.
The purpose of this study was to investigate the possibility of using freely available
optical remote sensing data (Sentinel-2 imagery) to predict tree species diversity
in the Amazon forest. The main objectives were (1) to investigate potential corre-
lations between species richness data from the ﬁeld and Sentinel-2 images over a
gradient of diversity within the Amazon rainforest, (2) to evaluate the use of Rao’s
Q diversity index in tropical forests using multiple vegetation indices and (3) to as-
sess the temporal variation in spectral diversity. The hypothesis of the study was
that a positive correlation between spectral variation and species diversity would
be found, based on the Spectral Variability Hypothesis.
Field diversity indices (effective number of species calculated for Shannon Diver-
sity Index) from plots in Brazil, Colombia, Ecuador and Peru were compared to
Rao’s Q diversity index calculated from Sentinel-2 images from 2016 and 2017.
Rao’s Q was calculated for the vegetation indices NDVI (Normalized Difference
Vegetation Index), NDWI (Normalized Difference Water Index) and EVI (Enhanced
Vegetation Index) as single-layer inputs and all the spectral bands of Sentinel-2 as
a multi-layer input (except band 1, 9 and 10). Furthermore, the temporal variation
in Rao’s Q was investigated for two of the ﬁeld locations on EVI time series from
2016 to 2018.
The results showed a signiﬁcant negative correlation between spectral diversity
and in-situ species diversity (R2 = 0.60), which was the opposite of what was
expected. It is hypothesized that the negative correlation is caused by a higher
canopy gap density in less diverse areas or differences in forest type among the
locations. It is therefore concluded that Rao’s Q calculated on optical Sentinel-2
imagery over tropical forests is not able to detect variations in species diversity
in a way that agrees with the Spectral Variability Hypothesis. Further studies are
required to understand the mechanisms behind the observed negative correlation.
Furthermore, Rao’s Q was not stable over time between the plots of each location,
which underlines the importance of the timing of remote sensing data.

INTRODUCTION
The Amazon forest is not only one of the most species rich places on Earth, it is
also one of the most important natural areas due to the multitude of functions it
provides to humans. Uptake of CO2, recycling of water and provision of food and
medicine are just a few of the offerings of the Amazon. However, despite being an
area of high value and importance to humans, it is also facing continuous threats
from different anthropogenic factors; deforestation and climate change being two
of the major ones. Since 1970, more than 15% of the Amazon has been cleared by
humans (Amigo, 2020; Lovejoy and Nobre, 2019). Scientists discuss how much
forest we can afford to lose before we reach a tipping point, potentially turning
the rainforest into tropical savannah (Lovejoy and Nobre, 2019). Climate change
is causing increased temperatures and reduced rainfall, which also leads to tree
mortality (Lovejoy and Nobre, 2019). It is clear that protection of the Amazon is
crucial.
Conservation efforts are often focused on highly diverse areas. Besides the intrin-
sic value of biodiversity, there are also other incentives for us to prioritize these
areas. Biodiversity has been linked with higher and more stable productivity in
ecosystems (Cleland, 2011; Poorter et al., 2015), which leads to a higher CO2 up-
take. To focus conservation on highly diverse areas, we need to be able to pinpoint
the biodiversity hotspots within the Amazon.
The traditional way of monitoring biodiversity, ﬁeld work, is both time-consuming,
costly and difﬁcult to carry out in such a densely vegetated area. Furthermore, it
is impossible to obtain ﬁeld data over a larger area, such as the entire Amazon
biome. Remote sensing, which is the process of investigating an object of interest
at a distance, offers a tool that can quickly and easily collect data over large ar-
eas. Using satellite images, it is possible to obtain continuous data over the entire
Amazon forest. Palmer et al. (2002) formulated the Spectral Variability Hypothe-
sis (SVH) which states that variations in remotely sensed data are expected to be
related to biological variation, i.e. biodiversity. This hypothesis has been investi-
gated in different ecosystems and with different sensors, with varying results (e.g.
==PAGE:13==
2
Oldeland et al. (2010); Torresani et al. (2018); Schmidtlein and Fassnacht (2017)).
This study aims to investigate the possibility of using freely available remote sens-
ing data to predict species diversity over the Amazon rainforest. The Sentinel-2
satellites collect optical imagery, have high spatial and temporal resolution and the
data is freely available to download (European Space Agency, 2021). Rocchini
et al. (2017) proposed the use of Rao’s Q diversity index to detect biodiversity on
remotely sensed images and provided an R-package to calculate the index. Rao’s
Q is a measure of spectral variation based on richness, abundance and pairwise
distance of pixel values. Rocchini et al. (2017) tested Rao’s Q on MODIS (Mod-
erate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer) images over Europe, which have a
lower spatial and temporal resolution than Sentinel-2 images. The present study
sought to investigate whether the same method works in tropical forests with im-
ages of higher spatial and temporal resolution, using ﬁeld data from Colombia,
Ecuador, Peru and Brazil. Ideally, this method could be used to extrapolate species
diversity from ﬁeld plots to the entire Amazon biome. It was hypothesized that the
results would be in line with the SVH, which would result in a positive correlation
between spectral variation and ﬁeld diversity.
The main objectives of this research project were: (1) to investigate potential corre-
lations between species richness data from the ﬁeld and Sentinel-2 images over a
gradient of diversity within the Amazon rainforest, (2) to evaluate the use of Rao’s
Q diversity index in tropical forests using multiple vegetation indices and (3) to
assess the temporal variation in spectral diversity.

THEORY AND BACKGROUND
2.1
Characteristics of biodiversity
2.1.1
Different types of biodiversity
Biodiversity can be deﬁned as the variety of life.
It can be divided into differ-
ent aspects, such as species diversity, genetic diversity and functional diversity
(Cavender-Bares et al., 2020c). The species diversity of an ecosystem, also called
taxonomic diversity, refers to the amount of species present. The genetic (or phy-
logenetic) diversity represents the ”evolutionary distance” between species in an
ecosystem, i.e. how long ago the species diverted from a common ancestor, or
how genetically similar they are.
The functional diversity of an ecosystem de-
scribes how different the functional traits of the species present in the ecosystem
are. In terms of tree species, the functional traits can for example be leaf size,
chlorophyll content and dispersal mode. Biodiversity is often measured solely by
species richness, and it is important to note that when doing so, only one aspect
of biodiversity is considered.
On the spatial scale, biodiversity can be divided into alpha, beta and gamma di-
versity (Whittaker, 1960). Alpha diversity is the local diversity within a community,
usually in smaller areas within a region. Beta diversity is the turnover between
communities, i.e. how different two areas within the same region are. This can
be measured by the amount of species that overlap between those two areas.
Gamma diversity is the total diversity of a region, and is quite difﬁcult to measure
due to time and resource availability constraints.
2.1.2
Importance of biodiversity
An ecosystem is said to have certain services, which beneﬁt humans. The ser-
vices are typically divided into four categories; cultural, regulating, provisioning
and supporting services (Sataloff et al., 2005). Cultural services are non-material
beneﬁts provided by ecosystems, such as spiritual or aesthetic gains. Regulating
==PAGE:16==
2.1
Characteristics of biodiversity
5
services maintain the ecosystems stable by for example countering global warm-
ing, preventing erosion and ﬁltering particles from the air. Provisioning services
include the supply of water, food and other resources. Finally, supporting services
provide the necessary foundations for the other services, such as nutrient cycling
and primary productivity (Sataloff et al., 2005).
These ecosystem services have been found to be negatively affected by biodi-
versity loss (Cardinale et al., 2012). Higher biodiversity has been linked to in-
creased productivity, which can be attributed to different concepts (Poorter et al.,
2015). Firstly, a more diverse community is more likely to contain highly productive
species, simply because more species are present. Secondly, with more species
present, more ecological niches can be ﬁlled, thereby increasing the total produc-
tivity of the system. Higher biomass production implies a higher uptake of carbon
dioxide from the atmosphere (Poorter et al., 2015). The diversity is also linked to
the stability of the ecosystem (Cleland, 2011). Stability refers to having low vari-
ability in productivity even under changing environmental conditions. Having more
plant species present can increase stability in case of a change in environmental
conditions, as it is more likely that one or more of the species can adapt to the
changing conditions, and maintain the level of productivity from before the pertur-
bation. If the diversity is lower, it may happen that none or only few of the species
can adapt to the new conditions (Cleland, 2011).
2.1.3
Measuring biodiversity
Alpha diversity
When measuring species diversity, the most simple metric is species richness,
i.e. how many species are present within a certain area. However, this metric
does not take the abundance (or evenness) of each species into account. The
abundance refers to the number of individuals per species, i.e. the distribution of
rare and common species (Cavender-Bares et al., 2020a). Some of the most used
alpha diversity indices that include both species richness and abundance are the
Shannon-Wiener Index (also known as the Shannon Index, Equation 1, Shannon
(1948)) and the Simpson Index (Equation 2, Simpson (1949)).

The Shannon Index calculates the uncertainty of predicting the species of a ran-

uncertainty of predicting the species will also be low and vice versa. This means
that a higher value of the Shannon Index indicates higher diversity. The Simpson
Index calculates the probability that two randomly selected individuals from the
study area will belong to the same species (Simpson, 1949). If this probability is
high, the diversity must be low. This means that a higher value of the Simpson
Index indicates lower diversity, as opposed to the Shannon Index. For this rea-
son, the Gini-Simpson Index is often used instead, which is the Simpson Index
subtracted from 1 (1 −D) (Jost, 2006).
There are some issues associated with the use of these two diversity indices as
indicators of biodiversity. Neither increases linearly with species richness, which
makes their interpretation less intuitive. Jost (2006) argues that the diversity in-
dices do not actually measure diversity directly, instead they measure entropy. En-
tropy can be used as a proxy of species diversity, but it should not be interpreted
directly as diversity. Instead, the effective number of species can be calculated
(Jost, 2006). This number is derived from the diversity indices, calculating the
amount of equally abundant species necessary to give the observed value of the
diversity index (Equation 3 and 4). In other words, given a certain value of the
diversity index, how many species would be present if there was the exact same
number of individuals of each species. Since the ”unit” of the effective number of
species is ”species”, this value is a lot easier to interpret. Furthermore, both the
Shannon and Simpson indices have a tendency to saturate in regions with high
diversity. The effective number of species is better at dealing with high diversity
(Jost, 2006).

Beta diversity
Beta diversity is the difference between the species composition of communities.
There are two different components to beta diversity, nestedness and turnover.

species found in another community. Turnover is when some of the species of one
community is replaced by other species in another community. The Sorensen Beta
diversity index incorporates both turnover and nestedness (Equation 5 (Sorensen,
1948)).

2.2
Introduction to remote sensing
To calculate the diversity indices, in-situ species data is required. Data collection is
usually both costly and time-consuming, especially when investigating remote and
densely vegetated areas such as tropical forests. Collecting data in the ﬁeld is also
not spatially representative, since it is close to impossible to sample an entire for-
est. It is therefore interesting to look into methods of estimating biodiversity where
extensive ﬁeld work is not required. One promising tool is remote sensing, where
information about the vegetation is obtained through sensors either mounted on

2.2.1
Electromagnetic radiation
Electromagnetic radiation is energy that travels at the speed of light in the form
of waves. The waves are characterized by their wavelength and frequency. The
electromagnetic spectrum is divided into different sections depending on the wave-
length. The shortest waves are X-rays and gamma rays (shorter than 10−8 m)
and the longest waves are micro- and radiowaves (10−3 to 1 m). The part of the
spectrum that is visible to the human eye ranges from 400 to 700 nm (Jones and
Vaughan, 2010).
When an electromagnetic wave hits a surface or an object, a number of different
types of interactions are possible. The ﬁrst one is transmission, which is when
the radiation passes through the object. In the case of forests, some radiation is
transmitted through the leaves of the upper canopy. Another type of interaction
is absorption, which is when the object absorbs the energy from the incoming ra-
diation and converts it into another type of energy, such as when plants absorb
solar radiation and use it for photosynthesis. The last type of interaction is reﬂec-
tion, where the radiation is simply reﬂected back to the atmosphere. Which type
of interaction occurs depends on the wavelength of the incoming radiation and the
properties of the inﬂicted object (Jones and Vaughan, 2010).
Some of these interactions occur when the electromagnetic waves travel through
the atmosphere. Some of the radiation is either absorbed or scattered by molecules
present in the atmosphere such as water vapor or ozone. In absorption, the in-
ﬂicted molecule absorbs the energy from the radiation. This energy is converted
and re-emitted as another type of energy, e.g. heat (with another wavelength). In
scattering, which is a form of reﬂection, the radiation is redirected and dispersed
in a different direction (Jones and Vaughan, 2010).
2.2.2
Remote sensing
Remote sensing is the process of obtaining information about an object without
being in direct contact with it, i.e. measuring something at a distance. Using the
knowledge that radiation with different wavelengths interacts with different surfaces
in different ways, remote sensing obtains information about the inﬂicted object. The
typical setup is a sensor measuring radiation emitted or reﬂected from the object of
interest. There are many different forms of remote sensing, both in terms of what
==PAGE:20==
2.2
Introduction to remote sensing
9
kind of radiation is measured and what kind of platform is used. Platforms can be
satellites, aircrafts or mounted on the ground (for example taking measurements
from a ﬁeld below the platform) (Jones and Vaughan, 2010).
As mentioned previously, electromagnetic waves interact with the atmosphere.
Some of the solar radiation will be lost on the way to the surface of the Earth.
Likewise, the radiation that is reﬂected or emitted from the surface of the Earth
will also be reduced on the way to the sensor measuring the radiation. Further-
more, radiation scattered by molecules from another area of the atmosphere may
come into the sensor’s view and confound the data. Consequently, it is important
to perform atmospheric correction when extracting information of the surface of
the Earth from remotely sensed images. Remotely sensed variables are therefore
sometimes called ”top-of-atmosphere” (TOA) or ”bottom-of-atmosphere” (BOA), to
indicate whether the atmospheric correction has been performed or not. BOA val-
ues can also be called surface values (Jones and Vaughan, 2010).
Remote sensors differ in spatial, temporal and spectral resolution. The spatial res-
olution refers to the size of the pixels the radiation is measured in, the temporal
resolution refers to how often the measurements are repeated and the spectral
resolution refers to which bands of wavelengths are measured. Most remote sen-
sors do not measure radiation at each wavelength. Instead, the wavelengths are
grouped in bands and the sensor measures the radiation received within a range of
wavelengths. The width of the bands varies as well. Multispectral sensors capture
information in a few bands, which are usually categorized in different groups (e.g.
in the optical spectrum, they are grouped into categories, such as the blue band,
the green band and the red band). Hyperspectral sensors record information in
hundreds or thousands of narrow bands, and can therefore pick up subtler details
and variations. However, having more bands also adds more complexity to the
data (Jones and Vaughan, 2010).
Because of scattering, some types of remote sensing are not possible under cloudy
conditions. How much of the radiation is scattered by the water droplets in clouds
depends on the wavelength, with shorter wavelengths having a higher tendency
to scatter than longer wavelengths. Therefore, remote sensing in the optical spec-
trum is not viable in cloudy weather, whereas the longer wavelengths, such as
microwaves, can still be used (Jones and Vaughan, 2010).
The amount of energy picked up by the sensors is called radiance. Radiance is
typically converted to reﬂectance, which is the ratio of the reﬂected radiation to the
==PAGE:21==
2.2
Introduction to remote sensing
10
total amount of incoming solar radiation.
Some remote sensors measure the naturally occurring solar radiation (passive
sensors) and some send out radiation and measure what comes back (active sen-
sors). Passive remote sensing either measures the radiation emitted by Earth or
solar radiation reﬂected by the surface of the Earth. The solar radiation reﬂected
by the surface is mostly shortwave radiation in the visible part of the spectrum. Ra-
diation emitted by the Earth is mainly longwave radiation. Active remote sensors
provide their own source of radiation. The sensor emits radiation in the direction of
the object of interest, and measures the amount of radiation reﬂected back. Since
active sensors do not depend on solar radiation, they can also be used at night
(Jones and Vaughan, 2010).
2.2.3
Remote sensing of vegetation
To be able to interpret the data obtained from remotely sensed images of vegeta-
tion, it is essential to understand the mechanisms and interactions that happens
between the radiation and the vegetation at a smaller scale (leaf level). As men-
tioned previously, the interaction depends on the wavelength of the incoming radi-
ation, but it also depends on the properties of the inﬂicted material. In the case of
vegetation, it depends on the structural properties, chemical composition and the
physical form of the canopy (Jones and Vaughan, 2010).
Green vegetation absorbs a large portion of the visible radiation (except for the
green wavelengths) and reﬂects a substantial part of the near infrared (NIR) ra-
diation (illustrated in Figure 1). The visible part of the spectrum (400-700 nm) is
also referred to as the photosynthetically active radiation (PAR), since these wave-
lengths are absorbed by chlorofyll or other pigments in the leaves and used for
photosynthesis. There is a sharp increase in reﬂectance right around 700 nm,
known as the red edge, which is caused by internal scattering in the leaves. In
the NIR region, there is high reﬂectance and almost no absorption, caused by the
structural properties of the leaf. In the shortwave infrared (SWIR) region, the high
reﬂectance is interrupted by water absorption bands (at around 1.4 µm and 1.9 µm
in Figure 1). The amount of radiation absorbed in these bands depends on the leaf
water content (Jones and Vaughan, 2010).
Optical remote sensing captures solar radiation reﬂected by the ﬁrst layer of the
vegetation. In a dense forest, optical remote sensing almost only records infor-
mation about the upper canopy. To get information on the lower vegetation and
the soil below, active microwave remote sensing would be more useful, since the

the angle (Jones and Vaughan, 2010).

2.3
Remote sensing of plant biodiversity
There are both direct and indirect ways to estimate biodiversity through remote
sensing. Measuring biodiversity directly implies being able to distinguish the differ-
ent species individually (Cavender-Bares et al., 2020a). This would require a high
spatial resolution of the remotely sensed image, where pixel size would match the
size of the object of interest. Indirectly, biodiversity can be measured using remote
sensing by investigating the relationship between reﬂectance and species diversity,
for example by studying spectral variability.
2.3.1
Spectral Variability Hypothesis
Spectral variability refers to how much the pixel values (such as reﬂectance in a
certain band or a vegetation index) differ from each other in one image. In other
words, it is the spatial variation in the radiation reﬂected by or emitted from a given
area.
Palmer et al. (2002) presented the hypothesis that spectral variation (or
spectral diversity) is correlated with species diversity. This is called the Spectral
==PAGE:24==
2.3
Remote sensing of plant biodiversity
13
Variability Hypothesis (SVH). It builds on the idea that spectral diversity is caused
by environmental heterogeneity, i.e. differences in landscapes or ecosystems. A
higher variety of ecosystems is expected to represent more different ecological
niches, which can support more species.
2.3.2
Importance of scale
Spectral diversity can represent species diversity in different ways depending on
the scale (Rocchini et al., 2010). Spatial scale refers both to spatial extent, i.e. the
size of the image or study area, and the size of the pixels.
In a large scale study, i.e. an image/study area that covers several different types of
ecosystems and landscapes, the spectral variation reﬂects differences in geology,
topography, land cover etc. These different landscapes provide different conditions
and different ecological niches, and are therefore expected to be linked to the
species diversity of the area. In a smaller scale study (such as one image over a
forest), the spectral diversity may be caused by different species of trees. However,
it might also be caused by a number of other factors, such as variation in biomass
production, different phenological stages in the same species and plant stress.
When using spectral diversity as a proxy for species diversity, species must be
spectrally distinct, which is not always the case. Furthermore, sometimes there
can be intraspeciﬁc variation, i.e. members of the same species that are spectrally
distinct, which adds noise to the data (Zorger et al., 2019). Pixel size can also affect
the results signiﬁcantly. If the pixel size is larger than the object of interest (such as
a tree crown), the remote sensor will measure a mixed signal from several trees,
potentially different species. It is therefore of the utmost importance to properly
deﬁne the scale (Gamon et al., 2020).
2.3.3
Rao’s Q diversity
There are several different ways of measuring spectral variation. The one that
has been investigated in the present study is Rao’s Q diversity index, which is a
measure of diversity similar to Shannon and Simpson diversity. A species diversity
index is usually calculated with species richness data, but it can also be calculated
on remote sensing data. In that case, each speciﬁc pixel value is considered a
separate species. It is therefore a measure of how many different pixel values are
present in the image and how they are distributed (species abundance). Rao’s Q
differs from the other diversity indices by also considering distance between pixel
values, in addition to richness and abundance (Rao, 1982; Rocchini et al., 2017).

2.3.4
Latest research
Since Rocchini et al. (2017) proposed the use of Rao’s Q diversity to predict
species diversity, it has been tested on various ecosystems and using various
remote sensors. Rocchini et al. (2019) suggested including temporal variation in
Rao’s Q, and calculated the index on a time series of NDVI from MODIS images
over Italy. Khare et al. (2021) tested this by looking at temporal turnover in NDVI
and MSAVI (Modiﬁed Soil-Adjusted Vegetation Index) from Landsat 7 images over
a moist deciduous forest in the Himalayas. They found that Rao’s Q was able to
distinguish between different vegetation classes. Torresani et al. (2019) calculated
Rao’s Q on NDVI time series from both Sentinel-2 and Landsat 8 images over an
alpine conifer forest in Italy, and found a strong correlation between ﬁeld based
Shannon diversity and Rao’s Q in June/July. They concluded that the correlation
between spectral diversity and species diversity is season dependent. Torresani
et al. (2020) used Rao’s Q on LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) images over
two different European forest types to test the Height Variation Hypothesis, which
is the idea that there is a correlation between species diversity and height hetero-
geneity, i.e. areas in a forest containing trees of different heights. They found a
positive correlation.
The SVH has been successfully tested in tropical forests using hyperspectral im-
agery (Somers et al., 2015; F´eret and Asner, 2014). Additionally, Sentinel-2 im-
agery has been used in tropical forests to estimate functional trait diversity (Aguirre-

2.3
Remote sensing of plant biodiversity
15
Guti´errez et al., 2021) and to discriminate different vegetation types in combination
with Sentinel-1 imagery (synthetic aperture radar (SAR)) (Erinjery et al., 2018).
The literature search did not yield any studies on the use of Rao’s Q as a spectral
diversity metric on optical imagery in tropical forests.

distance measure. The moving window calculated one value of Rao’s Q per pixel,
using the surrounding pixels in the calculation. The Rao’s Q values were then av-
eraged for each plot, resulting in one value of Rao’s Q per 1 ha plot (see Figure 6).
If all the pixels in the moving window have the same value, Rao’s Q diversity will
be 0. The upper limit of Rao’s Q depends on both the chosen distance measure
and the magnitude of the variation in the input values.
Rao’s Q can be calculated with different spectral images as input for the calcula-
tion, e.g. a vegetation index or raw spectral bands. It is also possible to calculate
multidimensional Rao’s Q for several input layers at once, by calculating the dis-
tance matrix dij as multidimensional distance. In the present project, Rao’s Q was
calculated for single layers of NDVI, EVI and NDWI, multidimensional combina-
tions of the different vegetation indices and a multidimensional combination of all
the spectral bands at once.
When both in-situ and spectral diversity indices had been calculated (one value
per 1 ha plot), their relationship was explored using Pearson correlation. The cor-
relations were evaluated based on their R2-values and p-values. The signiﬁcance
level α was set at 0.05.

DISCUSSION
5.1
Variation in in-situ and remote sensing data
Species diversity (ENS Shannon) was highest for co and br2, which is in line with
the ﬁndings of ter Steege et al. (2003), who state that the highest species diversity
in the Amazon is found in the western and central part. ec has the highest level of
precipitation, which is also associated with high species diversity (ter Steege et al.,
2003). This is also in line with the observed alpha diversity.
NDVI, NDWI and EVI were all calculated and EVI had the largest variation between
the different locations. NDVI is known to saturate in tropical forests (Darmawan and
Sofan, 2012), which also seems to be the case here. Rao’s Q is calculated using
the pairwise distance between pixels, and therefore depends on the variation in
the input data. Consequently, it is an issue to have vegetation indices that become
saturated and show very little variation. Torresani et al. (2019) calculated Rao’s Q
from NDVI over an alpine conifer forest in Italy, and their NDVI values ranged from
0.4-0.7. In the present study, NDVI ranged from 0.7-0.85, whereas EVI ranged
from 0.4-0.6. Rao’s Q calculated from EVI therefore showed the most promise in
terms of predicting diversity in tropical forests, compared to NDVI and NDWI. The
Rao’s Q values were also largest for EVI, ranging from 0.01 to 0.05. This order
of magnitude is comparable to other Rao’s Q spectral diversity studies (Torresani
et al., 2019; Khare et al., 2021).
The correlation between ENS Shannon and Rao’s Q was strongest when using all
spectral bands as input compared to using vegetation indices. This is likely related
to the saturation of the vegetation indices in tropical forests due to very dense
vegetation (Huete et al., 1997).
The correlation between spectral diversity and species diversity was negative, both
for alpha and beta diversity. The correlation was stronger with alpha diversity than
with beta diversity. The alpha diversity can be interpreted as the level of species
diversity within each plot, whereas the beta diversity is the spatial distribution of
==PAGE:49==
5.2
Spectral Variability Hypothesis
38
the different species within each plot. Beta diversity could therefore be expected
to have a stronger correlation with Rao’s Q diversity, which also describes spatial
variation. However, the results showed that the beta diversity was similar for all
the plots of the different locations, which indicates that the spectral variation is not
caused by the spatial distribution of species within each plot.
There was a positive correlation between species diversity and EVI. Since EVI is
an estimate of plant biomass, this correlation indicates that there is a link between
high levels of biomass and high species diversity.
5.2
Spectral Variability Hypothesis
All the observed correlations between the spectral diversity and species diversity
were negative. This is not in line with the SVH, which links higher spectral di-
versity to higher species diversity. Environmental heterogeneity is associated with
more different ecological niches that can support a higher species diversity (Palmer
et al., 2002). The observed negative correlation between species and spectral di-
versity indicates that species diversity is not the driver behind the spectral variation
measured in the present study. To look into what causes the negative correlation,
we need to investigate which other factors might contribute to the spectral hetero-
geneity in this case study.
5.2.1
Canopy gaps
One theory that might explain the negative correlation is that the higher spectral
diversity in areas with lower species diversity is caused by gaps in the canopy.
This hypothesis would mean that the areas with higher species diversity are more
homogeneous because of an even and dense canopy cover, whereas areas with
lower species diversity are more irregular. The results of the present study can
neither support nor reject this hypothesis, since the canopy gap density of the study
areas is unknown. The number of trees per plot is quite similar for each location
(see Figure 3), but it is unknown which of these trees belong to the understory
and which reach the upper canopy.
Gillespie (2005) found a negative correlation between the standard deviation of
NDVI and species richness in tropical dry forests. Those results are similar to the
ones of the present study, as the standard deviation is a measure of the variation in
NDVI, just as the Rao’s Q measures the variation in the vegetation indices or in the
spectral bands. Gillespie (2005) also found a positive correlation between NDVI
==PAGE:50==
5.2
Spectral Variability Hypothesis
39
and species diversity, which is in line with the positive correlation between EVI
and ENS Shannon found in this study. Gillespie (2005) hypothesized that density
of evergreen trees is the main reason behind these correlations. In tropical dry
forests, understory vegetation is denser in highly diverse areas. Therefore, if there
is a gap in the canopy in a highly diverse area, the dense understory will keep the
NDVI stable, whereas if there is a gap in a less diverse area, this might cause a
lower NDVI and thereby a higher standard deviation of NDVI. This was found to
be true for dry tropical forests, and the results from this study indicate that it might
also hold true for wet tropical forests.
Furthermore, Lucas and Carter (2008) studied species diversity using hyperspec-
tral imagery on Horn Island, Mississippi (over a gradient of woody coverage). Neg-
ative correlations between the coefﬁcient of variation for reﬂectance values and
species diversity were observed. The higher spectral variation in less diverse ar-
eas was hypothesized to be caused by increased soil exposure. Over the wood-
lands, a higher species diversity was related to a denser canopy with less variation
and less soil exposure.
These studies indicate that the negative correlation between species and spectral
diversity may be explained by irregularities and gaps in the canopy, leading to
increased soil exposure in less diverse areas.
5.2.2
Forest types
Another possible explanation for the negative correlation is that we might be look-
ing at different forest types, that have different spectral signatures. In Figure 14,
br2 and co are located in the upper left corner of the plot, while br1 and pe1 are
in the lower right corner. ec is in between the two groups. These groupings might
represent different forest types with distinct spectral signatures, and this could be
the cause of the negative correlation. If that is the case, it might be more construc-
tive to investigate the correlation between spectral and species diversity in each
forest type separately.
br1, br2 and co have the lowest elevation among the locations, while ec and pe1
are situated at higher elevations at the Andes-to-Amazon border (Table 1). Fig-
ure 16 shows that higher elevation is associated with higher spectral diversity in
these locations. Asner et al. (2014) studied forest structure over a gradient of el-
evation from lowland Amazonia to the treeline in the Peruvian Andes mountains
and found that the number of gaps in the canopy increases with elevation. This
is explained with decreasing productivity in higher altitudes, which means that it
==PAGE:51==
5.2
Spectral Variability Hypothesis
40
takes more time for a gap in the canopy to close. This could explain why spectral
diversity increased with elevation in the results of the present study.
In terms of annual precipitation, all of the locations are moist tropical forests with
high rainfall. However, ec and co have slightly higher mean annual precipitation
than the other locations, and br1 and br2 have lower mean annual precipitation
(Table 1). According to Figure 15, areas with lower rainfall have higher spectral
diversity. However, the correlation is weak, which can be contributed to the fact that
there is not a lot of variation in rainfall among the locations. Somers et al. (2015)
used hyperspectral imagery and LiDAR to predict species diversity in dry, moist
and wet tropical forests. They found that while spectral variability was generally
highest for the moist forests, spectral variability in the visible part of the spectrum
was highest for the dry forests. This was hypothesized to be due to the trees max-
imizing photosynthesis when water is available. In contrast, in wet forests, where
water availability is more constant, the level of photosynthesis would be expected
to be more stable, causing less spectral variability. All the locations in the present
study are wet forests, however, there is a small tendency that areas with less pre-
cipitation have higher spectral variability. A possible explanation could be that the
trees in drier locations vary more in their photosynthesis activity. Including ﬁeld
data from a larger gradient of precipitation could help investigate this hypothesis
further. It has not been investigated which spectral bands contribute most to the
spectral variability in this study.
In summary, we see that in this case areas with higher spectral diversity generally
have higher elevation and lower precipitation and that areas with lower spectral
diversity are situated at lower elevation and have higher precipitation.
5.2.3
Scale
As discussed in section 2.3.2, the SVH can be justiﬁed in two different ways.
When looking at larger study areas, spectral diversity might stem from environ-
mental heterogeneity, i.e. diversity of landscape or ecosystem types. A variety of
habitats is expected to support a variety of species. When looking at smaller study
areas, that perhaps only include one type of ecosystem, spectral diversity might
be caused by a diversity of functional traits and growth structure of the vegeta-
tion, depending on the spectral bands examined. An area with a larger diversity in
functional traits can be expected to hold a larger species diversity as well. Con-
sequently, the mechanisms driving the SVH can be different at different spatial
scales.
==PAGE:52==
5.2
Spectral Variability Hypothesis
41
Oldeland et al. (2010) studied alpha diversity in three different study sites of open
savannah vegetation in Namibia, and found that the relation between spectral di-
versity and species diversity was scale-dependent, with the correlation improving
with increasing study area size. When the study area is bigger, there is higher
spectral variability and higher species diversity, leading to a bigger range of diver-
sity. This is supported by Imai et al. (2012), who studied tree species diversity in
Bornean tropical forests, and argued that there is a higher probability of including
a small, highly diverse forest patch in a larger study area. In contrast, Marselis
et al. (2020) studied pantropical tree species richness using LiDAR and argued
that a larger study area includes more rare species, which do not contribute much
to the spectral signal of the canopy, but they do contribute to the species richness
numbers. In the present study, this effect is minimized by using diversity indices
(Shannon and Simpson), which take both species richness and abundance into
account.
In the present project, the effect of the study area size on the correlation has not
been investigated. The ﬁeld data comes from study areas of 1 ha, and Rao’s Q
is calculated for each pixel within these 1 ha plots on a moving window with the
size of 3x3 pixels. That means that Rao’s Q considers the spectral variation within
an area of 60x60 m surrounding the pixel in question. It is important to consider
what the spectral variation in a moving window of 60x60 m over a tropical forest
might tell us. The ratio between the pixel size and the size of the object of interest
will inﬂuence the interpretation of the spectral signal. In this case, each pixel is
20x20 m. The size of individual tree crowns is unknown, but it might be smaller
than one pixel. This means that each pixel will represent a mixed signal coming
from different trees (potentially different species). The bigger the pixels, the more
mixed will the signal be. When the signal is mixed, the group of trees in each pixel
must be spectrally distinct from the groups of trees in the adjacent pixels to cause
spectral variability.
When planning a study where remote sensing is used to predict species diversity,
it is important to be aware of what kind of spectral variation the species diversity
can be expected to be correlated with. For example, is the species diversity in the
study area expected to be driven by differences in landscapes and habitats? If so,
a larger study area might be required to detect these differences. However, if the
species diversity is expected to be driven by factors that are not visible in a satellite
image (such as soil quality or climate), the species diversity might not be correlated
with differences in landscape. Different types of landscapes can be species poor
==PAGE:53==
5.3
Temporal variation in Rao’s Q
42
or species rich, so the sole fact that there is a variety of landscapes does not
always mean a higher species diversity (Schmidtlein and Fassnacht, 2017). If the
species diversity is not expected to be caused by differences in landscape, it might
be more feasible to ﬁnd a correlation between functional trait diversity and spectral
diversity. When studying functional trait diversity, it might be necessary to use a
ﬁner spatial resolution, and therefore also focus on a smaller study area, to be able
to detect differences between species. For these reasons, it is very important to
consider scale and the expected spectral variation when planning a study.
5.3
Temporal variation in Rao’s Q
The temporal variation in Rao’s Q calculated from EVI time series for the available
Sentinel-2 images for ec and pe1 (2016-2018) was larger than the variation in
Rao’s Q between the individual plots of each location. Under the assumption that
species diversity remains more or less constant from one month to another, the
temporal variation in Rao’s Q should not be larger than the difference between the
plots, if it is to be linked to species diversity. On the other hand, temporal variation
in spectral diversity might be caused by seasonal changes, such as ﬂowering or
a change in leaf pigments. If this is the case, the temporal variation is driven
by functional diversity, not species diversity. This is supported by the observed
negative correlation between species and spectral diversity, which demonstrated
that spectral variation is not directly caused by species diversity in this case.
Torresani et al. (2019) calculated Rao’s Q from NDVI using Sentinel-2 images over
an alpine conifer forest in Italy and found that the correlation between species di-
versity and spectral diversity varied signiﬁcantly throughout the year. Some months
had higher R2-values than others, underlining the importance of the timing of the
chosen remote sensing data. Schmidtlein and Fassnacht (2017) studied spectral
diversity in MODIS images over the south of Germany and also found the strength
of the correlation between spectral and species diversity to be dependent on the
time of the year.
Khare et al. (2021) examined temporal turnover in three different vegetation classes
in the Himalayas by calculating the coefﬁcient of variation on time series of Rao’s
Q, and were able to distinguish between different vegetation types. Schmidtlein
and Fassnacht (2017) tried to use seasonal variation in combination with spa-
tial variation to predict species diversity over a large area in Southern Germany,
however, the correlation with seasonal and spatial variation was weaker than the
correlation with spatial variation alone. To investigate whether seasonal variation
==PAGE:54==
5.4
Applicability of Rao’s Q on Sentinel-2 images in tropical forests
43
can help predict species diversity in tropical forests, more complete time series are
needed over all ﬁeld locations. This is a challenge when using optical imagery due
to the frequent cloud cover over wet tropical forests.
Pouteau et al. (2018) studied correlations between NDVI and species richness in
tropical forests on New Caledonia, using both single-image NDVI and time series
NDVI metrics. They argued that when the NDVI-species diversity relationship is not
well understood, it is preferable to do a single-image screening instead of using
time series NDVI metrics.
Furthermore, they found that the best single-image
NDVI to predict tree species richness on New Caledonia was at the end of the dry
season.
These studies underline the importance of investigating which time of the year the
correlation between spectral variables and species richness is the highest in the
Amazon. The importance of the timing might depend on how large the seasonal
variation is in the study area. The temporal variation in the present study also
showed that the plots within one location shift in ranking. For example, the plot
which had the highest Rao’s Q in June 2016 in ec, no longer has the highest Rao’s
Q in August 2017. This indicates that Rao’s Q might not be stable over time. Even
if there is seasonal variation in the locations, plots within one location would be
expected to behave similarly.
5.4
Applicability of Rao’s Q on Sentinel-2 images in tropical
forests
5.4.1
Choice of spectral diversity metric
The choice of the spectral diversity metric might affect the results. Rao’s Q is
calculated using the distance between pixel values and their abundance, and for
multidimensional Rao’s Q, a multidimensional measure of distance is calculated.
The results from this study showed that the use of Rao’s Q on Sentinel-2 images
over the Amazon is able to capture some spectral variation, but not variation in
species. Since the present study did not test any other spectral diversity metric,
it is not possible to say whether another metric could have better captured the
species variation.
Schmidtlein and Fassnacht (2017) compared two different metrics to measure
spectral diversity on MODIS images over the south of Germany; Euclidean dis-
tance and a k-means classiﬁcation method.
The k-means based method was
found to be more stable throughout the year. This was hypothesized to be due
==PAGE:55==
5.4
Applicability of Rao’s Q on Sentinel-2 images in tropical forests
44
to the Euclidean distance not taking into account the abundance of different pixel
values, only the difference. Rao’s Q is not included in that study, however, it would
be interesting to test Rao’s Q against the k-means classiﬁcation method. Rao’s
Q is also based on Euclidean distance, but it does take abundance into account,
and can therefore be expected to perform better than Euclidean distance on its
own. Using a classiﬁcation method may also be useful in dividing the locations
into different forest types, depending on the number of clusters.
5.4.2
Choice of sensor
The aim of this study was to assess the applicability of Rao’s Q on optical imagery
in tropical forests. Rao’s Q measures the spectral variation in the input data, and
can therefore only ﬁnd spectral diversity if there is sufﬁcient variation in the input
data. The negative correlation found in this study questions the ability of Sentinel-2
images to detect variation between tree species in tropical forests when used on
their own.
No studies using solely Sentinel-2 images to predict the tree species diversity in
tropical forests have been identiﬁed. Vaglio Laurin et al. (2016) looked at simulated
Sentinel-2 images in tropical forests, and they were able to distinguish between
forest types and between areas with different dominant species. Aguirre-Guti´errez
et al. (2021) used Sentinel-2 images in combination with climate and soil data to
model canopy functional traits in tropical forests using ﬁeld data from Australia,
South East Asia, Africa and South America. Both Vaglio Laurin et al. (2016) and
Aguirre-Guti´errez et al. (2021) found that the image texture features were impor-
tant when using Sentinel-2 to distinguish forest types and functional traits. Texture
features measure the spatial distribution of pixel values within the study area (Har-
alick et al. (1973), as cited by St-Louis et al. (2009)). It is outside the scope of
this study to investigate the texture features of the images, however, it could be
included in a future study.
One of the reasons Sentinel-2 has been chosen for this study is that the data
is freely available. Being able to predict species diversity with free data would
facilitate identiﬁcation of biodiversity hotspots globally, increase accessibility and
potentially guide local conservation policies. More costly approaches have been
able to estimate the species diversity in tropical forests. Hyperspectral imagery
(F´eret and Asner, 2014; Sch¨afer et al., 2016) and the combination of hyperspectral
imagery with LiDAR (Somers et al., 2015) have proven useful in detecting tree
species diversity in tropical forests.
==PAGE:56==
5.5
Uncertainties and limitations
45
In the interest of exploring other open access options, Sentinel-1, a C-band syn-
thetic aperture radar (SAR) mission, could be tested. The advantage of using radar
imagery is that it can be used regardless of cloud cover. It would therefore be pos-
sible to obtain a more complete time series to study seasonal variation over all the
locations. Bae et al. (2019) use Sentinel-1 images to map forest biodiversity of
12 different functional groups in temperate forests across Europe. They found that
Sentinel-1 showed promise in predicting forest maturity and species composition.
Another option is to combine optical and SAR imagery, e.g. Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-
2. Erinjery et al. (2018) used classiﬁcation methods on Sentinel-1 bands, Sentinel-
2 bands, NDVI and texture features to distinguish between different vegetation
types in the Western Ghats in India (several different types of forest). They found
that the combination of Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 was promising.
The results from other studies indicate that it could be beneﬁcial to try predicting
tree species diversity in tropical forests using combinations of Sentinel-2 bands
with supplemental information, such as texture features, Sentinel-1 bands or cli-
mate and soil data.
5.5
Uncertainties and limitations
5.5.1
Unknown species
As one of the initial steps in the processing of the in-situ data, the unidentiﬁed
trees were removed from the data set since they do not contribute to the diversity
indices. The majority of the sampled trees in pe2 were unidentiﬁed (see Figure 3)
and pe2 was therefore removed from the analysis. br1, ec and pe1 also had some
unidentiﬁed trees, which might interfere with the results. The species accumulation
curves (Figure 4) showed that it is likely that the actual species diversity of these
areas cannot be accurately estimated from this data. An insufﬁcient number of
trees have been sampled (/identiﬁed). However, since there was a distinction be-
tween the species accumulation curves of the different locations, it was assumed
that the calculated diversity indices would still serve as reasonable estimates of
the actual diversity. The limited amount of trees sampled does represent a ma-
jor limitation in this study, as it is uncertain whether the actual species diversity is
substantially higher than what has been estimated here.

5.5.2
Field data collection
There are a few uncertainties associated with the collection of the ﬁeld data. First
of all, the data has been collected by two different organizations, TEAM Network
(2016) and ForestGEO (2020). Different data collectors may cause differences
in the data sets. co differs from the other locations by having more 1 ha plots,
but a smaller spatial extent since all the plots are located right next to each other.
Having more plots than the other locations may cause a bias in the correlations.
The fact that the locations have different study area extents (see Figure 19-24 in
Appendix A), may cause differences in the total amount of species present within
those areas.
Most of the ﬁeld data was collected in 2016, however some of it was collected as
far back as 2007. This also constitutes a potential uncertainty, since there might
have been some changes in species diversity since 2007. For the sake of this
study, the species diversity is assumed to stay relatively constant.
Additionally, for the ﬁve locations sampled by TEAM Network (2016), the orienta-
tion of the plots are unknown. This is thought to be a minor uncertainty since both
the species and the spectral diversity are similar among the plots in each location.
However, it could potentially have an effect and should therefore be taken into con-
sideration. There are also uncertainties related to the GPS measurements in the
ﬁeld. When measuring plots of 1 ha, inaccuracies in GPS measurements have a
higher impact on the results than with bigger plots.
The height of the trees and the canopy gap density have not been recorded. If
the height was known, it would be easier to estimate which trees could be ex-
pected to be visible on a satellite image. It is unknown how much of the recorded
diversity is present in the understory of the forest, and how much is in the upper
canopy. Knowing the canopy gap density would help in understanding the causes
of spectral variation.
5.5.3
Cloud cover
Initially, the aim of the study was to examine the seasonal variation in reﬂectance
and to investigate whether this was related to species diversity. This was not fea-
sible in all locations due to extended cloudy periods in the Amazon. The idea was
to include one Sentinel-2 image per month for each location, but many of the lo-
cations only had a handful of useful images per year. The temporal variation was
therefore only assessed in two of the six locations (pe1 and ec), and even for those
==PAGE:58==
5.6
Recommendations for further research
47
two, the time series were not complete with one image per month. The temporal
variation seen in these results are therefore only indicative of the true change that
happens in the course of a year.
Furthermore, due to the frequent cloud cover, it was not possible to obtain images
from 2016 from the same month for all locations. The images from 2016 range
from June to October (see exact dates in Table 2). The analysis of the temporal
variation showed that the ranking of the plots within a location (in terms of magni-
tude of Rao’s Q) changed from month to month. Using satellite data from different
months in the same correlation therefore represents an uncertainty. However, for
2017, all the images were from a period of approximately two weeks (July/August),
and the correlations between ENS Shannon and Rao’s Q (EVI) from 2016 and
2017 were similar. This indicates that using images from June to October instead
of from only one month may not be signiﬁcant for the resulting correlation.
5.6
Recommendations for further research
5.6.1
Classiﬁcation of forest types
The aim of this study was to extrapolate species diversity from ﬁeld plots to the
entire Amazon using simple models based on satellite imagery. However, while
the results indicate that it is not possible to use Rao’s Q on Sentinel-2 images for
this purpose, Rao’s Q (or spectral variation in general) might prove itself a useful
tool to divide the locations into groups that share similar characteristics. Once the
locations have been grouped, the SVH can be tested on each forest type. For this
purpose, it is recommended to try a classiﬁcation method (e.g. k-means clustering)
as a spectral diversity metric and compare with Rao’s Q.
Furthermore, it is recommended to continue the investigation of the characteristics
of the different groupings based on Rao’s Q values. In this study, the effects of
mean elevation and mean annual precipitation were discussed. Other factors could
be included in the characterization, such as soil data and canopy gap density.
This could help in understanding the mechanisms behind the observed negative
correlation between in-situ diversity and spectral diversity.
5.6.2
Data acquisition
Field data
In this study, the ﬁeld data was not adequate to accurately estimate the species
==PAGE:59==
5.6
Recommendations for further research
48
diversity of the different locations. It would therefore greatly improve the reliability
of the results if more extensive ﬁeld data was collected, both in terms of more
identiﬁed trees per location, but also including more locations from different tropical
forests.
Remote sensing data
The results of this study showed that it is not possible to detect variations in species
diversity from Sentinel-2 images alone. It is therefore recommended to supplement
the Sentinel-2 images with additional data. This could be Sentinel-1 images, tex-
ture features or climate and soil data.

CONCLUSION
The correlation between species diversity and spectral diversity was negative with
an R2-value of 0.60 (p < 0.05). This result was not in line with the original hypoth-
esis, stating that higher species diversity is linked to higher spectral diversity. It is
hypothesized that the negative correlation is caused by gaps in the canopy in less
diverse areas or differences in forest type among the locations. It is recommended
to consider both these factors in future studies. The most notable limitation of the
study was estimated to be the unidentiﬁed trees in the ﬁeld plots. If these trees
were identiﬁed, it might change the species diversity indices and the correlation
with spectral diversity.
Correlations between spectral diversity and species diversity were explored with
different Rao’s Q values, both calculated on single-layer and multi-layer inputs. Us-
ing Rao’s Q calculated from all the spectral bands of Sentinel-2 led to the strongest
correlation with species diversity. Based on the negative correlation between spec-
tral diversity and species diversity, Rao’s Q used on Sentinel-2 images over tropical
forests can be concluded to successfully detect some variation in the canopy, but
not variations between different tree species.
Temporal variation in Rao’s Q (EVI) was investigated for two of the ﬁeld locations.
The Rao’s Q values were not stable over time between the plots, which highlights
the importance of the timing of the remote sensing data.
In conclusion, the SVH did not hold when using Rao’s Q diversity on Sentinel-2 im-
ages over the ﬁve ﬁeld locations in the Amazon. No clear conclusion can be drawn
as to what causes the negative correlation between spectral and species diversity.
Further studies should be carried out with more complete ﬁeld data and potentially
supplementing the Sentinel-2 data with Sentinel-1 images or other environmental
data such as climate or soil information.
