Abstract 
Organisations must adapt to their environment to remain viable, if not organisations can fail. This thesis 
describes measures that organisations can take in a globalising and increasingly complex world with 
added uncertainty. Focussing on the economic measures of globalisation in respect to their decision-
making processes. The Global Financial Crisis (2008) serves as a reference point. The reason here for 
being is that it is a recent event that created a large amount of uncertainty. 
A qualitative empirical study has been performed to create a better understanding of the measures 
organisations have taken, and what can be learned from these initiatives for future purposes.  
The analysis involved comparing the literature to what has been applied in practise, to explore where 
they are in line with one another, where discrepancies lie, and the reasoning behind this.  
We learned that there are a large amount of external sources of uncertainty, mainly political and the 
market. The theory is summarised in our expectations for the empirical study. Research found that all 
the expectations were met. Although not all the expectations were equally substantial. A remarkable 
outcome was that not all organisations were affected, despite the fact that during a downturn it was 
expected that all organisations would face a decline in profits. Supposing data-driven decision-making 
is common practise, from our sample we concluded that 21% still uses experience or gut feeling in 
decision-making. From our results, we can also conclude that HR plays a major facilitating role in 
decision-making.  
 
Keywords: Managerial decision-making, decision-making under uncertainty, decision-making theory, 
recession, global financial crisis, economic downturn, qualitative empirical study, semi-structured 
interview, data coding, sources of uncertainty, organisational reactions to uncertainty, market 
environment characterisation, market uncertainty, political uncertainty

1 Introduction 
To remain viable, a great deal of organisational theorists emphasise that organisations must adapt to 
their environment. If not, organisations can fail, like Kodak, that failed to adapt to their environment 
and consequently went bankrupt (Mui, 2012).  The environment is defined as the totality of physical 
and social factors that are taken directly into consideration in the decision-making behaviour of 
individuals in the organisation. One of the central issues in this process is coping with uncertainty 
(Crozier, 1965; Thompson, 1967). 
1.1 Motivation 
The biggest organisations are no longer national firms, but multinational corporations with subsidiaries 
in many countries. More than ever, organisations all over the world are globalising. Many authors have 
attempted to define globalisation in a variety of ways. Al-Rodan & Stoudman (2006) proposed the 
following definition: “Globalisation is a process that encompasses the causes, course, and 
consequences of transnational and transcultural integration of human and non-human activities”.  
Examples of globalised firms are abound, ranging from natural resource companies such as Royal Dutch 
Shell to shipping companies like Maersk. Through increased globalisation, the world has become more 
complex inducing extra uncertainty in the decision-making process. This topic has also been highlighted 
by Friedman (2005), where globalisation is changing the core concepts of the economy. This thesis 
shall focus on the economic measures of globalisation in respect to their decision-making processes. 
Important drivers for globalisation are the world foreign direct investment inflows, FDI. Figure 1 below 
depicts world foreign direct investment inflows (USD billions).  
 
Figure 1: World FDI Inflows (USD billions). Source: European Commission 
The rationale behind this thesis is built on the presumption that globalisation brings with it a vast amount 
of additional factors to take into consideration in the decision-making process (there are not only local 
==PAGE:20==
2 
 
factors, but the whole world is a market and thus also the differences in cultures). To stay competitive, 
organisations must be able to make good decisions in a swift manner. This poses a paradox, as one can 
imagine this context spawns uncertainty and the ability to make good decisions diminishes with greater 
uncertainty. 
1.2 Aim of the thesis 
There exists a vast amount of scientific papers, journals, and articles available on the subject of 
uncertainty and uncertainty linked to decision-making. However, what is lacking is a study that ties it 
all together and compares theory to what is actually being done in practice. Our contribution and aim is 
to address this and point out where the discrepancies lie, what organisations can learn from each other 
and recommend further interesting areas for research.  
1.3 Opportunities for dealing with uncertainty 
As this subject is not an exact science, the ambiguous concept proves hard to grasp a full understanding. 
The purpose of the extensive literature review is to establish a good understanding of how scholars 
believe uncertainty should be dealt with in managerial decision-making. There are numerous reasons to 
alleviate uncertainty. By mitigating it, an organisation shall become faster in responding to problems, 
which could provide an edge over competitors and/or more benefit for its stakeholders (customers, 
suppliers, shareholders etc.).  
1.4 Logic behind the recent increase of global uncertainty  
The levels of uncertainty have increased over the past years due to the Global Financial Crisis (GFC) 
of 2008 according to the Economic Policy Uncertainty  (Baker, Bloom, & Davis, 2015). In short, banks 
and traders took advantage of a lack of regulations in the US housing market. What followed was the 
collapse of the US housing market, financed by huge corporations. The collapse of the big corporations 
forced the US economy into a recession. The interconnectedness of the global market saw other markets 
around the world collapse and fall into recession too. Banks and governments have requested and 
received bailouts to keep their economies afloat and to prevent a complete meltdown of the economic 
system. It is hard to put a number to the cost of the GFC. In July 2014, three economists at the Federal 
Reserve Bank of Dallas published their work (T. Atkinson, Luttrell, & Rosenblum, 2013). They 
estimate the cost of the crisis to be at a bare minimum of 20.000 USD for every American. Adding 
broader impacts such as workers well-being could raise this figure to 120.000 USD.  
The GFC has been felt almost throughout the entire world. To determine and identify sources of 
uncertainty in managerial decision-making, the GFC shall be used as a case study. The literature review 
shall also consist of research into the GFC, to provide a good understanding of how organisations could 
have been affected by it. Moreover, when comparing theory to practice, with the GFC being a relatively 
==PAGE:21==
3 
 
recent event with major impact, data on the subject should still be ‘fresh’ in the minds of people 
(employees) to help further the empirical research.   
When the economy is booming it is important to have managers that make the right decisions for 
sustaining and growing the company. This is even more so during a downturn. Managers have to deal 
with regular uncertainty, add the uncertainty of the market in its current state, and be able to make the 
right decisions at the right times to ensure not being victimised by the crisis. This is the reason why 
research into the topic of uncertainty in managerial decision-making is of the utmost importance, to 
save firms from drowning, to get the market back on its feet and learning lessons to prevent such an 
event happening again.  
1.5 Research focus 
Research in the form of an empirical study will show if and how organisations adopt guidelines written 
by scholars and academics on the topic of uncertainty in managerial decision-making. There is little to 
no literature that investigates what is occurring in practise since the GFC.  
Organisations are lead from a strategic level; boards of directors develop the strategy for the 
organisation. Consequently, this is where we expect to find out how the organisations have reacted to 
uncertainty best. Tactical level managers are of importance too, they shall be more able to explain to us 
how they have interpreted their firm’s strategy, how they have translated it into attainable goals and 
what methods they have adopted for coping with uncertainty.  
The research focus is broad. International organisations are preferred but smaller organisations shall be 
interviewed too. All participants shall be categorised for comparative reasons. Different kinds of 
organisations in various parts of the world may be able to learn how to cope with various types of 
uncertainty from each other.  
1.6 Structure of the thesis 
Section (1) should incite the reader to read the rest of the thesis. This will be done by clarifying the 
importance of the problem we are seeking to address. Section (2) shall consist of extensive research 
into the GFC and all literature surrounding the topic of uncertainty that has been identified as important 
to our research. Section (3) shall provide the reader with all our methods used to study this subject to 
the extent that it could be reproduced. Section (4) presents the reader with the findings of our empirical 
research. Section (5) discusses these findings and its limitations. Section (6) combines knowledge 
gained from (4) and (5) and draws conclusions from our research. Finally, section (7) proposes remedial 
action to help solve the problem and gives direction for future investigations in related topics.

2 Literature review 
This section shall present all the relevant information that should help us theoretically answer our 
research question, namely: ‘How can companies prepare themselves for uncertainty in managerial 
decision-making processes in a globalising world?’ We start by studying a recent major source of 
uncertainty in the global market (2.1). Then the concept of uncertainty is tackled (2.2). Thereafter, focus 
shall be turned towards organisational reactions to uncertainty (2.3) and how the literature proposes 
decisions be made under uncertainty (2.4). After gathering and processing all this information, our 
expectations for the empirical study are presented (2.5).  
2.1 The 2008 Global Financial Crisis 
To start with, this paragraph shall briefly explain the events leading up to the crisis, how and when the 
crisis hit, and how it spread from one point, all throughout the world.  
It is hard to pinpoint the exact starting point of the crisis, as there were numerous events leading up to 
it that are of importance. Typically, the collapse of the American property market is placed at the starting 
point (Commission, 2009). The beginning of 2007 in the USA is where the market started feeling the 
first effects of the crisis due to the housing market bubble bursting. The section below explains how 
this occurred. 
2.1.1 The collapse of the US housing market 
The following information was obtained from Dimartino & Duca (2007), Jarvis (2012) and McKenna 
(2011). Imagine a family wanting to buy a house. This is considered a good investment as house prices 
were always on the rise Figure 1Figure 2. The family goes to a broker; the broker in turn sells the 
mortgage on to an investor whilst claiming a fee. So far, all parties are happy, the family has a roof over 
their head, the broker has received a fee for selling on the mortgage, and the investor is now receiving 
mortgage payments from the homeowners. Investors then decided to buy as many mortgages as they 
could, by doing so receiving all the mortgage payments.  
To turn over even more profit, the investors decided to sell the mortgages to other parties. They did this 
by slicing the mortgages into three (safe, okay, risky), and boxing it together, giving it the term 
Collaterized Debt Obligation (CDO). CDOs operate in the following manner: As money from the 
mortgage payments comes in it fills the top safe tray. Once the safe tray is full, mortgage payments start 
filling up the okay tray. Whatever is left spills into the final risky tray. Therefore, if homeowners stop 
paying their mortgage payments (default), less money comes in, and the bottom tray may not be filled. 
To compensate this phenomenon, the risky tray yields a 10% Rate of Return (ROR). The safe tray yields 
about 4%. To ensure the safeness of the safe tray, banks will insure it (for a fee), called credit default 
swap. The reason the banks do this is to receive high ratings from credit agencies. AAA for the safe 
==PAGE:23==
5 
 
tray and BBB for the second tray, the risky tray went unrated. Mortgages from the safe tray were sold 
to investors only interested in safe investments. Those from the okay tray were sold to other bankers. 
Hedge funds and other risk takers invested in the risky tray. 
The investment bankers made a lot of money by doing this. They had found a good investment for their 
money. Even the safe tray yielded 3% more compared to the safest investments (treasury bills were at 
1% to stimulate economic growth). They were so pleased they wanted more CDO slices, however the 
mortgage broker was now unable to find any other families that qualify for a mortgage, as everybody 
in this category already owns a house.  
The investors had an idea, when homeowners default on their mortgage, the investor gains the house. 
Prior to 2007, houses were always increasing in value, see Figure 2, and since they were covered in the 
homeowners default, lenders were able to add more risk to mortgages. They decided to split mortgages 
in two. Prime mortgages, and sub-prime mortgages. These sub-prime mortgages were the most 
complex, and sold to lower-class people. They needed no proof of income and no down payment. This 
is the turning point, the moment the housing bubble burst. 
Eventually everybody started defaulting on his or her mortgages. The consequence of this is that the 
investment banker is no longer receiving mortgage payments but left with property. At this point, the 
market was flooded with property, so much so that house prices were in fact declining. This poses an 
interesting problem for prime-mortgage payers. For example, they could now be paying 300k USD 
Figure 2: US house prices 
==PAGE:24==
6 
 
mortgages when their property value has dropped down to 90k USD. Instead of paying overpriced 
mortgages, they now too default on their loans.  
The investment bankers now started to realise that their business was not as lucrative as before, and try 
to sell the mortgages. However, other investors and bankers are not naive; they have found themselves 
in the same position. The investment bankers have borrowed billions to buy the mortgages that are now 
worth nothing and are incapable of paying this back. The same goes for those that have invested in the 
CDOs. The entire financial system now starts collapsing and everyone involved is filing for bankruptcy.  
2.1.2 Spread to other countries 
Financial institutions all over the world had invested in the complex CDOs without fully understanding 
how risky they were. Even until this day analysts find it almost impossible to fully understand them. 
CDOs initially started in the USA, but the other housing markets soon followed the same concept. Real 
estate markets were built up like an unstable house of cards, once the American market fell, it dragged 
the rest of the world down too (Commission, 2009). It is not the first time a turn-around in a small part 
of the financial system has toppled the entire structure. Such events have happened before, in Japan and 
the Nordic countries in the early ‘90s and the Asian crisis in the late ‘90s. Countries in emerging Asia 
were probably less exposed to the financial crisis than countries with more mature economies because 
these countries had adopted relatively conservative financial practices after the 1997–98 Asian financial 
crisis (Hale & Kennedy, 2012; Hale, 2011). 
 
The alarm sounded in Paris. In the month of August 2007, BNP Paribas discovered that many of its 
investment funds were filled with these ambiguous insecurities. Other banks around the world followed. 
The British bank Northern Rock fell in September of 2007, creating the first great ripples of uncertainty 
throughout the global market. Because of banks realising how troubled their investments were, 
counterparty risk between banks increased dramatically, as reflected in soaring rates charged by banks 
to each other for short-term loans, see Figure 3. The vertical axis presents the BPS, the horizontal axis 
represents the date.  
==PAGE:25==
7 
 
 
Figure 3: Rates charged between banks. Source: Reuters, EcoWin 
When the financial services firm Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy in September 2008. The ripples 
of uncertainty transformed into tsunamis, affecting organisations all over the world. The last quarter of 
2008 is seen as the start of the GFC. At this moment in time, European countries and banks were being 
heavily affected. To keep the markets afloat, the EU leaders enacted a €200 billion stimulus plan. 
However, the stimulus package is not enough. 
In December 2009, Greece’s debt was 113% of its GDP, and Greek bank ratings and sovereign debt 
drop. In May of 2010, EU officials form a €500 billion fund to protect the European economy from 
further crisis. In November of that year, Ireland requests a bailout. Followed by another request for 
bailout in May of 2011 by Portugal and Greece. Italy now introduces austerity measures. In October 
2011, England pumps €75 billion into its economy through quantitative easing (printing money). In 
November 2012, the Spanish banks received bailouts (BBC, 2012). 
One must recognise that these bailouts are not long-term solutions; the debt will have to be repaid over 
time. Other measures such as quantitative easing, also known as printing money will only devalue the 
currency (e.g. the Zimbabwean Dollar in 2000 or Germany in the 1920s, referred to as hyperinflation). 
The reason money is being printed is to prevent a depression like the one we encountered in the 1930s. 
Printing money is just buying time and at this point, specialists are not even sure if we would survive 
without it. The debts will eventually have to be repaid. This GFC is unlike any we have ever encountered 
before and thus, uncharted territory. It is the deepest, most synchronous across countries and most global 
crisis since the Great Depression of the 1930s (Commission, 2009). Another noteworthy fact is that 
previous bubbles, like the Dot-com bubble, Japan’s bubble economy of the 1980s, the Florida real estate 
bubble etc. all emerged naturally from the stock market. The one we have encountered now is mostly 
due to lack of regulations and irresponsible banking. From the beginning of the actual recession 
(recession definition: two negative quarters of negative economic growth, measured by the country’s 
==PAGE:26==
8 
 
gross domestic product, GDP) until its nadir in 2009 it was responsible for the destruction of nearly $20 
trillion worth of financial assets owned by U.S. households. During this time, the U.S. unemployment 
rate rose from 4,7 percent to 10 percent. By 2010, college graduates fortunate enough to find a job were 
on average earning 17,5 percent less than their counterparts before the crisis (Goodwin, Harris, Nelson, 
Roach, & Torras, 2014).  
2.1.3 Affecting all industries 
Bankers, investors and traders failed to recognise the interconnectedness of the global market. 
(McKenna, 2011). McKenna further explains how the world is interlinked through banking; it is 
difficult to exist in complete isolation. Import and export are major factors in a countries worth. 
Moreover, small scares in one place can cause panic in other parts of the world (financial ripple effect). 
A good example of this interconnectedness is Iceland. Iceland allowed its banking sector to grow tenfold 
in terms of foreign assets compared to that of its own GDP. By doing so, Iceland’s survival was solely 
dependent on conditions abroad. The systematic risk of its three largest banks had also gone beyond its 
own borders as they had already fully expanded within their own. By opening up branches abroad, in 
particular the UK and the Netherlands, and offering higher interest rates than comparable banks in these 
countries. As Icelandic banks collapsed and faced major liquidity problems due to the American market 
collapsing, the UK invoked an anti-terrorism act to freeze the UK assets. Iceland as a country went into 
shutdown. Due in large part to reliance on its relatively large financial sector (Gissurarson, 2013; Viral 
V Acharya, 2011). 
This interconnectedness can further be explained by the term circular flow. As the construction industry 
fell, it was followed by huge layoffs. Unemployment is bad for the economy because people are no 
longer able to spend money. This is felt through the entire economy. As people still employed become 
aware of the financial crisis affecting everybody, they go into a saving-mode, only spending money on 
absolute necessities, effectively further worsening the problem. In addition to this, people are also being 
hit by austerity cuts, which translate into longer hours and less pay for employees. Consequently, more 
and more firms are filing for bankruptcy and all businesses resemble dominos waiting to be knocked 
down. Figure 4 below shows the unemployment percentage rate in the USA from January 2001 – 
January 2015. As can be derived, the unemployment rate has been gradually declining since 2010. 
However, self-employment has been on the rise, see Figure 5. Unemployment rates need to be combined 
with other factors before sound conclusions can be drawn and to prevent a false senses of optimism.

2.1.4 Long-term effects of this crisis 
Nobody is capable of assessing when the crisis will end, the global economic system is built on so many 
unstable variables that it impossible to comprehend the sheer complexity of it and how many relapses 
will occur before we are sailing in safe waters again (McKenna, 2011). The next recession could already 
be starting. Similar signs to the US real estate market are appearing in China, the emergence of empty

2.2 Uncertainty 
The first step is to analyse how scholars define the term uncertainty, and which definition is most 
relevant to this thesis. Uncertainty and risk are often treated synonymous (Gough, 1988). A clear 
distinction has been made within scholarly work by Knight (1921). Risky decisions are those decisions 
where the probabilities of the various possible outcomes are objective or known (for example, gambling 
on the flip of a coin or a roulette wheel). Uncertainty occurs where the decision-maker has to estimate 
or infer the probabilities of the various outcomes happening (for example, the weather being sunny for 
a barbecue at the weekend) (Beresford & Sloper, 2008).

2.2.2 Environmental uncertainty 
Perceived environmental uncertainty occurs when decision makers perceive unpredictability from their 
environment (Milliken, 1987). This occurs when discrepancies are found between required and 
available information. The GFC created a lot of uncertainty and thus, environmental uncertainty. 
Ripples sent through the market combined with circular flow has made most environments uncertain. 
For example, declining share prices for one firm could spell bad news for its suppliers. If they rely too 
heavily on this company, it could spell disaster for them, and their employees and their suppliers. It is 
easy to imagine how this knock-on effect continues and influences the rest of the market. 
Milliken (1987) describes three types of perceived uncertainty about the environment: state, effect and 
response. For the remainder of this thesis, when perceived uncertainty is mentioned it shall be targeted 
as the uncertainty perceived by the person (observer) unless mentioned otherwise.

Duncan (1972) identifies characteristics of the environment that contribute to decision unit members 
experiencing uncertainty in decision-making. Two dimensions of the environment are identified. The 
simple-complex dimension is defined as the number of factors taken into consideration in decision-
making, a homogeneous environment. The static-dynamic dimension is viewed as the degree to which 
these factors in the decision unit’s environment remain the same over time or are in a continual process 
of change, a heterogeneous environment. In his research, Duncan (1972) studies twenty-two decision 
groups in three manufacturing and three R&D organisations. Identification of the environment’s 
dimensions is necessary in order to make predictions about the kinds of environments in which different 
levels of perceived uncertainty are expected to exist. Results indicate that individuals in decision units 
with dynamic-complex environments experience the greatest amount of uncertainty in decision-making. 
The data also indicates that the static-dynamic dimension of the environment is a more important 
contributor to uncertainty than the simple-complex dimension. To create an understanding of the simple 
complex dimension, Duncan (1972) uses the following example: A low-level unit of a manufacturing 
firm needs to make a decision. They need to inform the parts and materials department and the 
marketing department. These two are both under the same environmental component, namely: the 
organisational function and staff units. This would be considered a simple environment. An example of 
a decision unit with a complex environment would be a programming and planning department. When

making a decision, they need to consider internal and external environment. Marketing and materials, 
but also customer demand, availability of raw materials, product parts of the supplier component, 
government regulatory control. To determine the static-dynamic placement of the unit, the focus must

instance, if the materials and marketing department demand were stable over longer periods of time, it 
would be considered static. If the factors from the programming and planning department were to 
fluctuate often, this would be considered dynamic.  
According to López-Gamero et al. (2011) the managerial perception of the environment can be mainly 
categorised into two topics: Information uncertainty and Resource dependence. Information uncertainty

2.3 Organisational reactions to uncertainty 
Organisations invariably need to respond to the uncertainty in their environment. In this section, we 
present suggestions from the literature on how theorists believe organisations should react to 
uncertainty. During a downturn there is little confidence in the market, it is never clear when it will end. 
Many decisions have to be taken under uncertainty. Organisations need to respond quickly to avoid 
losing business or even going bankrupt. 
During a crisis, organisations are under constant pressure. Less confidence in the market leads to lower 
consumer spending (Roberts, 2003). When customers stop spending their money, organisations lack 
revenue. If this leads to failure of making profits, organisations have to respond. These situations are 
extra delicate due to every dollar having to be spent wisely. This has implications on all environmental 
parties further down the supply chain, (e.g. such suppliers). Evidently, tighter price negotiations lead to 
delayed payments. This is just an example of what could happen in a downturn. There are many 
possibilities, thus it can be said that the situation is uncertain. 
2.3.1 Economic business cycle 
First, it is useful to know how the economy works and what kind of pattern it follows. As an example, 
we can take the business cycle. Business cycles follow the economy by having up- and downturns. 
Every recession is different and all arise without warning or indication as to when it shall end (Latham 
& Braun, 2011). A traditional cycle usually follows four phases:

Phases 3 and 4 are preferable and phase 1 is the first indication of a negative impact, phase 2 has the 
most impact and thus the one to be avoided most. A downturn will take place either way, but a 
depression can be avoided by using guidelines from the optimistic model created by Jayaramana et al. 
(2011). This model simulates a recessionary environment and uses five steps to bypass a depression: 
1. Need for diversification; investing in other areas, 
2. Scope for new opportunities; negotiate better prices, 
3. Paradigm shift; shift from not only laying people off to cost/asset reduction, and also revenue

4. Appropriate space creation; smaller or bigger firms, globalisation, 
5. Corporate governance; evaluation of organisational structure, restructure. 
The last step is supported by research from the success rate of organic management systems (lateral 
management) where employees are also able to participate in the decision-making (Keller, Slocum, & 
Susman, 1974). In this study it is examined which type of management system is affected more by 
environmental uncertainty, either the organic system or the mechanistic (classic approach) system. 
Results are in favour of the organic system. The results showed a correlation with a higher success rate 
in terms of economic success and task uncertainty (the uncertainty or unpredictability of occasions for 
corrective actions). 
If an organisation is aware of the business cycle, it should realise that a recession will follow after 
prosperous times, planning for declining sales should be incorporated into their strategy. To be less 
affected by a recession, an organisation could choose to use their strategic positioning to be active in 
multiple geographic locations, industries and markets and plan ahead when the economy will enter the 
recovery phase as stated in the above five steps (Pearce & Michael, 2006).

the short-term cost cutting is certain and the results of advertising and innovation in the slightly more 
distant future are uncertain. This reflects one of the propositions by Latham & Braun (2011). Proposition

2.4 Decision-making under uncertainty 
This section provides an overview concerning decision-making under uncertainty. An overview of 
models, theories, and phenomena that are linked to this topic are presented.  
Damghani et al. (2009) describe how most people often make choices out of habit or tradition, without 
going through the decision-making process steps systematically. Decisions may be made under social 
pressure or time constraints that interfere with a careful consideration of the options and consequences. 
Decisions may be influenced by one's emotional state. When people lack adequate information or skills, 
they may take less than optimal decisions. Even when or if people have time and information, they often 
do a poor job of understanding the probabilities of consequences. Even when they know the statistics, 
they are more likely to rely on personal experience than on information about probabilities. The 
fundamental concerns of decision-making are combining information about probability with 
information about desires and interests. 
In decision theory, there is a distinction between normative and descriptive theories. The distinction in 
these theories are described by Vroom & Yetton (1973). Normative theories are based on how decisions 
should be made on rationally choosing among alternatives. Descriptive theories look at the factors that 
determined the decisions between alternatives. In other words, descriptive models describe what people 
did. Normative models describe what people in theory should do (Dillon, 1998). There is a slight 
distinction as Dillon (1998) separates prescriptive (what people should do) and normative (what people 
can do). Baron (2004) uses the same definition and elaborates by giving several normative theories, 
whereas Dillon (1998) elaborates on descriptive theories.

In the political model, a decision proceeds incrementally, thereby minimising uncertainty. The process 
model and organisational model are similar in the sense that both make use of quantitative data and 
recognise behavioural aspects of decision-making. The differences are also important. The process

is less constrained by policy and procedure and thus designed to accommodate innovative managerial 
decision-making. Policy and procedure is more an end than a means. The rational model operates in an 
artificially closed environment. As such, making it of limited use in most real-world managerial 
decisions involving high levels of uncertainty. The principal shortcomings of the organisational model 
are that it has: a short-term horizon; a low tolerance of uncertainty, and; an all-consuming emphasis on 
following the established way of doing business. These shortcoming are similar to those of the rational 
model, it is best suited for situations with high levels of certainty (lower in the organization). The 
political model is characteristic of most organisations in the public sector and is almost totally 
behavioural driven. The outcome is a decision acceptable to many external constituencies. The process 
model of decision-making with its strong managerial emphasis and its objectives-oriented outcomes is 
the model recommended for decisions with discernible levels of uncertainty attendant on the outcome 
(middle and upper level management) (E. F. Harrison, 1993).

2.4.3.4 Decision-making models – Influencing factors 
Kahneman & Tversky (1979) argue that sole reliance on normative decision theory does not apply in 
practice. Prospect theory shows that people make decisions based on biases. People value losses and 
gains differently. When posed with two equal choices, one expressed as a possible gain and the other 
expressed as a possible loss. The one describing the gain will usually be chosen. Kahneman & Tversky 
(1979) argue that this is why the normative outcome is not always chosen and thus explains irrational 
choice. 
Another phenomenon that explains irrational choice is known as the sunk-cost effect. Arkes & Blumer 
(1985) prove that the greater the price paid for a product, the more likely people are to use or consume 
it, even in the face of risk or adversity. Thaler (1980) gives the following example. A man joins a tennis 
club and pays $300 in annual membership fees. After two weeks of playing, he develops a tennis elbow. 
He continues playing (in pain), saying: ‘I don’t want to waste the $300!’ Here the loss of a past 
investment drives the decision to continue to play despite terrible pain. Also known as throwing good 
money after bad.  
Keeping the GFC in mind the Resource Dependence Theory is extremely useful as every organisation 
depends on external resources in their environment. The GFC hit many organisations, thus hitting their 
environments. This means the ‘power’ of organisations that is under pressure during the GFC will be 
lower due to economic factors. This will lead to decisions that have to be made to survive and thus 
result in less power as companies that do not have to be in survival mode are now in charge. The power 
imbalance changes. In the research and data analysis, the difference of the power of organisations can 
be researched before and after the GFC and the consequences.

3.1 Literature Review 
To start with, an in-depth literature review was necessary for us to fully understand the concept of 
uncertainty and its interfaces with managerial decision-making in both a theoretical and a practical 
sense. We also conducted research into the 2008 Global Financial Crisis. This event caused major 
uncertainty in organisations all around the world, and as it occurred relatively recently, it was viewed 
as a good means for obtaining information on the subject of uncertainty in managerial decision-making. 
The primary sources of information were obtained through reading scientific journals, articles and 
books surrounding the subject. Moreover, when comparing theory to practice, with the GFC being a 
relatively recent event with major impact, quality data on the subject should still be ‘fresh’ in the minds 
of people (employees) to help further the empirical research.  
3.2 Data Collection 
As mentioned before, primary data was gathered through interviews. Participants for the interview had 
to fit the following profile: 
- 
Participants must be in a managerial/decision-making position in their organisation. 
- 
Ideal participants are at a strategic level in the organisation. However, as strategic level 
employees are generally very busy, tactical level managers with sufficient knowledge about 
strategic decisions are also accepted.  
- 
Participants must have sufficient knowledge of how their organisation was affected and how 
they responded to the GFC.  
- 
Participants are given the option to remain anonymous in the study. However, they must at least 
agree to provide a brief description of their business and industry, and state at which level the 
participant works for the firm (tactical or strategic).  
Secondary information was gathered through the internet or by consultation with field specialists. 
Sources that, in general, do not carry with them the credibility of scientific research, but do provide for 
interesting and alternative views on the subject that are relatively easy to understand. To ensure a good 
overview, Mendeley Desktop software was used for filing documents.

3.2.1 Interview process 
For getting interviews, we mainly used two sources. The primary source of interviews was through 
personal contacts. Personal contacts are often more motivated to help and proved a great source for

to explain swiftly and in simple terms what we were looking for and the potential benefits for their 
organisation. Once a potential participant had been spoken to, a follow-up e-mail was sent summarising 
the information and containing the primary questionnaire. The primary questionnaire was sent 
beforehand so that the participant was able to prepare his/her answers. After all, the questions 
surrounding the GFC are dating back approx. 7-8 years. Participants were told the interview would take 
up no longer than 30 minutes of their time, as managers of these levels generally have very busy 
schedules.  
The interview process had to be in person, through videoconference or by telephone. These means of 
communication (with direct feedback) give the ability to directly steer the direction of the interview. E-
mail and surveys were avoided due to the necessary semi-structured nature of the interview. Participants 
were often asked to elaborate on their answers for a better understanding of the ambiguous concept of 
uncertainty. There is a high risk of misinterpretation of this information if direct communication is not 
used. Furthermore, we did not limiting ourselves to a single industry, but gathered information from a 
vast range of industries. This meant certain questions had to be tweaked or asked in a slightly different 
way. A survey would have given us more quantitative data, but due to reasons mentioned above the 
data could possibly not have been of sufficient quality.  
Some of the questions might be altered to keep the flow, as not every participant might be able to answer 
the questions, additional questions can also be asked to give a more clarification. Although it is fully 
structured, the interview itself will flow like a semi-structured interview.  
The actual interview process was structured as follows. There were three roles: the interviewer, the 
participant, and the note-taker. The interviewer was in charge of the general conversation, flow, and 
questions of the interview. The participant was responsible for providing answers to the interviewer’s 
questions. The note-taker was in charge of recording the interview and taking notes as the interview 
progressed. If at any time during the interview the note-taker had any additional questions, he was free 
to ask them. Before starting the questionnaire, the participants were briefed on our background, thesis, 
and goal of the interview. 
After the interview had taken place, the interviewer and note-taker would transcribe the interview. The 
transcribing process is in accordance to Recording and Transcribing adapted by the University of 
Calgary, Psychology (J. Atkinson, 1949). The transcripts were then sent back to the participant for 
==PAGE:56==
38 
 
review. If anything had been misinterpreted by the interviewer or note-taker, this could now be corrected 
by the participant before starting data analysis.

3.2.3 Questionnaire 
The structure of the literature review provided the foundation for developing the questionnaire. The 
questions were formulated in such a manner that they were easy and short enough for managers to 
understand (e.g. containing no technical terminology found in scientific papers). Nevertheless, did allow 
us to gather sufficient in-depth data. Two questionnaires were developed, a primary and a secondary, 
see appendix A. The primary questionnaire was sent to the participant. The secondary questionnaire 
was for the interviewer; it contained the exact same questions as the primary did, but with additional 
information attached to the questions to ensure a complete answer is given and not to confuse the 
participant. 
3.2.3.1 Questionnaire structure 
The questionnaire is divided into five sections. After the participants were briefed on our background 
and interview process, the questionnaire could start. A summary of the aim of each of the sections can 
be found in Table 15. 
Table 15: Questionnaire structure (v3) section aim 
Section 1: Introduction of the organisation  
To put the participant at ease and get him/her comfortable with speaking English.  
Understanding the participant’s current role in the organisation, how long they have been in that role 
and how much experience they have in their field. To be able to judge their answering capabilities. 
Moreover, to give an overview of the organisation, the information here will be used for classification 
of the organisations.  
Section 2: Organisational reactions to uncertainty 
To find out and fully understand their organisational reactions to the GFC, the uncertainty it created 
and how they managed to survive it. To further pry into responses by asking how quick the decisions 
were taken, how much information was collected, how many resources were involved, and how 
fundamental these decisions were. The aim is to cover several initiatives that are mentioned in our 
expectations (2.5) and to compare practise with theory. 
Section 3: Uncertainty 
To characterise how uncertain the participant’s market environment is. On a simple-complex scale 
and a static-dynamic scale. To be able to further categorise answers according to Duncan’s (1972) 
model and review/compare answers. 
Investigate their firm’s major sources of uncertainty and if/how they overlap with other industries.  
Section 4: Decision-making under uncertainty 
To analyse the decision-making process. By prying with questions about their decision-making 
process, leadership style, if they consider where they lack of information and how they go about 
==PAGE:59==

collecting information. To study how their organisation deals with decision-making under 
uncertainty. Again to identify different models and leadership styles and to see if it reflects the theory.  
Section 5: Retrospective 
To find out what decisions they would have taken differently, something they gained from a lessons 
learned or review that was possibly caused by uncertainty.  
Section 6: Further questions 
This section gives the participant the opportunity to clarify or mention something that has not been 
covered and it is a reflection for the interviewees to see if all the topics have been covered (where 
applicable). This section might be useful for additional interviews as the participant might have 
contacts that are more capable of helping us get the optimal answers.   
During interviews, for sections 2, 3 and 4 it is up to the interviewers to steer the interview to cover the 
topics from the literature review. In section five participants are open to cover any topic they like, and 
is likely to uncover interesting topics. The questionnaire has been slightly altered (three versions) 
throughout interviews. The minor changes were mainly in structure and phrasing of the questions.   
3.3 Data analysis 
At this point, all interviews had been conducted and all transcripts had been reviewed by the 
participants. Before actual analysis could commence, the data needed to be prepared.  
3.3.1 Classification and Coding 
The first step consisted of classification, to be able to group the organisations. The classification table 
can be viewed in Figure 10.  
The reason for classifying the participants is to help us find patterns in the answers they had given. For 
example, if a number of participants say they are very fast in decision-making compared to their 
competitors. In addition, all these participants are classified as small businesses (<10), the argument 
would be strong to say that small businesses are faster in decision-making than bigger firms are. The 
next step would then be to investigate why this phenomenon occurs, and if/how, other industries could 
learn from this.  
The second step consisted of coding. Coding is the process of assigning a word or short phrase that 
summarises an attribute for a portion of data. That attribute is assigned to a node. For example, when 
participants are asked about their major sources of uncertainty, they could answer by saying:  
“… the market is extremely volatile, we find it hard to gage when and where we must focus on 
to maximise profit” 
This text could then be assigned to a node called: ‘Market/demand uncertainty’. To keep a clear 
overview of the analysis, nodes were structured into main and different levels of sub-nodes.  
==PAGE:60==
42 
 
3.3.2 Work method 
To start with, both students separately conducted their analysis. One of the students used the software 
programme QSR Nvivo 10. The other student did his analysis in MS Excel 2013. Reasons for this were: 
1. To find out which of the software programmes was better suited to coding.  
2. To study separately ensures that each student interprets the data in their own way. It is 
interesting to compare where students picked up on different things. After all, it is qualitative 
research and open to interpretation.  
Both analyses were compared and it was decided that QSR Nvivo 10 provided a better overview for 
analysis. Therefore, the next step was to assess and discuss both analyses and include the data from the 
Excel analysis into the Nvivo file. In the process of doing so a lot the node structure/names changed. 
The process of comparing is long and thorough with many discussions. When analysing the qualitative 
data and creating nodes it is essential to refer back to the literature. To see if both students are 
interpreting the same results the inter-observant agreement was used. This basic formula depicts the 
similarities between nodes. The similarities of different nodes will be measured. Afterwards both 
students decide together which nodes are useful for the thesis and decide on the final node structure. 
The outcome of which shall be further explained in the results section (4).  
When the final list of nodes is finished, the last task of the coding will be for both students to separately 
read all the transcripts again and code any data that may have been overlooked. The reason here for 
being is that it is easier to assign data to a final node structure, than trying to assign and develop nodes 
simultaneously.   
The final step in the work method consisted of the actual analysis. First, the students divided the main 
nodes between them for separate analysis. The idea behind doing this was similar to that of the previous 
analysis. By separately analysing the data and comparing the different methods, a discussion can 
commence about how to best merge the analyses and present the findings.  
3.3.3 Inter-observer reliability 
By consulting Krippendorff’s alpha, the inter-observer reliability was calculated, a statistical measure 
of agreement between the coders when coding a set of units in their analysis. In our case, this refers to 
the coding of the transcripts of the interviews. It must be noted that before actual coding there was no 
node structure agreed upon. Both students used different types of coding software to test which 
programme was better suited, and in doing so also created their own node structure. The similarities 
between these two were calculated (44%). Due to time considerations neither analyses were ‘cleaned 
up’. This explains the relatively low similarity percentage. Figure 11 and Table 16 below present the

5 Discussion 
This section shall discuss the findings presented in (4). Here we critically examine our findings and 
attempt to figure out what they mean, how valuable they are and why they’re significant. To do this, 
the results (4) shall be compared to the literature review (2) to give answer to the research question: 
‘How can organisations prepare themselves for uncertainty in managerial decision-making processes in 
a globalising world?’  
5.1 Environment characterisation and market analysis  
Results from Duncan’s (1972) study show that the majority of decision groups in R&D firms (7) 
perceive high levels of uncertainty (complex, dynamic) and that the majority of decision groups in 
manufacturing firms (7) perceive low levels of uncertainty (simple, static). As we only have one 
participant from a pure R&D organisation, this comparison is not representative. We observe from our 
analysis that no manufacturing firm has rated themselves lower than a five (on a 10-point scale). This 
observation is in direct contrast to Duncan’s (1972) findings. Reason for this could be that this research 
was conducted over 43 years ago. Technology has changed and has changed the way firms operate. 
However, we presume that this discrepancy is because our question was presented in an open manner, 
with minimal additional information to one participant of an organisation. Contrary to Duncan’s (1972) 
study, where multiple decision groups from one organisation were presented with a predetermined list 
of factors. Thereby mitigating the chances of over- and underrating. A phenomenon also highlighted by 
one of our participants.  
“Everybody you ask will be tempted to give a high ranking number. We all tend to exaggerate 
what we do. So let me explain our situation and then I’ll let you know what number I rate it 
as.” – Manuf_A. 
 For the few participants that did not rate themselves in the upper right corner, their answers suggest 
they have a sound understanding of their market environment, e.g.,  
“From our perspective it is quite simple; our organisation is based on skill. If I have 10 cards 
or 10.000 fuel cards. I still need more or less the same amount of people to work the system. 
For example: if tomorrow a very large client comes in, with 10.000 or even 50.000 fuel cards. 
I do not have to scale up my systems or labour capacity. In that sense rather simple. If you look 
at it from a strategic point of view I would say rather complex. Because you have the trend of 
electric cars, of autonomous cars and new payment services coming in. That is in our business, 
our strategy more and more complex. But overall I would give it a 2… I think the frequency of 
change is low. But the change itself is quite dramatic. In our industry, first there was a car. It 
was run on gasoline (or maybe diesel). But now you see that changes are coming. Not only 
from a car perspective but also from an energy perspective. So the change frequency is low, 
==PAGE:99==

but the change is significant. Because the changes are minimal, but the impact is still high I 
would say a 3 or 4.” – Admin_A.  
However, reproducing and comparing results to Duncan’s (1972) analysis was never the main objective. 
Concerning our research question, we can say that the majority of participants felt they perceive high 
levels of uncertainty. Additionally this section of the questionnaire did force the participant to think 
critically about their market environment and consequently provided us with interesting answers that 
have been coded in our analysis. We have learned that form our sample the majority perceived high 
levels of uncertainty.  
When posed with the question of how their respective organisations have fared against the GFC. 70% 
provided codable answers. Answers were coded into three nodes, namely: decline, stable, or growth in 
profits. The main pattern is that of decline in profits (63%). 80% of construction organisations and 
100% of financial organisations saw their profits drop. 21% of respondents saw their profits stabilise,

5.4 Data limitation 
The limitations in our research are mainly in the organisations that have been interviewed. It proved 
challenging to obtain a large sample, we contacted over 150 organisations and only had 40 responses, 
of which 27 interviews were held, including pursuing organisations several times. The time constraint 
of the thesis and the difficulty of finding organisations willing to be interviewed resulted in a broad 
sample of participants. The implications of this are that conclusions drawn across industries are not 
strong enough. Comparison for manufacturing organisations (7) and construction organisations (5) are 
most reliable from our sample.

6 Conclusion 
This study was conducted based on the presumption that globalisation brings with it an extra dimension 
in the form of complexity which ultimately leads to added uncertainty. The thesis set out to explore 
how organisations could prepare themselves for uncertainty in managerial decision-making processes 
in a globalising world. Our research has provided us with enough information to draw five sound 
conclusions.  
Findings from our empirical study show that the majority of our sample too saw a decline in profits as 
a direct result of the GFC. However, we can conclude that not all organisations were hit by the GFC. 
From those that stabilised or perceived growth: 
- 
Multi-national organisations have a higher chance of stabilising as a group 
- 
Not all industries have been affected (e.g. commodity products) 
- 
Industries with a low operating cost and strong ICT platform will likely not be hit severely  
- 
Organisations securing longer term contracts have more time to react accordingly and are less 
prone to feel fluctuations in the market 
- 
Some organisations benefit from currency rates and oil prices 
Our results show that the majority of sources of uncertainty are perceived externally. In these external 
sources of uncertainty, we can conclude that political uncertainty is the most important and surprising, 
as this was not picked up on in our literature review. The second most indicated source of external 
uncertainty comes from the market. This is in agreement with findings from the literature review.   
We can conclude that from our sample, organisational measures against a recession presented in the 
literature, match what occurs in practice. However, the literature does highlight the importance of 
investing during an economic downturn. The organisations we interviewed are all survivors of the GFC, 
and from that sample, only 37% invested. Thus investing was not a common reaction even though the 
literature does state this.  
Data-driven decision-making is common practice, that conclusion can be drawn from our study and its 
importance is backed up by the literature. However, from our sample we can conclude that not all 
decision-making is data-driven. 21% of decision-making is solely based on experience and/or gut 
feeling. This number is surprisingly high. We can conclude that experience and gut feeling still play a 
reasonable role in the decision-making process.   
When posed with a retrospective question on decision-making, participants indicated either being too 
confident or too doubtful in the past. These extremes could have been avoided by HR measures. We 
can conclude that HR plays a key role in facilitating other departments to help take the right decisions.

7 Recommendations 
Concerning sources of uncertainty, we recommend that organisations first map out where all their 
sources originate from (internal, external). Then tackle internal sources of uncertainty first, as these are 
influenceable by the organisation. Thereafter tackle external sources of uncertainty. These will often 
prove harder to mitigate than internal sources as organisations rarely exert power over these. In our 
study political and market uncertainty were most prevalent. We recommend a proactive attitude towards 
these predicaments by staying up to date on events that concern your organisation (in)directly. For 
political matters, an organisation could choose to lobby. However, resources must be available to do 
so, not every organisation can afford to commit resources to these matters.  
From our research, we saw that multi-national organisations had the ability to stabilise their profits by 
being spread out geographically. We also encountered the contrast of this, namely: organisations that 
were negatively impacted by the GFC due to being too dependent on local markets. From our literature 
review, venturing into international markets was also specified. These facts further highlight the 
importance of being active in a multi-national environment, and therefor receive our recommendation.  
As a result from our empirical study, we also observed the benefits of running a business with low 
operating costs on an ICT platform during a recession. This is related to investing in R&D and thereby 
improving operational efficiency, also mentioned in our literature review. This further highlights the 
importance of investing in R&D, and therefor receive our recommendation.  
Although HR is responsible for facilitating the needs of its employees, it seems that in decision-making, 
all lessons learned are directly related to HR responsibilities. We recommend that HR be more 
integrated into the decision-making process to help better facilitate in decision-making and thus 
ensuring decisions made are neither too confident nor too doubtful.  
The majority of our participants did well faring against the recession basing their decisions solely on 
experience and gut feeling. Nonetheless, we recommend they incorporate data-driven decision-making 
into their decision-making process. This is too in accordance with our literature review. By doing so, 
decision makers can come to new insights by being forced to crucially think about all the implications. 
Our research also highlighted a pattern that 80% of our interviewed construction organisations base 
decisions on experience and/or gut feeling. It could prove interesting to explore why this number is so 
significant, and if it is representative for the construction industry.   
Investing was not a prevalent measure against the recession from our sample. Its advantages have been 
scientifically proven. We recommend that organisations that chose not to invest during the last crisis, 
to do so in a next downturn to not only survive it, but to emerge successfully from it.  
==PAGE:111==
93 
 
Two final recommendations can be considered as general knowledge, but their importance has been 
highlighted in our research. That is securing long-term contracts, ensuring a steady fixed income over 
a longer period of time, giving an organisation more time to react to a crisis and less sensitive to market 
fluctuations. Moreover, the necessity of the product/service a business provides, e.g. participants that 
supply commodity products or services integral to daily life remained unaffected by the crisis.   
Interesting further areas of research lie in external sources of uncertainty, in particular political 
uncertainty. Political uncertainty is a key factor, but was not picked up on from the literature review.   
The inability to draw sound conclusions about industries is due to the fact of our sample size. We 
recommend that the results obtained through the semi-structured interviews be used as a base for 
developing a survey. The basis of which lies in the results of our research. By doing so, more 
participants can be reached.  
The HR aspect in our lessons learned node proved a conclusive finding. However, this was also not 
picked up on in our literature review. It might prove interesting to research why this aspect does not 
present itself when conducting research in managerial decision-making under uncertainty.

c. What is your background? (educational/professional) 
d. How long have you been working for the organisation? 
e. What have your previous job titles/departments been? 
f. How has the company been doing? (leading up to the crisis, when it hit, and 
currently)  
 
 Timeline of major events

iii. How many resources (persons etc.) were involved in the decision-making

iv. How fundamental were these decisions to your company’s future? How 
complex? 
Topics: Good/bad costs, sunk cost, strategic positioning, back to core, 
investments, R&D, lay-offs

dynamic
