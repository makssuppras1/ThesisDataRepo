II. Abstract 
Snakebite envenoming is listed as a Neglected Tropical Disease by the World Health Organization. Every 
year this truly awful disease is estimated to impact more than a million people worldwide, causing death and 
permanent sequelae. Current treatment relies on an old production method which has seen little development 
in the past 125 years. 
This project is focused on the development of recombinant antivenom against Red-tailed Coral Snake 
(Micrurus mipartitus) venom. Currently, the only antivenom available is made with venom from snake 
species related to M. mipartitus and shows a low neutralizing effect on M. mipartitus venom. One of the 
venom components contributing to the toxic effect of M. mipartitus venom is the toxin mipartoxin-1. In this 
project, mipartoxin-1 was recombinantly expressed, and used as an antigen in several phage display 
campaigns. 
Mipartoxin-1 expression was carried out utilizing an expression system capable of forming disulfide bonds 
de novo. The expression was analyzed on sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis and 
purified by affinity purification, followed by size exclusion chromatography. The purified toxin was 
biotinylated, immobilized, and used as the antigen for phage display. A control was set in place to evaluate if 
the binders (antibody single-chain variable fragments) discovered on a recombinant toxin had the capacity to 
bind a native toxin. The control consisted of side-by-side phage display campaigns on recombinant α-
cobratoxin and native α-cobratoxin and would prove if the recombinant antigen displayed the same epitopes 
as the native toxin, and if it was possible to generate binders to the native toxin using the recombinant toxin 
as the antigen during phage display. 
A total of four phage display campaigns were carried out: two on recombinant mipartoxin-1, one on 
recombinant α-cobratoxin, and one on native α-cobratoxin. The binders from all campaigns were screened 
for their antigen and cross-antigen binding abilities. 70 clones from the four phage display campaigns were 
send to sequencing. Out of the 70 sent, 9 unique clones were discovered from the two phage display 
campaigns on recombinant mipartoxin-1, two of which showed high mipartoxin-1 specificity, 10 unique 
clones were discovered in recombinant α-cobratoxin phage display campaigns, and 11 unique clones in 
native α-cobratoxin campaigns.  
This project has shown that using recombinantly expressed snake venom toxins can work as antigens in a 
phage display campaign. This proof of concept has the potential to reduce the amount of snake venom 
required in the pursuit of developing antivenom alternatives. Moreover, if the mipartoxin-1 binders 
discovered show binding to native mipartoxin-1 in future assays, they may have implications in the 
development of an efficient M. mipartitus antivenom.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Snakebites: a neglected tropical disease with an outdated treatment  
Snakebite envenoming is an underappreciated public health crisis that affects millions of people worldwide. 
It is estimated that at least 1.8-2.7 million people are bitten each year with an estimated 81,000 to 137,000 
snakebite-associated deaths [1]. This number is an underestimation of the true number of envenoming, as not 
all snakebites are documented [2,3]. In 2017, the World Health Organization recognized the impact of 
snakebite envenoming and reinstated it as a class A neglected tropical disease [4]. With this reinstatement, 
there is hope that snakebite envenoming will be more recognized, and, as a result, more resources will be 
allocated towards combatting this awful disease [5].  
The majority of snake species are native to tropical regions, such as sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, 
Oceania, and Latin America [1,3]. Consequently, the human population of these regions is at increased risk 
of snake encounters and snakebites. Cases of snakebites within these regions are unevenly distributed 
between social and economic classes, with more cases in poor and rural communities, due to poor housing, 
and limited access to education. Moreover, access to healthcare is also limited within these communities, 
reducing the access to effective treatments [1,5]. The main groups at risk are agricultural workers and youths 
because of their higher-than-average snake exposure while working on the fields or playing outside. Victims 
of snakebite envenoming often comprises the main breadwinners of these communities, and will not only 
risk dying, but also risk long-term sequela such as amputations, deformities, chronic ulcerations, and many 
more [1,5–7]. The combination of death or long-term disability and victims being the main breadwinners of 
these rural community, means that a snakebite not only has large implication of the quality-of-life of the 
victim, but has the potential of pushing whole communities further into poverty [5,8,9].  
The traditional treatment of snakebites involves serum-derived antivenoms. These antivenoms are made 
using a technique from 1894, developed by Albert Calmette (figure 1) [10,11]. This production technique 
revolves around immunizing large production animals such as horses or sheep through an intensive 
immunization process involving injection increasing amounts of the whole venom into the production animal 
throughout an extended period of time (months to years) [11]. As a result the animal will develop an immune 
response to the venom, and once the immunization process is over, the animal will be bled, and the 
antibodies (IgG) purified from the blood. The purified antibodies can then be digested by pepsin to produce 
antibody fragments (F(ab’)2 or Fab before being formulated and made ready to use as an antivenom [11,12].  
The decision on what venoms to use for immunization is based on the medical importance of the venom and 
the impact of snakebites of the specific snake. Moreover, there are several considerations to be taken when 
==PAGE:9==
6 
 
selecting the right venom, such as the inter- and intraspecies variability. Therefore venom reference pools are 
formulated, with a composition representative of the venom of the targeted snake(s) [13].  
Both monovalent and polyvalent antivenoms are produced using the method outlined in figure 1, depending 
on whether the goal is to neutralize the venom of one, or several snake species. Monovalent antivenoms have 
the benefit of being highly specific and thus requiring low amounts of antivenom during treatment, reducing 
the risk of adverse reactions. However, the use of monovalent antivenoms requires accurate snake 
identification – which is not always possible [14]. Polyvalent antivenoms have the capacity to neutralize the 
venom of several snake species and is therefore the preferred treatment method in regions where the majority 
of local snakebite envenoming can be treated by a single polyvalent antivenom [12]. Polyvalent antivenoms 
circumvent the need for correct snake identification and are easier to manufacture and more cost-effective. 
However, the treatment of snakebite envenoming using polyvalent antivenoms requires larger volumes with 
a potential risk of adverse reactions [12,14,15].  
While these antivenoms save numerous lives annually, they are associated with serious drawbacks, such as 
adverse reactions, batch-to-batch variability, and high cost. However, the benefit outweighs the drawbacks 
and serum-derived antivenoms remain the standard of care today [11,12].

1.2 Theoretical background 
1.2.1 Snake venom and three-finger toxins 
Snake venoms are diverse and complex mixtures of proteins, peptides, lipids, and other compounds, that 
elicit various biochemical and toxicological effects on the victims. Once injected, the venom components 
(toxins) act locally or spread through the body via the lymphatic system, blood vessels, or a combination [1]. 
The physiological effects of snakebite envenoming are dependent on the venom composition and can 
manifest itself as local tissue damage, neurotoxicity, and cardiovascular and hemostatic disturbances [1].  
Venom composition is extremely variable and dependent on the species of snake, diet, and age, among other 
factors [16]. There are four families of venomous snakes, one of which is relevant to be discussed in this 
report: the elapids (cobras, kraits, sea snakes, mambas, and coral snakes). The toxin content of snake venom 
from the elapid family is predominantly phospholipases A2 (PLA2), and three-finger toxins (3FTx), in this 
thesis, the focus will be on 3FTx [1,17,18]. 
With more than 700 entries into the UniProt database, 3FTxs comprises one of the largest family of snake 
venom toxins [19]. Envenomation by 3FTx is associated with neurotoxic and cytotoxic effects. However, in 
the context of this report, only the 3FTx that are neurotoxin and act on the nicotinic acetylcholine receptors 
(nAChRs) will be considered. 3FTx are small proteins (6-8 kDa) of 60 to 72 amino acids and are categorized 
as short-chain or long-chain α-neurotoxins depending on their size and number of disulfide bonds [20–22]. 
The structure of 3FTx is highly conserved with a globular hydrophobic core and three β-stranded adjacent 
loops resembling fingers (figure 2) protruding from the core [19–22]. The hydrophobic core is stabilized by 
four conserved disulfide bonds, and an additional disulfide bond can be found in the second loop of long-
chain α-neurotoxins (figure 2A) [19–23]. While both long and short-chain α-neurotoxins have a high affinity 
to muscle α1 nAChR, only long-chain α-neurotoxins are able to bind the neuronal α7 nAChRs [24]. Another 
distinction is the fact that short-chain α-neurotoxins have a faster association and dissociation to nAChRs 
than long-chain α-neurotoxins [19].  
As mentioned, 3FTxs act on nAChR by antagonistically preventing the binding of acetylcholine (ACh). 
Under normal physiological conditions ACh is released into the synaptic cleft, where it binds one of the two 
recognition sites of nAChRs. Once both recognition sites are occupied by an ACh molecule, the receptor 
undergoes conformational changes, and the receptor-associated ion channel is opened, this increases the 
permeability of the synaptic membrane to cations resulting in depolarization of the muscle end plate and 
muscle contraction [24]. However, under the antagonistic action of either long or short-chain α-neurotoxins, 
the binding of ACh is inhibited, preventing opening of the ion channel and disrupting neurotransmission 
[24]. The main amino acid residues of the 3FTxs that interact with the nAChRs, are located on the second 
loop of both short and long-chain α-neurotoxins [19].

1.2.2 Micrurus mipartitus, mipartoxin-1, and Micrurus spp. antivenom 
Terrestrial coral snakes from the elapid family is a diverse group of more than 120 subspecies, distributed 
from the southern United States to central Argentina [17,18,23]. The genus Micrurus is the most diverse 
genus within the coral snake group and is known to inhabit a wide range of habitats ranging from lowland 
rainforests and deserts to highland cloud forests [17,23,25]. Although Micrurus spp. envenoming is relatively 
uncommon constituting less than 2% of total snakebites in the Americas, they have medical significance, 
causing peripheral paralysis and in a worst-case-scenario respiratory failure and death [17,26]. 
The venom of Micrurus mipartitus commonly named the Red-tailed Coral Snake, contains a short-chain type 
1 α-neurotoxin which is the main toxin of interest in this thesis (figure 2B [27]). This medically relevant 
toxin named mipartoxin-1 (Uniprot id: B3EWF8), was first isolated and categorized in 2012 by Rey-Suárez 
et al. [25]. The toxin has a unique amino acid sequence that shares low sequence similarity to other short 
chain type 1 α-neurotoxins. When compared to 67 related 3FTxs from nine elapid snake genera the highest 
sequence similarity found was 70%. The identity value dropped to less than 38% when compared to proteins 
of elapid genera other than Micrurus spp. [25]. The toxin plays a major role in M. mipartitus venom toxicity 
and is therefore an important target for development of therapeutic antivenoms [25].

Treatment of Micrurus spp. envenoming consists of administration of a commercial available coral snake 
specific antivenom, manufactured in Brazil by Butantan Institute [15,17,18,28]. This antivenom is produced 
using venom from M. corallinus (50%) and M. frontalis (50%) as immunogens during horse immunizations 
[17,28]. The production of these antivenoms is hampered by a lack of venom, which stems from the limited 
venom that is obtained during extraction and the difficulty of capturing and keeping coral snakes in captivity 
[15,17]. Moreover, studies show that the antivenoms are not efficient and show limited snake species 
coverage [17,23]. The problem with limited species coverage and efficiency of Micrurus spp. antivenom is 
thought to be caused by a number of issues, one of which is the presence of 3FTx in the venoms used for 
immunization [29].  
Antibody content of antivenoms is governed by the immune response of the host and the immunogenicity of 
the venom components. It is well known that both toxic and non-toxic venom components can elicit an 
immune response, resulting in antivenom containing mixtures of medically relevant and irrelevant antibodies 
[30]. 3FTxs are not very immunogenic, attributed to their low proportional content in a whole venom and 
their small size, which results in a low number of 3FTx-specific antibodies in antivenoms [17,23,28]. These 
toxins have a major influence on the toxicity of the venoms, however the antivenoms available have a low 
amount of 3FTx neutralizing antibodies, and thus these toxins are the most poorly neutralized elapid snake 
toxins by current antivenoms [17,29,31].  
Several people have attempted to increase the amount of 3FTx-specific antibodies in serum derived 
antivenoms [31,32]. One of the most recent and relevant attempts was published in 2021, where Archundia et 
al., [31] immunized horses with either a recombinant short-chain consensus α-neurotoxin or the whole 
venom from M. tener. The neutralizing effect of the two antivenoms were tested against 11 Micrurus 
venoms. It was observed that mixing the two monovalent antivenoms had the best neutralization capacity: 
neutralizing 10/11 venoms tested. Interestingly, the antibodies from either monovalent antivenom were 
unable to neutralize the venom from M. mipartitus [31]. The antivenom produced using the recombinant 
short-chain consensus α-neurotoxin was also tested for its neutralization ability on multiple venoms, and was 
found to neutralize a broad spectrum of neurotoxic elapid venoms [30].  
1.2.3 Innovating antivenoms 
Current antivenoms leaves much to be desired in terms of adverse side effects, non-specific antibodies, 
batch-to-batch variation, and many more [11,33]. The work presented in this thesis is focused on developing 
alternative antivenoms that circumvent these drawbacks. One promising alternative is the development of 
recombinant antivenoms consisting of oligoclonal mixtures of fully human immunoglobulin G (IgG) that are 
discovered using phage display technology [34]. This concept has been proven to neutralize the toxic effects 
of black mamba venom in vivo, in a proof of concept [34]. 
==PAGE:13==
10 
 
Oligoclonal mixtures of fully human antibodies have the benefit of neutralizing venoms without the 
drawbacks of traditional antivenoms. The benefits include high compatibility with the human immune 
system due to human origin, resulting in fewer adverse reactions [33,34]. The production of these 
antivenoms facilitates high designability, as the IgG’s are specifically discovered and selected based on their 
ability to neutralize medically relevant toxins. The individual antibodies within the mixture can be designed 
to be broadly neutralizing or exhibit effector functions such as pH dependency, thus increasing the medical 
scope [34–37]. This novel approach to the treatment of snakebites could bring the field of antivenom into the 
modern era of biotechnology. 
1.2.4 Recombinant snake toxin production 
A large part of this project was dedicated to the recombinant bacterial expression of the snake toxin 
mipartoxin-1. As mentioned, mipartoxin-1 is a type 1 short-chain α-neurotoxin from M. mipartitus, 
containing eight conserved cysteine residues that form four disulfide bonds which are essential for correct 
conformation and stability (figure 2B) [25].  
The formation of disulfide bonds can be a major challenge in recombinant heterologous expression. Several 
expression systems have been developed to enable the production of disulfide-rich proteins. Some of these 
systems rely on targeting the toxins to a cellular compartment, such as the endoplasmic reticulum of 
eukaryotes or the periplasm of prokaryotes [38,39]. Compartmentalizing the toxins in the prokaryotic 
periplasmic space using a short signal sequence on the N-terminus of the toxin promotes disulfide bond 
formation due to the presence of chaperones and other catalysts [40]. A few downsides to this method exists, 
including the unknown effects on the yield caused by the presence of the signal sequence, or the difficulty of 
predicting the efficiency of periplasmic translocation when combining a signal sequence with novel 
recombinant toxins [40]. Despite these downsides this method has been successfully used to produce toxins 
from snakes, cone snails, spiders, and sea anemones [40].  
In contrast to the compartments that catalyze the formation of disulfide bonds, the cytoplasm of wild-type 
prokaryotes is unfit for disulfide bond formation due to its reducing environment and the absence of 
components that catalyze the formation of disulfide bonds [38–40]. Several expression systems have been 
designed to enable the production of disulfide-rich proteins in the cytoplasm, such as using specialized 
Escherichia coli strains. Two such strains exist: SHuffle® from New England Biolabs, and Origami™ from 
Novagen. These strains promote the formation of disulfide bonds by disrupting the reducing pathways 
present in the cytoplasm, which is achieved by genetic deletions of glutathione reductase and thioredoxin 
reductase [40]. The SHuffle® cells express a periplasmic disulfide isomerase in the cytoplasm which further 
promotes native disulfide-bond formation [41]. Both Shuffle® and an Origami™ derivative, Rosetta-gami™, 
have been used to produce toxins; however, they have been associated with low growth, yield, and limited 
efficiency at producing correctly folded disulfide-rich proteins [20,40,42]. 
==PAGE:14==
11 
 
The system used for the production of disulfide-rich proteins in this thesis is the csCyDisCo, which is an 
extension of the CyDisCo system [43]. The CyDisCo is a vector-based system, which allows for expression 
of two redox enzymes: the sulfhydryl oxidase (Erv1p) (UniProt ID: P27882), native to the inter-membrane 
mitochondrial space of Saccharomyces cerevisiae, and the human protein-disulfide isomerase (hPDI) 
(UniProt ID: P07237). The Erv1P oxidase allows for the de novo synthesis of disulfide bonds, and the hPDI 
isomerizes non-native disulfides [39,44]. This system efficiently expresses high yields of disulfide-bonded 
proteins in the cytoplasm of E. coli [39]. The csCyDisCo system adds a conotoxin PDI (csPDI) (UniProt ID: 
A0A140G913) to the vector, which was found to accelerate folding of conotoxins in vitro [43]. Both 
CyDisCo and csCyDisCo has advantages over the specialized E. coli strains: 1) they are compatible with any 
E. coli strain and 2) the system is non-disruptive, whereas the specialized strains disrupt pathways in the cells 
cytoplasm [40]. Because of the non-disruptive nature, efficiency at expressing animal venoms, and the ability 
to obtain high yields of disulfide-bonded proteins, the csCyDisCo system was chosen as the expression 
system. 
To utilize this expression system, E. coli BL21 (DE3) cells were co-transformed with two vectors: the 
csCyDisCo vector (pCsCyDisCo) and a vector containing the protein requiring disulfide bond formation 
(pET39Ub19-α-cobratoxin-AviTag or pETDuet-mCherry-BirA-MCS2-mipartoxin-1-AviTag). The vector

1.2.5 Phage display 
The main method utilized in this thesis for the discovery of mipartoxin-1 binders, was phage display. This 
technique combines ease of use, robustness, and low cost with a high throughput screening method capable 
of screening libraries containing billions of unique antibody single-chain variable fragments (scFv), in 
pursuit of discovering medically relevant antibodies [45]. Phage display technology has a high 
biopharmaceutical relevance, evident by the fact that 14 monoclonal antibodies currently in clinical use 
(number from 2020) were discovered using this technique [45].  
Central to phage display is the bacteriophage, specifically the M13 filamentous bacteriophage has been 
utilized extensively in phage display (figure 4) [46]. The M13 phage is highly specific and is only capable of 
infecting E. coli strains that express the F pilus. The F pilus is an essential component that enables infection 
and translocation of the phage genome into the cytoplasm of the bacteria [46–48]. After infection, the phage 
remains in the bacteria as a chronic infection, continuously secreting phages [46–48]. The 6407 bp long M13 
genome consists of nine genes that encode 11 different proteins: 5 coat proteins, and 6 proteins involved in 
phage replication and assembly [46]. The five coat proteins and the single-stranded phage DNA is shown in 
figure 4. 
In antibody phage display selections, the gene encoding an antibody scFv is incorporated into the DNA of 
the phage fused to the G3P gene (the gene encoding the pIII coat protein), which results in the scFv being 
displayed on the pIII coat protein (figure 5A) [46,49]. This coat protein is essential for infection; without it, 
the phage is unable to interact with the F pilus and rendered non-infectious [46]. Each phage particle 
contains three to five copies of the pIII coat protein. In the case of this project, the incorporation of the scFv 
on all pIII proteins is undesirable, as phages containing more than one scFv copy will have an artificially 
high avidity [46]. To prevent the multivalent display of scFvs, the phagemid system was used. These 
plasmids (figure 3E) contains three elements: 1) an antibiotic marker, 2) the gene encoding the scFv-pIII 
fusion protein, and 3) the phage origin of replication required for amplification and the production of the (+) 
DNA strand that can be packaged into a phage [46]. E. coli cells containing phagemids are incapable of 
Figure 4: Anatomy of the M13 phage. The five coat proteins and the phage single-stranded DNA is annotated. 
==PAGE:17==
14 
 
producing functional phage particles and need to be infected by a helper phage containing the complete 
phage genome. This ingenious design means that the G3P of the helper phage will compete with the fusion 
scFv-G3P gene from the phagemid, resulting in 90% of phages displaying pIII proteins without the scFv 
while the remaining 10% will display a single copy of the pIII-scFv, thus circumventing the problem with 
multivalence [46].  
During phage display, phages containing scFvs go through several rounds of panning against an antigen of 
interest. The antigen is presented to the phage either in solution or immobilized on a specific surface. For the 
phage display selections carried out in this project, the antigen was expressed with a biotin accepting linker 
(figure 3B, 3C). Once biotinylated, the antigen was immobilized on beads coated with streptavidin or 
neutravidin (figure 6B).  
The phage display panning cycle is shown in figure 6. Each panning consists of 1) deselection and blocking 
the phage display library. This step aims to remove any unspecific binders and streptavidin/neutravidin 
binders (figure 6A). 2) Incubating the phage display library with the immobilized antigen, allowing binding 
to the antigen (figure 6B). 3) Washing. Here any phages with scFvs displaying no or low binding will be 
removed (figure 6C). 4) Elution. Phages that bind the antigen are eluted using trypsin (figure 6D) [46]. The 
trypsin cleavage sites are highlighted in figure 5. The location of the trypsin cleavage site allows eluted 
phages that contain a copy of the pIII-scFv fusion protein to remain infectious, while phages without the 
pIII-scFv fusion protein will be rendered non-infectious (figure 5B, 5D). 5) The eluate is then used to infect 
E. coli cells (figure 6E). Thus all phages that displayed an antigen-binding scFv will be retained and ready 
for amplification and use in the next bio panning, or characterized (figure 6F, 6G) [46].

M13 phage without the G3P-scFv fusion gene. D) M13 without the fusion gene rendered non-infectious after trypsin digestion.

2.1 Preliminary work 
I cannot take credit for all the work done in this thesis, a large part of the preliminary work that made this 
thesis possible was carried out by members of the Tropical Pharmacology Lab - mainly Charlotte Rimbault 
and Anna Damsbo Jensen. This means that when I joined the group, all vectors were constructed, the 
expression, purification, and cleavage conditions were optimized, and several µL of toxins was already 
purified and ready to be worked on. The native toxin used as a control (α-cobratoxin) had already been 
biotinylated by Tulika and the biotinylation profile had been determined by MALDI-TOF (supplementary 
figure 3 section 8.2). 
2.2 Experimental strategy for the thesis 
The main objective of the thesis was to discover mipartoxin-1 binders. Due to the difficulty of procuring the 
toxin it was decided that it should be made recombinantly. In order to verify the success of the method, a 
control consisting of a recombinant version of another toxin was carried out in parallel. This control toxin (α-
cobratoxin from Naja kaouthia) has already been well characterized and the native toxin is readily available. 
Thus, if control selections on the recombinant toxin show comparable results to selections on the native toxin 
the conclusion might be extrapolated to the mipartoxin-1 selections. 
The overview of the experiments carried out in this thesis is presented in figure 7 below. The toxins were 
expressed using auto-induction, followed by affinity purification and cleavage of the solubility and 
purification tag. After cleavage, the toxin was purified a second time to remove the cleaved tag. For the 
second purification three methods were attempted, ultimately size exclusion chromatography was found to 
give the best result. The first phage display campaign was started using the recombinant and native toxins as 
antigens. The success of the phage display was estimated by counting the colony-forming units (CFU) and 
calculating the enrichment factor (EF), immediately followed by polyclonal phage enzyme-linked 
immunosorbent assay (ELISA). Confirming the success of the selection, the clones exhibiting binding were 
subcloned and separated. A monoclonal Dissociation-enhanced lanthanide fluorescence immunoassay 
(DELFIA) was carried out to characterize the individual clones. 
Throughout the next sections a lot of different figures, numbers, and names will be presented – hopefully in a 
way that is easy to follow. However, several steps had to be repeated and explanations may not be clear, 
which might be a cause of confusion. An explanation of the naming is in order.

3.2 Methods 
3.2.1 Recombinant expression of mipartoxin-1 and α-cobratoxin 
Co-transformation of competent BL21 (DE3) E. coli cells was carried out with pCsCyDisCo (pLE577) and 
pETDuet-mCherry-BirA-MCS2-mipartoxin-1-AviTag or pET39Ub19-α-cobratoxin-AviTag depending on 
the toxin to be expressed. 50 µL competent BL21 (DE3) E. coli cells were mixed with 1 µL of one of the 
toxin-containing vectors and 1 µL pCsCyDisCo, incubated on ice for 30 min, heat-shocked at 42°C for 45 
sec, and cooled on ice for 2 min. The cells were then suspended in 950 µL LB media and incubated at 37°C 
for 1 h (Multitron, Infors HT). 100 µL cells were streaked on LB plates containing 25 µg/mL 
chloramphenicol and 50 µg/mL kanamycin and incubated at 37°C overnight (ON). Precultures were made by 
inoculating 5 mL LB media containing 25 µg/mL chloramphenicol and 50 µg/mL kanamycin with colonies 
from the transformation. After ON incubation at 37°C, the precultures were used to inoculate 500 mL 
autoinduction media in 2800 mL Fernbach flasks. The expressions were incubated 5 h at 37°C with 200 rpm 
agitation, whereafter the temperature was set to 16°C, and 1 µg/mL biotin was added to cultures co-
transformed with pETDuet-mCherry-BirAMCS2-mipartoxin-1-AviTag. Expression was carried out for 20 h 
at 16°C to allow the cells to reach saturation. 
3.2.2 Cell lysis 
Expression cultures were centrifuged at 6.000 x g, 4°C, for 15 min (Sorvall Lynx 4000 centrifuge, Thermo 
Scientific). The supernatant was discarded, the pellet was weighed and resuspended in fresh lysis buffer on 
ice. The cells were sonicated (Qsonica, tip diameter 3,2 mm) on ice for 2 effective minutes at 40% amplitude 
with 2 sec on and 1 sec off. Immediately after sonication, 1 ug/10 mL benzonase and 1 µL/mL protease 
inhibitor cocktail set III were added. The cells were then centrifuged at 14.000 x g, 4°C for 30 minutes. The 
supernatant containing the soluble protein fraction was saved, while the pellet containing the insoluble 
fraction was discarded. 100 µL fractions from the expression culture, soluble fraction and the insoluble 
fraction were saved for analysis on sodium dodecyl sulphate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS 
PAGE) (section 3.2.7). 
3.2.3 Affinity chromatography 
For each protein expression batch, 4 mL Ni-NTA slurry was equilibrated with two column volumes (CV) (40 
mL) of Tris 20 mM imidazole. The equilibrated Ni-NTA slurry was mixed with the soluble fraction for 60 
min at 4°C with end-over-end rotation. The soluble fraction and Ni-NTA mix were centrifuged at 3.000 x g 
for 15 min, and the supernatant was collected; this was the flowthrough. The resin was resuspended in 40 mL 
Tris 20 mM imidazole, centrifuged at 3.000 x g for 10 min, the supernatant was collected - this was the 
wash. The resin was transferred to the gravity flow column, and 2 - 3 x 5 mL Tris 400 mM imidazole was 
==PAGE:26==
23 
 
used to elute the protein. After each addition of elution buffer, the solution was incubated for 10 min before 
collection. The purified proteins were dialyzed in 4 L PBS at 4°C for 2 h, moved to a fresh 4 L PBS batch, 
and dialyzed at 4°C ON. 
3.2.4 TEV cleavage 
Following dialysis, the proteins were concentrated to 40 uM using centrifugal filtration units. TEV 
endoprotease was added at a molar ratio of 1:20 (TEV:protein). The cleavage was carried out at 30°C for 1 h, 
whereafter it was moved to 4°C ON. The cleavage was analyzed using SDS PAGE. 
3.2.5 Size exclusion chromatography 
The size exclusion column (HiLoad 16/600 Superdex 75pg (Cytiva)) was equilibrated in water for two CVs 
on an NGC™ Chromatography System (BioRad). After equilibration the water was replaced with PBS. The 
proteins were concentrated to 1 mg/mL prior to loading. The separation was carried out using the following 
method: sample load and 100% PBS for 1,25 CV. 2 mL fractions were collected and analyzed on SDS 
PAGE. 
3.2.6 In vitro biotinylation 
Only the Rα-cobratoxin needed in vitro biotinylation. BirA was added at a molar ratio of 1:50 (BirA:Rα-
cobratoxin), along with 10 mM ATP and 300 uM biotin. The solution was left at room temperature (RT) for 
1 h, after which a new batch of 300 uM biotin and 10 uM ATP was added. The solution was moved to 30°C 
and incubated for 1 h. The biotinylation was analyzed with an electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) 
on a SDS PAGE. 
3.2.7 SDS PAGE 
Analysis of expression: Fractions from the expression culture (total cell protein (TCP)), soluble fraction 
(SF), insoluble fraction (IF), wash (W), and elution (E) was analyzed by SDS-PAGE. 15 µL of each fraction 
was mixed with 5 µL 4x SDS loading dye, the loading volume was normalized following the protocol from 
pET vector manual. The gel was run for 45 min at 200 V, stained in coomassie protein stain for 20-60 min, 
and destained in destaining solution for 60 min to ON. All gel pictures were taken on a ChemiDoc (BioRad) 
imaging system using the Image Lab Touch Software (v. 2.4.0.03). 
Biotinylation EMSA: The biotinylation profile of the two recombinant toxins was tested using an EMSA. 5 
µL 10 uM biotinylated toxin was mixed with 5 µL 4x SDS loading dye and 5 µL H2O. The mix was heated 
to 95°C for 5 minutes, allowed to cool, and mixed with 5 µL 50 uM streptavidin. The samples were run on 
an SDS gel at 100 V for 120 min. 
Disulfide bond EMSA: The presence of disulfide bonds in both toxins was tested using an EMSA with 
reducing and non-reducing conditions. 10 µL of 20-50 uM toxin was mixed with 4x SDS loading dye

containing a reducing agent, and 10 µL 20-50 uM toxin was mixed with 4x SDS loading dye without a 
reducing agent. The SDS PAGE was run at 200 V for 45 min.

Day 1 – Preparation of TG1 cells: TG1 cells from glycerol stock was streaked on a minimal media plate. 
The inoculated plate was then incubated at 37°C ON. 
Day 2 – First selection round: One colony of TG1 cells from the minimal media plate from day 1 was used

agitation. 
For each selection, 50 µL streptavidin beads were used for deselection. The beads were washed three times 
with 3% MPBS (changing the tube for the last washing step) and blocked in 3% MPBS at RT for 1 h with 
end-over-end rotation. The 3% MPBS was removed, and the beads were resuspended in original volume of 
3% MPBS (50 µL). Once blocked, the beads were used to deselect the phage library. 
Phages from the IONTAS λ naive library corresponding to 50x coverage (meaning each unique scFv is 
represented 50 times (50 x 4 x 1010 pfu/mL)), were blocked in 3% MPBS at RT for 1 h with end-over-end 
rotation. The blocked library was then used to resuspend the blocked beads. Once mixed, the deselection was 
carried out at RT for 1 h with end-over-end rotation. 
For each selection, 50 µL streptavidin beads were washed three times with 3% MPBS (changing the tube for 
the last washing step) and 100 nM biotinylated antigen (either Rmipartoxin-1, Rα-cobratoxin, or α-
cobratoxin) was added. The mixture was incubated at RT for 10 min with end-over-end rotation, after which 
the beads were captured, washed three times with 3% MPBS, and blocked at RT for 1 h with end-over-end 
rotation, followed by removal of the supernatant. For each selection, one control containing no antigen was 
also prepared – this control was referred to as the zero-antigen control. 
Supernatant with the deselected library was used to resuspend the beads containing immobilized antigen and 
the zero-antigen control. The selection was carried out at RT for 30 min with end-over-end rotation. After 
which, the supernatant was removed, and the magnetic beads were washed six times with PBS 0,1% Tween. 
The beads were then incubated twice in PBS 0,1% Tween for 5 min – each time removing the buffer and 
adding fresh buffer. To remove any residual Tween, the beads were washed three times with PBS. 
The phages were eluted with 100 µL phage elution buffer containing trypsin and incubated for 15 min. The 
eluate was used to infect 10x V/V log-phase TG1 cells, and the mixture was incubated for 1 h at 37°C with 
200 rpm agitation. Serial dilutions were made, and the 10-2, 10-3, and 10-4 dilutions of the selection, and the 
10-1, 10-2, 10-3, and 10-4 dilutions of the zero-antigen control were streaked on 2TYGA plates. As a 
contamination control: 2x100 µL non-infected TG1 cells with an OD600=0,5 was streaked on 2TYGK (cell

4. Results and Discussion 
4.1 Recombinant mipartoxin-1 and α-cobratoxin expression 
Several expressions of both Rmipartoxin-1 and Rα-cobratoxin were carried out during this project. Two 
important differences between the expressions were the fact that Rmipartoxin-1 was co-expressed with a 
biotin ligase BirA which allows for “in vivo” biotinylation, whilst Rα-cobratoxin requires in vitro 
biotinylating. Furthermore, to facilitate purification and improve solubility the toxins were coupled with a 
ubiquitin-his tag at the N-terminal, to facilitate biotinylation the toxins were fused with an AviTag at the C-
terminal. 
The expression was carried out using autoinduction, and the results were analyzed using SDS PAGE where 
the total cell protein (TCP), insoluble fraction (IF), soluble fraction (SF), flowthrough (FT), wash (W), and 
elution (E) were loaded. To get an accurate estimation of the protein content and a useful comparison 
between the different fractions, the volumes were normalized to the OD600, concentration factor, and gel 
factor before loading.  
4.1.1 Expression, purification, and biotinylation profiling of Rmipartoxin-1 
Figure 8 shows the gel from the first batch of Rmipartoxin-1. The recombinant protein consisting of 
ubiquitin-his-mipartoxin-1-AviTag is highlighted, and its position on the gel is consistent with the molecular 
weight (22 kDa). Thus, both the expression and purification were successful. However, with a large band in 
the insoluble fraction it seems not all the toxin is soluble and has formed inclusion bodies, a common 
occurrence during recombinant expression [51]. One thing to note (consistent across all expressions carried 
out) is the presence of a protein corresponding to the size of the ubiquitin-his tag, linker, and TEV cleavage 
site (12 kDa) in the elution. The fact that this band is positioned at the location corresponding to ubiquitin-his 
size, and it is eluted with the protein of interest, indicates that it is the ubiquitin-his tag without the protein of 
interest. This finding is not novel, and it is postulated to be caused by the fast expression of the ubiquitin-his 
tag that is halted by the fusion protein, leading to some ubiquitin-his tag missing the fusion protein. Fusion 
constructs similar to the one used here have also been associated with spontaneous cleavage [52]. 
An EMSA to determine the biotinylation profile of the toxin is also present in figure 8. The well annotated 
“E+strep” contains the elution mixed with streptavidin: the presence of a band in this well at the annotation 
“biotinylated Rmipartoxin-1” shows the presence of biotinylated toxins. With two clear bands at the 
annotations “Ubi-His-Rmipartoxin-1-AviTag” and “Biotinylated Rmipartoxin-1”, it can be concluded that 
the biotinylation is not 100%.

A brief conclusion to the expression and purification of Rmipartoxin-1: the expression and purification was 
successful. However, some things to note are: 1) the biotinylation is not 100% and 2) there are some

be drawn regarding the concentration of the biotinylated toxin: when measuring the concentration, the

4.2 N-terminal tag removal 
The ubiquitin-his tag was removed using the TEV endoprotease, that targets the E-N-L-Y-F-Q-G/S amino 
acid sequence and cleaves the between the Q and G/S residues [53]. Once cleaved, a reverse affinity 
purification was carried out; using this method, the protein of interest is found in the flowthrough. The 
protocol was carried out on Rmipartoxin-1 and Rα-cobratoxin batches. For simplification and due to 
similarities between the two batches, only the results from Rmipartoxin-1 are presented here, and the SDS 
PAGE gel for Rα-cobratoxin is presented as supplementary figure 2 in the appendix (section 8.1). 
Figure 10 shows the SDS PAGE of the TEV cleavage and the reverse affinity purification. From the wells 
annotated “Before TEV” and “After TEV” the result of TEV cleavage can be seen. The sample loaded in the 
well “Before TEV” is the elution from the first purification (figure 8). The well annotated “After TEV” is the 
sample after TEV cleavage. The result from TEV cleavage shows far for 100% cleavage efficiency, with a 
large part of the uncleaved toxin remaining. Several conditions for TEV cleavage were tested in an attempt 
to optimize the efficiency. Condition optimization included changing the target:TEV molar ratio, changing 
buffers, swapping the TEV with another version (SuperTEV) that does not require activation by DTT [54], 
and changing temperature (4°C – 37°C) and time to cleave (1 h to ON). The result presented in figure 10 
shows the most efficient cleavage. 
Also shown in figure 10 is the reverse affinity purification. The cleaved product annotated Rmipartoxin-1-
AviTag is not present in the flowthrough as expected but found in the elution. This result is undesirable and 
did not change in subsequent repetitions, which prompted an alternative purification method. 
After cleavage, the presence of disulfide bonds was confirmed using an EMSA. The result of this EMSA is 
presented in supplementary figure 2 in the appendix (section 8.1).

4.4.1 Enrichment factor 
A good method to estimate the success of the selections is the EF which is found by dividing the CFU from 
the selection by the CFU from the zero-antigen control. The zero-antigen control is included to determine the 
background, and thus the EF is an estimation of the number of antigen-specific binders in the selection. The 
EF for all selections is shown in figure 13. 
The immediate observation is the large reduction in EF between rounds 2 and 3 in all selections except 
Rmipartoxin-1 #2. This leads to the conclusion that the selections get progressively less antigen specific. 
This is not the case for selections on Rmipartoxin-1 #2 where an increase in specificity can be observed, 
which is likely due to swapping biotin binder in the second round. The EF is a good indication of the 
presence of background binders and if the selection was successful, but another test that can be carried out to 
better determine the success of the selection is the polyclonal phage ELISA.

4.6 Monoclonal DELFIA 
Binding characteristics of the individual clones was done using direct DELFIA. DELFIA is a type of time-
resolved fluorometry immunoassay and has advantages over traditional immunoassays such as ELISA, 
including a higher dynamic range and better sensitivity. These characteristics makes DELFIA an ideal 
immunoassay for the type of characterization needed here. The protocol for DELFIA is almost identical to 
that of ELISA. However, DELFIA utilizes a Europium lanthanide chelate-labeled antibody in place of 
horseradish peroxidase-coupled antibody. In this project a Europium labeled anti-Tri-FLAG antibody was 
used as the “tracer” binding to the scFvs. A signal was generated by exciting the Europium at 340 nM 
wavelength resulting in a measurable emission signal at 615 nM, which was read using a plate reader.  
All clones recovered from the selections were subcloned into the pSANG10-3F vector (figure 3D) which 
links two C-terminal tags (his6 and Tri-FLAG) to the scFv and guides the expression to the periplasmic 
space via the pelB signal sequence (figure 3D) [56]. The total number of clones recovered from subcloning 
was 184 from Rmipartoxin-1 #1 selections, 92 from Rα-cobratoxin selections, 128 from α-cobratoxin 
selections, and 192 from Rmipartoxin-1 #2 selections. A preliminary DELFIA was carried out to cherry-pick 
clones with specific antigen-binding (see supplementary figures 4, 5, and 6 in the appendix section 8.3). Any 
clones with a higher signal than a threshold of 10x negative control were picked and used for subsequent 
characterization. In total 66 clones from Rmipartoxin-1 #1 selections, 51 clones from Rα-cobratoxin 
selections, 24 clones from α-cobratoxin selections, and 20 clones from Rmipartoxin-1 #2 selections were 
cherry-picked.  
The first DELFIA on the clones from the Rmipartoxin-1 #2 selections included streptavidin as an antigen, 
and an additional threshold of streptavidin signal below 5x negative control was used for this cherry-picking. 
The results from the first DELFIA on Rmipartoxin-1 #2 selections can be found in supplementary figure 7 in 
the appendix section 8.3.

4.7 Sequencing of cherry-picked clones 
Cherry-picked clones from all selections were sent to sequencing to identify unique clones and to enable an 
analysis of the six complementarity-determining regions (CDR). To get the widest range of binding profiles, 
clones showing high specific binding, cross binding, and even some streptavidin binders were sent to 
sequencing. Out of the 70 clones sent to sequencing (20 from Rα-cobratoxin, α-cobratoxin, and 
Rmipartoxin-1 #2 selections and 10 from Rmipartoxin-1 #1 selections) 52 were successfully sequenced. Out 
of the 52, 30 clones were unique. 
Using a α-cobratoxin-specific clone (D11) from Line Ledsgaard with defined CDRs as a template, it was 
possible to compare the CDRs of the clones. The CDR sequences are listed in table 6 below. Clones tested in 
the 3rd DELFIA are highlighted in table 6 for easy comparison between the 3rd DELFIA and the sequencing. 
4.7.1 Clones from Rmipartoxin-1 selections 
Selections on Rmipartoxin-1 #1 contained 7 unique clones out of the 10 sent to sequencing. Unfortunately, 
this selection had high affinity towards streptavidin. There are five general streptavidin motifs described in 
the literature: HPQ, HPM, SVP, SVP(W/F)(S/T)V, and HP(Y/F)P [57–59]. However, none of the well-
known motifs are present in any of the clones, which signifies the presence of unknown streptavidin-binding 
motifs.  
Out of the 20 clones send to sequencing from the Rmipartoxin-1 #2 selection, 15 did not contain the scFv 
gene, which confirms the results obtained in 3rd DELFIA (figure 19) where the empty vectors show low-to-
no signal. Of the five clones that contained an scFv gene, two unique clones were identified. Clones 
TPL0574_01_D06, TPL0574_02_B05, TPL0574_02_B04, and TPL0574_02_F12 were identical and will be 
referred to as TPL0574_02_F12. The two unique clones have identical HCDR1, HCDR2 while LCDR1 and 
LCDR2 differ by 1-2 amino acids. It is well known that the HCDR3 is the most varied in terms of length and 
composition and has a large part to play in antigen binding [60], so the high variety in the HCDR3 region 
between the two clones is not unexpected.  
Comparing the unique clones from Rmipartoxin-1 #1 and #2 to the template and the other clones, there are 
some motifs which are repeated. The YY(G)MDV motif at the end of the HCDR3 sequence is shared across 
a large majority of the unique clones found here, such as TPL0574_01_E04, TPL0444_02_C08, 
TPL0444_02_B07, TPL0442_01_C04, TPL0441_01_H04, and TPL0441_01_F12 just to name a few. Thus, 
this motif seems to have an implication in binding with 3FTx. Clones TPL0574_02_F12 and 
TPL0444_02_F07 have very unique HCDR3 sequences that share no homology with other HCDR3 regions 
from the other clones. Both of these unique clones show specific binding to Rmipartoxin-1 with low binding 
to all the other antigens tested.

5. Conclusion 
 
The goal of this project was two-fold 1) expressing and purifying of the two toxins mipartoxin-1 and α-
cobratoxin, and 2) discovery and characterization of Rmipartoxin-1, Rα-cobratoxin, and α-cobratoxin 
specific antibody fragments. 
Several rounds of expression and purification was carried out, producing enough toxin to allow for 
characterization and using them as antigens for the subsequent phage display campaigns. However, 
purification proved somewhat troublesome, and several purification methods were tested. After an extensive 
troubleshooting phase, the method of choice was still unable to remove all contaminants from the 
recombinant toxins. The contaminant remaining in the solution was the ubiquitin-his tag, and its presence 
meant that toxin concentrations were never accurately determined. In the future it would be favorable to 
exchange the ubiquitin-his tag with a larger, alternative solubility tag that wouldn’t elude simultaneously 
during size exclusion chromatography purification. 
Four solid phase phage selections were carried out during this project. The initial results from the enrichment 
factor showed a high background in the first 3 selections. However, the polyclonal phage ELISA showed the 
presence of specific binders. Selection number four was carried out on recombinant mipartoxin-1 using an 
alternative selection strategy, in the hopes the alternative strategy would reduce the background. The 
background proved much lower using this selection strategy, thus any future solid phase phage selections 
should be done with this specific selection strategy. 
Clones were characterized using polyclonal phage ELISA and monoclonal DELFIA. Output from the Rα-
cobratoxin and α-cobratoxin selections showed high cross reactivity. However, the comparison was 
restricted by the concentration problem associated with Rα-cobratoxin, described above. Output from the 
first Rmipartoxin-1 #1 selection proved to contain a large number of streptavidin-binding clones. The second 
Rmipartoxin-1 #2 selection showed high Rmipartoxin-1 specificity. 
While the overall goals of this thesis were accomplished, a lot of work on characterization the recombinant 
toxins and the discovered clones still remains. Future work that I deem beneficial is described in the next 
section.

6. Future and Commercial Perspectives 
6.1 Future perspectives 
The work carried out in this thesis is applied research and should only be viewed as such. The clones 
discovered and the conclusions drawn, show promise in the context of this project but may not correlate to 
real-world scenarios. 
A lot of work is still required to support the conclusions drawn. The toxin concentration problem would be 
the first problem to tackle. To solve this, an alternative means of separating and purifying the biotinylated 
toxin would allow for accurate concentration estimates. Once accurate toxin concentration measurements 
were obtained, a new DELFIA could be carried out, where the scFv and toxin concentrations were 
normalized across all clones. Which would allow for an accurate comparison between the signals obtained. A 
simple test like that would result in more accurate conclusions on scFv-toxin specificity and cross-binding 
characteristics. Moreover, the scFvs showing binding to recombinant mipartoxin-1 should also be tested on 
the native toxin. Another characteristic of the scFvs that would be desirable to obtain, is the on- and off-rates 
which can be measure by methods such as Surface Plasmon Resonance. 
All the results presented in this thesis assume that the two recombinant toxins have the same structure and 
conformation as the native toxins. This has, however, never been confirmed. A basic means of testing the 
functionality of the recombinant toxin is testing their nAChR binding abilities in a binding assay. Such a 
binding assay would use the same principle as an ELISA, by using the nAChRs as the ligand, the 
biotinylated toxin as the analyte, and streptavidin coupled HRP as the quantifiable signal. Another way of 
assessing the functionality of the recombinant toxins is testing its activity to inhibit ACh-mediated signaling. 
This can be done by Patch-clamp electrophysiological assays which measures the change in current inside 
and outside cells in two conditions: when exposed to ACh and while under the antagonistic action of the 
toxin.  
The basic analysis of the toxic activity would be followed up by more an in-depth structural analysis. A 
preliminary test to assess the secondary structure such as Circular Dichroism, would elucidate on the 
efficiency of producing correctly folded toxins. To assess the cysteine connectivity, a sequential reduction 
and alkylation followed by mass spectrometric analysis as described by Anastasia et al., 2016 [61] could be 
applied. Ultimately the structure of the recombinant toxins should be assessed using X-ray crystallography 
and compared to X-ray crystallography data of the native toxin. X-ray crystallography could also be applied 
to the recombinant toxin-scFv interaction to discover the epitope on the toxins. Another method to elucidate 
==PAGE:57==
54 
 
the epitope-paratope interaction is the High-Density Peptide Microarray where binding of the scFv is tested 
on linear sections of the toxin to determine the binding site. 
A plethora of other analysis to characterize the scFvs and recombinant toxins could be carried out – too 
many to fully include in this report. However, the tests described above would strengthen the conclusions 
drawn in this project. 
6.2 Commercial perspectives 
The main binders found in this project are, in their current form, unfeasible as a future antivenom. With that 
said, focusing on improving the affinity, converting the scFv to IgG format, and testing in vivo neutralization 
would increase the potential as an antivenom. As mentioned, the antivenom available for M. mipartitus 
treatment has shown poor mipartoxin-1 binding. This creates an opportunity for us to boost the efficiency of 
the antivenom by supplementing it with the binders found here. However, due to the limited amount of M. 
mipartitus envenoming cases each year, the market for this antivenom is very small. As far as the author can 
tell, two attempts at producing a polyvalent antivenom for all coral snakes in the Americas have been made – 
this so-called Pan-American anti-coral antivenom has shown promise at neutralizing venom from M. 
mipartitus. However, it was never commercialized [15,62]. The fact that attempts at producing antivenom for 
M. mipartitus have been carried out but was never implemented, reduces the likelihood that the product 
developed here will be implemented in current antivenom schemes. 
Another aspect that lies beyond what has been done in this thesis – but ties in with the methods utilized, is 
the use of recombinant antigens as a means of discovering antibodies. This is not a novel approach to the 
development of antigens [63], but by using bioinformatics and modern molecular biology it is possible to 
create almost any antigen desired. Antigens designed to contain a consensus sequence can be used for the 
discovery of antibodies with high cross-reactivity to all proteins involved in the consensus sequence. This 
has a higher potential for commercialization than the antibody discovered here.  
But let’s entertain the idea of making a product of the specific antibody discovered here. The first order of 
business would be an extensive and expensive development phase, where the characteristics (neutralizing 
effect, pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic analysis, and many more) of the antibody would be assessed. 
After confirming the efficiency of the antivenom and pursuing implementing it as a biopharmaceutical, it 
would need to be protected. There are a few ways of protecting the product, 1) by patenting the product or 
the method used to obtain it. In the case of this thesis, the patent would likely be on the specific amino acid 
sequence of the antibody, or alternatively, the method of expressing disulfide-rich proteins in bacteria. 
However, for the process to be patentable, it must be novel, and the process of producing disulfide-rich 
proteins is not novel. 2) keeping the product, a secret from competitors, is a good way of ensuring that the 
==PAGE:58==
55 
 
specific product is not copied. This way of protecting the intellectual property is widely used in the biotech 
industry and is a cheaper alternative to patenting.
