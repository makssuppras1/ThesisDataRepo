Abstract
In line with the wishes of the Municipality of Copenhagen, this thesis 
aims to investigate the possibilities of utilizing plastic from waste 
generated in the Municipality of Copenhagen and implementing 
a circular economy to redesign its city furniture. The study, initially, 
accounts for a design brief with the Municipality of Copenhagen, 
where it is suggested that one of their city furniture should be 
redesigned and made more environmentally friendly  by using the 
plastic of the citizens of Copenhagen as material for the redesign,
Based on a pilot study of the redesign of Copenhagen city furniture, 
different aspects of the problem statement from the design brief 
are addressed through four phases:
The first phase researches the waste system in the Municipality 
of Copenhagen to evaluate what plastic waste sources are most 
relevant to use as a material supply for redesigning the city furniture. 
Relevant knowledge of plastic sorting, recycling, and material 
properties of recycled plastic is obtained through literature studies, 
interviews, and fieldwork. Additionally, relevant city furniture in the 
Municipality of Copenhagen is analyzed to find opportunities for a 
redesign. This leads to the conclusion that the highest contributor 
to the environmental footprint of the city furniture was the material 
consumption related to their design and use. Additionally, it is 
found that recycled HDPE from household waste can be used in the 
redesign of the city furniture as it has good mechanical properties 
for impact resistance and recyclability for closed-loop systems.
The study’s second phase selects the city furniture with the most 
potential for environmental improvements to be redesigned. The 
product with the most potential for a redesign was the Pizzakurv, 
which is a garbage bin. Then, based on various design methods, 
the Miløkurv, is conceptualized. The Miljøkurv prolongs the lifetime 
of its material by making a closed-loop maintenance system that 
reuses its plastic components.
The third phase of the study details the design of the miljøkurv 
through various alternative solutions that are evaluated by 
prototyping and simulation studies. This results in optimized 
strength, material reduction, and maintainability of the Miljøkurv.
Finally, the fourth phase evaluates the environmental and economic 
performance of the Miljøkurv. The redesign is expected to reduce 
the environamtal footprint of the Pizzakurv by 91.5% and its financial 
cost by 81.5%. Then, the redesign was evaluated by stakeholders 
from the Municipality of Copenhagen. Overall, they considered the 
redesign feasible and viable. Finally, based on the pilot study, a four-
step plan is proposed to guide the Municipality to implement the 
Miljøkurv fully.
Based on the four phases of the study, it is concluded that city 
furniture can be made from recycled waste generated by the citizens 
of Copenhagen and that the city furniture’s environmental footprint 
and financial cost can be reduced through the implementation of 
circular systems.

Preface
This is a master’s thesis in collaboration with the Municipality of 
Copenhagen. The project was supervised  by Torben Anker Lenau, 
and was done in the period from the 24th of January to the 24th of 
June 2022.
A set of appendices were created to be followed while reading this 
report to document calculations, research, and processes from the 
underlying work done throughout the project. Therefore, more 
detailed information will be referred to in an appendix, whereas only 
the most essential information and documentation were written in 
this report.
The thesis is based on a design brief with the Municipality of 
Copenhagen (see Section 1.3). A set of learning goals, seen in the 
project description, were made to scope the project initially. The 
project description is seen in Appendices A.1 and A.2.
The report is divided into four phases: Research, Conceptualization, 
Detailing, and Validation & Implementation. A set of questions were 
stated at the beginning of each phase. These questions were divided 
into topics and were answered in their corresponding sections as a 
topic summary. The questions were made to communicate what 
was considered relevant to study throughout the different stages 
of the project and to follow the development of the different parts.
A set of tools and methods were used throughout the project. When 
used, the report describes the reason for choosing a given method 
and its application. In addition, the introduction has a description 
of the empirical methods for gathering the information presented 
during the project.
Both group members have taken an equally part in the overall 
work of this project. However, throughout this report, areas of 
responsibility have been divided. The division of responsibility are 
seen Appendix A.3.

This paper accounts for the processes of redesigning a piece of 
existing city furniture in the Municipality of Copenhagen by utilizing 
plastic waste produced within the municipality. This master’s 
project, Environmental and sustainable handling of plastic waste in 
the municipality of Copenhagen - a pilot study, involves:
• Researching the current waste handling of plastics, recycled 
plastics potential, and the existing city furniture of the Municipality 
of Copenhagen.
• Choosing a piece of city furniture to redesign based on  
environmental impact, financial costs, and then proposing concept 
solutions based on the problems found in the existing product.
• Detailing the redesign of the chosen product based on 
simulations, mock-ups, and a functional prototype.
• Evaluating the final redesigns environmental and financial 
performance compared to the current solution and proposing an 
implementation plan for the redesign
A pilot study is a small-scale preliminary study before focusing on a 
large-scale implementation. A pilot study conducts research before 
a full-scale project, with the primary outcome being knowledge and 
data that provides insight into potential results in a shorter amount 
of time compared to implementing a product in full-scale (Simkus, 
2022). Therefore, pilot studies are also referred to as feasibility studies, 
as it is used to evaluate the feasibility of the project by conducting 
it on a smaller scale.
1.1 Motivation
Today, there is an incentive to burn less and recycle more. For 
example, for each recycled kilogram of plastic, 2.4 kg of CO2 is saved 
(AVL, n.d.). Furthermore, circular economy, resource efficiency, 
sustainable consumption, and production have been on the EU 
agenda in recent years (AVL, n.d.). The consumption of plastics 
produces both CO2 and other harmful waste. 60% of the generated 
plastic waste in Denmark in 2019 was incinerated (Ritzau, 2019) and 
remained a source of CO2 pollution and financial loss. 1.6 billion DKK 
annually could be earned from recycling instead of incinerating 
the plastic waste (Ritzau,2019). It is estimated that the goods and 
products being used in Denmark have a total plastic content of 
almost 2.1 million tonnes (DS, 2021), where household consumption 
stands for around 350 thousand tonnes. 
In 2015 Denmark collected 31% of its waste from households for 
recycling. Denmark is therefore in the lowest quarter for recycling 
the household waste in the EU, where the average collection rate was 
around 41% (Dakofa, 2018) while also being the country in Europe 
with the highest municipal waste of 844 kg waste generated per

1.3 Design Brief
A design brief was defined through a meeting at the TMF with Søren 
Mikkelsen and Kira Thume (Appendix A.4.1) to guide the project’s 
direction.
Background
The TMF acknowledges the problems that the Copenhagen 
Municipality has with handling plastic waste. The TMF wants to 
use the plastic generated within the municipality to become more 
sustainable and promote a sustainable image by utilizing HHW 
plastic as the material for their city furniture. The TMF describes a 
problem with the current city furniture being subjected to vandalism 
and wear. Moreover, the city furniture is scrapped whenever its 
appearance or functionality has been damaged, which does not 
align with the environmental image they want.
Goals & Objectives 
The project should propose a redesign of existing city furniture to 
document this pilot project’s possibilities and provide a success 
story, which could influence further work with sustainable redesigns 
of the MC’s city furniture. Furthermore, the redesign of the city 
furniture should utilize plastic generated within the municipality of 
Copenhagen by its citizens, as this will help create a green initiative 
towards more circularity. Additionally, the redesigned product 
should produce less CO2 while remaining financially viable. Finally, 
the redesign should not compromise the existing functions of the 
city furniture and should be implementable in the system around 
the existing city furniture.
Schedule
The duration of the project is set to start on the 24th of January 
and will be delivered at the 24th of June. In addition, a series of 
meetings and workshops will be conducted throughout the project 
to encourage co-design and validate the different stages of the 
product development process.
Project Deliverables
The study should present a quantification that compares the current 
city furniture and its redesign. The comparison should indicate 
significant CO2 reduction and cost savings. Also, a prototype should 
be made to prove the concept’s feasibility and viability. Lastly, a 
short and long-term implementation plan to put the redesign into 
practice should be proposed to the TMF.
Problem Statement
The following problem statement was formulated based on goals, 
objectives, and problems discussed during the design brief:
“How can the Municipality of Copenhagen recycle collected plastics 
to build city furniture?”

1.5 Sustainable Strategies
As this project mainly focuses on sustainability, it was essential to 
document what strategies that were used to verify the sustainable 
aspects behind the redesign. Therefore, two main sustainable 
strategies, Circular Economy (CE) and Design for ”X” (DFX),  are 
explained as they were the main drivers behind the sustainable 
mindset for this project. Furthermore, the strategies influenced the 
requirements of the redesigned product and its additional systems.
1.5.1 Circular Economy
One of the most well-known frameworks to tackle environmental 
challenges is the CE Framework (MacArthur et al., 2013).  The 
goal is to keep a product in the shortest loop possible, meaning 
maintaining, reusing, re-manufacturing, or recycling products 
instead of disposing of them for energy recovery or landfill. A product 
should be designed to stay in a loop for as long as possible instead 
of a linear approach that focuses on take-make-dispose. The CE

framework is seen in Figure 1.2. CE was the primary model for the 
project to secure a sustainable and economically viable approach.
1.5.2 Design For ”X”
CE is a strategy to guide future product and service development to 
create better environmental performance (MacArthur et al., 2013). 
Design for “X” (DfX) is a more tangible tool than CE and it expresses a 
wide variety of scopes, Product Scope, System Scope and Ecosystem 
Scope, that can be used within product design. These scopes can be 
directly linked to a specific life cycle stage (Chiu & Okudan, 2010). The 
model, ”Design for X”, is seen in Figure 1.3 and it shows eight overall 
approaches with examples such as Design for: Assembly (DfA) or 
Maintainability (DfM). The purpose of DfX is to design a product 
through a holistic approach where the entire product development 
process is influenced (Meerkamm & Koch, 2005, p. 308). Figure 1.3 is 
divided into two major subjects which are Design for Efficiency and 
Green Design. Both subjects have scopes that help ensure the right 
approach towards DfX and circular economy.
1.6 Project Plan
Iteration is a defining aspect of design theory and design practices. 
However, most planning tools do not implement iteration. Tools 
such as the double diamond describe iteration but do not visualize 
it in its visual time frame. Planning tools such as Gantt charts 
provide a linear approach to planning which goes against the 
unpredictability of designing through iterations. Gantt Charts are 
prescriptive about what should happen and do not consider what 
could happen (Wynn, Clarkson, et al., 2009). Therefore, the planning 
was divided into micro and macro planning.
1.6.1 Macro Planning
Macro planning ensures that the overall goals of a project are in 
place and what the vision of the result should be. Macro planning 
can also consider repeating tasks within different processes to 
accommodate new findings in the design process (Simicic, 2019). 
A design process model was developed from the activity time plan 
(see Appendix A.2) and the initial design brief to define milestones 
and common goals for this project. These were illustrated an 
activity plan for this pilot project and can be seen in Figure 1.4, and 
in Appendix A.6.1. Additionally, lists of the tools that were used in 
the different phases are included in the plan. However, these were 
added to the plan after finishing the project.

2.1 Sorting of Plastic In The Municipality of 
Copenhagen
This section creates an overview of the selected plastic waste flow 
from its disposal to where it can be recycled. This was done with the 
purpose of mapping a possible supply chain for the waste source in 
the MC as a material for redesigning their city furniture. Furthermore, 
this section looks at the CO2 footprint of transporting plastic to a 
sorting facility and its economic aspects. Lastly, an overview of the 
plastic fractions and the amount that can be used from the sorting 
facility is displayed.
2.1.1 Mapping of Actors Involved With Handling of 
Waste In The MC
Actors involved in the waste management system in the MC were 
identified along with the sequence in which the waste, financial 
agreements, and information sharing move between them. 
Understanding how the system works and is formed through the 
interactions between the actors (Callon, 1990) and the intent behind 
the work practices of the actors (Beyer & Holtzblatt, 1999, p. 96) 
gained a holistic insight into how a supply chain for the redesign 
could be formed. The information about the waste system in the 
MC and its actors was gained through two separate interviews with 
Martin Tilsted from PARC (Appendix A.4.5), and Søren Mikkelsen 
and Kira Thume from PKR (Appendix A.4.1). A map of the waste 
flow between the actors was created from a constellation between 
a sequence analysis and an actor/network. The map is seen in 
Appendix B.9 and in Figure 2.1.
The arrows in Figure 2.1 indicate a sequence of actions between 
the actors, including the flow of waste and finances between them. 
It was found that the Municipality of Copenhagen differentiates 
between three types of waste depending on if it originates from 
households, industries, or public spaces (Kommune, 2019, p. 11).
Industrial Waste
It was discovered that there was a clear separation between 
household waste (HHW), industrial waste, and general waste as 
they had few interactions. Industrial plastic waste has a short cycle 
because the plastic waste created from it has a high demand due 
to its low contamination. Industrial plastic waste is often recycled 
by the company itself and is a significant contributor to closed-loop 
systems for plastic products (see Appendix A.4.11).
Household Waste
HHW is segregated and later sorted into specific fractions at the 
sorting facility, Hündgen Entsorgung, in Germany. It was found 
that this waste source generates around 15 to 20 thousand tonnes 
of plastic a year and has low demand (see Appendix A.4.5). Lastly, 
as shown in Figure 2.1, there is a money/material flow between the 
recycled plastic and the TMF indicated with dotted lines. Through 
an interview with Britt Nielsen & Malene Møhl, it was discovered 
that the TMF could buy back its plastic waste from Hündgen at a 
price lower than the market price (see appendix A.4.9). However, 
this has not been initiated by TMF yet as they do not have a use for 
recycled plastic.

General Waste
The general waste generated has no sorting methods through 
the garbage bins in the public areas. All the waste created 
and gathered is considered hazardous waste and is therefore 
incinerated to provide energy. Also, the TMF estimates that only 
around 30 tonnes of plastics a year could be gathered through the 
public waste (see Appendix A.4.1). Therefore, the fraction of plastic 
is too small to investigate further compared to the other sources.
Legislation
Figure 2.1 shows a legislative decision framework. The company in 
charge of handling the plastic waste is decided through common 
EU regulations, meaning that the agreement of which company 
should handle the plastic waste must be put out to tender by the 
government. The government then states a list of requirements 
for companies to fulfill to apply for the task. In 2021, Hündgen 
Entsorgung won that bid and has since been the main recipient 
of the household plastics from the MC and six other surrounding 
cities.
Selection of Waste Source
The plastic generated from households was chosen as the most 
promising material supply. HHW was chosen based on the 
knowledge of waste from households, industries, and public 
spaces, together with a discussion with the environmental policy 
manager of Plastindustrien, Christina Busk (see Appendix A.4.11), 
and Søren Mikkelsen and Kira Thume (Appendix A.4.1). Plastic 
from HHW is legal to use, has a sufficient supply quantity, and 
low material demand. Furthermore, HHW is already sorted into 
different polymer fractions. Therefore, the current HHW man-
agement framework for the MC was further researched.
2.1.2 Household Waste Management
This section aims to map the flow of the household plastic in the MC. 
From the first step of disposing of the plastic by the citizens until it 
becomes a sorted fraction of plastic ready for recycling. Additionally, 
the CO2 footprint for transporting the plastic to the sorting facility 
was calculated to estimate the environmental footprint of a supply 
chain using recycled plastic from HHW. Lastly, an exposition of the 
sorted polymer fractions was presented.

As of the 1st of July 2021, all municipalities in Denmark has to segregate 
their household waste into nine different categories (Christensen, 
2021). These categories are seen in Figure 2.2.

The citizens of Copenhagen segregate their household waste 
differently depending on their address. In the populated areas 
of Copenhagen, most waste is segregated in the courtyards of 
apartment blocks and is emptied once a week. In less populated 
areas, the garbage must be segregated at a recycle station or in 
shared garbage containers. There is the possibility to sort the waste 
for all citizens; however, not all areas in Copenhagen sort their waste 
correctly, which results in considerable variation in the quality of the 
waste (Appendix B.4).
Preparing Plastic for Transportation
When the citizens have segregated the waste, it will be collected by 
different garbage trucks designated to each of the nine categories 
from Figure 2.2. The segregated waste will then be transported to 
different locations for recycling or other treatments (AVV, 2022). 
When the plastic waste has been collected, it will be transported 
by garbage trucks to either ARC or Vestforbrændingen (Appendix 
A.4.9). When the plastic arrives, it is compressed and stacked into 
400kg unsorted pallets. The compressed plastic waste is then 
stockpiled until it is ready to be transported to a sorting facility, as 
illustrated in Figure 2.3 and Appendix B.3.

Transportation to the Sorting Facility
The pallets of plastic waste are then transported to the plastic 
sorting facility, Hündgen Entsorgung, four times a week (see 
Appendix B.1). The waste is then sorted at the facility on Tuesdays 
and Wednesdays. This is so that the waste from the MC does not 
get mixed with waste from other sources. Furthermore, this secures 
transparency on the material the MC sends to Hündgen. The 
private company, Combineering (Combineering, 2022) handles the 
transportation of the plastics. Combineering has contact with all 
the haulers to ensure that the transportation is documented and 
timed correctly (See Appendix A.4.15). 
Furthermore, Combineering ensures that the waste is handled in 
a CO2-friendly manner and that it is being reported. A large part 
of the transport is done by trucks. These trucks will transport the 
compressed plastic from Copenhagen, down south to the fairy from 
Rødby to Puttgarden, and then to Hündgen. After an interview with 
Slawomir Edward Debski, a Project Manager from Combineering, 
a detailed report of Combineering’s calculations for the CO2-eq. 
footprint of their transport was obtained. An illustration of the data 
obtained is seen in Figure 2.4.
As of now, each truck driving from Copenhagen to Hündgen 
Entsorgung has a total footprint pr. truckload of 774.73 kg CO2-
eq. (see Appendix A.4.16). The MC pays 2,826 DKK per tonne of 
plastic waste sent to Hündgen (Andersen, 2019) while each truck 
can carry up to 22 tonnes. Currently, Combineering is testing some 
new transportation routes by train. If Combineering would use 
this alternative route with trains, then the final CO2 footprint of an 
equivalent truckload would have a CO2 footprint of 405.41 kg CO2, 
which is a decrease of 47.67%.

Sorting Effectiveness
A data sheet acquired from Vestforbrændingen made by Hündgen 
Entsorgung revealed how much plastic waste was sent to Hündgen 
Entsorgung in 2021  and how much was recycled, and what 
fragments it was sorted into. An overview of the data is seen in 
Appendix B.2. The data is represented in Figure 2.5 and it showed 
that 17,337 tonnes of plastic were sent to Hündgen, whereas 13.590 
tonnes (78,4%) were sorted for recycling, and 3.746 tonnes (21,6%) 
were sent to an incineration facility as it consisted of low-quality 
polymers, organic material, or wrongly sorted materials. The four 
main polymers collected from the households in Copenhagen 
and sorted at Hündgen were plastic films (LDPE) (23.44%), PP 
(7.99%), PET (5.61%), and HDPE (5.49%). The number of sorted plastic 
products containing more than one plastic-type was 30.29%, which 
naturally is much harder to recycle than monopolymers.

2.1.3 Topic Summary
Q1.1 - Who are the stakeholders managing the waste?
The stakeholders involved in handling waste in the MC were 
separated into stakeholders dealing with legislation, producing 
trash, or handling trash. The Danish government determines rules 
concerning handling and producing trash. The trash is organized by 
the facilities ARC and Vestforbrændingen.
Q1.2 - How is waste collected?
The waste collected in the MC is categorized into waste coming from 
households (HHW), public areas, and the industry. Waste collected 
from public areas is not sorted, as it is defined as contaminated, 
which makes it illegal to collect for recycling. Instead, this waste 
is transported directly to incineration. Industrial plastic waste is 
often sorted in pure fractions, making it desirable for companies to 
purchase. This plastic mostly ends up being recycled internally by 
companies. HHW from the MC is transported to Germany, where it 
is sorted and sold to different customers.
Q1.3 - How much CO2 is emitted from a supply chain of 
plastic made from waste?
According to the circular economy, a supply chain of recycled 
material should start with the end-of-life phase of the recycled 
material. Therefore, the supply chain’s cost is initially determined by 
the emissions from transportation. The company transporting the 
plastic waste is called Combineering. It was found that one truckload 
of material with their current route is 774.73 kg CO2. However, this 
will be reduced to 405.41 kg CO2 if they follow through with their 
plans of using trains.
Q1.4 - What fractions is the plastic sorted into?
The plastic from the MC is sorted at the sorting facility Hündgen 
Entsorgung. It is sorted into LDPE, HDPE, PP, and mixed polymers. 
However, they are capable of sorting in multiple different types.
Q1.5 - How much waste is recycled?
17,337 tonnes of plastic from the HHW in the MC was transported to 
Germany through the year 2021. Out of the 17,337 tonnes of plastic, 
13.590 tonnes were sorted. The recyclability efficiency for 2021 was 
therefore 78%. The remaining 22% was incinerated.
Q1.6 - How can the plastic from the citizens of Copenhagen 
be collected?
Household waste has been chosen as the most promising material 
supply based on the legislation, wishes from the MC, and the 
quantity of material generated.

2.2 Plastic Sorting & Recycling Theory
This section describes the underlying processes and technologies 
of sorting and recycling plastic to gain sufficient knowledge to 
create a feasible supply chain. Furthermore, the potential of the 
plastic fractions sorted at Hündgen was evaluated based on their 
recyclability, demand, suitable manufacturing processes, and 
mechanical properties to help choose materials and manufacturing 
methods during the redesign. Finally, other waste recycling solutions 
were researched for additional insight and learning, including other 
municipalities in Denmark, an earlier redesign of the city furniture in 
the MC, and different types of products made from various methods 
of recycled plastic.
2.2.1 Sorting Processes At Hündgen Entsorgung
The sorting process was examined to understand the quality and the 
fractions of the material that would be used in the future redesign. 
An in-depth description of the technical process will be explained in

Rotary Screening

Eddy Current Separator
The batch is then moved through a field of a charged ionizing 
electrode. This induces non-magnetic metals. If a magnetic 
induction changes a material over time, a voltage in the material 
is created. The magnetic induction creates a magnetic field in the 
non-magnetic metals as long as it passes through a field of charged 
ions. The magnetic field of the materials is then subjected to an 
opposing magnetic field that removes all metals from the batch.
Airflow Separation
The film and light plastics are then separated from the batch by 
an airflow separator attached to the conveyor belt. The material is 
moved into a chamber with an airflow circulating, blowing all light 
films and bags into a separate tube. This is an efficient process to 
separate low density and high density polymers, as polymers with 
high density will be heavier and, therefore, not be discarded.

NIR Spectrocopy Technology
Lastly, the high density polymers from the batch is sorted into 
the fractions: LDPE, PP, PET, and PE by a Near-infrared-scanner 
(NIR). A NIR scanner works by emitting infrared radiation from 
an incandescent bulb (SpectralEngines, 2019). This reflects the 
materials it hits into a receiver. Each type of plastic has its unique 
frequency band, which the receiver detects. Each NIR scanner is 
programmed to look for one specific polymer. This fraction is then 
blown away from the batch and into its separated chamber. A 
facility like Hündgen has multiple scanners and can separate each 
polymer type in one cycle.

The term additive describes a complex blend of materials that

One of the problems with recycled plastic is its resistance to 
degradation. Two types of stress happen during the recycling 
process: shear stress, which promotes molecular chain branching,

resistances for the HDPE and PP made from the HHW. Table 2.6 
shows that the HDPE made from the HHW has a higher resistance 
to thermal degradation than the PP. In the industry, antioxidants, 
a type of additive, are used to accommodate the continuous 
degradation of recycled plastic through closed-loop recycling

2.2.5 Topic summary
Q2.1 - How is plastic sorted for recycling?
After arriving at the sorting facility at Hündgen Entsorgung, all plastic 
bags containing trash are opened through ballistic separation. 
After that, the trash is manually sorted. The trash is then sorted 
into fractions by rotary screening. Next, an eddy current separator 
removes metals from the trash. Then, an airflow separator sorts out 
plastic films. Lastly, A NIR scanner sorts out one plastic fraction at a 
time.
Q2.2 - How is plastic recycled?
A mono-polymer fraction in the industry is commonly recycled by 
shredding the plastic material into smaller pieces. The shredded 
granulate is then washed with a high-speed friction washer. The 
material is then further sorted through density separation. The dust 
and other small particles are removed through a mechanical dryer. 
The granulate is then ready to use.
Q2.3 - What plastic fractions are suitable for a redesign?
The polymers HDPE and PP are the best fractions. This is because 
HDPE keeps most mechanical properties during the first ten times 
of recycling. Therefore, HDPE is the most recycled plastic fraction. 
PP has slightly worse recycling capabilities but is the least recycled 
fraction of the hard plastics. PET is recyclable, but its mechanical 
properties are not excellent. Therefore, its use is most commonly in 
food packaging. LDPE is also recyclable, but it is a low-cost material 
used for films and foams.
Q2.4 - What manufacturing methods are durable for 
recycled plastic?
Manufacturing methods for plastic is determined by its MFI. Mono-
fractions can obtain different MFI’s depending on their quality. It 
shows that the MFI for HDPE and PP made from HHW from the MC 
are different from each other. The PP is useful for blow molding and 
injection molding, while HDPE is more suitable for extrusion and 
blow molding.
Q2.5 - Are there any local alternatives to sorting plastic?
Whether or not using local sorting facilities has significant 
environmental benefits depends on the quality of the material 
provided by smaller local sorting facilities compared to the additional 
environmental and financial costs related to transportation.
Q2.6 - How does other products use recycled plastics?
An earlier redesign of the Copenhagen city furniture, the 
Københavnerbænk, showed constraints in manufacturing when 
using recycled plastics. A redesign using plastics from HHW 
could benefit from considering the most feasible production and 
manufacturing methods instead of mimicking the designs and 
shapes found in existing furniture. However, some companies 
like Polywood use additives to alter the mechanical properties of 
the products to mimic other materials. Another solution could 
be to control the ratio of virgin and recycled plastics to enhance 
the material’s mechanical properties and enable the use of more 
manufacturing methods.

2.4.2 Other Impacts
The CES analysis only looks at certain parameters that directly 
influence the CO2 footprint from the products. However, other 
impacts should be mentioned, as not only the CO2 footprint can 
justify if a material or process is environmentally viable. This section, 
therefore, focuses on other aspects that should be kept in mind 
when redesigning the city furniture.
Use of Steel & Primer
The primary steel material used for the Pizzakurv and the Tårnkurv 
is galvanized steel. The production of galvanized steel emits zinc 
fumes that are toxic to inhale. After the steel has cooled, a primer will 
be used to secure that the paint does not fall of (Contributors, 2021). 
The primer is also used to prevent rust and corrosion. Nevertheless, 
the primer used for galvanized steel is hazardous and toxic to its 
environment. Additionally, a similar problem is present when 
welding stainless steel. The fumes created from welding increase 
the risk of lung cancer if inhaled (Rasmussen, 2006, p. 23). Therefore, 
the redesign should focus on reducing the amount of steel, primers, 
paint, Etc.
Waste In Production
A sole source of impacts that can increase the environmental 
footprint, if not considered, is the waste in production. Production 
waste can be considered into eight different types which are 
defects, excessive processing, overproduction, waiting, inventory, 
transport, motion, and non-utilized talent (Gay, 2019). The term lean 
manufacturing focuses on waste in production. The goal is to remove 
the waste of resources within operations. Therefore, the redesign 
should also consider the waste of materials, time, and labor during 
manufacturing. The overall goal should be to reduce as much waste 
in production as possible.
Deforestation & Non-recyclability
Wood is a renewable resource if it is FSC certified. This certification 
proves that the wood is harvested from responsibly managed 
forests (Taylor, 2022). Additionally, wood is a valuable resource for 
building and manufacturing and has a low environmental impact 
compared to steel and plastics. (OkCredit, 2021). However, wood is 
not recyclable by itself. For example, in Denmark, wood would most 
likely be incinerated, and the carbon in the wood would therefore 
be released. Even if the wood emits less C02 in production, plastic 
might have a smaller footprint if it exists within a closed loop.

3.1.3 Requirement For The Redesign
The circular economy has been defined as an industrial system that 
is restorative or regenerative by intention and design (Michelini et 
al., 2017, p. 2). The requirements for the redesign of the mechanically 
emptied garbage bins were therefore made from a product-
service system (PSS) perspective to transition the current design 
and systems of the garbage bins from linear to circular economy 
(Michelini et al., 2017, p. 6).
From Hotspots To Requirements
Mont’s PSS framework states four PSS elements: Product, Service, 
Infrastructure, and Actor networks which lay the foundation of a PSS 
system (Mont, 2004, p. 142). The framework was included by mapping 
requirements related to each PSS element (Pigosso & McAloone, 
2021b). The requirements were generated from the hotspots in 
Section 2.5 related to the mechanically emptied garbage bins to 
ensure that all opportunity and problem areas were included when 
formulating the requirements for the redesign. The PSS elements 
were reworded as Product, Maintenance, Infrastructure, and

3.2 Ideation of Concepts
This section explains the ideation process of an initial concept and 
scope for redesigning the current mechanically emptied garbage 
bins, the Pizzakurv and the Tårnkurv. First, different concepts were 
generated to widen the search for new solutions (Cross, 2021, p. 
126). Then, the concepts were evaluated based on feedback from 
the TMF in correspondence with the requirement specification (see 
Appendix C.4) to then combine the concept variants (Cross, 2021, 
p. 37). Lastly, the CO2 footprint of the concept for the redesign was 
compared to the existing city furniture to verify the environmental 
improvements and identify optimization areas for further iterations.
3.2.1 Generating Ideal Concepts
Concepts were generated as variations of the established 
requirements to improve sustainability without compromising cost 
and function (Cross, 2021, p. 123). Concepts were created from those 
requirements as ideal concepts to ensure that each requirement 
was optimized (McAloone & Bey, 2009, p. 35). The concepts were 
then visualized to help communicate the concepts when evaluated 
by the TMF (Cross,2021, p. 9).
Morphology
A morphology chart was made to generate solutions related to 
the requirements defined in the requirement specification for 
the redesign (Cross, 2021, p. 126). The requirements were slightly 
reworded to get a similar level of generality and distinguish-ability 
(Cross, 2021, p. 124). The requirements for the morphology chart 
were: improve lifetime, improve maintenance, less CO2 impact, 
reduce financial cost, reduce other environmental impacts. To each 
Requirement, a row of sub-solutions were generated. Each ideal 
concept had to use solutions from each requirement row.
The workshop for creating the sub-solutions was done in sprints of 
ten minutes for each requirement (see Figure 3.7).
From the morphology chart, five ideal concepts were created as 
solutions for each requirement (see Appendix C.6). The five ideal 
concepts were: Easily maintainable, Extended life, Economically 
optimized, Reducing environmental cost, and Lowest CO2 impact 
from factory. The morphology chart is seen in Figure 3.8 and

The participants liked that the plastic panels were separable from

tiny to prevent pests from entering.  ́

Holtzblatt, 1999, p. 86).

4.3.3 Assembly of The Frame
When the steel frame is produced, it would consist of different 
assembled parts. Therefore, the most suitable assembly methods 
for connecting the steel parts were considered (see Appendix D.3.3). 
The assembly methods are bolts and screws,welding, and rivets (see 
Figure D.3.3).
The criteria that was set for the frame which can be applied to 
the assembly are that the assembly method should contribute to 
Making the steel structure robust, rigid, and maintainable:
Bolts and screws
Concerning maintenance, bolts and screws make the assembly 
and disassembly of the steel frame easy as they require few tools 
(Kuang, 2018). In addition, the usage of these makes the ”on-site” 
maintenance possible for the steel parts. Regarding criteria 1, bolts 
and screws have robustness but will become loose over time from 
stress (Kuang, 2018) as these bolts are affected by wear and thermal 
expansion. If the bolts or screws’ lifetime proves to be an issue over 
time, then self-locking bolts/screws or similar concepts could be 
used instead (Igus, 2021).
Welding
No matter the type, welding contributes to making a rigid structure 
with a long lifetime (Huang, 2022). Furthermore, welding is versatile 
and could make the assembly process from the factory easier or 
more difficult depending on the particular design (Kuang, 2018). 
Welding could also introduce product variability because it has to 
be done by an erector (Kuang, 2018). Regarding maintainability, the 
weld itself can be maintained. However, if the municipality could 
afford to have welding undone to fix the garbage bins, they would 
already do it on their current garbage bins. However, as previously 
mentioned in Section 4.3.2, the concept also wants to prolong the 
lifetime by having a robust frame. The environmental effectiveness of 
maintainability vs. robustness is a matter of maintenance frequency 
and how much steel needs to be scrapped from the repair.

4.4 Detailing of The Remaining Functions
This section documents the ideation and evaluation of the 
remaining functions marked with an equal sign from the function 
and means tree for the Miljøkurv (Appendix D.1). These functions 
were the door locking mechanism and the directions ramps. These 
were less important to the concept but necessary for the Miljøkurv 
to work as a mechanically emptied garbage bin. For that reason, 
they should be implemented without compromising the main 
aspects and requirements for the concept.
4.4.1 Door Locking Mechanism
The door on the garbage bins should not be locked with a key 
or anything that prohibits access, as previous cases have shown 
that deposit collectors will force the door open and damage the 
garbage bin in the process. The door should therefore have a 
locking mechanism that can be opened and closed by anyone (see 
Appendix C.4). Regarding maintenance, the Miljøkurv should have 
as many standardized components as possible (See appendix C.4). 
Furthermore, the panels have been designed to be interchangeable. 
Therefore, the locking mechanism should be mounted without 
altering the door shape.
Ideation of The Door Locks
Different lock shapes were sketched and can be seen in Figure 
4.26. Descriptions of the locks are found in Appendix D.4.1. Lock 2, 
3, 5, 6, 7 and 8 are all mounted directly to the door, which could be 
an inconvenience if the door is replaced and recycled. This means 
that the locking mechanism must be changed every time they are 
damaged. Lock 1 and 4 are not attached to the actual panel. These 
mechanisms are made from a similar principle where a piece of 
material blocks the door externally, whereas the other locks work 
internally. However, as seen in Figure 4.27, there was nowhere to 
mount the locks to the steel frame as the HDPE panels make up 
the entire external area. Mounting locks 1 and 4 to the top panel, as 
illustrated in Figure 4.27 will reintroduce the same problem as the 
other locks because changing the top panel will require the lock 
mechanism to be dismounted.
Figure 4.26: Sketches of different door locking mechanism.

Mounting To The Door
It became inevitable that the lock was to be mounted onto an HDPE 
panel with the design of the external HDPE panels and the internal 
frame. Lock 2, 3, and 7 require more than one hole to be mounted. 
This means that mounting them requires altering the shape of one 
panel. Lock 5 and 6 was handle designs that resemble the current 
locks on the Pizzakurv and the Tårnkurv. The preexisting mounting 
holes are meant for screws or bolts, which mean they are smaller 
than the current holes for mounting the handles on the Pizzakurv 
and the Tårnkurv. A handle fitted into a 6-8mm hole would likely 
break from rotating and pulling the lock multiple times. Lock 8 uses 
magnets which eliminates the need for moving parts and, in turn, 
makes them easier to mount into the panel’s mounting holes, as 
illustrated in Figure 4.28.
Additionally, mounting a magnet into all the free mounting holes 
creates a symmetric look. Finally, removing the magnets should 
be similar to removing the screws found on the other panels to 
make the magnets less inconvenient for the service workers when 
changing the door.
The Usage of Magnets
Permanent magnets are made from various metals depending on 
their specific strength and function. Their combinations of different 
nonrenewable materials make them challenging to recycle (Darcy 
et al., 2013, pp. 1381–1382). However, permanent magnets do not emit 
CO2 in their use phase and last a long time, making them suitable 
for reuse. Therefore, unless the magnets break, the permanent 
magnets should be reused even if the door is damaged. The 
permanent magnets should be stored by the service workers so the 
old magnets can be inserted into new doors to create a short closed 
loop. The use of magnets would be tested further in the Functional 
Prototype.

Pros For The Prototype
There was a broad consensus among the service workers that the 
solution of a maintainable product would reduce the usage of 
materials and help speed up the maintenance process. They were 
surprised by how sturdy the prototype was in comparison to the 
Pizzakurv. A test had them replacing a panel, which they did in under 
two minutes. The service workers approximated that they would be 
able to fix a panel (both side and top) in approximately  five minutes 
on an average work day. When asked, they approximated the time 
of demounting a Pizzakurv from its foundation to 10 minutes and 
mounting a new one to 20 minutes. They were also in favor of the 
removable frame and added that they could remove the frame parts 
as quickly as the panels.
Cons For The Prototype
The most significant concern of the Miljøkurv was that it seemed 
less resistant to fire hazards. It was discovered that a mechanically 
emptied garbage bin would accidentally set on fire at least 1-2 times 
a week in the summer period. Furthermore, it was mentioned that 
it would be a problem if the HDPE panels could melt, as they had 
that experience with the plastic containers inside the garbage 
bins. As previously mentioned in Section 2.2.3, adding additives 
to the HDPE would be possible to make it more resistant to high 
temperatures. Additionally, the service workers had concerns 
about the door being opened at the garbage inlet. They thought 
the sanitation workers would complain about the door not having 
a handle. Also, they thought bolts would be easier for them to 
work with as they had to aim more when mounting screws. In 
conclusion, during the workshop with the maintenance workers, 
there was a positive attitude towards the functional prototype, with 
some minor concerns. These concerns would be addressed in the 
implementation plan for further testing.

5.4 Implementation plan for the Miljøkurv
An implementation plan was proposed to ensure that assumptions 
related to the concept are tested before the MC invests money on a 
full implementation of the project. Additionally, an implementation 
that stretches throughout different phases bears less risks when 
implementing a new concept (Pigosso & McAloone, 2021c).
5.4.1 Assumptions
The primary assumptions were identified as the implementation 
plan was created based on assumptions surrounding the concept. 
The implementation plan could therefore be aimed at investigating 
unknown areas. Before making the implementation plan, it was 
assumed that:
Material Supply
•	
The HDPE fraction sorted from Hündgen has a pureness of at 
least 90%.
•	
That the legislation allows for the MC to buy back the HDPE 
sorted from their HHW (see Section 2.1.1).
Manufacturing
•	
The manufacturing methods described are sufficient for getting 
the right form of the panel (see Section 5.1.2).
•	
A fraction with a 90% - 92% pureness is enough for producing 
the desired form of the plastic part (see Appendix A.4.4 ).
•	
The plastic manufacturing stakeholder is able to recycle 
damaged parts from city furniture.
Maintenance
•	
The most parts of the Miljøkurv can each be replaced within 5 
minutes (see Section 4.5.3).
•	
The TMF can collect the sufficient data to effectively stock up on 
spare parts to enable on site maintenance.
•	
The TMF have space to store spare parts for the Miljøkurv.
5.4.2 Description of The Implementation Phases
The implementation was wade with the end goal of the concept in 
mind. The final implementation and outcome of the concept were 
therefore set as a final phase of the implementation plan. The final 
goal should be to produce a version of the Miljøkurv and the system 
that works as described and fulfills the requirements stated in the 
requirement specification. Additionally, the final goal should be to 
implement stakeholders for setting up the described infrastructure 
to support the product and its system to realize the overall vision 
of the concept. The first step of the implementation would be to 
ensure further testing of the concept without making extensive 
modifications to the current system. Then, if the tests show 
successful character, full implementation of the new system could 
be considered. The implementation plan consists of four phases: 
the first focusing on product elements, the second on maintenance 
elements, the third on stakeholders, and the last on combining the 
infrastructure. The implementation plan is seen in Figure 5.24.

Phase 1: Finalized testing and appearance

Phase 2: Test and verify the effectiveness of the

The second phase can be initiated when the TMF has a prototype 
with no apparent design flaws and the concept communicates its 
story convincingly to the public. The MC can make a pilot test of 10 
- 15 Miljøkurvs to place around Copenhagen to make a small-scale

Phase 3: Create the circular system and establish material

This implementation phase focuses on the stakeholders that will 
affect the circular system and supply chain for the Miljøkurv (see 
Section 5.1.3). First, the MC should agree to repurchase their plastic 
from their established plastic sorting facility (currently Hündgen 
Entsorgung), and involve plastic manufacturers that can process the 
sorted HDPE from HHW. The plastic manufacturer should have the 
capabilities to use the established manufacturing methods (Section 
5.1.2) and be capable of recycling broken HDPE components from 
the Miljøkurv. Additionally, a transportation line should be set up for 
transportation of the broken HDPE parts that are send back to the 
plastic manufacturer.
Phase 4: Estimate spare part usage and initiate mass 
production of the final product
The last phase focuses on out-phasing the Pizzakurv and the 
Tårnkurv. It is initialized when the agreements with stakeholders have 
been established, the Miljøkurv has been successfully made from 
HHW from the MC, and when it works in the current maintenance 
system. First, the MC should estimate how many spare parts the 
service workers need based on the tests done in phase 2 to stock 
up on spare components and to preorder a sufficient amount of 
Miljøkurvs for replacing the current bins. Stocking up on components 
is an essential step towards easy and fast maintenance of damaged 
products, as it must be possible to replace the most commonly 
damaged parts. Next, when the Miljøkurv has proven to work well, a 
scaled-down version of it can be made to also replace the Tårnkurv. 
Finally, the last step towards full implementation is to extend the 
side panels’ production to ensure that they are produced as defined 
in the concept description (see Section 5.1.1). Furthermore, a material 
reduction should be achieved from the product’s prolonged lifetime 
by utilizing the curved design. Currently, the Københavnerkurvs are 
replaced with the mechanically emptied garbage bins (see Section 
2.3.1) and during this phase, the Miljøkurv should fill that role while 
simultaneously replacing the Pizzakurv and the Tårnkurv, ensuring 
a gradual transition towards replacing the current garbage bins.
Having a gradual transition of the Miljøkurv ensures a less costly 
implementation. Additionally, it gives the stakeholders, in the 
manufacturing, a constant production flow which allows for 
slight adjustments in the production to increase the quality of the 
products over time. Also, gradually replacing the current garbage 
bins ensures that the current furniture is not wastefully scrapped 
before its end of life.

This thesis investigated the possibilities of making a redesign of the 
current city furniture in the Municipality of Copenhagen. To do so, 
the following problem statement was formulated from an initial 
design brief with the Teknik and Miljøforvaltningen:
“How can the Municipality of Copenhagen recycle collected plastics 
to build city furniture?”
The problem statement and design brief led to a series of initial 
research questions. The knowledge gained at the end of each 
phase during the project led to new research questions for the work 
ahead. The following sub-conclusions were drawn based on those 
questions:
1. The waste source in the Municipality of Copenhagen with the 
highest environmental potential and feasibility as a plastic supply 
source was the waste produced in households. Therefore, the 
material supply chain starts at the sorting facility handling the 
household waste generated in the Municipalities.
2. Previous studies and field work found that the redesign should 
utilize HDPE as it can be recycled multiple times and produced with 
good mechanical properties from extrusion. Additionally, HDPE 
produced from recycled plastic does not yet have an extensive 
industrial application.
3. The Miljøkurv was developed to redesign the mechanically 
emptied garbage bins; the Miljøkurv, and the Tårnkurv. The redesign 
describes the form of the product elements, proposed production 
methods, and mapping of the system and stakeholders that 
comprise the infrastructure contributing to the product’s circular 
system.
4. Based on the CES analysis comparing the CO2 emissions of the 
Pizzakurv and the Miljøkurv, it was found that reducing steel and 
utilizing recycled HDPE would reduce the CO2 emission related to 
the current city furniture.
5. Based on the approximation of the material and manufacturing 
costs of the Pizzakurv and the Miljøkurv, it was found that redesigning 
city furniture using recycled HDPE would be economically feasible.
6. Based on further calculation from the CES analysis, the estimated 
purchasing prices of the Miljøkurv and the Pizzakurv, and field work 
studies, it was found that implementing a maintenance system 
could reduce financial costs and environmental impacts related to 
the city furniture significantly.

7   Future work
This chapter suggests ideas for future work derived from the 
research and findings throughout the project.
Concept development
The Miljøkurv should be further developed and improved in regards 
to the following areas:
•	
Calculate optimal angle of the bended curve on the panel.
•	
Test how HDPE panels reacts to fire damage from burning 
containers and how improvements like using additives change 
the HDPE’s reaction to such damage.
•	
Test the durability of the curved designs through real life tests.
Utilize high quantity of recovered plastic
As stated in Section 2.1.2, 746 tonnes of HDPE was recovered for 
recycling at Hündgen Entsorgung in 2021. A Miljøkurv utilizes 25.2 
kg of HDPE per bin, meaning that around 19 thousand units could 
be produced annually. Therefore, there is potential for redesigning 
all the city furniture as the supply of recycled plastic from HHW 
would not surpass the demand. It was discovered that both HDPE 
and PP have great recycling potential, with HDPE being more 
suited for closed-loop systems. It could be interesting to investigate 
the potential of using fractions other than HDPE to prolong the life 
of those materials.
Use local sorting facilities
As described in Section 5.2, the transport phase had a higher 
environmental footprint for the Miljøkurv than the Pizzakurv. 
Therefore, it would be beneficial to look into using partners for 
sorting HHW plastic located closer to Copenhagen. However, a 
facility that can handle the same quantity as Hündgen Entsorgung 
in Denmark has not yet been built.
Expand to other municipalities or countries
It has been proven throughout this project that it is possible to 
build city furniture with recycled household HDPE. Therefore, it 
could be looked further into how to expand the findings to other 
municipalities or countries, as the municipalities’ problems are not 
unique. In addition, similar studies could be conducted focusing on 
reducing steel use by replacing it with plastic.
Find other ways to utilize the recycled Plastic
This project has primarily focused on utilizing the recycled plastic 
from the MC to be used in redesigning their current city furniture, 
as stated in the design brief. However, as discovered in Section 2.1.2 
13,590 tonnes of plastic were recovered for recycling at Hündgen 
in 2021. The HDPE fraction showed to have the best properties for 
closed-loop systems. HDPE was therefore chosen for the Miljøkurv. 
However, HDPE only makes up 5.5% of the total plastic generated 
after the sorting process at Hündgen Entsorgung. Therefore, looking 
into alternative ways to utilize the remaining fractions would be 
advantageous for sustainability in future product designs.

Find ways to sort for less contamination
The Miljøkurv’s plastic components are all extruded as this was 
the most promising production method for real-life application. It 
was found during the research phase (see Section 2.2.3) that the 
feasible production methods are determined by the stability and 
predictability of a material’s MFI. The MFI of recycled plastic material 
would be more consistent with the sorting technology, allowing for 
even purer sorting of the plastic fraction with shallow contamination 
values. This would allow for more uses of recycled plastic from HHW 
to design more products that contribute to sustainability.

8  Reflection
This chapter reflects on challenges in planning and scoping 
the project and learning goals (see Appendix A.2) that had to be 
achieved for the project.
8.1 Reflection on planning and scooping
Planning the project and collaboration with external 
stakeholders
This project was based on four phases with a rough time estimate 
for each phase. However, during the conceptualization phase, much 
pressure was build-up due to the time extend of communicating 
and planning with stakeholders. The time plan was set so that each 
phase had a limit. Therefore, it could have been beneficial to include
a buffer period for each phase to account for any unavoidable 
hiccups. According to the plan, the conceptualization should have 
lasted one month but ended up taking one and a half months. The 
time extension added pressure during the detailing phase, which 
was estimated to last two months. The detailing stage was reduced 
to one and a half months, but the time was compromised by 
contacting relevant stakeholders and setting up the construction 
of the functional prototype during the conceptualization phase. 
Fortunately, by thinking more initial steps into the project, the 
prototype was built faster than anticipated.
Scooping the project
Before going into the project, it was unsure what direction the 
project would go. Therefore, the beginning of the project became 
time-consuming as much time was put into understanding 
where to start researching. The MC had never mapped their waste 
management system as the tasks related to it are distributed across 
different departments (Section 1.2). Therefore, time was invested 
in understanding the current stakeholder map and material flow 
system as no one could provide a complete overview. Furthermore, 
a lot of information and data about the MC’s stakeholders was 
classified and, therefore, difficult to obtain. This also meant that 
data was left out of the thesis, and that alternative methods were 
applied to compromise the disclosed data. The data relating to 
Combineering and Hündgen was gained through contacting 
multiple stakeholders.

Copenhagen was created (Section 2.1.1). This model included actors 
transporting the waste, sorting the plastic waste, and making the 
legislative decision. Actors were involved in mapping the supply 
chain for the material used for the current city furniture (Section 
2.30). Additionally, a plastics manufacturing company that handles 
recycled plastic was involved in building a prototype to understand 
the real-life capabilities of plastic recycled from HHW (Section 4.5.1).
2. Can apply elements of current research within waste 
management at an international level to develop ideas and solve 
problems.
During the research of how the waste was handled in the 
Municipality of Copenhagen, it was discovered that an agreement 
was made with the sorting facility Hündgen Entsorgung, and with 
Combineering acting as the logistic company that transports the 
plastic waste (Section 2.1.2). This constellation became the basis 
for the proposed supply chain for how the new system should be 
made, with the possibility of utilizing more local facilities in the 
future. It was found that there was no foundation to look at waste 
management and sorting facilities at a greater international level 
as it is strictly constrained by the infrastructure of the individual 
municipality and country.
3. Will be able to make a science based holistic view and delimit 
a complex open issue within a broader academic and societal 
perspective, and propose a variety of possible actions towards 
making the waste management system more efficient at recycling 
plastics.
The project combines knowledge of the Copenhagen Municipalities 
waste management infrastructure, legislative foundation, and 
city furniture along with science based research on recycling 
plastic. Additionally, other projects utilizing recycled plastics were 
researched to create a holistic picture as a foundation for defining 
a design space. The redesign makes the current sorting process 
more effective by proving to the municipality of Copenhagen that 
HHW plastic can be an efficient material for designing city furniture 
if the proper production methods are considered during the early 
design stages of the product development process (Section 2.2.3). 
Additionally, the concept implements its waste management 
system for recycling plastic by making a closed-loop system of the 
plastic components to ensure the most extended lifetime for the 
material possible (Section 5.1.3).
4. Can develop relevant models, systems and processes for recycling 
plastics.
The project has produced models describing actors involved in 
the current waste management system in the MC (Figure 2.1), and 
developed new systems for recycling the plastic in closed-loop 
system, which are explained through step-wise processes in various 
models (Figures 3.24, 3.23, 5.10, and 5.11).
5. Can communicate and mediate research-based knowledge both 
orally and in writing.
All research-based knowledge regarding recycling of plastic, 
waste flow research, city furniture, production and manufacturing 
methods, legislation, and current solutions.
The knowledge has been presented at the specify parts of the thesis 
to serve as underlying arguments for the design decisions made 
throughout the project.

6. Can identify and explain leading international research within 
other municipalities/countries and their waste handling.
During the preliminary research of the waste handling infrastructure 
of the MC, it was found that most municipalities around the world 
use similar waste handling methods for their plastics. Additionally, 
Germany handles most waste in Europe because they have sorting 
facilities capable of handling waste on an international scale and 
utilize the newest sorting technology available. However, other 
companies that have worked with recycled plastics for product 
development were investigated and provided insightful knowledge 
(Section 2.2.4). Therefore, the research became more focused on 
how to make design decisions based on international studies of 
mechanical properties and manufacturing capabilities of recycled 
plastic.
7. Can work independently and reflect on own learning, academic 
development and specialization.
Literature from books, articles, and lectures on the circular economy, 
mechanical design, industrial design, system design, and PSS 
systems has been combined to provide the theoretical framework 
from which the project scope was defined, design decisions were 
made, and actions were taken during the redesign and course of 
the project.
8. Can master technical problem-solving at a high level through 
project work, and has the capacity to work with and manage all 
phases of a project - including preparation of timetables, design, 
solution and documentation.
Technical tools such as Granta CES, CAD, static stress simulations, 
technical drawings, and prototyping has been used throughout 
the project to evaluate design solutions and describe relevant 
elements of the concept. All appropriate research and findings 
were documented in appendices throughout the project. Project 
management was done through macro planning in the form of a pilot 
case activity plan (Appendix A.6.1), and micro planning timetables 
were generated as Gantt charts (Appendix A.6.2). Furthermore, 
there have been milestone presentations at the end of each phase 
described in the pilot case activity plan to mediate the findings up 
to that point and to receive feedback. Finally, a series of workshops 
were held with stakeholders to incorporate them into the project to 
receive feedback, co-create solution proposals, and gain first-hand 
knowledge on specific areas.
9. Can use pilot case studies as examples for how to solve a larger 
problem.
The purpose of doing a pilot case study was to prove the feasibility 
of using recycled plastic generated in the MC for redesigning 
the current city furniture within a short time frame and without 
investing money in up-scaled production. The implications of the 
case study could influence future concept development decisions 
of city furniture within the MC. The pilot case study was conducted 
by redesigning one chosen city furniture and creating a functional 
prototype as initial validation for the feasibility of a large-scale 
implementation of the concept. The pilot case study’s findings 
helped form the assumptions that the future implementation plan 
was based on. An implementation plan was formulated based on 
the findings from the initial pilot case. The implementation plan 
suggests further case studies. This therefore also proved, that to an 
extend, all the current city furniture has the potential to undergo a 
redesign just like the Pizzakurv did. The Pizzakurv was selected, as it 
had the highest potential in terms of implementation, and reducing 
financial cost and environmental impact.

10. Can conceptualize and develop prototypes to evaluate solution
and/or concept ideas.
In the conceptualization phase, five different ideal concepts were 
developed (Section 3.2) to visualize the ideas and functions derived 
from the modified morphology (Figure 3.8). Iterative and parallel 
rapid prototyping and digital CAD prototypes were used to evaluate 
alternative solution proposals during the detailing phase. Lastly, a 
functional prototype was built to verify different components of the 
detailed product elements of the Miljøkurv through the construction 
phase and user testing.
11. Can collaborate with external companies in the development of 
a concept.
It was possible to make the functional prototype because of 
collaborations with external stakeholders. The recycled household 
HDPE panels were created with the help of Epsotech. The panels 
were manufactured with the help of the people at the DTU workshop, 
who cut the plastic panels to the right sizes. The steel frame was 
developed with the metal shop J. Jensen, who provided expert 
knowledge on production and labor to create the steel structure 
for the Miljøkurv. Furthermore,multiple interviews were conducted 
with several external companies to provide extensive knowledge, 
which was indispensable for the project.
