PREFACE 
The reported work was a collaborative research in contamination risks in cleanrooms between The Technical 
University of Denmark and the consulting company Ramboll. Ramboll provided both financial and technical 
support to the project. 
The risk of contamination in a clean room environment under steady conditions with tracer gas and particles 
has been studied previously by Jaz Peoplez and Arpad Karolyi, where the subject of interest in this thesis 
concerns the risk of contamination under dynamic conditions, when the aerodynamic barrier between a clean 
room and a dirty room is weakened by the opening of a door. This was investigated with smoke tests and tracer 
gas measurements.

ABSTRACT 
4 
 
 
ABSTRACT 
In the pharmaceutical and electronic production industry along with the hospital sector a high level of cleanli-
ness in cleanrooms is required in order to avoid contamination of products or people. The level of cleanliness 
is obtained through several means such as high efficient air filters that removes most particles and very high 
air flowrates that ventilates the room efficiently. A typically cleanroom design consists besides the cleanroom 
of an airlock, which is a room used for passage between a clean area and a dirty area. Furthermore, pressuri-
zation by having an overpressure in the cleanroom and an under pressure in the airlock makes sure that that 
the air in the clean area will move towards the dirty area and not the other way around. Additionally the occu-
pational behavior of the personnel operating the cleanrooms are under strict restrictions. However, when the 
door separating an airlock and a cleanroom is open the differential pressure cease to exist and polluted air from 
the airlock can migrate towards the cleanroom due to the movement of the door and the persons entering the 
cleanroom. The air velocity from the cleanroom through the doorway to the airlock plays a crucial part in 
creating an effective aerodynamic barrier, but other parameters such as differential temperature between the 
cleanroom and the airlock, operation speed of the door and walking speed of the person also have an impact 
on the barrier. 
The guidelines in current standards dealing with the design of cleanrooms for pharmaceutical production are 
in some cases not that specific, where border values related to minimum differential pressure and minimum 
air velocity through a door opening seems to be overdesigned. The same applies to the recommended behavior 
of the persons operating in these environments and the protective air velocity through the door. Air velocity is 
known as an important design parameter, but the exact effect of contamination risk based on air velocity is not 
known. The potential overdesign may lead to high running cost for the HVAC-equipment servicing the clean-
room facility or result in disposal of healthcare products if the recommended design values are exceeded. The 
aim of the thesis was therefore to examine the contamination risk in an over pressurized cleanroom with non-
unidirectional airflow, when the door between the cleanroom and the airlock was opened and closed for an 
entering person. Research was based on full-scale tracer gas measurements with N2O together with smoke 
tests, and the test scenarios were designed to simulate realistic situations. The indoor parameters used were air 
velocity, differential temperature, differential pressure, operation speed of the door and movement speed of 
the personnel. The investigations concludes that air velocity was the most important parameter for minimizing 
the contamination risk, but it was also discovered that the door opening speed would neglect the effect of a 
high velocity compared to a low velocity. Furthermore, measurements with differential temperature between 
cleanroom and airlock showed that air temperature should be a design parameter equally compared to air ve-
locity. Research in operational behavior and air temperature can be further expanded to include design values 
just as for air velocity and differential pressure.

1.   INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Research area 
In the pharmaceutical and electronic manufacturing industry as well as in the hospital sector cleanrooms and 
control of contaminated air are of high importance, when there is a need for strict control of the air quality. 
Cleanrooms in hospitals are used for operations of humans, where it is important that the environment is sterile 
in order to avoid infections, while cleanrooms in the pharmaceutical industry are preventing the medicine or 
the electronics from being contaminated. Contaminated products possess a potential health risk for consumers 
and will be destroyed when contamination is detected resulting in significant economic losses, while contam-
inated people may die to the infection [1]. 
Maintenance of the air quality in cleanrooms within the pharmaceutical industry is done through different 
means, whereas the most important ones are induced through high efficient filters and high supply airflow 
rates. Other means for securing the cleanroom against contamination is having a higher differential pressure 
between a cleanroom and a single or several airlocks. The largest threat for contamination of pharmaceutical 
products occurs however when people enters the cleanroom, and because of this, airlocks are used as spaces, 
where people can undress and change into special cleanroom clothing before entering the cleanroom [2].

Entering a cleanroom from an airlock still move particles from the dirty zone to the clean zone though, and 
depending on the level of cleanliness up to several airlocks are installed right after each other [3]. Changing 
into cleanroom clothing results in high emittances of particles due to the high level of activity. The emitted 
particles will thereby led to a high concentration in the airlock due to the small size of that room [4]. Figure 
1.1 illustrates a typical suite of rooms for aseptic filling with the level of pressurization for each room indicated 
[5]. The highlighted area in the figure marks the area of research in the thesis, where the contamination risk 
from the airlock to the cleanroom is investigated as migrated air volumes from the contaminated zone to the 
clean zone. 
 
1.2 Objectives 
To secure a cleanroom for contamination of particles, the current guidelines offer various procedures concern-
ing the design and maintenance of the facility. However, information regarding the contamination risk in dy-
namic stances in a cleanroom is limited compared to the information for contamination risk under steady state. 
For example, the size of air changes and air velocities required to obtain a specific cleanliness class is described 
in details with minimum border values, when the door remains closed. However, when the door is open, the 
guidelines dealing with the operational behavior for professionals in a cleanroom lacks information regarding 
minimum values on door operation speed, walking speed, air velocity through the doorway under isothermal 
and non-isothermal conditions. 
 
Pressurized aerodynamic barrier (when door between cleanroom (CR) and airlock (AL) is closed)

Weakened pressurized aerodynamic barrier (when door between cleanroom (CR) and airlock (AL) is open)

The purpose of the thesis is therefore to identify the effect the listed parameters for creating and maintaining 
an aerodynamic barrier in a doorway under dynamic conditions to minimize the contamination risk, which will 
be simulated by full-scale tracer gas experiments in combination with smoke visualizations with realistic 
flowrates and operation speeds. 
Additionally, the research will also cover the setup of the ventilation system and the method of assessment of 
tracer gas measurements with fast-recording and slow-recording monitors, since the setup of the ventilation 
system and the experimental procedure can act as template for further research. Thus the main objectives of 
the study are the following. 
 
1) Identification of method for experimental procedure

2.   BACKGROUND 
2.1 Contamination sources 
The source of contamination comes from various elements as shown in Figure 4 with the areas of research 
highlighted in the thesis, which illustrates the many challenges with designing a cleanroom. Although all ele-
ments possess a risk for contamination, the biggest threat comes from the personnel entering the cleanroom 
[6]. Not only will the person going through the door led contaminated air into the cleanroom through the 
movement of the door and the person itself, but additionally once inside the cleanroom the person will emit 
particles depending on the level of activity. Personnel introduce particles through skin flakes, oil, hair, yeast 
and movement, while bringing in external particles from cosmetics, perfume, lotions and clothing [7].

2.3 Airflow distribution 
In the cleanroom industry, three types of ventilation principles are used as practice according to the needs of 
the room. These types of ventilation are unidirectional flow, non-unidirectional flow and mixed flow, which 
is a combination of the two first mentioned. The unidirectional flow also called laminar flow is movement of 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
BACKGROUND 
16 
 
air, where the air streamlines are parallel to each other, while the non-unidirectional flow also called turbulent 
flow is movement of air, where the air streamlines not are parallel to each other. The mixed flow combines the 
two principles, where in one part of the room the airflow would be unidirectional, while in another part of the 
room the airflow would be non-unidirectional. For a stringent cleanroom, unidirectional airflow is common 
practice, while for an intermediate cleanroom non-unidirectional airflow is used more [4]. Due to high costs 
for installation and running a system with unidirectional airflow, non-unidirectional flow in form of swirl 
diffusers from the ceiling is a much used method in the industry. 
The different airflows are illustrated in Figure 2.2, where the numbers indicates the location of the air supply 
and the air exhaust.

2.4 Design guidelines 
The investigation of parameters affecting the aerodynamic barrier are, as earlier mentioned, door opening time, 
movement time for personnel, differential pressure, air velocity through door and temperature differences be-
tween the clean room and the contaminated room. The recommended values are obtained from international 
ISO-standards, ASHRAE, FDA and books in the area. 
In general most of the knowledge regarding design of cleanrooms deals with the actual design, and the 
knowledge about operation of the clean rooms is more limited. This is reflected in the critical border values 
for the parameters, which either do not exist or are very conservative [9]. 
 
2.4.1 Door opening time for swing door 
For a cleanroom door that opens manually it is recommended to open it as slow as possible according to 
international standards [10]. The door opening time can be expected to be around 4-8 seconds from fully closed 
to open in an angle of 90°. Current research in the field of influence of door opening time on contamination 
level in a cleanroom shows a value of 6 seconds and below is recommended [11]. It is furthermore advised to 
have the door open in the shortest time possible in order to avoid the infected air entering the cleanroom. 
However, when a rotating door is opened quickly there is a potential of creating a vortex of air following the 
movement of the door and thereby letting polluted air inside the clean zone. Sliding doors seems according to 
various literature to be a better option in terms of maintaining the aerodynamic barrier in the doorway com-
pared to a hinged door, but components in sliding doors generates too many particles to make this the standard 
design [12]. Little information is provided regarding the effect of the opening angle of the door, and research 
investigating the difference of having a swing door or a sliding door takes an opening angle of 90° for a swing 
door into consideration. However, when a single person is entering a cleanroom through a swing door by 
manual force, the door might not be fully open in an angle of 90°. The vortex created when opening could 
perhaps be prevented by not open the door completely as shown in Figure 2.3, since the vortex from the move-
ment of the door depends on speed and angle. In summarization, tot much information is given on this matter 
except that the door should be opened slowly according to guidelines [4].

2.4.2 Walking speed for personnel 
Operational behavior is of interest in clean room environments for two reasons. Walking fast creates more 
particles from the person compared to walking slow, and secondly the fast movement may create another 
vortex following the person and leading the air from the contaminated room into the clean room [8]. The 
research in this thesis only takes the latter part into consideration, and the results concerning this parameter is 
therefore not fully transferable into real life situations. 
The entering time for personnel is closely connected with the door opening time and is expected not to be more 
than a few seconds. Normal walking pace is in the area of 0.5 – 1.0 m/s according to similar studies resulting 
in period of 4-8 seconds over a distance of 2.0 m [13]. Since the experiments involving the mannequin only 
consists of a movement distance of 2.0 meter, the amount of setups for this parameter is limited. A high and a 
low walking distance will therefore be the only two set points for the experiments in the thesis. 
 
2.4.3 Differential pressure  
Recommended differential over pressure lies in the range of 10-20 Pa according to the ISO-standard [4]. Too 
high differential pressure will make it more difficult to open the door towards the over pressurized zone, and 
the values between 5-20 Pa seems to be the minimum as a rule of thumb. The differential pressure between 
two rooms is most important under steady conditions, when the door is closed, since when the door is open, 
the differential pressure is neutralized immediately. The differential pressure will not be a parameter of inves-
tigation due to this and will be kept at an initial level of 15 Pa for all scenarios. 
When the door separating the cleanroom with the airlock is closed the overpressure in the cleanroom prevents 
air from the airlock to move into the cleanroom through openings. Depending on which literature used for the 
design the differential pressure level between the clean zone and the dirty zone should be in the range of 5-20 
to prevent air from less clean zone entering the cleanroom through leaks in the envelopment Pa [4]. The rec-
ommended range of 5-20 Pa allows the door to be opened manually and prevents crossflows due to turbulence. 
The minimum allowed differential pressure allowed to prevent contaminated air from moving into the clean-
room, was investigated prior to this thesis [14].

2.4.4 Air velocity through door 
The air velocity through the door opening depends on three parameters, which are the size of the hole, the 
flowrate and the air pattern distribution. The minimum air velocity to create and an effective aerodynamic 
barrier varies from industry to level of cleanliness, where the microelectronics production industry for instance 
have a higher recommended air velocity compared to the healthcare production industry and also rely on the 
use of air curtains to a higher degree. Detailed information about the amount of migrated air from one room to 
another for specific conditions is not described in ISO-standards. 
However, research that has been conducted in this area by different institutes have covered it somehow. The 
study investigated the invasion rate of air contaminated with particles from a dirty room into a clean room 
under different doorway airflow velocities, and discovered that an air velocity of 0.1 m/s were found to be the 
minimum for an invasion rate of 1% [15]. The value of 0.1 m/s corresponds to some extent with the number 
given in ISO-standards. The ISO-standards list a value of 0.2 m/s to achieve segregation of cleanrooms with 
low pressure differentials. However, values as high as up to 0.5 m/s have been demonstrated to be the case for 
a zero invasion rate in other studies [16]. 
In most literature concerning the minimum required air velocity to maintain an aerodynamic barrier does not 
distinguish between isothermal and non-isothermal temperature conditions. This creates a challenge, since the 
minimum air velocity value for non-isothermal conditions are expected to be higher due to thermal buoyancy. 
The minimum velocity for a scenario with no differential temperature between two adjacent rooms may not be 
sufficient for scenarios with temperature differences between the rooms. The air velocity for isothermal con-
ditions is illustrated as a vertical section between a cleanroom and an airlock in Figure 2.5 with the aerody-
namic barrier between the two rooms shown in the middle of the figure. The protective air jets from the clean-
room generated by the pressure differential between the two rooms tries to achieve segregation against the 
contaminated air caused by the movement of the door and the personnel.

2.4.5 Differential temperature 
The differential temperature between the clean room and the airlock affects the movement of air, when the 
door opens. As a result, the minimum required air velocity through the door varies in accordance with the 
temperature difference. The higher differential temperature is, the higher the air velocity must be in order to 
create an aerodynamic barrier in the door. 
Figure 2.6 illustrates the challenge with keeping the barrier with non-isothermal conditions due to the move-
ment of air under differential temperature. Unlike the scenario in Figure 2.5, the air velocity from the clean 
zone towards the dirty zone must both segregate contaminated air caused by the movement of the door and the 
personnel as well as temperature differences. 
The topic of differential temperature is of particular interest to the pharmaceutical industry, since manufactur-
ing processes often cause temperature differences in the surrounding air, but the problem also occurs in an 
airlock, where people changing into cleanroom clothing generates body heat. Furthermore, some pharmaceu-
tical products may require a lower air temperature than 20 °C, which also could result in a differential temper-
ature between two rooms. 
Literature by Bjengt Ljungqvist and Berit Reinmuüller explains the air migration between two rooms with a 
differential temperature and draws the conclusion that high very air velocities are required for non-isothermal 
conditions [5]. A value as high of 0.6 m/s for a single doorway is for instance representative for temperature 
difference of 4 K as seen in Figure 2.7 [17]. 
Similar conclusions is made by research by G.J. Farquharson and W. Whyte [18]. However, they conclude that 
2.0 m/s excesses supply airflow is needed for a differential temperature of 4 K for a single, which is three times 
as much. The value is derived from Figure 2.8.

3.1 Mathematical treatment 
Tracer gas measurements were carried out to measure the ratio of contamination in the cleanroom against the 
contamination in the airlock as well as to quantify the total volume of air transferred through the doorway. The 
tracer gas were supplied into the airlock and mixed with the air before opening the door. During the door 
operation the tracer gas flower across the doorway resulting in a sudden increase of concentration in the clean-
room. This was followed by a slow decay towards the initial background level prior to the door operation. 
Passage through the doorway was simulated with a mannequin attached on a remote controlled skateboard. 
The conversion of the measured tracer gas concentrations into a volume of migrated air for was used in a study 
with a similar setup with good results [19]. The method of calculation was based on the assumption that there 
were no leakages and that the tracer gas that entered the cleanroom were extracted only through the exhaust. 
Since the facility was sealed around the edges and corners this was considered a valid assumption. However, 
since the bypass would remain open during the entire door operation, it was likely that all migrated tracer gas 
would not be detected through the exhaust hole, if the tracer gas would stay inside the cleanroom for a longer 
period.

4.2.3 Airflow distribution 
A main challenge with the airflow distribution in the cleanroom model was to rely on very high air changes 
reflecting real life situations in a very small facility. The volume of the cleanroom was 11.5 m3, while the 
volume of the airlock measured 9.0 m3. Originally the model was equipped with two swirl diffusers, but CFD 
simulations showed very high turbulent air velocities through the doorway, which resulted in backwards air-
flow. The high air streams through the doorway would hit the other door in the airlock and thereafter return to 
the cleanroom along the floor illustrated in Figure 4.4. 
The swirl diffusers were originally used in an experimental setup, where the door separating the cleanroom 
with the airlock was closed. The purpose of the high air changes in that case was to keep contamination level 
on the same low level in the room, while the purpose of the high air changes in this research was to create an 
aerodynamic barrier in the doorway. Therefore two textile ducts spanning from ceiling to floor replaced the 
swirl diffusers. The low-impulse textile ducts made it possible to supply the cleanroom with very high 
flowrates but at the same time keep an air velocity within a range that prevented backwards airflow. The 
installation furthermore resulted in a more uniform airflow through the doorway. Figure 4.5 shows an example 
of air distribution from a textile duct, although the duct was installed vertically in this research.

Air supply in the airlock through the bypass created the same dilemma with backwards airflow, where air jets

placed inside the airlock could have been another solution that might have worked, but this wat not tested. As 
a result of the placement of the cardboard, three fans had to ventilate the airlock in order to get well-mixed air.

4.4 Procedure 
The experimental procedure with tracer gas was finalized as an iteration process, where specific procedures 
were repeated until the measurements could be used for further processing. Several issues had to be addressed 
in order to get any measurements at all in the clean room. Unlike static tracer gas experiments, the dynamic 
scenarios in this thesis were executed under a short amount of time with very high flowrates, as earlier men-
tioned. Since the concentration measured in the clean room was used in combination with the concentration 
measured initially in the airlock under steady state conditions, the initial value in the airlock was of high im-
portance. The concentration in the clean room would be derived as a fraction of the concentration in the airlock, 
which meant that the minimum initial concentration in the inlet room was interesting to find in order to save 
the usage of gas for dosing. Early test showed that an initial concentration of 600 ppm N2O in the airlock was 
sufficient in order to detect observations of 10-15 ppm in the clean room for scenarios with flowrates of 600 
m3/h and 1200 m3/h. Challenges that were addressed regarding the experimental procedure was the following 
five points. 
 
 
Validation of general test procedure and functionality of ventilation system with smoke tests 
 
 
Acquire well mixed air in the airlock before opening of the door 
 
 
Acquire steady state conditions in the airlock before opening of the door 
 
 
Being able to measure concentrations of 5-10 ppm during a period of 60 seconds in the clean room 
with Innova monitor 
 
 
Being able to measure concentrations of 5-10 ppm during a period of 60 seconds in the clean room 
with FCM monitor 
 
4.4.1 Smoke testing 
The test procedure for the smoke experiments was conducted in the following order. 
 
1. Place the laser sheet in the wanted heights as seen in Figure 4.13. 
 
2. Seal the door that leads into the airlock around the edges with tape. 
 
3. Apply desired flow rate to the system and adjust bypass dampers so ∆P is 15 Pa between CR and AL.

4.4.3 Tracer gas measurements 
The one-minute interval measurements were recorded with photoacoustic gas monitor Innova [21], while the 
fast measurements were recorded with a nondispersive infrared sensor FCM [22]. The fast gas concentration 
meter were able to measure 10 times per second, but suffered from some downsides such as an unsteady zero-
point and fluctuations of 10 +/- ppm for each measurement. 
In general various research has been conducted about contaminated air migrating from a dirty room to a clean 
room, but a substantial amount of the studies had not a ventilation systems running in order to create an aero-
dynamic barrier in the door [15] [23]. Therefore the impact of having a high air velocity contra a low air 
velocity is not fully highlighted, and as such the migration as a function of the air velocity does not exist. The 
experiments investigated therefore the change of the tracer gas concentration in the clean room under dynamic 
operations, when the door is opened and closed. This was achieved by a dynamic experimental setup, where 
the airlock first was filled with the tracer gas N2O for thereafter being fully mixed with the help of fans inside 
the room. When the concentration reached a certain concentration, which in this case was 600 ppm, the door 
would be opened from outside by a remote control and remain open for a short amount of time before being 
closed again. The 600 ppm were discovered to be the minimum concentration in the airlock that would allow 
the sensor in the clean room to detect a fluctuation, at least for the Innova monitor. The FCM monitor were 
not able to record the smallest concentration changes. The tracer gas procedure is seen in Figure 4.14, where 
the figure takes the different movement times of the mannequin and the different door opening times into 
account. The total time for the operation is 35 or 45 seconds and the procedure was the following. The proce-
dure for the tracer gas experiments was conducted in the following order and was performed separately from 
the smoke tests. 
 
1) Apply desired flow rate to the system and adjust bypass dampers so ∆P is 15 Pa between CR and AL. 
Adjust temperature in both rooms to the desired state. If non-isothermal conditions was desired, the 
fan coils or the space heaters were turned on. 
 
2) Start recording on the computers for both monitors. 
 
==PAGE:41==

3) Turn on the fans in the AL in order to get a well-mixed smoke concentration. 
 
4) Start dosing tracer gas and observer measurements. Once steady state was reached on the Innova mon-
itor, which was 600 ppm ± 5 % for three consecutively minutes, one experiment could start. The dosing 
of tracer gas would continue to run. The gas inlets pointed in the opposite direction of the clean room, 
which meant that the air from the textile diffusers would push the latest dosed tracer gas towards the 
exhaust holes. Therefore the measured concentration in the t-joint of the exhaust system would record 
higher values than 600 ppm, despite not adjusting the flowmeter for the gas. 
 
5) Turn off the fans and the smoke generator in the AL and wait for 10-15 seconds. The fan coils or the

5.1.1 Prevention of backwards airflow from bypass 
The potential risk of having backwards airflow from the bypass was discovered and eliminated at first. The 
risk existed due to the high air velocities that was generated from the bypass, which consisted of two holes at 
the outlet in the airlock. There were not any diffusers located that could have reduced the velocity, since an 
installation of those would have resulted in a higher pressure loss than 15 Pa through the bypass for scenarios 
with high flowrates from 1200 m3/h and up. The initial 15 Pa wanted to be kept for all scenarios. 
Without any resistance, the airflow from the bypass in the airlock would possibly have hit the nearest door and 
continue its path along the floor towards the door separating the cleanroom and the airlock. Scenarios with 
high flowrates would therefore have a less efficient aerodynamic barrier than scenarios with low flowrates, if

was placed inside the airlock and not inside the ducts. The door opening time was set to 20 seconds, since 10 
seconds would have resulted in air entering the cleanroom for both conditions, and the laser sheet was put right 
above the floor.

The measurements from the Innova monitor were able to record an increase in the concentration in the clean

in the recordings in Appendix B, where a small increase can be seen short after the dosing of tracer gas starts.

measurements. A small leakage in the facility or the small gap of 10 mm below the door separating the clean-
room from the airlock may offer an explanation for this. The initial increase of tracer gas is shown in Figure

7.2 Measurements – FCM gas analyzer 
Unlike the Innova monitor the FCM monitor were not able to record a steady transition from very low concen-
trations to low concentrations. The FCM monitor succeeded only in cases, where the increase of concentration 
was higher than 10 ppm. Scenarios with lower increases fluctuated too much to tell the increase apart from the 
fluctuation, and as a result of that only five scenarios were included in the data analysis, despite every scenario 
were recorded. 
The FCM monitors recorded concentration values in the same location as the Innova monitors throughout all 
experiments, but not all measurements were able for usage. Especially the monitor recording in the cleanroom 
that only measured values between 0 - 25 ppm had in certain scenarios too much fluctuation to differentiate an 
increase of concentration because of the door opening operation from the fluctuation itself. The measurements 
in the airlock in the range of 0 - 2500 ppm was however the same measured with the Innova monitor. The 
purpose of the usage of the FCM monitor were however not to compare it with the Innova monitor for all 
scenarios. Instead it was used to observe the actual increase and decay of concentration in the cleanroom over 
a short period, which the Innova monitor could not do. 
The recorded data were initially not clear enough for analyzing due to the 10 recordings per second and a 
baseline that measured -40 or -600 instead of 0. The first step was therefore to eliminate the high frequency of 
the measurements, while the second step was to convert the negative baseline to 0. The high frequency was 
reduced by taking the average for two seconds reducing the 20 recorded values over the two seconds to one 
value instead. The baseline was thereafter found by taking the average for the first five stable minutes with no 
dosing of tracer gas and then add that value to the remaining recordings. 
The comparison of the Innova analyzer and the FCM analyzer is shown in Figure 7.9, where the total sampling 
period for the cleanroom for scenario 1 is displayed. Each analyzer is able to measures the peak concentration

8.   DISCUSSION 
8.1 Sources of error 
The obtained results gives a good indication of the behavior of the aerodynamic barrier with different indoor 
climate conditions. The results shows a good correspondence between fast and slow movement of the door 
and the mannequin and the effect of having a high flowrate compared to a low flowrate. Several important 
conclusions can therefore be made based on the executed experiments. However, precautions must be taken 
due to a number of reasons as seen in the form below. 
 
 
Calculation method of migrated air from AL to CR 
 
 
Transformation of measurements from Innova monitor 
 
 
Processing of data from FCM monitor 
 
 
Comparison between FCM monitor and Innova monitor 
 
 
Comparison between smoke videos and tracer gas measurements 
 
 
Comparison between results from tracer gas experiments and particle experiments 
 
 
Comparison between results from tracer gas experiments and literature review 
 
8.1.1 Mathematical treatment 
The first issue to address is the formulas used for calculating the migrated air volumes. In Eq. 3, which is used 
for the air volume calculations, it is seen that it depends on three factors with a different uncertainty for each. 
The total exhaust flowrate Q in the clean room was based on the assumption that the facility had no leakages 
and all the air was extracted through the exhaust as well as under the door and through the bypass. The flowrate 
through the bypass was however not measured, and it is therefore recommended to measure the airflow in the 
bypass as well in future studies. 
==PAGE:78==
DISCUSSION 
77 
 
Furthermore, the measured concentration p(t) was recorded with a one-minute interval, which did not measure 
the highest concentration but the average concentration over 30 seconds. The inserted value in the migration 
calculations was therefore the average even though it was considered as the peak. 
Last but not least, it was assumed that the initial concentration p(0) in the airlock had fully mixed air before 
the door operation began. It was assumed that the air was fully mixed, which was also proven by the earlier 
setup, were recordings of tracer gas were performed in the t-joint and in three other locations in different 
heights in the airlock. This was executed for all the four different flowrate conditions and it was here verified 
that the values with the final setup with different fan positions and speed settings as seen in Figure 4.12 meas-
ured the same values ± 5 %. The assumption of fully mixed air in the airlock was therefore based on this 
together with smoke videos with the same setup that showed that the smoke was getting mixed. Nonetheless 
there could be mistakes in the setup. 
 
8.1.2 Comparison of different gas analyzers 
The FCM monitors were originally used with two things in mind. The first was to compare the measurements 
with the Innova monitor and observe the resemblance. Secondly, the monitor was able to record the actual 
process of a sudden rise of concentration and a slow decay during the door operation. The experiments showed 
however that the FCM monitors were not reliable for small concentrations and only few recordings could be 
used. Those recordings that could be used turned out to be very useful however and helped create the estimated 
curves in Figure 3.2. 
For the comparison between the measurements with an Innova monitor and a FCM monitor there were some 
obstacles in form of a high fluctuation and a negative baseline, which could change over time. The frequency 
of the fluctuation could be reduced, but creating a baseline turned out to be more difficult. The baseline rec-
orded in real time was as earlier described either around -40 ppm or around -600 ppm, and could even raise or 
recede during the experiments for no good reason. Changes of air temperature may explain this behavior, but 
this was not fully investigated to confirm for certainty. 
 
8.1.3 Resemblance of smoke testing with tracer gas experiments 
The smoke test visualizations performed prior to the final tracer gas experiments proved to be a good indicator 
of what would happen with the air, when the door was opened and closed. Although it was difficult to tell the 
exact amount of contaminated air that would enter the clean room from the airlock, the tests showed nonethe-
less the movement of air on both horizontally and vertically placed laser sheets. The buildup of smoke in the 
airlock before opening the door to the clean room had however some challenges. One of these, was the fact 
the smoke was dosed with the same flowrate for each flowrate scenario of 400 m3/h to 1650 m3/h, meaning 
that it was difficult to get the exact same amount of smoke in the airlock, when the flowrate of fresh air varied. 
The dosing of smoke was 15-25 seconds for every scenario investigated. For a flowrate of 400 m3/h 15 seconds 
were spend, while for a flowrate of 1650 m3/h 25 seconds were used to get a somehow similar amount of 
smoke concentration. 
 
==PAGE:79==
 
 
DISCUSSION 
78 
 
The obtained results, where the parameters as movement time for personnel, door opening time for swing door 
and air velocity through doorway under isothermal and non-isothermal conditions, shows significantly lower 
amount of migrated air compared to the read literature [19]. However, in those cases, there was nothing or 
only very little airflow through the door. In terms of defining that an effective aerodynamic barrier under 
dynamic conditions must have air velocities through the doorway of 0.2 m/s, the results may indicate that air 
velocities for isothermal conditions under 0.2 m/s is sufficient. Velocities below 0.2 m/s may even be sufficient 
for non-isothermal cases, especially opening of the door slowly is taken into consideration. 
 
8.1.4 Comparison of other research 
Another thesis conducted at the Technical University of Denmark also investigated the contamination risk with 
almost identical conditions [24]. The experiments were however performed with particles instead of tracer gas 
in order to observe the differences, and were similar to scenarios 1-9 for isothermal conditions conducted in 
this thesis. 
Particle measurements showed similar behavior for isothermal conditions compared to tracer gas. For instance 
did fast and slow door operations show large reductions in contamination risks, while moving the mannequin 
fast and slow also showed a difference. 
However, considering the invasion ratio or the correlated value of concentration in the cleanroom adherent to 
the concentration in the airlock, there was a significant difference. Invasion ratios from approximately 1 to 20 
% were the case for the particle experiments for isothermal conditions, while ratios for tracer gas experiments 
only spanned from 1 to 4 %. Different measuring locations in the cleanroom may explain the reason for this, 
where the measuring location for the tracer gas investigations was located at the exhaust, while the location 
for the particle investigations was located in the middle of the cleanroom. 
Furthermore the door operation was not completely identical, which also would have contributed to the differ-
ences in the observations. Summarization of the experiments with particles and tracer gas is the following. 
 
 
Similar reduction of contamination risk with increased airflow with particles and tracer gas 
 
 
Similar reduction of contamination risk with reduced door opening time with particles and tracer gas 
 
 
Different ratios of measurement in CR compared to measurement AL with particles and tracer gas 
 
8.2 Assessment and recommendations  
It was seen that air velocities based on flowrates of 400 m3/h and 600 m3/h showed an invasion rate of 1 %. 
The flowrates corresponds to 0.14 m/s and 0.15 m/s through the doorway, meaning that 0.2 m/s as a minimum 
air velocity can be questioned. If the door is opened slowly even lower velocities are sufficient to create and 
maintain an aerodynamic barrier in the door. In real life situations, where there is a non-isothermal condition 
or where the flowrate is considered low, adjusting the time to open the door can have a big impact. This is also 
==PAGE:80==
DISCUSSION 
79 
 
a solution that be implemented relatively easy by nudging in form of visualizations of preferred occupational 
behavior or by having a hydraulic pump on the door preventing the door from being opened to fast. 
Furthermore, it was demonstrated that it was possible to use the Innova monitor to produce reasonable results 
for a dynamic scenario in two locations with a low concentrations in one them. The combination of measuring 
with FCM monitor also proved to be helpful, although it is recommend to try to measure higher concentrations 
due to the constant fluctuation. The results demonstrated however that also the FCM monitor were able to 
record small peaks of 10 ppm, but not every time. 
The occupational behavior in form of the simulated walking speed also showed the importance of controlled 
movement in a clean room facility. Moving with 0.5 m/s compared to 1.0 m/s showed that approximately 60 l 
entered the clean room in the first case, while approximately 120 l entered the clean room in the last case. If 
the difference of 60 l can be converted to the other scenarios, which did not have the mannequin moving, it 
demonstrates that walking speed is also a big contributor, since the 60 l would be a high proportion in almost 
every other scenario. 
Lastly, differential temperature could be an important new design parameter due to low measurements for a 
warm cleanroom and a cold airlock. In terms of energy consumption it might be more effective to cool the 
airlock instead of increasing the differential flowrate between cleanroom and airlock. Summarization is seen 
below. 
 
 
Door opening time and walking pace have a large impact and can be implemented relatively easy 
 
 
Innova monitor and FCM monitor makes a good combination for dynamic tracer gas experiments 
 
 
Differential flowrates and air velocities is lower for isothermal cases compared to non-isothermal cases 
 
Future work would include further examination of the values between the fast moving motions and the slow 
moving motions, since only a high and a low value was considered in this thesis. Also further investigation of 
the minimum required air velocity through the door in order to prevent too much polluted air entering the 
cleanroom under non-isothermal conditions could be of interest. 
Additionally, the closing time of the door and the waiting time between opening and closing of the door could 
be further investigated. Other future work would be related to the setup of the experimental design and the 
experimental procedure for realistic dynamic scenarios. 
Lastly, it could be of interest to create a reference setup for a standard cleanroom similar to the reference 
houses used for energy calculations for buildings. Hereby the energy consumption and the contamination risk 
would have a reference value, which could be reduced by adjusting similar parameters related to this thesis.

CONCLUSION

9.   CONCLUSION 
The tracer gas measurements confirmed that it was possible to simulate very high flowrates in a small test 
environment and produce viable results. Coordination of experimental procedure must be timed very precisely 
though to measure any concentration in the cleanroom if the measuring point is close to the exhaust. The two 
gas analyzers complemented each other perfectly, despite the challenges with fluctuations with the FCM mon-
itor. In experiments in a cleanroom with very high flowrates it is recommended to rely on the Innova monitor, 
since the measured concentrations often will be too low to be detected for the FCM monitor. 
Good correspondence with smoke testing and tracer gas measurements were also confirmed. The smoke tests 
helped identify critical minimum values for door opening and flowrate. 
Door operation movement, especially door opening speed is of high importance, where it was demonstrated 
continuously that a low speed resulted in a small invasion of contamination. Movement speed for the manne-
quin also had a big impact. 
Air velocity was also identified as an important parameter for creating an aerodynamic barrier, and can be 
lower compared to current recommendations, when considering isothermal indoor climate conditions. For non-
isothermal conditions the air velocity should correspond to whether the cleanroom is colder than the airlock or 
vice versa. Temperature was according to the measurements just as an important parameter as differential 
flowrate, which was seen in scenarios 10 – 13. The migrated air volume was the lowest recorded of all scenar-
ios, when the cleanroom had a warmer air temperature of 4 °C more than the airlock. The contamination risks 
based on the investigation of the different parameters is seen below. 
 
 
Door opening time set to 20 seconds instead of 10 → low contamination risk and easy to implement 
 
 
 Movement pace set to 0.5 m/s instead of 1.0 m/s → low contamination risk and easy to implement 
 
 
Increase of differential flowrate and velocity → low contamination risk but expensive to implement 
 
 
Reduction of air temperature in airlock → very low contamination risk but expensive to implement
