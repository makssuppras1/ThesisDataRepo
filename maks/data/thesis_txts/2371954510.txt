Abstract
Pool-Seq raw data obtained by sampling honeybee workers from 6 different locations,
representing 3 subspecies and 2 evolutionary lineages of Apis mellifera, constitute the dataset
for whose processing and analysis a bioinformatic pipeline has been developed. The pipeline
aims at producing biologically meaningful results in connection with the objectives of the 3rd
Working Package of the European project SMARTBEES. Up to date, preliminary findings
have demonstrated the pipeline’s ability to determine population genetic structure of the
subspecies, detect selective sweeps and identify informative biomarkers to be used for the
development of a subspecies diagnostic tool.

Introduction
Apis mellifera
The study of the European honeybee Apis mellifera has an ecologic and economic importance
as is an important pollinator and the products from the honeybee hives, such as honey, wax
and royal jelly, are sold for commercial purposes . In addition, it is a social insect and it has
been intensively studied for its behavioral and social organization [12].
Based on morphometric studies, around 29 subspecies of A. mellifera have been identified
and divided into four major groups as following [11]:

The genome of A. mellifera extends up to around 247Mbp and it is arranged into 16
chromosomes containing in total around 10.000 genes [9]. One distinct characteristic of the
A. mellifera genome is that it has high CpG content and an absence of most major families of
transposons.
Honeybees are separated into male and female bees, with the latter divided into two
diploid bees, sterile workers and fertile queens, with the determination into worker or queen
being based on nutrition, hormones and gene expression. Each colony has one queen, which
has greater lifespan than workers, with a mission of laying eggs (around 2000 eggs per day).
One the other hand, workers come into ten of thousands per colony and their main activities
concern the maintenance of the colony and the feeding of the young. Male bees (drones)
result from unfertilized eggs, and thus they are haploid, and their main task is to fertilize a
new queen [2].

Introduction
SMARTBEES project
The project is part of the European research project "Sustainable Management of Resilient
Bee Populations" (SMARTBEES, FP7-KBBE-2013, Project ID: 613960), which is a col-
laborative multidisciplinary project across multiple universities, research institutions and
companies throughout Europe (www.smartbees.eu).
The SMARTBEES project aims to solve the two main problems related to the A. mellifera
populations. The first problem is related to the honeybee colonies losses by varrosis, a
disease caused by the parasitic mite Varroa destructor. Varroa is fed by getting attached to
the bees bodies and during this process a RNA virus that causes deforming wings gets spread
out to the bees. On the other hand, there are honey bee colonies that exhibit resistance against
Varroa, and therefore the identification of the genetic basis of these adaptations would be a
valuable asset for the fight against Varroa.
The second problem is connected to the economic importance of A. mellifera. Due to
commercial interest, many native subspecies in some regions have been replaced by non-
native ones. The drawback of this act is that it leads towards uniformity in the honeybee
populations and consequently to excessive inbreeding and loss of genetic diversity. This
loss causes a reduced capacity of the honeybee populations to adapt to new and challenging
conditions. For this reason, beekeepers should be encouraged to preserve the local adaptation,
and towards this goal it would be valuable the development of a fast and cheap molecular
diagnostic tool that could identify strains for breeding purposes [7].
Objectives
One of the scopes of Smartbees project is to focus on assessing honeybee biodiversity. This
is the main focus of the Working Package 3 (WT3), whose objectives can be summarized as
following:
1. To determine the genetic structure of Apis mellifera populations, accounting for
variation among and within subspecies. This way it will be possible to have an insight
into the allele frequencies of the subspecies, the genetic distance between them as well
as the amount of common and exclusive polymorphic sites.
2. To identify diagnostic molecular biomarkers to be used for genotyping. This will
allow the development of a fast and cheap molecular tool for strain identificaiton to be
used for breeding purposes.

3. To determine selective sweeps. Selective sweeps refer to the phenomenon where
a favorable mutation, that has occurred in a population, gets fixated altering the
frequencies of alleles that reside in close linked loci (hitchhiking) [10]. Selective
sweeps are strong indicators of positive selection and they may set the genetic basis of
adaptation, and especially adaptation caused by Varroa.
For achieving these objectives, a bioinformatic pipeline has been created for the process-
ing and analysis of Next Generation Sequencing data (NGS data).
Genetic measures
The analysis of genetic variation in the A. mellifera populations in based on the study of
Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP). In a population, a SNP refers to a single genomic
position that is being occupied by different nucleotides among individuals. For instance, for
a given genomic positions in some individuals that position could be occupied by Thymine
(T), whereas in others by Guanine (G). In this case the position is called polymorphic and
the two bases alleles.

Fixation index (Fst)
Fixation index (Fst) is a Wright’s F-statistic that is widely used in population and evolutionary
genetics as a convenient measure of genetic differentiation between populations. It is related
to the allele frequency and it can show the degree of resemblance among populations [3].
If the Fst between two populations is small, it means that the allele frequencies are similar
and the two populations exhibit great resemblance. On the other hand, if the Fst between two
populations in high, it means that the allele frequencies are different and the two populations
are quite dissimilar.
The Fst can be conveniently calculated as following:

In general, highly genetically diverse populations are able to adapt easily to changing
environments as individuals that carry specific variations of alleles will exhibit greater fitness
upon the arise of a new environmental challenge. On the other hand, low genetic diversity
renders a population vulnerable to certain diseases and is the major cause of species extinction.
One of the reasons of low genetic diversity in a population is selective sweeps which due to
the hitch-hiking effect lead to genomic regions with reduced HE.

Methods
Reference genome
The genome of Apis melifera has been sequenced by the Baylor College of Medicine (BCM-
HGSC) using a whole genome shotgun (WGS) method and the sequence, assembly and
analysis of the honeybee genome has been performed by the Honeybee Genome Sequencing
Consortium [12]. The fasta file has been downloaded by the following server:
• ftp address:
"ftp://ftp.ensemblgenomes.org/pub/release-34/metazoa/fasta/apis_mellifera/dna/"
• fasta file: Apis_mellifera.GCA_000002195.1.dna.toplevel.fa.gz
Sampling
The resulted raw data are based on Pool-Seq which is a sequencing technique where the
libraries are not prepared from the DNA of a single individual but from the DNA fragments
of different individuals (pool) [1]. This way Pool-Seq allows a cost effective genome-scale
comparison of populations compared to whole-genome sequencing of single individuals. On
the negative side, the main disadvantage of Pool-Seq lies on the fact that it does not yield
optimal results for pool sizes less than 50 individuals, and does not provide direct information
on the haplotypes.
For the current study pools of 100 individuals (A. mellifera workers) have been sampled
from 6 different locations, and the libraries preparation and sequencing has been performed
by Genoskan A/S (http://www.genoskan.com/). The libraries (Table2.1) were prepared by
using Illumina TruSeq DNA PCR-Free library preparation kit (HiSeq SBS Kit v4), whereas
the Illumina HiSeq 2500 was used for sequencing in 2x125 mode with an insert size of 350bp,
software HiSeq Control Software 2.2.58 and RTA 1.18.64.
All libraries were created using standard TruSeq LT (single index) adapters. The following
sequences were used during the trimming process:
• Read 1 adapter: AGATCGGAAGAGCACACGTCTGAACTCCAGTCA
• Read 2 adapter: AGATCGGAAGAGCGTCGTGTAGGGAAAGAGTGT

Subsampling and SNP calling
In order to avoid biases during the calculation of genetic measures (e.g. Tajima’s D) due to
sequencing errors in highly covered regions, the mpileups have been subsampled to uniform
coverage. Based on the depth coverage results (Fig.3.2.e), positions with coverage within the
range 50-200 have been subsampled to uniform coverage of 50.
During the SNP calling process, in order for a position to be considered polymorphic the
minimum allele count was initially set to 2. However, based on the results obtained during
the calculation of the Tajima’s D at 20Kb window size, it has been hypothesized that the
observed noise was due to the existence of excessive rare alleles (Fig.2.6). Based on this
hypothesis, the minimum allele count was set to 3 and the process was repeated giving the
results in Table2.4.
Subsequently, the pools have been compared to each other so as to identify the amount
of SNPs that are common between pools or exclusive to each pool (Fig.3.3.a). Moreover,
a python script has been written for the calculation of the minor allele frequencies (MAF)
which have been plotted after filtering out all the non biallelic sites (Fig.3.3.b).

Window size determination
For the calculation of the Tajima’s D and the Fst the value determination for the parameters
window size and step size is of great importance. The window size concerns the length of the
genomic region in base pairs to be used for the calculation of the genetic metric, whereas the
step size corresponds to the amount of overlapping between windows. The estimation of the
window size was initially based on a binomial distribution and the step size was set to 50%
overlapping in order to avoid missing selective sweeps due to windows split. The probability
for mutation (Pr = 1/166) was based on the median of the identified polymorphic sites in
all pools divided by the total length of the reference genome. The binomial distribution
showed that the smaller window size with probability of 95% of having more than 20 SNPs
is 4790bp (Fig.2.3).

Manhattan plot it has been hypothesized that this is due to high amount of rare alleles, and
for this reason the minimum count for calling a SNP was set to 3 in all measures (Fig.2.6).

Diagnostic Biomarkers
The process for the identification of diagnostic biomarkers has been performed in two stages.

been used once again, but setting this time the window size and step size to 1. For each
pool pair, genomic positions with Fst = 1 have been identified as diagnostic private alleles
(Table2.5) and the mean Fst has been used as the basis for building the Fst heatmap showing
genetic similarity between pools (Fig.3.4.b).

Results
After the verification of the raw files checksum the reads length has been calculated using Unix
awk commands and compared to the reads length after trimming showing an improvement
of uniformity. (Fig.3.1).
Following the pipeline steps of phase I (Fig.2.1), after filtering the bam files, the com-
mand flagstat of samtools has been called before and after removing the PCR dublicates
(dedubbing) showing that a small fraction of the reads has been removed from the dedubbing
process (Fig.3.2.a).
The phase I finishes by plotting the mapping quality of the reads, (Fig.3.2.c) the insert
size (Fig.3.2.d) and the amount that each pool covers the reference genome (Fig.3.2.b). The
majority of the reads has a mapping quality above 30, they do not overlap extensively nor are
located far apart from each other and each pool covers ≈90% of the reference genome.
In order to determine the appropriate coverage value to be used during the subsampling
process of phase II (Fig.2.2), the depth coverage has been plotted, after filtering indels, where
the value 50 has been identified as the target value (Fig.3.2.e).

Discussion
The current study is based on Pool_Sep raw data obtained by sampling worker honeybees
from 6 different locations representing 3 subspecies and 2 evolutionary lineages of A. mellif-
era. A bioinformatic pipeline has been built for the processing of the Illumina NGS data,
producing quality bam files regarding mapping quality, depth coverage and reference genome
coverage, and for attaining the objectives of the 3rd Working Package of the European project
SMARTBEES which could be summarized as 1) determine European honeybee population
genetic structure, 2) determine selective sweeps and 3) identify informative biomarkers to be
used as subspecies diagnostic tool.
Limitations
The determination of the window size parameter for the perl scripts of Popoolation 1 package
has been identified as the biggest bottleneck of the pipeline analyses due to high waiting time
for obtaining the results during testing. This process could be expedited by implementing
the process with a programming language that supports multiprocessing. Additionally, the
window size that yielded biologically meaningful results was 20Kbp which is considered
quite high value for the small genome of A. mellifera. This might signify that this type of
analyses is not very sensitive with small genomes, and the distribution plots of the genetic
measures showed that it is strongly dependent on normality.
Conclusions
The SNP calling process revealed 6618873 total SNPs with mel_russia having the highest
number of SNPs (1572358) and ibe_south the lowest (852239). Most SNPs were shared
amond the M branch (284100) whereas the cec_greece had the most exclussive ones (667637).
The Fst comparisons have shown great genetic similarity among the iberiensis subspecies
(0.020268 ≤Fst ≤0.0265453), but also among the mellifera subspecies (0.0624191 ≤Fst ≤
0.102297) whereas the subspecies that was proven to be the most genetically distant from the
rest was the cecropia (0.482265 ≤Fst ≤0.587547).
The Tajima’s D calculation revealed 155 significant windows which correspond to 130
genomic regions with 213 annotated genes.In general, most genomic regions exhibited
balancing selection instead of positive selection. The pool ibe_west was the one with the
Page 29
==PAGE:34==
Discussion
most significant windows (46) from which 39 were related with balancing selection and had
40 annotated genes with non-synonymous mutations, and only 7 with positive selection and
8 annotated genes with non-synonymous mutations. On the other hand, the pool mel_ireland
had the lowest number of significant windows (10), 7 of which presented balancing selection,
with 3 annotated genes encompassing non-synonymous mutations, and 3 positive selection
with only 1 annotated gene with non-synonymous mutations.
The functional annotation of the genes carrying non-synonynomous mutations using the
DAVID API did not produce meaningful results as most of the gene products are uncharacter-
ized, especially the ones belonging to regions with negative Tajima’s D. The API identified
only families of functions with the most prominent being the ones related to smell receptors,
sensory transducers and membrane components.
Finally, the allele count comparison displayed no private alleles for the iberiensis pools
and subspecies nor for the Irish mellifera pool. There were 34148 private alleles identified
for the Greek cecropia and only 1 for the Russian mellifera pool and mellifera subspecies.
Future research
While the pipeline has produced biologically meaningful results, nevertheless the findings
presented in this report are not definitive. The results are still in an ongoing investigation and
the ones presented so far consist a preliminary assessment of the pipeline’s ability to achieve
the project’s objectives as is going to be used for the processing and analysis of the rest of
the A. mellifera subspecies.
Moreover, interesting results obtained by plotting and comparing the MAF per chro-
mosome revealing probable highly conserved areas and common areas with high MAF.
It would be interesting to investigate the possibility of finding patterns that on one hand
could be attributed to a specific subspecies and on the other could explain the differentiation
between subspecies by using image processing and machine learning. This might lead to
the development of a model that could quickly explain the genetic difference between A.
mellifera subspecies.
