Abstract 
The worldwide brewing industry has yet to find a feasible and sustainable solution to the bi-product; brewers spent 
grains (BSG), for which it is currently considered as waste. Previous studies outlined the composition of raw BSG 
revealing how it contains concentrations of nutritional components, which benefit human health. With the aim of 
transforming BSG from waste to product, the use of a drum pressing filter machine developed by Dacofi® on BSG was 
investigated.  
Two filter mesh sizes of 100µ𝑚 and 300µ𝑚 were attached to each side of the filter in order to compare the machine 
performance. Four batches of BSG were prepared in total to generate consistent raw material. During the filtration 
procedure, four outputs were generated in total, two for each solid and filtrate fraction, respectively. Additionally, the 
two liquid filtrate fractions were split into two new products through centrifugation, supernatant and sludge. Only the 
liquid outputs were thus investigated in terms of particle size content, density, viscosity, and sedimentation time. The 
BSG of the fourth and last brew, as well as the solid fraction were both included when the nutritional content summary 
was made. The summary included the measurements of total protein and soluble protein concentrations, phenolic 
content, sugar concentrations, total dietary fiber1, as well as ash. Mapping the concentrations of lignin and lipids were 
excluded from this investigation. 
Initially, both particle size distributions and viscosity measurements confirmed that the 300µ𝑚 filter produced a liquid 
filtrate containing more particles of larger size compared to the 100µ𝑚. The distribution mode of the particle sizes for 
each filter in the fourth brew were found at 30.2µ𝑚 and 363µ𝑚 from the 100µ𝑚 and 300µ𝑚 filters, respectively. 
The difference was confirmed once more in the protein and fiber assay, where the nutritional value of the liquid stream 
was found to be directly proportional to the amount of particles. The TPC analysis however, showed how a significant 
portion of the phenols was kept in the solid fraction, indicating that certain hemicellulose fibers such as arabinoxylan, 
which tends to form peptide bonds with the phenols, were present. Here, the ability of the 300µ𝑚 filter to let larger 
particles into the liquid stream was identified, as the liquid fractions from this filter had a higher TPC content than the 
100µ𝑚 filter. 
However, as the 300µ𝑚 filter produced a liquid with higher particle density and therefore nutritional value, the 100µ𝑚 
filter counterpart proved adept in removing a larger fraction of water from the raw BSG, effectively preserving the 
insoluble nutrients in the solid fraction. From the soluble protein and sugar measurements, it was seen how the 
concentration of soluble nutrients found in the liquids with fewer particles, was primarily based on sugary 
carbohydrates. This indicated that the 100µ𝑚 filter had greater potential in extracting the soluble sugars located in the 
moist BSG.  
It was thus concluded, that the best filter size would depend on the nature of the task given to the Dacofi® filter machine. 
If the goal would be to generate a liquid rich in nutritional particles with a follow-up purification step, the 300µ𝑚 filter 
would be recommended. 
However, if the solid fraction from BSG was found to be the ideal food additive in a specific industry, the 100µ𝑚 would 
be preferable, as it removed more than 50 𝑤𝑒𝑖𝑔ℎ𝑡−% of water from the BSG input in a single throughput.

Project objective 
The main objective of this report was to investigate the separation performance of the Dacofi® Filter machine on 
Brewer’s Spent Grains (BSG), using two different filter mesh sizes. The measure of success was based on the ability of 
the filter machine to transfer the liquid fraction from BSG and to direct the flow of nutrients allowing both solid and 
liquid fractions to be used as potential human food sources.

Introduction 
Across the globe, beer constitutes approximately 12% of the total consumption of beverages, placing it as the 4th largest 
behind hot drinks, milk, and carbonated beverages [1]. Since the 1990’ies, beer consumption has steadily increased, 
reaching between 1.93 billion and 1.97 billion hectoliters worldwide in the timespan 2013-2015 [2]. 4.8% of this volume 
was covered by Germany alone, which produced approximately 95.6 million hectoliters in 2015 [3], [4].  
During the beer production, four separate materials are generated; wort, brewer’s spent grains, hops, and excess yeast. 
With wort being the main product for the fermentation process, the remaining material has considerably less value and 
is generally perceived as a waste. For every 100 liters of wort produced, brewer’s spent grains (BSG) is subsequently 
generated at a mass of approximately 20 𝑘𝑔, resulting in a 1:5 part runoff as waste [5]. Thus, BSG is the most abundant 
part of this waste at approximately 85 wt-% of the total runoff [6]. Based on this waste ratio, Germany produced 
approximately 1.91 million tons of BSG in 2015, which gradually increases with rising demand and expanded beer 
production.  
A logistical issue arises for production companies exceeding 1 million hectoliters of beer annually, as the sheer volume 
of BSG becomes neither feasible nor viable to transport and sell as agricultural feedstock [1]. Proximity of trade is a 
requirement, as wet BSG is subject to rapid molding due to anaerobic microflora within 7-10 days [7], [8].  
With limited shelf time and proximity requirements to the place of demand, an oversupply of BSG quickly reduces its 
value making it an expensive liability. Stocking methods have been developed to store large quantities of BSG over 
longer periods of time in silages by adding lactic acid, preventing butyric acid bacteria Clostridium Butyricum from 
spreading [8]. Despite the functionality, the silages must be kept free of air and properly insulated to halt the bacteria 
cultivation, which in turn only extends its limited shelf time if the material is desired as livestock feed.  
A window of opportunity exists at the end of the mashing process however, where BSG remains hot and unexposed to 
air. In this state, the microbial contamination is low enough for the BSG to be considered within the range of food usage 
[7]. This particular phase in the process, has received increased attention due to its potential to reduce the 
environmental impact while potentially easing the economic strain on the brewing companies.  
This attention gave rise to several investigations, which sought to find viability and profitability for BSG in various 
industrial applications. Recently, BSG has been promoted in the process of increasing the porosity of brick production 
[9], in addition to using it as a biosorbent for cleaning wastewater of copper(II) [10]. Furthermore, BSG has seen 
application in solid-state fermentation process to enrich the protein content, showing a 2-4 fold increase in both crude 
and soluble protein content [6]. Other experiments sought the use of both BSG and spent yeast (BSY), to hydrolyze the 
protein fraction found in the BSG, by using vacuole proteases found in BSY. Given optimal conditions, the protein 
content in BSG was successfully hydrolyzed by proteases activity in BSY, ensuring a higher total phenol content (TPC) 
and antioxidant activity (FRAP).  
However, due to the sheer scale of production currently existing in the beer manufacturing industry, it is unfeasible to 
implement none-continuous process lines, which require time and resources to operate properly while generating 
products that have no current value.  
Thus, it is the goal to outline the separation efficiency of the Dacofi® Filter machine, in terms of nutritional concentration 
in the two product fractions, as well as the ratio of solid fraction dewatering. Depending on the designated purpose as 
well as the feasibility and viability, either the solid or the liquid phase from the filtration could be the selected choice of 
human food additive. This choice is expected to rest with any industry that finds a use for either product stream.

The Dacofi® filter machine 
The Dacofi filter machine is the primary downstream process unit, and has been continuously developed at DTU Food 
for nearly 15 years. The design was originally made with intend of turning potato peels into pulp. However, with 
scientific involvement in beer craft, DTU food sought to experiment with the machine and used the separation mechanic 
on BSG to test its feasibility. The initial results proved the machine capable of separating liquids and solids at a 
reasonable degree, albeit with few and superficial details. However, the question remained whether the operation could 
advance to the industry, as a viable answer to the BSG waste problem faced by brewing companies. 
The machine functions as a basic filtration unit, equipped with four custom built cylindrical drums placed vertically inside 
a chamber holding. The placement of the four cylinders creates an empty space inside the filter chamber, where raw 
BSG is fed. The feeding mechanism is driven by a screw placed vertically on top of the cylindrical unit, which spirals 
downwards into the main chamber. The subsequent pressure built-up, forces the BSG feed outwards along the edge of 
the rollers. Through the combined effort of both rollers and the custom-built filter drums, the slow and sturdy rotation 
squeezes the feeding material between the narrow crevices, generating a liquid phase flow inside the filter drums. The 
remaining solid fraction is stuck on the outside of the filter, and is subsequently scraped off by two panels on both sides 
of the machine. An image of the working filtration machine is shown below in Figure 1 [11]. 
 
Figure 1 : Image of the Dacofi® filter machine and its functionality. BSG medium is fed from the top of the machine and 
administered by a revolving screw into the feeding chamber. The subsequent pressure forces the BSG outwards, squeezing it 
between the filter and rubber drums, generating two fractions; a solid and liquid material [11]. 
The reason for the unique design is based on the necessity of performing the basic separatory function to a competitive 
degree. This degree is measured in its ability to process BSG at a suitable rate, while transferring its nutritional content 
into the liquid fraction.  
Moreover, if the Dacofi® filter machine proves to create a commercially competitive product containing nutritional value 
for humans, it must comply with feasibility and viability if deployment to industry is to occur. Feasibility refers to the 
operation practicality in itself. From the point of its creation, BSG must be processed immediately to avoid 
contamination and extended oxygen exposure. The question of feasibility thus lies with the machine’s ability to process 
an adequate amount of BSG to a sufficient level of quality, with minimal downtime.  
==PAGE:9==
 
 
 
Page 9 of 74 
 
The implication of viability states that the overall operating performance must be economically appealing if the machine 
is to replace the current standard. This factor depends on a variety of parameters, such as the potential market value 
of the product, logistical expenses, operational cost, and filtration rate. 
As BSG is considered a waste in a modern brewery, it is treated accordingly and disposed of. Hence, to challenge this 
current practice norm, the filtration unit must be able to perform a competitive unit operation that potentially replaces 
the downstream process, despite it being new and untested. 
BSG 
The brewer’s spent grain (BSG) is the key component processed by the Dacofi® Filter machine. It is the byproduct left 
when mashing is completed, and the wort has been drained from the vessel. Barley malt comprises the main ingredient 
to the mashing process, and is produced on a worldwide scale of 134 to 144 million tons between 2011 and 2013 [12]. 
The total mass of BSG generated per brew varies from 20-30 weight-% of the original total mashing weight, depending 
of the lautering efficiency [13]. This relatively high waste fraction makes BSG an abundant and cheap material, suitable 
for exploitation. The bulk of BSG is mainly composed of water and insoluble components from the grist, such as the 
pericarp, husk, and cell walls.  
 
Figure 2 : Cross section view of a barley malt grain. It is seen how several cell walls form the outer shell, including the pericarp 
(seed coat) on the dorsal side, and husk (palea) located at the ventral side. They cover the core mass of starchy endosperm and 
plant embryo, divided by the scutellum epithelium [14].

study outlined a list of the material composition in BSG [7]. The study aimed to compile the potential application of BSG,

downstream processing of BSG, and exclusively dealt with the question of its nutritional value. On this basis, the study

Analytical methods 
Applying a unit operation to an industrial process is a technical challenge, where experiments are performed prior to 
the pilot plant scale. The experiments serve to indicate whether the operation is viable and feasible by interpreting the 
generated data.  
The performance of the Dacofi Filter machine is measured in its ability to not only fractionate liquid from the solid mass, 
but to direct the flow of proteins and fibers into the liquid phase. Ranking such performance requires a collection of 
data, which fully describes the properties of the solid and liquid. The methods and experimental approaches to obtain 
such properties are categorized and split into subsections further below.  
To ensure reliable and comparable data from the analytical investigations, a new brew was made at regular interval to 
keep fresh supplies at hand. Thus, each brew has been duplicated in terms of composition and execution, to ensure 
minimal deviations. Additionally, each brew was subject to different types of analysis, where the fourth and last brew 
was subject to all types. An overview is shown below. 
 
Brew 1 – Preliminary. 
o 
The first brew was made to test the validity of the brewing system, in terms of mass balancing and 
conservation. It was important to test whether a reliable balance of input and output materials to the 
system could be established, allowing for more advanced studies of the Dacofi® Filter machine. 
 
 
Brew 2 – Mass balance, size distribution, density, viscosity, protein content, disc centrifugation. 
o 
Next brew shifted focus towards the physiological properties of the liquid, as well as protein content 
measurement using the Kjeldahl assay. Size distribution, density and viscosity all contributed to 
outline the liquid behavior in industrial conditions, both prior and post Dacofi® Filter machine 
processing. 
 


Particle size distribution 
Despite both Dacofi® Filters being made with a fixed mesh size, it is undocumented whether they produce a liquid 
containing particles of their exact mesh size, or if a certain distribution of sizes is found in the liquid. By measuring the 
particle size distribution for the liquid filtrates and centrifuge products, the filtration quality can be determined, based 
on how far the size of the particles in the liquid deviate from the actual filter size. Initially, particles with a lower size 
than the filter is naturally assumed present in the size distribution measurements. As the filters are supposed to block 
particles up to a certain size, any case of detected particles with a higher size than their respected filter must be treated 
as one of two causes.  
Either the quality of the filtration process is less than optimal, or the particle size measuring equipment is 
inappropriately adjusted. The first cause is likely to have an influence, as not all physical experiments yield results that 
matches the theory precisely.  By investigating the distribution of particle sizes within the liquid fractions before and 
after centrifugation, the filtration performance can be highlighted in detail. Thus, the results from performing the 
particle size distribution will be presented after the centrifugation step has been described. Using the proper particle 
analysis equipment, the study will reveal how accurately the 100µm & 300µm filters produce liquids containing similar 
particle size as their mesh.  
Mastersizer 2000 
The particle distribution of both liquid streams and their centrifugation products were subject to refractory 
measurement. The measurement was performed using the Malvern® Mastersizer 2000 particle analyzer with model no. 
APA2000. Besides the Mastersizer 2000 acting as the optical unit, a Hydro 2000G sample dispersion accessory is also 
included [20]. The machine follows the light scattering theory known as the Mie theory, which is the most accurate 
description of predicting the light scattering behavior of all materials under all conditions [21]. 
However, the key point of this theory is that specific knowledge of the particle size and structure detail must be in place 
before any measuring can commence. Thus, one can accurately predict the way light scatters around the target particle. 
The optical unit of the Mastersizer 2000 works by capturing the very light scattering pattern detected from a field of 
particles. By subsequently applying the Mie theory, it calculates the size of the particles in the field that generated the 
specific light scattering. Three distinct steps must be taken in order to extract a particle size distribution from any 
sample.  
Initially, the sample must be dispersed such that the sample is properly mixed with the circulated mobile phase, or 
dispersant. If the sample generates chunks or clogs the optical unit, corrupt scattering will occur, rendering the particle 
size measurement incorrect. When the sample is properly dosed and correctly dispersed in the mobile phase, the light 
scattering step taken by the optical unit proceeds. The optical unit is made up of several individual detectors, where 
each detector collects information about the light scattering from different angles.  
To produce a single size measurement, the detector array continuously “snap-shots” the scattering pattern produced 
by the sample, and averages the result. Typically, more than 2000 “snap-shots” are taken during this phase, every 
millisecond. Finally, when the measurement is completed and all data is collected in the Malvern software, the size 
distribution pattern can be calculated following the Mie theory. An example of such measurement using the optical 
detector array based on the Mie theory of particle size distribution is shown below in Figure 4, alongside an explanation 
to the fundamental concepts of understanding the Mastersizer 2000 data output.

From Figure 4, each bar represents a particle size distribution, calculated from the light scattering caused by the 
dispersed sample being circulated in the mobile phase. Thus, the horizontal value of each bar is a size band of particle 
sizes, e.g. 56-61µm with a vertical value of an associated total volume fraction. It is important to note however, that the 
Mastersizer system fundamentally calculates the particle size distributions on a volume basis, and is likely to yield 
corrupt or incorrect data if transformed into a surface, length or number distribution [20]. A quote from the Mastersizer 
2000 operators guide interprets volume-based results as follows. 
“The first and probably most important point to remember when interpreting the results is that the fundamental size 
distribution derived by this technique (Mastersizer 2000) is volume based. This means that when the result indicates, 
for example, that 11% of the distribution is in the size category 6.97 – 7.75 µm, the total volume of all particles with 
diameters in this range represents 11% of the total volume of all particles in the distribution.” [20] 
In summary, the Mastersizer 2000 does not quantify nor detect particles of individual sizes, but rather fixes a volume 
fraction of the measured particles and relates it to the total volume of the dispersed sample. Applying the data 
interpretation stated by the Mastersizer 2000 operators guide on Figure 4 above, the graph shows how particle sizes of 
20-30µm are represented by 3 bars, collectively forming the volume distribution peak of the measured sample. The 
total volume of all particles within this size range, represented by the tallest three red bars, must be interpreted as 
occupying approximately 13.5 volume percent of the total volume in the distribution, calculated as the three bars 
multiplied by 4.5 vol-% for each. This concept is thus referred to as “volume based results”. 
It is essential to ascertain however, that the Mie theory presumes all particles to be perfectly spherical, which rarely is 
the case and tends to cause an issue when interpreting the particle size data [22]. The issue has considerable influence 
when describing the particle appearance, and thus prevents a detailed documentation of the actual particle shape. The 
Mastersizer 2000 simply states a unique value from its calculation that only fits the volume description of a perfectly 
spherical object. If the particle is rectangular, it is unknown whether the machine measured the diagonal or edgewise 
length. The spherical presumption technique concept is known as “equivalent spheres”.  
All derived distributions made by the Mastersizer 2000 are expressed in sets of size classes that are optimized to match 
the detector geometry and the optical configuration of the machine, aiming to yield the best resolution. The distribution 
parameters and the derived diameters from the presumed spherical shaped particles are calculated from the 
fundamental distribution using the summation of the contribution from each size band, or “red bar” as visualized in 
Figure 4. The calculation for the representative diameter for each size band was done using the geometric mean of the

The final requirement for the particle size measurement is the selection of a proper refractory index (RI) value. The RI 
is a factor, which determines how much light is bent or refracted when propagating a particle. It also states how much 
light is reflected when reaching the interface, as well as the critical angle for total internal reflection [23]. Consequently, 
an RI value of both the dispersant and particle type is necessary in order for the Mastersizer 2000 to distinguish between 
dispersant and sample. Demineralized water was used as dispersant, which has the specific RI of 1.33 [20]. In order to 
determine the RI value of the sample, it is necessary to outline the general composition by selecting the most 
significantly occurring particle. The refractory index encyclopedia primarily lists metals, alcohols, polymers, silicates, 
and oils. However, cellulose is listed as the only organic compound most closely tied to the sample composition, with 
an available RI value 1.46. As cellulose is considered a water insoluble high molecular weight dietary fiber (IHMWDF), 
classified as a dietary fiber in 2008 by the CODEX Committee [24], its refractory index of 1.46 is subsequently used when 
measuring the particle size distribution for all the samples produced by the Dacofi® Filter machine. An example of 
measuring 20µ𝑚 microcrystalline cellulose powder by three duplications using the specified refractory index was

The physiological behavior of a fluid system can be split into two general classifications, Newtonian or non-Newtonian. 
A fluid is deemed Newtonian when the viscosity tensor is constant and independent of the stress state and velocity of 
the fluid flow. Water, air and alcohol are systems, which closely resembles a Newtonian fluid behavior, albeit not 
perfectly [29].  
If a fluid is non-Newtonian, its viscosity changes by applying shear or tensile stress, spawning two additional sub-
classifications of this particular fluid behavior, shear thinning or thickening. The majority of non-Newtonian fluids has 
shear thinning or pseudo plastic properties, meaning that the viscosity decreases with increasing shear rate, effectively 
thinning the fluid when stirred or agitated.  
In specific cases however, a system is considered shear thickening when stirring increases its viscosity, making it thicker 
and nearly impervious to deformity. These fluid suspensions are defined as being dilatant, and are typically water mixed 
with high concentration of cornstarch. Examples of dilatant fluid behavior found in nature are quicksand, or wet beach 
sand.

Total protein by Kjeldahl assay 
The Kjeldahl assay is a total protein investigation developed by the Danish scientist and brewer Johann Kjeldahl, in 1883. 
Kjeldahl found that by using a three-step reaction involving digestion, distillation and back titration, the total nitrogen 
content in the organic sample could be calculated indirectly [32]. The Kjeldahl method and specialized equipment are 
universally applied and acclaimed for its relative accuracy and scope of sample size and condition. The equipment 
necessary to perform the Kjeldahl assay is available at the DTU brewery, and will be the applied assay to measure the 
total nitrogen and thus estimated protein content in the Dacofi® Filter machine products. The assay requires the use of 
highly concentrated chemicals; hence, all safety measures regarding equipment handling, personal protection and 
waste management are met. The Kjeldahl assay manual has been attached to the Appendix – Excel sheet: Kjeldahl 
Assay.xslx, (Danish).

Soluble protein by Bradford assay 
As the Dacofi® Filter machine was designed with the intention of producing commercially viable foods and food additives 
from BSG, the content of soluble protein influences the degree of product quality, as sedimentation in commercially 
sold liquids is undesired. Hence, the Thermo ScientificTM Coomassie (Bradford) Protein Assay was applied to estimate 
the concentration of soluble proteins in the liquid fractions generated by the Dacofi® Filter machine. The assay included 
the Coomassie reagent Brilliant Blue G-250, which was a dye that binds the protein in an acidic medium [36]–[39]. By 
binding the protein, an immediate shift in color from brown to blue occurs, with a concurrent change in the maximum 
absorption from 465nm to 595nm. By measuring the absorption in this wavelength interval, a soluble protein 
concentration can be deduced, if a standard curve with a fitting reference protein has been established.  
The reference protein used for the Coomassie Bradford assay was the protein Immunoglobulin (IgG bovine) of the -
globulin class from bovine blood ≥99%, supplied by Sigma Aldrich6. Despite Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA) being a 
commonly used reference material when measuring protein concentrations, its known inhibitory effect on enzymes at 
acidic conditions is undesired, and may potentially corrupt any protein measurements performed by either the Kjeldahl 
or Bradford assay [40].

content was thoroughly mixed and incubated at room temperature for 10 minutes. The spectrophotometer (Shimadzu

Total phenolic content  
The quality of nutrient concentration transferred to the liquid phase by the Dacofi® Filter machine risks the possibility 
of deteriorating by lipid oxidization. This degradation of lipids by oxidation not only reduces the nutritional quality of 
the processed food supplicant, but also contributes to human age processing, carcinogenesis, and atherosclerosis [41]. 
Despite the addition of synthetic anti oxidizing compounds such as propyl gallate, tertiary butyl hydroquinone, butylated 
hydroxyanisole, and butylated hydroxytoluene (BHT), lipid oxidation in foods remains uncontrolled and continues 
unhindered [42]. However, the use of synthetic additives to reduce the lipid oxidation in foods has been limited due to 
its toxicity and health risk potential [43], [44]. Hence, the importance and desire to replace commercially synthetic anti 
oxidizing agents with natural ingredients have increased.  
A study issued and executed at the DTU Food institute sought to investigate the natural anti oxidizing content of selected 
seaweeds off the Danish coast. The study included a list of assays, collectively serving to outline the total anti oxidizing 
ability of the different types of seaweed, through extraction of both water and ethanol. One such assay was the total 
phenolic content (TPC), whereby extracting the dried seaweed in a selected medium, the phenolic concentration could 
be identified, as described by Singleton and Rossi [45]. The method used Gallic acid both as the calibration reagent, and 
as the medium from which the TPC concentration would be calculated. An elaborated walkthrough to obtain the TPC 
concentration in Gallic acid equivalents is presented further below, while the result of the calibration sequence is shown 
below in Figure 16. The calculations are presented in Appendix – Excel sheet: Total Phenolic Content analysis.xslx.

Total dietary fiber assay 
The four principle carbohydrate groups are monosaccharides, disaccharides, oligosaccharides and polysaccharides. 
These groups are subsequently split into sub factions, in accordance to the number of monomers attached to the 
carbohydrate backbone [48]. Dietary Fibers (DF) are classified as carbohydrates that have a degree of polymerization 
exceeding 3. Thus, DF are classified as either oligosaccharides or polysaccharides. Additional segmentation introduces 
high or low molecular weight dietary fibers, (HMWDF) / (LMWDF) to the DF, which changes according to their solubility 
properties. The distinction is described in the following subsection, and is essential in order to outline the concentration 
of certain fiber sub groups, and thus the product stream quality.  
The overall division of fiber and their respective sub groups are shown below in the carbohydrate classification 
schematic in Figure 18 below.  It is by this division, fiber sub groups are named and labeled with various properties [17], 
[49].  
 
Figure 18 : Dietary fiber classification schematic. It is seen how the carbohydrates are split into factions depending on their 
degree of polymerization (DP). From here the factions are further split into fiber sub groups, depending on their water solubility 
[17]. It must be noted however, that this fiber grouping is not absolute, and certain exceptions must be considered. Evidently, -
glucan is marked as an water-insoluble fiber from yeasts or molds. However, as cereals are the source material, the -glucans 
measured by the AOAC 2011.25 method are presumed water soluble. 
 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
 
Page 33 of 74 
 
The source of nutrients in BSG stems from the barley malt that is rich in carbohydrates. The variety of carbohydrates 
found in malt includes cellulose, -glucan and arabinoxylan, where the latter is classified as a hemicellulose [50], [51]. 
Arabinoxylan serves mainly as structural tissue in plant cell walls, but is also a reservoir of ferulic and phenolic acids 
through covalent binding [52], [53]. Consequently, the binding of phenolic acids grants arabinoxylan the unique feature 
of having anti-oxidizing properties. This makes arabinoxylan an important class of fiber, as it is shown to possess various 
health benefits for humans [54].  
In the context of health benefits, several claims concerning the properties of various fiber types, including -glucan and 
arabinoxylan, were issues by the European Commission [55]. The subject was centered on the health benefits of 
digesting these particular fiber types, where a claim asserted that by consuming 3 gram of -glucans on a daily basis, 
the blood cholesterol would be maintained and even reduced to normalized levels [55]. Another claim argued that a 
daily intake of 8 grams of arabinoxylan would contribute to a reduction of post-prandial glycemic response, and overall 
improvement of fecal bulking [55]. The health claims were presented to and summarized by the authority of the 
European Union, the United States Food and Drug Administration, and the Australian/New Zealand Food Standards. By 
the effort of the commission and its health claims, the goal of the total dietary fiber assay presented below was to 
outline the concentration of the highlighted fiber types from the claims, in the various product streams generated by 
the Dacofi® Filter machine. Hence, -glucan and arabinoxylan will be of particular interest when judging the nutritional 
quality of the products. 
The dietary fiber assay performed in this investigation was according to the CODEX AOAC Method 2011.25 [56]. A 
partnership, with the Finnish Food Safety Authorities EVIRA under supervision of Senior Researcher D.Sc. Helena Pastell, 
was made, who agreed to carry out the total dietary fiber analysis of the Dacofi® Filter machine products. The two 
dietary fiber assays AOAC Method 2011.25 and AOAC Method 2009.01 share similar principle, use of reference reagents, 
enzymes and dietary fiber detection range. However, the newer assay 2011.25 has the distinct advantage of being able 
to separate water soluble and insoluble polysaccharides. This feature brings more detailed fiber content information of 
the filter products to light. The AOAC method 2009.01 detection pattern is shown in Figure 19 below.

The samples were sent by courier from Denmark to Finland after being freeze dried for approximately 48 hours. Upon

collected as residue. The two residues, IDF and SDFP, were each subject to summary drying, weighing and combustion.

through HPLC. Thus, the total dietary fiber was summarized as IDF + SDFP + SDFS.

based on the dietary fiber classification schematic from Figure 18 above. Arabinoxylan (AX) was mentioned as a

performed by D.Sc. Helena Pastell suggested that AX gradually decreased in solubility with fewer arabinose sugar units 
attached to the xylose backbone. However despite naturally occurring AX having fewer attached sugar units (500 −
3000) compared to insoluble cellulose (7000 −15000), it is not yet possible to specify the lower limit on the degree 
of polymerization where the hemicellulose species switches from water-soluble to insoluble. Nevertheless, as -glucan 
and arabinoxylan were shown to contain features that provide health benefits for humans upon consumption, attention 
must be given to the dietary fiber results in the SDFP category.

in higher solubility, as well as gums, galactomannans. In terms of water soluble oligosaccharides are inulin, resistant 
starch and polydextrose.

Experimental procedure 
Having established the scope of available analytical features and the overall purpose of the Dacofi® Filter machine 
performance, the experimental procedure from brewing to analytical work could commence. Immediately after 
completing the mashing procedure by approximately four hours, the filtration was carried out over the course of an 
hour. Next, samples from all process streams including the wort were collected and stored at freezing 
temperatures (−20°𝐶). This was done in order to ensure minimal degradation by bacterial activity, while preparing the 
analytical assay. However, a portion of the liquid filtrate from all brews save Brew 1 was put aside, and used as part of 
the centrifugation step. This generated two additional product streams from the filtrate, supernatant and sludge, which 
is included in the overall mass balance as seen in Figure 3. 
Conclusively, the experimental procedure was designed with the intention of generating batches of BSG by a duplicated 
mashing recipe, such that comparisons could be made between the progressively elaborating assays. 
Brewing 
The raw materials for mashing were composed of 500 liters of water and 90 kg barley malt, supplied by Sophus Fuglsang 
Maltfabrik A/S, with the following designation. 2-row spring, Pilsen malt 8070, production date 10-2016. 
The base malt has the following composition stated by the supplier [58], where the EBC value expresses the color 
scheme of the specific malt type, similar to the Standard Reference Method (SRM).

Prior to milling and adding the barley malt to the mash, 84.5g citric acid was added to the water to reduce the pH to 5.8 
[59]. Preparing BSG from the supplied malt followed standard procedure in terms of mashing and lautering, as stated in 
the general literature [59], while upholding proper brewing etiquette in terms of sanitation, documentation, and safety. 
The mashing procedure complied with the temperature profile shown below in Figure 20. The control log containing 
the full brewing procedure is attached in the Appendix – Brew Log. By following the Figure 20 profile, the enzymatic 
activity of the malt is realized through the following three phase steps [59]. 
 
Gelatinisation 
 
The milled malt mixed with hot water, increased the volume and caused the closely packed starch molecules to swell 
and burst. This dispersion of starch increases the surface area from which the enzymes can consume and produce 
sugars, accelerating the mashing process. 
 
 
Liquefaction 
 
This phase reduces the viscosity of the sample by the activity of the enzyme -amylase. The viscosity is at all times 
desired to be low, in order to avoid agglomeration of starch to occur, effectively slowing down the extraction of sugar. 
 
 
Saccharification 
 
The last phase is the process of sugar formation. As procedure is temperature sensitive the optimal activity of -
amylase and -amylase enzymes was set at 72-75°C and 60-65°C, respectively [59]. As the mashing completes, the

wort was extracted and transferred to the boiling kettle for further treatment. The remaining spent grains were 
weighed and fed into the Dacofi® Filter machine for filtration.

Centrifugation 
Due to the limited knowledge of the filtrate sedimentation behavior, the choice of centrifuge equipment was unique to 
Brew 1 & 2. A proposition using an industrial disc centrifuge on the two batches was made, to test whether a separation 
using this type of equipment was possible. The disc centrifuge mechanics, liquid phase flows and disc stack positioning 
as shown in the cross-section of Figure 22 below.  
 
Figure 22 : Cross-section of the industrial disc centrifuge used to make the initial test of separating the particles from the liquid 
filtrate generated by the Dacofi® Filter machine. 
This type of centrifuge was designed to create a two-phase system of a continuously fed liquid stream due to the 
difference in density. The centrifuge was fed by an automated pump system that fed the liquid through the top and 
down into the rotating chamber. During the rotation sequence, the relatively heavy component would be let through 
the “Rising channels” in the “Separating discs”, forced towards the inner wall of the chamber, and led up toward the 
“Dense phase” outlet. Meanwhile, the lighter fluid would remain closer to the axis and released through the “Light 
phase” outlet at the top. In addition, any particles found in the feed would be collected over time at the periphery, and 
discharge through the “Solids ejection” outlet using a manually controlled water jet. The centrifugation procedure 
allowed for the 100µm filtrate to be processed first, followed by the 300µm filtrate. 
Spinning at 10000 rpm and with a chamber size of 15.8𝑐𝑚 [60], the disc centrifuge was indeed able to generate a two-
phase separation of the filtrate. From the centrifuge specifications, the relative centrifugal force (RCF) was calculated 
using the following [61] in [31].

Results & Interpretation 
As the investigation objective was to compare the filtration performance of the two different filter sizes, the results will 
show both filter characteristics simultaneously. The goal of which is to provide an overview such that the verdict of 
choosing the appropriate filter is evident. The results will be presented in the following order. 
 
Particle size estimations, based on the distribution. 
 
Particle and liquid medium density. 
 
Viscosity of the liquid medium. 
 
Theoretical sedimentation time. 
 
Dry matter composition (Protein, fiber, ash, sugar, total phenolic content) 
Initially, the fluid dynamic characterization and the corresponding theoretical sedimentation time will be examined. This 
will give a preceding impression on the difference between the two filter product lines. Subsequently, the dry matter 
composition will provide the overarching evaluation necessary to decide which filter size yields the product line most 
suitable for further research and potential application in the brewing industry.  
 
Particle size estimations 
Following the mass balances, an estimation of the particle sizes found in the various liquid streams was made, based on 
the measurements done by the Mastersizer 2000. The sizes were given in distributions as described in the Mastersizer 
2000 section above for all Brews. By using two different centrifugation system between Brew 1 & 2 and Brew 3 & 4, it 
is expected that the distribution of particle sizes will follow two general themes. Since significant sedimentation was 
observed on the products generated by the industrial disc centrifuge, their corresponding particle size distribution is 
assumed to have a considerable concentration of larger particles in the lighter phases. A reminder must be made, of 
the choice of refractory index (RI) and its effect on measuring the size distribution of pure cellulose. From the labeled 
size of 20µ𝑚, the pure cellulose powder was instead measured to approximately 29 −30µ𝑚. This particle size 
overshoot must be considered when interpreting the following distributions. Firstly, Brew 1 & 2 are presented 
simultaneously due to their expected similarities in particle size distributions.

Figure 29 : Viscosity profile of the Brew 4 fluids. Water has been included as an indicator of the equipment accuracy. Theory states 
that the viscosity of water is not shear rate dependent. Consequently, the graph is expected to be horizontal for this liquid, but 
show signs of significant turbulence in the initial measurements, and an increase of viscosity in the later stages. The initial 
turbulence is presumably caused by inaccuracy of the equipment, while increased friction due to higher shear rate at the later 
stages of the measurement affects the temperature, and thus the viscosity. 
The theory states that due to being a Newtonian fluid, the viscosity profile of water was expected to keep a horizontal 
development along the increasing shear rate, as the difference in shear stress should have no effect on its fluidity. The 
expectation is not met however, as water seemingly has a turbulent profile in the initial stages of the measurement, as 
well as in the latter. The turbulent beginning may be caused by inaccuracy of the instrument, as the piston needs to 
maintain a low shear rate. In the shear rate interval of 20𝑠−1 −100𝑠−1, the instrument seemed to accurately measure 
the expected viscosity profile of water at 0.001𝑃𝑎∙𝑠, whereas it begins a gradual increase past 100−1.  
The gradual decrease of viscosity for all involved fluids indicates that they all possess rheopectic properties, in that they 
show shear-thinning fluid behavior. This confirms that the fluids are Non-Newtonian as expected, as viscosity increases 
with higher shear stress. If an estimation of the two theoretical sedimentation time scenarios of the Brew 4 filtrate was 
to be made, the appropriate viscosity value would be at the point of intercept along the y-axis. An explanation follows 
next.

sheet presented in the Appendix – Excel sheet: Mass Balances.xslx – Tab: Brew 4 Mass Balance. However, passing the 
dense sludge through the required 0.2µ𝑚 filter before being added to the HPLC equipment proved not worthwhile. As 
the sludge needed centrifugation treatment, it would presumably generate a supernatant similar to what was tested in 
advance. Therefore, the sludge sugar concentration stated in the mass balance sheet was considered equal to that of 
the supernatant.  
As sugars are carbohydrates designated as either monosaccharides or disaccharides, caution must be exercised when 
interpreting the results given by the HPLC chromatography, as an overlap in the concentration measurement between 
carbohydrate types can occur. Namely, the concentration of fibers could interfere with the final sugar estimation, 
leading to misinterpretation of the actual content. The sugar concentrations given above do not provide sufficient 
results to base the decision on which of the two filter types would be most suited for filtrating BSG.

thus resulting in more particles of bigger sizes passing through. The remaining IDF found in the filtrate was subsequently 
passed to the sludge after the centrifugation treatment, showing that the sedimentation procedure was effective in 
separating the IDF particles from the liquid medium.  
The hemicellulose fiber, arabinoxylan, was mentioned to have a positive health impact upon consumption. It was 
categorized as an SDFP fiber type, and was seemingly split into both the solid and liquid phase after filtration by an 
approximately equal degree. The 100µ𝑚 filter however, was able to generate a liquid filtrate with a greater 
concentration of SDFP fibers than the solid, pointing towards how the use of this filter mesh would be fitting if the SDFP 
fibers in a liquid solution were the target nutrient. The exact solubility of arabinoxylan in water has not been fully 
covered as previously mentioned, as it depends on both the degree of polymerization (DP) and degree of substitution 
(DS). The DP for naturally occurring arabinoxylan has been mapped to approximately 2500, making it soluble in water. 
However, the exact lower threshold of DP that turns arabinoxylan insoluble in water has not been confirmed.

yielded a higher concentration of SDFS fibers in the particle heavy sludge fraction, than in the supernatant fluid. 
Although, despite the supernatant amounting for approximately 70% of the mass after centrifugation, its lower 
concentration made the yield difference between the two centrifugation products miniscule.

Discussion 
Disclosure of dry mass balances in Stage 1 & Stage 2. 
The significant discourse of the first mass balance was possibly due to inexperience of handling the equipment, as well 
as not keeping in line with the mashing procedure. From the first brew, not the entire solid fraction from both filters 
was collected from the intermediate space inside the filter machine, causing an underestimation of the solid output. 
Additionally, a crude weight with two decimals was used when doing the dry matter balance of Brew 1, further deviating 
the mass balance disclosure of Brew 1. However, the consecutive brews were made with significant care for the details, 
yielding a closed dry mass balance at the fourth and last brew. 
Choice of centrifuge system. 
The limited knowledge of the filtrate particle behavior led to an intermediate experimentation stage, where the 
industrial disc centrifuge was used to test whether it could produce a complete phase separation. As the result was not 
satisfactory due to the observed sedimentation in the light phase, a resorted solution was found using the lab centrifuge. 
As such, the centrifugal products generated in Brew 2 may have caused a mass balance deviation compared to Brew 4, 
which experienced a clear separation of the particle concentration in the two phases using the lab centrifuge. 
Uncertainties with the centrifugation degree of separation in Brew 4. 
The degree of separation was necessary to close the Stage 3 section of the Brew 4 mass balance. However, as Brew 3 
was not balanced in terms of mass, using only the lab centrifuge separation data gained from Brew 4 may have yielded 
uncertainties for the actual separation degree.  
Deviation in total Nitrogen results of Brew 1. 
Compiling the total nitrogen content using the Kjeldahl assay in Brew 1 was done using wet samples, while the other 
brews were done using dried samples. The Kjeldahl assay was designed to include the use of wet samples, as the 
degradation step proceeds no matter the sample condition. However, if direct cohesion between the Kjeldahl results 
and the final dry mass composition charts were desired, it was deemed necessary to use dry matter samples only. Yet, 
as wet samples were used in the Kjeldahl assay of Brew 1, the sample mass was adjusted by using the corresponding 
dry matter fraction. However, the procedure was ultimately subject to errors, as the Brew 1 dry matter balance was not 
closed within satisfactory limits.  
Fiber content interpretation accuracy 
It was found that the degree of solubility was dependent on both the degree of polymerization and the degree of 
substitution. Calculations based on the fiber results have been done while assuming that some individual fibers 
constituted the total concentration of that particular fiber group. Hence, every calculation regarding a particular fiber 
type would be a rough overestimation of the actual content of that specific fiber. However, the calculations were as 
accurate as the findings they were based on.  
The -glucans found in cereal were designated to have water-soluble properties. This addition was considered a valid 
approach when calculating the theoretical number of people having their daily fiber intake requirements met through 
a single batch of 120𝑘𝑔 BSG. 
 
 
==PAGE:67==
 
 
 
Page 67 of 74 
 
“Unknown” dry mass fraction in pie charts. 
The dry matter compositions generated by the various assays exclude the presence of both the cell wall material of 
lignin, and lipid as oils. Summarizing these two components from the literature study performed by Mussatto et. al. 
2005 yields a mass fraction of approximately 34%. This fraction was much in line with the missing mass fraction stated 
in Table 27 above, where the assays required to identify the concentration of lignin and lipid have not been included. 
This results in a similar missing mass fraction of approximately 35%. An implication could be made, that the exact 
missing mass fractions from the conducted experiments in this study marked 𝑈𝑛𝑘𝑛𝑜𝑤𝑛, are the cell wall components 
lignin, and the fatty acids, triglyceride oils, lipids. 
Total phenolic content (TPC) measurement 
The total phenolic concentration measured by the TPC assay, combined the use of the Folin-Ciocalteu reagent with 
Sodium Carbonate. However, as stocks of Sodium Carbonate were depleted during the TPC measurements between 
Brew 3 and 4, a switch in reagent was required. The use of Sodium bicarbonate was thus applied, which had one sodium 
atom replaced by a hydrogen atom. The change in reagent composition between the two brew measurements was 
perceived as a significant error source. Therefore, using the remaining stock of Sodium Carbonate a comparison was 
issued, to test whether a shift in the assay reagent would have a significant effect on the result.  
It was found through testing that the only considerable change between measurements was not based on the choice of 
reagent, but due to agitation of the liquid sample. By stirring the liquid in the micro cuvette, before measuring by 
spectrophotometer, the visible offset in color due to phase formation would break, equalizing the color difference.

Conclusion 
Depending on its assigned purpose, the Dacofi® Filter machine can be used to either transfer nutrients into the liquid 
phase, or remove excess water from the solid phase. 
If the Dacofi® filter machine was assigned to extract nutrients found in the BSG into the liquid phase, the results suggest 
that using the 300µ𝑚 filter would be more profitable, as a larger degree of particles would be transferred into the 
filtrate. However, if the functionality of the filter machine was to separate water from the main bulk of BSG, the 
100µ𝑚 filter would be a better choice, as it produces a solid fraction with a less amount of water.

Future works 
If an implementation of the Dacofi® filter machine was deemed lucrative to a brewing company of high production 
capacity, the addition of one certain unit operation would be recommended if the products reviewed in this report were 
to be duplicated. As the Dacofi® filter machine was proven to continuously process relatively large amounts of BSG 
compared to its size, the volumetric scale of liquid products being generated would exceed the size of any batch 
designed centrifuge system.  
Assuming that the Dacofi® filter machine was active 24 hours a day, it would have processed 18.5 tons of BSG, and 
generated approximately 6.5 tons of liquid filtrate. In order to maintain a downstream process rate of this magnitude, 
the use of a decanter centrifuge may provide the necessary particle separation from the liquid medium. 
A decanter is a centrifuge system, designed to continuously separate two density phases in a liquid stream. It has 
application in petrochemical, wastewater food, and mineral processing industries, where slurries require centrifugation 
in order to purify the liquid fraction. The decanter is characterized as having its centrifugal axis placed horizontally, while 
the feed is continuously injected into the machine. Consequently, the high rotational speed forces the dense phase of 
the feed into the periphery where it is gradually pushed to the opposite end of the lighter density phase. Below in Figure 
39 is an example of a 4000𝑔 generating decanter developed by Hiller A/S.
