Abstract 
The thesis is about the reuse of bricks as a building material, with a focus on the Norwegian market 
because the author is from, and future workplace is in, Norway. Findings can also be used elsewhere. 
The background for this topic came out of six interviews held with people from the industry in Norway, 
January 2019. The six people came from a producer, contractor, people from relevant organizations 
and others with relevant experience or influence. The outcome of the interviews was a broad 
agreement on the importance of circular economy, and that bricks could contribute as a reusable 
construction product more than before. Therefore, the focus became about this topic.  
A masonry building built before 1960, can be torn down and the bricks can be reused. The reason for 
the year 1960 is that cement-based mortar was broadly used after this, and the cement in mortar 
makes it harder to separate mortar from the brick. Lime-based mortar mainly used prior to this point 
are much easier to separate from the brick and the brick could easier be reused. There are rules for 
certifications on used building materials, and certification is difficult for materials without a 
harmonizing standard. A material without a harmonizing standard can be certified through an EAD 
(European Assessment Document), that forms the basis for an ETA (European Technical Assessment). 
This is made for used bricks in Denmark, and the EAD is currently in the publishing process of the EU 
commission. The ETA is a European document and can be used in Norway as well.  
The thesis contains of several tests done on bricks, determination of densities, voids, water 
absorptions, frost resistance and includes a suggestion for a new test that determines the salt content 
in an uncomplicated and non-destructive way, developed in accordance with this thesis. All results are 
linked to the requirements in the ETA and checks whether it’s possible to certify according to the 
requirements. The bricks that where tested was five different batches, two picked from a renovation 
project of an old building in Copenhagen, two batches from a company selling used bricks in Denmark 
and one batch of new bricks, so a comparison to a reference series could be made. The thesis also 
includes research on the existence of a market for used building materials and includes a checklist that 
attempts to make it easier to choose reuse, above new bricks.  
The results indicate that the colour difference on the bricks have a quite huge impact, and it tells 
whether the bricks are burnt at a high or low temperature. A darker red shade on the brick indicates 
burning at a higher temperature. A brick burnt at a higher temperature have coarser pores and lower 
porosity, which for example makes it more resistant to frost, and the frost resistance is often a decisive 
requirement if the brick is to be applied outside. The used bricks are more expensive, because it 
requires the assemblage of bricks, a machine or manpower to clean them and stack them on pallets. 
The salt test shows that there is a clear difference on the measured resistance on the salt exposed 
bricks compared to the ones without any salt content. The test cannot determine the exact salt content 
of a brick, but it can say if further testing should be considered. The salt test is seen as a contribution 
to the industry that does not have a non-destructive test like this. Regardless of quality, used bricks 
can almost always be used for inside walls that aren’t load bearing. The results conclude with that all 
the bricks being tested could be certified and CE-marked according to the requirements in the ETA.

1 - Introduction 
In part one of this thesis, the scope of the project is narrowed down and settled. Bricks is a building 
material which have been used for centuries, made of clay and fired in kilns. The process of burning 
the bricks have impact on the environment, since the heat source will vary from coal to natural gas 
(Grønn Byggallianse, 2017). The burning process, and the transportation from the producers in Europe, 
can both contribute into a negative CO2-impact. A wish to get people’s opinions and try to identify 
where the greatest challenges where located and see how a contribution could be made, naturally 
drove the first part into an interview section. 
To define a master thesis around this subject, it was necessary to talk more organized with several 
people and have a set of questions ready. This led to contact with Norsk Murforum in Norway, and 
Øyvind Buset. Norsk Murforum is a workgroup in the brick-industry in Norway, and Øyvind is working 
there as a consultant with his background within masonry. Norsk Murforum are working to promote 
bricks and masonry work, and cooperates with organizations, producers, suppliers, universities and 
associations within the industry, and strengthens masonry as a solution in buildings (Byggutengrenser, 
2017). 
The focus of this thesis will be on the construction industry in Norway. This is because the author is 
from Norway, and the contacts and future employer are located there. It is also seen as beneficial to 
narrow down the area to one country, since the market and industry with building methods could be 
different from country to country. Denmark still have long experience with bricks, so contacts and data 
from Denmark will also be used, because findings also can be used elsewhere. 
1.1 - Qualitative method 
A qualitative method is used when interviews with a non-representative selection of interviewees are 
held. The interviews seek the opinions in the industry and does not give solid conclusions. The method 
gives opinions and picks up what people think, which also is the purpose. The interviewees are 
strategically selected, and a broad perspective are emphasized.  
The method does not give answers that can be analysed in graphs, and people in different companies 
and organizations have dissimilar knowledge, so an alteration in the questions often occur. On the 
other hand, the interviewees have the possibility to elaborate their views on the subject and 
emphasize what they think are important, and they also know what the industry are focusing on today.  
1.2 - Quality assurance and reflection 
The reason for the interviews was to map the different opinions from the industry, and the six 
interviews with different parts of the industry, gave coverage of a broad part of the business. Longer 
qualitative interviews gather a large amount of information, and recordings was done in the face-to-
face interviews, so the focus would be on the interview and not on taking notes. Two of the interviews 
was conducted by e-mail. Six people does on the other hand not get a unifying opinion from the 
industry, but the selection of people gives a tolerable assemblage of opinions.  It was emphasized to 
not ask leading, but broad questions, to get honest and personal answers. Different people still 
emphasis different things and this is also natural since they work in different parts of the industry. 
Stories and opinions are something that have been listened to, and it is known that most of the 
interviewees are from the masonry industry. The nuanced picture has been emphasized.

1.3.1 - Interview nr. 1  
The interview with Rannveig Ravnanger Landet took place January 10th. She is the director of Climate- 
and environmental policy in The Federation of Norwegian Construction Industries (BNL). BNL is an 
interest group for companies and employers of a total fifteen organizations in the construction 
industry in Norway. Rannveig and BNL has shown to be influential political as well, and have earlier got 
breakthroughs with sustainable and environmental recommendations to the government (Byggfakta, 
2016). 
Her knowledge about masonry and bricks is not that high, but her background made it interesting to 
talk to her. She stated early that her and the company’s approach to materials and products are all 
based on facts. Therefore, it was clear for her that materials should be chosen based on facts, and not 
myths and feelings. Her earlier work experience in The Norwegian EPD Foundation, have given her 
knowledge about EPD’s. She said that brick producers are a bit behind in the industry when it comes 
to the environmental product declarations. The importance of circular economy was repeated in the 
interview, and the advantage with low maintenance and the long lifetime compared to other materials, 
was something she saw as positive with bricks. The maintenance is also considered in the Life Cycle 
Assessments, that actually is regulated in Norway, but different methods of doing the analysis makes 
comparison difficult.  
 
With the climate change we are facing, she believed that masonry façades would be a good solution. 
Bricks are used in many buildings in Norway today, especially in Oslo, and she believes that traditions 
in the cities will stand strong in the future. Although the environmental and climate focus are high 
these days, she still thinks that materials will be chosen based on architectural expressions and not 
environmental considerations. Myths of materials is something she believes comes from people 
without knowledge and people who wants simple explanations.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
“Brick has a great advantage when it comes to a lifetime perspective. And 
longevity is coming in as more and more important in the circular economy. 
Longevity is one of the most important parameters, and that is environmentally 
good.”  
Rannveig Ravnanger Landet, 10.01.2019

1.3.2 - Interview nr. 2 
An e-mail interview was held with Fredrik Slapø. He has long experience with bricks as a building 
material and are now doing research, and he is educated with a Master of Science within the field and 
answer the questions as a private person. He was contacted because he has a lot of experience and 
have been working as a mason for several years, and therefore have the experience from craftsman to 
research. He means that the industry is positive to masonry buildings, and that people sees bricks as 
an aesthetically and lasting solution. He thinks that people in general sees bricks as an appealing and 
exclusive material, but also an expensive building material.  
Fredrik thinks that there can be some misconceptions around bricks, and have heard arguments 
against bricks, for example that the walls needs to be thicker and that bricks are not environmentally 
friendly. He sees bricks as a sustainable material, but it depends on the use, what it is intended for and 
that the benefits are utilized. Even though some sees bricks as a costlier façade solution at the time of 
investment, he means that the advantages with low maintenance and longer lifetime, also must be 
taken into consideration. He means that, if gutters and other fittings on the buildings, are designed 
and done right, a masonry façade will have good terms to have a long lifetime. 
If things are done the right way, it will have the right terms for 
a long lifetime. And Fredrik says that hundred years old bricks 
could have better quality then bricks from today. In today’s 
market, the bricks are chose based on the look, and not the 
quality as it was hundred years ago. But he also says that 
bricks have a natural variation in quality, but in general, new 
and old bricks will have long existence if gutters and other 
fittings, as said, are done right. Nevertheless, the reuse level 
is low. His possible explanation on this, is that the 
development of mortar. The increased strength in the mortar 
makes it more difficult to separate.  
 
 
 
“Brickwork of good quality that 
are not exposed to more than it 
should (leakage on the gutter / 
unfortunate runoffs) will have a 
very long life.” 
Fredrik Slapø, 18.01.2019

2 - Theory and Background 
Bricks in Norway are known as burnt clay and have the dimensions 228 x 108 x 62 mm (Madsø, 2001b). 
The history of bricks goes way back in time, and the building tradition is said to go back 5000 year. The 
moulded clay are formed to bricks, and then burned at approximately 1100 ̊C, and the drying process 
is 5-7 days (Thue, 2017).  Bricks have become a tradition in Oslo Norway, and after several town fires, 
bricks were coerced in new buildings from 1624 because it was considered as fire-resistant. The laws 
was not followed by all since bricks was an expensive building material, but the fire proof considered 
material was implemented early and has built building traditions for the city (Hoel & Rimstad, 2017). 
The coercing of masonry was later a demand in all the big cities in Norway and could explain why the 
traditions for masonry are large in the cities.  
Today, approximately 15-20 million new bricks are being built within Norway every year. There has 
been a slight increase over the last years, after the decline in the construction industry in 2008. The 
percentage of reuse are difficult to estimate, but the main importer of used bricks in Norway has since 
their beginning imported 5-6000 bricks from Denmark, but says that the demand is increasing 
(Woxmyhr, 2019). There are no companies in Norway that handles used bricks as a profession, and the 
import of used bricks is from Denmark. If old bricks are to be reused in a building today, it came out in 
the interviews that it is possible to use the used bricks within the same company or on the same plot, 
but the Construction Product Regulation (CPR) has demands that essential building materials must 
have technical approval, a product certificate or similar. Bricks is countable as an essential building 
material (Sørnes et al., 2014).  
2.1 - Bricks 
Depending on who you ask, there will be pros and cons of building with bricks. The interviewees 
disagree with each other on some points but agreed on others. The brick producers would lift the 
benefits with masonry, and a spokesman in the timber industry would talk about the benefits of wood. 
Each project needs its own life cycle assessment if numbers are to be compared, and the most 
environmentally friendly material are to be chosen. There are still some benefits with masonry which 
cannot be omitted, such as low maintenance and the fact that it is a natural material. The building 
method is old, and the variety of patterns and different types of bricks are numerous. Masonry 
buildings have good qualities when it comes to fire resistance and is one of the reasons it was used as 
much as it was in the beginning. Now it is the expression of the façade which often are decisive, and 
traditions lives on.  
Old buildings and traditions have in recent times become a popular thing to see in Oslo, and guided 
tours are arranged around the city. People have interest in this and are listening to art historians talk 
about old buildings and traditions. This keeps the tradition going.

2.2 - Mortar 
The most common mortar today consists of cement, sand aggregate and water, and is used as a binder 
between the bricks. The history of mortar goes far back, but the development from a lime-based 
mortar to the cement-based one used today, is the one of interest in this thesis.  
A lime-based mortar is made with lime, sand aggregate and water. This kind of mortar are more flexible 
and has a limitation when it comes to strength compared to cement-based (Nielsen, 2011). Elder lime-
based mortar have normally a compressive strength between 2 – 5 N/mm2, but values up against 20 
N/mm2 are also found (Kvande & Waldum, 1998).  The lime-based mortar has a higher degree of vapor 
permeability and are diffusion-open compared to the cement based one, because of higher porosity 
(Nielsen, 2011).  Cement-based mortar is stronger and therefore more rigid and much more used 
today. With the development of mortar, the way of doing masonry work also had to change. The old 
walls made with lime-based mortar were more flexible and cement-based mortar makes stiffer and 
more rigid walls. This led to the need of dilatation joints that lets the construction move. Movement 
in the building that was previously taken care of in the lime-based mortar will with cement-based 
mortar be taken care of in the dilatation joints. The development of mortar is presented in more detail 
in Chapter 2.3.4 - Old mortar and cleaning.

2.5 - Characteristics and specifications  
This part will contain a description and explanation of the various parameters and characteristic of a 
clay masonry brick.  
2.5.1 - Compressive strength 
The compressive strength of clay masonry units is determined after DS/EN 772-1 and provides the 
strength of the bricks. An increased density also gives an increased compressive strength (Madsø, 
2001b) and the compressive strength is given by the failure load divided by the area surface tested. 
The combination between mortar and bricks gives that the wall is no stronger than the lowest 
compressive strength of these two.  
2.5.2 - Density 
The density is the mass divided by the volume and gives the volumetric mass density. The densities in 
this thesis are divided into two, gross and net dry densities, and the volume to which it is divided, is 
determined by the density. The gross dry density takes into account the dry weight and the geometric 
volume into account, and the net dry density are determined with the weight found by hydrostatically 
weighing.  
For bricks, gross densities are divided in over and under 1000 kg/m3, and the bricks with a density 
≤1000 kg/m3 are considered as light weight (Madsø, 2001b), but are not given any further attention in 
this thesis. Density does also affect the compressive strength. The lower the density, the higher the 
porosity. A high porosity gives lower compressive strength.

2.5.5 - Colour difference 
Difference in colour can be with intention, such as yellow, red, etc., or with different shades in the 
same series of bricks. The difference in the shade on the bricks comes from the difference in burning 
temperature. For example, a higher temperature will give a darker red brick, compared to a brick burnt 
at a lower temperature. The different temperature does not only affect the colour, but also on the 
properties of the bricks. A brick burnt at a lower temperature have finer pores and higher porosity 
(Maage, 1984). Colour difference are to be expected from used bricks (Teknologisk Institut, 2017). 
The colour difference was more common in the older ovens. The same batch with bricks could have a 
colour difference, because it depends on where in the oven it was burnt. The bricks closest to the heat 
source, were exposed to the highest temperatures, and would have a greater degree of burning. These 
bricks were earlier chosen to be façade bricks, because they had better frost resistance with lower 
porosity and coarser pores. The bricks where sorted by half-, full-, clinker burnt, and wrecked bricks. 
The harder the burn, the more sounding bricks were, when hitting them brick to brick (Jessing, 1969).

2.5.6 - Frost resistance 
In the Nordic countries, bricks are expected to be exposed to frost during winter season. It is therefore 
important that bricks that are in these environments are frost resistant. Parameters that affect the 
brick during frost are the density, pores and rate of water absorption. The porosity increases when the 
density decreases, and a porous material has more capacity for water which sooner or later can freeze 
and can cause possible scaling. The rate of water absorption also has an impact, the higher the rate of 
absorption, the faster the water is absorbed in the bricks. In the lifetime of a brick, it can be exposed 
to several freeze/thaw cycles during winter time and should be able to resist it.  
2.6 - Weathered bricks/Old bricks 
Materials will over the years meet weathering in one way or another. Some more fatal than others, as 
well as the exposure have an impact. Materials near the sea or in chemical environment, such as 
exposure class MX4-5 would be affected rougher than inside walls and other constructions with 
moderate exposure to moisture, MX1-2. If a brick is to be reused, it is important to know the 
characteristics of the bricks, so that a brick with given specifications does not end up in a different 
environment as intended. 
2.6.1 - Salt 
All bricks and mortar have a naturally content of salts and it exist in various types. The different types 
have deliquescence point at different levels of relative humidity, in different types of bricks and have 
dissimilar water solubility. Salt has a hygroscopic effect, which means that in binds moisture. Salt from 
the environment can also influence the bricks and infiltrate the bricks together with moisture. A 
document about salt and damage caused by salt are made by Danish Technological Institute (H. 
Hansen, 2010) and describes the most common types of salts with examples, and are here presented: 
Sodium chloride (NaCl)

create weathering on red bricks and mortar, but not in yellow bricks according to Hansen.

2.6.3 - Renovation and preservation 
Knowledge and experience have led to new building techniques. Thicker walls with more isolation are 
a huge difference when comparing old and new masonry buildings. When renovating elderly buildings, 
the outer wall should be isolated better, and the question is divided into two: Exterior or interior 
change.  
With external isolation, an old ornate building can lose its expression and history will be lost, on the 
other hand, it will have none or little technical issues. Also, the bricks do not have to be frost resistant.  
Internal isolation will reduce some of the area inside, but the expression and the cultural part of the 
façade will be preserved. It is not the decrease of internal area that are the biggest problem, it is the 
of the commutation of the “zero-point” of the external wall that is the problem. When internal 
isolation is applied, the point of where the temperature changes from minus to plus degrees in the 
façade, are moved further in. This will lead to a façade with a higher exposure to frost than earlier, and 
the bricks and possible plaster may behave different and be damaged when exposed to frost. Some 
bricks in a façade wall without isolation may never have been exposed to frost before because of the 
heat loss through the wall and is something to take into consideration. 
A thorough examination should be done before doing anything, because the bricks may have 
parameters that do not fit for a change of the “zero-point”. This could in turn lead to scaling of plaster 
or scaling of the bricks itself.

3.1 - The certification process 
When an essential building material is to be used in Norway, the Construction Product Regulation  
(CPR) requires a technical approval, product certificate, or similar (Sørnes et al., 2014). Today, there is 
no standard that can be used to certificate used building materials, but it is possible to CE-mark a used 
building material without a standard.  
The CE-marking of materials was introduced to ensure the conformity with health, safety and 
environmentally protections standards, and was introduced in 1985. The certification mark applies in 
EEA and Turkey, and the intention with this chapter is to explain how a product can be certified. Danish 
Standard has a step-by-step guide on how to CE-mark materials that have their own standards, but 
since used bricks does not have that, another method have to be used.  
A material without a harmonizing standard can be certified through an EAD (European Assessment 
Document), which forms the basis for an ETA (European Technical Assessment). The EAD must be 
approved by EOTA (European Organisation for Technical Assessment), then the ETA is published. So, 
the EAD is sent to the EU commission to be published. The EAD is a harmonizing European technical 
specification according to CPR at the same level as standards. With an ETA made for a specific product, 
the product can be CE-marked (ETA-Danmark, n.d.). 
News agencies in Denmark and Norway have posted news that claims that it is possible to CE-mark 
used bricks in Denmark. There is still no guideline, standards, or an ETA that has been found by 
research, hence contact was established with companies both in Norway and Denmark. 
Communication has been established with Norwegian standards, a company in Norway that works 
with certifications of building products called Kontrollrådet, Danish standards, and ETA-Denmark. The 
aim was to check whether it is actually possible to certificate and declare used building materials, and 
then especially bricks, and whether there were any methods available.

4.6 - The cleaning process 
All the bricks from the Central Post building had old mortar on them, and supervision from a mason on 
site was preferable before the cleaning started. The mason showed his skills and techniques with the 
hammer, and the bricks were cleaned after his instructions. The bricks would then be as close to reality 
as possible and be just like a mason would have done it and could have reused them.  
The mason told to put the brick in the one hand, with a hammer in the other one. To remove the 
mortar the mason instructed to hit the brick with the hammer on all sides, so the roughest parts of old 
mortar would fall off first. The mortar that was still on the brick would then be removed when the flat 
part on the back of hammer is used repeatedly back and forth to scrape the remains off, as illustrated 
in Figure 19.  When the flat head on the hammer had done its job, and the surface was flat and not 
rough, the stone was good to go. The progress is shown in Figure 18 and 19, and two cleaned bricks 
are shown in Figure 20.

5 - Method 
In order to perform the different tests, work in the laboratory was necessary. Working environment 
and safety are important at the laboratory, and a video with a subsequent test was carried out before 
the start-up. This is also a requirement before working at the laboratories at DTU. This chapter contains 
of methods, relevant standards and quality assurance with reflections.   
5.1 - Quantitative method 
A quantitative method is used when data is to be presented in numbers, graphs and tables. In this part 
of the thesis, were the bricks are to be tested by standardised methods in a laboratory, the quantitative 
method helps when the numbers are to be analysed and compared up against each other.  
To classify bricks, several tests must be performed. Today the standard DS/EN 771-1 Clay masonry 
units defines which tests are to be followed to classify new bricks. A product standard for classification 
of reused materials, and especially bricks, does not exist in Denmark or Norway. This does not make it 
possible to declare them as a used building material according to a standard. An experience document, 
made by BYG-ERFA in Denmark, comes with recommendation of which characteristics to test (BYG-
ERFA, 2018). The document was received by an academic director from the Danish Technological 
Institute, who has extensive knowledge of tests on reused bricks. The recommendation in the 
document was as follows: 
• 
Format 
• 
Compressive strength 
• 
Densities 
• 
Rate of capillary suction and adhesion 
• 
Frost resistance 
 
Based on this, the following tests have been conducted:  
• 
DS/EN 772-1, Determination of compressive strength (European standard, 2015a).  
• 
DS/EN 772-3, Determination of net volume and percentage of voids of clay masonry units by 
hydrostatic weighing (European standard, 1998a). 
• 
DS/EN 772-7, Determination of water absorption of clay masonry damp proof course units by 
boiling in water (European standard, 1998b). 
•

concrete, manufactured stone and natural stone masonry units due to capillary action and the

•

•

small collection of specimens, when having in mind that the building is quite large, and the building

5.2 - Quality assurance and reflection 
All tests are done at the laboratories at the Technical University of Denmark, in accordance with 
approved standards and known methods which are mentioned in the relevant chapters. In advance of 
all the tests, the relevant standards and methods are read and understood. The results are expected 
to have the same outcome if tests are done again with the same bricks, and there were no ambiguities 
along the way. The irregularities, the way they were cleaned and the different degree of burning of the 
bricks all have influence on the results, but this is also expected on the used bricks. Gross deviations in 
the results are taken into account and commented in the relevant cases.  
In order to verify the way in each test, and have a broader basis for comparison, new bricks are also 
tested in the same conditions and with the same methods. It cannot be omitted that the author had 
no experience with testing of bricks before the tests started. With the supervision from staff in the 
laboratory, experience and knowledge with laboratories and tests done on other type of materials 
from earlier, made it unproblematic. By testing new bricks that have product information and 
specifications given from the producer, the procedure on the other tests are even more likely to be 
correct.

6.7 - Frost resistance 
The standard 771-1 does not specify which method to use to classify a brick against frost, a variety of 
methods can be used. This frost resistance test is developed on a project in Norway, as mentioned in 
Chapter 2.3.1.  
This test, hereby called the FR test, uses the rate of water absorption on exposed surface/stretcher

The compressive strength results had a vast deviation. The results show that the bricks with highest 
compressive strength also are the one with darkest shade of red and the highest net dry density.

makes it difficult to categorize some of the series with bricks in one absorption category.

one with most variables. The FR test does approve the fewest bricks, nor the new bricks that have a F2 
declaration from beforehand. The new bricks, as mentioned, have been declared with another test by 
the producer.  
The void filling number are used to check the frost resistance in bricks, and according to the demands 
in the Norwegian catalog (Madsø, 2001b), more bricks where determined as frost resistant, then with 
the previous test. The demands according to the void filling mentioned in the ETA, pf ≤ 0.9, declare all 
the bricks tested as frost resistant.  
The surface on the bricks are varying, and some have plaster and paint residues. This will naturally 
have an impact on the result, and the two batches with most damages and minor flaking, are the one 
from the façade and the machine cleaned GM bricks.  
The ETA has wide tolerances according to density and dimensions, and all bricks tested would be 
accepted. The frost resistance measured by void filling have approved all the bricks, and the normalized 
compressive strength measured on the façade bricks have also all proven to be higher than 15 N/mm2.

7 - Salt determination  
The previous focus has been on what exist today and what demands that exists to declare used bricks. 
A non-destructive method that checks possible salt content on bricks without efflorescence is not 
found by research and gave the idea to develop a test like this. This chapter includes a suggestion for 
a new test that determines the salt content in an uncomplicated and non-destructive way, developed 
in accordance with this thesis. The test will meet the demands in determination of possible salt content 
in bricks in a non-destructive way.  
7.1 - Assessment of potential salt content 
A test to determine the active soluble salt content in clay masonry units exist and is explained in DS/EN 
772-5. The test consists of crushing a brick sample, which means a destructive test. An idea for a non-
destructive, uncomplicated and on-site applicable test is attempted developed and executed within 
these fields.  
The idea behind the test is that a brick with a high salt content should have a lower resistance and 
therefore have higher conductivity. A multimeter can measure resistance and is used in the test to 
measure the resistance from stretcher to stretcher, and header to header.  
The bricks used in the test are all new from Wienerberger, and the same type as the new bricks used 
earlier in the thesis. Before the test the bricks were tested for water absorption, according to DS/EN 
772-7 and DS/EN 772-21 as the other bricks, so information about the absorption was known.  
7.1.1 - Test procedure 
As mentioned, a brick exposed to salt should have lower resistance than a brick without salt content. 
To ensure bricks with salt content, the bricks would need to be exposed to salt. The bricks were dried 
in an oven for 24 hours at 105 ̊C, so constant mass is reached. The dry bricks were then submerged in 
saline water for 24 hours, and the purpose being that the brick reaches saturation and absorbs the 
saline solution and a measurement can be made. The salt chosen was sodium chloride (NaCl) because 
it is in sea water and widely used when de-icing roads and pavements. 
The basis for the percentage of salt in the water was calculated based on the critical values of salt in a 
brick, which is 1 % of the dry weight (H. Hansen, 2019b). And since the absorption is known, the content 
of salt in each solution can be calculated by the dry weight percentage. As an upper limit, the salinity 
was chosen to be 2 % of the dry weight and as salinity, the highest level was 16.5 % salt in the salt 
solution. The rest of the values were decided step by step, so the results would make sense in the 
different intervals. The twelve different salinities with expected dry-content of salt in the different 
bricks can be seen in the result chapter. 
After 24 hours of submerging, the bricks were measured. The established measuring point on each 
side is made with fine steel wool, and it covers the entire surface on each side, see Figure 42 and 43. 
Since there is no knowledge on which point of the two faces have the lowest resistance, the 
measurement is done this way from the one entire surface to the other. In this way the electrons find 
the fastest path through the brick, as electrons do. The two brass angle irons used as support, are 
insulated to avoid contact between the brick and the bench and minimizes error measurements.

when the stretcher resistance was measured. Before the bricks were exposed to salt solutions the 
resistance was measured through the bricks in the same manner after an immersion in pure water of 
24 hours, so a comparison could be made after the salt bath.

Figure 43 - Test setup 2 
After the measurements, the bricks are dried in an oven at a temperature of 60 ̊C, until the absorbed 
water had evaporated. The temperature was put to 60 ̊C because more salt is believed to stay in the 
bricks, stronger salt crystals are also expected when the drying process is done slower. The drying time 
in the experiment is recommended to be minimum 72 hours, by experience within this test. The 
specimens were weighed every 24 hours, until the weight difference was less than 1 %, because it is 
expected at this point that the bricks are at a constant mass. Before drying, the bricks were wrapped 
in a laboratory film, Parafilm “M” from Bemis, on five sides and one of the stretcher sides was exposed. 
The laboratory film was attempted to be sealed on top with tape, see Figure 44. In this way the 
evaporation and efflorescence are partly controlled to this one surface, as it would be in a façade.

mm of water, as seen in Figure 46. The brick will then absorb water, so it will be possible to test the 
conductivity on the different levels without washing the salt out. Out-washing may be the case if the 
bricks were fully submerged again. The bricks were not submerged after drying at 60 ̊C, so a possible 
wash-out of the salt could not have happened during the test.

7.1.2 - Verification 
Measurement with multimeter was also done on the other batches of used bricks. The bricks where 
submerged for 24 hours, and the same procedure were performed as on the new bricks. Indications 
on high or low resistance could then tell whether the old bricks had an existing salt content, and the 
results can also be used to a further verify the resistance on the new bricks without salt exposure. 
Several tests have been done on these bricks, and there is a possibility that any salt could have been 
washed out during previous submerging/drying cycles. To try to quality assure this, a couple of bricks 
that were in back-up was also tested. The back-up bricks, one of each of the series from the central 
post building, had not been submerged earlier. The two bricks were put in a tray in 5 mm of water on 
spacers until saturation (Figure 50), in this way the salt would have a lower chance of wash out. The 
resistance measurement with the multimeter was then done on the two bricks, so the results from the 
other used bricks could be more credible.

8 - Application and market 
This chapter will look into the industry and try to investigate if there is a market for used bricks. This 
are done by looking into a method called the triple constraint, application and will at the end contain 
a checklist that can be used if reuse is considered.  
8.1 - Market  
The past year, the focus on the environment and sustainability in the world have been growing. The 
medias have news articles every day about the subject, and the construction business are no exception. 
Web sites with focus on engineering and construction business are weekly having articles on how the 
business can be more sustainable, and that shows a business that are aware of the problem and have 
started to deal with it.    
Some of the initiatives are for example Rehub which is a collaborative with stakeholders from the 
whole value chain and focuses on a higher recycling rate and reuse. The goal is to make a platform, 
open for everybody, and encourage people to share their knowledge on the field. (Byggeindustrien, 
2019) Another project is a reuse project in Norway that promotes how we can build sustainable and 
environmentally friendly in the future. The project have used old materials from projects nearby to 
build a communal house, and is considered as a pilot project (Byggmesteren, 2019). 
This is only two of many projects that shows an industry that are willing to try and shows that it is 
possible. Small initiatives and pilot projects could make the difference in the end, which also was 
mentioned in the interviews.  
8.2 - The triple constraint 
Who decides which materials to use? And what determines the focus of a project? When handling a 
project, a model called the triple constraint can be used to explain the choices and focus that has been 
made. The triple constraint model includes cost, scope and time, and describes how a project can be 
managed by using these three parameters. The main concept is that all three constraints cannot have 
all the focus at the same time and when choosing focusing on only one, it goes on expense of the other 
two. (ProjectManager, 2019) Juggling between these three is the key, and the balance is important. 
Two examples are now following, with focus on a sustainable scope with reuse of bricks. 
 
Figure 64 - The triple constraint 
 
==PAGE:87==
79 
 
When demolishing a building, and the scope is to reuse the bricks, the choices can be based on the 
triple constraint model. One can choose to increase the cost and send the bricks to another place that 
separates the brick and mortar, stacking them on pallets and return them. According to the model it 
will focus on the scope, and let the cost and time be on expense. If focus is placed on cost, one can 
spend more time and separate the bricks and stack them on pallets on site. 
Another example of balancing the constraints is when a new construction is being built. If bricks are 
going to be used as a building material in some way one can chose between used or new bricks. New 
bricks are cheaper, and the focus will be at cost rather than a sustainable scope. Used bricks will be at 
the expense of cost, since the price is higher.  
These two examples are designed to illustrate the model. The model explains how one choice often 
are made, and there will in some way be at the expense of the other constraints. Projects can often be 
cost- or time driven, and it can make a sustainable scope more difficult to focus on.  
8.3 - Applicability 
Bricks and masonry façades can be used on almost every building, but some are more expedient than 
others. The same applies to reused bricks, but with reasonability. Bricks or not, the used bricks should 
be able to be applied in the same constructions as new ones, as long as they are certified and approved 
for the conditions.  
Different work and papers from the different countries, have different opinions about where used 
bricks can be applied. The experience document made by BYG-ERFA in Denmark states that used bricks 
should not be plastered at all because of the risk of scaling, cracking, colour differences and weathering 
(BYG-ERFA, 2018). On the other hand, the experience document from Lilleborg in Norway has made a 
table with varying requirements including walls both with and without plaster, in and outside (Madsø, 
2001a), and SINTEF’s written recommendation document says that used bricks can be plastered in a 
façade, and does not even need to be frost resistant (Sørnes et al., 2014). As mentioned earlier from 
two sources, the bricks should not be exposed to exposure classes higher than MX3.1, unless a 
declaration for higher exposure are present.  
A certain risk of using old bricks, still needs to be taken into consideration. A Danish paper refers to 
what one must have accept that several of the bricks may need to be changed when reused because 
of frost or salt weathering, compared to whether new bricks are used (BYG-ERFA, 2018). SINTEF in 
Norway also writes that you must take into consideration that some reused bricks most likely needs to 
be changed over the first years if applied (Sørnes et al., 2014).  
Used bricks is not widely used in Norway, but several buildings have been built in Denmark (Gamle 
Mursten, 2003b). Norway does not have the same facilities as in Denmark and could make the 
difference when it comes to reuse of bricks.  
The benefits with the used bricks are that it is a sustainable solution, that does not require recovery of 
new clay and you don’t get any emissions from the burning process. Reused bricks can also make 
authentic expressions and less visible repairs on existing old constructions. The emission saved is 
equivalent to 1 ton of CO2, if 2000 used bricks are reused (NIRAS, 2015). The solution is therefore green 
with low material emission.  
 
==PAGE:88==

There are also some challenges with used bricks. The fact that there is little or no knowledge about the 
raw materials, and a datasheet with the material specifications does not exist. The price is also 
expected to be higher, and you have to agree on a façade with differences on the surface, because the 
old bricks have that as the original expression. A façade with a varying expression may also be 
something an owner or architect want, so that does not necessarily need to be a disadvantage. Large 
quantity of the same type of brick could still be difficult to obtain.  
When using old bricks, there is no clear recommendations on what mortar to use. The only 
recommendations on the type of mortar to be used are on Gamle Mursten’s website, where they 
recommend using lime-based mortar when handling used bricks, but they also write that other types 
of mortar can be used (Gamle Mursten, 2003b). If repairing an elder construction with used bricks, one 
should strive to use the same mortar that is already in the construction. A mortar suitable for the rate 
of water absorption should always be sought. Finally, using lime-based mortar makes it easier to 
separate the bricks from the mortar later on, and it makes the reuse possible in the future.

9 - Discussion 
From the interviews at the beginning of the thesis, various answers appeared when quality about old 
and new bricks was asked about. There was some disagreement about the quality before and now, but 
the control regime in the new kilns is better today. The variation on the used bricks can at some degree 
confirm this, but the inner wall bricks had on the other hand more uniform results. Assumptions where 
done on how old the bricks are, so it could be that the inner wall bricks are newer then the rest.  
The information from Gamle Mursten gives that the machine cleaned bricks came from Otto Busses 
Vej in Copenhagen. It cannot be excluded that the bricks could have been collected from more than 
one building or construction, and the distinction between the bricks could be explained by this, 
together with the colour difference. 
The compressive strength of bricks is said to be rarely decisive, and the test done on the façade series 
of bricks shows that this is the case. Still, some of the bricks are close to the normalised compressive 
strength value at 15 N/mm2 stated as the declaration value in the ETA. The gross dry densities are all 
within the boundaries of 1650 kg/m3 +/- 30 %, and still, some of the series are significant different from 
one another. One can discuss if the boundaries are put too wide in the ETA.  
It is mentioned that a rougher surface and colour difference must be expected on used bricks, and this 
can affect the rate of water absorption. The results from the rate of water absorption chapter 
substantiates this, and some of the bricks have plaster and old paint on the stretcher sides, but residues 
of mortar on the bricks has little or none impact on the rate of water absorption. Still, great variations 
within the same series of bricks could make it more difficult to choose a suitable mortar.  
The two different methods used to determinate frost resistance in this thesis is the FR- and void filling 
test and the two tests are done on the basis from a catalog and not a standard. The catalog is from 
2001, and even though this is a few years ago, the test is used by laboratories in Denmark today. The 
FR test takes the void filling number, net dry density and the rate of water absorption of the exposed 
surface into account and does not approve all the bricks. Many of the bricks from the FR test are in the 
category of an unsure frost resistance and some not satisfying at all. Still, many of the same bricks are 
seen as frost resistant when using the void filling test. The standard DS/EN 771-1 makes no 
requirements as to which standard should be used when frost resistance is determined, and the void 
filling test are used in the ETA. The new bricks, on the other hand, are classified to severe frost exposure 
(F2) by the producer by using DS/EN 772-22 freeze/thaw testing, but these bricks do not have an 
ensured frost resistant when using the FR test. The FR test has the most parameters to determine the 
frost resistance, and since it does not approve the new bricks, either the new bricks are not frost 
resistant or the requirements can be considered as too far on the safe side.  
The frost resistance requirements in the ETA are based on the void filling number, and the 
requirements from the Norwegian document are adjusted upwards to approve all bricks with void 
filling ≤ 0.9, instead of ≤ 0.8. This makes it easier to declare used bricks but could also directly explain 
why you have to expect to change some of the bricks in the façade the first years if applying. Experience 
from the use of bricks according to these requirements in Norway are not found, and whether this is 
sufficient in Norway is uncertain.  
 
==PAGE:91==
83 
 
The ETA is developed by ETA-Denmark together with Gamle Mursten. Gamle Mursten is the only 
company with its cleaning technology and have great experience with used bricks. The boundaries in 
the ETA are wide, and the frost resistance demands are put lower than other requirements in the 
original test. This leads to a higher percentage of CE-marked bricks but can also lead to bricks with a 
non-sufficient quality are put in façades and needs to be changed within the first years. This risk is 
mentioned in several papers but can also lead to market that sees bad experiences with used bricks 
and choses new bricks instead.  
The standards used are based on new clay masonry units, and no standards exist for used bricks. The 
standards set requirements on how many bricks that should be tested, and six bricks can as seen have 
vast divergence. If a construction is considered reused, and an inspection of the construction is done, 
there is no requirements on how many, or where in the construction, bricks should be tested from.  
Salt can cause scaling on bricks, and the different papers mentions that salt affected bricks should be 
sorted out. This can on the other hand be difficult if efflorescence is not visible, and a uncomplicated 
non-destructive test that detects this would be beneficial. The assessment of potential salt content 
test was developed during this thesis and was considered as a nescesarry contribution since salt can 
cause considerable damage. The results shows that salt content can be detected by resistance 
measurement in bricks. The test has clear results on low resistance in the salt exposed bricks, and a 
tendency shows that a higher salt content calculated by dry weight, gives a lower resistance. The 
pictures after drying the specimens also shows that efflorescence are slightly visible on bricks with 
measured dry weight of salt content on 0.7 and 0.9 %, and this is close to the critical content. Still, the 
salt content based on the dry weight measurements are varying, and does not support the gradually 
increase of efflorescence on the bricks. 
The theory about remaining life time of salt affected bricks are showing a figure that shows that the 
damage rate is descisivce on bricks at the propagation stage. It is difficult to estimate the damage rate 
of a brick, but the lower the resistance measured on the brick, the steeper angle on the damage rate 
is expected to be. With a steep angle, shorter remaing life time is expected.  
The market for used materials is growing, and examples show that used building materials are being 
used. Still, used materials can still be symbolic and for a niche market. Price and availability could be 
decisive factors, but the environmental benefit cannot be omitted.  Masonry brick buildings are being 
demolished and the handling process by crushing the bricks could be favourable since no system exist, 
and this does not speak favourably for the reuse of bricks in Norway at the moment. The interviews 
still show a picture of an industry that are willing to change. The niche and symbolic projects could be 
pushing this in the sustainable direction, if the market is willing to use bricks that have variations 
appearance wise.

10 - Conclusion 
The production of bricks nowadays, compared to the one used a hundred years ago, are different. The 
properties of used bricks are varying, and according to the results, the interviewees are right in that 
there is better control now with new and improved kilns where workers does not adjust the 
temperature manually with coal, only by looking at the flame. This is for example seen on the new 
bricks, as they have the smaller deviations in the results compared to the used older ones. The CE-
marking and standards ensure this and gives a more even quality.  
From the results it is clear that the colour difference has great impact on the properties. In chapter 
2.5.5 it’s mentioned that a higher degree of burning gives a darker colour and shrinkage. That gives 
changes in the properties, and this is clear on the series of bricks with these variations. The darkest 
brick has the highest normalised compressive strength at 98.5 N/mm2 compared to the brightest one 
at 17.6 N/mm2. The brightest brick was also 21.9 % bigger than the darkest one. The density is 17.9 % 
higher in the darkest brick and did also have the best result in the frost resistant test and the slowest 
water absorption. The comparison is taken from the façade brick four and five.  
The ETA and the upcoming EAD and its requirements make it possible to declare and CE-mark used 
bricks. The boundaries are wider then what new bricks are declared with, and wide limits is also 
necessary since greater variety are expected on used bricks. The used bricks tested in this thesis have 
results with varying standard deviations and are all within the requirements. The density requirements 
show that all the bricks could have been CE-marked with the same ETA, and great variation must then 
be accepted when ordering these kinds of bricks. The frost resistance requirements in the ETA are 
based on the void filling number, and the original requirements from the void filling test are adjusted 
from ≤ 0.8 to ≤ 0.9. This makes it easier to declare used bricks but could also explain why you must 
count on to change some of the bricks in the façade the first years if applied. 
The importance of testing the bricks is clear, and this is substantiated by the great variation in the 
results. One cannot count on that an old brick in a façade have sufficient frost resistance, even though 
it has “confirmed” its resistance over the last 100 years. This is because the heat loss in an elder 
building can have led to a façade that never have been exposed to freezing temperatures severe 
enough. The results from the rate of water absorption are widest on the used bricks, where the façade 
bricks and the bricks from GM had a mean standard deviation of 0.6 kg/(m2 • min) while the new bricks 
had 0.1 kg/(m2 • min). The choice of mortar should not have a too big impact, and if there is uncertainty, 
a test wall should be considered to ensure adhesion. 
The assessment of potential salt content test was developed so a non-destructive method can find salt 
content in bricks in a less complicated way. The test shows that there is a clear difference on the 
resistance to the salt exposed bricks compared to the ones without any salt content. The test cannot 
determine the salt content of a brick, but it can say if further testing should be considered. The test 
meets the demands as intended for by determine possible salt content in bricks and is seen as a 
contribution to the industry that does not have a non-destructive test like this.

The interviews gave the direction of this thesis, and news articles shows an increasing focus for 
application of used building materials. Standard Norway replies that a standard for used building 
materials is most likely to be developed and ETA-Denmark together with Gamle Mursten have 
developed an ETA that can be used for future CE-marking of used bricks. The industry is starting to pull 
in the same direction, and used bricks are considered as a contribution to this. The upcycling of used 
bricks contributes in an industry with growing focus on circular economy and sustainability.  
If used bricks are to gain a bigger market share in Norway, a company with cleaning methods and 
machines such as Gamle Mursten could be of great help, since this does not exist in Norway today. In 
Denmark, Gamle Mursten solves this by receiving bricks for free, sort them out, clean them, stack them 
on pallets and sell them. This could gain the demolisher and a possible company in Norway as well.
