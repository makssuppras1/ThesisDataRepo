Abstract
The safe operation of large offshore wind farms relies on the implementation of electrical pro-
tection systems. These systems are conducted by protection philosophies designed to address
common faults and maintain design integrity across wind farms. The protection philosophy
has evolved through experience from each wind farm project and ongoing operational feedback.
Given the unpredictable and rare nature of electrical faults, it is challenging to predict the
responses of protection systems under all possible conditions, demanding a compromise in pro-
tection system engineering.
This project aims to investigate the current protection philosophy applied to the array cable
protection system, specifically implemented in the REL670 relay hardware. The study will
utilize a hardware-in-the-loop (HiL) test setup and Real-Time Digital Simulator (RTDS) to
verify the protection philosophy, with a system and fault logic implemented in RSCAD.
The goal of this project include establishing a laboratory setup with configured relays based on
standard configurations from Ørsted, modeling an offshore wind farm focusing on array cable
systems within an RTDS environment, and selecting realistic fault scenarios and grid configu-
rations for testing.
Case studies will be conducted using the relay in the hardware-in-the-loop setup to archive
disturbance records, where various fault types are applied and the behaviour of the relay is
analyzed. The results will be used to verify protection philosophy from Ørsted, identifying any
discrepancies. The testing will be conducted to verify the effectiveness of these mitigations.
This approach aims to provide valuable insights into the hardware and protection strategies,
potentially revealing areas for optimization and enhancing the overall reliability of offshore
wind farm operations.

1.1
Background
Researchers have explored changes in the atmosphere, ocean, cryosphere, and biosphere due to
increased carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. [20] Therefore, The Paris Agreement was framed
in December 2015, which stands as a global commitment to tackle climate change. Its core aim
is to limit the rise in global temperatures to well below 2 ◦C. [43]
Thus, without extreme climates, humans have to change their way of thinking and focus on
systems that are important for life and minimize their emission of carbon dioxide.
The knowledge of a future power system is thus crucial as renewable large-scale energy projects
expand, ensuring their safe and reliable operation is of importance. There will also be lower
inertia supplied into the grid from these non-dispatchable sources. Renewable energy will play
a pivotal role in a future power system where Wind Europe has set a goal that the EU will
install 29 GW a year on average over the years from 2024 to 2030, which will bring the installed
wind capacity to 393 GW at the final period.[45] The sharp increase of wind power will reduce
the reliance on fossil fuels, offering a promising solution to combat climate change.
This master thesis delves into distance protection for offshore wind power. The safe operation of
large offshore wind farms is secured by the implementation of electrical protection. Protection
philosophies are used to cope with the most probable types of faults and to ensure design
integrity across the wind farms.
Using hardware-in-the-loop and RTDS, it is possible to test and verify a protection philosophy
implemented in a specific relay. This gives valuable insight into the hardware and the protection
philosophy deployed. It may also reveal areas of concern and/or opportunities for optimization.
This project will investigate the current protection philosophy used on the array cable protection
system and implemented in the hardware REL670.

The key questions that will be addressed during this thesis are
• How does the current protection philosophy detect and respond to different types of
electrical faults in array cable systems?
• What are the potential discrepancies between the expected and actual behavior of the
protection system?
• What are the limitations of the current protection philosophy in handling rare and un-
predictable fault scenarios?
1.3
Methodology, scope and conditions
This project will use a combination of HiL and RTDS technologies, which enable testing and
verification of the protection philosophy under realistic conditions. The methodology includes,
• Connection of the configured REL670 relay into the HiL and RTDS setup.
• Model offshore wind farm in RSCAD with an emphasis on High Voltage Alternating
Current (HVAC) array cable systems.
• Select realistic fault types and locations, operation scenarios, and grid configurations.
• Simulate the response of the relay in the HiL setup.
• Analyze the result to verify the protection philosophy.

Thesis Outline
This thesis is structured into seven chapters, each focusing on a critical aspect of the research.

Chapter 2 lays the groundwork for the thesis by introducing the models of the various equipment

components to the algorithm used in RSCAD for transients.

Chapter 4 delves into the fundamental functions of a distance protection, which is used in the
protection of power systems to disrupt a fault or overcurrent leading to change in network
topology.
Chapter 5: Hardware-in-the-loop Testing
Chapter 5 introduces hardware-in-the-loop testing, an advanced method for validating the
performance of power system equipment and protection schemes. This chapter explains the
setup and execution of HIL tests.
Chapter 6: Simulations
In Chapter 6, the thesis explores the use of simulations to study power system behavior and
validate the models and protection strategies. This chapter details the simulation tools and
techniques used, the scenarios tested, and the results obtained.
Chapter 7: Summary and Discussion
Chapter 7 concludes the thesis with a summary of the key findings and a discussion of their
implications. This chapter synthesizes the research, highlighting the contributions made and
suggesting directions for future work.

The conductor, usually composed of copper or aluminum, primarily serves to carry electrical
currents from one point in space to another. The choice between the two conducting materials
is influenced by the price and physical advantages.
Copper has a greater tensile strength (mechanical property) and lower resistivity (electrical
property) compared with aluminum, thus for the same current a smaller cross-sectional area is
possible. Conversely, aluminum has lower density than copper which influences the weighing
which is advantageous for installing lengthy cables on a drum. [38]
The ability of thick-stranded conductors to carry current effectively can be reduced by elec-
tromagnetic phenomena known as skin and proximity effects. The skin effect describes the
==PAGE:16==

inclination of electric current to flow along the outer surface of a conductor, influenced by the
internal magnetic field within the conductor. The proximity effect is the tendency of an electric
current to flow along one side of the conductor. It occurs due to the interaction between the
magnetic field generated by the current flowing through the conductor and the currents present
in nearby conductors.
These effects cause an uneven distribution of current across the cross-section of the conductor,
thereby raising its resistance. [29]

The metallic sheath is to neutralize the electric field surrounding the cable, and a pathway
for the return of charging current, and conduction of fault currents to the ground. It is also
a mechanical protection against accidental contact and reducing the proximity effect.
The
dimensions of the metallic screen depends on the zero-sequence short circuit current that will
flow in a faulted scenario. [38]
Since one is considering a three-core cable, the short-circuit current will have three different
parallel paths to ground.
In submarine cable installations, metallic armour is typically installed to have higher tensile
strength. The material used for armour is normally copper, steel or stainless steel. It must
handle the pulling tension (T) which is T = W · d + H, where W is the cable weight in water,
d is the depth of water, and H is the bottom tension. [4]

3.1
Power system operating states
The power system contains equipment located in an environment that are susceptible to faults
arising from external circumstances or from the equipment themselves. Thus, for further in-
spection it is worth mentioning that the system can be classified into five operating states,
illustrated in figure 3.1.

The power system can move into various states in a direction according to the arrow pointer,
depending on the function.
When operating in a normal state, all variables are not violated and every equipment is safely
operating below its rated value for longer periods.
If there are increased probabilities of a disturbance due to hazards, a system might be trans-
ferred into alarm state where contingency or overloading of equipment is threatened.
If the system is changed into emergency state due to faults or overload of equipment, then the
system is able to either heal or lead to further isolation of the system, depending on the success
of defensive response.
If it fails to restore it will enter in extremis state which causes cascading outages and possibly

3.6
Neutral Grounding Method
Grounding serves the overall aim of protecting both life and property in instances of faults
and transient events arising from switching operations. It can also allow high sensitivity of the
relays operation.
The fault current flowing depends upon both the impedance of the fault loop and the equivalent
rated voltage. The response of a single-phase to earth depends on the type of the network
neutral grounding. System grounding, serves the purpose of regulating the voltage to earth
within predictable boundaries. Also, when there is a phase-to-earth fault, a flow of current
makes the detection. The detection of the fault relies on the portion of the fault current, with
solidly grounded system or with lower grounding impedance as long as there is a fault between
phase(s) and ground. When the busbar connecting N feeders has a delta connection on the
MV side of the step-down transformer and a solid ground on the primary side, a more eﬀicient
solution is obtained when installing a separate grounding zig-zag connected transformer. [35]
==PAGE:38==
32
CHAPTER 3. POWER SYSTEM FAULTS
The transformer’s impedance is high for balanced three-phase voltages to ensure that, in the
absence of system faults, only a minimal magnetizing current flows in the transformer winding.
The transformer’s impedance to zero-sequence voltages is low. This design allows high ground-
fault currents to pass through. The transformer effectively splits the ground-fault current into
three equal components, each in phase with the others, and directs them to flow through the
three windings of the grounding transformer. [17]
It is placed on the secondary side of the step-down transformer to detect fault currents since a
delta connection has an ungrounded part, allowing the protection system for defense response. If
it would be ungrounded, then the voltages of the unfaulted phases would degrade the insulation
of the connected equipment since it increases to line-to-line voltage. The zig-zag topology also
has the advantage of eliminating third harmonic voltages in the feeder cables. [34]
One might consider reversing connection topology on the step-down transformer, with the solid
grounded star topology on the secondary side and a delta connection on the primary side.
Eliminating the grounding transformer, since no extra equipment is required. However, that
can lead to high ground-fault currents and may cause high touch or step voltages. For higher
grounding impedance, the capacitance of the system becomes dominant. [35] When there is no
neutral point where the system is isolated, the zero-sequence impedance will be equal to the
pre-faulted phase voltage over the non-resistive capacitive earth fault current. [2]
The grounding topology for this thesis is used for a wind farm collector grid. In order to detect
an earth fault the system has to have a path for fault current to flow. The grid transformer
has a delta winding towards the wind farm. This is primarily to suppress triple harmonics.
The earth fault current cannot flow in the delta winding, and there is not a neutral point that
can connect to the ground. Hence, the system needs grounding path from somewhere else.
One can use a dedicated grounding reactor with or without a neutral resistor. Alternatively,
as in this case a combined earthing and auxiliary transformer are used.
The earth fault current flowing during a single-phase-to-ground fault is determined by the
transformer zero sequence impedance and a possible impedance located in the neutral. If the
transformer zero sequence impedance is low and the neutral is connected directly to ground a
relatively ”high” current will flow during faults. This makes it ”easy” to detect fault, because
the phase current will be several magnitudes higher than the normal load current. However,
the downstream equipment has to be rated for this current. The impact is often that the array
cable wire screen has to have a larger cross-section and hence become more costly. The earth
fault current can be reduced by adding an impedance (e.g. a resistor) in the neutral and/or
increasing the zero sequence of the transformer. The resulting earth fault current should be
high enough for the relays to measure, and low enough to not make additional cost to the
equipment. The required transient performance (over voltages and damping) also affects the
design of the earthing transformer. At last, the design of the grounding system affects the
angle of the earth fault current (resistive/inductive). The inductive part of the current should
be higher than the capacitive part comming from health feeders. This is important considering
distance protection.

7.1
Summary and Discussion
The natural aesthetics of distance protection in a feeder in an offshore wind farm has been
introduced, using equivalent grid, array cables, wind turbines, power, grounding and instrument
transformers. It offered significant validation from steady-state calculations settings in the relay
to Dommel’s algorithm for REL670 distance protection using various scenarios at a defined
location within the wind farm.
Overall, there were five scenarios with grounding isolation, various fault resistances, pre-fault
generation, grid equivalent parameters, and loss in power production while a fault was applied.
These scenarios made it possible to address the key questions that were introduced.
When rare and unpredictable faults were applied to the model, there were challenges of the
relay which led to maloperations when ground fault types were involved. That included when
the grounding transformer is not connected to the grid, there was no indication that the zero-
sequence current was isolated until the fault was applied. The impedance trajectory did not
reach any distance measuring zone and thus that function was not activated. However, back-up
overcurrent and overvoltage protection were able to start and eventually send a trip signal.
When the fault impedance increased significantly, the impedance trajectories were also unable
to reach the distance measuring zones. Thus, at the boundary of the first array cable section, the
fault location was influenced by the fault resistance when it increased significantly although the
distance is estimated by the positive-sequence reactance. The worst case was phase-to-phase
and three-phase to ground faults when the fault current was small and the voltage was not
distorted, where the zero-sequence current was small. They also rely heavily on the interaction
with the equivalent grid since the power transfer from the faulted point on the wind farm is
limited.
The reference scenario, with a bolted fault resistance and zero pre-fault generation, was tested
at various locations within the wind farm with the types of faults and proved to be in line
with the protection philosophy in terms of measuring the distance zones. However, the backup
reference varied between steps, but was always able to send a trip signal. The negative phase
sequence overcurrent did not trip for any scenario, but started when there was a measure of
unsymmetry in the phases.
When the wind farm is connected to a weak equivalent grid, there are frequent deviations in
voltage and current that were found to have malfunction of the relay only for phase to ground,
where the current transformer was saturated and a greater deviation of the healthy voltage

7.2
Results
It is Important to use communication schemes where the interaction with other relays might
be beneficial for further clarification of the state of the system.
It is necessary to have a reliable backup protection along with other relays when the distance
measuring zone is not able to correctly detect a fault location.
Neighboring array loss did not affect the faulted feeder when the wind farm was already not
able to transmit power through the fault point.
When fault resistance increases, the fault location along the cable will mostly be affected by
the resistance, which makes the detection of the 80 % boundary harder for the first array cable
section. For a certain resistance, the relay is not able to use accurately the distance measuring
zone.
It is important to adjust the residual compensation factor with the grounding resistance in-
volved for accurate fault location, when there is a pre-fault generation of the wind farm.
The location of F2 and F3 proved to have the same distance impedance measure since in the
model it is the same point. However, in reality there might be short cable in between that must
be considered.
When the grid equivalent is disconnecting from the wind farm, there is an additional zero-
sequence current flowing prior to the fault when the ground fault type is involved. Thus, it
will increase the fault current to ground. When the fault has been cleared and the grid is
disconnected, it will be zero.
When fault occurs on a wind farm and it disconnects from the grid equivalent, the wind farm
is not able to reach steady state on its own after the fault has been cleared. Thus, in reality
if the circuit breaker on the faulted feeder will disconnect, it is beneficial to wait for the grid
to be connected again. The islanding detection of the relay was not analyzed for disconnected
equivalent grid, which might have been able to trip as well.
Saturated instrument transformers are critical for accuracy when measuring the current and
voltage. The fault current can easily saturate the instrument transformers if the flow becomes
unsymmetrical. Saturation will affect the binary function in the relay, where they might be
deactivated for some time.
There is a low short-circuit current contribution from the wind farm, and the protection phi-
losophy is mostly based on steady-state short circuit current, their transients are usually dis-
regarded.
When there is a change in network topology, a fault evolvement between distance measuring
zones might happen.
The zero-sequence current flowing in a three phase to earth fault is only flowing when the fault
is going to its steady-state, which is much less than the single-phase to earth fault.
