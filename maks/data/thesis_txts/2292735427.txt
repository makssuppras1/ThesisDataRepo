Abstract
A combination of waste incineration and geothermal energy is used for district heating
in Thisted, S├Ėnderborg and Copenhagen, Our energy production, e.g. waste incin-
eration is relatively constant whereas the need for district heating is highest in the
winter months and much lower in the summer months. This creates surplus energy
in the summer months but the Heat storage in Hot aquifers(HeHo) project is set out
to explore the possibilities of storing the surplus energy, in form of heated water, in
geothermal reservoirs.
Batch experiments are performed on 30 sandstone samples at the reservoir pressure
and at dierent temperatures to investigate the consequences of geothermal heat stor-
age. To quantify the potential changes in uid composition several chemical analyses
are performed on the sample water.
PHREEQC is used to model mineral equilibrium concentrations and especially look
at the dierences between quartz and chalcedony when modelling silicium concentra-
tions at dierent temperatures. Mineral saturation indices are also determined to help
clarify the potential mineral dissolution or precipitation.

Introduction
In Denmark, a large part of our waste management strategy is to burn the waste and
use of energy from combustion into electricity and district heating, thus achieves a
very high energy recovery of waste (Agency (2015a)). Although Denmark does not
have particular warm summers, the need for district heating is signicantly lower in
the summer than for the winter, hence there is a production of surplus heat in the
summer. To avoid the surplus heat to be wasted, it is appropriate to examine if it
can be stored until the winter, where waste incineration cannot provide enough heat
to district heating only.
Deep sandstone aquifers with temperatures around 75┬░C are today providing the
Danish district heating with renewable energy (Lund et al. (2011)). To make the win-
ter periods of district heating more environmental sustainable and economical cost-
eective geothermal heat storage is considered. Surplus heat from waste incineration
plants in the summer months can be used to heat up groundwater in heat exchangers
and store it in the respective aquifer. The groundwater can be extracted in the winter
months when need for district heating is bigger.
Presently, Denmark has three producing geothermal plants so implementing geother-
mal heat storage would be relatively cheap, cost wise (R├Ėgen et al. (2015)). There are
two plants, Thisted and S├Ėnderborg, which are using a reservoir in the Gassum Forma-
tion for producing geothermal energy to district heating with wells in a depth of 1.25
km and 1.2km, respectively. The last plant, Amager, are producing from a reservoir in
the Bunter Formation at a depth of 2.6km. The Gassum and Bunter Formations are
suitable for heat storage, not only are geothermal wells already implemented in these
formations but also because they are the most common deep sandstone formations
in Denmark. Furthermore, their physical properties(e.g. permeability) are suitable
(Mathiesen et al. (2009)).
The Heat storage in Hot aquifers(HeHo) project, started in 2010, has a purpose
to evaluate the potential of heat storage, and possibly develop a new technology, by
looking at all the responses a potential reservoir would have when subjected to a heat
injection. This thesis is a part of the HeHo project performing geochemical experi-
ments and numerical modelling of the experiments in PHREEQC.
The main part of the presented data in this study is from a previous project (Ja-
cobsen (2015)) where batch tests were carried out on eight core samples from the
Gassum Formation and eight core samples from the Bunter Formation, a total of 16
samples. The batch tests had durations from one day to six months. To simulate the
predicted reservoir conditions with geothermal heat storage, the experimental batch
set up was subjected to 170 bars of pressure and 150 ┬░C. To quantify changes in the
uid composition chemical analyses were performed on the sample water.

3.1
Geological setting
3.1.1
Bunter Formation
The Bunter Formation is mainly deposited in the lower Trias era, 240-251 million
years ago. The formation is widespread in the North German Basin, and further out in
the Norwegian-Danish Basin, with varying thicknesses of 200 - 900 meters (Mathiesen
et al. (2009)). The samples used in this study, and in Jacobsen (2015), are from the
exploration well T├Ėnder-3, which was completed in 1980 and is located in the south-
ern part of Jutland, see Figure 1. The well is 1834 m deep, and the samples used in
the present study are taken from a depth interval of 1654 m to 1655 m (GEUS (2015a)).
The Bunter formation sandstones are uvial, well sorted and are mostly ne to
medium grained (Weibel and Friis (2004)). The formation is made up mostly of arkoses,
a group of sandstone which has at least 25 % feldspar in it, and sub arkoses, a group
of sandstone where over 75 % of it is quartz (Olivarius et al. (2015)). K-feldspar is
more common than Plagioclase(Ca,Mg-feldspar) but overall are Albite(Na-feldspar) is
the dominant feldspar mineral (Weibel and Friis (2004)). Other minerals are calcite,
dolomite, anhydrite and the most common clay minerals, such as illite, kaolinite and
smectite.
Bunter sandstones cover a broad spectrum of porosities and permeabilities, the sand-
stones in this present study has a porosity around 30% and they are high permeable,
over 200 mD (Rosenbrand (2014)).

3.1.2
Gassum Formation
The Gassum Formation is deposited in the last part of upper Trias, around 200 million
years ago. The Formation runs from the North German Basin through the Danish
Basin and ends in the Norwegian-Danish Basin. In the North German and Danish
Basins the thickness is around 50 - 100 meters, but increases to over 300 meters thick
in the Norwegian-Danish Basin(Mathiesen et al. (2009)). The samples are from the
gas storage investigation well Stenlille-2, which was completed in 1988 and is located
in the western Sealand, see Figure 1. The well is 1662 m deep, and the samples used in
this present study are taken from a depth interval of 1538 m to 1539 m(GEUS (2015b))
The Gassum formation sandstones are shallow marine and very ne to medium
grained. Clay clasts can be present(Weibel et al. (2010)). The sandstones are sub-
arkosic to arkosic, meaning a bigger part of it is quartz when comparing to the Bunter
formation (Weibel et al. (2010)). K-feldspar is the dominant feldspar mineral. Other

3.2
Aquifer thermal heat storage
Aquifer thermal energy storage(ATES) is essentially storing surplus energy, e.g. from
a waste incineration plant, as heated groundwater in aquifers. Heat surplus, typically
in the summer months, are used to heat up groundwater, extracted from aquifers, in a
heat exchanger and afterwards returned, and stored, into the aquifer. In periods of con-
sumption the storage groundwater can be extracted and cooled in the heat exchanger.
The hot and cold wells are located in the same aquifer to avoid any complications due
to dierent groundwater composition and also to maintain the hydrostatic equilibrium
in the aquifer.
In Denmark ATES can potentially become an important part of the Danish Cli-
mate and Energy Policy Plan, which is set out to phase out the fossil fuels in the entire
energy supply(Agency (2015b)). 54 % of the Danish households are being heated from
co-generated plans. A co-generated plan produces both electricity and heat (Agency
(2015a)). So savings both economical and environmental could potentially be big.
The potentials of ATES is also being studied in the Netherlands which has resulted
in the research project  Environmental and Chemical Aspects of Thermal Energy Stor-
age in Aquifers and Research an Development of Water Treatment Method started
by the International Energy Agency in 1987(van Mourik and Snijders (1990)). Most
of the contractors and research facilities were from/in the Netherlands but participat-
ing countries were: Canada, Denmark, Finland, Germany, the Netherlands, Sweden,
Switzerland and the U.S.A
Problems with ATES, seen with geochemical eyes, are mainly related to the forma-
tion of precipitates which accumulate around the heat exchangers, thus reducing their
eciency. Furthermore precipitates will, when pumped back into the aquifer, reduce
the permeability by clogging up the pores, thus, decreasing the potential maximum
injection rates. Precipitates are most often composed of carbonates or Fe and Mn oxy-
hydroxides but when the storage temperatures are above 100┬░C the likelihood increases
that silicate precipitates will form (Jenne (1990)).
Large amounts(600-800 kg per cycle) of aragonite precipitated at the St. Paul(USA)
site, a test facility designed by the University of Minnesota, due the fact that a large
fraction of the aquifer sediments are dolomite. The reason for aragonite precipitation
was that the groundwater used for heat storage was already in equilibrium with cal-
cite. Upon heating of the groundwater to 87┬░C during the rst heat storage cycle, the
groundwater became supersaturated with respect to aragonite, which is what caused

the precipitates(Jenne, 1990). This is obviously not suitable of the for the heat stor-

addition of energy will speed up the process (Masteron and Hurley (2004)).

3.4.1
Collision theory and the Arrhenius equation
In order for a chemical reaction to proceed, there must be a number of successful
collisions between molecules, which not only have to have an energy level equal to or
higher than the activation energy but also have to collide at the right places on the
molecules. When the concentration increases an increase in the number of collisions
will occur due to the fact that there are a larger number of molecules in the solution.
The increase in concentration leads to the reaction happening more quickly. Simi-
larly, if the temperature is increased the reaction will occur faster. The primary reason
is the larger number of molecules which have a higher energy level than the activation
energy. The rate of successful collisions will therefore increase (Palandri and Kharaka
(2004); Masteron and Hurley (2004)). Arrhenius used in 1889 the collision theory and
its expression of the rate constant as the basis for the temperature-dependent Arrhenius
equation.

3.6.3
Comparison
Methods based on the Debye-H├╝ckel principle are easy to use since they require fewer
information about species and solution. There is also an extensive amount of literature
on ion reactions available, which facilitates the creation of fairly consistent databases
for geochemical modelling purposes. But when the ionic strength is above 0.1 most of
these methods tend to become inaccurate. Pitzer equations are remarkably accurate
at high ionic strengths but has its limitations in the lack of data, especially the lack of
chemical reactions for aluminum and silicate (Bethke (2007)), thus making it dicult
to use when doing geochemical modelling of sandstones.

4.2.1
Limitations of PHREEQC
PHREEQC is a groundwater program used to do calculations on general chemical
problems. The limitations, relevant to this study are listed in the sections below
Quartz vs. Chalcedony
It is interesting to see how the model handles silicate dissolution and precipitation
due to the fact that both our types of sandstone are quartz rich. Several studies show
that when modelling quartz dissolution and precipitation with equilibrium constants
for crystalline quartz too low silicium concentrations are obtained compared to what
is seen in reality (Azaroual and Fouillac (1997); Gislason et al. (1993, 1997)). Based
on the studies chalcedony will be implemented in our model to see if chalcedony may
give more realistic silicium concentrations than quartz would.
Chalcedony is a micro crystalline quartz structure mixed with moganite (a silicate
mineral) crystals. Gislason et al. (1993), showed that moganite is signicantly less sta-
ble than quartz which could be one reason for chalcedony's higher solubility than pure
quartz. Another reason could be that moganite has a much larger specic surface area
than quartz (Gislason et al. (1997)). When looking at the dissolution rate of moganite

Choice of database
PHREEQC has dierent databases for dierent situations, but this study will only
consider the following three databases.
Pitzer.dat has the most precise aqueous model and is best at handling highly saline
solutions. It does so by incorporating the Pitzer virial coecient approach, mentioned
in section 3.6.2, when correcting the activity coecients. But Pitzer.dat only includes
the most common ions, such as Na-K-Mg-Ca-H-Cl-SO4-H2O (Plummer et al, 1988).
Silica and aluminum are not included in the present available version of pitzer.dat and
as silica and clay precipitation/dissolution may potentially be important in our case,
the pitzer.dat database has been discarded.
Phreeqc.dat uses the Debye-H├╝ckel equation to calculate the activity coecients
and has a vast sum of ions and minerals included. As mentioned in section 3.6.1, the
Debye-H├╝ckel equation cannot handle highly saline solutions. As exemplied in Table
5 this causes the activity of the ions to be small and thereby results in lower modelled
concentrations than would be expected in reality.
llnl.dat (Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory Database) is the reasonable in-
between database handling both the high saline solution while still being able to do
calculations on the needed ions and minerals. The llnl.dat database uses the b-dot
equation, see section 3.6.1.2, for calculating the activity coecients. As mentioned
in section 3.6.1.2 the equation is only accurate up to around 3 in ionic strength, but
the brines used in this study has ionic strengths of over 3.5 and close to 5(Tables 3
and 4). So there may be some uncertainties with the modelled concentration from the
llnl.dat database, but nevertheless it is the database that is going to be used in this
study.
It should be stressed that no database is a suitable t for every situation, they
consist of dierent coecients, constants and elements, taken from a variety of exper-
iments. So they are ideal for dierent purposes. Therefore, any modelling will only be
an approximation to what is happening in reality.

Discussion
Batch experiments
Two Gassum samples, at room temperature is not presented in this study. One of them
was sampled late in June and the analyses could not be nished in time and the other
sample is still waiting to get sampled(scheduled August 28th). But even though two
samples are missing from the data set, a base line for the room temperatures can still
be made when comparing with the Bunter samples.
In this study there were not any problems with leaking during the heat or cooling
period, compared to the many problems in the previous study(Jacobsen (2015)). So
that cannot explain why all the sample CBEs are so far from the acceptable level of
┬▒10%. On a side note, most of the 150┬░C samples has the lowest CBE. A qualied
guess could be that the much higher sodium and chloride concentration(Table 3 and
4) are controlling the outcome of the speciation. So the analyses, of these ions, are
not extremely accurate the CBE will give a wrong percentage. CBEs for the brines,
which are made to be electrically neutral, were done and gave a satisfying results, so
the method/program is working.
Modelling
Unfortunately, only silicium concentrations were successfully modelled with kinetics.
Generally when more than one mineral was added the the model, together with one
of the brines, it started to behave oddly. I do not know if was my lack of expertise
or the brine composition which cause it. Maybe a bit of both. But I do know that
the Bunter and Gassum brines has higher ionic strengths than what is suggested for
solutions when working with llnl.dat database.
Quartz vs. chalcedony
Chalcedony seems like a suitable choice when trying to modelling the observed sili-
cium concentrations with a reasonable equilibrium concentration. Bringing quartz to
equilibrium with the respective brines resulted in an equilibrium concentration which
was around 60% of what was seen when using chalcedony. But a peculiar thing is
that when the mineral SIs are plotted for quartz and chalcedony (Figure 11 and 20),
quartz seems to be the phase controlling the silicium concentration and are nearest
equilibrium. But when modelled quartz equilibrium are furthest away from the cor-
rected silicium concentrations. Perhaps the fact that chalcedony has a smaller dened
enthalpy of reaction in the llnl.dat database can explain it.

Consequences of the observed quartz dissolution could be lowered permeability
due to clogging of pores.
Which could lead to heat storage no longer being prof-
itable/ecient due to the energy needed to inject the heated water. This could lead
to formation skin damage if the wellbore pressure exceeds the formation pressure by a
lot. As mentioned earlier, silicium precipitate could also reduce the eciency of heat
exchangers by accumulating around the heat exchangers. In the long run if quartz keep
getting dissolved and silicium precipitate are getting remove from the heat exchangers
by acidication or another way, the aquifer could get unstable and potentially collapse
as a wort case scenario.
The high concentration of sodium and potassium, for Bunter 150┬░C samples could
likely be due to fast dissolution of albite and k-feldspar, respectively. The rst two data
points on both albite and k-feldspar SIs for Bunter 150┬░C samples are undersaturated.
Gibbsite could likely be the reason for low aluminium concentration. If gibbsite
precipitation from start to nish then it would explain why aluminium concentrations
are almost 0. Gibbsite SI for Bunter and Gassum 150┬░C samples is oversaturated. But
the concentration are extremely small so the could be close to the detection limit.
Carbonates
As mentioned earlier, ankerite, (Ca, Mg, Fe)CO3, could be the phase controlling cal-
cium, magnesium and iron. Though ankerite is only observed in Rosenbrand (2014)┬┤s
Bunter samples, it is still possible that ankerite present in this study's samples. Ankerite┬┤s
associated minerals, calcite, dolomite and siderite, are in fact present.
Magnesium concentrations decreases for Bunter 150┬░C samples which could be at-
tributed to the increasing oversaturation of dolomite in the samples.
The calcium concentrations for both Bunter and Gassum 150┬░C samples lie around
SI=0 and can likely be explained by the calcite SIs which also uctuates around SI =

7
Conclusion
The total of 30 samples helped established a concentration baseline for room tem-
perature and give suggestions to what could happen when the aquifer is heated up
during heat storage cycles.
This study shows clear changes in ion concentrations when the samples are sub-
jected to temperatures of 150 ┬░C. This apply to both Bunter Formation samples and
Gassum Formation samples. The most clear changes was the increase in silicium con-
centration to over 100 mg
L over time periods of 4 and 6 months(Gassum and Bunter,
respectively).
The modelled concentrations show that chalcedony is more suitable to use than
quartz when trying to model the observed concentrations. Modelled quartz equilib-
rium concentrations reached only around 60% of the the observed(and chalcedony's)
equilibrium concentrations.
Reasonable ts where observed when trying to t chalcedony to the corrected sili-
cium concentrations at 150┬░C. Though the specic surface areas(SSAs) used to mod-
elled with mineral kinetic were not actual silica SSAs, but SSA from rock samples. The
Gassum SSA were within 30% of another study's SSA for quartz.
With respect to heat storage in Gassum and Bunter Formations, the fact that large
increases in silicium concentration are observed after relatively short time may be bad
news. Precaution has to be made to ensure that the silicium does not end as precipitate
when in the geothermal system when cooling down by transferring heat to the district
heating water through heat exchangers.
