Abstract
With the world looking ever more towards adopting more renewable energy generation methods and
investing heavily in emerging technologies, storing the energy that is produced has also become vital.
A power plant that is able to store excess energy and generate electricity which is not carbon based, is
an attractive solution. This study focuses on the Solid Oxide Cell technology as a main contributor to
this solution, as it is able to provide energy storage through the electrochemical process of electrolysis
(SOEC), and produce electricity when operating as a fuel cell (SOFC). The fuel utilized is hydrogen,
allowing the only waste products of the process to be air and water. In conjunction with waste heat
energy recovery components, a direct contact membrane distillation (DCMD) unit that desalinates
seawater, and an absorption chiller (AC) that can provide cooling, a high capacity polygeneration plant
was designed to investigate the performance in northern and southern Europe. The plant is powered
by a photovoltaic park that provided 204 MW and 156 MW of electricity at peak solar irradiation for
southern and northern Europe respectively. When the plant operation is reversed to fuel cell mode
and runs on the hydrogen that has been stored throughout the day, it was able to provide up to 42
MW for the south, and 28.6 MW for the south. This was indicative of the potential output in fuel cell
mode as it is based on average daily accumulation of hydrogen. In reality it is able to provide up to 80
MW of power at high fuel rates. The plant also provided enough water to power the electrolysis cells
and also provide water to the district water network. The cooling eﬀect of the AC was much higher
and more eﬀective when the plant was run in SOFC mode, due to the burner that is used to combust
the oxygen with the hydrogen and water mixture that cannot be utilized. The winter and autumn
seasons were the most challenging for the northern European scenario since the plant was not able
to produce enough hydrogen to sustain the operation of the plant in fuel cell mode. Therefore the
recommendation for the operation of such a plant to be viable, is to consider the amount of hydrogen
storage available for oﬀ-peak production months, how it is stored, and which renewable energy source
is powering the plant at a speciﬁc location.

Chapter 1
Introduction
As the global energy demand keeps rising and is expected to rise by 1% up until the year 2040 [1],
the need for more renewables to be introduced across multiple sectors is necessary if the goals of
the Paris Climate Agreement are to be met [2]. As the capacity of renewable energy sources (RES)
increases, the demand for energy storage and conversion systems will be paramount for balancing
the national and intergovernmental grids. There are several electrical energy storage (EES) systems
that can be applied, some are currently available and some are in development. The most common
large scale energy storage as of 2019 is Pumped Hydroelectric Energy Storage (PHES) [3], however
this type of storage depends a lot on the geography of a country and despite being cheap in terms
of $/kW of capacity installed, it also has its ﬂaws. Other than PHES, some other forms of EES
include Compressed Air Energy Storage (CAES), Battery Energy Storage (BES), Fuel Cells, Flow
Battery Energy Storage (FBES), Superconducting Magnetic Energy Storage (SMES), Flywheel En-
ergy Storage, Super Capacitor Energy Storage (SCES) and Thermal Energy Storage (TES). All have
their subcategories and are in diﬀerent stages of maturity or development. H. Chen et al. [4] gives a
very critical review of these systems and their potential, as well as their advantages and disadvantages.
According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), electricity is expected to grow at 2.1% per year
until 2040, twice the rate of the primary energy demand [1]. This means that the share of electricity
in the total ﬁnal energy consumption will increase from 19% in 2018 to 24% in 2040, based on the
“Stated Policies Scenario.” In the “Sustainable Development Scenario” it is estimated to reach 31%
by 2040 [1]. There is no technology that fulﬁls all the criteria as a solution by itself. Technologies are
suited diﬀerently to either improve power quality, reliability, generation, storage or a combination of
these. To reach the set targets, a mix of all these solutions is needed. As the share of RES increases,
the power production on a given day will have varying peaks and valleys, especially when based heavily
on solar and wind - currently the cheapest forms of RES [5]. This paper focuses on the use of fuel
cells, as they are ﬂexible systems that can be used for various power to gas projects, are reversible,
scalable and show great promise in the near future.
When an electrochemical cell converts a fuel directly into electricity by means of an oxidising agent,
it is called a fuel cell. The most common type of fuel cells for larger applications are Solid Oxide Fuel
Cells (SOFC), which operate at high temperatures and use hydrogen as a fuel, but can potentially use
methane, propane and butane, although these are still in development. Other types of fuel cells are
touched upon brieﬂy in the report. The electrochemical process requires a fuel, in this case hydrogen,
which although is the most abundant element in the universe, is not so abundant on earth due to the
fact that it bonds easily with other elements, forming other molecules and organic compounds, such
as water. To date, approximately 90% of the produced hydrogen is done by steam reforming natural
gas, the rest is done using other production methods using a variety of fossil fuels [6]. Recently the
electrolysis of water using electrolysis cells - similar to fuel cells but the working principles are reversed
has - the potential to produce zero-carbon or “clean” hydrogen if the source of electricity comes from
RES. Like SOFCs there are several types but the one studied in this paper is a Solid Oxide Electrolysis
Cell (SOEC). It is more energy intense than steam reforming, however has the potential to be more
eﬃcient [7], especially if it is a Reversible Solid Oxide Cell (RSOC) [8], allowing it to work as an
1
==PAGE:12==
electrolytic cell and a fuel cell depending on the need. In fact a study by Christopher Graves et al.
[9], shows that not only is RSOC technology possible, but it can help slow or possibly prevent the
degradation of the cell and in turn extend its lifetime.
At 1 atm and 25 ◦C, hydrogen has a speciﬁc energy (or energy density by weight) of 33.3 kWh/kg [10],
whereas gasoline and diesel have a speciﬁc energy of 12.7 kWh/kg and 11.6 kWh/kg respectively [11,
12]. However since hydrogen is a gas at ambient conditions and petroleum fuels are liquid, their energy
density by volume is very diﬀerent - 0.003 kW/L for hydrogen [13], 8.76 kWh/L for gasoline and 9.7
kWh/L for diesel. This is why hydrogen is usually stored as compressed hydrogen or liquid hydrogen
in cryogenic tanks. Pressurised hydrogen contains about 0.5 kWh/L at 200 bar, 1.1 kWh/L at 500 bar
and 1.4 kWh/L at 700 bar [13]. But the storage needs depend on the end use. For transport, where
the volumetric size of the tank needs to be as compact as possible, it makes more sense to pressurise.
Some examples are vehicle propulsion such as fuel cell cars & forklifts. For long-distance and big
volume transport, cryogenic tanks are the preferred option. However, for stationary hydrogen storage,
a large cavern or a low pressure vessel (<200 bar), is a viable option and the one explored in this paper.
Furthermore, the high operating temperatures of solid oxide cells means that a portion of the surplus
thermal energy can be utilised for other processes. In this study, the proposed technologies for captur-
ing this excess energy are a Direct Contact Membrane Distillation (DCMD) and an Absorption Chiller
(AC). Water puriﬁcation and desalination is becoming an increasingly hot topic as certain areas of the
world, such as California, Australia and South Africa, experience severe droughts that are expected to
worsen in the future [14]. To date the most known and widely used method for desalination has been
Reverse Osmosis (RO) - a process that uses a partially permeable membrane to remove ions and larger
molecules from drinking water. However, the DCMD method takes advantage of the heat available
in the feed solution (the saline water), and has been proven to work at temperatures above 100 ◦C
[15]. In an experiment by Shirazi et al. [16], where optimum conditions were met and the optimum
membrane material had been selected, 99.99% of the salt was separated from hot water at 80 ◦C. A
lot of areas in the world where there is a high need for water, there also tends to be a need for indoor
climate control, more speciﬁcally cooling. Here an AC that runs on a RES can provide carbon-neutral
cooling for residential or oﬃce spacing. AC technology has proven to be a relatively good method
of recovering waste heat, and as long as certain criteria for its operation are met, such as the usage
per annum and correct choice of refrigerant, they present a competitive advantage in eﬃciency and
economic viability over other technologies [17].
The combination of the aforementioned technologies entail that the plant will be generate multiple
uses for the energy it produces and stores. Such a plant is called a polygeneration plant. In theory the
plant can be powered by the grid, but this would not guarantee the carbon-neutrality of the hydrogen,
puriﬁed water or the cooling system. It would also make the output and price of the other systems
dependant on the spot price of electricity. To solve this issue, this study includes an onsite solar
photovoltaic (PV) park, that will power the entire plant. Globally solar PV has been plummeting in
price the past decade, surpassing oﬀshore wind in 2014 (0.16 USD/kWh for PV vs. 0.18 USD/kWh
for oﬀ. wind) and is currently the cheapest form of electricity production alongside onshore wind
(both lying between 0.047-0.051 $/kWh) [5, 18]. Solar PV is now a very competitive way of producing
electricity (in price/kWh) even compareed to fossil fuel based technology. This should help keep the
costs of a polygeneration plant low, and theoretically be applicable to diﬀerent sized islands or coastal
communities where oﬀ-grid or decentralised power production and storage makes sense.

2.1
Photovoltaic Cells
Unlike a wind turbine, a photovoltaic (PV) cell has no moving parts, it is a static way of producing
electricity. It uses the sun’s energy to create a ﬂow of electrons, known as the photovoltaic eﬀect. This
eﬀect occurs when a junction made up of two diﬀerent materials creates a current or electric potential
due to light exposure (i.e. the sun).
The most common type of solar cell is silicon based. There are various types such as monocrystalline,
polycrystalline, thin ﬁlm and amorpheus cells to name a few. However, the monocrystalline and poly-
crystalline are the ones most widely used due their longevity, eﬃciency and cost aspects. By nature
silicon is a semiconductor, making it a poor conductor of electricity. To improve this, impurities, also
known as ’dopants’, are introduced. For example by incorporating boron for the positive (P) side and
phosphorus for the negative (N) side, the charge of the silicon will be diﬀerent in each layer of the
wafer. This is known as “doping”. In between these layers is a P-N junction, which serves as a barrier
between the two layers where one has excess ’holes’, the P-layer, and the other has excess electrons,
the N-layer. This is depicted in Figure 2.1.

When the photons hit the surface of the cell, they either reﬂect oﬀthe surface, pass through the silicon,
or if their energy is higher than the silicon band gap value, they are absorbed. This energy is given
to electrons, which are knocked loose of their atoms due to the higher level of excitement. The excess
electrons from the N-layer can now only move in one direction; towards the holes in the P-layer. If the

2.2.1
Solid Oxide Fuel Cells
The way SOFCs work is by converting the chemical energy from a fuel into electricity. Since SOFCs
run at high temperatures, they take a relatively long time to start up (10 minutes for SOFCs vs. 1
second for PEMFCs) in turn causing thermo-chemical stresses on the cell structure. This is why ide-
ally an SOFC system should be shut down for maintenance only to avoid the thermal cycles as much
as possible. All SOFCs share the same basic parts to function: an anode, a cathode, an electrolyte,
9
==PAGE:20==
an interconnecting plate, a fuel and an oxidant. Since hydrogen is the fuel of choice in this study, the
oxidant will be oxygen (from air). When the hydrogen is pumped into the cell, the molecules react
with the anode catalyst to produce ions and release electrons. These free-moving ions move towards
the electrolyte but cannot pass through it, while the electrons ﬂow from the anode, to an external
circuit and then to the cathode. Simultaneously the air ﬂowing through the cathode side reacts with
the incoming electrons to form oxygen ions. These ions pass through the electrolyte and bond with
the hydrogen ions to form water molecules. As long as the hydrogen and oxygen are supplied at a
constant rate, the chemical reactions provide a steady electricity generation. This process is depicted
in Figure 2.10.
Figure 2.10: Working Principle of a Fuel Cell
The chemical reactions that occur at the electrodes and overall are given in equations 2.17, 2.18 and
2.19.
Anode (oxidation) : H2 + O2−−→H2O + 2e−

A SOFC system, better known as a “stack”, is comprised of multiple cells connected in series. These
cells need to have the correct materials chosen for their parts if they are to work optimally.
• Electrolyte: The task of the electrolyte is to conduct anions of O2−while not allowing any
fuel molecules to pass through. Additionally, it needs to have a low electric conductivity to
prevent any internal currents from occurring. This part of the oxide cell is usually made of
dense Yttrium-stabilised Zirconia Dioxide (YSZ). This is the material of choice because it can
provide long-term (several years) stability at 1000◦C [27] and has the desired mechanical and
electrical properties.
• Anode: Otherwise known as the fuel electrode in a fuel cell, the anode needs to be a good electric
conductor to transfer electrons from the oxidation reaction, and it needs to act as a catalyst
for the oxidation process while allowing the movements of anions to and from the electrolyte.
10
==PAGE:21==
Since the electrolyte is made of YSZ, the anode is usually made of a cermet called Nickel-YSZ
(Ni −Y SZ), fulﬁlling the requirements described.
• Cathode: Also called the air electrode of a fuel cell, and just like the anode, it needs to
have good electric conductivity.
This helps transfer the electrons coming from the external
circuit to the oxygen molecules. It also acts as a catalyst for the reduction process and has to
allow the cations to move through it freely to reach the electrolyte, where they then continue
through it to bond with the anions on the other side. Them most common cathode for this
application is Strontium-doped Lanthanum Manganite (La0.84Sr0.16MnO3 where the subscript
numbers describe the doping level). Despite being a good candidate, it has relatively poor ionic
conductivity, so sometimes Mixed Electronic-Ionic Conductor (MIEC) materials are used, like
ceramics such as perovskite.
• Interconnector: This part allows the electrons produced by the electrochemical process, to
ﬂow between cells so that an electric current may be generated. This material needs to have
a low electric resistance, have similar thermal expansion coeﬃcients with each layer in the cell,
not react with the fuel or oxidising agent and have good chemical stability within the operated
temperature range of the cell. For these reasons ceramics are usually a good selection, however
they tend to be expensive compared to for example metals.
Though metal alloys are only
capable of operating at lower temperature ranges (600-800◦C) so this is something that should
be taken into consideration. Recently metal-ceramic and metal-glass-ceramic composites have
been undergoing research to improve the durability and costs of the fuel cell seals [28].
• Fuel & Oxidant: These depend on the criteria set by the user of the system. Currently SOFC
systems are capable of running on hydrocarbon fuels such as methane, propane and butane,
which make them very ﬂexible. Nonetheless, if the goal is to produce carbon-neutral electricity
that can power a cleaner grid, then hydrogen is the better option since it emits no harmful gases.
The fuel does however aﬀect which materials are used as electrodes, since some are better suited
for particular fuels, so this should also be taken into account.
Currently commercially available SOFCs come in two structural forms; planar and tubular. Figure
2.11 [27, 29] illustrates the diﬀerent types of designs.
(a) Planar SOFC Design
(b) Tubular SOFC Design

In the planar design, a single cell is made up of several layers of rectangular disks. It starts with
an end plate, then the anodic and cathodic plates on either side of the electrolyte, and ﬁnally either
another end plate or an interconnecting plate that functions as a series connection to the next cell.
The end or interconnection plates have grooves that allow for the ﬂow of the fuel and air. The tubular
design consists of a hollow tube that is closed at one end. Inside this tube is another hollow tube
in which air passes through. This inner tube stops before the closed end of the tube so that the air
returns in the next concentric layer that is constructed with the cathodic material. The next layer is
the electrolyte and then the anodic layer as the ﬁnal outer tube (the initial hollow tube). The fuel
ﬂows on the outside of this layer and the tubes are connected via interconnecting plates at each end,
that are in contact with all the electrodes in each tube. The advantage of the tubular designs is that
they do not necessitate high temperature seals to isolate the oxidant from the fuel, which evidently
leads to stable performance for prolonged periods of time [29, 30]. Despite this they have a lower
power density compared to planar cells [29, 30] and are also the least common of the two. Therefore
the planar SOFC design was chosen for this study.

3.4.1
Plant operation
This study looked at the feasibility of a large polygeneration plant, to see what the possible outcome
could be, based on current knowledge. Particularly for the oxide cells, it has not factored in other
inﬂuential parameters such as the amount of hours in each mode and the potential improvements in
materials or production of the system. The reversibility of an oxide cell seems to show promise in
terms of less degradation of the cell [9], however other parameters such as the electrode and electrolyte
choice are also important when constructing such a system [41].
The irradiation data used to map the plant’s location is based on average daily values. Actual daily
values will produce more precise results that can also show how the plant would operate during ex-
treme conditions, i.e. several days without PV power. This would have great eﬀects on the hydrogen
and oxygen contents of the storage tanks.
It was mentioned at the beginning of the results section that the demand for electricity was assumed
to be constant throughout a 24hr period. In reality it would work certain periods of the day in both
modes, using a certain number of stacks for electrolysis and the others as fuel cells, to accommodate
the grid demand. Figure 3.17 from Quattrini M. [42] depicts a similar scenario for a small-scale RSOC,
and could be used to investigate the eﬀects on a polygeneration plant to improve the accuracy of the
output parameters of the model on an hourly basis.

3.4.2
Plant location
The plant clearly has an advantage when placed in the southern European region. Since PV is the
main power source, this was expected. However, the plant could theoretically be powered by other
RES technology depending on the region it is operating in. For example in Denmark it would make
sense to investigate how the plant also works with wind power, since this is its primary source of
renewable energy. Collela L. [43] bases his study on this principle and how several RES (PV, wind &
ﬂat plate solar collectors) can work in tandem to power a power-to-gas (P2G) plant.
33
==PAGE:44==
Another important factor for the location of the plant, is to have access to the commodities it requires,
namely freshwater for electrolysis and seawater for desalination. So even though it can work as an
oﬀ-grid polygenerator, it still needs access to a district water and heating network to dispose/consume
freshwater and deliver the cooling eﬀect where needed.
3.4.3
Hydrogen storage
In the overview the importance of the hydrogen storage strategy is emphasized. Depending on the net
amount available on a daily basis, the volume of hydrogen will be a very important factor. Through-
out the study it was assumed that the hydrogen was stored at low pressure, meaning that either salt
caverns or very large storage vessels would be necessary. With the information in section ?? on page
?? in mind, storing the hydrogen at relatively low pressures assures the least amount of energy loss
during conversion and storage. Studying how the storage pressure aﬀects the eﬃciency of the plant
and the costs, would serve as a good addition of knowledge to this study and would make the case for
a real-life pilot plant more attractive. The emerging technologies such as metal hydrides, also show
promise but are still in early stages of development and will take time to be commercially available
and economically viable. A recent study by Andersson J. and Gronkvist. S, investigates the potential
solutions for large-scale hydrogen storage, conﬁrming that if hydrogen is produced via electrolysis
and stored using low electricity prices, then the variable costs of high-density storage (such as liquid
hydrogen) may be more tolerable.
Apart from determining the correct seasonal storage to provide the plant with fuel the whole year, any
surplus amount of hydrogen could potentially be sold to industries that require them. For instance
economies that will turn to more hydrogen based vehicle propulsion, such as Japan and Australia [44],
can use a polygeneration plant to store large amount on site at lower pressures (so that electricity
production of the plant remains at low cost) and then if the transport sector requires hydrogen it can
either transport it via pressurized pipes to the ﬁlling location or pressurize it onto a transportable
container (on a truck/train). That the energy content of the hydrogen produced by the plant will only
be compromised when necessary.
3.4.4
Absorption chiller
The absorption chiller performed poorly in SOEC mode compared to fuel cell mode. After having
gone through several iterations of the design to capture as much waste heat as possible, it seemed
wise to place such a system in place. Due to time limitations there was not an opportunity to present
cases where either the AC unit was placed elsewhere in the system, or if it could be bypassed during
colder seasons and use the excess heat for district heating instead. Such a study, where comparing the
eﬀect of having the energy used for district heating vs. cooling, can enhance the analysis of the plant
both in terms of energy eﬃciency and cost eﬀectiveness. A thesis by Chaudhary S. [45] examines the
implications of running an absorption chiller and providing district heating, although this is using a
micro - combined heat and power - plant and not RES.

Chapter 4
Conclusion
The aim of this study was to design a polygeneration plant powered by photovoltaics, capable of
sustaining a coastal municipality with renewable energy, drinking water and cooling. This was to be
demonstrated by mapping the plant at two diﬀerent locations to see how the performance of such a
plant would diﬀer according to the amount of solar irradiation at the given locations.
The design of the polygeneration plant turned out to be successful in producing adequate amounts of
hydrogen and freshwater in both locations. However the cooling eﬀect of the absorption chiller was
not as high as expected. This is likely due to the fact that the heat recovered in electrolysis mode was
shared between the DCMD and the AC, causing the desorber to absorb less heat than it potentially
could. In contrast, the cooling eﬀect was very large in fuel cell mode. Given that a burner is needed
to burn the products of the fuel cell process, large amounts of heat are available to drive components
that use heat as an energy source. An important note regarding these outcomes though, is that the
dimensioning of both the desalination plant and chiller in fuel cell mode, will have to be large enough
to handle the extensive mass ﬂows required to utilize the heat.
Regarding the mathematical models of the various components, there are certain conditions that have
been assumed to simplify the process. Despite being validated with data from other studies, this limits
the accuracy of the results. Hence it is of interest to alter the input parameters within the code of
DNA (not just the user interface). This study focused on existing models available in DNA and could
therefore be beneﬁcial to change other input parameters of the components, so that progress in the
technology can be reﬂected in the output parameters of the model.
In SOFC mode the plant is able to produce a peak power output of 28.6 MW and 42 MW, for Denmark
and Cyprus respectively, during the summer season. The output is based on the amount of hydrogen
storage available before starting the fuel cell process, so these values are only indications of what the
output could be at a given mass ﬂow rate of fuel. The model was capable of producing up to 81.2 MW
of power at ideal conditions, indicating that with the right hydrogen and water storage management,
the plant is able to cover the electricity demand of the grid. The eﬃciency in SOFC mode ranges from
0.36 - 0.43 and the eﬃciency in SOEC mode ranges from 0.75 - 0.78, depending on the location and
season. These values fall in line with those of other studies and represent the use of fuel and power
well. Although it is important to note that the cooling eﬀect of the AC in fuel cell mode has a large
eﬀect on the overall eﬃciency, and therefore needs to be revised before taking it into account at face
value.
The study shows the technical feasibility of such a polygeneration system. The comprehensive outcome
is that such a plant will operate better under conditions available in southern European countries. This
does not mean that the possibility of building it in a northern country should be omitted, it should
instead be evaluated with other waste heat recovery methods, such as district heating, and a mix of
other renewable energy sources, such as wind turbines. An economic analysis of the cases presented
would improve the level of the study, as it can provide vital data when comparing the location and
size of the plant, as well as the cost of storing hydrogen at diﬀerent conditions.
