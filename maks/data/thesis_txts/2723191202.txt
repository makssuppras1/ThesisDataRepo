Abstract
In this thesis, we explore different CNN architectures for pothole detection and compare them
with some state-of-the-art solutions while also analyzing them in depth. The dataset used
for this study was collected from different sources and modified before training the models to
make it as robust as possible. Hyper parameters were fine-tuned in a systematic manner to
obtain the best performance. The main aim of this study was to create a high accuracy model
for pothole detection that can be useful in maintaining road safety.

Introduction
Surveillance cameras and their applications have been on the rise since the past decade[1].
Naturally, the more computer vision-based applications have gained a lot of popularity, espe-
cially for security, construction, surveillance etc. Such data from surveillance cameras contains
a lot of key information. For instance, it can play a vital role in detecting congestion, accidents
and other anomalies on the roads that need to be taken care of by the relevant authorities.
Potholes are a common road maintenance problem that can be caused by several factors[2].
This includes weather conditions (cold and heavy rainfall), heavy traffic, road age and deterio-
ration and lack of proper maintenance[2]. The combination of these factors can cause potholes
to form and grow, which can lead to hazardous driving conditions and damage to vehicles.
Anomalies on the road surface can be troublesome for a lot of reasons.
They can cause
accidents, stress and discomfort for drivers, cyclists and pedestrians. According to a news
article from Indiatimes, 5626 people died from road accidents caused by potholes in the period
from 2018- 2020[3]. While roads in India may not be comparable to Danish roads, it still
leaves a concern about the severity of the possible damages done by potholes if not properly
maintained.
As of 27th of January 2023, Danish broadcasting cooperation, DR, published an article re-
garding the increase of potholes found on roads in Denmark[4]. Because of the rainy and
cold weather, more potholes have appeared than normal and are a nuisance for many drivers
according to Jesper Vesterlyng, director in Gundsø Entreprenør Forretning[4].
It is therefore important that the respective municipal authorities are timely informed about
the road situation so they can act accordingly. A road condition/anomaly tracking system
can help governments plan and schedule road maintenance in a way that creates the least
disurbance on people’s daily life, while an anomaly warning system for drivers can increase
anomaly awareness, which may decrease the number of accidents, costs in car maintenance
and driver stress. Also, when planning a trip, information about roads’ condition can increase
comfort for both the driver and the passengers.
The process of detecting potholes in Denmark is currently done a few times a year by the
responsible municipality, where potholes are found by walking or driving on the roads[5]. They
are only responsible for fixing municipal roads. Citizens can also contact the municipality and
report anomalies on the road[5]. Both of these are manual work and are very time consuming
for the municipalities.
This thesis proposes an automatic pothole detection system that utilizes video data to verify
if the road contains a pothole or not. The problem can be seen as a classification task that
aims to classify a given image of a road as either a normal road or a road with potholes/man-
holes. To solve this, a convolutional neural network (CNN) model is created with the intent
of detecting potholes on roads. This would make it possible to automate the detection and
make the verification less time consuming.
Chapter 2 is a theoretical explanation of how neural network works, and it highlights the most
important theory related to them. Related work and the state-of-the-art of road anomalies
detection are stated and explained in Chapter 3. Chapter 4 highlights the data set used and
how it was created. It further elaborates on the preprocessing and the choices made for testing
and further improve the CNN model. The design of the system and how it could be applied
are shown in Chapter 5. It also includes an explanation of implementation choices. Chapter
6 holds the performances and results of various trained models which will be compared to

Introduction to Neural Network
This section aims to give an introduction to neural networks and explain all the theory behind
this project in detail. The section begins with an introduction to the first form of neural
network, known as the perceptron, and explains the idea and mathematics of how it works.
Afterwards, key aspects of a modern neural network are shown and explained, such as back-
propagation, activation functions etc. The section ends with an overview of the neural network
architecture used for solving the classification problem. This is done to give a general insight
and understanding of the theory use throughout this thesis.
2.1
The First Neural Network
In 1943, a paper[6] was published by neurophysiologist Warren S. McCulloch and logician
Walter Pitts who described a mathematical view of how a nervous system processes infor-
mation. This system is what connects the brain with the body and allows for signals to be
passed around between these. The nervous system is described as a net of neurons, which is
the origin of a neural networks[7].
With inspiration from their work, the first type of artificial neuron was created in the late
1950s and early 1960s by psychologist Frank Rosenblatt[8]. This type of neuron is called a
perceptron. An illustration of a perceptron is illustrated in Figure 1.

The currently most popular and recommended activation function to use in hidden layers
[10][9] is ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit). ReLU is also bounded to zero for negative values as
a sigmoid function, but conversely it is not bounded on values above 0. ReLU suffers from a
disadvantage when the input is a negative value since it will always return zero and can cause
gradient disappearance[11]. When this occurs, the ReLU neuron may "die" and only return
zero, which will harm the whole network because it is not suited to handle negative values.
Different variants of ReLU have been created to try and combat the issue of the original. The
common theme of these is to handle the dying ReLU problem by adjusting the equation to be
able to handle negative values. Parametric ReLU (PReLU) and LeakyReLU are variants of
ReLU that have shown promising results in other deep learning tasks[11]. Both of the forms
can be seen in Table 1. LeakyReLU adds a value to the formula that makes it possible to
return a small value for negative values. This value is commonly set to 0.01 [12] and gives the
curve a sloping look. PReLU is doing the same, but instead of having a fixed value, it utilizes
a learnable parameter that the model learns and changes.
Exponential Linear (ELU) and Swish are other types of functions that seem to produce good
results in different tasks[13][11][14]. They are both unbounded for positive values. ELU shares
similarities with LeakyReLU since it also follows a small slope, which returns a variety of values
for small negative values. Swish shares the same property and uses a learnable parameter to
adjust the curve.

The following sections 2.3, 2.4 and 2.5 summarizes the principles of two different papers’
explanations of loss functions, minimization and backpropagation[8][9]. To evaluate how well
a model (with given weights and bias) is performing when predicting labels, a cost function
J(θ)(where θ is equal to weight and bias) will be defined. The cost function outputs an error
of the model, which measures the performance. The error gives an indication of how well the
model can modelize the data. To optimize our model, an algorithm is needed to calculate the
best set of parameters (weights and bias). This is done to reduce the given cost function so
it can approximate match with the desired output of the classification. For this task, binary
cross-entropy is chosen as the cost function. This is an optimal choice regarding classification
problems with two classes. The cost function is given as:

2.4
The minimization problem
The optimization problem stated in Section 2.3 indicates a minimization problem where the
aim is to minimize the cost function from Eq. 9 concerning finding the optimal parameters θ
(being the weight and bias). This is done by taking "steps" in the function until a local (or
better, global) minimum is met. The step length is decided by the learning rate parameter,
η. A high value of this would make the algorithm take large steps, while a low value makes
it take smaller steps. This would increase the time before convergence, but would ensure that
the global minimum is not skipped. The global minimum is the absolute lowest point in the
gradient, while a local minimum is the lowest compared to its neighbor points.
The first algorithm that is considered is stochastic gradient descent (SGD), which performs a
parameter update for each training example[15]. The equation is given as

hit. This method helps to lower error rates and increase the generalization of the model by

Convolutional neural network
CNN is a type of network architecture that is specified and widely used in image classification
and recognition[8][16]. It is a type of FFNN where the information flows in one direction, but
it utilizes different types of layers that work better with images.
Figure 3: Illustrates an CNN with one convolution layer, one pooling layer, one flatten layer
and then two fully connected layers where the last one is the output layer. The first square is
the input image, and the small boxes are the kernels.
Table 3 shows the flow of a small CNN network. Features is extracted from the original image
by the convolutional layer with 3 filters. A kernel is used to fetch the features. A max pool
filter is then applied which takes the highest value inside the kernel and sends it the next
layer. Then a flatten layer is used to make the output one-dimensional so it can be used by a
fully connected layer which is the same ones used in regular FFNN. These are then connected
to the output layers. The rest of this section will give an detailed description of some of the
most important points related to CNN.

one neuron in the hidden layer as in Figure 4. The selected region that the hidden neuron
learns is known as the local receptive field[8].

When working with deep learning models, it is good practice to have a high multitude of

4.1.1.1
Guidelines
Before starting the initial data gathering, some guidelines have been set with the super- visors.
This was in order to collect quality data relevant to the use case while also making it possible
for others to add on to the dataset with similar quality data. The guidelines are:
1. Images should be taken in daylight.
Images should be taken during the daytime to get a clear image of the road/pothole. It
does not matter whether it is sunny or cloudy as long as the road/pothole is visible.
2. Images should be focused on showing paved roads.
The main focus on the images should be the road or pothole which is recognizable for
a person. This means that when a person looks at it, they should know that the image
is of the road and not a tree or a house. A minimum ratio of road to background is set
to be 70%. Images can be cropped to fit this requirement, for example by removing the
sky or houses if these are visible.
3. Paved roads should be made of asphalt or concrete
This rule essentially means that the type of road in question matches the road pavements’
structure in Denmark. This would mean roads on dirt, brick or other materials is not

4. Large enough cracks are considered as potholes.
Because of the difficulty of finding fitting potholes, cracks of significant size are catego-

bigger classification task where cracks and potholes are in different categories. No rules of the

4.1.2
Identifying Class Imbalance
Class imbalance refers to a situation in which the number of samples in one class is significantly
different from the number of samples in another class. To identify class imbalance in an image
dataset, one can simply count the number of samples in each class and compare the counts.
An imbalanced dataset in a CNN could result in poor performance for the class with less
representation. This is a problem because the training or validation set could contain more
images of one class and be trained to get a bias towards that class. If the dataset contained a
majority of pothole images (80%), the model would most likely be more prone to predict that
the image contains a pothole. This could mean that the model predicts that every image has
a pothole because it has only learned a selected set of features. This is something that should
be avoided as it can have negative effects on the model’s performance.

4.3
Batch training
When training the model, a batch size is selected, which determines how many images are
used per training per iteration. Common strategies are to either use the whole dataset, one
image or a user defined size between these two values. When using a smaller batch size, there
is a risk that the model accuracy becomes worse because it learns less from the images. By
using a high batch size, it may increase the training time because more memory is required,
but it could also decrease the time because it would loop through less times because it takes
more images at a time.
A paper [40] tries to use different batch size to test the effects on a large dataset. The results
show that the optimal batch size for their model lays around around 64 to 256. It shows that
a smaller batch size is worse, which may be due to the fact that the gradient is less accurate
because of fewer samples. Since their dataset is a lot larger than ours, we would try with a
smaller batch size in our reference CNN and see the effects of increasing and decreasing it.
Different batch sizes are tested, including 32, 64 and 128. The batch size of 64 will be used
in our reference since this is the middle value of the 3 chosen values. In the cases where the
batch size does not align with the dataset size to distribute the images equally, the last batch
will contain the remaining images.
4.4
Activation Function
For the choice of the activation function in the hidden layer, we have chosen to use ReLU
for the reference CNN. While ReLU has some drawbacks, it is still used by many as the
go to default activation function[11], and it still shows competitive results compared to other
functions as seen in different papers in Section 3. ReLU is also the choice of activation function
for the hidden layers for many YOLO variants seen in Section 3. The other activation function
mentioned will be tested to see if they can perform better than ReLU for our task.
For the final layer, we use the sigmoid function instead of ReLU because we want a bounded
output of two values only. As stated in Section 3.1, the sigmoid function produces a bounded
result of 0 or 1, which is ideal for our classification task of two classes where they can correspond
to the output numbers.
4.5
Optimizer & Learning Rate
As mentioned in Section 2.4, the learning rate is an important hyperparameter since it directly
dictates how well the model learns. By using a high value, it could end up in an inadequate
local minimum and worsen the learning process, and with a low learning rate it could take a
long time to reach a good local minimum. For the testing, we will experiment with different
learning rates starting with a middle value of 0.0005 and then increasing or decreasing by a
factor of 10. By doing this, the effects because of the the learning rate can be observed and
adjust to the right direction. We will keep lowering/increasing by this factor until it does
not improve the learning of the model. From here, we will adjust with smaller values to find
the most optimal learning rate over a range of 100 epochs for the given optimizer. Epoch is
a number for how many iterations the model will be training for of the entire dataset. The
reason for optimizing with 100 epochs is to have a starting point for further testing of other
hyperparameters. Adam, as mentioned in section 2.4 will be used as the main optimizer, while
SDG will also be tested to see which of these yields the best results.

6.2
Learning Rate and Optimizer
The learning rate is one of the most important parameters that can have a heavy effect on
the model performance. As mentioned in subsection 2.4 the learning rate dictates the jump
that the optimization function makes to find a good local minimum or preferably the global
minimum. The speed of convergence is also decided by this value which is why its necessary
to find an optimal one.
Since this is an important parameter which may need to be changed several times during
testing, it is decided to find the most optimal value for 100 epochs. The optimal value is one
where the model starts to converge right before the 100 epochs mark. The chosen value will
be used throughout the rest of the testing process. This is done to give each model the same
starting point and see how they function with the same optimizer and learning rate.
Adam
Adam is the selected initial optimizer to be used. The previous results have shown a large
amount of overfitting which may be because of a poorly chosen learning rate which makes it
converge to a bad local minimum. By increasing the learning rate to 0.0005 we observed no
positive improvements on the results. However, lowering the learning rate showed improve-
ments against the overfitting with a significant amount. This can be observed in subfigures
33c and 33d which shows the losses being close together.
The value of 0.000008 is selected for the three chosen models.

Major Learnings

YOLO is one of the most popular models used for pothole detection which our model outper-
forms when comparing with the papers in Table 3. Anyways, it would be really interesting
to see how YOLO can perform when trained with our data which is why it will be done in
the future and compared with HACNN. As seen in section 3, YOLO performs both object
detection and classification in a single forward pass, making it faster and more efficient than
other models. This is because YOLO divides the input image into a grid and applies a single
neural network to the entire image, which simultaneously predicts the bounding boxes and
the class probabilities for each box in one go.
For our study of pothole detection, YOLO can be beneficial because potholes can be relatively
small in size and can occur anywhere in the image. Additionally, YOLO can be trained on
relatively small datasets, which is important when dealing with rare or unique scenarios such
as pothole detection on roads.
6.8
Major Learnings
It is clear that choosing three different model depth is a good initial step to do to get an idea
of the most optimal depth to solve the given classification task. By using two different types
of optimizers, we got an insight into how they worked and what it took to make them work
well. Adam seemed like a good choice because of the automatic parameter update and good
results with no overfitting. SGD were tried with both learning rate decay and momentum
(which helps moving the gradients in the right direction) but became too complicated tuning
for 3 parameters to get optimal results. Changing the batch size had minimal affect when
compared to the effects of other hyperparamters. Further tests were done with HACNN with
all three batch sizes which yielded similar results for different activation functions.
Using a higher image size caused overfitting for both the LeNetHA and VGGHA. It seemed
that the two models were not optimized to work well with a bigger image size. HACNN did
output promising results, but because of the limitation on hardware it was not trained further
with.
Trying different activation functions showed us that both ReLU and PReLU were the best
performers for all model architectures.

Future Work

7
Future Work
The camera that is going to be used to gather video data of the road should be augmented on
the busses in such way they film the entire road without including the unwanted information
like the sky or buildings. This should be an upfront view so the camera can get as much width
of the road as possible. Filming with the camera pointing with high angles downwards could
result in narrowed down videos. This could result in missing filming potholes to the far sides
of the road because of the narrowed vision. Having a standardized position for filming would
also make it easier to cut the video on specific coordinates to cut out unnecessary details
such as the sky, payment, bike lane etc. We would also recommend deploying and train the
model with the help of cloud computing. Data storage should also be done on the cloud itself.
Making a server room to hold the large amount of video data and training a neural network
can be both expensive and require space and maintenance. By using a cloud service all of
these concerns are on the service provider. The providers often offer pay-as-you-go solutions
and will help with scaling and other problems that may occur.
To verify the model’s accuracy, we recommend that the predictions are looked by a worker
to confirm whether or not the predictions are accurate. All the cases where the model have
predicted a positive on if the image contains a pothole and a couple of random selected images
that have been predicted as a clean road should also be examined so that the model can be
improved for the future with such feedback. The worker should also report the potholes to the
appropriate authorities so the potholes can be dealt with. The images would then be saved,
accordingly to the workers examination, and be used to improve the dataset and used for the
next training iteration of the model. This way Frederiksberg municipality ensures that the
predictions are right and gather free variable data that otherwise could be costly and time
consuming to acquire.
The solution could further be extended to be used on bike lanes which are also paved. It is not
hard to imagine the potential damage that could be done to a bike and its rider when colliding
with a pothole compared to a car. The bikes are potential in more danger since smaller holes
can effect the wheel. Our dataset also contains smaller pothole images which is a great start
to further improve the detection for all size holes.
7.1
GDPR
The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is a comprehensive data protection law
that applies to all organizations that process personal data of EU residents. Since the pothole
detection project involves collecting, storing, and processing personal data, such as images
of individuals or license plates, we need to be aware of GDPR compliance involving storing
and processing of data that can be categorized as personal data. By Art 4.1[49] the term
‘personal data’ is referring as information that can be used to identify a person. This includes
the person’s name, images of the person, an identification number etc. When taking videos
of the road there is a potential of getting people’s faces or number plates in the frame, which
could lead to legal actions if not dealt with. To ensure compliance with GPDR is to ensure
that these potential issues are either blurred or cut from the videos/images. This can either
be done manually or by acquiring services from a third party.

Conclusion
In conclusion, this thesis has demonstrated that the use of a CNN or similar architectures is
a promising approach for detecting potholes on roads. Along with model building, the thesis
also covers other important factors such as accurate data collection techniques, data analysis
prior to model building etc. as well as final model validation. By investigating and testing
different CNN architectures, we were able to create a model with high accuracy as seen in
the results Section 6, that can reliably detect potholes in images and videos. The process of
manual data collection and data modification played a crucial role in improving the accuracy
and performance of the model.
Additionally, finding the best hyperparameters and comparing our model to other state-of-
the-art solutions, we can confidently say that our model is competitive and can perform as
well or better than existing methods in certain conditions. Selecting the best parameters for
the model is of course, the most crucial step in the analysis section and long hours were spent
just to settle down with the final ones.
All of the research questions posed are answered in detail throughout the report including the
most important one, which was to achieve an accuracy of more than 80%.
Overall, this study has important implications for road safety, as the detection of potholes
can enable quick repairs of damaged roads. The models developed in this study can be used
to develop real-time pothole detection systems that can be installed on vehicles, enabling
more effective and efficient monitoring of road conditions. Further, this study demonstrates
the effectiveness of CNN architectures for pothole detection and highlights the potential for
further research in this area.
