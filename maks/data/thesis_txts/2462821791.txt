Abstract  
The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) agreed by the global community in 2015, consist of 
17 goals with 169 targets that aim to obtain peace and prosperity for all people as well as respect 
and preservation of the planet by 2030. In order to attain the Agenda 2030 and a better world, all 
governments, civil-sector players and businesses are urged to deliver innovative and sustainable 
solutions, which they can achieve by launching and implementing different projects that 
contribute in addressing SDGs.  
the main purpose of this master thesis is to show how we can achieve a better understanding of 
the different types of projects that can be contributed to any industry categories and to what 
extent they lead any industry toward addressing any SDG.  
To achieve this purpose, 82 reports of projects across different business sectors were investigated 
through an empirical survey research methodology. 65 of those were done and completed in 
Danish companies.  
Since Denmark is recognized as a top rank country in terms of the SDGs in 2019, only Danish 
business sectors were included in analysis of the results of this study.  
In this study, the investigated projects were categorized according to the “Global system for 
categorizing projects”, and the relevant business sectors were classified in different industry 
categories based on” International Standard Industrial Classification (ISIC)” classification. 
The results in this study show – perhaps not unsurprisingly- that the different organizations or 
business sectors do a variety of projects and activities. It appears that all business sectors can 
contribute to achieving SDGs through their core projects and activities, and thus all industries do 
contribute to the implementation of Agenda 2030 and the impact of different industry categories 
on SDGs can also be considered.  
Furthermore, it is concluded that some industry categories contribute much more than others in 
attaining the SDGs, depending on the type of projects they have embarked on.  For instance, in 
the Construction industry, 37% of the investigated industries with a variety of projects in 
different business sectors, managed to address almost all SDGs.  
On the other hand, some SDGs such as Goal 8 and Goal 9 have been addressed by multiple 
==PAGE:5==
 
 
Page 5 of 120 
 
industry categories. It shows that, most of the industry categories contribute to creating job 
opportunities for people and strive to achieve higher levels of economic productivity through 
their projects, technological upgrading and innovation. 
Although in 2019 Denmark has recognized as the best performing country in Agenda 
2030, but some challenges have remained and they should be considered more in different 
industries to solve.

1 Introduction 
This section presents the background and the problem statement together with the research 
questions which define the purpose of this Master Thesis.  
 
1.1  Background 
Although working towards achieving sustainable development goals (SDGs) and a more 
sustainable world is improving these years, many challenges yet remain and will also do so by 
2030. The challenges such as competition over resources, corruption, lack of environmental and 
social justice are and allegedly still will be major issues worldwide. But pursuing the Global 
Goals should lead the world to face these challenges better (Business and Sustainable 
Development Commission , 2017, p. 24).  
Business sectors have been recognized as necessary global partners to achieve a sustainable 
development. In order to connect business strategies with those global priorities, the SDGs as a 
framework can help companies to shape and steer their strategies, goals and activities. 
In this respect, factors affecting sustainable development topics such as climate changes, 
poverty, health and education, all relevant to all companies, should be covered by SDGs. 
Therefore, it should be considered to collaborate with businesses and help them apply their 
creativity and innovation in order to solve sustainable development challenges, thus minimizing 
negative impacts and maximizing positive impacts on people and the planet (SDG Compass, The 
guide for business action on the SDGs, 2015).  
SDGs are a global agenda and they urge all governments, players in the civil sectors and 
companies to join forces to create and to implement on-scale sustainable solutions. In order to 
contribute tangibly to the results of sustainable development, the Danish government is also 
firmly committed to using SDGs as a platform for their development initiatives in developing 
countries. In this commitment, Denmark draws on its wide societal experience, and the 
government is actively seeking to participate and cooperate with private Danish actors who can 
contribute their expertise, skills and technology to solve the challenges in the sustainability 
(Dalberg, 2019).  
==PAGE:12==
 
 
Page 12 of 120 
 
 
Since the business sectors, among other actors in different industry categories, play such an 
important role in contributing to SDGs, this master thesis focuses on studying their role in 
Denmark as the best performing country in addressing SDGs. 
 
1.2  Problem statement and research questions 
According to the definition of CSR, organizations are responsible for the impact of their 
decisions and activities on society and the environment, through a transparent and ethical 
behavior. 
By increasing the expectations on corporate responsibility, sustainability is becoming more 
important as a business approach for all companies to create long-term value and to foster 
company longevity. In this regard companies should  take into consideration their activities in 
the ecological, social and economic fields. 
SDGs are an ambitious vision by 2030, but it is also a vision that offer significant growth for 
responsible businesses through solving the world’s biggest problems. On one hand, businesses 
can implement the Global Goals as part of a compelling growth strategy for themselves, on the 
other hand the Global Goals need the businesses. Therefore, this vision is chosen by an 
increasing number of businesses as a roadmap to growth, and so reaching the Global Goals can 
be a powerful incentive for companies, governments and other stakeholders to plan by and invest 
accordingly .Thus, the Global Sustainable Development Goals have already been created as a 
priority by an increasing amount of businesses on their strategic agenda (Business and 
Sustainable Development Commission , 2017).  
Although the social and environmental dimensions of sustainability can be challenging to 
incorporate in programs and projects as vehicles to execute an organization’s strategies 
(Sánchez, 2014), according to Ban Ki-moon, United Nations former Secretary-General, 
businesses can contribute through their core activities to achieve the SDGs (SDG Compass, The 
guide for business action on the SDGs, 2015), therefore, it is interesting to see to what extent the 
SDGs are affected by the projects that are being implemented in different industry categories.  
==PAGE:13==
 
 
Page 13 of 120 
 
Deliverables of a project depending on the business sector and the type of project have social, 
economic and environmental impacts that far outlast the projects themselves (Sánchez, 2014). 
Therefore, as the SDGs are important and my personal interest in this topic, the main purpose of 
this master thesis is to show how we can achieve a better understanding of the different types of 
projects that can be contributed to any industry categories and to what extent they lead any 
industry toward addressing any SDG. Since Denmark has achieved the top rank in terms of 
SDGs in 2019, therefore the role of different Danish industries in their success is analyzed. 
 
This master thesis aims to respond the following questions: 
1. What type of projects contribute to the development of industries? 
2. To what extent does any industry category contribute to address the SDGs by 
implementing their projects?

2 Methodology 
This section presents the methodology adopted to address the purpose of research as well as an 
overview of the steps taken to respond to the research questions. the limitations of this study are 
presented at the end of this section. 
 
2.1  Research Methodology 
This master thesis adopts a research approach using an empirical research survey methodology in 
the industries. Required information to respond to the survey questions has been gathered from 
existing reports provided by students in the project management course from 2016 to 2018 at 
Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The accessibility to the survey and relevant reports for 
the author of this master thesis has been made by Christian Thuesen, the professor of this course.  
 
2.1.1 Survey Design 
Qualtrics Survey Software has been used in this survey to gather the responses from the students 
who had investigated the projects in terms of project management concept. The design of the 
survey has been based on the taught content in one of the courses at DTU called project 
management. It has been organized based on a combination of quantitative and qualitative 
questions in which most of them are qualitative questions and a modified Likert scale has been 
used for the answers. 
The questions have been divided among six thematic blocks: 
1. Background information: This block looks into the basic information of the group 
of students who have done the project for this course. 
2. Case information: This block gathers data on the business sector, its industry 
categorization, the investigated project in this company and the type of the 
project. 
3. Method and empirical material: This block deals with the method and materials 
that students have used to conduct their research in the relevant project. 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
Page 15 of 120 
 
4. Findings (SDGs): This block attempts to gather information about the projects’ 
impact on the different sustainable development goals. 
5. Findings (Success): This block gathers data on the project success from a project 
manager’s perspective and in terms of project management criteria. 
6. Findings (Practices): This block attempts to gather information about the 
challenges and the success that project managers have faced in managing the 
project in terms of project management perspectives such as purpose, people, 
complexity and uncertainty.  
The survey can be found in the Appendix (Appendix A). 
 
2.1.2 Data Collection 
The original survey was designed to gather the responses given by the students who investigated 
the projects in terms of the project management concepts including Purpose perspective, 
people perspective, complexity perspective, uncertainty perspective and connecting the dots, 
but for this study, the author of this master thesis has read their project reports in order to collect 
required data in the survey. 
For this purpose, I have read 82 student project reports from different business sectors.  79% (65 
reports) of these are related to companies in Denmark. The rest of 21% (17 reports) of the 
investigated projects have been done in other countries such as South Africa, Brussels, Belgium, 
Chile, China, Germany, Norway, Finland, Hong Kong, Palestine, Spain, Sweden, The United 
Arab Emirates, The United Kingdom. 
Since sustainability and sustainable development goals are the most important and the most 
interesting topic to investigate for these days, the acquired data from Findings (SDGs) block 
were considered as the main focus of this master thesis. After figuring out the main focus of this 
study and according to the formulated problem statement and research question, a data set 
consists of industry categorization, type of projects and their contribution to the SDGs have been 
extracted from the main survey. 
In this study, in order to gather data in terms of SDGs, the purpose perspective of each 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
Page 16 of 120 
 
project has been investigated. From investigating through the purpose of forming each 
project, we can identify how this project has impact on the SDGs. 
In this survey, five different effects can be considered for each SDGs: 
• Direct positive effect: The SDGs which have been addressed directly in the 
purpose of the project and this project has positive impact on this SDG. 
• Indirect positive effect: The SDGs which have not been addressed directly in the 
purpose of the project, but from investigating the project purpose it is identified 
that, this project has positive impact on SDGs.  
• Neutral effect: The SDGs which neither have identified in the project purpose nor 
can be addressed by projects indirectly, therefore these SDGs considered as 
neutral. 
• Indirectly negative effect: The SDGs which have not been addressed directly in the 
purpose of the project, but from investigating the project purpose it is identified 
that, this project has negative impact on SDGs. 
• Direct negative effect: The SDGs which have been addressed directly in the 
purpose of the project with negative impact on the SDGs. In this study, there are 
not any project with this type of impact.  
For instance, constructing a power plant with the purpose of recycling waste to provide 
electricity and heating as well as reducing the CO2 production has positive directly 
impact on SDGs 7 and 13. Furthermore, although it has not mentioned anything about 
providing job opportunity for people in the project purpose but this project is an 
opportunity for people job and therefore has positive indirect impact on SDG 8.  
This simple example can explain how to consider the different impacts of the projects on 
SDG.

This section constitutes a review of the literature to make a foundation of knowledge associated

3.1 Sustainability 
Sustainability and sustainable development are the most important challenges of our time. 
In order to clearly address the concept of sustainability and understand what Sustainable 
Development means and how sustainability emerged as a global concern, we have to go back to 
the 18th century in which the Saxon Mining Officer Hans Carl von Carlowitz was concerned 
about out of the timber shortfall for the Saxon mines so that in his first comprehensive treatise 
about sustainable yield forestry (Sylvicultura oeconomica), he suggested to log only as many 
trees as grow back and used the term “sustainable” in this context (Sustainability teories, 2019). 
In 1972, a book called “The Limits to Growth”  (Meadows, Meadows, Randers, & Behrens , 
1972) was published in which simulating the consequences of mankind's use of earth's natural 
resources has been done by the authors. Although they have concluded that with the continued 
growth of  the world's population and economy at their current exponential speed, uses of the 
planet's natural resources would overshoot their capacity but economists have rejected it ( Meyer 
& Nørgård, 2010) so that it caused a public debate leading to formation of the UN World 
Commission on Development and Environment, called  the Brundtland Commission. 
Therefore, a widely accepted definition of sustainability and Sustainable development, as defined 
by the Brundtland Commission’s Report ‘Our Common Future’, is the development that meets 
the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own 
needs (World Commission on Environment and Development, 1987). This report, by mentioning 
“sustainable development strategy, in its broadest sense, aims at promoting harmony between 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
Page 22 of 120 
 
humanity and nature”, it points that sustainability next to the economic perspective, requires also 
a social and an environmental perspective on development and performance (Silvius, Kampinga, 
Paniagua, & Mooi, 2017). In the other words, according to (Keating, 1993), there are widely 
accepted opinion in which all of the development goals of economic prosperity, social well-being 
and prudent use of natural resources, cannot be reached without considering and effecting the 
other two.  
In this respect, (Elkington, 1997) in his book named “Cannibals with Forks” says: The Triple 
Bottom Line of 21st Century Business” identifies a broader concept of sustainable development 
based on the integration of three dimensions: economics, environment and society as the ‘triple 
bottom line’ or ‘Triple-P (People, Planet, Profit)’ concept.  
(Silvius & Schipper, Sustainability in project management:A literature review and impact 
analysis, 2014), in their literature review, found that 86% of the publications address 
sustainability in terms of the Triple Bottom Line concept. Therefore, this concept extracts the 
essence of sustainability ( Savitz & Weber, 2006) which is about the balance or harmony 
between economic sustainability, social sustainability and environmental sustainability (Silvius 
& Schipper, Sustainability in project management:A literature review and impact analysis, 
2014).  
According to Brundtland definition, in order to achieve “the ability of future generations to meet 
their needs”, a balanced orientation on both short and long term is required (Silvius, Kampinga, 
Paniagua, & Mooi, 2017). According to (Gilbert, Stevenson, Girardet, & Stren , 1996), 
sustainability implies that “the natural capital should remain intact which means the source and 
sink functions of the environment should not be degraded. Therefore, the extraction of renewable 
resources should not exceed the rate at which they are renewed, and the absorptive capacity of 
the environment to assimilate waste, should not be exceeded” (Silvius, Kampinga, Paniagua, & 
Mooi, 2017). As so, (Dyllick & Hockerts, 2002) conclude that sustainability is about consuming 
the income and not the capital. 
In 2015, all United Nations Member States adopted the 2030 agenda for Sustainable 
Development, named "The Sustainable Development Goals" which  provided a shared blueprint 
for peace and prosperity for people and the planet, to achieve a better and more sustainable 
future for all (The United Nation, 2015). 
The above definitions and concepts discuss sustainability, or sustainable development in general.

However, in order to achieve the goal of this study, our discussion should be continued about 
sustainability within the SDGs, businesses and projects. 
 
3.1.1 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) 
In 2000, a list of eight goals called Millennium Development Goals (MDGs), was prepared by 
the leaders of 189 countries who agreed on a vision in order to tackle extreme poverty in all of its 
forms in 15 years. 
As a result: 
• 
More than 1 billion people have been lifted out of extreme poverty (since 1990). 
• 
Child mortality dropped by more than half (since 1990). 
• 
The number of out of school children has dropped by more than half (since 1990). 
• 
HIV/AIDS infections fell by almost 40 percent (since 2000).  
Valuable lessons and experiences achieved by the MDGs encouraged leaders to start 
working on a set of new goals on sustainable development including new issues such as 
climate changes, economic inequality, innovation, sustainable consumption, peace and 
justice, among other priorities. Therefore, in 2012, the Sustainable Development Goals 
(SDGs) were born in the United Nations Conference on Sustainable Development in Rio 
de Janeiro and in September 2015 a set of new universal goals were agreed in order to 
help to finish the work, leaders started in 2000 to tackle some of the most serious 
challenges facing the world today (United Nations Development Programme, 2019). The 
SDGs are an urgent call for worldwide action to end poverty, protect the planet and ensure 
that all people enjoy peace and prosperity as well as shifting the world onto a more 
sustainable path (Thuesen & Opoku, 2018). 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
Page 24 of 120 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
These 17 sustainable development goals with their 169 targets are providing a more 
sustainable, prosperous and safer planet for all humanity while ensuring that no one is 
left behind by 2030. This is the greatest chance in order to improve life for future 
generations (United Nations Development Programme, 2019). 
In order to achieve “global partnership for sustainable development”, 17 SDGs explicitly 
recognizes business as essential as they are taking these Goals as serious signals of future 
policy and market direction, so that these Global Goals for Sustainable Development 
have been already made as the priority on the strategic agenda of a growing number of 
companies (Business and Sustainable Development Commission , 2017). 
Today, according to (Global Compact, 2018), the Goals are adopted by all 193 UN 
member states and explicitly addressed by more than 9,900 companies in 170 countries, 
both development and developing, representing nearly every sector and size (Thuesen & 
Opoku, 2018). 
 
3.1.2 Sustainability in Business 
The general definitions on sustainable management of organizations which have been elaborated 
by The International Institute for Sustainable Development, focus on: “Adopting business 
strategies and activities that meet the needs of the enterprise and its stakeholders today while 
protecting, sustaining and enhancing the human and natural resources that will be needed in the

==PAGE:25==

future” (Deloitte & Touche, 1992). In this regard, (Silvius, Kampinga, Paniagua, & Mooi, 2017)  
reflect on the statement  “…needs of the enterprise and its stakeholders today…”, and believe 
that the authors seem to make the point that without profitability on the short term, care for the 
environment and humanity cannot be sustained on the longer term.  
The Dow Jones Sustainability Indexes define ‘corporate sustainability’ (CS) as “a business 
approach that creates long-term shareholder value by embracing opportunities and managing 
risks derived from economic, environmental and social developments” (Dow Jones 
Sustainability Indexes , 2009) .  Furthermore, ( Baumgartner & Ebner, 2010) demonstrate the 
relation between concept of Corporate Sustainability and actions related to the business 
environment which means sustainable development while combining by the organization is 
called Corporate Sustainability  in which the same as sustainable development,  it contains all 
three pillars or dimensions of sustainability : economy, ecology and society. 
Obviously, regarding to definition of Corporate Sustainability, on one hand, The Dow Jones 
definition, mentions the interests of shareholders (Dow Jones Sustainability Indexes , 2009) 
and/or stakeholders (Deloitte & Touche, 1992) that according to developed notion of 
‘stakeholder theory’ by (Freeman, , 1984) have the right and legitimacy to receive sufficient 
management consideration that takes into account their interests (Julian, Ofori-Dankwa, & Justis, 
2008). Therefore, a win-win situation should be sought by the organization with embracing all 
stakeholders’ interests (Eskerod & Huemann, 2013). On the other hand,  Corporate Sustainability 
definition also mentions opportunities and managing risks (Dow Jones Sustainability Indexes , 
2009) which motivate sustainability ( (UNEP Finance Initiative , 2006); ( Godfrey, Merrill , & 
Hansen, 2009); ( Yilmaz & Flouris, 2010); (Silvius, Kampinga, Paniagua, & Mooi, 2017)). 
Another concept in the context of business which is related to Sustainable Development is called 
“Corporate Social Responsibility” (CSR) (Ebner & Baumgartner , 2006), defined by the 
International Organization for Standardization (ISO) 26,000 as: 
 “Responsibility of an organization for the impacts of its decisions and activities on society and 
the environment, through transparent and ethical behavior that:  
• Contributes to sustainable development, including health and the welfare of society; 
• Takes into account the expectations of stakeholders;  
• Is in compliance with applicable law and consistent with international norms of 
behavior; and   
==PAGE:26==
 
 
Page 26 of 120 
 
• Is integrated throughout the organization and practiced in its relationships 
(International Organization for Standardization, 2010) ”. 
Thus, according to this definition of a CSR concept, each organization or company is responsible 
for the impact of its decisions and actions, the transparency and ethicality of its behavior on the 
society and environment.  
In this respect, regards to normative and ethical aspect, values and ethical considerations of 
society can be reflected by sustainability as a value-based concept ( (Robinson, 2004); (Martens, 
2006))and according to (Silvius, Kampinga, Paniagua, & Mooi, 2017) integration of 
sustainability into business decisions and actions should go beyond being compliant with legal 
obligations. 
On one hand, the statement, “…Takes into account the expectations of stakeholders”, in the ISO 
26000 definition, implies that in order to meet the stakeholders’ expectation, a business is 
responsible for its decisions and impacts of activities, and on the other hand the statement “… is 
integrated throughout the organization and practiced in its relationships” shows that a business is 
also responsible for the environmental and social impacts of its partners actions in the entire 
value chain. 
Organizations influence their international stakeholders such as competitors, suppliers or 
customers or they are influenced by them. The geographical area that organizations influence is 
affected by the increasing globalization of economies (Silvius, Kampinga, Paniagua, & Mooi, 
2017). Therefore, the behavior and actions of organizations have an effect on economic, social 
and environmental aspects, both locally and globally (Gareis, Huemann , Martinuzzi , Sedlacko , 
& Weninger, 2011). 
 
3.1.3 The reason that all businesses should embrace sustainability 
By increasing expectations on corporate responsibility, sustainability is becoming more 
important as a business approach for all companies to create long-term value and to foster 
company longevity by taking into consideration how a given organization operates in the 
ecological, social and economic environment (Haanaes , 2016). 
McKinsey (the consulting company) in their sixth survey on executives in 2011, asked why and 
how their companies were addressing sustainability and to what extent executives believed it

wanted to make a positive contribution to their companies’ short- and long-term value by

can help companies to shape, steer their strategies, goals and activities by covering a 
wide spectrum of sustainable development topics relevant to companies such as climate 
changes, poverty, health and education. 
Furthermore, the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) as a definition of worldwide 
sustainable development priorities and ambitions for 2030, call for universal action 
among governments, businesses and civil societies and seek to mobilize global efforts 
around a common set of goals and targets to end poverty and create an honorable life and 
an opportunity for all, within the boundaries of the planet. 
Therefore, in order to achieve global agenda for the development of our societies and 
SDGs success, leading companies to demonstrate how their business helps to advance 
sustainable development as well as an action and collaboration by business to apply their 
creativity and innovation in order to solve sustainable development challenges and

3.2 Role of projects in business sectors 
Projects as a vital form of organizing deliberate changes, are considered everywhere in 
our society, not only as solutions to technical problems but also, they have become 
necessary to our lives in which in addition to impressive economic impact, each of the 
United Nations’ 17 global sustainable development goals are considered and can be 
reached through a portfolio of projects taking place globally and locally (Geraldi, 
Thuesen, Oehmen, & Stingl, 2017). 
      “Projects in the future will no longer be just operational tools for executing strategy—they        
     will become the engines that drive strategy into new directions (Shenhar, Dvir, Levy , & 
Maltz, 2001)”. 
 Furthermore, according to (Jensen, Thuesen, & Geraldi, 2016) projects are essential as a 
key vehicle to economic and social action in any business sector in many industries and 
the industrialized society has become a ‘project society’ (Geraldi, Thuesen, Oehmen, & 
Stingl, 2017). 
Projects are regarded as temporary organizations in this context ( ( Lundin & Söderholm, 
1995); ( Turnera & Müller, 2003)), most often across organizational structures and borders, 
directed at achieving a defined deliverable or outcome, logically or preferably connected 
to the strategy or goals of the organization, with assigned budget and resources (Silvius 
G. , Schipper, Planko, Van den Brink, & Kohler, 2012). Project management is "the 
means of planning, managing and controlling the resources allocated to the temporary 
organizations to bring about the beneficial and useful change" (Turner, 2014, p. 29). 
In projects, the success is meeting the primary goals and objectives and other benefits 
planned for the organization as a whole, while in project management, success is mainly 
related  to the direct activities of the project manager’s success, applying relevant tools 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
Page 31 of 120 
 
and methods, and also  it is in regard to the triple constraints of any project, including 
scope, time and cost ( Wit , 1988). 
Therefore, all criteria that deal with the effects of the project’s final product or service 
which help executives measure the effectiveness at obtaining customer satisfaction are 
considered as project success criteria. 
In this regard, in addition to achieving project goals and value, meeting all end-users and 
clients’ requirements as well as stakeholders' satisfaction are essential toward project 
success. It means that even if a project is about to be known as a failure in terms of 
project management (unpredicted delays, overworked resources, unprofitable project for 
the organization, and so on), some clients might be entirely satisfied with the result of 
that ( The Beeye Team, 2018). 
(Silvius G. , Schipper, Planko, Van den Brink, & Kohler, 2012) identifies failed and 
unsuccessful projects as waste and concludes that businesses should learn from their 
projects so as not to waste energy, resources and equipment due to their project mistakes 
and errors. 
All in all, projects with a business perspective in mind in order to succeed need to have 
goals and project managers as strategic leaders must take on responsibility in order to 
conduct them toward better results and organizational performance, more proﬁts, 
additional growth, and improved market position (Shenhar, Dvir, Levy , & Maltz, 2001). 
 
3.2.1 Different types of projects 
Most organizations acknowledge that the projects they finance and implement fall into 
distinct categories, but project management discipline has not fully acknowledged that 
these distinct kinds of projects often display different life cycle models and involve 
distinct governance techniques: prioritization, authorization, planning, execution and 
control. Obviously, it is true that the fundamental principles generally apply to all kinds 
of projects, and there are also many variations in how these principles and methods are 
best applied to different categories of projects.

3.3 The Role of business sectors and their projects in 
achieving SDGs 
Business organizations are researching and investing heavily in technology, hoping to 
achieve competitive advantages in today’s dynamic markets. As the third pillar of society 
(Wartick, Wood, & Czinkota, 1998) , they should perform significant changes and new practices 
including the use of projects and benefits management methods in order to align more to reality, 
looking for a better use and control of existing resources and capabilities (Gomes & Romãoa, 
2016). 
There are some issues that organizations are increasingly urged to respond to in order to attempt 
to create more sustainable businesses: 
• Governments awareness of climate issues and their reaction by stricter regulation 
(Epstein, 2008). 
• Choosing workplaces by employees according to how they account for social and 
environmental responsibilities ( Keeble, Topiol, & Berkeley, 2003). 
• Shareholders consideration to sustainability as an indicator of financial 
achievement (Lynelle , 2001). 
It is asserted by (Marcelino-Sadaba, Gonzalez-Jaen, & Perez-Ezcurdia, 2015) that 
projects play a vital role in achieving sustainability in organizations and society and that 
according to (Zainul Abidin & Pasquire, 2007, p. 275) "sustainability elements would 
lead to enhancing project value such as enhanced production quality, increased 
productivity, increased profitability, reduced cost of living and  business improvement ". 
In order to guarantee project success, the issues of social, environmental and 
economic activists must be closely regarded as part of project decision-making (Aaltonen 
& Kujala, 2010). 
The environmental, social and economic dimensions of sustainable development, is 
balanced by sustainable development goals (SDGs) (UNEP, 2013). Contributing to the 
SDGs, can help a business defines its aspirational purpose 
and is a way of creating shared value for all stakeholders in a manner that is important 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
Page 34 of 120 
 
and inspirational to them and allows purpose of becoming the basis of its strategy, 
causing long-lasting beneficial change that may boost long 
term social, environmental and economic value (EY Global, 2017). 
All in all, in order to cope with current competitive business environment, projects are 
considered as a powerful strategic weapon (Shenhar, Dvir, Levy , & Maltz, 2001), 
initiated in addition to not only solutions to technical problems, a way to improve business, 
implement changes, create economic value and competitive advantage (Andersen & Jessen, 
2003), but also as mentioned earlier, any of the United Nations’ 17 global sustainable 
development goals are considered and can be reached through projects taking place 
globally and locally (Geraldi, Thuesen, Oehmen, & Stingl, 2017). 
Establishing a connection between projects and sustainability, today, managers of projects 
not only fulfills traditional project management responsibilities, but also according to 
(Association for Project Management, 2006) they are considerably positioned in order to 
contribute to sustainable management practices, and since (Goedknegt, 2013) concludes 
they have a significant impact on the implementation of the principles of sustainability to
  the project, therefore according to ( Hwang & Ng, 2013) they have to handle the project 
in the most efficient way in terms of sustainability. 
 
3.4 Sustainable development goals in Danish industries 
The worldwide demand for sustainable products and services is growing quickly. Governments, 
companies and consumers around the world are seeking solutions that make a useful and positive 
contribution to people and the planet. 
SDGs as a global agenda urges all governments, players in the civil sectors and companies to 
join forces to create and implement on-scale sustainable solutions. In this regard, in order to 
contribute tangibly to the results of sustainable development, the Danish government is firmly 
committed to using SDGs as a platform for their development cooperation in developing 
countries. In this commitment, Denmark draws on its wide societal experience, and the 
government is actively seeking to participate and cooperate with private Danish actors who can 
contribute their expertise, skills and technology to solve the challenges in the sustainability.  
==PAGE:35==
 
 
Page 35 of 120 
 
Danish businesses are well-positioned to take a part of the SDG-related business 
opportunities and they have sustainability as a core element of their business culture. 
Therefore, Denmark is maintaining strong global positions in industries which are 
important to development such as food, water and clean energy. 
Moreover, many Danish businesses in different sizes from small to large companies have 
a powerful focus on innovation, design and technology, further enhancing the capacity to 
exploit emerging opportunities. If Danish companies effectively exploit a share of 
emerging market opportunities that corresponds to Denmark's present share of global 
trade in appropriate SDG industries, emerging potential SDG opportunity could be worth 
60 billion USD a year in 2030 for Danish businesses. In order to exploit these markets, 
Danish businesses are well-suited by establishing a basis for business development and 
attracting talents as well as making a crucial contribution to the SDG agenda. They are 
aware of the SDGs and are presently examining how they can affect their company 
positively. A study on 160 Danish businesses in 2018, which previously involved in 
markets outside Europe and North America demonstrates that, the SDGs topic has been 
heard by about 60% of these businesses and it has been incorporated by more than half of 
these businesses. Four out of five Danish exporting companies believe that, the future of 
their company will be shaped by SDGs (Dalberg, 2019).

4.1.1 Manufacturing 
This category consisting of chemicals, pharmaceuticals, computers, electronics, 
machinery, equipment, motor vehicles…, involves the physical or chemical conversion of 
materials, substances or parts into new products. Transformed materials, substances or 
parts are raw materials that are agricultural, forestry, fishing, mining or quarrying 
products as well as other manufactured activities products. Manufacturing can be defined 
as substantial alteration, refurbishment or reproduction. 
In the Manufacturing industry, six different business sectors were investigated with three 
different project types consisting of one project in Business & Organization Change, one 
Information Systems (Software) Projects and four Product and Service Development 
Projects. This means that 67% of business sectors were interested in developing their product 
or service in this type of industry. 
 
4.1.2 Electricity, gas, steam and air conditioning supply 
This industry category includes the activity of providing, utilizing, controlling and 
distributing electric power, natural gas, steam, hot water and the like through a permanent 
infrastructure (network) of lines, mains and pipes, in industrial parks or residential 
buildings.

5 Discussion 
Today, keeping the projects and their potential impacts in accordance with the business strategy 
is basically performed by business. Of course, business focus on profitability is clear, but 
sustainability is of greater importance today.  
In this section, the importance of projects in the industries in achieving SDGs, the application of 
different projects in different industries, implementing SDGs in different industries and finally 
the role of industries in achieving SDGs in Denmark are discussed. 
 
5.1 The importance of projects in the industries in achieving 
the SDGs 
For many, many years, the business sectors viewed sustainability and economic growth as two 
polarities that could not be bridged. In the past decade, however, this attitude has changed 
considerably – particularly so within the last few years. 
Thus, sustainability is now associated with business success and beneficial economic factors 
such as enhanced resource efficiency and energy consumption. Nowadays, transformational 
managers in projects and business sectors can be seen to incorporate social and environmental 
sustainability in their core strategies to ensure growth but also to ensure a greater degree of social 
and environmental responsibility. 
A 2017 survey among 600 Danish companies conducted by the Confederation of Danish 
Industry (DI) showed that approximately one third of these companies have heard and known 
about the SDGs and one third of these companies that knew about the SDGs, worked with these 
Goals strategically (Dalberg, 2019). 
Since projects can operate as the engines which drive business strategy into directions that lead 
toward sustainability and achieving Agenda 2030, it might be essential for companies to find 
some ways for their projects to commit to the SDGs in order to lead the companies, finding long-
term sustainable strategic goals to address their business. By investigating the SDGs and their 
relation to separate areas among the business sectors and their projects in different industries, the 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
Page 59 of 120 
 
company can identify which SDGs are more relevant to focus and then these SDGs will be 
adjusted to business strategies and required projects are formed in line with them.  
In this study, 65 reports of the students in different business sectors have been analyzed based on 
their purpose perspective. In the purpose statement, the reason why a project is done is stated but 
the strategy of business sector is not mentioned on it. By investigating the purpose perspective of 
these projects, it can be seen that their direction was toward addressing the SDGs and at least two 
SDGs were addressed directly or indirectly in these project purposes. In addition to one or some 
SDGs, projects impact on other Goals were considered. 
According to investigated projects in this study, it is identified that, in order to find ways to 
commit the SDGs, management team and project manager can be responsible to choose the 
relevant SDGs and then to put pressure on them in line with the project success direction. 
Because achieving the SDGs depends on how projects are done, therefore considering the 
direction of the projects toward success is important. Subsequently, in addition to project 
success, the adaptation and development of the projects in relation to the SDGs and looking for a 
better use and controlling the existing resources and capabilities can be the project management 
team’s responsibility. 
 In this study, almost all of the investigated projects have been considered as a successful project 
from the project manager’s point of view. Therefore, relevant SDGs were attained by achieving 
the project success. 
 
5.2 The application of different projects in different 
industries 
From literature reviews and Table 2, it can be seen that most business sectors in different 
industry categorization recognize that the projects they fund and implement fall into different 
classifications.  
Projects in any type are more similar to those projects that produce the same sort of product or 
service even if they belong to different industry category. Vice versa, any type of projects has 
less similarity to others in the same industry category. But in terms of project contribution 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
Page 60 of 120 
 
towards addressing SDGs, the main focus of similar projects in different industry categories is 
different according to the core purpose of those industries. For instance, in the human health 
industry and the Education industry, although there is much more similarity between projects for 
Research and Development in these different kinds of industries, but in terms of SDGs, these 
types of projects have different impact in these two-industry categories. For instance, since the 
main purpose and responsibility of human health industry is to respect the health of human, SDG 
3 is addressed in Research and Development project, however Education industry with 
responsibility in educating people, mainly contributes to SDG 4 by doing Research and 
Development project. On the other hand, although there are differences between three kinds of 
projects such as Research and Development, Facility, and Event projects in Education industry, 
but since the main focus of this industry is on educating people, therefore, different projects 
in this industry mainly contribute to SDG 4. Other SDGs can be addressed based on what 
have been stated in the purpose statement of different projects. 
 
5.3 Implementing SDGs in different industries 
Table 3 presented an overview of to what extent any SDG has been addressed in any industry 
category. From this table and relevant analysis, it can be concluded that, commitment to any 
SDG in any industry differs a lot and it depends on different types of contributed projects in 
them.   
For instance, from the Table 2, it can be seen that the number and variety of projects in 
Construction industry is the most within other industry category in this study. Therefore, this 
category stands out to address all of the SDGs because of its variety of projects in this study.  
According to Figure 7, 90% of industries (nine out of ten industry categories) in this study have 
addressed SDG 9. This is an industry chance in which by encouraging sustainable 
industrialization and innovation during their activities and projects, by upgrading local 
infrastructure, in addition to contributing to SDG 9, they can contribute to develop initiatives in

7 Conclusion 
The main purpose of this master thesis is to show how we can achieve a better understanding of 
the different types of projects that can be contributed to any industry categories and to what 
extent they lead any industry toward addressing any SDG. Since Denmark has achieved the top 
rank in terms of SDGs in 2019, therefore the role of different Danish industries in their success is 
analyzed. 
From this study, we can conclude that projects that different industry categories fund and 
implement fall into different classifications. There is connection between different industries and 
relevant business sectors and their commitment to contributing to the SDGs by doing different 
projects. 
Any industry category consists of different organizations or business sectors which do variety of 
projects and activities. Since business sectors, can contribute to achieving SDGs through their 
core projects and activities, it can be concluded that industries have contribution to the work with 
implementing Agenda 2030 and the impact of different industry categories can also be 
considered in terms of SDGs.  
In order to have a successful outlook of sustainable thinking within different business sectors in 
different industry categories, a framework is needed for transformational managers to give them 
the possibility to compare their company with the relevant industry category in respect to achieve 
SDGs. 
Generally, this master thesis proposes a helpful framework to the ministry of industry, 
organizations, business sectors and so on in which the relationship between different industry 
categories and SDGs is identified and they can use this framework as an inspiration for their 
future contribution to a sustainable path. 
Furthermore, by measuring different industry contribution in addressing each SDG in order to 
achieve Agenda 2030, this study can be used to support the current tools, such as SDG compass. 
This study contributes to the available literature to show how different kinds of projects in 
several business sectors which belong to different industry categories lead to addressing different 
SDGs. Therefore, it is concluded from the results that the contribution of addressing the SDGs 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
Page 71 of 120 
 
was broader in some industry categories than the others and it depends on the projects they have 
contributed to.  For instance, Construction industry with 37% of total investigated industries 
consisting of variety of projects in different business sectors, have contributed to address almost 
all SDGs and can be used as an inspiration by other industries to see how they can contribute to 
Agenda 2030 through their projects. 
This master thesis also contributes to investigate which SDGs are addressed more by different 
industries in Denmark. Although SDG 8 with 65% and SDG 9 with 71% have been addressed by 
most of investigated projects in this study, but some challenges with growth rate under the rate 
needed to achieve by 2030 have remained so that should be considered more with different 
industries to solve them. Therefore, it can be concluded that, selecting which of SDGs to 
consider as the purpose of the projects in line with business sector strategy and solving the 
remained challenges in Agenda 2030 can be seen as an obstacle for managers. After this step the 
responsibility of project managers to lead the projects toward success and addressing SDGs 
according to what have stated in the purpose statement of the project should be considered 
significantly.
