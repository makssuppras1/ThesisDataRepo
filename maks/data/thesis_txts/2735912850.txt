Abstract
Space debris poses a significant challenge to the safety and sustainability of current and
future space activities, jeopardizing satellites, spacecraft, and missions. Addressing this
issue is critical for preserving the viability of space exploration. This thesis presents a
novel solution by developing a robotic arm equipped with the ”CHASE” algorithm for tar­
geted space debris removal. This algorithm enables a satellite to identify and pursue
specific debris, enhancing the precision and effectiveness of removal strategies.
The research methodology centers on designing, developing, and testing the robotic arm
within a simulated Earth orbit environment. This simulation aims to closely replicate the
conditions and challenges of removing space debris, providing a robust platform for as­
sessing the arm’s capabilities. A series of experiments and simulations validate the robotic
arm’s design, functionality, and performance, focusing on its precision, efficiency, and
adaptability for potential use in actual space missions.
This study’s objectives are detailed as follows:
• The robotic arm system’s design and development focus on creating a durable and
flexible device capable of performing complex maneuvers, grasping, and manipula­
tion tasks in the zero­gravity conditions encountered during space debris removal.
• Validation of various debris capture techniques and strategies is conducted through
simulated scenarios, evaluating their effectiveness and reliability based on debris
characteristics and operational considerations.
• The robotic arm’s performance and efficiency are assessed, comparing traditional
debris removal methods with metrics like capture time, energy use, and success
rate to evaluate the proposed solution’s improvements.
• The feasibility of scaling and implementing this approach for future space missions is
examined, considering cost, technological readiness, and compatibility with existing
space infrastructure.
Incorporating advanced imaging and spatial analysis techniques, such as neural networks
for debris identification and point cloud transformations for accurate 3D mapping, this re­
search enhances the robotic arm’s functionality. These technologies improve the system’s
ability to detect, track, and precisely capture space debris.
The end point of this work aims to contribute valuable insights and advancements to the
field of space debris mitigation. By pioneering innovative removal techniques and tech­
nologies, this research aims to clear the path for a safer, cleaner, and more sustainable
space environment, supporting ongoing and future space exploration.
Keywords: Space debris removal, Robotic arm, CHASE algorithm, Simulation, Neural
networks, Point cloud, Efficiency, Sustainability, Space exploration.

Introduction
In the vast expanse of outer space that surrounds our planet, a hidden danger lurks, one
that threatens the very fabric of our space infrastructure and the future of astronomical
exploration: space debris. This debris, comprising defunct satellites, fragments of disin­
tegrated spacecraft, and the remnants of orbital collisions, poses a significant and grow­
ing threat to operational space assets and the safety of space missions. As we stand
on the brink of a new era in space exploration, with ambitions ranging from commercial
satellite constellations to manned missions to Mars, the issue of space debris demands ur­
gent attention and innovative solutions. ”Robotic Arm­Assisted Space Debris Removal: A
Drone­based Approach” is a master’s thesis that aims to address this pressing challenge,
proposing a novel methodology for the active removal of space debris to safeguard and
sustain future space endeavors.
The advent of space exploration and the proliferation of satellite technology have been
hallmarks of the 20th and 21st centuries, revolutionizing communication, navigation, and
our understanding of the universe. However, this progress has not come without its con­
sequences. The accumulation of space debris in Earth’s orbit has escalated to a critical
point, with current estimates indicating hundreds of thousands of objects, each capable
of causing catastrophic damage to satellites and manned spacecraft. The potential for a
cascade effect, known as the Kessler Syndrome[1], wherein collisions between objects
could lead to an exponential increase in debris, thereby rendering large areas of space
unusable, underscores the need of actions for proactive debris removal strategies.
This research aims an innovative approach to tackling the space debris problem, em­
ploying drone technology equipped with robotic arms to simulate the conditions and chal­
lenges of debris removal in space. By creating a controlled Earth­based environment
where drones act as both the debris and the remover, this study seeks to explore the
feasibility, efficiency, and precision of using robotic arms for debris capture and removal.
This method offers a unique blend of robotics and aerospace engineering, providing valu­
able insights into the dynamics of space debris interception and the potential for real­world
application in orbital debris removal missions.
To lay the groundwork for this investigation, the thesis begins with a comprehensive review
of the origins, types, and dangers posed by space debris. It delves into the complex
dynamics of space orbits, guided by the principles of Keplerian mechanics, to understand
how debris moves and interacts in the space environment. This foundational knowledge
is critical for developing effective strategies for debris tracking and interception.
The research then advances to the development and analysis of algorithms designed to
guide a debris removal satellite to its target. It explores two principal strategies: one that
methodically adjusts the satellite’s orbital elements to align with the debris and another that
utilizes advanced PID controllers for finer, more responsive adjustments to the satellite’s
trajectory. By comparing these approaches, the study aims to discover the most effective
method for guiding debris removal missions, contributing to the broader field of aerospace
engineering with practical insights into satellite maneuvering and control.
A significant focus of the thesis is the design, development, and testing of the robotic
arm, which represents the cornerstone of the proposed debris removal approach. This
section examines the engineering considerations involved in creating a robotic arm ca­
pable of operating in the challenging conditions of space. It covers the arm’s degrees of

Theoretical Background
In the expanse of space, the legacy of human ingenuity and exploration is shadowed
by an ever­growing challenge: space debris. This theoretical background embarks on a
detailed examination of the issue of space debris, the fundamental principles of orbital me­
chanics that govern their unpredictable dance around Earth, and the robotic technologies
mobilized to face this challenge. It gets deeper into the origins, types, and implications of
space debris, laying the complexities of their dynamics and the risks they pose to current
and future space missions. Furthermore, it explores the sophisticated sector of robotic
engineering, spotlighting the evolution of space robotics and the systems designed for de­
bris removal. This section aims to weave together the rich historical context, the rigorous
scientific principles, and the innovative technological advancements that frame the critical
need for effective space debris management strategies. Through this comprehensive ex­
ploration,the magnitude of the challenge at hand is not only grasped, but also the way for
a deeper understanding of the proposed robotic arm­based system for debris removal is
paved, highlighting its potential as hope for securing the safety and sustainability of space
activities for generations to come.
2.1
Space Debris
2.1.1
Definition and Types of Space Debris
Space debris, often referred to as space junk or orbital debris, cover a wide array of man­
made objects orbiting Earth that no longer serve any functional purpose. This includes
everything from entire defunct satellites and spent rocket stages to smaller fragments like
bolts, tools, and even paint flecks [2]. As humanity’s footprint in space has expanded, so
too has the volume of debris, turning Earth’s orbit into a crowded and more dangerous
environment. Understanding the types and classifications of space debris is crucial for
assessing the challenges and strategies associated with its removal and management.
• Large Debris:This category includes sizable objects such as non­operational satel­
lites, empty rocket bodies, and large pieces of equipment that were part of space­
craft or launch vehicles. These objects are often the easiest to track due to their size
but pose significant risks due to their mass and potential for causing catastrophic
collisions, leading to the generation of more debris.
• Small Debris: Small debris comprises objects ranging from the size of a softball to
that of a grain of sand. This includes screws, springs, broken satellite pieces, and
other fragments resulting from collisions, explosions, or the disintegration of larger
debris. Despite their smaller size, these pieces travel at high velocities and can
cause considerable damage to spacecraft and satellites, much like bullets.
• Microdebris: The smallest category of space debris, microdebris, consists of parti­
cles smaller than 1 mm, including paint chips, metallic flakes, and other small scale
fragments. These particles, while tiny, pose a significant risk to spacewalks and the
external surfaces of spacecraft, as they can erode materials and penetrate protec­
tive shielding [3].
The diversity in size and composition of space debris necessitates a multifaceted ap­
proach to monitoring, tracking, and removal. Large debris, while easier to detect and
track, requires substantial effort and technology to capture and deorbit safely. Small and
microdebris, though more challenging to monitor due to their size, represent a significant

2.1.2
Origins and Causes of Space Debris
The landscape of Earth’s orbit tells the story of human ambition in space exploration,
marked not just by groundbreaking achievements but also by the gradual gathering of
space debris. This accumulation, often overlooked in the early excitement of space ex­
ploration, has grown into a significant challenge for current and future missions. Under­
standing where this debris comes from helps in crafting strategies for its management and
removal.
The Start of the Space Age
The launch of Sputnik 1 in 1957 inaugurated the space age, setting off a chain of events
that would fill Earth’s orbit with a variety of objects. The subsequent space race, char­
acterized by rapid advancements and intense competition, contributed significantly to the
early collection of orbital debris. Each mission, while a step forward in exploration, left
behind spent rocket stages, defunct satellites, and other materials, laying the groundwork
for the current debris situation.
Satellite Operations and Expansion
The reliance on satellites for communication, navigation, and observation has only inten­
sified over the decades, leading to a crowded orbital environment. Satellites that end their
operational life without a de­orbit plan contribute to the growing population of space debris.
Furthermore, operational incidents, such as unintended collisions or satellite breakups
due to system failures, add to the debris cloud.
Significant Collision Events
Notable collisions, like the 2009 crash between Iridium 33 and Kosmos­2251, have dra­
matically highlighted the risks and consequences of space debris. Such events not only
generate thousands of new debris pieces but also emphasize the interconnected nature
of space activities, where the actions of one entity can have widespread impacts on the
orbital environment.
Anti­Satellite Weapon Tests
The testing of anti­satellite (ASAT) systems by various countries has been a controversial
practice contributing significantly to space debris. By intentionally destroying satellites,
these tests create large fields of debris that can endanger other operational satellites and
future missions across multiple orbits.
The increase of space debris is a direct consequence of human activities in space, from
the earliest days of exploration to contemporary satellite operations and military tests.
This scenario is crucial for understanding the urgency and complexity of debris removal
efforts. As we look to safeguard and sustain the space environment, recognizing our
role in its current state is the first step towards responsible care and the development of
innovative cleanup solutions.
2.1.3
Risks and Impacts of Space Debris
The expansion of space debris in Earth’s orbit presents significant risks to operational
satellites and manned space missions, with the potential to trigger the Kessler Syndrome—
a cascade of collisions leading to an exponential increase in debris, making certain orbits
unsustainable for future space endeavors. Space debris, moving at velocities up to sev­
eral kilometers per second, can inflict catastrophic damage upon impact, threatening the
integrity of satellites that underpin global communication, weather forecasting, and nav­
igation systems. For manned missions, even tiny debris poses a lethal hazard, neces­
sitating complex and costly avoidance maneuvers. The looming specter of the Kessler
Syndrome underscores the urgency of addressing space debris, as it could lock human­
ity out of space by surrounding Earth with an impassable barrier of orbiting fragments,

Orbital Dynamics and Debris Dynamics

lenges posed by space debris. This understanding begins with the foundational principles
of orbital dynamics, primarily through Kepler’s laws of planetary motion, which also apply
to the motion of objects in Earth’s orbit, including space debris.

Control Systems
Integrating robotic arms for space debris removal necessitates an exploration into the

Within this domain, several control strategies have been developed and deployed, each

Some standard used control systems are:
1. Feedforward Control Systems:These anticipate the needs of the robotic system
based on a predefined model, applying control actions without relying on feedback
from the system’s current state. While useful for predictable environments, they
may not be ideal for the unpredictable dynamics of space debris.
2. Feedback Control Systems:Operate by continuously monitoring the system’s out­

3. Adaptive Control Systems: These systems adjust their parameters in real­time

4. Robust Control Systems:Designed to maintain performance despite uncertainties
and variations in the system’s model, robust control systems are valuable for ensur­

space.
Among these, the PID Controller specializes in error correction and is particularly revered
for its straightforward yet effective approach. It adjusts a robotic arm’s movement by
calculating the difference between a desired setpoint and the actual position, then applying
corrective actions based on three key components:
• Proportional (P) Term:Directly responds to the current error, with the adjustment
proportional to the error size.
• Integral (I) Term:Addresses the accumulation of past errors, providing a corrective
action that’s proportional to both the magnitude and the duration of the error.
• Derivative (D) Term:Predicts future errors based on the rate of change, helping to
preemptively correct the path of the robotic arm.
The PID controller’s adaptability to varying tasks and conditions makes it a cornerstone
for the precise control required in space debris removal operations. By fine­tuning the PID
parameters, engineers can optimize the robotic arm’s responsiveness, ensuring accurate
and efficient debris capture and manipulation without exacerbating the debris problem.

2.6.2
Inverse Kinematics and Dynamics
In the theoretical exploration of robotic systems for space debris removal, a comprehen­
sive understanding of inverse kinematics and dynamics is essential. These concepts are
the base of the structure of robotic motion planning and control, enabling precise manip­
ulation and interaction with space debris.
Inverse Kinematics
Inverse kinematics (IK) is a crucial computational technique in robotics that deals with cal­
culating the joint parameters necessary to place the end of a robotic arm (the end­effector)
at a desired position and orientation in space. Unlike forward kinematics, which straight­
forwardly computes the end­effector’s position based on given joint parameters, inverse
kinematics works in the opposite direction. It solves for the joint angles that achieve a
specific task position from the end­effector’s perspective, a process essential for directing
a robotic arm to capture space debris accurately.
In the context of space debris removal, IK allows for the precise positioning of robotic
arms to grasp or interact with debris, taking into account the unique challenges posed by
the space environment, such as microgravity and the absence of atmospheric drag. IK al­
gorithms must be robust and flexible, capable of handling the arm’s complex movements
and configurations to ensure successful debris capture without generating additional frag­
ments or risking collision.
Inverse Dynamics
While inverse kinematics focuses on the geometric aspects of motion, inverse dynamics
delves into the forces and torques required to achieve a certain movement. This analyt­
ical approach calculates the necessary actuator forces and torques at the robot’s joints
to produce the desired motion as determined by inverse kinematics. Inverse dynamics is
pivotal in understanding and controlling the behavior of robotic systems under the influ­
ence of various forces, a critical consideration in the vacuum of space where every action
has an equal and opposite reaction.
For space debris removal, mastering inverse dynamics means ensuring that robotic arms
can maneuver debris with the required finesse and strength, considering the mass and
inertia of both the arm and debris. It involves predicting how the arm should move to
minimize unwanted forces that could destabilize the debris or the servicing satellite, thus
ensuring safe and controlled debris manipulation.

Collision Detection Algorithms
Building upon the theoretical foundations laid out by the study of inverse kinematics, dy­
namics, and the control systems that guide robotic arms in space, we transition to a crucial
aspect of collision detection algorithms. These algorithms are vital for ensuring the safety
and efficacy of robotic operations, especially in the unpredictable environment of outer
space. Among various strategies developed for this purpose, the Rapidly­exploring Ran­
dom Tree (RRT) algorithm stands out for its effectiveness and adaptability in navigating
complex spaces.
Collision detection algorithms are a cornerstone of robotic system safety, particularly in
applications like space debris removal, where the margin for error is exceedingly small.
These algorithms enable robotic systems to identify potential collisions with debris or other
objects in their operational environment and adjust their course to avoid contact. This ca­
pability is critical not only for preventing damage to the robot and the objects it is designed
to interact with but also for ensuring the success of debris removal missions by avoiding
the creation of additional debris through collision.
Examples of Collision Detection Algorithms:
1. Voronoi Diagrams:This method partitions space into regions based on proximity
to a set of predetermined points. By constructing Voronoi diagrams around objects
or debris, robots can navigate through the largest available spaces, avoiding the
closest obstacles. This technique is particularly useful in densely populated debris
fields, guiding robots along safer paths that maximize distance from potential colli­
sions.
2. GJK (Gilbert­Johnson­Keerthi) Algorithm: The GJK algorithm is efficient in de­
termining the closest points between convex shapes, making it valuable for collision
detection between robotic arms and irregularly shaped debris. By iteratively refining
the search for the minimum distance, GJK can quickly confirm or deny the presence
of a collision, allowing for real­time adjustments in the robot’s trajectory.
3. Sweep and Prune (Broadphase Collision Detection): This algorithm efficiently
identifies potential collisions by tracking the axis­aligned bounding boxes (AABBs)
of objects as they move. By ”sweeping” a line across the scene and ”pruning” object
pairs that do not overlap in all dimensions, this method narrows down the list of col­
lision checks to only the most likely candidates, improving computational efficiency
in dynamic environments.
4. Rapidly­exploring Random Tree (RRT): excels in efficiently exploring complex,
high­dimensional spaces by incrementally building a branching structure that reaches
towards unexplored areas. Its strength in quickly generating paths in dynamic and
unpredictable environments, like those encountered in space debris fields, makes it
invaluable for plotting safe trajectories for robotic arms tasked with debris removal.

3.3
Computer Vision and Neural Network
For the neural network the investigation lies on the construction of the Unet. The mini­
UNet architecture, inspired by the U­Net model for semantic segmentation, employs a
sequence of convolutional operations for feature extraction and spatial downscaling, fol­
lowed by upscaling operations to restore the original image dimensions with semantic
class labels for each pixel. Implemented in frameworks like PyTorch and TensorFlow, this
model demonstrates efficient feature learning from image data for segmentation tasks.
The model takes an input tensor of shape (nchannels, H, W), where nchannels is the number
of input channels (e.g., 1 for grayscale or 3 for RGB images), and H, W are the height
and width of the input image, respectively.
The downsampling path consists of four sequential operations that halve the spatial di­
mensions while doubling the feature depth, starting with 64 features to a maximum of 512.
Each downsampling step is performed by the Down module, which combines max pool­
ing with a double convolution operation (DoubleConv), incorporating batch normalization
and ReLU [19] activation functions. The upsampling path sequentially restores the spa­
tial dimensions back to the original size, halving the feature depth from 512 to 64. This
is achieved through transposed convolutional layers or bilinear upsampling followed by a
double convolution operation, facilitating the precise localization of semantic information.
The final layer of the model is a 1×1 convolution (OutConv) that maps the 64 feature chan­
nels to the number of target classes nclasses, producing a tensor of shape (nclasses, H, W)
where each pixel is classified into one of the nclasses categories.

Discussion
The escalating challenge of space debris presents a formidable threat to the sustainability
of space activities and the safety of orbital assets. Addressing this issue, this project
developed a robotic arm designed specifically for the efficient removal of space debris,
underscoring the urgent need for advanced solutions in this domain.
The robotic arm represents a significant application of the technological developments
of robotics, featuring gripping mechanisms, autonomous debris identification capabilities
and efficient removal. Its ability to recognize, capture and remove the specified debris
proves the efficiency of the simulation. Also, the ”CHASE” algorithm is a great represen­
tation of orbital dynamics and the possible dangers encountering through space.
5.1
Results Interpretation
5.1.1
Chase
The conducted study starts with the implementation of two distinct approaches for control­
ling a robotic arm intended for space debris removal: a Proportional­Integral­Derivative
(PID) controller integration and a simple approximation algorithm referring firstly at the in­
clination. The performance of these methods was evaluated based on their accuracy, as
indicated by the percentage difference in completeness, and their efficiency, as reflected
by the number of iterations required to reach the goal.
The PID controller was found to exhibit superior precision, with a minimal difference in
completeness noted at 0.0006388382% for the inclinations.This level of precision is critical
in the domain of space debris removal, where small errors can lead to mission failure or the
generation of additional debris. The effectiveness of the PID controller’s feedback mech­
anism, which continuously adjusts control inputs to minimize error, was demonstrated
through these results.
In comparison, the simple approximation method resulted in a larger deviation from the
target inclination, with a difference in completeness recorded at 0.0010441176%.The find­
ings suggest that while the simple approximation method may suffice for tasks with lower
precision requirements, it is inadequate for applications like space debris removal, which
necessitate exact positioning.
Furthermore, the PID controller was observed to reach the intended target within 77 itera­
tions, suggesting greater efficiency compared to the simple approximation method, which
required 99 iterations 4.1.
The algorithm, as said, initializes with the out of plane maneuvers for adjusting the in­
clination and the Right Ascension of the Ascending node with two different approaches.

to the complexity of the simulation.
First and foremost executing neural network operations within a robotics framework in­
volves substantial computational challenges, particularly due to the high memory de­
mands of such models. Training robust neural networks, capable of performing tasks like
image recognition or sensory data interpretation, requires vast datasets that necessitate
extensive memory and storage. Furthermore, the computational intensity of processing
and learning from this data often means that only systems with powerful GPUs and high
level processors can handle the workload efficiently. This not only poses a challenge for
real­time robotics applications but also restricts the ability to iterate and improve models
quickly. In addition, the obstacle of ensuring compatibility between the advanced neural
network frameworks and the robotics operating systems can lead to further complexities
and a need for significant troubleshooting. Collectively, these difficulties can impose lim­
itations on the scope of research, affecting the development of sophisticated robotic sys­
tems designed for complex operations such as autonomous navigation and manipulation
in unstructured environments.
Regarding the complexity of the simulation itself, it should be noted that a lot of things
need to be simplified in order to create a viable project in the scope of a master thesis.
For example, in the orbital dynamics algorithm, the perturbations are not considered in the
analysis as it would upgrade the computational demands and the difficulty to a level way
above the needed. In addition to this, the orbital dynamics by themselves are considered
extremely harsh and although the results of the algorithm are acceptable, this algorithm
can only be used for academical purposes as such algorithms that are used in the industry,
have been organized and created by whole groups of scientists as they require extreme
precision and collaboration between the different aspects. Also, the ”CHASE” algorithm
is mainly used for Low Earth and ”Mid” orbits because although it is mentioned that the
algorithm is not strictly considering the fuel budget, higher orbit to lower orbit and vice
versa demand a huge amount of delta vee.
In the context of the robotic arm configuration, the project initially was supposed to be
mounted into drones in order to simulate the space orbital motions. Due to time limitations
this point was not reached in the project and is left for future work. Also, for a robotic arm
to be viable in space projects, materials must be carefully selected to ensure durability,
functionality, and safety under the unique and harsh conditions of space. Here are some
materials commonly considered for space robotics, along with their key attributes:
1. Titanium: Known for its high strength­to­weight ratio, resistance to corrosion, and
ability to withstand extreme temperatures. Titanium is often used in the structural
components of space robotics. Its lightweight nature helps minimize the overall
mass of the spacecraft, which is critical for launch efficiency.
2. Aluminum Alloys: Aluminum alloys, especially those treated for improved strength
and durability, are widely used due to their low density, good corrosion resistance,
and excellent thermal conductivity. They are suitable for various parts of a robotic
arm, including the frame and joints.
3. Carbon Fiber Reinforced Polymers (CFRP): CFRP materials are prized for their high
stiffness, strength, and low thermal expansion, making them ideal for structural com­

In a more general aspect, the simulations conducted in this thesis are tailored to the oper­

motion planning algorithms. Moreover, space debris removal presents unique challenges,
including size, shape, and material composition of the debris, which may not be entirely
compatible with the debris models used in this thesis. As such, there is a significant need
for customization before these methodologies can be applied to alternate scenarios. A
nuanced approach that considers the specifics of each new application is crucial for ex­
tending these simulation results to other domains.
Simulated Environment vs. Actual Space Conditions
Space is a dangerous place with more risks than can be predicted in a simulation. In this
thesis, a simulated environment provided a controlled and idealized setting to scrutinize
the mechanics and control systems of the robotic arm. Nevertheless, several space­
specific factors remain unaccounted for, such as zero­gravity conditions that influence the
dynamics of movement and operation. Real space environments also contend with high­
energy radiation, which can impair electronic systems and sensors, leading to unforeseen
operational anomalies that are not replicated within the simulations’ scope.
Moreover, the simulated environment cannot perfectly emulate the wear and impact of
micrometeoroids or the thermal extremes experienced in orbit, each of which can dras­
tically affect the performance and structural integrity of a robotic arm. The simulations
also fall short of capturing the complex interactions between the robotic arm and debris,
which in reality may include unpredictable spin rates, tumbling motions, and potential for
fragmentation.

Conclusions
The exploration into the realm of space debris removal through the deployment of a robotic
arm equipped with neural network­based image recognition and point cloud transforma­
tions represents a significant stride towards enhancing the safety and sustainability of
space operations. This thesis has demonstrated the feasibility and effectiveness of an
innovative approach, underscored by the integration of the CHASE algorithm, which en­
ables precise tracking and removal of space debris.
Central to these findings is the robotic arm’s improved capability for debris identification
and capture, facilitated by the advanced neural network model trained on a diverse dataset
of supposed space imagery represented by a green cube. This model has shown excep­
tional accuracy in recognizing debris pieces, a critical step for any removal operation.
Equally important, the use of point cloud data to generate three­dimensional models of
the debris has offered insights into the spatial dynamics of debris fields, enabling more
strategic planning and execution of removal tasks.
Through rigorous testing in a simulated Earth orbit environment, the design, functionality,
and operational efficiency of the robotic arm were validated. The experiments confirm
not only the technical viability of the system but also its adaptability to varying conditions
and debris types, marking traditional debris removal methods. The system’s adaptability,
a key focus of the study, suggests that such technology can be scaled and modified to
address a broader range of space debris scenarios, extending its applicability beyond the
initial proof of concept.
The study also shed light on the critical role of the CHASE algorithm in enhancing the
robotic arm’s efficiency by dynamically adjusting the satellite’s orbit to match that of the
targeted debris. This capability allows the approach to a certain debris with a relative
precision in the scope of the master thesis.
Looking ahead, apart from certain limitations, this research opens several avenues for
future work. While the current study confirms the potential of integrating neural networks
and point cloud transformations with robotic arm technology for debris removal, ongoing
advancements in machine learning and robotics promise to further enhance these ca­
pabilities. Future research could explore more complex neural network architectures for
improved debris classification, as well as the incorporation of real­time data processing for
adaptive mission planning. Additionally, the scalability and integration of this technology
with existing space mission frameworks present new areas for exploration, particularly
in terms of cost­efficiency, operational logistics, and compatibility with other satellite sys­
tems.
In conclusion, this thesis not only contributes valuable insights into the technological ad­
vancements necessary for effective space debris removal but also highlights the impor­
tance of continuous innovation in preserving the outer space environment. By paving
the way for more efficient, adaptable, and scalable debris removal solutions, this work
supports the broader goal of ensuring the long­term sustainability and safety of space
exploration and utilization.
