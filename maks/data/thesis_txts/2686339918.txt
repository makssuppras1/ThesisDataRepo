Abstract
The use of a membrane-aerated bioﬁlm reactor (MABR) for Simultaneous Nitriﬁcation and
Denitriﬁcation with Phosphorus Removal (SNDPR) can potentially lead to savings in terms of
oxygen and carbon sources utilized. Also, less sludge would be produced, reducing the cost for
the disposal. In this study, the application of a MABR for SNDPR has been investigated at
the Ejby Mølle Water and Resource Recovery Facility. Batch tests at the MABR with primary
eﬄuent and mixed liquor from the anaerobic stage have been performed. Furthermore, anaer-
obic/anoxic and anaerobic/aerobic laboratory batch activity tests have been carried out on
the activated sludge to investigate the abundance of Denitrifying Polyphosphate-Accumulating
Organisms (DPAO) and Polyphosphate Accumulating Organisms (PAO) as well as on the
Glycogen Accumulating Organisms (GAO) and PAO dominance. SNDPR has been success-
fully achieved in the experiments with mixed liquor. A phosphorus uptake rate of 0.61±0.45
mg P g VSS−1 h−1 has been obtained and an ammonia removal rate of 0.79±0.15 gN m−2 d−1
has been observed. Also, high SND eﬃciency of 95% has been achieved. As a DPAO/PAO
ratio of 0.92 has been found for the activated sludge, the phosphorus removal in the MABR has
been linked to the activity of DPAO. Regarding the denitriﬁcation, it has been linked to the
activity of Denitrifying Glycogen Accumulating Organisms (DGAO), as they have been shown
to be more abundant than DPAO. The Ordinary Heterotrophic Organisms (OHO) living in the
bioﬁlm might have played a secondary role as the available Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD)
has been low throughout the experiments. Regarding the primary eﬄuent experiments, no
phosphorus removal has been observed. This suggests that the DPAO do not live in the bioﬁlm
but in the mixed liquor where the nitrate and nitrite diﬀuse. Nevertheless, SND has been
achieved with an ammonia removal rate of 0.82±0.39 g N m−2 d−1 and a SND eﬃciency of 90
%. However, the system has been highly unstable, and inhibition has been observed. This has
been linked to the high initial soluble COD concentration which has aﬀected the nitriﬁcation
process. Also, an initial phosphorus release in the ﬁrst 90 minutes has been observed which,
although it might be linked to hydrolysis, needs to be further investigated.

1
Introduction
1.1
Phosphorus problematic
Phosphorus (P) and nitrogen (N) are fundamental nutrients for the growth of living organisms:
the former is essential for the formation of DNA, RNA and energy transfer, while the latter is
fundamental for biomass synthesis [12].
However, an excess of these nutrients has been shown to cause the overgrowth of plants in
surface waters, which leads to oxygen depletion due to death and decay, and eventually to the
presence of dead zones. This process is called eutrophication [9].
Phosphorus and nitrogen are dispersed in the environment as a consequence of anthropogenic
activities. One of this is the extensive use of P and N in the production of fertilizers worldwide.
The nutrients enter the water reservoirs as a consequence of the runoﬀfrom agricultural lands
[48] or through manure runoﬀ. Furthermore, another potential source of these nutrients are
the eﬄuents, both industrial and domestic, of Wastewater Treatment Plants (WWTPs).
In fact phosphorus, despite being a non-renewable source, has been introduced in the form of
phosphates or phosphorus containing compounds in various products for human use. As an
example, until the ban of the European Union in 2013 [15], detergents containing phosphorus
were used in private households and ended up into the wastewater treatment system.
Eutrophication is today one of the biggest pollution concerns aﬀecting the quality of surface
waters [27]. It has economical consequences as it aﬀects the quality of the drinking water, the
water used for recreational purposes and the water used for ﬁsheries [2].
Due to the more stringent regulations such as the European Directive Framework [16], the
Danish WWTPs must comply with the water quality goals introduced for the content of P and
N in the eﬄuents. The need to preserve the already scarce water resources and the ecosystems
which depend on them, has created the urge to manage the input of these elements in the water
bodies.
Thus, it has been fundamental to ﬁnd cheaper and more eﬃcient ways to remove P from the
wastewater and ways to recycle it.
1.1.1
Phosphorus removal in WWTP
Phosphorus removal, in most WWTPs, is achieved chemically by using alum or lime for pre-
cipitation. However, this process generates the need for extra handling and disposal of the
chemicals[32].
Another option, which is being more and more integrated with conventional methods in
WWTPs, is the Enhanced Biological Phosphorus Removal (EBPR) [53]. The EBPR can rep-
resent a cheaper and more eﬀective alternative. It produced less solids than the traditional
chemical treatment [32] and it is has been shown to concentrate 10–50 times the phospho-
rus concentration [84]. The EBPR takes advantage of the observation that certain types of
microorganisms, in aerobic or anoxic environments, uptake more phosphorus than what is nec-
essary for the metabolic activities [83]. These organisms are called Phosphorus Accumulating
Organisms (PAO).
The metabolic ways adopted by PAO are complex and still not fully understood and are sub-
ject of research still today. As a consequence, system failures are commonly experienced in
9
==PAGE:10==
full-scale WWTPs, which raises the need for skilled water treatment operators. [8]
However, the potential beneﬁts from the implementation of this process and the possibility of
applying it in multi-functioning systems urge the need for deeper research.
1.2
The EBPR process
The EBPR is achieved in the WWTPs by recirculating activated sludge between anaerobic and
aerobic or anoxic tanks. This shift is fundamental to boost the metabolic activities of PAO.
This is because PAO store carbon and release phosphate in anaerobic conditions, while they
utilize the stored carbon and replenish the phosphate pool in aerobic or anoxic conditions.
Because PAO grow in aerobic/anoxic conditions, they are able to uptake more phosphate than
what they released.[26] Then, a fraction of the sludge is recycled and a fraction is disposed.
In aerobic conditions, a group of organisms called Ordinary Heterotrophic Organisms (OHO),
which have a higher yield than PAO, utilize oxygen and carbon sources for growth. However,
in conditions of scarce carbon resources, PAO can outcompete OHO, despite having a lower
yield, by relying on the stored polyhydroxy-alkanoates (PHA) as a source of energy.
1.2.1
Metabolic pathways of PAO
Anaerobic metabolism
The absence of electron acceptors in the anaerobic tank is a stress-
ful condition for PAO [73]. Thus, they do not grow but store carbon sources. A representation
of the anaerobic metabolism is shown in ﬁgure 1. PAO uptake the Volatile Fatty Acids (VFA)
into the cell, in the form of acetate, derived from the fermentation processes, and they con-
vert it to Acetyl-CoA. The Acetyl-CoA is then converted into the storage carbon compounds
PHA, such as polyhydroxybutyrate (PHB). The breakage of the phosphodiester bonds of stored
polyphosphates (poly-P) provides the energy to form Adenosine Triphosphate (ATP) and as
a consequence releases ortho-phosphate molecules in the bulk liquid. The ATP produced from
this process and from the degradation of glycogen, is utilized for the transport and the conver-
sion of the acetate to PHB. [68]
As PHB are more reduced compounds than VFA, a reducing power is needed. On this aspect,
diﬀerent metabolic pathways have been proposed. One hypothesis is that the reducing power
is obtained through the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle and the other hypothesis is that the
NADH is instead produced from the metabolism of glycogen.
The production of reducing power through glycolysis was proven in diﬀerent studies [60][44],
but according to the stoichiometric calculations, it was not enough to fully justify the synthesis
of PHB. In a study by Martin et al. 2006 [43], it was shown that Accumulibacter sp., which
are the most known PAO, presented the genes to both obtain NADH through the TCA cycle
and through glycolisis. It has also been reported in literature, that PAO might be able to even
shift between these two pathways [1].
Aerobic metabolism
In the presence of an electron acceptor (e.g. oxygen), the PHA is
consumed and the phosphate is uptaken. The oxidation of the polymer produces the reducing
power in the form of NADH, which then, through oxidative phosphorylation, forms ATP.
The ATP is utilized for biomass growth and maintenance, for the transfer of phosphate from
the bulk liquid through the membrane, for the synthesis of poly-P and for the synthesis of
10
==PAGE:11==
glycogen. Furthermore, the synthesis of glycogen and biomass both release more NADH. [55]

1.2.2
The EBPR communities
The EBPR process has been studied since 1959, when it was observed for the ﬁrst time in India
[70]. Initially, it was thought that the main responsible for the removal of phosphorus was
the Acinetobacter [11]. However, recently, the focus of the research has shifted on Candidatus
Accumulibacter Phosphatis from the order Rhodocyclales. That is, because Accumulibacter have
been identiﬁed in successful EBPR processes worldwide in WWTPs but also in laboratory batch
experiments. In a study conducted in 28 Danish WWTPs [46], Accumulibacter and another
genus belonging to PAO, Tetrasphaera, have been detected as the most abundant over all
other bacteria in EBPR processes. Despite this, researchers have not yet succeeded in isolating
the Accumulibacter.
The maximum concentration reached of these bacteria has been 90%
[42]. This is partially due to the presence of another type of microorganisms called Glycogen
Accumulating Organisms (GAO).
1.2.3
Factors aﬀecting EBPR performance
Competition between GAO and PAO
GAO have a similar metabolism to PAO: they
are also able to store carbon in anaerobic conditions for periods in which the substrate is
not available.
The diﬀerence is that all the energy needed is taken from the consumption
of glycogen.
Speciﬁcally, in anaerobic conditions they uptake VFA and they convert it to
PHA. In the presence of an electron acceptor, they utilize the reserve of PHA for growth,
cell maintenance and to restore the reserve of glycogen. Thus, they do not require to uptake
phosphate to form poly-P compounds. This translates into GAO competing with PAO for VFA
in anaerobic conditions, without contributing to phosphorus removal. For this reason, they are
considered to be detrimental for the EBPR process [46]. The presence of GAO has been indeed
associated with failures of the EBPR in batch experiments as well as in real-scale WWTPs.
However, in the presence of electron acceptors other than oxygen (i.e. nitrate and nitrite),
recent studies suggest that there might be a collaboration between GAO and PAO. In fact, it
has been hypothesized that PAO might not be able to utilize nitrate for phosphorus removal
11
==PAGE:12==
but only nitrite instead. Thus, they need GAO to convert the nitrate to nitrite to carry out
phosphorus removal in anoxic conditions. [62]
Eﬀect of pH
In a study by Fukushima et al., 2010 [18], a deterioration of the EBPR as well as
a decline of the population of PAO has been observed at pH equal or lower than 6.5. Similarly,
it has been shown in another study that PAO perform more eﬃciently, both in anaerobic and
aerobic conditions, at higher pH (≈8), thus out-competing GAO [54]. In particular, in Filipe
et al., 2001 [17], it was suggested that the pH of the aerobic phase and anaerobic phase should
not be lower than 7 and 7.25 respectively, in order to obtain PAO dominance.
The reason why GAO are in disadvantage at higher pH is that the ATP required to transport
acetate through the membrane increases with pH [69]. Both PAO and GAO need more energy
to perform acetate uptake. However, a simpliﬁed explanation of why PAO are beneﬁted might
be that they can rely on the poly-P pool to produce ATP and not only on glycogen. When in
aerobic conditions, because less PHA is needed to produce the poly-P than what is needed to
replenish the glycogen, PAO have more PHA available for growth than GAO. [53]
Thus, an optimum pH value for the EBPR process that would beneﬁt PAO is between 7.2 - 8
[19].
Eﬀect of temperature
In a study by Tian W.D. et al., 2013 [52], Accumulibacter Type I
out-competed GAO at a temperature of 10℃. This has been attributed to the inhibition of
the metabolism of glycogen at low temperatures. At a temperature of 30℃instead, PAO have
been out-competed by GAO.
Although in literature it is possible to ﬁnd contradicting results due to diﬀerent experimental
settings, what is been observed in most studies is that PAO have an advantage at lower tem-
peratures, while GAO at higher ones [39][38]. Thus, PAO can be considered to be psychrophilic
bacteria while GAO mesophilic [40].
Organic Load
The performance of the EBPR process is highly inﬂuenced by the ratio be-
tween the organic and phosphorus content of the inﬂuent. Many studies have been conducted
in order to understand what the optimal ratio would be to beneﬁt PAO over GAO. Although
the presence of VFA such as propionate and acetate is fundamental for EBPR, high values of
COD/P ratio are not suitable for EBPR due to the proliferation of GAO [67]. The VFA/P
required in the inﬂuent has been suggested to be 14-16 by Barnard and Abraham, 2006 [6].
In a study by Ong et al., 2013 [57] conducted at high temperature (≈30℃) in which acetate
was the only source, higher removal rates have been found with a COD/P ratio of 3 compared
to a COD/P ratio of 10. In other studies a high COD/P ratio of ≈50 mgCOD/mgP has
been associated with GAO dominance [28] which creates the risk of EBPR failure. Thus, an
intermediate COD/P ratio of 10 has been recommended by Schuler and Jenkins, 2003 [64].
1.2.4
Denitrifying PAO
The EBPR has been observed in literature also in the presence of electron acceptors other
than oxygen i.e. nitrate and nitrite. The organisms performing this type of metabolism have
been called Denitrifying Polyphosphate Accumulating Organisms (DPAO). In anoxic condi-
tions, they are able to consume PHA using nitrate or nitrite as electron acceptors to provide
12
==PAGE:13==
energy for the phosphate uptake. Again, the metabolic pathways of DPAO are not completely
understood. Many studies have been done to investigate whether it is the same organisms
switching between anoxic and aerobic metabolism or whether it is an entirely diﬀerent com-
munity of microorganisms.
In several batch experiments carried out in diﬀerent studies [45][30], phosphorus uptake has
been observed in anoxic conditions until it ceased. When switching sequentially from anoxic to
aerobic conditions, the phosphorus uptake continued. This might indicate that DPAO utilized
all the PHA pool and were not able to uptake more phosphate. In aerobic conditions instead,
PAO, which were before inhibited by the absence of oxygen, could now uptake the phosphate
utilizing their reserve of PHA.
In a study by Carvalho et al., 2007, two diﬀerent morphologies have been identiﬁed: a rod-
like morphology which belonged to the most denitrifying communities able to denitrify from
nitrate, and a coccimorphology belonging to communities which were able to denitrify only
from nitrite [13]. Later genetic studies suggested that these two morphologies might belong to
two diﬀerent clades (I and II) of Accumulibacter which are divided themselves in sub-clades
[61]. Furthermore, PAO belonging to the II clade, do not contain the enzymes necessary for
the reduction of nitrate, but only the ones for the nitrite [43]. However, there have been con-
tradicting studies on the nitrate reducing capacity of PAO clade I.
In a recent study by Rubio et al., 2017, it has been shown that a highly enriched PAO I clade
culture has not been able to denitrify from nitrate. Instead, the culture which contained both
DPAO and GAO, has been able to denitrify from nitrate. Thus, in the same study it has
been hypothesized that DPAO need to work in synergy with other denitrifying organisms, that
can carry out the partial denitriﬁcation from nitrate to nitrite. Also, these results highlight
the possibility of a competitive/synergistic relation between GAO and DPAO. [62] In fact, in
a even more recent study, it has been found that PAO clade IC could not carry out mere
denitriﬁcation from nitrate. The high diversity that characterizes PAO communities and the
apparently conﬂicting results reported in literature imply that more investigation is needed in
this sense. [63]
1.3
Nitrogen removal in WWTP
Nitrogen can be present in wastewater in two main forms: biodegradable and non-biodegradable.
Regarding the biodegradable part, nitrogen can be found in form of ammonium (NH+
4 ) and
contained in organic compounds such as amino acids.
[71] Ammonium, if not removed, is
transformed to nitrate or nitrite in the receiving water bodies, causing a lack of oxygen and
contributing to eutrophication. Furthermore, the un-ionized state of ammonium, ammonia
(NH3), which is formed at higher values of pH, is highly toxic to aquatic organisms [75]. The
average content in wastewater can range between 30 to 100 mg N/L in terms of total nitrogen
and between 20 to 60 mg N/L in the form of ammonia nitrogen (NH3 and NH+
4 ) for low to
high strength wastewater [25]. The requirement for the average concentration of nitrogen in
eﬄuents from WWTPs in Denmark is 8 mg/L for total nitrogen [31]. Thus, eﬃcient meth-
ods are needed in order to guarantee such low concentrations. The removal of nitrogen from
wastewater is achieved eﬃciently through biological processes which are also considered to be
cheaper than physio-chemical processes [71].

Nitriﬁcation
The nitriﬁcation is a two-step process in which ammonium is converted to nitrate by ammonium-

1.3.3
Simultaneous Nitriﬁcation and Denitriﬁcation
As nitriﬁcation and denitriﬁcation require diﬀerent conditions, aerobic and anoxic respectively,
in conventional WWTP processes they are carried out in separate reactors. In most cases, the
treatment is based on suspended growth biological systems which require large tanks, a recy-
15
==PAGE:16==
cling system of the sludge, extensive oxygen supply and often addition of carbon substrates.
In numerous studies, the possibility of achieving Simultaneous Nitriﬁcation and Denitriﬁcation
(SND) in one reactor at low concentrations of DO has been shown [50][20]. Performing these
processes in only one tank lowers the environmental footprint of the treatment process. Also,
the need to add chemicals such as acids and bases for pH control is reduced.
As described previously, the nitriﬁcation reduces the alkalinity of the system. Thus, as the
denitriﬁcation produces alkalinity instead (3.5 g in terms of CaCO3 per g of NO−
3 reduced), it
contributes to mitigate about half of the eﬀect on the pH from nitriﬁcation [21].
Furthermore, the need for carbon sources is also reduced by approx. 22–40% with respect to
conventional treatments [65].
The nitriﬁers are slow-growing microorganisms which in conventional suspended growth sys-
tems require a long solid retention time. In this sense, attached growth technologies are ben-
eﬁcial as they retain the biomass and avoid the wash-out of nitriﬁers. For this reason, many
types of technologies have been developed which utilize bioﬁlm to accomplish nitriﬁcation.
1.4
Membrane bioﬁlm reactor
The membrane bioﬁlm reactor or membrane-aerated bioﬁlm reactor (MABR) is an innovative
wastewater technology which provides bubble-less aeration to the bioﬁlm, via direct oxygen
supply.
The main conﬁgurations are ﬂat sheet and hollow ﬁber MABR. The hydrophobic membrane
separates the liquid phase on the outside of the membrane from the lumen in which the gas
is supplied. [41] The membrane has the double function of being the surface for the attached
growth and to diﬀuse aeration into the bioﬁlm through the pores present in the ﬁber [35]. The
oxygen diﬀuses from the inside of the membrane to the bioﬁlm. The latter is in contact with
the bulk liquid on the shell side. A simpliﬁed representation of a MABR conﬁguration is shown
in ﬁgure 2.
16
==PAGE:17==
Figure 2: On the left, a vertical section of a typical MABR conﬁguration. The pollutants diﬀuse from the bulk
liquid to the bioﬁlm, while the oxygen counter-diﬀuses from the lumen of the membrane to the bioﬁlm. On the
right, a cross-section showing all the layers of a MABR.
In conventional systems, the bioﬁlm is characterized by both aerobic and high substrate con-
ditions, whereas in a MABR, the oxygen is counter-diﬀused from the lumen side to the shell
side, where it is in contact with a substrate-rich bulk liquid.
Due to biomass growth and increase of the bioﬁlm thickness, the oxygen cannot reach the
bulk liquid. This stimulates the stratiﬁcation within the bioﬁlm, allowing diﬀerent types of
microorganisms to exist. This phenomenon is an advantage for nitrogen removal and makes
the SND possible.
In particular, the nitriﬁers grow close to the gas supply where the organic load is the lowest
and the concentration of oxygen is the highest. The denitriﬁcation on the other hand, occurs
in anoxic conditions close to the bioﬁlm-bulk liquid layer, where the concentration of nitrate,
nitrite and carbon sources is the highest [35]. A representation of a typical stratiﬁcation within
the bioﬁlm layer is shown in ﬁgure 3.

Many are the advantages of MABR systems, which is the reason why it has attracted attention
in the recent years.
In a conventional system, oxygen is provided through ﬁne bubble diﬀusers which show a gas
transfer eﬃciency as low as 5-25% and it accounts for 40-60% of the energy consumption of a
WWTP [4]. In a MABR instead, the oxygen is diﬀused through the hydrophobic membrane
speciﬁcally to the nitriﬁcation bioﬁlm. By monitoring the contact time of oxygen with the
bioﬁlm, it is possible to obtain an oxygen transfer up to 100%, reducing enormously the energy
consumption and the environmental footprint of the WWTP [35].
Furthermore, higher biomass thickness has been observed for MABR systems than conventional
ones. However, this has to be carefully controlled in a MABR in order to avoid membrane foul-
ing. In order to obtain a successful SND, the bioﬁlm must be thick enough to create diﬀerent
aerobic and anoxic conditions to allow the growth of nitriﬁers and denitriﬁers. However, a
high bioﬁlm thickness is limiting to substrate diﬀusion, causing a reduced removal rate of the
pollutants. Thus, a bioﬁlm thickness of 1-2 mm was suggested [66].
Advantages from having a bubble-less aeration also include minimal foam formation [72], re-
duced stripping of volatile organic compounds [10] and de-coupling of the mixing and aeration
processes.
1.4.1
MABR for phosphorus removal
The possibility to successfully combine Simultaneous Nitriﬁcation and Denitriﬁcation with
Phosphorus Removal (SNDPR) [34][74] has been shown already in several studies. The mi-
croorganisms responsible for this process are DPAO. As seen in section 1.2.4, DPAO uptake the
VFA in anaerobic conditions utilizing the pool of poly-P. In anoxic conditions, they consume
the stored PHA by utilizing nitrate/nitrite as electron acceptors and replenishing the poly-P.
Most importantly, they consume carbon sources from raw wastewater, denitrify and remove
18
==PAGE:19==
phosphorus during the process. The combination of phosphorus removal with SND can lead to
high savings in terms of both oxygen and carbon sources needed [34]. Furthermore, as DPAO
have a lower cell yield than PAO, less sludge would be produced, reducing the cost for the
disposal.
SNDPR has been achieved mainly through Sequencing Batch Reactors (SBR) [74]. An example
is the anaerobic/oxic SBR conﬁguration. However, long aeration periods are needed to allow
the nitriﬁers to proliferate, due to their low growth yield. This causes the inhibition of DPAO
in the aerobic phase, favouring PAO instead.
Due to the fact that denitriﬁers need to be
close to the source of electron acceptors (nitrite and nitrate), it is necessary that they are
adjacent to nitrifying organisms. Thus, it would be advantageous to have a single reactor for
both denitriﬁcation and nitriﬁcation with direct oxygen supply exclusively to the nitrifying
organisms. These issues can be overcome through a MABR system.
The possibility to perform SNDPR with MABR through DPAO is still unexplored in research,
despite the potential beneﬁts that could be achieved.
In fact, the MABR is characterized
by an internal stratiﬁcation in the bioﬁlm which allows the coexistence of both nitrifying
and denitrifying organisms. However, DPAO need alternating anaerobic-anoxic conditions to
perform successful phosphorus removal. Thus, the conditions within the bioﬁlm seem not to
be suitable for their growth. Furthermore, the bioﬁlm of a MABR is also populated with OHO
which feed on organic carbon and in presence of high organic load would out-compete DPAO.
As a consequence, DPAO are more likely to grow in the bulk liquid, where they can consume
the nitrate and nitrite that diﬀuses from the bioﬁlm, utilizing the carbon storage. To achieve
SNDPR, it is indeed beneﬁcial to boost the storage of PHA from DPAO in anaerobic conditions.
In this stage, it is important to control the growth of GAO as they compete with PAO for
VFA. Thus, operating conditions that are favourable for PAO should be chosen, such as low
temperature (< 30℃), a pH of 7.2-8 and a mixture of VFA (acetate and propionate) rather than
one single source of carbon [80]. In this way, it is possible for the enriched populations of DPAO
to outcompete the denitrifying OHO in the MABR at low concentrations of carbon sources [79].
In this scenario, it is not excluded that the growth of DGAO could also be observed in the mixed
liquor. As described in section 1.2.3, in anoxic conditions, the DGAO could cooperate with
DPAO, performing the ﬁrst step of denitriﬁcation from nitrate to nitrite. These hypotheses
need to be further investigated. There have been only few studies on the potential of MABR
to be used for phosphorus removal. More investigation is needed to understand whether DPAO
are able to grow in these conditions and whether they can compete with OHO living in the
bioﬁlm. The role of DGAO in the system should also be investigated: it is not clear whether
it would represent a threat for the EBPR or whether there could be a collaboration between
these types of microorganisms. Furthermore, it would be interesting to investigate the role
of the bioﬁlm alone in the EBPR. Pilot scale studies on MABR for SNDPR are necessary to
investigate further the applicability on real-scale systems.

Aim
The research on MABR for phosphorus removal is limited and no studies have been conducted
yet for a MABR at a pilot scale.
The objective of this research is to evaluate the MABR at Ejby Mølle WWTP for combined
phosphorus removal and simultaneous nitriﬁcation and denitriﬁcation; using mixed liquor, with-
out addition of carbon sources and at low DO concentrations.
Batch tests will be conducted on the activated sludge both at the MABR pilot plant and in
the laboratory.
From the experiments at the MABR pilot plant:
• The MABR tank will be run ﬁrst with mixed liquor and then with primary eﬄuent to
investigate the phosphorus removal potential of the bioﬁlm with mixed liquor and of the
bioﬁlm alone.
• The process performance of the MABR will be evaluated by monitoring the main pa-
rameters relevant for EBPR and SND. Thus, the concentration over time of ammonium,
nitrate, nitrite, phosphorus and soluble COD will be measured.
• The ammonia removal rate and SND eﬃciency will be calculated in order to assess the
nitriﬁcation and denitriﬁcation capacity of the bioﬁlm.
• Analogously, the uptake rate for phosphorus removal will be calculated to assess EBPR.
• Other relevant parameters will be monitored to investigate the conditions existing in the
MABR such as pH, DO, temperature and oxidation-reduction potential (ORP).
From the laboratory batch experiments:
• The phosphorus uptake/release ratio will give information on the GAO/PAO dominance
within the activated sludge.
• The phosphorus uptake in anoxic and aerobic conditions will be assessed to gain infor-
mation on the presence of PAO/DPAO.
• The phosphorus uptake rate from the laboratory experiments with mixed liquor will be
compared with the uptake rate from the MABR.

4.2
Laboratory batch activity tests
The EBPR process performance has been evaluated through batch activity tests on the acti-
vated sludge mixed with primary eﬄuent. As previously described in section 1.2, PAO are able
to uptake phosphorus in anoxic or aerobic conditions, after they store carbon in the anaerobic
phase. Thus, it has been decided to perform two types of batch tests: anaerobic followed by
aerobic conditions and anaerobic followed by anoxic conditions with nitrate as electron accep-
tor. The objective of these tests is to calculate the phosphorus uptake and release rates, which
will give information regarding the presence of GAO/PAO and DPAO/PAO dominance in the
activated sludge. Furthermore, it will be possible to assess the EBPR potential in standard
conditions and without carbon limitation.
4.2.1
Set-up
The batch activity tests have been conducted following the guidelines present in the book
”Experimental Methods in Wastewater Treatment” [37].
The lab experiments have been conducted on fresh activated sludge in the laboratory at the
EMWRRF, thus performed in situ and no later than 2 hours after collection. As the activity
tests all start with an anaerobic phase, the activated sludge has been collected at the end of
the aerobic phase. The primary eﬄuent has been used as substrate for the experiments. It has
been either freshly collected at the same time as the activated sludge or stored in the fridge at
5 ℃. When stored at low temperature, it has been ﬁrst brought to the same temperature as
the activated sludge before starting the experiment.
The bio-reactor has been provided with ports both for sampling and addition of substrates
or pH regulation. The bio-reactor has been equipped with a stirring plate and a magnetic
stirrer to provide adequate mixing. It has been inserted in a water bath to ensure an adequate
temperature, that has been checked with a thermometer. It has been connected to a multi-
probe for monitoring of pH and DO which has been calibrated before each batch experiment.
For the experiments, 2 L of activated sludge and 1 L of primary eﬄuent have been mixed in
a 3 L airtight bio-reactor. The primary eﬄuent and the activated sludge have been brought
to the desired temperature of 20 ℃. To favour the EBPR process, the pH should not be lower
than 7 (see section 1.2.3). Thus, the pH of the mixed liquor has been between 7 and 7.5 and
the variation during the experiments has been kept within a ±0.1 variation via HCl and NaOH
addition.
The stopwatch has been started right after the addition of primary eﬄuent to the sludge.
The samples have been collected at the beginning of the experiment and every 15 minutes
to calculate the substrate speciﬁc rates. They have been immediately ﬁltered through a 0.45
µm ﬁlter and stored in the fridge at 5 ℃. Then they have been measured the same day or
maximum after 24 hours. The MLSS and MLVSS values have been measured at the beginning
of the experiment.
4.2.2
Anaerobic-aerobic batch activity test
The test consists of an anaerobic phase, followed by an aerobic phase of both 1 hour. Through-
out the anaerobic stage, airtight conditions have been maintained to keep suitable conditions
25
==PAGE:26==
for COD uptake and phosphorus release. After 1 hour, the bio-reactor has been brought quickly
to aerobic conditions. Thus, concentrations of DO above 2 mg O2/L have been kept throughout
this phase, in the range of 4-6 mg/L.
4.2.3
Anaerobic-anoxic batch activity test
The test consists of an anaerobic phase of 1 hour, followed by an anoxic phase of 2 hours. As
before, airtight conditions have been maintained throughout the anaerobic stage.
To obtain anoxic conditions, a nitrate standard solution with a concentration of 1000 mg NO3/L
has been used. As suggested by Van Loosdrecht et al., 2016 [37], the concentration of nitrate
in the bulk liquid should not exceed 20 mg N/L. Thus, a suitable volume of the solution has
been calculated to be 100 mL, which would result in an initial concentration of 7.45 mg N/L in
the bio-reactor. However, in a preliminary experiment, it has been observed that a too rapid
decrease of nitrate occurs for the complete phosphorus uptake process to take place. Thus, it
has been chosen to introduce nitrate in two stages: 100 mL of nitrate solution at the beginning
of the anoxic phase and 50 mL after 1 hour.

4.4
Calculations
4.4.1
Speciﬁc kinetic rates
Phosphorus release and uptake rates
The phosphorus release and uptake rate are repre-
sentative of the speed at which the phosphorus is released in the anaerobic phase and uptaken
in the aerobic phase respectively.
The experimental concentration of phosphorus has been projected over time and a linear regres-
sion has been ﬁtted to the sampling points. In this way, it is possible to calculate the volumetric
kinetic rates which correspond to the slope of the line. For this purpose, it is suggested that
the statistical determination coeﬃcient (R2) should be above 0.90 [37]. Considering the con-
centration in milligrams per liter and the time in hours, the unit measure of these rates can
be expressed in mg L−1 h−1. As the experiments have been carried out using freshly collected
activated sludge, it has been possible to calculate the phosphorus release and uptake rates by
dividing the volumetric rates by the concentration of VSS in the bulk liquid.
Ammonia removal rates
The ammonia removal rate can be used to evaluate the nitriﬁca-
tion potential of the MABR. If nitrite or nitrate accumulation is not observed in the reactor,
then simultaneous nitriﬁcation-denitriﬁcation is occurring and the ammonia is completely re-
moved from the system. The decrease in ammonia concentration over time is used to calculate
the volumetric kinetic rate as described before. Because the nitriﬁcation is done by the mi-
croorganisms living in the bioﬁlm, the volumetric kinetic rate for ammonia is multiplied by
the volume of the MABR and divided by the bioﬁlm surface. Thus, the ammonia removal rate
would be expressed in g N m2 d−1.

4.4.4
GAO-PAO dominance
As described in section 1.2.3, GAO have a similar metabolism to PAO, meaning they can store
carbon but they do not release or uptake phosphorus. It follows that if the system would be
dominated only by GAO, carbon uptake would be observed during the anaerobic phase with
no phosphorus release. On the contrary, if the P-release would be higher than the C-uptake,
this could be linked to the abundance of PAO. [81] Thus, from the lab batch activity tests, to
obtain information on the population of GAO and PAO in the system, the P-release/C-uptake
ratio has been calculated.

5.1.2
PAO-GAO dominance
In all the lab batch experiments, the soluble COD has been decreasing over time considerably
in the anaerobic phase and little to no decrease has been observed in the aerobic/anoxic phase
(see ﬁgure 5 and ﬁgure 6 as examples). This shows that the heterotrophic populations present
in the system might have been mainly PAO and GAO, as not enough carbon would have been
available for normal heterotrophs in the aerobic/anoxic phase. Furthermore, it has been found
that the most abundant population between GAO and PAO in the system is likely to be GAO.
According to the stoichiometric model proposed by Smolders et al., 1995 [68], the P-release/C-
uptake ratio for the PAO metabolism is 0.5 P-mol C-mol−1, while the one for the GAO
metabolism, according to Zeng, R.J. et al., 2003, is 0 P-mol C-mol−1 [85].
As the P-release/C-uptake obtained from the lab batch activity tests resulted to be 0.23 P-mol
C-mol−1, it can be hypothesized that the most dominant are GAO. Furthermore, it has been
suggested in literature that for standard conditions (i.e. pH=7, Temperature=20℃, acetate as
substrate), a value <0.25 for the P-release/C-uptake rate is associated with a GAO dominated
metabolism, a value > 0.50 is associated with a PAO dominated metabolism and ﬁnally a value
between 0.25 and 0.50 with a mixed population [37]. As a rough estimate, this might again
indicate that the activity sludge subject of the experiments is populated mostly by GAO. The
uncertainties related to the COD and phosphorus uptake and release rates previously discussed
should however be kept in mind.
Nevertheless, also in previous experiments at the EMWRRF, it has been found from 16s am-
plicon sequencing analysis of the sludge that the known GAO Candidatus Competibacter are
the dominant phosphorus removal related genera [76].
In two preliminary experiments performed on nitrite as electron acceptor in the anoxic phase,
nitrite and nitrate decrease has been observed but no phosphorus removal. Also, no COD
decrease has been observed in the anoxic phase, thus indicating that the responsible for nitrate
decrease might have been mainly GAO, pointing to the fact that they are more abundant in
the sludge.

Simultaneous nitriﬁcation-denitriﬁcation combined with phosphorus removal has been observed

been found that GAO are the main responsible for the denitriﬁcation in the mixed liquor
experiments, having the possibility to use the carbon storage formed in the anaerobic stage.
For the primary eﬄuent, the high denitriﬁcation activity observed can be linked to OHO living
in the bioﬁlm, as the concentration of MLVSS in the bulk liquid has been very low.
Regarding the SND process, a higher average ammonia removal rate has been observed for the
experiments with primary eﬄuent when considering the ammonia removal rates obtained from
the SCADA. When considering the values measured manually, ammonia has been removed at
the same rate as in the experiments with mixed liquor.
However, the experiments with primary eﬄuent showed more instability in the system. A wide
variance and a constant increase in the pH values has been observed. Also, an initial inhibition
of the SND in one of the replicates with primary eﬄuent occurred. It should be also taken
into account that in the normal operation of the MABR, the bioﬁlm is not exposed to such
high concentrations of COD, which might have contributed to the instability that has been
observed. The nitriﬁcation might have been the main aﬀected process. Nevertheless, linear
removal of soluble COD throughout the experiment has occurred for the experiments with
primary eﬄuent, suggesting a high denitrifying activity in the bioﬁlm.
39
==PAGE:40==
6
Conclusion
In this study, SNDPR has been successfully achieved in a MABR present at the EMWRRF.
• Successful SNDPR has occurred in the experiments with mixed liquor. The ammonia
removal rate has been equal to 0.79±0.15 gN m−2 d−1 and the SND eﬃciency to 95 %.
The average phosphorus uptake has been 0.61±0.45 mg P g VSS−1 h−1. The DGAO
are found to be main responsible of the denitriﬁcation process, while the DPAO have
carried out phosphorus uptake.
The P-uptake rate found in the experiments at the
MABR is lower than the one found in the lab batch tests. This could be both due to the
competition of PAO with GAO, which are more abundant in the activated sludge, and
to the competition with the OHO in the bioﬁlm.
• The presence of DPAO in the mixed liquor has been conﬁrmed from the lab batch activity
tests, in which it has been shown that they are considerably more abundant than PAO.
As phosphorus removal has not been observed in the experiments with primary eﬄuent,
it has been hypothesized that the DPAO do not grow in the bioﬁlm. As aeration has
been continuously diﬀused through the bioﬁlm during the experiments, there has been
no alternation between anaerobic and aerobic or anoxic conditions which is needed for
phosphorus uptake. Thus, the DPAO likely live only in the mixed liquor, where they
utilize the NOx diﬀused from the bioﬁlm for phosphorus uptake. This should be conﬁrmed
by microbiological analysis both on the bioﬁlm and the activated sludge.
• Successful SND has occurred in the experiment with primary eﬄuent. The ammonia
removal rate has been 0.82±0.39 g N m−2 d−1 and the SND eﬃciency has been 90 %.
However, the system has shown more instability than in the experiments with mixed
liquor and inhibition has been observed. This has been linked to the high organic load
of the primary eﬄuent.

==PAGE:41==
7
Future Perspectives
Several aspects need to be further investigated regarding the potential of the MABR at the
EMWRRF to be used for SNDPR:
• In this study, it has been hypothesized that both DGAO and DPAO coexist in the mixed
liquor of the MABR but not in the bioﬁlm. Also, it has been shown that DGAO might
be more abundant than DPAO. Molecular techniques need to be applied both on the
bioﬁlm and the mixed liquor to reveal the microbial communities present in the MABR
and conﬁrm these hypothesises.
• Diﬀerent P-uptake rates have been obtained in the diﬀerent replicates of the experiments
in the MABR with mixed sludge. Thus, more experiments are needed to obtain a better
evaluation of the phosphorus removal potential.
• The speciﬁc kinetic rates obtained through the experiments can be utilized for the con-
struction of a bioﬁlm model of the MABR to test diﬀerent operational conditions such
as higher organic loads and higher concentrations of ammonia.
