Abstract
The green transition emphasizes the role of Power-to-X (PtX) technologies, particularly
Power-to-Methanol (PtMeOH), as transformative levers for reducing environmental impacts
within the chemical industry. This study investigates the environmental implications of pro-
ducing formaldehyde via PtMeOH pathways, integrating prospective Life Cycle Assessment
(pLCA) with Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) to address the methodological chal-
lenges posed by emerging, data-scarce technologies. A novel framework is introduced to
model technological evolution from First-Of-A-Kind (FOAK) to Nth-of-a-kind (NOAK) con-
figurations by quantifying explorative environmental learning rates (eLRs) and constructing
environmental learning curves (R2≥0.91). This is achieved by sequentially developing and
scaling eight e-formaldehyde configurations that integrate methanol production, hydrogen
generation and formaldehyde synthesis. Generative AI models, Qwen3-8B and DeepSeek-
R1-Distill-Qwen-7B, are deployed to enable AI-guided flowsheeting and creative synthesis,
proposing innovative configurations based on engineering constraints and feasibility. These
designs are validated via Aspen Plus V14 simulations and their environmental impact is as-
sessed using the IMPACT World+ life cycle impact assessment methodology. The foreground
system’s evolution is mapped over four doublings of cumulative experience (2025-2065), while
background transformations are aligned with Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSP1, SSP2,
SSP5) and RCP1.9 climate scenarios. Results show that integrating environmental learning
substantially improves learning-enabled environmental performance forecasting, with reduc-
tions of up to 71% in climate change impact and significant gains across other categories such
as freshwater acidification, particulate matter formation and ozone depletion. The study re-
veals critical burden-shifting phenomena, where decarbonization efforts may inadvertently
intensify mineral resource use or human toxicity unless paired with technological advance-
ment. Regardless of the selected SSP pathway, burden-shifting tendencies are counteracted
by advancements in the foreground system. Ultimately, this study demonstrates that com-
bining GenAI-driven knowledge-based design space expansion with pLCA offers a robust
methodology for forecasting the environmental trajectories of emerging technologies. It pro-
vides valuable insights for decision-makers in policy, engineering and investment, supporting
strategic navigation under deep uncertainty.

Highlights
• Developed a hybrid framework that integrates generative artificial intelligence with
prospective life cycle assessment (pLCA) to evaluate emerging technologies under fu-
ture uncertainty.
• Applied a novel approach to quantify environmental learning rates (eLRs) without

• Embedded environmental learning dynamics within the pLCA framework to capture

Introduction
The industrial transition towards climate neutrality has intensified interest in Power-
to-X (PtX) technologies for the sustainable production of chemicals and fuels [11]. Among
these, Power-to-Methanol (PtMeOH) systems are gaining recognition as a promising plat-
form for synthesizing methanol-based derivatives using renewable electricity [12]. Methanol
is not only a versatile platform molecule widely used across the chemical industry, but also
increasingly regarded as a key energy carrier and carbon-neutral feedstock in PtX pathways
[13].
A particularly impactful application within the concept of methanol economy is linked to
the production of formaldehyde [14]. Currently, more than 35% of global methanol output
is directed toward formaldehyde synthesis, making formaldehyde the largest direct down-
stream product of methanol use [15]. Derived through methanol oxidation, formaldehyde
is one of the most widely produced chemicals globally and serves as a critical intermedi-
ate in the manufacturing of adhesives, plastics, coatings and high-performance resins [16].
Formaldehyde is most commonly stored and distributed as an aqueous solution called for-
malin, containing around 37 wt% formaldehyde in water [17]. Formalin can be produced
from a plethora of raw materials such as natural gas, coal, biomass and other carbon-based
sources. The most common production process is carried out through steam reforming of
natural gas, proceeding to methanol synthesis from the produced synthesis gas and finally
formalin synthesis. More than 90% of formalin production globally is produced by the natu-
ral gas reforming via the methanol synthesis route [18]. As such, reducing the impact of its
fossil fuel-based production could generate substantial downstream environmental benefits,
with positive spillover effects across multiple industrial value chains. Methanol, which is
required to produce formalin, can also be obtained using alternative production pathways.
While natural gas-based production pathways are most common, there has been a significant
increase in research of more renewable pathways [19]. Companies with global reach such as
LEGO Group, Novo Nordisk and A.P. Moller-Maersk are exploring green alternatives to
conventional methanol in order to reduce their fossil fuel emissions [20]. A key milestone
is the inauguration of the first commercial-scale e-methanol production facility in Kassø,
Denmark in 2025 by European Energy, which produces e-methanol via the PtX pathway
[20].
Despite their environmental potential, PtX-derived chemical systems remain at an early
stage of commercial maturity, with most demonstration projects operating electrolyzers be-
low 6 MW, which translates to hydrogen production capacities of under 3 tonnes per hour
[21]. These first-of-a-kind (FOAK) technologies are typically marked by limited process op-
timization, suboptimal efficiencies and elevated environmental burdens when compared to
more refined Nth-of-a-kind (NOAK) configurations [22]. Assessing their long-term environ-
mental performance is therefore, methodologically challenging. Studies on methanol synthe-
sis showcase that the footprint of conventional methanol production is considered to be high,
ranging between 0.6 kg CO2-eq. per kg of methanol for natural gas-based processes [23] and
up to 3.8 kg CO2-eq. per kg of methanol for coal-based synthesis routes [24]. Integrating
renewable energy sources into methanol synthesis has proven effective to reduce environmen-

tal impacts as shown by Chen et al. [25] who conducted a life cycle assessment (LCA) of
an integrated atmospheric biomass gasification cycle and reported net-negative greenhouse
gas emissions of -109.2 kg CO2-eq. per kg of methanol. Additional studies have reported
that Power-to-Methanol pathways utilizing renewable solar energy can achieve climate ben-
efits, with GHG emissions between -325 and -654 kg CO2-eq.
per kg of methanol [26].
Conventional sustainability assessment methodologies, such as LCAs, predominantly rely on
retrospective data and mature system boundaries, making them inadequate for evaluating
emerging technologies like PtX, where data availability is limited, technological pathways are
still under development and future application contexts remain uncertain [27, 28]. As a re-
sult, assessments that fail to incorporate foreground system evolution risk producing flawed
projections. A more robust approach calls for learning-enabled sustainability forecasting,
which explicitly accounts for the progressive technological improvements that unfold as ex-
perience accumulates [29]. These conventional approaches lack the dynamism to map how
the environmental profile of emerging technologies might evolve as they scale and mature,
limiting their relevance for prospective assessments.
To address this limitation, recent advancements in prospective life cycle assessment
(pLCA) enable the evaluation of future environmental performance by integrating projected
changes in background conditions, such as regional energy mixes and sector-specific emission
factors [30], through the use of integrated assessment models (IAMs) [31]. This approach is
often implemented using future-oriented life cycle inventory databases that align with explic-
itly defined Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) [32] and Representative Concentration
Pathways (RCPs) [33]. However, most existing pLCA applications remain confined to mod-
eling background changes, overlooking the dynamic evolution of the technologies themselves.
This lack of foreground life cycle foresight limits the ability of such assessments to capture
the full spectrum of sustainability outcomes for emerging systems undergoing learning, scal-
ing and design optimization. At present, despite their relevance, quantified environmental
learning rates that link technological evolution, scaling effects and experiential learning to
reductions in environmental impacts are still largely unavailable. As a result, there is a
strong need to systematically derive and apply such learning rates to enable more robust
environmental assessments of emerging technologies.
Chemical process modeling plays a fundamental role in evaluating the environmental
and operational performance of emerging technologies. By simulating process parameters,
energy and material flows, it provides a systematic perspective on how design modifications
and scaling influence environmental performance. However, the exploratory identification
and design of future process configurations is often complex and time-consuming, due to
the large design space and interdependencies of advanced systems. This presents a major
bottleneck in evaluating prospective system behavior and learning trajectories. These chal-
lenges highlight a growing need for expedited process design strategies that can streamline
configuration development and broaden the scope of feasible design pathways. In this regard,
the integration of artificial intelligence (AI) presents a compelling opportunity to augment
traditional modeling by enabling faster, more adaptive and data-driven process innovation
by reducing the trial-and-error approaches and automating the process design [34].
Artificial Intelligence has been applied in chemical and process engineering through
classic machine learning techniques such as artificial neural networks (ANNs) [35] and ran-

dom forest (RF) algorithms [36]. These models have been successfully utilized for various
chemical engineering tasks such as modeling hydrothermal gasification of biomass [37], reac-
tor modeling [38], reaction kinetics [39], transport phenomena [40], fault detection [41] and
multi-objective optimization [42]. By learning patterns from experimental or simulated data
[43], they offer valuable predictive capabilities across a wide range of other industries such
as controlled drug delivery [43] and chemistry [44]. However, as the dimensionality of the
input space increases, due to the growing number of process variables, operating conditions
and constraints, these models require large [45], high-quality datasets [46] for effective train-
ing. This data requirements limits their applicability in early-stage and low-data contexts,
particularly in the case of emerging technologies. In contrast, the recent surge in generative
AI (GenAI) tools introduces new opportunities for modeling and design. Applications of
GenAI models in domains such as drug discovery [47], protein design [48] and materials
science [49] have indicated promising capabilities in learning complex patterns, generating
novel designs and expediting research workflows. Generative AI models offer a promising
pathway for exploring a broader design space and identifying alternative process configu-
rations. Recent developments have demonstrated the capability of generative transformer
models in flowsheet design and optimization, particularly when chemical processes are en-
coded as structured sequences or graphs [50]. These models are capable of capturing complex
relationships between process inputs and outputs, enabling the generation of feasible and
innovative design alternatives [50]. Large Language Models (LLMs) have been investigated
for tasks such as flowsheet autocompletion, error correction and novel process synthesis [51].
Yet, as also highlighted by Life Cycle Initiative hosted by UN Environment Program [52],
their potential remains untapped in the fields of environmental science and process systems
engineering, marking a significant opportunity for innovation. Exploring the use of GenAI
in this domain constitutes a key novelty of the present study.
This study addresses this gap by introducing a hybrid framework that quantifies and
applies explorative environmental learning rates (eLRs) to the foreground process of e-
formaldehyde production via Power-to-Methanol pathways. Instead of relying on histori-
cal performance data, the framework simulates a sequence of FOAK-to-NOAK transitions
through virtualized technology evolution, where each successive configuration reflects im-
provements in energy and material efficiency, emissions control and utility demand. These
eLRs are then embedded into the prospective life cycle inventory, enabling the construc-
tion of prospective learning trajectories that capture the environmental progression of the
foreground system over four doublings of cumulative experience, projected from 2025 to
2065.
A distinguishing feature of this study is the integration of generative artificial intelli-
gence (GenAI) into the process design and optimization workflow. By guiding generative
models with engineering constraints and process feasibility boundaries, the AI facilitates
design space expansion beyond conventional boundaries, uncovering novel NOAK config-
urations that are often inaccessible through traditional heuristics. This AI-guided design
space expansion is validated through process simulation and the resulting configurations are
assessed using life cycle modeling to quantify their environmental performance. The collab-
oration between computational creativity in engineering and established simulation methods
enables a dynamic exploration of technological possibilities. More importantly, it introduces

a novel mechanism for embedding learning-by-innovation within the pLCA framework, of-
fering a forward-looking approach to sustainability assessment that captures not only what
is known today, but what might emerge through engineered discovery.
The case study models a Power-to-Formaldehyde system that utilizes green hydrogen
produced via commercial alkaline water electrolysis. The FOAK plant is assumed to com-
mence operations in 2025 with a capacity of 1 metric ton of e-formaldehyde per hour. En-
vironmental impacts are modeled under three SSPs (SSP1, SSP2, SSP5) and one RCP
scenario (RCP1.9) to capture the combined effects of foreground learning and background
transformation. In parallel, the study evaluates the influence of technology doubling time
and experience accumulation rate on long-term environmental performance.
While prospective life cycle assessments have been carried out for various e-fuels such
as e-ammonia, e-methanol, Fischer Tropsch synthetic diesel and liquefied e-natural gas [53],
the environmental performance of formaldehyde remains unexplored.
As a high-volume
platform chemical with significant industrial importance, e-formaldehyde represents a critical
gap in the current understanding of Power-to-X systems. This study addresses that gap by
presenting a methodological framework that can be applied to emerging technologies lacking
historical data, incorporating both foreground technological learning and background system
transformation in a unified prospective assessment.
Ultimately, the results reveal that omitting foreground technological learning leads to
substantial underestimations of future environmental benefits. In contrast, the combined
application of AI-guided design exploration and environmental learning modeling provides
a more comprehensive and realistic projection of environmental trajectories. Moreover, this
study advocates for moving beyond the prevailing climate-centric tunnel vision that often
characterizes prospective assessments. While decarbonization remains essential, environmen-
tal assessments must consider a broad range of impact categories rather than focusing solely
on climate change. By explicitly incorporating multiple environmental impact categories
into the modeling process, the framework enables more nuanced trade-off analyses and helps
ensure that climate mitigation strategies do not unintentionally exacerbating other planetary
boundaries. The proposed approach aligns with broader system-level assessment paradigms
and fosters decision-making grounded in systemic insight and multi-dimensional impact nav-
igation, ensuring that technological advancement supports not only climate objectives but
also respects ecological limits and resource boundaries.
This study advances both methodological robustness and real-world relevance in prospec-
tive environmental assessment, offering a decision-support tool for guiding clean technology
development, policy design and strategic planning under deep uncertainty.

2.1
Goal and scope of the study
The primary objective of this study is to evaluate the environmental impacts and scal-
ability associated with formaldehyde production from e-methanol (eMeOH), while conduct-
ing a long-term prospective life cycle assessment spanning the period from 2025 to 2065.
Page 9 of 101
==PAGE:16==

To quantify environmental learning and the accumulation of process-related knowledge, a
range of techniques in process synthesis and modeling are applied to design, optimize and
scale the production pathway from synthesis gas to eMeOH and ultimately to formaldehyde.
The environmental learning rates derived from this analysis serve as a basis for assessing
progress within the assessed system. In addition, these rates are integrated into prospec-
tive environmental impact assessments, in which background system evolution is aligned
with descriptive Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) and Representative Concentration
Pathways (RCPs), allowing for consistent scenario development across both foreground and
background systems.
The functional unit in this study is defined as 1 kg of formaldehyde produced in Power-
to-Formaldehyde systems located in Denmark. The assessed plant capacities range from
approximately 1 to 250 metric tons of e-formaldehyde per hour. E-formaldehyde is syn-
thesized via the catalytic BASF process using e-methanol, which is derived from synthesis
gas combined with green hydrogen produced through alkaline water electrolysis. A cradle-
to-gate system boundary is established, encompassing raw material extraction, electricity
consumption, methanol production, hydrogen generation and formaldehyde synthesis across
the relevant life cycle stages. The system boundary also includes in-process emissions derived
from in silico process simulations, as well as the associated utility demands.

2.3
Quantifying environmental learning rates
The overall methodological framework used for quantifying environmental learning and
scaling effects follows the explorative approach outlined by Fozer et al. [1]. Wright’s founda-
tional observation on learning effects suggests that production costs decline by a consistent
percentage with each doubling of cumulative experience, a pattern that can be expressed
mathematically using a power-law relationship [57]. Technological advancements contribute
to enhanced eco-efficiency by reducing environmental burdens and costs. In this study, learn-
ing curves are utilized in place of experience curves to minimize the uncertainties associated
with price volatility, providing a more stable basis for assessment [1]. This approach over-
comes the absence of historical environmental learning rates from literature, especially for
early-stage technologies [1, 58]. To address this gap, we rely on established economic learning
proxies and rules of thumb from similar technologies [22] and derive environmental learning

[1, 58]. This methodology enables the systematic estimation of environmental improvements
where empirical data is limited, making the assessment more reliable and transparent for
emerging technologies [59, 58].

Artificial intelligence is employed to aid with the optimization and scaling up of the
NOAK configurations. More specifically, the DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B [60] and Qwen3-

[63]. RAG enables domain-specific knowledge without altering the underlying model weights,
allowing for faster integration of new information while reducing computational and data
demands [64].

of memory. These hardware specifications impose limitations on the deployment of more

ing engine with a model capacity of 7.62B parameters, optimized for efficient deployment
on mid-range hardware such as the M2 MacBook Air. Despite its smaller size, it achieves
strong performance on math and text-reasoning benchmarks due to its distilled architecture
and instruction-tuned training process. The model supports a maximum context window of
32,768 tokens, making it suitable for many reasoning-intensive applications without requiring

2.5.1
Background system modeling
Prospective evaluations are conducted to assess anticipated techno-economic and envi-
ronmental progress, which depend on a range of influencing factors. These include potential
changes in socioeconomic systems, climate change mitigation and adaptation strategies, de-
velopments in the energy sector, demographic and cultural shifts, advancements in infrastruc-
ture and technology and the implementation of emission reduction measures [68]. Integrated
Assessment Models (IAMs) offer valuable insights into long-term development challenges by
representing key processes within human and earth systems and capturing their interactions
across economic, energy and climate domains [69]. These models quantify Shared Socioe-
conomic Pathways (SSPs), which consist of alternative trajectories for global socioeconomic
development and are often used alongside Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs)
to explore future climate scenarios. In this study, the background system is established using
cut-off datasets from the ecoinvent v3.8 database [70], while prospective life cycle inventory
databases are generated using the premise v1.8.3 framework [30]. This approach enables
the integration of IAM-based projections into the prospective life cycle assessment (pLCA),
aligning foreground technological developments with broader systemic transitions.
Prospective life cycle inventories are developed based on three SSPs, 1,2 and 5. These
SSPs are selected to cover a broad range of plausible trajectories for future global devel-
opment [71]. These SSPs are widely used in integrated assessment and climate modeling
to characterize differing levels of climate policy ambition, socioeconomic development and
environmental outcomes [32]. SSP1, represents a sustainability-oriented trajectory often re-
ferred to as the "green road", reflects a world making rapid progress towards sustainability,
with low challenges to mitigation and adaptation [68]. It aligns with scenarios that aim to
limit GMST increase to below 2°C, consistent with the goals of the Paris Agreement [72].
SSP2 reflects a continuation of current trends and is characterized as the "Middle of the
Road" scenario [68]. It results in GMST increases of approximately 2.6-3.2°C by 2100 if
no new climate policy is implemented, corresponding to a radiative forcing level of around
6.5 W m-2 in 2100 [72, 73]. SSP5 depicts a development path heavily reliant on fossil fuels,
commonly described as the "highway" approach, when coupled with high radiative forcing
pathways [68]. This scenario can lead to GMST increases exceeding 4°C by 2100 [74]. SSP2
scenario is also assessed in conjunction with the RCP 1.9 climate policy target to reflect
the influence of strong climate policy intervention, representing ambitious climate mitiga-
tion efforts. RCP 1.9 targets a radiative forcing level of 1.9 W m-2 by 2100, consistent with
net-zero CO2 emissions by mid-century and limiting GMST increase to approximately 1.5°C
[72]. This combination enables exploration of strong mitigation effects within a "middle of
the road" socioeconomic setting, enhancing policy relevance. These three scenarios capture
a wide spectrum of plausible socioeconomic and climate policy futures, enabling the analy-
sis of environmental and technological performance under varying boundary conditions. By
evaluating e-formaldehyde production across these contrasting pathways, the study exam-
ines how future systemic developments may influence both foreground and background life
cycle impacts.

3.1
Developing the FOAK-to-NOAK stages
The Power-to-Formaldehyde process is a multivariable system, characterized by a vast
configuration space and numerous degrees of freedom, each of which can be leveraged to
enhance process performance and scalability. In this study the critical variables identified
as influencing the production rate and overall process performance include the synthesis gas
mass flow rate ( ˙msyngas (kg h−1), the synthesis gas composition, specifically the fractions of
H2 (ZH2 (-)), CO (ZCO (-)), CO2 ( ZCO2 (-)) and CH4 (ZCH4 (-)), the number of reactor
tubes in the methanol production reactor (NMeOH (-)), the catalyst loading in the methanol
production reactor (Ccat,MeOH (kg)) and the number of reactor tubes in the formaldehyde
production reactor (NHCHO (-)).
At each scale-up stage, these parameters are adjusted to optimize the process perfor-
mance while also reducing production costs. The stage development relied on a traditional
trial-and-error method, known as the manual approach, where sequential experimentation
guided the tuning of the process variables. Table 2 presents the range of the optimization and
scale-up during the manual approach. More detailed process parameters and performance
from FOAK to NOAK8 for the Manual Approach can be seen in Table A20. The molar com-
position of the synthesis gas remains within realistic and industrially achievable limits, as
supported by existing literature [77]. The number of tubes selected in this study falls within
a realistic range observed in industrial applications. Smaller-scale reactors may contain sev-
eral hundred tubes, while large, world-class reactors can operate with several thousand. A
typical example is the catalytic methanol synthesis reactor developed by Topsoe, which is
used for converting synthesis gas, primarily composed of hydrogen, carbon monoxide and
carbon dioxide, into methanol [78]. The reactor is designed as a vertical tubular unit, with
catalyst material packed inside the tubes. Industrial-scale designs generally include between
2,000 and 4,000 tubes, each approximately 7 meters in length [78]. When a large number
of tubes is required, the configuration can also be achieved by dividing the design across
multiple reactor shells to ensure operational feasibility and flexibility [79]. It is important
to note that in the NOAK 8 configuration, the higher heating value (HHV) efficiency of the
electrolyzer is improved from 0.75 to 0.82 [80].

Three distinct strategies are applied to carry out the optimization: (1) manual trial-
and-error-based tuning and process design, (2) technology development with DeepSeek-R1-

ducted manually to set a benchmark as described in Section 3.1. Figure 5a illustrates the
economic learning curve constructed manually. Even though the manual approach show-
cased a satisfactory coefficient of determination (R2=0.9749) for the economical learning
curve complying with the learning targets, the technological learning curve showcased a R2
of 0.863 for energy conversion efficiency and R2 of 0.835 for electricity consumption which
does not comply with the high standards set suggested by Thomassen et al. [22]. The issue
lies in the NOAK5 configuration, where the energy conversion efficiency and electricity con-
sumption deviate from the expected technological learning targets, as illustrated in Figure
7a. To enhance NOAK5 configuration performance, the DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B gen-
erative AI model is deployed for the NOAK5 configuration, resulting in an improvement over
the manual approach in the economic learning rate (LR = 12.48% vs. LR = 12.35%), the
technological learning rate for energy conversion efficiency (tLRη = 3.75% vs. tLRη = 3.56%)
and the technological learning rate for electricity consumption (tLRε = 10.89% vs. tLRε =
10.4%). Of great importance is the improvement of the R2, which increased to 0.916 from
0.863 for energy conversion efficiency and to 0.908 from 0.835 for electricity consumption,
which is borderline satisfactory. However, a relative error of 16.95% from the theoretical
learning target is also observed, as indicated by the green marker in Figure 4.
In pursuit of constructing more robust and representative learning curves and reducing
the relative error from the theoretical learning target, the Qwen3-8B model is deployed
across the NOAK3, NOAK4 and NOAK5 configurations, which has a higher model capacity,
making it suitable for deeper reasoning and multi-turn analysis. This benefits tasks such as
engineering design optimization, where logical planning and multi-hop analysis are essential.

reliance on wind turbines, solar PV and battery storage increases the demand for critical

case, climate change impacts continue to decline, yet mineral resource use either increases
or remains elevated, revealing a disconnect between decarbonization and material efficiency.

changes not only offset earlier increases but also align material use more closely with decar-

4.1
Reflections on the use of GenAI
The application of Generative AI in this study aligns with recent calls for integrating
machine learning into early-stage chemical process design, particularly in cases where the
number of possible design combinations becomes too large to evaluate individually using
conventional trial-and-error methods [87]. In this study, upon constructing the initial eco-
nomic and technological learning curves, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B and Qwen3-8B are
deployed to improve the curves. The use of these LLMs expedites the manual process of
exploring the design space and is targeted to satisfy specific goals, in this case, specific
production costs, energy conversion efficiency and electricity consumption. The use of the
LLMs is straightforward given the LM studio interface. A critical aspect of this work lies in
the prompt engineering strategy used to engage the large language model (LLM) in process-
related predictions. To enhance the relevance and accuracy of the outputs, the model was
prompted to have a specific role to provide contextual and specialized responses, for ex-
ample, "You are a process engineer working on a project where formaldehyde is produced
from methanol." This approach helped frame the model’s reasoning within the context of
chemical process design. Furthermore, the prompts were constructed in a highly descriptive,
narrative style to facilitate understanding. The critical parameters were stated in detail.
Process conditions and relationships were conveyed through detailed textual descriptions as
well as the economic and technological learning targets. This linguistic framing enabled the
model to better understand dependencies and propose new configurations. To effectively
implement the RAG approach, the input documents must be properly sanitized. This in-
volves ensuring that the documents contain all relevant technical details while minimizing
irrelevant content. Well-structured and concise documentation enhances the model’s ability
to retrieve accurate information and generate high-quality responses. Poorly formatted data
can lead to hallucinations, thereby compromising the reliability of the RAG workflow.
DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B model required multiple runs to produce outputs that
were usable in the Aspen Plus simulation environment. The primary issue was its tendency
to generate answers too quickly, without engaging in sufficient reasoning over the process
context. This resulted in unsatisfactory suggestions that failed to meet the learning targets.
The quality of the output improved when remained in "thinking" mode more. On the other
hand, Qwen3-8B, a model more suitable for deeper reasoning and multi-turn analysis, pro-
vided more accurate results faster that aligned with the economic and technological learning
targets that the model was instructed to give. This model spent more time in "thinking"
mode before generating an output.
The parameters generated by the model were first evaluated for technical feasibility.
If deemed reasonable, they were implemented and tested within the Aspen simulation en-
vironment. A limitation observed in both models concerned the handling of synthesis gas
composition. Despite explicit prompt instructions to ensure that the component fractions
sum to one, the models failed to enforce this constraint. Instead, they predominantly fo-
cused on optimizing only the hydrogen (H2) fraction, without appropriately adjusting the
mole fractions of carbon monoxide (CO), carbon dioxide (CO2) and methane (CH4). While

4.2
Generative AI in Life Cycle Assessment and process systems
engineering
Life Cycle Assessment is a data-intensive methodology, requiring detailed inventory data
across multiple life cycle stages. However, one of the challenges in applying LCA, especially
for emerging technologies, is the lack of high-quality, comprehensive life cycle inventory data
[88]. Available information is limited to lab-scale or pilot-scale operations, which may not
reflect the conditions or performance of full-scale deployment [89]. For prospective or ex ante
assessments, where technologies are still in development and datasets are limited or absent,
traditional modeling approaches often fall short [89].
Generative AI tools offer opportunities to address these limitations by helping to fill
data gaps associated with scaling emerging technologies. Traditionally, analysts have used
known specific features of existing technologies to synthetically develop foreground data
for the emerging technology under assessment [90]. By leveraging LLM models fine-tuned
on specific domains, GenAI can assist in generating representing data and create plausible
LCI based on process descriptions or analogical reasoning from related technologies. For
instance, models can propose missing input-output flows, estimate resource consumption
and even propose proxy datasets when direct measurements are unavailable.
Beyond filling data gaps, GenAI shows potential in generating process flowsheets. In this
study, GenAI was used to construct digital twins capable of suggesting process configurations,
which were validated through ASPEN simulations. This integration demonstrates a shift in
LCA workflows, from relying solely on bottom-up data collection to incorporating top-down
scenario generation assisted by GenAI.
Despite these advances, the integration of GenAI into LCA practice requires further
validation, especially in regard of the accuracy and reproducibility of AI-generated datasets.
While GenAI is not a replacement for expert-driven LCA modeling, it represents a powerful
complementary tool. With further research and development, it can accelerate sustainability
assessments in LCA and process systems engineering.

4.3
Assessing the environmental and techno-economic potential of
e-formaldehyde production
Formaldehyde is one of the most important chemicals, as it is primarily used as a
precursor in the production of a wide range of value-added products, highlighting the need
for a transition from fossil-based production to environmentally conscious feedstocks and
pathways [91].
According to Puhar et al.
[92], the highest climate change impacts are
obtained when utilizing fossil sources as main feedstocks. These impacts are reduced by
41% when switching to natural gas and up to 79% when renewable and waste feedstocks are
utilized [92]. A previous study reported that formaldehyde production using captured CO2,
a renewable feedstock, as input results in a climate change impact of approximately 3.344
kg CO2-eq. per kgHCHO [93], which aligns closely with the findings of the present study.
In this assessment, the climate change impact associated with the Power-to-Formaldehyde
pathway is estimated at 3.46 kg CO2-eq. per kgHCHO in 2025 under the SSP1 scenario where
emissions are projected to decline to 2.41 kg CO2-eq. per kgHCHO. Under the fossil-based
SSP5 scenario, climate change impacts are expected to be higher, ranging from 3.69 kg CO2-
eq. per kgHCHO in 2025 to 3.12 kg CO2-eq. per kgHCHO in 2065. However, these figures can
be further mitigated by taking into account learning effects. For instance, under the fossil-
intensive SSP5 scenario, the integration of learning effects reduces climate change impacts
to as low as 1.56 kg CO2-eq. per kgHCHO by 2065.
The formaldehyde production cost is linked to the methanol production feedstock [94].
A techno-economic study on conventional formaldehyde synthesis using natural gas over a
silver catalyst route reported a production cost of 0.86 EUR per kg [95]. The present study
estimates the production cost for the FOAK technology at 5.53 EUR per kgHCHO, but with
the evolution of the technology, the production cost is projected to decrease to 1.92 EUR
per kgHCHO by NOAK8. Taking into consideration the large-scale production and mature
infrastructure of natural gas, the price difference is to be expected [96]. As a PtX pathway,
the e-formaldehyde technology remains in the early stages of technological development and
is not yet cost-competitive. As technology improves, the e-formaldehyde production cost can
be reduced further.
4.4
Addressing burden shifting through foreground learning
While most midpoint impact categories such as climate change, fossil and nuclear energy
use, freshwater acidification, marine eutrophication, freshwater eutrophication, particulate
matter formation, ionizing radiation and photochemical oxidant formation, follow a declining
impact trend, this study uncovers trade-offs in other midpoint categories, revealing the
burden-shifting phenomenon. Specifically, mineral resource use impacts, land transformation
impacts and toxicity-related impacts do not decrease over time. In some cases, they even
increase, despite broader environmental progress.
A clear example is mineral resource depletion, which increases in sustainability-oriented
scenarios such as SSP1, SSP2 and SSP2-RCP1.9. Although these pathways prioritize decar-
bonization, they also accelerate the deployment of material-intensive technologies like solar
panels, wind turbines and battery storage [85]. As a result, SSP1 showcases a 6.85% increase
Page 46 of 101

in mineral resource depletion between 2025 and 2065. Toxicity-related impacts showcase the
burden-shifting phenomenon during the SSP2-RCP1.9 scenario due to the extensive deploy-
ment of low-carbon technologies and energy systems in SSP2-RCP1.9 [86]. This stands in
contrast to the average 29.63% reduction observed under SSP1, SSP2 and SSP5 scenarios
over the same period.
This illustrates the concept of "carbon tunnel vision" which is the tendency to focus
exclusively on reducing climate change impacts while overlooking other environmental di-
mensions. The issue arises when climate mitigation is pursued without considering its side
effects across broader environmental impact indicators. Although climate change impacts
are critical, they should not be treated as the sole metric of environmental performance,
especially in prospective assessments where systemic shifts are modeled.
The integration of environmental learning effects into the foreground system offers a
pathway to neutralize burden-shifting issues. Their inclusion led to substantial benefits be-
yond decarbonization. For instance, mineral resource depletion showcased a 23.6% reduction
during the SSP1 scenario between 2025 and 2065. During the SSP2-RCP1.9 scenario, im-
plementing environmental learning leads to reductions in environmental and human health
impacts by 2065, including a 25.63% decrease in freshwater ecotoxicity, a 25.11% reduction in
human toxicity (cancer) and a 24.39% reduction in human toxicity (non-cancer). These find-
ings emphasize the importance of applying foreground learning in achieving environmental
improvements across multiple impact categories.
4.5
Suggestions
To further improve the modeling accuracy and environmental performance of
e-formaldehyde configurations, the following methodological and design improvements are
recommended:
• This study did not incorporate pinch analysis.
Applying such an approach could
enhance thermal energy efficiency by optimizing internal heat recovery, which is par-
ticularly relevant for high temperature unit operations within the process.
• In industrial practice, formaldehyde is most commonly stored and distributed as an
aqueous solution known as formalin, typically containing around 37 wt% formaldehyde
in water. To accurately reflect reality, state-of-the-art thermodynamic models can be
utilized to simulate formaldehyde with water interactions and to represent process
conditions that more closely align with real-world operations.
• In this study, renewable electricity from wind power is considered exclusively for the
operation of the electrolyzer. However, the compressor is also an electricity-intensive
component of the process. Its integration with renewable electricity sources should be
further explored.
• The assumption of fixed technology doubling times may oversimplify real-world inno-
vation dynamics. Incorporating variable doubling times could potentially yield more
realistic projections

5
Conclusion
This study introduces a combined explorative and normative framework to quantify
environmental learning and scaling effects for emerging technologies in the absence of his-
torical data by integrating Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) with Prospective Life
Cycle Assessment (pLCA). It demonstrates its usability for assessing the future environmen-
tal performance potential of e-formaldehyde synthesis. Nth-Of-A-Kind(NOAK) Power-to-
Formaldehyde configurations are developed in accordance with theoretical learning targets
defined by the theoretical economic learning curve. The capacities of the plants range from
approximately 1 to 250 tHCHO h-1 and cover knowledge accumulation and scale-up of up to 8.0
doublings of experience. The incorporation of GenAI models, Qwen3-8B and DeepSeek-R1-
Distill-Qwen-7B, enabled knowledge-based design space expansion and process optimization,
outperforming the conventional trial-and-error approach in constructing robust technolog-
ical and economic learning curves with high explanatory power, meaning that the model
captures the dominant trends in performance improvements with scale, thereby enabling
confident forecasting. Prospective environmental progress is evaluated based on quantified
environmental learning rates and technology doubling times in the foreground system and
considers shared socioeconomic pathways (SSP1, SSP2, SSP5) along with a representative
concentration pathways SSP2-1.9 in the background system. The climate change impact of
the First-Of-A-Kind (FOAK) technology is reduced from 2.77-3.69 kg CO2-eq. per kgHCHO
in 2025 to 0.8-1.56 kg CO2-eq. per kgHCHO in 2065 when environmental learning rates are
applied. The importance of applying foreground learning in prospective life cycle assess-
ment is also highlighted by other midpoint categories, which showcase the burden-shifting
phenomenon. The application of environmental learning rates proves to be a consistently
effective approach to neutralize burden-shifting issues that occur in background system pro-
cesses.

7.1.1.1
Synthesis gas to Methanol
Synthesis gas (syngas) is a versatile feedstock that can be used to produce a wide
range of chemical products (e.g., alcohols, hydrocarbons and ethers) [97]. The synthesis
gas in this study is derived from the hydrothermal gasification of wet microalgae biomass
(Chlorella vulgaris), a renewable resource that captures atmospheric CO2 during growth,
thereby making it an integral part of the biogenic carbon cycle. This biomass is catalytically
enhanced with NaOH and upgraded via reforming processes to produce a high-quality syngas
feedstock for sustainable e-methanol synthesis as per Fozer et al. [37].

reaction (RWGSR), as shown in Equations 13-15, were considered for the synthesis of e-

simulation of this hierarchy block as it accurately models non-ideal phase behavior and
thermodynamic properties of the syngas-to-methanol system under high-pressure conditions
by combining an equation of state with activity coefficient predictions [98].

7.1.1.2
Alkaline Water Electrolysis
Water electrolysis can utilize renewable resources such as wind and solar energy to
decompose water into oxygen and hydrogen and the purity of the produced hydrogen can
reach up to 99.9%, which can be directly used for energy storage [101]. The Alkaline Water
Electrolysis (AWE) technology was selected for green H2 production due to its advanced
technological development, cost-effectiveness and its appropriateness for large-scale produc-
tion. As this study focuses on scalability and economic feasibility in hydrogen production
for e-methanol synthesis, AWE was considered the most viable and well-established method
[102].
For the modeling of the electrolysis process, the build-in electrolyzer reactor of As-
pen V14 was used.
For the property method, the Electrolyte Non-Random Two-Liquid
(ELECNRTL) model was selected, as it is well-suited for the simulation of aqueous solutions
and mixed solvents [103]. In the electrolyte wizard interface, the apparent component ap-
proach was used. The Aspen Plus flowsheet model of industrial scale alkaline electrolyzer
is presented in Figure 1.
The core component of the system is the alkaline electrolyzer
(ELECTRLZ), where water is split into hydrogen and oxygen at the cathode and anode,

is not predefined. To address this, a Design Specification (DS) was implemented, where the
required H2 for methanol synthesis is defined according to Equation 1. Given the specified
conditions, the total power demand and the feed water flow rate were calculated accordingly.

Capital expenditures (CAPEX) refer to the costs incurred in the construction of a new
plant or the modification of an existing one. CAPEX represents the total assets required
to establish a plant, which includes two primary components: the Fixed Capital Investment
(FCI) and the Working Capital (WC). The methodology employed to estimate the CAPEX

a sophisticated market, such as synthetic fibers. A typical value for petrochemical plants is
15% of the fixed capital, which was adopted in this study, in the sense that it is commonly
observed in large-scale industrial processes [112].

7.2.2
Life Cycle Inventory
The life cycle inventory (LCI) data and the corresponding SimaPro inputs are presented
in Tables A23 to A27 for the electrolyzer, Tables A28 and A29 for the Cu/ZnO/Al2O3 cat-
alyst used in methanol production and Tables A30 to A33 for the complete e-formaldehyde
production system for the FOAK to the NOAK8 plant configurations. The electrolyzer data
and SimaPro inputs were adapted from the work of Koj et al. [126], where the original
LCI, developed for a 6 MW electrolyzer, is proportionally allocated to match the capacity
of the electrolyzer used in each configuration. The inputs were expressed per functional unit
(FU), defined as 1 kg of formaldehyde produced in Power-to-Formaldehyde systems located
in Denmark. The electrolyzer and the Cu/ZnO/Al2O3 catalyst for methanol production
were modeled as separate processes within the LCI framework. The catalyst for methanol
production was defined per 1 kg [127]. It was assumed that the compressor operates us-
ing electricity supplied by the Danish grid, while the electrolyzer is powered by electricity
generated from onshore wind farms in Denmark. The entire methanol production line is
modeled using a dataset from the Ecoinvent database, which is based on a reference plant
with a production capacity of 2,700 tons per day and an assumed operational lifetime of
30 years. This dataset was proportionally allocated to reflect the capacity associated with
each NOAK configuration. Additionally, it was assumed that the same methanol production
dataset from Ecoinvent can be applied to represent the formaldehyde production line.
