Abstract
This thesis is built upon the predictive and stochastic reduced-order model of wind turbine
wake dynamics currently being developed at DTU using proper orthogonal decomposition.
The model in question has proven to be computationally efficient and accurate, but currently
only covers the thrust coefficient in the parameter space, and for practical purposes, it is
desired to predict the flow inside wind farms over a diverse range of operational conditions.
Consequently, this thesis expands the model to include the turbine number as a new parameter.
In order to do this, the global modes of the decomposition are calculated including information
from several turbine numbers, which provides the basis for flow reconstruction across a larger
region in the parameter space. Afterward, the dynamic aspects of the wake, such as the
meandering, velocity fields, the evolution in time of the modes in the flow decomposition,
and the correlation between modes, are studied as the flow enters the wind farm up to high
turbine numbers. This analysis reveals relevant physical characteristics of the wake and how
the model captures them, which allows an educated decision on what information to include
in order to expand the model in the turbine number dimension. Subsequently, the model
predicts the flow at certain turbine numbers using linear interpolation, and the results are
compared with the model using the LES data at these turbines. The result comparison
shows statistical agreement between the results, and the physical notions developed provide
insights into where in the parameter space more data is required to expand the model in
future iterations.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The demand for large-scale wind farms has grown at an accelerated rate during the past few
years and is expected to keep growing during the following decades, especially offshore [1]. To
reduce installation and maintenance expenses offshore turbines are located in clusters, but this
increases the importance of wake effects, which increase mean and fatigue loads, and reduce
power production [2][3][4]. Therefore, it is of high interest for developers and manufacturers
to have a clear and reliable understanding of the wind flow inside wind farms [5]. For this
purpose, numerous models have been developed with simplified physics, but it remains a
challenge to develop a fast and accurate model capable of predicting the dynamic nature of
the turbulent flow inside wind farms [6][7][8].
When it comes to wind turbine wakes the flow is divided into two regions, the near and
far wake. The near-wake typically extends 2-4 radii [2] and is primarily characterized by the
tip vortex from the blades in a helical structure, which is inherently unstable and eventually
breaks down [9]. After the breakdown, the far-wake starts and it extends up to where the wind
has recovered its initial streamwise momentum [8]. This region is characterized by a velocity
deficit in the streamwise direction, a significant increase in turbulence intensity with respect
to the atmospheric level, a reduction of the turbulent scales, and wake meandering [8][10].
Moreover, the far-wake is of particular interest in the design process because, inside wind
parks, turbines are located at a distance of 3 to 10 rotor diameters from each other, so they
operate in the far-wake of neighboring turbines [6][7].
There are different approaches for the modeling of the flow in the far wake. Several
engineering models, such as Jensen [11], Frandersen [12], and Bastankhah and Porté-Agel [13],
are used to predict the average flow of a single turbine. On top of them, when several wakes
are combined, a superposition model is implemented to predict the flow [14][15][16][17][18].
The main advantage of these models is their low computational cost and relative simplicity,
which makes them ideal for initial assessments in planning and development of wind farms.
However, these models are unable to predict the dynamic aspects of the wakes, which are
relevant for predicting the loads on the turbines [7][8].
To capture the dynamic effects of the wakes there are tools such as the Dynamic Wake
Meandering model (DWM) [10]. This model decomposes the wake in its three main blocks:
velocity deficit, turbulence in the wake, and meandering. Each block has its own model, some
of them introducing stochastic properties [19][20], and the final flow is the atmospheric wind
plus the sum of the contributions from each block. This model points in the right direction,
as it includes some of the most important dynamic features of the flow. However, it has
some inherent limitations, such as the implicit assumption that the contributions of each
block are independent of each other. From turbulence theory [21], it is known that assuming
independent contributions is a good first approximation, but it is not entirely correct as large
==PAGE:14==
2
1 Introduction
turbulent structures pump energy into smaller eddies, i.e. the energy cascade, and also small
turbulent scales can influence back into larger ones. A second limitation arises from the
model used for the meandering, which is assumed to be purely a consequence of the wake
behaving as a passive tracer. This has satisfactory results in the case of a single turbine.
However, as it has been suggested [22], when several turbines are included, the periodic cut
of the wake by the turbine spacing constrains the large turbulent scales, and further into the
wind farm self-organized motions strengthen.
The shortcomings of models such as DWM are, to some extent, a consequence of trying
to represent phenomena as inherently complex as turbulent flow with an inexpensive model.
This complexity emerges due to the chaotic and highly non-linear nature of the equations
describing the flow, which makes it a particularly tough challenge not only in engineering but
also in physics and math [6][23].
An alternative option to capture the wake dynamics is using CFD. This is the most
accurate and detailed alternative as it directly aims to solve the fundamental governing
equations of the flow, Navier-Stokes. Some studies have already proven that methods such
as Large Eddie Simulations (LES) can accurately model the flow and performance of large
wind farms [4][24][25][26]. However, it requires specialized engineers to generate reliable
simulations, and, more importantly, the computational cost of CFD makes it prohibitively
expensive for regular industrial applications.
To bridge the gap between practicality and detailed results from rigorous CFD simulations,
data-driven methods can be applied to databases constructed from CFD results to uncover
key aspects of the flow, and subsequently, use them to develop alternative models. There
are multiple methods to decompose large data-sets [27], and one of the most extensively
used in fluid mechanics is the Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD), which provides an
optimal linear subspace in terms of turbulent kinetic energy, and consequently, it is desirable
for studying turbulent flows [28][29]. In the context of wind farm flows, this decomposition
has been proven useful to reveal the underlying mechanisms of energy entrainment and wake
recovery [30][31][22][32].
Furthermore, when the flow has been decomposed, it is possible to build Reduce-Order
Models (ROM) by truncating the number of modes used in the decomposition. Reduced-Order
Models have been used to reconstruct single flow cases [33][34], and they have tried to be
expanded to generate stochastic flows [35][36][37]. However, these attempts have failed to
use POD to develop a predictive and stochastic reduced-order model [6]. In response to this,
a recently developed model hypothesizes that with a ROM based on flows with different
operational conditions, the flow can be predicted under new conditions without having to
run more CFD simulations [38].
This proposed reduced-order model exists in space as a function of the parameters that
produce the flow. These parameters are the operational conditions for the wind farm, such as
thrust coefficient (CT), turbine number inside the wind park (TN), atmospheric turbulence,
boundary layer stability, and yaw, among others. Each of these variables represents one
dimension in the parameter space of the reduced-order model, and for each point in this
space, there is a corresponding flow in the wind farm, which is dynamic and stochastic. The

problem with creating such a model is that it would require a large number of extensive
CFD simulations to produce the flows that generate the ROM. For this reason, a robust
understanding of the flow physics in terms of the reduced-order model is desired, as it could
be used to explore the parameter space more efficiently.
The model in question [38] has been developed at DTU with a database built with LES
using the actuator disc technique [39], and POD to decompose the flow and generate the
ROM. The model has proven to be computationally efficient, as it can generate flows faster
than the Mann turbulence box [19][20], used to generate turbulent atmospheric flows required
by DWM. Furthermore, results show that the model is capable of accurately recreating the
flow, especially at low frequencies, and it can reasonably reproduce the statistics of the loads
on the turbine. The main limitation of the model is that currently only covers CT in the
parameter space, which is the dominant parameter in the far-wake [40], but covering a larger
portion of the parameter space is required to implement the ROM in real applications. This
is where this thesis aims to contribute.
1.1
Problem Analysis and Research Questions
With the reduced-order model developed at DTU as a start point [38], this thesis aims to
expand it to include the turbine number as a new parameter. This is an important parameter
as describes how the flow transitions from single wakes to a deep array, which indicates if
and when the flow dynamics achieve convergence as the average characteristics of the flow do
[8]. Researching this evolution in turbine number is important as it can point towards the
zone of the parameter space where more simulations are required.
Furthermore, the physical characteristics of the flow as it enters the wind farm are
described in terms of the features of the reduced-order model, such as meandering and
cross-spectral density of the POD coefficients. Finally, these results are explored as a tool to
help better predict the flow in conditions that have not been simulated.
This research objective can be synthesized in the following question:
How does the flow change across different turbine numbers and how are these changes
manifested into the POD coefficient’s shape and dynamics?
To answer this question, it is split into different sub-questions that are interconnected:
1. How are the main aspects of the wake, such as meandering, captured by the reduced-
order model? For instance, what are the main physical roles of the spatial modes and
their cross-spectral density (CSD)?
2. How do the global modes of the ROM change when the turbine number is included?
3. How do the modes’ spectra vary as a function of the turbine number? For instance:
How are the effects of the atmospheric turbulence present in the spectra across the
parameter space?
Do the spectra converge far into the wind farm as seen in other flow properties?
4. How are these physical notions useful for predictions with the model? For instance:

How do the physical notions aid to predict the flow in intermediate conditions of CT or
turbine number?
Does this physical understanding provide any useful insight when expanding the model

1.2
Contributions
As this thesis was developed, several contributions were made to the ongoing research. The
most relevant are the following:
• Showing that the global modes do not change significantly when the new dimension,
turbine number, is included. This is a significant contribution due to the scaling of
computational resources required to execute POD when larger data sets are considered.
• In addition to the CSD and mode correlation analysis, the wake dynamics were depicted
in terms of meandering and its corresponding phase space.
• An analysis of the wake dynamics evolution as it enters the wind farm in terms of the
POD coefficients. This provides insights into where in the parameter space the flow has
larger gradients and consequently more data is required.
• A better understanding of the metrics and filtering that should be implemented to
compare the spectra yield by the model.
• It has been shown to what degree of accuracy the reduced-order model can be used to
predict flows in a parameter space including the turbine number and small changes in
CT using linear interpolation.
1.3
Thesis Outline
The second chapter provides a detailed description of the reduced-order model, which includes
the characteristics of the CFD simulations that generated the data, the available parameter
space, a brief introduction to proper orthogonal decomposition, and the structure of the
ROM. Chapter 3 contains the wake aerodynamics in the context of POD, which means that
aspects of the flow, such as velocity deficit and meandering, are presented as a function of
the number of modes used to recreate the flow. Chapter 4 explores the dependence of the
global modes on the turbine number. Once the global modes have been settled, the flow can
be constructed at any turbine number and the results are directly comparable. Consequently,
Chapter 5 explores the flow across the different turbine numbers. Chapter 6 describes the
proposed interpolation points in the parameter space including CT and turbine number, and
compares the interpolated with the original flows. Every chapter has a discussion of the
results immediately presented, but further discussions are presented in Chapter 7. Finally,
the conclusions are presented in Chapter 8.

CHAPTER 2
Reduced Order Model
Given that this thesis is built upon the Reduced Order Model (ROM) developed at DTU [38],
it is imperative that the reader understands the model. Therefore, this chapter describes the
simulations that generated the flow, specifies what data was extracted to build the model,
and the parameter space covered. There is a breve explanation of the proper orthogonal
decomposition (POD) and how it is used in the current model. Finally, the structure of the
ROM is explained, and it is specified where it has to be modified to carry out this thesis.
2.1
CFD Simulation Description
The data used to construct the model was generated using the incompressible 3D flow solver
EllipSys3D [41][42][43], and is the same data used for previous studies of the ROM [38].
EllipSys3D solves the discretized Navier–Stokes equations in general curvilinear coordinates
using a block-structured finite volume approach in a collocated grid arrangement.
The Navier-Stokes equations are numerically solved using Large Eddies Simulations (LES).
This means that EllipSys3D applies a spatial filter to the equations; everything above this
filter is directly solved, and everything below it, is modeled through a sub-grid scale (SGS)
model. Importantly, this method does not rely on time averages and, although is more
expensive, it typically produces more detailed results than RANS models.
The implementation of a Large Eddy Simulation is required for the kind of analysis
desired. Given that the objective is to study and predict the wake dynamics, it would be
inadequate to use a computational method based on time averages, like RANS. Alternatively,
methods such as direct numerical simulation (DNS) can almost perfectly predict the physics
but are far too computationally expensive. Therefore, LES was chosen because it can predict
the relevant dynamics and is computationally viable.

2.2
Proper Orthogonal Decomposition
The principal component analysis (PCA), closely related to the singular value decomposition
(SVD), is the fundamental dimensionality reduction technique for probability and statistics,
which is commonly used in data science and machine learning applications [27]. This is closely
related to the proper orthogonal decomposition (POD), first introduced by Lumley [28], with
a more general view provided by Berkooz [29].
In the context of fluid mechanics, the proper orthogonal decomposition yields a linear
subspace optimal in terms of variance, which corresponds to turbulent kinetic energy [22][27].
To explain the reasoning behind this decomposition, take as an example the Kármán vortex
street of the flow around a cylinder presented in Figure 2.5. With the POD, the original
flow is decomposed into a mean flow and several POD modes. In this manner, the original
flow can be reconstructed by summing the mean flow with the POD modes vibrating at
certain frequencies; which is analogous to a Fourier decomposition of a signal, but with the
decomposition in a space optimal in turbulent kinetic energy instead of frequency space.
Figure 2.5: POD in the laminar flow around a cylinder, image taken from [45].
The advantage of this decomposition is that a good approximation of the flow can be
achieved without having to sum up all the modes. Therefore, a model can be truncated at a
certain point, hence the name reduced order model.
Further details with regard to the mathematical model behind POD are presented in the
Appendix A.
When this decomposition is applied to a single flow, including data from only one CFD
simulation, the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the auto-covariance matrix of the data are

2.3 Reduced Order Model Structure
11
2.3
Reduced Order Model Structure
The main structure of the reduced order model is divided into five steps depicted in Figure 2.7:
1. The input to the model are the LES results for different flow cases to cover a range of
the parameter space. The current model covers three values of thrust coefficients. The
data is extracted at a plane parallel to the rotor in front of each turbine as is shown in
Figure 2.2. The simulation was saved after a very long time to ensure stationarity and
ergodicity, and a long time was saved to capture the behavior at low frequencies.
2. The proper orthogonal decomposition is performed on the data to calculate the global
modes discussed in section 2.2. This step allows a truncation at a chosen number of
modes, which reduces the number of dimensions required to represent the flow. As the
number of data sets included in the decomposition increases with the expansion of the
parameter space, the computational cost of the decomposition increases significantly.
However, this step has to be executed only once to create the reduced-order model.
3. The flow of each data set is projected into the global modes to determine the time series
that describes the contribution of each mode in time. The complex cross-spectral density
matrix (CSD) covering all the modal time series is used to describe the second-order
statistics of the flow in terms of cross-covariance functions for different modal time
series.
4. Given the CSD representation of the flow at several points in the parameter space, new
points, which correspond to flows that have not been simulated, can be predicted. This
is done by interpolating the CSD within the parameter space. This is the predictive
aspect of the ROM.
5. Once the CSD of the new flow has been obtained it has to be translated into a time series
for each mode. This is done with white noise to apply a random phase to each frequency
component and use the inverse Fourier Transform. This provides the stochastic nature
of flow generation. There is a more detailed description of the flow recreation process
from a spectrum in Appendix B.
Further details about the model structure are presented in the model’s article [38].

==PAGE:26==

3 Wake Aerodynamics in the ROM
As Figure 3.1 shows, the flow around the turbine is divided in three regions, the induction
region upstream, and the two sections downstream, the near-wake and the far-wake.
The induction region is the result of the flow reducing its velocity and increasing its
pressure due to an imminent obstacle, the wind turbine. The reduction in velocity is mainly
a function of the distance to the rotor, the rotor’s diameter and the turbine’s induction
factor (a) [46]. Where the latter is intimately related to the thrust coefficient. As an example,
for a wind turbine close to rated conditions (a ≈0.3), the wind speed is gradually reduced
as it approaches the rotor from u0 (streamwise velocity) up to 0.7u0 at the rotor, and it is
approximately 0.91u0 one radius upstream [46].
The near-wake is the region immediately after the turbine and typically extends 2-4 rotor
lengths [2]. This zone is characterized by the tip vortex in a helical structure and the hub
vortex. The tip vortices can reduce the entrainment by separating the inside region from the
outer flow [47], similar to how the vortex created by the front wing of an F1 car seal the
flow around the sides of the car. Consequently, the average velocity deficit is nearly constant
close to the center and it bleeds out until it becomes a smooth distribution in the far-wake.
The near-wake is particularly susceptible to parameters such as the tip-speed ratio (λ), pitch
angle, and geometry of the blades, hub, nacelle, and tower. However, these dependencies
decrease as vortices break and the flow becomes more uniform in the far-wake [2].
The far wake is the main concern of this study as it is the zone where downstream
turbines are located, and it is where the data from the simulations is extracted. As the
tip-vortices no longer exist in this region, it is easier for turbulence to transport momentum
from the atmospheric flow into the wake, which eventually recovers the wind’s velocity.
The main characteristics of this region are the velocity deficit in the streamwise direction,
significant increase in turbulence intensity with respect to the atmospheric level, and the
wake meandering. The first two characteristics are reflected in the average flow, but the last
one, the meandering, is a purely dynamic phenomenon in the wake.
The streamwise velocity deficit is a direct consequence of the extraction of kinetic energy
by the turbine. Once into the far-wake, the time average deficit is approximated by an
axisymmetric self-similar Gaussian distribution [48][49][8]. Engineering models have been
developed to capture this deficit, as it is the most relevant in the first stages of designing a
wind farm. Some models capture the average deficit with a non-physical approach [11][12],
and others can predict the self-similar Gaussian distribution [13]. As expected, the LES
results also predict the later distribution as shown in Figure 3.2.

transporting momentum back into the wake, which determines the wake’s growth rate [2][4][8].

(in this case in the streamwise direction) and its mean at a given point.

the wake is next to the highest atmospheric velocities, which cause large shear stresses and
consequently higher turbulence. Finally, notice that approximately after TN4 the profiles
appear to be fully developed.

The meandering, depicted in Figure 3.6, relates to the random unsteady oscillations of the
entire wake with respect to the time-averaged wake centreline [8]. It is a strictly dynamic
characteristic of the wake and has a significant impact on turbulence and fatigue loads of

the wake center is calculated as the weighted average in each dimension as shown in Eq. 3.2.
In this context, the meandering is the evolution of the wake center in time, so it is determined
by YC(t) and ZC(t).

system’s attractors.

Global Modes
The global modes are the basis of the flow decomposition. Once the modes are known, it is
only required to know how each of them should evolve in time to recreate the flow. In this
case, the global modes are non-dimensional, have u, v, and w components in the plane where
the flow is reconstructed, and are ordered based on their turbulent energy content (variance)
in descending order.
A POD decomposition from a single simulation provides the modes1 that are optimal
to decompose the variance, TKE, of the flow in the data provided. If the decomposition is
done over two or more simulations, the resulting modes are on average the optimal basis to
decompose the turbulent kinetic energy of all the simulations included, hence the name global
modes. These global modes may not be the optimal basis for each simulation separately but
are optimal when all are considered. The advantage of using global modes is that all the
simulations have the same basis, so their dynamics only differ by how the modes evolve over
time, which is their CSD.
From the ROM already developed [38], it was possible to compare the modes of each
simulation with the global modes when one turbine number is analyzed at three thrust
coefficients (3 simulations included). From here it was noticed that the overall shape of the
modes remains almost the same and only small differences exist. This is shown for the first
6 modes in u direction in rows 1 and 2 of Figure 4.1, where the first row corresponds to
one simulation, and the second row includes information from 3 simulations (1 TN at 3 CT
values). There are minor differences such as the order of the modes, notice that mode 1 in
the first row corresponds to the shape of mode 2 in the second row, but this is simply a
reordering of which modes contain more energy in the flow reconstruction; the main takeaway
is that their shapes are almost the same.
This result suggested that if more turbine numbers were to be added to the decomposition,
the global modes would also be very similar. Which is the case, as illustrated in the third row
of Figure 4.1. This row has the modes when turbines 2, 5, and 9, each of them at 3 CT values
are included in POD. As expected the overall shape of the modes is very close to previous
cases.
This is a very advantageous result due to how the computational time required scales with
the number of simulations included in POD. As an example, when the modes in Figure 4.1

across the wind farm. However, for a detaedil comparison including frequency, the spectra
must be compared, but this is not entirely trivial. As spectra in a log-log scale have a lot
of oscillations in the medium and high-frequency region, a smoothing filter is desired. The
problem is that simple filters and rolling mean methods that take a constant window of data
tend to lose very valuable information at low frequencies and much of the undesired noise at

high frequencies remain. For this reason, all the spectra comparisons in this thesis include a
filter of the spectrum with a rolling mean in logarithmic scale, shown in Figure 5.9.

is the point i in ascending order of the frequency in the spectrum, the filter averages data
between points 10log10(i)−0.01 and 10log10(i)+0.01 to calculate the filtered value at i. As shown in
Figure 5.9, the logarithmic rolling mean preserves the data at low frequencies and takes a
reasonable average at high frequencies.

To quantify the difference between two spectra it is necessary to introduce the error
metrics, where “error” only makes reference to the difference and not necessarily something

The first metric: M1, named metric 1, is defined in Eq. 5.1, where ˆSi is the filtered
spectra i. This is meant to sum every difference between the spectra and normalize by the
area of one of them. This metric is exclusively additive, meaning that errors do not cancel
each other, which can be desirable. The disadvantage is that it includes simple stochastic
variance as errors and the final result can be misleadingly high. Therefore, this is the most
strict metric.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
The large flow characteristics, meaning the velocity field which is a sum of all the modes, the
meandering which is an average of the deficit in the velocity field, and the lowest modes, which
shape the largest turbulent structures and have the most TKE, present converging tendencies
as a function of the turbine number. Their convergence depends on the thrust coefficient and
occurs at different rates for different characteristics. These results, together with the evolution
of the correlation between modes, describe the transition of wake dynamics from single wakes
to deep array wind farm flow. However, in order to conclude that the flow dynamics are
developed and the deep array flow has been achieved, a converging criterion is required.
Depending on the criterion, CT, and the flow characteristic observed, the convergence is
achieved at different points across TN, and some of them may require extending the flow
beyond TN11.
On the other hand, smaller flow characteristics, such as the evolution of medium and
high POD modes present non-monotonous dynamics, which could be related to self-organized
motions in the wake. However, given that the large flow characteristics only converge after
approximately TN7, only four turbines can be used to describe the deep array dynamics as a
function of the turbine number. This is insufficient to determine if the self-organized motions
are actually occurring or are just fluctuations in the results.
Additionally, according to the results in Chapter 6, this behavior in medium and high
modes introduces errors in the interpolated flows. But they seem to be smaller than the
stochastic error in the realizations because the overall distributions of the results seem to be
unaffected.
Concerning how to compare the flow, the statistical comparison presented is based on the
spectrum of a given parameter, velocity, meandering, or a given mode. However, the analysis
with the modes can be complemented if also the cross-spectral density between all realizations
are compared, which is related to the coherence. But to do it some technical obstacles must
be considered, one of them being the size of the data stored. For the realization presented, in
each seed the spectra of the three velocity components at the hub, the two components of the
meandering, and the spectra of 50 modes were saved, this is 55 spectra per seed. If instead of
the spectrum of each mode, the entire CSD is stored, 1280 spectra per seed would have to be
stored1, which quickly depletes the storing capabilities, at least available to students. And
notice that this data must be saved because the statistical comparison requires comparing
every realization to every other realization.
Additionally, another flow aspect not included explicitly in this thesis that could hold
valuable insights into the flow’s evolution with the turbine number is the turbulent scales

With respect to the interpolation, the accuracy of the results in the present study suggests
that including less than four turbines in TN may be feasible. Furthermore, the largest
gradients in the phase space found so far are at high CT and low TN, which is where
future iterations of the reduced order model should include more data, and is an interesting
proposition to test in future studies.
Moreover, the next step in testing the interpolation performance is comparing it with the
validation case used previously to validate the ROM. This case is from a different simulation,
where the thrust coefficient is intermediate between the high and middle CT values used
to develop the model. Comparing against this case is a tougher challenge than the cases
presented in Chapter 6 because the differences in CT are much larger, but it can confirm or
provide new information about the zones in the parameter space where more turbines are
required.
With respect to the interpolation methods used, there are three aspects in which the
results can be improved. The first is to replace the linear with non-linear interpolation
methods; these utilize more of the available data in the parameter space and are capable of
better predictions. The second is the implementation of interpolation in non-uniform grids,
as the phase space itself is not uniform, and more data is desired where the gradients are
larger, like at high CT where the convergence in TN is slower, it is beneficial to efficiently
interpolate in non-uniform data-sets. Finally, the model should also include extrapolation,
currently something breaks when the model tries to predict outside the defined range, even if
it is just differing by an infinitesimal amount; for some reason extrapolation method produces
Not a Number (NAN) in the new CSD and it does not work. All of these three aspects are
currently being developed in the research group at DTU.
Finally, this study has provided valuable insights into the evolution of the flow and the
model as a function of the turbine number, but there are still big challenges ahead of a
reduced-order model like this one. The main one is the inclusion of several other dimensions
in the parameter space, for which the data required grows alarmingly fast. For this purpose,
future iterations may consider the use of Fourier-Bessel functions or auto-encoders as an
alternative to POD. Some of these methods, or others, may prove better or more efficient in
overcoming the immense challenge of modeling turbulent flows in an extensive parameter
space, a challenge that is not unique to wind farm flows.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusions
This thesis has built upon the reduced-order model developed at DTU by including the
meandering analysis and exploring the expansion of the model including the turbine number
dimension with linear interpolation.
The global modes from POD only present minor differences when several turbine numbers
are included in the computation. Furthermore, it was shown that recreating the flow with
different sets of global modes, including data from three or nine points in the parameter space,
has significant effects only at low frequencies when the number of modes used to recreate the
flow is very small, around five. Due to most flow reconstructions using over five modes, the
global modes chosen for the model are not a critical decision. This can potentially reduce the
computational power required to build a larger reduced-order model, as it implies that fewer
simulations have to be included to find the global modes.
The meandering analysis proved that it is captured by the first modes of the model. As
these modes are the main contribution of the large turbulent structures in the flow, it is
consistent that they are the main component to capture the wake center movement. It was
also found that the convergence of the meandering as a function of POD modes is significantly
faster than the convergence of the velocity field. So, to capture the meandering and velocity
field with the same level of accuracy, fewer modes are required by the meandering.
The analysis of the meandering probability distribution showed how the domain of the wake
center changes depending on CT and TN. As the turbine number increases, the meandering
domain increases when CT is high and decreases when CT is low. Furthermore, the phase
space analysis showed that the velocity of the wake center tends to decrease as it enters the
wind farm. And in polar coordinates, two or four stable points can be found in θ depending
on the vertical deflection of the wake, which depends on CT and TN.
The analysis of the correlations between modes showed that there is a significant change in
the correlations as the turbine number increases, which is consistent with previous findings [38].
Furthermore, it was shown that the correlations tend to converge as the turbine number
increases.
The statistical comparison of the spectra showed that characteristics of the reconstructed
flow, such as the velocity field and meandering, tend to converge as the TN increases. This
convergence is faster when the thrust coefficient is low, which is attributed to the fact that
when CT is low, the overall differences between low and high TN are smaller. Furthermore,
the convergence was found to be faster for the velocity fields than the meandering.
The statistical comparison of the modes spectra showed that low modes tend to converge
monotonically, resembling the behavior of the meandering and velocity fields. Nonetheless,
medium and higher modes behave differently. Their overall difference between low and high
==PAGE:58==

8 Conclusions
TN is smaller, but as TN increases they do not have a monotonous evolution. However,
despite different modes presenting different dynamics as a function of TN, the aggregated
measure of all of them suggest a convergence, which is also a function of the thrust coefficient.
The relatively smooth converging tendency with TN of some dynamics characteristics, such
as the first modes, meandering, velocity field, and the modes’ coherence, are indications of the
transition from single wakes to deep array wind farm flow. This converging process depends
on the thrust coefficient having smaller gradients when CT is low. In order to determine
if the flow has converged or not, a criterion of acceptable variance should be implemented.
And some characteristics converge at different rates, for example, the velocity field tends to
converge faster than the meandering. Therefore, the convergence criteria implemented should
have insights into what aspects are desired to capture.
Nonetheless, the statistical convergence of some flow characteristics does not imply that
all its dynamics converge as a function of TN. It was shown that medium and high POD
modes have a non-monotonous evolution with the turbine number, and, at least up to TN11,
convergence was not apparent.
Finally, there was an excellent agreement between the flow constructed from the projected
LES data and the flow constructed from interpolating the CSD mainly across TN. The
statistical agreement shows that it would be impossible to tell from which case a random flow
sample was taken. This means that even though some flow characteristics are not monotonous
across TN and introduce error in the interpolated result, the total error introduced is smaller
than the stochastic variance in the original flow and there is an overall good statistical
agreement. The accuracy of the interpolated results suggests that it may be feasible to
construct the ROM with less than four points in TN, which is even more plausible as non-
linear interpolation in non-uniform grids is included in the model. And whether or this is a
good approximation should be tested against the validation case previously used to validate
the model’s prediction capabilities.

Due to the chaotic nature of turbulent flows, statistical tools are almost always used to
analyze them. For this purpose, the first four statistical moments are common tools to study

scales [21]. The time and space correlations can be related if the turbulence intensity is not
too high with Taylor’s frozen hypothesis approximation.

are used to compare the flow in two points separated by time or space. For example, the

These turbulent scales are used to estimate the size of the eddies in the flow. In this sense,
the macro scale is of the order of magnitude of the large eddies which inject energy into
the flow. The micro-scale is in the order of the eddies in the inertial range, which tends to

eddies, in the dissipation range the Kolmogorov-scale is found, in this range most of the
energy is transformed into molecular dissipation as heat. Here the Kolmogorov scale is where
the Reynolds of the eddies is reduced to 1 [21]. Furthermore, scales in the inertial subrange
or bellow behave independently from the macro movement generating the flow, which gives
them the name of universal equilibrium range. The three scales and three zones mentioned

turbulent flows to contain eddies that expand in size across several orders of magnitude [48].
In this way, the spectral analysis shows how the flow evolves across multiple scales and how
the turbulent energy is distributed.

B.1
Flow recreation
The reason why spectral analysis is tremendously valuable is not only because it allows
an easier analyze the flow, but also because it is a two-way street. This means that if the
spectrum is known, it is possible to recreate the flow. More precisely, it is possible to create
a flow that is statistically the same as the original flow.
To recreate a turbulent flow the first ingredient is the spectrum of the original flow.
This can come from experimental data, detailed computational simulations, or validated
models such as the Frost, von Karman, or Kaimal spectra [54]. Then the inverse Fourier
transform has to be calculated from the spectrum to recreate the flow. To do this, a series of
random numbers is generated to provide a random phase in complex space to each frequency
==PAGE:70==
58
B Spectral Analysis in Turbulent Flows
component of the spectrum. This means that the flow created depends on which random
numbers were used, which depends on the seed number used by the software generating the
numbers. As different seeds generate different flows the stochasticity of the transformation
arises, but the main takeaway is that the flows are statistically the same as they have the
same spectrum.
A very simple example in one dimension of flow recreation is provided by David R.
Fuhrman and B. Mutlu Sumer in their book on turbulence [21]. Here a short Matlab program
called TurbGen.m shows how random phases are added to the original spectrum, the inverse
Fourier transform is applied, and the flow is generated.
This task of recreating the flow becomes remarkably more difficult as the number of
dimensions increases. This is because the four-dimensional velocity field (dimensions: x, y, z, t)
must respect the conservation of mass and momentum to be physically valid. Therefore, the
recreation of velocity in any direction is linked to the others. To do this correctly, the spectral
velocity tensor (Φij, Eq. B.4) has to be taken into account. This was done under some
assumptions by Paul S. Veers in 1988 [54], and then more generally, including anisotropic
distributions of turbulence due to shear, by Jakob Mann in the 90s [19][20].
Nowadays the stochastic reconstruction of the three-dimensional flow, referred to as a
turbulent box, is a fundamental building block of almost every aeroelastic simulation in
the industry and research. To implement it, usually, the Mann method is used to create a
so-called Mann-box, which contains the wind that is applied to the turbine. The importance
of this method has made it part of the industry standard IEC 61400.
So far, the turbulent boxes recreate the atmospheric flow and a model like DWM has to
be used on top of that to recreate the wake conditions inside of a wind farm. The purpose of
the research project, of which this thesis is part, is to create a tool capable of generating the
flow inside of a wind farm just like the Mann-box does it with the atmospheric flow.
