Summary
The medical field has always benefited from the latest technological headways such
as radiography, robotics or more recently augmented reality. Indeed, the progresses
in image analysis and augmented reality have led to major therapeutic progresses in
the surgical field as well as in the diagnosis field. Thus, computer vision and more
precisely image registration could greatly improve the effectiveness of mini-invasive
surgery such as laporascopy. In this master thesis, I present a methodology to perform
3D non rigid registration on elastic organs, notably the liver.
I compare various
associations of rigid, affine and non-rigid algorithms such as CPD and RANSAC in
order to determine the most efficient combination depending on the case.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Minimally invasive surgery was first introduced in 1987. In the last thirty years, this
technique has proven its benefits both for the patient and the surgeon. Mini-invasive
surgeries (MIS) are less painful, leave less scars, necessitate shorter recovery time.
They are also more precise, reduce the bleeding and the risk of post-operative com-
plications. To perform such operations, the surgeon only performs a few numbers
of small incisions (unlike open surgery), to introduce cameras and miniature instru-
ments. In particular, MIS are used to perform liver’s resection in the case of liver’s
cancer.
Indeed, cancer is one of the first cause of death in the world and liver’s cancer is
one of the principal kind of cancer. During a laparoscopy (a type of MIS) to resect
a liver’s tumor, the surgeon will only be able to see through the two cameras of the
laparoscope that are inserted in the patient abdomen, resulting in a reduce field of
view. Moreover, the surgeon doesn’t have a direct access to the liver and therefore
loose the information obtained by touch.
To compensate this loss of information, computer vision and augmented reality
would present a real interest. Indeed, there exist some techniques to superpose a 3D
model of the liver with its internal structure to the cameras’ images. However, the
specific case of the liver presents some difficulties. First, during the surgery, a gas is
injected in the abdomen of the patient to increase the surgeon’s room for manoeuvre.
This also causes an elastic deformation of the liver which makes it harder to register
the model on the cameras’ images. Then, the liver is a very smooth organ, presenting
very few salient points, which complicates the registration. Finally, we have only a
partial vision of the liver. Indeed, in the best case, the cameras’ images show 50 %
of the total surface of the liver.
In this thesis, I propose a solution that could be used during surgery to perform
efficient non-rigid registration of an organ such as the liver.
1.1
Constraints
Throughout this thesis, I tried to develop a solution that could be easily used in an
operating room (OR). Indeed, the interest of this project is to improve the efficiency of
surgeons during laparoscopy. Therefore, the solution must be easy to use for surgeons
or nurses, meaning that it must not need any technical, computational expertise.

Moreover, it can only uses data and source of data that are already available
in the OR. If we want the solution to be used, we must differ as little as possible
from the existing procedure of laparoscopy, considering that surgeons are usually not

included in the procedure. Now that I have explained the constraints induced by the
surgery, I will defined more precisely what is image registration.
1.2
Registration definition
Image registration is the process of matching two or more images. More concretely,
it consists in finding the transformation that minimizes the difference between two
images. One image will be fixed, the target, and we apply the transformation to the
second one, the source, in order to best match the two images.
Figure 1.1: Image registration process.
==PAGE:13==
1.2 Registration definition

We distinguish different types of registration depending on the form we choose
for the transformation. Indeed, it can be rigid, affine, elastic... For example, a rigid
transformation is constituted of only translations and rotations.
1.2.1
Steps of registration
Regardless of the type of the registration, the registration process usually follows the
same steps.
First, we need to decide on the feature detection. Indeed, registration will try
to best match the corresponding features in the two images. This is the reason why,
depending on the images we want to match, we have to choose on which features we
will perform the registration. For example, those features can be lines, if we want to
match contours, or regions, if we want to match high contrast closed boundary region,
or points if we match corners. According to the features we try to match, we dispose
of different methods to detect them. For instance, to detect corners, we can use the
Harris corner detector [H+88] or Förstner corner detector [FG87]; to detect lines, we
dispose of the Canny edge detector [Can87]...
Then, we need to match those features. Indeed, for each feature in the source,
we need to find the corresponding feature in the target image. To do so, we can
use different similarity measures such as the euclidean distance, the normalized-cross
correlation or the mutual information.
Next, using the established pairings, we can estimate the transform model. We can
choose a mapping function that will best match the pairs of points and determine its
parameters. A mapping function can be global, meaning that the same transformation
will be applied to all the points in the source image, or local, meaning that the
transformation will differ along the source image. For instance, rigid transform are
global transform whereas the weighted least square is a local transform [Gos88]. There
also exist local mapping functions that are able to handle local distortions.
Finally, we need to apply the computed mapping function to the source image.
This also means choosing an appropriate interpolation technique for the case where
pixels of the source image are mapped to non-integer coordinates. There exists dif-
ferent interpolation techniques of various complexity. The simplest one is the linear
interpolation. It consists in taking the value of the two closest points and weighting
them with their distance to the point we calculate. In the case of two images, we
need bilinear interpolation that works in a similar way as linear interpolation except
that we take the four closest neighbours instead of two. In a more formal way, if
we consider Figure 1.2, E is the point of which we want the value, A, B, C, D are
known points. Let f(A) be the value of the point A. We have the following bilinear
interpolation formula.
f(E) = f(A)·(1−dx)·(1−dy)+f(B)·(dx)·(1−dy)+f(C)·(1−dx)·(dy)+f(D)·(dx)·(dy)

1.2.2.2
Affine registration
An affine transformation is of the form :
f(X) = AX + D
(1.6)
with X and D vectors of size n and A a square matrix of dimension n × n.
It preserves collinearity, ratios of distance and consequently parallelism [Wei]. A
combination of affine transformation is still an affine transformations. Most known
affine transforms are rigid transform, scale, shear, dilation and reflection.
Thus, for an affine registration, we will search the affine transformation between
the source and the target that minimizes the error.
1.2.2.3
Non-rigid registration
Non-rigid transformations are not as easy to define and describe. Indeed, they usually
cannot be formalized with a matrix notation and involves a high number of parameters.
However, we usually define constraints to ensure the smoothness of the displacement
and the coherence of the points motion.
1.3
Pre-processing
The registration procedure is quite complex.
Indeed, before moving to the pure
registration, we need a few pre-pocessing steps. First, in order to have a registration
as precise as possible, we want our model to be specific to the patient. To do so,
we need to build a 3D model from the patient’s MRI. Then, during the surgery, we
have only access to a stereo camera as a source for our registration. That is to say
that we only dispose of 2D images as sources. Therefore, we have to perform a 3D
reconstruction of the source from the stereo source. Finally, once we dispose of the
3D model of the liver that is patient specific and of the 3D reconstruction of the liver
during surgery, we can perform the registration.
In this thesis, we will only focus on the registration part.

Method
Throughout this thesis, I have tested different registration methods and algorithms. I
have first experimented on 2D data before moving to 3D data. Indeed, handling two
dimensions is easier to start and allows to introduce a lot of concepts and techniques
that can be used in three dimensions. Depending on the data, I had to use different
pre-processing such as filtering, thresholding, cropping...
2.1
Two dimensions
For the two dimensions registration, I used three different sets of images. First, I
used a simple red rectangle on a white background as a source and the same rectangle
translated and/or rotated as a target. Then, I used a scan of a brain as a source, and
the brain scan with various deformations (translations, rotations, scales, shears) as a
target. Finally, I used the camera of the computer to take pictures of yellow ball, that
I used as targets and I took a yellow circle as a source. The first set of images presents
the interest of being very simple, and is a good example to start experimenting on
registration.
The second set is a bit more complex and is medical relating.
The
third set is more realistic in the sense that it will present interferences (background,
reflection of the light...) and the ball can undergo more complex deformations such
as flattening.
For each set of images, I have tried different algorithms and model of transforma-
tions. I have first tried to obtain satisfying results on rigid registration before moving
to non-rigid registration.

2.1.1.1

relies on a simple principle and idea but has been proved to be quite efficient. It allows
refining the alignment of two point clouds that are roughly aligned. To give satisfying

As for the Canny Edge detector [Can87], it presents a computational approach of
the edge detection problem. It is constituted of the following steps:
• Smooth the image with a gaussian filter in order to reduce the noise
• Compute the intensity gradient of the image
• Suppress the pixels that are not local maxima using the gradient (we compare
the pixels to its two neighbours in the gradient direction and check if it is bigger
than its neighbours)
• Identify the edges using hysteresis thresholding (we fix two values minV alue

then it is an edge i and only if it is connected to a sure-edge).

edge detection. For example, a brain scan will not present a lot of corner whereas it
shows a lot of exploitable edges (Figure 2.2).

2.1.2
Other image analysis methods
Most image registration methods necessitate to have two images of good quality,
without noise or parasitic objects. This is the reason why we sometimes need to apply
different image analysis methods to the source or target prior to the registration.
First of all depending on how the data were acquired, we might need to filter
them in order to reduce the noise for example. Then, the images we acquired can
have other objects that we are not interested in, in addition to the object we want to
register. Thus, we might need to perform a template matching before performing the
registration in order to know exactly in which part of the image the registration must
be applied. Template matching consists in finding a template (an image) in an other
image. The algorithm behind template matching consists in moving the template

2.2.1
Rigid registration
For rigid registration, I have mainly used two algorithms : the Iterative Closest Point
algorithm (ICP) and a global registration algorithm based on the RANSAC (RANdom
SAmple Consensus) method.
The ICP algorithm in 3D works along the same principles as in 2D. However, as
in 2D, it is only able to perform a good rigid registration if a good initial alignment is
provided. In case of major rotations, the algorithm will not perform the registration.
This is the reason why we need a global registration algorithm that will be able to
roughly align the source and the target before executing the ICP algorithm. To this
end, we use the RANSAC method.
The principle of this method is the following. At each RANSAC iteration, we pick
n random points in the source point cloud. We get the corresponding points in the
target point cloud by querying the nearest neighbour. To define the nearest neighbour,
we use the FPFH (Fast Point Feature Histograms) feature. It is a 33-dimensional
vector describing the local geometry of a point. It allows getting points with similar
local geometrical feature as neighbours.
Once we get the nearest neighbour, we
use fast pruning algorithms to reject false match based for example on the distance
between two matched points.

4.1
Error measures
My evaluation protocol aims to evaluate the precision and speed of the registration
results. To evaluate the precision of a registration I have used several tools. First, I
have used the Hausdorff distance [DKR93]. This metric is very useful to compute the
distance between two meshes. Let X and Y be two meshes, the one-sided Hausdorff
distance from X to Y is defined by :
dH(X, Y ) = max
x∈X min

From this formula, we can define the two sided Hausdorff distance as:
DH(X, Y ) = max(dH(X, Y ), dH(Y, X))
(4.2)
To compute this distance I have used the meshlab software [CRS98] which computes
the one sided Hausdorff distance. Thus, to get the two-sided Hausdorff distance, we
only need to compute the two one-sided Hausdorff distances and to keep the maximum.
Meshlab gives the results in the mesh’s unity but also with respect to the bounding
box diagonal. I chose to keep the value with respect to the bounding box in order to
have a value that is more universal.

fitness and the inlier RMSE of a given registration result. The calculus of this two
metrics relies on the calculus of matches between the target and the source cloud.

that they are the closest and the distance between them is not greater than a defined

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
Throughout this master thesis, I have studied the different registration techniques
in both 2D and 3D before determining the most appropriate one for the case of a
laparoscopy. I found the Coherent Point Drift algorithm and the RANSAC method
to be the most successful methods for our case. Thus, a combination of rigid and non
rigid registration has been proven to be efficient to perform registration on rotated,
translated and deformed models.
However, the results were less conclusive with cropped models depending on the
percentage of the organ remaining. In order to improve the performance of our process,
it becomes essential to have access to real medical data. Indeed, the large battery
of test that I implemented made it clear that one algorithm could not be used for
all cases. It must be tailored for each specific case. With access to the real medical
data, we could adapt the process depending on the rotation, deformation and visible
surface of the organ.
In addition, if we want to deploy the solution in the operating rooms, we would
also need to have an efficient solution for the 3D reconstruction from the laparoscopic
images.
Finally, the computation time is far from real time. A major further work would
be to optimize the computation time in order to make real time registration a reality.
This could be done by optimizing the number of points in the target, as a first step.
To sum up, I have obtained satisfying results in performing non rigid registration
on deformable organ even though access to real medical data would be primordial to
improve the performance of the process.

Abstract 
ChainMail is a physically-based deformation algorithm that has been successfully used in virtual surgery 
simulators, where time is a critical factor. In this paper, we present this algorithm for a minimally surgery 
use. This method enables a simple visualization of model deformation. 
Keywords: Non Rigid Registration, Deformation, VTK, 3D Chainmail, Computer Vision 
 
Introduction 
Minimally invasive surgery is seen as one of the 
major advances in surgery considering that it 
limits the operative trauma. It allows the 
surgeon to reach his target by centimetre 
incisions through the use of instruments 
coupled to a video imaging system, which is 
also an issue. During laparoscopic liver surgery, 
the surgeon, on the screen, doesn’t have a view 
as precise of the tumours and vessels location 
as during normal surgery. So the goal is to use 
the Augmented Reality to help them by 
visualizing this important information. 
A 3D mesh should be registered in real time 
over the liver. The 3D model has to follow the 
deformation that the liver underwent during 
surgery, which is nonlinear. However, the 
complexity of soft biological tissues’ mechanical 
behaviour makes the simulation in real-time a 
very challenging task. Several computational 
methods and models have been developed to 
simulate soft tissue deformations in real-time, 
the Finite Element Method (FEM) and the Mass 
Spring Model (MSM) are the most often used. 
FEM is carried out by solving an optimization 
problem using a system of linear equations 
which take into account the elasticity of the 
material, but the computational complexity is 
too heavy for real time, while MSM is less 
realistic but allows real time. In this paper an 
alternative method has been used, the 
Chainmail algorithm proposed by Friksen 
Gibson [1] in 1997. This algorithm is easy to 
implement and the computational complexity

authors for medical applications, such as 
angioplasty 
simulation 
heterogeneous

performed, but they are not realistic for the 
human liver behaviour.

Principle 
PyCPD is a python library presenting a numpy

The CPD algorithm relies on a Gaussian

Rigid Registration with Open3D 
Anaïs CHAMBRIAL, Marine COLLET, Audrey LEONG-HOI 
IDD : Interactive self-guided systems 
Project : Non rigid registration 
EDC : How to register 3D models ? 
 
Abstract 
Medical imaging has allowed great medical advances in surgery as well as diagnostics. For both those 
fields, image registration is essential. The Open3D library offers performant tools for image registration 
that can easily be adapted for medical application. In this paper, we detail the different registration 
techniques of Open3D and how they can be used on medical data.  
Keywords: Rigid-registration, 3D, Open3D, ICP algorithm, global registration. 
 
Introduction 
The medical field has always benefited from the 
latest 
technological 
headways 
such 
as 
radiography, 
robotics 
or 
more 
recently 
augmented reality. Indeed, the progress in 
image analysis and augmented reality have led 
to major therapeutic progress in the surgical 
field as well as in the diagnosis field. Thus, one 
of the most important technique of medical 
image analysis is the registration. Image 
registration is the process of matching two or 
more images. More concretely, it consists in 
finding the transformation that minimizes the 
difference between two or more images. The 
transformation can be rigid (composed of 
rotations 
and 
translations 
only), 
affine 
(composed of rotations, translations and 
scales), or non-rigid. Even though rigid 
registration can seem quite easy to perform, 
developing and implementing solutions that 
realise fast, precise and robust rigid registration 
on 
complex 
objects 
is 
still 
challenging, 
especially when we deal with 3D objects. One 
of the most known and used rigid-registration 
algorithm is the Iterative Closest Point algorithm 
that has been implemented notably by the 
Open3D library.     
 
Principle 
Open3D[1] is an open-source library that allows 
rapid development of software that deal with 
3D. It was first released in February 2018 and 
the latest version was released in January 
2019. Open3D mainly relies on the point cloud 
data structure. A point cloud can be extracted 
from PLY file as well as XYZ file or PTS file. 
Then, the library offers different tools to act on 
the point cloud objects. Thus, we dispose of 
visualization, filtering, geometric, segmentation 
and of course registration tools. In this paper, 
we are mostly interested in the registration 
tools.  
Indeed, 
Open3D 
implements 
several 
registration algorithms. The first of them is the 
Iterative Closest Point algorithm (ICP)[2]. It 
allows refining the alignment of two point clouds 
that are roughly aligned. Indeed, to give 
satisfying results the deformation between the 
two point clouds cannot be too important. One 
point cloud will stay fixed (the target) and the 
other will be transform to match the target (the 
source).  The major steps of this algorithm are 
the following:  
- 
Associate the points of the point cloud 
by pair based on the Euclidian distance, 
the points that are the closest will be 
associated. 
- 
Estimate the rotation and translation 
parameters that minimize the root 
mean square error (RMSE).

However, all those ICP variants needs a good 
prior alignment, which is not always the case in 
medical application. Thus, Open3D offers two 
global registration methods that can provide a 
first rough alignment. The first method is an 
implementation of the RANSAC (RANdom 
SAmple Consensus) method. The principle of 
this method is the following. At each RANSAC 
iteration, we pick n random points in the source 
point cloud. We get the corresponding points in 
the target point cloud by querying the nearest 
neighbour.  To define the nearest neighbour, we 
use the FPFH (Fast Point Feature Histograms) 
feature. 
It 
is 
a 
33-dimensionnal 
vector 
describing the local geometry of a point. It 
allows 
getting 
points 
with 
similar 
local 
geometrical feature as neighbours. Once we get 
the nearest neighbour, we use fast pruning 
algorithms to reject false match. Open3D 
proposes three different algorithms, based on 
the distance, the normal or the edge length. 
Finally, we compute a transformation based on 
the remaining matches. This technique is quite 
robust. 
However, 
it 
can 
take 
a 
long 
computational time. This is the reason why 
Open3D has implemented a fast global 
registration.  
 
  
 
Results 
We have tested the different functionality of 
Open3D on medical examples since it is one of 
the fields where registration is the most used.  
First, we have tried to determine the limits of the 
registration technique on the 3D model of a jaw. 
Then, we have imitated the conditions of a mini-
invasive surgery by recording with a depth 
camera a 3D impression of the jaw before 
registering the extracted point cloud with the 
model.  
First of all, we have determined the limits of the 
registration techniques in order to be able to 
know the form an input point cloud can take 
(numbers of points compare to the model, 
repartition of the points…) to have a satisfying 
registration. Our registration algorithm was 
composed of two steps: first a RANSAC global 
registration 
then 
a 
local 
point-to-point 
registration to refine the result of the global 
registration.  
Our first test consisted in verifying that the 
global registration was efficient when the source 
underwent important rigid transformations. To 
this end, we have rotated and translated the 
model of a jaw.

Discussions 
 
Thus, we have seen that the solutions offered 
by Open3D can provide impressive results in 
medical applications. The methods are quite 
robust since, even with a lot of missing points, 
the registration is still effective. Moreover, 
registration on partial objects also demonstrates 
satisfying results. However, on very noisy point 
clouds the registration does not offer the same 
precision and can lack robustness. Indeed, the 
RANSAC method relies on a randomised 
approach. Consequently, the result of the 
registration of the same very noisy point cloud 
can be different from one time to another. 
Hence, it is essential to have a good 3D 
reconstruction. 
In addition, Open3D only offers rigid registration 
tools. Therefore, if we want to perform a 
registration on a deformable object, like organs, 
Open3D will not be able to match perfectly the 
source and the target. It will at best be able to 
place the source at the right position compared 
to the target. 
 
 
Conclusions 
Open3D is very complete library that enables 
performing efficient rigid registration. It can 
easily apply to medical applications. However, it 
does not supply any non-rigid registration tool.

Perspectives 
As exposed previously, Open3D does not 
handle non-rigid deformation. Therefore, we 
could use it together with another library offering 
non-rigid registration such as PyCPD. Indeed, 
PyCPD present an implementation of the 
Coherent Point Drift (CPD) algorithm that 
handles non-rigid deformations.

Discussions 
We have proven the limits of Open3D on very 
noisy point clouds. Indeed, the registration can 
lack precision and robustness when the point 
cloud presents many outliers, since the 
RANSAC method picks points randomly. 
Consequently, the result of the registration of 
the same very noisy point cloud can be different 
from one time to another depending on whether 
the picked points are outliers or inliers. Hence, 
it is essential to have a good 3D reconstruction. 
 
Nonetheless, our approach is still able to 
perform a good quality registration when the 
source is deformed on a non-rigid way. Even 
though Open3D is not able to reproduce the 
non-rigid deformation the source underwent, it 
is still able to place the organ at the right place 
regarding to the target. Thus, it is a valuable tool 
to be used together with a non-rigid registration 
technique. 
 
 
Conclusions 
To sum up we have seen how Open3D can be 
used to perform rigid registration on elastic 
organs. In order to have an efficient registration, 
the source point cloud need to be of good 
quality, which cannot be achieve with a depth 
camera. However, Open3D shows interesting 
result when applied to sources deformed with 
non-rigid transformations.  
 
Perspectives 
Even though Open3D is performant on rigid 
registration, it lacks non-rigid registration tools. 
This is the reason why it would be interesting to 
use it together with a library offering non-rigid 
registration on point clouds such as PyCPD. 
Indeed, PyCPD presents an implementation of 
the Coherent Point Drift algorithm (CPD) relying 
on the numpy library.
