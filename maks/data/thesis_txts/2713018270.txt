Abstract
Stroke is getting more and more relevant as the average age increases in developed coun­
tries, and there is a lack of medical professionals to conduct therapy. Technology could
be a solution to increase therapy efficiency, such as user­optimised dashboards. This is
why a dashboard was developed following the principles of the iterative design process.
A dashboard for rehabilitation is supposed to have a scheduling system and a way to
track patient progress, for which the three­dimensional digital twin visualisation was also
used with a corresponding heatmap. The final dashboard resulted in three main menus
providing an overview and allowing the therapist to observe the status and progress of
patients individually based on proven medical scales and schedule exercises for them.
Furthermore, the digital­twin visualisation has received good feedback, providing a novel
way to visualise key aspects of stroke therapy. Overall, the reception of the dashboard
was positive, however, based on functional and usability testing, certain aspects need
refinement in the future.

1.1
Motivation
In today’s society, life without personal computers, high­speed internet and smartphones
is barely impossible to imagine. During the last decades, uses and users for personal
computers and advanced technology in general skyrocketed. It became even more pro­
nounced with the covid pandemic started in 2019, which encouraged remote work and
made product and food delivery services much more commonplace. This, in turn, also
encourages interaction with devices connected to the internet since most new services
are primarily available using the web. [1] [2] [3]
Another phenomenon observable throughout recent years is the rising number of health
issues. As people tend to focus more on their careers, most couples will likely have only
one or two children during their lifetime, and with the growing quality of medicine and
healthcare, more and more people are reaching older ages, pushing the average life ex­
pectancy higher. With higher average age, however, health issues like chronic diseases
and sicknesses also become more frequent. One example is stroke, a medical condition
with insufficient blood flow to brain cells, causing them to die. Depending on the severity
of the stroke, it can cause complications ranging from bad vision, difficulty speaking and
understanding, numbness of the body to lifetime paralysis and death. Worldwide in 2015,
approximately 42.4 million people suffered a stroke, the cause of death for 6.3 million of
them. Those who survive, however, do not necessarily return to their ordinary pre­stroke
lives. In many cases, different body functions can become paralyzed, most commonly
speech and limb functions like grabbing, gripping and holding things. Moreover, approxi­
mately 30 to 50 per cent, especially those who also suffer from impaired speech after the
stroke, suffer from post­stroke depression. [4] [5] [6] [7] [8]
Therapists help stroke patients using stroke rehabilitation to guide patients to relearn the
suddenly lost skills to brain damage. This also helps prevent the development of new
medical conditions like pneumonia, urinary tract infections, injury due to falls, or clot for­
mation in large veins. The outcome of an individual’s rehabilitation progress depends on
the severity of brain damage, age, home environment, family and friends, other concurrent
medical problems and, of course, the quantity and quality of the rehabilitation program.
[9] [10]
Rehabilitation becomes increasingly difficult as there are more older people for fewer
young medical professionals. To solve this issue, therapy efficiency has to be increased.
We believe that new technological rehabilitation systems might contribute to an improve­
ment of the situation. However, to bring this technology closer to the users and to facilitate
interactions, appropriate dashboards are required. Following the trends mentioned above,
with more and more services becoming available, our goal is to create a web dashboard
for the stakeholders in stroke rehabilitation, like therapists, patients and patients’ signif­
icant others, like family members. A fundamental prerequisite for the success of such
systems, however, is the seamless interaction between the user and the system, pro­
vided by a user experience­oriented user interface. We also aim to increase the quality
of rehabilitation by making it more fun and motivating for the users through simple games
­connected to the dashboard­ we create to simulate hobbies or everyday tasks. A study
in 2018 also found that many survivors of stroke experience post­stroke depression and

2.1
Stack
As mentioned in section 1, this thesis aims to develop a user experience optimised dash­
board for therapists in stroke therapy. There are many ways to design and implement
such a system, and before implementing, it is essential to choose the right set of tools,
also referred to as the stack. A stack is the set of libraries, tools, and frameworks which
are used to build and run an application. Stacks define the deep foundation of an appli­
cation, such as:
• How the app works
• App behaviour regarding the future
• How scalable, extendable the product is
• The purpose of the project
• The choice for data storage
• Server system capacity (it can change if it made for high read or write rate)
Since software development trends are constantly changing, it is important to stay in the
loop with popular stacks. There are many new options coming every year, but trends are
easy to follow if one puts in the time and effort. Some popular stacks as of 2022 include:
• The MEAN stack (MongoDB, Express.js, AngularJS, Node.js)
• The MERN stack (MongoDB, Express.js, React, Node.js)
• The MEVN stack (MongoDB, Express.js, VUE.js, Node.js)
• The LAMP stack (Linux, Apache, MySQL, PHP)
• Serverless stack
• Flutter for web
There is no ”best” approach for a given problem or idea, but it is better to work with
something very popular, supporting indispensable third­party libraries specifically created
for a given library or framework. Moreover, the popularity of these stacks ensures that
they are constantly updated, and users are also updated in case vulnerabilities are found.
It is also important to consider that the frameworks and libraries used for the web ap­
plication are constantly changing, being updated from time to time, which can change
fundamental logic inside the code. Usually, it will not cause problems; however, radical
changes might occur from the developers, so our project must also be changed and up­
dated accordingly. It is also important to consider what happens if the application has
to be run on a different computer. In such a case, it has to be made sure that the other
system is also running the same frameworks with the same version, which can be tedious
when multiple components are present in the system. One way to solve this issue is using

2.2
Backend
The backend, sometimes referred to as the data access layer, is often called server or
server­side, especially from an architectural point of view. Usually the goal of the back­
end is to expose functionalities to the outside world to be executed on something on the
inside, like a database or to return valuable data to be displayed on the frontend. By send­
ing requests to the API interfaces, it is possible to interact with a database for example,
but in a controlled manner, since usually there are restrictions related to data access, like
authentication, authorisation and so on. Another important aspect is input checking. It
is bad practice to put everything into the database without checking, for example nobody
can be negative years old. Checking input on the backend side is especially useful when
complicated constraints have to be applied before the actual operation on the database.
Our system uses the frontend I created to communicate with the backend, which commu­
nicates with the database, to modify or return data. [15]

Express

works and purely in Javascript, it becomes complicated to follow with even slightly bigger
projects like this one. [16] [17]
2.2.2
Middleware
A middleware is a kind of software which provides services to software applications which
are not available on the operating system. It is sometimes also called ”software glue”.
In express, for example, when dealing with requests, especially those with a JSON re­
quest body, it is impossible to directly get the values from the JSON included in the body.
Some kind of parsing is necessary. However, it is always tedious to implement parsing
for every endpoint implemented in the system. With an extensive system, maintenance
can become impossible over time. For this case, express has a built­in middleware, ex­
press.json, which is called before the data reaches the function we are using to handle
the request. It is parsing the data so that we can work with it more easily, so we do not
have to focus on parsing since the middleware deals with it. This kind of approach is often
used in input/output systems. [18] [15] [17]
2.2.3
Routing
When requests are posted to a given server, those requests must be directed to the appro­
priate endpoint API’s function that handles the request. This process is called routing and
is done using routing methods. Routing methods specify a callback function or handler
function, which is called whenever the application receives a request to a specific route (or
endpoint) and HTTP method (for example, POST request to route app.com/post). What
happens is that the application is comparing the requests it receives to see whether they
match the specified endpoints and methods, and in case of a match, the specified callback
function is called. Moreover, it is also possible for routing methods to have multiple call­
back functions as arguments which can be used for creating middleware­like behaviour. It

2.2.4
Database
In computing, an organised collection of data stored and accessed electronically is called
a database. Depending on the type of the database, storage methods might differ from
file systems to computer clusters and cloud storage. There are many different designs
and approaches, and choosing the correct one depends a lot on the problem at hand. In
short, the goal is to organise and store related data so later they can be used for various
purposes. Especially in our case, another role of databases is to store data persistently.
In the previous section, it has already been mentioned that with docker, it is possible to
create volumes to keep the stored data even after the database is terminated. However,
in this section, I would like to go into more detail about our choices for databases and how
they compare.
Relational databases
The relational database is the most commonly used database type based on how the data
is modelled. It is based on the relational model of data. It consists of four main parts:
• Tuple or record: Represents a single item in a relation. For example: John Smith,
1960. 01. 01.
• Attribute or field: Labeled element of a record. For example: name, date of birth.
• Relation: Set of record with the same attributes
• Result: Any set of records, response to a given query. For example: people born
before 1999. 01. 01.
To represent relational data, tables are used, where the columns correspond to the at­
tributes, rows correspond to records, and the whole table is the relation. When storing
data digitally, query languages are used to retrieve data based on a given query written
in a query language. Structured Query Language or SQL is the standard query language
for relational databases. It has been the standard of the International Organization for
Standardization (ISO) since 1987 and is revised every five years.
Various extensions exist to standard SQL, which implement functionalities not present in
standard SQL. For example, SQL/PSM, PSM stands for persistent stored modules. It
is also an ISO standard which defines an extension of standard SQL with a procedural
language for stored procedures. Using these procedures, it is possible to create and ex­
ecute SQL queries dynamically. They are useful when given parameters, like the date of
birth depends on some external property. Other SQL extensions are similar in function­
ality and syntax, but different databases use them. For example, Oracle databases use
PL/SQL or procedural language SQL, Microsoft databases use Transact SQL, MySQL
uses SQL/PSM, and the list goes on. These SQL extensions also support the creation of
triggers. Unlike stored procedures, triggers are called automatically when an event hap­
pens in the database. They are useful when enforcing rules related to the database. For
example, when relation A uses the records from relation B, relation A needs to recalculate
when new data is inserted into relation B.
Relational databases support the ACID principles which is an acronym defining the im­
portant transaction properties for databases, each letter meaning the following:
• Atomicity: Every transaction statement (read, write delete) is treated as a single unit.
Either the whole statement is executed or none of it. This prevents data corruption
and loss when the source fails during data streaming.

• Consistency: Transactions only make changes to relations in predictable ways. It

• Isolation: Transactions done by multiple users do not interfere with each other.

• Durability: It ensures that changes made by successfully finished transactions are

Most programming languages also provide libraries to connect to relational databases
and execute queries. However, depending on the data structure used in the database
and the data structure used by the application or program, conversion can be difficult and

relational databases, however, depending on the project’s scope and the data structures
used, implementing a system using relational databases is sometimes difficult. [20] [21]

Non­relational databases
The other primary type of database is the non­relational database, often called the NoSQL
database. Many non­relational databases exist, document­oriented databases, graph
databases, etc.
Document­oriented databases
Document­oriented databases are used for storing, retrieving, and managing semi­structured
data. They represent data in a schema­less flexible way, thus providing more flexibility for
data modelling. They use documents for storing data, for example, XML or JSON. Due to
the flexibility of XML and JSON, it is easier to manipulate data than relational databases.
Usually, documents are stored in collections. Some examples include MongoDB, Amazon
SimpleDB, Couchbase Server, etc.
To store information, labels are used, which describe the data and values in a record.
New information can be directly added to an existing record without modifying the entire
schema.
Due to the quickly changing nature of web­based, mobile, social, and IoT devices, data
schemas can also quickly change. In relational databases, these changes also necessi­
tate the modification of schemas by modifying tables and adding or removing columns.
The flexibility of document­oriented databases eliminates the need to force­fit the data
into predefined attributes and tables.
In conclusion, Document­oriented databases are useful for creating web­based services,
as they use the same document types like JSON and XML to store data, which are also
often passed between HTML requests. [27]
Graph databases
A graph database is a database that uses graph structures with nodes, edges, and prop­
erties to store data. The graph relates the data items to a set of edges and nodes, where
edges represent relationships between nodes. Data can be linked to each other directly
and, in many cases, can be retrieved with one operation. The priority of graph databases
is the relationships between records. Querying relationships is fast because they are per­
petually stored in the database. They are useful for heavily interconnected data, as graphs

2.3
Frontend
The frontend or user­side of a system is responsible for visualising data in a user­friendly
way. For the users it is an important part of a system since they usually only interact with
this.
Since many projects in Rehyb used Virtual Reality or VR and Augmented Reality or AR
technologies, the first concept was to create the frontend using these technologies. How­
ever, having worked with the technologies mentioned above, we found that creating a
frontend using AR or VR would be much more tedious, and the user experience would
also be much worse.
In the case of AR, hand movements are sometimes not properly registered by the headset
(we experimented with Hololens 2) and the placement of user interface elements is also
difficult. In particular, we found that using a heads­up display or HUD for a dashboard is
difficult to use and disorienting. The other idea we had was a virtual monitor ”floating”,
where the user can select different options, and it would display an avatar in the middle of
the room. The main issue with this idea is difficult calibration, and the equipment needed
to make such a system work is considerably more expensive than the value it adds to
it. We also considered voice control for a dashboard. However, in noisy places, it is
impossible to use, and sometimes voice recognition is difficult depending on the user’s
accent. In conclusion, AR is primarily useful for passive heads­up displays, for example,
with pilot helmets, where only a tiny bit of extra information is provided for the user.
On the other hand, VR technologies seemed to make more sense since the whole en­
vironment the user is in is also completely virtual, and the controllers are much more
responsive in our experience (with the Valve index set), so it feels more natural to use it.
We also considered the exact two solutions as with AR. However, heads­up displays are
also disorienting in an entirely virtual environment and interacting with them using con­
trollers is practically impossible. Also, having a virtual monitor in virtual reality to select
a menu, for example, the digital twin and then interacting with the digital twin in VR is
a possibility; however, setting up the room for VR and the space needed for it makes it
tedious to use, not to mention the extra cost this also carries. All in all, if VR support is
implemented in such a system, it will only be used as an option and not a mandatory piece
of equipment.
The last option we considered is a regular monitor for display and a web­based dashboard
to be visualised. It makes the most sense since everyday objects like PCs and mobile
phones already use web­based dashboards, and everybody is familiar with them. Also, a
well­designed, responsive web­based dashboard can be used with devices with different
sizes and aspect ratios, such as phones, TVs, computer monitors, etc. They are simple
to set up and use, and most applications are made with monitors in mind. We also found
that all research regarding dashboards assumed an ordinary screen to display the fron­
tend, so building on already existing research was the logical way to go. It lacks a bit of
immersion compared to VR, but no extra equipment is needed since most users already
have monitors at their workplace and home. [15] [30] [31]

2.4
Web­based development
When creating a web application, it is important to consider how the different components
will interact and how the users can interact with the components. Moreover, component
deployment on different systems and maintenance are essential aspects of development.
2.4.1
Docker
As mentioned in the previous section, it is important to be able to deploy the application
on as many types of computers as possible. Docker is a free Operating System­level vir­
tualization tool that allows running programs in isolated environments called containers.
Because Docker containers use a shared Operating System Kernel to work, it uses less
computational resources for the same task, and they are also more easily deployable on
machines with weaker hardware. However, the software provided for docker uses a con­
siderable amount of computing power, so it is advised to check the system requirements

Docker Hub

Communication
For communication between containers, computers or the outside world, Application Pro­
gramming Interfaces or APIs are used. When designing a system, the communication
protocols can also be factors that significantly influence the architecture of the final sys­
tem, so choosing the most useful one is an important task. The two most common ap­
proaches are Representational State Transfer (REST) and Simple Object Access Protocol
(SOAP), which I will describe in detail and compare and explain how we chose one. [42]
SOAP
Simple Object Access Protocol or SOAP is a messaging protocol specification for ex­
changing structured information in web service and computer network implementation.
It uses the XML information set for its message format and relies on application layer
protocols such as HTTP or SMTP for message negotiation and transmission. It allows
developers to call and execute processes and functions running on a separate operat­
ing system to communicate using XML or Extensible Markup Language. SOAP heavily
relies on HTTP protocols, which are installed on most, if not every, operating system.
Thus SOAP can be used to language and platform independently invoke web services
and receive responses.
Its three major characteristics are:
• Extensibility: SOAP is extensible with Web Services Addressing and Security for
example
• Neutrality: SOAP can use any protocol to communicate and operate, for example
HTTP, SMTP, TCP, UDP
• Independence: SOAP can be used with any programming model, which often closely
ties to programming languages, allowing requests in Java, Python etc.
Despite being relatively old and the last stable release being 15 years ago (as of 2022),

REST
Representational state transfer, or REST, is a software architectural style created for the
guidance of the design and development of World Wide Web architectures.
As of 2022, most APIs in the world are RESTful, meaning that they follow a set of ar­
chitectural rules and constraints called representational state transfer or REST in short,
which was created for the guidance of the design and development of World Wide Web
architectures. It has been the de facto standard for API development since the beginning
of the new millennium. [43]
A RESTful API organises resources into URIs or Uniform Resource Identifiers that differ­
entiate different entities and resources on the server. A client can use the HTTP protocol
to request data from a given endpoint on the server. The message has a specific and
defined format, URI or path of the request and the method of the request, which carries
the intent to do with the resource. The 4 most common request methods or sometimes
also called ”verbs” are:
• GET: They are used to retrieve data about the REST API resource. Browsers make
GET requests on the server URL specified to display the information returned. Nor­
mally, nothing is altered or deleted when simply opening a website. It is important to
note that GET requests cannot have bodies, which are custom data payloads often
used to send data to the server, since GETs should only be used to retrieve infor­
mation. It is possible, however, to have parameters inside the request URL, which
can be used to specify which user’s data is necessary to be returned, for example.
• POST: They are used to create a new REST API resource. One common example
is the creation of a new user in a system.
• PUT: They are used to update already existing REST API resources. One such
example is when changing the password for an already existing user.
• DELETE: They are used to delete an existing REST API resource or a related com­
ponent.
It is important to note that POST, PUT, DELETE requests have bodies that can then be
used by the server to execute a given function. So it is completely possible to create an
endpoint, which can only be accessed by a POST request, but it does something com­
pletely different, like deleting an entry from a database. It is also possible to functionally
use DELETE requests as GET requests; the application will compile and execute with­
out problems, so no syntax error is present. However, the example mentioned above is
a semantic error, which in these cases makes reading the code and understanding the

Status codes
After the request arrives and the code for the request is executed, the server returns a
response. This response contains the payload, information the client requested, and the
status code of the request. The client uses the status code to understand what happened
to its request. There are 5 different levels of status codes:
• 1XX informational response: Informational responses indicate that the request has
been received. It allows the server to inform the client that the request is still being
processed and that the client has to wait a bit more for the final response, which can
be something among the other levels listed below. Example responses include 100
continue and 102 processing.
• 2XX success: A success status code indicates that the server has received the
request and the action has been understood and executed without problems. For
different types of requests, the status code might be different. It allows the client to
verify that the request was successful. Example responses include: 200 OK, 201
Created, 202 accepted.
• 3XX redirection: This level of status codes indicates that the client must perform
an additional action to complete the request. It allows the server to notify the client
of this additional action, commonly redirection. Example responses include: 300
multiple choices, 301 moved permanently.
• 4XX client errors: This status code level is for situations where the client probably
caused the error. In the response, the server attaches an explanation of the error,
noting whether the problem is permanent or temporary. This way, the client knows
that the request was faulty and that the server is working properly. These status
codes can happen with any request. Example: 400 bad request, 403 forbidden,
404 not found.
• 5XX server errors: Indicates that some error happened on the server­side, so the
server could not perform the request. In many cases, the returned error code will
only be a 500 internal server error, and the error can only be resolved on the server­
side by checking the log files, for example. It allows the client to know that the
problem was on the server side. Examples: 500 internal server error, 503 service
unavailable, 501 not implemented.
There are official and unofficial status codes at each level, and it is also possible to ”define”
our own when creating the application. They can be especially useful for input checking
when adding new data to a database by specifying which data structure is necessary for
the endpoint to function correctly. [46] [47]

• Communicational Cohesion: When two or more elements work with the same input

On the frontend, routing is important and indispensable when building complex applica­
tions. The default router for React is the Reactrouter, but it is necessary to install sep­

react­router. Firstly, it subscribes and manipulates the history stack. The history stack is

rendering nested user interfaces according to route matches. For a specific URL, there
can be different renders. For example, it is possible to create a site where the homepage
URL of the average user and the administrator are the same, but that will not mean that
the admin site will be rendered for the user and vice versa.
There are many different routers and components included in react­router. However, only
used ones will be described.
• BrowserRouter: It stores the current location in the given browser’s address bar and
utilises the browser’s built­in history stack to navigate
• Routes: Contains routes. If the location changes, this looks through all its route
elements to find the best match to render it
• Route: As mentioned above, it contains a path and an element to render if the given
path was received. Similar to an if statement (if path then element)
• Link: It is an element that can be used to go to another page by interacting with it
by clicking for example.

way styles and markups and connected using CSS rules. A CSS rule starts with a selector,
specifying which element should behave the way it is specified in the rule. For example,
every paragraph defined with the <p> tag should have red font. The rules are contained
between braces after the selector in the so­called declaration block. The rules themselves
are key­value pairs that describe how the element should look. As the name implies,
these style sheets cascade, which means that there can be multiple rules which apply to
the same element. Hierarchy is used according to specificity to decide which style should
be present on a given element. More specific rules will overwrite the parents’ rules. The
order of hierarchy from highest to lowest is as follows:
• Importance: It is possible to add the ”!important” annotation which overwrites the
previous priority types
• Inline: Using the style attribute it is possible to specify style inside of an HTML file.
This is the second highest priority for rules.
• Media Type: For all media types there is a property definition, which stays the same
unless specified otherwise.
• User defined: With most browsers it is possible to define custom CSS. For example
if the user doesn’t want links to be underlined, it is possible to specify that by creating
a file containing a relevant user defined rule.
• Selector specificity:Generic definition is overwritten by specific contextual selectors.
For example specifying paragraphs inside headings in more specific than para­
graphs in general.
• Rule order: If there are multiple rules declared with the same selector, the last de­
clared rule will be applied
• Parent inheritance: Non specified properties are inherited from the parent element

2.4.10
Package manager
Since there can be many libraries and frameworks or shortly packages for a project, it is
standard for many runtime environments to have a package manager by default.
A package management system or package manager is a set of software tools that allows
the automation of the different computer program­related tasks like installation, upgrad­
ing, configuration, and removal. The biggest benefit is that it all happens consistently,
which also helps with deploying code onto other computers. On principle level, it is simi­
lar to Docker, which also allows such management, but usually on a higher level, meaning
instead of libraries, it is used more often with runtime environments, etc. These packages
contain specific metadata like the given software’s name, description, version number,
vendor, checksum and also the dependencies necessary for the software of the package
to run properly. This metadata is stored in a local package database when the package is
installed. Package managers also usually maintain a list of software dependencies and
version information to avoid mismatches. They work in close contact with app stores,
repositories, and managers to which even ordinary people can upload their packages so
others can use their code. It is similar to how the aforementioned docker hub works on
principle. When moving a program from one computer to another, it is only necessary
to download the package manager. It will access the local package database and auto­
matically download the necessary packages and dependencies, so the application can
quickly be deployed elsewhere.
The package manager of we used was NPM or Node Package Manager. It is the default
package manager for the NodeJS runtime environment. In NPM, the local database con­
taining the versions and dependencies is a JSON file called package.json. Without going
into too much detail, the package file can contain the following metadata: name, version,
description, engines, dependencies, main, scripts, author, and license. There is also a
package­lock.json file which further helps eliminate version mismatch­related problems
when moving the application to other machines. [69] [70]

Cookies
HTTP cookies or cookies are small pieces of data that are put onto the user’s computer
while browsing or using a service. There are different kinds of cookies, some of which
are essential for web functions and allow web servers to store stateful information. They
are most commonly used to store user­related data, so the same data can be presented
later when the user returns to a page. One common example is the ”remember me” or
”keep me logged in” button for websites, which stores authentication data so that the user
does not have to log in every time the browser is closed or the site is left where the user
was logged in. Another common example is the use of online stores, which can store
the basket’s contents, for example, or billing information and other forms filled, like credit
card information forms. There are also tracking cookies which are used to track the user’s
activity for commercial purposes. This is a privacy concern, so in the European Union,
storing non­essential cookies requires the user’s consent first. [71]

4.1
Iterative Design Process
Throughout the whole development process of this project, we followed the methodology
of the iterative design process. An iterative design process is the periodic process of
prototyping, testing, analyzing, and refining the given product or process. Using the re­
sults from testing and feedback from potential users, small changes and refinements are
made to improve the overall product by functionality and quality. To gather feedback, in­
teractions, discussions and questionnaires regarding the evolving product are used, and
then the gathered feedback is used to make the necessary changes by strengthening
or adding aspects and weakening or removing others. Iterative design is especially im­
portant in user interface design as the feedback received from actual would­be system
users is the most important as the focus lies on user experience. As such, a first design
sketch was developed using insights from analyzing existing dashboards. Following this,
we asked for the users’ feedback about that design sketch, prototype or implementation
because users on their own, in most cases, cannot describe what they exactly want; they
only know what they do not want when they see it.

smaller, but important hand movements might completely disappear from the heatmap,

collection and define the shape of the documents for that particular connection. These
Schemas then can be used to get data from the database while also applying filters, ag­
gregating, counting or whatever is necessary.

be included in the request body.
• GET ”/exercises”: It returns the contents of the Exercises collection.

• POST ”/users/:name/postdata”: This endpoint is responsible for storing exercise
data in the database. Currently, for testing purposes, it accepts the whole session’s
data to be stored. The from, data and score fields are necessary, and the ”position”
field inside the data field is recommended to send hand rotation data for visualisation
purposes. If the ”from” field does not correspond to any exercise’s start time and
date stored for the given patient, a new session will be added. This is because we
wanted to provide the possibility to create a session from the Unity games as well on
the fly. In this case, the ”to” and ”exercisename” fields are also necessary since we
are creating a new session, and these pieces of information are not known, unlike
with an already stored session.
There are also a few REST endpoints only used for testing and some which are not used at
all. They are, for example, endpoints for creating a therapist, patient, and login functions
for different stakeholders. Each of these endpoints defines a good foundation for future
work.
Before every request, the specific request bodies are passed to the express.json middle­
ware, which is responsible for converting the incoming data into easy to work with JSON
object format, so parsing is unnecessary in every request’s beginning. [92]

such a heatmap is feasible or not, the results also showed some limitations. Conceptual
limitations are discussed in the discussion chapter of the thesis, however now, only the
technical limitations we encountered are detailed. However, conceptually and design­
wise, there were no plans for implementing this, although such ideas might have been
mentioned beforehand. This simplification, which we did mainly for demonstration and
testing purposes, introduced a severe limitation related to data upload, as there is a limit
to the size of the body of the POST request. Another technical issue is the size of the
model we use in the avatar. The blue doll we are using, which Syncsense has approved
as a prototype, is rather small compared to the average person. Since we are only track­
ing hand data, it is often the case that the arm of the user is considerably longer than
the arm of the virtual doll. This leads to inaccuracies with visualisation. When testing
the system, it was also important for us to test one particular part of the system, to try to
isolate different issues while everything else was working properly. For example, when
doing the dumbbell exercise, I deliberately positioned myself so the system would ”think”
that my hand was much sorter. This was done to test the heatmap only and to see how
realistic arm movements would look with proper elbow tracking. This also highlighted an
issue related to recording. In order to have the doll and person move consistently, first,
the patient’s position must be calibrated, as the hand position is not relative to anything,
only to the base station. When testing, we decided to mark the floor with an X. However,
this method might not be the best with other headsets. The other idea for this issue was
to have the hand position relative to the head’s position. The problem is that the head is
also not stationary, so using it as an anchor point is challenging.

6
Discussion
6.1
Database
MongoDB, which we decided to use for this project, is a suitable database for this project,
as we found. It was easier to use than SQL­based databases, and storing dates and
images were also easily done.
It must be noted that proper stress testing is still necessary, especially when data are
coming from multiple patients doing exercises simultaneously, because the system might
be unable to handle it, which might cause crashes. One such possibility would be to
send data as small batches or bundles instead of one by one as, by default, connections
are opened and then closed with every request. Other protocols might also be useful for
communication, especially ones that do not close the connection after every request, so
a new connection does not have to be established with new data.
6.2
Backend
The backend results also show that using ExpressJS and node to create an application
like this is feasible. Stress testing is also necessary here, but most of those issues can be
solved by deploying the server on a proper computer for server hosting. As mentioned in
the 4th section, for prototyping, we simplified the data upload to the server. This means
that with long sessions containing massive data, it is impossible to upload everything at
once. If this is not handled correctly, then the data recorded by the unity game might be
lost on the game’s side. A solution could be to first send the data only related to creating
a new session and then the actual movement data in small batches or to increase the
request body’s maximum size, making it resource­intensive to upload a long session all
at once.
It has to be noted, however, that the endpoint names and types are not semantically
correct as there are requests that should be GET requests instead of POST. While tech­
nically it works, it should be corrected to avoid confusion. The reason for these semantic
problems is that when connecting the Unity game and the dashboard, the requests were
constantly changing and when the two systems were connected, changing and restruc­
turing the code on both sides was tedious, and we wanted to focus on creating a working
prototype to get feedback on whether the direction we had decided to take is correct or
not.
6.3
Design & frontend
For the average user, most important part of the dashboard is the user side which is
highly dependent on the design. Implementing an actual working website from a non­
functional design or drawing can be very tricky as there are specific interactions which
are not defined in the design as it is not animated or it is not possible to interact with them.
This also creates challenges for the designers themselves, as some parts of the system
might ”look good” when simply drawing it, but when implementing the actual functionality,
there might be differences between it and the design.
The most apparent differences are distances, sizes and ratios for and between different
dashboard components. In many cases, it is not easy to quantify sizes since the distance
described in an absolute unit is different from the display device to the display device.
Figma helps developers by providing CSS code to every design component. However,

for the parts implemented than including another page for only the WHO scale, for exam­
ple. Therapists could also use the scales when substituting for another therapist. Using
scales only, it is virtually impossible to get to know the patient, and usually, therapists
have regular meetings where they can discuss these issues.
Another really important issue that we have not touched on is responsiveness. Good
dashboards can adapt to different screen sizes and aspect ratios. For this dashboard,
the viewport’s width was used (so the browser window’s width) to change different as­

For example, when there are five boxes next to each other on a 16:9 aspect ratio screen,
it is fine, but when opening the same page using a smartphone, the aspect ratio also
changes drastically, in which case these five boxes might better be arranged below each
other, so instead of horizontally, vertically. Responsiveness can only come after the ra­
tios of different components are decided, which can be challenging when functionalities
can constantly be changing, which was the case with us. Also, on very old monitors,
sometimes even the most commonly used websites like Youtube fail to adapt properly.

6.4
Heatmap
A novelty of the dashboard is the heatmap and the 3­dimensional avatar visualising the
digital twin. The concept of the heatmap is to visualise the range of motion for the hand of
the patient as a three­dimensional cloud­like shape. A big issue related to this is the colour
selection. It is challenging to set the colour gradient so that they visually and intuitively
represent the range of motion. For example, the densest areas will probably be the parts
”inside” of this cloud, while the least dense areas are the ”shell” of the cloud. Geometrically
this means less dense areas (so green cubes) than dense areas (red cubes). When
selecting the colour for the different areas, we found it important to define them in terms
of percentages, like the ”rarest 10% should be green” and so on. Finding an overall
good colour distribution is a challenge on its own, and it might not be possible to find
a ”universal” solution. The size of the cubes which define the areas reached can also
differ. If the size of the cubes were too small, then every point in space was only touched
once because tracking is that precise, and we are not assigning colours to regions which
have not been reached. The heatmap colour scheme and cube size could change from
exercise to exercise or even from session to session. Whichever the case is, according
to the feedback, it is necessary to have some label indicating what the colours mean.
Listing the colours and the percentages for them might be sufficient, but more discussion
is necessary with medical professionals.

Another interesting question related to the heatmap is how the data is collected. Right
now, we are only collecting data from exercises, however, it is most likely, that these
exercises do not cover the whole range of motion for the patient’s hand. Intuitively it might
sound correct to assume that it is possible to make assumptions given the data collected.
For example, if the patient can draw a big circle with their hand in front of them according
to the recording, it can probably be assumed that they can also draw a small circle, but
they did not move their hand that way in a particular exercise. However, making these
kinds of assumptions is incorrect because the medical conditions are different from patient
to patient, so the assumption might not be correct. The solution could be to collect more
data or possibly aggregate multiple sessions’ data to create one big heatmap. A possible
solution could be placing the patient in a big virtual sphere which is larger than the range
of motion for even a healthy person as well, and instruct the patient to move their hand
around this sphere to cover most areas inside it. This, however, changes how data is
collected and the heatmap is calculated and would be more useful for overall assessment
and not session­only assessment, as we have it right now.
The therapists also mentioned that scales, like pain, depend on what the patient is doing.
The heatmap could be extended, so it is possible to visualise which parts of space were
reached how many times and indicate when it hurt the most. The two heatmaps would
be different as a patient may easily be able to move their arm somewhere, but it might
painful to do so.
Despite these issues, the heatmap could be a useful tool for therapists. Therapists are
trained professionals who can detect the smallest change in muscle spasticity, however,
as mentioned in the introduction, it might not always be possible for therapists to be in
the same room as the patients. For this, a heatmap would be a good solution, but it is
important to note that much more research is necessary for this one component of the
system, as we only created a prototype, which also gave us many new ideas we could
implement in future iterations for the dashboard.
6.5
Avatar
A three­dimensional avatar is an excellent choice to use for movement visualisation. With
more sensors, especially elbow and shoulder sensors, it should be possible to replay
the patients’ movements more realistically. This avatar’s technical details should also be
customisable; however, as explained in the results chapter, the digital figure we are using
is rather small compared to the average person. To solve this issue, patient dimension­
related data should also be stored, for example, arm length or height. The most difficult
part about tracking is how tracking is done with or without anchor points. Installing base
stations to serve as stationary anchor points is not the best idea, especially when we
consider that patients should be able to do exercises at home as well. However, using a
base stationless VR headset like the Pico is also challenging as relative motions might ruin
the data collected. For example, fixing hand movement relative to the hand would mean
that moving the head while the hand is stationary could be misinterpreted by coordinates
alone, that the hands were moved instead. This issue must be further considered.
In the end, we believe the benefits outweigh the issues. Especially when the patient is
doing the exercises at home, there were multiple ideas to record the patient’s movements.
The most trivial one, of course, is using the camera built into the headset if it exists and
using that to get the point of view perspective from the patient. This, however, violates
patient privacy, so only recording the virtual world might be the more optimal way to go.

7
Conclusion & Future work
Stroke therapy will play an increasing role in future healthcare. Due to the lack of ther­
apists and the increasing demand for highly­effective therapy, technology will play a key
role in increasing the therapy’s efficiency and effectiveness. This paper aimed to imple­
ment a dashboard for a future rehabilitation system based on Exoskeleton, Serious­games
in VR and Human Digital Twin. The implementation process followed an iterative design
process providing continuous feedback for improvement.
The resulting dashboard consists of 3 main menus. Each menu is responsible for different
aspects of rehabilitation, and patient interaction, which therapists told us are important to
them.
The results have shown that despite the basic functionality, the heatmap and avatar show
some limitations already which need to be addressed in the future. However, the patient
assessment is definitely headed in a good direction, but it could also be expanded with a
timeline or history feature, for example.
Given the results and final implementation, a few other features could be developed using
the same methodology.
For example, a slider could be included instead of clicking on an exercise to compare
previous sessions to more current ones, which would change the heatmap on the fly. For
this kind of idea, an aforementioned more general heatmap would also be a good idea.
For example, when not selecting any exercise session, the heatmap and avatar could
show the combined heatmap from the last few sessions.
Furthermore, for the heatmap and avatar, it is another idea to be able to click on different
body parts if sensor data is provided. This would enable progress tracking for the hand,
elbow, and shoulder, which are already assessed on the assessment page. Also, a pos­
sible future work is having different heatmaps for the different assessment metrics, like a
pain­related heatmap.
To sum up, we managed to create the foundation of a rehabilitation system that can and
should be expanded. An enormous future work for the whole system is related to two other
dashboards, the patient dashboard and the family or third­party dashboard. However, the
most immediate future work is to fix the issues mentioned in the results section and get
related feedback.
