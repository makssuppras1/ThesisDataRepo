Abstract
Advancements in collaborative robotics and automated systems have led to a demand
for more general-purpose robotic solutions capable of handling unstructured and chang-
ing environments. The Mohamed Bin Zayed International Robotics Challenge in Abu
Dhabi is a biennial international competition that focuses on solving real world robotic
problems. The work presented in this thesis, is in association with early preparations
for the upcoming challenge in 2023.
In this project advanced object detection and tracking methods are explored with the
end goal of a control paradigm that enables a suﬀicient grasping technique for agricul-
tural harvesting. Towards this end, the most prominent state-of-the-art methodologies
are evaluated based on inference speed to ensure a fast and robust solution.
With this goal in mind, a object detection model based on the YOLOv4 framework
is trained on a custom dataset using an automated data generation technique. The
temporal evolution of the detector predictions are combined with a Deep SORT tracking
algorithm to provide contextual information about the objects seen in the image. A
robotic grasp detection system tasked with learning the object descriptors based on
multi-modal RBD-D data is trained using a GR-ConvNet architecture as backbone.
The full system description is cast as a position-based visual servoing control strategy
with a combined average attempted grasp success rate of 96.36% on the chosen objects.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Technological advancement in field and collaborative robotics is a core driver for the
Mohamed Bin Zayed International Robotics (MBZIRC) challenge taking place in Abu
Dhabi.
MBZIRC is a biennial international competition that challenges universities
around the world to solve real world robotic problems in areas such as agriculture,
marine and hospital applications. The upcoming challenge in 2023 is set around the
aforementioned areas and include tasks such as crop harvesting, precision delivery, bed
side assistance, sterilization, surface and sub-surface capture [1]. The development of
tools to perform such tasks may contribute positively in the aid of human rescue and
living condition.
DTU and the automation and control institute aims to partake in the activities and
be recognized as an elite university which is on the forefront of engineering. MBZIRC
along with other major robot competitions serves as a great platform to showcase the
competences of both professors and students. DTU have previously attended the chal-
lenge (in 2017 and 2020) with great results and recognition. As of September 2021 the
documentation and specification of the tasks are in a preliminary state, however early
steps to ensure a robust and feasible solution to each individual sub-task is paramount
to achieving a top result.
1.2
Project Description
The project aims at exploring one or more advanced detection and tracking methods for
the design of a control paradigm to enable a suﬀicient technique for grasping an object
for agricultural harvesting. Towards this end, a detection strategy for object recognition
is to be derived and validated to work, this includes gathering of suﬀicient amount of
image data. Further, the detected objects must be tracked such that frame-by-frame
knowledge is maintained. Last, the project should address the issues revolved around
achieving a suﬀicient grasp of the objects.

2.1
Limitations
Given the sparse challenge description, the physical layout and complexity of both chal-
lenge and objects are unknown. Thus, the following assumptions are made to ensure
that further investigation upon subject is both viable and feasible.
- In efforts to provide a robust yet feasible solution, considerations on the chosen
objects is paramount for achieving a generic solution that works across multiple va-
rieties of different crop types and sizes. The definition of soft fruits and vegetables
is a very broad description leaving a multitude of different objects to be chosen
from. In this research, the objects were limited to that of cucumbers and tomatoes.
The softness of the tomato and the many shapes and sizes of the cucumber allows
a generic encapsulation of the many challenges involved with crop harvesting.
- In the field, vegetation has a broad spectrum of growth patterns that are very
specific to that type of crop and in general requires a very specialized solution to
ensure a suﬀicient grasp and harvest. To not enforce a solution that only works
for a specific type of stem or growth pattern, the notion of objects hanging from
a stem or a plant is disregarded from the analysis and the objects are limited to a
flat surface on a table. This both simplifies and limits the physical aspect of the
research objective, however solving the entirety of challenge in full is far beyond
the reach of a single thesis.
- Given that the objects are constrained to lie on the surface or plane of the table,
the prospects of incorporating a motion control system that drives a mobile robot
into close proximity of one or more of the graspable objects is not considered in
this thesis.
Thus, it is assumed that the manipulator tasked with acquiring a
grasp is positioned such that the object is within its reachable sub-space prior to
initializing the task sequence thereby neglecting the need to solve the Simultaneous
Localisation and Mapping (SLAM) motion planning problems that arises with
mobile robots.
This further implies that the experimental setup is completely
stationary and does not change over time.

Manipulator Kinematics
3.1
Introduction
In order to properly operate and control the chosen manipulator, an understanding of
the kinematics of the system is required. Towards this end, in the sections that follows
a detailed derivation of the forward, inverse and velocity kinematics will be outlined
followed by a series of tests to ensure that the provided solutions suﬀiciently describe
the kinematic relationships of the manipulator.
3.2
Forward Kinematics
The forward kinematics model of a serial manipulator deals with describing the position
and orientation of the end-effector based on known joint angles. Under the assumption
that each joint link only has a single degree of freedom, the actuating variable of each
revolute joint can be described by a single number: the angular rotation about the
attached frames z-axis. The analysis of this section is based on rigid transformations,
and thus a frame is rigidly attached to each link which rotates according to the links
joint variable, qi. This ensure that when the end-effector moves, the coordinates in each
of the kinematic links, i, are constant when expressed in terms of the ith frame. The
first link, i.e. link 0, is often referred to as the inertial or base frame of the configuration
and is fixed such that it does not move when the joints are actuated [7]. The position
and orientation of the end-effector, n, in relation to the base frame, 0, is given by a
three-dimensional coordinate vector 0pn and a 3×3 rotation matrix 0Rn which together
define a homogeneous transformation matrix

CHAPTER 4
Camera Calibration
4.1
Introduction
The experimental layout described in section 2 gives rise to a series of complications in
terms of acquiring a good estimate of the camera movement in relation to the end-effector
position. Recall from section 2.3.3 that the camera is mounted using the eye-in-hand
approach and thus the camera position is non-stationary in terms of the world coordinate
system. The transformation between camera frame and end-effector frame is required
to perform motion control based on sensory feedback in the image, fortunately this
transformation remains constant provided that the assembly does not shift over time.
The estimation of this transformation is known as extrinsic calibration, or specifically
for this task, hand-eye calibration and in general requires that the camera frame position
relative to a calibration target is known. The conversion from camera pixel coordinates
to world coordinates requires what is known as the intrinsic parameters of the camera
sensor to be known and must therefore be estimated before the hand-eye calibration can
take place.
Calibration of the camera sensor as well as finding an accurate estimate of the hand-
eye transformation is paramount for achieving good grasping results. Incorrect or inaccu-
rate parameters will propagate through the entire image analysis pipeline and ultimately
hinder the prospects of controlling the arm towards a target goal. The camera calibration
in this chapter thus comes two-fold: first, a calibration sequence that seeks to estimate
the intrinsic parameters of the camera sensor is conducted. Last, the instrinsics of the
camera along with known joint and end-effector positions of the manipulator will be uti-
lized to guide the optimization problem of finding the transformation between camera
and end-effector frame.
4.2

three-dimensional point onto the image plane of an ideal pinhole camera with no lenses
used to focus light, equations of which will be derived later. The algorithms that deals

5.1
Introduction
In this chapter an automated data generation sequence is developed based on the manip-
ulator dynamics derived in chapter 3 and the calibrated camera using methods described
in chapter 4, the approach is theorized under the assumption that large amounts of image
data is required to drive the data driven image analysis. Machine Learning principles re-
quires considerable efforts to ensure a robust solution and the acquisition of suﬀicient and
descriptive image data is a cumbersome process if done manually. The method derived
in this chapter is developed such that the solution can be utilized in future alterations of
the on going MBZIRC research project (and thus, by other students). Consequently, the
method allows for fast addition of new objects in the image processing pipeline, or com-
pletely resampling the entire image dataset to perform detection in a new environment
or rectify the data based on changes in the challenge description. Traditionally classical
computer vision analysis can be highly dependant on changes in the environment or
deviations in the light intensity imposed on the scene. These problems are solved by
controlling the speed at which images are sampled over a given time period to allow the
natural changes in lighting conditions to be captured and rejected.
The structure of the proposed solution is based on placing targets, e.g. objects, in
known locations on a planar surface and translating the manipulator such that either the
end-effector or camera frame is aligned towards the target, resulting in a solution that
can ultimately be used to automate the camera calibration sequence previously derived.
An element of randomness is applied in both the translation as well as orientation of the
aligned frame to ensure that no image is similar. The derivation and implementation
of the algorithm is divided into two parts: first, an overview of the algorithm method-
ology describing the sequence of operations needed to automate the process is outlined.
Secondly, an extensive description of the implemented method for generating randomly
sampled points using an exhaustive iterative approach is explained in the section that
follows.

5.2
Algorithm Methodology
It is important that the algorithm does not stop abruptly, as that would require a manual
interaction to resume the process. The manipulator has several safety measures that can
be diﬀicult to circumvent or completely safeguard against, as some triggers are based
on the torque loads of the joints which can occur when the end-effector approaches
singularity configurations causing an unbounded spike in joint velocities, additionally,
due to the mounting orientation of arm, the manipulator must uphold the full weight
of the arm as well as the torque produced in all of its joints which can severely limit
the maximum commanded velocity possible when the arm is fully extended. For this
reason, the commanded velocity is kept within reasonable bounds to keep the arm from
stopping.
A consistent stream of both manipulator and camera data is required to ensure
smooth operation. For this reason, a threaded server based approach that offloads the
communication with the physical arm and the camera is theorized. This is beneficial
as it allows the script to be run from any machine so long as the servers are reachable
over Ethernet. This setup is depicted in figure 5.1. Both servers pulls data continuously;
the manipulator can deliver positional data of the all joints at a rate of 125 Hz and the
camera delivers a stable stream of aligned image and disparity (or depth map) frames of
approximately 30 FPS. Additionally, the arm server incorporates functionality to halt
operation until the commanded move of the manipulator has been completed. This is
achieved on older manipulator models by employing a moving average filter on the read

The core functionality of the data generation sequence can be simplified into the
following five steps:
1. Before any translation or rotation of the end-effector takes place, an initial check
of the live joint angles of the manipulator is performed. This is required as the
evaluation of the joint angles derived from the inverse kinematics does not take into
account the potential of saturation in terms of minimum and maximum rotation
of each of the joint axes.
The joints impose a physical constraint and should
the commanded angle of a given joint exceed the boundaries [−360; 360] degrees
the manipulator throws an error. For this reason, joint angles that approaches
the outer boundaries are rotated clockwise (CW) or counter clockwise (CCW) to
ensure that the manipulator does not throw an error exception. Additionally, if the
arm is extended such that the correction of the joints would collide with the base
frame construction itself, an extra step is performed to ensure that manipulator is
properly corrected by first rotating the shoulder and elbow joint to fold the arm.
2. A point given in relation to the base frame of the manipulator is randomly gener-
ated using an iterative approach based on the Las Vegas algorithm presented in
[32]. In broad terms, the (x, y, z) coordinates are generated from a uniform distri-
bution function within a predetermined workspace surrounding the robot. Based
on the generated point a series of steps are introduced to correct and find the
orientation of the end-effector frame, i.e. the rotation around the (x, y, z) coordi-
nates axes, such that it is aligned towards a target on a planar surface. Last, an
iterative search using the inverse kinematics derived in section 3.3 is performed
along a straight line formed by equidistant points from the current position of the
end-effector to the corrected generated point. This seeks to test if the manipula-
tor approaches singularity configurations, a point outside the reachable workspace
or most crucially, if the gripper and camera assembly will collide with the arm
itself during translation. If any of the performed checks raises a warning, the se-
quence is repeated until an acceptable solution derived from the current position
of the end-effector is generated. As a result, the generated point is guaranteed
to be reachable and without camera collision during movement. The Las Vegas
algorithm guarantees a correct solution, but the runtime is not fixed and thus
it is possible for arbitrarily much time to surpass before a solution is generated.
Further explanation of the random point generation follows in section 5.3.
3. Given a reachable generated point, the arm server receives and commands the
manipulator to move the end-effector to the desired location. Additionally, further
computation is halted until the movement of the arm has been completed. The
time spent in the move command is further used to sense the unlikely event that a
fault has been raised on the manipulator. This is a crucial step of error handling,
==PAGE:68==
50
5 Automatic Data Generation
as the computation of random points and sampling of images would otherwise
continue generating a large quantity of similar data.
4. When the arm is properly situated in the generated point, a command is sent to
the camera server to save both the color and depth map images in the assigned
folder location. Additionally, the location of the end-effector and angles of the
joints are logged by requesting the data from the arm server.
5. Steps 1-4 are repeated until a suﬀicient amount of image data has been generated,
resulting in a fully automated approach.
These five steps clearly indicate that an automated data generation sequence is feasi-
ble. Other methods for solving the path planning problems between current end-effector
position and the generated point was initially investigated by use of an alternative search
strategy known as D∗presented in [33] that works by searching backwards from the tar-
get position and associate neighboring points in a grid with a cost based on the relative
position to the starting position. This algorithm generates a path that is traversable
in the reachable joint configuration space based on the inverse kinematics and does not
include points associated with singularities. Such a solution is presented in [34] based on
a two-degree-of-freedom manipulator and a static scene to construct a collision free path.
Significant efforts are required to adapt the solution to a 6-DOF manipulator and under
the assumption that large amounts of data is required, the trial-and-error approach of
the Las Vegas algorithm will over time converge to similar results as a generated point
that is discarded initially might be traversable again after multiple iterations. Addition-
ally, the movement of the end-effector frame is commanded linearly in tool-space, which
generally is prone to singularities as the on board software use a numerical solution of
the inverse kinematics. Fast joint movements can be achieved by shifting the analysis
to joint space and command positions using joint angles derived from the inverse kine-
matics and selecting the joint configuration that requires the least amount of rotation
to achieve the target position. This has certain caveats as it can be diﬀicult to access
in which order and direction the rotation of the joints are performed, which can be dev-
astating for the camera collision avoidance. For this reason, the movement of the arm
was kept linear in the end-effector frame.

The avoidance of collisions between camera assembly and manipulator is important to

6.1
Introduction
In recent times, machine learning algorithms have surpassed conventional computer
vision methodologies for image classification and detection strategies.
Traditionally,
solutions based on well-established computer vision techniques such as Scale Invariant
Feature Transform (SIFT) [37] or Speeded Up Robust Features (SURF) [38] were used to
generate feature descriptors of local image patches containing information such as edge
detection, corner detection or threshold segmentation. This step is generally known as
feature extraction and can be used later to search for similarities in other images. It
is generally hard to acknowledge which of the extracted features best describe a given
object, and can be a cumbersome process based on trial and error to achieve good
classification results and is further complicated for large number of classes.
Artificial Neural Networks, or ANNs for short introduce an end-to-end data oriented
approach that on the basis of provided image data seeks to estimate the underlying pat-
terns and most descriptive or salient features of the objects. This approach has certain
caveats as it is heavily dependant on the provided data and the computational resources
available. The analysis of the classification shifts from knowledge of the intricate details
of the objects using hand-crafted features, to providing suﬀicient amount of input data
to the model. Provided enough variety in the data, a classification or detector model
trained using state-of-the-art machine learning methods far exceeds the accuracy achiev-
able using conventional methods [39]. Modern models contain enough parameters that
inference, i.e. calculating the forward pass of a model given an input, is a cause for
concern for applications that requires fast execution time and is generally much slower
than inferior methods such as SIFT or SURF.
In this chapter an object detector featuring both a classifier and a detector stage is
trained using state-of-the-art machine learning methods with data provided by the auto-
mated data generation sequence derived in chapter 5. First, an analysis of the available
methodologies is described followed by an overview of the selected method. Secondly,
based on the selected method, suﬀicient amount of data is generated and augmented to
improve results of the trained model. Last, a series of the test are conducted to validate
the eﬀiciency of the trained object detector.

6.2
Analysis
The general methodologies for state-of-the-art object detection can according to [40]
in general be split into two categories: two-stage detectors and single-stage detectors.
Common for all are the use of bounding box regression methods to localize the objects
in an image based on the classification output. The architecture of the methods differs
greatly and can summarized as follows
Two-stage detectors splits the classification and localization problem into two stages:
first, a region proposal network (RPN) finds regions of interest in the image that
could belong to an object and based on these performs object classification and
bounding box regression. The most prominent architectures within region proposal
networks are Faster-RCNN (Faster Region-Based Convolutional Neural Network)
presented in [41], R-FCN (Region-Based Fully Convolutional Network) in [42] and
Mask R-CNN (Mask Region-Based Convolutional Neural Network) in [43].
Single-stage detectors simplifies the object detection problem by treating feature ex-
traction and bounding box prediction purely as a regression problem handled in a
single pass of the model. Common examples of single-stage detectors are YOLO
(You Only Look Once) presented in [44] and SSD (Single Shot Multibox Detector)
in [45].
Comparative studies between two-stage and single-stage detectors are presented in [46]
and [47].
In general a trade-off between prediction precision and execution time is
the main deciding factor between the methodologies. Due to the simpler structure of
one-stage detectors the inference time is considerably lower than two-stage solutions
albeit with lower overall precision, SSD suffers from approximately 10-20% loss in mean
average precision compared to two-stage solutions and YOLO is tuned for even higher
speed suffering even greater in precision [48]. [46] states that if the dataset is relatively
small and real-time results are of little importance, Faster-RCNN is the optimal choice.
On the contrary, YOLO is best suited for analysis on real-time image data as it is fast
and shows best overall performance. Figure 6.1 shows an illustration of the average
precision of the most commonly used object detectors trained on a large public dataset
known as Common Objects in Context (COCO) containing 80 different object classes.
It is evident that YOLO performs well and at considerably higher speeds compared to
other detectors.
Based on the formulation of visual servoing, the total execution time of the pipeline
necessitates the choice of fast inference time as the bandwidth of the final system is
dependant on how fast the velocity kinematics can be evaluated from the Manipulator
Jacobian derived in section 3.4. For this reason, the YOLOv4 framework presented in [44]
is chosen as the optimal comprise between inference speed and accuracy. Consequently,
the solution will suffer in accuracy compared to two-stage solutions but will uphold a
much higher sampling time required for suﬀicient motion control of the manipulator.

Network Architecture
The YOLO architecture has gone over several alterations since it was first presented in
[51]. The general approach involves splitting the image into multiple grids and analyz-
ing each separately. In particular, the feature map of the detection layer is designed to
output bounding box coordinates, objectness scores and class scores to enable both clas-
sification and localization of multiple objects within one frame in a single forward pass
of the model [50]. The concept of grid analysis had the drawback of severely limiting the
size of objects that was detectable. This was later rectified in [52] and [49] by applying
up-sampling and concatenation techniques to perform detection at three different scales.
The backbone of the YOLO architecture is solely composed of convolutional layers with
1 × 1 and 3 × 3 kernels making YOLO a FCN (Fully-Convolutional Neural Network).

note that the detection layer is handled at 3 different scales (layer 139, 150 and 161).

tion of Spatial Pyramid Pooling, or SPP for short that allows the network to calculate
a fixed-length representation regardless of image size or scale and improves robustness
to object deformation caused by e.g. resizing or reshaping of images. Additionally, the
feature extractor (or backbone) is based on CSPDarknet53 improving detection accu-

the associated class label in relation to the bounding box which severely hinders the
evaluation process. Additionally, consistent labelling across all images are important
and imposes a decision on how the detector should localize objects, e.g. if only a small
portion of an object is visible within the image frame, a choice to label it or not is bound
to have an impact on the trained model.

6.7
Evaluation
Evaluation of object detectors differs from conventional methods as the detection in
general consists of three different outputs: The predicted label of a class, the associated
bounding box and the confidence score usually given between zero and one. The per-
formance assessment is based on comparison between the predicted bounding box and
the ground truth labelling. A perfect matching, ignoring confidence, is achieved when
the area and location of the predicted bounding box and the ground truth is the same
and completely overlapping. This is assessed by evaluating the intersection over union
(IOU), otherwise known as Jaccard Index, which is a coeﬀicient of similarity between
sets of data [57]. Let A denote the area of a predicted bounding box and B the area of
the ground truth label, then the IOU for object detection is defined as the area of the
overlapping region divided by the area union
IoU(A, B) = A ∩B
A ∪B

ing to equation (6.1) occurs when IOU = 1, and if no overlap then IOU = 0. This can be
used to classify detections as being correct or incorrect by setting a detection threshold.
A higher IOU threshold is more restrictive and requires near-perfect detections to be
classified as correct. The threshold is usually given in terms of a percentage of overlap,
commonly set at 50%, 75% or 95%. The IOU can be reformulated as a loss-function
and used for back-propagation of the neural network during training. IOU gives no
indication on how close in proximity a discarded sample is in terms of the ground truth
label, and for this reason it is often used in combination with other losses.

given in terms of the two concepts: precision and recall. Precision is the models ability
to predict and classify only relevant objects and is the percentage of correct positive

CHAPTER 7
Tracking Algorithm
7.1
Introduction
Keeping track of the temporal evolution of objects in between frames is often omitted
from the analysis of many projects. This project aims at exploring visual servoing for
anti-podal grasping purposes on the basis of the derived object detector in chapter 6
and therefore necessitates the need for temporal information. This is further motivated
in cases were more than one of the same object is present in the same image frame, as
without tracking, the two is unable to be distinguished from one another. Additionally,
under the assumption that only singular objects, i.e. one of each class, can ever be
present in the image, false positives can be detrimental if used for visual servoing. A
major problem of object detection methods is the presence of false positives and the
trained YOLOv4 model is no exception. Therefore methods for misclassification rejection
is paramount for achieving acceptable results.
In this chapter, a state-of-the-art multiple object tracker is presented that on the
basis of the predicted bounding boxes actively rejects misclassifications and provides
contextual information of the tracked objects to be used in conjunction with visual
servoing to select and translate a manipulator towards a target of interest. First, an
analysis of the available methodologies is presented followed by a motivation for the
selected approach.
Last, a series of tests are conducted to illustrate the robustness
and effectiveness of the tracker when combined with the predicted output of the object
detector stage.
7.2
Analysis
The general methodologies for state-of-the-art multiple object tracking (MOT) can ac-
cording to [59] be diﬀicult to categorize, but can be compared according to three differ-
ent criteria: initialization method, processing mode and type of output. Common for
all methods is the underlying mathematical formulation of the target objective, that is
solved by some alteration of a multi-variable estimation problem. The majority of meth-
ods can in general be split into two categories according to how objects are initialized:
detection-based tracking (DBT) and detection-free tracking (DFT).
Detection-Based Tracking requires an object detector for initialization of trajectory
==PAGE:94==
76
7 Tracking Algorithm
tracks. This is also referred to as a tracking-by-detection strategy that given a
sequence of type-specific object detections applies a hypothesis to link the object
motion into trajectories. The caveat of this method is that the precision is highly
reliant on the performance of the object detector and requires that it is trained
beforehand.
Detection-Free Tracking requires that the initialization of a fixed number of trajec-
tory tracks is performed manually by annotating a bounding box location of the
area to be tracked.
Such a method is described in [60] and suffers if multiple
locations with similar appearance are to be tracked or if the object deforms over
time.
Based on the formulation above, the processing of trajectory tracks can be performed
either online or offline. The main differences is both processing speed and whether future
frames are utilized in the handling the current frame.
Online tracking relies solely on the past information available up to the current frame
to provide observations and trajectories. For this reason, this type of processing is
also known as sequential tracking and works by gradually extending the trajectories
based on current observations and is therefore best suited for real-time usage.
Offline tracking uses the future detections in entirety (or batches) to estimate the
trajectory.
Therefore it requires that detection is handled on all frames prior
to being jointly analyzed. This requires substantial computational resources and
memory to perform on large datasets.
the output type of a tracking method depends on the randomness of the output and can
be classified as either deterministic or probabilistic depending on the chosen optimization
strategy adopted to solve the multi-variable problem.
The use of offline trackers is
not relevant for the research conducted in this project and have therefore not been
investigated. Additionally, DFT trackers are not suited for the envisioned pipeline as
the manual track initialization and diﬀiculty tracking multiple similar objects are severe
limitations. As a result, only online detection-based trackers with fast inference speeds
have been considered.
Traditionally, methods adopting a detection-based tracking framework have been
solved using Multiple Hypothesis Tracking (MHT) presented in [62] or Joint Probabilis-
tic Data Association (JPDA) in [63] that based on the estimated uncertainty delays
association decisions in the object assignment when the uncertainty is high. The com-
plexity of these algorithms scales exponentially with the amount of objects in the frame
and as a result are computationally slow making them a bad choice for real-time applica-
tions. Simple Online and Real-Time Tracking (SORT) presented in [61] features a much
simpler solution based on a framework that incorporates a Kalman filter in the image-
space and a data association (or assignment problem) solution based on the Hungarian
method [64] using the intersection over union (IOU) as metric. The precision of SORT

CHAPTER 8
Anti-Podal Grasping
8.1
Introduction
Recent advancements in collaborative robotics and automated systems have led to a
demand for more general-purpose robotic solutions capable of handling unstructured
and changing environments. Traditionally, manual methods involving hard-coded solu-
tions have been utilized to program robots with the necessary instructions to perform
a required task and thus requires considerable efforts to account for changes in the
environment. Such methods have in general achieved acceptable results but has sig-
nificant limitations for generalized applicability [70]. For this reason, an abundance of
approaches based on data-driven deep learning methodologies have been explored in the
literature [71].
Based on the classification, localization and temporal information provided by the
object detector and tracking algorithm derived in chapter 6 and 7, respectively, a robotic
grasp detection system is developed to define how the manipulator end-effector can be
arranged such that a successful grasp of an object can be achieved. Towards this end,
an analysis of the available methodologies concerning deep learning methods in robotic
grasp detection is presented followed by a motivation for the selected approach. Secondly,
the selected method is described in further detail as well as the adaptations required
to conform with the requirements of this research. Additionally, due to limiting avail-
ability of suitable datasets, an entirely new dataset is generated and annotated on the
basis of the automated data generation derived in chapter 5. The dataset undergoes
considerable augmentations by use of an synthetic data augmentation approach to ex-
pand and generalize the trained model. Last, a series of tests are conducted to verify
the robustness of the detected grasps and the feasibility of using the anti-podal grasping
network for closed-loop path planning by mapping the image plane coordinates of the
grasp candidate to the robot world coordinate system.
8.2
Analysis
Robotic grasp detection is in [72, 73, 70] identified as the ability to detect both grasping
points and poses for an object given any image. Human perception generally have no
trouble immediately identifying the most optimal grasp for an arbitrary object, often
without it being directly in vicinity. In the same manner, visual perception techniques

88
8 Anti-Podal Grasping
attempt to map and classify each pixel of a scene to a group belonging either to a suc-
cessful or unsuccessful grasp. In this regard, the literature is divided in terms of the
representation or definition of a successful or suﬀicient grasp. Early methods in [74]
represented grasps as a singular three-dimensional point to infer the spatial location in
Cartesian space. This approach was severely limiting as no orientational information or
the width required to securely grab the object was determined. Instead, a vast major-
ity of state-of-the-art methodologies apply a common orientated rectangle description
presented in [75]. This configuration denotes a seven-dimensional grasp in terms of a
grasping point, grasping orientation and the opening width of the gripper. This was
further simplified to a five-dimensional space in [76, 77] by recognizing that a suﬀicient
prediction in 2D space, i.e. image coordinates, can be translated to 3D space by applying
proper calibration or depth mapping. Such a grasp can be derived in image coordinate
space as Gi = (u, v, w, h, θ) and is illustrated in figure 8.1 when placed on shapes sharing
resemblance to the objects of this research.
The rectangle description is objectively analogous with object detector methods that
was previously described in chapter 6 albeit with an additional dimension due to the
orientation angle. For this reason, deep learning methods generally involve the use of
convolutional filters to classify if patches in the images contain regions of a potential
grasp. According to [70] detection based grasping can be carried out using two tech-
niques: detecting structured grasp representations based on a rectangle configuration or
learning a grasp robustness function. In [78] a Fully Convolutional Grasp Quality Con-
volutional Neural Network (FC-GQ-CNN) is presented that on the basis of synthetic
point cloud data evaluates millions of grasp candidates and predicts the most robust
grasp based on the evaluation of a quality function and can plan grasps within 0.625s.
Similar methods is proposed in [79] that utilizes a Grasp Quality Convolutional Neural
Network (GQ-CNN) to rank grasp candidates based on a pre-trained model of grasp
robustness. These method is solely dependant on depth disparity maps, but most recent
research involves inclusion of RBG-D data.
Grasping based on multi-modal data is first presented in [76] that formulates the
detection problem without the use of traditional sliding windows or region proposal
techniques by splitting an image into N×N grids and associate each cell with a bounding
box in the center location and train a model to predict whether that cell contains a
successful grasp. Many alterations of multi-modal grasp planners have been presented
in literature [80, 81], common for all is relatively slow inference speeds due to two-step
approaches.

end-goal for the entirety of the image processing pipeline, and therefore methods for
real-time detections are explored in further detail. To decrease the computational load,
in general a much simpler network architecture must be proposed. Such a lightweight
model is presented in [82] and denoted a Generative Grasping Convolutional Neural Net-

to [82] at the cost of 1ms computational load when both color and depth map is used.

The architecture of the proposed Generative Residual Convolutional Neural Network

8.4.1
Annotation of data
The process of ground truth annotation shares close resemblance to the labelling per-
formed for the object detector stage. This time however, the annotations must be rotated
rectangles with an angle association. This requires a specialized tool presented in [84]
that is a fork of the original LabelImg software that has the capabilities required for
rotated bounding box assignment.
The use of the ground truth grasp rectangles comes two-fold. First, the output of
the neural network requires ground truth feature maps for the quality score Q given as
the quality of the grasp at every pixel in the image between zero and one, the angle Θ
of the grasp point in each pixel and the required width W for every pixel in the image.
Secondly, the annotated rectangles are used as the back-propagation loss based on the
Huber loss function in equation (8.3) and to access whether the prediction candidate
is adequate.
The internal use of the grasp candidates are important, however most
methods in the literature (and datasets) assigns every annotated candidate with the
same quality of one. This assumption is not necessarily the case for all annotations, and
therefore an encoding strategy is employed during labelling to allow for augmentations
on a later stage.
The annotated oriented rectangles are assigned a value, e.g. TomatoE0 or TomatoE5,
between zero and five to indicate the robustness of the grasp, where five denotes regions
with highest amount of confidence. Robustness in this setting, is an area where the object
is unlikely to escape the gripper mechanism during translational movement. Therefore,
labels assigned with a low encoding score is graspable, but likely to fail during movement.
Figure 8.3 shows an example of the exhaustive labelling process for both object classes.
Note that in general the annotation of cucumbers requires more labels as the objects
are larger in the image frame, additionally, the tomato is diﬀicult to label when images
are taken directly from above, because an arbitrary amount of rectangles can be formed
around a circle as indicated previously in figure 8.1.
The cucumbers have the highest encoding score label in the centroid of the object
with decreasing values moving outward. This tries to enforce the model to learn always
to grasp in the center if it is visible in the image frame. When tomatoes are seen from
above, all grasp candidates are assigned the same encoding score as any predicted grasp

8.5
Data Augmentation
With common data augmentation techniques such as the ones applied for the detector
stage, it is hard to completely avoid a model that is unbiased towards the trained
environment. Given the static configuration of the experimental layout, it is unfeasible
to collect data in outdoor or other intended environments. Therefore, another way of
achieving robustness towards change in environments is explored.
A vast majority of public dataset are entirely constructed and labelled by use of
synthetic data generation techniques, some of which applies multiple state-of-the-art
neural network architectures just to handle the data augmentation alone. Such a concept
was first presented in [86] and is known as Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN) and
involves training of multiple models to detect whether a generated image is synthetic or
==PAGE:115==
8.5 Data Augmentation
97
real. In doing so, a secondary model learns to best generate images such that the detector
is tricked into believing that the generated images are real [87]. Such methodologies are
considered out of scope for this project and instead a simpler solution is envisioned
based on cut and paste techniques presented in [88] that shows relative performance
increases of up to 21% on benchmark datasets. The core of the algorithm involves using
a segmentation cut of object instances to paste them onto new background environments
using a variety of different blending techniques to avoid a model that is trained on
the pixel artifacts that appear along the edge of the pasted objects. The three main
components of the solution includes: backgrounds, objects and blending functions:
Background images of the target environments that object segmentation cuts are to
be pasted on. These do not necessarily have to be photo realistic scenes but can
also be abstract shapes or structures.
Object segmentation cuts of the objects from real data that are to be pasted onto a
new background.
Blending functions are a variety of different filter functions that ensure that the model
does not learn to find the objects by locating the pixel artifacts from the pasting
process.
In the work presented by [88], distractors in the form of randomly chosen cut of objects
that should not be predicted are also pasted at random to further occlude the image
frame. These are not considered in this research and therefore the key framework consists
of achieving a suﬀicient instance segmentation of the objects prior to pasting.
It is
entirely possible to manually segment object cuts to paste them onto new backgrounds,
this however, is yet another manual and time consuming process. Instead, an automated
approach for generating an arbitrary amount of synthetic images from each image in the
dataset is presented in the sections that follows.

8.5.2
Flattened depth map
The data generation sequence captures images of the object on the surface, but a huge
part of the image contains irrelevant information in the background. In [88] the syn-
thetic data solution is solely based on color images, and therefore did not have to take
into account the augmentations on the depth map. A completely flattened depth map
is envisioned that only protrudes upward towards the camera in the region of the afore-
mentioned instance segmentation. Therefore, the estimated plane model from equation
(8.7) is required for this operation to succeed.
The proposed solution is based on generating an arbitrarily large grid of points that
are co-planar with the plane description followed by an exhaustive iterative search to
find correspondences between plane and image.
In general depth maps provided by
the stereo camera will have regions with faulty data due to reflections or incorrect
measurements, such points are returned as having zero depth and therefore contains
no spatial information in some pixel locations. The synthetic dataset should be fully
descriptive in the regions outside the instance segmentation and therefore the projections
is handled in three separate steps to combat these issues:
1. Initially, a grid of points extended from the dimensions of the original point cloud
is generated with an equal distribution of points along each dimension. This is
deliberately oversized to extend far beyond the field of view of the camera to avoid
situations where the orientation perspective of the camera in relation to the plane
requires much larger dimensions than the original point cloud.
2. For every point, the re-projection of the camera world coordinates to the image
plane is calculated from the inverse position relationship of equation (8.5) and
==PAGE:121==
8.5 Data Augmentation
103
(8.6). A multitude of points are rejected due to being outside of the image field
of view. If a point in the grid corresponds to a pixel coordinate in the image, the
length of the z-coordinate axis is calculated to denote the distance.
3. The re-projected depth map will have several values that did not achieve a suﬀi-
cient remapping and therefore no depth association. To remedy this, a series of
interpolation techniques are applied to fit the remainder of the depth map. First,
cubic interpolation is applied that best fits the missing central points, but gener-
ally have issues along the edges. Secondly, to interpolate the edges the values are
assigned based on the nearest points to complete the depth map.

of the solution. Additionally, a point cloud perspective of the steps are illustrated in

most research in the literature apply a rectangle metric presented by [75] that defines a
predicted grasping candidate to be valid if it satisfies the following two conditions:
1. The angle difference between ground truth grasping candidates and the predicted
result is less than 30 degrees
2. The intersection over union between ground truth grasping candidates and the
predicted result is more than 25%
while these conditions seem overly conservative, the intersection over union overlap is
bound to be considerably less than bounding box locations predicted by say, the YOLO
architecture. This is in part due to the structure of the conversion from ground truth
grasping candidates to ground truth feature maps previously described in 8.4.1. The
model does not predict the length of the grasping rectangle, i.e. the vertical displace-
ments that in physical interpretation denotes the length of the gripper plates which
is always constant, and therefore the bounding box representation from the model is
predicted using a third of the width to represent the height. This implies that a dis-
crepancy between manually labelled annotations and predicted oriented rectangles will
always be present since no consideration for the aspect ratio between width and height
were performed during labelling similarly to public grasping datasets.

CHAPTER 9
Visual Servoing
9.1
Introduction
Autonomous and reliable grasp planning relates to the iterative path planning process
required to successfully and securely grab hold of an object. Typically, open-loop solu-
tions assumes a static scene where objects are immobile upon retrieval, such methods
are usually prone to calibration errors and incorrect sensor measurements. To perform
grasping with robustness, the assumption of no moving targets is a poor approxima-
tion, and therefore a closed-loop solution that utilizes the image processing pipeline in
its entirety is theorized. Planning usually involves conversion of the detected grasping
candidate to robot world coordinates and perform motion control based on the error
between current position of the end-effector and the target approach vector.
In this chapter, the motion control system that seeks to actuate the manipulator on
the basis of the combined system of the YOLOv4 object detector, tracking algorithm
and anti-podal grasping network is described. Towards this end, the velocity kinematics
derived in chapter 3 will be used to calculate the joint velocities required to move the
end-effector frame towards the target. In the section that follows, an analysis of the
available methods followed by an overview of the selected approach. Lastly, a series of
tests are conducted on the real system to test the performance of the closed loop system
when the full image processing pipeline is used.
9.2
Analysis
In general there are two methods for solving the problem of vision-based control, pre-
sented in [7] as position-based visual servo control (PBVS), and image-based visual servo
control (IBVS). The main difference is the way the feedback data is provided by the
vision system. In PBVS, a vision system attempts to build a target approach vector for
the end-effector in the global coordinate system, and therefore requires the perspective
projection, camera intrinsic parameters and consequently the hand-eye transformation
matrix to be known quantities. For this reason, this method is prone to camera calibra-
tion errors and is highly reliant on real-time implementations of the three-dimensional
representation of the world.
Additionally, this methods assumes control of the end-
effector directly and therefore does not take into account if the object of interest leaves
the camera frame during control. This is solved in IBVS [89, 90] by calculating the
==PAGE:132==
114
9 Visual Servoing
target error in the image frame directly and performing control in the image space. This
method is therefore robust against camera calibration errors, however is prone to task
failure if the motion required in the image frame exceeds the reachable workspace of
the manipulator. The main diﬀiculties of IBVS is that while the image error is low, no
control is exerted to ensure that the trajectory trace of the image motion ensures that
a grasp can be achieved once the object is centered in the target location in the image
frame.
Based on the considerations above, a solution build on PBVS is the most suitable ap-
proach in this research as the controlled motion of the end-effector position is paramount
for achieving a suﬀicient grasp of the objects. Therefore considerations must be made
to ensure that the motion of the manipulator is handled such that the object is kept
within the field of the view of the camera. This will ultimately constrain the achievable
results as the stereo depth camera a have finite minimum sensing distance as described
previously in section 2.3.3. Therefore the final motion of the grasp must be performed
in open-loop as grasp candidate predictions are not calculated.
In the section that follows, a principle overview of the visual servoing strategy is
outlined using the derived object detector, tracking algorithm and anti-podal grasping
network. Lastly, a series of tests are conducted to verify that the feedback loop suﬀi-
ciently achieves grasp of moving objects.

10.2
Robustness test
The robustness of the proposed solution is validated by attempting 166 grasps in succes-
sion in a cluttered and unstructured environment. The potential causes of failure can
be divided into four separate situations:
1. If an object collides with the box container during placement. This can occur if the
grip is adequate to hold the object stable in the gripper, but is not properly centered
around its center of mass. It is arguable whether or not this metric is meaningful
for crop harvesting as usually harvested crop is dumped in containers that far
exceed the physical dimensions of the crop itself. However, in this research an
assessment of the precision of the grasping candidate is an important assessment.
2. If an object is lost due to the tracking algorithm causing an error by reassigning
a prediction to a new identification. This error is unlikely to occur, however large
rotational movement in the image plane, such as when an object is rotated 90
degrees can cause the linear velocity model of the Kalman filter to lose track of the
aspect ratio, especially for cucumbers that have very differently sized bounding
boxes when seen from different angles. This is detrimental to the visual servoing
strategy as it relies on predicting on fixed targets.
3. If an object is not appropriately contained in the space between the gripper plates
upon finishing the grip manoeuvre. The cause of this error can be an accumulation
of a wide array of errors: incorrect camera calibration, Hand-Eye transformation,
bounding box prediction and grasping candidate. The main cause of error can
likely be attributed to the anti-podal candidate grasping network giving a bad
final target vector due the object not being fully visible in the image frame.
4. If an object is mistakenly placed in the wrong assignment bin. This is an extreme
error and would have to be a cause of a cascade of errors in the image processing
pipeline.
The results from performing the robustness test is depicted in table 10.1 where the afore-
mentioned misclassification situation have been excluded due to no occurrences. It is
evident that the system have a 100% success rate at picking up tomatoes and therefore
did not fail an attempted grasp. The cucumber saw numerous errors, approximately

Graphical User Interface
As a side project, a graphical user interface (GUI) has been developed on the sideline with
the prospect of presenting a unified solution that can be used by other students to iterate
on the results presented in this thesis. The GUI incorporates the fully threaded server

is consistently streamed. A small collection of image samples can be seen in appendix

10.4
Future Work
The final system test proved that the implemented solution is suﬀicient at providing
optimal grasp candidates.
This section presents considerations concerning potential
improvements of the existing system
10.4.1
Additional Feedback
The stereo camera provides sensory information of the objects in the scene, but gives
little feedback on the achieved grasp due to the position in relation to the grasping point.
A force torque sensor reads the torque produced in each of the coordinate frame axes
of the end-effector and can therefore be used to access whether or not the gripper holds
onto an object when lifted off the surface. Therefore if no additional torque is produced
in the direction of the gravity vector, the grasping candidate was inadequate and a choice
==PAGE:144==
126
10 System Test
between re-orientating the manipulator to attempt a re-grasping or completely exclude
the object from future grasp attempts can be made.
Additionally, force feedback in the gripper plates directly would enable a wide variety
of complex grasping options by controlling the amount of grip force to securely hold on to
an object. Such a solution is presented in [91] by use of a Fuzzy Impedance Controller
and an iterative learning process to estimate the amount of torque required to avoid
slippage. This would also generalize well to a wider range of objects than presented
here.
10.4.2
6-DOF Position Estimatation
The current grasp candidates are based on a 2D representation in the image frame
and therefore only finds the rotation angle around the z-axis of the image frame. During
implementation it was attempted to estimate the normal of the points in the point cloud
using open source libraries, but the results were both slow and a poor approximation of
the true normal. Instead, a different type of neural network structure that predicts a 6
degree-of-freedom representation of the grasping rectangle could be explored as presented
in [92], however most state-of-the-art solutions are too slow for real-time applications as
the model structure is quite complex for such systems.
10.4.3
Additional Synthetic Data
In the case of this research, an attempt to generate a fully complex 3D representation of
the objects could be done to generate a dataset solely based on synthetic data. Vegetables
are common 3D objects and a quick search reveals a wide range of publicly available
models that can be introduced into different scenery or environments to produce datasets
that would otherwise not be achievable.
10.4.4
Improved Tracker
The deep appearance descriptor used in the tracking algorithm is trained on extracting
features specifically for person re-identification. Therefore, efforts in retraining the cosine
matching model on a dataset of the objects would most likely aid in the removal of
grasping failures due to lost tracking identification.

tomatoes was reported to be 100%, and 87.36% for cucumbers resulting in an average

CHAPTER 11
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to explore advanced detection and tracking methods to de-
sign a control paradigm that enables a suﬀicient grasping technique for agricultural
harvesting. Towards this end, a YOLOv4 object detector model was trained on the
basis of a comparative study between available state-of-the-art methodologies on a cus-
tom dataset using an automated data generation technique. The trained object detector
model proved eﬀicient at correct label assignment of the predicted bounding boxes with
a mean average precision score mAP@0.5 of 99.57% and mAP@0.75 of 95.89%.
To keep track of the temporal evolution of the objects, a tracking algorithm was
presented using the Deep SORT state-of-the-art architecture modified to work with the
object detector model. The tracker proved to be eﬀicient at misclassification rejection by
requiring a prediction to be made for at least 3 consecutive samples of an object before
it was classified as a confirmed track. The combined object detector and tracking stage
proved to have fast inference speeds of approximately 48ms, suitable for real-time usage.
A robotic grasp detection system was implemented adopting a Generative Residual
Convolutional Neural Network as an additional image processing layer that provided
suitable grasping candidates for the tracked objects with a precision score of 99.11%
evaluated at IoU@0.25 when trained on a dataset with synthetic data augmentation.
The image processing pipeline was combined to perform position-based visual servo-
ing control of a Universal Robots manipulator. The feedback control system delivers a
stable stream of grasping candidates for a target object at 15Hz sampling time. The sys-
tem was successful in both tracking and grasping of moving targets on a planar surface
and was rigorously tested for robustness. The system was reported to showcase 100%
accuracy when grasps are attempted on tomatoes that are within the field of the view
of the camera, and 87.36% on cucumbers yielding an average precision for attempted
grasps based on visual servoing control of 96.36%.
