Abstract
This paper investigates virtual reality-based gaze-controls with the goal of discovering met-
rics of measurement for optimizing the telerobotic controls of the user interface. The study
covers a selection of telerobotic- and gaze-control applications before introducing the exist-
ing project that is sought improved, and establishes a problem statement based on these
factors.
Designs and implementations are made for an alternate, threshold-based turning setup of
the user interface, a method of collecting gaze-data in real-time and lastly a modiﬁcation of
an existing heatmapping design for visualizing the gathered gaze-positions.
The developed software is tested across 12 participants, using a gaze-controlled robot to
navigate an oﬃce space. A statistical analysis is carried out on the results, with three de-
pendent variables and four independent variables.
The analysis reveals that diﬀerent control-grid sizes have a signiﬁcant impact on the driving
performance of the users in relation to two of the four investigated independent variables.
From these results the null hypothesis of the paper is rejected and a theorized enhancement
for the robot controls can be established. The problem statement concerned with optimizat-
ing the gaze-controls is not answered as a need for an additional testing phase is required,
to apply the newly discovered gaze-trends to form a new UI design to compare with the
original.

1.1
Motivation
The ﬁeld of telerobotics is continuing to evolve and is now at the point of having a multitude
of application areas for a wide variety of reasons. These areas include providing telerobot ex-
periences to manage physical handicap[25], maintenance of professional responsibilities[21],
as well as social- and academic relations[13], sometimes in the event of prolonged hospital-
ization or homestay[15] or the desire to be present at distant events, such as conferences[18].
Some adaptations of these use cases include the implementation of 360 degree camera views
for the pilot of the robot[11] so as to produce a more immersive experience than that of a
standard wide ﬁeld-of-view (FOV) type camera.
With the speciﬁc scenario for each case having diﬀerent approaches to the particular topic,
the underlying reason appears to be that people do not simply want the experience of seeing
environments far away from their own location, but instead desire the sensation of being
there, by being able to move around the viewed area, while also having their telepresence
acknowledged by the surroundings of the on-site robot.
Just like telerobotics, so too is the technology surrounding eye-tracking, and its uses in
gaze-control, growing to the point of interest for interacting with a variety of vehicles, such
as controlling motorized wheelchairs[7], unmanned ground vehicles (UGV)[20] or unmanned
aerial vehicles (UAV)[10]. These designs thereby keep the arms and hands "out of the equa-
tion", in terms of sending input to command to the machinery, either as an incapability to
factor arms and hands in or to free them up for alternative functionality, such as gesticula-
tion[3] that may improve the telepresence experience.
Taking a step back from the telerobotics and gaze-tracking, the underlying framework for
the software is of equal importance, and therefore the combination of Unity with robotics is
a topic of interest.
While Unity has had success emerging on the game developing market since its release in
2005, the engine is now also utilized across other technical ﬁelds.
Most importantly, in
regards to this project, Unity is being applied in collaboration with robotics[5], or more
speciﬁcally the open-source, meta-operating system of ROS (Robot Operating System) for
creating and managing software functionality in robots[6]. As a game engine allows for the
creation and structuring of a 2D or 3D digital world, the framework of the engine is also
applicable for the use of constructing a virtual simulation, VR interface, or both combined,
which in turn can then be incorporated with ROS by "weaving" the robot input-command
design together with the generated scene in Unity.

1.2
Scope
The overall setting of this project revolves around partaking in the development of an inter-
active virtual reality (VR) telepresence implementation, with the purpose of allowing for the
experience of a scenario at a distance from the stationary user’s location via a gaze-controlled
robot.
The paper focuses on the the user’s gaze-behavior in VR as a type of control for human-
robot interaction, the development of software to collect and visualize data pertaining to
the gaze-tracking taking place.
With this in mind, one main goal will be pursued, which will be to further optimize the
user interface (UI) of the existing system, with the purpose of improving user performance
for the target group of people in search of telepresence as a solution to the aforementioned
variety of diﬀerent application-areas of interest.
The approach for accomplishing this will be to collect data from the users’ focal points, while
interacting with a telerobotic setup. Subsequently, these points will be used to generate
heatmaps to grant a visual representation of the user-robot interaction, as a supplement to
the statistical analysis, so as to establish the optimum control grid among the diﬀerent UI
versions.
1.3
Outline
The research of literature will mainly focus on the subjects of anatomy- and behavioral trends
of the human eye, eye-tracking methodology, present day telerobotic applications. Through
these focus areas the problem statement for this paper become is set as the following:
"Can the existing development of VR gaze-tracking controls for telerobotics be improved
upon through the comparison of UI control-grids?"
From research and software development, a practical approach is carried out concerning itself
with looking into the aforementioned user interaction when utilizing the UI to maneuver the
robot’s movements. This will revolve around user testing, in order to acquire data samples,
statistical analysis and visualization for further inspection and the subsequent estimation of
trends across the data.
In summation, in order to successfully investigate a possible answer to the established prob-
lem statement, the study will need to assess the eﬃciency of the gaze-behavior of users
driving the robot. In order to achieve the necessary insight into the gaze-behavior of the
user, the software implementations will perform the following list of tasks:
– Collect gaze-data in real-time
– Alternate between input-grid sizes
– Generate heatmaps
– Process collected data
With these tools, a test scenario can be constructed, and the resulting data can be analyzed
and visualized, so as to further enhance the control schematics of the robot’s UI.
The structure of this paper will be to establish a foundation of information relating to eye-
tracking and telerobotics, deemed relevant for the scope of this paper and its goals, while

Hardware

main plastic frame being three 3D printed plastic discs, placed atop one-another, and held

design allows for the camera eye to be positioned 1.17m from the ground, when placed atop
the robot, while simultaneously having a ﬂexible structure, which allows for customizable
positioning of the camera in relation to the main robot structure. A table containing all the
equipment and gadgetry of interest for this paper can be seen in Figure 1.4.2[19][1][14][24],

ROS
The ROS-side of the project consists of an interrelationship between multiple ROS nodes,
existing in a network of process [17]. As the entirety of the ROS graph setup is somewhat
extensive only the functionality surrounding the connection to Unity is covered, and can be
seen in Figure 1.4.3.
Figure 1.4.3: The ROS system connecting the robot to Unity via a rosbridge
As seen in the illustration, two types of blocks are present, speciﬁcally rectangles (topics)
and ellipsoids (nodes). The structure of such blocks function by having a node responsible
for computing a certain task, and then output a message, which in turn is then published
into a topic. Subsequently, another node is then able to subscribe to the topic, thereby al-
lowing for it to receive messages from the previous node. Some of the ellipsoids are nodelets.
These work by connecting node-messages in such a way that, while both nodes are on the
same computer, a pointer is utilized, as opposed to packaging, sending and unpackaging the
message, as two regular nodes would.
With this syntax in mind, the setup for the ROS connection to Unity for this project can then
be elaborated upon. The node called /cmd_vel_mux (velocity command multiplexer) is
responsible for mixing the input velocity signal, so as to smoothen the movements of the
robot by avoiding rapid velocity starts. Meanwhile, the /mobile_base_nodelet_manager
nodelet then runs the multiple processes of of vital functionality for sending and receiving
information in between the controls and the robot - e.g. the ultrasonic sensors. Next, the
cmd_vel holds the ﬁnal velocity command messages, which are then seen by the subscribed
/arlobot and this node then sends the output signals to the robot motors.

Chapter 2
Design and Implementation
The design for the software expansions to the existing code seeks to determine whether new
structuring of the input grid, within the UI, can be made to check for dissimilarities between
a user’s expected point-of-turning and the actual one, and thereby how the tuning of control
input for turning can be further optimized. With this goal in mind, the following hypothesis
is formed:
"Changing the UI control-grid size has a signiﬁcant eﬀect on user performance when to
turning the robot"
with the null hypothesis
"Changing the UI control-grid size has no eﬀect on user performance when to turning the
robot"
In accordance with the established development goals of the introduction chapter, the new
expansions to the system needs to extract the real-time gaze-position. The grid structure of
the UI needs to be easily interchangeable for the developer, so as to enable quickly switching
between setups in between test runs. And lastly, software capable to generate a graphical
representation of the collected data must be developed, with the purpose of using this method
of data visualization to interpret the performances of test participants across tests.
In the pursuit of achieving the aforementioned functionalities, the focus of interest for de-
velopment is placed on the classes titled VRController and RobotInterface (highlighted
in Figure ??) in order to obtain the desired values, at the points in the system, deemed
most prudent for data extraction and UI modiﬁcation, respectively. Following the expansive
developments of the two existing classes, the newly created HeatmapPresenter class is
covered in detail.
The following sections cover the design thoughts behind approaching the tasks involved with
each of the software expansions to the project, before elaborating on how the implementa-

The implementation should be able to place the vertical grid lines of the control UI at spe-

the center-point of the VE’s input-square, to trigger the turning of the robot while steering

boundary between going straight and turning, and then subsequently checking a collection
of gaze-data for whether the expectation of the users parallel that of the UI established grid
size - more speciﬁcally, the width of the grid.

2.2
Collection of gaze-data in real-time
The entirely of the code responsible for collecting gaze-data is located in the VRController
class, with the functionality being designed in such a way that the data is collected at any
time when a user is steering the robot via gaze-positioning. The data collected is the x-
and y position of the user’s gaze-position within the four orange corners, situated in in the
front view of the VE. The coordinate values are all within the range of -1 and 1, depending
on the gaze-position, with the center of the perceived image being 0,0 as a result of the
FOVE VR headset design. Alongside the coordinate values, a timestamp is included in the
generated ﬁle, indicating the exact time of the given gaze-point’s extraction during the test.
Two options are available for stopping the collecting of data. The ﬁrst is in the event of
terminating the Unity simulation, while the other occurs if the amount of sample points
reaches a pre-set total amount. Should either of these two options turn out to be true then
the data is stored in a newly generated and labeled ﬁle.
For the sake of generating a ﬁle and writing the gathered coordinates to it, the _ﬁle and
_writer variables are deﬁned for later use, along with a simple boolean to be used for
checking whether or not the stream of data is closing. This setup is presented in Listing 3.
The next ﬁve variables are declared to set up two counters keeping track of data samples and
frames, as the relationship between the two are utilized for establishing the sampling rate
via _framesPerSample variable, while _samplesTotal and _frameMax sets limitation
values. These are included for the sake of avoiding excessively large datasets or out-of-bounds
system errors, respectively.
The current setup takes one sample for every 10th frame, leading to 5-6 samples per second,
depending on the frame rate of the overall system during a test. With the assumption that
a maximum of six samples are collected per second, the total amount wanted is set equal to
3600, in order to set an automatic ending of data extraction after approximately 10 minutes.

private List<Point> FindHeatArea(HeatPoint heatPoint, int radius) {
List<Point> circumferencePointsList = new List<Point>();
for (double i = 0; i <= 360; i += 10)
{
Point circumferencePoint = new Point
{
X = Convert.ToInt32(heatPoint.X + radius *
Math.Cos(ConvertDegreesToRadians(i))),
Y = Convert.ToInt32(heatPoint.Y + radius *
Math.Sin(ConvertDegreesToRadians(i)))
};
circumferencePointsList.Add(circumferencePoint);
}
return circumferencePointsList;
}

The experiment is designed to have four focus-points for the developer throughout the
process of having a participant perform a successful test. The grid UI software, the collision-
count of the robot hardware, the responsiveness of the gaze-controls in the VE and the
archiving of the generated data ﬁles.
The testing period is set up over the course of ﬁve days, with 12 participants partaking in
steering the robot through a pre-set path conﬁned within an oﬃce space. The demographics
of the participants vary from a wide variety of study subjects and professions, having the
age-range consist of a spectrum from 19 to 67 years old, with the majority positioned between
the ages of 23 and 28. The male-to-female ratio is 3:1, with all test subjects having a driver’s
license, 50% having tried VR before and none having experienced gaze-tracking.
The following sections go through a pre-test executed prior to the ﬁnal testing phase, before
moving on to the schematics of the experimental area and the technical- as well as practical
setup of both gadgetry, general software as well as the environment of the participant and
robot respectively, before moving on to the analyzation and visualization of the results.

Preceding the ﬁnal testing, a pre-test is performed with a 15th participant, whom is granted
the test ID number 1, leaving the following participants numbers ranging from ID #4 through

The pre-test keeps a less formal tone than that of the main testing period, so as to maintain

3.3.1
Counterbalancing
The ﬁnal part of the protocol is to look into the sequencing of test parameters across robot
runs, so as to prevent tendencies, parallel in between participants, from arising within the
ﬁnal collection of data - e.g. if a speciﬁc sequence order could prove to improve or worsen
the performance of the test. This kind of test issue is called an order eﬀect and can be
addressed through the implementation of counterbalancing[12]. This methodology concerns
itself with randomizing sequences of test conditions, for the sake of avoiding skewed results
brought on by the order eﬀects.
With the inclusion of counterbalancing to the test setup, the matrix of participants and their
test runs will even out the order eﬀect to some degree. The application of counterbalancing
to any n x n matrix is also called a Latin square. However, the random counterbalancing
may produce sequence pairs, such as two participants within the same Latin square having
setup B immediately followed by setup A. Consequently, the order eﬀect is still present,
though to a lesser degree.
Therefore, in the pursuit of compensating the data for this issue thoroughly, the balanced
Latin square[2] design is sought implemented. By setting up the algorithm for the sequencing
of matrix values in accordance with Figure 3.3.2[12] a balanced Latin square is capable of
preventing pairs of test conditions from occurring more than once.
Figure 3.3.2: Algorithm for setting up a balanced Latin square matrix[12]
With this in mind, the sequence of test matrix across participants is created with a online
random grid generator capable of preventing test parameter pairs [22].
By dividing the test subjects into two groups of six, a balanced Latin square design is
achieved across the 12 participants, as can be seen with the iteration of the sequence for
test ID #4 and test ID #10 in Table 3.1. As the two squares ﬁt the Latin square design,
the implemented design accomplishes balanced Latin squares, thereby counterbalancing the
experiments.
Additional order eﬀects can potentially be brought on by practice or fatigue[12], as a test
subject has the potential for unpredictably performing increasingly- well or worse, as the
test scenario is experienced multiple times - this will be investigated in the analysis of the
results under the topic of "Training". As these eﬀect may occur, the participants are not
permitted a trial-run prior to performing the six robot runs, but instead allowed breaks in
the event of experiencing headache or nausea.

Chapter 4
Results
Following the testing phase, the accumulated data is analyzed, visualized and interpreted.
The search for trends within the quantitative data is covered ﬁrst, followed by the qualitative
data of the test subjects’ feedback.
The main focus of the quantitative data is placed on the correlating behavioral patterns
between the dependent- (DV) and independent variables (IV). In summation, the tests are
performed on 12 test participants, with each one circumventing the robot’s environment
either clockwise or counterclockwise a total of 6 times.
With this, the total amount of
observations from the testing phase is 72. The perfect performance for driving the robot
would result in driving straight four times, with four turns of 90°. Naturally, it is no surprise
that the test participants do not fare so well, and for this reason, the following aspects are
investigated across the total amount of observations for analyzing the data:
• Independent variables
- Grid size (Large, Medium, Small)
- Direction (Left, Right)
- Training (Round 1-6)
• Dependent variables
- Completion time
- Collision count
- Percentage of going straight
- Percentage of correcting heading
The collected data is split up depending on the method of analysis. For the various generated
charts visualizing user behavior, the Y-axis is discarded, as the testing focuses on the turning
of the robot - i.e. the x-axis. For the heatmaps, the time-recording is discarded, as these
visualizations are used to get an idea of the users’ gaze-behavior overall.
Prior to the analysis of the IV variables’ behavior throughout the tests, the summed up DV
are covered, so as to allow for a fundamental impression of the data.

4.2
ANOVA
While the charts’ mean- and SEM values grant an indication of the diﬀerence in between
grids is signiﬁcant, it is prudent to analyse these results further, for the sake of validating
this indication. To do this, an analysis of variance (ANOVA) test is performed respectively
on the percentages for driving straight, and those for correcting heading, with the percent
values being grouped up for the three grid sizes. This method is then able to ascertain
==PAGE:39==
Chapter 4. Results
33
whether or not a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the three samples is present. A one-way
ANOVA test is used for the IV (grid size) and its three groups, set with the alpha value of
0.05 (5%) it should equal the value of 1 when added with the conﬁdence value[16].
The ANOVA reveals the "driving straight"-data as having a P-value of 9.18E-17 and the
"correcting heading"-data a P-value of 7.56E-07. As these values are below the alpha value
of 0.05, the ANOVA conﬁrms that the results are signiﬁcant, and that the null hypothesis
of "Changing the UI control-grid size has no eﬀect on subject performance related to turning
the robot" can be rejected.
4.3
T-test
The ANOVA does however not specify where in between the three IV groups the signif-
icant diﬀerence resides. Therefore, the inclusion of ad hoc testing is necessary, which is
accomplished via t-tests. Multiple t-tests are performed with two samples each (e.g. the
comparison between the Large grid and the Medium grid) and assuming unequal variance,
as the variance in between the observations groups is unknown. The alpha value is still set
to 0.05 for every t-test performed.
Six t-tests are carried out in total, with three checking the diﬀerence between grid size
groupings in correlation to the average percent of driving straight, while the other three
investigate the signiﬁcance in between the grid groups for the average percent of correcting
heading. The following values are revealed:
– Driving straight
Large-Medium, Two-tail P-value 3.01E-05
Medium-Small, Two-tail P-value 3.63E-08
Large-Small, Two-tail P-value 1.64E-12
– Correcting heading
Large-Medium, Two-tail P-value 0.001
Medium-Small, Two-tail P-value 0.002
Large-Small, Two-tail P-value 5.36E-05
With these t-test P-values the grid size groupings are more clearly interpretable in relation
to one-another, with the change to how much time is spent driving straight being the most
signiﬁcant of the two DVs, and the pattern of both the DVs being that the largest diﬀerence
is between the Large and Small grid sizes.

Chapter 5
Discussion
Throughout the development and testing for this study a variety of problematic aspects
arise, which potentially causing a negative impact on the functionality of the developed
code, or erroneous test data, due to unexpected factors. A selection of these discovered
aspects are covered, and reﬂected upon, for a better understanding of the produced study
and the room for future improvements.
The literature research for alternative approaches to telerobotics and gaze-controls is limited,
thereby leaving room for error in developing a testing design, robot- or UI functionality that
has potentially already been performed and deemed disadvantageous for a setup such as the
one covered in this project. For future work on this study, the amount of literature reviewed
can be expanded upon, to investigate existing trends discovered by other similar works, and
to ascertain the need for this speciﬁc type of design on the market.
The 360°camera handle is ineﬀective throughout the tests, as a result of some unknown
damage to the screw-grip at the bottom, thereby necessitating the need for duct tape to
keep the camera in place atop the ﬂexible arm. This may have caused slight movements to
the positioning of the camera, leading to the collected gaze-data being aﬀected. To address
this issue, the grip of the camera can either be ﬁxed, or the 360°camera can be replaced.
Saving the gaze-data, intensity maps and heatmaps will, with the current implementa-
tion, save on top of one-another, as an incrementor for the naming of the ﬁle is not cur-
rently included, thus making the need for re-labeling or re-locating the generated data-ﬁle
of paramount importance in between tests, to avoid losing data. This can be addressed, as
mentioned, with the implementation of an sequentially incremented value for the labeling
of the data-ﬁle.
Data for test ID #2 and #3 are damaged and therefore discarded, and another two test
subjects are called in for the sake of acquiring two perfect Latin squares to counterbalance
the test data. The reason behind the damaged C-test data for the two test participants is
very like a cause of the aforementioned data-saving procedure, and so the solution to these
issues are linked.
The necessity of the FileStream.Flush() function is based on the need for the buﬀered data
in the ﬁle system to be written to the data-ﬁle. This manual call to handle the buﬀered data
can be eliminated with the implementation of the FileOptions.WriteThrough. However, this
approach is very taxing on the overall processing of the program, and is therefore not used,
as it negatively aﬀects the real-time latency of the system.
In terms of the testing setup, the participants might navigate more calmly in open spaces at
the ends of the table setting as opposed to at the sides, as these side-sections appear thinner,
despite the white borders being set with a constant path width along the entire route. This
36
==PAGE:43==
Chapter 5: Discussion
37
issue can be countered by selecting a diﬀerent oﬃce space furniture setting, so as to aid the
test participant visually to experience the same feeling of room for maneuverability across
the entire test.
Additionally, the test participant seating is located in a public space, and therefore external
sounds during testing may aﬀect the performance of the individual runs to some degree. For
future testing, the setup can include not one, but two rooms, with one isolating the robot,
as with this current setup, and another for isolating the test subject.
The IVs of completion time and collision count are validated as having insigniﬁcant diﬀer-
ences between grid sizes and across training. However, with more extensive collecting of test
data across a larger participant group can illuminate additional tendencies of signiﬁcance
across the relations between the established DVs and IVs.
While the direction shows little to no diﬀerence between the DVs, the observation and anal-
ysis of it is nevertheless prudent, as tests similar to the one performed in this study will
continue to require test subject going through the robot course in both directions, so as
to not exhaust the muscles of the eyes by only steering in one direction. In extension of
this, the analysis of the directional IV can highlight the presence of one or more unexpected
factors inﬂuencing the data causing the user experience to change.
The heatmaps of the collected parameter data (A-F) do not show the calculated statistical
trends very clearly, thereby suggesting it is possible that the sample-rate can be increased,
to gather more points for each test, possibly making the tendencies more clearly visible.
The main concern of the project development is the need for setting up a second testing
phase, so as to utilize the discovered trends in the data for altering the UI and the testing
for an eﬀect, thereby successfully checking for whether or not an optimization of the system
is achieved. For this reason, the most logical next step for future works is to set up a new
test based on the ﬁndings of this report, to check for improved user performance between
the original setup and the newly designed one.
==PAGE:44==
Chapter 6
Conclusion
The study succeeds in implementing the three designated development goals, with a thresh-
olded setup for the control-grid of the UI, the collection of timestamped gaze-position co-
ordinates, and a modiﬁed heatmapping program, able to handle and display the collected
data.
Through testing the design is partially successful as it is able to show that alternating
threshold-based control-grids aﬀect the performance of turning the robot for two of the four
measured IVs, as the average completion times and collision counts prove insigniﬁcant. The
discovered behavioral data gaze-trends in turn grant insight for estimating an enhanced
control setup for the telerobotic VR UI.
This paper can not however deem the problem statement answered, as an additional iteration
of testing is required, to adjust the control-grid in accordance with the ﬁndings of the this
testing phase, so as to pinpoint this approach as a success or failure to optimize the steering
capabilities. Therefore, the study only succeeds in performing the groundwork in terms of
testing for steering optimization.

Test Protocol
Upon a test subject’s arrival, the person is seated and presented with refreshments, the
consent form and the pre-test questionnaire.
1. Subject reads through the consent form and signs it, before proceeding to ﬁlling out
the pre-test questionnaire [5min]
2. Subject is equipped with VR headset and a gaze-calibration is performed, while the
conductor verbally gives a brief instruction regarding the steering interactivity of
changing one’s gaze vertically or horizontally, the goal of circumventing the oﬃce
space without collisions and the presence of additional features in the VE. [2min]
3. Subject performs six test runs [18min]
(a) Subject navigates the robot around the room, while the conductor observes both
the subject’s VR point-of-view from a monitor and the robot in the room, noting
down any collisions
(b) Conductor terminates the simulation, collects the generated data ﬁle, changes the
grid setup and resets the robot position for the next run, while the subject is able
to rest
4. Subject removes VR headset, and the conductor inquires about any signs of nausea or
headache, before the subject ﬁlls out the post-test questionnaire [5min]
Total test time: 30min
