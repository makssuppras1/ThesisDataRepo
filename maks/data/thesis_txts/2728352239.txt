Background
Unlocking the mysteries beneath the waves, this thesis delves into fish compositions and size distribu-
tions in seven confined areas in Danish waters. While stock assessments may be indicating sustainable
stocks, still local fishermen and anglers are experiencing decreases in catches. Only one flounder ap-
peared in a 72 hour video in Vejle Fjord (Politiken, 26. april 2023), and at the Danish Championships
in Under Water (UW) hunting in Storebælt in 2022, only 17 out of 42 contestants came back with a
catch (Danmarks Naturfredningsforening, november 2022). Moreover, a mere eight out of 117 coastal
waters are currently classified as "Good Ecological State" by the Danish Environmental Protection
Agency 2019.
Through extensive data mining of large data sets from international trawl surveys, I will use
statistical modelling to shed light on the researched area’s historical states and current conditions
through six species. The data, areas and species are described below.
0.1
Description of data
The main data utilized in this study has been sourced from the International Council of Exploration
of the Sea’s (ICES) online Database of Trawl Surveys (DATRAS) which is primarily collected by
ICES expert groups from bottom trawl fish surveys (ICES 2023d). It covers a large area of Nothern
Europe with more than 45 years of continuous time series data. The data is high quality with already
standardised data formats and calculations (ibid.). Two surveys, namely The North Sea Internaional
Bottom Trawl Survey (NS IBTS) and the Baltic International Trawl Survey (BITS) were employed.
Historically, theses surveys have provided distinct data on different species, obtained through varying
methodologies. However, both surveys employ the standardized ’cpue per length per hour per haul’
which will be the main variable assessed in this study.
0.1.1
Baltic International Trawl Survey (BITS)
BITS was initially established with the primary objective to coordinate national surveys in the Baltic
region, with a specific focus on the cod recruitment. The first meeting of the BITS working group
took place in 1996, marking the initiation of the development of appropriate manuals and development
of new standard fishing gears. The new coordinated survey design was implemented in 2001 where
quarter 4 (Q4) has been included since (ICES 2017).
The standard species targeted in the survey now comprise Atlantic cod, European flounder and
plaice, and to some extent, sprat and herring. The standard fishing gear employed since 2001 is the
TV-3 bottom trawl which comes in two variations (ibid.):
• The smaller variant with a circumference of 520 meshes (TVS) which is designated for the vessels
with engine power less than 600 kW.
• The larger variant with a circumference of 930 meshes (TVL) which is designated for vessels with
engine power exceeding 600 kW.
All trawl types adhere a standard tow duration of 30 minutes (ibid.). The survey covers ICES
subdivisions 21-32(ibid., pp. 44). Figure 1 shows the timeline of gears used from different ships and

North Sea International Bottom Trawl Survey (NS IBTS)
NS IBTS has been conducted in the first quarter of the year since the beginning of the 1960s. Tra-
ditionally, it was aimed at the juvenile herrings and later extended geographically to Skagerrak and
the Nothern Kattegat. In the 70s other species were also included. From 1991-1996 surveys were con-
ducted in all quarters but from 1997 only in quarter 1 (Q1) and 3 (Q3). Surveys in Q1 are conducted in
February, and in August in Q3. The standard species include: Atlantic cod, plaice, European flounder,
herring, whiting, haddock, sprat, norway pout, saithe and mackerel. There has been no major changes
since 1991 (ICES 2020). The survey has covered the Standard Roundfish Areas 1-9 since 1991 for all
standard species (ibid., pp. 55). All trawl types adhere a standard tow duration of 30 minutes. There
was no standardisation of gear prior to 1977, but after ICES recommended that all ships should use a

Research areas
Seven areas were chosen for further analyses. The characteristics of each area are described below. See
Figure (3a) for placement of areas.
0.2.1
Area 1
Area 1 is placed in the northern part of Kattegat, east of Jutland. It has a minimum depth of -50 meters
(GEUS 1997) and primarily consists of muddy sediments (Figure 3b). The fishing pressure is high with
an average annual fishing effort from 2012-2020 between 5-10 in Total Swept Area Ratio (SAR) in the
eastern part of the area (Ritzau and Reijden 2023). Both BITS and NS-IBTS surveys were conducted
in this area with most hauls from BITS (Figure 3a). Mean monthly sea bottom temperatures have
been increasing since 1993 (Figure 4).
0.2.2
Area 2
Located west of Læsø, this area has the steepest depth gradient reaching depths down to -100 meters
(GEUS 1997). The sediments consist predominantly of mud and sand (Figure 3b). The fishing pressure
is high with an average annual fishing effort from 2012-2020 between 1-5 in SAR in most of the area
(Ritzau and Reijden 2023). Surveys from both BITS and NS-IBTS were used for this area (Figure 3a).
Mean monthly sea bottom temperatures have been increasing since 1993 (Figure 4).
0.2.3
Area 3
This area is located north of Øresund and was designated as a no-take zone (NTZ) in 2009. Depths
ranging from -20- -30 meters (GEUS 1997), with sediments primarily consisting of mud (Figure 3b).
The surveys conducted in this area include both BITS and NS-IBTS. Mean monthly sea bottom
temperatures have been increasing since 1993 (Figure 4).

Species
0.3.1
Plaice
Plaice (Pleuronectes Platessa) is a demersal species and common in Danish waters. It is primarily
living on sandy or mixed sediments from the coast and until -200 meters depth (Kottelat and Freyhof
1995). The older, the deeper the occurrence (Frimodt 1995). Female plaice matures around ages 3-6
years and males around 2-4 years. Migration towards spawning grounds, which are at -20- -40m. depth
begins at the end of winter (Vinnikov, Thomson, and Munroe 2018). Eggs and larvae drift with the
currents from the spawning grounds to inshore, shallower waters along the coasts. In Kattegat, the

most important growing areas. After 1-2 months the larvae metamorphose into a flat fish (Murua
and Saborido-Rey 2003). The largest plaice can become up to 90-100 cm. long with a weight of 7kg.
although they are rarely seen larger than 50cm. (Nielsen 1986; Muus and Dahlstróm 1975).
According to ICES 2022a landings were highest between 1999-2003 with a maximum catch of 8.000

large increase. Plaice is a large bycatch in the Nephrops and cod fishery (ibid.). The minimum landing
size of plaice in subdivisions 21-23 (Kattegat, Belt Seas, and the Sound) is 27cm.
0.3.2
European flounder
European flounder (Platichtys flesus) is a common species in Danish waters with an increase in indi-
viduals from Kattegat to Østersøen. Flounder can, unlike plaice, live in brackish waters and thrive
well in Danish fjords as juveniles. Flounder is also very tolerant in waters with high temperatures and
fluctuations in salinity (Hansen 2019). Flounder prefers sand and muddy sediments without vegetation
(Kottelat and Freyhof 1995). During spring and summer, it mainly stays near the coast to hunt for
food. During fall and winter, it seeks deeper waters to spawn pelagic at -20- -100 meters depth (Balon
1990). After spawning the eggs and larvae drift to the growing areas near the coast(Rochard and Elie
1994). They often start living at the bottom before they arrive at the growing areas at the beginning of
summer. The males become mature as 3-year-olds when they’re about 20-25 cm. long whereas females
are 4 years old and lengths between 20-35 cm. (Hansen 2019).
0.3.3
Atlantic cod
In Danish waters Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) is found in Skagerrak, Kattegat and the Baltic. It can
live with salinities as low as 6 ‰but the genetic variations are large ranging from the North Sea to
the Baltic. It is primarily found at temperatures around 2-10 ℃but can handle temperatures up to 20
℃(Stubgaard 2017).
Spawning occurs in Danish waters around January- March. In Øresund there’s a 0-catch quota
during the spawning period as this, and north of Øresund and along the Swedish coast, are the common
spawning grounds. The mature cod gathers at -30- -60 meters depths to spawn pelagically (Cohen
et al. 1990). After 3-4 weeks the eggs hatch into 4-5mm. larvae at the water surface. When they
are about 3 months old they seek towards the bottom at shallower waters and among eelgrass and
seaweed. In the fall, when the offspring is around 12-15cm they seek towards stone reefs where it is
possible to hide from predators. Cod can get up to 150cm long and weigh around 40kg but this is rare
(ibid.; Frimodt 1995).
Cod in the Danish waters are included in two stocks: The Kattegat and Western Baltic. The
HELCOM Red List for the Baltic Sea and Kattegat has categorized cod as Vulnerable (ICES 2023a).
For the Kattegat stock, there is no targeted cod fishery, although cod is mainly taken as bycatch in
the Nephrops fishery. Looking at the Catches for the Kattegat Stock, they have been declining since

0.3.4
Herring
Atlantic Herring (Clupea harengus) is a common schooling fish in Danish waters. It is living pelagically
in schools with thousands of herrings from different stocks. The juveniles can also join schools with
sprat and Norway pout. They are known for their long migrations between spawning and feeding areas
but can be found around the Danish coasts all year round (Narberhaus, Krause, and Bernitt 2012).
Herring typically mature around ages 2-3 when they reach a size of 25 cm. (ICES 2009) Depending
on genetics, herring spawns either during spring or fall. In Danish water, during spring spawning
happens on lower waters around eel grass and seaweed. The eggs stick to the eelgrass and seaweed and
hatch, depending on temperature, until one month after. The larvae drift until they metamorphose
into juveniles (Hildebrand 1964).
Spawning during fall, on the other hand, often occurs on stone and gravel in deeper waters. For the
North Sea Herring, the larvae drift towards the Danish Westcoast, Skagerrak and Kattegat until they
mature and migrate back to the spawning areas of the British Eastcoast (ibid.; Deurs and Bekkevold
2023).
The stock development over time from ICES of the western Baltic spring-spawning herring in
subdivisions 20-24 shows a sharp decline in catches since 1992-2011, going from 180.000 tons to ap-
proximately 30.000 tons. A small increase from 2012-2016 of 20.000 tons followed by a decrease until
2022 (ICES 2023c). SSB has been below Blim since 2005 (ibid.).
0.3.5
Sprat
Sprat (Sprattus sprattus) can be found everywhere in the Danish waters but is most common near
the coasts of Fyn and Zealand and around Læsø. It is a pelagic schooling fish often seen swimming
with younger herring of the same size. Sprat is very tolerant towards temperature changes and salinity
(Whitehead 1988; Deurs, MacKenzie, and Henriksen 2022).
Unlike herring, sprat does not migrate to reach spawning areas but forms local spawning populations
in different areas. The spawning period runs from spring till late summer. It reaches maturity already
after a year of being spawned itself. Eggs are pelagic, and larvae drift until they are large enough to
be part of a schooling (Parmanne, Rechlin, and Sjöstrand 1994; Whitehead 1988).
Research shows that the spawning time for sprat is very dependent on temperature. More fish fry
is seen in years with temperatures exceeding five ℃in months where eggs and larvae are developing
(Deurs, MacKenzie, and Henriksen 2022).
The SSB are large enough to maintain the stock, although the fishing pressure now is too high to
maintain a sustainable stock. It has been above Blim since app. 1994. The catches have varied a lot,
with a significant decrease from 1976-1986 followed by fluctuations until 2022. (ICES 2023e).

Whiting
Whiting (Merlangius merlangus) is also a common species in Danish waters and commonly found from
-30 to -100 m. depth. Mainly on mud and gravel bottoms (Cohen et al. 1990). Females are larger
than males and mature around age 2 (Zheng et al. 2002) when they are <20cm (Hislop et al. 1991).
Eggs are pelagic and can take up to 10 days to hatch (Russel 1976). Part of the Skagerrak population
is probably spawning in the North Sea in summer.
A large amount of whiting caught is as discards, and there has been almost no catches since 1997.

INTRODUCTION
Examining trends in fish species compositions
and size distributions using Generalised Additive
Models (GAM): A focus on local dynamics
Line Overgaard Bering
Aquatic Science and Technology, Technical University of Denmark
Abstract
Marine ecosystems face severe pressure from human activities such as over-exploitation and pollution.
Despite international initiatives, changes in species abundance and size distributions persist. While
stock assessments encompass large areas, they potentially overlook fine-scale spatial dynamics. To
look at the fine scale dynamics this study employs data from two international trawl surveys: The
North Sea International Trawl Survey (NS IBTS) and the Baltic International Trawl Survey (BITS).
Through Generalised Additive Models (GAM), the trend in fish abundance in seven confined, local
areas in Danish waters has been analysed using fish as indicators of the area’s conditions. Although
high variance between areas, species and size groups is inevitable, the results also highlight distinct
patterns. Øresund has high adult abundance, which may be due to a trawl ban since the 1930s. Areas
in the northern Kattegat show abundant juveniles and young adult pelagic species but low abundances
of flatfish. Storebælt, Mecklenburg Bay, and southwest of Bornholm have increases in flatfish, notably
plaice. Some of these local dynamics challenge the broader stock assessments. The study underscores
the importance of local research and sea preservation. Protective measures hold promise, but a deeper
exploration of local habitats and environmental factors is essential.

marine ecosystems to achieve good and sustainable
states(Borja et al. 2017; Danovaro et al. 2016).
Denmark is no exception to the global trend of
threatened marine ecosystems. Only eight out of
117 coastal waters in Denmark have attained GES
(Danish Environmental Protection Agency 2019).
The degradation is attributed to factors such as
the influx of nutrients from agriculture (Hansen
and Rytter 2022), the trawl fisheries’ negative ef-
fects on the seabed (Feekings, Lewy, and Madsen
2013) and over-exploitation of marine resources
(kilde). Consequences include the loss of eelgrass
because of high nutrient influxes resulting in fewer
nursery habitats and lowered water quality (Staehr
et al. 2019). Furthermore, events of hypoxia are
increasing in Danish waters deriving many nega-
tive effects such as collapse or mass mortality of
marine benthic macrofaunal communities (Josef-
son and Widbom 1988; Conley et al. 2007). Ad-
ditionally, the persistently low Spawning Stock
Biomass (SSB) of Atlantic Cod (Gadus morhua)
since the 2000s has resulted in the disappearance of
large spawning aggregations in Kattegat/eastern-
Skagerrak (Cardinale and Svedäng 2004).
Despite international trawl surveys and fish
stock assessment, little is known about the fish
communities in smaller confined areas. Stock as-
sessments evaluate stocks across broader regions
like the North Sea, the Kattegat/Skagerrak area,
and the Western Baltic Sea. These may be sus-
tainable, while local habitats and fish populations
might be adversely affected.
This issue is ad-
dressed in coastal regions by the Danish volunteer-
based program, Key Fisher, which is collecting
data through citizen science (Støttrup et al. 2018).
Still, knowledge of local areas on deeper waters is
needed as this may help understand the states and
potential effects on local fish communities.
The present study aims to shed light on se-
lected, confined, smaller areas on deeper waters
to obtain a local view of fish species composition
and size distributions. By analysing the histori-
cal states and the current conditions, this study
provides a new foundational understanding vi-
tal for effective protection measures and informed
decision-making.
The possibility of using international trawl sur-
veys to scrutinize and elucidate the dynamics of
fish communities across seven areas in Danish wa-
ters have been investigated. Employing statistical
modelling techniques through Generalized Addi-
tive Models (GAM) will help to provide compre-
hensive insights into the status of fish populations
and their trends by using fish as an indicator.
2
Materials and methods
2.1
Data
For the analysis of spatiotemporal changes in the
fish community in confined, local areas in Dan-
ish waters, available information from two specific
surveys have been used: the North Sea Interna-
tional Bottom Trawl Survey (NS IBTS) and the
Baltic International Trawl Survey (BITS), both
from the International Council of Exploration of
the Sea’s (ICES) online Database of Trawl Surveys
(DATRAS). Among others, the data contains in-
formation about the catch per unit effort (CPUE)
per length, location, time, trawl depth, ship and
gear.
The study focused on following six commer-
cially important species with sufficient information
within the study areas: Cod (Gadus morhua), Eu-
ropean flounder (Platichtys flesus), plaice (Pleu-
ronectes platessa),
herring (Clupea harengus),
sprat (Sprattus sprattus) and whiting (Merlangius
merlangus).

2.3
Data analysis
The relative abundance of the six species was in-
vestigated over time, between 5-year periods from
2000 to 2022, while accounting for confounding
factors.
For this, Generalised Additive Models
(GAMs) were employed.
The trend in relative
abundance over time is likely not linear and GAMs
allow to estimate non-linear effects by the use of
smooth functions(Wood 2017). It provides a ro-
bust approach to uncover nuanced trends and pat-
terns so the temporal dynamics of CPUE can be
better understood.
In Equation 1 the mathematical notation of the
model is depicted.
g(CPUE) = f1(year) + f2(depth) + Quarter+
Sediment + Ugear
(1)
Where f1 is a cubic regression spline on year and f2
is a thin-plate regression spline on the trawl depth.
Quarter and Sediment are fixed effects and U is
a random effect of Gear. g(CPUE) is a log-link
function specified by the tweedie family, indicating
that the response variable was transformed to log-
scale during modelling and back-transformed when
completed. The maximum allowed wiggliness (as
defined by the number of knots, k) for f1 and f2
has been specified individually in each model. If
only one level of a random effect was available (eg.
only GOV gear), it was excluded from the model.
Finally, the minimum best model was determined
based on the Aikaike Information Criteria (AIC)
and confidence intervals of 95% were calculated.
CPUE was predicted assuming median depth for
each area, quarter 4, gear type "TVS" and sedi-
ment type "Mud".
A total of 126 models were constructed. See
Figure 6. All model assumptions were thoroughly
checked (7.3), and in case any random effects ex-

4
Discussion
In the following, I will discuss the differences in
species compositions and size distribution between
areas. Furthermore, I will look into the limitations
of the method and future work.
4.1
Differences between areas
Looking at the overall abundance of all species size
groups in all areas, a clear picture emerges: Øre-
sund (area 4) is the area where all species’ adult
size groups have their highest abundances in 2022
according to models (except plaice- it’s doing best
in area 5 and 6), while area 1 is the area where
all juvenile and young adult pelagic species show
to have highest abundances. Simultaneously, area
2 is especially good for juvenile and young adult
herring while still being the area where all plaice,
flounder and sprat have the lowest abundances in
2022 according to models (Refer to Appendix 7.4).
The abundance does not seem to be correlated
with the number of hauls as these areas are not
the ones with the most hauls (Table 3).
There are a number of potential reasons for
the observed differences in the relative abundance
between areas. First, the areas might fall under
different management regimes. In fact, the area
with the highest abundance in adults for 5 out
of 6 species (area 4) has been under the protec-
tion of a trawl ban since the 1930s due to shipping
traffic (Øresundsakvariet 2019).
The lower fish-
ing pressure in comparison to the other areas is
likely to have positive effects on the large individ-
uals that are most sensitive to the fishing gear.
This result is in line with the findings by Ander-
son et al. 2008 and Boyce, Sinclair, and White 1999
who found that larger individuals disappear when
the fishing pressure is high. Recent findings from
Øresundsakvariet in Øresund indicate an increase
in juvenile cod (Øresundsakvariet 2023).
These
observations are consistent with the observations
in area 4 when compared to the 2010-2014 time
frame; since then, both juvenile and young adult
cod populations have experienced positive growth.
Additionally, compared to the period of 2000-2004,
a rise in young adult cod is apparent, which may
be attributed to the new protection measures for
catches of cod by recreational anglers (EU 2022).
This is contradicting to the stock assessment of
cod: It is experiencing a collapse (ICES 2023a),
but at the same time, it is doing well, locally, in
Øresund.
However, the changes in fish abundance are not
uniform. While the small area north of Øresund
(area 3) has the fewest hauls (Table 3), only cod
has this area as the one with the lowest abundance
in 2022.
While the high abundance in area 4 seems to
support the positive effect of management mea-
sures on fish abundance, the other protected area
(area 3) does not show consistently high abun-
dance for the species studied here. In fact, this
area is one of the lowest abundance of juvenile
and adult cod in comparison to the other areas,
although this area is being protected as a No Take
Zone (NTZ) since 2009. These results are in line
with the findings by Sköld et al. 2022 who found
stagnating cod abundances. Comparing the most
recent period (2018-2022) in area 3 to the period
immediately following the initiation of protection
(2010-2014), the results of this study also show
a decline in juvenile cod. Interestingly, this area
exhibits an upward trend in adult flatfish, young
adult plaice, and juvenile and young adult whit-
ing. Coherently, Sköld et al.(2022) point out that
the increase in abundance of these species could
be attributed to the protective measures.
The findings regarding area 3, and to a lesser
extent also regarding area 1 and 4, have to be in-
terpreted with caution as these areas have very low
sample sizes of below five hauls per year for area
3 and below 10 for areas 4 and 1 (See Table 3).

DISCUSSION
While the area east of Læsø (area 2) shows
to be an area of high abundances of juvenile and
young adult herring, the area is simultaneously the
area with the lowest abundance of flatfish in 2022.
This can potentially be explained by the very deep
waters (Table 1), where large individuals of plaice
are expected to dominate (Frimodt 1995).
The
largest size of plaice observed in area 2 is merely
36cm. This could be an indication of the larger in-
dividuals having disappeared because of overfish-
ing (ICES 2022a;Anderson et al. 2008).
On the other hand, areas 5, 6 and 7 are ar-
eas where flatfish are generally increasing and have
high abundances in 2022, according to the mod-
els.
In particular, plaice with an increase of a
factor of around 145 in areas 6 and 7 from 2000-
2004 to 2018-2022. This finding aligns well with
the stock assessment of the species. According to
ICES 2022a, landings of plaice were highest be-
tween 1999-2003, where SSB at the same time was
low. Since 2017 the SSB has experienced a large
increase. The same pattern is seen in my results;
more adults today than in 2000-2004.
The reasons for the increase in flatfish can be
many. A study by Rau et al. 2019 shows that the
availability of benthic prey organisms in the stud-
ied area (The Baltic) was verified as a biotic driver
for flatfish, especially for plaice. Another reason
could be the low abundance of other species, re-
sulting in fewer competitors or predators.
Conversely, area 5 is aligning with accounts
from anglers and underwater hunters reporting
challenging conditions in the region in recent years,
particularly in the context of cod populations
(Støttrup et al. 2020; Guldagger and Dahlager
2023). Their observations of decreases in this area
fit with the results of this study’s decrease in cod
compared to 2010-2014. However, an increase in
young adults and juveniles is seen compared to
the later period of 2000-2004. This demonstrates
one of the intended purposes of this study (actu-
ally the shiny app): Facilitating the recognition
of such patterns and the need for a nuanced un-
derstanding of the temporal fluctuations and the
broader historical context.
Area 1 showed the highest abundances of the
juvenile and young adults of the pelagic species
in 2022, showing an increase in abundance for
all species except juvenile herring since the 2000-
2004 period. Looking into the stock assessments,
the same rise in whiting since 2010-2014 is de-
tected (ICES 2022b). Sprat exhibits many fluc-
tuations, making interpretations more challenging
(ICES 2023e). However, another picture emerges
for herring.
A substantial decline in herring’s
SSB is evident from 1992 to 2011 within subdivi-
sions 20-24. Despite this decline, the SSB during
that period remains higher than the present lev-
els (ICES 2023c). The results reveal a contrasting
trend when compared to the period of 2000-2004.
While juveniles exhibit a general decrease, the two
adult size groups experience an increase, except in
area 3. This divergence from the stock assessment
could be attributed to the focus solely on quarters
4 and 1, while the advice pertains to spring her-
ring. Removing quarters 2 and 3 limits the analy-
sis to a six-month autumn and winter period, po-
tentially affecting results, particularly for species
with a strong summer presence. Conversely, in-
cluding quarters 2 and 3 could skew models due to
limited data.
In conclusion, it seems like tendencies are more
pronounced over the longer-term (since 2000-2004)
than the short-term (2010-2014). This rationale is
well-founded, as changes since the most recent pe-
riod are not drastic. Nevertheless, while certain
areas might appear to show positive or negative

4.2
Limitations and future work
Although some trends are emerging, there is a gen-
eral picture of high variability across species and
areas. This gives rise to three principal topics of
discussion: The rejected models and their under-
lying patterns, the utilization of two surveys, and
the use of multiple sub-models rather than a single
comprehensive model.
4.2.1
Non-converged models
Many juvenile and adult size groups lack explana-
tory results due to rejected models.
Either the
models did not converge or, in some cases, the
minimum best model did not include the time vari-
able, i.e.
the abundance is constant over time.
This could either be due to the fact that time did
not significantly improve the model fit or lack of
data. In either case, the difference in abundance
between periods is not shown in Figures 7 and 8
as the abundance is constant over time.
This is especially apparent for juvenile plaice,
sprat and flounder, young adult flounder and adult
cod and whiting. For juvenile plaice and flounder,
this aligns with their preference for shallower wa-
ters (Frimodt 1995). However, a unique exception
is herring, where models for all areas and sizes are
robust, with small confidence intervals. This can
be attributed to a generally higher number of in-
dividual herrings across all areas, explained by the
prominent schools in autumn.
The exclusion of models for the aforementioned
species’ size groups is often linked to the presence
of a substantial proportion (>50%) of hauls where
no fish of the specific species and size group were
caught, as detailed in Appendix 7.2. The poten-
tial option of omitting these instances from the
analysis has been considered. However, removing
them entirely could introduce a distortion, skew-
ing the results in a more optimistic direction by
potentially inflating estimates of fish abundance
(Hyndman and Athanasopoulos 2021, ch. 3).
Nevertheless, despite the many zero-catches,
the models were excluded primarily due to non-
convergence issues. Consequently, our analysis po-
tentially overlooks valuable insights regarding the
scarcity of these species where this happened. This
omission could potentially obscure an underlying
trend, which might have been shown if the models
were better equipped with more extensive histori-
cal data.
Yet, during the data pre-processing phase, the
decision to cut off a substantial part of the tem-
poral data series was primarily motivated by di-
verse gear types, especially in the BITS data se-
ries (Figure 1 in Background). One could argue
that by extending the temporal span, comparisons
could be enabled across a more extended period
instead of only a medium (2000-2004) and shorter
(2010-2014). But as the objective of the research
centres on optimizing model fit to the available
data, thus facilitating optimal inter-temporal com-
parisons, the models would be more sensitive to
including years with irregular data quality.
To sum up, the lack of models can result from
many factors:
Few individuals, lack of histori-
cal data or too many hauls with zero catches of
species’ size groups. The general pattern suggests
that the models are doing well at picking up trends
in juvenile roundfish, whereas, for flatfish, it’s the
adults.

4.2.2
Multiple surveys
The gear type variable also reflects the surveys
conducted. Although heightened gear significance
might be anticipated in areas with two surveys (ar-
eas 1, 2, and 3), this is rarely prevalent in areas 1
and 2. However, the significance is often in areas
6 and 7, the two gear types deployed by Dana and
Havfisken, namely TVL and TVS, respectively, are
the reason for this. Both are in the BITS survey.
This approach of dual surveys aims to augment the
data pool, enhancing models’ ability to elucidate
historical trends due to increased data availability
(Singh 2020).
Yet, concerns arise regarding po-
tential biases from mixing data sources and gear
types, potentially leading to imprecise estimations
(ibid.). However, I contend that the methodology
remains robust and serves the overarching aim of
examining species trends in different areas through
time.
4.2.3
Numerous sub-models
Lastly, my approach utilizes numerous sub-models
to account for spatial heterogeneity.
While a
spatio-temporal model would also account for the
spatial dimension and could utilize the data from
all areas at the same time, this more complex
model is likely to require more data and
However, an important thing to keep in mind
is that the models may indicate trends within local
habitats, yet they do not fully capture dynamics
in coastal areas.
5
Conclusion
Despite stock assessments, our understanding of
fish communities in smaller confined areas remains
limited. To address this gap, I have utilized inter-
national trawl survey data and GAMs with fish
as indicators of the area’s conditions.
This ap-
proach has allowed for a unique insight into local
dynamics, and cases where a stock’s overall status
contradicts its local performance.
The most northern area in Kattegat (area 1)
shows high abundances of juvenile and young adult
pelagic species. Area 2, east of Læsø in Kattegat,
has increases in juvenile and young adult herring
but low flatfish abundances. The two protected ar-
eas, Øresund and the NTZ north of Øresund (areas
4 and 3 respectively), show different results. While
Øresund stands out, according to models, with
high abundances of adult size groups in 2022, the
NTZ shows stagnating cod abundances since the
initiation of protection although an upward trend
in other species is seen. Storebælt, Mecklenburg
Bay and southwest of Bornholm (areas 5, 6 and 7)
witness increased flatfish abundances, particularly
plaice. While the stock assessment of cod shows a
collapse, the finding of this study indicates that it
is increasing in some local areas.
The study acknowledges limitations tied to
data quality, non-converging models, and the use
of multiple surveys and gear types. Despite these
complexities, the models still shed light on local-
ized dynamics and GAMs have proven a useful tool
for the analyses of trends in the fish communities.
Future work could involve exploring rejected
models’ insights, addressing potential biases, and
considering spatio-temporal models. A larger his-
torical dataset, more focus on environmental pa-
rameters and tailored survey designs could en-
hance precision.
In conclusion, this study emphasizes local vari-
ations in fish communities across areas, showcasing
the importance of diving into local areas and their
dynamics.
