Abstract
The need for smaller and more eﬃcient power supplies has also reached the railroad indus-
try. Rosenfelt & West Engineering are currently using an older linear AC/DC converter
for their level crossing systems, this is both an ineﬃcient and unnecessarily large solution,
providing poor power density. A bridgeless dual SEPIC is proposed as a replacement, as
this oﬀers great power factor, and high eﬃciency as the losses through the diode bridge
rectiﬁer is avoided. A circuit analysis of the proposed converter is carried through to ﬁnd
expressions for the voltage and current stresses experienced by diﬀerent components. A
wirewound and an interleaved planar solution for the transformer is explored with each
being described in terms of their parasitic elements. The converter is modelled using the
State Space Averaging method in MATLAB/Simulink, and a controller using a compen-
sator and notch ﬁlter is implemented, and veriﬁed using SPICE simulations. The ﬁnal
converter was never able to achieve full power level because of robustness issues with the
switching devices, however, it showed promising results regarding the power factor and
THD, as well as the expected level of eﬃciency. Further work has to go into the topology
in order to solve the fundamental issue of large output voltage ripple, for the converter to
be usable with Rosenfelt & West’s equipment.

Introduction
1.1
Present converter
Rosenfelt & West Engineering A/S is a company based in Ølstykke, Denmark, that spe-
cialises in rail safety technology, mainly level crossing systems and marshalling yards for
the Danish and Swedish railroads.
Rosenfelt & West operate and maintain all 300 level crossings owned by private railroads
in Denmark, with the usual level crossing system involving the following components:
Beams, and beam drivers, often equipped with lights. Masts with warning lights for road
vehicles and trains, as well as bells, either of bronze, or electronic. Axle-counters on the
train tracks and inductive sensors under the road for detecting traﬃc. Lastly, the ”hut”
housing the electronics.
In older, simpler systems that utilize relays, the electronics are housed in a cabinet instead
of a hut.
Inside the hut, a variety of diﬀerent components are housed, from the circuit boards
operating the crossing itself (ZPAS), to boards operating the inductive road sensors, a
circuit breaker, batteries, to a diagnostics PC monitoring the system in real time. All of
this is supplied by a single AC/DC converter located in the hut.
There are currently several projects in the Danish rail industry involved with reducing
the scale of these large huts, into more compact cabinets, doing so, naturally, involves
minimizing the volume of each component. While this is not something Rosenfelt & West
is involved with directly, it is an indicator of the direction the industry is headed, and
underlines the need for high density power supplies, also in the rail industry.

occur. This has let to a series of EMC standards being implemented, namely the EN 61000-
3-2 standard supplied by the EU [2], which this converter falls under. The standard lists
the maximum permissible harmonic current for each harmonic order of the input current,
up to 2 kHz.
For higher frequencies, between 150 kHz and 1 GHz, there are standards
regarding the conducted and radiated emissions from electronic devices (CISPR).
There are currently no regulations in the 2 kHz to 150 kHz range for AC/DC converters
because currents at these frequencies are dampened suﬃciently by the impedance in the
power grid to not cause major issues for suppliers, however, there are numerous downsides
to introducing these high frequency current emissions [3, 4], and they should be avoided.
The downsides include interference with nearby equipment, audible noise, and interference
with communication via the power grid, which also takes place at these frequencies. This
means, that while not being enforced in a legal sense, high frequency current emissions
should be avoided regardless.
To comply with regulations, either passive or active power factor correction (PFC) is
implemented in most AC/DC converters.
Passive PFC usually involve an LC ﬁlter in the form of an π-ﬁlter before or after the
rectiﬁer, and while this is a simple solution to the problem, the cost and size of this ﬁlter
at higher power levels become an issue for most applications. The beneﬁts of this is also
limited, as PF from passive PFC is limited to around 0.7 - 0.75 [5], when it is used with
a conventional bridge rectiﬁer.
Active PFC is the most popular solution for applications of higher power.
It usually
involves a boost converter before the actual power supply, these can often achieve PF of
up to 0.99. The downside of this option is obviously the decreased eﬃciency and increased
complexity involved in having two power supplies in one.

On of the targets of this project, is using the isolated SEPIC without diode bridge or PFC
ﬁlter, and operate the converter during the entire line cycle, achieving a near sinusoidal
current proﬁle, and thus PF close to 1 [6].
This is done by taking two conventional
isolated SEPICs, and having one operate during the positive half-line cycle, and the other,
the negative half-line cycle. As the converter would negate the need for a rectiﬁer bridge
and smoothing capacitor, the current of this converter should be approaching a sinusoidal
shape, requiring no PFC ﬁlter.
There are numerous beneﬁts of this, as the eﬃciency is not hampered by either bridge
diodes, large ﬁlter elements, or active PFC. However, the main downside is the added
components, almost doubling the amount in a conventional isolated SEPIC, with especially
the added transformer being an expensive addition.

As for most converter topologies, it is important to evaluate if the converter should be
operating in CCM or DCM early in the process, as both usually come with their own
challenges, so the two modes are compared.
The advantages of the circuit operating in DCM, is primarily three things:
1) Input current will have same phase as the input voltage, and assuming a constant load
and duty cycle, be near sinusoidal except for current ripple, as it will function as a voltage
follower [6]. Further explanation of this will follow.
2) Since the secondary side inductor current will reach zero before the main switch is
turned on again, the secondary side diode will experience zero current switching when
turning oﬀ, minimizing switching loss.
3) Easier to control, given that monitoring of the input currents is necessary to achieve
stability in the CCM case.
The disadvantages of DCM is mostly added losses:
1) Higher current stress on components, because of the higher crest factor of the currents
associated with DCM.
2) Higher ﬂux excursions for the transformer because of these higher current peaks, re-
sulting in a need for a larger transformer.
Despite this, because the power factor is an important aspect of the converter, DCM
operation is chosen.

Transformers have many parasitic components, given their complex electric and magnetic
structure, the main ones usually depicted is the magnetizing inductance, the leakage in-
ductance for the primary side, the inter-winding capacitance as well as various parasitic
resistances.
The magnetizing inductance (Lm) is the inductance that couples to the magnetic core,
transferring energy into this when excited by a current.
Inter-winding capacitance (Cw1, Cw2) is, as the name suggests, the capacitance created
by having the conductors from the primary and secondary side close together, causing
these to act as opposing poles in a capacitor. This causes a current ripple on the primary
side of the transformer. Inter-winding capacitance can be found both from the primary to
secondary side, but also between each winding on either side.
Leakage inductance (Llp, Lls), is the inductance resulting from not exciting a magnetic
ﬂux in the core, and is undesired in most applications. The primary leakage inductance
causes a voltage ripple across the switch, as well as a current ripple on the secondary
side of the transformer, both in the d2Ts period. While the secondary leakage inductance
results in a voltage ripple across the diode during d1Ts.
In both cases the current ripple is undesirable, but manageable, only causing additional
conduction losses in the switch, transformer and diode. The more critical issue is the
voltage ripple, as maximum voltage needs to be kept below the maximum ratings for the
switch and the diode, thus both should be reduced as much as possible.
Parasitic resistance (Rp and Rs) account for conduction losses in the transformer, these
are usually so small that they do not account for a signiﬁcant amount of loss.
There are generally two types of transformers suitable for this application: wirewound and
planar, each with their respective strengths and weaknesses in terms of parasitics elements.

5.1
Input voltage sensor
One of the issues with testing and verifying a dual SEPIC with AC input, is that special
measures has to be taken in order to operate it in open loop, because of the aforementioned
need to have one switch on, while the other is in it’s switching mode. Three options were
considered:
• Placing diodes in parallel with the switching devices. This is the simplest solution, as
it would simply bypass the non-operating switch, enabling the circuit to be operated
by a single external function generator. This has numerous drawbacks, including
losses in the diode from the forward voltage, and added parasitic inductance across
the switching device, that could potentially lead to malfunction of these.
• Sampling the input voltage, and using a DSP to generate two signals. The main
drawback of this from a prototyping perspective, is that it relies on a DSP for open
loop operation.

6.3.2
Gate drivers
As GaN transistors are very sensitive regarding VGS, great care must be taken in designing
and choosing components for the gate drive. [20]
As recommended by the manufacturer of the switch, a gate driver with separate outputs
for pull up/down was selected, and values for RG,on and RG,off were selected to be within
the recommended ranges of 10-20Ω and 1-3Ω. Higher values for these increases the turn-
on/oﬀtimes, and lower values will increase the voltage ripple, the low value for RG,off is
to avoid Miller-turn-on in particular.
The gate drivers are placed as close as possible to the gate of the switch, to lower the gate
inductance as much as possible, as this will lower the voltage ripple on VGS.

7
Conclusion
The proposed replacement converter for the present AC/DC converter used by Rosentfelt
& West was analyzed in terms of the diﬀerent operation modes and the negative eﬀects
on power factor from CCM operation was outlined. Diﬀerent types of transformers were
analyzed in terms of the parasitic elements and their eﬀect on the performance of the
converter.
A voltage mode controller using a compensator, notch ﬁlter, and DSP, in combination with
an input voltage sensor was designed to control the converter. This was done by creating a
State Space Averaged model of the converter using MATLAB/Simulink and later veriﬁed
using simulations in SPICE.
A prototype was realized, that showed promising results in terms of power density and
early test results, however, many issues involving the robustness of the GaN switches were
encountered, and thus no tests were carried out for higher power levels with either of the
transformer types.
In terms of the EMC of the realized converter, satisfactory results were achieved for the
power factor and THD in the required frequency band.
The eﬃciency of the realized
converter, while being higher than the eﬃciency of the battery charger currently in use,
is not on par with modern switch mode power supplies used as AC/DC, however, a clear
path is outlined for improving this in future designs.
The main drawback of the converter topology - the large output capacitance - was identi-
ﬁed, but not immediate solution to this has been proposed, as all those that were found,
would negatively inﬂuence the power factor or the eﬃciency. This in the end means that
the topology is not suitable for applications requiring low voltage ripple unless further
research is carried out.
