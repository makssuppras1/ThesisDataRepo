Abstract
In order to solve complex problems in the field of geophysics, it is necessary to create efficient techniques
to solve strongly nonlinear, coupled systems when there is incomplete information available on the
systems. In this thesis we investigate the possibility of using Physics Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)
in order to address this challenge; both in settings analogous to inverse problems, where some information
of the true field is available, and in settings relying only on the knowledge of the initial condition, the
boundary condition, and the governing equations of the system.
In the tests we have carried out we find that in the setting of an inverse problem based on a 1D MHD
system, our PINN was not able to learn the solution to the governing equations of the system, but rather
interpolates the fields. Additionally, classic approaches to increase performance, such as increasing the
number of collocation points or changing the activation function, does not resolve the issues of training
the PINN properly. In order to force the PINN towards the true solution of the governing equations,
it is necessary to apply more advanced training techniques. Based on the literature, we choose to test
both Batched Training and Causal Training, which have been suggested to aid in the process of training
PINNs. Both techniques seem to have advantages when training the PINN, namely that the methods
are able to better balance the terms of the loss function. Based on the findings of this thesis, these
approaches appear to be necessary if PINNs are to be successfully utilized in solving complex nonlinear
systems relevant to geophysics.

Introduction
In this thesis, we investigate an application in geomagnetism of a new numerical tool, Physics Informed
Neural Networks (PINNs) [29], suitable for efficiently finding forward and inverse solutions to complex
equations. The aim is to test whether this technique is suitable for solving nonlinear coupled equations
of the form describing the dynamo process in Earth’s core.
Understanding the dynamics of the Earth’s core have been a question that scientists has been dealing
with for centuries [26, Chap. 1]. Since we cannot directly observe the core itself, we are limited to
observe the effects of the core. One of the most significant effects of the Earth’s core is the Earth’s
magnetic field. The Earth’s magnetic field can be broken down into: the Main field from the core,
the Crustal field from magnetized rocks in the crust, the External field from electric currents in the
ionosphere and magnetosphere, and the magnetic field that is induced in the crust and upper mantle
by external magnetic field time variations [17, Chap. 1]. When attempting to understand the dynamics
of the core, we are interested in the Main field. Specifically, what the generation mechanism for the
Main field is. We know that it cannot be a result of remnant magnetization, since the temperature of
the core exceeds the Curie temperature [17, Chap. 1.4]. From seismology we know that the Earth has
a liquid outer core. This led to theories of highly conducting liquid material in the inner Earth, which
can produce electric currents, resulting in a magnetic field, i.e. a Dynamo [17, Chap. 1.4]. However,
since the magnetic field is still present today, the magnetic field can not be the remnant results of a
dynamo from the early formation of the Earth. It can be shown that due to ohmic dissipation, such a
dynamo would decay within 1000 - 10.000 years [17, Chap. 1.4.4]. This has led to the hypothesis that
the Earth’s core consists of a self-excited dynamo. In brief terms, a self-excited dynamo is a dynamo
that is configured such that given a seed magnetic field, B0, if the speed of the moving conductor, v,
is higher than some critical value, vc, the dynamo is able to induce a magnetic field, B, strong enough
to keep the dynamo going. This makes the seed magnetic field, B0, redundant and only necessary to
start the process. This means that as long as v > vc the self-excited dynamo will remain running due
to the induced B field, regardless of the seed magnetic field [26, Chap. 3.1.1.]. Simple models have been
constructed that show that self-excited dynamos are possible, see e.g. [26, Chap. 3.1.1.] for a thorough
example of a simple case. However, the goal is to reconcile it with the observations of the Earth’s
magnetic field.
For instance, we could try to solve this as an inverse problem; we can observe the magnetic field, and
based on these observations attempt to infer knowledge of the origin of the field, namely the core. In
order to construct such a model, it is necessary to solve the nonlinear, coupled, equations of Magnetic
Induction, Heat, and Conservation of Momentum [26, Chap. 3]. These are given as:
1. The magnetic induction equation which describes the evolution of the magnetic field and is
described as [26, Chap. 3.2.3]:

the possibilities of a new approach in order to find a numerical solution to a nonlinear coupled system
of Partial Differential Equations (PDEs). The chosen approach is to utilize a PINN. The foundation of
PINNs and neural networks in general, are described in section 2.4 and section 2.5.
In this thesis, rather than dealing with the full set of 3D equations governing the dynamics of Earth’s
core, we choose an initial step to investigate a simpler 1D system containing similar physics. We choose
to simplify the equations, eqs. (1.0.1) to (1.0.3) by omitting the temperature dependency, which means
we only consider eq. (1.0.1) and eq. (1.0.3). Additionally, we choose to limit the system to the 1D

presented by [6] comprising a 1D system of two coupled PDEs which describe the relation between the

in practice. The results of the tests are presented in section 4, a discussion of the results and their
interpretation is presented in section 5, and finally a conclusion is presented in section 6.

In this section a brief introduction to inverse problems is presented. First, the forward problem can
formally be defined as [34, Chap. 1.3.]
d = g(m)
(2.3.1)
d are the observable parameters, m are the model parameters, and g(·) is typically called the forward
operator. However, when solving an Inverse Problem, the goal is to determine the model parameters, m,
based on some observations, d, in addition to any prior information of the physical system. In the linear
case, the forward operator can be described as g(m) = G m. By assuming independent and identically
distributed (iid) errors the least-squares solution is obtained as [34, Chap. 1.5.]:
m =
 GT G
−1 GT d

For more general applications of inverse problems, moving away from linear problems with iid errors,
this is not possible. This is a problem that often arises in real-life applications of geophysics, and it is
necessary to use probabilistic inversions in these cases, which take on the form [34, Chap. 1.5.]:
σ(m) ∝L(m)ρ(m)
(2.3.3)
ρ(m) is the prior distribution of the observed data and the mode, and L(m) is the likelihood function

σ(m), for instance by using the Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method [34, Chap. 2.3.5]. The
problem is, that this is slow as it requires many forward solves. For this reason, it is necessary to
look into new methods of solving general inverse problems. It is required that these new methods can
efficiently find models, m, that are compatible with observations, d, and can be constrained by physical
constraints, such as the governing physical equations. As discussed in section 2.5, PINNs are constructed
to satisfy these criteria, which is the motivation for applying PINNs to problems that are relevant in
geophysics in this thesis.

Neural Network Architecture
With the neuron defined, it is possible to look into the architecture of neural networks. The simplest
case of a neural network is a Feedforward Neural Network (FNN) [10, Chap. 6]. The name comes from

only passed forward through the network. This is not true for all neural networks, but only FNNs are
of relevance. The FNN consists of layers, made up of sequences of neurons, where the output from
the neurons in the first layer, are used as inputs to the neurons in the next layer. This can be visually

From the Universal Approximation Theorem it is known, that a sufficiently large FNN, with at least one
hidden layer, a nonlinear activation function in the hidden layer, and a linear output layer, is able to
approximate any continuous function on a closed and bounded subset of Rn, from one finite dimensional
space to another, with a nonzero error [10, Chap. 6.4.1]. If the FNN has n neurons in the input layer,
hidden layer, and output layer, and attempts to approximate a function of degree n−1 it is effectively an
interpolation problem. The problem then lies in learning the correct weights and biases of the neurons
in the network, in order to approximate a given function. The Universal Approximation Theorem does
not tell us whether a FNN is able to learn the approximation, only that it is able to represent it.

This section presents a closer look at the choice of activation function. There are a wide variety of
activation functions, and no well defined way of picking "the right one" [23, Chap. 3]. Instead, the task
of choosing the right activation function requires a heuristic approach. Therefore, this section is mainly
concerned with activation functions that are of interest to the application of PINNs.

The Sigmoid function and its derivative is defined as [25]:

2.4.8
Batch Normalization
As shown earlier, quite a few activation functions suffer from saturation issues, especially Tanh and
Sigmoid, but also ReLU. One way to combat this is the initialization of the weights. However, this is
not the only solution. The issue of more neurons moving towards the saturation points in deep neural
networks, is referred to as Internal Covariant Shift by [14]. [14] argues that instead of resolving the
Internal Covariant Shift by careful initialization, or low learning rates, the issue can be resolved by
incorporating normalization as a part of the neural network architecture. This process is called Batch
Normalization [14]. A batch, sometimes mini-batch, is a randomly sampled smaller subset of the the
total dataset. Training on batches is sometimes preferred to introduce additional variability to neu-
ral networks. However, letting the size of the batch equal the total dataset is equivalent to not using
batched training, which means that batch normalization can also be applied to training on full datasets.
The goal of Batch Normalization is to counteract the Internal Covariant Shift by fixing the input to
a given layer through normalization of the input [14, Sec. 2]. However, one has to be careful when
applying the normalization; the representational ability of a given layer in a neural net comes from
the non-linearity of the activation function. For instance, if a Sigmoid unit is normalized to the [-1,1]
interval, it would be restricted to the linear regime of the function, which limits the unit [14]. To combat
this, the batch normalization has to satisfy the criteria, that the transformation is able to represent the
identity transformation [14, Chap. 3]. In order to do this, two parameters are introduced which are
treated as parameters to be learned by the neural net; γ and β. The idea is that these can scale and
shift the normalized activation values, which allows a unit to maintain its representational power [14].
It has to be mentioned, that it is necessary to introduce a γ and β for each activation in a batch, in
order to maintain each activation individual representational power [14, Chap. 3]. The general idea is to
first compute the mean and variance of a given batch, and then use these to normalize the values of the
batch. Finally, they are shifted and scaled by γ and β in order to maintain representational power, by
avoiding hard restrictions of the activations to the activation functions. The algorithm describing the
application of Batch Normalization can be found in algorithm 5, where ε is a small constant introduced
for numerical stability [14, Algo. 1].

Physics Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)
PINNs were first introduced in [29], where the foundations of PINNs were presented. The goal is to
utilize the prior knowledge of a physical system when training neural networks, allowing training in
cases with sparse data availability [29].
This is achieved by modifying the loss function to enforce
physical constraints on the neural network during training [29]. This is unlike other common neural
networks, where the change of neural network architecture is the defining feature.
For instance in
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) or Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), where the introduction
of Convolutional layers and Recurrent connections in the architecture gives rise to the name. This is
not the case of PINNs; the examples presented by [29] all use a simple FNN architecture, however the
defining change is the modification of the loss function. The PINNs shown in [29] extend the classic
MSE loss function, see section 2.4.3, to also incorporate constraints from the physical information of
the system. The paper presented by [29] is not the first time the term physics-informed neural networks
has been used; [36] also uses the expression when describing their turbulence modelling. However, [29]
argues that the distinction is that [36] treat the neural network as a black-box model, whereas [29] delves
into the loss-function to move away from this approach. [29] are focused on nonlinear, parameterized,
differential equations on the form [29]:
∂tu + N [u; λ] = 0,
x ∈Ω,
t ∈[0, T]

In this formulation, t is the time domain, x the spatial domain, Ωis a subset of RD, u is the latent
solution to the PDE, and N [·, λ] is the nonlinear operator which is parameterized by λ [29]. In this
regime [29] proposes two different kinds of applications of PINNs [29, Sec. 2]:
1. Data-driven solutions of PDEs: ∂tu + N [u] = 0, x ∈Ω, t ∈[0, T]

case of the data-driven solution, the goal is to learn something about the hidden underlying solution to
the PDE, u(x, t) [29]. This case is closely related to how we usually perceive neural networks, in the
sense that all the learnable parameters of the PINN are the weights of the FNN. The incorporation of
physical constraints in the loss function is what defines it as a PINN.

2.6
Automatic Differentiation
Computing the partial derivatives of the governing equations, which are key when training PINNs, re-
quires the use of Automatic Differentiation (AD). AD has two different modes: forward and reverse
mode [2]. Forward AD is efficient at computing derivatives of functions mapping f : Rn →Rm where
m ≫n. Reverse AD is likewise most efficient when m ≪n. The first step is to understand Forward
AD, as this is the "simple" case. Reverse AD is more complicated, however it is a generalization of
backpropagation [2], which is covered in section 2.4.5. Backpropagation is also exactly the case where
m ≪n, since the goal is to compute the derivative of the loss with respect to all weights in the network.
First, it is important to convince oneself that any numerical computation is, at its core, a finite se-
ries of elementary operations [2, Sec. 3]. These operations are the algebraic operations (addition,
subtraction, division, multiplication, powers, and roots), and the transcendental functions
like exp, sin, cos etc. As an example, consider the function f(x1, x2) = ln(x1) + x1x2 −sin(x2), with

2.7
Challenges of Training PINNs
The idea of using neural networks ability to behave as a universal approximators, in order to learn the
solutions to complicated, non-linear, PDE systems is quite clever. However, it turns out that PINNs
cannot be treated purely as black-box models to solve all our problems. PINNs have been shown to face
several issues during training, e.g. [40, 38]. However, we will focus on a few of these issues, which also
relate to how we intend to test our PINN implementation.
The PINNs originally presented by [29] all use the Tanh function. As discussed in section 2.4.6 ReLU
is computationally less expensive than Tanh, in addition to less prone to suffer the vanishing gradients
problem. An exact analytic answer to whether ReLU or Tanh yields the best best results is difficult
to determine. However, [5] investigate the error bounds of neural networks when used to approximate
analytic functions. They prove that shallow and wide neural networks with Tanh perform similar, or
better, than deep, narrow neural networks with ReLU. They argue that this could be a justification for
Tanh in favor of ReLU in function approximation.
Additionally, the PINNs by [29] also train on the entire data set at once; in other words, they do not
utilize batches during training. [15] explores the effect of utilizing batched training, when solving the
Biot’s Equation. Based on their experiments, they conclude that using batches during training may
increase the performance of the PINN, at the cost of longer training times. They recommend a batch
size of 8 or higher to avoid training time becoming excessively long. At the same time, increasing the
batch size too much comes at the cost of lower model accuracy, but faster training time. At the same
time, varying the batch size also requires adjustment of the learning rate, according to [15]. This means
that applying batched training adds both the hyperparameter of the batch size, and the requirement of
additional tuning of the learning rate.
Another study on the training issues is [42]. In addition to the application of batched training, they
propose adding weights to the loss function. Specifically, for reactive-diffusion equation they recommend
adding a weight on the Initial Condition [42]. The reason being that if the PINN fails to learn the the
solution at time t = t1 (first time step), it will never be able to learn it at t = t2 (with t2 > t1). Using
the loss function from eq. (2.5.2), this would result in the loss function:
L = λICLIC + LBC + Lf
(2.7.1)
Where λIC is some positive integer, e.g. λIC = 100 in [42]. Furthermore, they recommend two different
adaptive sampling strategies of collocation points to improve the PINN. The first sampling technique is
called Adaptive Sampling in Space, and the goal is to improve the resolution of poorly resolved regions.
They start out by randomly sampling the entire spatio-temporal field and train the PINN. After training,
a forward pass is made to predict the field. The error of the field prediction is computed, and in the
regions with the highest error, additional collocation points are added [42]. This can be repeated for
several iterations, effectively increasing the resolution of the PINN in poorly resolved regions, in order
to increase overall performance [42]. An illustration is shown on fig. 11.

Methodology
The goal is to solve the MHD system presented in section 2.1 by employing a PINN which is able
to learn the latent solution to eq. (2.1.1). However, this is not straight forward; there are difficulties
when implementing PINNs as discussed in section 2.7. Therefore, it is necessary to take a structured
approach when testing the PINN in order to determine, which setup gives the best results. Additionally a
reference solution is necessary, to allow a baseline that the tests can be validated against. In this section
the first step is to introduce the Reference PINN, which is heavily inspired by the PINN presented in
[29, Sec. 3.1.1]. After the Reference PINN is defined, the planned tests will be introduced, and their
relation to the Reference PINN described. There is a heavy focus on how to implement the test and
carry it out in practice. For this reason, code snippets will be included when describing the tests. A
section covering the implementation of the most important functions of the PINN (the FNN, the PDE, and
the Loss function) is included, which gives the basis of the following implementations of the proposed
tests.
3.1
The Reference PINN
In order to determine how well a test performs, a reference implementation is necessary. When making
the reference PINN, the example of a Data Driven Solution PINN used to solve the Schrödinger problem
presented by [29, Sec. 3.1.1] is used as inspiration. The first step is to create the dataset. For this purpose
the forward model is solved, as described in section 2.2. From this dataset, the training data can be
sampled to train the PINN. When sampling the training data, it is necessary to decide how much training

When implementing the Reference PINN there are three important functions that have to be defined in

vector. The additional arguments are grad_outputs which should be a vector of ones with the same
size as the input, in order to get the right output dimensions. This is because torch.autograd.grad

a linear combination of the partial derivatives [2, Chap. 3.1]; Let the Jacobian Matrix of x and f be

as the linear combination Jxfr = ∂xf [2, Chap. 3.1]. Setting the optional argument grad_outputs
is equivalent to defining r. Finally create_graph=True is used to create a new computational graph,
which allows second order derivatives to be computed. If this is set to False a new computation graph
is not made, and it is not possible to apply automatic differentiation again. Finally, the PDE of the
MHD system on the form of eq. (3.1.2) is defined on Lines 22 and 23 and returned as the output.

3.3

The examples presented in the original paper by [29] showcase examples utilizing only the knowledge of
the Initial Condition, Boundary Condition, and the Governing Equation (Data-driven Solution case),
similar to the Reference PINN we choose to employ. However, the Data-driven Discovery case employs
a loss function that also uses knowledge of the field in some locations; a data-misfit term. We wish to
combine these two approaches, to arrive at a loss function closer to a classic inverse problem. Since the

we would typically observe the magnetic field in order to learn something about the velocity field, such

3.5
Batched Training
The effects of training on batches of data, instead of on the entire dataset at once, is a classic approach
when training neural networks. However, the PINNs presented by [29] do not utilize batches during
training. Instead they train on the entire domain all at once. As described in section 2.7, advantages to
training PINNs with batches have been shown in literature. For this reason, we wish to test the effects of
applying batched training to the PINN. In the Reference PINN setup, the PINN is trained for 200.000
iterations, which means that the optimizer takes 200.000 steps, or rather, there are 200.000 weight
updates. We choose a batch size of 202, since it is an even divisor of the training data size of 80.396
which avoids incomplete batches during training. This means that we have 80.396/202 = 398 iterations
per epoch. To hit 200.000 iterations, resulting in the same number of weight updates as the Reference
PINN, we need 200.000/398 = 502.5 epochs. We choose to round up to 503 epochs. We recognize that

There are a wide variety of ways to implement Causal Training. We choose to employ the same im-
plementation as that of [39], found in [37]. We wish to emphasize the function used to compute the
temporal weights here, and the modification of the loss function. Specifically, it is important to main-
tain temporal order at all times. This could be achieved by doing the forward pass in a loop; looping
over the number of time-steps, i, each step being the size of the temporal resolution, δt, until the final
time-step of the temporal domain is reached, T. This means that we forward pass all input (xi, ti),
for i = 0, 1δt, 2δt, ..., T. This requires N forward passes in the loop, for N = T/δt, and is expensive if
δt ≪T such as in our case. Instead it is favorable to vectorize the computation. This requires con-
catenating all input vectors covering the entire tempo-spatial domain, and constructing the two input
vectors; X = [x0, ..., xi, ..., xN]T and T = [t0, ..., ti, ..., tN]T . Both vectors can then be passed to the
FNN, which means that it only requires 1 forward pass instead of N forward passes. However, this also
outputs (u, b) in the format (u, b) =

5
Discussion
5.1
The Reference PINN
Creating a baseline is a necessity when determining how well a PINN performs. For this purpose the
Reference PINN we created is heavily inspired by the Data Driven Solution PINN for the one-dimensional
Schrödinger problem, presented by [29, Sec. 3.1.1.]. As shown in table 13 the Reference PINN performs
poorly when attempting to learn the solution to the one-dimensional MHD system from eq. (2.1.1).
The relative L2 error of the u-, and b-field are 0.979 and 0.770, respectively, and 1.011 and 0.720 for
validation.
From the entire tempo-spatial field predictions, fig. 18, a noticeable cone-shape appears in both fields.
From fig. 19 it is apparent that the initial condition is well resolved, but is then compressed towards
x = 0, as opposed to diffusing as expected. This leaves the boundaries stationary, resulting in a cone-like
structure in the tempo-spatial domain, as the initial condition is pushed towards x = 0. Interestingly, it
appears to flip around t = 0.25s, from which point the cone-shape expands again. From the loss curve
of the Reference PINN, fig. 21, it is clear that the loss of the governing equations is several orders of
magnitude smaller than the remaining loss terms, and slightly increasing during training. This could
explain why only the initial condition and the boundary conditions are satisfied; since they are several
orders of magnitude larger than the governing equations, it is favorable to minimize the loss on these
components first. It also implies that properly balancing the loss terms is crucial when training the
PINN. However, it also seems that the training is not yet fully converged, as the loss on the governing
equations are still increasing.
However, due to a rather long training time of 26 seconds per 100 iterations, corresponding to roughly
14.5 hours training for 200.000 iterations, it was chosen during this thesis to keep the maximum number
of iterations at 200.000. In future work longer runs with more iterations should be performed.
5.2
Effects of Changing the Loss Function
Modifying the loss function to include the knowledge of the b-field makes the PINN behave similar to
an inverse problem. As seen from fig. 22 the resulting field predictions of the b-field is significantly
improved. However, this improvement is unfortunately not reflected in the u-field, which we wish to
retrieve, as it still suffers from the same cone-like structure found in the Reference PINN. The same is
reflected in the relative L2 norms which is only improved for the b-field, reduced from 0.770 to 0.016
table 14.
The "Both Fields Known" case reveals a similar trend; significantly improving the results on the u-field
when the data-misfit is added to the loss, albeit the L2 norm is still high on the u-field at 0.363. The
Both Fields Known case, does not show the troublesome cone-structure in the spatio-temporal evolution,
however fig. 27 reveals issues with resolving the fields close to the boundaries. Since we are dealing with
a stiff PDE with sharp gradients, especially towards the boundaries, it makes sense that these regions
are poorly resolved. It could seem that the issues stem from the extra information given on the u-field,
which propagate into the b-field prediction, since the issues are not present in the Inverse Case.
The loss function of both approaches, fig. 25 and fig. 28, share similarities: The added data-misfit loss
term is on the same order of magnitude as the initial condition and the boundary condition, which
is significantly higher than the governing equation. This would indicate that the PINN in this case
essentially interpolates the fields, rather than actually learn the solution to the governing equation.
This again implies a problem in the terms in the loss function, or perhaps with the initialization not
being sensitive to the governing equations, and the training not being run for long enough.
The spikes towards the boundaries of the "Both Fields Known" test are not physical of nature, which
further indicates that the PINN attempts and interpolation, rather than learning the true solution to
the system. Interestingly, both loss curves suddenly increase towards the end of training, indicating
that perhaps much longer training sessions are necessary in these tests.
In early tests of the "Both Fields Known" case, with much deeper and wider FNN architectures, e.g.
10 layers of 1000 neurons per layer, the information of u does not propagate into the b-field. This could
indicate that the information being propagated from one field to the other is an issue caused by not
having enough available weights in the network to represent both fields, when attempting to interpolate
the two fields.

5.3
Conventional neural network optimization methods
Methods such as adding training data, changing or adding optimizers, and changing the activation
function are classic approaches to optimization of neural networks. However, in the case of this PINN
there is no significant improvement in these tests. Especially the test with more, or less, collocation
points, and the test with adding the L-BFGS optimizer are almost identical to the Reference PINN.
They all suffer from the cone-shaped structure in the field predictions, which are the results of the initial
condition being compressed towards x = 0, instead of the fields diffusing, as seen from fig. 29, fig. 30,
fig. 32, fig. 33, fig. 42 and fig. 43.
Their loss functions are also similar to the Reference PINN; they are dominated by the initial condition
and the boundary condition. The activation function test interestingly does not suffer from the cone-
shape, as seen in fig. 45. However, the relative errors are comparable to the other tests, as seen from
table 18. However, since the loss is still dominated by the initial and boundary condition, it would
indicate that the reason the PINN is not performing properly is not directly related to cone-shape, but
rather due to the unbalanced loss terms.
5.4
Batched and Causal Training for PINNs
The best performing PINN is achieved by applying Batched training, as seen from table 16. The test
training for 4250 iterations is also significantly better than the one training fro 503; this is encouraging,
as it confirms that the PINN is not converged in tests that were possible to perform given the time
available in this thesis.
As seen from fig. 38 and fig. 39 the Batched training does not suffer from the cone-shape. Instead, from
fig. 39 it appears that towards time-step t = 0.040s and t = 0.080s the PINN starts having trouble with
sharp edges in both the u- and b-field predictions. This error seems to be in the same category as seen
in the Both Known Fields tests; the PINN has issues resolving the sharp structures and gradients of the
fields. It might be worth pursuing network architectures with more hidden units, in order to increase
the representational power of the PINN, in the future.
The loss function reveals that the loss terms are closer to being balanced in the batched approach.
Especially the loss term on fb, as seen from fig. 41, which is 3-5 orders of magnitude greater than for the
Reference PINN. This would agree with the fact that balancing the loss terms is important to achieve
proper training.
Based on the observation of the apparent importance of balancing the loss terms, it is also important
to highlight the Causal Training approach. From table 19 it is apparent that the test does not perform
well, but actually worse than the Reference PINN. However, the loss function fig. 51, reveals that this
approach is able to balance the loss term by itself.
The reason that the method behaves so poorly, despite being properly balanced, seems to be because the
method is very far from converged, and because the initial condition has not been sufficiently heavily
weighted, which allowed training to progress from the initial step too fast. As discussed by [39], in
order for the method to converge, all causal weights should have converged to 1. From fig. 52 it is clear
that this is not the case. Additionally, the temporal loss also increases as training progresses, which
is a reflection of the balancing of the loss terms. However, since the initial condition is not weighted
sufficiently high, the PINN seems to be stuck trying to balance the poorly resolved initial condition
and the following time-steps, jumping back and forth time-steps, as shown by the temporal weights.
From fig. 49 it is apparent that the method has not yet properly learned the initial condition. This is
in agreement with the the fact that the method is stuck, and never reaches the later time-steps during
training. Interestingly it, seems like the time-step at t = 0.080s performs better than the initial condition
from fig. 49. This could be because the causality parameter is not properly tuned, allowing later time
steps to be learned too early.
The prediction at t = 0.2s also shows a very clear spike around x = −0.15, which is not physical. This
is the same issue as seen in earlier tests, which is suspected to arise from the difficulties of resolving
the stiff system, in particular the sharp gradients of the u-field. This could also indicate that FNN
architectures with more hidden units are necessary. This could also be combined with more advanced
sampling techniques in order to better resolve the sharp gradients in the domain.
It is impossible to determine whether these would be resolved with longer training, however; the examples
by [39] involve changing the causality parameters up to 5 times during training, with significantly longer

5.5
Future Prospects of PINNs
Applications of PINNs in highly non-linear systems with stiff PDEs such as those describing the magne-
tohydrodynamics of Earth’s core seems promising, but finding a correct implementation is difficult based
on the tests carried out in this thesis. The approach presented by [29] does not seem to be powerful
enough to deal with the stiffness of eq. (2.1.1), and conventional optimization methods are not sufficient
to resolve these issues.
However, more complex training methods, such as Batched Training and Causal Training seems to be
a step in the right direction. The Causal Training method is more in line with conventional numeric
solutions, e.g. the BPR-353 method, which we use to solve the Forward Model. In the BPR-353 method,
we are forced to compute the solution step by step, obeying the underlying physics of the system. Intu-
itively it makes sense, that a similar approach to learning the right solution in the PINN is an advantage.
However, these come at the cost of significantly increased computational complexity, which means that
more processing power is desirable. Increasing the weight on the initial condition, and training for
sufficiently longer times, for instance with batched training, could help the training. It could also be
interesting to apply other sampling schemes, such as the Adaptive Sampling in Space sampling scheme
discussed in section 2.7.
We have observed issues of spikes, that are not physical in nature, in the predictions of the PINN. These
could arise due to regions of sharp gradients being poorly resolved. Adding collocations points in these
regions, as proposed in the Adaptive Sampling in Space scheme, in addition to increasing the number
of hidden units in the FNN architecture, could help resolving this issue.
If through these suggestions it is possible to train a PINN to obey the governing equations to a suffi-
ciently small error, a significant advantage comes from the fast forward pass of the neural network. One
could also imagine a scenario whereby the PINN could be constructed in such a way, that it can take
an initial condition as input, and we could thus time-step the system quickly. This would most likely
require modifying the way the training of the PINN, e.g. to train on many different initial conditions,
instead of just one, like we have done in this thesis. Running such PINNs in ’Inverse’ mode for different
initial conditions could perhaps also be used as part of a sampling scheme, in order to probabilistically
solve the inverse problem.

Conclusion

6
Conclusion
In this thesis the possibilities of using PINNs to solve a one-dimensional nonlinear MHD system has
been investigated. The chosen MHD system is the system proposed by [6], which is a coupled system of
PDEs consisting of prototype 1D versions of the Navier-Stokes and Magnetic Induction Equations. In
order to solve the system and generate a training and validation dataset, the BPR-353 time-stepping
scheme, in combination with Chebyshev Differentiation Matrices to compute the spatial derivatives, was
applied to discretize the MHD system.
A Reference PINN was then constructed, as a baseline for all other tests on the PINN, based on the
PINN presented in [29] for their solution to a one-dimensional Schrödinger system. The Reference PINN
achieves a relative L2 error of 0.979 and 0.770 on the u-, and b-field respectively, and 1.011 and 0.720
on the validation time-steps.
We performed a series of tests related to classic optimization of neural networks; changing the loss
function, increasing training data (analogous to collocation points in PINNs), and testing different opti-
mizers as discussed by [29]. Unfortunately none of these tests were successful in significantly increasing
the performance of the PINN, indicating that the problem cannot be resolved by classic neural network
setup approaches, but instead the problems are fundamental issues when training the PINN.
We investigate the effects of modifying the loss function, e.g. adding knowledge of the b-field through
a data-misfit loss term which makes training the PINN analogous to solving an inverse problem. This
significantly improves relative L2 error, bringing the error on the b-field down from 0.770 to 0.016.
However, by investigating the components of the loss function, it is suspected that the PINN does not in
this case learn the solution to the governing equations, but rather performs interpolation by minimizing
the loss on the data-misfit term.
This is further reflected by the error on the unknown u-field not
decreasing. This implies that properly balancing the terms of the loss function is crucial when training
PINNs.
More advanced training techniques have been shown to improve performance of PINNs, as discussed in
section 2.7. Namely tests involving Batched Training, and Causal Training. Batched Training achievs
the best performance with a relative L2 error of 0.787 and 0.094 for the u-, and b-field, and for the
validation time-steps an error of 0.825 and 0.098. Since no data-misfit term is present in the Batched
training approach, this is interpreted as the PINN converging towards the right solution, but requiring
more training time. Causal Training achieves relative errors of 1.059 and 0.861 for u and b, which is
worse than the Reference PINN. However, the Causal Training approach is able to properly balance the
loss terms on its own, which is very promising. The poor performance is suspected to be because the
PINN is far from converged, as seen from the temporal weights, and requires much longer training time,
which is not feasible given the time-frame of the project. It may also require higher weight on the initial
condition.
In the future, further exploring the possibilities of both Causal Training and Batched Training is ad-
vised, while possibly also increasing the number of hidden units in each layer, such that sharp gradients
can better be represented. Other kinds of adaptive sampling techniques for the collocation points could
also be a possibility. It is suspected that the PINN has troubles resolving the sharp gradients of the u-
field, which an adaptive sampling technique like the Adaptive Sampling in Space scheme might resolve.
Despite the disappointing results obtained in this thesis, it seems possible that PINNs might still prove
useful in geomagnetism, provided sufficient time and computational resources are available for training
the neural networks.
