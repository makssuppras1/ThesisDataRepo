Introduction  
Being a widely used mean of transport within a highly competitive system of transportation 
modes, railways (and thus the railway customer services) are called to be optimized. Key 
factors, such as the provision of reliable services and so the empowerment of the robustness 
levels shall be reached. A possible way to achieve the latter is through a deeper analysis of the 
operations of these services. Therefore, a statistical description of the daily collected data, 
alongside with the stochastic results that can be produced through a simulation model may 
lead to such an achievement.  
An in-parallel statistical analysis and microanalytic simulation of the Danish “Kystbanen” – the 
coastline connecting Copenhagen and Helsingør- was held within this study. The relevant data 
needed for the analysis were collected at the initial phase, provided by the Danish railways 
infrastructure manager (BaneDanmark), the railway operator (DSB) as well as from the 
supervisors’ databases. The timetable data (and thus the period to which this study refers to) 
relate to the autumn period of 2014 (01/09/2014 – 30/11/2014). All data were interpreted 
and filtered before utilized, and two software were mainly used: SAS Enterprise Guide and 
OpenTrack for the statistical analysis and the network simulation respectively.  
It has to be stated that the stations København H and Nørreport were excluded both from the 
statistical analysis and the simulation modelling. This was done in order to avoid dealing with 
the additional “noise” that trains not related to the Kystbanen, and that terminate at 
Østerport, create.  
The objectives of this study can be synopsized into three categories. Firstly, the successful 
creation of a simulation model that describes in a microscopic level the coastline (Copenhagen 
- Helsingør), allowing the user to manipulate the scenario analyzed based on timetable factors. 
Secondly, the statistical analysis of the available timetable data of the line, obtaining 
descriptive tables and graphs referring to the arrival, departure and dwell times of the line, as 
well as an attempt to fit certain distributions to the data analyzed. Lastly, the application of 
Key Performance Indicators related to the timetable of the line, aiming to an evaluation of 
timetable factors, such as the homogeneity and the travel time prolongation. Thus, 
conclusions regarding the quality of the current operations were made, as well as proposals 
for further work and development of the created model.  
Analyzing the objectives in depth, the simulation model was created in OpenTrack simulation 
software, including information regarding the infrastructure within a high level of accuracy, 
information regarding the used rolling stock as well as the timetable data that were provided 
for the whole 3-month period of study. Lacking a clear separation of the delay values into 
primary and secondary delays, the model scenarios that were created were restricted into 
ideal cases where incidences are absence. Nevertheless, a comparison between the ideal and 
the actual scenario can be of high interest, especially concluded by the application of KPIs 
using both cases. 
The statistical analysis, based on the available timetable data for the autumn period of 2014 
focuses on the arrival (with an additional separation into late arrivals) and departure delays, 
as well as on the dwell time at all the thirteen stations under analysis. Thereinafter, the data 
analysis includes a distribution fitting to the delays and dwell times, followed by key-statistic 
measures such as the mean and the standard deviations values. Moreover, a general 
description of the study line is achieved by the extraction of multiple diagrams and basic 
statistical information (e.g. percentages of platform use in case that more than 1 platforms

are being used in a stations per direction). The disaggregated results will be finally interpreted, 
identifying the most sensitive parts of the line. 
Finally, identified relative Key Performance Indicators were applied. The latter refer to the 
evaluation of the timetable used in the line, evaluating the performance of the three train 
types running on the line. Thus, the homogeneity level of the northbound direction of 
Kystbanen will be assessed based on the SSHR and SAHR Key Performance Indicators (based 
on the technical, scheduled timetable and compared to the ideal one, as calculated in the 
simulation software). Additionally, a calculation of travel time prolongation of the three 
running train types took place, comparing to other national values and leaded to conclusions. 
Finally, the study includes a sensitivity analysis of the arrival delays based on the train 
performance factor, at all stations, per train type, based on the modeled ideal scenario. 
Consequentially, recommendations regarding the train running time supplements are drawn.

Abstract  
The Danish “coast line” (Kystbanen) is a regional railway line that connects the Danish capital, 
Copenhagen, to a northern city of Sjælland, Helsingør. It consists of 46.2 kilometers of 
electrified double track, a total of 15 stations, and serves around 10 million passengers 
annually. Modern train detection systems and computing facilities have enabled a high-
precision measurement of the daily operations, providing with numerous analysis 
opportunities. Additionally, railway-specific simulation software can be used in order to 
output results regarding the same line, in an optimal scenario, enabling comparisons and 
useful conclusions.  
The simulation modelling of Kystbanen was done in three stages: The infrastructure, the 
rolling stock and the timetable modelling phase. All data were collected in a high level of 
accuracy and the final product consists of a high level of detail model including the 
infrastructure and rolling stock data of the whole line, as well as the itineraries for the 
northbound direction of the morning rush period of the day. Nevertheless, delay incidences 
were not included in the model, resulting into optimal scenarios formulations only.  
Using the operation related data that were provided for the means of this study, after the 
needed filtering and preparation, an analysis of the arrival, departure and dwell times was 
held, with a final emphasis on the northbound direction. The results include a disaggregation 
of the initial information, based on the train type, the station, both the train type and the 
station at the same time, as well as the analysis of the latter level of detail when referring only 
to the rush periods of the day. The results for the different delay types indicate the need of 
the separation, as major differences where observed when disaggregating the data. On the 
other hand, when analyzing the dwell times, a high level of disaggregation is not needed, as 
the results per train type or per time period do not vary among them.  
The hypothesis that the arrival, late arrival and departure delays would fit an exponential 
distribution, as well as dwell times a normal distribution was rejected. This was done after 
testing a fitting for the whole time period of a day as well as the isolated rush periods of the 
day alone. Nevertheless, a further analysis based on the type of the day is suggested before 
making a final conclusion.  
The analysis based on timetable-related Key Performance Indicators specifies that the 
scheduled timetable is scheduled as a balance between optimality and heterogeneity level in 
order to achieve a desired level of robustness. Additionally, all three train types’ travel times 
appear to be highly prolonged, a fact that can be explained based on the high density of the 
stopping pattern.  
Finally, the sensitivity analysis, held on the optimal scenario and referring to the arrival delays 
(based on the train performance factor) output some interesting conclusions regarding the 
running time supplements. Based on the results, the arrival delays are connected to the length 
beforehand the station as well as the performance factor (the higher the length, the more 
sensitive the arrival delay values when dropping the performance factor). Thus, it is suggested 
that it could be considered to include the realized train performance factors when assigning 
running time supplements to a timetable.

Chapter 1: Theoretical Background 
 
1.1 
Terminology 
 
Throughout the study, railway-specific terminology is presented. Due to lack of normalization, 
it was deemed necessary to present the explanation of the terminology used at this initial 
face, in order to ensure the conveyance of clear messages to the reader.  
A major part of this study is devoted to a microanalytic simulation of a line. As microanalytic 
simulation (more commonly referred as “microsimulation”) is defined the category of 
computerized analytical tools, performing analyses of various activities with a high level of 
detail. In the case of this study, these activities refer to the railway traffic flowing through the 
study line (kystbanen). The objective of microsimulation is often the evaluation of the effects 
that different scenarios would have, before they are implemented into the real world. 
During the statistical analysis of the data, a separation between the arrival and the “late 
arrival” delays took place. As late arrival delays is defined the time difference between an 
observed delayed arrival and the scheduled arrival time (Goverde, 2001).Thus, all the early 
arrivals are excluded, leaving only the positive valued delays. Additionally, a major general 
separation on any kind of delay could be made between primary and secondary delays. As 
primary delays are defined those delays that are caused by disturbances to the railway traffic 
and the source of which is not another delayed train. Timetable supplement times are used 
to reduce or absorb this kind of disturbances. (Vromans, Reliability of Railway Systems, 2005). 
As secondary delays are defined those delays that are caused by earlier delays. They are also 
known as “knock-on delays”. The dependencies between the train services, alongside with the 
crowdness of the lines may result to the propagation of a primary delay to a second (or more) 
trains, resulting into the secondary delays. (Vromans, Reliability of Railway Systems, 2005) 
While interpreting different timetable scenarios, based on Key Performance Indicators, the 
terms “robustness”, “capacity” and “heterogeneity” were used. As defined in (Goverde, 
2013), timetable robustness is the ability of the timetable to remain feasible in cases where 
trains deviate from their scheduled paths, up to a certain level. The deviations can refer to 
design errors, parameter variations and changing operational conditions. When referring to a 
rail context, capacity can be defined as: “A measure of the ability to move a specific amount 
of traffic over a defined rail line with a given set of resources under a specific plan” (Krueger, 
1999). Finally, as heterogeneous is defined the railway traffic, that is characterized by large 
running time differences on the same track sections (and thus homogenous is defined the 
traffic with the same train running times on the same track sections) (Vromans, 2004).  
 
1.2 
Key-Performance Indicators (KPIs) used for the evaluation 
 
In order to obtain an idea of the status and the quality of a given railway network and its 
respective timetable, or even to compare to different scenarios of a given network, KPIs can 
be proven a useful solution. The parallel illustration of the results on a map, enables a 
macroscopic (or microscopic, depending on the level of the analysis) perspective, making 
==PAGE:13==
Page | 13  
 
easier the identification of possible weak spots, or generally spots of interest within the 
network. (Lindfeldt, 2015) 
Separating the network into parts (usually based on key-geometric points like the stations), 
four different kind of KPIs might be applied. These KPIs are referring to: 
 
Infrastructure 
 
Timetable 
 
Traffic 
 
Delays 
Considering the needs and the time limits of this study, KPIs referring to the timetable were 
only selected. Thus, a comparison between the theoretical (simulation) and the actual 
(existing data) will be accomplished.  The chosen KPIs are listed below. 
The Sum of Shortest Headway Reciprocals (SSHR) and the Sum of Arrival Headway Reciprocals 
(SAHR). These two heuristic measures are introduced by Vromans (2004) and used to evaluate 
the homogeneity in a timetable. SSHR applies to track sections, representing the distribution 
of the trains in an hour on a track, while SAHR applies to stations, taking into consideration 
only the arrival headways between every pair of subsequent train. (Vromans, 2004)

1.3 
Introduction to Software 
 
1.3.1 SAS Enterprise Guide 
 
SAS Enterprise Guide is a component of SAS (Statistical Analysis System) software. Among 
others uses, it is used for advanced and predictive analytics, as well as data management. It is 
Java based, and the interface consists of a very user-friendly data-flow layout. The software 
provides the user with multiple supported options, minimizing the need of self-programming 
the demands needed to complete the desired analyses.  
Multiple process flows can exist for the same root-data sheet, while reports and outputs can 
be printed out throughout the procedures. The graphical representation helps the user 
understand any relationship between tasks and outputs, as shown in the example in Figure 1 
 
Figure 1: Example of SAS Enterprise Environment (Source: (Getting started with: SAS Enterprise, 2017) 
Among other features, the software allows the user to filter, sort or manipulate an existing 
dataset using its default demands, or, if deemed necessary, to “interfere” with a self-
programmed part. Most importantly, a big variety of graphs is provided to describe the 
resulting tables, as well as the opportunity to check possible distribution fittings, both visually 
(producing the respective graphs) and numerically (producing different normality and 
goodness-of-fit tests). 
 
1.3.2 Opentrack 
 
OpenTrack is a widely used railway planning software that provides to the user the functions 
of modelling, simulation and analysis of various railway systems. Within the large variety of 
==PAGE:15==
Page | 15  
 
tasks it provides, it includes the determination of the requirements for the network’s 
infrastructure, running time calculations, timetable construction, designing of signaling 
systems, analysis of delays and extraction of different type of results into graphs.  
The input data needed in order to create a model can be separated into three categories:  
 
Network Data 
 
Rolling Stock Data 
 
Timetable Data  
The Network Data (the information of which is carried by each of the elements of the network 
designed graphically), include information regarding different attributes: length, gradient, 
maximum speed etcetera. Additionally, data regarding signals, switches, stations and routes 
are included in this category.  
The Rolling Stock Data are organized into locomotive groups, called depots. The information 
needed for the different locomotives refer to technical characteristics (i.e. tractive 
effort/speed diagrams, length, loads, adhesion factor and power system). Using a locomotive 
from the existing depot-database, the user is able to simulate it together with the needed 
number of cars (carriages or wagon).  
The Timetable Data store information regarding each train individually at each station (for 
both arrival and departure times), connection to other trains as well as minimal stop times.  
After finishing the determination of the input data explained above, the Simulation takes 
place. As the network data are designed graphically at the 1st step, the animation base is 
defined, allowing the user to observe the simulation while running. Being executed under the 
constrains of the signaling system and the existing timetable, the simulation finishes by 
printing the resulting data in the form of various diagrams, graphs and statistics. Some 
examples of useful results for interpretation that OpenTrack offers are: Speed/Distance 
diagrams, train graphs, distance/time diagrams, occupation statistics and delay statistics.

1.4.2 Normality Tests 
 
Throughout the statistical analysis of this study, a distribution fitting was attempted to specific 
sets of data. Apart from the graphical methods, that can be proven useful when it comes to 
checking the fitting of the data to a certain distribution, a formal conclusion regarding the 
fitting should be given based on statistical tests. Considering the used software, SAS Enterprise 
Guide, three tests could be used in order to interpret the goodness-of-fit of the analyzed data 
sets. These three tests are the:  
 Kolmogorov – Smirnov (KS test) 
 Cramer-von Mises (CVM test) 
 Anderson-Darling (AD test) 
All three tests were used in order to answer the question: “are the data exponentially 
distributed?” or “are the data normally distributed”? The abovementioned is being done by 
checking on the “p-value”, with an assumed significance level (in this case taken equal to 0.05). 
The null hypothesis (the data follow the assumed distribution) shall then be accepted, in case 
that the calculated p-value is observed to be higher than 0.05 (Aggelidis, 2017).

2.3 
Modeling with OpenTrack 
 
After gathering all the needed data and information, the formation of the line in OpenTrack 
took place. The procedure was in accordance with the software manual, and it includes the 
logical order of data input: infrastructure  rolling stock  timetable data.  
The basics of how the data were input in the software, the numbering that was followed as 
well as exceptions and assumptions that were made throughout the modelling process are 
listed in the following paragraphs.  
 
2.3.1 Creation of the infrastructure  
The first stage of the modeling process was the establishment of a highly accurate 
infrastructure basis. All the gathered data were input as descriptive information of lines or 
points (edges or vertices as they are called in OpenTrack). Thus, any milestone within the line 
was represented with a vertex (that primarily carries the information of the chainage where a 
point of interest is located) and the edges that are connecting those points of interest would 
carry all the descriptive information of that part.  
In Figure 15 an example of a random vertex and a random edge is displayed. The Vertex refers 
to a Turnout before Snekkersten St. and it includes its kilometer point and its given name in 
order to identify it easily. The edge (that always connects two vertices) connects the latter 
turnout with the station vertex of Snekkersten St. of platform 1. The information that it carries 
include its length, the gradient and the maximum allowed, in this part, passenger train speed 
for both directions. While the length is being calculated automatically (since it is the difference 
of the kilometer point of the two connected vertices), the gradient and the speed limits are 
assigned manually.

Page | 26

In order to create the main body of the line, the TIB-S schematic layouts were used. Thus, the 
stations were added (for each station the number of vertices equals the number of platforms) 
alongside with the Switches&Crossings. The exact kilometer point of the S&C was based on 
the data acquired from the infrastructure manager, while the stationing is known from the 
TIB-S data. Additional vertices were added in order to include all signals and stopping boards. 
Finally, vertices that realize the end of a speed limit or the end of a gradient were added. 
While “translating” the line’s schematic layout into the simulation model, some parts of the 
infrastructure were excluded. A good example of such an exclusion is given in Figure 16. While 
tracks 1 and 2 are included in the model, the rest of the station area is not (rectangle shadows 
with pink color).  In general, shunting areas or other irrelevant to the Kystbanen line parts of 
stations were not included in the simulation model produced, in order to save time and avoid 
additional complexity.

The last step of modeling consists of the insertion of the timetable data. The latter consist of 
two main components: The itinerary and the time information, meaning the paths that each 
train type has to follow on the created infrastructure, as well as the time that it (according to 
schedule) has to cross the station components included in the created course.  
In order to create the itinerary for each train type, a specific hierarchy structure has to be 
followed: 
1. Creation of the routes. The routes are the smallest possible parts of a course, and they 
start and end at a signal vertex. 
2. Creation of paths. The paths are a string of consecutive routes. They can be used, for 
instance, in order to group into one big part. These parts are one-way solution for an 
operating train (i.e. parts of the infrastructure without turnouts) 
3. Creation of itineraries. As a result from the created paths, a sequence of the latter will 
form an itinerary. Thus, based on the stopping pattern of each train, the respective to 
each train type itinerary was created. 
The usefulness of the different grouping of routes applies also to this study, as one of the train 
types (ØK train type) uses as terminal station, the Nivå station.  
The last step of modelling was the insertion of the timetable data. After assigning an itinerary 
to the inserted trains, the stopping pattern as well as the scheduled arrival and dwell times 
for each itinerary is filled. The scheduled arrival times are known (Appendix 02 – Kystbanen 
timetable), while the dwell time for all the 13 stations included in the study is equal to 30 
seconds (based on the statistical analysis that is analyzed in the next chapter). As a result, the 
departure times for all stations were easily calculated. The result was 3 base-itineraries with 
the respectful timetable assigned (1 for each train type, for northbound direction only).

2.2.4 Simulation parameters & results 
 
After completing the modeling of the line (an illustration of which is shown in Figure 19), the 
user is able to define some key-parameters before running the desired simulation period. One 
of these parameters, that was the one chosen to interpret on further on this study, is the 
“performance factor”. The performance factor is actually controlling the acceleration, braking 
and constant speed efficiency, meaning the percentage of which these three are applying, 
relevant always to the maximum possible.  
Taking into consideration that the model created does not include incidences (i.e. delaying 
incidences), it was deemed as a reasonable decision to run the simulation with a 100% 
performance factor, taking results that correspond to the theoretical optimal running times, 
within a given timetable. This creates early arrival delays (i.e. negative values of arrival delays) 
as well as long dwell times, in order for the trains to catch up to the scheduled departure 
times. 
These long dwell times are visible at one of the simulation results, the train diagram 
(distance/time diagram) displayed as Figure 20. While the schedule dwell time for all stations 
is equal to 30 seconds, in this “ideal scenario” the duration is quite larger, in order for the 
trains to respect the departure times at each station.  
Taking a look at Figure 21, Figure 22 and Figure 23, the meaning of the 100% performance 
factor can be realized easier. The trains are reaching the maximum allowed speed in all cases, 
while they are breaking at the exact right moment in order to stop at the stopping board of 
each station. Additionally, the acceleration is maximized, in order to reach the maximum 
allowed speed as soon as possible. This result can be considered not realistic, when inserting 
in the system the human factor (real life). Still, this ideal outcome can be characterized as 
important study outcome, as it is shown in the 4th chapter of this study.  
Finally, another interesting simulation result is the comparison between the scheduled and 
the actual (simulation results) timetable. Each of the train type was analyzed separately, and 
the results are being presented in Table 8, Table 9 and Table 10, including the time stamps for 
arriving and leaving the stations, the comparison (time difference), as well as the actual 
average speed between each section. Thereafter, a first impression regarding the most 
“tight”, in time supplements, sections of the line can be obtained.

2.3 
Conclusions 
 
Concluding, simulating a railway line can be proven a time consuming process that demands 
a high level of caution and reliable data sources. On the other hand, the successful creation of 
a simulation model can provide the user with multiple tools, referring for example to the 
timetable operations.  
In the “Kystbanen” case, the simulation model created during this study includes a high level 
of detail and accuracy in the input infrastructure data, while the northbound direction for all 
three train types for the morning rush period of the day was simulated. Due to lack of the raw 
delay data separation into primary and secondary delays, the delivered model does not 
contain an incident occurrence distribution. Nevertheless, interesting output can be obtained 
by this “ideal scenario” (i.e. not containing delay-generating events). 
As expected, simulating the line with trains that are able to run at their 100% capability, 
created negative arrival delays (early arrivals) at all of the stations of the line. Since the 
inserted time schedule includes fixed departure times, the trains expand the scheduled 30-
seconds dwell times until they can depart on time for each station. Trying to gain this “lost” 
time (the extra dwell time), another scenario was created, where the trains do not have to 
respect a departure schedule, rather than only their stopping pattern  
The final results indicate that, if the three different train types were able to run in such a way 
that they could utilize their characteristics at a maximum level, ØP and ØK train types could 
gain a 13.4% reduction in total running time, while the ØD type a 12.7% reduction. The latter 
results could be proven as an interesting starting point for a further investigation of assigned 
running time supplements to the line. Nevertheless, a more detailed analysis was attempted 
in this study, and it is presented further on, in Chapter 4.

3.1 
Raw data description 
 
Throughout a daily operation, a big amount of data is being collected at the under analysis 
line. These can contain numerous different information, including information regarding the 
arrival and departure times, the train ID, the used platform and so on. Based on these 
collected raw data, statistical analyses are becoming possible through various statistical 
software. For the needs of this study, the software “SAS Enterprise Guide” was used, while 
different sets of raw data were provided from the railway operator.  
The number of the data sets provided were three, and for the needs of this study they were 
separated into “preliminary” and “main, with two and one data sets respectively. Included in 
the preliminary data sets were the information regarding the train sets compositions, from 
which a matching of train type and ID was able to be output, as well as a second data set 
including the platform usage, based on the train ID.  
Most importantly, the main data set included a big set of information, for the period of June-
December of 2014, including among others: 
 
Time and Train IDs 
 
Planned and actual dates and times  
 
Station information  
 
Record type (Entrance or Exit of a station) 
 
The realized time between each point where there is a measurement  
 
Delay values (before and after the correction needed)  
It has to be mentioned that the correction of the delay values was held beforehand. Thus, the 
differences between the correction factors among the different station was not necessary to 
be considered in this study, as the data set was provided directly with the corrected values.  
 
3.2

excluding from the analyzed data sets any stations apart from those that belong to the study

whether this number was even or odd, separating the northbound and southbound direction

year needed, alongside with easily exploitable data regarding the time and the direction.

3.5 
Distribution fitting & Normality tests 
 
The last part of the statistical analysis of the available train running times data of the 
“Kystbanen” consists of an attempt to fit the data to known distributions. Based on the 
existing literature, and more specifically a publication carried out within “The Netherlands 
TRAIL Research School for Transport, Infrastructure and Logistics”, an attempt to apply the 
distribution fitting held throughout the latter publication in this study took place. Note that 
the distribution fitting analysis was limited to the northbound direction only. 
Some of the results of the abovementioned study (Goverde, Statistical Analysis of Train Traffic: 
The Eindhoven Case , 2001), are summarized below, and will be analyzed as categories, 
applying them in the Kystbanen case: 
 
No general distribution fits all arrival delays 
 
Late arrival delays follow an exponential distribution 
 
Departure delays fit well to a single exponential distribution  
 
Dwell time fits well to a normal distribution 
At the first three categories, an additional disaggregation of the day in order to isolate the two 
rush periods and analyze them separately was held. Nevertheless, in none of the categories a 
separation based on the different day type was held, even though it is possible that the day 
type may affect the result, and thus, in an optimally detailed analysis, an alongside separation 
based on the day type (weekday, Friday, Saturday) shall be held.  
The analysis is presented separately for each of the categories, and it consists of two stages: 
The graphical evaluation based on the histogram plots, and the final evaluation based on the 
relevant goodness-of-fit tests.  
 
3.5.1 Arrival Delays 
 
Starting with the arrival delays, the expected based on the literature outcome was that no 
general distribution fits all arrival delays. In this case, the lognormal and the exponential 
distributions were checked for fitting. Initially, as shown in Figure 43, a graphical evaluation 
was held, accepting the hypothesis based on literature. The tests held double-checks this 
approval, as shown in Table 16: Distribution Parameters and goodness-of-fit results (referring 
to arrival delays, NB direction), where all the “p values” are extremely low, both for the 
lognormal and the exponential distribution fitting. 
An additional disaggregation took place, testing the fitting for each distribution, for each 
station of the line. The results are included in “Appendix 16 – Attempt to fit an exponential 
and a lognormal distribution to the arrival delays – Northbound direction, per station. 
Relevant goodness-of-fit tests included”, and the result remain the same: No general 
distribution fits all arrival delays.

3.6 
Conclusions 
 
A statistical analysis took place in this part of the study, focusing on the northbound direction 
of the Coastline (Kystbanen). Based on the results, that include the analysis of the delays and 
dwell times in different levels of disaggregation (based on the time period, the train type, the 
direction and the station) it can be securely stated that the separation of the delays into arrival 
and departure delays is crucial and needed, as they were found to have major differences. 
Additionally, many interesting results were found regarding their values and the patterns they 
follow through the different levels of disaggregation. 
Starting with the arrival delays, the differences between the northbound and southbound 
directions appear to be pretty high with northbound direction having double, or even higher 
average values (1.472 and 1.973 minutes for arrivals and departures respectively, versus 0.766 
and 0.681 for the southbound direction).  Nevertheless, the very high standard deviation 
values point at a numerous events high much higher values that the average.  
Separating based on the part of the day, isolating the rush periods, the evening rush appears 
to have the most incidences with arrival as well as departure delays higher than 2 minutes, 
higher than the daily average percentage. On the other hand, the morning rush appears to 
have a percentage lower than the one observed on average. 
After separating the events also based on the train type, the result suggest that the separation 
should be made, as the results vary significantly, indicating a clear hierarchy in delay among 
the trains. In both arrival and departure delays, the ØP train type appears to have the highest 
average delay values, followed by ØD type and finally the ØK train type.  
Looking at the delays separated be station, high differences are observed between the station 
with the highest average departure delay and the one with the lowest, which are respectively 
Nivå and Østerport station with values 2.274 and 1.135 minutes. Nevertheless, very high 
standard deviation values are observed here as well. When testing a separation both by train 
type and station at the same time, a major new outcome is not drawn, apart from the very 
high arrival delay at Helsingør for the ØD type, that is topping the list of all stations and train 
types. 
A final additional separation was done based on the time period of the day, on the top of the 
lastly mentioned level of disaggregation. It appears to be that it is of high interest to analyze 
the late arrival delays separately, as the hierarchy changes significantly. Østeport station 
appears as the highest in average arrival delays for the ØP train types (from last when not 
separating the late arrivals), and at the same time the rest of the stations tend to balance the 
average values among them.  
When analyzing the dwell times, Østerport were found to have the highest average value 
(1.133 minutes). Three more stations have relatively high values (Espergærde, Kokkedal and 
Hellerup). The rest of the stations could be grouped in a same group of lower values, around 
0.8 minutes. Even though all the relevant separations were applied in this case as well (by 
station, train type, together), the behavior of the dwell time appears to remain stable and 
nothing major can be extracted out of such separations.  
As for the final part of the statistical analysis, an attempt to fit specific known distributions to 
the data took place. The results verify that no general distribution fits all arrival delays of the 
==PAGE:71==
Page | 71  
 
northbound direction. At the same time, an exponential distribution is not able to describe 
the arrival delays, neither generally nor when referring to the rush periods of the day only. 
The rejection applies to departure delays of the line as well. At the same time, the attempt to 
fit a normal distribution to the dwell times of the stations, separately, appears to reject the 
hypothesis that it would.  
Nevertheless, it has to be mentioned that an additional attempt to fit the abovementioned 
distributions to the data should be made, when separating the data based on the type of the 
day type. Additionally, it is suggested that a future development of this study could filter out 
the possible outliers of the data, as the statistical analysis that was held points at the possible 
existence of such values when looking at the 99th percentiles and the scatter plots. Finally, it 
could be interesting to investigate the behavior of the different train types separately as well.

4.2 
Simulation Model calibration 
 
The final simulation product of this study was a model that describes in very good detail the 
infrastructure of the coastline, being able to simulate the three different train types of the 
line, during the morning peak hour of the day. Nevertheless, the simulation results that were 
exported until now were referring to the ideal scenario where the train performance of all 
courses of the line was equal to the maximum one (100%). In other words, based on the trains’ 
characteristics, the acceleration, braking and constant speed parts are, in every case, the best 
possible ones, following the speed limits of the line.  
==PAGE:78==
Page | 78  
 
The latter simulation parameter leaded to relatively big dwell times (in some cases larger than 
1 minute), so that the actual departures would match with the inputted scheduled departure 
times (Table 8 - Table 10, p.38). In an attempt to make the model more realistic, a sensitivity 
analysis of the arrivals delays was held, based on the Performance Parameter, for each station 
of the line, for each different train type. The different stopping patterns as well as the different 
engine types for each train type lead to different “bottlenecks” for each train type. The 
analysis was stopped at the % area where at least one station started having positive arrival 
delays values, and the results are presented in Table 26. 
Following the cells painted with orange color, the bottleneck for each train type is noted. It 
appears to be that the ØK type is the least affected by a lower than maximum performance, 
while the ØP type is the most sensitive one. Nevertheless, it has to be noted that these results 
do not contain delay incidences, and an optimized version of the same model, containing them 
might lead to different results. 
Based on the results of Table 26, a measure in order to summarize the size of the change, 
based on the absolute difference between the arrival delay values with 90% minus the value 
referring to 100% performance factor was held and presented in Table 25. Clearly, the non-
stop distance before each station affects the sensitivity of the arrival delay of the station. That 
explains the reason why the middle stations, such as Espergæde and Snekkersten appear very 
small changes throughout the analysis.  
The abovementioned observation can be explained easily, through the fact that, having a 
larger non-stop distance with a lower performance factor, in means that the train will operate 
in the specified % of its maximum allowed speed, and thus it will not utilize its potentials as it 
could, for that long distance. This fact leads to the conclusions that the assigned travel time 
supplements must be a more complex procedure, considering that in real life, trains do not 
operate under 100% performance, and probably should not be done based only on the 
theoretical travel time, nor on the distance between the stations alone.

4.3 
Conclusions 
 
After combining the simulation results with the scheduled, real data, it is concluded that 
simulation can be proven a useful tool in order to evaluate the quality of a given timetable, 
through the use of Key Performance Indicators. The results of the different scenarios that can 
be designed and executed in the simulation software, and the further application of the latter 
in chosen, proper KPIs can lead to interesting results regarding a given timetable.  
More specifically, for the “Kystbanen” case, two different KPIs were applied, as well as a 
calibration of the simulation model based on the performance parameter took place. The 
results of the KPIs, both referring to the evaluation of the timetable, can be concluded as 
following:  
As for the homogeneity level of the line (northbound direction), the level is lower than the 
optimal value that was observed during a simulation run with optimal train function, without 
timetable departure time restrictions. Nevertheless, the result is evaluated as reasonable, as 
in order to reach a high level of robustness, the optimality level of the operation has to be 
balanced alongside with the heterogeneity and the capacity levels.  
Regarding the travel time prolongation, a calculation of the value of the three different train 
types (and thus their different timetable) was held. That was done with the help of a 
simulation model that is able to calculate a stop-free running time for each of the train types. 
The results, compared to other Danish known results, were found to be high, or very high 
valued, indicated that the lines have already a dense stopping pattern.  
Finally, the sensitivity analysis of the arrival delays, based on the train performance factor 
helped on the identification of the most and least sensitive stations to the arrival delays 
values, based on the performance factor. The results imply that the arrival delays are affected 
by the length of the stop-free distance beforehand, and thus, the need of a more complex 
running time supplements calculation, considering the actual performance factor is 
suggested.

Chapter 5: Conclusions and further development 
 
Throughout this study, a simulation model using “Opentrack” simulation software, a statistical 
analysis using “SAS Enterprise Guide” software and a calibration using Key Performance 
Indicators and simulation scenarios took place, in order to evaluate the train running times in 
the “Kystbanen” of Denmark.  
The successful collection of highly detailed data leaded to the formulation of a simulation 
model of the study line, able to run for the northbound direction, including all three different 
train types of the line. Nevertheless, the model is restricted due to the lack of the raw delay 
data separation into primary and secondary delays, and thus it is able to produce only 
incidences-free results. This optimal scenario was evaluated and compared to the scheduled 
timetable, revealing a reduction of 13.4% when analyzing the ØP and ØK train types and 12.7% 
reduction for the ØD type. 
Raw datasets for a big part of 2014 was provided for the needs of the study. After the proper 
filtering and preparation, a broad statistical analysis was held, focusing on northbound 
direction of the line, in different levels of detail. The detail latter began with an absence of 
separation of the delays, and included step by step a separation of the: delays (arrival and 
departure), train type (the three train types were analyzed differently), stations (stations were 
analyzed separately while keeping the train type not separated), both stations and train type, 
and finally isolating only the rush periods of the day. The results showed that such a level of 
detail is important, as, each level of detail provide with additional information, indicating 
differentiations between the diverse train types, different stations and period of the day. 
Nevertheless, the analysis of the dwell times indicated that there are not major differences 
throughout the day, nor among the train types, and the level of detail can be restricted to a 
station-separation level. 
An attempt to fit known distributions to the given data was held as the last part of the 
statistical analysis. The results indicate that an exponential distribution is not fitting the data 
neither for the arrival, late arrival or departure delays even when the rush periods of the day 
were isolated and tested individually. Additionally, the normal distribution is not describing 
the dwell time data. Nevertheless, a separation based on the day type was not held, and thus 
a final conclusion cannot be drawn for the data.  
Finally, a set of Key Performance Indicators were used in order to evaluate the timetable 
quality. The results of a homogeneity-related KPI, comparing the optimal timetable as 
calculated using the simulation model created in this study to the scheduled timetable, show 
that the optimal timetable comes with a higher level of homogeneity. Nevertheless, the result 
is interpreted as reasonable, as one the timetable’s objectives is a high level of robustness, 
that requires a balance between optimality and heterogeneity. Additionally, using the 
scheduled timetable and the ideal scenario once more, the travel time prolongation of each 
of the train types was evaluated, based on other known Danish lines values. The results lead 
to the conclusion that all three train types have highly prolonged travel times that can be 
explained due to the dense stopping patterns.  
Furthermore, a sensitivity analysis based on the train performance factor was held on the 
optimal scenario for each train type, using multiple simulation runs. Based on the results, the 
non-stop length before the stations appears to affect the sensitivity of the arrival delay values, 
==PAGE:82==
Page | 82  
 
making the delay value higher when the length is large and the factor value drops. The latter 
leads the possible proposal of a more complex train running time supplements assignment, 
including realized train performances. 
The produced simulation model of this study covers scenarios of optimality. Nevertheless, it 
is proposed that a future development of the current version would be able to simulate the 
line closer to a real operation by including incidences of delays. At the same time, itineraries 
covering all time periods of the day and treating the days differently is proposed. A reality-
matching model could be proven then, a strong tool on furtherly evaluating the line on its 
results and a wider variety of KPIs.  
Finally, an equally detailed analysis of the southbound is proposed for further work, while at 
the same a separation by the type of the day could provide with useful results, when testing 
for instance the distribution fitting of the different type of delays and dwell times.
