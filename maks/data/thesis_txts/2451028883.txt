Abstract
This master thesis aims to explain the negative effect of the nonlinear transmission
impairments of the optical fibre and describe how they could be dealt with using artificial
neural networks. Evaluation of different scenarios has been done using a simulation
model that imitates point-to-point transmission over an optical fiber. The model was
developed in MATLAB in order to perform numerical analysis.
In this project, the signal is transmitted using a single-channel and single-polarization
lightwave over a long-haul system of length L = 1000 km. In this scenario nonlinear
phase noise occurs as a consequence of self-phase modulation and is the effect that
needs to be overcome. For this purpose neural networks have been chosen due to their
capabilities of performing certain tasks by considering examples and the use of nonlinear
activation functions for modeling. Their performance depends on the training method
in use and in this project a few conventional second-order algorithms are used, as well
as extended and unscented Kalman filter algorithms. Two architectures were considered
when implementing the neural networks, one-sided single hidden layer network and end-
to-end learning which had two single hidden layer networks on each side of the channel.
With the aim of evaluating the quality of different neural network setups and training
methods, the bit error rate is calculated for different launch powers. The bit error rate
is used for the analysis as the most typical measure of signal distortion. The simulations
were performed using Monte Carlo simulation in order to achieve accurate numerical
approximations.
Using neural networks showed improvement of the performance of the system. The
best performing method was one-sided neural network trained with the extended Kalman
filter achieving BER = 9.8×10−7 at launch power Pav = 0 dBm. Even though it achieved
the minimum BER, end-to-end learning outperformed it for specific power values.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In the last three decades, an extremely rapid technological development has been wit-
nessed, especially in different areas of telecommunication. Needs of telecommunication
system users for information exchange are constantly growing, as well as providing addi-
tional services. Therefore, the capacity and data rate requirement are growing from year
to year. Development of fiber-optic communication is one of the factors that led to revo-
lutionizing telecommunication. Along with microelectronics, fiber-optic communication
was the driving force that started the ”information age” during the 1990s.
Telecommunication as it is known today, would not be possible without optical com-
munication systems. They use high carrier frequencies, around 190 THz, which allows
them to transmit bandwidths of order of magnitude in THz. Furthermore, optical com-
munication systems are suitable for long distance transmission due to their property of
having low loss, thus achieving a high bandwidth-distance product, which is a commonly
used measure of quality in communication systems. Improvements are constantly worked
on in order to increase the distance, bandwidth or reach lower bit error rate. Optical
fibers transmission impairments, such as dispersion and nonlinear optical effects, are
the main obstacle to increase the bandwidth-distance product. They are tackled using
different methods of digital equalization [IK10], which are well-developed linear equaliza-
tion techniques. In practice some of the impairments are nonlinear, thus the remaining
bottleneck for increasing of the capacity and transmission range are fiber nonlinearities
[Ess+10]. In this project, the focus is going to be on compensation of these nonlinear
effects, more specifically nonlinear phase noise.
Even though neural networks were created more than 40 years ago, their presence
in research has boosted incredibly in the last ten years.
This is due mainly to the
great improvements in the computational power of modern computers and the immense
increase in the amount of data collected worldwide. Neural networks are quite complex
and sometimes hard to interpret, but they offer flexibility and performance that are
unprecedented among machine learning methods. They particularly stand out in their
ability to model nonlinearity.
Therefore, in recent years their application in optical
communication is blooming, as it overcomes the limitation of traditional methods and
their reliance on theories and models.
Based on [PS17] Kalman filtering has the potential to diminish several optical im-
pairments simultaneously, therefore it has drawn huge attention in the field of optical
communication. Originally developed to handle linear dynamic systems, its capabilities
have been extended to nonlinear as well, in the form of extended Kalman filter (EKF)

and unscented Kalman filter (UKF). EKF is currently under wide investigation in opti-
cal communication systems, due to low complexity, fast convergence, joint tracking and
compensation of fiber impairments [PS17]. Since Kalman filtering methods can also be
used for parameter estimation, using them as training methods for the neural networks
to tackle the fiber impairments seemed like a logical approach.
1.1
Project motivation
Having in mind the attention given to neural networks in optical communication, as well
as using nonlinear Kalman filtering methods to tackle optical fiber impairments, the idea
was to see how would neural networks trained using EKF and UKF algorithms perform
on a nonlinear optical channel. The investigation would be conducted on a one-sided
neural network as an equalizer and end-to-end learning as a pulse shaping adaptation
for a nonlinear channel. Work done in [Jov18] showed the potential of both of these
approaches when a BPSK signal is transmitted, but the problem was quite basic to
make proper conclusions. Therefore, in this thesis, the goal was to use 16-QAM signal
as a higher level modulation, and evaluate the performance by examining the bit error
rate.
1.2
Project description
The goal of the project is to implement neural networks, trained by EKF and UKF
algorithms, as equalizers and assess the benefit they provide when transmitting over a
nonlinear channel. The two training algorithms will be compared to more classical deep
learning algorithms to determine if their use proves to be valuable. The comparison will
include the bit error rate and convergence speed.
Furthermore, end-to-end learning neural networks trained as well by EKF and UKF
algorithms are implemented and compared to the previously mentioned setup.
The
intention of this comparison is to see if it provides a more optimal solution since the
transmitted signal would be processed before it is exposed to the negative influence of
the nonlinear optical channel. This method can be considered as an adaptation of the
pulse shaping for the nonlinear channel.
For the purpose of analyzing such systems, a MATLAB implementation needs to be
developed. It should model a transmitter-channel-receiver chain in which the effects of
different techniques can be numerically evaluated in various working conditions.

CHAPTER 2
Fiber-optic communication
Fiber-optic communication systems are lightwave systems that employ optical fiber for
information transmission [Agr10]. Optical fibers are one of the main components in
modern communication and the base for high capacity core networks. The main charac-
teristic of optical fibers is low-loss and wide bandwidth which makes them suitable for
long distance, high-demand applications. Yet, transmission over optical fibers has room
for improvement, to achieve higher bit rates and cover longer distances, by overcoming
some of the limitation they possess, such as dispersion, fiber losses and nonlinear effects.
In this thesis, only long-haul systems are taken into consideration and their perfor-
mance limiting factors are going to be introduced in this chapter. Fiber dispersion is a
problem that is tackled by dispersion management, therefore it will not be considered
as a limiting factor. To achieve long distances, fiber losses, which are introduced in
section 2.1, need to be compensated by periodically inserting optical amplifiers along
the link as shown on Figure 2.1. There is a limitation on the number of cascaded inline
amplifiers, due to the noise they introduce, which will be discussed further in section 2.2.
Section 2.3 will cover nonlinear effects that occur in all optical fibers and are the main
limiting factor of these systems.

2.2
Fiber Loss Management
Optical fibers are characterized as having low-loss and the ones used in practice have an
attenuation coefficient of α = 0.2 dB/km. Even though, the fiber losses are low, given
that the long-haul system links spread over thousands of kilometers, compensation of
these losses must be performed.
The compensation of fiber losses, so-called fiber loss management, is performed by
periodically employing optical amplifiers along the optical link. The amplifiers overcome
the cumulative effect of fiber losses and are positioned such that the information carried
by the signal could still be retrieved. Example of such a link is presented on Figure 2.1
and it shows how the amplifiers are cascaded periodically to achieve longer distances of
transmission. One might think that, using this technique these links can achieve arbi-
trarily long distances. But this is not the case, because the optical amplifiers introduce
amplified spontaneous emission (ASE) noise.
Most of the modern systems use erbium-doped fiber amplifiers (EDFAs), therefore
only they will be considered while modelling the optical channel in this thesis. Amplifiers
are characterized through their gain G and amplifier noise figure Fn. Amplifier noise
figure is a parameter that quantifies the degradation of the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR),
due to ASE noise, and is defined by Equation 2.4:

increased. Thus, the launch power represents an important design factor in an optical
link, because it has to be chosen such that the best performance of the system can be
achieved.
2.3
Nonlinear Optical Effect
Nonlinear optical effects are considered the most limiting factors when it comes to de-
signing an optical communication system [Agr10]. These effects can be divided into two
groups, stimulated light scattering and nonlinear phase modulation. Raman scattering
and Brillouin scattering belong to the first group, and their effect becomes important at
high powers. Therefore, they will not be taking into consideration in this thesis.
From the nonlinear effects, nonlinear phase modulation is singled out. Three phenom-
ena belong in this group, self-phase modulation (SPM), which can be a major limitation
for long-haul single-channel lightwave systems, cross-phase modulation (XPM), which
can occur when wavelength-division multiplexing (WDM) technique is used, and four-
wave mixing (FWM), which occurs as a fourth optical field when three optical fields
are in the fiber simultaneously. They limit the system performance and introduce a
data-dependent nonlinear phase shift know as nonlinear phase noise (NLPN). In this
project the signal is transmitted using a single-channel single-polarization lightwave sys-
tem, thus the propagation of the optical signal through the fiber can be described using

CHAPTER 3
Channel equalization using
artificial neural networks
In the previous chapter, ASE noise and NLPN have been introduced as the main limiting
factors of long-haul optical systems. Noise is a limitation that cannot be overcome and
presents the theoretical boundary in the performance of a communication link. Since
the amplifiers are the ones that add the noise, it would be best that not many of them
are used. In order to decrease the number of amplifiers and still cover long distances,
the launch power should be increased, which will result into more severe degradation on
behalf of the NLPN.
In this chapter, channel estimation and equalization will be introduced in Section 3.1
as a method that can be used to overcome the negative effect of the NLPN, therefore
allowing the increase of the launch power. Simultaneously, overcoming the issue of the
number of amplifiers in use. Since the considered distortion of the signal is nonlinear,
artificial neural networks are suggested as a state-of-the-art approach, as they are con-
sidered to provide good approximations of nonlinear models. Artificial neural networks
are described in Section 3.2, while in Section 3.3 end-to-end learning is presented as new
path for architecture of communication systems.
3.1
Channel estimation and equalization
Channel estimation and equalization play an important role when receiving a signal. In
order to eliminate or decrease the negative effects that occur while the signal propagates
through the channel, in this case optical fiber, a highly precise estimation of the channel
is required for the purpose of further signal processing. Two types of estimation can be
distinguished, non-data-aided and data-aided.
Non-data-aided estimators function based on known statistic parameters of the re-
ceived signal. In this type of estimator, the information symbols that are transmitted
are unknown and it requires a significant amount of information to estimate the chan-
nel. On the other hand, data-aided estimators rely on information received from referent
signals, that are known both to the transmitting and receiving side, comparing them
with the received signals. Since the referent signals are known to both sides, they do
not carry any information, therefore part of the transmitted signal is not useful, which

is not the case in the non-data-aided estimators.
Since the negative effects of concern, that occurs in optical fiber, in this project is
NLPN, instead of using classical estimation and equalization methods, artificial neural

This method uses a known training sequence, therefore it can be considered as a data-

Neural networks use backpropagation, to update their weights iteratively until con-
vergences. Backpropagation calculates the derivative of the error in respect to all the
weights and biases in the network. Most commonly it applies gradient descent to a
cost function and minimizes it, to achieve the desired relation between the input and

algorithm. Many methods have shown superior performance when it comes to training
speed, mapping accuracy and generalization, and they are presented in section 4.1.
Neural networks can have different architectures and purposes depending on the
hidden layers in use. They can be divided into four classes:
• Feed-forward neural networks are the simplest neural networks. The data moves
only in one way, as suggested by their name: forward. As shown on Figure 3.1,
data goes from the input, through the hidden layers and to the output layer.
• Convolutional neural networks consist of convolutional layers, usually used for
image processing.
• Recurrent neural networks are different form feed-forward neural networks, due to
the cycles they form. They have internal memory to store previous states and are
suitable to process sequences and mimic dynamic behaviour.
• Autoencoders are a type of artificial neural networks that are mostly used for
dimensionality reduction and can learn a representation of data. They are trained
to copy the input to the output, but in a restricted way, such that the output
cannot be a perfect copy, just an approximation. Having a perfect copy would not
prove to be useful. Autoencoders consist of two parts: encoder, which transforms

CHAPTER 4
Training algorithms
Chapter 3 provided insight on channel equalization, neural networks, as well as different
architectures.
Their performance depends on the training procedure in use.
There
have been many enhanced training methods developed during the years, providing high
training speed, mapping accuracy, generalization and overall performance. Due to the
complexity and high computational requirements, most of these training methods are
not applicable in real time. Therefore, in this chapter, some of the superior training
algorithms will be explained and compared to Kalman filter algorithms, that are provided
as alternative training methods for ANN, since they have advantages of low complexity
and faster convergence.
Section 4.1 provides a brief description of training methods used from the MATLAB
deep learning toolbox, while in section 4.2, Extreme learning machine algorithm will be
presented as the fastest training algorithm. In section 4.3.1 principles of Kalman filter
are described and the state space notation provided, as well as the recursive equations.
Furthermore, in subsections 4.3.2 and 4.3.3, extended and unscented Kalman filters are
discussed, respectively, as nonlinear variations of the Kalman filter. Subsection 4.3.4 de-
tails the application of these methods for parameter estimation, where they estimate the
weights of neural networks, and can therefore be used as a training algorithm. Finally,
they are considered as training algorithms for end-to-end learning in section 4.3.5.
4.1
Conventional ANN training algorithms
Over the years, many training algorithms for neural networks have been developed in or-
der to enhance the training procedure and improve on the overall performance compared
to the standard gradient descent method. Most of these alternative methods use second-
order derivative information. The goal of this thesis is not to implement these training
algorithms, but just to review and compare their performance. Therefore, MATLAB’s
Deep Learning toolbox has been used, since it offers several of these training algorithms.
Of those, the Levenberg–Marquardt (LM) algorithm and Bayesian Regularization (BR)
deserve to be noted.
The Levenberg–Marquardt algorithm [HM94] is a very fast training algorithm to
minimize the square error. It is an iterative process by which the parameters are updated
with a step approximated by its linearization. The linearization is calculated from the
gradient of a cost function with respect to the parameters. It is important to mention
==PAGE:28==
18
4 Training algorithms
that this technique will only find local minima, and it will only find the global minima
if it exists and the initial guess for the parameters is in a close neighborhood.
Bayesian Regularization is a training algorithm provided by the MATLAB Deep
Learning toolbox. Bayesian probability is based on the use of a prior distribution or
model that is updated with a likelihood function (calculated with observed data) to-
wards a posterior distribution that is used for prediction. Regularization is a technique
widely used in Deep Learning that is mainly used to control the complexity of the prob-
lem (by reducing the number of parameters used) and avoid overfitting. In the case
of this toolbox, with Bayesian Regularization, they refer to a training technique that
updates the weights and bias according to the aforementioned Levenberg–Marquardt op-
timization. It tries to reduce the number of weights by finding the correct combination
of minimizing the error as well as the weights. The found combination usually produces
a network that generalizes well since the regularization helps to avoid overfitting.
Most of the training algorithms require high computational power, they are quite
complex and iterative, therefore not adequate for real-time implementation. In the fol-
lowing section, another training algorithm that overcomes these limitations is described.
4.2
Extreme learning machine (ELM)
A new learning algorithm for a single-hidden feed-forward neural network has been
presented in [HZC06], known as Extreme learning machine (ELM). This algorithm over-
comes a major bottleneck in training neural networks such as their learning speed. ELM
does not require a gradient, nor are the network parameters tuned iteratively, therefore
it is faster than traditional learning methods.
ELM algorithm is an extremely fast algorithm for training a single hidden layer
feed-forward neural network with Nh nodes in the hidden layer. It is based on Moore-
Penrose Generalized Inverse, of which a detailed explanation can be found in [HZC04].
ELM requires to have more training samples than the number of neurons in the hidden
layer, Nh ≤N. In this algorithm, the input weights are randomly chosen and fixed. The
output of the hidden layer H is calculated with these weights and an activation function
g(x). Using the outputs of the hidden layer and fitting them with the target dataset,
output weights can be analytically calculated as given in Equation 4.1. This process is
only done once, therefore such fast computation can be achieved. The summary of the
ELM algorithm is given in Algorithm 1.

and provides only an approximation of the optimal estimation. Since the first-order

4.3.5.1
Parameter setting
EKF and UKF algorithms require some initial parameter setting, such as weight initial-
ization and choosing Q and R values. In this project, the weights were drawn from a
normal distribution. Parameters Q and R are represented by scaled identity matrices
and they influence the overall performance of the algorithm. Since they are constrained
to be diagonal matrices, it means that an assumption of independent parameters is
made. Usually R is set to a fixed value, and there are several options on how to choose
Q.
• Set Q to a fixed value;
• Set Q to an initial value, which will be ”annealed” towards zero during the training.
Lower limiting value of the order of 10−6 should be established.
• Set Q = (λ−1
RLS −1)Pw, where λRLS ∈(0, 1] is often referred to as the ”forgetting
factor”, as it is defined in the recursive least-square (RLS) algorithm. This provides
for an approximate exponentially decaying weighting on past data [Haw01].
• Set Q such that it is consistent with the actual update model:
Qk = (1 −αRM)Qk−1 + αRMKk[(dk −ˆdk)(dk −ˆdk)T]KT

This method may be used for R as well.
For the purpose of this project only the first option, fixed values of Q and R through-
out the whole training process, was used. Parameter optimization was performed for Q
and R, when EKF training method was used, and it is explained in detail in section
5.2.3.
Parameters α, β and κ are essential for the UKF algorithm. The constant α is usually
set to a small positive value (10−4 ≤α ≤1), while κ is usually set to 0 or 3 −n, where
n is the number of weights in the ANN, and β = 2 is optimal if the prior distribution of
the weights is a Guassian distribution [Haw01]. Therefore, these parameters were fixed
to α = 1, κ = 0 and β = 2 and were not optimized.

repeated three times for each combination of parameters and the mean of these values
was taken as the result.

5.2.4
Issues with UKF
It was aforementioned that issues were encountered when UKF was used as a training
method for neural networks once the bias was added to it. The source of the problem is
the fact that the UKF requires the calculation of the square root of a matrix such that
the following statement is fulfilled:
A = LLH,
where H represents conjugate transpose, also known as Hermitian transpose. The square
root of a matrix can be found by using lower-triangular Cholesky factorization, but it
requires matrix A to be positive semi-definite. Loss of this property results into the
algorithms failure.
There exist a few ways this can be overcome. Looking at the definition of a positive
semi-defined matrix from a perspective of eigenvalues, it is said that A is positive semi-
==PAGE:61==
5.2 Performance analysis
51
definite if and only if all of its eigenvalues are non-negative.
Therefore, every time
before a Cholesky factorization should be performed, eigenvalues of the matrix can be
found and set to non-negative values, if there is a need for it. This method would be
the simplest one, but would probably damage the performance of the algorithm. Other
optimization methods can used, such as line search. But, doing deeper research on the
topic and implementing such optimization methods would increase the complexity of the
algorithm and is out of the scope of this thesis.
However, there is suggestion that if efficient square-root UKF method, presented in
[Haw01], is used, the loss of the positive semi-defined condition may be avoided, since it
updates the Cholesky factorization, rather than recalculating it every time. There is no
formal proof of this statement.

6.1
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to analyze the capabilities of neural networks to compensate
for the negative effect of nonlinearities introduced in the optical fiber when a 16-QAM
signal is transmitted. Nonlinear Kalman filtering techniques were used as training meth-
ods for the neural networks and compared to more traditional learning algorithms, as well
as how they performed compared to ideal estimation and equalization. The achievement
of end-to-end learning architecture was also investigated, to try and find pulse shaping
more suitable for a nonlinear channel. As the main quality indicator bit error rate (BER)
was used, as the most important quality indicator in digital telecommunication systems.
The analysis was performed using a simulation model developed in MATLAB, in
which apart from the transmission system, neural networks trained using EKF, UKF,
ELM and end-to-end neural network architecture trained with EKF and UKF, as well
as the channel model and zero-forcing were implemented. The traditional blocks of the
transmitter and receiver were provided by the research group and the conventional neural
network training algorithms were used from the MATLAB Deep Learning toolbox. The
influence of the channel on the transmitted signal was reviewed and the performance
analysis of all of these methods was conducted, as they have been tested over a launch
power interval. The interval was chosen in such a way that it covers all the possible
scenarios.
Based on the results of the analysis, it was noticed that depending on the launch
power the channel behaves differently. For low powers, the influence of NLPN is barely
noticeable and the distortion made by ASE noise is dominant. Intensifying the power
the impact of NLPN increases, rotating the constellation diagram and spreading the
spectrum of the signal.
Since the transmitted signal uses 16-QAM modulation, the
power is not equally distributed among the symbols. Therefore, symbols with higher
power suffer more under the effect of the NLPN. For the purpose of eliminating the
negative effects of the nonlinear channel, equalization using neural networks has been
used. Additionally, end-to-end learning was applied with the aim to modify the pulse
shaping to match the nonlinear channel, as a different approach.
Moving on to the inquiry done on the performance of the neural networks as equal-
izers, it was noticed that all of the applied methods improve the performance of the
overall system. Proper conclusions about the UKF algorithm cannot be made due to
the implementation restrictions it encountered. The model using classification displayed

that for the cases when the system is operating in the linear or optimal regime, the

the input power to the channel, which made the analysis a bit more difficult.

the cases and in this area the BER varied from 1 × 10−3 and 5 × 10−5, most of the time

interval, it reached values of more than 20 dBm and had BER around 0.5. Looking at
the spectrum of the encoder output signal, it was realized that it broadens the spectrum

still recognizable and the decoder restored it to its original shape. Acknowledging that
BER was notably different for similar launch power, it was presumed that the acquired
results were not the optimal ones and to investigate this, re-training of the decoder was
done. The test done with re-training the decoder achieved improvement and therefore
confirming the assumption that the optimal performance was not achieved.
Taking all of the results into consideration, neural networks proved to have high
potential as equalizers of the nonlinear channel, especially when they are trained using
EKF, due to its low complexity and fast convergence. Further improvements can be
made by adding another neural network at the transmitter but at the cost of having an

6.2
Future work
Numerous different setups, tests and adaptations have been left for future work due
to the lack of time. Future work provides possible improvement guidelines and new
proposals, as well as simple curiosity. Suggestions for further development of the model
are presented:
• Investigating the performance of ANN trained with EKF, if the mean of the ANN
output signal is subtracted.
• As a basic improvement of the EKF training method, implementation of parameter
update methods explained in section 4.3.5.1 for Q can be done and tested in order
to see if they actually improve the performance and provide a more fine parameter
tuning.
• End-to-end learning method showed instability with regards to the launch power,
this can be improved by normalizing the weights of the encoder, enabling control
over the input power to the channel. It would make it feasible to have a deeper
understanding of the outcome of the method, possibly acquiring optimal pulse
shapes for each launch power.
• Implementation of methods to overcome the issues UKF suffers from explained in
section 5.2.4. If these methods were to provide a solution, UKF could be used in-
stead of EKF as a superior method. It would relieve the requirements of derivation,
thus also the need for accurate mathematical models when it comes to end-to-end
learning.
• Implementation and testing of the efficient square-root UKF method.
• It was mentioned that nonlinear Kalman filtering algorithms were used to approach
more optical fiber impairments at the same time, thus a logical extension to this
model would be including a wider span of negative effects to the channel model.
It would be interesting to see how would Kalman filtering methods would per-
form if the memory was added to the channel since they are considered recursive
algorithms.
