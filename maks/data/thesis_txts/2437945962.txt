Abstract 
Diabetes mellitus is becoming a worldwide concern, and the current treatment with insulin 
involves administration via the subcutaneous route. Oral delivery is the preferred route of 
administration. The small intestine is often targeted for drug delivery of biopharmaceuticals, 
nonetheless these exhibit poor bioavailability. Obstacles such as poor absorption and intestinal 
mucus must be overcome for effective oral drug delivery. Thus, developing novel oral drug delivery 
systems as e.g. microcontainers for treating diabetes mellitus is incentivised. 
The aim of this project was to explore how spray coating of lids onto microcontainers with the 
functional polymers poly(lactic-co-glycolic) acid (PLGA), chitosan and poly(ethylene glycol) 
(PEG) may increase intestinal absorption and prolong residence time of the model protein, 
lysozyme in the small intestine. Coated microcontainers, loaded with insulin and excipients such 
as a permeation enhancer, might be an efficient drug delivery system for treating diabetes mellitus. 
The protein drug is protected by the external environment, and is assisted across the intestinal 
barrier. Initially microcontainers loaded with the model protein lysozyme were spray coated with 
polymers employing different functions. To protect the protein from proteolysis inhibition, the 
microcontainers were all coated with PLGA. An additional coating of either PEG or chitosan was 
applied on top, both providing mucoadhesive properties, with chitosan also promoting permeation 
enhancement. The thicknesses of the coatings were measured and was 32.5±7.96 µm for 
PLGA+PEG and 22.49±3.78 µm for PLGA+chitosan. The morphology of the different coatings 
was evaluated and showed lid-like appearances. The mucoadhesive properties of the coatings were 
assessed on porcine intestine with a simulated peristaltic flow and were evaluated by their 
retention. Both chitosan (35.69±14.09 %) and PEG (40.85±5.53 %) coatings demonstrated 
increased mucoadhesion, and may be able to prolong residence time in the small intestine. Release 
profiles were produced using microdissolution and showed a slower release for PLGA+chitosan 
(0.76 Δ%/Δt) compared to PLGA and PLGA+PEG. The lids were investigated in combination 
with the permeation enhancer C10 for its ability to promote absorption across Caco-2 and Caco-
2/HT-29 cell monolayers. The PLGA+chitosan coating demonstrated the ability to open tight 
junctions for both lysozyme and insulin. Notably, this effect was more pronounced for Caco-2/HT-
29 cells than for Caco-2 cells. The flux of insulin with C10, coated with PLGA+chitosan in Caco-
2/HT-29 cell studies was 24.32±4.59 *10-6 nmol/min/cm2. These results however need validation, 
as reversibility was not consistently observed. Conclusively, coating microcontainers with 
functional polymers was found to be promising for oral drug delivery of insulin.

Introduction 
The financial incentive for pharmaceutical companies to develop small molecules has rapidly 
decreased the last decade. This has resulted in a revelation in the pharmaceutical industry. The 
advancement of biotechnology is still on the rise and an increasing focus has been employed on 
the development of biopharmaceutical drugs, such as enzymes, antibodies, and recombinant 
therapeutic proteins and peptides [1]. In the years between 1989 and 2012 marketed 
biopharmaceuticals increased from 13 to 210 products, while the worldwide sales increased to US$ 
163 billion. Moreover,  biologicals accounted for 7 % of the revenue generated by the ten top-
selling pharmaceuticals in 2001 to 71 % in 2012  [1]. 
Biopharmaceutical drugs compared to small molecule drugs offer higher specificity and potency, 
which ultimately leads to reduced side effects and more effective treatments resulting in decreased 
healthcare costs [2]. However, biopharmaceuticals are structurally and functionally complex 
macromolecules making their formulation and delivery challenging. When exposed to external 
environmental interactions, a biopharmaceutical may lose its activity and has reduced 
permeability across biological barriers due to their high molecular mass and often high 
hydrophilicity.  
Currently, this means that the primary administration routes are  intravenous or subcutaneous 
injections [1]. Oral delivery however, is by far the most desirable route of administration [3], but 
is challenging for proteins and peptide drugs. Often these drugs have a maximum oral 
bioavailability of 1-2 %, caused by enzymatic degradation and poor permeability over the 
intestinal membrane. Therefore studies are ongoing to develop advanced drug delivery systems 
that enable effective oral drug delivery [4][5]. Such studies are currently being investigated for 
treatment of numerous diseases, including Alzheimers [6], cancerous diseases [7] and Diabetes 
mellitus [4]. The motivation for developing these new formulations is high, especially for a disease 
like diabetes mellitus, which is an ever growing concern world-wide. It is estimated that the 
condition affects 415 million people globally, a number expected to increase to 642 million by the 
year of 2040. In addition, around 193 million people remain undiagnosed as the condition is often 
asymptomatic or only causes mild symptoms [8]. Hence, novel treatments of diabetes is becoming 
a huge and increasing market in the foreseeable future and thus, the potential revenues are 
immense for innovative solutions. However, developing effective oral protein and peptide delivery 
systems an extensive understanding of the mucosal microenvironment and intestinal physiology 
is therefore required [9].

1.1 Gastrointestinal Tract 
The human gastrointestinal (GI) tract comprises the mouth, oesophagus, stomach, small intestine 
and colon (Figure 1-1) [10]. The main function of the GI tract is the digestion of food which 
subsequently can be absorbed as nutrients, and concurrently provide an efficient barrier restricting 
entry of toxic substances like peptides, viruses and bacteria to the systemic circulation [11]. 
Different locations in the lumen of the GI tract are associated with different challenges to orally 
formulated drugs, especially proteins and peptides [12]. Most notable are the large pH span 
throughout the GI tract, from down to ~1 in the stomach, up to ~8 in the ileum, the digestive 
enzymes and the barrier formed by the mucosal layers [13].  
The main function of the oral cavity is to guard the stomach by moisturising food into a soft 
bolus. The mucosa is tough and functions to protect, rather than to absorb. Likewise, the mucosa 
in the oesophagus has low absorption and passage of the bolus is normally complete within 10 
seconds [14]. The stomach constitutes a harsh environment for macromolecules, due to the low 
pH and the presence of numerous degradation enzymes, such as pepsin [15]. In fasted state the 
pH varies from 1-3 in and 3-6 in fed state. When drugs are orally administered, absorption mainly 
occurs in the small intestine [13], though the colon gains increased attention as potential 
absorption site, having a relatively low proteolysis activity [16].

1.1.1 Small Intestine 
The small intestine is composed of three different parts, each with distinct absorptive capabilities. 
The first part is the duodenum (pH of 6-7), which is 25-30 cm long making passage time very 
short. This results in low degree of drug absorption [18]. The second part is the jejenum (pH of 
6-7.7), which is 3 m long and has a highly active peristalsis, favouring absorption. The third and 
final part is the ileum, (pH of 6.5-8). This part is also  3 m in length offering the same absorption 
capability as the jejenum [18][19]. 
For a drug to reach the lamina propria and enter the systemic circulation, it has to cross the 
intestinal epithelium [13]. It consists of mucus covered polarized cells, structured into crypts and 
villi, which are further arranged with microvilli [13] (Figure 1-2). This increases the inner surface 
area of the small intestine enormously to 300-400 m2 [12]. The villi are covered by epithelial cells, 
mainly absorptive enterocytes, specialized to absorb digested nutrients by either passive or active 
transport, and mucus secreting goblet cells[18]. Less abundant are the phagocytotic M cells present 
in regions called Peyer’s patches, Paneth cells and enteroendocrine cells [13].

Adjacent epithelial cells are interlinked via protein networks that form a junctional complex close 
to the apical surface. This complex consists of three components: Desmosomes, adherence 
junctions and tight junctions [20]. Desmosomes and adherence junctions mechanically associate 
adjacent cells [20]. However, most notable are the tight junctions, which provide the denseness 
and tightness of the epithelium [13] and act as gatekeepers to restrict entry of molecules to the 
systemic circulation [21].  
Tight junctions comprise a complex of transmembrane integral proteins, including junctional 
adhesion molecules, claudins and occludins. These are anchored to the actin cytoskeleton by 
intracellular plaque proteins, forming structural support for tight junctions, and regulatory 
proteins responsible for signal transduction in permeability regulation [20]. Thus, tight junctions 
form the barrier that regulates the passage of water and electrolytes from the lumen, but 
concurrently prevents entry of infectious and inflammatory agents into the systemic circulation 
[22]. 
In epithelial cells layers, the transport can be facilitated through two pathways; the paracellular 
pathway (aqueous) or the transcellular pathway (lipoidal). The paracellular pathway is the 
transport between adjacent cells, through the tight junctions [23]. Transcellular transport is the 
transport through cell membranes and comprises passive transport via facilitated or simple 
diffusion by a concentration gradient, active transport against a concentration gradient and 
endocytosis by engulfment [21] (see Figure 1-3). Before a drug can enter either of the pathways, 
it needs to pass through the mucus layer lining the epithelial surface.

1.1.2 Mucus 
Mucus is a complex hydrogel consisting of carbohydrates, lipids, bacteria, salts, cellular debris 
and proteins [12]. It constitutes the first line of defence throughout the GI tract and covers the 
lining of both the stomach, large and small intestine. In addition to protecting it, mucus also 
lubricates the inner surface of the GI tract, facilitating the passage of chime, food and faeces [12]. 
It is tenacious and tends to cover particles in a cloak, preventing contact with the epithelial cell 
surfaces [24]. The primary mechanism by which mucus traps foreign particles and pathogens is 
by forming polyvalent adhesive interactions [25]. In this way, the mucus layer to some extent 
protects the epithelium from toxins and pathogens [26], but enables the exchange of nutrients 
[13]. 
 
The thickness of mucus changes throughout the GI tract and is thickest in the stomach, ranging 
from 40 µm to 450 µm and in the colon, ranging from 100 µm to 160 µm. There are also variations 
across the small intestines, with mean values of 476 µm in the ileum, 123 µm in the jejenum and 
170 µm in the duodenum [19]. The M-cells in the small intestine are believed to be important in

drug delivery, as they are covered in a thinner layer of mucus compared to other epithelial cell 
types [12]. The mucus thickness differences are listed in Table 1-1. 
The intestinal mucus layer consists of an inner firmly adherent layer and an outer loosely adherent 
layer. The loosely adherent layer is by far the thickest [12]. The thickness also varies according 
to a person’s fibre intake, which leads to an increase in loosely adherent layer thickness [12]. In 
general, the thickness is influenced by the balance between the rate of secretion, shedding and 
degradation [24]. 
Mucus undergoes a continuous secretion, followed by shedding, then discarding or digestion and 
lastly recycling [27]. The turnover time is highly variable, but it is usually fastest at the surfaces 
with the thinnest layer of mucus. In the GI tract, peristaltic forces cause the continuous secretion 
(nearly 10L per day) and clearance of mucus. The general turnover time has been observed to be 
4-6 hours in rats, and is likely to be similar for humans [27].

The main protein component of mucus are the mucins. These are responsible for 2-5 % of the 
total mucus mass [13]. Mucins are large highly glycosylated glycoproteins, consisting of an 
extended central protein core, densely coated with oligosaccharides. These accounts for around 80 
% of the total mass of mucins [28]. Mucins’ backbones are rich in proline, threonine and serine, 
forming PTS domains. The threonines and serines are O-linked to oligosaccharides of varying 
length and composition. These include N-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc), N-acetylgalactosamine 
(GalNAc), fucose, galactose and sialic acids (Figure 1-4) [29]. Mucus has a net negative charged 
provided by the carboxyl groups and carbohydrate-bound sulphate residues of sialic acids [30]. 
The PTS domains are flanked by cysteine-rich domains, including a C-terminal cysteine knot 
domain and four D domains of von-Willebrand factor. These are involved in the dimerization and 
oligomerization, respectively, leading to the viscous mucus-gel network [31].

Mucins exist in either secreted or membrane-bound forms. At least 20 mucins are encoded in the 
MUC gene family, of which MUC2 is the major secreted mucin in the large and small intestine. 
These are exclusively secreted by goblet cells, whereas membrane-bound mucins are expressed on 
the apical side, of both enterocytes and goblet cells [31]. Secreted mucin monomers (300-500 kDa), 
connect via disulphide bridges into large mucin molecules that entangle and form the viscoelastic, 
shear-thinning gel, which is essential for protecting and lubricating the luminal surface of the GI 
tract [12]. Transmembrane mucins cover the apical surface of the enterocytes and reach out into 
the lumen and produce a glycan-rich well-attached diffusion barrier called the glycocalyx, whose 
function is to protect the enterocytes from foreign particles and regulate the local milieu [32].

1.2 Oral Delivery of Peptide and Protein Drugs 
Advances in the last few decades in biotechnology and high-throughput screening have cleared 
the way for the discovery of an increasing number of promising drug candidates [33]. Better 
understanding of biochemistry and molecular biology, has opened the enormous potential of the 
therapeutic use of proteins and peptides [34]. 
 
Often, treatments from biologicals offer considerable advances over conventional small molecule 
drugs for similar treatments [34], including high specificity leading to less chance of adverse effects 
and interference with normal biological processes. As many of the proteins used in treatments are 
naturally produced by the body, they are most often well-tolerated and are less prone to elicit 
immune responses [35]. Protein therapeutics are often approved by the Federal Drug and Food 
Association (FDA) faster than small molecules. This owes to their uniqueness of shape and 
function, which enables targeting of new therapeutic areas, making it easier to attain patent 
protection [35]. 
 
The oral route remains the most attractive route of administration of drugs [33], as it is less 
invasive, increases patient compliance, and eases administration [36]. In general however, this 
route is not feasible for protein-based drugs. Despite their benefits, protein therapeutics are often 
associated with challenges regarding their administration. At present, protein and peptide drugs 
are mainly administered intravenously through the parenteral route, either subcutaneously or 
intramuscularly [34]. Treatments involving injections often require trained personnel and exact 
aseptic techniques, while self-administration normally is discouraged. The fear of needles is 
widespread, especially for young people. Thus, despite being effective, the parenteral route does 
not represent an ideal route of administration [36].  
Due to the hydrophilicity of proteins and peptides, the majority having negative logP values, 
their passive diffusion through the transcellular pathway is immediately hindered [37]. Normally, 
large hydrophilic molecules like proteins are limited to carrier-mediated transport or the 
paracellular pathway [3]. However, passage via the paracellular pathway is usually restricted to 
molecules in the size range of 100-200 Da (molecular radii lower than 11 Å), by the narrow space 
that traverse between adjacent enterocytes. The paracellular pathway, however, does not rely on 
lipophilicity to the same extent as the transcellular pathway, as it is mainly an aqueous channel 
[37].

Generally, there is an undiminished ambition in overcoming the obstacles of oral protein delivery 
[38]. Thus, various strategies have been explored to target both the transcellular and paracellular 
pathways [3] with the use of specialised drug delivery systems [39]. One protein for which this 
interest is greater than ever is insulin [38]. 
 
1.3 Insulin and Diabetes 
Insulin is an anabolic peptide hormone existing in a monomeric, dimeric and hexameric form [4]. 
Insulin is initially secreted by pancreatic β-cells, after which it is partially cleared by liver 
hepatocytes, and delivered to the vascular endothelium. Following this, it is transported primarily 
to the brain, muscle fibres and adipocytes and is eventually degraded and cleared by the kidneys 
[40][41]. In this pathway, insulin binds to insulin receptors. This lowers the blood glucose levels 
and facilitates the cellular uptake of glucose, including amino acids and fatty acids. In addition, 
it increases the expression of enzymes which catalyse glycogen, lipids and protein synthesis, while 
lipolysis and proteolysis is inhibited [40]. 
In non-diabetic individuals, the basal insulin accounts for around half of the daily output and 
keeps the blood glucose levels stable. Prandial (bolus) insulin is on the other hand released in 
bursts in response to food intake. This burst release suppresses the hepatic glucose production, 
but stimulates hepatic glucose uptake and the uptake by peripheral tissues to rapidly regain 
normal glucose levels [4][42]. A dysregulation of insulin is the cause of diabetes mellitus of which 
there are two types; type 1 and type 2. Type 1 diabetes mellitus is an autoimmune disorder which 
causes the destruction of β-cells resulting in no or minute secretion of insulin. Type 2 diabetes 
mellitus is a metabolic disorder in which the β-cells progressively loses their functionality to 
adequately suppress hepatic glucose release [4]. Diabetic patients require administration of insulin, 
which is currently administered via the parenteral route as a subcutaneous injection. The site of 
injection, however can affect the onset and duration of action. In addition, subcutaneous delivery 
does not mimic the normal dynamics of endogenously secreted insulin as it directly enters the 
systemic circulation via the hepatic portal vein and many patients often fail to maintain stable 
glucose levels [4]. Patients with irregular glucose levels risk long-term complications such as 
retinopathy and adult blindness, cardiovascular diseases, non-traumatic amputation, and diabetic 
nephropathy [9]. Oral administration of insulin has a better risk/benefit profile and better mimics 
insulin secretion in the endogenous pathway [4]. However, like other proteins, insulin has low 
bioavailability when orally administered, and oral dosage forms are extensively investigated [9].

1.4 Drug Delivery Systems for Oral Administration 
The incorporation of drugs into specialized drug delivery systems has been widely studied to 
overcome the challenges of delivering proteins and peptides orally [43]. Drug delivery systems for 
oral administration come in many shapes and forms, comprising liposomes, microspheres, polymer 
hydrogels, polymeric micro- and nanoparticles and microemulsions [39]. Polymer-based systems 
are widely used, as these are easily functionalized and many polymers are biodegradable [39]. A 
considerable number of polymers have been used in various carrier systems, including natural 
polymers like chitosan, gelatin, dextran, agar and alginate, but also synthetic polymers such as 
poly(lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA), polycaprolactone (PCL) and polyethylenimine (PEI) [43].  
Several systems have been designed for oral delivery of insulin, providing structural protection 
[9]. They also may enhance the absorption by including permeation enhancers, enzyme inhibitors 
and applying chemical modifications [21]. Implementing excipients, with functional properties is 
essential in obtaining an acceptable level of bioavailability [33]. 
 
1.4.1 Permeation Enhancement 
The intestinal absorption of peptides can be improved by altering the paracellular or transcellular 
pathway, with the use of permeation enhancers.  These can function by various mechanisms of 
action, including changing of the mucus viscosity, induction of tight junction opening and 
modulation of plasma membrane fluidity [44]. 
One strategy, that has been shown to promote increased absorption [45] is the inclusion of 
mucolytic agents such as papain, bromelain or trypsin, that all cleave the mucin network, 
decreasing the viscoelasticity of the mucus [46]. Another strategy is modulating tight-junction 
permeability. Many substances have demonstrated this capacity, including Zonula Occludens 
toxin, pz-peptide, thiolated polymers and chitosan and its derivatives [47][48][49].  
 
 
1.4.2 Mucoadhesion 
Several strategies have been pursued for overcoming and/or exploiting the intestinal mucus layer 
for more effective delivery of drugs [45]. Mucoadhesion is an important property for a drug delivery 
system and is defined as the capability of a material to adhere to mucosal surfaces [50]. The 
constant mixing by the peristalsis in the GI tract can be exploited by employing mucoadhesion 
==PAGE:22==
 
11 
 
in a drug delivery system, as the mixing ensures eventual contact between the system and the 
mucosal barrier [10]. This is an attribute commonly used for carrier systems targeting 
transmucosal delivery, as it increases residence time in the GI tract and may result in higher 
absorption and bioavailability [12][51]. Adhesion to mucus arises from electrostatic interactions, 
van der Waals interactions, hydrophobic interactions and interpenetration from polymers. Many 
well-known polymers such as chitosan and poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) have demonstrated 
mucoadhesive abilities [52][53]. 
 
 
1.4.3 Proteolysis Inhibition 
A different approach for improving the bioavailability of an orally administered peptide drug is 
to apply enzyme inhibition. More specifically, lowering the local pH can cause the deactivation of 
local digestive enzymes [54]. Several substances have been employed in drug delivery for this 
purpose, such as citric acid [55].  The drawback of enzyme inhibition, is that it may compromise 
the long-term protection that the inhibited enzymes normally provide, and may lead to the 
absorption of unwanted molecules [56]. 
 
1.5 Microcontainers for Oral Drug Delivery 
Orally ingested therapeutic dosage forms traditionally deliver and release the active ingredient in 
an omni-directional manner, resulting in inefficient delivery. High drug doses are therefore needed, 
which may cause serious side effects for the GI tract of patients [57]. However, advances in 
microfabrication have recently contributed to the development of innovative drug delivery 
systems [57][58]. One example of such a system is SU-8 polymer reservoir-based and cylinder 
shaped containers in the micrometre range, called microcontainers (Figure 1-5) [59][60]. SU-8 is 
an epoxy resin-based polymer, which is a commonly used material in micromechanical processing. 
It displays excellent mechanical strength, chemical resistance and biocompatibility [61]. Therefore 
it has been proposed to be suitable as an implant material and applicable for encapsulating 
biomolecules [62]. These microcontainers can provide protection against enzymatic or acidic drug 
degradation in the GI tract for fragile molecules [57][58], such as therapeutic proteins. 
Microcontainers also enable an unidirectional release for the therapeutic drug as the reservoir is 
exposed only on the top of the cylinder [57][63]. It has been shown that microcontainers increase 
drug absorption by interacting with intestinal mucus [58][64]. The unidirectional release increases 
==PAGE:23==
 
12 
 
the drug concentration at the interface of the microcontainer opening and the epithelial cell 
surface, which in turn improves the drug permeation and bioavailability [65]. This has been shown 
for furosemide [58] and ketoprofen [64]. In addition, the microcontainers can be both surface 
treated and coated to provide added functionality and enhance oral bioavailability [58][62].  
 
 
 
1.6 Microcontainer Filling 
To prepare the microcontainers for oral administration, they initially need to be loaded with drug 
and possibly excipients, with an appropriate method. Ideally, this should be a method by which 
the filled amount can accurately be determined [66]. Generally, a substantial mass of a protein-
based drug is required to exert a detectable therapeutic response. Thus, for a drug delivery system 
to obtain commercial use in the field of bio pharmaceuticals, it is critical that a proper amount 
of drug can be loaded into the transport system, while its stability is maintained [10].  
Various methods have been employed to load drug into microcontainers. These include inkjet 
printing, which is able to dispense precise amounts of drug into microdevices [66]. Super-critical 
impregnation [67] has demonstrated reproducible and accurate loading, which is easily controlled 
by tuning of time, temperature, pressure and drug concentration [68]. Another method is powder 
embossing [69], in which a shadow mask is applied onto the chip to avoid residual powder between 
containers, followed by embossing. Similar but more manual methods using a mask have also been 
employed, in which drug powder is brushed into the microcontainers [70]. Even simpler methods, 
in which drug powder is distributed manually without the use of a mask have also been used [58]. 
The best suited filling method depends on the properties of the drug and excipients [68][69].

1.6.1 Lysozyme 
Lysozymes, also referred to as muramidases are a group of glycoside bond hydrolysing enzymes, 
commonly used as model proteins [71]. Hen-egg white lysozyme is the best characterised of all 
proteins and its reaction is well described, making it appropriate as a model protein [72][73]. It is 
well-known for having antibacterial and immune functional properties exploited in medicines and 
food additives [72][74]. It can perform lysis of the cell walls of gram-negative bacteria [75] and is 
part of the innate immune system in humans, where it is produced by the Paneth cells of the 
upper intestinal tract [76]. 
 
1.6.2 Sodium Caprate 
A well-studied excipient for inclusion in drug delivery systems is sodium caprate (C10) [77]. C10 
is the sodium salt of capric acid and is a medium chain fatty acid, naturally found in dairy 
products and oils such as milk and coconut oil (see Figure 1-6) [78]. It has been approved as a 
food additive with no daily limit set by the World Health Organization (WHO) and the FDA 
[79][80]. While its mechanism of action is not fully understood, C10 is thought to promote 
permeability of macromolecules via both the transcellular and paracellular pathways. The latter 
by modulating the tight junctions [79]. Albeit not widely studied, it has been suggested that the 
monomeric, micellar or vesicular form of C10 non-covalently interacts with the drug. This 
interaction changes C10’s physicochemical properties, which improves drug absorption across the 
intestinal epithelium [79]. In human studies it has shown to cause a transient effect on the 
intestinal epithelium and cause reversible membrane perturbation while being well-tolerated 
[78][79][81].

1.7 Spray Coating 
A second way to add excipients and functionalities to the microcontainer systems is by coating 
with  functional polymers [63].  An ultrasonic spray coating instrument can be utilized in the 
formation of thin polymer films. A polymer solution is pumped and nebulized into a fine mist 
through a small orifice by applying a pressure of nitrogen gas, while mechanical vibrations are 
produced by a piezoelectric transducer inside the ultrasonic spray nozzle [82][83][84]. The small 
droplets of the polymer solution is directed towards a substrate and are deposited where they 
coalesce to form a coating. The substrate is placed on a hot plate which can be temperature 
controlled. In general, ultrasonic spray coating has good transfer efficiency and produces precise 
conformal coatings on both planar and nonplanar surfaces and has good reproducibility [84] 
(Figure 1-7). However, many parameters must be controlled and optimized to obtain a uniform 
film. Parameters such as the concentration of the polymer solution, solvent, number of spray 
passes, flow rate, temperature of the substrate and its distance to the spray nozzle, the movement 
speed of the nozzle during the spray and the air pressure must all be considered [84]. The optimum 
of the parameters is narrow and thus, a balance must be found between the time it takes for the 
droplets to coalesce on the substrate and the next spray passage.

on a temperature controlled hot plate [83]. 
 
Several studies have successfully included coatings of pH-sensitive polymers on microcontainers, 
and have shown that polymer coating of microcontainers can provide protection and functionality

1.7.1 Chitosan 
Chitin, poly(b-(1®4)-N-acetyl-D-glucosamine), is a naturally occurring polysaccharide, 
synthesized by a vast amount of living organisms. The primary source of chitin, is from 
crustaceans such as crab and shrimp shells [85]. When chitin is deacetylated to a degree around 
40%, it is soluble in aqueous acidic solutions and is called chitosan [85]. This is the most important 
derivative of chitin and comprises copolymers of N-acetylglucosamine and glucosamine, the 
deacetylation product of chitin [86] Chitosan exists in many different molecular weights (2-50 
kDa) and degree of deacetylation (40-98%) which influences its viscosity and solubility properties 
[86]. The chemical structures of chitin and chitosan are presented in Figure 1-8 A and B 
respectively. 
 
 
Figure 1-8: Chemical structure of (A) chitin and (B) chitosan [87]. Chitosan is the deacetylated form of chitin. 
 
The pKa value of the primary amine of glucosamine is approximately 6.5. Thus, it is primarily 
protonated in acidic pH’s and will be in a soluble cationic form [88]. However, generally chitosan 
is soluble in aqueous acidic solutions and dimethylsulfonide [89]. Numerous acids can form water-
soluble salts with chitosan, including formic acid, maleic acid, acetic acid, lactic acid and glycolic 
acid [90]. The protonated form of chitosan has demonstrated the ability to increase paracellular 
permeability across mucosal epithelia for peptide drugs [86]. It does so by reversibly modulating 
the integrity of the epithelial tight junctions [91]. The signal mechanism by which chitosan induces 
opening of tight junctions has been studied and found that multiple mechanisms may be involved 
in the process [92][22]. Results indicate that chitosan interacts with the transmembrane claudin

protein CLDN4 to trigger the opening of tight junctions [22] Moreover, chitosan is well-known as 
a mucoadhesive agent, enabling prolongation of residence time in the small intestine [91]. These 
properties make chitosan a popular polymer to include in drug delivery systems [91]. 
Studies on Caco-2 monolayers have shown more pronounced permeation enhancement of high 
molecular weight chitosans and high degree of deacetylation [86]. The dependence of protonation 
degree, has also been demonstrated to assert its permeation enhancing [28] and mucoadhesive 
features [93]. Chitosan’s mucoadhesive properties arises from its strongly positive charge, 
attracting the negatively charged mucins of the mucus [94]. Chitosan has the capability to form 
both hydrogen bonds and covalent bonds, via its -OH and -NH2 groups. This enables an 
electrostatic interaction with the negatively charged mucus components such as sialic acid [95]. 
Also, hydrophobic interactions and hydrogen bonding plays a role in chitosan mucoadhesive 
properties [52]. 
 
Several derivatives of chitosan have been explored to address the solubility limitations of native 
chitosan at neutral and alkaline values. The derivatives N-trimethyl chitosan chloride and 
thiolated chitosan have, among others, shown promising results as permeation enhancers [86]. 
 
 
1.7.2 Polyethylene Glycol 
One of the most used polymers in drug delivery systems is the FDA approved polyethylene glycol 
(PEG). It is a relatively chemical inert and non-biodegradable polymer. It is uncharged and 
amphiphilic, meaning both hydrophilic and hydrophobic in nature, and is consequently soluble in 
water and organic solvents [96]. PEG is available in various molecular weights and it is divided 
into groups either containing free hydroxyl groups (-OH) at both ends or chain terminated with 
one or two methoxy groups (-OCH3) (mPEG) [97][98]. The structure of a hydroxyl terminated 
PEG is shown in Figure 1-9.

PEGs express stealth behaviour and is therefore used to conjugate with numerous molecules, 
including proteins, to prolong their blood circulation [96][97][99]. This type of conjugation is called 
PEGylation and increases the bulkiness and reduces protein-protein interactions by steric 
hindrance and the possibility of degradation [96][99]. Furthermore, PEG has good permeability, 
retention effects and it is widely used and studied as a mucoadhesive agent [100]. The 
mucoadhesive properties are believed to arise from its hydrophilic nature and ability to inter-
diffuse with the mucus gel network. In addition, it is suggested that formation of hydrogen bonds 
between the ether oxygens and sugars on glycosylated mucins enhance the diffusion of PEG coated 
particles [53]. The molecular weight and density of PEG seem to play a role in its interaction 
with the mucus mesh. Low molecular weight and high density of PEG surface coverage is required 
for rapid mucus penetration [53][101]. A high molecular weight of PEG increases mucoadhesion 
and a transition seems to appear in the range of 5 to 10 kDa, where PEG changes from being 
mucopenetrating to mucoadhesive [53]. Higher molecular weight PEG has longer chains resulting 
in better entanglement with mucins, by which the number of intermolecular interactions such as 
hydrogen bonding is increased. The opposite is applicable for low molecular weight, in which the 
PEG-mucin interactions are reduced. A higher surface coverage of PEG adds a shielding effect 
from mucins and hydrophobic adhesive interactions are blocked since the displacement of water 
becomes entropic prohibitive. This enhances the mucuspenetrating effects of PEG [53][101]. 
 
 
1.7.3 Poly(lactic-co-glycolic) acid 
Poly(lactic-co-glycolic) acid is the copolymer of poly lactic acid (PLA) and poly glycolic acid 
(PGA).  PLGA is considered one of the most successfully developed and used polymers, owing to 
its very attractive properties. Firstly, it is biodegradable and biocompatible. Secondly, it is 
approved by FDA and European Medicines Agency (EMA) approved and thirdly, it is very well 
studied, meaning that formulations and production methods are well-described for various types 
of drugs. These count both hydrophobic and hydrophilic small molecules as well as 
macromolecules. PLGA’s attributes have been utilized to protect drug molecules from 
degradation, thereby facilitating sustained release. Finally, PLGA can be used to modify surface 
properties of biological materials [102].

PLGA is generally stable in its dry state, but when exposed to aqueous environments such as the 
in vivo environment, it degrades [103]. This degradation occurs by hydrolysis of the ester linkages 
in PLGA’s backbone [104]. PLGA is first broken down into small oligomers and further into its 
monomers. Eventually, these hydrolysis products are metabolized and cleared from the body with 
minimal systemic toxicity associated [102]. The structures and the hydrolysis is schematised in

degradation [104].

1.11 Epithelial Cell Models 
A well-established method for estimating the intestinal absorption of drug in vitro is by 
determination of the permeability across cell monolayers [37]. Several epithelial cell lines are 
available, of which the Caco-2 cell line by far is the most widely used. This shows good correlation 
for drug absorption in the human jejenum [109]. The Caco-2 cell line is derived from a colon 
carcinoma, but differentiate spontaneously into a cell monolayer with properties close to those of 
absorptive enterocytes [110]. Differentiation takes a total of 21 days, after which the cells will 
have tight junctions and brush borders with microvilli on the apical side. Moreover, they express 
enzymes typical for enterocytes like lactase and aminopeptidase-N amongst others [26]. Still Caco-
2 cell monolayers have some limitations, most notable the lack of a mucus layer. A cell line known 
to produce mucus is the HT-29 cell line [111]. A drawback of the of HT-29 cells however, is a 
==PAGE:32==
 
21 
 
much lower enzymatic activity compared to that of normal intestinal levels and that they do not 
express lactase [111]. 
 
By co-culturing the two cell lines described above, expression is achieved of the two most 
abundant cell types; the enterocytes and the goblet cells. A co-cultured Caco-2/HT-29 cell line 
makes a very useful model for studying the transport across intestinal human epithelium [110]. 
Still, some of the carriers and transporters, normally expressed by human epithelial cells, are not 
expressed by either of these two cell lines. Caution should therefore be taken in translating results 
directly to actual human effects. However, several reports have found good correlations between 
these in vitro models and in vivo human models. Altogether, this model makes an easily available 
approach for attaining a decent representation of alterations in human intestinal epithelia [26]. 
Normally, in terms of physiologically relevant abundancy, the two cell types are mixed in ratios 
of 90:10 to 75:25, Caco-2 and HT-29, respectively [110]. 
 
1.12  TEER Measurements 
It is vital for the physiology of epithelia to maintain their barrier integrity [112]. Measuring 
Transepithelial electrical resistance (TEER) is a well-established method used to evaluate the 
integrity of tight junctions in cell models. TEER is measured in Ohm and calculated on the basis 
of Ohm’s law, given as the ratio of the current and the voltage [112]. TEER is generated by the 
prevented passage of small electrolytes, by tight junctions. When these are opened, electrolytes 
are allowed to pass through the paracellular pathway, which results in a decrease in  TEER [22]. 
GI epithelia can be classified into categories based on their TEER values. A TEER of around 
2000 Ohm*cm2 is denoted as tight, 300-400 Ohm*cm2 as intermediate and 50-100 Ohm*cm2 as 
leaky [112]. However, a generalization in cell models could be misleading, as large variances occur 
across different laboratories and TEER measurements can be affected by factors such as 
temperature, cell passage number, composition of cell culture medium and period [112].

1.13  Ussing Chamber 
Ussing chamber systems are used to study transport across tissue and constitutes a useful method 
to assess its barrier function, as it makes a good simulation of the physiological environment [113].  
An Ussing chamber system is made of solid acrylic (Plexiglas) into two similar halves that are 
assembled and are connected via either a vertical or horizontal port creating an open two-chamber 
system (see Figure 1-12). Each chamber has its own inlet for gas (commonly a 95 % O2 / 5 % 
CO2 mixture), which is forced under low pressure through the inlet valves, enabling circulation of 
the system. A two-piece slider containing a small aperture is accommodated into a crevice between 
the two chambers, which effectively connects them. In the aperture of the slider, a piece of 
intestine can be mounted in between the two chambers [114].

1.13.1 Tissue Integrity Assessment 
When the permeation of tissue has been modulated, its integrity after ended experiment can be 
assessed [115]. Radioactive labelled compounds can be employed as markers for passage through 
epithelial cell layers. A commonly used marker used to evaluate the integrity of tissue is D-[1-
14C]-mannitol. Mannitol is a small hydrophilic molecule [116], with very low cell membrane 
permeability [117]. Radiolabelled molecules are measured by liquid scintillation counting. The 
molecules are dissolved in a suitable solvent with a scintillator, an organic compound able to 
absorb radiant energy. This absorption of energy results in the formation of exited atoms, rapidly 
returning to the ground state, leading to the release of photons. The number of emitted photons 
can be translated into the concentration of the radioactive labelled molecule [118].

1.14  Reversed-phase High-performance Liquid Chromatography 
Samples obtained in cell studies and Ussing chamber studies are normally analysed by liquid 
chromatography [114][119]. 
Proteins in solution absorb UV light at 280 nm. Mainly aromatic amino acids are responsible for 
the signal at this wavelength. The linear relationship between a protein’s absorbance and its 
concentration, derived by Lambert-Beers law (Equation 1) can then be used to quantify proteins 
in a solution [120]. 
Reversed-phase chromatography (RPC) is the most common method for high-performance liquid 
chromatography (HPLC). RP-HPLC is a very flexible method in which separations can be 
performed using either isocratic or gradient elution [121]. The latter, however, is often preferred 
to minimize run-time. In gradient elution, initial conditions are primarily aqueous, which favours 
a high degree of organisation of the water molecules surrounding the molecules in the mobile 
phase [122]. The separation relies on the equilibrium between the surface of the stationary phase 
and the mobile phase. Separation occurs according to hydrophobic interactions between the 
stationary phase and the analytes present in the mobile phase. The stationary phase is made of a 
hydrophilic matrix of silica beads, onto which hydrophobic carbon chains are attached, usually n-
alkyl or aromatic hydrocarbons. Initially, all analytes are bound to the stationary phase through 
multi-point attachment. To initiate elution, the amount of organic solvent (typically acetonitrile) 
in the mobile phase is gradually increased. This makes conditions increasingly hydrophobic and 
causes detachment of the sample molecules from the stationary phase [121][122]. To enhance the 
hydrophobic interactions, an ion pairing agent is typically used, most commonly trifluoroacetic 
acid (TFA) [122].

2 Hypothesis 
Loading microcontainers with a permeation enhancer and coating them with a lid of permeation 
enhancing and mucoadhesive polymers will increase intestinal absorption and prolong residence 
time in the small intestine for lysozyme and insulin.  
3 Aim 
The aim of this project is to load SU-8 microcontainers with lysozyme and C10, and subsequently 
spray coat with combinations of the polymers PLGA, PEG and chitosan. The morphology is 
assessed for each coating, and the coated microcontainers are evaluated for their impact to 
increase lysozyme transport through a Caco-2/HT-29 cell model, by evaluating the flux and TEER 
values. The coated microcontainers’ mucoadhesive properties are demonstrated with an in situ 
experimental setup, and Ussing chamber studies are further carried out as an ex vivo model for 
lysozyme absorption across porcine small intestine tissue. Additionally, the lids influence on 
lysozyme release from the microcontainers is assessed by microdissolution.  
Lysozyme is used as a model protein for insulin to provide prove of concept, and in vitro cell 
transport is further studied with insulin, with the most promising polymer coating to evaluate 
their combined effect on absorption in combination with C10.

5.2 Filling Procedure 
The chips were weighed prior to and after loading to assess the amount of lysozyme and insulin.

5.8 In Vitro Cell Transport Studies 
In vitro transport studies were carried out on T6 cell plates of both Caco-2 cell monolayers and

RP-HPLC methods described in section 5.10.

Ex vivo transport studies were performed using a modular EM-CSY-6 Ussing chamber system 
(Physiologic Instruments, USA), which includes a temperature controlled metal rack for mounting 
Ussing chambers in series (Figure 5-6 A). The Ussing chambers containing a vertical port were

==PAGE:47==

an airflow of 2 bubbles/second was applied to each chamber. A piece of porcine intestine was

remnants of muscle and the epithelium [126]. After equilibration, the buffer was removed, the

5.10 HPLC Method Development 
Samples from in vitro and ex vivo transport studies were analysed by RP-HPLC on a Prominence 
Ultra Fast Liquid Chromatography (UFLC) instrument system (Shimadzu, Japan). The column 
used was a Kinetex 5 µm XB-C18 100 Å, with proportions of 100x4.6 mm (Phenomenex, USA). 
Prior to each experiment, a stock solution of 5 mg/mL lysozyme in HBSS buffer was prepared. 
Standards of 8 different concentrations in the range of 3 µg/mL and 100 µg/mL were prepared 
from the stock solution and were run before and after each experiment. The method used for 
quantification of lysozyme was derived from the method described by A. Kafka et al. [127] but 
was further optimised. The mobile phases were acetonitrile with 0.1 v/v % TFA (Solvent A) and 
MiliQ water with 0.1 v/v % TFA (Solvent B). For lysozyme, the analysis was carried out at 30 
°C, and solvent A had a steady gradient from 29 % to 71 % over 15 minutes at a flow rate of 1.4 
mL/min. The total runtime per sample was 27 min. From the standard series of lysozyme, the 
limit of detection (LOD) and limit of quantification (LOQ) was calculated.

6.3 Ex Vivo Mucoadhesion Study 
Each coating was tested for its mucoadhesive properties and retention on porcine intestine by 
being exposed to a peristaltic flow mimicking the in vivo situation. Both single microcontainers 
and clusters of microcontainers stuck to the tissue (Figure 6-7). This behaviour was similar among 
all the different coatings to a greater or lesser extent. 
 
 
 
Figure 6-7: Photo of uncoated microcontainers on dried porcine small intestine 24 h after experiment. 
 
 
An average of 177±35 microcontainers were applied to the tissue in each experiment. For control 
samples (uncoated and not loaded), the average fraction of microcontainers remaining on the 
tissue was 13.03±2.29 %. Microcontainers coated with PLGA showed an adherence of 19.31±8.82 
%, for PLGA+PEG this number was 40.85±5.53 %, while PLGA+chitosan showed an adherence 
of 35.69±14.09 % microcontainers (Figure 6-8). 
The microcontainers coated with PLGA+PEG and PLGA+chitosan demonstrated a higher tissue 
adherence than the controls. However, this difference was only significant for PLGA+PEG. This 
corresponds well with literature [53][91], and indicates that PEG and chitosan are useful for 
increasing the retention time and adherence to the mucosae in the small intestine. The lack of 
significance for the PLGA+chitosan coating can be explained by the large SEM. This error would 
assumedly become smaller with more empirical data from additional replicates. A reason for the 
large variation in the results most likely owes to the variations across the luminal surfaces of the 
small intestine in terms of both structure and mucus thickness [19]. This is important to consider, 
as the original location in the intestine, of the used pieces of tissue was unknown.

6.4 Release Studies 
The dissolution of lysozyme (or any protein drug) from the coated microcontainers is important 
to investigate. Most of the lysozyme should be released and not become trapped inside the coated 
microcontainers. As the loaded mass of lysozyme varied across samples, the results are presented 
as the average change in % of loaded mass over time. Figure 6-9 displays the dissolution of 
lysozyme over 14 and 2-hour time periods.  
The controls (uncoated) showed an initial rapid dissolution of lysozyme followed by a much slower 
dissolution. The initial rapid dissolution was expected due to lysozymes’ high solubility in aqueous 
solutions [130]. However, the second slope was more elusive and may be explained by a quick 
dissolution of the lysozyme in the top of the microcontainers, but a slower diffusion into solution 
of the lysozyme deeper in the microcontainers. The SU-8 may also provide an adhering surface 
for lysozyme, such that the local lysozyme concentration becomes saturated within the 
microcontainers, slowing the release. The slopes of these two different dissolution rates were 15.21 
Δ%/Δt (R2 = 0.98) for the initial slope and 0.85 Δ%/Δt (R2 = 0.99) for the second slope.   
The dissolution of lysozyme from PLGA and PLGA+PEG coated microcontainers, initially 
seemed similar. However, the curve for PLGA dropped off quicker than that for PLGA+PEG. 
==PAGE:57==
 
46 
 
These observations were supported by the slopes, which for PLGA was 2.87 Δ%/Δt (R2 = 0.98), 
and for PLGA+PEG was 3.46 Δ%/Δt (R2 = 0.99). According to these results, the release for 
PLGA+PEG was faster than for PLGA, this in spite of the additional PEG layer. The chemical 
properties of PEG (see section 1.7.2) may provide an explanation to this. The amphipatic nature 
of PEG may aid in the dissolution of the PLGA by simultaneous interactions of PEG with the 
surrounding water molecules and the hydrophobic PLGA. As PEG binds to PLGA, it may 
facilitate their collective dissolution, resulting in a faster dissolution rate compared to PLGA 
alone. 
The slowest release of lysozyme was observed for the PLGA+chitosan coating. The slope of the 
dissolution was 0.76 Δ%/Δt (R2 = 0.99), which was considerably slower than for PLGA and 
PLGA+PEG. On the contrary to these coatings the PLGA+chitosan coating procided a sustained 
release, which is favourable in drug delivery. Chitosan is not readily soluble in PBS buffer (pH = 
7.4). Nonetheless, all lysozyme was released after a 10 hour period (100.67±9.10 %). The release 
may generally be slowed by the formation of a chitosan hydrogel [44].

This section presents results obtained from the three distinct cell studies (see section 5.8). Firstly, 
lysozyme studies were carried out for Caco-2 cells. More data exists for Caco-2 cell studies, which 
gives opportunity to compare our results with the literature. Secondly, Caco-2/HT-29 studies 
were performed for both lysozyme and insulin, for a closer resemblance to the in vivo situation 
due the presence of mucus. The experimental design for the insulin studies was based on results 
obtained in the lysozyme studies. 
 
6.6.1 Caco-2 Cell Studies with Lysozyme 
The initial TEER values for all the cells were at a normal level between 215-255 Ohm*cm2 [112]. 
After ended experiments, all TEER values, except for the blank, dropped to around 90-120 
Ohm*cm2, and were not significantly different from each other (Figure 6.11). 
Interestingly, the TEER value for the PLGA+chitosan-C10 coating was similar to samples 
containing C10. It was significantly lower than the end TEER of the blank indicating that 
chitosan effectively promotes an increase in the permeability. This complies with previous findings 
in the literature, stating that TEER is reduced across Caco-2 cells following treatment with 
chitosan [22]. The PLGA+PEG+C10 coating did not seem to add any effect on the permeability 
when comparing with the end TEER among the other samples. After 24 hour recovery, TEER 
values returned to normal levels for all samples, except PLGA+chitosan+C10. These samples 
were part of another cell batch, from which no cells recovered, which may explain this observation.

The average flux was calculated for each analysed sample in units of nmol/min/cm2*10-6. The flux 
for the control, PLGA+C10, and PLGA+PEG+C10 were 4.78±1.12, 5.33±1.35, and 4.93±1.63, 
respectively (Figure 6-12). These values overlap, and do not show any significant difference. The 
samples containing PLGA+chitosan-C10 had a flux of 3.05±0.17 and do not overlap with the 
control, but was calculated to be insignificantly lower. A larger sample size however, is expected 
to narrow the SEM of the controls (and the other samples), thus making the difference appear 
significant. The SEM for both chitosan samples is narrow and the flux values are assumed close 
to the actual average.  
The lower flux values for the chitosan samples are likely to be explained by the slower release of 
lysozyme (see section 6.3). It was not possible to assess the release rate in the cell studies, but 
this should ideally be investigated as it could seem that the flux highly depends on the amount 
of lysozyme being released [124]. Interestingly, the PLGA+chitosan+C10 samples showed an 
extremely significant difference from the PLGA+chitosan-C10 (among other samples), having a 
much lower flux of 0.31±0.09, this despite containing C10. The TEER droped to similar levels as 
the other samples after the experiment. Thus, an increase in permeability was promoted, but the 
transport of lysozyme was very limited.

6.6.2 Co-cultured Caco-2/HT-29 Cell Studies with Lysozyme 
The initial TEER for Caco-2/HT29 cell monolayers were for all experiments at quite consistent 
levels in the range of 345-390 Ohm*cm2, all being above 250 Ohm*cm2, which is considered 
suitable for experiments [132].  
TEER values dropped for all the samples after ended experiments, however not significantly for 
the blank (Figure 6-13). The end TEER of the control, PLGA+C10, and PLGA+PEG+C10 
samples were insignificantly different and all dropped to levels around 200 Ohm*cm2. As PLGA 
and PEG are not known for having permeation enhancing abilities, the presence of C10 is believed 
to cause these drops.  
The TEERs of the chitosan samples differed from theses samples. The TEER of PLGA+chitosan-
C10 though, was not significantly different from that of the controls, but close to the limit of 
significance (P=0.0504). Comparing with the blank, the difference becomes very significant, 
showing that chitosan promoted an increase in permeability.

(Blue) End TEER. (Green) 24 h recovery TEER. The columns represent mean ± SD in triplicates, ns = non-significant, ** =

After a recovery of 24 hours, no samples recovered to normal TEER levels and values either 
dropped or remained within the SD of the end TEER. Caco-2 cell monolayers have shown to 
recover within 4 hours of exposure to C10 [78]. Under the assumption that Caco-2/HT-29 cells 
recover within the same time frame, the recovery TEERs were expected to have returned to 
normal levels, as it was seen for the Caco-2 studies (except PLGA+chitosan+C10). In this study, 
even the blank did not recover. A reason for this could be that the timeframe exceeded the optimal 
limit for recovery. Whichever factors responsible for this observation, all cells seemed to have 
been contaminated with either a bacterial or fungal infection. The Caco-2/HT-29 cell monolayers 
contained threads or filaments after overnight ‘recovery’, indicating the presence of an infection 
(see Figure 6.14). A way to prevent this could possibly be by changing the working procedure of 
the experiment. The experiments took place in an open laboratory and not in a closed environment 
like a LAF bench with efficient ventilation. Another possibility is contamination originating from 
the incubator itself. Ideally, contamination should be confirmed by viable methods. If bacteria 
were present, they would grow exponentially when fed with the nutrient rich cell culture medium 
[133]. Thus, the bacteria may have consumed a vast amount of nutrients which were then made 
unavailable to the cells. In addition, the cells were exposed to a lot of stress during the procedure.

PLGA+PEG+C10 samples were 6.18±3.45 and 2.66±1.15, respectively. The PLGA+chitosan-

6.6.3 Summarising Discussion on Lysozyme Studies 
The lowering of TEER for PLGA+chitosan+C10 and PLGA+chitosan-C10 in both studies highly 
indicated chitosan interactions with cell monolayer. 
The flux for the PLGA+chitosan+C10 coating was observed to be higher across Caco-2/HT-29 
than Caco-2 cell monolayers (P=0.0101). A reason for this could be the presence of mucus in 
Caco-2/HT-29 cells and chitosan’s well-known ability to form hydrogels. It is speculated that the 
interaction of the chitosan and mucus could disrupt the rigid structure of the polymer network in 
chitosans’ hydrogel. This could allow for faster diffusion of lysozyme through the coating. 
Generally, it seems that the flux highly depends on the dissolution of lysozyme from the 
microcontainers, indicating kinetics of a two-compartment system [134]. Thus, the slower flux of 
lysozyme across Caco-2 cell monolayers for PLGA+chitosan+C10 could indicate that C10 retains 
lysozyme within the chitosan hydrogel. 
 
Chitosan provided permeation enhancement both with and without C10.  The effect however, 
was in Caco-2/HT-29 studies slightly more pronounced when C10 was included. Thus, a 
combination of chitosan and C10 may be favourable for absorption studies on Caco-2/HT-29 cells. 
Regarding the unsuccessful recovery of the Caco-2/HT-29 cells, the method should be optimised 
to validate the observed results and avoid contamination. It is essential that the modulation of 
tight junctions is reversible and the integrity of the cells are not permanently compromised  [135].

a delivery system for a highly relevant protein drug like insulin was the initial motivation for this 
project. Insulin is a much smaller protein compared to lysozyme and differs in solubility, stability

extended to three hours due to the slow release of lysozyme in PLGA+chitosan coated

6.7 Ex Vivo Ussing Studies 
To mimic the in vivo situation as much as possible, absorption studies were also carried out on 
porcine intestine with the Ussing chamber method. Adsorption studies on Plexiglas did not show 
any difference in concentration of lysozyme after three hours and lysozyme does not adhere to the 
chamber walls (data shown in Appendix 10.2). 
==PAGE:67==

The absorption was insufficient for chitosan coatings and a flux could not be calculated. This is 
believed to be caused by a combination of the slow release of chitosan coating, and because of the 
chitosan hydrogel, retaining the lysozyme. This hydrogel behaviour was very clear after ended 
Ussing chamber studies (Figure 6-18). 
 
 
A flux of lysozyme was however detected for all samples not coated with chitosan.  The flux was 
3.93±0.40 for the control, 7.6±1.58 for PLGA+C10 and 5.29±0.26 nmol/min/cm2*10-6 for

indifferent. Accurate quantification of the number of microcontainers applied to the tissue in each 
experiment was not possible. Thus, these flux values should be validated with additional 
experiments. However, the reproducibility is poor and the method needs optimisation to viably 
investigate the flux of proteins from coated microcontainers across tissue. 
Ideally, the experiments should be carried out with insulin and the amount of the protein should 
be accounted for. The insulin cell studies implied a faster diffusion out through the chitosan 
hydrogel and a higher flux across the Caco-2/HT-29 cell monolayer compared to lysozyme. Thus, 
applying insulin may result in the absorption of detectable amounts of drug, for the chitosan 
coatings.

7 Conclusion 
The aim of this study was to evaluate the polymers PLGA, PEG and chitosan for their respective 
functionalities implemented into a microcontainer delivery system for insulin. This hypothesis 
however, was predominantly tested with lysozyme as a model protein. 
The loading of lysozyme and insulin into the microcontainers was successful with both methods. 
Characterization of the morphology of the obtained coatings revealed that the coating of PLGA 
in combination with either PEG or chitosan resulted in a smooth lid-like appearance, with 
thicknesses of 32.5±7.96 μm and 22.49±3.78, respectively.  
Ex vivo mucoadhesion studies demonstrated the mucoadhesive properties of PEG and chitosan 
coated microcontainers. Their retention was similar and not significant different, and their 
retention was better than uncoated controls of SU-8 microcontainers. 
Dissolution studies showed a sustained lysozyme release with a slope of 0.76 Δ%/Δt from 
microcontainers coated with PLGA+chitosan. This was much slower compared to 3.46 Δ%/Δt 
for PLGA+PEG and 2.87 Δ%/Δt for PLGA. 
Caco-2 and Caco-2/HT29 cell studies demonstrated chitosans ability to promote opening of tight 
junctions. A drop in TEER was generally more pronounced for samples coated with chitosan but 
did not show the highest fluxes. This is thought owed to the slower lysozyme dissolution from the 
microcontainers. The combination of a chitosan coating and C10 demonstrated increase in the 
cellular absorption of both lysozyme and insulin. The results need validation as the recovery was 
unsuccessful.

nmol/min/cm2*10-6 for insulin, which was higher than the values from the equivalent lysozyme 
studies. However, the chitosan coatings did not show any absorption of lysozyme across porcine 
small intestine in the Ussing chamber studies. This is believed to be caused by issues with the 
experimental setup.  
Chitosan displayed its hydrogel forming behaviour, which is speculated to have a great influence 
on the results seen across different experiments. 
Conclusively, it was shown that PEG and chitosan can be applied as potential lids on 
microcontainers. Both polymers were able to induce increased mucoadhesion and chitosan 
demonstrated the ability to open tight junctions in cell monolayers and increase the absorption 
of insulin. Best results were seen with the inclusion of C10.

8 Future Perspectives 
Oral delivery of insulin may transform the current treatment of diabetes mellitus in the future as 
more research is carried out and more knowledge is accumulated. This project provides basis for 
the development of a polymer coated microcontainer drug delivery system for treatment with 
insulin and potentially other protein and peptide drugs. Generally, the methods used in this 
project need optimisation and the obtained results from in vitro cell studies and ex vivo Ussing 
chamber studies require validation. 
  Appropriate methods for accurate quantification of protein and number of microcontainers 
should be developed for future Ussing chamber studies. And hereafter the results should be 
revaluated. 
  More extensive mucoadhesion studies may be performed, in which the angle of the supporting 
slide is variated, to evaluate the how this might have an impact on the retention of coated 
microcontainers. 
  The chitosan coatings showed promising results. In the future however, modified chitosan 
coatings could be investigated. These modifications may further improve chitosan’s permeation 
enhancing abilities, but may exhibit alterations in their solubility, which consequently affects drug 
release. 
  Proteolysis (inhibition) studies should be performed to investigate the ability of PLGA to lower 
local pH and thus the activity of trypsin and chymotrypsin. Assays do exist that are designed to 
monitor proteolysis, but they will need to be adapted to the microcontainer system. A more 
extensive study of PLGA’s possibilities as a microcontainer lid, could look into the co-polymer 
ratio of PGA and PLA. This may be tailored to control PLGA’s degradation rate and the lowering 
of pH. As an alternative to PLGA, a well-studied proteolysis inhibitor like citric acid could be 
added to the microcontainers, maybe in combination with the chitosan lid, to have the effects of 
both. 
  Ultimately, in vivo animal studies should be performed, to assess the effects of the lids in a 
model with closer resemblance to the human body. This would however require studies of the 
microcontainers’ release from and stability in enteric coated capsules to provide protection from 
the stomach.   
These future research suggestions will hopefully lead to improved understanding of oral drug 
delivery of protein drugs as well as to increased bioavailability of insulin via the oral route for 
coated microcontainers utilised as a delivery system.
