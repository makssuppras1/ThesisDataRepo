Abstract 
The popularity of Antibody-Drug Conjugates (ADCs) has gained increased interest in the recent years be-
cause of their great efficacy and specificity compared to standard chemotherapeutics for cancer. Despite the 
major development of ADC, there is still room for improvements. Current ADC in clinical trials is focused 
on low drug to antibody ration (DAR) using potent drugs and less site-specific conjugation to antibodies 
leading to a heterogenous release of the cytotoxic drug. A heterogeneous release suffers from lack of control 
over the number of cytotoxic drugs attached and released. There is a current interest in designing strategies 
for production of homogenous ADCs with a high DAR allowing use of less potent cytotoxic payloads. A 
high DAR can potentially entail the risk of immunogenic reactions and rapid clearance rates due to high hy-
drophobicity and risk of aggregation. However, this can be solved by either incorporation of PEG-chains or 
modifications of peptide linkers.  
The aim of this Master Thesis is to design and develop a method for synthesis of homogeneous site-specific 
ADCs with DARs of both 4 and 8. The design is based on the already known GGFG-Dxd peptide-drug 
linker, and the main core of the branching structures is designed to contain stable amide bonds allowing the 
structures to be obtained through well-established amide couplings via the use of orthogonal protecting 
groups allowing selective deprotection and further branching of the core.

I de seneste år har der været øget interesse for Antistof-lægemiddelkonjugater (ADC’er fra engelsk ”An-
tibody-Drug Conjugates”) til bekæmpelse af kræft på grund af deres effektivitet og specificitet sammenlig-
net med standard kemoterapier. På trods af den store udvikling af ADC er der stadig plads til forbedringer. 
Nuværende ADC’er i kliniske forsøg fokuserer på en lav lægemiddel-til-antistof-ration (DAR) ved brug af 
potente lægemidler og mindre specifik konjugation til antistoffet, hvilket fører til en heterogen struktur af 
ADC’et og en heterogen frigivelse af lægemidlet. Der er således ingen kontrol over antallet af lægemidler 
som sættes på antistoffet og frigives fra antistoffet. I nyere tid er der en forhøjet interesse i at designe homo-
gene ADC’er med høj DAR, som inkorporerer mindre potente lægemidler. En høj DAR har været associeret 
med en potentiel risiko for immunogene reaktioner og hurtig elimination grundet høj hydrofobicitet og risiko 
for aggregering. En løsning på dette problem kunne være at inkorporere PEG-kæder eller modificere potenti-
elle peptidlinkere. 
Hensigten med dette projekt er at designe og udvikle en metode til at syntetisere en homogen høj-DAR anti-
stof-konjugationsplatform; mere specifikt at opnå DAR værdier på henholdsvis 4 og 8. Designet er baseret 
på den allerede kendte GGFG-Dxd peptid-drug linker, og forgreningen tager udgangspunkt i den samme ba-
sisstruktur, som opnås gennem simple amidkoblinger og anvendelsen af ortogonale beskyttelsesgrupper, 
hvilket tillader selektiv afbeskyttelse og yderligere forgrening.

1. Introduction 
Cancer is a major public health problem worldwide and treatment remains a challenge. Cancer can result 
from abnormal proliferation of cells in the body. Rather than responding appropriately to signals controlling 
healthy cell behaviour, cancerous cells grow and divide rapidly and uncontrolled, leading to invasion of  nor-
mal tissues and organs spreading through the body. There are many distinct types of cancer, which can vary 
in their behaviour and response to treatment; the most common treatment being chemotherapy.1 Chemother-
apy is a therapeutic option for cancer treatment with poor specificity towards tumour cells, which is associ-
ated with poor therapeutic response and toxic effects also on to healthy cells, leading to side-effects.2,3 
As early as the beginning of 20th century, Paul Ehrlich proposed the concept of “magic bullets” as com-
pounds that were able to directly access desired targets in the cell to cure diseases.4 These compounds are  
today referred to as Antibody-Drug Conjugates (ADCs). ADCs aim to offer patients safer and more effective 
treatment, i.e. cancer chemotherapies. Progress in synthetic biochemistry methods, including remodelling of 
mAb production, linker technology and novel payload discovery, has paved the way for a new generation of 
ADC with the potential to improve the activity and lower toxicity profiles compared to earlier generations of 
ADCs.5,6 However, there is still room for improvement and the commercial market is expanding at the speed 
of light.  Despite the fact that there are over 100 ADCs in clinical trials only 14 are approved by FDA for 
clinical use because of off-target toxicity and/or low efficacy.7 The targeted drugs both pose technical and 
developmental challenges. One key factor of an ADC is the Antibody-Drug Ratio (DAR). The majority of 
ADCs in clinical trials are “marked” heterogeneous conjugates with an average DAR of 3-4 because higher 
DAR were reported to impact the pharmacokinetic properties of the ADCs such as toxicity, reduced half-life, 
and a narrow therapeutic index. However, the most predominant ADCs on the market uses very potent drugs, 
such as Monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE) playing a role in maintaining a DAR of approximately 4. Previ-
ously ADCs have conventionally been cytotoxic drugs which either damage DNA or block mitosis by micro-
tubule inhibition. These potent drugs show strong dose-limiting toxicities which has limited their clinical de-
velopment, thus recently therapies focus on less potent drugs, including the development of topoisomerase 1 
inhibitors, Deruxtecan (Dxd), meaning the need for a higher DAR to increase the overall potency of the 
ADC.8,9 
Increasing the DAR and homogenous conjugation are crucial properties for the next generation of ADCs as 
well as the selection of the cytotoxic payload. The lack of methods to produce homogenous ADCs have led 
to development of site-specific conjugation methods to improve and control the ADC homogeneity by meth-
ods such as the GlyCLICK method.10 This thesis will focus on the development of a method allowing synthe-
sis of ADCs possessing high DARs of both 4 and 8 with homogenous conjugation to a target antibody.

2. Antibody-Drug Conjugates (ADC) 
Antibody-Drug Conjugates (ADCs) are targeted agents composed of a cytotoxic drug, attached to an anti-
body via a synthetic linker (Figure 2). ADCs combine an advantage of highly specific targeting and a strong 
cytotoxic effect to achieve accurate and efficient clearance of cancer cells. 
 
Figure 2, composition of an Antibody-Drug Conjugate (ADC) 
An ideal ADC remains stable in blood circulation until it reaches its therapeutic target and releases the cyto-
toxic drug. When developing/working with ADCs it is important that the ADC is selective towards the can-
cer cells and does not affect the healthy cells. Also, the linker must be stable during circulation, while being 
efficiently  cleaved after internalisation to the target cell. Furthermore, the antibodies used in the ADC must 
not elucidate an immune response.11,12 
The ADC can kill cancer cells through different pathways.4,13 The main mechanism can be seen in Figure 3. 
 
Figure 3, the core mechanism of how ADCs work. Created in BioRender. 
The ADC will circulate in the plasma until recognition of a specific target antigen on the surface of the can-
cer cells forming an ADC-antigen complex. The complex is internalised into the cell by endocytosis, then 
fused with the lysosome, where degradation of the complex and release of the attached cytotoxic drug oc-
curs. After release of the drug in the lysosomes, the drug can perform its cytotoxic effects, resulting in cell 
apoptosis and death via either targeting of DNA or microtubules. With cell permeable payloads, the so-called 
bystander effect may be induced, i.e. off-target on-tumour cell killing. This enhances the efficacy of the 
ADC. In addition, the antibodies can bind to effector cells. The term effector cells are applied to certain cells 
in the immune system and cover cells which are relatively short-lived activated cells that depend on the body 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
4 
 
in an immune response, i.e. B-cells and T-cells. When antibodies is bound to the effector cells it can result in 
depletion of the specifically bound target cell and induce an possible immune response.4,14 The bioconjuga-
tion, choice of payload, choice of antibody and linker properties will be covered in section 2.3-2.6. 
2.1 Target antigen and antibody 
Antigens are markers that tell the body that something is foreign and cause the immune system to produce 
antibodies against it. Antigens are recognised by surface antibodies (B-cells) or by T-cell receptors when as-
sociated with major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules. All antigens can be recognised through 
lymphocytes/antibodies, however only a small quantity can activate lymphocytes to produce the antibodies 
and induce an immune response. Ideally target antigens are mainly present on cancerous cells and not or only 
in limited quantities present on normal healthy cells. Commonly targeted tumour antigens include human ep-
idermal growth receptor 2 (HER2), guanylyl cyclase C (GCC), mesothelin, and carcinogenic antigen-related 
cells adhesion molecule 5 (CEACAM5).15,16 
The efficacy of ADCs is highly dependent on the choice of an appropriate target antigens; both with respect 
to its expression as well as its ability to endocytosis. Targeted ADCs exploit the differences in protein ex-
pression on healthy cells and cancer cell to select the most suitable target antigens. The target antigen should 
be homogenously and selectively expressed on the surface of tumour cells to avoid or limit on-target off-tu-
mour toxicity.17 
2.1.1 Immunoglobins (Igs) and Monoclonal antibodies (mAb) 
Antibody molecules are Y-shaped molecules comprised of three equal-sized parts connected by a flexible 
tether as represented in Figure 4. All antibodies are constructed identically with paired, heavy (H), and light 
(L) polypeptide chains and are termed immunoglobins. The amino terminal ends of the polypeptide chains 
has variation in amino acid composition and are referred to as the variable (V) regions to distinguish them 
from the constant (C) regions. The hinge region in between the H chains, Cγ1 and Cγ2 is held together by 
disulfide bonds. The hinge is flexible for a majority of immunoglobins which allows the distance between 
the two antibody-binding sites to vary. The immunoglobins can be divided into IgM, IgD, IgG, IgA and IgE. 
18,19 
Each (group of) immunoglobin(s) has its own role: IgMs appear on the surface of B cells and is the first to be 
secreted, IgDs appear as surface immunoglobin on mature native B cells (function unknown), IgAs are se-
creted mainly by mucosal lymphoid tissues, IgEs are involved in allergic reactions and IgGs are the most 
abundant and will be further shortly discussed below.19  
 
Figure 4, schematic representation of Immunoglobin G. Made in BioRender. 
==PAGE:17==

As the main component of immunoglobin in serum the antibodies currently used in ADCs are the immuno-
globin G (IgG) antibody which includes the subtypes IgG1, IgG2, IgG3 and IgG4. IgG1 is the most com-
monly used since it is most abundant in serum and can induce strong Fc-mediated effector functions; anti-
body-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC), Antibody dependent phagocytosis (ADCP), and com-
plement dependent cytotoxicity (CDC) by high binding affinity via the Fc receptor. The Fc-mediated effector 
functions play crucial roles in anticancer activity of antibody drugs and will shortly be touched upon in sec-
tion 2.3. The other three subtypes are not used due to rapid clearance, short half-life, aggregation, and inef-
fective target effect.4,20 The functions of the constant and variable regions include antigen binding fragment 
(Fab) which recognise the antigen, and the crystallisable fragment (Fc) which interacts with other elements 
of the immune system to promote the removal of an antigen.14 
At present, ADC drugs mainly use IgG1 molecules in humanised monoclonal antibody subtypes. Monoclo-
nal antibodies (mAb) are antibodies designed to specifically target a certain antigen. Monoclonal refers to 
“cloned” versions of just one antibody binding to one antigen only. mAb works by recognising and finding 
specific antigens on the cell surface, where they trigger the immune system to attack and kill the cancer cells 
or act on the cells.21 
2.2 The Bystander Effect 
The ADC bystander effect exploits the ability of the payload to cross cell membranes and reach neighbour-
ing cells (heterogeneous tumour cells and metastatic tumour cells) around the cancerous cells to exhibit their 
cytotoxic effect. How much an ADC mediates bystander killing will depend on the extent of internalisation 
after binding to the target antigen as well as the hydrophobicity of the cytotoxic payload. This will again de-
pend on whether the ADC include a non-cleavable or cleavable linker. Non-cleavable linkers do not produce 
efficient bystander killing since the drug release by metabolic degradation will form a cell membrane imper-
meable charged payload. In contrast, cleavable linkers will release the native drug, which are cell-membrane 
permeable, depending on the drug, and thus are not only able to kill the antigen-positive target but also the 
surrounding antigen negative cells. The bystander effect is especially advantageous for heterogeneous tu-
mours with a low target antigen expression.17,22 
Toxins can also affect the bystander effect. Toxins with low polarity can easily penetrate the cell membrane, 
after release and can therefore diffuse through the cell membrane to and act on surrounding cells. Many 
drugs incorporated into ADCs today is able to diffuse across the cell membrane when released (e.g., 
MMAE). Toxins with high polarity might not be able to penetrate the cell membrane, leading to no bystander 
effect.23  
2.3 Fc-mediated Effector Cells (ADCC, ADCP and CDC) 
The Fab segment of the ADC antibody is responsible for binding to the antigen epitope of virus infected cells 
or tumour cells while the Fc segment binds to FcR on the surface of killer cells (NK cells, macrophages, etc.) 
of the innate immune system, mediating direct killing effects.20 The interaction with Fc-receptors can lead to 
killing of virus-infected cells and recent studies have emphasised the importance of Fc-mediated antibody 
effector functions in pathogenesis.4  
Fc-mediated effector cells functions include Antibody-Dependent Cell-Mediated Cytotoxicity (ADCC), An-
tibody-Dependent Cellular Phagocytosis (ADCP), and Complement-Dependent Cytotoxicity (CDC), a depic-
tion can be seen in Figure 5. ADCC is induced by the mAb Fc-domain which binds to viral proteins on the 
surface of the infected cells. The interaction leads to release of cytotoxic granules, resulting in killing of the 
infected cells. ADCP refers to the uptake of virus-antibody complexes by phagocytic cells, resulting in clear-
ance of immune complexes and trafficking of the virus-antibody complexes to lysosomes for degradation.

tion either directly by CDC or indirectly through phagocytic clearance of inflammatory response.24

2.4 Linkers in ADC 
The linker possesses a key role in an ADC because its characteristics has a substantially impact on the thera-
peutic index efficacy and pharmacokinetics of the attached drugs. The therapeutic index describes the rela-
tionship between the therapeutic and toxic dose of a given drug calculated using the ED50 and TD50. The rela-
tion between the dose required to produce a specific effect in 50% of the population (ED50) and the dose re-
quired to produce a toxic effect in 50% of the population (TD50) will give the therapeutic index.25 The linker 
possesses a key role in an ADC because its characteristics has a substantially impact on the therapeutic index 
efficacy and pharmacokinetics of the attached drugs. The therapeutic index describes the relationship be-
tween the therapeutic and toxic dose of a given drug calculated using the ED50 and TD50. The relation be-
tween the dose required to produce a toxic effect in 50% of the population (ED50) and the dose required to 
produce a toxic effect in 50% of the population (TD50) will give the therapeutic index.26  
The linker is the “bridge” between the antibody and drug. In addition, stability of linkers in the blood circula-
tion is important to ensure that the drug is not released before the ADC has reached its target, which would 
result in minimum off-target effects. Linkers can be divided into two main types of linkers, non-cleavable 
and cleavable.27  
Non-cleavable linkers do not rely on cleavage by a defined mechanism and the cytotoxic payload is only re-
leased upon endocytosis and lysosomal processing most often with a small part of the antibody still attached 
to the payload. However, attachment of a small amino acid appendage will introduce a charge in the payload, 
which prevents the released payload to participate in bystander killing, limiting the potency. In contrast, 
cleavable linkers are cleaved in response to specific chemical of enzymatic signals making them less stable 
and more prone to off-target toxicity, but more versatile since they release the native drug, and such allow a 
greater number of payloads to be used.28 Currently, a number of cleavable linkers have been introduced, 
which are cleaved in response acidic conditions in the lysosome, reduction of linker in cytoplasm or cleavage 
by specific proteases.17 In this thesis, the focus will be on cleavable linkers.

2.4.1 Cleavable Linkers 
Cleavable linkers can be defined as a chemical group which can be cleaved in a controlled manner to release 
the cytotoxic drug. Cleavable linkers can be divided into two subclasses, chemically labile linkers, and en-
zyme-cleavable linkers, which will be touched briefly upon in the following sections. 
2.4.1.1 Chemically Labile Linkers 
Chemical labile linkers include acid-cleavable linkers and reducible linkers. These undergo fracture either 
due to the, increase acidity in the endosomal-lysosomal pathway or because of the high concentration of glu-
tathione (GSH) inside the cells.29 
Acid-cleavable linkers consisting of a hydrazone moeity are designed to utilise the acidic environment of en-
dosomes (pH 5.5-6.2) and lysosomes (pH 4.5-5.0), while maintaining stability in circulation under physio-
logical conditions (pH 7.4). The linkers are designed to undergo acidic hydrolysis and release of the cyto-
toxic drug at pH 5, but unfortunately also slow release at pH 7.4 is seen. This leads pre-release of the drug in 
the bloodstream, causing off-target toxicity.29–31  
 
Figure 6, mechanism of hydrazone hydrolysis of Mylotarg. Hydrazone moeity is highlighted in red. 
The first FDA-approved ADC using an acid-cleavable acyl hydrazone linker was Pfizer’s gemtuzumab ozo-
gamicin (Mylotarg®), however, it was withdrawn due to poor stability, which caused preliminary release.12,32 
The hydrolysis of hydrazone with water in Mylotarg® can be found in Figure 6. 
Reducible linkers (also referred to as GSH-cleavable linkers) exploit the difference in the reduction potential 
in the intracellular compartment versus in plasma. Reduced GSH is present in higher concentration in tumour 
cells compared to healthy cells. Compared to the bonds in acid-cleavable linkers, disulfide bonds are stable 
at physiological pH and are easily reduced by nucleophilic attack from thiols. Consequently, disulfide bonds 
can be selectively cleaved by high intracellular concentration of GSH, releasing the active drug at the tumour 
site. Increasing the steric protection around the disulfide bond makes the linker less susceptible towards re-
duction and reduces any preliminary release of the drug before it reaches the high GSH level at the tumours 
site. Based on these considerations, methyl groups in the disulfide alfa-position have been introduced. 
Linker-drugs involving disulfide bond hydrolysis mostly contain maytansinoids and disulfide carbamate pay-
loads.12,31 A release mechanism of disulfide carbamate drug release can be seen in Figure 7.

2.4.1.2 Enzyme-Cleavable Linkers 
In the mechanism of action of an ADC, the ADC is trafficked to the lysosome, which contains a high con-
centration of hydrolytic enzymes. This has been exploited for selective cleavage of the linker within the tar-
get cell.14 
==PAGE:20==

Dipeptide-containing linkers are present in a majority of the ADCs that have reached clinical trials, including 
the approved brentuximab vedotin (Adcetris®). Dipeptide linkers are cited to be targeted by cathepsins, es-
pecially cathepsin B, a cysteine protease that is commonly implicated in tumour progression with overex-
pression and extracellular activity observed in a wide range of cancers. The activity of cathepsins enables it 
to cleave a dipeptide linker with a payload linked to the C-terminus. For dipeptide-cleavage two trends have 
been discovered.12 
1) Hydrophilic residue at P1 is required and hydrolysis rates increase with basicity. 
2) Hydrophobic residues Phe, Val, and Ala at P2 enable cleavage by cathepsin B whilst imparting 
plasma stability.12 
The two most well-known dipeptide linkers include Val-Cit or Val-Ala sequences, since they provide high 
stability in plasma and efficient release of the toxin in the lysosome. The FDA-approved Adcetris® (brentux-
imab vedotin) uses the Val-Cit linker to conjugate MonoMethyl Auristatin E (MMAE) to an antibody. Due 
to steric reasons para-aminobenzyl carbamate (PABC) unit has been introduced as a self-degrading spacer, 
which undergo spontaneous 1,6-elimination after proteolysis, releasing the payload, CO2 and azequone meth-
ylate,12,33 as seen in Figure 8. 
 
Figure 8, Adcetris® structure consisted of the dipeptide, Val-Cit linker, a PABC-spacer, and the potent drug MMAE. The drug is 
released through protease cleavage followed by spontaneous 1,6-elimination of PABC.34 
The Val-Cit-PABC linker system has proven good stability in human serum, however many studies have 
shown that the linker is susceptible to premature degradation in mouse plasma caused by unspecific prote-
ases, which hampers preclinical evaluation of ADCs.32,33,35 Other than linkers with a dipeptide motif, the ca-
thepsin-cleavable Gly-Gly-Phe-Gly (GGFG) tetrapeptide motif has been employed in Enhertu (also known 
as Trastuzumab Deruxtecan or T-Dxd) acting on HER2. The GGFG linker is conjugated to the hydroxy 
group of exatecan payload (Dxd, a topoisomerase I inhibitor) through a self-immolative aminomethylene 
linker. The GGFG linker follows the trends of the dipeptides, however it is less hydrophobic and the stability 
of the GGFG linker is enhanced at a high Drug-to-Antibody Ratio. Furthermore, other tetrapeptide motifs 
have been tested, e.g. Gly-Phe-Leu-Gly and Ala-Leu-Ala-Leu, however compared to GGFG these showed 
unfavourable limitations , including relatively slow drug release and a tendency to aggregate upon payload 
attachment.36,37 
The Drug-to-Antibody Ratio (DAR) is the average number of payloads attached to each antibody. The size 
of the DAR has several implications with respect to the stability and drug activity of a given ADC. If the 
mAb is heavily conjugated, it will have a high DAR, and the hydrophilicity, biodistribution, and pharmacoki-
netics of the ADC will be affected. This is the main reason why current ADCs have an average DAR of two 
to four to retain hydrophilicity and avoid aggregation. To overcome this hydrophilicity-enhancing linker 
technologies are under development. The GGFG linker was designed to reduce hydrophobicity and aggrega-
tion of the ADCs. The GGFG linker lends hydrophilicity to the ADC, resulting in higher stability and slower 
clearance making it a promising tool for linkage of ADCs with a high DAR.38,39

ADC linkers designed to rely on cleavage mechanisms of other lysosomal enzymes have been investigated 
including phosphatases, pyrophatases, sulfatases, β-galactosidase and β-glucuronidase.36 Since the ADC de-
sign in this thesis rely on cathepsin cleavable linkers, the other possible lysosomal enzymes will not be dis-

2.5 Cytotoxic Payload 
The cytotoxic payloads are important factors and affect both the efficacy and adverse reactions of the ADCs. 
An ideal payload should fulfil certain criteria, in order to be an effective treatment method. These include 
high cytotoxicity, low immunogenicity, high stability of storage during preparation and circulation, and wa-
ter solubility. The intrinsic potency of the payload must be high since accumulation of the payload in target 
cells is limited due to permeability of mAbs into tumour tissue. If the ADCs, which only carry limited cyto-
toxic payloads, need to have a therapeutic effect, the toxic payload needs to be able to kill the tumour cells 
with the limited payload delivery.6,40 
The characteristics of the payload include a relatively small molecular weight to reduce an immunogenic re-
action. In addition, it should have an appropriate water solubility to facilitate conjugation with the antibody 
and ensure efficient solubility of the conjugate under physiological conditions. A hydrophobic cytotoxic pay-
load can cause aggregation, while greater hydrophilicity increases membrane permeability and favours the 
bystander effect, which was discussed in section 2.1. The payload should show high stability, since mAb 
have long half-lives in circulation and the ADCs should remain stable to avoid release or decomposition. 
Lastly, the payload should contain a functional group suitable for coupling to an antibody through linkers.6,40

Payloads used for ADCs include two broad classes of nearly all natural-product based agents: microtubule-
disrupting and DNA-damaging agents. Microtubule-disrupting drugs are represented by auristatin analogues, 
maytansinoids, and tubulysins, which all inherently kill rapid dividing cells by inducing mitotic arrest. These 
types of agents limit off-target toxicities in non-dividing cells  i.e. normal/normal cells but also allow tumour 
cells to escape from treatment effects. Cancerous cells can adapt mechanisms to evade detection and destruc-
tion by the host’s immune system, which allow the cells to survive and replicate in the microenvironment of 
the host.14,41 On the contrary DNA-damaging agents are represented by calicheamicins, duocarmycins, camp-
tothecin, and pyrrolo-benzodiazepines (PBDs) which bind to the minor groove of DNA and exert their mode 
of action. A representation of the payloads can be seen in Figure 9. In the ten approved ADC drugs, six dif-
ferent small molecule payloads are used. Three ADCs deliver MMAE, two deliver calicheamicin, whereas 
MMAF, DM1 and the two CPT derivatives SN-38 and Dxd.6,40,42 
Microtubules are an important part of the cytoskeleton in eukaryotic cells, and are dynamic polar polymers 
composed of alpha, beta tubulin heterodimers. Microtubules maintain various cellular functions including 
cell morphology, signal transduction, organelle transportation, cell motility, cell division, and mitosis, mak-
ing it an important target for tumour therapy. Tubulin inhibitor drugs, such as MMAE and DM1, show strong 
toxicity against rapid dividing tumour cells, making them popular ADC payloads. However, most of the 
ADCs containing microtubule inhibitors that have been under investigation failed their clinical development 
due to dose-limiting toxicities (DLTs).40,42 
Compared to tubulin inhibitors, DNA inhibitors can act on the whole cell cycle by destroying DNA through 
double-strand breakage, alkylation, chimerism, crosslinking etc. causing cytotoxic effect and have also 
shown a good therapeutic effect on solid tumours. DNA inhibitors have fewer targets than tubulin inhibitors 
but can also target tumour cells with low antigen expression, increasing the interest in using these in many 
next-generation ADCs. 
The latest development of ADCs focuses on using less cytotoxic drugs, with lower IC50 values. Among 
these, the most promising class of payload is represented by camptothecin, which is a class of anticancer 
agents targeting topoisomerase I (TOP1).39,43 TOP1 is an important ribozyme for genomic stability and DNA 
structure preservation, and it is associated with innate and adaptive immune responses, suggesting that it may 
contribute to antitumour immunotherapy. The natural pentacyclic product camptothecin (CPT) induces dou-
ble-stranded DNA breakage in cells by formation of a stable TOP1 and DNA complex, leading to apoptosis. 
CPT itself shows low solubility and under physiological condition the lactone ring will reach equilibrium 
with the ring-opened form caused by hydrolysis. This so-called open-loop form is not active and therefore, 
this results in a decrease of the antitumour activity preventing its use in cancer treatment. Several modifica-
tions of CPT have been made including SN-38 and Dxd which are presented in Figure 10. SN-38 shows the 
desired potency, but due to its hydrophobic nature and limited number of conjugation sites, it is challenging 
to use. Dxd is a novel, highly membrane-permeable TOP1 inhibitor which can overcome multidrug re-
sistance and shows stronger efficacy for various tumour transplant models.39,44

2.6 ADC Bioconjugation  
Commonly, conjugation of cytotoxic payloads to antibodies involves reactions with exposed amino acid resi-
dues, including the epsilon-amino group of lysine residues, or reduction of disulfide bonds to expose free in-
terchain thiol groups of cysteines on a therapeutic IgG antibody. Additional recent approaches introduce site-
specific conjugation handles to the mAb via glycan conjugation (GlyCLICK®), cysteine engineering, incor-
poration of unnatural amino acids for short peptide tags.49 This thesis will briefly touch upon some of the 
conjugation methods mentioned above. 
Lysine conjugation eventuates that the lysine residues on the mAb contain a nucleophilic primary epsilon- 
amine which  reacts with the linker via N-hydroxysuccinimide esters. However, the IgG scaffold contains 
over 80 lysine residues of which most are exposed on the surface. Furthermore, among the surface lysine res-
idues, more than 20 have been shown to be highly solvent-accessible, thus, all of the surface lysine residues 
may serve as potential ADC conjugation sites leading to an uncontrolled heterogeneous mixture.49,50 
Cysteine thiol conjugation is slightly better and reactive thiol residues will be exposed upon the reduction of 
four possible interchain disulfide bonds. Upon reduction under specific conditions two, four, six or eight  
sulfhydryl groups are exposed. The nucleophilic thiols can link the antibody and the linker via reaction of the 
antibody thiol groups by electrophilic maleimides on the linker by forming a thiosuccinimide linkage. How-
ever, the thiosuccinimide linkages are unstable in vivo because they can undergo two competing reactions in 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
12 
 
plasma: elimination of maleimide through retro-Michael reaction resulting in loss of drug-linker, and hydrol-
ysis of the thiosuccinimide ring resulting in a derivative resistant to elimination. The instability of maleimide 
might result in drug loss during prolonged circulation in the blood stream leading to systemic toxicity.51

The heterogeneity of both cysteine and lysine makes it difficult to predict the efficacy, stability, pharmacoki-
netics, and therapeutic window of the ADCs. To enhance control and attain a homogeneous ADC population, 
a new conjugation technic has been a topic of much interest in current research. The new technique take ad-
vantage of the two glycan chains presented on IgG 53 and will be discussed in the following section. The 
three mentioned conjugation techniques can be seen in Figure 12. 
2.6.1 A New Generation of Antibody Conjugates – GlyCLICK® 
Glycan-mediated conjugation (the method describe here is referred to as GlyCLICK®) is a site-specific con-
jugation for labelling of native IgG. Almost all IgG-type antibodies have only one glycan conserved site in 
the Fc-region. GlyCLICK® refers to chemo-enzymatic installation of the linker-payload moeity onto the con-
served N-glycosylation sites of the antibodies. The installation is obtained by glycan remodelling and attach-
ment of the linker-payload moiety.54 A schematic presentation of the conjugation process is shown in the 
Figure 13.

GlyCLICK® is a technology marketed by Genovis AB, where the first step involved deglycosylation. Fc gly-
cans are hydrolysed to the innermost GlcNAc moiety on the IgG by an immobilised endoglycosidase en-

relatively large DARs (greater than 2) can have improved potency, caused by the increased delivery of pay-

2.6.2 Drug-to-Antibody Ratio and Conjugation Site 
Given the limited number of ADCs that each target cell can internalise, it is desirable to increase the DAR to 
further increase the potency. However, the cytotoxic payloads used to achieve the desired potency tend to be 
large, lipophilic species, so an increase of the DAR can result in protein aggregation and ADC clearance. 
Hence a balance needs to be set to obtain the desired increase in the activity without eradicating the pharma-
cokinetic properties of the antibody.49

3.3.1 
Peptide Linker through Repetitive Solution-Phase Peptide Synthesis (RRSPS) 
To synthesise the wanted GGFG tetrapeptide linker the Repetitive Solution-Phase Peptide Synthesis 
(RRSPS) was used. RRSPS is a methodology which aims to produce peptides via a repetitive procedure 
without the need for purification of intermediates, allowing synthesis of peptides in gram-scale. For each 
coupling, super-stoichiometric amounts of the activated amino acids were used to drive the reactions to com-
pletion, and water-soluble nucleophilic reagents are added to neutralise the excess of the activated species, 
followed by removal of byproducts by acidic and basic aqueous extractions. The N-terminal protecting 
group, Boc, is subsequently cleaved and the coupling cycles are repeated until the desired peptide sequence 
is obtained. The described methods can be seen in Scheme 3-4 and followed the procedure described by 
Meneses et al.58 
 
Scheme 3, Repetitive Solution-Phase Peptide synthesis (RRSPS) based on the method described by Meneses et al.58 
To avoid purification steps throughout the peptide synthesis Boc-protected Pentafluorophenyl esters were 
used as the activated amino acid components, because they are hydrophobic, easily accessible, stable to stor-
age and known to rapidly undergo aminolyses with amino acids to yield epimerisation-free peptides. The 
peptide products would be hydrophilic enough to be poorly soluble in non-polar organic solvents, like petro-
leum ether, diethyl ether or pentane. Hence excess of the activated esters can be removed by simple precipi-
tation of the peptide product.

Self-immolative spacers by copper-catalysed decarboxylative acetoxylation 
Incorporation of self-immolative spacers covalently attached to the drug, will release the drug in its native 
form upon enzymatic cleavage. The GGFG-AM linker incorporating the self-immolative AM spacer, which 
upon lysosomal cleavage initiates an electronic cascade leading to a spontaneous and irreversible release of 
the attached drug. To install the AM spacer, the C-terminal glycine residue in the synthesised peptide 3c is 
transformed into an acetoxy group before incorporation of the drug. 
 
Scheme 8, reaction overview of oxidative decarboxylation of peptide sequence 3c 
Incorporation of the acetoxy is done by a variation of the Kochi reaction, which involves the use of excess 
amounts of Pb(OAc)4 as an oxidant and CuII as a catalyst.61,62 Decarboxylation of aliphatic acids follows the 
reaction: 
PbIV(OAc)4 + RCO2H →PbII(OAc)2 + ROAc + HOAc + CO2 
Acids can be decarboxylated by PbIV(OAc)4 via a free-radical chain mechanism with Pb(III) implicated as 
an intermediate: 
Initiation:                                   PbIV(OAc)4 + RCOOH →PbIII(OAc)3 + CO2(g) + R •  
Propagation starts with the formation of the PbIII carboxylate followed by decarboxylation in presence of 
acetic acid, which enhances the rate of decarboxylation. Kochi et al.62 rationalised that presence of acid re-
leases a carboxylate ligand as a carboxylic acid thus forming a coordinative unsaturated metal carboxylate. 
The decarboxylation to give the alkyl radical can proceed directly or via the acyloxy radical. It is proposed 
that under thermal reaction conditions the alkyl radical is formed directly, whereas under the photochemical 
reaction conditions, the alkyl radical is formed via the acyloxy radical. In the reaction conditions applied to 
obtain 3d the reaction is heated to 60 °C, thus expecting direct formation of the alkyl radical. The following 
oxidation of the alkyl radical to the alkyl carbocations is slow with PbIV; therefore, CuII is applied to in-
crease the oxidation rate. The CuII is rapidly re-oxidised by PbIV,which regenerates the metastable PbIII and 
sustains the chain62–64: 
CuI + PbIV →PbIII + CuII

Scheme 29, Mechanism of phthalimide deprotection with hydrazine 
Hydrazine is used as a nucleophile to open the ring through a nucleophilic acyl substitution. The protonated 
hydrazine is deprotonated by an intramolecular N-N proton transfer. The unreacted NH2 of hydrazine then 
attacks the other carbonyl through another nucleophilic acyl substitution to “kick-off” the amine. The pri-
mary amine is then formed by protonation and the cyclic by-product phthalhydrazide formed.

4. Conclusion 
The aim of this thesis was to design an easy synthetic pathway for the two target structures for either DAR4 
or DAR8 homogeneous site-specific bioconjugation, as illustrated in Figure 1. The synthetic pathway has 
been revised, optimised and the structures have been synthetised in relatively few steps with overall good 
yields. Through RRSPS the peptide linker, GGFGG, was synthesised in gram scale, and attachment of a vari-
ety of model drugs and Dxd was tried out. Unfortunately, the final step for drug attachment onto the GGFG-
AM linker proved to be difficult and the final attachment to obtain the final structures was not reached in the 
limited timeframe of the project. Therefore, the linkers have not been tested in enzymatic studies. The next 
step will be to synthesise the peptide-drug linker structure and attach it to the core branching structures. 
The linker was designed and inspired from the novel Enhertu® ADC incorporating the GGFG-Dxd peptide-
drug linker, which are designed to be cleaved by lysosomal enzymes for the release of Dxd drug. The synthe-
sised branching will allow the possibility of attachment of 2 or 4 peptide-drug linkers to obtain DAR4 and 
DAR8 through site-specific homogeneous bioconjugation, which is preferable in drug development. There-
fore, the synthesised ADC branched linker structures would be a good candidate for development of a high 
DAR homogenous ADC for treatment of cancer.

6.2 General Procedures for RRSPS 
For synthesis of the peptide sequence by RRSPS a general procedure was used.58 
Procedure 1: Synthesis of Pentafluorophenyl Esters 
PfpOH (1 equiv.) was added to a stirred solution of Boc-AA1-OH or Boc-AA2-AA1-OH  in DCM (0.2M). 
Followed by addition of EDC∙HCl (1.2 equiv.). To the reaction solution silica (10 times the mass of 
EDC∙HCl used was added), and the suspension was filtered through a bed of celite and silica. The solvent 
was removed, and the crude directly used in the next step. 
Procedure 2: Synthesis of Boc-AA2-AA1-OH 
Boc-AA2-OPfp (1 equiv.) in THF (0.2 M) was added to an aqueous solution (0.1 M) of AA1 (2 equiv.) with 
constant stirring. DIPEA (2 equiv.) was added, and the reaction was stirred until completion was reached 
(monitored by TLC). THF was removed, and the aqueous phase was acidified to pH = 2-3 using 10% citric 
acid solution, followed by extraction with EtOAc. The organic phases were dried over MgSO4, filtered, and 
concentrated. The crude was subjected to USE with pentane or diethyl ether to give desired peptide.  
Procedure 3: Boc Deprotection 
Boc-peptide (1.2 mmol) was added to an ice-cold solution of 4 M HCl in dioxane (10 mL). The mixture was 
allowed to warm to room temperature and stirred until completion. The solvent was removed and the HCl 
salt was washed with diethyl ether (x3). The deblocked peptide was used in the coupling step without further 
purification. 
Procedure 4: Coupling Protocol 
To a solution (0.1 M) of the Boc-protected amino acid or peptide Pentafluorophenyl ester (1.1 equiv.) in 
THF was added to an aqueous solution/suspension (0.2 M) of deblocked peptide (1 equiv.) containing DI-
PEA (2.1 equiv.), pH of the aqueous solution = 9, giving a homogenous mixture. The coupling was moni-
tored by TLC, and when reached completion (approx. 3-4 h), the THF was removed and the aqueous solution 
was acidified to pH = 2-3 using 10% citric acid solution, followed by extraction with EtOAc, leading to pre-
cipitation of the Boc-protected peptide. The organic phases were dried over MgSO4, filtered, and concen-
trated. The crude was subjected to USE with pentane or diethyl ether to give desired peptide.
