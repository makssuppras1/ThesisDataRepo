Abstract
Syngas biomethanation has emerged as one of the most promising alternatives to fossil fuels,
combining the advantages of thermochemical and biochemical processes, with the value of
biomethane as a fuel. The literature review reveals the advanced performance of trickle-bed
reactors, as means to overcome the low gas-liquid mass transfer and biomass cells concen-
tration. Moreover, thermophilic conditions have been shown to enhance biomethanation.
In this context, this study focuses on the evaluation of a 180mL laboratory scale trickle-
bed bioreactor operating at 60°C, applying a mixed microbial consortium. The bioreactor
was supplied with syngas consisting of 45% H2, 25% CO2, 20% CO, 10% N2 and tested at
varying inﬂow rate (Fin,syn) and hydraulic retention time (HRT) conditions. Moreover, the
parameters affecting the process and the fermentative behavior of the culture were investi-
gated in more depth in batch reactors.
The bioreactor achieved high conversion of H2 and CO, along with high CH4 productiv-
ity, up to 3.54 mmol L−1 h−1; the highest reported for the particular syngas composition. At
the selected optimal operating conditions (Fin,syn=1.6mL/min and HRT=5d), the conversion
was over 95% for both CO and H2 and the CH4 productivity stood at 2.80 mmol L−1 h−1.
The CH4 content in the produced gas reached up to 24.9%, which is a promising starting
point for its future integration to the natural gas grid or its use as a transportation fuel. Hy-
drogenotrophic methanation was the main metabolic pathway followed by the culture, while
acetic acid was the main by-product. Adjusting the HRT was proved to successfully control
the two sensitive parameters of pH and acetic acid accumulation.
Additionally, increasing the buffering capacity of the growth medium did not affect
biomethanation. Experiments under different pH and/or acetic acid concentration revealed
the ability of the culture to maintain its performance, unless both low pH and high acetic
acid concentration were applied. No aceticlastic methanation was exhibited by the culture.
Finally, growth experiments under either only CO, CO/H2 or CO2/H2 mixtures conﬁrmed
the high hydrogenotrophic methanogenic potential of the culture, and showed that the con-
sumption of CO is the main source of VFAs production.
This study proves the effectiveness of syngas biomethanation in trickle-bed bioreactors,
succesfully providing a reliable set of data and increased knowledge to enhance the future
up-scaling and improvement of the process.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The scientiﬁc ﬁeld of renewable energy has attracted great interest the last few decades, due
to the attempt to deal with two major challenges of the modern world; energy security, as
energy has pivotal role in the quality of people’s life, and environmental pollution. Nowa-
days, mankind is dependent on fossil fuels at a range of around 80%. Consequently, at this
high rate of industrial and economic growth, oil, natural gas and coal reserves are estimated
to last for another century at most. [1]
The tremendous increase in population and industrialization has led to a 17-fold increase
in the energy consumption worldwide during only the last century. As a consequence, the oil
prices are increasing and there is an established oligopoly that makes countries dependent
on other countries’ oil reserves [2]. Environmentally, that has led to an enormous increase
in CO2, NOx and SO2 emissions. As in 2009, 37% of the worlds CO2 emissions were directly
generated from oil combustion [1].
Biofuels are a promising alternative to fossil fuels, exhibiting at the same time several
advantages in terms of sustainability, reduction of greenhouse gases (GHG), regional devel-
opment, social structure and agriculture and security of supply [1].
The term "Biofuel" refers to a liquid or gaseous fuel that is predominantly produced from
biomass. The main features of biomass, that make it a special alternative to fossil resources,
are that it is renewable, it has no net emissions of CO2, it has a low sulphuric content and it
will always be much more economic, considering that fossil fuel prices will increase in the
near future. For these reasons, biofuels provide a sustainable alternative, and their use is
more and more promoted in developed countries [1]. In the 21st century, the production of
biofuels has bloomed. Indicatively, until 2009, there has been 4-fold and 18-fold increase in
the production of bioethanol and biodiesel, respectively, than in 2000 [3].
Legislation and policies that were established in many regions of the world were crucial
for this positive development, mainly tax exceptions and subsidies. In the European Union
(EU), a minimum binding target of 10% for biofuels in transport by 2020 has been estab-
lished (Directive 2009/28/EC on renewable energy). Moreover a minimum 50% reduction
in GHG emissions has to be achieved by biofuels in a lifecycle perspective. The EU Directive
on Renewable Energy of 2009 introduced a target of 20% share of renewables in energy con-
sumption by 2020. In several countries tax reductions or exemptions have been implemented,
in order to support production or consumption of renewable energy. Directive 2003/96/EC

Biofuels can be categorized into three types, based on the feedstock; namely Ist, IInd and
IIIrd generation biofuels [2]. Currently, biofuels are industrially produced mainly by edible
sugar, starch and oil-based feedstocks, such as corn, cassava, sugar cane, soybean, palm fruits
and rape seed. These are referred to as Ist generation biofuels and they have triggered a dy-
namic debate over the morality of producing fuel from food sources [4]. Biofuels have been
considered the main cause for the increase in food prices between 2003 and 2008. Moreover,
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) studies have shown that many Ist generation biofuels have a
negative contribution to the reduction of CO2 emissions, with corn-based ethanol showing
the worst outcome [3]. Eventually, non-edible feedstocks have to be promoted to increase
the societal value and sustainability of biofuels. The most applicable alternative so far is to
use lignocellulosic sources, such as agricultural residues and by-products, non-edible crops
and organic waste, for the production of the so called IInd generation biofuels, as they do
not compete with food [4]. IIIrd generation biofuels refer to those that can be produced by
algae, and are still in a premature technological stage [2].
1.1
Comparison of Bio- and Thermochemical Production of Biofuels
Biofuels can be produced via either biochemical or thermochemical processes, but also via a
combination of the two. Conventional biochemical processes consist of a pretreatment step,
to break the interactions of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin, a hydrolysis step, to obtain
the fermentable fraction of the biomass, i.e. the sugars, and a fermentation step to obtain the
desired biofuel [4].
Thermochemical processes use heat and catalysts to convert biomass into biofuels or
other products. Thermochemical processes are considered as mature technologies and they
include combustion, pyrolysis, gasiﬁcation, hydrothermal processing and hydrothermal hy-
drolysis of biomass into sugars. [5]
The biggest challenge with biochemical processes is that the pre-fermentation steps are
highly expensive, while only a small fraction of the obtained sugars is fermentable and there
is a high production of inhibitory compounds during those steps.
Those characteristics
lead to low energy yields and high downstream costs for the biochemical production of
biofuels. The answer to these challenges comes by thermochemical processes, especially
gasiﬁcation of biomass into synthesis gas (syngas) and the conversion of it to biofuels, via
catalytic processes, such as the Fischer-Tropsch (FT) or by syngas fermentation, i.e. the use
of microbial catalysts. [4]

1.2 Gasiﬁcation

1.2
Gasiﬁcation
Bain and Broer (2011) [6] deﬁne gasiﬁcation as the "thermal conversion of organic material to
combustible gases under reducing conditions with oxygen added in sub-stoichiometric amounts com-
pared with the amount needed for complete combustion to carbon dioxide and water". The gaseous
product of gasiﬁcation, called producer gas, is a mixture of CO, H2, CO2, CH4 and some
minor short-chain hydrocarbons and N2 [6].
These minor components consist the impurities of raw producer gas and include ashes,
tars, acetylene, ethylene, ethane benzene, H2S, SO2, ammonia and H2O.
Syngas is the
gaseous mixture obtained after the removal of the impurities and consists mainly of CO,
H2, CO2. The most widely used technologies for producer gas upgrade to syngas include tar
cracking, ﬁltrations, water scrabbing, cyclones and electrostatic ﬁlters, as well as chemical
and catalytic methods, like amine or cobalt and nickel treatments. [7]
According to Bain and Broer (2011) [6], the gasiﬁcation process consists of four main
stages which rapidly succeed each other in almost 1sec; Heating and Drying, Pyrolysis, Gas-
Solid Reactions (GSR) and Gas-phase Reactions (GR) (see Fig. 1.1 [6]).
The heating and drying stage aims to decrease the moisture content of the biomass, in
order to reduce the energy needed for the gasiﬁcation. Pyrolysis refers to the rapid thermal
decomposition of the feedstock in the absence of oxygen. The temperature of pyrolysis can
reach up to 400-500°C. This stage results in the production of volatile compounds (water,
CO, CO2, H2 and volatile tar) and solid char. Based on the feedstock and the gasiﬁer type,
this stage can convert up to 80% of the feedstock into gaseous products. [6])

1.4
Advantages of biochemical against catalytic syngas upgrade
Focusing on the advantage of gasiﬁcation that syngas can be further processed to a wide
spectrum of valuable products, it should be noted that this is widely applied through Fischer-
Tropsch (FT) processes. The FT process is a catalytic group of reactions that convert a mixture
of CO and H2 into a mixture of gaseous and liquid products, such as diesel and gasoline,
using metal catalysts, such as iron, cobalt, ruthenium and nickel. [11]
Apart from its undeniable advantages, the FT process has certain drawbacks that have
driven the research towards other alternatives. According to Liew et al (2016) [10] the high
costs of the FT process is one of the main disadvantages. The process takes place in high tem-
peratures and pressure (150-350°C, 30 bar), coupled with expensive metallic catalysts, which
can be easily contaminated by potential syngas impurities. Thus, the biochemical conver-
sion of syngas into low-carbon molecules, through syngas fermentation offers a number of
advantages [10]. Daniel et al. (2012) [12] summarize the superiority of syngas fermentation
into the limitations of the FT process in terms of robustness, ﬂexibility and selectivity of the
metal catalysts; facts that generate an economical disadvantage for the FT process. They also
emphasize that during the FT process around 45% of the substrate results in production of
by-products, making more difﬁcult the downstream separation of the products. Indicatively,
comparing the production of ethanol from biomass, Grifﬁn et al. [13] refer to the three com-
panies that currently use gas fermentation for the production of fuel or chemicals, namely
Coskata, IneosBio, and LanzaTech. They reveal that the process of LanzaTech achieves an
overall energy efﬁciency of 57% compared to 45% of the reference thermochemical process.
Moreover, the carbon conversion to fuel is 51.6% for gas fermentation against only 40.7% for
the thermochemical process, leading to a higher ethanol yield, standing at 117gal/dry US
ton for the former against 83.8gal/dry US ton for the latter. On top of that, they suggest that
the bioprocess generates lower CO2 emissions than the thermochemical; 24.8 against 30.2 lb
CO2/gal. [13].
One of the major advantages is that syngas fermentation occurs at milder temperature
range (37-80 °C) and ambient pressure, reducing the energy cost compared to the FT process.
Moreover, unlike the FT process, it does not strictly require a ﬁxed H2:CO of 2:1, being more
ﬂexible in terms of syngas composition. Biochemical conversions are also more selective
in a speciﬁc desired product than the FT process which results into a spectrum of products
which need further puriﬁcation. Finally, and most importantly, the microorganisms involved
in syngas fermentation can be more tolerant to syngas impurities, reducing the need for pu-
riﬁcation of the syngas, compared to the easily inhibited catalysts of the FT process. [10]

Syngas Fermentation
Phillips et al. (2017) [15] deﬁne syngas fermentation as "a hybrid thermochemical/biochemical

fermentation process to deliver ethanol and potentially other chemicals". According to Munasinghe

bioethanol. Other targeted products include acetone, butanol, 2,3-butanediol and biopoly-

In their extensive review on commercial biomass-derived-syngas fermentation, Daniell
et al. (2012) [12] describe the reductive acetyl-CoA pathway, which was ﬁrst proposed by
Wood and Ljungdahl in 1966; thus also known as the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway for aceto-
genic microorganisms, which is illustrated in Fig. 1.2 [12]. They claim that there is a number
of researchers that have thoroughly investigated this pathway and it is believed to be one of
the ﬁrst metabolic pathways met on Earth, as it was followed by microbes to preserve life

even 3.8 billion years ago, long before enough O2 existed in the atmosphere of the planet.
Those microorganisms utilize CO and H2 as energy sources and CO2 as an electron acceptor.

The Wood-Ljundahl pathway is divided into two branches; the methyl and the carbonyl
branch, also referred to as the "eastern" and "western" branch respectively; terms introduced
by Ragsdale (1997) [17] in his review over the research that led to the elucidation of the
metabolic pathway. He suggests that while all organisms utilize the Eastern branch for the
metabolism of one-carbon molecules, the Western branch is distinctive for the anaerobic
acetogenic and methanogenic microorganisms that consume CO2 or CO through the Wood-
Ljungdahl pathway. [17]
Daniell et al. (2012) [12] provide a comprehensive description of the steps of the Wood-
Ljungdahl pathway, as a collective of the previous research of various scientists. Following
Fig. 1.2, they describe the two ways that CO enters the metabolic pathway. CO can either be
introduced directly to the Western branch, while at the same time a monofunctional carbon
monoxide dehydrogenase (CODH) oxidizes another molecule of CO to CO2, through the
water-gas shift reaction, forming also a reduced ferredoxin. A part of the formed CO2 enters
the Eastern branch, depending on whether CO is the only carbon and energy source, or there
is another energy source (e.g. H2) to serve in a hydrogenace reaction. Thermodynamically,
electrons are more likely to be produced from CO than H2, while hydorgenases can exhibit
reversible inhibition from CO. Thus, when the concentration of CO is high, H2 consumption
is ceased until a part of the CO is consumed and its concentration is below inhibitory levels.
[12]
The CO2 entering the Eastern (or methyl) branch of the metabolic pathway is reduced
to formate by a formate dehydrogenase (FDH). With the act of a 10-formyl-THF synthetase
(FTS), formate is attached to tetrahydrofolate (THF) and the resulting compound is reduced
gradually by several enzymes (MTC, MTD, MTRS) until the methyl group of the reduced
methyl-THF complex is transferred by a Methyltransferase (MTR) to a corrinoid-FeS protein
(Co-FeS-P), which eventually carries the methyl group to the Acetyl-CoA pathway. [12]
In the Western branch, CO results in formation of Acetyl-CoA, irrespectively of whether
it is used directly or formed by CO2. The most important enzyme of the metabolic pathway
is the bifunctional metaloenzyme CO dehydrogenase (CODH)/Acetyl-CoA synthase (ACS)
which can both reduce CO2 to CO and accept the methyl group carried by the Co-FeS-P. The
enzyme then condenses the carbonyl and the methyl units with a CoA group producing a
molecule of Acetyl-CoA. [12]

Scope of the current project
Coupling thermochemical and biochemical processes is a key factor to the future develop-

CHAPTER 2
A review on Syngas biomethanation
Methane production from one-carbon molecules was ﬁrst suggested by Sohngen in 1910
[22]. Later, Fischer et al. (1931) [23] achieved the biomethanation of carbon monoxide via
fermentation using a culture obtained from mud. They suggested that carbon dioxide and
hydrogen were produced as intermediates. The same authors suggested that if CO/H2 is
more than 1/3, then CO2 and CH4 were formed, while higher excess of H2 resulted in higher
methane concentration in the produced gas. Following their work, Stephenson et al. (1933)
[24] used a culture obtained by mud of River Ouse and achieved the conversion of CO to
methane by H2 (CO:H2=5:95) at 37°C, at a CO to CH4 molar yield of 0.86 and H2 to CH4
molar yield 0.27.
In the same work, they achieved the conversion of CO2 with H2 into
methane, at a yield of almost 1 mol CH4 per mol of CO2 utilised and 0.25 mol CH4 per mol
H2 consumed. They were the ﬁrst to suggest the following hydrogenotrophic methanation
reactions:
CO + 3H2 →CH4 + H2O
(2.1)
CO2 + 4H2 →CH4 + 2H2O
(2.2)
Some years later, Kluyver et al. (1947) [25] conﬁrmed that biomethanation of CO could
also occur by M. barkerii in the absence of H2, under the carboxydotrophic methanation
reaction:
4CO + 2H2O →CH4 + 3CO2
(2.3)
These primary studies have been the inspiration for further investigation of the biometha-
nation potential, using gaseous substrates for the microbial cultures.

The eﬀect of operating conditions on Syngas Biomethanation
Despite the great potential of syngas fermentation, there are several bottlenecks that have
to be overcome in order to achieve the establishment of syngas biomethanation at industrial
scale. The parameters that affect syngas fermentation have been extensively reported by a
number of authors and recently reviewed by Mohammadi et al. (2011) [39] and Ramachan-
driya et al. (2016) [40]. Grimalt et al. (2018) [14] have focused more on the parameters and
the drawbacks of syngas biomethanation. They all agree on the fact that syngas upgrade
into either liquid or gaseous biofuels is dependent on operational parameters such as pH,
temperature, syngas composition, and, most importantly, on the gas-liquid mass transfer
and the cells concentration in the bioreactor. Those parameters affect the microbial growth
on synthesis gas and it is even more critical, but also more difﬁcult, to optimize them when
working with MMCs, where there is no common optimal conditions for the different strains,
as stated by Grimalt et al. (2018) [14]. It is notable, though, that as reported by Rittmann

2 A review on Syngas biomethanation
et al. (2015) [41], hydrogenotrophic methanogens are very resilient to environmental shock
and are able to recover, even in case of exposure to O2.
pH One of the most inﬂuential parameters on syngas biomethanation is the pH. Grimalt
et al. (2018) [14] state that the pH is the key when attempting to regulate the metabolism of
the microorganisms. According to Liu et al. (2008) [42] hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis
is less effective at low pH values, as the homoacetogenic bacteria hold a selective advantage
to produce acetic acid. Additionally, they suggest that methanogenic microorganisms grow
optimally in a pH range of 6.8-7.5, except from Methanobrevibacter acididurans, which can
tolerate pH conditions as low as 5. Pereira et al. (2013) [43] thoroughly investigated the
pH effect on syngas biomethanation by MMCs. They surprisingly concluded that among
pH values of 5.8, 6.9 and 7.6, at ambient pressure and mesophilic temperature, the highest
syngas to CH4 molar yield was achieved at pH 5.8, corresponding to 0.89. In his review on
syngas biomethanation though, Grimalt et al. (2018) [14] observe that most processes are
operated in almost neutral pH, ranging from 7.0-7.6.
2.3.1
Temperature
Another important factor is the temperature of the process. Grimalt et al. (2018) [14] point
out that the temperature regulates the interactions among the species in a MMC, and, con-
sequently, the metabolic pathway that leads to the production of methane. According to
Guiot et al. (2011) [44] methanogenesis by mixed microbial cultures mainly occurs through
hydrogenotrophic routes, either in mesophilic or thermophilic conditions.
On the other
hand, Sipma et al. (2003) [45] suggest that under mesophilic conditions the main precur-
sor to methane is acetate, while above 45°C and especially around 55°C the CO to methane
conversion shifts to hydrogenotrophic pathways. A thorough investigation of the metabolic
pathways, through inhibition studies, by Navarro et al. (2014) [46] conﬁrmed the latter sug-
gestion. As concluded also by Grimalt et al. (2018) [14], it is in accordance with several
studies, both of syngas biomethanation and biogas upgrade, that methanation shifts from
aceticlastic, under mesophilic conditions, to hydrogenotrophic, under thermophilic temper-
ature ranges. They propose that this shift happens either because under thermophilic con-
ditions, the diversity of carboxydotrophic hydrogenogenic bacteria is higher, or because the
hydrogenogenic reactions are more exergonic in higher temperatures, favoring the respective
hydrogenotrophic methanation pathway. They also conclude that direct carboxydotrophic
methanation, i.e. direct conversion of CO to methane, is negligible when using mixed micro-
bial cultures.
The effect of temperature on the microbial interactions leads to an increase of the metrics
of the process under thermophilic conditions. Indicatively, Guiot et al. (2011) [44] concluded
that the methane productivity was almost 5.6 times higher at 60 °C than in mesophilic tem-
perature. Sipma et al. (2003) [45] had already noted that the conversion rate of CO exhibits a
0.6 to 1-fold and 2-fold increase moving from 30 to 55°C, depending on the type of the initial
sludge and the diversity of the species that exist in it.

Finally, as underlined by Grimalt et al. (2018) [14] the solubility of the syngas components
is decreasing with elevating temperature, sharpening the drawback of the gas-liquid mass
transfer limitation.
2.3.2
Syngas composition-Partial pressure of gases and impurities
Syngas composition is a factor which affects multiple aspects of the biomethanation process.
Grimalt et al. (2018) [14] emphasize on the possible inhibitory effect of CO and H2 in ele-
vated partial pressures and the effect of the impurities that might be present in the supplied
syngas.
Regarding the effect of CO, Pereira et al. (2013) [43] concluded that increasing the syn-
gas pressure from 1 to 2.5atm, under mesophilic conditions, has a negative effect on the
syngas to methane molar yield. Coupled with low pH values, they showed that the inhibi-
tion is worsened. Navarro et al. (2016) [47] performed a kinetic study of carboxydotrophic
methanogenesis under mesophilic conditions (35°C), at CO partial pressures (pCO) ranging
from 0.1 to 1.5atm. The maximum CO consumption was observed at pCO=0.5atm, while the
methane production rate achieved a maximum at pCO=0.2atm and was totally inhibited at
1atm. It should also be noted that the acclimation of the culture to pure CO for 45 days,
resulted in increasing the methane yield to almost 90% at PCO=1atm. It was proved that
this improvement was due to a shift from the aceticlastic to the hydrogenotrophic pathway,
suggesting a better adaptation of those populations to high CO concentrations.
The partial pressure of H2 is also affecting the performance of hydrogenotrophic metha-
nation, according to Lopes et al. (2015) [48]. Their experiments in increasing the H2:CO2
ratio from 1:0 to 5:1, showed a 105% increase in the methane production rate. According to
Skidmore et al. (2013) [49] this is due to the enhanced activity of hydrogenase with increas-
ing partial pressure of H2.
As already mentioned, syngas fermentation is proposed as an alternative to catalytic pro-
cesses due to the signiﬁcantly higher tolerance of the microbial catalysts to the impurities of
raw syngas. It is a fact though, according to Xu et al. (2011) [7], that some of the impurities
can affect either the microbial activity directly, or some of the process parameters. They sug-
gest that certain impurities can affect the activity of some enzymes, especially in acetogenic
bacteria, such as hydrogenase and CODH. Guiot et al. (2013) [50] examined the effect of
HCN, NH3, H2S and aromatic hydrocarbons on the overall methanogenic metabolism and
concluded that only HCN has a signiﬁcant effect in low concentrations, while the other impu-
rities affected the process only in high concentrations. Ahmed et al. (2007) [51] investigated
the effect of NO on syngas fermentation, reporting that its presence at concentration greater
than 40ppm inhibits the hydrogenase activity, as it binds to the enzyme. In their review, Xu
et al. (2011) [7] suggest that NH3 can either inhibit directly the methanogenic microorgan-

isms or affect the pH of the process and is one of the most dangerous inhibitors. In general,
Grimalt et al. (2018) [14] conclude that each microbial group can react in a different way
to the presence of impurities in the syngas and further work needs to be carried out on the
inhibitory effect of these impurities on syngas biomethanation.
2.3.3
Gas-Liquid mass transfer and cell concentration
The main restricting parameters that affect the syngas biomethanation potential are the low
gas-liquid mass transfer of the gaseous components of syngas, especially for CO, and the
cell concentration in the bioreactors, as explained by Grimalt et al. (2018) [14]. The main
strategy investigated to overcome those limitations and increase the volumetric mass transfer
coefﬁcient (kLa) of the gases is the evaluation of different reactor conﬁgurations for biometha-
nation of syngas. Those conﬁgurations include stirred-tank, trickle-bed, bubble column and
gas-lift reactors, while there have also been some promising studies on multi-oriﬁce oscilla-
tory bafﬂed reactors and reverse membrane bioreactors.
Klasson et al. (1992) [52] suggested that the most suitable reactor for syngas biometha-
nation would be one achieving high gas-liquid mass transfer and high cell density. They
compared the effectiveness of continuous stirred-tank reactors (CSTR), packed-bubble col-
umn reactors (PBCR) and a co-current trickle-bed reactor (TBR). The CSTR achieved much
higher kLa values, and, notably, a 50% increase of the agitation rate led to a 360% kLa in-
crease, showing that the agitation substantially enhanced the gas-liquid mass transfer. Lower
values were obtained for the two other conﬁgurations, 2.1h−1 for the PBC and 55.5h−1 for
the TBR. Surprisingly, they also showed that despite the higher mass transfer rate in the
CSTR, the TBR achieved higher CO conversion, probably, as they believe, due to its almost
plug-ﬂow operational behavior. To overcome the drawback of low productivity in CSTR
bioreactors, Wise et al. (1978) [53] had already introduced the concept of cell retention in
the bioreactor, through a recycle stream for the microbial cells in a continuous CSTR. At
mesophilic temperature, they achieved a 3-fold increase in cell concentration followed by
an almost 4-fold increase in productivity. In another study on CO/water gas-liquid mass
transfer, Riggs et al. (2006) [54] claim that the range of the kLa obtained (0.003-0.043s−1 for
agitation rates of 200-600rpm) is the higher obtained in the literature, taking into account the
fact that in contrast to other studies, they measure the parameter in the absence of microor-
ganisms, excluding additional resistances to mass transfer.
As referred by Nalakath et al. (2011) [55], a number of studies on syngas fermentation
conclude that the gas-liquid mass transfer depend mainly on the geometry, conﬁguration
and operating conditions of the reactor, as well as the properties of the liquid phase. They
add that there are a number of additives that have been used to efﬁciently increase the (kLa)
of CO, including surfactants, alcohols (e.g. ethanol in a concentration of about 1%), salts,
catalysts and small particles (e.g. nanoparticles). Regarding the reactor size, Kimmel et al.
(1991) [56] compared two different trickle bed reactors for syngas biomethanation, showing
==PAGE:33==

19
that using a smaller size and porosity packing material led to an enhanced liquid medium
distribution that enhanced the methane productivity and to a kLa of 53h−1. In another study
of Klasson et al. (1990) [57], they achieved a CO/water kLa equal to 780 h−1 for a trickle-bed
reactor, while only 3.5h−1 for a packed bed bubble column reactor. The latter low value
was considered to be obtained due to the mass transfer limitations and the porosity of the
packing material, which seems to be an inﬂuential factor for packed-bed columns.
Bubble columns and gas-lift reactors have been reported to achieve high gas-liquid mass
transfer rates, due to the high gas-liquid interfacial area, with the advantage of avoiding me-
chanical agitation [14]. Munasinghe et al. (2010) [58] compared the kLa values for CO/wa-
ter in eight different conﬁgurations, achieving a value of 91.1 h−1 for an air-lift bioreactor,
equipped with a 20-µm bulb diffuser for enhanced mass transfer. For gas-lift reactors, Guiot
et al. (2011) [44] suggested gas recirculation as a measure to increase the CO conversion by
67% and a 5-fold increase of methane productivity, with a recirculation:feed ratio of 18:1.
Overall, it seems that the type of reactor conﬁguration greatly affects the gas-liquid mass
transfer, with CSTR and trickle-bed bioreactors achieving the higher kLa for CO/water. The
latter type shows also a better performance in terms of methane productivity and yields,
due to the plug-ﬂow operation style, while the absence of mechanical agitation offers an
extra economical and energy consumption-related advantage, as underlined by Grimalt et
al. (2018) [14]. More research is necessary, though, on the bioﬁlm growth and the porosity
of the packing material for the development of this reactor conﬁguration type.
2.4
Recent advantages on syngas biomethanation process
The research on syngas fermentation focuses mainly on ethanol and acetate production.
However, due to the value of biomethane as a product, there is a considerable amount
of studies investigating the potential of this technology, ultimately aiming to achieve the
commercialization of syngas biomethanation. Thus, an overview of the state-of-the-art pro-
cesses that have been proposed so far would provide an insight to the progress maid on the
ﬁeld until now, reporting the maximum productivities and yields that have been achieved in
relevant studies.
2.4.1
CO2/H2 as substrate
Wise et al. (1978) [53] investigated the potential of a MMC, coming from sewage sludge and
enriched on CO2 and H2, under different pressure and temperature conditions, in a CSTR.
Implementing a cell recycling technique, in order to achieve a high cell concentration in the
reactor, they achieved a CH4 productivity of 163.58 mmol L−1 h−1 under mesophilic (37°C)
and 409 mmol L−1 h−1 under thermophilic conditions (60°C), with the feed composition
being a CO2/H2 mixture of 1:4 stoichiometric ratio. With the same inﬂow gas composition,
Nishimura et al. (1992) [59] achieved a higher methane productivity, 1280 mmol L−1 h−1

2.4.2
CO and CO/H2 as substrate
Bugante et al. (1989) [62] investigated the potential of CO biomethanation in a column reac-
tor, using crystobalite PQ-10 as a supporting material under thermophilic conditions (55°C).
They achieved a methane productivity of 120 mmol L−1 d−1 and a CH4 yield of 0.25, using
a mixed microbial culture enriched from cow pasture sludge. Guiot et al. (2011) [44] investi-
gated the biomethanation potential of an anaerobic granule sludge from a wastewater treat-

a 1:2 CO:H2 gaseous mixture into methane. They achieved a productivity of 6.8 mmol L−1
h−1 and an outﬂow gas methane contenct as high as 72%. Luo et al. (2013) [64] proposed
a novel thermophilic process (55°C) in a CSTR equipped with Hollow Fiber Membrane for

a thermophilic wastewater treatment plant. The methane productivity reached up to 3.08
mmol L−1 h−1, while the CH4 content in the gas was signiﬁcantly lower compared to a
CSTR. It should be noted that the innovation of this process was the simultaneous sludge
treatment and biomethanation of CO. A similar study by Jing et al. (2017) [65] was per-
formed under mesophilic conditions (37°C) in an Upﬂow Anaerobic Sludge Blanket Reactor
(UASB) using sludge from a cassava stillage treating plant. The CH4 productivity reached
up to 3.6 mmol L−1 h−1, accompanied, though, by a low methane content in the produced
biogas. In the last two cases, CO2 was in higher concentration in the produced gas, a fact
that could be resolved with the addition of H2 to enhance the methanation, as stated by the
authors.

Klasson et al. (1990) [57] investigated the potential of a triculture consisting of R. rubrum,

with almost 100% conversion of CO, while the respective values for the PBC reactor reached

2.5
Objectives of current study
As described in Section 1.7, this study aims to evaluate syngas biomethanation in a trickle-
bed bioreactor and to investigate the operational parameters and the fermentative behavior
of the mixed microbial culture applied.
Speciﬁcally, the most important objectives of the study include:
• Achieving Steady State operation under different syngas inﬂow rates (Fin,syn) and hy-
draulic retention time (HRT). The achieved speciﬁcations of the operation under those
conditions, i.e. substrate conversion efﬁciency, methane productivity and composition
of outﬂow gas, will provide the basis for the future evaluation of the process compared
to mesophilic operation, and the design of a pilot scale process.
• Investigation of the effect of several parameters, such as the syngas inﬂow rate (Fin,syn),
the HRT, the pH and the production of VFAs on the biomethanation process. Those
observations will provide a better understanding on the interaction between the pro-
cess parameters and their signiﬁcance to the optimization of syngas biomethanation
applying the speciﬁc MMC. Those interactions will determine the techniques that can
be applied to control the operational parameters in the future upscaled process.
• Investigation of the fermentative behavior of the applied enriched MMC. It is impor-
tant to identify the metabolic pathways, leading to the products and by-products of
the process. The advantage of syngas is that its composition can be pre-determined
according to the operational conditions of gasiﬁcation. Thus, knowing the optimum
composition for subsequent syngas biomethanation is a key to achieve the desired out-
ﬂow gas composition.
The above objectives constitute the key factors that should be taken into account in the
future development of a pilot-scale and commercial syngas biomethanation continuous pro-

components over a solid bed, which is usually the catalyst. The liquid reactants trickle over
the catalytic bed, in the form of either ﬁlm, rivulets or droplets. Optionally, if necessary the
gas can alternatively ﬂow upwards in the reactor.

Eﬀect of pH and Acetic Acid concentration on methanogenic activity
As already explained, the pH is one of the most affecting parameters in syngas biometha-
nation (see section 2.3). The change in the pH of the liquid medium in the course of the
process is affected mainly by the production of VFAs, which lower the pH and in higher
concentration can drop it outside the reported pH range in which syngas biomethanation is
feasible.
In this experimental set, it is aimed to identify if the presence of VFAs, mainly acetic
acid, affects the consumption rates of the substrates and the methane productivity only via
lowering the pH of the process or also by inhibiting some methanogenic population in the
culture. For this reason, syngas biomethanation under a speciﬁc concentration of acetic acid
(2.5g/L±0.2) and different pH values (6.50±0.1 and 7.00±0.1) has been investigated in batch
reactors.
For the conﬁguration at pH 7.00, 20mL inoculum and 80mL TMC-BA medium were in-
jected in 330mL serum vials, along with 0.250mL of 100% acetic acid solution. Syngas has
been supplied to the vial up to 1.55 bar. The pH needed no further adjustment.
For the conﬁguration at pH 6.50, 20mL inoculum and 80mL TMC-BA medium were in-
jected in 330mL serum vials, along with 0.250mL of 100% acetic acid solution. Syngas has

3.1.4.4
Aceticlastic potential of MMC
There are studies suggesting that CO conversion to methane also occurs through acetic acid
formation which is subsequently converted to methane by aceticlastic methanogenic bacteria.
The presence of such microbial population in the enriched mixed microbial culture used in
this study has been investigated. Speciﬁcally, in a 120mL vial, ﬂushed with N2, 25mL of

The experiment has been performed in duplicates. Gaseous (0.1mL) and liquid (5mL)
samples were withdrawn roughly in a weekly basis for one month.

The liquid medium circulating in the conﬁguration contains the dissolved part of the biomass
cells that is not attached in the solid bed to form bioﬁlm, as well as the liquid by-products of
the process (mainly VFAs and alcohols). In the batch reactors, the biomass and by-products
are obviously only dissolved in the liquid reactive volume. In order to assess the concentra-
tion of the microorganisms in each reactor, measure the pH and the by-products concentra-
tion in the conﬁguration, a liquid sample was used for the analysis, which varied in volume
for the batch reactors (2-5mL), and for the lab-scale bioreactor it was obtained from the daily
medium replacement described previously (see Section 3.1.3).
The concentration of the microorganisms was determined with the typical Optical Den-
sity (OD) method, using a DR2800 Spectophotometer (Hach Lange, Denmark). The method
is based on the fact that light scatters, passing through the cell suspension. As a results, the
concentration of cells is higher when the measured scattering is higher. Under the assump-
tion that the cells were at their exponential growth phase when the sample is withdrawn,
the OD was measured at 600nm (single wavelength, OD600).
For the OD600 measurement, a 0.9ml amount of the liquid sample was placed in a cuvette.
The Spectophotometer was calibrated to measure light absorbance up to 0.700 Abs. For this
reason, whenever necessary, an appropriate dilution of the sample with distilled water was

It should be noted that the consumption of CO2 is very dependent on the inﬂow rate.
CO2 is both a substrate and a -intermediate- product of the process, when CO is converted
to CO2 via the water-gas shift reaction. It should be noted, though, as also stated by Wise et
al. [53] that the determination of CO2 present in the process and a calculation of its utiliza-
tion by the microbial culture can be considered as inaccurate, because it has a high solubility
compared to H2 and CH4 and signiﬁcant quantity can be removed in the liquid efﬂuent. In
Fig. 4.1, in lower syngas inﬂow rates, up to 1.4mL/min, there is a net consumption of CO2,
along with H2 consumption of over 90%, indicating that biomethanation primarily occurs
through hydrogenothrophic methanation, directly from the consumption of CO2 in the syn-
gas. This is also veriﬁed by the fact that the H2 to methane yield during the ﬁrst Steady States
achieved is closer to the stoichiometrical yield of reaction 2.2 (0.25 mol/mol). As the syngas
inﬂow rate increases, the CO2 consumption decreases, until there is a net production of the
gas, while the H2 to methane yield also increases closer the stoichiometric yield of reaction
2.1 (0.33 mol/mol). This veriﬁes that CO2 is both a product and a substrate of the process,
a fact pointed out as well by Westman et al. [67]. As clearly noticed, the H2 to methane
yield is always between these two values (0.25 and 0.33 mol/mol), as expected, speciﬁcally
between 0.26-0.31 mol/mol. The H2 to methane yield is in most cases higher than the one
reported by Klasson et al. [57] (0.214 mol/mol).
As observed in Fig. 4.2, the electron yield to methane is not signiﬁcantly affected by the
inﬂow rate or the HRT, when the reactor operates at steady state, achieving values close
to 75-80%. The CO to methane yield also follows a similar trend, ranging mostly around
65-70%mol, compared to the stoichiometrical maximum of 1.00 mol/mol. The remaining
electrons and carbon cover the needs for energy and cell maintenance and reproduction, as
well as are utilized by the acetogenic populations in the culture. As the H2 to CH4 yield
appears to be usually higher than 0.25 mol/mol, it indicates that the primary metabolic
mechanism resulting in methane production is the water-shift gas reaction accompanied by
hydrogenotrophic methanation. This metabolic pathway is also reported to be more favor-
able for some known methanogenic archaea, as reported by Kimmel et al. [56], than the

CHAPTER 5
Conclusions and Recommendations
Coupling the advantages of thermochemical and biochemical processes for the production of
biofuels can be the dawn of a new era for the ﬁeld of renewable energy. Syngas biomethana-
tion stands as one of the most promising alternatives to conventional fermentation processes
and anaerobic digestion; however, there are still limitations that need to be addressed until
the technology is mature enough for large scale establishment.
In this study, biomethanation of syngas (45% H2, 25% CO2, 20% CO, 10% N2) was in-
vestigated under theromphilic conditions (60°C) and ambient pressure in a laboratory scale
trickle-bed bioreactor, applying an enriched mixed microbial consortium. The conﬁguration
achieved steady state operation under different operational conditions of syngas inﬂow rate
(Fin,syn) and HRT, resulting in high methane productivity and substrate conversion. The max-
imum productivity at steady state reached up to 3.54 mmol L−1 h−1, which is the highest
reported for reactors utilizing syngas containing the same components. At the selected op-
timal conditions (Fin,syn=1.6mL/min and HRT=5d, see also Table 4.1), the productivity was
also high, standing at 2.80 mmol L−1 h−1. Under these conditions, the methane content in
the gas (24.9%) reached 84.4% of the theoretical maximum and the CO content was only
1.8%. As this methane content is much lower than the minimum required for integration
to the natural gas grid, altering the initial composition of the syngas should be considered.
Initially providing 10% CH4, instead of N2, would increase the methane content to almost
45%. Applying, subsequently, the conventional CO2 removal technologies used for biogas
upgrade, the methane content can reach up to 90%, which is the minimum required for inte-
gration of the produced gas to the natural gas grid or for use as a transportation fuel.
The H2 to methane yield at steady state was closer to 0.25 mol/mol at lower Fin,syn, while
at higher Fin,syn, the molar yield was increasing towards 0.33 mol/mol. This indicates that
as the syngas supply is increasing the water gas-shift reaction rate is increasing respectively,
prior to hydrogenotrophic methanation, which is identiﬁed as the main metabolic pathway
for the production of methane.
During the operation of the reactor, the production of VFAs was observed, mainly acetic
acid, which was the most inﬂuential parameter to the pH of the process. However, at steady
state operation, the performance of the reactor was not severely affected, unless pH dropped
below 6.80 and acetic acid accumulated at higher concentration than 2.50g/L. The increment
of the acetic acid, which resulted from the increase in Fin,syn, provoked signiﬁcant decrease
of the methane productivity and substrate conversion. The HRT was the most effective way
==PAGE:74==
60
5 Conclusions and Recommendations
to overcome the high VFAs accumulation and the low pH, as the metrics of the process im-
proved when the HRT was decreased, at constant Fin,syn.
In batch reactors, the biomethanation process proved to be intact by the concentration of
phosphates used to increase the buffering capacity of the growth medium. Evidently, the
provision of the necessary nutrients is the crucial parameter when considering the composi-
tion of the medium. Moreover, methane activity experiments at different pH (6.50 and 7.00)
and/or at the presence (2.50g/L) or absence of acetic acid showed that a single environmen-
tal shock, i.e. either low pH or high acetic acid concentration, has no signiﬁcant effect on the
methane production and the substrate conversion. This fact reveals a potential synergy or a
mechanism possessed by the strains in the culture to regulate the fermentation environment.
On the other hand, both low pH conditions and high acetic acid concentration were proved
to have a negative effect on the substrate conversion, although slightly affected the methane
production rate.
The acetogenic and aceticlastic potential of the culture was also evaluated in batch re-
actors. Acetogenic activity was almost undetected in batch mode, indicating an important
difference in the fermentative behaviour of the culture in batch conditions than in the TBR,
where the existing acetogenic bacteria recover. As expected, the results indicated that there
is no active aceticlastic methanogenic population in the MMC applied.
Methanation under either only CO, CO/H2 or CO2/H2 mixtures showed that the VFAs
production comes mainly from CO, via carboxydotrophic acetogenesis or indirectly via ho-
moacetogenesis. The experiments also veriﬁed that the main methanogenic population in
the MMC are the hydrogenotrophic species, as the methanation was greatly enhanced in the
presence of H2.
The aforementioned results provide a signiﬁcant insight to the syngas biomethanation
process in trickle-bed reactors. They consist a reliable set of experimental data to compare
with mesophilic operation and provide the optimal design for a future up-scaled bioreactor.
The need for controlling the operational parameters, especially the pH, is highlighted, while
the importance of exploring the versatility of gasiﬁcation to provide the optimal substrate
components ratio is also emphasized.
In this context and with the objective to make this process commercially appealing, mea-
sures of improvement and future work are suggested.
Firstly, focusing on the operational conditions, the use of a pH controlling system, to
monitor the pH and adjust it accordingly with acid or alkali buffers, is strongly encouraged.
It is also important to examine the potential of further decrease in the HRT and of even
higher syngas inﬂow rates, in case a new optimal set of conditions can be achieved that will
==PAGE:75==
5 Conclusions and Recommendations
61
eventually enhance the bioﬁlm formation and increase the methane potential of the process
as it is. To achieve this, an investigation on potential increase of the nutrients concentration
in the growth medium is proposed. Investigating the effect of the liquid medium recircula-
tion rate is also recommended, as it affects the formation of the bioﬁlm and could be another
crucial parameter for the process. At the same time, a change in the efﬂuent removal/growth
medium replacement should also be considered. An automatic pumping system is recom-
mended in order to continuously replace the medium, or at least at portions within the day.
This will assure the constant provision of nutrients and continuous removal of VFAs, instead
of performing this action once a day, as in the course of this study.
Secondly, regarding the substrate, it is recommended to investigate the potential of al-
tering the syngas composition, either to increase the initial methane content in the gas or
adjust the ratio between CO, H2 and CO2 closer to stoichiometric optimal. As discussed
earlier, another possibility is the enrichment of the feed gas in H2 generated via electrolysis
utilizing the surplus electricity from other renewable energy technologies (e.g. wind farms).
The identiﬁcation of the microbial diversity in the culture, which is already scheduled as
future work, will reveal the primary strains existing in the MMC. This can assist the sub-
strate composition planning, as according to their methanogenic type (hydrogenotrophic or
carboxydotrophic) the ratio between the components can be optimized.
Finally, the comparison with mesophilic syngas biomethanation, as well as the design
and operation of a pilot-scale TBR, will provide greater insight to the effectiveness of the
process in industrial scale. An LCA study on the process is also encouraged to be performed
in order to quantify the sustainability of the process and reveal any possible environmental
hot-spots that might need further handling. Overall, syngas biomethanation in trickle-bed
reactors exhibits great potential to serve as a substitute for natural gas. This study serves as
the starting point for future investigations and enhancement of the potential of the process
under thermophilic conditions.

In this Appendix, the results for the daily analysis of the trickle-bed bioreactor are presented
in terms of CH4 productivity, yields, as well as the cells concentration and VFAs concentra-
tion in the efﬂuent, in Fig. 1-6.
In the graphs, it can be observed that there are irregular ﬂuctuation and days with no
results, as a consequence of some incidents that occured or malfunction of the reactor. Specif-
ically:
• Exp day 25: There was a power outage in the building for about 15min, while the pump
was off for about 30min. The measurements of the day were performed 3h after the
reactor started to work normally again. This had caused some small ﬂuctuation to the
metrics of the reactor, who was at the time working at steady state. Those ﬂuctuations
were not severe and the Fin,syn was increased at this day to 1.4mL/min.
• Exp day 32: A part of the tubing used for the medium recirculation was found broken
in the morning. The liquid medium had leaked almost entirely out of the reactor, while
air was inserting in the reactor due to the peristaltic operation of the pump. The tubing
was changed, the liquid medium was replaced with fresh TMC-BA medium and the
conditions were kept as before. It should be noted that at this time the reactor had
almost achieved steady state and it had to remain at those conditions for a long period
to achieve almost the same performance. Apart from that, it is surprisingly observed
from the graphs that the hydrogenotrophic population of the bioﬁlm recovered the
heavy O2 shock in only 2 days (high CO2 and H2 conversion).
• Exp day 42: The septum of the liquid sampling was excessively worn out and had to
be changed. During the replacement, 45mL of the liquid medium leaked out of the
reactor and were replaced by fresh TMC-BA medium. The disturbance caused was
overcome in only one day as observed from the graphs.
• Exp day 71: The tubing of the waterbath pump broke, causing a severe drop in the
temperature of the reactor, as it was found in the morning. The whole pump was
replaced and no measurements were performed at the day. The conﬁguration seems to
have recovered immediately to the temperature shock.
• Exp day 74: Clogging of the non-return valve used between the gas sampling port and
the gas outﬂow meter. That provoked a disturbance on the pressure inside the reactor,
causing a ﬂuctuation of the measurements for the next couple of days.
