Abstract
Human Serum Albumin (HSA) has been used in a variety of therapeutic settings
for about 70 years. Nevertheless, due to a lack of supply, its wide applicability
is somewhat hampered. Additionally, there is a need for alternative purification
techniques to replace the current resin¬≠based solution, due to long retention times
and difficulty in scaling up.
In this work, a recombinant strain of the methylotrophic yeast Pichia Pastoris con¬≠
taining the HSA expressing gene has been applied in an effort to reach the highest
yield possible. High¬≠density fermentation was employed in a bioreactor using a
novel media, which resulted in the yield of 1.4 g/L following 60 h of methanol in¬≠
duction.
The optimal conditions for purification of HSA using a N¬≠Benzyl¬≠N¬≠methyl ethanol
amine (BMEA) as ligand. At optimal pH 8.5, the maximum equilibration binding ca¬≠
pacity was found to be 389 mg¬≠HSA /g¬≠membrane corresponding to 114.7 mg/mL,
while the dissociation constant was found to be 2.16 mg/mL (32.2 ¬µM). Further¬≠
more, the flow permeability was tested at different flowrate, resulting in capabilities
of high flowrates at low pressuredrop < 0.15 MPa (1.5 bars).
The purification of HSA was done using fibrillated glycidyl methacrylate (GMA)
grafted cellulose membranes, with the BMEA ligand, as an alternative to conven¬≠
tional resin chromatography. The conditions for HSA purifications were optimized
to which a membrane with 18% weight gain from grafting had a binding capacity
of 24.4 mg/mL at 1 minute residence time, purifying from commercial HSA dis¬≠
solved in buffer. Another membrane with 17% weight gained from grafting had a
binding capacity of 14.4 mg/mL at 0.5 minute residence time, purifying from Pichia
supernatant.

Introduction
As a result of the overall population growth, an expanding geriatric sector and the
the rise in common chronic illness as such as cancer, diabetes, and cardiovascu¬≠
lar disease, the demand for biopharmaceuticals is rising. The use of recombinant
biopharmaceuticals is the fastest¬≠growing segment of the pharmaceutical sector,
creating a wide range of commercial expression platforms, including both micro¬≠
bial and mammalian cell hosts, have been created for the industry [1].
Even though the creation and optimization of the production organism is in rapid
development, the production of the protein of interest (POI) is limited by expensive
bottleneck, which is the downstream purification process. In some studies it is sug¬≠
gested that the expenses associated with recovery and purification might account
for up to 80% of overall production costs [2]. Moreover, a substantial amount of
the production expenses are contributed by packed bed column chromatography
alone.
1.1
Human Serum Albumin
Human serum albumin (HSA), the main protein found in human plasma, is used
in many in vitro experiments and medicinal formulations [3]. The molecule can be
seen in Figure 1.1, and is described as a single non¬≠glycosylated peptide chain
of 585 amino acids (66.5 kDa) with isoelectric point (pI) of 4.7 [4], makes up the
monomeric multi¬≠domain macromolecule [5]. HSA has a wide range of uses, in¬≠
cluding nanodelivery of certain medication, cell culture medium, the creation of
vaccines, cryopreservation, and many more cutting¬≠edge applications. The most
important use is as an expander of blood volume [6].
The therapeutic demand for HSA in 2014 exceeded 500 metric tons globally due
to the high standard doses (above 10 g/dose) [7]. As all medical grade HSA now
in use is generated from whole blood or donated human plasma, its usage is
constrained by a lack of availability, a possible danger of infection from blood¬≠
derived pathogens, and the difficulties of mass manufacture [8].
1.2
Production of HSA
Technology for heterologous expression has been extensivily used in resent stud¬≠
ies and several expression methods, including Escherichia coli [11], Saccharomyces
cerevisiae [12], and Pichia pastoris [13, 14, 15], have been utilized for high produc¬≠
tivity of recombinant HSA (rHSA) in the last decades due to the non¬≠glycosylated
property of HSA.
However, it is currently very difficult to produce rHSA on a big scale. The minimal
required for rHSA secretion looks to be several grams per liter given the cheap
unit cost and huge market volume [8]. Among the various bioreactor systems,
Pichia pastoris exhibits the most promising potential for HSA expression due to

1.3
Purification of HSA
Purification of HSA and proteins in general is usually done through resin¬≠based
packed bed chromatography, where multiple studies and commercial product has
shown the current capabilities of HSA purification.
In most of these studies, the purification of HSA from supernatant is done through
multiple step, where the supernatant has undergone buffer exchange, following
a 1¬≠2 steps of packed bed chromatography, using either affinity, hydrophobic
chromatography and/or ion exchange chromatography, where the latter is rec¬≠

In this theory section, I will give an introduction to biopharma and its upstream
and downstream components. The first section after the introduction focussed on
the upstream process, comparing production organisms, before focussing on the
organism of choice, P. pastoris.
The following segment will be discussing different chromatography modes, while

2.1
Introduction to biopharma
The vast majority of biopharmaceuticals that have received regulatory authority
approval for use in therapeutic applications are proteins that were made using
recombinant DNA technology in a variety of expression systems. They make
up around one¬≠sixth of the whole pharmaceutical market and are the industry‚Äôs
fastest¬≠growing sector. Biopharmaceuticals are typically utilized to make up for a
shortage or deficit of body proteins necessary for the organism to operate normally.
Blood factors, thrombolytics and anticoagulants, hormones, enzymes, growth fac¬≠
tors, interferons and interleukins, vaccinations, and monoclonal antibodies are the
basic groups into which they may be separated.
Depending on the recombinant protein in question, an expression system should
be chosen. It is typical to utilize the least labor¬≠intensive expression method that
nonetheless yields the appropriate protein quality and quantity. The most straight¬≠
forward and affordable expression system is E. coli, however because to issues
with proper folding and a lack of post¬≠translational modification (PTM), its usage
is restricted.
Despite frequently having an unfavorable glycosylation pattern, yeasts are use¬≠
ful in protein folding and post¬≠translational modification because they are simple
eukaryotes. Mammalian cells have the highest degree of protein quality and post¬≠
translational modification, such as glycosylation, but are also the most difficult and
expensive to maintain [22].

2.2.2
Yeast
The unicellular organism yeast has several sub¬≠cellular characteristics with higher
eukaryotes. These comprise the nucleus, vacuoles, microbodies, mitochondria,
endoplasmic reticulum, and Golgi apparatus. In addition, some yeast species,
such as Saccharomyces cerevisiae, have long been a part of the human diet. In
the process of developing recombinant pharmaceuticals, yeast is a helpful organ¬≠
ism since it is ‚Äùgenerally recognized as safe‚Äù (GRAS) and has undergone signif¬≠
icant research. Other commonly used yeast species include H. polymorpha, K.
lactis Y. lypolytica, S. pombe, K. lactis and P. pastoris.
Due to extensive study, the vast molecular and biochemical characteristics of
yeast is known as well as how to manage it. The second most well¬≠known yeast,
P. pastoris, is employed extensively in industry and in laboratories. One of the rea¬≠
sons it is utilized so frequently is that it has the capacity to generate more protein
than S. cerevisia in terms of increased secretion and production.
2.2.3
Mammalian cells
They are the expression technique for biopharmaceuticals that are now used the
most frequently, accounting for two¬≠thirds of the income from biopharmaceuticals
generated in mammalian cells. The great majority of approved proteins have been
produced using Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells. Two other rodent cell types
are mouse myeloma (NS0) and baby hamster kidney (BHK). Human¬≠derived cells
have also been employed, including human embryonic kidney (HEK¬≠293) cells
and retinal¬≠derived cells. These cells can all be grown in suspension culture in
bioreactors and are all scalable [25]. The main advantages of mammalian cells are
the capacity to synthesize complex proteins modified post¬≠translationally and the
capacity to generate proteins in great yields. Human¬≠like proteins, including those

Chromatography materials: resin and
membranes
Resin chromatography is a form of chromatography that separates molecules us¬≠
ing a solid support, usually a spherical bead. Usually, a polymer, such agarose
or polyacrylamide, is used to create the beads, which are then functionalized with
certain ligands that can attach to the target molecules.
After that, the beads are arranged into a column, through which a mobile phase is
conveyed. While the contaminants travel through the column, the target molecules
will bond to the ligands on the beads. The molecules of interest are then eluted
using a new mobile phase after the column has been cleaned to get rid of the
contaminants.
Packed bed chromatography has a number of drawbacks, including restrictions
on diffusion, significant pressure dips across the column, limited throughputs, ex¬≠
pensive resin prices, and difficulties scaling up. In particular when separating big
molecules like antibodies, membrane chromatography is a viable alternative to
packed bed chromatography for lowering the intraparticle diffusions that signifi¬≠
cantly increase transport resistance.
This highlights the significance of developing low¬≠cost separation methods like
membrane chromatography.
Due to the larger porous structures and greater
permeabilities, pressure drops across membranes are smaller than those across
packed beds, enabling higher flowrates and, as a result, better product through¬≠
puts. Furthermore, membrane chromatography is a simple process to scale up.

supporting material for purification [2, 41].

4.1.3
Sampling
Samples are taken twice daily at 11 am and 5 pm. The sample size is 10¬≠15 mL
and is used for optical density, glycerol measurements, wet cell weight, and dry
cell weight. The sample area is sprayed with 70% ethanol before sampling, to
keep a sterile environment.
Optical Density
Optical density at 600 nm (OD600) is used to measure cell growth in culture. The
absorbance of 1 mL of cells is measured in a spectrophotometer cuvette. The
measurement is taken at less than 0.8 OD units. If the value is greater, a series
of dilutions with supernatant is performed to bring it within the accepted reading
range. The final OD value is calculated by multiplying the measurement by the
dilution factor. The supernatant is used to blank the samples.
Glycerol measurement
For observing the glycerol measurements and making sure, that glycerol has been
completely depleted before beginning the methanol fed¬≠batch, the glycerol content
is measured by a technique for figuring out the amount of glycerol in a solution
is the YSI glycerol measurement. It is carried out with a YSI 2900 Biochemistry
Analyzer, a biosensor¬≠based device that can quickly and accurately test a wide
range of analytes.
A glycerol¬≠specific mounted enzyme electrode is used to detect glycerol. The en¬≠
zyme catalyzes the reaction of glycerol to form hydrogen peroxide when the sam¬≠
ple is placed on the electrode. The electrode then detects the hydrogen peroxide,
and the signal produced allows one to calculate the concentration of glycerol. The
method is conducted by triplicate measuring 25 ¬µL of cell¬≠free supernatant made
by centrifuging 1.5 mL of fermentation broth for 10 min. at 10.000 rpm. All sam¬≠
ples are made in duplicates. Glycerol measurements are only done from the batch
setting and until the glycerol fed¬≠batch has ended.
The YSI equipment has a lower detection limit of 0.75 mg/mL ¬± 0.75 mg/mL [65].
Cell density

4.5.1
SDS¬≠PAGE and imageJ
While the SDS¬≠PAGE is seen as a qualitative method, for visualizing protein con¬≠
tent, the software imageJ was used as a method of quantification. The methods
are used in tandem to visualize protein content, before quantification. The soft¬≠
ware can be downloaded from: [64].
The samples were calculated to have approximately 5 ¬µg of protein in each well,
for comparison among each other as well as for calculation using the imageJ
software. Together with each gel, a 3¬≠4 point standard curve of 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, and
0.6 mg/mL of HSA dissolved in pure water was made for reference to the software.
1. In a microtube, 150¬µL of each crude sample material (collected fractions) is
mixed with 50¬µL (4x) Laemmli sample buffer and 5¬µL of beta¬≠mercaptoethanol
(BME).
2. The microtube is sealed (to avoid evaporation) and heated at 95‚ó¶C for 5
minutes in order to unfold the protein, whereafter the tubes are cooled at
¬≠20‚ó¶C for 10 minutes.
3. The tubes are vortex vigorously before the samples are added to the wells.
4. The gels are run with a 200 V current, and whereafter the gels are washed
3 times, before staining for 20 minutes using Bio¬≠Safe Coomassie G¬≠250
stain.
5. After 20 minutes, the gels are destained using pure water and left overnight
in a gel box on a shaking table set to 50 rpm.
A standard curve is used for the calculation of HSA content in samples and is
made with pure HSA dissolved in pure water
4.5.2
PicoGreen¬Æ assay
Removal of dDNA is important in protein purification since unknown proteins might
be recognized by the immune system and cause an immune response. In order
to quantify the double¬≠stranded¬≠DNA, before and after the product capture step,
the PicoGreen Assay was used.
PicoGreen is a fluorescent dye¬≠based test for determining the quantity of double¬≠
stranded DNA (dsDNA) in a sample. The test is based on the idea that PicoGreen
dye binds selectively to dsDNA, and its fluorescence increases when attached to
dsDNA.
The test is carried out by adding PicoGreen dye to a dsDNA sample. After that,
the dye and dsDNA are combined and incubated for a length of time. The fluores¬≠
cence of the dye is measured using a fluorometer after it has been incubated. The
quantity of fluorescence in the sample is proportional to the amount of dsDNA.
The PicoGreen¬Æ assay was prepared using the procedure found through the sup¬≠
plier Thermo Scientific [69], but the overall process is described below.
==PAGE:62==

1. Prepare the PicoGreen reagent and the DNA samples.
2. Add the PicoGreen reagent to the DNA samples.
3. Incubate the samples for 2¬≠5 minutes at room temperature.
4. Measure the fluorescence of the samples using a plate reader.
5. Calculate the DNA concentration of the samples using a standard curve.

Fermentation
The fermentation was run as two separate 1.5 L bioreactors under identical condi¬≠

BFM21, before switching to the glycerol fed¬≠batch and then switching to methanol
fed¬≠batch. Between the glycerol fed¬≠batch and methanol fed¬≠batch a starvation
phase is implemented due to glycerol‚Äôs inhibitory effect on the AOX genes, that
initiate methanol metabolism.
Since the fermentation was run under identical conditions, they should therefore
act similarly. However, since it‚Äôs a biological system, batch¬≠to¬≠batch variations are
common [72]. As seen in Figure 5.1, 5.2 and 5.3, bioreactor 1 is still consuming
methanol, when bioreactor 2 has consumed all and is going into the starvation
phase.
Figure 5.1: Fermentation timeline ¬≠ The visual presentation of the step in the fer¬≠
mentation, where the experimental results can be seen in Figure 5.2 and 5.3.
Looking closer at the initial BFM21 batch, it is seen that while there are some
disturbances in the measurements of dissolved oxygen, the agitation is steadily
increasing, due to the growth of the cells. After, a drop is seen in agitation, simply
due to consumption of the feedstock, that is left in the batch. The batch is not
starving, but what‚Äôs left is being consumed. Going over to the glycerol fed¬≠batch,
the DO and agitation are changing rapidly. This is done in order to increase the
oxygen transfer, that is resulting in glycerol being dropwise added to the batch. In
this stage, the agitation is steadily increasing to supply oxygen to the increase in
biomass.
The decision to increase in methanol feed rate from 0.1 to 0.2 rpm is based on the
negative slope seen on the agitation figure for both bioreactors 1 and 2. The batch

6.2
Removal of pigment in supernatant
As a way to understand the problem of unknown contaminants, another literature
study is conducted. While initially thought of trace metals in the supernatant to
be the sinner, multiple studies report problems in downstream processing as a
result of the green/yellow color. According to one study, the pharmaceutically
undesirable green pigment released by P. pastoris made it challenging to purify the
recombinant protein. During downstream processing, these unwelcome colors in
the supernatant reacted with the target protein as well as other protein impurities,
which lowered the yield of the target protein [75].
Studies show, that alcohol oxidase (AOX), which is produced in high cell density,
methanol¬≠induced Pichia fermentations, is the cause of the green hue in super¬≠
natants. A homooctamer (crystalloid) of AOX forms, serves as a prosthetic group
for the binding of flavin¬≠adenine dinucleotide (FAD) [76], where it is known that
AOX crystalloids, despite being produced inside the cell, give Pichia fermentation
supernatant its characteristic yellow¬≠green hue due to continual leaking into the
environment [77].
6.2.1
Precipitation, chromatography & active carbon
Different methods of removal of the pigment have been tried. The removal of
these pigments from a culture supernatant containing Staphylococcal Protein A
has been attempted using ammonium sulfate precipitation and ethanol precipi¬≠
tation, but these methods have been ineffective because the pigments could be
precipitated by both ethanol and ammonium sulfate [77].
Contrary results of removal of pigment using active carbon, indicate efficiently
removing pigment, but one study shows removal of pigment at the expense of
protein content, which is reduced by 25%, while another does not show removal
of protein up to 10% active carbon content [78].
Another method, that has worked was the chromatography¬≠based purification meth¬≠
ods, such as size exclusion and ion exchange chromatography, which are effec¬≠
==PAGE:91==

tive in the removal of the pigment [77] [79] [80]. These methods should be used
before the purification of the target protein for optimation. Chromatography meth¬≠
ods are however rather expensive and should be evaluated, since the concept
of ‚Äùfrom fermentation¬≠to¬≠purification‚Äù is a main factor for membrane¬≠based chro¬≠

6.2.2
Diafiltration
The most cost¬≠effective and efficient methods seem to be a series of diafiltration.
The three main steps in this study‚Äôs sequential cross¬≠flow filtration were (i) pretreat¬≠
ment of culture supernatant using a microfiltration membrane with a pore size of
0.2 ¬µm, (ii) cross¬≠flow filtration using an ultrafiltration membrane with a molecular
weight cut¬≠off of 10 kDa followed by 2 kDa, and (iii) diafiltration of the 10 and 2
kDa retentate fractions with 500 mL.
The buffer used in the study is PBS, due to the optimality of the experiment, but
should most likely be able to substitute for the equilibration buffer used. The target
peptide in this study was the antimicrobial peptide (CAP) with a molecular weight
of 4.1 kDa, while HSA has a molecular weight of 65.5 kDa, vastly exceeding the
need for a second diafiltration at 2 kDa, reducing cost and complexity.
Furthermore, it has tremendous scalability, especially when dialysis is added. Ac¬≠
cording to research, the permeability of commonly used membranes normally
rises as membrane area grows, enabling larger process capacities and a lower
cost¬≠per¬≠volume ratio [81]. Due to this method‚Äôs low operational cost, it also pro¬≠
vides an economic benefit. The membranes, which run about 563 USD per cas¬≠
sette of Vivaflow 200 membrane, are the main expense of this procedure [82].

7
Conclusion
‚Ä¢ It was possible to express HSA production in P. pastoris, in high concen¬≠
trations (1.4 mg/mL), although not near titers that other studies present (17
mg/mL).
‚Ä¢ For further use in the downstream process, the supernatant was diluted due
to too high conductivity and buffering conditions, and spiked with commercial
purified HSA, to increase its concentration, to simulate a natural high titer.
‚Ä¢ While successful in producing HSA in Pichia, the resulting supernatant had
a green/yellow tint and reached a high conductivity of approximately 23
mS/cm, which was highly buffered to a state where a 1:1 dilution with pure
water was necessary for pH adjustment and to bring it within the working
range of the ligand (15¬≠20 mS/cm).
‚Ä¢ The optimal binding pH for binding HSA was found to be between pH 8 and
pH 9.5. The pH of 8.5 is chosen due to protein instability at high pH.
‚Ä¢ The dissociation constant from the Langmuir isotherm binding capacity study
was found to be 2.16 mg/mL (32.2 ¬µM)
‚Ä¢ The maximum equilibration binding capacity was found to be 389 mg¬≠HSA
/g¬≠membrane corresponding to 114.7 mg/mL
‚Ä¢ The permeability decrease as the grafting degree increases. The biggest
decrease in permeability is seen when going from 18% WG to 20% WG,
although none of the sample exceeded 0.15 MPa (1.5 bars).
‚Ä¢ The use of a regeneration step, when purifying from supernatant is not only
important but highly efficient to keep the binding capacity steady after multi¬≠
ple binding cycles.
‚Ä¢ The fact that no proteins show up in the elution after running an SDS¬≠PAGE
from the 5 consecutive runs with regeneration, strongly indicates that some¬≠
thing else in the supernatant is responsible for the drop in binding capacity,
as seen in the 5 consecutive runs without regeneration. Other studies indi¬≠
cate the green and yellow pigment, common in Pichia fermentations [77].
‚Ä¢ Membranes at 18% WG had a binding capacity of 24.4 mg/mL (1 min. RT)
of HSA dissolved in equilibration buffer, compared to membranes at 17%
WG which had a binding capacity of 14.4 mg/mL (RT = 0.5) of HSA in the
supernatant. While pigment caused by AOX and FAD in the supernatant is
highly suspected, the precise cause has not been verified.
‚Ä¢ The optimized membrane had a binding capacity of 14.4 mg/mL at a low
retention time (RT = 0.5 min) at 17% WG. The recovery process had an
elution and a recovery yield at around 100%, with the removal of dDNA and
HCP of 82% and 96% respectively.

‚Ä¢ Compared to industrial resins, the membrane‚Äôs low residence time results

Capto‚Ñ¢Blue/ Blue (high sub) and POROS‚Ñ¢CaptureSelect‚Ñ¢HSA Affinity

8
Future studies
‚Ä¢ Although variations in biological systems are common, the resulting conduc¬≠
tivity of the supernatant was higher than reported in litterature (23.7 com¬≠
pared to the normal 15¬≠20 mS/cm [37, 38, 30]). An investigation by replica¬≠
tion of the fermentation conditions might show wether the conditions needs
to be reevaluated.
‚Ä¢ Fermentation studies of Pichia show that by using a complex media of methanol
and the sugar sorbitol under methanol fed¬≠batch, result in higher yield under
longer fermentation. Sorbitol does not repress the AOX genes [27].
‚Ä¢ On the basis of the highly buffered, high conductivity supernatant, a study in
reducing the resulting buffered state and conductivity in supernatant might
result in a better downstream process.
‚Ä¢ A thorough investigation of the content of the supernatant, that might influ¬≠
ence the binding capacity of the membranes, wether that might be trace
metals in the solution, pigments or both.
‚Ä¢ In extension, due to the coloring of the supernatant contributed by the fer¬≠
mentation, and more specifically, the trace metals, an IEX chromatography
before the purification might remove contaminants from the supernatant.
Prior purification of the proteins will most likely increase the membrane‚Äôs
binding capacity as contaminants will no longer compete and interfere with
the ligands. This will however increase the overall workload and production
cost of the purification and will have to be evaluated.
‚Ä¢ In continuation of previous statement, alternative methods of removing the
pigment is available and should be investigated [77].
‚Ä¢ The 10% breakthrough study in equilibration buffer would need another mea¬≠
surement using the 20% WG BMEA¬≠GMA membrane, to see wether it would
cause a decline in binding capacity.
