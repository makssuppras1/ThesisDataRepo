Abstract 
This report maps the benefits and challenges concerning the design and use of 3D-reinforcement in a 
complex geometry structure. This was done by comparing qualitative data obtained both by 
investigating the modelling and design phase as well as aspects of construction and manufacturing of 
reinforcement. Ultimately, the project sought to compare the challenges of designing and using 3D-
reinforcement models in complex structures with the value it gives to the main stakeholders in the 
concrete reinforcement supply chain.  
The challenge in the design and modelling phase was exclusively connected to solving the issue of 
complex geometry, where a generative design tool was applied. This is a more advanced modelling 
technique as generative design tools rely on a programming language, in this case a visual programming 
language.  The purpose of applying such tools was to investigate how designers can solve the modelling 
aspect of 3D-reinforcement in complex structures, and whether it is achievable to expediently model 
the reinforcement in such structures.  
The report is connected to a reference project – a double-curved support wall leading up to a small 
highway bridge. The construction of the wall and bridge were already completed at this project’s start. 
Still, it was a representative structure for the scope of the project. With access to data from COWI, 2D-
reinforcement drawings and bending schedules were given for the reference project. This served as the 
basis for an extensive modelling process where a replica of the rebar design was created in a BIM. The 
wall’s geometry was given by COWI through a shell in a BIM and used as the host for the digital 
reinforcement.  
To investigate the benefits and challenges concerning the use and implementation of 3D-reinforcement, 
several qualitative interviews were performed. The interviewees represent the main stakeholders in the 
concrete reinforcement supply chain. The majority of the interviewees had to a certain degree 
experience with 3D-reinforcement in the construction phase. Building owners were also interviewed to 
investigate what they require for implementing 3D-reinforcement in their projects.  
Additional data was retrieved by performing a case study on a full-BIM project, where the consultants 
only deliver information and data through BIM to the contractor and building owner. The case is a 
hydropower-plant extension project with several high complexity concrete structures and is pr. July 
2016 still in construction. Data was collected through an inspection, meetings and through three 
qualitative interviews. Additionally, new software releases with new functionalities were investigated 
to map the development of 3D-reinforcement design tools and how multiple tools can improve the 
design phase. 
The report concludes that designing 3D-reinforcement in a complex geometry structure is achievable 
and that generative design tools bring several benefits. Not only does it handle the complex geometry, 
but also increases automation of the design and gives the designer a high reuse value. Furthermore, the 
swiftness of changes in BIM can reduce time spent in the design phase as well as may reduce manual 
errors. Reinforcement in BIM also gives benefits in the construction phase by improving ordering, 
planning and coordination. Still, generative design tools pose a competence challenge for designers and 
BIM is a technology unfamiliar for many conservative AEC professionals. Furthermore, the 
implementation is governed by building owners, who can be skeptical and unwilling. Additionally, the 
use of 3D-reinforcement out in the construction pits also pose a challenge. However, as the case study 
shows, it is possible to achieve.

1. Introduction 
This project was written in cooperation with the bridge department of COWI Norway, an Oslo-based 
engineering consultancy firm. It was requested to map the benefits and challenges of 3D-reinforcement 
in complex structures as the routines and processes of this today are in many cases limited or non-
existent. This is especially the case in structures in relation to infrastructure projects, such as bridges.  
To achieve a result, a study of processes concerning both modelling and the usage of reinforcement in 
BIM was performed. Due to the collaborator’s role, the project is performed from a consulting 
engineering perspective. However, to be able to map the full range of benefits and challenges, data has 
been collected from both contractors, reinforcement manufacturers, consulting engineers and 
professional building owners. The focus of the project lies on which challenges and possibilities are 
connected to the modelling process and how a reinforcement BIM is, and can be, used by the different 
stakeholders in the AEC industry. 
The implementation of 3D-reinforcement is not common in the AEC industry. This is backed by the 
McGraw Hill survey [1, p. 15] discovering that among BIM users who model structural elements, 80% 
model concrete elements most frequently, while only 28% reported frequent modelling of 
reinforcement, indicating a gap that may be in need of improvement [2]. However, these numbers 
represent modelling by both contractors and structural engineers and do not include civil and 
infrastructure projects. Moreover, as the same survey found a rate of only 14% for frequent modelling 
of bridges it is reasonable to assume a low-level of reinforcement-modelling in infrastructure/bridge-
projects, projects with typically complex geometry structures.  
The implementation of 3D-reinforcement in concrete structures can automate processes and increase 
productivity, thus reducing costs. This can help the AEC industry build more efficiently as information 
from reinforcement models can be extracted and used by several of the stakeholders in the reinforcement 
supply chain [3]. This includes the possibility of prefabrication of rebar cages, in which a case study by 
M. Shamsai et. al. [4] on concrete columns found a 33.3% reduction of time and 7.1% reduction of cost 
per column when implemented. This project has also investigated the possibilities of prefabricating 
rebar cages for complex geometry structures.  
 
1.1  Background 
BIM is a well-known term implemented in the AEC industry. It is used by all disciplines through all 
phases of projects in all sizes, and the possibilities in BIM comprehend an extensive list. However, as 
these possibilities can reap potentially huge rewards, BIM is still a relatively new technology and 
approach in AEC. This is especially the case in reinforcement design where little work and research has 
been performed. Therefore, discoveries of unfamiliar terrain are necessary to extract valuable

information on how to improve today’s processes. However, there are examples where 3D-
reinforcement has been implemented, such as the extension of Oslo Airport and the case study of this 
report.  
The present structural design process is largely seen to only comprehend traditional 2D-reinforcement 
drawings, especially in infrastructure projects. For instance, in bridge design, drawings are produced by 
structural engineers based on a structural 3D-model. Sections are extracted from the model in which 
reinforcement is designed and drawn. An issue here is that these sections and drawings are independent 
of each other, meaning that a change in one section/drawing is not reflected in other corresponding 
sections/drawings. In BIMs, if an object is changed or deleted in one view or section, the object is 
changed or deleted in all other views and sections as it applies to the full model. The traditional 2D-
workflow usually consists of many revisions resulting in redesign of reinforcement, thus requiring many 
changes of sections and drawings.   
The implementation of BIM in construction projects is governed by the building owner, which in 
infrastructure projects usually is a governmental directory. These directories typically follow protocols 
and guidelines when ordering a project, such as the Norwegian Public Roads Administration bridge 
design handbook [5]. Over the last few years such protocols have governed increasing requirements 
and demand of BIMs, resulting in higher accuracy of the models. Furthermore, the intention of the 
protocols or handbooks aim amongst different things at contributing to the use of 3D-models as the 
basis for construction on site [6].  
The concept of 3D-reinforcement is a relatively new branch in BIM. It has advanced through the 
continued development of BIM-tools and is implemented in several software-programs. The use of this 
has over the last few years shown an increasing trend and several projects have been carried out where 
3D-reinforcement have been applied in full scale [7], [8]. However, BIM-tools for concrete 
reinforcement are not as developed as for other areas within the field of 3D-modelling. This is especially 
the case for reinforcement in structures with complex geometry. The BIM-tools of today have 
capabilities to handle reinforcement in simple geometry structures but display challenges when 
structures have curved and complex shapes. There are however certain tools with a computational 
design approach that allow complex 3D-modelling of reinforcement in combination with traditional 
BIM-tools. This is very helpful with regard to tackling the challenges of complex structures. However, 
it is a relatively new and untested approach with a lack of research to back its application, also being 
the case for reinforcement BIM-tools regardless of model complexity [2]. This master project 
investigated the capabilities and limitations of such software-programs and aims at helping the 
construction industry evolve to apply better processes in future work in relation to 3D-models of 
reinforcement in complex concrete structures. 
The concrete structures in infrastructure, such as bridges and tunnels, generally have complex 
geometries and are usually unique in design. This differs from traditional buildings that are mass 
produced for housing or office facilities with repeating and identical non-complex geometry structural 
elements, where the application of 3D-reinforcement can flow more seamlessly. The uniqueness of the 
complex structures and the corresponding restrictions in current BIM-tools do not allow the application 
to flow as smoothly as for traditional buildings. The design of 3D-reinforcement in complex structures 
therefore requires additional tools. 
The computational power of computers has surpassed the capabilities of the human mind by a large 
extent and the capabilities in modern software serve as platforms for increasing design possibilities. 
With regard to 3D-reinforcement, generative design (often referred to as computational design) is a tool 
that can help increase the degree of automation and give designers extended abilities in their design.

Generative tools are based on rules, or algorithms, generating design solutions that can be altered by 
controlling parameters, allowing designers to redesign fairly easy. The use of such programs is also 
relatively easy as new software offer visual programming possibilities rather than explicit coding, 
requiring little or no previous experience with programming of the user.  
 
1.2 Limitations, Purpose and Problem Statements 
It is important to note that the author of the project has no commercial interest in any kind of software 
or BIM-programs, thus is the report fully objective with regards to the applied tools.  
 
1.2.1  Limitations 
The concrete reinforcement supply chain consists of many stakeholders and processes, and the process 
of tension-reinforced concrete (pre-stressed or post-stressed) differs from regular slack-reinforced 
concrete. This project has its focus on the link and processes between consulting structural engineers 
and project owners, as well as how contractors can apply reinforcement in BIM on site and how the 
reinforcement manufacturer can aid the processes. Still, the report has its main focus on the design 
phase and is written from a consulting engineering perspective. Therefore, the physical construction of 
reinforcement from a BIM on site is investigated as a secondary objective. The reinforcement 
considered in this project is slack-reinforced, i.e. not pre- or post-stressed. This is due to the complexity 
of the stressed-reinforcement itself, including the jacking connections, complicating modelling 
processes. Furthermore, this also means that the concrete structures considered are cast in-place (in-
situ) structures.  
As the project revolves around the implementation of 3D-reinforcement and evaluates the design and 
implementation process around it, no structural analysis or structural properties is performed or 
examined. The applied reinforcement design is given through a reference project from the collaborator 
COWI.  
This project has been performed in Norway and Denmark, thus not necessarily representing the status 
in other countries or regions. 
 
1.2.2 Software Limitations 
There are several BIM vendors producing BIM tools capable of modelling 3D-reinforcement. This 
project applied Autodesk Revit 2016 as a main platform with the Dynamo 0.9 add-in as an additional 
software for generative modelling and automation purposes. This add-in was the key in this project as 
it was essential to handle the complex geometry at hand.  
The reason for applying Revit was the program’s ability to handle 3D-reinforcement and the author’s 
previous experience with the software. This was intended to reduce unnecessary training time. Dynamo

3. BIM, Reinforcement Design & Complex 
Structures – Applied Theory  
BIM is short for Building Information Model or Building Information Modelling, concerning a model 
and the processes connected to a model respectively. The model aspect concerns a 3D-model consisting 
of building elements with a set of properties and their relation to other building elements in the model. 
A building information model is in this regard not just a 3D-model, but a 3D-model with information 
attached to every single element as well as the elements’ relation to each other. As the concept and 
processes of BIM have evolved, models can now contain several dimensions of information such as 4D 
(time) and 5D (economy) [12] as well as 6D (facilities maintenance and management) [13]. The process 
aspect of BIM concerns the 3D-model based processes equipping AEC-professionals with insight and 
tools to plan, design, construct and manage building projects efficiently [14]. 
In construction projects it is important that stakeholders can communicate and share information 
efficiently. With a BIM process, this means that the different stakeholders must use the same systems 
or rely on open BIM. Open BIM has its foundation on three pillars [15]: 
• 
Unified open file format 
• 
Mutual terminology 
• 
Standard process and delivery specifications 
A common unified open file format is IFC (Industry Foundation Classes), which is supported by most 
BIM-software, allowing users to interact across platforms. Several freeware programs exist that support 
such file formats, allowing users to access information regardless of licensing and which programs they 
are accustomed to.  
Mutual terminology concerns exchange of data and information across software programs and assures 
that information is not lost between different stakeholders. This terminology can be standardized from 
standards such as the ISO 12006-3, as the International Framework for Data Dictionaries (IFD) is.  
Standard process and delivery specifications describe the stakeholders, procedures and requirements of 
deliveries in projects. Such descriptions are intended to aid the AEC industry to cooperate efficiently. 
Standardized process descriptions define the services from, and the interface between, stakeholders in 
projects. Process and delivery specifications can be standardized from standards such as the ISO 29481. 
BIM can be utilized in all phases of a construction project. This report is limited to processes and 
interfaces between consultants, building owners, manufacturers and contractors. These processes and 
interfaces are included in the design and build phases, as seen in Figure 3.

From the beginning of time mankind has produced structures with complex geometry. Ancient cases

3.2 Reinforcement Design in BIM 
Reinforced concrete is a composite material consisting of concrete and steel. It combines the low tensile 
strength and ductility of concrete with the counteracting high tensile strength and ductility of steel. 
Reinforcement is usually placed passively in concrete but can also be stressed by pre- or post-
tensioning. This project only considers passive (slack) reinforcement.  
The design process of reinforced concrete consists of a structural analysis, where structural engineers 
calculate the required amount of reinforcement in a structure on the basis of acting forces. The amount 
is typically given as a cross sectional area. The structural engineer then based on this amount determines 
a design, where types (shapes and diameter) and amount of bars are chosen to fulfill the cross sectional 
area requirement. Reinforcement design has traditionally resulted in 2D-drawings following Eurocodes 
and national standards, such as NS-EN-8332 in Norway. Furthermore, state directories acting as 
building owners such as the Norwegian Public Roads Administration require 2D reinforcement-
drawings in their bridge projects [5]. These drawings are in most cases quite complex and require both 
training and experience to master, see an example in Figure 5.  However, following the technological 
evolution of CAD and BIM-tools, opportunities to evolve from 2D to 3D have risen.  
 
 
Figure 5: Conventional 2D rebar drawing (the reference project). 
 
There are several BIM platforms (or software) supporting and enabling the design of 3D-reinforcement. 
These platforms have been around for some years, providing designers with opportunities to model 
reinforcement in BIMs. An example of 3D-reinforcement can be seen in Figure 6. Such models can be 
used to produce drawings, bending schedules and aid contractors with regards to placement and

construction [3]. As several BIM platforms are renewed with new versions annually, the process of

3.3 Generative and Parametric Design in BIM 
“Designing and modelling free-form surfaces and curves as building elements which are associated 
with different components and have multiple patterns is not an easy job to do with traditional methods” 
– Z. Khabazi [20]. 
 
The purpose of this project is to investigate the benefits and challenges in modelling of 3D-
reinforcement and to compare it to the value in the usage of reinforcement in BIM. The key investigation 
point concerns complex geometry structures. As traditional BIM-tools display challenges when element 
geometries get complex, generative and computational design tools are needed. A survey amongst 
researchers, professionals and students by Abrishami et. Al. [21] found that the majority believe 
generative design tools can aid designers and BIM processes. The results are shown in Figure 11. 
Furthermore, solving difficult tasks and sparking new design ideas is the aim of generative systems, 
providing design assistance and extending designers’ current capabilities [22]. The introduction of 
generative design tools in the project was therefore introduced as a necessity.

Generative design is a design method where the output is generated or determined by a set of rules or 
an algorithm, normally by using a computer program. This is a more automated process than traditional 
design techniques, where designers model elements individually.

Generative design tools require little computer power compared to the complexity of the geometry it 
creates [24, p. 13]. This is because it replaces 3D-objects with object generating operations, where a 
shape is described by a sequence of definitions, rather than a large amount of data. This allows 
generative design tools to handle issues of e.g. complex geometry structures.  
Generative design is often, and in this project, combined with parametric modelling. The term 
parametric modelling can be defined as a process in which an element is modified and adjacent or 
connecting elements are automatically updated and adjusted to maintain the previously determined 
relationship [25]. This can for example be the size and position of a window in a wall or the position of 
a rebar in a concrete host element. Parametric modelling and BIM is in this regard closely related terms.  
This project mainly applies two BIM-tools; a generative (Dynamo) and a parametric (Revit). The 
generative methods in Dynamo controls the parameters in Revit, in this case the reinforcement design.

Dynamo is an open source generative design tool, and is also a freeware. Due to this, no licensing is

Dynamo is a visual programming tool applying a visual programming language (VPL). This separates 
it from traditional programming tools relying on the user’s competence in programming languages such 
as HTML, Java or C++. Such traditional scripting environments are usually text-based, where the user 
types in algorithms using a precise syntax. Such syntaxes are prone to omissions as they usually does 
not allow typos. VPL is a programming tool allowing the user to create programs and algorithms 
graphically, rather than defining it textually [27]. Dynamo, and many other VPLs, are based on boxes 
and strings, where the boxes represent entities and the strings represent the relations. This is exemplified 
in Figure 12, where the boxes (called nodes in Dynamo) are connected to form an arc by defining three 
points. This geometry can then be pushed to Revit and used for modelling purposes.

Dynamo is applied in this project as it is an incorporated add-in in Revit and the two programs are 
closely connected. Dynamo is specifically used to push a defined reinforcement design into Revit, 
where the reinforcement is geometrically placed in the reference project geometry. The generative 
features of Dynamo can also handle the complex geometry of the reference project, which is a key point 
for its application. Additionally, the VPL requires less programming knowledge of the designer (the 
author).

4.1 Building a Reinforcement Script in Dynamo 
To create rebar in Revit from Dynamo, one has to “push” geometry from Dynamo to Revit. The Dynamo 
for Rebar package contains a node that does this automatically, see Figure 15. The output of this node 
is rebar (can also be rebar container, grouping several rebar into a rebar set), which is created in Revit. 
This node is in most cases the final node in a rebar script and is therefore a good starting point to 
determine what is needed from the rest of the script. 
 
 
Figure 15: Rebar.ByCurve node in Dynamo. 
 
There are several inputs to the node. However, it is the curve and vector input that require the most 
work. The other inputs can be selected by drop-down selection nodes directly linked to the Revit content 
of the project. However, efforts can be put in to creating more automated processes here as well.  
The curve input is the geometry that Revit will use for rebar generation and the vector input defines the 
orientation of a plane in which e.g. rebar hooks should follow. A curve is defined as all sorts of curved 
(even if straight) shapes [30]. The curves can be created in several ways and this project utilizes two 
methods: one with the rebar following surface node from the Dynamo for Rebar package, and one where 
the curves are extracted and defined manually. The latter being most tedious is described first.

(lines) for rebar generation is later created on the selected face. These curves are the centerlines of the 
rebar (midpoint), representing the center placement of the rebar.

4.2.1 Modelling of Transverse and Longitudinal Reinforcement 
Rebar scripts for the modelling of transverse and longitudinal reinforcement were developed as 
described in section 4.1, where selecting a face and inputting number of rebar, the cover and bar 
diameter governs the design generation from Dynamo.     
Transverse reinforcement is the rebar that are placed perpendicular to the defined length of the structure, 
while longitudinal reinforcement is the rebar that follow the length of the structure. Such rebar is usually 
straight rebar (shape 00) or curved with a constant radius (shape 67). 
As previously shown, the rebar scripts in Dynamo can generate rebar fairly easy. However, one of the 
most important things of a rebar design is to ensure that the reinforcement cover is correct. If the 
reinforcement cover is incorrect when constructed it could lead to a weakened structure, while in a BIM 
the available room for placement of bars may be compromised – leading to an undesired design, 
affecting constructability and the quality of the structure itself. Therefore, efforts were invested to make 
scripts outputting correct cover for the reinforcement.  
As previously described, in order to get the cover relative to the selected surface correct one can apply 
the SurfaceOffset node in Dynamo. However, this only assures that the cover is correct toward one face, 
while two faces still remain, see Figure 29.

equipment that handle curved structures as well.

Regarding the accuracy of a model, reinforcement will be affected by building plans, where the 
requirement is that it have to be buildable. Therefore, if anything is modelled, it has to be buildable, 
TST explains. That means that there cannot be deviations between positioning of objects (heights, 
intersections). Furthermore, objects have to be connected so that the contractor does not have to wonder 
and ask; "what is thought here?", "what shall we do here?". Therefore, the model should be designed as 
it should be built. Hence there are two requirements; the solid geometry of the modelled object and the 
survey data (positioning of the object).  
TST says that every time the contractor is more effective in a project it should affect the contractors 
tendering/pricing in the next round/project. So, if the contractor can more efficiently place rebar and

the profit. However, as long as more contractors can deliver the same efficiency prices will drop, says

The design of a structure purchased from consultants is cheap compared to the contractor’s construction 
cost. TST says that if something construction related increases efficiency by 5% it has a big impact. 
This will usually not even come close with the associated increase of costs from the consultants. So, if 
the contractor builds more efficiently it does not really matter if the consultants have to spend more 
time. 
As a state directory, NPRA cannot say that the best software has to be used in their projects, because 
that will steer the market and hinder competition, TST continues. Therefore, they have to be soft-edged 
and say that data can be delivered in one way, but also in another. In this regard, NPRA account for 
both 2D-processes as well as BIM-processes. However, they try to push toward the information models. 
To summarize, TST says that if it is possible to reduce time spent on construction and build more 
efficiently (reduce costs and material waste) then 3D-reinforcement is very welcome. That is the case 
for everything that increases effectivity. However, if it is hard to design and only a few specialists can 
do it then there is the problem with compromising competition - so, the market must be mature for such 
requirements (multiple contenders have to be able to play).

The purpose of the interview was to gather information on a conventional (2D) reinforcement process

delivered to the contractor and building owner is on the format of 2D-drawings. The Revit model is also 
delivered, however only for visualization purposes. The contract on the project has 2D-drawings as the 
legally binding format of data exchange. 3D-reinforcement is therefore not implemented in the project 
and thus not included in the Revit model.  
BHK explains the procedure on how reinforcement drawings and bending schedules are produced in 
the project. The Revit model is used as a basis for generating the 2D reinforcement-drawings and the 
corresponding bending schedules. This is done based on a structural analysis of the static system. Plan 
views and sections are extracted from the model and annotated with the correct information required 
for the reinforcement workers to construct the rebar design. Thus, BIM is used in the reinforcement 
process, however only as a basis for 2D-data – a so called hybrid-solution.  
Drawbacks of the hybrid-process is that it is time consuming compared to full-BIM, BHK continues. 
First, the model is designed and then sections and plans are extracted from it, in which details are 
designed. The extracted sections and details are accurate representations of the model, and thus 
represent how the structure should be built. However, they do not correspond to current building codes 
and norms, as required in the project contract. Therefore, the consultant engineer has to annotate and 
add correct visualization features to the retrieved details from the model. This is a tedious and somewhat 
irrational process, BHK underlines. 
A benefit of the hybrid-process is the feature of BIM where if an object is changed in one project-view, 
then all corresponding views (sections, details, drawings) are updated with the change. In a 2D-process 
with no BIM, the designer would have to update every single corresponding drawing manually, 
increasing the risk of errors. This is an important point, as there are often many changes and revisions 
in a project, BHK continues.  
A hybrid solution is a better alternative than a 2D-process, BHK explains. There are by far many 
benefits by using BIM-tools in the design process. However, as the BIM is only used to generate 2D-
data, BHK feels that the benefits of using BIM is not fully achieved and points toward implementation 
of full BIM. BIM is not a new tool for production of conventional data sets, but rather a new way of 
working, BHK adds. 
BHK believes that when a model is made (e.g. in Revit), there is a small gap to bridge to implement 
3D-reinforcement, in which BHK feels there are several benefits. One is that it is a more intuitive way 
of designing, aiding less experienced reinforcement designers in their work as they might have little 
experience with 2D-drawing processes. Furthermore, with implementation of 3D-reinforcement in a 
full-BIM project, drawings would be eliminated. BHK explains that one drawing usually is estimated 
to be designed, quality checked and delivered within 20-25 hours. The current project she is involved 
in contains approximately 700 drawings. Additionally, full-scale BIM is a strong coordination tool 
between AEC-stakeholders and can eliminate costly additions from contractors based on unresolved 
issues.

element is updated or changed in the model. Both EN and LH experience that they save a lot of time by 
not having to quality check 2D-drawings, but rather having good checking routines in a BIM.

modelling, they add. The representation of reinforcement in BIM allows the designer to intuitively

little experience with 2D-drawings.

modelled in a BIM, a lot of work remains to extract 2D-drawings that meet the requirements in the 
Eurocode. On this basis, LH and EN believe that both conventional 2D-reinforcement as well as 3D-

7.2.1 Benefits and Challenges of the 3D-Reinforcement Modelling Process 
As shown, both in figures and in the model in the appendix, a replica of the 2D-reinforcement data from 
COWI has been modelled in 3D. As the reference project is defined as a complex structure, the model 
confirms that technology and procedures of today enable designers to design 3D-reinforcement in such 
structures. Still, challenges have been encountered in the modelling process toward obtaining this result.  
Modelling of rebar in a complex structure relies on additional generative design tools, such as Dynamo, 
as applied in this project. With the introduction of such tools, demands of the designer’s capabilities are 
set. However, as experienced in this project, the challenges in this workflow and process are overcoming 
for BIM-users even with basic programming skills and understanding.  
Concrete challenges have been found with the modelling of stirrups. As described in 4.2.2, there are no 
nodes in Dynamo that generate stirrups. The workaround for this was to model straight rebar and change 
the shape code in Revit. However, it was experienced that many of the stirrups in the project were 
automatically oriented by the east-west direction in the Revit project, as seen in Figure 58 (stirrups = 
green). To solve this, the stirrups were manually rotated in Revit – a time consuming process, where a 
better Dynamo code could solve the problem. However, the author assessed it as more important to 
replicate the 2D-data than to invest time in a script which ultimately could fail, resulting in the reference 
project not being modelled with 3D-reinforcement.  
It is not only the stirrups that require manual adjustments in Revit. As the model consists of a lot of 
rebar, it was necessary to adjust both longitudinal and transverse rebar to get a good visual 
representation.

8. Discussion 
The purpose of this project was to investigate where the benefits and challenges of implementing 3D-
reinforcement in a project with complex geometry lie. The problem statements were “In what ways can 
3D-reinforcement in complex structures enrich the concrete reinforcement supply chain?” and “How 
can stakeholders benefit from 3D-reinforcement in complex structures?” 
Through the applied methodology, results were obtained as qualitative data giving indications as to 
where the aforementioned points of interest were found. As the report is structured into two main parts 
of data retrieval, modelling and interviews, the discussion will follow the same structure. The two data 
sets of the modelling and the interviews represent the design phase and the construction phase.  
 
8.1  Design Phase Implementation 
The difference between a 2D and a 3D design phase, as defined in this report, is that a conventional 2D-
process relies on traditional 2D-reinforcement drawings and bending schedules for sharing of data, 
while a 3D-process utilizes reinforcement modelled in a BIM for the same purpose. A goal of 
implementing a BIM-process is to build more efficiently and to reduce time waste. Through both the 
modelling process of the reference project and the interviews, the 3D-modelling process was identified 
as time consuming. However, the design phase of a project is relative to the construction phase a very 
cheap part of a project. Therefore, increased time spent on designing and developing a BIM instead of 
2D-data is not a major issue. Furthermore, the application of reinforcement in BIM has been found to 
be time saving in the construction phase, thus compensating for the increased time investment in the 
design phase. Additionally, the abilities in BIM to more efficiently apply changes to a design than in a 
2D-project may as a project progresses catch up some of the time spent on the initial design. Still, these 
easy-change abilities in BIM may, without good quality checking routines, give unwanted changes to 
elements. Therefore, it is important to have the competence and routines necessary to ensure a sound 
reinforcement design in BIM.  
Implementing 3D-reinforcement requires some restructuring of resources and building of software 
competence. This is something that applies through all phases of a construction project and for all 
stakeholders. However, consulting engineers are familiar with CAD-tools as they play a big part of their 
everyday work. Therefore, restructuring and competence issues are seen to pose a bigger challenge for 
other groups than the consultants, which will be discussed in section 8.2. Still, when introducing 
complex geometry and generative design tools, the users’ abilities can be tested. There was found bugs 
and incompatibility issues in the modelling process which required workarounds or manual 
adjustments. This furthermore challenges the implementation in the design phase. Hopefully, and most 
likely, the continuous development of BIM-software will eliminate this issue. Furthermore, as

the need for generative design tools. Some add-ins for Revit incorporate such technology in a low 
threshold interface allowing the generation of rebar on complex surfaces without the need for additional 
programming tools. Still, these add-ins are relatively new and may not solve all geometries, whether it

There were found several benefits in the modelling process in this project concerning the design of 3D-
reinforcement. One was that the reuse value of rebar scripts in Dynamo is high. When a functioning 
rebar script has been developed it can be reused in the designer’s current project as well as for any 
future projects. This is something that increases the efficiency of designing rebar in BIM compared to 
a 2D-process where tools for 2D-drawing production have not got the same reuse ability. Furthermore, 
a rebar script can be shared with co-workers, increasing efficiency across a department or a design team. 
Increased efficiency has also been found as a result of the high degree of automation when applying 
generative design tools. The user can with a few mouse clicks, governing that a functioning rebar script 
is available, generate rebar without manually adjusting or placing any bars. Still, the automatically 
generated rebar design can compromise practicality and give buildability issues on site. Therefore, good 
quality checking routines including buildability considerations are important aspects of the 
implementation. Anyhow, as long as the generative design script is functioning, it cannot only increase 
efficiency but also reduce or eliminate sources of manual design errors.  
One interesting observation in the modelling of the reference project was that longitudinal bars 
following a curved surface was generated as shape 67 bars. Shape 67 defines the shape of the rebar with 
a length (A) and a constant radius (R), see Figure 64.

8.1.1  Hybrid Solutions 
Sørbye et.al. [3] proposed a hybrid process as an ideal process as they found both benefits and 
challenges in both 2D- and 3D-processes. However, the results in this report suggest that a 3D-process 
is a more superior workflow. Still, whether a hybrid solution is better than a 2D-process is uncertain. 
Results from the interviews in this report are somewhat inconclusive. Hybrid solutions require the 
development of two data sets (2D and 3D), which is a time consuming process. Still, reinforcement in 
BIM can be used to extract 2D-drawings and bending schedules. However, arguments indicate that the 
abilities the user have to more easily perform design changes in BIM give a hybrid solution a 
competitive edge over a 2D-process. Many also believe that the full potential of reinforcement in BIM 
is not achieved unless full-BIM and no 2D-data is applied.  
Interview 1 in the case study (section 6.2.1) revealed that an undergoing hybrid project contained 
approximately 700 drawings, where one drawing is estimated of being designed, quality checked and 
delivered within 20-25 hours. Still, by implementing full-BIM all these hours are not freed up as it also 
takes time to design reinforcement in a BIM. Therefore, it would be interesting to see a quantification 
of data in future projects. However, such quantifications would most likely require two identical data 
sets in the same project, or two adequately similar projects, which could be hard to come by and to 
realize. This is one of the reasons why this project utilized a qualitative data approach. Furthermore, 
researching such quantification projects in a lab with e.g. a beam both designed in 2D and 3D will most 
likely not be representative as there are many factors affecting the real life construction on site. These 
arguments therefore challenge the possibilities of getting good quality quantitative data regarding the 
subject.  
 
8.2 Construction Phase Implementation 
When implementing BIM in the construction phase, one meets the challenge of utilizing BIM outside 
an office environment – on the construction site. However, it has been found that by applying tablets 
and BIM-containers it is achieved to get the required data all the way out into the construction pits. Still, 
it is concerning coordination and organizing where the largest benefits are found. The use of BIM 
physically on the site is only a consequence of eliminating 2D-drawings. Nevertheless, to achieve a full 
implementation, solutions concerning this are also important.  
The construction phase involves the contractor, building owner and rebar manufacturer. The consultants 
are also involved, however their role and work processes do not differ from the design phase to any 
mentionable degree for the purpose of this project. The contractor is found to have benefits in planning 
and organization with 3D-reinforcement. The digital reinforcement allows foremen and planners to 
accurately and quickly retrieve the amount of reinforcement they need and to order it from the 
manufacturer. With BIM-tools such as NavisWorks, the contractor can keep a better overview than with 
traditional 2D-tools. From the interview with Aas Jakobsen it was found that for a part of the concrete 
structures, the contractor spent one afternoon calculating the quantity of rebar he needed, whereas in a 
traditional 2D-process this would take around two working weeks. This is something that came up in 
all the interviews, that 3D-reinforcement is a superior tool for quantities and information takeoffs 
(ITOs) compared to traditional 2D-data. Furthermore, through the interview with NCC (contractor) it 
was found that by ordering reinforcement from a BIM, the exact number of rebar needed was delivered. 
In a 2D-process, there is typically ordered one or two too many rebar per position number. These extra 
rebar not only increases cost, but increase time-waste as workers need to dispose the extra rebar.

Isolated, this waste and increased cost is not significantly high. However, this could amount to notable 
figures when accumulated. Moreover, the NPRA (building owner) indicate that if it is possible to reduce 
time spent on construction and build more efficiently, reduce costs and material waste, then 3D-
reinforcement is a technology that is welcomed. 
With changes and revisions in projects the contractor can have a good overview in BIM, ensuring that 
the he does not have excessive amounts rebar laying all over the site. This helps to not binding capital 
in construction materials at stages where it is unnecessary and ensuring that he has not ordered rebar 
for later construction stages that end up being changed and no longer needed. Furthermore, the 
contractor can color mark rebar in the model by different status parameters, e.g. ordered, delivered, 
constructed, and by this keep a good overview of the construction process. Moreover, the same goes for 
the consultant that can color mark a model after what data is delivered and what is not, typically parts 
of a structure that has or has not been quality checked yet. Changes have also been found to be time 
consuming for consultants in 2D-processes. By implementing 3D-reinforcement, changes can be 
handled more efficiently.  
BIM, and 3D-reinforcement, is a solid tool for coordination. The BIM can be used in meetings where 
all stakeholders intuitively can see and explain through the visualization on a big screen. This allows 
for effective planning of complex areas that may cause problems on the site. This is beneficial for both 
the contractor and the building owner as problems on site usually causes delays, which are expensive. 
Furthermore, the elimination of 2D-drawings reduces the preparation time prior to meetings as one does 
not need to review any drawings. The amount of drawings that need to be reviewed can require several 
hours of work prior to coordination meetings. When BIM and 3D-reinforcement is implemented, these 
hours can be spent on other purposeful tasks.  
Prefabrication of rebar cages is profitable for contractors, and thus for building owners as well, as a 
rebar design can be built more efficiently. 3D-reinforcement can be used as data for this. Still, there 
have not been found any data favoring reinforcement in BIM over 2D concerning prefabrication. Rebar 
manufacturers cannot use the BIM to automate their prefabrication processes in addition to 
prefabrication for complex geometry structures being very limited. As of today, curved structures are 
irrational to prefabricate rebar cages for. The prefabrication processes favor assembly line structures, 
such as columns and beams. There is also an issue with transportation. The reference project is divided 
into three independent sections all weighing in at about eight metric tons with an unfolded length of 
eight meters. The highest section is almost five meters high. Transporting these sections as prefabricated 
rebar cages would pose challenges. Another question is also if the rebar cages would maintain their 
shape.  
An issue in the construction phase is the implementation of BIM out on the site. However, as seen in 
the case study it is achievable. Still, lack of competence complicates the use of a 3D-reinforcement 
model, as well as competence may fade if not conserved. It is therefore important to have competent 
persons at the various stakeholders ensuring that the competence is maintained as well as spread out to 
more.

8.3 Requirements for Implementing 3D-Reinforcement 
8.3.1 Who Holds the Key? 
Through this project it has been found that building owners are the stakeholders holding the key for 
implementing 3D-reinforcement in structures with complex geometry. Especially in bridge projects, 
where the Norwegian Public Roads Administration (NPRA) require 2D-drawings in their projects. This 
only opens up for hybrid-solutions as the use of conventional methods cannot be avoided. However, 
complex concrete structures are found in other projects than road and bridge projects, such as the 
hydropower plant in the case study. The building owner here is not steered by governing handbooks 
and can more freely fund what they want. It is the building owner that funds a project and what they 
order is therefore the governing factor. Still, from the interview with the NPRA bridge department it is 
seen that there are forces, if not pulling toward implementing 3D-reinforcement, then at least 
investigating the possibilities for it. However, it is found that the NPRA depend on positive experiences 
from other projects to apply new technology in their own.  
The NPRA is a governmental institution and is therefore obliged to not hinder market competition. This 
is something that slows down implementation of 3D-reinforcement as the general level and capabilities 
in BIM-software must be equal for the majority of BIM-software and not just only for one. This applies 
to all software the NPRA utilizes, not just concerning reinforcement, which means that they must wait 
for the general software level to be sufficiently high before requiring the available technology. In other 
words, they cannot require that data to be delivered through a format only one program can offer. 
However, private building owners do not need to take this consideration and stand freely to choose that 
the best available technology should be used in their projects.  
 
8.3.2 Contract Types 
As different types of building owners can set different requirements, it is also found that the different 
types of contracts a project is based on can serve as better for implementation of 3D-reinforcement. 
Through a turnkey contract the contractor is responsible for the design of the project and not the building 
owner. This means that the contractor can decide what type of data the consultants shall deliver. If both 
the contractor and the consultant have the competence and willingness of implementing 3D-
reinforcement, a turnkey contract seems more realizable for the implementation. Built to order contracts 
are contract types where the building owner has the responsibility for the design phase and for anything 
that goes wrong. Such contracts may therefore fuel reluctance toward new and untested technology to 
a larger extent as building owners can be conservative and may have rules on how data is to be delivered.  
 
8.3.3 General Requiring Factors 
For a successful implementation, communication between stakeholders and good coordination is a key 
factor. As discussed, this is something that can be achieved better trough BIM. However, it is also 
important to have good routines ensuring an optimal workflow through the project phases. This includes 
the building owner (or the stakeholder responsible for the design) setting clear requirements for what 
should be delivered, while the consultants produce data to these specifications. The data must be of a 
sufficiently high standard so that the contractor can build what is described. To ensure this, quality is

warranted through quality checking routines and communication between stakeholders to solve 
potential issues and challenges. Still, there are competence and willingness issues that has to be 
overcome for contractors and building owners. 
BIM-competence is also a requirement. As the technology has developed over the last years, so has the 
competence. Still, the AEC-industry is a conservative industry, where many participants are 
comfortable with conventional procedures and are reluctant toward new technology. It is therefore 
important to have high competent persons at all the stakeholders, supporting the ones that are less 
competent. Furthermore, the software need to be able to handle all challenges and requirements that are 
set for building as described. Through this project, it has been found that the BIM-software of today is 
able to handle 3D-reinforcement to a sufficiently high degree. The continuous development of the 
software is also seen as a positive trend in this regard.  
Lastly, positive experiences are important to further fuel the willingness to implement new technology 
such as 3D-reinforcement. If a project goes bad, the project participants may for future projects want 
known procedures and data such as 2D-methods.  
 
8.4 Discussion Summarized 
Through the project both benefits and challenges have been discovered by implementing 3D-
reinforcement both in complex geometry structures as well as regular, non-complex, structures. The 
data retrieved in this report is qualitative. Concrete benefits and challenges have therefore not been 
quantified, which could give more explicable results. However, the benefits of implementing 3D-
reinforcement are seen to reduce waste in the form of time and material use, as well as it can reduce or 
eliminate the chance of manual errors. Furthermore, it allows for a more intuitive coordination between 
stakeholders as well as provide designers with a strong quality checking tool. Therefore, 3D-
reinforcement is seen to add more value to the AEC-industry than traditional 2D-reinforcement. 
Concerning the implementation on site it is seen through the case study that 3D-reinforcement can be 
used on site to build reinforcement. The development over the last years has also been positive, cf. 
Figure 56, indicating that more and more see the value and that the competence is increasing. Still, 
implementation come with certain challenges. Conservatism or reluctance as well as competence issues 
are found to be key aspects here. Therefore, the implementation of 3D-reinforcement comes with certain 
conditions for an optimal workflow.

9. Conclusion 
This project has investigated and mapped the benefits and challenges of implementing 3D-
reinforcement in a complex structure. Both aspects have been found for all the main stakeholders 
(building owner, consultant, manufacturer and contractor) at both the design phase and in the 
construction phase. If implemented properly, 3D-reinforcement have several benefits over a 
conventional 2D-process. Still, the stakeholders need to see these benefits to accept the technology.  
The modelling of 3D-reinforcement in a complex geometry structure has been found through this 
project to be time consuming but achievable. The introduction of generative design tools increases the 
threshold for designers but can automate processes and have a high reuse value. Once a functioning 
rebar script is made it can be used for similar rebar throughout a project as well as in coming projects. 
Still, consultants initially may have to restructure processes to adopt the new ways of working. It has 
also been found that the modelling of a structure with 3D-reinforcement is more time consuming than 
if it was designed only in 2D. This is because all reinforcement has to be modelled in 3D, where the 
2D-process only require the modelling/drawing of a representative selection. Still, the time spent on 
changes in a BIM is far less than in 2D-projects, reducing the difference in favor of 3D-reinforcement. 
In fact, through a project life cycle this may result in less time spent in total on the production of the 
construction data. However, the time and cost spent in the design phase is relatively low compared to 
the cost of construction. Therefore, even if 3D-reinforcement comes with an increase of the design cost, 
it can result in increased efficiency in the construction phase and lead to a lower total cost of a project. 
And, this report found that the implementation of 3D-reinforcement can lead to savings in the 
construction phase.  
The contractor is dependent on a solid data basis for construction, regardless of the format in which it 
is delivered, to build efficiently. Delays and production stops on a construction site are very expensive, 
both for the contractor and the building owner. Therefore, the quality of the data delivered by 
consultants must be high, not only for the rebar workers but also for communication and planning 
between stakeholders and for foremen. This is where 3D-reinforcement has been found to be a better 
tool than 2D-drawings. It is visually stronger, as it does not rely on codes and symbols to represent 
rebar. Furthermore, all the rebar can be found in one place – the model, and not on multiple drawings. 
The reinforcement model can be shared on a projector screen and elements in the model can be 
temporary hidden, tagged with information and ordered in a building sequence. By applying 3D-
reinforcement, these aspects have shown to increase the efficiency of meetings and communication 
between stakeholder. Furthermore, issues and challenges in the construction phase are discovered at an 
earlier stage and can be planned and avoided, avoiding construction stops.  
The contractor can benefit from 3D-reinforcement in the planning and ordering phases during 
construction. The digital reinforcement allows for exact ordering of rebar, reducing both the risk of 
89

manual error as well as reducing the time spent on calculating the desired quantities. This also leads to 
precise deliveries of rebar on site, where logistics and handling can be improved by the implementation.  
Rebar manufacturers are also interested in implementation of 3D-reinforcement as it optimizes their 
workflow by reducing manual labor and automating their processes. They are also served by offering 
additional services, such as prefabrication of rebar cages. Still, prefabrication of rebar cages is pt. not 
seen to be enhanced with the implementation of 3D-reinforcement. Furthermore, complex geometry 
structures complicate the usefulness of prefabricated rebar cages and its construction. Still, contractors 
can use the 3D-reinforcement for small-scale prefabrication on site.  
To be able to implement 3D-reinforcement in projects with complex geometry, one cannot pt. avoid the 
requirements and regulations set by building owners such as in the bridge handbook of the Norwegian 
Public Roads Administration (NPRA). Therefore, the key of the implementation is held by the building 
owners. They need to order or require 3D-reinforcement for it to be implemented. Still, there are 
contract types leaving the contractor with the design responsibility, opening up for an easier 
implementation. This governs that the contractor has the competence and the willingness to work in 
full-BIM, which also applies generally. Furthermore, there are also other types of projects with complex 
geometry, such as hydropower plants, where the building owner is a private stakeholder. These building 
owners usually do not govern certain requirements on which format the building data should be 
delivered, also opening up for an implementation of 3D-reinforcement.  
The two major challenges of implementing 3D-reinforcement in the design and construction phase of a 
project is software competence and conservative regulations. For a successful implementation it is 
therefore important to have key persons with high competence in positions where they can assist and 
train others. This both applies to the consultants and the contractors. Low competence transmits to 
reluctance and can be the deciding factor in a project. Therefore, the key competent persons need to 
communicate, not only internally but also to the other stakeholders in the project, so that good routines 
and processes are established for a solid implementation through all phases. Still, the conservative 
regulations are hard to come by, but sharing of positive experiences and successes through projects are 
two factors seen to influence opinions.  
Ultimately, this project sought out to compare the challenges of implementing 3D-reinforcement in a 
complex structure to the value and benefits of its use. The design of digital reinforcement in a reference 
project investigated the challenges of the design process itself, while interviews investigated the benefits 
and challenges through other parts of a typical project. It is concluded that current software programs 
allow designers to successfully design digital reinforcement in a complex geometry structure. There are 
competence and time issues connected with this. However, a high reuse and automation value is found, 
and the ease of performing changes in BIM is superior compared to a 2D-process. Furthermore, the 
main stakeholders of a project can utilize 3D-reinforcement as a stronger communication and 
coordination tool. It is a better tool for construction planning than traditional 2D-data and it can 
eliminate manual calculation errors and significantly reduce the time required for ordering rebar, aiding 
both contractors and manufacturers. This is something that can affect the prizing contractors offer in 
tenders, reflecting in a reduced cost for the building owner in coming projects. Still, the main challenges 
in the construction phase are software competence and conservatism. AEC professionals are 
comfortable with traditional working methods and the industry is conservative. Positive experiences, 
successful projects and competent persons are factors that can aid the industry to move toward a change 
in work methods and embracing 3D-reinforcement. There is currently an increase in projects utilizing 
the technology, indicating that wheels are set in motion toward digital reinforcement.

10. Future Work 
The limitations of this project allows for investigation of other aspects of implementing 3D-
reinforcement in future work and studies: 
 
In-depth case study, where a quantification of results is obtained. 
This project has obtained qualitative results. To get a more direct basis for comparison, 
quantification of time and economy in a 2D- and a 3D-project or processes can be performed.  
 
FEM-based generation of 3D-reinforcement 
Recent software releases enable the user to automatically generate reinforcement in a structure 
based on test results from a FEM-analysis, such as Sofistik [35]. Investigation of such 
automation tools for increased efficiency in the design phase can be of interest. 
 
Contract types 
Through this project it was discovered that some contract types may be more suited for the 
implementation of 3D-reinforcement. As this was something that was not initially defined as a 
research area, little study was done on the subject. 
 
Use of models on the construction site 
This thesis’ main focus was on the design and modelling phase. It could be interesting to take 
a deeper dive into how the model is used on a construction site as it may be the contractor that 
has the highest potential benefit.  
 
Pre/post-stressed reinforcement 
Investigate modelling aspects of pre-/post-stressed rebar.

Execution plan – Case Study: Vamma 12 
Research Goals 
The goal of the case study is to gather experiences and information from an ongoing project where 3D-
reinforcement is fully implemented. This will serve as the main objective of the case study and is 
believed to give valuable data for the thesis. Furthermore, an investigation of additional software 
beyond Dynamo will be taken on as a secondary objective. The purpose of this will be to investigate if 
an optimized design process could include multiple software add-ins, or if a one-program-process is 
adequate. The investigation of the secondary objective relies on the availability of such a program, 
initially thought to be Sofistik, and is dependent of the availability at Norconsult.  
With this background, the following research goals have been developed. 
• 
Identify the benefits of implementing 3D-reinforcement from an ongoing project. 
• 
Identify the workflow of implementing 3D-reinforcement from an ongoing project. 
• 
Explore new software for implementation of 3D-reinforcement (Sofistik). 
 
Research Questions 
To meet the research goals of the case study the following research questions have been developed. 
• 
How does the contractor use the implemented 3D-reinforcement? 
o How has this affected the contractors work process? 
o What benefits/challenges are there? 
• 
How is the workflow in the project? 
o How is the interaction between the different participants in the process? 
• 
In what ways can multiple BIM-software tools enrich the concrete reinforcement design 
process? 
 
Methodology 
Inspections will be carried out to get firsthand experience with 3D-reinforcement implemented on a 
construction site. Initially, one inspection is thought sufficient, however more can be included if seen 
valuable for the thesis. In these inspections, observations and conversations will be carried out where 
key figures at the contractor (and any other relevant project participant) will be approached for 
information retrieval. These figures will typically be concrete foremen, reinforcement workers and 
project leaders.  
Additionally, the author will partake in the project by physically being present at Norconsults project 
office in Sandvika, Norway. Experiences from observing and sitting in the project environment is 
presumed to give valuable data for the report. Mapping of the consulting engineers work process will 
be determined by carrying out conversations/interviews.  
This methodology is intended to supply information on the full process of implementing 3D-
reinforcement on a full scale, between all participants and out into the construction pit. This will suit as

a data basis in which is believed to serve beneficial for the mapping of experiences in projects with 3D-
reinforcement.  
The secondary objective of the case study will be investigated by applying different software to a Revit 
model to develop a reinforcement design. This methodology is intended to provide information on how 
to optimize the design process of a 3D-reinforcement model.  
 
Limitations 
The case study will investigate the reinforcement process defined as the process associated with in-situ 
cast structures. This process consists of: 
• 
The design phase, at the consulting engineer. 
• 
The production and delivery of reinforcement, by the reinforcement steel manufacturer. 
• 
Placement of reinforcement in the formwork and casting of concrete, by the contractor. 
As the master thesis only considers the modelling aspects of the reinforcement design process, the case 
study will not include a structural analysis to determine the reinforcement design in the design phase. 
However, if there is not developed a reinforcement design there will be made assumptions and estimates 
to create a plausible reinforcement design. 
The software limitations will only include Revit and Sofistik as well as a potential combination with 
Dynamo. This is due to the relative short time available for the case study.

EB emphasizes that in projects were disciplines and stakeholders are dependent of each other's work 
(almost every project), it is important to eliminate time waste. One concrete potential time waste is the 
production of bending schedule at the consultants, which is something that needs to be manually 
produced and then also quality controlled. Time wasting is also found in changes and revisions of the 
data basis. The benefits will correspond to the size of a project, where big projects will have big benefits 
and so on, with eliminating time waste. Reducing time waste can be done by eliminating bending 
schedules, EB feels.  
The important point is that you don't only eliminate the production time, but also dependencies of the 
process of making e.g. the bending schedule or revisions, EB underlines. The overall goal is to reduce 
waste, time waste, and optimize the processes for the consultants, contractors and building owners by 
eliminating unnecessary time spending on tasks that shouldn't be necessary, working more efficient.
