Abstract
Clinical research show that the left atrial appendage is correlated with
several cardiac diseases such as atrial thrombus formation in atrial fib-
rillation. Occlusion of the left atrial appendage from the left atrium is
preventing strategy to reduced the risk of cardiac diseases. However, the
impact of the left atrial appendage on the left atrial hemodynamics has
not yet been fully elucidated. Recent advances within numerical analysis
have made numerical modelling an essential tool within diagnostics and
research in the cardiovascular field. So far, numerical models which serve
the purpose of investigating the hemodynamics of the left atrium lack
clinical relevance.
In this thesis, a realistic computational fluid dynamics model and a fluid
structure interaction model are developed with the aim of investigating
the impact of the left atrial appendage on the left atrial hemodynamics
in healthy subjects. Within each numerical technique, a model of the left
atrium with and without the left atrial appendage is created and compared
on several hemodynamic parameters. A realistic geometry is created by
the segmentation of cardiac computed tomography scans and physiologi-
cal boundary conditions are obtained by processing data from magnetic
resonance imaging.
The results show that the models represent the in vivo flow environment
of the left atrium. By assessing several hemodynamic parameters, it is
concluded that the left atrial appendage has an insignificant impact on
the left atrial hemodynamics. The models developed in this project are
furthermore proposed as a tool in contributing to novel clinical research
and diagnostics within cardiovascular diseases.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Cardiovascular diseases(CVD) are the number one cause of global mortality and are
also a major contributor of disabilities causing a reduced quality of life [56]. With an
increase in CVD deaths of 21% from 2007 to 2017 and 17.8 million deaths worldwide
in 2017, CVD is a major health issue [56].
Diagnosis of cardiac diseases is mainly done through different imaging modalities like
Computed Tomography(CT), Magnetic Resonance Imaging(MRI) or Doppler echocar-
diography. However, the imaging techniques have limitations and might be invasive.
In recent years, numerical studies open a new chapter in clinical studies and im-
proved diagnostic procedures by helping clinicians conduct thorough investigations
on cardiac dynamics and hemodynamics [76][1][10]. In this project, the impact of
Left Atrial Appendage(LAA) on the Left Atrium(LA) hemodynamics is investigated
through numerical analysis. To this aim, a realistic holistic LA geometry is recon-
structed from CT images on which Computational Fluid Dynamics(CFD) and Fluid
Structure Interaction(FSI) simulations are performed. Finally, hemodynamic indices
of the LA model with and without LAA are compared.
1.1
Motivation
The LA and LAA are related to many CVDs like LA fibrillation, LA enlargement, LA
hypertrophy, pulmonary hypertension etc. [6]. The local hemodynamics of the LA
and the LAA are closely related to the function of the heart and can act as a biomarker
for cardiac health. The development within imaging techniques has provided useful
tools for evaluating cardiac hemodynamics. Yet, these techniques contain limitations
given by the relatively low spatial and temporal resolution[9]. Clinical research also
shows that measurements in some regions of the heart like the LAA is diﬀicult to
perform[76]. By combining computer aided numerical analysis and medical imaging
modalities, it is possible to simulate physiological and pathological scenarios to inves-
tigate cardiac hemodynamics in different conditions. Numerical analysis constitutes
a non-invasive method for obtaining and predicting important local hemodynamic pa-
rameters such as vorticity, pressure and velocity, which can have great clinical impact
[40]. Simulation of in vivo cardiac environments can therefore act as a powerful tool
for diagnostics and increasing knowledge in cardiovascular physiology.
Most numerical studies of the left heart focus on the left ventricle[9][66].
In con-
ducted studies of the physiological LA, it is often seen that chamber morphology or
boundary conditions have been simplified[76]. These model simplifications question

2.1
Clinical Background
To model the LA and LAA, it is essential to acquire a thorough understanding of
the anatomy and function of these structures. In this section, the physiology of the
human heart and the anatomy and function of the LA and LAA are presented as a
theoretical foundation for the numerical analysis.
2.1.1
Physiology of the Human Heart
The human heart can be divided into the left side and the right side, each consisting
of 2 chambers (figure 2.1). The two atria are the upper chambers, which receives
the blood before it is pumped into the ventricles. One cardiac cycle is considered
as the cardiac muscles performance from one heartbeat to the next. Each heartbeat
consists of a diastolic period, where the ventricular muscles relax and the ventricles fill
with blood and a systolic period, where the ventricular muscles contract and blood is
ejected. Deoxygenated blood returns to the right atrium via the superior and inferior
vena cava. During the diastolic phase, the ventricular muscles relax and blood fills
the right ventricle through the tricuspid valve, which connects the two chambers.
When the right ventricle contracts, the blood is sent to the lungs via the pulmonary
artery through the pulmonary valve. The gas exchange occurs in the lungs, where
carbon dioxide is removed and oxygen is absorbed into the blood. Oxygenated blood
then enters the left atrium(LA) through the pulmonary veins(PVs). During early
diastole, the mitral valve(MV) opens and the left ventricle(LV) fills with blood. In
the late diastole the LV contracts and pumps blood through the aortic valve and into
the aorta, from where the blood travels through the systemic circulation. The total
volume within the heart is found to be constant during the cycle [2].
==PAGE:28==
4
2 Theory
Figure 2.1: Anatomy of the human heart. The arrows show the direction of blood
flow. Illustration from [53].
The cardiac cycle can be divided into 7 phases. The phases are distinguished by the
spatial pressure gradient across the chambers due to contraction and relaxation of
the cardiac muscles, which cause the atrioventricular(AV) valves to open and close.
The phases are explained below and the respective pressure profiles of the left heart
can be seen in figure 2.2.
1. Atrial contraction
The atria contract and the atrial pressures increase within the chamber, which
forces the AV valves to open and rapidly fill the ventricles with blood. The
peak in the left atrial pressure(LAP) is called the ’a-wave’.
2. Isovolumetric contraction
Due to filling of the ventricles, the intraventricular pressures exceed the atrial
pressures, which force the AV valves to close. The ventricular pressures con-
tinue to rise rapidly since the ventricles contract whilst all valves are closed.
It is said to be isovolumetric since the ventricular volumes remain unchanged.
However, the shape of the ventricles changes with an increase in circumference
and decrease of the atrial base-apex length. Bulging of the mitral valve(MV)
leaflets cause a minor increase in the LAP seen as the ’c-wave’.
3. Rapid ejection
When the ventricular pressures exceed the pressure within the pulmonary artery
and aorta, the respective valves open and blood is ejected. Since the MV plane
is pulled down and the LA expands, the LAP initially decrease. However, LAP
quickly returns to normal state, when the chambers are filled with blood.
==PAGE:29==
2.1 Clinical Background
5
4. Reduced ejection
The ventricles repolarize, meaning that the active contraction reduces. Outflow
of the ventricles still happens due to the kinetic energy of the blood. Yet, the
flowrates and ventricular pressures decrease. The LAP continuously increases
due to inflow from the PVs.
5. Isovolumetric relaxation
The pulmonary and aortic valve close, whenever the ventricular pressures have
decreased suﬀiciently. The relaxation of the ventricles occurs isovolumetric due
to all valves being closed. A peak in the LAP is seen at the end of the phase
and is called the ’v-wave’.
6. Rapid filling
The AV valves open when the ventricular pressures are below the atrial pressures
causing a passive filling of the ventricles. The LAP drops significantly, triggered
by the MV opening, which produces the ’v-wave’.
7. Reduced filling
The filling of the ventricles are continued in this phase, causing the intraventric-
ular pressures to slowly rise. This induces a reduced pressure gradient across
the AV valves, which reduces the rate of the filling. 90 % of the ventricular
filling occurs passively during the diastole and before the atrial contraction.

2.1.2
Anatomy of the Left Atrium and Left Atrial Appendage
The LA is one of the hearts four chambers and forms the posterior base of the heart.
The LA is superiorly situated relative to the right atrium and they are separated by
the inter-atrial septum [71]. The oval-shaped chamber is positioned anterior to the
esophagus and located at the 5th to 7th thoracic vertebra above the LV. The aortic
root including the aortic valve is positioned at the left ventricular outlet, whereas the
ascending aorta runs along the wall of the LA chamber [48] [71]. Four PVs enter the
LA chamber at the posterior wall, with veins from the right lung entering the septal
side and veins from the left lung entering the lateral side of the chamber. However, the
precise location and branching of the PV’s contain large inter-patient variability[10].
The PVs perfuse the chamber with oxygenated blood from the lungs to be pumped
into the LV through the MV, which is positioned at the base of the LA chamber.
The MV consists of an anterior and posterior leaflet surrounded by a fibrous ring
called the mitral annulus, which acts as an insertion site for the leaflets. The area
surrounding the LA outlet is called the vestibule.

by [71].
The LAA protrudes from the left atrial cavity via a narrow orifice and wraps around

Atrial tissue is a highly compliant material and exhibits huge elastic deformations
during the cardiac cycle resulting in a nonlinear stress-strain response [26].
The total wall thickness is non-uniform and ranges from 1.1 to 6.5 mm [62][25][68]

2.1.3
Left Atrial Function
The main function of the LA is the left ventricular filling. In diseased hearts, where
remodelling of the LV chamber impairs the cardiac function, the LA is able to com-
pensate to maintain an optimal cardiac output. The cardiac performance, therefore,
relies highly on the mechanical functions of the LA. The mechanical functions of the
LA consist of passive and active filling and can be divided into three phases, which
modulates the filling of the LV [55]. In the first phase, the LA chamber acts as a reser-
voir, where oxygenated blood from the PVs fills the chamber. The reservoir phase
occurs during the ventricular systole, where the MV is closed and LV contracts. The
contraction of the LV causes the MV plane to descend due to the shortening of the LV,
==PAGE:32==
8
2 Theory
which increases the volume of the LA and decreases the atrial pressure. This creates
a suction effect, which allows the atrial filling. The contraction of LV during systole
is therefore responsible for the left atrial filling [2]. The venous return then causes the
left atrial pressure to rise and the volume to increase while the chamber expands [5].
At this point, the LAP is larger than the LVP, which creates a pressure gradient that
causes the MV to open. The second phase occurs during the early diastole, where the
LA functions as a conduit, passively filling the LV due to the pressure gradient. The
conduit phase constitutes the main part of the left ventricular filling. Eventually, the
intra-atrial pressure equalizes with the LVP, where the left ventricular filling is highly
modulated by the diastolic properties of the LV [41]. In the third phase, during late
diastole, the LA functions as a pump as the LA contracts and empty the remaining
blood into the LV. As the atrial muscles contract, the pressure within the LA exceeds
both the LVP and the PV pressure, allowing blood to fill the LV and cause minor
backflow of blood into the PVs [41]. The left atrial function is commonly described
by the relationship between the LAP and volume change during one cardiac cycle,
given by figure 2.5 and is used to assess the atrial function. The relationship creates
a closed loop, consisting of two lopes; the left lower volume lope, called the ’A-loop’,
and the right higher volume lope, is called the ’V-loop’. The A loop is caused by the
active phase of the LA and the V-loop is caused by the passive phases of the LA. In
cardiac diseases, where the LAP and/or volume is affected, LA remodelling occurs
and alters the pressure-volume loop [70].

2.1.3.1
LA Appendage Function
The LAA has a unique movement pattern due to two phases of inflow and two phases
of outflow during the cardiac cycle. During atrial contraction, a clear outflow is seen,
which is followed by the systole, where inflow is seen. The final two minor flow waves
are created by the ventricular relaxation and the elasticity in the LAA wall. The
cardiovascular role of the LAA has been considered relatively insignificant [57]. How-
ever, since the LAA is more compliant than the remaining LA chamber [11], it can
act as a blood reservoir yielding homeostatically adaptive responses, when the pres-
sure or volume of the LA increases either physiologically or pathologically, with the
purpose of improving important cardiac parameters as the circulating blood volume
and the cardiac output [51]. It has become well-known that the LAA structure is
correlated with different cardiac diseases. The LAA is a common site of thrombus
formation, especially in patients with Atrial Fibrillation(AF) and induce a great risk
of stroke for those patients. The elongated shape of the appendage, predispose the
structure for blood stagnation, which is highly associated with thrombus embolism.
Yet, the association between AF and LAA dysfunction has not been fully elucidated
[57]. On the contrary, it has been suggested that the washout effect of the LAA in
healthy subjects prevent the blood stagnation associated with thrombus formation
[51]. Most patients with AF are treated with anticoagulant drugs to reduce the risk
of clots forming. This treatment has shown to be very effective, yet some patients
have contraindications to blood-thinning medicine. For these patients, LAA obliter-
ation is an alternative strategy to reduce the risk of stroke. Surgical amputation or
implantation of occlusion devices, which limit or fully exclude blood from entering
the LAA have shown promising results [37]. The physiological and hemodynamic
effects of LAA obliteration are not fully evaluated, yet some studies show that the
procedure is associated with increased LAP and transmitral flow velocity [11][31].
2.2
Fluid Dynamics
Fluid dynamics is a sub-branch within fluid mechanics and investigates the motion of
fluids. In the human circulatory system, complex flow patterns arise, where blood is
the fluid of interest. In this section, the foundation of what to take into consideration
when modelling blood flow in a heart chamber is established.
2.2.1
Blood as a Fluid
Blood is a heterogenous fluid consisting of deformable cellular elements immersed
into plasma fluid. The cellular elements consist of Red Blood Cells(RBCs), White
Blood Cells(WBCs) and platelets, where RBCs are volumetric the most dominating
blood cell. Plasma mainly consists of water with a suspension of particles like pro-
tein, electrolytes and organic molecules [3]. Blood and its mechanical properties can
therefore be modelled as a fluid containing particles. Both of these phases and their
ratio towards each other affect the blood properties.
A fluid is considered newtonian, if the viscosity of the fluid is constant and act as the
constant proportionality between shear stress and shear rate. This applies for water,
==PAGE:34==
10
2 Theory
which is the main content of blood plasma. As a whole, blood have shear-thinning
properties.Hence, depending on the shear rate, the viscosity of the blood changes,
which makes blood act as a non-newtonian fluid. The non-newtonian properties of
blood are depending on the presence and interaction of cellular elements and are
mainly significant, when the particle sizes are comparable with the lumen size. In
this project, the blood flow is investigated in a chamber. When there is a relatively
large distance between the solid boundaries, the thermal loss and changes in the vis-
cosity of the fluid flow can be neglected. This means that the deformability of RBCs
and their ability to pass through small diameter vessels is negligible. Hence, since
the viscosity variation is mostly significant in smaller vessels(<0.5mm), blood can
be assumed newtonian, which simplifies the constitutive models used to describe the
motion of blood in numerical analysis. Furthermore, blood is practically an incom-
pressible fluid, which indicates that when a force is applied to the fluid, the density
remains the same to a considerable degree [3].
2.2.2
The Navier-Stokes Equations
In the field of fluid dynamics, Navier-Stokes equations are partial differential equa-
tions(PDEs), which describe the motion of fluid expressed by conservation of physical
properties. By solving these equations together with a set of boundary conditions for
a complex numerical model, parameters such as fluid velocity and pressure can be
computed for a specific geometry.
The equations can be simplified depending on
the characteristics of the fluid, e.g. whether the fluid flow is laminar or turbulent,
newtonian or non-newtonian or if the fluid is compressible or incompressible. In this
section, the characteristics of blood will be used as a reference.
The first of Navier-Stokes equations, which are solved for in the COMSOL interface,
are called the continuity equation and simply describe the conservation of mass. For
incompressible fluids the conservation of mass is described as [22]:
∇· ⃗u = 0,
(2.1)
where ∇is the differential operator, nabla, and ⃗u is the velocity vector given in m/s.
Equation 2.1 express how the derivative of the velocity components (u, v, w) with
respect to its coordinates is equal to zero, meaning that the mass is conserved.
The second of Navier-Stokes equations originate from Newtons second law(F = m·a)
applied on fluid motion and describes conservation of momentum, given by equation
2.2 for incompressible newtonian fluids [22]:
ρ · (∂⃗u
∂t + (⃗u · ∇)⃗u) = ρ⃗g −∇p + µ · ∇2⃗u,
(2.2)
where ρ is the fluid density in kg/m3, p is the fluid pressure in Pa, µ is the dynamic
viscosity of the fluid in Pa·s and g is the gravity in m/s2. The left side of the Navier-
Stokes equation constitutes ’the mass times acceleration’ part of Newtons second
law, whereas the right side constitutes the external and internal forces affecting the
fluid. Mass is in relation to fluids given by its density. The acceleration term is split

2.2.4
Vorticity and Vortex Ring Formation
Vortices are an important fluid phenomenon contributing to a physiological cardiac
function due to its affect on blood motion and tissue stress [33]. Vorticity is the local
rotation of fluid particles, which depend on velocity gradients. This means that the
boundary layer, which develops in viscous flow due to the velocity gradient between
the wall and the bulk fluid is considered as an elongated layer of vorticity and is
therefore called a shear layer. Vorticity, ω, is proportional to the wall shear stress,τw,
by the wall shear rate, sr, giving by equation 2.4 [33]:
τw = sr · ω
(2.4)
The vorticity magnitude is a measure of the velocity gradient ∆u between adjacent
fluid layers. The vorticity vector is defined as the cross product between the velocity
field(u(t, x)) and the differential operator ∇(2.5) [33].
ω(t, x) = ∇× u
(2.5)
Vortex formation is an accumulation of vorticity, which appears in cardiac chambers
and larger vessels, where the thin shear layer constitutes a smaller part of the entire
flow field. In local regions where the flow decelerates, the shear layer will thicken and
eventually separate from the wall, which initiates the formation of vortex structures.

The velocity of the side of the shear layer adjacent to the wall is lower than the
velocity on the farther side, thus the detached layer creates a spinning motion. The
deceleration of flow often occurs due to geometric changes such as stenotic vessels,
aneurysms or bifurcations. Additionally, vortex structures which come close to the
wall can also give rise to shear layer separation and the formation of new vortex
structures. At sharp edges, as seen at the entrance of the PVs to the left atrium,
the shear layer leaves the edge tangentially and immediately forms a spiral structure.
Due to the circular shape of the orifice, a vortex ring forms at the inflow. Vortex rings
are circular tubes with axial symmetry. It can be diﬀicult to identify vortex rings,
since the highest magnitude of vorticity mostly appears at the shear layer [33]. Several
vortex definitions have been proposed through time. A now established method using
the λ2 criterion to identify vortex structures was introduced by Jeong and Hussain
[28].
By decomposing the velocity gradient tensor ∇u into a symmetric and antisymmetric
part, an expression for determining local pressure minimums is obtained:
S2 + Ω2,
(2.6)
where S is the symmetric strain rate tensor and Ωis the antisymmetric rotation rate
tensor. [28] defines a vortex ring from the eigenvalues of equation 2.6. The expression
2.6 contains the real eigenvalues λ1, λ2 and λ3 ordered as λ1 ≥λ2 ≥λ3, which is
calculated at each point in the velocity field. A point is defined as a part of the
vortex core, being a connected region of local pressure minimums, if the requirement
λ2 < 0 is fulfilled, given that at least two eigenvalues are negative. The λ2 criterion
is implemented using the external MATLAB functions given in appendix B.4.

order the eigenvalues and outputs the λ2-values, which is used for plotting isosurfaces
visualizing vortex rings.

The heart provides an amount of external work to pump blood under pressure around
the body. Internal work refers to the cardiac work performed during systole within the
wall of the ventricles when the contraction happens [74]. The external work is mainly
composed of stroke work, which is the product of stroke volume and average pressure
during ejection, required to move the volume of fluid. The remaining part of the
external energy is constituted by Kinetic Energy(KE), which is a measure of energy
used to accelerate blood due to inertia [2]. Atrial KE in the left heart is generated
by the longitudinal contraction of the LV and a smaller fraction is generated by the
LA contraction. The amount of atrial KE can be obtained by equation 2.7 [2].

in their correlation with cardiac diseases and has an established clinical relevance.
Atrial KE has become an unaccounted part of external ventricular work. Thus, the
conservation of KE may be affected in cardiac diseases, where the blood pressure is
normal. Since KE is a pressure-independent measure of energy used to move blood,
this is an important hemodynamic parameter in the investigation of the LAA flow
effects. [2] suggests that a smaller atrium causes lower KE during systole, which is
found due to the comparison between the LA and RA. KE is not only determined by
atrial volume, yet it is proposed that diseases affecting the atrial volume, will affect
the atrial KE. Findings of the work by [2] also suggest that rotational flow in the
atria contributes to conserving the energy.
2.3
Numerical Analysis
CFD is a technique within the mechanical engineering field, where computer-based
simulations are used to analyze fluid flow, heat transfer and associated phenomena
in order to solve complex engineering problems. This methodology can be used to
develop new devices, optimize existing or gain an inside in phenomenons, which were
otherwise not possible. In biomedical engineering, CFD can be used to obtain clini-
cally relevant knowledge on how body fluids perform.
Fluid motion can be described by mathematical PDEs, which can be solved by high-
level computer programming to finally obtain numerical solutions. A CFD simulation
is set up by initially preprocessing the fluid flow problem including specifying the ge-
ometry, generating a high-quality mesh, selecting appropriate physics, definition fluid
properties and finally specifying the boundary conditions [40]. Numerical solutions
can be obtained using different techniques, yet in this thesis, the finite element method
is applied (see section 2.3.1).
With a FSI model, it is possible to simulate the multiphysic interaction between a
fluid domain and a deformable solid domain describing both the fluid dynamics and
structural mechanics. In this thesis, FSI is used the mimic the blood-muscle interac-
tion within the LA. Pressure and velocity changes within a fluid structure will cause
stresses and strains to be exerted on the solid structures, which will cause defor-
mations of the solid depending on the material properties. Likewise, will structural
deformations of the solid body or forces exerted on the fluid domain affect the flow and
pressure field within the fluid domain [77]. The COMSOL Multiphysics™software is
used to perform the numerical analysis in this project [65].
2.3.1
The Finite Element Method
To quantify spatial and time dependent problems within physics, these phenomenons
can be described with PDEs. In most cases, these equations cannot be solved an-
alytically with an exact solution due to too complex geometries, materials or loads.
The Finite Element Method(FEM) is a technique to solve these equations numerically
by iteratively approximating solutions. FEM is used within many engineering fields
==PAGE:38==
14
2 Theory
to solve problems of solid mechanics, fluid mechanics, heat transfer and electromag-
netics. The concept of FEM involves the discretisation of a geometry into smaller
elements connected at nodes, called the mesh. The PDE solutions can thereby be
approximated over a finite number of discrete elements at each nodal point. Each
node has a number of displacements called degrees of freedom(DOF) which can be
constraint by defining the boundary conditions [38].
2.3.2
The Arbitrary Lagrangian Eulerian Formulation
Problems within physics can be described differently according to which mathematical
approach is used the interpret the physical equations. Each approach is appropriate
for a specific engineering application. The Eulerian and Lagrangian formulation are
fundamental mathematical approaches within continuum mechanics. The Eulerian
method is advantageous within fluid dynamics and electromagnetics. This technique
describe dynamical problems within the spatial frame, which express variables as
functions of fixed coordinates. The approach formulates how physics are naturally
observed, where the deformation of an object is observed from a fixed point in space
without any reference to the physical system itself. The Lagrangian method is used
within structural mechanics. Here the deforming object appears within the material
frame, where the coordinate system is referenced to individual volumes within the
material and deforms together with the material. This is advantageous when display-
ing stress and strain within anisotropic materials. The Arbitrary Lagrangian Eule-
rian(ALE) formulation combines the Lagrangian and Eulerian methods to account
for the expression of both fluid dynamic and structural mechanic equations, which
plays an important role of FSI modelling. This methodology uses a mesh coordinate
system, which is not fixed in space or attached to the material, to solve the physics
equations. The Lagrangian method is applied at the boundary of the solid domain,
whereas the Eulerian method is applied in the fluid domain. Additional equations
are then in this method added to solve the deformation in the boundary between
the Eulerian and Lagrangian domains. In COMSOL these equations are defined by
adding a Moving Mesh, where the velocity of the fluid is bound to the deforming
mesh [13].
2.3.3
Mesh
Mesh quality, suﬀicient accuracy and computational resources are factors to be con-
sidered when meshing a geometry for finite element analysis. The quality of the mesh
is often measured by the skewness of the mesh elements, which is a measure of the
length to width ratio and angular element distortion. A perfect mesh element is in
the COMSOL software indicated by the quality of 1 in relation to skewness. As a rule
of thumb, the minimum element quality should be above 0.1[23]. A low growth rate is
also a typical indicator of a good quality mesh. The accuracy of the solution can be in-
creased by refining the mesh in challenging areas of the geometry. A 3D geometry can
be meshed using mainly four different kinds of mesh elements consisting of tetrahe-
drons, hexahedrons, prisms and pyramids, where only tetrahedrons are able to mesh

mesh consists of unstructured highly isotropic elements, which accommodate complex
geometries. Hexahedrons and prisms are anisotropic elements, which can have a very

2.3.4
Solver Settings
When solving nonlinear problems in multiphysics simulations, the system of equations
can be solved with a segregated or a fully coupled approach. The segregated solver
divides the problem into steps and solved e.g. for one physics at the time. The steps
are solved iteratively, where the unknowns are computed for each sequence. The fully
coupled solver computes the unknowns for the entire system of equations within a
single iteration, which therefore requires more memory than the segregated approach
for each iteration [47].

2.4
State of the Art
CFD modelling has in a great extent been used to investigate the function and struc-
ture of the heart in relation to CVD. Modelling the fluid dynamics of the left heart
is most often focused on the left ventricle [58]. Hence, limited research focus of the
impact of the LAA structure.
Unrealistic boundary conditions and assumptions in the numerical simulations can
lead to conclusions, which are not applicable in a clinical setting. In numerical simu-
lations of the left heart, most studies isolate the left ventricle due to the complexity
of the atrial geometry. The LA is in some works simplified by using in vivo flow
measurements as the inlet boundary condition for the LV at the mitral valve. The
LA chamber plays an important role in the ventricular filling and the complexity of
the LA do not allow morphological simplifications or to be fully eliminated. The
simplification of numerical studies, therefore, limit the clinical implications.
In some studies, a highly simplified atrium is included in left heart model to serve
the purpose of letting the flow develop in a chamber-like structure before entering the
ventricle. An example of geometrical simplifications is seen in the Karlsruhe Heart
model by[61], where the geometry of the LA is modelled as a sphere with only two
PVs entering the chamber. Other studies, such as the CFD framework of the LA and
PV by [10], simplify the computational model by assuming rigid walls. The volume
of the atrial chamber changes a lot during the cardiac cycle and the result of the
investigation will, therefore, be highly influenced by this incorrect assumption. A
more correct mimicking of the properties of the endocardium in a CFD simulation is
to use dynamic meshing. A dynamic mesh can be implemented in the model by using
different methods. Masci et. al. [44] accounts for the LA wall movement in a CFD
investigation of AF in the LA. By using image registration, a motion model can be
estimated from CT images to be used in the creation of a dynamic mesh series[44].
Applying a prescribed velocity to the mesh is highly relevant when investigating the
entire cardiac cycle, where the wall deformation is large and influenced by several
functionalities. However, even when a dynamic mesh is applied, CFD modelling do
not account for the interaction between the LA tissue and LA blood pool.
In a study by Zhang et. al[76], the function and hemodynamics of the LAA in sinus
rhythm and AF are investigated using a FSI computational model of the LA with
and without the LAA. A simplified geometry of the LA and LAA is constructed on
the basis of TEE measurements. The solid domain is created as a rectangular bulk,
wherein the atrium is immersed, and defined with mechanical properties mimicking
the myocardium. The study shows no difference in transmitral velocity when com-
paring the model with and without appendage in sinus rhythm. The model without
appendage shows slightly weaker vortex generation. The results suggest that the LAA
is not functional in sinus rhythm. This study act as a great foundation for investi-
gating the impact of the LAA, yet the simplified geometry hinder the applicability of
the results.

3.1
Magnetic Resonance Imaging
Flow can be measured using different techniques, where Doppler echocardiography
and phase-contrast MRI are used most commonly since they are noninvasive. Phase-
contrast MRI are the most accurate technique since you can orient the imaging plane
relative to the vessel of interest and account for the different velocities occurring
across the lumen [4].
A MRI scanner consists mainly of a strong electromagnet, gradient coils, Radio Fre-
quency(RF) coils and a receiving computer. MR is a noninvasive imaging technique
used to visualize soft tissue in our bodies using magnetic fields and RF radiation
by measuring the amount of water in the tissue. Hydrogen nuclei in our body are
spinning(precessing) around their own axis. However, when they are exposed to a
strong magnetic field caused by the superconducting magnet in the MR scanner, the
precessional axis will be oriented according to the orientation of the magnetic field.
The precessional frequency is dependent on the strength of the primary magnetic
field. A magnetic gradient coil is therefore used to alter the primary magnetic field
by applying a second magnetic field, which varies throughout the body so that only
the area of interest is targeted.The gradient coil also allows for spatial encoding by
producing poles in the x, y and z-direction.
Most of the atoms are low energy atoms and will be aligned longitudinally in the
direction of the primary magnetic field and a smaller proportion will be aligned anti-
parallel to the primary field. The longitudinal net magnetization generated from the
hydrogen protons is measured using a RF coil. The tissue specific RF coil sends RF
waves of resonance frequency into the area of interest. The energy is absorbed by
the low energy atoms, which causes them to move into a higher energy state and
changes the direction of the precession axis to be anti-parallel to the magnetic field.
The RF pulse also causes the photons to be in phase, which results in a transverse
net magnetization and can be measured in a receiving coil as an electrical current.
When the RF pulse is turned off, the protons releases the absorbed energy and return

to their normal state in two different processes simultaneously. When the signal is
received, it is deconstructed using Fourier Transformation to assign each voxel with
a location.
Phase-contrast MRI exploits the information about phase-shift of the precessional
spins and frequency for each signal, which occur due to the magnetic gradient. Since
the gradient affects the phase-shift along the vessels, the phase-shift is proportional
to the blood flow velocity. The flow measurements are made by orienting the slice of
the scan perpendicular to the vessel of interest.

ventricular filling. Atrial contraction, A, causes a negative inflow of the PVs [15].

Computed Tomography
The geometry used for modelling the LA is constructed by the segmentation of cardiac
CT images. Medical CT is an imaging technique used to produce detailed internal
images of the body. The scanner consists of a rotating X-ray source, an X-ray detec-
tor and a computer unit for data processing, reconstruction and visualisation. The
principle of CT imaging consists of how X-rays is absorbed within different parts of
the body. When the attenuated X-rays interact with the detection device, a planar
image of the patients internal tissues can be generated. X-rays are electromagnetic
waves with a wavelength smaller than visible light. X-rays are generated in an X-ray
tube, where accelerated electrons are emitted from a cathode to an anode containing
a target material like Tungsten. When the electron is decelerated by the anode, atoms
of the target material are excited and excess energy is emitted as X-rays [8]. X-rays
can interact with matter in different ways, where the intensity is reduced depend-
ing on the attenuation coeﬀicient of the respective tissues. A number of radiograms
are recorded at each angle around the patient, where reconstruction algorithms are
used to create the cross-sectional slice of the body. The tissues represented on the
grey-scale image are quantified by Hounsfields units(HU), given by equation 3.2 [24].

Modeling the Left
Atrial geometry
The geometry of the left atrium is irregular and complex and the volumetric changes
vary greatly during the cardiac cycle. It is therefore not a valid assumption to simplify
the geometry to a regular shape [43]. In this chapter, it is thoroughly explained how
a realistic geometry of the LA is obtained. Two different geometries are created from
CT images. One geometry of the blood domain to be used in the CFD model and
another model containing the blood domain as well as the solid domain of the LA
wall to be used in the FSI model.
4.1
Segmentation of the LA and LAA
To model a realistic geometry, manual segmentation of the LA and LAA from CT
images was performed in the open-source software 3D SLICER [17]. Image segmenta-
tion is the process of masking voxels within the region of interest in medical images to
create a volume of an anatomical structure. The segmentation is performed manually
using a variety of techniques, since the quality of the geometry is prioritized above
automation.
For the CFD model, the fluid domain of the LA, LAA and part of the PVs was
segmented. For the FSI model, a segmentation of the solid domain is used, which
includes the wall of the LA, LAA and PVs. The segmentation of the fluid domain
is used as foundation for the segmentation of the solid domain. For simplification
purposes, all segmentations are performed using CT images of the chest captured in
the systolic phase, to minimize the number of geometries to be created. As mentioned
in section 2.1.2, the morphology of the LAA varies greatly among subjects. For the
models created in this project, CT images from a patient with a ’Cauliflower’ LAA
morphology was used for the segmentation, since this morphology has the simplest
geometry, which reduces the complexity of the numerical study.

Geometry Modifications
The segmented geometries are modified in SOLIDWORKS. The modifications only
include the PVs and the MV annulus and the geometry of the LA and LAA remains
unmodified. The purpose of modifications is to achieve uniform inlets and outlet.
The segmentation at the inlets and outlet are irregular shapes. For the simulations, a
planar face is needed at the inlets and outlet to be able to define boundary conditions.
Furthermore, a regular shape with uniform thickness, corresponding to the size of the
PVs from which flowrate data is used, is required in order to not affect the velocity
of the fluid in the simulation. The entrance of the PVs is modified so they consist of
a uniform oval shape with measurements extracted from the MR images. A circular
outlet is made at the MV plane using measurement from the literature, since images
at the MV plane was not included in the MRI scan. The procedure for modifying
the segmentation of the fluid domain and the solid domain is equivalent, yet the solid
domain includes a few extra operations. The steps performed are described below(1-7)
and the features used are illustrated in figure 4.7 and 4.8.

CHAPTER 5
Computational Fluid
Dynamic Simulation
This chapter describes the CFD model of blood flow in the LA and LAA. The flow
is simulated in the early diastolic phase of the cardiac cycle, where the MV is open
and oxygenated blood from the lungs passively fills the LV. The objective of this
simulation is to acquire initial results regarding the hemodynamic effect of the LAA
on the flow in the LA. For this purpose, two versions of the CFD model are created.
Each model has identical setups. However, in one model, called Case 1, the LAA is
included and in the other model, called Case 2, the LAA is occluded using an interior
wall at the LAA orifice. See figure 5.1. The boundary conditions for the interior wall
correspond to the remaining LA wall and is described in 5.2.
Furthermore, this study aims to first test the ability to perform flow simulations on the
realistic LA and LAA geometry segmented in this project. Secondly, the study serves
the purpose of breaking down the complexity of the numerical study by performing
a simplified simulation of the fluid domain before including the solid domain in the
FSI simulation. To compare the results of a model with and without the LAA, two
studies are run in parallel with equivalent setup. A description and justification of
the simulation setup will be presented in this chapter.

By reducing the size of the mesh elements, the accuracy of the results will increase.
However, since this severely increases the number of elements used, it will simulta-

simulation computes mathematically accurate results and that the mesh size does not
influence the results, a mesh convergence analysis is performed. In COMSOL, you
can choose from nine predefined physics-controlled mesh element sizes from ’extremely

and is appropriate for most models, whereas the more extreme element sizes most
likely will not be able to be solved. In this study, the ’normal’ elements size will be
compared to the ’fine’ element size. Figure 5.8 shows the two different mesh element

CHAPTER 6
Fluid Structure
Interaction Simulation
A FSI model of the LA for evaluating the effects of the LAA on the LA hemodynamics
is developed. Physical properties and boundary conditions of the model are described
in this chapter.
The development of a complex FSI model as presented in this thesis is not a linear
process. Several model setups, boundary conditions and solver settings have been
tested and evaluated before the following model was able to be presented.
Many
gained experiences have become the foundation of why this is presented as the sug-
gestion for a representative and realistic simulation model of the LA. It is well-known,
that within the human body, the PVs are stay fixed at the LA entrance and the MV
plane descend as the LV contract. However, the iterative process of evaluating differ-
ent model setups resulted in an alternative model setup. The final model, therefore,
includes a fixed MV plane, which is compensated by the ability of the model to move
at the PVs.
Similar to the presented CFD model, two versions of the model are created; Case A,
which includes the LAA and Case B, where the LAA is occluded.
6.1

6.1.1
Geometry
The geometry of the FSI model is defined from the imported mesh of the model
created in SOLIDWORKS including both a solid domain and a fluid domain. A few
initial repairing procedures are applied to the mesh to fix inverted mesh elements.

placed in (x, y, z) = (0, 0, 0). The faces of the PV ostia are extruded 15 mm distally to

constraint is added to the partitioned face as described in section 6.2. This procedure
is performed to avoid solver problems, since the fully developed flow applied at the
inlets is incompatible with the moving mesh. The MV face of the fluid domain is
extruded 40 mm distally. In this way, the LV is mimicked as a long tube of which the
LV pressure profile can be applied at the end, where the outlet is placed. To avoid

CHAPTER 9
Discussion
This chapter will independently discuss the results of the CFD and FSI models.
Strengths and limitations of the models will be pointed out and considered in a
clinical relevance. Furthermore, key hemodynamics and mechanical characteristics
will be compared with clinical results and similar models reported in the literature.
The discussion will give rise to a concluding evaluation of the impact of the LAA on
the LA hemodynamics.
9.1
Computational Fluid Dynamics Model
The main objective of the CFD simulation was to perform a preliminary study of the
flow within a realistic geometry of the LA and LAA. With the CFD study, essential
experiences were successfully derived, which eased the modelling of the FSI simula-
tion.
The CFD models contain several assumptions and limitations, which makes the model
not valid for clinical applications. The model simulates the LA and LAA chamber
with rigid walls, which is inconsistent with the hyperelastic properties of the wall
tissue. During the early diastolic phase, the LA experiences large volume changes,
due to the emptying of the chamber. These changes in the shape of the chamber and
the hemodynamic effects are, therefore, not included in the CFD analysis. Another
limitation of the model is the insuﬀiciency in only modelling a part of the cardiac
cycle. This eliminates the possibility of running several cycles and making the results
repeatable and stable. The results obtained from the early diastolic phase is as a
consequence not stabilized. Moreover, especially initial results are affected by the
onset of the simulation. Another consequence of only modelling one phase the is elim-
ination of retrieving the hemodynamic effects of the different phases on each other.
Since most reported CFD models retrieves measurements from the whole cycle, only
few measurements are able to be directly compared with the literature. As another
limitation of the LA shape, it should also be mentioned, that the geometry used for
CFD modelling of the early diastolic phase, originate from the systolic phase of the
cardiac cycle.
In spite of the limitations of the CFD model, the results indicate that the LA flow
is modelled realistically. Table 9.1 summarizes essential quantifying results obtained
from the CFD analysis in comparison with reported values from the literature.

9.2
Fluid Structure Interaction Model
The main purpose of conducting a FSI analysis on a model of the LA with the LAA
and on a model, where device occlusion of the LAA is mimicked, was to investigate
the impact of the LA on LAA hemodynamics. To evaluate this, it is necessary that
the FSI model is a valid representation of the LA function. The pulmonary inflow is
an essential boundary condition of the model. In this project, a physiologic flowrate
profile was measured at each PV using MRI. Only very limited research has measured
==PAGE:109==
9.2 Fluid Structure Interaction Model
85
the physiological flowrate profile since most work is based on PV velocity profiles ob-
tained by Doppler echocardiography. In section 3.1.2, it was evidenced that the trend
of the flowrate profiles clearly shows the characteristics of the LA function and that
the flowrate magnitude correspond with reported flowrate magnitudes [15]. The FSI
inlet boundary conditions are created with four unique PV flowrate profiles which
simulates the unique inflow at each PV. Similar studies [76][1] use one generic pre-
dicted PV velocity profile or constant pressure as an inlet boundary condition, which
is not fully representative for the in vivo inflow. A limitation of the inlet boundary
condition of the presented FSI models is that each PV flowrate profile is measured at
different HRs, which creates a small variation in the onset of the cardiac phases.
The FSI model presented in this work is based on a realistic LA geometry obtained
from CT scans.
When compared to similar models investigating the LA flow dy-
namics, simplified geometries and/or CFD modelling are most often used [76][10] [1].
CFD models do not mimic the hyperelastic tissue properties and deforming charac-
teristics of the LA chamber, unless a predesfined velocity is added at the wall. These
characteristics as well as a realistic LA and LAA geometry with a non-uniform wall-
thickness are incorporated in this project. The muscle fibers of the myocardium are
however not included as a part of this study. Thus, the LA relaxation is not modelled.
To increase the clinical relevance and expand the studies performed in this project,
it is suggested that future work include the modelling of the muscle fibers and the
geometry and motion of the MV leaflets. It should also be noted that this model uses
data from different subjects, since the data from the CT scan, MRI and the LV outlet
pressure profile does not originate from the same individuals. By combining data
from several individuals, the risk of obtaining pathological results arise. However,
the results presented in chapter 8 does not indicate pathological behavior of the LA
function. The FSI models are evaluated for three cardiac cycles. To fully investigate
the stabilization and reliability of the results, it would be optimal to run the models
for more cycles. Yet, the results presented in table 8.1 and the comparison of all three
cycles, shown in appendix A.6, demonstrate that the presented results are reliable.
The changes in pressure within the chamber is an essential measure, since pressure
gradients between the LA and PV and LA and LV are one of the key mechanisms
controlling the blood flow including the opening and closure of the MV. The pressure
curve of the FSI models properly illustrate that key phasic functions are obtained.
Yet, it becomes clear that the limitations of not including the MV leaflets in the
model affect the pressure curve, where the c-wave, which is caused by the bulging of
the leaflets, is absent. When comparing the mean pressure and peak pressure values
between the model with LAA and without LAA, it is seen that the pressure within
the chamber is not affected by the occlusion since only a negligible difference is seen
between the measurements. These findings contradict the work of [11], which found
a significant pressure difference, when occluding the LAA of canine hearts. The reli-
ability of the models is as well verified by finding that the pressure values lie within
the range of clinically reported measurements of the LAP.
When investigating the volume changes of the FSI models, the key phasic characteris-
tics of LA filling and passive and active emptying are demonstrated. Table 8.3 shows

that the difference in volume between the models due to the LAA occlusion does
not affect the volumetric parameters used for accessing the cardiac function. E.g. it
is seen that the total emptying volume is identical for both models indicating that
LAA occlusion in healthy individuals do not affect the LV stroke volume, which is
an important hemodynamic parameter for assessing cardiac function. It is as well
evidenced that all obtained volumetric parameters corresponds well with clinically re-
ported values. The pressure and volume relationship is illustrated on the conducted
pressure-volume loop. The active emptying creating the A-loop is well obtained in
both cases. Yet, it is observed that the concave trend of the reservoir phase, creating
the V-wave is absent. This is a consequence of the lack of modelling the muscle fibers
and the fixation of the MV. In the reservoir phase, the muscle fibers relax and the
MV descends due to the contraction of the LV, which create a pressure drop within
the LA chamber. Since these mechanisms is not modelled, the initial pressure drop
which happens at the onset of systole is not present in the FSI models. Instead a
linear increase in the volume and pressure is seen. However, when calculated the LA
stroke work, which is the area of the active emptying phase, it is seen that the stroke
work corresponds well with clinically reported values. Furthermore, no significant
difference between the models is observed when comparing the pressure-volume loop
and the stroke work, which indicates that the LAA occlusion does not affect the LA
hemodynamics.
Similar to previous findings, no significant difference is found between the models,
when observing the E- and A-peak of the transmitral velocity. The magnitude of
the E-peak of Case B, is slightly larger than the E-peak of Case A as found by [31].
Yet, the difference is of negligible magnitude which further confirms that the vortex
formation, which is generated by the PV inflow has the affect of conserving the ve-
locity of the blood flow before entering the LV. The magnitude of the transmitral
velocity parameters shown in table 8.5 confirms that the modelled LA hemodynamics
corresponds well with a healthy subject.
Profoundly limited research has been done in investigating the vorticity magnitude
within the LA chamber which limits the possibility of making a direct comparison
with the literature of this parameter. The average vorticity magnitude curve pre-
sented shows a great correlation with the trend of the average KE curve, which
suggest that vorticity contribute in conserving the KE of the domain and that the
parameters are closely related. The λ2 method successfully captures the formation
of vortex rings and clearly visualize how the vortex rings are generated by the PVs
and how the blood is transferred through the MV. Moreover, it is observed that the
λ2 method is not only limited to capturing vortex rings but is also capable of vi-
sualising shear layers. It is furthermore observed, through the visualization of the
vortex structures, that the maximum vorticity magnitude is highly affected by the
shear layers. The location and timing of the vortex rings correspond well to what is
reported in similar investigations of the vortex formation in the LA [21]. In appendix
D.3, an illustration of vortex ring size and time of formation, observed in the work
by [21], are shown. It illustrates that two significant vortex rings are formed during
the cycle; one in the end-systole and another in mid-diastole. This corresponds with

the findings of this FSI model, which show that the vortex rings are generated by

Finally, the LA stress and stretch have been investigated in this project to asses the
mechanical function of the LA and the impact of the LAA. Very limited research has
been conducted in this area, which limit the possibility of comparing these results
with similar work. [43] has however investigated the wall stress in a porcine LA using
a computational model, which allows the displacements of the MV annulus.
The
location of maximum stress agrees with the findings in this work, which suggest that
the fixation of the MV do not affect the stress distribution. Maximum stress and
stretch is located in the same areas, when comparing Case A and B. Yet, in the late
diastole a high stress distribution is found at the LAA in Case A. Stress alteration
has been evidenced to have a strong correlation with the onset of cardiac diseases,
which in relation to this work shows that the LAA occlusion does not contribute to
increased risk of cardiac disease. The results of the CFD and FSI models correspond
well with each other, especially when comparing important flow characteristics as the
formation of vortex rings, which indicate that both models could be beneficial in car-
diovascular research. The results of both models lead to the conclusion that the LAA
do not impact the hemodynamics of the LA in healthy subjects. In a study by [31],
the impact of LAA exclusion on the LA function was investigated in a clinical study
of dogs. The findings showed a small affect on the reservoir function, yet concluded
that a more clinically relevant model was needed, which has been accomplished in this
project. The conclusions of the work by [76], which uses a simplified FSI geometry,
agree with the analysis of this project, which suggests that the LAA is not functional
in sinus rhythm. [1] has published the most recent work within this topic which inves-
tigates the impact of the LAA shape on LA hemodynamics using CFD. The overall
findings of this work also suggest that the LAA causes insignificant alterations of the
LA hemodynamics. Yet the work is inconclusive in the effect of the morphological
impact of the LAA and request further investigations to be performed. The work of
this project, therefore, act as a necessary continuation of former works.
The results show that the FSI model developed in this project is a great representation
of the in vivo left atrial environment and hemodynamics. The model could therefore
be highly beneficial in clinical applications and contribute in the development of new
biomarkers of cardiac health and new methods to differentiate pathological patients
from healthy subjects. Recent research by [2] suggests that the conservation of KE

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
The work of this project consisted of developing two realistic numerical models of the
LA to investigate the impact of the LAA on the LA hemodynamics. By using clinical
data, a CFD and FSI model was successfully developed and important hemodynamic
parameters has been assessed. The CFD acted as important tool in the development
of a holistic FSI model. The results of the FSI establish that this model accurately
represents the in vivo flow environment within the LA.
As being the aim of this project, it can, based on the results of the FSI models, be
concluded that the LAA do not affect the hemodynamics of the LA within healthy
subjects. The model serves a high relevance in further cardiovascular research, where
it can be used in to obtain further knowledge within cardiovascular diseases.
Current research within cardiovascular biomechanics have been used in the identi-
fication and understand of this particular scientific issue. On this basis, scientific
methodologies, theories and tools have been used to develop the presented models.
This project is a result of independent and cooperative work, where it in this disserta-
tion has been shown that the main project goals are successfully accomplished. Thus,
it can furthermore be concluded that the learning objectives of this thesis has been
meet.

1
Introduction
Cardiovascular diseases(CVD) are the number one cause of global mortality and are also a major
contributor of disabilities causing a reduced quality of life. With an increase in CVD deaths of
21% from 2007 to 2017 and 17.8 million deaths worldwide in 2017, CVD is an increasing threat
to global health[5].
Diagnosis of cardiac diseases are mainly done through diﬀerent imaging modalities like Com-
puted Tomography(CT), Magnetic Resonance Imaging(MRI) or Doppler ultrasound. However,
the imaging techniques have limitations and might be invasive.
In recent years, numerical
studies open a new chapter in clinical studies and improved diagnostic procedures by helping
clinicians conducting thorough investigations on cardiac dynamics and hemodynamics. In this
project, the impact of Left Atrial Appendage(LAA) on the Left Atrium(LA) will be investi-
gated through Computational Fluid Dynamics(CFD) analysis. To this aim a realistic holistic
LA geometry will be reconstructed from CT images and the CFD analysis would be performed
on the reconstructed geometry. Finally, the hemodynamics indices like pressure, velocity and
vorticity etc of the LA model with and without LAA will be compared.
2
Background and motivation
The LA and LAA are related to many cardiovascular diseases like LA ﬁbrillation, LA enlarge-
ment, LA hyperthrophy, pulmonary hypertension etc. The local hemodynamics of LA and the
LAA are closely related to the function of the heart and can act as a biomarker for cardiac
health. The development within imaging techniques have provided useful tools for evaluating
cardiac hemodynamics. Yet, these techniques contain limitations given by the relatively low
spatial and temporal resolution[2]. Clinical research also show that measurements in some re-
gions of the heart like the left atrial appendage is diﬃcult to perform[1]. By combining CFD
and MRI and CT imaging modalities it is possible to simulate physiological and pathological
scenarios to investigate cardiac hemodynamics in diﬀerent conditions
CFD constitute a non invasive method for obtaining and predicting important local hemody-
namic parameters such as vorticity, pressure and velocity, which can have great clinical impact.
CFD simulation can therefore act as a powerfull tool for diagnostics and increasing knowledge in
cardiovascular physiology. Most CFD studies of the left heart focuses on the left ventricle[2][4].
In conducted studies of the physiological LA, it is often seen that chamber morphology or bound-
ary conditions has been simpliﬁed[1]. These model simpliﬁcations question the relevance and
use-fullness of the results in clinical context. A thorough description of the LA hemodynamics
and the impact of the LAA is still lacking.
3
Project hypothesis and objective
The anatomical properties of the LAA including the irregular geometry and a narrow oriﬁce
contribute in promoting blood stasis and thrombus formation. The LAA is therefore one of the
primary source of thromboembolisms causing ischemic and cardioembolic stroke.[7]. Studies
also show that the unique and patient speciﬁc shape and size of the LAA aﬀects the preva-
lence of thrombus formation[8] [3] [6]. The strong correlation between the LAA and cardiac
abnormalities or drug therapy contraindications has yielded the need for alternative preventive
methods for cardiovascular diseases such as percutaneuos or surgical LAA occlusion. Exclud-
ing the LAA have shown encouraging results, yet the eﬃcacy, future eﬀects and safety of the
procedure is not fully understood.
2
==PAGE:163==
September 23, 2021
The anatomically realistic 3D LA and LAA ﬂow model developed in this project aims to con-
tribute to the investigation of how the LAA shape and size aﬀects the ﬂow patterns and risk of
thrombus formation and to increase the knowledge within the eﬀects of LAA occlusion. This
knowledge will as well be relevant in the construction of a physical LA phantom to be used in
an experimental study for further research of the hemodynamics in the left heart. The model
is generated from cardiac CT images, where image segmentation is used to reconstruct a 3D
model. The model will be modiﬁed as the pulmonary veins are replaced with circular tubes for
simpliﬁcation purposes. The simulation study will be split into two separate analysis; a ﬂow
simulation of the LA with and without occluding the LAA. PC-MRI data is used for creating
boundary conditions. Both models are validated by comparing the results to experimental and
numerical studies documented in the literature. The objective of this project is to model the LA
and the LAA and using CFD to investigate the impact of LAA on the LA hemodynamics. The
hypothesis of this project states that by constructing a holistic physiological model of the LA
with and without the LAA using realistic data, it is possible to mimic in vivo hemodynamics
in a ﬂow simulation study and analyze the LA and LAA ﬂuid dynamics during systolic phase
of the cardiac cycle.

The ﬁrst task in this project consist of performing a literature study to acquire knowledge

in this project by performing studies on simpliﬁed models.
4.2

Initially, a realistic 3D CAD model of the LA and LAA is created to be used for investigation of
the hemodynamics in a ﬂow simulation. The model is reconstructed from CT scan images of a
healthy heart in the atrial systole. The images are acquired from Cardiovascular Biomechanics
Group at DTU and Magnetic Resonance Research Center at Aarhus University Hospital(AUH).
An image segmentation is performed in the open source program 3D Slicer, where a combination
of diﬀerent segmentation tools is used to create a mask of the LA and LAA. The mesh imported
into SolidWorks in order to perform modiﬁcations on the model. The processes in SolidWorks

The modiﬁed reconstructed 3D geometry of the LA and LAA is imported as the ﬂuid domain
in COMSOL Multiphysics, where ﬂow simulations will be executed. Boundary conditions for
the simulation is set using physiologic ﬂow rate proﬁles extracted from MRI data using the
software SimWin. The ﬂuid domain will be deﬁned with blood mimicking properties and time
dependent inﬂow at the pulmonary veins. Since, only the systole phase will be simulated, no
outlet is included in the study and the ﬂuid domain will be simulated using a moving mesh.
First, the simulation will be focused on the LA geometry including LAA and ﬁnally the results
will be compared to the hemodynamics of the LA geometry in which the LAA ostium is occluded.
A mesh convergence study will also be performed to make sure that the results are not
aﬀected by the mesh.
If time allows, the investigations will also be performed during the diastolic phase as well
by included the mitral valve in the model and adding an outlet to the simulation.
4.4
Model validation
The ﬁnal part of the project, besides documentation, is validation of the results obtained in
the simulation study. The ﬂow models should be compared to similar models conducted in
acknowledged studies and a conclusion should be drawn whether the LAA have an impact on
the hemodynamics of the LA.
