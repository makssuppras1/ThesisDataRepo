Abstract
The phase retrieval problem occurs in both optics and sound processing and consists of reconstructing
the phase of a function from only the modulus of linear transformations of the signal.
Since the modulus of the Fourier transform is proportional to the input a sensor gets from a signal,
phase retrieval from the modulus of the Fourier transform is useful when reconstructing the original
signal from a measurement.
Phase retrieval can also be necessary in signal processing, where the
Fourier transform and also the wavelet transform are used.
In this paper we examine the uniqueness and stability of the phase retrieval problem from the modulus
of the Fourier and wavelet transforms. In the case of the Fourier transform, uniqueness does not occur
for functions in L2(R), since a wide range of real-valued phase functions will solve the same problem.
If f has compact support, non-uniqueness can occur in the form of scaling, translation, mirroring and
zero-Ô¨āipping. If f is zero on the negative half line, solutions can diÔ¨Äer by scaling, translation and in
the zeros of the holomorphic extension of ňÜf.
When doing phase retrieval from the modulus of the wavelet transform, we will follow the proofs
from [1], where we consider the Cauchy wavelets. To get examine uniqueness, we Ô¨Ārst prove that a
holomorphic function in the upper half complex plane can be reconstructed uniquely, up to a global
phase, if it is known on only two horizonal lines. This is used to prove that a holomorphic or real-valued
function in L2(R) or a distribution with compact support is uniquely determined up to a global phase
by the modulus of the wavelet transform of f with two distinct Cauchy wavelets. It is also shown
that if the function is a periodic, holomorphic discrete function, it is unique up to a global phase and
addition by a constant if the modulus of the wavelet transform is known for two Cauchy wavelets.
Finally, we have examined the stability of the phase retrieval from the modulus of the wavelet transform
by using an algorithm and code provided by [1]. Here we see that the stability depends on the variation
of the wavelet transform, and thus that the stability depends on which wavelet coeÔ¨Écients occur in
the reconstruction.

Introduction
Is it possible to reconstruct the phase of a complex-valued function from the modulus of its Fourier
transform? Is it possible to make a unique reconstruction? How about a stable reconstruction?
These are questions of theoretical interest, but also of practical importance in Ô¨Āelds such as optics and
sound processing.

If the function describes an optical signal, its Fourier transform,
which will be deÔ¨Āned properly in Section 2.1, is proportional to
the far Ô¨Āeld measurement. Since a sensor is typically insensitive
to phase, the modulus of the Fourier transform is thus propor-
tional to the measurement. In order to reconstruct the signal,
we therefore need to be able to reconstruct its phase. Here ques-
tions of uniqueness and stability are important for the quality of
the reconstructed signal. In Ô¨Āelds such as medical imaging being
able to reconstruct a signal with accuracy can be crucial to get
the correct diagnosis.
The problem of reconstructing the phase of a function from the modulus of its Fourier transform is a
special case of the Phase Retrieval Problem:
Reconstruct f from {|Lif|}i‚ąąZ

The simplest variant of the problem is to reconstruct the phase of a function from the modulus of
the function. In this problem it is clearly not possible to Ô¨Ānd a unique solution, since in general the
modulus does not hold information about the phase.
Phase reconstruction from the modulus of the Fourier transform of a function is less intuitive and the
uniqueness of a solution may depend on the type of function. This is one of the things we will examine
in this thesis.
The Fourier transform is also widely used in signal processing as it transforms the time signal to a
frequency spectrum. It is however a method with limitations in signal processing, since it does not
provide the frequency location of a signal and thus works best for stationary signals. As a way to cope
with this, the short term Fourier transform was constructed, where the signal is divided into smaller
parts by a time window and each part is Fourier transformed individually. This type of transformation
does however still assume local stationarity, which is not necessarily the case in general. The wavelet
transform suggests a way to cope with this. Here the moving time window is swapped with a wave that
vanishes outside a certain interval. In the wavelet transform it is then possible to not only move the
wave but also to change the width of the wave, thus making it possible to capture diÔ¨Äerent frequencies.
This allows for a spectrum showing frequency and time simultaneously, which is called a scalogram [2].
Sometimes in sound processing, we will eliminate the phase, e.g., during Ô¨Āltering and then we need
phase retrieval to reconstruct the signal.
The wavelet transform also has a speciÔ¨Āc meaning in speech processing. When doing speech recognition,
we often use MFCCs which are coeÔ¨Écients trying to imitate the way humans speak. When converting

a sound to MFCCs, the signals are Fourier transformed and later they are Ô¨Āltered according to the

The phase retrieval problem is generally ill-posed, thus uniqueness and stability of the solution is not
ensured. The problem of uniqueness has been examined widely for the case of the Fourier transform
[4, 5, 6, 7] and more recently also for the wavelet transform for speciÔ¨Āc wavelets [1].
In this thesis we are interested the uniqueness of the phase retrieval problem from modulus of the
wavelet and Fourier transforms. We will also examine the stability of the solution through numerical
tests.
We start by deÔ¨Āning the Fourier transform on functions in L1(R), L2(R) and on distributions and go
through some properties of it in Section 2.1. Then in Section 2.2 we will deÔ¨Āne the wavelet transform
and Ô¨Ānally, we will dig into the class of holomorphic functions deÔ¨Āned on the complex unit disc and
in the upper half complex plane in Section 2.3. With all this theory in place, we can lay out the
results of uniqueness on this problem. First we will consider the problem of retrieving the phase from
the modulus of the Fourier transform of a signal in Section 3.1 and examine uniqueness if various
restrictions are posed on the function. In Section 3.2.1 we examine an example of the phase retrieval
from the modulus of the wavelet transform studied in [1]. Afterwards, in Section 3.2.2 we prove that a
holomorphic complex function in the Hardy space H2(H) is uniquely determined from its value on two
horizontal lines in the upper half complex plane [1]. This result is used to get a uniqueness result for
the wavelet transform, in the case of Cauchy wavelets for functions in L2(R) in Section 3.2.3, as was
done in [1] as well, and distributions in E ‚Ä≤(R) in Section 3.2.4. In Section 3.3 we prove the discrete
equivalent of the uniqueness result. Finally, in Section 4, we examine the stability of the reconstruction
of the phase from the modulus of the wavelet transform using numerical simulations made from the
code from [8].

5
Conclusion
In this thesis, we have examined the uniqueness and stability of the phase retrieval problem.
We
Ô¨Ārst considered the problem of reconstructing the phase from the modulus of the Fourier transform
of a signal. Here we do not have uniqueness for general L2(R) signals, and neither for compactly
supported or holomorphic signals, since apart from trivial non-uniqueness such as translation, scaling
and mirroring, zero-Ô¨āipping can occur.
We also studied the uniqueness of the phase retrieval from the modulus of the wavelet transform of a
signal with Cauchy wavelets. In order to do so, we showed that two H2(H) functions are unique up to
a global phase, if they are equal in modulus on two horizontal lines in the upper half complex plane.
This was used to prove that a holomorphic or real-valued signal in L2(R) can be determined uniquely
up to a global phase if the modulus of the wavelet transform of the function is known for two diÔ¨Äerent
wavelets. This result was also expanded to include distributions with compact support.
Finally, we examined the stability of the phase retrieval from the modulus of the wavelet transform by
using the algorithm for phase retrieval given in [1] and the code provided by [8]. Using this we saw
that the stability depends on the type of signal and the choice of wavelets in the reconstruction. A
smooth signal such as a sum of sine functions is prone to perturbations, if enough wavelets are used
in the reconstruction, since the low-frequency signals are highly disturbed by the noise. A signal with
many zeros also gives an unstable solution.

6
Future work
In this thesis we have presented some of the uniqueness and stability results of the phase retrieval
problem, but there are still many open questions.
When doing phase retrieval from the modulus
of the wavelet transform, we used only the Cauchy wavelet. It would be interesting to be able to
expand the class of wavelets, where the uniqueness result holds. The crucial matter is to connect the
wavelet transform of the signal f with a complex function, such that each convolution with a wavelet
corresponds to evaluation of the complex function on a horizontal line. If a similar relation can be
made with other wavelets, the result is straightforward from Theorem 3.3.
Another interesting way forward is to expand the class of signals, where the solution holds. So far,
we have proven the uniqueness for functions in L2(R) and distributions with compact support, but
because of the strong convergence of the Cauchy wavelets, it could be possible to expand the class of
functions even further while still having the needed convergence.
Furthermore, the uniqueness result for a holomorphic function in H2(H) from Corollary 3.4 can be
used on the problem of phase retrieval from the modulus of the Fourier transform if ňÜf can be extended
to a function F ‚ąąH2(H) and we have data on R and on some complex horizontal line as well.
We have also examined numerically the stability of the problem in the case of the wavelet transform.
Here it would of course be interesting and a natural next step to try to derive theoretical results for the
stability. Using the concrete form we have for the functions in H2(H) it may be possible to examine
the stability by perturbing the zeros of the function.
In the numerical investigations of stability, it would be interesting to examine further how to choose
the best wavelets to reconstruct a certain signal, as we have seen indications that the stability of the
reconstruction depends on the choice of wavelets.
Furthermore, it would be interesting to examine new and better ways to solve the problem numerically.
The algorithm works by computing an approximate solution and then reÔ¨Āning the solution by two
diÔ¨Äerent methods, thus it may be possible to compute a better approximate solution from the beginning
to avoid some of the tedious reÔ¨Ānement afterwards.
