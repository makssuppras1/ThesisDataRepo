II ABSTRACT 
Current design methods for the structural fire design of car parks are based on the consideration of a local 
fire involving 3-4 cars, according to what is indicated in a widely used European guideline published at the 
beginning of the millennium [1]. Nevertheless, large fires in car parks have been observed all over Europe in 
the past two decades, where the extent of the fire spread and impact on the structural elements have been 
more severe than what was anticipated on the basis of previous knowledge.  
The cases of Stavanger [2] and London Luton airport [3], which are just two recent examples of car fires 
that gained the attention of the media, both for the large number of cars involved in the fire (ca. 300 and 
1300 cars, respectively) and because they both resulted in a major collapse of the steel structures. 
However, they are neither the only cases that resulted in structural collapses (e.g. the garage roof collapses 
in Pontoise, FR, in April 2020, and in Märsta, SW, in August 2021) nor the most recent cases of large car 
park fires (see e.g. Rijsenhout Parking fire, NL, in November 2024). These large and severe fires in car parks 
seem to have reached an alarming frequency of ca. 2 per year in the last decade, almost double that in the 
previous decade. Such increased frequency has been correlated with the increment of car size [4] [5] [6], 
which corresponds to a shorter distance between parked cars and a larger fire load from cars, which is 
further increased by the higher amount of plastic and foams in the car interior and by increasing presence 
of BEVs with large Li-Ion batteries [7] [8]. Both aspects are determinant factors in promoting the fire spread 
as well as the severity of the fire. It is therefore needed to update and quantify with respect to the actual 
European car fleet.  
This project is therefore aimed at assessing the average and design fire load, pertaining floor area, and the 
fire load density of current cars. In particular, the fire load per kg of an average ICEV and BEV has been 
determined by analyzing the mass and calorific value of the single car components [7] and then the 50%, 
80%, 90% and 95% fractile of the car fire load has been determined on the basis of the car weight 
distribution obtained for newly registered cars in Denmark [8]. In addition, the average and design car 
density are being investigated with respect to open-air, open, semi-open, and closed car parks on the basis 
of design recommendations [9] as well as personal inspections of several car parks in the Copenhagen area.  
The results provide a reliable value of the fire load density to be used for the structural fire design of car 
parks in Denmark and, with some variation related to the percentage of BEVs in other countries, in all of 
Europe. 
 
Keywords: fire safety, fire load, fire load density, car park, car density.

III RESUME 
De nuværende designmetoder for den konstruktive brandsikring af parkeringsanlæg er baseret på 
antagelsen om en lokal brand, der omfatter 3–4 biler, som det fremgår af en udbredt europæisk design 
guide udgivet i begyndelsen af årtusindskiftet [1]. Ikke desto mindre er der i de seneste to årtier observeret 
omfattende bilbrande i parkeringsanlæg over hele Europa, hvor både brandudbredelsen og påvirkningen af 
de bærende konstruktioner har været væsentligt mere alvorlig end hidtil antaget. 
Brandene fra Stavanger [2] og London Luton-lufthavn [3] er blot to nyere eksempler på bilbrande, der 
tiltrak stor medieopmærksomhed – dels på grund af det store antal biler involveret i branden (henholdsvis 
ca. 300 og 1.300 biler), og dels fordi begge hændelser resulterede i omfattende kollaps af 
stålkonstruktionerne. Disse hændelser er dog hverken enestående eller de eneste tilfælde med 
konstruktive svigt (f.eks. kollapsede taget i parkeringsanlæggene i Pontoise, Frankrig i april 2020, og i 
Märsta, Sverige i august 2021), ligesom de heller ikke er de seneste større bilbrande (se f.eks. branden i 
Rijsenhout Parkering, Holland, november 2024). Disse store og alvorlige brande i parkeringsanlæg ser ud til 
at opstå med en alarmerende frekvens på omkring to tilfælde om året i det seneste årti – næsten dobbelt 
så mange som i det foregående. Denne stigende hyppighed er blevet kædet sammen med den generelle 
vækst i bilernes størrelse [4] [5] [6], hvilket medfører kortere afstand mellem parkerede biler og en øget 
brandbelastning per bil. Dette forstærkes yderligere af det stigende indhold af plast og skum i bilernes 
interiør samt af den voksende andel af elbiler (BEV’er) med store litium-ion-batterier [7] [8]. Begge forhold 
er afgørende faktorer for både brandens udbredelse og størrelse. Der er derfor behov for at opdatere og 
kvantificere brandbelastningen med udgangspunkt i den faktiske europæiske bilflåde. 
Formålet med dette projekt er at vurdere den gennemsnitlige og den dimensionsgivende brandbelastning, 
det tilhørende parkeringsareal samt brandbelastningstætheden for nutidens biler. Særligt er 
brandbelastningen per kilogram for en gennemsnitlig konventionel bil (ICEV) og elbil (BEV) blevet bestemt 
ved analyse af massen og brændværdien for de enkelte komponenter [7]. Herefter er 50%, 80%, 90% and 
95% fraktilen af bilernes samlede brandbelastning bestemt på baggrund af vægtfordelingen for 
nyregistrerede biler i Danmark [8]. Derudover undersøges den gennemsnitlige og dimensionsgivende 
bildensitet i forhold til åbne, semi-åbne og lukkede parkeringsanlæg på baggrund af gældende 
designanbefalinger [9] samt egne inspektioner af flere parkeringsanlæg i Københavnsområdet. 
Resultaterne giver et pålideligt grundlag for brandbelastningstætheden, der kan anvendes til den 
konstruktive brandsikring af parkeringsanlæg i Danmark – og, med visse tilpasninger afhængigt af andelen 
af elbiler, i resten af Europa.

1 INTRODUCTION 
Over the past two decades, there has been significant urbanization [10] and an increase in the number of 
cars in Denmark's largest cities. Furthermore, at the same time, the design of cars has been undergoing 
large changes in size and materials [11] [12]. This has led to a high demand for parking spaces in a relatively 
small area. In order to have enough space for the existing infrastructure, such as buildings, living areas, 
gardens, etc., at ground level, parking areas are being established as parking buildings or underground 
parking. This may be a fine solution for urban planning, but from a fire protection perspective, the covered 
parking spaces have become quite a challenge.  
The current design methods for structural fire design of car parks are based on the assumption of a local 
fire of 3-4 cars, because of the good ventilation, which should limit the fire spread and prevent a flashover 
from occurring. This was demonstrated in a large research study made by the European Commission in 
2002 [13], where results of several experiments and a statistical study from the Fire Brigade of Paris on fires 
in car parks were presented. The fire experiments were performed for open and closed car parks with cars 
from 1980s and 1990s and a common distance between the cars at that time. The results from the tests 
and survey showed that 3-4 cars can be on fire at the same time and location. Since then, guidelines have 
been using the assumption of a localized fire with 3-4 cars as design fires. The report further showed a 
theoretical total heat release ranging from 6 GJ to 12 GJ, depending on the car model. However, the tests 
resulted in a heat release of 2.1 to 6.8 GJ. 
The European Commission also released a guideline for design rules for steel structures in closed car parks 
[1], which included some of the results from the research study. The guideline stated that the structure can 
be designed so that the parts exposed to high temperatures and weakened by the fire are supported by 
other structural elements located farther from the fire, which are less affected by the heat, since the fire is 
localized. The assumption of a localized fire has led to the construction of several car parks with 
unprotected steel structural elements in Europe e.g. Tobaksbygningen in Gladsaxe in Denmark. 
However, large fires in car parks have been observed all over Europe in the past two decades, where the 
extent of the fire spread and impact on the structural elements have been more severe than what was 
anticipated on the basis of previous knowledge. In some remarkable cases, these fires resulted in a 
structural collapse, such as in the case in Stavanger in 2020 where 300 cars burned out and resulted in 
structural collapse [4]. A Fire in an underground parking in Grentzbach 2004 where seven firefighters lost 
their lives because of the concrete ceiling collapsing, Liverpool in 2018 where 1,400 cars burned out and 
the damage to the building was so severe, that it was demolished, and Märsta, Stockholm, where 200 cars 
burned out and the building collapsed. In September 2024, a fire in an underground parking area at 
Skovlunde in Denmark resulted in severe damage to the concrete structures. Even though only 10 cars 
were burned out and 10 others were damaged, the temperature reached more than 1000 degrees Celsius 
in the parking space, which resulted in the concrete cracking, exposing the reinforcement, and a possibility 
of collapse. In Table 1 is a list of examples of some notable fires in car parks around Europe, where several 
cars were on fire at the same time. 
The number of large fires in car parks seems to have reached an alarming frequency of ca. 2 per year in the 
last decade, almost double that in the previous decade. Such increased frequency has been correlated with 
the increment of car size [4] [5] [6], which corresponds to a shorter distance between parked cars, thus a 
more dense parking area, and a larger car fire load. The increase in the car fire load is not only caused by

the higher amount of plastic and foams in the car interior, but also by the increasing presence of BEVs with 
large Li-ion batteries. These aspects are some of the determining factors in promoting the fire spread as 
well as the severity of the fire, and need to be updated and quantified with respect to the actual European 
car fleet.  
The aim of this thesis is therefore to assess the average and design fire load, pertaining floor area, and fire 
load density of current cars. This is done by finding the fire load per kg of an average ICEV and BEV by 
analyzing the weight and calorific values of the single car components [7]. The 50%, 80%, and 90% fractile

Based on the determined values of the fire load and the car density, it is expected to provide a reliable

variation related to the percentage of EVs in other countries, in all of Europe.

2.1 FIRE LOAD AND FIRE LOAD DENSITY 
To assess the fire load density of a car park, the fire load of a single car is investigated. In this thesis, this is 
carried out by finding a theoretical fire load by using the heat of combustion of the car material and the 
mass of the respective material. The total heat release is given as:

The heat of combustion is a measurement of the amount of heat released or absorbed when a chemical 
reaction occurs and is given in kJ/kg or MJ/kg. In fire safety and fire chemistry, the most common reaction 
is the combustion reaction. This reaction consists of the oxidation of substances (gas, liquid, or solid fuel) to 
produce light and heat. The combustion reaction can be divided into two types: complete or incomplete 
combustion. For the complete combustion, the exact proportions of reactants are completely converted to 
the products. This is also known as a stoichiometry reaction. An example of a complete combustion 
reaction of methane is given in Equation (2.2).  
 
𝐶𝐻4 + 2𝑂2  →𝐶𝑂2 + 2𝐻2𝑂 
(2.2) 
 
For an incomplete combustion, some of the material may not be combusted, or the chemical reaction also 
produces byproducts such as soot. The production of byproducts typically happens when there is a lack of 
oxygen in the combustion. This is also called an under-ventilated combustion. An example of an incomplete 
combustion reaction of methane is given in Equation (2.3).

combustion is also divided into two types. In the literature, the heat of combustion for complete

value and denoted Δ𝐻𝑛𝑒𝑡 or Δ𝐻𝑐, and is based on the values measured in the combustion bomb 
calorimeter and is not necessarily appropriate for a realistic fire, but can be used as a conservative 
assessment for the potential heat release. The heat of combustion for an incomplete combustion is called 
the effective heat of combustion or chemical heat of combustion and is denoted Δ𝐻𝑒𝑓𝑓 or Δ𝐻𝑐ℎ. This 
considers the production of soot, humidity in the material, and some residue of the material is left. The

2.2 CAR COMPONENTS 
The modern car as we know it today consists primarily of metal, glass, plastic, rubber, fuel, and textile. The 
majority of cars are running on diesel and gasoline and are categorized as ICEV (Internal Combustion Engine 
Vehicle), but the electric cars with batteries, which are categorized as BEV (Battery Electric Vehicle), are 
gaining momentum and are increasing in numbers every year. In the first quarter of 2025, BEVs accounted 
for more than 50% of all newly registered cars in Denmark [30] [31]. Each car types are basically built the 
same, with some slight differences in material compositions, except for the engines. An illustration of the 
different parts in a modern car is shown in Figure 1. 
==PAGE:11==
Page 5 of 49 
 
Even though modern cars are basically built of the same materials, they come in many different types, 
shapes, and weights depending on the manufacturer and the purpose of their use. It has therefore been 
necessary to categorize the different types of cars, which have been done by governments and private 
organizations such as the US EPA, the Euro NCAP, and the European Economic Community (Euro Car 
Segment) [32]. The classification is used for description, regulation, market place etc., and is based on a 
variety of factors. The boundaries between the segments are not only clarified by the dimensions or the 
weight of the car, but also by price, extra accessories, etc. [33]. The classification is therefore typically 
related to a brand's model, such as a VW Golf is a C-segment car corresponding to a medium car. An 
overview of the Euro Car Segment is specified in Table 2. It is observed from the table that the ranges of the 
weight for the different segments have blurry boundaries, resulting in overlapping. The data for the 
average car weight in Denmark presented in Section 0 is provided by Danmarks Statistik and is categorized 
in accordance with the Euro Car Segment. In this thesis, only passenger cars are considered; cars as trucks, 
buses, disability vehicles, etc. are neglected. 
 
 
Figure 1 - Components in a modern car. [34] 
According to Mills Equipment Company [35] fuel tank for an ICEV can range from 30-90 L, where the tank 
size typically increases with car size. A B-segment car, such as a VW Polo, has a tank of 40 L, a C-segment 
car, such as a VW Golf, has a tank of 45-53 L, and a J-segment car can have a tank of 60-90 L.  
For almost all BEVs, the used batteries are produced as lithium-ion batteries. The batteries vary in size, 
internal chemistry, and build-up. The batteries contain cells, which have a certain amount of energy. In 
order to get a longer driving range, more cells have to be added, which results in a larger battery. Batteries 
have a relatively high density compared to other materials in the car, and at some point, the weight of the 
battery will be the limiting factor in achieving range parity. The fuel in the batteries is given as Wh. The 
combustible energy from the batteries varies depending on the different chemicals in them and their state 
of charge (SOC). The build-up of the batteries and the different chemical components will not be 
investigated further in this thesis.

2.3 CAR DENSITY 
As explained earlier, the car density is a key part of the fire load density. In this section, a gathering of 
assessed car densities in existing studies and a review of the design manuals are performed.  
2.3.1 
Guidelines for parking spaces 
Parking areas are a crucial element in ensuring smoother traffic management. This is done by managing 
where the concentration of traffic is planned to be, and where it is supposed to be limited. In principle, a 
parking area consists of one or more access streets, maneuvering area/lanes, and parking spots. The 
layouts can vary a lot and need to be suited for the specific area, building, and needs. The simplest parking 
area consists of one lane with parking spots and a parking street, which in this case also acts as the 
maneuvering lane. A more complex layout consists of multiple elements, vehicles, and floors. The vehicles 
can be cars, buses, bicycles, scooters, etc. An illustration of a simpler parking area only for passenger cars is 
shown in Figure 2, and an illustration of a parking layout with different elements and parking spaces for 
different vehicles is shown in Figure 3.

The parking areas can be in many different places, such as along a street, a separate area on ground level, 
in a basement, in a parking house with floors, or a fully automatic elevation parking facility. When designing 
a parking area, it is important to secure a flow based on a circulation principle. The principle should be 
determined based on whether the driving direction is one-way or two-way, if it is a drive-through traffic 
principle or a dead-end street principle, and whether the parking spot is parallel, perpendicular, or angled. 
Some examples of circulation principles are shown in Figure 4. 
 
Figure 4 - Principle of circulation from Vejdirektoratet. One-way circulation with angled reverse-in parking is the least common. [9] 
Looking at the detailed design of the parking areas, it is important to keep in mind the size of the area, the 
needed capacity, and the traffic safety of the users. In Denmark, there are no regulations for designing a 
parking area or the parking spots. It appears from Bygningsreglementet 2018 (the Danish building 
regulation) [36] that there should be a sufficient parking area for vehicles in connection with a building for 
its residents, users, visitors, customers, etc. Since there are no regulations, the parking areas are designed 
based on recommendations and design manuals. These manuals have changed in line with the evolution of 
the cars. As an example, the recommendations for a perpendicular parking spot were 2.3x5 m in 1991 and 
2000, but changed to 2.5x5 m in 2009, and with the latest design manual edition from Vejregelrådet (The 
Road Standards Council) from 2023 [9], the recommended size is 2.6x5 m long.   
The parking spots can be established as parallel, perpendicular, or angled parking. Each type of layout 
principle has its pros and cons:  
- Parallel parking is great where the parking street is narrow, because the parking spot takes up less street 
space. However, in most cases, it is necessary to perform a reverse maneuver on the street in order to park 
the car. Furthermore, the street has to be fairly long to have a large capacity. The parallel parking is 
therefore often used to fill out spaces where it is possible to narrow down the street. The recommended 
dimensions of a parallel parking spot are 2.3x5.1 m from center to center of the parking spots marking, and 
the recommended maneuver requirement (maneuver lane) is 3.5 m.  
- Perpendicular parking will, in most cases, have the best utilization of the parking area in terms of the most 
parking spots. The layout requires a larger area, especially the width, than the other layouts, and the 
visibility can be limited when reversing from the parking spot. The recommended dimensions of a 
perpendicular parking spot are 2.6x5.0 m from center to center of the parking spots marking with a 
recommended maneuver requirement (maneuver lane) of 7.0 m. If the parking spot is 2.6x5.2 m from 
center to center of the parking spots marking, the recommended maneuver requirement (maneuver lane) 
is 6.9 m.  
 
==PAGE:15==
Page 9 of 49 
 
- Angled parking can, in some cases, result in more parking spots than parallel spots, thus a higher density. 
The spots can have different angles, but the most common are 30°, 45°, 60  or 75 .  They are typically used 
when the parking street is one-way and the drivers are supposed to leave in a direction other than the 
arrival direction. The recommended dimensions of an angle parking spot are 2.7x5.2 m from center to 
center of the parking spots marking, with a recommended maneuver requirement (maneuver lane) of 3.5 
m for a 30  angle and 45  angle, and 4.0 m for a 60  angle and 5.5 m for a 75  angle.  
An illustration of the recommended dimensions of the different parking spot layouts is shown in Figure 5 
and Figure 6. The design recommendations are based on driving tests with a dimensioning design vehicle. It 
is estimated that 10 % of the sold cars in Denmark were larger than this vehicle in 2023. The design manual 
further recommends that the parking requirement should be given an additional reserve capacity of 10%. 
Although the design manual exists and secures a comfortable and safer parking area, it isn’t a provision 
according to the law, and it is therefore not certain that it will be followed. History shows that new design 
manuals will eventually be followed; however, it will take time for existing parking areas to be updated to 
the new recommendations. It is therefore expected that there still are parking spaces with a width of 2.3 m 
because the layout hasn’t been updated since the introduction of the new design manuals.

Just like the layout, there are no direct design rules for the rest of the parking buildings in Denmark. It is up 
to the designer and engineer to ensure a comfortable and safe environment. This includes ceiling height, 
ventilation, etc., which have a major impact on the fire safety design of the structural elements, especially 
in an enclosed parking area. As shown in Figure 3 and Figure 7, a parking area can consist of many different

elements. This thesis aims to assess a fire load density for a parking area for passenger cars. In the 
following, elements that are not necessary for the parking spaces for passenger cars are neglected.  
The floor area of the parking spots will depend on the actual building and is therefore subject to relatively 
large variations, which also leads to a variation in the car density. Butcher [37] and Spearpoint et al. [38] 
performed a survey of parking buildings and open-air parking areas and found a floor area per car between 
18.5-26.8 m2/car and 23.8-34.0 m2/car, respectively. Spearpoint used a density of 25 m2/car to assess a fire 
load density.  
Chrest et al. [39] used an approach of classification of the cars and the level of service (LOS). LOS is a 
classification of the degree of congestion of traffic, with the highest LOS (category A) being traffic with free 
flow with no delay, and the lowest LOS (category F) resulting in “gridlock”. Based on the LOS, the density 
was between 16-40 m2/car, where the densest area was for 100% “small” cars. 
Hill [40] suggested that the parking area should be between 20-35 m2/car for 45  and 90  parking angles in 
order to have an efficient layout and still maintain a good flow.  
Hertz et al. [4] made a survey of a number of parking buildings in Copenhagen, and found a density 
between 18-23.5 m2/car. However, after comparing with the design manual at that time, a density of 18.0 
m2/car was used to assess a fire load density. Giuliani [41] performed an investigation of the generic 
models of parking spots based on the design manual at that time. The density was found between 14-25 
m2/car and was suggested to be 18.0 m2/car with an 80% fractile of 22.5 m2/car.  
2.3.2 
Open and closed car parks 
When performing fire safety design for a car park, it is important that the ventilation conditions are 
assessed, as they have a significant impact on fire development and fire spread. This is because low-
ventilated buildings present a higher risk of flashover, whereas well-ventilated buildings tend to experience 
faster-developing but shorter fire durations remaining the fire localized. The inclosing structures are 
therefore of great important when designing a car park. As mentioned in the previous section, the car parks 
are categorized as open-air, open, semi-open and closed. In [4] the open car parks are defined as a building 
where at least 1/3 of the façade walls are open, and for the closed car park less than 1/3 of the façade walls 
are open. A definition of a semi-open car park not made, but most be in between the open and closed.  
Based on the above definitions the open-air and open car parking should be less critical compared to the 
closed car park. However, most of the fires listed in Table 1 is fire in open-air and open car parks. Since 
major fires and fire spread have been observed in real chases, an assumption of a local fire, which is 
discussed in Section 2.5, can be questionable.   
A further investigation of the definition of the open, semi-open, and closed car parks are not permed in this 
thesis. 
2.4 FIRE LOAD AND FIRE LOAD DENSITY IN LITERATURE 
As part of the process of making designs for a fire safe environment, fire tests have been performed to 
provide useful data. These tests can be used as benchmark tests, and can provide critical data for design 
manuals and rules for particular structures or materials, such as heat release, as well as smoke generation 
and species composition, or to gather information on specific hazards. The results gathered from the tests, 
especially the heat and smoke release, are of great importance in the design of structural elements, 
ventilation, and evacuation systems. The full-scale tests can give a large amount of data, but they are also 
expensive to perform. As earlier descripted, the car models have changed a lot throughout time, and with

common cars. Only a few full-scale fire test results are available in the literature for BEV, but in the last

Fire load density 
One of the first assessments of the fire load density was done by Butcher [37], who found the amount of 
wood to be 17 kg/m2 for the equivalent fire load density for a car park in the 1960s. By using a heat of 
combustion for wood of 17-20 MJ/kg, which was found by Thomas [62], and an area per car of 18.5 m2 the 
fire load density was between 290-340 MJ/m2 per floor area. Since then, only a few assessments have been 
performed for the fire load density for car parks. However, Gewain [63] later suggested that the wood 
equivalent fire load wouldn’t in general exceed the corresponding wood of 9.75 kg/m2, which would lead to 
a fire load density between 166-195 MJ/m2 pr floor area.  
In 1986, Thomas [62] gathered information from German and Swiss data. The data was obtained from tests 
and gave an average fire load density of 190 MJ/m2 pr floor area with a standard deviation of 105 MJ/m2 pr 
floor area and the 80%, 90% and 95% fractile of 270 MJ/m2 per floor area, 340 MJ/m2 per floor area and 
420 MJ/m2 pr floor area respectively based on a log-normal distribution. Based on the obtained values from 
the German and Swiss data, Thomas suggested that the design fire load density should be for the 80% or 
90% fractile. This has been widely used in Europe since, and will also be applied in the thesis by providing 
an assessment of the 50%,  80%, 90%, and 95% fractile.  
Spearpoint et al. [38] made a probabilistic approach to assess a fire load density by using existing studies in 
2015. The methodology accounted for the probability of the distribution of car segments, the occupancy 
within the car park building, and the fuel load of the cars. This approach gave a fire load density between 
230-280 MJ/m2 per floor area, with the majority of the 80%, 90% and 95% fractile between 258 MJ/m2, 261 
MJ/m2, and 263 MJ/m2 per floor area, corresponding to a 129 MJ/m2, 131 MJ/m2, and 132 MJ/m2 in closed 
surface (floor and ceiling). 
In recent years, Hertz et al. [4] used an average fire load of 12 GJ/car together with a car density of 18.0 
m2/car. This led to a fire load density of 660 MJ/m2 per floor area and 330 MJ/m2 per enclosed surface. 
Giuliani [41] also assumed a fuel load of 12 GJ/car but used a density of 22.5 m2/car, which led to a fire load 
density of 300 MJ/m2 pr enclosed surface.  
Looking at the regulations, the fuel load density for a car park isn’t specified in DS/EN 1991-1-2:2024 [64] or 
the Danish Annex. In the Swedish Boverkets författningssamling (BFS) [65], which is the Swedish national 
board of Housing, building and planning’s collection of statutes, the 80% fractile for the fuel load density of 
a car park is given as 400 MJ/m2 floor area. In New Zealand, the building code gives a design fire load 
density of 400 MJ/m2 floor area based on the verification method C/VM2 [66]. The Swedish and New 
Zealand building codes are aligned. 
2.5 DESIGN RULES FOR STRUCTURES IN CAR PARKS 
During the 1970s to the 2000s many full-scale fire tests were performed for the fire spread, rate of heat 
release, and temperature development around the world. In 1999, the European Commission collected the 
information from the newest studies at that time. This resulted in suggested design rules for closed car 
parks subjected to natural fires in the EUR 18867 EN [1]. Since then, these design rules have been widely 
used throughout Europe.  
The design rules stated that the fire spread and the severity of the fire depend on the duration of the fire in 
each car, the distance between the cars, and the total heat release of the cars. Based on the available 
studies at that time, using cars from the 1980s and 1990s, it is assumed that the fire in a car park is local, 
meaning that the cars will gradually burn out during the fire. This assumption was based on the test results 
in [13], where no more than 3-4 cars were burning at the same location and time, and led to a “wave” 
==PAGE:21==
Page 15 of 49 
 
hypothesis where each car starts to burn one after another with a delay of 12 min. However, the fire 
spread depends on the distance between the cars. The fire tests leading to a fire spread were performed 
with a distance of 0.5 m between the cars. A newer study [6] using CFD shows that the fire spread is 
expected to be less for a distance of 0.4 m and 0.6 m.  
Furthermore, the EUR 18867 EN stated a potential heat release of 12 GJ for an ICEV of 1,400 kg from the 
1980s. Since then, many fire tests have been performed for newer cars to find the total heat release of a 
car. This is discussed in Section 2.4. 
The design of car parks has therefore been performed based on the assumption that the fire is local, with 3-
4 burning cars spreading like a wave, with a delayed ignition time between the cars of 12 min, and a total 
heat release of a car of 12 GJ. 
With the increase in size and combustible materials, and the fires listed in Table 1, the reliability of the 
design rules can be questionable because it is no longer up to date with the actual parameters. 
2.6 FIRE DESIGN  
When designing structural elements for fire safety, a performance-based requirements approach is used. 
The requirements are typically in terms of the time equal to the time at which the element is expected to 
have sufficient load bearing capacity. The required time is often given as the European classification system 
e.g., R60, R120, etc. To ensure that this requirement is fulfilled, the fire scenario and a suitable design fire 
need to be selected. It is important to choose an accurate fire scenario and design fire, since the load 
bearing capacity is affected in both the heating face, at the fully developed fire, and in the cooling face.   
From the previous section, the fire scenario can be assumed to be a local fire. Since the 1900s, different 
time-dependent fire curves have been developed. The fire curves can be categorized as the standard fire 
ISO 834, and parametric fires.  
The standard fire ISO 834 is a temperature-time curve based on a severe fire in a compartment, and it only 
depends on the time of the fire; thus, the gas temperature will keep increasing. As a result, the curve does 
not take the cooling face, potential fire load, and the material properties of the enclosed surface area into 
account. Therefore, the curve does not represent a realistic fire behavior. Nonetheless, the time for the 
European classification system is determined based on ISO 834. The standard fire ISO 834 is given as:

3 ANALYSIS 
As mentioned in the previous section, the car industry has developed a lot and is still advancing with new 
technology and materials to secure driving comfort, economic fuel consumption, more effective engines, 
variation in fuel types, safety, and much more. However, this has led to larger and heavier cars with 
potentially more combustible materials and a larger fire load than what was expected previously. To assess 
the fire load density of a car park with modern cars, the fire load of a single car is considered based on a 
theoretical approach by analyzing the car components and their calorific value. The analysis will be 
performed for both the actual car fleet and the newly registered cars in Denmark. Since the cars have 
increased in size and mass, it is expected that the fire load will be higher for the newer cars. 
Furthermore, the car density is assessed based on established and recommended dimensions of the car 
parks. The recommended dimensions of car parks have not changed in the past 15 years. It is therefore 
expected that the car density corresponds to the values obtained in [4], even though newer 
recommendations for parking spots have been introduced. 
3.1 FIRE LOAD OF CARS 
The assessed fuel load of a single car is done by using Equation (2.1). The mass of each component in a car 
and the corresponding calorific value are found in the literature.  
Looking at the mass, the American Chemistry Council has conducted a large analysis of quantifying the 
chemical components in the average car produced in America and has made a report based on the analysis 
[7]. According to the report, the average weight of an American car has been rising since the late 1980s, 
with only small decreases in weight in some years. In the past decade, the weight growth has intensified, 
bringing the total average weight to the highest ever. Just in 2023, the average weight increased by 62 kg 
(136 lbs.) to 2013 kg (4,439 lbs.), which correspondence to an increase of 3.1 % of the total weight. In 
Figure 9, the development of the average mass of an American car is shown. It is observed from the figure 
that the average weight dropped significantly in the late 1970s, which was probably caused by the demand 
for fuel economy-efficient cars due to the higher gas prices caused by the oil crisis. Cars began to increase 
in weight in the late 1980s and continued throughout the 1990s, which could be caused by the introduction 
of SUVs. The weight then hovered around 1,800 kg (4,000 lbs.) from 2004 to 2017, where the weight 
==PAGE:23==
Page 17 of 49 
 
increased again and is still rising, which may be caused by the growth of the BEVs due to advances in 
batteries and manufacturing. 
However, in the period from 2004 to 2023, the average American car has increased its weight by 213 kg, 
corresponding to 11% of its total mass. Since the average weight of American cars has increased, it is 
expected that the cars in Denmark have done the same. 
3.1.1

load of a car, the increase in size and mass of the cars is important to investigate, as it is expected to lead to 
more combustible material, thus a larger fire load.

Materials 
The car consists of many different parts, as described in Section 2.2. As technology advances, more and 
more existing components are being replaced by lighter materials, primarily plastics or polymer 
composites, typically developed by the chemical industry. This has led to plastics accounting for 50% or 
more of the total volume in cars, while representing only about 10% of the total mass. The increased use of 
plastics in vehicles benefits fuel economy and helps reduce greenhouse gas emissions. However, from a fire 
safety perspective, this trend may pose challenges. Replacing non-combustible materials with combustible 
plastics increases the calorific value and, consequently, the potential heat release in the event of a fire.

3.2 DENSITY OF CARS 
When the specific fire load per car is determined, one must assess the density of the cars, thus determining 
the number of cars per area. As mentioned in Section 2.3.1, the layout of the parking area can vary a lot 
and consist of elements other than parking spots, such as maneuvering lanes, access streets, ramps, etc. 
The parking area can also be adjacent to buildings or basement functions, such as storage rooms, etc. Since 
this thesis aims to assess a simplified fire load density for the car park itself, areas with other functions are 
excluded, as they have different fire load densities and do not contain vehicles. Within the car park, cars 
are primarily located in parking spaces and only remain in other areas, such as driveways, ramps, or 
entrances, for short periods of time. When designing a specific parking area, the car density can be 
calculated as the number of cars divided by the total surface area of the floors, ceilings, and surrounding 
walls, depending on the layout and design. For the open-air parking areas, only the floor surface will 
conduct energy, since there are no walls or a ceiling. The car density in this case is therefore the floor 
surface. For open and large closed car parks, where the distance to an enclosed wall is large, the enclosing 
surface is the floor and the ceiling, since the wall will not dissipate heat from the combustion. However, for 
small closed parking areas, the walls will dissipate heat, and the enclosed surface will be the floor, ceiling, 
and walls. The point at which the closed parking area is considered large is not investigated in this thesis.  
In this thesis, the worst-case scenario for the parking area layout is investigated. This is done by finding the 
floor area per parking spot. The investigation is therefore done for a single parking spot with relevant 
maneuvering area, where it is assumed that the cars are only inside the parking spot and the access streets, 
ramps, etc., are neglected and thereby don’t contribute to the enclosed or floor surface area. Thus, only 
the maneuvering area and the parking spots will be included in the density calculation. An illustration of the 
considered area of a 90  parking spot is shown in Figure 20. The area is defined by the boundaries of the 
adjacent parking spots and the middle of the maneuvering street. As it is observed from the figure, this 
area represents both the case where there are multiple parking spot lanes and where the spot is positioned 
against a wall. The same area is considered for the parallel and angled parking shown in Section 2.3.1. 
In the following, the area represented in Figure 20 is investigated both for the recemented dimensions 
given in the design manual and actual parking spaces in Copenhagen.

3.2.3 
Occupancy 
When assessing the car density, some authors, e.g., Spearpoint et al. [38], suggest considering or applying a 
percentile of the occupancy of the car park building. The percentile of the occupancy can be used to 
prevent an overdimensioning of the structural elements. However, applying such a percentile of the 
occupancy must be considered carefully, and should only be performed for the actual car park. Anderson 
and Bell [82] performed a survey of parking space occupancy at a shopping mall over two weeks. The 
results for the particular car park showed that the peak period was in the middle of the day, where 90% of 
the parking spots were occupied. The results further showed that the highest occupancy level was at 99%. 
These results are relatable for the shopping malls in Denmark e.g., for the suburban areas, where the stores 
open at 10 am and close at 20 pm, and there are many visitors during Christmas time or after payday. 
However, Hertz et. al [4] claimed that by using the 90% fractile of the average car density, the average fire 
load of the car should be increased from the 50% to the 90% fractile, which gives almost the same fuel load 
density as the 100% filling with the 50% fractile fire load per car.  
Since the thesis aims to assess a fire load density for the worst-case scenario, it is reasonable to assume an 
occupancy of 100%, which was also the approach from Nigro et. al [83], Hertz et. al [4] and Giuliani [41].

Even though the fire load of the cars is different. This is because the ratio between the fuel load and the 
density is almost identical, where the used fuel load in this thesis is larger, but the layout is likewise less 
dense. The values are a little higher than the values from Giuliani [41], which is caused by the density and 
fuel load being lower in that study.   
Based on the current assumption, a fire load density of 650 MJ/m2 floor surface area and 330 MJ/m2 
enclosed area for the floor and ceiling is recommended and is regarded as a reasonable assessment for the 
current car fleet. Based on the used assumption, it is expected that the fuel load of cars will increase in the 
coming years. If the car park layout does not change at the same rate as the fuel load, a fire load of 830 
MJ/m2 floor surface will probably be reached in a couple of years. A further increase of the BEV will 
probably contribute to an even higher fire load density since these cars have a generally higher fuel density 
than the ICEV.

4 DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION 
In the present study, a fire load for a car park was assessed. This was done by a theoretical approach by 
assessing a fire load for the average Danish car and the car density for the parking area layout. From these 
values, a fire load density was assessed. 
The fire load of the cars was assessed by identifying the mass of individual combustible components and 
their respective calorific values. The investigation revealed that the mass of the Danish car fleet has 
increased by nearly 400 kg over the past two decades, reaching 1,530 kg for the 80% fractile. In the past 
decade alone, the weight of newly registered cars has risen by almost 500 kg, reaching 1,929 kg for the 80% 
fractile. This increase in car mass is primarily due to a general trend toward larger car sizes, changing 
consumer preferences favoring larger cars, and the growing number of BEVs. Alongside increased car size, 
the incorporation of new and more combustible materials, such as plastics and polymers, has further 
contributed to a higher total heat release and increased fire load. 
Based on a chemical report detailing the quantities of chemical substances in a typical car, the specific fire 
load (excluding fuel) was determined to be 8.17 MJ/kg for ICEVs and 8.34 MJ/kg for BEVs. For the current 
Danish car fleet, the resulting total heat release was calculated as 10.4 GJ for the average car and 12.5 GJ 
for the 80% fractile. For newly registered cars, the total heat release was found to be 13.7 GJ for the 
average car and 15.9 GJ for the 80% fractile. 
In 1999, the European Commission published a guideline introducing a reference fire load of 12 GJ per car, 
which has since been widely adopted. However, the results of this study indicate that current total heat 
releases are significantly higher. It can therefore be concluded that the 12 GJ fire load assumption is 
==PAGE:47==
Page 41 of 49 
 
outdated, and it will undoubtedly become even more inaccurate if current trends in car development 
continue over the coming years. 
The density of cars in a car park was investigated by analyzing a single parking space along with its 
associated parking and maneuvering area. The study considered various parking angles, with 90°, i.e., 
perpendicular parking, being the most common. Based on Danish design manuals for parking layouts and 
survey data, the average car density was found to be 20.6 m²/car, with the 80% fractile at 23.3 m²/car. As 
90° parking is the predominant configuration in car park buildings, further analysis focused specifically on 
this angle. For 90° parking, the average density across all occupancy levels was found to be 20.0 m²/car, 
and 21.5 m²/car for the 80% fractile. These values are within the range reported in the literature, although 
they are slightly lower than those recommended in the design manuals. It is therefore concluded that using 
20.0 m²/car and 21.5 m²/car for the average and 80% fractile, respectively, is reasonable based on the 
assumptions applied in this study. 
It is further concluded that, in terms of assessing a fire load density for a design fire, an occupancy of 100% 
is an appropriate assumption.  
The results for the car density show that the car density changes depending on the parking angle and the 
occupancy. However, this was not investigated further. 
Based on the values assessed above, the average and 80% fractile fire load densities can be determined for 
both the current car fleet and newly registered cars. For the current car fleet, the fire load density is 
calculated to be 298 MJ/m² of enclosed area for the average car, and 326 MJ/m² for the 80% fractile. For 
newly registered cars, the fire load density is 393 MJ/m² for the average and 416 MJ/m² for the 80% 
fractile. 
It is recommended to adopt a fire load density of 325 MJ/m² of enclosed surface, comprising both floor and 
ceiling, for the design of car parking buildings. A similar value was obtained in [4] and [41]. 
It was possible to find an equivalent design time and the corresponding resistance class when a standard 
fire exposed is assumed, based on the assess fire load density and the parametric Danish parametric fire 
curve. 
It is concluded that a reliable and conservative assessment of car fire load, car density, and fire load density 
can be achieved for use in fire safety design, based on the theoretical approach and assumptions applied in 
this study. It should therefore be noted that the values are only applicable to conventional car park 
buildings for passenger cars. Parking facilities for handicap vehicles, vans, trucks, and automated parking 
systems are not included in the scope of this project.
