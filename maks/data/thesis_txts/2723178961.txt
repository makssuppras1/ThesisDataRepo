Abstract
This thesis studies the effects of Fluid Inertia on the Dynamics of Mechanical Face Seals.
This is done by developing a 1-Dimensional Transient Axisymmetric Two-Phase Thermo-
Hydrodynamic model of a simplified seal with the inertial terms included in the Fluid Dy-
namics. The model is multi-physical in nature using multiple different areas of engineering
to cover all aspects of the seal.
This thesis focuses on the Fluid Dynamics part of the model, which is implemented using
the Finite Volume Method on the Incompressible Navier-Stokes Equations with a stag-
gered velocity grid to avoid Checker-Board Oscillations. The model also includes a Finite
Volume implementation of the Energy Equation and a Transient Equation of Motion for the
dynamic opening of the seal. Furthermore, the Heat Transfer in the seal rings is deter-
mined using the Finite Element Method, and the Heat Fluxes between the Fluid and the
seal rings are formulated to connect the Finite Volume grid and the Finite Element Mesh.
Furthermore, a Continuous Boiling Model is used for Phase Change and the Fluid Prop-
erties are evaluated using a Thermodynamic Saturation model based on the IAPWS-97
standard.
Combining the resulting equations yields a Differential Algebraic (DAE) system of equa-
tions, which is implemented and solved in MatLab using the ode15s-function.
To validate the program and study the effect of inertia, three validation cases are struc-
tured. This is done with the primary purpose of comparing the model with and without
inertia. It is found that the program performs reasonably well in the three cases, however,
the program has two significant shortcomings in its ability to solve a given system. It is
unable to solve Two-phase flow for film heights greater than 5µm, and dynamic simula-
tions where the lower seal ring is allowed to move. It is therefore not possible to make
any definitive conclusion on the effects of inertia on the dynamics of the seal.

Introduction
Pumps are a fundamental technological cornerstone of modern society, as they constitute
a core part of any application of the manipulation of fluids. The importance of pumps in the
world that we live in today cannot be understated, and the amount of different applications
is truly staggering. From water treatment, sewage movement, irrigation, and flood control
to the motor industry, oil and natural gas industries, cooling systems, and many more.
Estimates suggest that pumps account for over 10% of the world’s energy consumption
[1, 2]. It is therefore very important to understand how pumps work, how to make them
more efficient, and how they fail. While the mechanical and fluid dynamics parts are quite
well-understood and thoroughly modeled, the seals which are responsible for minimising
leakage of the pumped fluid are less well-understood. It is estimated that around 40-50%
of failures in pumps occur due to seal failure [3]. While there are many different types
of seals, the ones of interest in this thesis are the subcategory called Mechanical Face
Seals, exemplified in figure 1.1.
Mechanical face seals are in many ways similar to hydrodynamic thrust bearings studied
in Tribology, in that they both use a very thin fluid film and two disc-like objects to achieve
their respective design goals. However, the primary objective of a mechanical face seal
is to minimise leakage, which is done by minimising the fluid film height. This means that
the expected film height in a mechanical face seal is in the order of 1µm [4], whereas
the expected film height of hydrodynamic thrust bearings is around 100µm. Not only is
this small film height hard to measure for experiments, but it also introduces numerical
problems in computer simulations.
This thesis focuses on mechanical face seals in water pumps, where under certain oper-
ational conditions the fluid film can evaporate, which can give way to instabilities.

1.1
Project Outline
This project is a continuation of the work done by Michael Gani [5] and William Steeval
[6]. Gani started with his PHD-thesis, where he in collaboration with Grundfos devel-
oped a program to simulate two-phase flow in mechanical face seals. Gani gathered the
foundational assumptions and equations for steady state and introduced the monolithic
approach to solving the equations. Steeval, for his master’s thesis, expanded the equa-
tions to be transient and developed a new program to simulate transient two-phase flow
in mechanical face seals, with the ultimate goal of studying puffing.
The goal of this project is the expand the program developed by Steeval to include fluid
inertia. Inertia is often neglected in the analysis of mechanical face seals because it is
assumed that the flow is dominated by viscous forces. This project seeks to examine the
effect of inertia to see if this assumption is reasonable and under which conditions the
inertia becomes important.
Because this project is an extension of the work done by Steeval, it was decided early on
to change Steeval’s program instead of developing an entirely new one. Since the goal
of this project was to study inertia, most of the work went into the development of a fluid
dynamics model that includes inertia. As a consequence of this, a lot of the equations,
and most of the program, were developed by Steeval. These include the Equations of
Motion, the Energy Equation, and the Heat Transfer equations. However for the sake of
completeness, and to ensure that this report stands on its own, all of the fundamental
equations which constitute the model that the program is based on will be covered in this
report.

Due to the inherent multi-physical nature of the problem, it is decomposed into its essential
mechanical, thermal, and fluid components. These are illustrated by the numbers in figure
2.1 and are as follows:
1. Force Balance: The opening and closing of the seal is governed by a force balance
described by Newton’s 2nd law.
2. Fluid Properties: The fluid properties are evaluated using the Ideal Gas Law and
the saturation functions by Gani [5]. These properties can have very large variations,
due to the possibility of two-phase flow.
3. Fluid Dynamics: To determine the dynamics of the fluid the Finite Volume Method
(FVM) is applied to the fundamental fluid conservation laws (Conservation of Mo-
mentum, Mass, and Energy) with the goal of developing a model that includes the
fluid inertia.
4. Heat conduction: Due to the importance of temperature for phase change the
conduction of heat in the seal rings must be modeled, which is done using the Finite
Element method.
5. Heat Transfer between Fluid and Rings: The wall heat fluxes are modeled to cou-
ple the fluid temperature from the finite volume grid to the seal ring heat conduction
in the finite element mesh.

2.2
Fluid Dynamics
The flow between the seal rings is modeled using the fundamental conservation laws in
fluid dynamics - the Navier-Stokes equations (Conservation of Momentum and Mass) and
the Energy equation (Conservation of Energy). In order to be able to solve the equations
the following assumptions are made about the fluid flow:
1. Small film thickness: The film thickness is assumed to be very small, at around
„ 1–50µm. Mechanical face seals are designed to operate at small film thicknesses
to minimise leakage.
2. Axisymmetry: The seal is assumed to be axisymmetric, meaning that there is no
change in the tangential direction, B
Bθ = 0.
3. No axial velocity: Due the to small film thickness it is assumed that there is no
velocity across the fluid film, w = 0, as there is little space for the fluid to accelerate
and decelerate. The exception to this is the lower ring, which is allowed to move
axially, however, this is governed by the equations of motion as the change in film
thickness.
4. Fluid properties and pressure are constant across the fluid film: Due to the
small film thickness, it is assumed that any gradient across the film would be so
large that they are thermodynamically unstable and therefore disperse quickly. The
exception to this is the temperature and radial velocity.
5. Temperature distribution across film thickness: The temperature across the film
is assumed to be a 2nd order polynomial. The boundary conditions are given by the
temperature on the seal ring surface resulting from the heat transfer model.
6. Radial velocity across film thickness: It is assumed that inertia only affects the
magnitude of the velocity and not the velocity distribution. It thereby follows that
the radial velocity across the film is a 2nd order polynomial with no-slip boundary
conditions. Furthermore, film thickness averaged velocities are used to evaluate
the velocity at a given point.

allows for the construction of a system of equations, that can be solved numerically, yield-

There are two different ways of dealing with checker-board oscillations [8]. The first is

this requirement. This is solved by using the ghost point method, where an imaginary vol-

ghost point values are defined so the prescribed value appears on the boundary volume
face.

operational conditions for the seal and are applied as boundary conditions.

3.2
Numerical Considerations
Using a pre-built solver like ode15s comes with the advantage of taking care of the majority
of the numerical considerations. However, there are still a few key areas that have to be
done manually, including normalisation and the pattern of the Jacobi-matrix, which will be
covered in this section. For further information on numerical considerations see Steeval
[6].
3.2.1
Normalisation
Normalisation is a critical step in making the equations solvable and is used in two different
ways in the program. The first of which is in regard to the system parameters. Due to
the nature of the system, there are large differences in parameter values - e.g. pressure

„ 1µm = 10´6m. This can lead to numerical difficulties when solving the system, yet,
these problems can be alleviated by normalising the parameters so that they are as close
to unity as possible. This is done by applying the following equation to the parameters,

the continuity equation is normalised with respect to the mass flow rate since it essentially
is a balance of the mass flow rate.
3.2.2
Jacobian
Although it is possible for the program to calculate the entire Jacobi-matrix each time
a simulation is run, it is very computationally expensive, especially for larger grid sizes
where the matrix is very big. Yet, it often does not improve the solution significantly.
The alternative is giving the solver the pattern of the Jacobi-matrix, that being a sparse ma-
trix that indicates where the Jacobi-matrix has values. This Jacobi-pattern only changes
with grid and mesh dimensions, since only the positions of the Jacobi-matrix values are of
interest and not the values themselves. This means that the Jacobi-matrix for the system
can be calculated once, from which a mesh-dependent script that reproduces that pattern
can be formulated. For more information on exactly how the Jacobi-matrix for the sys-
tem is found see Steeval [6]. The Jacobi pattern is different in the new program because
velocity is included as a state variable. The new pattern is shown in figure 3.1.

4.2
Program Limitations
The results presented so far are for the cases where the program works. However, the
program has two major gaps in its ability to solve a given system, which will be covered in
this section. Those two gaps are two-phase flow for larger film heights and with free film
height.
4.2.1
Two-Phase Flow
The program has difficulties solving two-phase systems for film heights above around
5µm. It is able to solve these cases for a short time but stops at a certain point for some
unknown reason. It seems that the problem is numerical in nature since the two-phase
simulations below 5µm, as shown in the validation section 4.1, seem to be similar enough
to the results from the old program that they can be deemed physical. The inclusion of two-
phase flow adds another layer of complexity to the system. It introduces large differences
between the values in the velocity vector, which only become larger as the film height
increases. These differences are hard to take into account using normalisation as they
are differences inside the vector itself. It is possible that this could be solved by introducing
two different normalisation values, one for liquid velocities and one for vapour velocities.
It should be noted that the reason that this is not a problem for the old program is that it
did not include velocity as a state variable.
An example of this kind of simulation failure can be seen in figure 4.10, which was made
using the parameters in table 4.6. In figure 4.10a the pressure and quality are plotted.
The pressure seems to be somewhat reasonable, but the quality is too continuous to
be correct. The film thickness should be low enough that boiling still would be discrete,
unlike what is seen in the figure. Figure 4.10b shows the difference in the velocity vector,
mentioned before. The maximum value after the boiling point is around 1500 m/s, while
the liquid part is still 1 m/s. It is this difference that is speculated to be the cause of
the problem, as the differences were not this large in the results for the low film height
simulation in section 4.1.1. Figure 4.10c shows the normalised enthalpy as a function of
time and is included to show how much the variables change per time step. The enthalpy
initially changes a lot each time-step, but eventually enters a region where the change is
more steady. This lasts up until a point near the end of the simulation where the enthalpy
again starts to change rapidly just before the program breaks and stops working. Lastly,
figure 4.10d shows how the boiling point location changes as a function of time, and is a
good representation of how the program handles these two-phase simulations. It seems
that the program really wants the boiling point to be near the ends of the seal rings, where
the fluid is either fully vapour or fully liquid.

4.2.2
Free Film Height
As mentioned earlier, the primary interest of this project is to study the effect of inertia
on the dynamics of mechanical face seals. However, the program seems to be unable to
solve the most interesting cases of dynamic behaviour, i.e. those where the film height is
not locked, but free to move as determined by the equation of motion, 2.7.
The equation of motion is a force balance dependent on the pressure distribution and
the balance ratio of the seal. Ideally, this means that with free film height the seal would
eventually find an equilibrium position where the force balance would be zero, whereby
the film height would not change. Not obtaining this equilibrium position means that the
seal fails, as the force balance always will be either positive or negative, respectively
resulting in either the seal popping open or the fluid film collapsing.
An example of these types of seal failures is shown in figure 4.11. The results are found
using the parameters in table 4.7. In figure 4.11a the simulation stops because it has
reached the end time, but even in that short period the film height has grown exponentially
to 2000µm. In figure 4.11b the simulation stops before the end time because the solver
can not take another time step without the film height dropping below zero.

5
Further Work
This section covers the possible solutions to the program’s unsolved problems, presented
in the previous section, and suggestions on how to develop the program in the future.
These suggestions are a combination of the features that were intended to be included if
time permitted and recommendations on what direction the overarching project of simu-
lating mechanical face seals should take.
5.1
Program Problems
The program has two significant problems, those being solving two-phase and dynamics
simulations as covered in section 4.2. It would therefore be prudent to try to resolve these
underlying problems and make sure that the model works as intended, before expanding
the program and adding new features.
As mentioned, the two problems seem to be numerical in nature, and should therefore be
able to be resolved by changing the implementation of the model. The first problem, of not
being able to solve two-phase flow for larger film height, appears to be a consequence of
large differences in the values of the velocity vector. It is possible that this could be solved
by implementing a velocity vector normalisation, that changes based on the phase of the
fluid. To do so one would need two different normalisation values, one for the vapour
velocities and one for the liquid velocities, which would be applied to the velocity vector
using the quality vector. This would allow the large vapour velocities to better match the
low liquid velocities, which should improve the solvability of the system.
The second problem manifests itself as unreasonably fast vibrations with negative damp-
ening when the system should find an equilibrium position. Due to the character of the
vibrations, this problem also seems to be numerical in nature. While the exact source of
this problem is unknown, the problem can be alleviated by removing the viscous friction
term from the momentum, as it seems to dampen the vibrations. However, this is not a
good approach, since this is one of the most important terms in the equation. The ap-
proach to solving this problem should be first to find the underlying causes of it, and then
devise a plan to fix them.
However, it is also possible that the problems are symptoms of a faulty model. The model
implemented and presented in this project is my best guess at a fluid dynamics model with
inertia and is by no means final. Over 6 different models were derived and implemented
over the course of this project. This trial and error approach was necessary due to the lack
of literature on this particular combination of subjects - Inertia is most often neglected in
the analysis of mechanical face seals and axisymmetric CFD formulations of the Navier-
Stokes equations are rarely discussed in detail.
Lastly, it should be noted that throughout this project the majority of the time has been
spent on deriving a new fluid dynamics model to include inertia. This means that the
inclusion of fluid inertia effects in the rest of the equations could have been somewhat
overlooked. Specifically, in the derivation of the energy equation 2.71, there is a step,
that can be seen in Steeval’s thesis [6], as it is not included here, where a derivative of
the velocity is neglected. While the effects of this should be minimal, it is important that
the assumptions are applied consistently to the equations. This was not corrected due to
time constraints.

5.2
Excluded Features
In this section the features that were originally considered for implementation, but ulti-
mately disregarded, will be covered. They were not included in the model due to time
constraints and a desire to make the program functional before adding more complex
features to it
The first of these features is the inclusion of the derivative of the velocity with respect to
time in the momentum equation. In the current model, this term is neglected to simplify the
system as much as possible while still including the inertia terms. The inclusion of this term
is a little more tricky than the rest of the inertial term, as it turns the momentum equation
from an algebraic equation to a differential equation in the DAE system. This is in itself
not hard to implement, one would only need to change the mass matrix for the momentum
equation to have ones in the diagonal, like the other differential equations. However, there
is still the consideration of how the term should be handled in the finite volume analysis,
which is the primary reason it was excluded from the model. The overall effect of including
this term should be small in theory, but would nevertheless be interesting.
The second excluded feature mentioned by Steeval [6] regarding the implementation of
an empirical model for heat flux between the fluid and the seal rings. The argument is
that the current formulation of the heat flux is reversely proportional to the film thickness,
which introduces numerical stiffness to the problem at low film heights.

The most logical next step for the evolution of the overarching project is an expansion into
2-dimensions. This can be done in one of two ways, depending on what area of study is
of interest.
The first option is to expand the model in the axial direction. This would allow for an exam-
ination of the fluid properties and state variables across the fluid film and the assumptions
that are often had in this regard. E.g. the assumption that the fluid properties are constant
across the fluid film, with the exception of temperature and velocity, or the assumption that
inertia only affects the magnitude of the flow and not the velocity profile itself.
The second option is to expand the model in the tangential direction by removing the
assumption of axisymmetry. This would allow the program to simulate more complex and

6
Conclusion
This thesis represents an extension of the work done by Steeval [6], who developed a
transient thermo-hydrodynamic model for simulating the dynamics of mechanical face
seals. The goal of this thesis is to expand this model to include inertia in order to study
the effect thereof on the dynamics. This is done by including the velocity as a state variable
and by developing and implementing a new fluid dynamics model, that includes inertia.
The model is derived from the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations using the Finite
Volume Method and the equations are reduced to a 1D axisymmetric formulation to fit
the preexisting model. To avoid the problem of checker-board oscillations, velocity is
implemented using a staggered grid approach. If this is not accounted for the results
would be non-physical as they would contain two different solutions superimposed on one
another, due to the pressure dependencies arising from the Finite Volume formulation.
The new fluid dynamics model is implemented as a modification to Steeval’s program
rather than as a standalone program. Since this thesis focuses on one particular aspect
of the dynamics many of the governing equations remain the same. Hereby changing
the preexisting program would be easier and would allow the programs to be more easily
compared.
The new system of equations governing the dynamics of mechanical face seals is a type
of different equation called Differential Algebraic Equations (DAE), as it contains both
differential and algebraic equations. Such a DAE system is solved using MatLab’s ode15s
function. To solve the system all parameters and the equations themselves are normalised
to reduce the numerical stiffness.
To validate the program it is compared mostly to its previous iteration in three different
validation cases using seal data from Lau et Al [11]. The first validation case compares
the program to Gani’s [5] and Steeval’s [6] programs. This is done by a single two-phase
simulation with locked film height. The purpose of this comparison is to examine the
effect of inertia when the film height is small, where the differences in the models should
be small in theory. The results show that the general shapes of the results match, but
they do not match exactly when the simulation parameters are the same. However, by
changing the temperature boundary condition in the new model from 207C˝ to 204C˝,
the results become very similar. The velocity distribution is almost identical to the other
models, but the pressure is underestimated.
The second validation case compares the new and old programs to determine the relative
error which is made by not including inertia at different film heights. To do so 6 different
film heights are simulated in one-phase flow. The results show that for film heights in the
range of 1µm ă h ă 15µm the relative error is almost constant at 9-10%. However when
the film height increases above 15µm the error starts to grow exponentially reaching 100%
at a film height of around 50µm. Comparing this the to maximum film height reached by
Steeval’s puffing simulation, around 30µm, this corresponds to a maximum error of around
40%, assuming the error is the same for two-phase flow.
The third simulation case compares the effect of the assumed taper angle. The results are
in accordance with both the theory and the old program. The resulting velocity distributions
match very well, but the pressure is still underestimated in the new program.

While the results from the validation cases suggest that the implementation of the inertial
effects is successful, the program still has two major shortcomings in its ability to solve
a given system. Firstly, the program has trouble solving two-phase flow for film heights
larger than 5µm. It is speculated that this problem arises from the large differences in
velocities due to the presence of phase change. As the film height increases these differ-
ences become very large and would therefore introduce numerical stiffness to the system.
Secondly, the program has trouble with the most interesting dynamics cases, where the
lower ring is free to move. The exact cause of this problem is unknown but seems to be
numerical in nature.
All in all, when the program functions the results seem to be reasonable and in accordance
with the other programs. However, due to the numerical issues, I remain hesitatant to
make any concrete conclusions regarding the effects of inertia, before these problems
are resolved.
