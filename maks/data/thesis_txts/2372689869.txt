Abstract
Robustly measure the absorption coeﬃcient of materials at low frequencies is a chal-
lenge, because the diﬀraction on the edges of the samples bias the measurement. In
order to minimize this ’size eﬀect’, an endﬁre loudspeaker array can be used to achieve a
sound beam, focusing the sound in the center of the sample. To investigate this possible
solution, a directive array is designed from simulation, and successfully realized exper-
imentally from free drivers.
The surface impedance of an absorptive sample is then
measured using the directive array designed and a monopole-like source, and the results
compared. This document describes the process of the experimental realization of the
array. The loudspeaker units of the array were designed using the PSPice software and
their frequency response measured. The array is designed via mathematical simulation
on Matlab and its parameters adjusted after experimental tests to ﬁt real-world physi-
cal limitations, such as the limited sensitivity of the driver used. The devised array is
then used as source to measure the surface impedance of an absorptive material with
a pressure-velocity sensor on various sizes of sample, and the results compared to ones
obtained with a single, monopole-like source. The results show that a better agreement
with the theoretical values of the impedances is obtained with the array source, mainly
at low frequencies. However, the size of the sample used proves to still inﬂuence the
’array’ measurements at a signiﬁcant extent.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The project aims at designing a procedure to robustly measure the random incidence
absorption coeﬃcient at low frequencies. The current ISO 354 [8], based on reverbera-
tion chamber method, is highly unreliable and unreproducible. A more precise way is
to measure the normal and oblique absorption coeﬃcients in anechoic conditions with
a suﬃcient angular resolution and to use Paris’ law to compute the random incidence
absorption coeﬃcient. However, below approximately 800 Hz, this method shows sig-
niﬁcant errors due to diﬀraction on the specimen’s edges [2].
Loudspeaker arrays are systems made of several loudspeaker units that can generate
a narrow sound beam when properly designed.
The use of an array might allow to
focus the sound beam in the center of the sample tested, minimizing the inﬂuence of
the unwanted edges diﬀraction. The purpose of this study is to report whether a di-
rective array can be successfully designed, built and used to robustly measure surface
impedance, from ’elementary’ mathematical tools and equipment. For simplicity, the
study is narrowed to the normal incidence case, but such a setup would allow to measure
oblique incidence absorption if success is met.
The ﬁrst chapter of this report shows how the loudspeakers units of the array are de-
signed to ensure a successful experimental realization. The second chapter describes
how the beamforming array was devised and realized experimentally based on an ex-
isting near ﬁeld beamforming algorithm. Finally, the third chapter describes how the
impedance measurement setup was designed and compares the measurements of surface
impedances and absorption coeﬃcients obtained using a directive array with the ones
obtained using a single loudspeaker.

Chapter 2
Driver enclosing
In order to build the loudspeaker array, proper loudspeaker units have to be designed and
built to ensure a suitable frequency response in the frequency range investigated, from
100 Hz to 700 Hz. This chapter describes how the prototype of loudspeaker unit used
was designed to satisfy the purpose of the project, built and validated experimentally.
2.1
Theory
A driver radiating in free air has poor low-frequency radiation, because the low-frequency
volume velocities emitted from the front and the rear of the diaphragm are out of phase
and tend to cancel. A simple solution to avoid this problem is to mount the driver on
a portable enclosure. Indeed, when the driver is mounted on one wall of the box, the
radiation of the rear is trapped in the enclosure and does not cancel the front radiation
anymore, allowing a better low-frequency response.
Since the study investigates the low frequencies range (from 750 Hz down to 100 Hz),
it is needed to enclose free drivers to ensure a suitable low-frequency response, which is
dependent on the box dimensions, as it might help to reach a signiﬁcant beamforming
at low frequencies.
On the other hand, it is aimed at keeping the radiation pattern at the frequencies inves-
tigated close to a monopole radiation, i.e. omnidirectional, by keeping the dimensions
of the boxes small compared to the wavelength. The reason for this is that it allows a
more realistic simulation and an easier experimental validation.
Another reason to keep the boxes small compared to the wavelengths of interest is that
it prevents unwanted interactions between the several speakers when they are put close
together in array.
This chapter presents the design of the enclosures for the drivers in regards to these
considerations.

Several pieces of information can be taken from these results:
- The resonance frequency of the 3 inches driver (133 Hz) is already above 100 Hz, and
thus indicates the 5-inch driver as the best driver to reach a good frequency response
down to 100 Hz (fs=79 Hz). This was expected considering the sizes of the drivers;
- Both drivers exhibit a quality factor greater than 0.7. Enclosing them will not allow
reaching the minimum lower cutoﬀfrequency as described in section 3.2.2.
From these results, it is decided to design the enclosed loudspeakers by using the 5
inches drivers.

This chapter presents the design of the directive array, realized through mathematical

3.1

3.1.1
Loudspeaker arrays
Two types of loudspeaker arrays were considered to achieve the beamforming:
- Broadside array;
- Endﬁre array.
Broadside array is a conﬁguration in which a number of loudspeakers are arranged in
a row, and the primary radiation is in the plan orthogonal to the row. This is the typical
subwoofer arrangement seen in most outdoor music venues, either stacked (horizontal
row) or ﬂown (vertical row). However, for the purpose of the project, a two-dimensional
Broadside array would be needed to create a beam in one single direction.
Figure 3.1: Schematics of broadside arrays

In endﬁre conﬁguration, the loudspeakers are placed in a spaced row pointing at the
desired direction of radiation, with speciﬁc delays between the diﬀerent drivers in order
to achieve a narrow beam pattern.
For the simplicity of the study, only arrays with a regular spacing between the drivers
were considered.
The goal is to design an array that provides an eﬃcient enough beam forming in the

range investigated (100-700 Hz), while keeping an experimental validation easy enough
to setup and to reproduce.
With this perspective, and in order to narrow the ﬁeld of study to the simplest case, it

direction (in the direction of the line array) with a simple geometry. In comparison, a
broadside array would allow steering the sound in a single direction at the price of a
more complex, two-dimensional geometry (such as a cross-shaped array).

3.1.3
Array performance parameters
The performances of loudspeakers array are usually quantiﬁed with two parameters:
their power gain, deﬁned as the ratio of the output power in the steered direction to the
input power, and their directivity, the ratio of radiated power in the steered direction to
the radiated power in all directions. These parameters are usually translated into the
dB scale as Power Index (PI) and Directivity Index (DI), respectively.
In order to quantify and compare the performances of the simulated arrays, two
parameters are investigated: the brightness ratio and the contrast ratio.
With the same notation as in the previous section, the brightness ratio is computed as

which is a source a real-world issues because it challenges the robustness of the drivers
to deal with high amplitude input signals without distortion and self-noise.

simply quantiﬁes the ability of the system to direct the sound in the preferred direction.

3.4
Conclusion on array design
From the experimental results, it appears that the simulation algorithm used allows
designing a directive array. It means particularly that the monopole source assumption
is close enough to the reality in the frequency range investigated to allow a performing
beamforming. However, the simulation does not take accurately into account some of
the physical limits of the real-world array, such as the lower frequency limit of beam-
forming set by the limited length of the array and the limited precision of the speaker
when dealing with high inputs. Special attention must be given to the power index per-
formance parameter, as it quantiﬁes how demanding in power the algorithm is. These
current limitations constitute clues to improve the simulation. For this project, a sig-
niﬁcant part of the array design is adjusted experimentally. This is made by varying
the tuning factor β and ﬁnding the values that allow a suﬃcient directivity while not
overdriving the speaker to distortion. According to the results presented here, an array
design with a tuning factor β = 70 and an array length of 2 meters provides a signiﬁcant
beamforming, with a SPL drop of more than 15 dB eighty centimeters away from the
axis, in all frequency bands.

4.1.2
Impedance measurement procedure
The method used to measure the impedance in this project uses a pressure-velocity (PU)
microphone, which allows to directly measure the particle velocity at the location of the
sensor. The use of that sensor allows having the measurement point very close to sample
tested, as compared to the method using two pressure microphones (P-P method).
By measuring the acoustical impedance at the location of the microphone, the surface
impedance is however not exactly measured. Obtaining the surface impedance from
the measured impedance requires more advanced calculation, based on the sound ﬁeld
properties and the distance from the surface to the probe. As the properties of the
sound ﬁeld are not known, plane wave approximation (PWA) deduction method can
be used to retrieve the surface impedance from the measured impedance. Using this
method, the hypothesis is made that the sound beam locally behaves as a plane wave
propagating in the z-direction (direction of the array’s axis). As seen in [6], the surface
impedance ZS is then derived from the impedance measured ZM via

the measurement point is located at a distance from the sample that is not negligible
compared to the wavelength.
However, as the distance from the PU sensor to the sample is very small compared to
the wavelengths investigated, this correction has very small inﬂuence, and the surface
impedance can thus be approximated by the measured impedance.
4.1.3
Inﬂuence of edge diﬀraction on measurements
The eﬀect of the size of a sample for the normal absorption coeﬃcient measurement
leads to errors with most of the mathematical model, as most of them assume a sample
of inﬁnite size.
Indeed, the ﬁniteness of the sample implies diﬀraction on its edges,
thus interfering with the sound ﬁeld. As investigated in [1], the size eﬀect shows as an
"oscillatory behavior" of the absorption coeﬃcient and surface impedance measured, and
can be interpreted physically as constructive and destructive interferences caused by the
diﬀraction on the edges of the sample. The dimensions of the sample determine at what
frequency the oscillations are found. In the ideal case where the microphone is located
in the center of a square sample, the wavelength of the frequency at which the ﬁrst lobe
appears corresponds to the dimension of the sample, as seen on ﬁgure 4.1. Locating

the microphone near the geometrical center of the sample is also meant to increase the
amplitudes of the lobes, as the eﬀects of the several edges add up. When the sensor is
not located in the center, the frequency at which the ﬁrst lobe becomes less predictable.

4.3.1
General comments
From the results obtained, several comparisons are made and presented in this section.
For reason of visibility, the results presented in this section are smoothed by averag-
ing over 10 Hz bands. The impedance plots are displayed in international units, here
Pa · s/m
On the one hand, the measurement obtained for the three diﬀerent sizes of sample

types of sources are compared for each size of sample to see how the array source aﬀects

The results obtained deviate signiﬁcantly from the model used, especially at low fre-
quencies. At the very low end of the range investigated (below 130 Hz), some of the
measurement look corrupted, as some real parts of the measured impedances diverging
or going to zero. An unexpected ’oscillatory behavior’ of both surface impedance and
absorption coeﬃcient are constantly found in every measurement and appears as the
main source of error. These issues can be explained by several factors:
- The oscillatory behavior of the results is likely due partly to an inaccurate calibration,
as the lobes of the oscillations correspond quite well to the ones found in the modulus
and phase of the calibration function, seen on ﬁgure 4.3.
- The soundcard used to send the several signals through the loudspeakers could only
handle signal length of a few seconds. It was thus needed to repeat the signals sent, so
that a ’click’ sound could be heard periodically in some measurements. It is not clear
at what extent this issue has biased the measurements.

4.4
Conclusion on impedance measurements
The measurements performed show that the use of a directive array has an inﬂuence on
the measurement of the surface impedance and absorption coeﬃcient, as the measures
realized with an array source provide signiﬁcantly diﬀerent impedances measurements
below 400 Hz, where the array source provides a better directivity than the monopole.
It can especially be observed for a given sample size, the directive array source provides
a better agreement with the reference Miki model.
However, the trends regarding the eﬀects of sample size are not completely in agree-
ment with the expectations. Indeed, the measurement performed with the array are
even more dependent on the size of the sample than the monopole below 200 Hz, while
the higher directivity is meant to provide more consistency. The reason why the mea-
surement with the array source is more aﬀected by the size might be due to the fact that
the sample and the rigid backing aﬀect unexpectedly the array sound ﬁeld, which has
not been investigated via simulation. Moreover, the oscillations related to the size eﬀect
behavior cannot be identiﬁed clearly from the results, as signiﬁcant deviations from the
model may be related to the measurement uncertainties (uncertain calibration of the
PU sensor, few clicks in the sent signal).

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The results of the project show that it is possible to successfully design and realize
experimentally a highly, low frequency (100-600 Hz) directive loudspeaker array, based
on an existing beamforming method. In this project, the loudspeaker were made of 5
inches drivers enclosed into cubic boxes with edge length of 16 cm. Several prototypes of
directive arrays were simulated and tested, varying diﬀerent parameters relative to the
beamforming algorithm or the geometrical parameters of the array. A highly directive
array with a signiﬁcant low frequency directivity compared to the monopole situation
was achieved, and used to measure the surface impedance and absorption coeﬃcient on
acoustic samples of various sizes. Comparing the obtained results to the values obtained
with a single loudspeaker, it appears that the array provides results closer to the cho-
sen reference model. However, the size of the sample is unexpectedly inﬂuencing the
results obtained with the array, which might be linked to the fact that the presence of
the absorptive sample and rigid backing were not taken in account in the beamforming
implementation.
A certain number of aspects in the project could be improved for further investiga-
tion. Experimentally, The use of more precise and robust drivers could allow a better
beamforming ar low frequencies. In the simulaton, the beamforming algorithm could be
improved by adjusting the tuning factor for every frequency and modelling the source
as pistons instead of simple monopole sources. Morevover, more advanced modelling
could be used to take the presences of the absorptive sample and the rigid backing in
the beamforming implementation. Finally, a more robust calibration of the PU sensor
should be beneﬁcial to achieve more accurate and reliable surface impedance measure-
ments. If consistent results are eventually obtained for normal incidence, further work
could study at what extent oblique incidence cases can be handled by the method.
