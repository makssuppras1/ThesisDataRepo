Abstract
Unicellular plankton comprise half of the primary producers in the biosphere, hence
they play an important role to the removal of atmospheric CO2. At the same time,
copepods are the most abundant mesozooplankton and they contribute to carbon se-
questration through the production of fast-sinking fecal pellets. This thesis presents
the implementation and analysis of the “NUM” (Nutrient-Unicellular-Multicellular)
framework extending it with the addition of diatoms, dividing thus the unicellu-
lar component of the model into classes of diatoms and mixotrophic protists. The
trait-based approach was employed with size being the key-trait for the description
of the physiology and predator-prey interactions of mixotrophs, diatoms and cope-
pods. The aim of the thesis is to quantify the relative contribution of mixotrophs,
diatoms and copepods to the carbon export. The mechanisms governing the ecosys-
tem structure were explored through dynamic simulations for diﬀerent environmen-
tal conditions. Picophytoplankton is shown to account for the biggest fraction of
carbon export, with diatoms being the phytoplankton with the least ﬂuctuating
contribution to it. This model enlightens the mechanistic processes that shape the
size-structure of the epipelagic plankton community, which sets the strength of the
biological pump. The ﬁndings of the model improve the understanding of the eco-
logical mechanisms that drive the carbon cycle and climate processes.

Introduction
The biological pump is the biologically driven carbon sequestration from the at-
mosphere to the deep ocean.
In the ﬁrst step of this process, photoautotrophs
(photosynthetic organisms) ﬁx carbon dioxide from the sea surface and convert it
into organic matter. This organic carbon can be respired in the euphotic zone or
below and it can reach the deeper layers as particulate organic carbon that can
sink passively (gravitational biological pump), be injected to depth through phys-
ical processes or through vertical migrations of organisms that feed on the surface
and respire at depth (i.e. copepods, ﬁsh). At the seabed, a fraction of organic carbon
can be sequestered for hundreds of years [18, 56]. Estimates show that the biological
pump removes more than 10 billion tons of carbon from the ocean’s euphotic zone
(the uppermost layer of the ocean where sunlight can penetrate enabling photosyn-
thesis) per year [11]. This amount accounts for roughly two-thirds of the vertical
transfer of carbon in the sea [47]. Therefore, the biological pump plays a major
role in the ocean’s carbon cycle and the gravitational biological pump is the process
addressed in this study.
All the carbon in the biological pump originates from unicellular plankton. Au-
totrophic unicellular plankton are responsible for approximately half of the plane-
tary primary production (the assimilation and ﬁxation of CO2 into organic matter)
[21] and more than 90% of total marine primary production. Unicellular plankton
include diverse marine organisms of diﬀerent sizes, such as diatoms, dinoﬂagellates,
ciliates and coccolithophores and also some cyanobacteria (Fig.1) [38]. Diatoms are
a key group for sequestration among these organisms. That is because they have
bigger mass and density due to their silicate shell and therefore higher sinking ve-
locities. Moreover, diatoms appear in extensive spring blooms and contribute about
40% of the primary production of the oceans [19]. For theses reasons, diatoms are
an integral part of the biological pump.
The second step of the biological pump is the ﬂux of diﬀerent forms of organic matter,
including dead unicellular plankton. This ﬂux out of the euphotic zone is described
by the term carbon ﬂux. The carbon ﬂux from the surface to the deep sea is divided
in “export ﬂux” that is leaving the bottom of the euphotic zone and the “sequestration
==PAGE:7==
4
ﬂux” from the bottom of the mesopelagic zone (the layer below the water’s surface
between 200 and 1,000 meters); that is because when carbon sinks deeper than the
sequestration depth, it stays there for more than a century [47]. The export ﬂux
from the euphotic zone is in the form of particulate organic carbon (POC) which
consists of phytodetritus, which is detritus (dead organic matter) originating from
phytoplankton, fecal matter from zooplankton and ﬁsh and macroscopic organic
aggregates known as “marine snow”, that include all of the above plus dead animals,
soot, sand and other inorganic dust [66]. Each of these forms of POC contribute
to the carbon ﬂux to varying degrees according to their state, in particular the
abundance, size distribution and sinking velocity of the particles. Hence, in order to
understand carbon sequestering, we need to examine the diﬀerent forms that carbon
comes from and their diﬀerent properties.
1.0.1
Phytodetritus
Phytodetritus comprises phytoplankton aggregations and makes an important con-
tribution to the sequestration ﬂux of carbon [66]. Under certain conditions, phy-
toplankton from the euphotic zone may aggregate, sink rapidly through the water
column and build up, relatively intact on the seabed [25]. It is called phytodetritus
because it is primarily composed of phytoplankton, such as diatoms, dinoﬂagellates
and coccolithophorids [25]. Bacteria and organic slime cause the phytodetrital ma-
terial to stick together in large clumps [25]. The diversity and abundance of the
components of phytodetritus indicate its signiﬁcant role in the carbon ﬂux.
Figure 1: Diﬀerent taxonomic groups of phytoplankton. Size here is not to scale. Figure
from [41].
Diatoms are considered to be one of the dominant parts of the seasonal phytodetritus
sedimenting. This stems from their relatively bigger mass and density due to the
silicate shell, that allow them to sink faster and reach directly the seabed. Therefore,
diatom phytodetritus augments the export and sequestration ﬂuxes of carbon. This
==PAGE:8==
5
eﬀect is enhanced a few weeks after the occurrence of spring blooms at the sea
surface, when their abundance is very high [8]. However, recently the idea that
the higher proportion of the carbon ﬂux originates from diatoms and other large
phytoplankton has been re-evaluated. Findings from models [52, 64] and data [64]
suggest that the contribution of picoplankton to the carbon export is proportional
to their contribution to primary production. Accordingly, picoplankton, being the
most abundant group of primary producers [15], are responsible for most of the
carbon export.
1.0.2
Zooplankton fecal pellets
The input of zooplankton fecal pellets to the carbon export ﬂux is important but
also very variable.
This variability derives from their highly ﬂuctuating sinking
rates. Their importance lies on the fact that the fraction of the total sinking of
POC originating from identiﬁable zooplankton fecal pellets can reach up to 100%;
however, it can be as low as 1% with the majority of the values hovering below
40% [18, 17, 23]. These ﬂuctuations emerge from the diﬀerences in sinking rates
that depend on the size of the fecal pellets, as larger pellets sink faster. The food
that is consumed by the zooplaknton should also be considered. For instance, diets
that consist of diatoms appear to result in larger fecal pellets [7]. Moreover, the
pellet size scales with the size of its producer [68, 43]. Overall, the carbon ﬂux from
zooplankton depends both on the composition of the zooplankton community, but
also on the prey.
Among the diﬀerent zooplankton taxa, copepods are the most abundant metazoan
zooplankton in the ocean. They produce fast sinking fecal pellets and, because of
that, they fuel a considerable proportion of the vertical ﬂux of zooplankton fecal
pellets [32]. Additionally, their fecal pellets can remain intact while descending the
water column because they are surrounded by a peritrophic membrane [24]. Many
sediment-trap studies of POC export have shown that most of the fecal pellets that
have been produced in the ﬁrst hundred meters of the water column are retained
there [66]. This is not due to microbial decomposition, since the decomposition rates
are slower compared to the fecal pellet sinking velocities, but due to coprophagy
(the ingestion of fecal pellets), coprohexy (fragmentation of fecal pellets to smaller
==PAGE:9==
6
particles) or coprochaly (the breaking of the pellet peritrophic membrane and the
subsequent dispersal of the pellet content) performed mainly by copepods [27, 40].
In this context, copepods aﬀect substantially the variation in the vertical ﬂux of
organic carbon, thus focus should be put on this group.

1.0.3
Marine snow
Marine snow is a major part of the biological pump as well. While it comes from
masses of phytodetritus, marine snow also includes microbes, living zooplankton,
zooplankton carcasses, discarded appendicularian houses, fecal matter and inorganic
mineral particles [61]. Marine snow is widespread and can be abundant depending on
the environment. In general the abundances ﬂuctuate between 1 and 100 aggregates
per liter [61] and in certain areas marine snow constitutes more than 90% of the
vertical ﬂux of particulate matter to depth [60]. Thus, marine snow contributes
signiﬁcantly to the oceanic carbon ﬂux and particularly the sequestration ﬂux.
1.0.4
Variability in carbon export ﬂux
There is notable variation both in time and space in the relative contribution of
phytodetritus, zooplankton fecal pellets and marine snow to the carbon export ﬂux.
The variation depends on the abundance and structure of the microbial, phyto-
plankton and zooplankton communities in the euphotic zone [26, 71]. The time
variability can be seasonal and/or interannual [26] or due to climate phenomena
such as El Niño-Southern Oscillation [34]. Typically, only a fraction of primary
productivity (PP) is exported, known as the export eﬃciency (export ﬂux:PP) [31].
Therefore, estimating the levels of PP is not enough to calculate the resulting ex-
port ﬂux. Nevertheless, it is suggested that higher transfer eﬃciency to depth is
==PAGE:10==
7
positively correlated with diatom cells abundance due to their heavier silica shells
[12]. The export ﬂux variability emerging from seasonal and spatial diﬀerences can
change the abundance of the phytoplankton community at the euphotic zone [42].
This leads to changes in the detritus food delivered to the sea-ﬂoor, thus altering
the abundance and biodiversity of the benthic communities [55]. Taking this into
account, the physical properties of the examined environment are essential for valid
assessments and predictions of the carbon ﬂux.
1.1
Aim of the thesis
The motivation for this thesis is to understand the biological pump. “The surface
ocean is where the ‘strength’ of the biological pump is set [...] in setting the mag-
nitude of, and controls on particle export” [11]. Since the strength of the biological
pump is in the epipelagic zone, this thesis aims to estimate the carbon export from
three groups of the pelagic plankton community, namely unicellular mixotrophs, di-
atoms and copepods, and assess their relative contribution to the export ﬂux. In
particular, the overarching question aimed to be answered is: “What is the ratio
of carbon export from unicellular mixotrophs, diatoms, and copepods under vary-
ing environmental conditions?”. This study will be carried out by implementing a
size-based model of unicellular plankton [14, 2] coupled with a diatom model [28]
as already developed in [1], with the addition of a population of copepods including
their diﬀerent life stages, as described in [59].
Through the trait-based modeling approach (see 1.2), with size being the key trait,
and the Nutrients-Unicellular-Muliticellular framework [59], an ecosystem that in-
cludes the most signiﬁcant contributors in carbon export from the pelagic zone
(unicellular plankton, diatoms and copepods, as reviewed above) is described. It is
aimed to quantify the carbon export from pelagic plankton, analyse the dynamics
and mechanisms behind the results and investigate changes in the export ﬂux under
varying environmental conditions.

1.2
The trait-based approach to ocean ecology
The size of an individual organism is a key trait to describe its physiology, trophic
strategies and ability to survive [4]. These functions performed at an individual
level, aﬀect the population and the dynamics of the entire community. In the trait-
based approach, all the biological properties can be described as a function of a
trait. Hence, since size is a key trait, an ecosystem and the dynamics therein can be
accurately represented based on the organism size. An alternative way of describing
ecosystem communities is by examining diﬀerent species and their species-speciﬁc
biological properties (clearance rates, growth rates, mortalities, etc.).
Yet, this
approach requires a vast amount of parameters for the description of each species
and the complexity increases even further with the addition of more species. Thus,
using size as a master-trait for the characterization of an organism, instead of adding
several diﬀerent species, provides a more eﬃcient way to describe an ecosystem and
its dynamics.
Moreover, the trait-based approach takes into consideration mixotrophy. Mixotro-
phy is a combined trophic strategy (mode for resource acquisition) that enables an
organism to acquire nutrients and carbon through both autotrophy (resource acqui-
sition through photosynthesis and diﬀusive uptake) and heterotrophy (feeding on
prey). On the contrary, other models, such as the NPZ-type model [20], disregard
this trophic strategy because they are built under the assumption that autotrophy
and heterotrophy are mutually exclusive strategies. The inclusion of mixotrophy
leads to a more realistic representation of the plankton dynamics [22] and has a sig-
niﬁcant eﬀect on the trophic transfer [69]. Unicellular plankton engage in all three
trophic strategies and their resource uptake can be expressed as a continuous trait
that derives from cell size [3, 14]. Employing the trait-based approach is therefore
suitable for modelling unicellular plankton communities.
1.3
The NUM framework
The planktonic community of this model is described by the Nutrients-Unicellular-
Multicellular (NUM) framework [59]. NUM is a size- and trait- based model that
includes the nutrient dynamics, unicellular plankton, multicellular zooplankton and
==PAGE:12==
9
the fecal pellets produced by the latter ones. The inclusion of multicellular zoo-
plankton in the model is substantial for a realistic description of the ecosystem,
since it is the component linking primary producers and ﬁsh. Moreover, the NUM
model introduces ontogeny of multicellular zooplankton, videlicet the developmental
stages of an individual within its own lifetime. This element has previously been
overlooked by other models (such as NPZ), but it can have a signiﬁcant eﬀect in the
ecosystem dynamics. In particular, it aﬀects the predator-prey interactions across
diﬀerent size-classes of the zooplankton community, as zooplankton exert cannibal-
ism on smaller individuals from both diﬀerent populations and the same as theirs.
Here, the multicellular component is represented by copepods for the reasons de-
scribed in [1.0.2]. The NUM framework is able to describe the entire planktonic
community and basic bio-geochemichal dynamics, thus it enables the description of
important processes, such as carbon export.

2
Methods
The model deployed for this thesis is the Nutrient-Unicellular-Multicellular (NUM)
model presented in [59], where the unicellular compartment is a combination of two
diﬀerent unicellular plankton models as described in [1]. The ﬁrst model describes
unicellular plankton that are assumed to perform all trophic strategies (photosynthe-
sis, diﬀusive uptake of nitrogen and DOC and phagotrophy [see 2.1.1]) [2], referred
to here as “generalists”, and the second model describes diatoms [28]. Both models
include the uptake of resources and synthesis processes. The nutrient compartment
is introduced with a biogeochemichal model that comprises pools of light, nitrogen,
dissolved organic carbon, all taken up by all cells, and silicon, used only by the
diatoms. Next, multicellular zooplankton is represented by a population of cope-
pods, consisting of multiple life-stages. Lastly, copepod fecal pellets are added to
the model. Hence, the model consists an end-to-end representation of the plankton
community and can describe the concomitant carbon export of their activity.
2.1
Cell model
The cell model describes the functions of unicellular plankton at an individual level.
The trait describing the cell is its size mG and mD (µgC) for generalists and diatoms
respectively. The generalist and diatom communities are both discretized in eight
size-classes that are logarithmically equally spaced with mG, mD ∈[10−8, 10−1]
µgC. The size of the generalist cell can deﬁne its trophic strategy and the chosen
range spans across osmo-heterotrophs, light-limited phototrophs, nutrient-limited
phototrophs, mixotrophs and heterotrophs [4], while for the diatoms the size aﬀects
the level of diﬀusive uptakes and photoharvesting.
Diatom and generalist cells in the model have some similarities and some diﬀerences.
They are both assumed to be spherical, consisting of cytoplasm that is surrounded
by a membrane, whose thickness h is assumed to be ﬁxed at 8nm [1]. However,
the structure of the diatom cell is more complicated (Fig. 3) and that is partly the
reason why diatoms stand out among other photoautotrophs [1.0.1]. The original
model formulations for the cell size description of the generalist and diatom are
diﬀerent. Nevertheless, here a common formulation is used as derived in [1] that

2.1.2
Flux balance, combination and growth
Before the cell combines nutrients and carbon - and silicon for the diatoms- the
uptakes undergo a regulation process. The uptakes of nitrogen and silicon should
correspond stoichiometrically to the net carbon uptake. For this reason, JSi and JN
need to be down-regulated. Nevertheless, in the case of nitrogen- or silicon- limited
uptakes, the uptakes will not be stoichiometrically balanced and Liebig’s Law of the
minimun is applied, such that the growth will be determined by the most limiting
resource and the excess recourse will leak from the cell [16](Fig. 4).
The eﬀective carbon uptake ﬂux for diatoms is the sum of the carbon ﬂux taken up
through photoharvesting and the DOC uptake ﬂux minus the losses due to respira-
tion, while generalists also acquire carbon (and nitrogen) via phagotrophy. More-
over, nitrogen and silicon uptakes are costly processes, as well as photosynthesis
[49, 48], the cost of which has to be paid from the cell’s incoming ﬁxed carbon [28].

2.3
Copepod model
The copepod community modeled, consists of one population which is structured
in discrete size-classes or life-stages. Here, J = 1 populations are modeled, with
S = 10 life-stages in each, where the ﬁrst stage s = 1 represents nauplii and the
last stage s = S represents adult copepods that can reproduce. In the intermediate
stages, copepods eat in order to increase their size and move to the next size-class.
Copepods (Fig. 5) acquire the energy needed to grow and reproduce from the food
they assimilate by ingestion [30]. It is assumed that all the copepods in the model
are active feeders.
The copepods nourish themselves through cannibalism by feeding on smaller individ-
uals, originating from earlier life-stages of their own population (or from diﬀerent
populations if J > 1). Additionally, they eat protists, that are introduced as an
input from the combined unicellular plankton model of generalists and diatoms, de-
scribed in 2.2. Moreover, they perform coprophagy, meaning that they ingest fecal
pellets. Lastly, they consume detritus (POC). Copepods select their food from all
these sources according to a preference that varies with size.

Unicellular plankton
First, the results for two scenarios of eutrophic and oligotrophic situations are pre-
sented for the unicellular plankton model. In the eutrophic situtation (Fig. 7) looking
at the top panels, we observe that all size-groups of generalists are carbon-limited,
due to the abundance of nutrients. Small generalists acquire nutrients exclusively
through diﬀusion, while generalists with mass larger than 10−6µgC also perform

therefore they are osmoheterotrophs. As the cell size increases, carbon is taken up
through photoharvesting. Generalists larger than 10−5µgC have limited ability for
light uptake due to self-shading, but they ﬁnd additional carbon from their prey. The
size-classes from 10−6 to 10−2µgC are light-limited photoautotrophs. Hence, there
is a shift to mixotrophy, as they perform both phagotrophy and photoautrophy to
meet their feeding needs.
The diatoms acquire nutrients only from diﬀusive feeding and carbon from DOC
and photoharvesting, while they also need silicon to build their shells.
Looking

down-regulation process, all the size-classes of the diatoms end up taking up as
much nitrogen and silicon as carbon, such that no excess resources leak from the
cell. Moreover, the size-classes following the second one, grow less. For the larger
diatoms, the explanation is again the eﬀect of self-shading. Even though there seems
to be enough light for the diatoms, the lower growth that we see on the top-right
ﬁgure, suggests that there are additional respiration costs. These are caused by
the down-regulation of silicon and nitrogen uptakes, thus decreasing the net carbon
uptake by the cell.
The size distribution at the bottom-right panel shows a clear dominance of general-

by their lower growth rates compared to the corresponding generalist size class and
the predation pressure. As their size increases, the biomass of both protists increases.
The peak of the diatoms’ biomass coincides with their highest rate of photoharvest-
ing, while for the generalists it occurs when they can take up a lot of light but
also feed on prey. Regarding the mortality, the generalists overcome the predation
pressure, which is much higher than the one of diatoms (silica shell protection), by
enhancing their growth through mixotrophy. Since diatoms are autotrophs, as they
get bigger than 10−5µgC, their growth decreases and they are subject to predation
and viral lysis losses, which inevitably lead to their death.

Eutrophic conditions

drastically the size-structure of the unicellular plankton community (Fig. 10e).

it is necessary to examine the resource uptakes and mortalities of all plankton. The

To quantify the fecal pellet ﬂux, which is another important component of the
carbon export ﬂux, it is essential to understand the processes within the copepod
community, as shown in ﬁg. 11. The fecal pellet production rate scales with the
feeding level (Eq. 51). First of all, the feeding level of the copepods needs to be
above the critical feeding level (Fig. 11a), otherwise they die of starvation. The
magnitude of the feeding level is determined by the food ingested by the copepods.
At the early nauplii stages most of the food is acquired by predation on protists
(Fig. 11c) and this energy gain gC is transformed into somatic growth γ (Fig. 11b).
Therefore the biomass of the following life stages increases (Fig. 10d). However,
protists are not a sustainable source of food for larger nauplii due to the very low

their main source of food, the consumption of which gradually declines with size

is induced by higher trophic levels (i.e. ﬁsh) which is lower from the middle to
the penultimate life-stage of the population.
The trends in the higher trophic-
level mortality, since it is density dependent, are explained by the size-spectrum of
copepods in ﬁg. 10d. For instance, young copepods of 100µgC have higher biomass
than the younger copepods and therefore higher trophic-level mortality (Fig. 11d).
This overview of the feeding level and the size-distribution of copepods enables the
estimation of the fecal pellet ﬂux (Eq. 2.5.1).

Discussion
I have implemented and analysed the Nutrient-Unicellular-Multicellular model (NUM)
[59], which encapsulates generalists and diatoms in the unicellular component. The
trait I selected for the description of the epipelagic community was the size of plank-
ton, in terms of biomass-distribution at diﬀerent size-classes of generalists, diatoms
and copepods. I have used the model to estimate the ratio of the carbon export
originating from these three organisms under changing environmental conditions. In
the process of producing this result, the importance of the interplay between plank-
tonic organisms through predator-prey interactions and other trophic strategies in
determining the contribution of diﬀerent sources to the carbon export ﬂux became
evident. That is because these functions aﬀect drastically the size-structure of the
community [31]. The size-structure of the community plays a major role to the
abundance and origin of POC and it is suggested that there is a positive correlation
between the POC concentrations and the carbon export ﬂux in the euphotic zone.
Unicellular plankton represent the greatest fraction of carbon export in the model
for both eutrophic and oligotrophic regimes (Fig. 14). The size-spectra in ﬁgs. 12,
10 and 17 show that picophytoplankton dominate both among protists and in the
entire plankton community. Since POC is density dependent and carbon export
scales with POC (Eq. 59), we can infer that these tiny primary producers account
for the majority of the carbon export. This result is surprising compared to the
common belief, that the magnitude of carbon export follows the emergence of larger
unicellular organisms [18, 66]. Moreover, one could argue that the small size of the
dominant POC producers and their concomitant low sinking speeds render them
==PAGE:44==
41
negligible drivers of the carbon sequestration, as they would dissolve before they
would manage to reach deeper zones. However, their high density that is promoted
by the absence of large copepods [9], allows them to coagulate and form aggregates
that sink rapidly out of the upper ocean [31, 10]. This way, the phytodetritus be-
comes part of the sequestration ﬂux [52]. Therefore, the contribution of picoplankton
to the unicellular plankton biomass is reﬂected on their contribution to the export
of carbon (Figs. 16, 17)[64, 52].
The scenarios of both the eutrophic and the oligotrophic conditions show high con-
tribution of diatom detritus in the carbon export.
This is not surprising, since
ﬁg. 16 shows that diatoms contribute signiﬁcantly to the primary production [19]
under most light and diﬀusive rate conditions, which reasonably induces a high
contribution to the carbon export as well, for the given assumptions of the model.
The novel inclusion of copepods is shown to have an impact on the total carbon ex-
port. That is because copepods change the size-structure and increase the biomass of
the unicellular plankton community through predator-prey interactions. The preda-
tion exerted on protists is in turn determined by the size-distribution of the copepod
community. Moreover, copepods produce fast-sinking fecal pellets proportional to
their size, an important component of the carbon export ﬂux (Fig. 14)[31]. The con-
clusion is that copepods increased the total carbon export indirectly by increasing
the biomass of the protists and directly through the production of fecal pellets.
Varying environmental conditions (light levels, diﬀusive rate, nutrient availability)
modify the size-structure of the ecosystem and the ratio of carbon export from gen-
eralists, diatoms and copepods accordingly. This stems from the positive correlation
of picophytoplankton and phytodetritus biomass. In particular, the diﬀusive rate
has the biggest eﬀect on the total carbon export, as the biomass of the protists
increases with the diﬀusive rate, and the POC produced by them likewise. The
concentration of fecal pellets scales with the diﬀusive rate, as well. For low diﬀusive
rates < 0.04 d−1 the relative contribution of diatoms to the carbon export is the
highest among all the producers, and at these rates and at higher light levels the
relative contribution of copepods becomes as high as it can get. The eﬀect of light is
not as intense in general. However, the detritus from generalists is maximized at 40
µmol photons m−2s−1 and it remains fairly constant for values > 50 µmol photons
==PAGE:45==
42
m−2s−1, while the diatom detritus is maximized at 100 µmol photons m−2s−1. In
general, the carbon export maximum coincides with that of the maximum picophy-
toplankton biomass.
There are certain unexpected results in the model which are likely due to the absence
of large copepods. For instance, in a eutrophic system, larger unicellular plankton
should dominate [5], a trend that is not observed in the model. That is probably
because a larger copepod population was not included; these large copepods would
have eaten the large generalists that contributed to the elimination of medium size-
classes of generalists. Without those medium-sized generalist, there is no predation
pressure exerted on the smallest protists. Hence, the dominance of small unicel-
lular plankton emerged. Still, nutrient-rich conditions in the full model give rise
to nutrient- and light-limited phototrophs [35]. Likewise, the expected presence of
mixotrophic classes under oligotrophic conditions [4], does not occur from the re-
sults. This is probably due to the predation by only one copepod population with
eating preference for these size-groups. However, had these mixotrophs survived,
we would observe a wide range of mixotrophs, as ﬁg. 12a indicates. Next to this,
the addition of macrozooplankton would have changed the relative contributions to
the carbon export. One reason for this is that large copepods produce large and
fast-sinking fecal pellets. Additionally, large copepods consume phytoplankton and
the fraction of phytodetritus in the export ﬂux is reduced. That is because the
phytoplankton biomass, which is lowered due to grazing, would then decrease the
density dependent background mortality of phototrophs µb.G,D, that leads to the
production of phytodetritus. At the same time, the contribution of copepods to the
export ﬂux would increase through their large fecal pellets [63]. For these reasons,
we may assume that the inclusion of large copepods would lead to a decoupling of
primary production and carbon export [31].
Diatoms are typically represented as microdiatoms (rD>20 µm) [39], yet picodi-
atoms are the dominant group of diatoms in the model. This is surprising, since
vacuolated cells have been shown to dominate among nanoplankton [13]. However,
their presence in the model is justiﬁable by their growth, as it follows the nutrient
availability. In the oligotrophic and eutrophic scenarios, the ﬂux of nitrogen JN
decreases with size, opposite to what the size-scaling of nitrogen aﬃnity suggests
==PAGE:46==
43
(Eq. 6). This is because, at both cases, there is enough nitrogen available only for pi-
codiatoms which use it up, leaving nothing for nanodiatoms. Moreover, the absence
of nanodiatoms in the model can be partially explained by the choice of parameters.
Namely, in the model, the maximum synthesis capacity Jmax of diatoms is lower for
diatoms than generalists, opposite to what is argued in [28]. Thus, an increase of
the maximum synthesis rate amax of diatoms could be useful. Additionally, the pre-
dation pressure by the selected copepod population, targets medium-sized diatoms,
therefore they become eliminated. A more complete representation of the copepod
community with more populations would probably resolve this discrepancy as well.
Notwithstanding, it has been shown [59, 65] that in most systems, small copepods
tend to have higher biomass than larger copepods.
Therefore, even though the
inclusion of larger copepods is essential to the ecosystem dynamics and the fecal
pellet export, the current version of the model with one population of small copepods
is based on realistic assumptions and can yield cautiously reliable results.
The abundance of fecal pellets scales with the copepod biomass. The copepod size
spectrum is dominated by adult copepods and large fecal pellets are expected to be
the prevailing size of fecal pellets. However, ﬁg. 15 shows that small fecal pellets are
more abundant, which can be explained by the eﬀect of coprophagy on larger fecal
pellets. Yet, the fecal pellet export ﬂux with the sinking speed vs, is expected to be
dominated by the fast sinking larger fecal pellets, as shown in [62].
4.1
Strengths, weaknesses and improvements
The main advantage of the model is its ability to describe the entire community of
plankton in the photic layer. Next to this, it provides an insight of the processes
that aﬀect the size-structure of the unicellular plankton and copepod community.
Additionally, I have included diatoms as a state variable on its own, diﬀerentiating
them from other unicellular plankton. This allows for a thorough overview of the
role of these important primary producers and quantify separately the phytodetritus
originating from them.
Moreover, with the inclusion of copepods, their life-stages and the fecal pellets they
produce, I completed the set-up of a model that can estimate the carbon export
==PAGE:47==
44
from the euphotic zone by calculating the concentrations of its most important
components [see sections 1.0.1 and 1.0.2]. At the same time, it is still a minimal
ecosystem model with many simpliﬁcations and with the ﬂexibility that trait-based
models provide [1.2].
Overall, the results of the model correspond well to natural processes. Even though
we have pinpointed certain unrealistic results, the reasons behind most of them have
been identiﬁed and solutions are suggested. These can be found together with the
discussion of the results above and below in the section. The conﬁdence on the
model’s functionality emerges from the accuracy of the described mechanisms, such
as resource uptakes, predation mortalities and growth rates. Therefore, potential
ﬂaws in the results can be tracked down by investigating the plots describing the
mechanisms, which can allow -if not the resolution of the problematic results- at
least the identiﬁcation of the part where further work is required. Lastly and most
importantly, the ﬁnal results indicate dominance of picophytoplankton, which is in
alignment with the latest ﬁndings in the ﬁeld [64, 52, 54, 53].
So far, the results of the model have been discussed and certain unexpected outcomes
have been attributed to the absence of larger copepods. Thus, it has been underlined
that the inclusion of a large copepod population is likely to improve those results.
The acquisition of suﬃcient resources for the survival of copepods has been a major
challenge in the implementation of the model. This is because the preferred protist
size-groups of the selected copepod population do not reach constantly the threshold
that prevents copepods from starvation for the conditions and parameters that have
been tested. Perhaps, after appropriate adjustments of parameters this issue could
have been resolved. However, for the sake of investigating the role of copepods in the
model within the available time frame, starvation was prevented by decreasing the
critical feeding level by a factor of ten. This modiﬁcation corresponds to a lowering
of copepod metabolism. Clearly, this is a problem of the model that should be ﬁxed
in the future.
Another improvement of the model would be changing the formulation of the prey
size preference function of copepods (Eq.40). Namely, the preference for prey should
be based on the prosome length for copepods and cell radius for protists, similarly
==PAGE:48==

copepods on protists, due to the fact that diatoms of the same size as generalist
have bigger radius, an advantage against predation. This formulation would lead to
diﬀerent preference of copepods between generalists and diatoms of the same size.
The carbon export in the model is proportional to a constant sinking velocity inde-
pendent of the size and origin of POC, an assumption that simpliﬁed the model but
does not correspond to the reality. Since the size-distribution of POC has also been
presented in the results, a sinking velocity as a function of size would have been
very useful for the calculation of the carbon export.

5
Conclusion and perspective
The aim of this thesis was to estimate the carbon export from unicellular general-
ists, diatoms and copepods and their relative contributions. To achieve that, the
NUM framework was deployed using size as the key trait. The analysis was based
on mechanistic descriptions of resource uptakes, growth rates and predator-prey
interactions. The major ﬁnding is that picophytoplankton are the dominant con-
tributors to the carbon export with picodiatoms prevailing under both eutrophic
and oligotrophic conditions. Thus, it is shown that the previously disregarded tiny
unicellular plankton play a key role to the ecosystem dynamics and the strength
of the gravitational biological pump. This is in aggreement with more recent stud-
ies that promote further research focusing on the role of picophytoplankton in the
biological pump.

Knowledge of the biological pump will enable a better understanding of how the
oceans function and how the processes in marine ecosystems aﬀect the climate,
acting as buﬀers of climate change through carbon sequestration.
