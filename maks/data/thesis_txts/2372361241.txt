Abstract
Owners of various cable-stayed bridges around the world are always solicitous about
the weather conditions during the winter that can lead to ice and snow accretions on
the bridge cables. Besides changing the aerodynamic properties of the cables it poses
a signiﬁcant risk for the traﬃc below. The problem occurs when these accretions
start to melt and consequently shed into chunks of ice with a considerable mass,
which forces the authorities to close the bridges until the risk has ceased. For this
reason various technologies have been investigated and tested both in laboratories
and on the bridges in service in the past few decades. Even though many of them
have shown promising results or a potential, their main shortcomings are durability
and cost-eﬀectiveness.
This thesis presents a statistical comparison between a conventional high-density
polyethylene (HDPE) pipe and an HDPE pipe with innovative surface modiﬁcation
in ice shedding. The experimental work was performed in the Climatic Wind Tunnel
(CWT) at FORCE Technology in Lyngby, Denmark. Both pipes were tested in two
diﬀerent inclination conﬁgurations, speciﬁcally 25 or 75 degrees, which represent the
extreme inclinations of cables on the cable-stayed bridges. The acquired testing data
allow to compare the mean values of accreted ice mass, duration of ice shedding,
melting rate of the accreted ice, and mass of the individual ice chunks falling oﬀthe
cable.
Based on the results and extensive photo documentation it is shown that the inno-
vative surface modiﬁcation of HDPE stay pipes brings an improvement as a passive
deicing method in terms of ice shedding. By being capable of retaining the accreted
ice on the cable longer than the conventional stay pipe solution, it prolongs the melt-
ing phase of ice shedding and considerably reduces the mass of ice accretion before
falling down, thus mitigating the risk.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at the Department of Civil Engineering at the Technical
University of Denmark in partial fulﬁllment of the requirements for the degree of
Master of Science in Civil Engineering. The work was done from January to June
2017 under supervision of Christos T. Georgakis, Professor at Aarhus University and
Holger H. Koss, Associate Professor at the Technical University of Denmark.
Chapter 1 introduces the problem of ice accretion and ice shedding on bridge cables
and reviews icing events from various cable-supported bridges all over the world.
State of the art of anti-icing and deicing technologies from the industries experiencing
problems with icing is provided in Chapter 2. The ice mitigation methods for bridges
are described in more detail.
Chapter 3 is dedicated to the theoretical background of atmospheric icing and ice
shedding.
Chapter 4 describes the equipment and instrumentation used for the experimental rig
and wind tunnel testing. A detailed description of the test setup and methodology
are introduced here as well.
The results of the experimental investigation followed by a discussion comparing the
investigated cable surface types are presented in Chapter 5.
Chapter 6 summarizes the ﬁndings of the experimental investigation and provides
suggestions for further research.
Appendices are the last part of this work including detailed results and extensive
photo documentation.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Icing on structures has always been causing problems to the owners and engineers
by imposing additional live and wind loads as well as changing the aerodynamic
properties of the structure or just its speciﬁc members. Many structural failures
have been dated up to now, mostly including collapsed roofs and power lines due
to an excessive ice or snow load followed by other slender structures such as lattice
towers and structural cables, where the main cause of failure was an increased wind
load or ice-induced vibrations.
A diﬀerent icing-related issue aﬀecting especially bridge cables is ice shedding. From
the two main types of cable supported bridges, suspension bridges and cable-stayed
bridges, the latter ones owing to their inclined arrangement of the cables have been
investigated with escalating interest in terms of ice shedding in the past two decades.
Since the cable itself consists of a compacted bundle of strands protected by a pipe
made either of metal or high-density polyethylene (HDPE), which among other pur-
poses serves as a waterproof casing, the ice forms on the surface of this outer pro-
tection. Under speciﬁc weather conditions ice forms on the cable sheathing, creating
an ice shell with a thickness up to 15 millimeters. As the ice starts to melt, it breaks
into pieces and sheds all these fragments on the deck below (Fig. 1.1). Some of these
chunks might be blown away by the wind across the whole width of the deck, hence
jeopardising the safety of the traﬃc on the bridge and endangering the pedestrians.
For this reason, the bridge owners often face a diﬃcult decision of closing the bridges
in order to manually remove the ice from the cables or wait until the risk has been
eliminated by natural forces. Besides the considerable ﬁnancial losses, the reputation
of the bridge and its owner may suﬀer as well.

1.1
Reported icing events
As mentioned before, speciﬁc weather conditions must be met in order to initiate
ice accretion and to trigger ice shedding. This happens especially in a cold climate
characteristic for countries in Northern latitudes like Canada, the United States,
Russia, Japan and countries in Northern Europe. The vast majority of the icing
events on the bridges and their closures have been repeatedly reported in these cold
climate regions not only in engineering journals, but in the TV news as well.
Among the aﬀected cable-stayed bridges in Asia, the known reported cases are In-
cheon Bridge in South Korea with weather-related guidelines on its website [1],
Russky Bridge near Vladivostok in Russia, where the ice can build up to 70 cm
in the winter [2], and Japanese bridges Hukacho, Mihara, and Tokachi [3].
Figure 1.2: Ice accretion on a stay of Incheon Bridge in Korea [4]
Reported icing events in Europe include Uddevalla Bridge in Sweden, Second Severn
Bridge in the United Kingdom, Øresund Bridge and Great Belt Bridge in Denmark,
Rhine Bridge Bridge Wesel in Germany, and Dubrovnik Bridge in Croatia.
The
suspension Great Belt Bridge has been in service since 1998 and experienced a series
of problems with ice and snow, especially between 2004 and 2007. During this period
the average time of bridge closures was 14.3 hours a year, out of which 12 hours were
due to chunks of ice falling from the cables [5]. For the same reason Denmark’s
longest bridge, Øresund Bridge, had to be closed six times in the last decade [6] with
the last ice-related closure of the bridge on March 18, 2011.
Many bridges in North America have been likewise aﬄicted by unfavourable weather
conditions with the traﬃc being paralyzed by ice shedding referred to as "ice bombs"
by various media in these countries. Bridges like East Huntington Bridge in West
Virginia, A-25 Bridge in Montreal, Penobscot Narrows Bridge in Maine, Charles
River Bridge in Massachusetts, Ravenel Bridge in South Carolina, Alex Fraser Bridge
and Port Mann Bridge in Vancouver, Veteran’s Glass City Skyway in Ohio have all
appeared in the TV news because of their problems with ice or snow and consequent
closures. The Arthur Ravenel Jr. Bridge is one of the recent medialized structures
because of falling ice (Fig.

2.1
Solutions from related industries
Due to the severe impact of the icing events on the economy, traﬃc safety, and
structural integrity, bridge designers and engineers have been questioned many times
about resolving this persisting problem. This has naturally lead to extensive research
based also on the experience from the other industries facing problems with icing.
The main engineering ﬁelds known for investigation and development of anti-icing
and deicing systems are aeronautics, energetics, telecommunications, oﬀ-shore oil
and wind energy.
In the case of aeronautic industry the focus is on the eﬀects of ice accretions on the
aerodynamic performance of aircrafts with innumerable works, papers, and reports.
It is worth to mention a great review elaborated by Lynch and Khodadoust [10] with
many references related to ice accretion and a technical report based on experimental
investigation studying the eﬀect of ice accretion on the aerodynamic performance of
a swept ﬁnite wing requested by the U.S. Department of Transportation [11].
The specialists in the energetics and telecommunication industry deal with solutions
to prevent collapses and overloads of the power networks. Ice and wet snow formation
on the overhead power and transmission lines (Fig. 2.1) can lead to their overload
by an excessive additional weight or vibrations as well as cause a short circuit of
the conductors. Various technical reports and papers dedicated to this problem deal
with its individual aspects as ice accretion [12] or ice shedding [13] and wet snow
shedding [14].
A comprehensive and complex work from Farzenah [15] combines
knowledge ranging from meteorology and atmospheric icing through numerical mod-
elling of icing, anti-icing and deicing techniques to a design of transmission lines for
atmospheric icing. Another interesting work documenting state-of-the-art on deicing
methods for power lines was written by Laforte et al. [16].

Regarding the wind engineering industry, the critical issue is a signiﬁcant reduction
of the performance and working life of a wind turbine when ice accumulates on
the wind turbine blades. The ﬁrst attempt to ﬁnd a feasible deicing solution for
wind turbines was made in the early ’90s.
However, it was being overlooked by
many manufacturers due to a small portion of the market occupied by the cold-
climate problem. Nevertheless, the rising demand for renewable energy has brought
developers to take the cold regions into account as many of them have excellent
wind conditions and therefore the question of ice has induced the manufacturers to
develop eﬀective countermeasures. Various contemporary solutions and techniques
have been reviewed by Parent and Ilinca [17], whose work discusses detection and
measurement of ice accretion on wind turbine blades as well as icing mitigation
systems. These systems are supposed to actively or passively prevent the ice from
building up on the blades (anti-icing) or to be able to remove the ice from them
(deicing). In order to understand the icing process and to reduce the amount of the
accumulated ice on wind turbine blades, Kraj and Bibeau [18] performed a series of
icing experiments during which they were controlling the ice accumulation rate. By
employing mitigation strategies as coatings and heat treatments and comparing it
with non-mitigated scenarios they were able to deﬁne the diﬀerent icing phases and
observe which of these mitigation strategies are eﬀective for diﬀerent types of ice.
Based on a similar series of tests were Kraj and Bibeau [19] able to determine the
adhesion force of ice on the curved surface of a wind turbine blade and control its
rate by incorporating various ice mitigation strategies. Another reviews of currently
available solutions for wind turbine blades used also in other industries can be found
in [20] or [21], where the aim is to actively control the deicing process with a help of
accurate ice sensing methods.
An increased demand for oil and a reduction in sea ice cover have resulted in an
extensive oﬀshore platform construction in Arctic waters and subsequently in icing
issues caused by sea spray and atmospheric precipitation in these areas (Fig. 2.2).
Despite not causing any serious structural failures, icing has a negative impact on
safety and productivity of oﬀshore platforms. A major work addressing the problems
of icing on oﬀshore platforms was elaborated by Ryerson [22] and further converted
into a journal paper [23]. The report assess the icing hazard, analyses the impact
on the individual locations and operations of oﬀshore platforms, and classiﬁes the
anti-icing, deicing, and ice detection technologies in fourteen categories. These tech-
nologies include all above mentioned industries as well as bridges.

2.2
Ice prevention and removal systems on bridges
As mentioned before, the technologies and research pertaining to icing on bridges
utilise the same knowledge and experience as the other engineering ﬁelds. Many of
these technologies have been tested for diﬀerent structures and modiﬁed to suit the
speciﬁc needs of the structure and/or its individual parts. Since the main safety
concern related to icing on bridges is ice falling oﬀthe cables, the focus is on the
methods which are potential to be used on these particular structural members. A
review of such technologies was presented by Kleissl and Georgakis [24].
In this
paper they have reviewed and classiﬁed many ice and snow prevention and removal
systems known to be tested on cables into two main categories: active and passive.
These are further classiﬁed into three more speciﬁc categories:
• Mechanical methods - based on a physical ice or snow accretion removal
• Thermal methods - based on heating the cable surface followed by ice melting
• Passive methods - based on natural forces
Following this classiﬁcation, some of the known mechanical systems are Electro-
mechanical Expulsion De-icing Systems (EMEDS), Electromagnetic-Impulsive De-
Icing (EIDI) and pneumatic deicing boots in form of inﬂatable rubber blades. The
EIDI system, developed in 1985 [25], was succesfully tested on hangers (Fig. 2.3) of
the Great Belt East Bridge in Denmark [26] but eventually proved ineﬃcient during
an extreme icing event. One of the contemporary mechanical removal techniques is
cable collars. This system, which employs heavy metal chains scraping oﬀthe ice
from the cable, has been in use for more than three years on the Port Mann Bridge
in Canada [27]. Nevertheless, there are some major drawbacks for the cable collars
as potential damage of the stay sheathing and sliding over the ice accretion/getting
stuck.
Figure 2.3: EIDI de-icing wires on the hangers of the Great Belt Bridge
A more sophisticated technology, developed by engineers in South Korea to secure
the traﬃc safety on the Incheon Bridge, utilizes robotics and IT technologies. A
remotely controlled Snow-Removal Robot (SRR) (Fig. 2.4) was designed to remove
the ice and snow accretions by climbing and sliding down the cable based on the
information from various sensors and a digital image processing technology while
estimating the dynamic properties of the stay cables [4].

Even though the thermal systems are eﬀective and reliable solutions their energy
consumption and therefore the operational cost is too high. Most of these systems
utilize electrical current to produce heat but systems using hot air have been tested as
well. An example of such a system was tested on the Uddevalla Bridge in Sweden with
a high-pressure system pumping hot air inside the cable and keeping its temperature
above freezing [28]. Some of the previously tested electric-based technologies are
Pulse Electro-Thermal De-icer (PETD) [29], heating foils and tubes [30], and ice
dielectric heating [22]. PETD is a thin ﬁlm conductor (Fig. 2.5) which reduces the
ice adhesion by melting only the interfacial layer on the surface of the cable and the
ice is then removed by external natural forces (wind and gravity). The system was
successfully installed, tested on the Uddevalla Bridge in Sweden in 2005 but it is not
in operation anymore. Ice dielectric heating combines high-frequency excitation (8-
200 kHz) and skin eﬀect, which causes alternating electrical current to ﬂow near the
surface of a conductor and melts the ice [31]. Despite having demonstrated eﬀective
deicing of transmission lines this system has never been tested on bridges due to high
power consumption.

both anti-icing and deicing technologies. Numerous types have been reviewed by Ry-

and agricultural-based chemicals. Speciﬁcally, calcium chloride, magnesium chloride,
potassium chloride, calcium magnesium acetate, urea, and agricultural-based chem-
icals are the commonly used ones. However, chemicals are in general not a preferred
and viable solution for ice protection of bridge cables due to many environmental

Coatings as a passive anti-icing protection have been divided by Kleissl and Georgakis
[24] into four categories: thermal absorbent coatings, hydrophobic coatings, solid
icephobic coatings, and viscous products and anti-icing greases.
The purpose of
coatings is to reduce the adhesion strength and to prevent ice formation on the cables.
The most common are hydrophobic (water repelling) and icephobic (preventing ice
formation) coatings which are often commercial products of private companies. A
review and description of various coatings developed mainly for naval and aviation
industry as well as overhead transmission lines can be found in [22, 15].
Recent
research on evaluation of certain types of coatings has been done by Kulinich and
Farzenah [32] for hydrophobic coatings and by Susoﬀet al. [33] for icephobic coatings.
The prior-mentioned work involved application of two diﬀerent hydrophobic coatings
on aluminium alloy plates and evaluation of the adhesion strength of artiﬁcially
created glaze ice accretion by spinning the samples in a centrifuge until they detached.
The authors of the second work investigated icephobic properties of various coatings
by performing a 0° cone test. The most recent ﬁeld tests of icephobic coatings on
bridge cables were conducted at the University of Toledo (Fig. 2.6) as reported by
Abdelaal et al [34]. None of the three tested coatings functioned eﬀectively on the
stay cable sheaths made of stainless steel which are currently used on the Veteran’s
Glass City Skyway bridge in Toledo. In addition, under the same conditions, the
ice accretion on the specimens with icephobic coating was even thicker than on
the uncoated specimen.
In summary, the main issue with coatings besides their
unsatisfactory eﬃciency is a low durability resulting into a costly reapplication, but
their further development has a promising potential since they are of a great interest
in several industries.

A more detailed and recent classiﬁcation of anti-icing and deicing methods has been
done by Nims et al. [9] as a part of the research addressed to ﬁnd a solution to the ice
shedding problem of the Veteran’s Glass City Skyway bridge (VGCS) in Toledo, US.
In order to ﬁnd the most suitable and practical anti/deicing technology for VGCS’
metal stay sheaths a technology matrix consisting of 75 potential technologies in 13
diﬀerent categories was constructed. The categories are as follows:
1. Chemical and chemical distribution: agricultural, chloride, acetate, and
glycol-based chemicals and systems for their distribution
2. Coatings: surface treatments for reduction of ice adhesion
3. Design: preventing ice accumulation by modifying the stay-sheath surface
4. Electrical deicing systems (electro-expulsive): ice removal with explosive
forces caused by repelling forces between conductors
5. Pneumatic expulsive deicing systems: breaking oﬀthe ice by inﬂating
rubber bladders
6. Heat: thermal systems preventing ice accumulation or removing accreted ice
7. Infrared radiant heat: infrared heating systems inhibiting ice buildup or
melting accumulated ice
8. Heating the ice-substrate interface: heating up the interface between the
ice and the stay to reduce the power consumption
9. High-velocity water, air, or steam: mechanical removal of the ice with
high-pressure stream of ﬂuid
10. Manual deicing methods: mechanical ice removal using hand powered tools
11. Piezoelectric: breaking the ice-surface bond with piezoelectric actuators
12. Vibration and covers: breaking the bond between the ice and the stay with
vibrations or covering the stay to prevent ice buildup
13. Ice detection: monitoring and detecting the presence of ice by using sensors
It was expected that by adapting or combining the existing technologies a viable
solution can be found. However, none of the solutions was deemed practical for use
on VGCS and the research team focused on development of a real-time weather-
monitoring system [9, 37]. This method together with ice-detection systems [22] is
becoming very crucial for the bridge owners to assess the necessity of bridge closures
and possible use of any active deicing technology during the closure. In order to
predict the occurrence of ice/snow accretions on stay cables Roldsgaard et al. [38]
did a probabilistic analysis illustrated with an example of a cable-stayed bridge
between Denmark and Sweden, the Øresund Bridge.

Chapter 3
Atmospheric icing: The nature
behind ice shedding
The whole problem of icing can be divided into two major parts: atmospheric icing
(ice accretion) and ice shedding. Both of these have a negative impact on the safety,
reliability and performance of the structures. While the ﬁrst one poses a problem
for any aﬀected structure in operation, the latter one aﬄicts primarily bridges and
overhead power lines. Nevertheless, the risk of ice shedding from cables is of a bigger
concern for the cable-supported bridges than for the overhead lines in terms of the
safety of its users. Cable-supported bridges with cables as one of the most important
load-bearing members of the structure are very susceptible to these atmospheric
changes. Except the increase of static live load on the cables and the structure itself,
ice accretion may impose a signiﬁcant dynamic load in form of ice-induced vibrations
due to a change in geometry [39].
In general, any form of water freezing either in the atmosphere and sticking to objects
or freezing directly on objects is called atmospheric icing. Despite a considerable
eﬀort that has been made in research of atmospheric icing [40, 41, 42] resulting into
very detailed atmospheric models, it is still not possible to predict and describe all the
parameters associated with it. In order to successfully develop a method to eliminate
or mitigate the risk it is necessary to understand the nature of this phenomenon.
3.1
Atmospheric conditions
Water in the atmosphere can exist in three diﬀerent states: liquid (water), solid (ice),
and gaseous (water vapour). The transformation from one state to another requires
energy, called latent heat. This energy must be either absorbed or released by water
molecules in order to undergo a phase state transformation. There are six known
water transformations:
• Melting - from solid to liquid
• Evaporation - from liquid to gas
• Sublimation - from solid to gas
• Freezing - from liquid to solid
• Condensation - from gas to liquid
• Deposition - from gas to solid

Each transformation requires a certain amount of latent heat to be exchanged with its
surroundings. A latent heat of approximately 334 J/g needs to be released from water
molecules in order to create bonds between them that form ice crystals (freezing)
and the same amount of energy must be added to break this bonds (melting). These
transformation together with other processes are a part of the water cycle on Earth.
One of the main transfer processes in the water cycle contributing to atmospheric
icing is precipitation. The three states of water can be present in various precipitation
forms in the atmosphere and these are govern by many variables in the process of
ice accretion (Fig. 3.1).
3.2
Ice accretion
Atmospheric icing in any form of frozen water substance as accretion is generally
divided into three categories [43]:
• In-cloud icing
• Precipitation icing
• Hoar frost
As can be seen in Fig. 3.1 each of the icing categories is linked with certain phe-
nomena in low atmosphere. These phenomena are diﬀerent forms of precipitation in
the atmosphere, where water vapour transforms either by condensation or deposition
into its liquid or solid state. The phenomenon of hoar frost involves direct trans-
formation of water vapour or humidity into the solid phase (deposition) in form of
light, nice-looking crystals [15]. Since this type of ice accretion has a very low den-
sity and weight it is deemed as harmless for most structures and can be neglected
when compared with growth rate of ice formed from liquid water droplets and snow
particles [44]. Accreted atmospheric ice as a product of the other two icing classes
can be classiﬁed into four types based on its properties according to ISO 12494 [45]:

The Liquid Water Content (LWC) describes the water mass per volume of air and
has a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the shape and size of the ice accretion. Speciﬁcally,
the heat balance of the aﬀected surface depends on the LWC due to the latent heat
released from the droplets upon the impact with the surface. The higher the LWC
is, the more energy is released and the surface temperature raises. Regarding the
density and therefore the type of accreted ice two main factors come into play: wind
speed and air temperature. These two factor together with LWC determine if the ice
growth is either "dry" or "wet". In "dry growth" the whole volume of water droplets
freezes before the next droplet of the same trajectory impinges on the surface. This
type of growth usually produces rime ice which is characteristic for containing air
pockets, thus varying in density. Conversely, when the water ﬂux is higher than
the freezing rate, the growth is "wet" and forms glaze ice characteristic for its high
density and transparency (Tab. 3.1). The dependency of the ice density and therefore

3.2.1
In-cloud icing

structure is usually formed [46]. The decisive factors for the the type of ice produced

3.2.2
Precipitation icing
Precipitation icing occurs at places where precipitation is combined with freezing
temperatures.
The two main sources of precipitation icing are freezing rain and
snow. Freezing rain consists of supercooled water droplets which, compared to in-
cloud icing, are considerably bigger (0.5-3 mm). As discussed by Theriault et al. [48],
this phenomenon occurs only during a speciﬁc vertical temperature distribution (Fig.
3.5) when a warm air layer (temperature above 0 °C) forms above a freezing layer
near the ground (temperature inversion). If the temperature of the warm melting
layer is high enough, then the precipitation in form of snow falling through this
layers melts into raindrops. As these raindrops further fall into the lower cold layer,
they turn into supercooled water droplets and follow the airﬂow in a liquid state.
Subsequently, the droplets freeze upon impact with objects or the ground.

3.3
Ice shedding
Ice shedding is an undesirable phenomenon for both bridge cables and transmission
lines. It constitutes a risk for the traﬃc on the bridges as well as for the vehicles
where power lines cross over the road. In the case of the overhead power lines the
ice "unzips" along the line length (Fig. 3.7) and sheds into small pieces which leads
to high dynamic loads on the lines [15]. On the other hand, ice accretions on bridge
cables fall oﬀin ice chunks capable of damaging wind shields on the passing cars and
harming people [8], while inducing negligible vibrations. The problem of "unziping"
or propagation of ice shedding on transmission lines has been further researched and
numerically modelled. Kollar et al. [50] show diﬀerent consequences of ice shedding
on the conductors linked with spacers and without them.
==PAGE:31==

An important work on the topic of ice shedding from cables was done by Druez et al.
[13] who have described diﬀerent parameters of ice shedding. Four of the parameters
(wind speed, wind direction, air temperature and relative humidity) correspond to
the atmospheric conditions in the same way as ice accretion does. The other three
speciﬁc factors of ice shedding are ice load variations, ice shedding duration, and
ice strength. Based on these seven parameters they have identiﬁed three physical
phenomena responsible for the reduction of ice mass:
• Ice melting
• Ice sublimation
• Mechanical ice breaking
Ice melting is the most common cause of ice shedding on bridges (Fig. 1.3) where the
main triggering factors are air temperature, solar radiation and wind speed. These
three factors are crucial for the ﬁrst phase when the ice melts and the occurrence
of shedding is minimal.
This phase is initiated by air temperatures above 0 °C
and penetration of shortwave solar radiation through the ice. As a result the cable
surface warms and creates a thin liquid layer of melted ice at the ice-surface interface
and breaks the adhesive bonds. The prevalent inﬂuence of wind and gravity forces
characterize the second phase when the ice shell covering the cable sheds into falling
chunks of ice.
Ice sublimation occurs at the ice-air interface and is govern by three main mete-
orological parameters: air temperature, wind speed, and relative humidity. It has
been observed that a decrease in relative humidity connected with an increase of
temperature and wind speed lead to an increased rate of sublimation. In addition,
the bigger the external surface of ice accretion is, the higher the sublimation rate is.
Mechanical ice breaking occurs when the stress/strain conditions of the ice crystals
exceed their rheological limits and cause a failure of adhesive or cohesive forces of
the ice accretion. Such conditions are induced by static and dynamic loads triggered
by atmospheric factors like wind speed, temperature, ice strength, and ice load [52].
This phenomenon is the most frequent cause of ice shedding from transmisison lines.

4.2
Additional equipment and data acquisition system
Compared to the author’s previous experimental work on ice shedding [36] this ex-
perimental study required a few changes and expansion of the testing rig. The main
improvement was done in monitoring of the ice shedding process by a real-time mea-
surement of the melting and falling ice mass. Hence, measurements and data on
accreted ice mass, duration of ice shedding, melting rate of the accreted ice and
weight of the falling ice chunks were obtained. Any additional systems or equipment
necessary for performing the tests and making such measurements are described
below.
4.2.1
Spraying system
As mentioned in the previous section, the spray bar can simulate only in-cloud icing
and therefore a diﬀerent system, capable of creating glaze ice, was chosen. One of the
main atmospheric parameters of freezing rain diﬀerentiating it from in-cloud icing is
the droplet size, which ranges between 0.5 to 3 mm. This was achieved by utilizing
a couple of spray nozzles with adjustable spraying pattern (Fig. 4.2). The nozzles
were set for a pattern producing ﬁne mist, but no detailed information about the
droplet size, referred to as Median Volume Diameter (MVD), or LWC was obtained
from the manufacturer. The particular pattern was set based on a set of trial tests
until the required type of ice accretion (glaze ice without air pockets) was reached.
Two nozzles were installed into the wind tunnel through two openings in the ceiling
of the test section, approximately 2 meters from the test model. They were further
positioned in a way that the center of the water stream would be in line with the
==PAGE:34==
Experimental setup
22
axis of the cable and each of them would cover one half of the cable in order to get
a uniform accretion.
Figure 4.2: Adjustable watering nozzle used to simulate freezing rain
The spraying system was supplied with desalinated water ﬁltered by reverse osmosis
and electro-deionisation [39] from a water supplier located outside of the test section.
The supplied water was under a pressure of 4.2 bar produced by a 1 kW water

spraying system had a water ﬂow meter installed (Fig. 4.3b). The average recorded

4.3
Innovative cable surface modiﬁcation
The presented modiﬁcation of the cable surface is another step in developing an
innovative surface modiﬁcations that have been originally developed in cooperation
with VSL International Ltd. to reduce the rain-wind induced vibrations (RWIV) of
bridge cables [54] and later tested to assess their performance in ice shedding [36].
As shown in Figure 4.7, the main object of this experimental study is a surface mod-
iﬁcation previously referred to as Innovation 2. The double-helical pattern geometry
with laterally staggered strakes has remained unchanged as well as the diameter of
the pipe. The outer diameter of the pipe is equal to 160 mm and the inner one is
equal to 146.7 mm. The pitch angle of the helical pattern is 60° and every strake
starts at a mid-length level of the neighboring strake at a 20 mm distance along the
cable axis. Each strake is 83.8 mm long (circular arc, central angle 60°) and has a
trapezoidal cross-section with concave ﬁllets converging with the surface of the pipe.
The top of the strakes is 0.9 mm thick and their edges are angular. Based on the
previous preliminary tests, the strake height was chosen to be 6.3 mm.

4.4.2
Test method and program
The ﬁrst part of every conducted experiment was to simulate atmospheric conditions
for freezing rain and consequently create glaze ice on the surface of the cable. The
target accretion thickness for the helical ﬁllet pipe at 25-degree inclination was set to
1.3-1.5 kilograms of ice per one meter of the cable length. Although the length of the
test specimens is 1.53 m, the eﬀective length for ice shedding is only 1.45 m due to the
enclosing spacers, which insertion length is 4 cm on each end of the cable specimen.
Therefore, the target mass of ice accretion for the above-mentioned conﬁguration
was 1.9-2.1 kilograms. Two governing factors of creating glaze ice in the wind tunnel
are the air temperature and the wind speed. Since freezing rain occurs in a certain
range of these two factors, the experimental conditions were based on a set of trial
tests. As a result, the glaze ice was created at the air temperature of approximately
-5 °C while the wind velocity in the test section was 7 m/s. The simulation consisted
of 11-12 sets of icing sequences based on the water pressure. One icing sequence
comprised 2 min interval of freezing rain followed by another 2 min interval without
the rain. This sequence was determined based on the trial tests, during which an
excessive amount of runoﬀwater produced by the spraying system was observed.
Therefore, the second 2 min interval, during which the spraying system was inactive,
allowed this excess water to freeze and gradually build up glaze ice.
Once the ice accretion process was done, any excess ice accumulated outside the
eﬀective-ice-shedding length of the cable was removed by a heated metal plate with
semicircle section. The temperature in the wind tunnel was raised to +1 °C and the
wind speed was set based on a melting process according to the test program below.
Figure 4.18: Test program
==PAGE:44==

As can be seen from the test program, both angle conﬁguration were tested in two
diﬀerent wind conditions during the melting process of ice shedding.
The main
referential condition was set to simulate windless conditions as to observe the air
temperature and simulated solar radiation eﬀects. However, without the wind tunnel
and hence the cooling unit turned on the air temperature raises rapidly. Therefore,
the "windless" condition was simulated at the lowest possible wind speed of 2 m/s, at
which the cooling unit was able to maintain the air temperature relatively stable. As
mentioned before, the starting temperature for the ice shedding was 1 °C and during
the process it slowly increased to 2-2.5 °C. The wind speed for the melting process
with wind was 5 m/s, the same as in the preceding experimental study [36]. Also,
the heating system inside the cable had two diﬀerent temperature conﬁgurations.
The ﬁrst set of tests for the 25-degree setup was conducted with the thermostat set
to 8 °C. Due to a limited time schedule of the wind tunnel and the duration of each
test, the second temperature conﬁguration for the 75-degree setup involved setting
the thermostat to 20 °C to speed up the melting process with wind and 8 °C for
the melting process without wind in order to make a direct comparison with the
25-degree setup.
Each test required various measurements and settings and these are described in the
following test procedure:
1. Make a zero reading of the load cells
2. Launch the thermocouple data logger
3. Start the wind tunnel at 3 m/s and cool the test section to -5 °C
4. Increase the win speed to 7 m/s and wait for the air temperature to stabilize
5. Prepare the camera and start the remote shutter
6. Open the water pump and start the freezing rain simulation
7. For a water ﬂow of 1.6-1.9 l/min do 12 icing sequences, for any higher water
ﬂow do 11 icing sequences
8. Close the water pump and turn oﬀthe camera
9. Turn oﬀthe wind tunnel, heat up the metal plate and remove any excess ice
from the cable
10. Log the load cell data - mass of the ice accretion (10 s, 30 Hz)
11. Start the wind tunnel at 5 m/s and raise the air temperature to 1 °C
12. Set the wind speed according to the test program (2 or 5 m/s)
13. Set the thermostat temperature according to the test program (8 or 20 °C
14. Set the target temperature of the cooling unit to 2.5 °C
15. Start logging system for the load cells (15000 s, 30 Hz)
16. Turn on the heating system and start the camera
17. Stop or turn oﬀeverything after all the ice from the cable has shed and fallen
18. Save the thermocouple and load cell readings
19. Clean the wind tunnel and prepare everything for the next test

5.3
Discussion of the results
The ﬁrst observations pertain to the diﬀerent wind speed conﬁgurations tested on the
same surface type. In all four cases the higher wind speed conﬁguration has shown
an increased melting rate compared to the "windless" conﬁguration. It is assumed
that this might be caused by a higher friction produced by the higher wind speed
and a horizontally-acting downforce generated by the wind. As the ice melts at the
ice-surface interface and creates a small gap between the ice and the pipe surface
(Fig. 5.18), this force pushes the ice shell towards the warmer surface of the pipe
and speeds up the melting process. Another aforementioned diﬀerence is the number
==PAGE:54==
Results and discussion
42
of shed ice pieces, where the experiments performed on the innovative surface at the
higher wind speed resulted in a higher number of falling ice pieces.
The reason
behind this is the inﬂuence of natural forces triggering the ice shedding. While for
the "windless" conditions the main driving force is gravity, ice shedding at higher
wind speeds is aﬀected by both gravitational and wind forces. Furthermore, in the
case of the 75-degree setup, the two wind speed conﬁgurations had also a diﬀerent
temperature of the heating system, which resulted in shorter shedding time for the
higher temperature conﬁguration. Nevertheless, the other ice shedding parameters
of the 25-degree setup series are comparable.
Figure 5.18: Detachment of the ice accretion from the pipe by simulated solar
radiation
Further observations might be done by comparing the two angle conﬁgurations of
the test setup. When analysing the traditional surface, the only noticeable diﬀerence
is the ice accretion mass, which is about 20-25 % higher in the case of the steeper
inclination. As observed during the testing, the increased ice accumulation is caused
by the unfrozen water rivulets that have to travel a longer distance to run oﬀthe
cable in the case of 75-degree inclination and therefore they have more time to release
their energy and freeze on the surface of the cable. The same increase of ice accretion
was observed for the innovative surface as well. Another distinction between the two
conﬁgurations of the innovative surface is shown in the histogram representing a mass
distribution of the falling ice (Fig. 5.15). While the falling ice mass distribution of
the 25-degree setup is relatively even in the range of 0-1100 g, the results from the
75-degree setup show that the majority of the ice pieces falling oﬀthe cable are very
light, hence posing smaller risk. This fact is also associated with a longer shedding
time observed for the comparable sets of test with higher wind speed, where the
melting time prior to ice shedding of the innovative surface inclined at 75 degrees
was 17 min longer in average.
At last, the principal comparison has been done between the traditional and the
innovative surface in both angle conﬁgurations. A direct comparison of the mean
values of ice shedding time-history is shown in Figure 5.19 and Figure 5.20. The
most signiﬁcant diﬀerence is the mass of falling ice. In each of the 26 experiments
performed on the traditional surface the test model shed the ice accretion in one

piece. Since this mostly happened within the ﬁrst 30 minutes of the simulation, the
ice shell fell while having around 85-95 % of its original mass before melting. On
the contrary, the main feature of the innovative surface is the capability of retaining
the ice for a long period of time. This behaviour is attributed to the shape of the
strakes and their arrangement.
The strakes act as supports for the detached ice
with grooves shaped according to the strakes (Fig. 5.18). The average melting time
of the ice before any shedding occurred exceeded two hours and during this time
the ice accretion lost around 55-75 % of its original mass, depending on the ice
shedding conditions. One drawback of the innovative surface in comparison with
the traditional surface can be noticed from the tables with ice shedding parameters
presented in the previous section. It is a higher rate of ice accumulation, most likely
caused by an increased cable surface area associated with the strakes.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
Herein presented experimental investigation of the innovative surface modiﬁcation
for high-density polyethylene sheathing of stay cables involved a statistical compar-
ison with the contemporary stay pipe solution utilizing near-round helical ﬁllets.
Although the primary function is to protect the structural members of the cable and
reduce the aerodynamic vibrations, their employment as a passive deicing protec-
tion might be of a great importance in cold climate regions, where any viable and
cost-eﬀective solution to ice shedding from bridge cables is required. Therefore, their
performance in ice shedding was assessed by a series of wind tunnel tests in various
angle and wind speed conﬁgurations. The evaluation is based on the force sensor
readings and photo documentation.
In order to conduct the experiments a successful adaptation of real weather conditions
was necessary. An atmospheric precipitation phenomenon known as freezing rain
was replicated in the climatic wind tunnel, thus creating ice accretion in a form of
glaze ice. Another factor and one of the actuators of ice shedding in nature is solar
radiation, which was simulated by a heating system situated inside the pipe.
Figure 6.1: Ice shedding performance of the presented innovation
Four series of tests on each stay pipe specimen has revealed a remarkable capability
of the innovative surface modiﬁcation to retain the ice accretion for a considerably
longer time than the conventional HDPE sheathing solution as depicted in Figure 6.1
above. This allowed the ice to reduce in mass and size by a prolonged melting phase
==PAGE:57==
Conclusions
45
and consequently shed into smaller pieces. While the accreted ice fell in one piece
from the conventional pipe in each test, the innovative surface was able to shed the
ice into 1-5 pieces during low wind speed experiments and 7-13 pieces in the case of
higher wind speed. However, it has been observed that the innovation accumulates
more ice in the same rain conditions than the traditional pipe with helical ﬁllets.
Furthermore, the acquired data and pictures have shown that a steeper inclination
also has an eﬀect on the rate of ice accretion. Despite being able to accumulate
more ice, the ice shedding simulation of the innovative stay pipe inclined at 75
degrees retained the ice for longer and shed the ice into smaller pieces than the same
conﬁguration at 25-degree inclination.
In summary, the existing and widely-used HDPE stay cable sheathing doesn’t provide
any protection in terms of ice shedding, whereas the innovative surface modiﬁcation
of HDPE pipes seems to be an applicable passive deicing solution for cable-stayed
bridges capable of mitigating the risk of falling ice.
6.1
Future research
Various technologies from all the industries facing icing issues have been reviewed,
but despite numerous anti-icing and deicing methods neither of them is in active
use or employed on more than one bridge. A very limited literature and research
on the design of bridge cables as a passive ice protection is available and therefore
constitutes a great opportunity for further research.
However, it is necessary to
keep in mind that any change of the shape of the cables or just their surface might
signiﬁcantly alter their aerodynamic properties.
Regarding the presented surface innovation, suggestions for further improvements
and investigation are as follows:
• Using a spraying system with a constant water pressure and known values of
LWC and MVD. These parameters together with a constant air temperature
during the freezing rain simulation can considerably improve repeatability of
the ice accretion tests.
• Changing the position or the type of a heating system. A diﬀerent heat distri-
bution inside the cable might change the ice shedding pattern.
• Isolating any metal parts of the testing rig that might be in contact with
the heating system and causing any noise or ﬂuctuations of the force sensor
readings.
• Changing the yaw angle of the experimental setup to simulate ice shedding for
diﬀerent wind directions.
• Utilizing a snow distribution system to simulate wet snow accretion and inves-
tigating the ice shedding performance of the innovative surface modiﬁcation in
wet snow conditions.
