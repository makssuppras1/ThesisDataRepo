Summary (English)
The goal of the thesis is to ﬁnd out if we can measure a diﬀerence in immersion
from handmade to procedurally generated games. By utilizing several diﬀerent
data of the player, both conscious and subconscious, we hope to be able to
create a more whole picture - and to create a more tangible understanding of
immersion.
We base our thesis on prior studies, both in gaming and other ﬁelds. We hope
to be able to take their approaches and expand upon them, by combining their
methods with new ones. More speciﬁcally we try to introduce blinks as a mea-
sure to subtract fatigue from the immersion measured through eye movement.
Furthermore we hope to create a realtime 3D heat map, to further reinforce the
understanding of the results.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at DTU Compute in fulﬁlment of the requirements for
acquiring an M.Sc. in Engineering.
This thesis investigates the diﬀerence in player experience from procedurally
generated games to handmade games, using eye-tracking as a measure of inves-
tigation. We hope to see a correlation in measurements, by using an eye tracker
to follow the player’s eye movements in diﬀerent ways, while playing a computer
game.
The thesis consists of an introduction, then explains the eye tracking setup,
followed by a technical documentation with game design and game architecture.
We then write about the algorithms we used and ﬁnally explain the diﬀerent
experiments we performed and discuss the results.

Chapter 1
Problem Formulation
Can we see a change in gamers’ immersion, going from procedurally generated
content to handmade content? (and vice versa)
The focus of this thesis is to research if gamers’ gain or lose interest whilst
playing a game with procedurally generated content, compared to a game with
handmade content.
As young game developers ourselves, we are interested to see how people react
when being exposed to the diﬀerent scenarios. At the same time this research
can be valuable to gaming companies, as PCG has been well adopted in the
gaming industry in order to save time and oﬀer more content to their customers.
However, it is crucial to ﬁgure out the point where the exchange of quality for
quantity is still proﬁtable without having gamers losing their interest.
We hope to be able to see that subjects show at least a subconscious preference
to handmade content. One reason for this, is for the large game companies to
appreciate their human game designers.
It is not certain that we will be able to measure a diﬀerence at all, due to
the fact, that the procedural level only diﬀers from the handmade in content
layout. The results might also show that subjects enjoyed one level over the
other, simply because they could not relate to that one procedural layout.
==PAGE:16==
2

To this extend we use an eye tracker to measure the player’s blinks and points
of interest in the game and from the data that we gather, in a typical 5 minute
session, we try to ﬁnd possible trends that verify our hypothesis.
1.1
Delimitation
We do not investigate all eye movement classes. We restrict ourselves to mea-
surements of ﬁxations and saccades. Smooth pursuit will be translated as "wan-
dering ﬁxations" and glissades are not taken into account at all. Neither do we
go deeper into micro saccades or tremors. But as long as we treat our data the
same, we should be able to use the results. See Discussion on page 60 for more
information about eye movement classes.
As an additional idea, we thought about looking into the local trend, when
subjects were reading the clues in the game, to see if we could identify reading
passages in time, through eye movement and blinks alone. The idea was to color
the background of the plots, whenever our LookedAtFurniture algorithm told us,
that the subject was reading. Unfortunately, due to time constrictions, we did
not implement this.
Further we created an algorithm and gathered data to create 3D heat maps for
all the sessions of the subjects, resulting in 50 3D heat maps. Initially, we had
hoped that we only had to scrap the dynamic part of the heat map, and thus
have time to investigate the 50 static 3D heat maps. However since our original
thesis focus on immersion took all our time, we were not able to go deeper into
this matter.

Chapter 2
Introduction
2.1
What is immersion and why do we want to
measure it
Immersion could be described as when your mind leaves your body. Most people
have experienced it, yet few can put exact words on it. Immersion is a fuzzy
term and incorporates subjects such as attention, interest, sense of time and
awareness of surroundings. Many have made attempts at deﬁning immersion,
however "Even within the term immersion, there are competing deﬁnitions."
[CCB+06a]
We do not pretend to be able to describe exactly what immersion is and it is
not the goal of the thesis to do so. But we intend on trying to create a more
tangible understanding of immersion. One could ask why we use the word in
the title and the answer would be: because it is what best describes what we
want to measure. The word ’attention’ or any other would give the wrong idea
to the reader.
We want to investigate if it is possible to measure a diﬀerence in any of these
terms that goes into immersion, from playing a procedurally generated game to
a handmade game. And we are not alone. [CCB+06b] wanted to investigate "if

it is possible to quantify the experience of immersion through more objective
measures of the cognition of an immersed person such as eye movements."
2.2
Why procedural content generation
"Procedural content generation (PCG) refers to the algorithmic generation of
game content with limited or no human contribution. “Game content” is here
understood widely as including e.g. levels, maps, quests, textures, characters,
vegetation, rules, dynamics and structures, but not the game engine itself nor
the behaviour of NPCs." [TCL+13]
PCG is nothing new in gaming.
In fact "PCG has been part of published
games since the early eighties, with landmark early examples being the runtime
generation of dungeons in Rogue and the extremely compressed representation
of hundreds of star systems in Elite." [TCL+13] Each of the 8 galaxies in the 8-
bit game Elite contained 256 planets, however originally the developers intended
to have 248 galaxies, which would also have been possible within the 8 bit.
PCG has many uses, one of which is to create variety, "[...] reducing development
time and development costs, saving space in transmission or on disk, augmenting
human creativity and enabling adaptivity in games." [TCL+13]
In its infancy, PCG was mainly used to conserve memory usage - whereas the
modern PCG game is about creating diversity with few human resources. How-
ever according to [TCL+13] PCG has never shown transformational creativity,
only exploratory creativity. Which is why we would like to investigate if the
diﬀerence in experience can actually be measured.
2.3
Why eye-tracking
[YT11] and [YT15] describe diﬀerent approaches to Experience Driven Procedu-
ral Content Generation (EDPCG). On which subject they discuss three diﬀerent
approaches to Player Experience Modeling (PEM). Subjective, Objective and
Gameplay-based, of which we use the latter. In short, it is respectively
• Data expressed by the player
• Data obtained from the aﬀective state of the player during play
==PAGE:19==
2.3 Why eye-tracking
5
• Data obtained through interaction between player and game
"Subjective player experience modeling can be based on either players’ free-
response during play or on forced data retrieved through questionnaires." "Sub-
jective player experience modeling may yield very accurate models of self-reported
aﬀective states". However "there is usually signiﬁcant experimental noise in the
responses of players; this may be caused by player learning and self-deception
eﬀects." [YT15]
Objective player experience can be based on player emotions, physiology and
motion tracking. "The key limitations of the objective PEM approach include its
high intrusiveness, low practicality (combined with high complexity) and ques-
tionable feasibility. Most modalities are still nowadays not technically plausible
within commercial computer games. For instance, existing hardware for phys-
iology requires the placement of body parts (e.g. head, chest or ﬁngertips) to
the sensors making physiological signals such as EEG, respiration, blood vol-
ume pulse and skin conductance rather impractical and highly intrusive for most
games." [YT15]
"The main assumption that drives gameplay-based PEM is that player actions
and real-time preferences are linked to player experience since games may af-
fect the player’s cognitive processing patterns and cognitive focus." "Gameplay-
based PEM is certainly the most computationally eﬃcient and least intrusive
PEM approach of all three but it usually results in a low-resolution model of
playing experience and its aﬀective component. The models are often based on
several strong assumptions that relate player experience to gameplay actions
and preferences." [YT15]
We were interested in measuring the eﬀects on players, as they were exposed to
diﬀerent gameplay. Our main concern was ﬁnding out if there was a measurable
diﬀerence in the player’s experience between handmade content and procedu-
rally generated content. To that extend we found that it made most sense to
measure subconscious data from the player, since the purpose of the procedural
content would be to subtly imitate the handmade content.
Furthermore, for the purpose of this experiment, there was no need to look
into the data during gameplay. This led us to choose the gameplay-based PEM
method. The gameplay-based method would provide us with the least intrusive,
data-driven approach. For this purpose we would need an eye-tracker.
“A device equipped with an eye tracker enables users to use their eye gaze as an
input modality [...]” [Eye14c] See ﬁgure 2.1 on page 6.

2.4
Report overview
So to investigate if the diﬀerence in experience can actually be measured we start
out by measuring people’s eye movements while they are playing two versions of
the same level. One level is procedurally designed, while the other is handmade.
The thesis starts with a short presentation of related research, giving a glimpse
of how diﬀerent ﬁelds exploit the use of eye tracking - all the while providing
valuable results to use and expand upon. Then the more practical technical-
ities are explained, followed by a description of the game architecture, game
design and afterwards we have a section on the algorithms we used for diﬀerent
purposes.
To describe the overarching subject, we divide the thesis in three subsections,
where we explore diﬀerent areas of the player experience.
First we measure eye blinks, in order to determine the player’s level of fatigue.
In order to process the data, we use a Hampel ﬁlter to avoid outliers and also
use the reversed combined exponentially weighted moving averages (EWMA) to
plot the results.
Second we measure ﬁxation duration and count, in order to determine the
player’s level of immersion. We use an alternative to DBSCAN to cluster the
data and then we apply the Hampel ﬁlter to remove outliers and use the reversed
combined EWMA to plot the results, same way as the blinks.
After the two sections, we calculate the best polynomial level to represent the
trend lines of the data, by comparing the explained variance across polynomial
levels.
==PAGE:21==
2.4 Report overview
7
Third we calculate what objects the player looked at and map it in 3D space,
creating a 3D heat map, in order to compare the player experiences.
Once all the results from the experiments have been presented, we discuss the
results and try to ﬁnd correlations in the data. We conclude the thesis and
ﬁnally future research is discussed.

Chapter 3
Related Work
There are many diﬀerent approaches to using gaze data. Ranging from passively
surveying a chauﬀeur’s state of driver’s fatigue, to utilizing an active approach
where the user interacts with a screen via eye motions, to create an eye-controlled
keyboard.
[NMHB14] did not use an eye-tracker, however they did use a face detection
technique proposed by [VJ03], to collect real time data on the driver’s iris, in
order to detect eye blinking. The eye data was used to determine the driver’s
tiredness.
They found that 99.45% of the time the eyes were detected and
blinking was detected correctly in 89.69% of the cases.
Using a micro cam-
era sensitive to near-IR, [AJM09] managed to get an eye status accuracy of
92%. According to several sources [AJM09], [NMHB14], [SB10], [HDP11] it is
generally accepted that eye motion is the most representative reaction for de-
tecting fatigue and eye blinking is commonly used as the basis for driver fatigue
detection by researchers.
[KL03] utilized an eye-tracker to detect saccades and ﬁxations, and used hidden
markov models, in order to interpret the eye-movement. They found that "The
signal from Smart Eye Pro is suﬃciently steady and has enough accuracy for
the eye typing application." [KL03]
[Bli10] used heat mapping to investigate how chess players memorized a conﬁgu-

ration of chess pieces. [Moo11] used heat mapping to optimize the eﬀect of signs
in both commercial and highway scenarios. Whereas [SsLcH13] examined the

The ﬁrst scenario is the blinks detection. The blinks have been separated in
normal and long blinks. A normal blink is less than half a second and a long blink
is more than that. The tracker itself cannot provide us of a blink track or count.
Hence, we developed our own algorithm based on the state of the tracking.
Initially in our ﬁrst iteration, we only calculated the time it took between each
lost state back to the tracking state to measure the blinks. Although this seemed
to work for us, it was very prone to errors and outliers based on the individual
subjects eye color and size, eyelash size and lastly the room illumination. This
way we experienced brown eyed people having 32 blinks per minute (1 blink per
two seconds) and green eyed people having the extreme amount of 140 blinks per
minute (2.3 blinks per second ! ) both of which are deﬁnitely impossible. From
that point we moved to our second iteration where we made sure that for a blink
to count, both eyes need to close and open within the same frame. We took this
approach as we noticed that the eye tracker lost track of one eye sometimes,
based on the lighting of the room. From the second iteration we achieved a
more reasonable amount of, approximately, 6 blinks per minute regardless of
eye color, gender, glasses or contact lenses. "Adults can blink anywhere from
six to thirty times per minute with a mean of sixteen per minute. The duration
between blinks varies depending on the users activities. Reading and computer
use reduce the average number of blinks per minute, which is why users’ eyes
often feel fatigued after an extended amount [of] visual focus." [Ama13] Thus, as
people are generally concentrating, searching for or reading quest notes, the total
of 6 blinks per minute is indeed viable. Together with the blink type (normal or
long), we are recording the time-stamp of the actual in game blink. The blinks
are being used for each individual to research their fatigue and attention levels.

The Hampel identiﬁer is a variation of the three-sigma rule of statistics, which

not values of the whole session.

and not for each sample, checking 30 seconds in front and 30 behind which of the
data points fall inside the window. The ones we ﬁnd we count up, divide by the
current interval and multiply by 60 seconds to get data (blinks or ﬁxations) per
minute. The result of the data per minute calculation is then passed through

A moving average is normally used with time series data, to smooth out short-
term ﬂuctuations and expose longer trends. The moving average is created by
moving through the data while returning local averages.
The weighted moving average attaches decreasing weights linearly, the further
you move from the current data point the less weight.
The EWMA attaches decreasing weights exponentially, and therefore includes
all prior observations. The EWMA for a series Y may be calculated recursively:

For instance, the biggest advantage LOESS has over many other methods is the
fact that it does not require the speciﬁcation of a function to ﬁt a model to all
of the data in the sample. Instead the analyst only has to provide a smoothing
parameter value and the degree of the local polynomial, similarly to EWMA.
However, it makes less eﬃcient use of data than other similar methods. It re-
quires fairly large, densely sampled datasets in order to produce good models.
This is because LOESS relies on the local data structure when performing the
local ﬁtting. [Cle79] Thus, LOESS provides less complex data analysis in ex-
change for greater experimental costs and since our sessions varied, (from 2.5
minutes to 15 minutes) we had to make sure we would not end up in a situation
that the algorithm could not ﬁt the data, thus we refrained from using this
approach that the statsmodels python package oﬀers.
On the other hand, an algorithm like kernel smoothing, is a statistical technique
for estimating a real valued function by using its noisy observations, when no
parametric model for this function is known. The estimated function is smooth,
and the level of smoothness is set by a single parameter.
This technique is
most appropriate for low-dimensional (p < 3) data visualization purposes and
although we aimed to have a polynomial degree smaller than 3, we had to
calculate that for each data set, without knowing if we would have a need for a
larger one (which was the case initially, until we resized our ’walking window’
and cleared out the outliers).
For these reasons we chose to go with the EWMA that would allow us to ﬁt
any kind of size of data set and with the possibility to have a result with a
polynomial degree higher of 3.

Below are described diﬀerent ﬁxation identiﬁcation algorithm types.
Velocity-based algorithms "[...] use the fact that saccades have a signiﬁ-

12.1.1
A lack of long blinks
Our original intend was to measure long blinks over time. However there are
cases where we do not measure any long blinks at all, and in the other cases we
detect very few.
In [CEU03] "The increased proportion of long closure duration blinks observed
[...] was signiﬁcantly higher (almost 20%), and in the drowsy condition repre-
sented on average more than one-quarter of all spontaneous blinks."
Since the proportion of long blinks is very high in tired subjects, our measures
can only mean one of two things. Either our 5 minute sessions are too short to
measure fatigue via proportion of long eye blinks or the subjects are not showing
any sign of fatigue. Since most of our subjects only played for 5-10 minutes, it
does make sense that they did not suﬀer from fatigue. One subject played for
12 and 20 minutes and only began showing a slight increase in long blinks after
around 12.5 minutes.
==PAGE:70==
56
Discussion
Since most long eye blink trend readings were either constant or close to con-
stant, it could be argued that the subjects who showed no long blinks, actually
had a constant long blink count of zero - and therefore returned no data. In
any case, it means that the trend of the long blinks were mostly constant. Or in
other words, that there’s little fatigue of signiﬁcance to detract from our mea-
surements of immersion. For this reason we chose to disregard the missing data
as constant trends, and did not include them in the histograms.
12.1.2
Long blink duration and fatigue
The polynomial trend of the long blinks showed that subjects generally had a
decreasing trend of fatigue on their 2nd playthrough - which correlates with the
measurements of attention from the ﬁxation duration. In addition, compared
to the PCG level, we saw that subjects in the handmade level were twice as
likely to have a decreasing trend of fatigue. And twice as many in the PCG

From the linear trend, subjects showed less increase in fatigue during their 2nd

players reacting more to the handmade level - for better or worse.

probably not an indication of a new exciting task, since the only diﬀerence was
the furniture arrangement. Further it showed that 5 times as many subjects in
the PCG level, as in the handmade level, had a decrease in attention during

The polynomial trend for ﬁxations per minute revealed an overall attention gain

12.2
Eye-tracking issues
12.2.1
Blue eyes
We were warned, that the eye-tracker has a hard time detecting blue-eyed sub-
ject’s blinks, due to the weaker contrast in their eyes.
We experienced this
early on, as brown-eyed subjects had no problem calibrating, whereas blue-eyed
subjects would sometimes have to re-calibrate once or twice to also obtain a
’perfect’ calibration score.
Further we experienced a diﬀerence, when the eye-tracker would detect up to
ten times the number of blinks for blue-eyed subjects. Earlier we had noticed
that the eyetracker UI had displayed the test subject with one eye ﬂickering.
Using this fact, we circumvented the error, by requiring our algorithm to only
accept blinks, if both eyes were blinking.
==PAGE:72==
58
Discussion
This was a choice between detecting all measured blinks (not actual blinks) and
accepting a lot of noise (up to 240 noise blinks) - or detecting around half the
measured blinks and accept no noise. We chose the latter, since it made blue
eyed subjects comparable to brown eyed individuals. However by choosing so,
we also ran into situations, where we would have as little as 15 detections during
a 3 minute session. Which is arguably few data points to work with. But since
our subjects were very focused, 6 blinks per minute are actually a very realistic
amount.
12.2.2
Non-simultaneous eye blinks
"Assessing the timing and amplitude of left and right eyelid movement with
cortically controlled voluntary blinks and brain stem–generated trigeminal reﬂex
blinks demonstrates the presence of functional eyelid asymmetry. Comparison
of the eyelid asymmetry present in voluntary and reﬂex blinks showed that a
diﬀerence in motoneuron excitability is the primary source of eyelid asymmetry
in both voluntary and reﬂex blinks." [KE06]
It was suggested that we extended the detection of blinks to allow for blinks
overlapping each other with as little as 80% in time.
However, due to time constraints we did not implement this algorithm enhance-
ment. Note that we should still get practically the same trend line, of course.
So it was not a major issue.
12.2.3
Indirect lighting
[AJM09] warns, that indirect light could aﬀect the eye-tracker’s ability to detect
the eyes, recommending IR for the best result. The eye-tracker we used, do take
advantage of IR, however we still experienced a markedly worse result if we did
not take indirect lighting into account, when setting up the hardware.
According to [HDP11] gaze recognition problems have been reported when using
eyeglasses or contact lenses.
"The system works robustly at night time and
for users not wearing glasses, yielding an accuracy percentage of 92%.
The
performance of the system decreases during daytime, especially in bright days."
And "Moreover, at the moment the system does not work if users wear glasses
because diﬀerent bright blobs appear in the image due to the IR reﬂections from
glasses." [AJM09] However, we experienced no problems with glasses.
==PAGE:73==

12.2.4
Trackbox
Setting up the experiment, we noticed how the distance to the screen strongly
aﬀected the blink detection. The documentation mentioned that the trackbox
exists between 45-75 cm - so we did expect to see an error when approaching
the limits of the trackbox, however the eﬀects far surpassed our anticipations.
Most remarkably was a ﬁve fold increase in detected blinks. Test subjects were
therefore speciﬁcally instructed not to move around unnecessarily.
12.2.5
Long eyelashes
A few of the subjects were completely unable to obtain a calibration score, even
though they had brown eyes. We noted, that these subjects all had very long
eyelashes. It is not certain if that was the actual cause, however it was outside
the scope of our thesis to investigate this further. We decided to skip these
subjects after four unsuccessful calibrations for each.
12.2.6
Body movement
As we were measuring gaze data, we noticed that if the test subject moved their
head or body around too much, our algorithm based on the tracker data would
measure it as a blink. A quick way around this problem, would be to discard
data while measuring body movement. However as we were interested in the
few long blinks that occur, we thought it better not to discard too much data
too early. So in order to counter wrong data, we incorporated taking one eyed
blinks, and a distance to screen into account in the algorithm, to get a better
result. In addition to that, we instructed test subjects not to move around.
12.2.7
Non-intrusive measuring
As the tracker is calibrated to the test subject, it also requires that the test sub-
ject more or less stays in place, which one could argue aﬀects the test subject’s
natural behaviour. The eye-tracker does allow for some movement, but it is an
area that could be improved upon.

12.2.8
Non-intrusive environment
Our tests usually took place in an open library environment and we did not give
special consideration to introvert subjects. All subjects were treated the same,
which means that the results of the introverts might have been aﬀected by this

Eye Movement Classes
There exists more than the two eye movement classes that we used for our
experiments.
12.3.1
Glissades
"For simplicity, [...] we use the term glissade for all types of movements that hap-
pen due to over-undershoots." [NH10] The paper criticizes current algorithms
saying "In the literature, there is no consensus on how to choose the minimum
duration [of ﬁxations]" and proposes to introduce glissades as a separate eye
movement class.
However because of the resolution of our data, we can’t use glissades, as it’s
hard to read the velocity clearly, when we only have 60 Hz samples.
12.3.2
Micro Saccades and drift
"The micro saccade is the movement that keeps the image on the retina. When
the drift movement causes focus to move away from the object the micro saccade
return the target back to the fovea. Drift is a slow movement with an average
velocity of ﬁve arc minutes per second and a top velocity of about 30 arc minutes
per second. The amplitude of the motion is normally one to ﬁve arc minutes.
More than 95 percent of a ﬁxation consists of drift movement and this movement
increases in amplitude as well as speed for the duration of the ﬁxation. Micro
saccades adjusting the ﬁxation in response to drift do not bring the image of
the target back to the exact same spot. The reason is a decrease in speed and
amplitude of the micro saccades as the ﬁxation proceeds. A micro saccade has
a duration of about 10 to 25 milliseconds." [KL03]

12.3.3
Tremor
"Tremor is a very fast movement with high frequency and small amplitude in
both vertical and horizontal direction. The average amplitude of a tremor is 20
arc seconds and the maximum velocity is 30 arc minutes per second. Tremor is
a superimposed movement, not eﬀected by other movements." [KL03]
12.3.4
Smooth Pursuit Movement
"To be able to retain a ﬁxation on a slowly moving object the eye performs a
smooth pursuit motion in order to keep the image of the target on the fovea
through the entire motion." [KL03]

Chapter 13
Conclusion
Immersion is a fuzzy term and incorporates subjects such as attention, interest,
sense of time and awareness of surroundings. Through our eye tracking experi-
ments, we have been able to measure a diﬀerence in fatigue and attention above
our expectations. Our data results show that eye tracking seems to be a de-
pendable means of investigating attention and fatigue, however other measures
must be taken, in order to look into other areas of immersion.
Moreover, we hope to have shown, that one should not jump blindly into cre-
ating a game procedurally, since we were able to measure a diﬀerence in player
experience.
From our measurements of ﬁxed duration, all subjects showed increased atten-
tion during gameplay. In accordance with this, the ﬁxations per minute showed
that 70% of the subjects gained attention during gameplay. Nonetheless, we do
not want to translate that into the fact that they liked the game, but rather
that they focused on the task at hand.
Judging from the decrease in fatigue in our long blink duration results, correlat-
ing with the increase in attention, measured from the ﬁxation duration trend,
during the subjects’ 2nd playthrough - one could argue that either attention or
another unknown component of immersion is actively countering the subjects’
fatigue.
==PAGE:78==
64
Conclusion
Comparing the PCG level with the handmade level, subjects seemed to react
more to the handmade, as they were twice as likely to have a decreasing trend
in fatigue. Correlating with this, we found less indiﬀerence to the level and
ﬁxation duration revealed that 5 times fewer had a decrease in attention during
gameplay. In addition to this, the ﬁxations per minute showed that 60% of the
subjects gained attention in the level, whereas that accounted for only 30% in
the PCG level. Based on this we conclude that subjects related and connected
more with the handmade level.
13.1
Ideas for future implementations and work
From our experience with lack of long blinks, we suggest that for the sake of
measuring a trend in long blinks, it would make sense to have sessions longer
than 10 minutes, in order to ensure that each subject have at least 1 long blink
measured. We also suggest to have subjects play several levels of both handmade
and PCG, divided over several session.
Game companies could utilize this knowledge to further test their games before
release, for immersion, in a more quantiﬁable manner.
For the purpose of creating new gameplay experiences, gameplay could adapt
real time, according to the player’s level of immersion and fatigue, simply by
adjusting the diﬃculty level or even reconstruct the entire layout.
We propose to look into 3D heat mapping, as we think there’s potential in
linking it to the eye movement results, to shed more light on immersion in
games.
Furthermore, it could be interesting to create a dynamic heat map,
where one could trace a subject in time and space, allowing to investigate what
draws their attention, as a parallel to how we were going to illustrate the reading
of the clues based on the local eye movement trend.

A.1.1
BlinksDetector
We have created our own algorithm to track and separate short from long blinks.
By fetching the last valid user position from the GazeDataValidator instance to
make sure that the player is looking at the screen and using the states of the eye
tracker (STATE_TRACKING_TRUE = 7, STATE_TRACKING_FAIL = 8,

blinks.

more than that and up to 2.5 seconds, it is considered a long blink. In addition,
due to the EyeTribe tracker’s trackbox, we are measuring the distance the player
has to the screen, and if that changes very often, we are again discarding any
possible blinks, as the tracker gets confused with rapid movement. Also, due
to the frequent loss of eye contact, we are checking if the tracker has access to
both eyes, this way we are discarding blinks in frames where this occurs.
Then we are using a nested class called Blinks, to save the time that the blink

This class is used to read the csv ﬁle that was created from the LookedAtFur-

Special rules apply when placing mini-objects inside the armoire and also when
placing certain mini-objects in order to align them correctly within their parent’s
bounds. We are using a dictionary in this class to keep track of all the objects
that we need to place and a list for the objects that we have already placed
successfully. On level Reset, we destroy all SpawnableMiniObjects and stop any
still running coroutines.

A.1.27
SpawnQuests
This class is used to spawn the correct amount of quests in the scene. Initially
it ﬁnds all possible furniture in the scene, places them in a list and shuﬄes that
list to randomise the order. Then it loads all quest papers from the resources
folder and separates them in two lists, one for the valid- and one for the fake
quest papers. After that, all the quest papers are spawned into the scene and
divided into two dictionaries, one for the fake and one for the valid again. This
is used to keep check that we have sets of three story parts for all the quests
and also to know what the key that characterises each set is. As an example a
quest paper is named QuestA1. This quest paper’s key is A, and it’s the ﬁrst
part of a story. We then pick one of the dictionary keys and place one set of
three valid quest papers in three random ClickableFurniture and delete the rest
of the valid papers. Then we parent the quests to the canvas and reset their
scaling to 1. The reason to use canvas as parent, is due to the quest papers
using canvas overlay rendering to overlay the 3D graphics rendering, keeping it
on top of the 3D content and thereby avoid clipping through in-game objects.
The same process happens for the fake quest items also. The only diﬀerence is
that we can select manually to have more than one set of fake quest papers. On
level Reset the script will stop all coroutines and destroy all quests in scene.
A.1.28
SpawnWallObjects
This script ﬁnds free spots to place furniture against the walls based on the
SpawnableBoxes and then instantiates the wall furniture.
The possible wall
objects are manually placed in a public list through the inspector and the class
creates a dictionary, based on their selected placement number before spawning
them. After the dictionary is ready, the class randomly spawns a wall object,
renames it and removes it from the dictionary, the furniture are saved in a list for
reference. The object is placed, aligned and registered in the SpawnableBox. If
all the possible wall spots are occupied, the remaining objects get self disabled.
On level Reset the class stops all coroutines and destroys all related objects.
