Unilateral climate policy and carbon leakage: Carbon pricing vs.
green finance
Yann-Ilya Ouzone, MSc Environmental engineering
July 2025
Abstract
This thesis investigates the mechanisms of carbon leakage induced by unilateral environmental
policies, by extending the theoretical framework of Fullerton et al.
(2014).
While the existing
literature has primarily focused on carbon taxes, this study offers a novel perspective by comparing
the economic and environmental impacts of a capital tax.
The analysis relies on a two-country, two-sector model with a fixed global capital supply in the
short-to-medium term. It incorporates both carbon and capital taxes, accounting for agents’ behavior
and the substitutability between inputs (capital and emissions) as well as between consumption goods.
Analytical solutions of the model yield closed-form expressions for changes in output, emissions, and
carbon leakage under each tax regime.
Results reveal fundamental structural differences between the two types of taxes. A key contribu-
tion is the inversion of the sign of the abatement ressource effect (ARE): while this effect mitigates
leakage under a carbon tax, it systematically increases it under a capital tax. The analysis demon-
strates that a capital tax always induces positive leakage, whereas a carbon tax can, under certain
elasticity conditions (specifically, when the elasticity of input substitution exceeds that of utility,
σH > σU), lead to negative leakage and thus a net reduction in global emissions.
A comparative assessment of deadweight loss (DWL) shows that capital taxation may introduce
additional distortions by affecting the price of a mobile input, potentially increasing welfare losses.
An extension of the model to account for a non-perfectly elastic carbon supply introduces the fuel
reallocation effect (FRE), which partially mitigates leakage under the capital tax regime.
These findings highlight the need for numerical applications to real-world scenarios to precisely
quantify tax effects and identify efficiency thresholds. Finally, the model’s current limitation—its
simplification to two countries—suggests promising future research directions involving Computable
General Equilibrium (CGE) models, to capture more realistic carbon leakage and welfare dynamics.

1
Introduction and litterature review
1.1
Context and Motivation
Climate change is one of the most pressing challenges of the 21st century.
Greenhouse gas (GHG)
emissions, primarily caused by the use of fossil fuels, generate a global environmental externality. In
response, many governments have implemented climate policies aimed at internalizing this externality,
particularly through economic instruments such as carbon taxes or emission trading systems.
A carbon tax is an environmental policy instrument designed to internalize the social costs of
GHG emissions. It charges economic agents a price for each ton of CO2 emitted, thereby reflecting the
environmental cost of their activities. By increasing the cost of carbon-intensive goods and services, such
a tax encourages substitution towards cleaner technologies or products [1, 2]. It is rooted in the logic of
externality correction, in line with the welfare economics paradigm.
Conversely, a capital tax seeks to correct the externality of emissions by targeting the capital required
for production. This type of tax can influence firms’ investment decisions and the location of capital in
an open economy [3, 4].
Despite their differences, both types of taxes share certain economic mechanisms. In a context of free
capital mobility, they can both lead to the international reallocation of production factors, particularly
capital, as they alter the relative profitability of economic activities. This phenomenon can, in the case of
either a carbon tax or a capital tax, lead to an increase in emissions in untaxed sectors or countries—what
is known as carbon leakage.
1.2
Carbon Leakage and Environmental Policies
When a carbon tax or permit price increases in one sector of a country, the price of that sector’s out-
put rises. This prompts consumers to shift their demand toward the untaxed sector of another country,
potentially increasing emissions there—a phenomenon known as the “terms-of-trade effect” (TTE). This
substitution raises concerns about carbon leakage, where emissions are not reduced overall but merely
shifted across sectors or borders [6].
In some cases, carbon leakage through trade—referred to as the ”tradable effect”—can be so sig-
nificant that it offsets, or even exceeds, the domestic emissions reductions targeted by the policy. For
example, evidence from the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) suggests that increased
emissions abroad due to trade exposure may counteract domestic gains [5].
However, the taxed sector also responds by reducing emissions per unit of output. It does this by
increasing its use of cleaner inputs or technologies—this is the “abatement resource effect” (ARE). This
effort to cut emissions draws resources away from other sectors or countries, thereby reducing their
capacity to produce and emit. The overall impact on leakage depends on the balance between consumer
flexibility and firms’ ability to abate. If consumers can easily substitute between goods, leakage may be
high. But if firms are more flexible in adjusting production to lower emissions than consumers are in
changing their preferences, then total leakage could actually be negative [6].
1.3
Short-term Model with Fixed Capital Supply
In this study, we adopt the assumption of a globally fixed capital supply, which reflects a short- to
medium-term view of market responses to taxation. This assumption is based on the idea that, while
capital is mobile in the long run in a globalized world, its immediate adjustment is constrained by several
factors. In particular, investment decisions take time, incur adjustment costs, and may be hindered by
uncertainty or institutional frictions.
As noted by Obstfeld and Rogoff (1996), it is common in open economy models to assume an inelastic
capital supply in the short run to better represent these temporary limitations. Goulder and Hafstead
(2013), in a study on the economic effects of climate policies, also emphasize that physical capital is
predetermined in the short run. This rigidity has important implications for the sectoral allocation of
production and thus for GHG emissions [7][8].
The purpose of this assumption is to allow for a clearer analysis of the mechanisms behind environ-
mental leakage. By assuming a fixed capital supply, we simplify the calculations to focus on leakage

analysis while remaining within a realistic framework—considering that the market’s immediate response
to taxation takes time and the global capital supply remains fixed in the short to medium term. This ap-
proach complements Fullerton’s framework by introducing a realistic constraint that is often emphasized
in the literature.
1.4
Existing Literature and Positioning
In a recent article, Fullerton et al. (2014) propose a theoretical framework to formalize the different
sources of carbon leakage in a world with two countries and two production factors (capital and emis-
sions).
Their main contribution is to show that, in addition to the traditional leakage effect linked
to the redirection of emissions to the untaxed sector (positive leakage), there is also another leakage
term—negative—linked to the substitution of inputs in the taxed country. Firms in the taxed country
consume more of the untaxed input (e.g., if carbon is taxed, they substitute by using more capital). Since
the model assumes a globally fixed capital supply, less capital is then allocated to the untaxed country,
reducing its production and thus its emissions [6].
This thesis aims to extend and adapt this framework in an original direction: instead of analyzing
the impact of a carbon tax, we investigate the effects of a policy taxing the other input—capital—and
its impact on leakage. Indeed, a unilateral tax on capital can trigger a reallocation of capital abroad,
thereby altering the production structure of both countries and potentially their emission levels. The
goal is to conduct a comparative analysis of the different leakage components under a capital tax and a
carbon tax.
1.5
Objective and Structure of the Thesis
The main objective of this thesis is to analyze, within a two-region model inspired by Fullerton, whether
and how a unilateral capital tax can lead to carbon leakage. More specifically, we aim to:
• Extend the Fullerton model to incorporate a capital tax;
• Determine the impact of this tax on the production and consumption of the polluting good;
• Evaluate the magnitude and direction of the induced leakage;
• Compare these results with those obtained under a carbon tax;
• Extend the model to the case of a non-perfectly elastic supply of carbon;
• Conduct a comparative analysis of the welfare effects of the two taxes.
The thesis is structured as follows:
Section 2 introduces the theoretical framework and key assumptions. We present a two-region, two-
sector model in which the production factors are capital and carbon emissions. This framework allows
us to model two environmental policy instruments: the carbon tax (as in Fullerton et al., 2014) and a
capital tax, which constitutes our main theoretical contribution. Introducing this relatively unexplored
tax in the literature allows us to study the environmental implications of a policy targeting a mobile and
“clean” input in an open economy context.
Section 3 is devoted to the analytical resolution of the model’s system of equations. This step allows
us to explicitly express changes in production and emissions in each region, as well as the carbon leakage
induced by each type of tax. We extend Fullerton et al.’s results by deriving, to our knowledge for the
first time, analytical expressions for leakage under a capital tax. This enables a direct comparison with
the carbon tax.
Section 4 analyzes these results and highlights the structural differences between the two instru-
ments. The central result of this section is the reversal of the sign of the abatement resource effect (ARE)
between the two taxes: while this effect can reduce global emissions under a carbon tax, it systematically
increases them under a capital tax. This reversal, directly related to the nature of the taxed input, is an
original contribution of this thesis. We also show that the capital tax always leads to positive leakage,
in contrast to the carbon tax, which can lead to negative leakage under certain elasticity configurations.
6
==PAGE:7==
These results are formalized through several theorems specifying the conditions under which each tax is
environmentally effective.
Sections 5 and 6 offer two extensions of the model. Section 5 relaxes the assumption of perfectly
elastic carbon supply by introducing an upward-sloping supply curve, allowing us to study a new ef-
fect—the fuel reallocation effect (FRE)—and examine its moderating role on leakage, especially under
a capital tax. Section 6 is dedicated to welfare analysis. It derives the deadweight loss associated with
each tax and introduces the marginal abatement cost to compare the efficiency of the two instruments
from a social perspective. The results show that the capital tax, by affecting the price of a mobile input,
generates additional distortions and potentially higher welfare losses. Theorems formalize these conclu-
sions and identify the conditions under which the policy can improve or harm global welfare.
Finally, Section 7 concludes by summarizing the main findings of the thesis.
It highlights the
contribution of this work to the debate on unilateral climate policies and opens several avenues for future
research, notably dynamic analysis or the introduction of market imperfections.

Model Assumptions and Justifications
2.1
Model Structure
Based on the model developed by Daubanes and Henriet, we consider two regions. The domestic open
economy, which is the main object of analysis, is called Home, and the variables related to it are denoted
with the superscript H. The rest of the world is treated as a single other open economy, called Foreign,
with variables bearing the superscript F. [9]
The model assumes that countries H and F each have two competitive sectors using a specific pro-
duction factor, denoted Ki, as well as carbon emissions Ei. Each sector follows a constant returns to
scale production function with diminishing marginal returns. In other words, production in each country
depends on these two inputs, according to the following relationships:
YH = YH(KH, EH)
and
YF = YF (KF , EF )
These inputs include what is referred to here as a specific input, whether capital, labor, or a mix of
both. This input is perfectly mobile between sectors and earns an identical return pK in each sector.
This means that firms can move their capital between sectors at no extra cost, implying a uniform return
on this factor. The total amount of this factor is fixed and given by:
K = KH + KF
where KH is the capital of sector H and KF is that of sector F.
As explained in section 1.3, the global capital supply is considered fixed because the study focuses on
a short- to medium-term analysis of the effects of taxes on the global reallocation of inputs.
This modeling assumes that goods YH and YF are not perfectly substitutable. Thus, a change
in the relative price leads to a reallocation of consumption, but not complete substitution. Therefore,
two distinct prices pH and pF can be defined.
2.2
Tax Implementation
2.2.1
Carbon Emission Taxes
The model assumes that there are taxes on carbon emissions in each sector, denoted τH and τF re-
spectively. The model thus considers a fixed (exogenous) cost of emissions, which does not
depend on the market and is perfectly elastic. An extension of the model considers a pos-
itively sloped supply, to account for increasing scarcity of fossil resources. These taxes are
applied per unit of carbon emission EH and EF in sectors H and F. Firms choose their level of emissions
Ei taking these taxes into account.
7
==PAGE:8==
2.2.2
Capital Taxes
Symmetrically, the model introduces taxes on the use of capital in each sector, denoted rH and rF . These
taxes represent the cost per unit of capital used in production. Like carbon taxes, they influence firms’
production decisions, encouraging them to reduce their capital demand if it becomes more expensive. In
the analysis, these capital taxes function exactly like carbon taxes in terms of economic incentives: they
change the relative cost of inputs and induce a reallocation of resources between sectors and technologies.
2.2.3
Tax Response
When there is an increase in the carbon tax in sector H, firms in this sector can respond by substituting
part of the carbon emissions EH with the specific input KH, in order to make emission reductions
(for example, by shifting from high-carbon production to less polluting technologies such as natural gas,
solar, or wind energy). This reflects the possibility of technological substitution, allowing emissions
to be reduced while maintaining production.
This logic also applies in the case of an increase in the capital tax rH, which encourages firms to
substitute capital with another input, or to modify their production technology. Thus, the model high-
lights a symmetry in the economic effects of taxes on carbon and on capital, both of which can guide
technological choices and sectoral activity levels.
Finally, the response to a carbon tax can also be interpreted as a system of emission quotas, where
the number of permits is implicitly fixed and the emission price corresponds to the tax τH.
2.2.4
Tax Revenue Recycling as a Lump-Sum Subsidy
The revenues generated by the carbon tax (τXCX + τY CY ) are then redistributed to households in the
form of lump-sum subsidies R. This redistribution is not conditioned by the behavior of firms but is
a fixed transfer to support household purchasing power.
2.3
Utility and Agent Behavior
Utility and Consumption Choices:
Households consume goods YH and YF , while maximizing
their utility, which is a function of the consumption of goods YH, YF , and carbon emissions E (as
these emissions negatively affect their well-being). The utility function is homothetic, which means
that households make proportional choices based on their available resources, aiming to maximize total
satisfaction while respecting the budget constraint:
max
YH,YF U(YH, YF , E)
subject to
pKK + R ≥pHYH + pF YF
The household budget consists of capital income K (the production factor) and tax rebates (R). House-
holds choose their consumption of YH and YF based on the prices pH, pF , and total income.
Agent Behavior:
All consumers are identical, allowing their behavior to be aggregated using a single
representative utility function. This function is assumed to be homothetic, implying that the budget
shares allocated to each good do not depend on the income level.

2.4.4
Elasticities of Substitution
Elasticities of substitution measure how firms and consumers adjust their choices in response to changes
in relative prices. They quantify the sensitivity of the ratio between two inputs (or two goods) to a
change in the ratio of their prices.

• σF and σH for the production sectors, representing the substitutability between capital and emis-

• σU for consumption, representing the substitutability between goods YF and YH.

4.2
Key Parameters and Sensitivity
From previous results, we note that there are two effects that influence the effectiveness of taxes on
leakages:
• The Term of Tradable effect: This term highlights one of the two key parameters, the substi-
tution between products YH and YF , represented by σU. Recall that σU is defined by the relation:
ˆ
YF −ˆ
YH = σU(ˆpH −ˆpF )
Thus, the more the price changes tend to be the same between the two goods, the larger σU grows
and the stronger the TTE effect.
• The Abatement Resource effect: This term highlights the other key parameter, the substitution
between inputs KH and EH, represented by σH. Recall that σH is defined in the case of a carbon
tax by the relation:
ˆEH −ˆKH = σH(ˆpK −ˆτH)
and in the case of a capital tax by:
ˆEH −ˆKH = σH(ˆrH −ˆτ)
Thus, the more the price change of capital is similar to that of emissions, the easier it is to substitute
the two inputs, and the stronger the ARE effect.
We have just seen in the previous subsection and Table 1 that what differentiates the two taxes on
emissions EH and EF is the sign of the Abatement Resource effect. From this analysis, we deduce that
the more substitutable the inputs are (i.e., the larger σH), the higher the leakage from the capital tax
will be compared to that of the carbon tax.
Similarly, the less substitutable the inputs are (i.e., the smaller σH), the closer the leakage from the
capital tax will be to that of the carbon tax.

Welfare analysis
Applying a tax on a market modifies its natural equilibrium and generates distortions, which lead to a
loss of utility for both the producer and the consumer (deadweight loss). The objective of this section is,
based on the work of Fullerton et al. (2014), to explore the economic distortions caused by the carbon
tax and the capital tax and to compare them. We will rely on results stated as theorems, in the same
manner as Fullerton.
The results of the above model allowed us to identify two types of distortions caused by the taxes.
First, they cause a production distortion, because firms use too little carbon compared to what would be
optimal without the tax. Second, the difference between the carbon tax rates of the sectors modifies the
relative prices of the produced goods, which leads to a consumption distortion.
In this analysis, we assume that the environmental damages related to carbon are separable in the
utility function. This allows us to focus solely on the loss of utility coming from consumption, i.e., the
economic cost associated with the abatement effort. We consider the utility of a single global consumer,
without distinction between countries.

a market is not at its optimal equilibrium, i.e., when supply and demand do not meet at the point where
total surplus (consumer surplus + producer surplus) is maximized. It is related to utility by the relation:

7
Conclusion
7.1
Summary of Contributions
This thesis aimed to deepen the understanding of carbon leakage mechanisms caused by unilateral envi-
ronmental policies, building on the theoretical framework established by Fullerton et al. (2014), while
proposing a significant extension of it. While their work focused exclusively on the effect of a carbon
tax, we introduced an original perspective by rigorously and comparatively analyzing the economic and
environmental effects of a capital tax. This innovative approach allows for an evaluation of the relevance
of different fiscal instruments in an open economy context, characterized by international capital mobility
and the need for effective climate policies.
The first part of the thesis laid the theoretical and empirical foundations of our analysis. After re-
calling the context of climate change and the necessity of internalizing the externality of greenhouse
24
==PAGE:25==
gas emissions, we presented the main mechanisms of carbon leakage, particularly those linked to the
terms-of-trade effect (TTE) and the abatement resource effect (ARE). The model by Fullerton et al. was
presented as a structuring contribution, as it demonstrates that leakage can be either positive or negative
depending on production trade-offs. Building on this foundation, we introduced our main contribution:
the integration of a capital tax into a similar theoretical framework, under the assumption of a fixed
global capital supply in the short to medium term—an assumption consistent with the goal of compara-
tive analysis.
In a second stage, the construction of a neoclassical model, notably inspired by Daubanes and Hen-
riet, made it possible to represent two regions (Home and Foreign) and two sectors using capital and
carbon emissions as inputs. This model explicitly incorporates the application of taxes on both carbon
emissions and capital and models agent behavior through homothetic utility functions. Substitution elas-
ticities—between capital and emissions in each country (σH, σF ), and between goods in consumption
(σU)—were identified as central parameters in the dynamics of leakage and the sensitivity of the results.
Solving the system of equations then allowed us to derive analytical expressions for changes in output
(bYH) and emissions ( bEH, bEF ) in each region. In the case of the carbon tax, the analysis revealed that
the TTE and ARE effects interact in a complex manner: the TTE tends to increase emissions in the
foreign country through demand substitution, while the ARE can reduce them via capital reallocation
toward the taxed country for abatement purposes. The direction of net leakage thus depends on the
ratio between σU and σH. Our contribution here lies in deriving equivalent expressions for a capital tax,
enabling a direct comparison between the two fiscal regimes.
The analysis of the results highlighted fundamental structural differences between the two types of
taxes. The most striking result is the reversal of the sign of the ARE effect: under a carbon tax, capi-
tal—considered a ”clean” input—is released and transferred to the foreign country, thereby helping reduce
leakage; conversely, under a capital tax, capital becomes relatively more expensive in the Home country,
which increases the attractiveness of carbon as an input and leads to a greater release of capital toward
the Foreign country, thereby increasing leakage. This reversal constitutes a key finding of our analysis.
Moreover, it was shown that the greater the substitutability between inputs (i.e., high σH), the larger
the leakage associated with the capital tax compared to the carbon tax.
Three theorems formalized these results. Theorem A establishes that, while the carbon tax can, in
some cases, result in negative leakage—and thus a net reduction in global emissions—the capital tax
systematically leads to positive leakage, thus pointing to a potentially lower environmental effectiveness.
Theorem B specifies that, in both cases, the presence of a carbon-intensive sector in the Home country
promotes global emission reductions. Theorem C explicitly links the variation in the capital stock in the
Home country to the increase in emissions in the Foreign country, in the specific case of a capital tax.
In an extension of the model, we relaxed the assumption of a perfectly elastic carbon supply to intro-
duce an upward-sloping supply curve. This extension made it possible to analyze an additional effect—the
fuel reallocation effect (FRE)—which modifies the leakage expressions under both fiscal regimes. It was
found that the FRE helps mitigate the leakage generated by the capital tax, reinforcing the relevance of
this extension in more realistic contexts.
Finally, the welfare analysis completed the evaluation of the two instruments by studying the dead-
weight losses (DWL) generated by distortions in production and consumption. The study revealed that
the capital tax induces an additional distortion through the change in the capital price, thereby increasing
relative DWL. Two additional theorems (D and E) formalized the conditions under which DWL increases
or may become negative—the latter case corresponding to a net welfare gain. The introduction of the
marginal cost of abatement (MCA) enabled a harmonized comparison of the efficiency of the two taxes,
taking into account both their environmental and economic effects.
Ultimately, the central contribution of this work lies in the extension of Fullerton et al.’s (2014) model
to a less-studied fiscal regime: the capital tax. This extension revealed differentiated leakage dynamics,
notably the reversed role of the ARE effect, and demonstrated that the capital tax systematically gen-
erates positive leakage, unlike the carbon tax, which can, under certain conditions, produce negative
leakage and reduce global emissions. In parallel, the evaluation of welfare effects—through DWL and
25
==PAGE:26==
MCA—provided a robust analytical framework to compare unilateral climate policies in open economies.
These results call for caution in designing environmental fiscal instruments and open up important avenues
for future research on the effectiveness of climate policies in a globalized world.
7.2
Limitations and Research Perspectives
The limitations of this study lie primarily in the nature of its model and the scope of its analysis. First,
although the functional forms of the carbon and capital tax expressions are similar, a direct analytical
comparison proved impossible due to their differing units. This highlights the need for numerical ap-
plication to concrete cases in order to precisely quantify and compare the effects of these two types of
taxes. Such an application would also allow for sensitivity analyses on key parameters, thereby identi-
fying thresholds at which one tax becomes more advantageous than the other depending on specific values.
Second, the current model, although rigorous, is simplistic in that it is limited to two countries (Home
and Foreign).
For a more comprehensive and realistic understanding of carbon leakage and welfare
dynamics, it would be beneficial to develop this model by incorporating more complex frameworks, such
as Computable General Equilibrium (CGE) models, which allow for the representation of a larger number
of economies and sectors.
