Abstract
The first part of the report will determine analytical expressions for the harmonic oscillator and
the parabolic potential barrier when it is assumed that the potential energy in each case can
be described using a quadratic function. A temperature dependence will be added to the
expression for the parabolic potential barrier, and this temperature dependence will lead to a
rate constant that diverges at certain temperatures, and thus is inconsistent with the physical
interpretation of the rate constant. The report will then continue with a different subject, namely
time-resolved X-ray diffraction, where the analytical expressions for the probability density func-
tions will be used to calculate a “perfect” signal, i.e. a signal without noise. Noise will be added
to this signal, and four different filters will be used in the attempt of finding an optimal filter for
the two different cases. The optimal filter for the harmonic oscillator turns out to be a method
developed from singular value decomposition analysis, where a certain amount of information
will be removed systematically in order to find the optimal filter when noise is added to our data.
The optimal filter for the parabolic potential barrier turns out to be a combination of a moving
mean filter and a Gaussian filter, which both take a weighted average over a certain amount
of data points in order to smooth out the noise. Concluding, the second part of the report will
show, that it is possible to return the most crucial information for the probability density functions
in the two different cases when noise is present, without too much information being lost.

Resumé
Den første del af rapporten vil fokusere på at bestemme et analytisk udtryk for sandsynlighed-
stæthedsfunktionen for en harmonisk oscillator og en parabolsk potentiel barriere når det an-
tages at den potentielle energi i begge tilfælde kan beskrives ved hjælp af en kvadratisk funk-
tion. En temperaturafhængighed er introduceret til udtrykket for den parabolske potentielle
barriere, og denne temperaturafhængighed resulterer i, at den korresponderende hastighed-
skonstant kan blive divergent i bestemte værdier for temperaturen, og derfor følger den ikke den
fysiske forståelse af en hastighedskonstant. Rapporten vil derefter fortsætte med et nyt emne
- tidsopløst røntgendiffraktion. Dette emne omhandler opsamling af et signal når et molekyle
er bestrålet med røntgenstråler, og de analytisk fundne sandsynlighedstæthedsfunktioner fra
første del vil blive brugt til at simulere eksperimentel data, hvor kunstig støj da vil blive introduc-
eret til det beregnede signal. Efterfølgende er målet at fjerne denne tilføjede støj ved hjælp af
et filter - der vil blive undersøgt fire forskellige filtre. Det optimale filter for den harmoniske oscil-
lator viser sig at være et filter lavet ud fra singulær matrixanalyse, hvor singulære værdier i en
diagonalmatrix D bliver fjernet efter størrelse. Det optimale filter for den potentielle barriere viser
sig at være en kombination af et moving mean filter og et Gaussisk filter, som begge tager et
vægtet gennemsnit af en mængde punkter fra datasættet. Derfor vil anden del af rapporten
vise at det er muligt at filtrere den værste støj væk fra signalet uden at miste vigtig information
om sandsynlighedstæthedsfunktionerne.

Equation 1.3 is found using recognized methods from quantum mechanics and chemistry - yet
the term (u/2)/sin(u/2) still makes the result divergent for specific u, e.g. for u(T) = 2π. Since
systems are allowed to have any temperature, the actual possibility to use the result to describe
the rate constant for a reaction becomes very limited. Therefore the interest in investigating
the occasionally divergent rate constant determined using known, recognized methods with
no apparent limitations.
The rate constant described in Equation 1.2 is found when assuming the potential energy of the
reaction has the shape of a parabolic barrier. Thus, initially, the report focuses on the properties
for a harmonic oscillator, by analysing both the “classical” case, where the energy is described
as a parabolic potential well, as well as the case where the energy has the shape of a parabolic
potential barrier.
A harmonic oscillator in quantum mechanics is defined as the vibrating movement between two
bonded atoms. The potential energy for a harmonic oscillator can be described as a quadratic
function with a local minimum. Normally, the ground state of a wave function is plotted in the
bottom of this potential well to show where in the well the particle is most likely to be found. This
is illustrated on Figure 1.1, where the blue graph is the potential energy and the red graph is the
wave function for the ground state of the harmonic oscillator.
Figure 1.1: An illustration of a parabolic potential energy well with minimum energy in the
bottom of the well (the blue graph) as well as the wave function for the ground state of the
harmonic oscillator placed in the bottom of well (the red graph).
The parabolic potential barrier can best be described as an intermediate stage where, at time
zero, the system consists of an atom A and a molecule B-C. Enough energy is applied for the
system to surpass the maximum potential energy, but on the other side of this barrier a new
connection of the atoms has been formed - now C is the atom and B-A is the molecule. At
the top of this barrier, all three atoms are “half-connected”, and the energy has reached a
maximum. Though, since systems tend towards low energies in general, the system is not stable,
and unless it is placed exactly in the maximum with no velocity, the system will tend to the side

Both the potential well and the potential barrier will be investigated in details in subsequent sec-

The second part of the report concerns X-ray diffraction. Time-resolved X-ray diffraction is a way
of describing the structure of the molecule, or in a quantum mechanical manner, the structure
of the probability density function, i.e a way to describe the most likely position of a particle
using X-ray diffraction. To do this, we need to know which cases to investigate, thus the first
part of the report comes in handy, since the harmonic oscillator and the parabolic potential
barrier cases are the ones of interest when performing time-resolved X-ray diffraction - either the
molecule becomes unstable and starts to vibrate when energy is applied, or the bond between
the molecule breaks because the applied energy was higher than the maximum energy of the
barrier.
Time-resolved X-ray diffraction can be described in two steps - first the molecule is vibrationally
excited with a pump at time t = 0, in our case a laser pulse, and then the molecule is affected by
the probe, in our case X-rays, at time t = tp, where tp is the delay time from when the molecule
is first affected by the laser pulse to when it is affected by the X-rays. A detector located behind
the molecule from where the X-rays is placed captures the signal as a way of accounting for
all the X-rays that bend or scatter due to interaction with the electrons in the molecule. The
way the X-ray was scattered is used to determine the position of the particle at this delay time
([Atk14], [K B11]). This procedure is illustrated in Figure 1.3.

In general, if we consider the harmonic oscillator, the molecule will return to the original state
after becoming vibrationally excited, which means that it is possible to use this procedure to
capture all the different motions within the molecule from when it gets excited and until it is
stable once again, by using different tp’s. For the parabolic potential barrier, the molecule will
break and thus not return to the original form.
The sample of molecules we analyse using time-resolved X-ray diffraction can be in the form of
either a crystal, a liquid, or a gas, but the less internal vibration within the molecule, the more
precision upon the retrieved data, which means less noisy experimental data. Thus, the most
efficient way to do this analysis is by using a crystal as the sample.

Normally, the rate constant for a chemical reaction would be a function of temperature, but
since some values for the temperature T makes our rate constant divergent, the definition of
our rate constant does not match the expectations, and thus not the physical interpretation of
a rate constant. Therefore, we must conclude that even though all the methods used has been
well known, recognized methods, the result is not functional in practise. This is due to the diver-
gence dependent on temperature, and we must declare the attempt to include temperature
dependence for our parabolic potential barrier problem as failed.

3.3
Smoothing the experimental data
In an ideal case, the experimental data would most likely be close to the data illustrated in Fig-
ure 3.1 and 3.7, but it is naive to believe it is possible to obtain experimental data that fits so well,
thus we need to introduce noise to our calculations of the signal. Also note, that even though
our data is considered as “perfect experimental data”, oscillations still occur as long as the max-
imum value of q is small. These oscillations are due to the fact that in Equation 3.4 and 3.5 our
integral goes to infinity, but this is not possible in practise because we would be lacking data,
so truncation errors will naturally occur, and in our case it occurs as oscillations. This truncation
error can be considered as noise in our system, but if we assume our data is not “perfect”, other
cases of noise will also be introduced.
Noise can also be considered as the disturbance coming from i.e. the instrument used to vibra-
tionally excite the molecule, the instrument used to shoot the X-rays or the laser pulse, and/or
the detector that captures the scattered X-rays, as well as some possible noise coming from
the interaction between atoms. This noise in the data makes the “actual” experimental data
different from the “ideal” experimental data that has been used up til now in the previous sec-
tions. [Haz03] states that the noise to expect in an X-ray image will have the overall shape of a
Poisson distribution, thus randomly picked numbers following a Poisson distribution is added to
the “ideal” experimental data in order to make the “perfect” experimental data noisy, and thus
better represent actual detected experimental data. In matlab this is done using the build-in
function poissrnd, which selects numbers randomly from a Poisson distribution, and then both
add and subtract these numbers in order to get noisy data for a mean value of λ = 1 in the
Poisson distribution. A plot of noise added to CS2 at different times are plotted for both the har-
monic oscillator and the parabolic potential energy barrier in Figure 3.13.
When noise has been added to the “perfect” experimental data, we have to filter it out again in
order to subtract the tendency of the data, which can be done in many different ways. Define
a filter as a method that filters out the irrelevant information, and leaves the important tendency
of the data. We will focus on four - a filter on our data that takes a weighted average of an
amount of points from our data, two filter that takes advantage of the fact that the numerical
integration that has been used can be described as a matrix, which can be analysed using sin-
gular value decomposition (SVD), and a method that multiplies a damping function to our data
in order to ensure that the data cannot increase infinitely as time goes. Furthermore, unless else
is noted, all filters are performed with qmax = 20 Å
−1, and a constant on poissrnd of 0.05 for the
harmonic oscillator and 0.005 for the parabolic potential barrier problem is multiplied in order
to downscale the numbers we get from the Poisson distribution such that they match our signal.
The reason why these numbers are different is because that if the noise is larger on the barrier
problem, it will become larger than the actual signal, and when a filter is used, all information
will be lost, and the filter will return the null vector as the optimal solution.
Also we note, that only the calculations for CS2 will be shown in the report since the definition
of CS2 is more complex than that of CS0, and both expressions should, in theory, give the same
result, thus we assume that if the more complex expression returns reasonable results, so will the
less complex expression. Yet, the same calculations has been performed on CS0 and some
chosen results can be seen in Appendix A.

3.3.1
Gaussian and moving mean filter
From previous approximations of the probability function (see the Figures 3.4, 3.5, 3.6, 3.9, 3.10,
3.11, and 3.12), the truncation error we introduced by limiting qmax is visible in the calculations as
oscillations. Thus, even when no noise is added to the system, we can assume that the tendency
of the experimental data is many oscillations, whereas a suitable filter would be a low-pass filter,
since the general purpose of a low-pass filter is to dampen the high values in an area [Cha73].
We find that the filter gives the best result if performed on the calculated probability density func-
tion instead of the experimental data directly, thus this has been done for the moving mean and
Gaussian filters.
Since the probability density function has the shape of a Gaussian function, the Gaussian fil-
ter would be suitable, and according to [G W95] the Gaussian filter has proven the most effi-
cient low-pass filter. Furthermore, in order to remove the worst oscillations before applying the
Gaussian filter, we use a moving mean filter that simply dampens the whole function, as this has
proven to give a better result when compared to the real probability density function in our case.
The moving mean filter, also known as the moving average filter, is characterized such that for
every point, it takes the average value of a small neighborhood around this point, that is spec-
ified by a certain amount of data points, where every point has the same weight. This filter is
available as a build-in function in matlab, called smoothdata, where the input is our data as a

Discussion of filters
In order to recreate our probability density function when noise is added, four different methods
have been used on both the harmonic oscillator and the parabolic potential barrier problem,
which all had its strengths and weaknesses.
In our moving mean/Gaussian filter, the optimal filter depends on the amount of points consid-
ered in the moving mean and Gaussian filter. It is, of course, a lot harder to find the optimal
amount of points for these filters when it is not possible to compare with the real probability den-
sity function, but it is not hard to simply try out some values, and even though the filter might
not be optimal, it can still emphasize the tendency of the function beneath all the noise. Thus,
it might not be the optimal filter for all cases, but it is a build-in function in matlab that does
not require much to implement and use once the data is loaded into the script. Also note, that
even though the filter was not completely successful for the harmonic oscillator (Figure 3.15), it
certainly was for the potential energy barrier (Figure 3.17), where it also became the filter with
the lowest error compared to the real probability density function.
The filters involving matrix analysis and the SVD analysis require more implementation than both
the dampening filter and the moving mean/Gaussian filter, but once it has been implemented,
the procedure is quite straight forward. The amount of values that should be removed from the
diagonal matrix D before calculating the modified F matrix could be a trial and error procedure,
simply investigating when the shape of the probability density function we seek (either for the
harmonic oscillator or the parabolic potential barrier problem) is most prominent. Especially the
method where we remove an amount of the largest elements in the diagonal matrix D is quite
straight forward to implement, and it has also proved to be more efficient than the filter where
we remove elements in D corresponding to when ˜S2 = |V TCS2| is smallest, since the error in both
the harmonic oscillator and the parabolic potential barrier case is smallest for this method. Fur-
thermore note, that this filter where we remove values in D according to largest values is the
optimal filter for our harmonic oscillator. We support this claim by referring to Figure 3.21, 3.24,
3.26, and 3.27 for the corresponding smallest errors, and Figure 3.22, 3.25, 3.28, and 3.29 for the
probability density function.
The dampening filter might be the simplest of the filters to implement - for each qi the function
exp(−kq2
i ) is multiplied on our experimental values. Once again an trial and error procedure
can be used to find an close to optimal value for k when the real probability density function
is unknown - fairly quickly the extra oscillation will be dampened, and only the “spike” from the
probability density function will be visible. Even though this filter seems like the optimal filter, it
comes with a cost to remove all the oscillations - the characteristic “spike” in the probability
density function will become wider and get a smaller amplitude, thus a high value for k will kill
all tendencies for our probability density function and simply return a straight line. However, if
we compare the calculated optimal errors, this filter has the second lowest error for both the
harmonic oscillator and the parabolic potential barrier. To support these claims we refer to Fig-
ure 3.31 and 3.33 for the error and Figure 3.32 and 3.34 for the probability density functions.
When comparing the four different filters, more than just the error decides which filter is the
optimal to use, e.g. how difficult it is to implement, how easy it is to find the optimal error or
even just the tendency of the probability density function, thus which filter is the optimal highly

In the first part of the report, chapter 2, the probability density function for a harmonic oscillator
was investigated, it was justified how a result known to be true for real values for the frequency
could be expanded to an imaginary value, and a temperature dependence for the rate con-
stant when we consider the parabolic potential energy barrier problem was implemented. The
first part of the report resulted in a rate constant that did not match the physical interpretation
of it, since it was divergent in certain temperature values. Thus, we must assume, that some-
where when the temperature dependence was introduced for the parabolic potential barrier,
a contradiction with some part of an approximation or assumption must have occurred.
During the derivation of the probability density function for the parabolic potential barrier, it
was assumed that K found for the harmonic oscillator could be used for the parabolic poten-
tial barrier as well, by simply inserting an imaginary frequency instead of a real frequency. This
procedure had to be justified in order to continue with the corresponding expression for the
probability density function, thus the expression for the wave function found using K and an
imaginary frequency was tested in the time-dependent Schrödinger equation. Furthermore,
it was assumed that the potential energy could be approximated using a quadratic function
instead of a Gaussian function. This approximation is due to the fact that it is used for the har-
monic oscillator where it does not seem to cause any trouble, and since the calculations for a
quadratic function in general are simpler than that of a Gaussian function. But is this approx-
imation of the potential energy using a quadratic function really what made the calculated
rate constant from Equation 1.2 useless in practise? The main difference between a Gaussian
function and the quadratic function used for this approximation is that as the Gaussian function
has a shape where it is zero everywhere except a peak, the quadratic function would have
the same peak, but instead of being zero everywhere else, it would approach −∞on either
side of the peak. In general it is assumed that a chemical system seeks to approach the lowest
energy, thus this quadratic function will allow the lowest energy to be −∞, which could explain
the divergence of the calculated rate constant.
The calculations for the probability density functions have only been for systems with one posi-
tion variable, a one dimensional system, but in reality at least two position variables are needed
to describe the correct movements of the system. A second position variable could in principle
make the calculations more precise and possibly eliminate the divergence of the rate constant.
These further investigations would have been at the expense of the second part of the report,
since the calculations for a two dimensional system would be more tedious than that of a one
dimensional system, and also, there is no guarantee that it would have solved the problem.

4 Discussion and conclusion
The second part of the report, chapter 3, mainly focused on how to retrieve as much knowledge
as possible about the structure of a molecule from artificial experimental data. “Perfect” data
was calculated using the two probability density functions, but the integration from this calcu-
lated data back to the probability density function was found to introduce some truncation
errors in the shape of oscillations. Some oscillations could be dampened using the dampening
filter for the harmonic oscillator and qmax = 10 Å
−1 (Figure 3.36), and for the parabolic poten-
tial barrier this dampening filter was also helpful in order to reduce the extra oscillations when
qmax = [4,10] (Figure 3.38 and 3.39).
When noise was added to our signal and integrated in accordance with Equation 3.4, naturally,
the probability density function was very noisy - to remove this noise four different filters were
investigated. The first filter was the moving mean/Gaussian filter, which is a low-pass filter that
works by neutralizing the big oscillations by using a weighted average of an amount of data -
the amount of data points to consider for both the moving mean and the Gaussian filter had to
be optimized. When the optimal amount of points had been found, the error was calculated
by comparing the approximated probability density function with the real probability density
function using Equation 3.7. The error was quite high for the harmonic oscillator, which means
that the optimal solution was not ideal (Figure 3.15), but for the parabolic potential barrier prob-
lem, the error was so small that it ended up being the optimal filter (Figure 3.17).
The next two filters originated from the same “integration” matrix F, which was determined us-
ing the trapezoidal integration, such that for each Ri the corresponding row of the F matrix
was calculated using Equation 3.8. SVD analysis was used on matrix F, which in practice meant
that Equation 3.9 was used to decompose our matrix into three new matrices, where the middle
matrix, the diagonal matrix D, contained the singular values ordered after size. The first filter in-
volved finding the optimal amount of elements in the diagonal matrix D to set equal to zero, by
starting from the largest value and then the second largest etc. This filter proved more efficient
for the harmonic oscillator than the parabolic potential barrier, and ended up being the filter
with the lowest error for the harmonic oscillator (Figure 3.22 and 3.28). The next filter involved in-
troducing a new vector, ˜S2, that consisted of our original signal multiplied with the transposed V
matrix found using Equation 3.9, whereas an optimal amount of the smallest values for the new
vector ˜S2 was removed from where they corresponded to in the diagonal matrix D. This filter
proved to be the worst filter of all for both the harmonic oscillator and the parabolic potential
barrier (3.25 and 3.29).
The last filter was the dampening filter which works by multiplying an decreasing exponential
function to our experimental data before they are transformed, using the trapezoidal integra-
tion method. This method proved to be the second best filter for both the harmonic oscillator
and the parabolic potential barrier (Figure 3.32 and 3.34), but if k became too large it would
completely kill of any tendency for the probability density function, and even when k was op-
timal, the approximated probability density functions had lower amplitudes and wider “spikes”
than the original probability density function.
The pros and cons were discussed in order to find the filter that was easiest to implement and
most certain to return a useful result even if the approach to finding the approximation was trial

and error, when the real probability density function was unknown. In the end, the optimal filter
depends on the one implementing it, if there is some initial knowledge about the probability
density function we are seeking, or in the case where there is no knowledge, a certain amount
of patience has to present in order to try a lot of values before the optimal combination for
a filter is found. Yet note, that the filter does not have to be optimal for it to be useful, since
it could still provide the necessary information about the probability density without using the
best combination of variables for that filter.
However, there are some things about the approach that will be investigated further, e.g. is the
trapezoidal integration a good approximation for our integral? What amount of noise can we
expect on our data, and what is the magnitude of this noise? Does it even make sense to use
a filter after we have integrated our experimental data to get the probability density, and was
the filters even implemented correctly?
Up till now the noise on our data has been chosen such that it was large enough to be visible in
Figure 3.13, but small enough so our filters would not return the null vector as the optimal solution,
but what do we even know about the noise? [Haz03] describes that the noise on our experimen-
tal data should follow a Poisson distribution, but our real experimental data is Sinst, which we only
see once in Equation 3.1. Sinst is related to the signals CS2 and CS0, which are the quantities we
analyse, but since they are only connected to Sinst via a differentiation, can we really assume
that the error has to follow a Poisson distribution? And how much noise could we even expect
on our experimental data, is the factor 0.05 for the harmonic oscillator too large? Is the factor
0.005 for the parabolic potential barrier too small? The easiest way to determine the distribution
of the noise is to investigate actual experimental data and compare with our “perfect” data,
which was not possible for this report, thus we can only assume that the right noise has been
added. The factor 0.05 for the harmonic oscillator might seem quite large compared to 0.005
for the parabolic potential barrier, but the filters were not able to handle a higher noise for the
potential barrier, and thus all information from the probability density function would be lost if
the factor was larger than 0.005, but that might not have been the case if the noise followed
another distribution.
Regarding the numerical integration method we have used - the trapezoidal integration method
is an easy way to integrate our data numerically, especially since it allows us to write the inte-
gration as a matrix, which might not be the case with another integration method. However,
the integration method might have been more precise if it was non-linear (without the ability to
write the integration calculations as a matrix transformation directly), which could lead to less
truncation error for smaller qmax, which means that there might not be as many oscillations. Yet,
the trapezoidal integration method provides two filters by using SVD analysis on the integration
matrix F, whereas the results from one of these are rather convincing, therefore it is definitely
a numerical integration method worth using - especially compared to the small effort of imple-
menting it.
Another speculation is whether or not the filters was used at the right time - the approach in the
report was to first add noise to the “perfect” experimental data, integrate them, and then use
the filter for the moving mean/Gaussian filter, but can this procedure be justified? Initially, the
idea was to filter the data right after noise has been added, but this way proved to be worse

than filtering on the calculated probability density function. A possible explanation could be
that from Figure 3.13, it is illustrated that for both the harmonic oscillator and the potential barrier
case, many oscillations occur forCS2, and the main purpose of the moving mean/Gaussian filter
and the dampening filter is to reduce those, thus a lot of information would be lost if the filters
was used directly, but it is possible that another filter might have worked on the experimental
data directly.
A naturally occurring question could be if our filters have been implemented correctly, and the
answer to this question is quite short - there are only two filters that could have been imple-
mented incorrectly, since the moving mean and Gaussian filters are build-in functions, and the
dampening filter is just a function multiplied to our data. However, the F matrix depends on the
trapezoidal rule and should not be too difficult to implement, but when SVD analysis is used to
remove elements in the diagonal matrix D, the amount of points that should be removed has to
be implemented correctly. This could be a challenge for the inexperienced programmer, but
this filter turn out quite well in our calculations, which is reason to believe that it is implemented
correctly. For the filter where we remove elements corresponding to ˜S2, it is quite a different story.
This filter turn out to be the worst filter for both the harmonic oscillator and the potential barrier
case, but in reality is should only remove data that is not important, thus we have to suspect
that the implementation might not be quite right. Yet, for all the filters, we can compare to the
real probability density function, and it is clear that all filters returns a function that is closer to
the real probability density function than before, thus the filters still provide crucial information.
However, no literature supported that these filters are the optimal filters for these two cases, thus

Overall, the report succeeded in determining an analytical expression for the harmonic oscilla-
tor and the parabolic potential barrier case. The additional temperature dependence for the
potential barrier suggested that a quadratic function might not be the best approximation of a
parabolic potential barrier, and it would be better to consider an approximation that was closer
to a Gaussian function in order to investigate whether or not a temperature dependence would
result in a divergent rate constant again. Furthermore, when the trapezoidal integration method
was used to return to our probability density function, a truncation error was introduced as os-
cillations, since there is a limitation on the maximum value of q. This truncation error could be
slightly improved if the dampening filter was used for the harmonic oscillator when qmax = 10 and
for the parabolic potential barrier when qmax = [4,10] if no noise was added to the data. When
noise was added to the “perfect” experimental data, it had to be removed again. Four differ-
ent filters were used to remove the noise on our probability density functions calculated from
our experimental data. The optimal filter for the harmonic oscillator was to use SVD analysis and
systematically remove singular values from the diagonal matrix D until the filtered probability
density function had a minimum error compared to the real probability density function. For the
parabolic potential barrier problem, the combination of a moving mean filter and a Gaussian
filter was the optimal filter in order to subtract the important data from the probability density
function, when compared to the real probability density function.
