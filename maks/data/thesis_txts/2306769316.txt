Abstract 
 
Commercialization of otherwise inaccessible products in electroencephalography (EEG) and 
electromyography (EMG) has opened up new possibilities in the development of biomedical 
solutions. By using EEG signal processing to already existing assistive technologies (AT) it is 
possible to enhance the robustness, accuracy and performance while providing additional 
degrees of freedom. The thesis seeks to establish the possibility of adapting multimodality in a 
hybrid control scheme to achieve better prosthetic control for the user through commercialized 
products. A hypothesis of enhanced brain computer interface (BCI) control for people who 
meditate frequently was explored. The thesis consists of three objectives: (i), to evaluate whether 
combining EEG and EMG for prosthetic control in a hybrid control application offer more grips, 
(ii), to eliminate the sequencing of input to move between grips through multimodality, and (iii) 
to establish the feasibility of EEG and EMG control in a hybrid scheme based on experimental 
work and literature review. The meditation group and the non-meditation group in the 
experiments showed no statistically significant difference in BCI performance, however with a 
weak positive tendency towards being statistically significant. Furthermore, none of the variables 
(age, level of gaming and task difference) showed any statistically significant difference in 
controlling a virtual prosthetic hand. Multimodality in a hybrid control scheme for prosthetic 
arms exceeds the number of distinct grips made possible by single modality control from EMG. 
Sequencing is also eliminated through multimodality control. The success rate from the control 
group was 91 % in a 60-second period and the overall mean time of task completion was 16.89 
seconds. Indications of a learning curve showed that reduction in the response time could be 
accomplished through more training. Multiple factors have to be accounted for when adapting 
BCI as a modality in prosthetic control. These factors include, but are not limited to, mental 
state, autonomy, type of BCI, stigma of prosthetics, and the adaptation of enhancing 
technologies. Multimodality in prosthetics through EEG and EMG based control is feasible, 
however still limited. The response time from an intention to move the hand and reaction of the 
prosthesis has to be improved before it can be deemed feasible for prosthetic control.

Resumé 
 
Kommercialisering af ellers utilgængelige produkter i elektroencefalografi (EEG) og 
elektromyografi (EMG) har åbnet nye muligheder i udviklingen af biomedicinske løsninger. Ved 
at bruge EEG signalbehandling til allerede eksisterende teknologiske hjælpemidler er det muligt 
at øge robustheden, nøjagtighed og ydeevnen, samtidig med at give ekstra frihedsgrader. 
Specialet søger at afdække muligheden for at tilpasse multimodalitet i en hybrid kontrol 
applikation for at opnå bedre protesekontrol for brugeren gennem kommercielle produkter. En 
hypotese omkring en forstærket brain computer interface (BCI) kontrol for folk, som mediterer 
ofte blev undersøgt. Specialet består af tre objektiver: (i), at vurdere, om en kombinering af EEG 
og EMG for protesekontrol i en hybrid kontrol applikation giver flere greb, (ii), at fjerne 
sekvensering af input for at flytte mellem greb gennem multimodalitet, og (iii) at undersøge 
gennemførligheden for EEG- og EMG-kontrol i en hybrid applikation baseret på eksperimentelt 
arbejde og litteratur. Meditationsgruppen og kontrolgruppe i forsøget viste ingen statistisk 
signifikant forskel i BCI præstation, dog med en svagt positiv tendens til at være statistisk 
signifikant. Endvidere viste ingen af variablerne (alder, mængde af spil og opgaveforskel) ingen 
statistisk signifikant forskel i styring af en virtuel protesehånd. Multimodalitet i en hybrid 
applikation for en protesearm overstiger antallet af distinkte greb muliggjort ved enkelt-modalitet 
kontrol fra EMG. Sekvensering elimineres også gennem multimodalitetskontrol. Succesraten for 
kontrolgruppen var 91% i en 60-sekunders periode, og den samlede gennemsnitlige tid for 
opgaveløsningen var 16,89 sekunder. Tegn på en indlæringskurve viste, at reduktion i 
responstiden kunne opnås gennem mere træning. Flere faktorer skal tages højde for i forbindelse 
med integrering af BCI som modalitet i protesekontrol. Disse faktorer omfatter, men en ikke 
begrænset til, mental tilstand, autonomi, type af BCI, stigmatisering af proteser og integrationen 
af forstærkende teknologier. Multimodalitet i proteser gennem EEG- og EMG-baserede styring 
er muligt, dog stadig begrænset. Responstiden mellem en hensigt om at flytte hånden og til at 
protesen bevæger sig skal forbedres før det kan siges at være gennemførlig for protesekontrol.

Chapter 1 
Introduction 
 
 
Commercialization of otherwise inaccessible products in electroencephalography (EEG) 
and electromyography (EMG) has opened up new possibilities in development of 
biomedical solutions.       This opens up for development in bio-robotic applications such 
as prosthetic arms. Current methods for controlling prosthetic arms uses EMG signal 
processing to manipulate the users’ prosthetic arm. While this offers an array of gestures, 
the limitations, both physical and internally, calls for other approaches in controlling 
prosthetic arms. 
       Advancements in brain-computer interfaces (BCI) give researchers the ability to 
process the generated EEG signals by the devices to classify specific patterns or 
intentions. Using EEG signals to control devices in different contexts has already been 
demonstrated by controlling a wheelchair (Li, Lei, Su, & Li, 2013; Millán et al., 2010a), a 
robot (del R. Millan, Renkens, Mourino, & Gerstner, 2004) and steering a tractor 
(Alonso-Garcia, 2011), just to mention a few. 
       By using EEG signal processing alongside existing assistive technologies (AT) it is 
possible to enhance the robustness, accuracy and performance while providing additional 
degree of freedom (Lalitharatne, Teramoto, Hayashi, & Kiguchi, 2013a; Millán et al., 
2010b). 
       Companies such as Emotiv Systems (USA) (Emotiv, 2016) and NeuroSky Inc. (USA) 
(NeuroSky, 2016) offer easily accessible EEG headsets. The company Thalmic Labs (CA) 
(Thalmic Labs, 2016b) has developed an armband with wireless EMG sensors as the only 
commercial product on the market specifically made for everyday use.  
       The Emotiv EPOC headset and Thalmic Myo armband will be the technology used in 
this thesis. Finally, an overview of the current state of the art and challenges in BCI for 
hybrid control scheme in prosthetics will be reviewed in regards to feasibility of 
applicability.

1.1 Motivation 
Technology is advancing faster than ever. New knowledge, methods and ways of solving 
complex problems are creating the foundation of tomorrow’s technology. Quantum 
computers already exist that can potentially disrupt the way technology works – a huge 
leap towards a more advanced daily life. However, some areas are not as progressive as 
others are. Some technology is developed with narrow applications as well as lack of 
imagination. In this study, the possibility of adapting BCI as a way of controlling a 
prosthetic arm is explored.  
       Current prosthetic control for lower arm amputees relies on EMG sensors attached to 
the residual limb of the lower arm. The EMG sensors built into the socket of the 
prosthetic arm are used to pick up muscle movement from the extensor and flexor muscle 
group. However, due to the limited number of EMG sensors, the user sometimes has to 
make illogical sequences of muscular activities to perform a gesture. It also makes for a 
counterintuitive way of controlling due to this sequencing.  
       By using BCI together with EMG signals, it is demonstrated here how the newest 
state of the art in technology can be combined to control a prosthetic arm. 
 
1.2 Objectives of the study 
The study seeks to establish the possibility of adapting multimodality in a hybrid control 
scheme to achieve better prosthetic control for the user. The specific objectives are:  
 To evaluate whether combining EEG and EMG for prosthetic control in a hybrid 
control application offer more grips.  
o Will multimodality offer a wider range of grips while maintaining the same 
level of control? 
 To eliminate the sequencing of input to move between grips through 
multimodality. 
o Can sequencing between grips be avoided by adapting multimodality in a 
hybrid control scheme? 
 To establish the feasibility of EEG and EMG control in a hybrid scheme based on 
experimental work and literature review.

o Which requirements, considerations and methods are necessary to achieve 
the goal of hybrid control scheme for prosthetic control?

1.4 Clarification of content 
The following section contains a short introduction to EEG, EMG and hybrid control 
scheme. 
 
1.4.1 Electroencephalogram 
EEG signals are the signatures of neural activities (Sanei & Chambers, 2007, p. 35). The 
readings are gathered from non-invasive electrodes placed on the subjects scalp. Through 
this, algorithms can decode the neural activity to classify specific actions based on the 
signals from the electrodes. The technology used in this project is the Emotiv’s EPOC 
headset. Through the Cognitiv Suite software developed by Emotiv, the user can train the 
system to detect specific thoughts. The EPOC headset can hold up to 13 different 
thoughts, but the engine currently only allows four at a time.  
       The default automatic classification algorithm made by Emotiv is used as the 
classification method for EEG signals. The thesis investigates the feasibility of hybrid 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
4 
 
control schemes for prosthetic devices, thus, a custom classification algorithm will not be 
made. 
 
1.4.2 Electromyogram 
EMG signals here refers to the electrical activities produced by skeletal muscles when the 
muscle cells contract (Gauthaam & Sathish Kumar, 2011). Much like EEG, the signals are 
gathered by placing electrodes on the surface of specific muscle groups. 
       The majority of myoelectric prosthetic hands are based on a simple design that limits 
motion to one degree of freedom (Joshi, Atreya, Arora, & Anand, 2009). Sensors around 
the user’s arm detects signals that are either active or not, and translates this signal into a 
pre-defined action (Touch Bionics, 2016a). Strength and speed of movements can also be 
controlled by variations of the muscle activity. One of the biggest flaws of pure EMG 
control in prosthetics is the sequential behavior in control when needing to move several 
joints (Ottobock, 2016). The user cannot move several joints at the same time or with the 
same signal. Instead, they have to move each joint separately. This is mainly because of 
the larger degrees of freedom for the human hand and the necessary versatility in 
movement needed to interact with the world (Saudabayev, 2015). The human hand is a 
complex piece of machinery, which is why complex systems are necessary to achieve the 
same level of operability. 
       The technology used in this project is Thalmic’s Myo band. The classification 
algorithm from Thalmic is used for classification of the EMG signals. 
       The Myo armband and the EPOC headset both use non-invasive electrodes for 
gathering data.  
 
1.5 Hybrid control scheme 
A hybrid control scheme is the concept of having two methods of control in a joint 
system. It can be either two or more of the same modalities e.g. two EEG devices, or it 
can be two different modalities e.g. an EEG and an EMG device. 
       A fusion between the modalities must be accomplished before it can be categorized as 
a hybrid system. Hybrid systems already exist in various other contexts such as cars 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
5 
 
(Staedter, 2002), energy sources (“Hybrid systems,” 2003) and robotics (Du, Zhang, 
Wang, & Mu, 2012). 
       In this study, the Emotiv EPOC EEG headset and the Thalmic Myo EMG armband is 
used in a hybrid control scheme.

Chapter 2 
Background and State of the art 
 
 
2.1 Electroencephalography (EEG) 
EEG has, until recently, predominantly been used in medical applications, especially in 
the field of epilepsy. The first case of electrical activity recorded from the scalp was 
performed in 1875 by Hermann Von Helmholz (Sanei & Chambers, 2007, p. 1). EEG has 
since then evolved into a technology available for other purposes than medical 
applications (Abdulkader, Atia, & Mostafa, 2015; Emotiv, 2016; NeuroSky, 2016; 
Nicolas-Alonso & Gomez-Gil, 2012a). EEGs are frequency related activities and the most 
commonly categorized frequencies of brain wave rhythms are delta (0.1-3.5 Hz), theta (4-
7.5 Hz), alpha (8-13 Hz), beta (14-30 Hz), and gamma (>30 Hz). 
 
2.2 Brain-computer interfaces (BCI) 
A BCI is a hardware and software communications system that permits cerebral activity 
alone to control computers or external devices (Nicolas-Alonso & Gomez-Gil, 2012a). 
The goal of BCI research is to provide capabilities to a group of people with disabilities. 
This group consists of a variety of disabilities such as paralysis, neurological 
neuromuscular disorders such as sclerosis, stroke, and spinal cord injuries. The latest 
addition being amputees.  
       Through an artificial intelligence system, a BCI has the capability of recognizing 
certain set of patterns in the brain signals. The recognition follows five consecutive 
stages: signal acquisition, preprocessing or signal enhancement, feature extraction, 
classification, and the control interface (Nicolas-Alonso & Gomez-Gil, 2012b).  
       BCI can be categorized into four types. P300, which is a positive detectable peak in 
activity that appears approximately 300ms after some infrequent auditory, visual or 
somatosensory stimuli (Nicolas-Alonso & Gomez-Gil, 2012a). P300 has been shown to 
work as a method of typing through visual stimuli (Klobassa et al., 2009a). However, the 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
8 
 
EPOC headset used in this study was shown not to perform ideally in rehabilitation and 
prosthetic control for P300 based applications.  
       Visual Evoked Potential (VEP) are brain activity modulations that occur in the visual 
cortex after receiving a visual stimulus (SSVEP), an auditory evoked potential (SSAEP), 
or a somatosensory evoked potential (SSSEP) (Abdulkader et al., 2015). Applications of 
VEP mainly revolve around SSVEP, where users are presented with visual flashing 
stimuli at unique rates (Lang, 2013). Slow Cortical Potential (SCP) are detectable 
changes in voltage that can take up to several seconds. It belongs to EEG signals below 1 
Hz. Unlike P300 and VEP, SCP requires extensive training in order to achieve an 
acceptable performance. Users can learn to control (increases and decreases) their cortical 
activity which can be translated into a response. It is, however, constrained to the mental 
state of the user as well as their motivation (Nicolas-Alonso & Gomez-Gil, 2012a) which 
makes the performance sensitive to the users state and the surroundings. Sensorimotor 
Rhythms (SMR) are oscillations in the brain activity localized in the mu band (7-13 Hz) 
and the beta band (14-30 Hz). The amplitude of the sensorimotor rhythms varies when 
cerebral activity is related to any motor task, although actual movement is not required. 
This means, that users can learn to control BCIs through voluntary modulations in the 
sensorimotor rhythms (Nicolas-Alonso & Gomez-Gil, 2012a). Decrease and increase of 
these rhythms are known as event related desynchronization (ERD) and event related 
synchronization (ERS), which are detectable in the EEG across multiple channels. In order 
to detect these decreases and increases of brain activity, a comparison of base state 
activity is necessary. This mode of control requires extensive training and can be 
challenging for the user.  
       Restoration of muscle groups in the hand for tetraplegics through functional electrical 
stimulation has shown to work with beta waves gathered from the subject (Pfurtscheller, 
Müller, Pfurtscheller, Gerner, & Rupp, 2003). Although the experiment required months 
of training of imaginary movement of the feet, the subject managed to differentiate 
thoughts in a scheme for opening and closing the hand and thumb. This experiment used 
two electrode pairs (2.5 cm anterior and posterior to position C3 and 2.5 cm anterior and 
posterior to position Cz) to locate right hand and over the foot area, respectively.  
       Newest research in non-invasive BCI for prosthetics has demonstrated a real-time 
closed-loop neuro-prosthetic control of grasping by an amputee. The subject managed to 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
9 
 
perform two grips, a cylindrical whole hand grasp and a lateral precision grasp, solely by 
the use of EEG signals (Agashe, Paek, Zhang, & Contreras-Vidal, 2015). The researchers 
also dismissed the notion of EMG interference, which is a known factor of artifacts in 
EEG readings, with the use of a low-pass filter as well as removing data segments 
corrupted by movement artifacts such as eye blinks and eye movement. Although the 
research was done on one subject and further research is needed, the possibility of using 
non-invasive cortical mapping of activity to decode grasp patterns is a big leap towards 
reliable BCI systems.  
 
2.3 Hybrid EEG and EMG 
Research in hybrid EEG and EMG based control schemes has shown that through 
multimodality, subjects can achieve a higher performance level than from single modality, 
however no standardization of EEG in prosthetics exists (Lalitharatne, Teramoto, 
Hayashi, & Kiguchi, 2013b). 
       Multimodality in prosthetics is not only focused on EEG and EMG. Other 
technologies such as the Microsoft Kinect as an assistive input technology to prosthetics 
has been shown to work in a multimodal control scheme along with EEG signals (Katyal 
et al., 2014a). The subject was equipped with a neural decode controller which would read 
neural data from a pair of electrode arrays on the subjects scalp. Through a learning 
session, the subject could manipulate an object with a robotic arm. The Microsoft Kinect 
was responsible for identifying the degrees of freedom of manipulable objects within the 
range of the robotic arm as well as planning the limb trajectory and grasp pattern for the 
desired object. Results of the experiment yielded a 97.6% success rate with a mean 
completion time of 42.2 seconds. While the success rate is impressive, the mean 
completion time is a difficulty which constraints the use of such a systems in real-time 
applications for prosthetic wearers.  
       Other research involves enhancing the responsiveness of one modality with the use of 
a second modality. The setting is different from prosthetics, but the fusion of modality in 
a hybrid scheme is the same nevertheless. People with muscle fatigue have diminishing 
muscular strength throughout the day. The same can be noted for some prosthetic wearers 
(Vigneswari, 2015). In the cases of pure residual muscle control through EMG signals, 
the prosthetic arm wearer has to actuate these muscles multiple times during the day. With 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
10 
 
an assistive modality, the user could attain the same level of control regardless of a 
diminishing effect in EMG signals (Leeb, Sagha, Chavarriaga, & Millán, 2011a; Leeb, 
2010a). 
       Research in muscle fatigue for prosthetic wearers needs to account for the decline in 
signal strength due to strenuous or repetitive activities of the muscle group. By using 
neural networks in a continuous learning environment in real-time, the system can detect 
muscle fatiguing during the day and increase the amplitude necessary for a grip to occur 
(Vigneswari, 2015). EEG can also aid in prosthetic control by increasing the level of 
control assigned to the EEG. By assigning EEG, an increasingly weighted amount of 
decision proportional to the decrease of amplitude from EMG sensors, a user could obtain 
the same level of control (Leeb et al., 2011a; Leeb, 2010a). 
       Currently, there is no system on the market that uses pure EEG signals in a 
multimodal setting along with EMG signals to control a prosthetic arm. 
 
2.4 Development of prosthetics 
The Modular Prosthetic Limb (MPL) from John Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory 
(JHAPL) is the latest in prosthetic arms. It has 26 degrees of freedom, 17 built-in motors 
(degree of control) as well as accelerometer, force sensors, temperature sensors and 
contact sensors among others. The arm is part of the DARPA Revolutionizing Prosthetics 
program, which started in 2009 and set out to deliver highly accurate motor skills as well 
as sensory feedback (Bridges, 2011; Johannes, 2011; Moran, 2011; Smith, 2011). 
       Latest state-of-the-art in use of the MPL is the Myo-controlled robotic hand from 
JHAPL (Johns Hopkins University, 2016a). The team attached a robotic arm through 
osseointegration into the subject’s residual limb. By using the Myo armband from 
Thalmic Labs the subject controls the arm with the remaining muscles in his upper arm. 
Through this, the subject can access a wide range of grips just by activating different 
muscles in the upper arm. However, the subject had to undergo Targeted Muscle Re-
innervation (TMR) surgery (Tenore, 2011). Targeted Muscle Re-innervation Surgery is 
necessary for the full movement offered by the MPL from John Hopkins (Johns Hopkins 
University, 2016b). By using multimodality, this procedure could become obsolete since 
additional input would outweigh the need for a TMR surgery. 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
11 
 
       As previously described, the challenge is to offer the same degrees of freedom as the 
biological human hand. The MPL from JHAPL offers 17 degrees of control with a 26 
degree of freedom. The hardware necessary to provide the foundation for advanced 
prosthetic control is therefore already present. The technological evolution of prosthetic 
hands has steadily increased and especially within the development of sensors between 
2010 and 2015 (Saudabayev, 2015). The limitation of prosthetics is thus not on the 
technology itself but rather the algorithms used in obtaining the wanted degrees of 
freedom necessary to perform activities of daily living. 
2.5 State of the art offered in Denmark 
In Denmark, the most recent state-of-the-art in prosthetic arms offered to people is the 
iLimb from Touch Bionics. Two electrodes placed inside the socket control the iLimb 
prosthetic arm. With the use of a smartphone, the user can change between a range of pre-
defined grips, however only four active grips at a time. These grips can also automatically 
change depending on which setting the user is in. Small chips called grip chips placed in 
the arm and at key locations control this with a range sensor depending on the situation 
i.e. work place gives the type- and mouse grip (Touch Bionics, 2014). 
       Controlling the iLimb arm relies solely on impulses generated by muscles. While this 
makes it simple to use, it also constraints the user. Moving between grips requires 
sequencing, changing setting on a smartphone or being near the range chips.

2.6 Ongoing research 
With a renewed focus on prosthetics, especially since 2009 with the Revolutionizing 
Prosthetics program at JHAPL, the attention on multimodality and EEG in prosthetic 
control could soon become a reality. Complex control is necessary due to the large 
degrees of freedom in the human hand, which is why EMG signal processing alone is not 
enough. In order to provide detailed control for activities of daily living, the hand needs to 
not only access a range of pre-programmed grips, but also have the ability to control each 
finger separately. To do this, multimodality with EEG signal processing could be the 
solution to this problem. There is still a long way from the current state of prosthetic 
hands to a clear imitation of the human hand. However, the preliminary research shows 
that EEG signal processing in BCI could achieve a higher degree of prosthetic hand 
movement than by using single modality alone (Lalitharatne et al., 2013a; Millán et al., 
2010b).

3.1 Electromyogram (EMG) 
Muscle activity is gathered from eight EMG sensors evenly placed around the forearm. It 
includes a nine-axis IMU containing a three-axis gyroscope, three-axis accelerometer and 
a three-axis magnetometer. Haptic feedback to the user is given through vibrations. 
Classification of gesture is calculated within the armband with an ARM Cortex M4 
processor before being sent to the adapter through Bluetooth 4.0 (Thalmic Labs, 2016a).  
 
Figure 2: Built-in gestures recognized by the Myo armband 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
14 
 
       The five gestures are as following: double-pinch, fist, spread fingers, wave in and 
wave out. Along with the five built-in gestures, a sixth state can also be used; the rest state 
when no signal is sent. 
       The gesture recognition algorithm in the armband is not publicly known. The 
manufacturers behind the Myo armband have stated, that the algorithm uses machine 
learning to guess the correct gesture. They collected EMG data from a multitude of 
individuals to determine the best way of making an algorithm that would work on all 
people. Due to the difference in structure and physiology of our muscles, the team had to 
generalize the algorithm since the EMG signal would differ based on each individual. 
Factors such as arm circumference, forearm strength, arm hair, and even body fat, have an 
influence on the signal measured at the surface of the skin (Thalmic Labs, 2014). 
       The Myo armband sends one signal of a gesture when that gesture is guessed. This 
makes it suitable for one-signal applications such as changing music in Spotify or change 
slides in a PowerPoint presentation. In the case of controlling a prosthetic arm, you need a 
continuous signal. Arguably, a one-signal approach could work as well, but that would 
only change the state of a grip in a binary mode i.e. either open or close. This means that a 
subject cannot stop the hand halfway through a grip if they chooses to. Due to this 
problem, the input handling from the Myo states a continuous replication of last known 
input until either a new input is received or rest state is active. 
By replicating the input signal at the speed of the Update-function in Unity a real-time 
movement of the hand based on the input, or lack thereof (rest), can be achieved. With 
this approach, the user can maintain an input until the hand is closed in a sufficient 
manner.

3.2 Electroencephalogram (EEG) 
The brain activity was acquired via 14 EEG channels over the motor cortex (AF3, F7, F3, 
FC5, T7, P7, O1, O2, P8, T8, FC6, F4, F8, AF4 – international 10-20 electrode locations) 
with two reference locations on P3 and P4. These are default channels when placing the 
EPOC headset on the subjects head. The software used to handle the EEG signals is the 
Cognitive Suite by Emotiv. The software detects the user’s real time brainwave activity to 
discern the user’s conscious intent to perform distinct physical actions on a real or virtual 
object. The detection engine works with 13 different actions: six directional movements 
(push, pull, left, right, up and down) and six rotations (clockwise, counter-clockwise, left, 
right, forward and backwards) plus an additional action where the user has to imagine an 
object disappear (Emotiv, 2014). The Cognitive Suite engine allows the user to choose up 
to four actions at any given time. However, more can be accessed by opening multiple 
control panels wherein the Cognitive Suite option can be launched. 
       In order to gain access to the different actions, the user must first train a neutral state, 
which is the idle state of the EPOC headset. Neutral state is the user’s passive mental 
state; one that is not associated with any of the selected, or intended, cognitive actions. 
Through another built-in program in the control panel of the EPOC headset, EmoKey, 
actions generated by thoughts can be sent to any given program. EmoKey links actions 
from the Cognitive Suite to a combination of keystrokes or simply a single keystroke in a 
given time interval. When the Cognitive Suite is recognizing an action, the assigned 
keystroke is sent to the application in focus, e.g. Unity. 
       The EPOC headset used in the study are not using any specific type of BCI for 
control in the experiment, although SMR is the most related type. The headset does not sit 
naturally across the sensorimotor cortex, since it was designed to gather as much 
information as possible and not only one type of BCI. Rather, it distinguishes between the 
trained thoughts and neutral (Emotiv, 2013). Participants imagined kinesthetic movement 
and were given visual feedback. The system processes the brain waves and tries to match 
them with the patterns learned in the training period.

Blender is an open-source 3D graphics software product primarily used for creating

3.4 The program 
This section consists of all the objects and scripts necessary to create the software for the 
experiments.  
       The Myo hub listener (Hub – 1 Myo) is an invisible prefabricated (prefab) object 
containing the prefab Myo. The Hub – 1 Myo prefab contains the script ThalmicHub, 
which is responsible for allowing access to one or more Myo armbands. Only one Myo 
armband was used, hence the Hub – 1 Myo prefab in the Unity scene. The Myo prefab, 
which is a child to the Hub – 1 Myo GameObject, contains the script ThalmicMyo. This 
script represents the Myo armband in the Unity scene. It contains information of the pose, 
accelerometer, gyroscope, synchronization and direction of the Myo armband on the users 
forearm. Spatial data is transformed to Unity standards, making it possible to track the 
Myo armband in real-time based on the sensors. 
 
       The invisible object ArmContainer contains the prefab object hand_9_animations_v3. 
The reason for having an invisible object containing a prefab, is to adjust the pivot point 
of the x, y and z direction when handling quaternion rotations. If the object has an odd 
pivot point with directional vectors of x, y and z non-relative to the vector lines of the 
Unity scene, then handling of quaternion rotations will be difficult. Instead, the prefab can 
be placed in an empty object with the correct pivot point and directional vectors. The 
GameObject ArmContainer contains the script JointOrientation. This script handles the 
rotation of a GameObject relative to the movement of the Myo armband. Real-time 
transformation of the GameObject with the use of the Myo armband makes it possible to 
imitate the movement of the arm for the wearer of the Myo armband. Although this 
feature is smart for movement of objects in a gaming environment, it was decided to not 
use in the experiment. The participants had to control a prosthetic arm with the use of two 
technologies they did not know prior to the experiment. That alone requires extreme 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
18 
 
effort, which is why it was decided to keep the virtual prosthetic arm static in terms of 
movement in a quaternion space. The three scripts mentioned up until now (ThalmicHub, 
ThalmicMyo and JointOrientation) were developed by Thalmic Labs for use in Unity. 
Modifications were made to JointOrientation for it to work with the virtual prosthetic 
arm, but ultimately it was dismissed due to the aforementioned situation. 
       The virtual prosthetic arm contained nine animations created in Blender. These 
animations were divided into separate animations and labeled accordingly since the 
animations in the Blender file was in one loop. Only six of the animations made it to the 
experiment. The three animations excluded from the experiment were fist, wave hand in 
and wave hand out since it was assumed that these grips were not commonly used. 
 
       Two graphical user interface (GUI) text scenes existed in the scene as well. 
SampleSceneGUI is an invisible GameObject in the Unity scene containing the script 
GUIScene. The script is attached to the Myo prefab from the Hub – 1 Myo prefab. It 
contains information relative to the state of the Myo armband. If the Myo armband is not 
initialized through the hub, is not synchronized or a Myo armband is not paired, then an 
appropriate text is displayed for the user to fix the problem. For example, if the Myo 
armband has not been synchronized then the text Perform the sync gesture will be 
displayed. If no problems are detected, then a message can be displayed for the user. A 
cookbook of the different combinations of EEG and EMG signals was displayed for the 
user once the Myo armband was initialized, paired and synchronized. 
       ActiveText is an invisible GameObject in the Unity scene containing the script 
ActiveInput. Just as GUIScene, this script is also linked to the Myo prefab. Once a correct 
combination of EEG and EMG signal are received by the system, a text will appear with 
the input detected. The script first detects which EEG input was last sent. It will then 
listen for a new gesture from the EMG and display the EEG and EMG combination once a 
continuous EMG signal is present. The reason for this was to have a visual debugger for 
the EMG signal. Participants had a tendency to involuntarily tighten the muscles in their 
forearm once a task was challenging. This could produce incorrect outcome since the Myo 
armband would stream a continuous signal from a gesture without the explicit intend of 
the participant. 
       The final component of the software is the main script, Brain. It is connected to the 
virtual prosthetic arm prefab. The Myo prefab is also connected to the arm in order to send 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
19 
 
gestures. The Brain script can be divided into three distinct features: Input detection, 
animation constraints and animation playback. 
       Input detection sorts the input sent from the Myo armband and EmoKey from the 
Emotiv EPOC Control Panel. The input detection works in a sequential manner. First, the 
script detects the gesture of the Myo armband e.g. Fist or Wave In. Then it checks for the 
last sent input from the Emotiv EPOC Control Panel. If no EEG input has been detected, 
then the loop stops before assigning the appropriate animation to play. When no EMG 
signal is being streamed to the computer, the gesture recognition changes to rest. The rest 
input makes all animations stop. This is ideal when you want to stop an animation 
halfway through i.e. halfway through a grip. If there was no rest statement, then the 
animation for each grip would play all the way through. 
       Animation constraints prevents the animation from skipping frames between the end-
frames of the active animation. An animation can loop from the end-frame to the start 
frame if the animation is allowed to play all the way through. Constraints were 
implemented to stop at the last frames and prevent the animation to play any further from 
a specific point. The hand would then remain in an end-position regarding of a continuous 
signal from the Myo armband. 
       Animation playback plays the animation based on the input from the EEG and EMG 
device. Eleven different input statements were necessary to differentiate between nine 
animations, and eight different input statements for the six animations in the experiment. 
Apart from the distinct animations there were two other states of the algorithm. One being 
the rest state, as mentioned earlier and the other being the rewind (fingers spread gesture 
from the Myo armband). The participants could, from any grip currently active, move the 
hand back in the idle position (open palm and fingers spread). All animations were made 
with crossfading. With crossfading the animations blend between each other which made 
the rewind feature an optional feature, but not a requirement to differentiate between 
grips. 
 
3.5 Data approach 
Lalitharatne et al. discusses two different categories in hybrid EEG-EMG-based control 
applications: sequential or simultaneous. 
       Each category provides benefits and constraints that the other does not necessarily 
offer. The context of the application helps to understand which category to pursue. 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
20 
 
       Simultaneous control offers, according to the study by Lalitharatne et al., a higher 
accuracy of the overall system if the fusion is done appropriately (Lalitharatne, Teramoto, 
Hayashi, & Kiguchi, 2013, p. 6). However, simultaneous control does not necessarily 
offer a switching mechanism. 
       The main benefit of the sequential approach is the reliability since either the EEG or 
EMG can act as a switching mechanism for the other (Lalitharatne et al., 2013b). 
       Sequential EEG-EMG fusion approach was established in the core algorithm due to 
stray thoughts, which would make it possible to have unwanted movement of the 
prosthetic hand.       Emotiv’s EPOC headset labels learned thoughts as well as neutral in 
order to distinguish between them, however; an action within the proximity of a learned 
thought i.e. thinking of lifting a physical object would sometimes trigger the lift thought, 
if learned by the system without the intent of doing so.  
       By verifying with an EMG signal in a sequential approach, biased movement by EEG 
is cancelled out. EMG verification makes it robust in terms of false-positive input, but 
limits the number of possible grips since pure EEG signals is restricted. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
       The notion of having stray thoughts ultimately led the systems category of hybrid-
based control to that of sequential even though that imposed limitations on the number of 
possible grips.

The procedure for the meditation group experiment differed from the experiment for the 
control group. An initial experiment was established to determine if the meditation group 
performed statistically significantly better than the control group. If a statistical test 
showed a significant difference, then the meditation group would go through the same 
experiment as the control group.

training and EEG control in a virtual environment. EEG training was done the same way 
as the control group in order to maintain consistency and produce reliable data for the

virtual scene with the text No input yet. The experimenter chose a thought at random out

trigger the EEG to get a visual response. The visual response consisted of the text 
changing correspondingly to the thought registered. The text on the screen would change

if they replicated the thought for push. The time was stopped when the participant 
triggered the correct thought. As with the control experiment, time was stopped if they did 
not trigger the correct thought within 60 seconds.  
 
3.9 Statistical analysis 
Multiple linear regression was used to test the mean response time between the two 
groups with age, level of computer/console gaming and difference between tasks as the 
explanatory variables. Level of computer/console gaming was divided into a binary factor 
class consisting of 1-2 as no, and 3+ as yes. Tasks were divided into a factor class with 
levels of 1 to 6 corresponding to the six tasks in the experiment. The analysis on response 
time between the control group and the meditation group was based solely on response 
time with age and gaming as explanatory variables. The response time was split into two 
analyses; one for push and one for lift. Since the experiment for the meditation group was 
not identical with the control group, the statistical analysis can only be used to give an 
indication of whether a statistical significance is present (with a p-value less than 0.05). If 
such value was obtained, the meditation group would be asked to participate on the same 
terms as in the control experiment. 
       All effects are reported by an estimate with 95% confidence intervals (CI) and a 
corresponding p-value. 
       All analysis and graphical presentations were performed using the statistical software 
R 3.2.5.

4.9 Artifacts and experience 
The first person in the experiment was the one who performed best. He had an overall 
mean of 1.75 seconds which was much lower than the accumulated overall mean from the 
control group of 16.89 seconds. The test case where he did worst was test case 4.4 (lift + 
wave in) with a mean time of 4.4 seconds. Test case 4.4 and 4.1 (push + wave in) were 
also the test cases in general with the worst mean time of 18.58 seconds for both. Wave in 
was the assigned EMG gesture for both these test cases, which could explain why this test 
case had a slightly higher mean time than the other test cases. However, test case 4.1 was 
also the test case where participant 1 did the best with a mean time of 0.6 seconds. Some 
EMG gestures were harder for the participants to perform than the others were. Ranging 
from easiest to hardest for all the participants were: fist, wave out, wave in, spread fingers. 
The difference in how difficult the various EMG gesture were, had no impact on the test 
case response time. The difference in mean time for participant 1 on test case 4.4 and 4.1 
could hence be random or with another underlying bias.  
==PAGE:49==
 
 
38 
 
       The reason why participant 1 performed so well in the experiment was due to 
artifacts. It was both observed and later discussed with the participant, that he used the 
muscles above the ears to produce EMG signals. It was not intentional, but rather what 
felt natural when he trained the thoughts. The electrodes around the ear picked up these 
signals and it provided a response strong enough to clearly indicate a “thought” signal. 
Due to this muscle movement during EEG training and under the experiment, participant 
1 was able to produce an EEG response immediately. Participant 1 also reported use of 
thoughts along with the muscle movements, however it is questionable how much of an 
effect these thoughts had when there was EMG activity from the ear muscles. Artifacts 
like this are generally not acceptable in BCI, but instead of actively filtering the signals 
from muscle movement around the ear, it could be used as a means of control in 
prosthetic control. 
       The movement of the muscles around the ear is a remain from evolution, human 
vestigiality (Darwin, 1890, p. 13). The need for moving the ear has diminished throughout 
evolution, but has not completely disappeared. Using these muscles to produce a strong 
signal could be beneficial, rather than an artifact needed to be filtered out. Movement of 
the ear is also hardly noticeable which aids in the concealment of ATs. Using EMG 
signals by acquisition through the EPOC headset has previously shown to be possible. 
Subjects were able to steer a tractor by clenching the jaw and looking right or left 
(Alonso-Garcia, 2011). This type of control is context specific for the driver inside the 
tractor. The driver does not have to interact with other people and he is focused on the 
task at hand i.e. steering the tractor. For a prosthetic user, the use of EMG has to be more 
flexible. Clenching the jaw and looking to the sides could be more constraining than it 
would be helpful. A prosthetic user has to be able to operate their robotic arm regardless 
of the context. This type of EMG based control that are visible would definitely not work. 
Instead, utilizing the concept of ear muscles could provide an extra method of movement 
in a hybrid build for prosthetic wearers. It does not constrain the user from engaging in 
everyday activities like speaking or looking at a specific direction. This method of control 
is not context specific and could work regardless of the situation.  
       Participant 1 had no prior experience with EEG equipment along with 14 of the other 
participants in the experiment. Three participants had prior experience with EEG 
technology. Two of them were helping with beta testing the software prior to the 
experiment to discover bugs. The last participant had previously participated in an

experiment regarding muscle stimuli on the legs and brain signals. The three participants

Discussion 
 
 
This chapter contains areas of interest regarding feasibility of hybrid control scheme in 
prosthetic devices. Discussion of equipment, procedures and methods will also be 
addressed in this chapter. 
       The last objective of the study is answered based on the considerations that arose 
during the experiments and during the creation of the thesis. Each assumption is evaluated

5.1 Stigma in prosthetics 
Feasibility in hybrid control schemes is not only the technical aspect and challenges, 
which must be taken into account. The feasibility and applicability of EEG in hybrid 
control schemes also requires a focus on the aesthetics for the user, sometimes even the 
concealment of the technology. The Emotiv’s EPOC headset used in the experiments 
offered the ability to map thoughts to different actions, but required 16 electrodes placed 
throughout the scalp of the participant. During the experiments, several of them expressed 
confusion regarding the aesthetics of the EEG headset:  
“Is it intended that people has to wear this headset to be able to control the arm? (Interviewee, 
male, age 26)” 
       Clearly, the use of a visible headset upon the participants head was stigmatizing and 
the participants expressed reluctance of wearing the headset in public.  
 
==PAGE:57==
 
 
46 
 
 
 
Current prosthetic devices have the concealment of ATs in mind when they design their 
products. Assistive products are made with the intention of modelling the human hand. 
Here, aesthetics are valued along with the technical components to hide the robotic 
components.  When a person receives a prosthetic hand, they have the option to coat it 
with a silicone layer.  This silicone layer functions as a mirror of the other hand, making it 
near identical to the other. While this offers the appearance of a real human hand, it is at 
the cost of functionality. Aesthetic prosthetics offers none or very low functionality with 
the intention of offering unnoticeable prosthetics. Whether a person chooses the aesthetic, 
low-function prosthetic or a high-functioning non-aesthetic hand depends on a few 
factors. This includes age, degree of amputation, health, and what the user expects from 
the prosthesis (Sahva, 2016).

Figure 26: Myoelectric hand with basic silicone layer.

==PAGE:58==

If the subject chooses an advanced prosthetic, they compromise the aesthetics offered for 
such a prosthetic. Due to the degrees of movements offered in advanced prosthetics, the 
aesthetic look is reduced to a simple silicon layer. Before applying a new technology in 
conjunction with an existing AT, it is important to understand the current level of 
stigmatization in prosthetics. It is important to understand how prosthetic users experience 
their situation and how they cope with this stigmatization. Any new enhancing technology 
in conjunction with existing technology has to apply the same level of aesthetic and non-
visibility before it can be integrated and accepted by the wearers. Investigation of 
psychological effects of humanlike prosthetics was conducted in an effort to study how 
wearers of prosthetics felt and experienced during rehabilitation. The research showed 
that subjects felt severe psychological effects during the rehabilitation period. 
“Any disfigurement of the hand, which is among the easily noticed part of the body, certainly 
affects the psychological well-being. The reported effects of amputation are depression, feelings of 
hopelessness, low self-esteem, fatigue, anxiety and sometimes suicidal ideation of the patient 
(Desmond, 2002)” 
Adding to the psychological effects of amputees is the social stigmatization of having a 
prosthetic device. Through concealment of prosthetics, wearers of prosthetic devices can 
ward off social stigmatization, which in turn will enable their social integration and 
reduce the level of emotional problems (Murray, 2005). Taking the psychological effects 
and stigmatization into account makes it clear, that enhancement of existing assistive 
devices through new technology must be in accordance with these factors (Parette, 2004). 
Although a lot of focus has been emphasized on the negative effects of prosthetic devices, 
some think differently of adopting new technology in aid of disabilities. In an effort to 
promote BCI as a tool, or even an embodiment of the user, the stigmatization of these 
individuals could diminish. With BCI growing more popular and the commercialization 
of easily accessible BCI equipment, stigmatization based on use of EEG equipment such 
as the Emotiv EPOC could likely become lesser than it is now. 
“We have suggested that whether BCIs always remain tools or sometimes become parts of our 
bodies, disabled people stand to gain if they are widely adopted. (Aas & Wasserman, 2015)” 
 
To summarize – stigmatization when adding EEG as an enhancing technology is based on 
the adaptation of BCI in everyday life. As of now, such technology is not widely known, 
which is why EEG technology should be concealed if added to any assistive technology.

5.2 Adaptation of enhancing technologies 
Adapting to a new prosthetic device varies depending on the situation of the subject 
(Racy, 1992). Coping with the reality of living without a natural functioning limb and 
having to teach yourself the basic task of everyday living is something many people are 
dealing with. In the United States alone there are nearly 2 million people living with limb 
loss (Amputee Coalition, 2016).  
       Training of an artificial arm in order to obtain a level of function close to that of a 
natural biological hand is a process that takes extensive training. The sensors present in 
the modern day prosthetics offers the ability to manipulate motors based on touch or 
electrical signals from the nerve endings. These signals are, compared to EEG signals, 
more reliable in both accuracy and time (Lalitharatne et al., 2013b). The experiment in 
this thesis also showed during the training period, that the participants had a much greater 
response rate with the Myo armband than with the EEG headset. The time it took to adapt 
to the Myo armband was also much quicker than with the EEG headset. This is of course 
due to the technological aspect since the Myo armband did not require any training, but 
also because the participants could easier replicate the same muscle signal required to 
activate the gesture recognition, compared to the EEG signals. 
       When installing a proposed enhancing solution (e.g. EEG) in conjunction with an AT 
(e.g. EMG in prosthetic arms), then the response time and general ability to control the 
overall solution must not diminish. The intention to use an enhancing technology in 
prosthetics is to offer additional degrees of freedom or give alternatives to control. If the 
enhancing technology increases the response time between an intention to move and a 
response, then the enhancing technology is not offering a suitable solution for the user. 
       Another aspect in developing new solutions for prosthetic wearers is the change in 
known procedures and/or technologies. If a new solution is radically different and offers 
no combination with existing ATs, then the user is set back to the lowest level of 
applicability. The lowest level of applicability refers to a level of use where the user, from 
baseline, has to learn a technology again. This stage of applicability is either when 
introduced to a new technology, or when an existing technology has drastically changed 
due to the adaptation of an enhancing technology. 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
49 
 
 “Furthermore, if our goal is to offer solutions to these people, and to augment their capabilities, 
then BCIs must be combined with existing assistive technologies (AT), especially those they 
already utilize. (Millán et al., 2010a)” 
 
If an enhancing technology can utilize on the expertise a user has achieved throughout the 
extensive learning period, then that technology stands much stronger for the overall fusion 
with the AT.  
       Adding enhancing technology also requires a person-specific evaluation on the 
context of their understanding. Enhancing technologies are only enhancing if the user 
knows how to utilize the new procedure or technology. 
“To minimize the probability of harm, investigators and practitioners must educate users on the 
potential positive effects and limits of BCIs. They should also adopt strict selection criteria and 
include only those with largely preserved cognitive functions who could give informed consent 
and would more likely be trained to successfully operate it. (Glannon, 2014)” 
While this example covers all types of disabilities such as paralysis, the selection criteria 
mentioned also applies to a group of prosthesis wearers. This may seem unfair for those 
with an impairment of cognitive function such as elders or people with cognitive 
disabilities, but it could protect their emotional state as well as maintaining their level of 
applicability. A user can be deemed unsuitable for an enhancing technology, such as BCI 
in conjunction with their prosthetic arm, if they do not meet the requirements to fully 
operate the type of enhancing technology. Choosing individuals based on these criteria 
may be justified due to the overall adaptation of the enhancing technology. 
       To summarize – adaptation of enhancing technologies in conjunction with ATs 
requires that the overall responsiveness of the system is maintained. In addition, selection 
criteria on individual basis are needed to ensure that the user complies with the offered 
technology, especially when that technology is BCI. 
 
5.3 The future of hybrid control schemes in prosthetics 
Since 2009, when the JHAPL started their focus on prosthetics, has the field of prosthetics 
grown drastically. Especially their work on prosthetic arms and new ways of operating 
them has improved the field of prosthetics (Burck, 2011; Johannes, 2011; Levy, 2011; 
Moran, 2011; Tenore, 2011). 
==PAGE:61==
 
 
50 
 
       However, the use of hybrid EEG-EMG-based control in prosthetics is very scarce 
with only a few articles mentioning hybrid EEG-EMG as a control scheme for people 
suffering from muscle fatigue or severe motor disabilities (Leeb, Sagha, Chavarriaga, & 
Millán, 2011b; Leeb, 2010b; Rouillard, 2015). The previous sections have given a look 
into the challenges and opportunities of hybrid control schemes using multimodality. In 
order to achieve a solution for better prosthetics, with the use of hybrid control schemes, 
manufactures must understand the need of incorporation rather than extension.  
       De Preester, H. (2010) distinguishes between incorporation and extension for the 
users understanding of their prosthesis (De Preester, 2010). Extensions are perceived as 
tools for the user. A means of getting from A to B which otherwise would not be possible 
without the aid of the extension i.e. a prosthetic arm. Not all extensions, or tools, are 
perceived as just a means to achieving something. Some tools might also feel close to not 
being there, which Preester describes as quasi-transparent i.e. almost invisible. 
Nevertheless, while a tool might inhibit the perception of being quasi-transparent, it is still 
seen as a tool by the user.   
“One condition for being a part of the body, is that the prosthesis becomes a knowing body part, 
in other words, something that is no longer a mere object attached to the body, but something that 
withdraws into the sensorimotor apparatus of the body. (De Preester, 2010)” 
 
Users have to feel the prosthesis as a body-part in order to achieve incorporation. One of 
the main reasons for not achieving incorporation is the imperfect mobility solutions. 
 
“Many prosthesis users consider their prosthesis indeed as an instrument, and not as a body part. 
One of the reasons is that the prosthesis provides imperfect mobility solutions. (De Preester, 
2010)” 
 
A solution to achieve incorporation of prostheses is by using sensory input. By offering 
tactile feedback, users are more likely to feel the prosthesis as a part of their self-image, 
providing that a prosthesis becomes an integrated body part, and not just a tool (Marasco, 
Kim, Colgate, Peshkin, & Kuiken, 2011). The development of sensors in robotics for use 
in prosthetics has grown steadily in the past decade (Saudabayev, 2015) and new research 
has integrated sensors able to give haptic feedback to the user (Armiger, 2013; Tenore, 
2011). By adopting feedback to the user, it will give them a better feeling of incorporation 
of their prosthetic device. 
==PAGE:62==
 
 
51 
 
       The notion of having technology that becomes an incorporation of a person’s self-
image is also important when adding BCI as a modality to prosthetic control. In the 
experiments for this thesis, a specific cognitive thought was used to change the input from 
the EEG headset. When referring to Preester and the discussion regarding extension and 
incorporation, it is clear that the solution becomes an extension – a tool for manipulating a 
technology. The ideal solution, when using BCI, is to decode the brain patterns of 
intention to move objects, like we normally do when manipulating objects. The user of a 
prosthetic device should be unaware that they are operating a piece of technology. 
Instead, it should be a natural integration to their body and the control should feel as 
natural as moving an actual biological limb. 
       To summarize – when developing hybrid control schemes for prosthetic devices, 
researchers has to be aware of the distinction between extension and incorporation. The 
mode of control has to rely on the natural decoding mechanisms of intention to use, rather 
than distinct cognitive thoughts as an input.  
 
5.4 Mental states in BCI 
Physical fatigue sometimes happen when the muscles are stressed too much without 
proper restitution. This typically happens for users of prosthetics in the training period 
when they first receive their prosthesis, however not from experienced users2. Mental 
fatigue is the equivalent of muscle fatigue for BCI. Several factors can affect the user’s 
ability to perform a task through BCI control. These factors are mental workload, stress 
level, tiredness, attention level and cognitive process (Millán et al., 2010a). Other factors 
which could cause variation in BCI performance are frustration and attention (Myrden & 
Chau, 2015). In the latter case, a statistical significant relationship between frustration and 
BCI performance was established, while fatigue approached statistical significance. The 
research by Myrden & Chau (2015) showed that an optimal level of frustration, fatigue 
and attention was found to be most appropriate for effective control of BCI. More 
specifically, when the subjects had self-reported low level of mental fatigue, they did 7% 
lower than average, and 7% higher than average when self-reported frustration was 
moderate. Their work suggested that an appropriate level of moderate mental fatigue as

baseline with attention as a compensatory mechanism to increase frustration yielded the 
best BCI results. 
       When the mental state affects the BCI performance for individuals, then algorithms 
has to account for the changes. Mental fatigue, stress level, tiredness and attention level 
changes throughout the day and is context specific based on what you are doing. When 
developing algorithms for BCI and especially in hybrid builds for control schemes in 
prosthetics, it is important to maintain the same consistency in BCI performance 
regardless of the mental state. BCI decoding has to be dynamic and account, or even 
detect, these mental state changes that occurs daily. The Emotiv EPOC’s detection 
algorithm does not account for mental changes – it is static in its detection algorithm. The 
Emotiv EPOC Control Panel does, however, have the ability to detect a small range of 
subjective emotional responses, but they do not cover the full spectrum of mental states. 
The Affective Suite measures levels of interest, excitement and engagement while the 
headset is active on a subject (Emotiv, 2014). These levels could be used in the 
experiment to account for the level of engagement (attention), which was shown by 
Myrden & Chau (2015) to have an effect.  
       To summarize – mental states has to be accounted for when designing hybrid control 
schemes for prosthetics where BCI performance is an active input method. Having static 
decoding algorithms could produce bias, or negatively affect the BCI performance leading 
to poor performance and a worse response rate when trying to move the prosthetic arm 
with EEG signals. 
 
5.5 Learning curve in BCI 
The assumption of increased performance for people who meditate frequently was not 
statistically significant in the analysis. It did show a weak positive tendency towards 
being statistically significant, and with the small sample group of five people it can be 
argued if the result would shift towards statistical significance if more people were 
included in the meditation group. One of the five people in the meditation group did 
worse than the others with an overall mean time of 21.72 seconds. A possible explanation 
for this could be due to a handicap for that particular participant. The participant was deaf 
and had to read the lips of the experimenter during the experiment. This could have 
caused some confusion and the level of details in explaining the experiment could have 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
53 
 
been worse than for the other participants. The other participants in the meditation group 
performed much better, with an accumulated overall mean of 8.43 seconds. The 
assumption of increased performance has also been proved in scientific papers (Jacko, 
2009; Lo, 2004; Tan, 2014) where a statistical significance between control groups and 
meditation groups showed enhanced abilities to control their EEG in BCI application for 
the meditation group. The hypothesis in this thesis stated that by increased focus of 
thoughts, subjects might be better to control BCI technologies. This assumption is also 
known as a learning curve for BCI. Meditation is a factor that potentially could give a 
better baseline performance than people who do not meditate. The learning curve for both 
groups would be the same, but the meditation group would start with a better performance 
at baseline. 
       Only 10 training sessions for each thought were recorded in the control and the 
meditation experiment. This was due to the time required to set up, train data, perform the 
experiment and the questionnaire afterwards. The amount of necessary training data to 
achieve a sufficient result varies greatly for each individual. Most of the factors have been 
established in the discussion section already. All the participants were told to hold a 
thought of something being pushed or lifted, which for some could be an abstract thought. 
The technique they used could have been suboptimal since they only had five minutes to 
come up with a technique. It was unknown whether the subjects had achieve a sufficient 
level of accuracy and performance on the 10 sessions or if their learning curve was 
sufficiently sated before moving to the experiment. 
       Research in learning curves for BCI shows that subjects were able to achieve better 
performance with increased number of sessions/trials in various experiments (Fuchs, 
2008; Klobassa et al., 2009b; Pineda, Silverman, Vankov, & Hestenes, 2003). Pineda et 
al. (2003) described a linear relationship between performance and sessions with a steep 
learning curve during the first six training sessions and leveling off during the final four 
training sessions. Each training sessions consisted of three 15-minute periods.  
“… subjects show an almost linear increase in learning across training sessions…” (Pineda et al., 
2003) 
 
The subjects had to differentiate between low-level mu wave and high-level mu waves. 
After the sixth training session did the learning stop to progress linearly. This was after 
4.5 hours of training. Klobassa et al. (2009) also showed a significant improvement 
==PAGE:65==
 
 
54 
 
throughout a four to six week period. Their participants endured 12 sessions each lasting 
60 minutes. 
 
“Group A performed significantly better in the final sessions than in the initial ones. Hence, 
Group A participants improved over the course of the study.” (Klobassa et al., 2009b) 
 
 
Finally, Fuchs et al. (2008) showed a linear relationship in BCI performance between 
success rate and number of trials. Subjects underwent 170 trials and improved their 
success rate by ~20 % (from 55% to 75% and from 73% to 88%). The amount of 
sessions/trials needed to achieve a high level of accuracy is much greater than the 10 
training sessions the participants had in the experiments. The low amount of training most 
likely had an effect on their BCI performance.  
       Ideally, it would have been beneficial for the experiments to perform training sessions 
with two or three day’s interval to observe the learning curve of the participants. For each 
training sessions the response rate of various tasks and success rate would be established 
and matched with previous results. This would repeat until no progression could be 
observed i.e. the learning curve was sufficiently sated. At that point, all the participants 
would theoretically be at the desired level of BCI performance to establish the feasibility 
of BCI in a control scheme for prosthetics. Due to time constraints, this could not be 
achieved, but it would have been highly advantageously.  
       The overall response rate from the participants would most likely be less if enough 
time were provided to achieve better BCI performance. This hypothesis of a progressing 
learning curve also aids in the matter of feasibility of EEG in prosthetics control. The 
reaction time for the prosthetic arm should be close to instant, when a prosthesis wearer 
desires a grip for a given task. The overall mean time of 16.89 seconds for the control 
group was far from instant, but with the assumption of better performance and success 
rate through increased learning it could be achieved. 
       To summarize – a learning curve is clearly present in BCI where users has to replicate 
brain signals in order to achieve a response. Indications of a learning curve was also 
present in the experiments. Acknowledging the learning curve when creating experiments 
with BCI as a control modality is essential before an assumption can be established.

5.6 Autonomy in hybrid control schemes 
A shared autonomy framework should also be considered when combining modalities in a 
hybrid build. The outputs of BCI and EMG in combination with information about the 
environment and the technology itself could possibly estimate the user’s intent better than 
solely by a hybrid build. By using sensors in the technology i.e. the prosthetic arm, it 
would be possible to identify manipulable objects within the proximal range of the 
prosthetic arm (Katyal et al., 2014b). If the system knows which object the prosthetic arm 
is supposed to interact with, then the appropriate grip can be activated without the user 
having to explicitly choose it. This level of autonomy could benefit the user in a series of 
ways. For example, it could reduce the amount of distinct input combinations required to 
control the arm as well as making it easier to do activities of daily living. If the system 
can detect whether a cylindrical grip or a pinch grip is necessary, then the user would only 
have to perform the opening and closing of the pre-assigned grip. Machine learning can 
aid in this matter in combination with image recognition algorithms. Image recognition 
algorithms detects if the object is e.g. spherical, cylindrical or rectangular. In addition, it 
can calculate the height and width of the object to better asses the necessary grip and grip 
force (Katyal et al., 2014b). 
       The position and velocity of the prosthetic arm can also be used in the assessment of 
grips. This is demonstrated by Touch Bionics with their i-mo™ technology in the iLimb 
quantum prosthetic hand (Touch Bionics, 2016b). An automated grip can be accessed by 
moving the prosthetic hand in one of four directions e.g. moving the arm straight ahead 
might open the hand for a greeting grip.  
       The use of sensors in a shared autonomy framework has been demonstrated by 
steering a brain-controlled wheelchair (Millán et al., 2010b). Obstacles ahead of the 
wheelchair were identified as well as position and velocity of the wheelchair. 
“In a shared autonomy framework, the outputs of the BCI are combined with the information 
about the environment (obstacles perceived by the robot sensors) and the robot itself (position and 
velocities) to better estimate the user’s intent.” (Millán et al., 2010b). 
 
The use of sensors in the field of exoskeletal robotics has also been reported (Du et al., 
2012). A shared autonomy framework existing of EEG, EMG, foot pressure sensors and 
an optical-fiber motion capture system was created. The sensors worked together with 
EEG and EMG to propose a better hybrid control scheme for exoskeletal movement. 
==PAGE:67==
 
 
56 
 
       A third option of autonomy is using the concept of time in relation to context. Some 
grip might be more prevalent than others throughout the day. These predictive capabilities 
through sensors would strengthen and become more accurate through extensive use, thus 
improving the accuracy and performance when controlling the prosthesis.  
       The technology used in the thesis, the Myo armband and EPOC headset, both have 
built in sensors capable of utilizing autonomy. The Myo armband has an accelerometer 
and a gyroscope capable of detecting velocity and position in space through the built-in 
nine axis IMU (Thalmic Labs, 2016a). The EPOC has the capability to detect where the 
subject is looking through the built-in 2-axis gyroscope (Emotiv, 2014). It could have 
been advantageously to adopt autonomy in the experiment by using the built-in sensors 
from the technology in predicting grips.  
       To summarize – through shared autonomy framework, users could be able to obtain a 
higher degree of control, thus making hybrid control schemes more feasible. Developers 
of prosthetics should incorporate the use of passive sensors in control schemes, in 
addition to focusing on active control input such as EEG and EMG. 
 
5.7 Alternative to multiple linear model 
Cox’s proportional hazard model (Cox’s regression) is a well-known model for analyzing 
survival data (Crichton, 2002). The model is widely used in epidemiological studies 
where the analysis seeks to establish the impact of a risk factor on the time to the 
occurrence of an event. The Cox regression method is similar in some ways to multiple 
regressions, which was the applied statistical model in the study. The dependent variable 
is the hazard at a given time and the independent variables thought to explain variation in 
the hazard. 
       The data gathered throughout the experiment constituted a time of completion for a 
participant. If the participant did not finish within the period of 60 seconds, then right 
censoring occurred. Cox’s proportional hazard regression would have been suitable for 
the analysis between the groups as well as the independent variables. Three distinct 
variables needs to be in place for the Cox’s regression to work time, event indicator and 
variables. Time is the response variable in the data i.e. how long it took them to complete 
the task. Event indicator is the right censoring i.e. if they did not complete the task within 
60 seconds. Finally, the variables containing the group-difference (control group or 
meditation group) and the other independent variables age, gaming and tasks. The hazard 
==PAGE:68==
 
 
57 
 
ratio modelled by the Cox regression then indicates whether an independent variable has 
an effect on the dependent variable. However, since each participant had multiple times 
for each task it would have been extremely time consuming to use this model. On 
standard epidemiological analyses there only exists one time for each subject i.e. the time 
from inclusion and until end of follow-up or death, which makes it easier to calculate. 
Due to this, the Cox’s proportional hazard model was not chosen as the proposed analysis 
since it would be too time consuming for this thesis. 
       To summarize – although the Cox’s proportional hazard model would have been 
suitable due to the structure of the data, it would not have been beneficial for this study 
due to time constraints. Instead, a multiple regression on the mean time for each 
participant on each task was used for the analysis.  
 
5.8 Reliability of Emotiv’s EPOC headset 
The Emotiv EPOC headset is mainly manufactured for gaming and meditation purposes. 
Its low cost of $799 for the EPOC headset and $299 for the Insight headset (5 channel 
EEG headset) makes it accessible for most people. Emotiv brands themselves as 
manufacturer of an EEG headset capable of various tasks such as EEG research, usability 
testing, cognitive performance and BCI development (Emotiv, 2016). Researchers has 
tested the EPOC headset in the field of EEG research and BCI development to determine 
if the EPOC headset is capable of performing as well as medical-grade equipment. As 
previously described, the electrodes from the EPOC headset are placed at key locations in 
order to achieve the performance necessary for their full software suite (expressive suite, 
affective suite and cognitive suite). These locations have been tested in relation to the 
different types of BCI. 
       The performance of low cost EEG equipment in relation to research purpose has been 
questioned by its ability to produce reliable and true EEG signals. Medical-grade 
equipment is too expensive for end-users, especially disabled people for whom the use of 
BCI might aid them in their situation. The Emotiv EPOC headset has been tested on two 
of the four BCI types – P300 and ERP. 
       The Emotiv EPOC headset was compared to a medical-grade system to determine its 
performance in a P300 BCI using the same electrodes for both systems (Duvinage, 2013). 
==PAGE:69==
 
 
58 
 
A quantitative comparison between the two equipment showed that the Emotiv EPOC 
headset performed suboptimal, thus not suitable for rehabilitation or prosthesis control. 
This was mainly due to the reliability of the EPOC headset. While the subjects were 
sitting down there was a medium significant effect, whereas a large significant effect was 
observed under walking conditions. The study only focused on P300 and not the other 
types of BCI.  
       A different study also confirmed the lack of reliability for a P300 experiment 
compared to medical-grade equipment (Ekanayake, 2010). The EPOC headset was 
verified to record true EEG signals, however not reliable enough for a P300 type 
application. 
       In the field of auditory event-related potentials, the EPOC headset has shown 
promises as a reliable and valid alternative to medical-grade equipment for recording 
auditory ERPs (Badcock, 2013; Badcock et al., 2015). The Emotiv EPOC headset was 
compared to Neuroscan, a research and medical grade equipment costing $60.000. Both 
the Neuroscan headset and the Emotiv EPOC headset was measured simultaneously and 
showed no significant difference between the two. The authors also noted that due to the 
quick and clean set-up procedure and portability of the Emotiv EPOC headset it would 
make a promising tool for measuring auditory processing for people unable or unwilling 
to be tested in an experimental laboratory.  
       Some technicalities do exist preventing a low-cost EEG headset such as Emotiv’s 
EPOC from being an alternative to the medical-grade equipment. 
“Our data suggest that, depending on the paradigm used, wireless headsets like the Emotiv may 
be a viable alternative to traditional ERP collection methods. However, issues like low sampling 
rate and limited scalp coverage currently still limit their broad applicability.” (Weed, 2014) 
Another factor limiting the EPOC headset is the saline solution. Each electrode has a 
semi-dry pad that needs to be soaked in a saline solution before the electrode can get 
contact with the scalp. This saline solution will evaporate on a full-day basis or even less. 
This was also noticed during the experiments where some electrodes had to be soaked 
halfway through the experiment. A potential drop in performance can occur if the 
electrodes are not sufficiently soaked. 
       The detection accuracy of the Emotiv EPOC headset for mental actions (Cognitive 
suite) also shows promising results. An empirical evaluation of the Emotiv EPOC headset 
==PAGE:70==
 
 
59 
 
for detection of mental actions showed that the EPOC headset was able to detect mental 
actions with an accuracy and sensitivity significantly greater than chance (Taylor & 
Schmidt, 2012). 
 “Despite its reduced cost relative to traditional research-grade EEG systems, the EPOC system 
appears to be capable of recording brain activity for the detection of various mental actions 
successfully. This system will provide BCI capabilities to a whole new field of personal users, who 
use a BCI system not because it is a last and only option, but because it provides a novel and 
enjoyable means of interacting with technology.” (Taylor & Schmidt, 2012) 
To summarize – the EEG headset chosen for the experiment was justifiable although not 
as reliable as medical-grade equipment for all types of BCI. The detection performance of 
mental actions shows above random accuracy and sensitivity, thus acceptable as the 
method of EEG control in the experiment. The Emotiv EPOC headset is still not precise 
enough as an end-user application for prosthetic control, however precise enough as a 
proof of concept equipment – as shown in this thesis. 
 
5.9 Reliability of Thalmic’s Myo armband 
The Myo armband is made for gaming and control of applications on the computer or 
smartphone. It is the first input device in the world to use the electrical activity of the 
muscles to control technology (Thalmic Labs, 2016b). Not much research exist on the 
market since the Myo armband is a new piece of technology with the first alpha release in 
January 2014 and the first final release in mid-2014. Since then, the market for Myo has 
grown with lots of new applications made available through their free software 
development kit. The Myo armband’s accuracy in prosthetic control was assessed in a 
study where training data was generated based on finger movement and different grip of 
the subjects. The study showed, that the Myo armband’s accuracy was insufficient to 
assist hand amputees with enough capability to recover significant lost hand functionality 
(Abduo & Galster, 2015). However, another study by Johns Hopkins Applied Physics 
Labs (JHAPL) shows that the Myo armband is indeed capable of offering a prosthetic user 
with enough control to control their prosthetic arm (Thalmic Labs, 2015). In contrast to 
the former study showing insufficient capabilities, the latter does require targeted muscle 
re-innervation and a custom detection algorithm in order for the Myo armband to function 
properly. The subject in the JHAPL’s is using the Myo armband on his upper-arm – 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
60 
 
sometimes multiple Myo armband at the same time – to control the MPL, also developed 
by Johns Hopkins. Whether the Myo armband can be deemed suitable for prosthetic 
control is dependent on the preliminary setting i.e. targeted muscle re-innervation and 
custom detection algorithm. Nevertheless, the Myo armband is capable of detecting 
enough distinct signals with its eight EMG sensors that it can be considered acceptable as 
the EMG modality in the experiment for this thesis. 
       To summarize – the EMG armband chosen for the experiment was justifiable 
although only highly accurate for prosthetic control based on custom algorithms and 
potential targeted muscle re-innervation. The Myo armband is intended to work on the 
lower-arm, which was the location during the experiment. The gestures recognized by the 
default detection algorithm in the Myo armband were used in the experiment. Since the 
Myo armband was used in the intended context, it can be accepted as justifiable as the 
proof-of-concept equipment for EMG input. 
 
5.10 Robustness of BCI 
It is clear from the previous sections that the development of BCI has to improve before it 
can act as a reliable control modality in prosthetics. Studies show that multimodality 
increases the performance of systems (Lalitharatne et al., 2013b), but is BCI making a 
hybrid build robust, or is the other modalities making BCI robust? The former speaks for 
adaptation of BCI; however, the latter might imply that BCI only works well enough 
when combined with other modalities. BCI as a stand-alone modality in prosthetic control 
performs worse compared to EMG signals, and with only a slight increase when 
combined together (Lalitharatne et al., 2013b). Research in best practice for prosthetic 
control are still on going and until a standard or shared model for correct BCI adaptation 
in prosthetic control are established, the use of multimodality with BCI should be used 
with caution. In the future, BCI could be the only means of interaction with a prosthetic 
device and thus become identical to how we control a biological arm. This solely relies on 
BCI’s ability to perform the same, or better, level of control than the current state of the 
art with EMG signals. Implementation of brain signals as direct control to the bionic arm 
would remove the constraints of control – possibly enhance the individuals capabilities 
beyond that of a biological limb. 
==PAGE:72==
 
 
61 
 
       To summarize – BCI as a control modality in prostheses needs to be reliable enough 
for it to enhance the hybrid control scheme. It should be unambiguous that the BCI offers 
a robustness to control and that BCI enhances the overall performance of the system 
without constraining the user in more ways. When BCI can provide a reliable level of 
control, the control scheme for prosthetics might shift to single modality again with BCI 
as the only control modality.

Chapter 6 
Conclusion 
 
 
The conclusions derived from the present study are highlighted in this chapter. 
Multimodality in a hybrid control scheme for prosthetic arms exceeds the number of 
distinct grips made possible by single modality control from EMG. With limited training, 
the participants were able to access six different grips, which is more than the i-limb from 
Touch Bionics. The learning curve suggests that by extensive training, users will be able 
to access numerous grips, thus eliminating the need for sequencing between grips. This is 
done through independent movement in the hybrid build, where each grip is pre-defined 
with a distinct EEG and EMG signal. The experiment showed a high overall success rate 
when moving the prosthetic arm of 91 %. The response rate however, was not ideally for 
prosthetic control with an overall mean time of 16.89 seconds. Further researches in 
reducing the response rate are needed before multimodality in prosthetic control is 
feasible.  
Several factors regarding the overall feasibility when adapting BCI as a modality in 
prosthetic control also has to be considered: 
 EEG as an enhancing technology in prosthetic control should be concealed to 
avoid unnecessary stigmatization. 
 Adaptation of enhancing technologies in conjunction with assistive technologies 
requires that the overall responsiveness of the system is maintained. 
 Selection criteria on individual basis to ensure compliance with the offered 
technology – especially when that technology is BCI. 
 Distinction between extension and incorporation. Future prosthetics has to be 
incorporation rather than an extension and rely on natural decoding rather than 
distinct cognitive thoughts as input. 
 Necessity of dynamic decoding algorithms to adjust for mental states throughout 
the day as well as general adjustment. 
 Acknowledgement of a learning curve in BCI control. A sated learning curve has 
to be accomplished before BCI can be deemed reliable. 
 Shared autonomy framework to obtain a higher degree of control. Incorporation of 
sensors in the control scheme to aid in determining the correct grip. Controlling a 
==PAGE:75==
 
 
64 
 
prosthetic device will become more reliable when passive sensors work together 
with active control input such as EEG and EMG. 
 BCI needs to make a control scheme robust and not the other way around. If 
highly accurate BCI controls are accomplished in the future, then BCI might 
become the only modality in prosthetic control.  
In conclusion – multimodality can, with enough training, access more grips and remove 
sequencing in prosthetic control. The overall feasibility of hybrid EEG-EMG-based 
control scheme for prosthetic devices relies on important factors which all aids in making 
BCI in a hybrid scheme possible.

11. Test protocol – control group 
Test protocol for Hybrid EEG-EMG-based control scheme for prosthetic 
devices 
Version: 1.1 
Author: Martin Søes Johansen, master’s student 
Approved by: Sadasivan Puthusserypady, supervisor 
Introduction 
This test plan describes dependencies, preparation, test cases and post experiment activities to 
be undertaken for Hybrid EEG-EMG-based control scheme for prosthetic devices. 
Purpose 
To validate EMG and EEG in a hybrid build for controlling a prosthetic arm. 
Furthermore, to verify that each component of the software is performing the expected output. 
2. Testing objectives 
An alpha test was performed prior to the execution of the test plan. 
Testing type: Beta. 
General scope: Whole product in environment. 
Opacity: Black box. 
Who does it: Independent tester outside the developer team. 
3. Preparation  
Preparation of the EMG and EEG technology prior to the experiment. Will approximately take 40-
45 minutes. 
3.1 EMG training 
Unit to test: Myo armband. 
Assumptions: Subject can access all four gestures used within 10 minutes of training. 
Training environment: Development page from Thalmic Labs. 
Expected result: 90% accumulated success rate when asked to access the gestures. 
Training method: Visual and haptic feedback when performing a correct gesture. Subject will be 
asked to access all four gestures at least 10 times for each. 
 
3.2 EEG training 
Unit to test: EPOC headset.

second intervals) for each 15min session up to 20 times for each cognitive state. Training will
