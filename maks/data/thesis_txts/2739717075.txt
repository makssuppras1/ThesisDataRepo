Abstract
This thesis presents the design, simulation, and analysis of a Primary Parallel Isolated Boost
Converter (PPIBC) for application in electric and fuel cell vehicles. The choice of the PPIBC
topology was decided on its capacity to manage high input currents at the same time as produc-
ing better efficiency and equal current distribution through the circuit. Experimental simulations
were performed to confirm the reliability of the theoretical models, focusing on the switching
behavior of the converter, the ripple current of the inductor, and the performance of the trans-
former. Idealized component models offered valuable guidance for the design, therefore laying
the groundwork for future practical implementation.
An important groundbreaking component of this project is the intended use of Gallium
Nitride (GaN) transistors, that offer great switching properties and thermal efficiency in com-
parison to conventional MOSFETs. Future research will focus on this topic to try to improve
converter efficiency further. Simulation results show the proposed PPIBC design fulfills the re-
quirements for high-power DC-DC conversion, making it suitable for electric and fuel cell vehicle
applications.
Furthermore, it also lays the foundation for the experimental setup and later creation of a
printed circuit board (PCB) for the converter. The next steps will focus on the transformer op-
timization, using actual GaN transistor models in simulation, and building a physical prototype
to confirm the converter’s performance in real-world scenarios.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
As a consequence of globalization, growing urbanization and industrialization, and the con-
sequent rise in the number of passenger vehicles, the modern society is extremely reliant on
transportation methods that are based on fossil fuels.
But because there are only so many
resources available, the vehicles of the future will have to be emission-free and powered by re-
newable energy sources. For the purpose of lowering the amount of energy that is consumed,
the efficiency needs to be improved or optimized. This is the only method that can be utilized
to sustain a high level of individual mobility over an extended period of time.[1] Over the course
of the last few decades, these technologies have made remarkable developments; ever since then,
car manufacturers have been concentrating their efforts on developing vehicles that are powered
by electric electric motors. One solution that shows promise in this situation is the fuel cell
vehicle. Isolated DC-DC converters are utilized in order to establish a connection between a
high-voltage battery for the fuel cell stack and the DC bus. [2]
An example of a high-efficiency converter that is able to handle high input currents while
maintaining balanced current distribution and improved reliability is the Primary-Parallel Iso-
lated Boost Converter, also known as the PPIBC. The integration of Gallium Nitride (GaN)
transistors into converters like these results in improved performance, including a lower on-
resistance, higher switching speeds, and superior thermal performance. As a result, these con-
verters are ideal for applications that require high efficiency [3].
Efficiency improvements in DC-DC converters, such as GaN transistors, have pushed con-
verter efficiencies over 95%, reducing energy losses and enabling faster charging [4]. GaN tran-
sistors can improve converter efficiency by up to 10% compared to silicon-based alternatives.
In conclusion, the transition to electric vehicles is supported by advancements in technology,
particularly in DC-DC converters. Efficient, high-performance converters like the PPIBC with
GaN transistors are crucial for meeting the growing demand for EVs and ensuring the reliability
and efficiency of charging infrastructures.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.2
Project Background
Efficient and reliable DC-DC converters are essential for electric vehicle (EV) charging sys-
tems, especially in fuel-cell-based vehicles where the insufficient output voltage from the fuel
cell must be increased to levels suitable for battery charging. Although conventional boost con-
verters are simple and economical, they frequently encounter high switching losses and thermal
management difficulties when operating at high power levels. These issues can lead to a decrease
in overall efficiency and reliability [5].
In order to address these difficulties, the Primary Parallel Isolated Boost Converter (PPIBC)
topology has been chosen for this project because of its capacity to manage large currents at
low input voltages with a higher efficiency. The proposed design results in improved efficiency
and dependability, making it especially suitable for high-power applications in electric vehicle
chargers [6].
Furthermore, the integration of Gallium Nitride (GaN) transistors into the PPIBC topology
offers significant improvements over traditional silicon-based transistors. GaN transistors fea-
ture lower on-resistance, faster switching capabilities, and better thermal performance, which
collectively contribute to reduced switching losses and improved thermal management. These
advantages make GaN-based converters more efficient and compact, aligning with the project’s
goals to develop a highly efficient and reliable DC-DC converter for EV charging applications
[3].

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.3
Topology
The design of the PPIBC integrates several parallel power stages on the primary side to
evenly distribute the input current, so minimizing the current strain on constituent components
and enhancing the overall reliability of the system.
An essential characteristic of the PPIBC is the addition of transformers on the secondary
side, which serve as a galvanic isolation between the input and output stages.
By isolating
the high-power input from critical output loads, this isolation is necessary to improve safety.
The parallel configuration of the main stages guarantees effective current stability and less heat
dissipation, making it especially well-suited for high-power applications [8].
One significant benefit of the PPIBC is the ability to utilize Gallium Nitride (GaN) tran-
sistors. The selection of these transistors is based on their electrical characteristics, including
reduced on-resistance and accelerated switching operations in comparison to conventional silicon-
based transistors. The outcome is a noticeable reduction in switching losses and better thermal
efficiency. The reduced switching losses facilitated by GaN transistors directly contribute to
the improved efficiency of the converter, a critical factor in applications requiring compact and
high-efficiency solutions [9].

1.4
Delimitation
This project focuses on the design, simulation, and validation of the PPIBC for EV chargers.
The scope includes the selection and optimization of components, thermal management, and
efficiency analysis. However, it does not cover the experimental set-up, detailed design of the
fuel cell or battery management systems.
Additionally, the detailed design and modeling of the fuel cell system, the development and
optimization of battery management systems and the reliability testing and lifetime analysis of
the converter components are outside the scope of this project.

2.1.1
Stages of the circuit
The two principal stages function simultaneously, using the same control signals. The pri-
mary switches S1–S4 are hard switched and are operated in pairs S1–S2 and S3–S4. The driving
signals exhibit a 180° phase shift. In order to guarantee a switch overlap and a continuous
current path for the boost inductor current, the duty cycle D of the switch transistor needs to
be set above 50%.
Stage 1 (T1)
• Switches: All switches Q1 −Q8, are on, allowing the inductor L1andL2 to charge.
• Diodes: All rectifier diodes D1, D2, D3, D4 are off, preventing current flow in the trans-
former secondary winding.
• Transformer: Transformers T1 and T2 are inactive as no current flows through the primary
or secondary windings.
• Inductor: Inductors L1 and L2 are active, storing energy as current flows through them.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
5.1
Conclusion
This project has successfully completed the design, simulation, and analysis of the Primary
Parallel Isolated Boost Converter (PPIBC). The selection of the converter topology was based on
its capacity to manage high input currents, leading to improved efficiency and even distribution
of the current. The usage of Gallium Nitride (GaN) transistors provides improved switching
speed and thermal control, which is is the goal with the objectives of developing a very efficient
DC-DC converter suitable for electric vehicle chargers. In addition, the PPIBC topology has
been studied in the framework of fuel cell vehicles, where the highest priority is given on achieving
high efficiency and power density.
During the project, the theoretical models and calculations were verified by a simulation,
showing that the results generated were consistent with the expected theoretical behavior. The
inductor ripple, switching waveforms, and transformer dynamics all matched to the expected
design specifications. The simulations, although using ideal components, provide a strong basis
for future practical implementation. Although the converter demonstrated acceptable outcomes,
reaching an efficiency of 94.73%, shows potential for improvement. More specifically, by opti-
mizing the switching devices and transformer design, it should be possible to further decrease
the losses, and allowing the converter to produce better efficiencies. This would enhance its
potential for challenging high-power applications.
This thesis laid the foundation for the experimental arrangement and future printed circuit
board (PCB) design. It also confirms that the PPIBC topology is a robust solution for efficient
DC-DC conversion in electric vehicle and fuel cell vehicle applications due to its high-power
capabilities.
5.2
Future Work
Several aspects of the converter design can be optimized and further developed in future
work:
• Component Optimization: While the component designs in this project fulfilled the per-
formance criteria, further improvement is necessary to minimize losses and enhance effi-
ciency, particularly when dealing with shifting load conditions. More consideration should
be given to the reduction of core and copper losses of the transformer, together with the
guarantee of improved thermal performance during continuous operation.
• GaN Transistor Simulation: Due to technical constraints, these simulations were carried
out using optimal MOSFET models.
In future research, it is suggested to re-run the
simulations using actual GaN transistor models in order to precisely evaluate their influence
on the efficiency of the converter.
This analysis will offer a further understanding of

switching losses, thermal efficiency, and overall system effectiveness in the setting of GaN
transistor technologies.
• Experimental Setup: The work made in this project provide a foundation for building
an experimental configuration. Following steps should include building a prototype based
on the simulation findings and theoretical analyses. Comparing of the physical model’s
performance with simulation results is required to verify the design’s suitability in real-
world scenarios.
• PCB Design: Future work includes also the design of a printed circuit board (PCB) for
the PPIBC converter.
In order to minimise parasitic effects, decrease electromagnetic
interference (EMI), and guarantee efficient heat dissipation, especially when operating at
high power levels, it is essential to carefully optimize the layout.
By addressing these areas in future work, the converter can be further optimized for efficiency
and reliability, making it a strong option for use in next-generation electric vehicle and fuel cell
vehicle charging systems.
