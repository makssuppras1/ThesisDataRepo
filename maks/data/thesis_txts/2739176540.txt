Abstract 
Riparian vegetation plays a crucial role in shaping water flow dynamics and the ecosystem 
health of rivers. Naturally varied vegetation patterns, organized into distinct patches, intro-
duce uncertainties in hydraulic modeling and management practices. This study investigated 
the drag and velocity distribution of flexible woody vegetation patches with understory 
grass arranged in different configurations of shape (elongated and compact) and density 
(Leaf Area Index (LAI) of 1.0-2.4). A new force instrumentation setup was developed to 
measure patch-scale drag forces, while an Acoustic Doppler Velocimeter (ADV) captured 
point-based velocities. Drag measurements were conducted for mean velocities of 0.1-0.6 
m/s and at two relative submergences (H/hd = 1-2, where H is the water level and hd is the 
patch deflected height), representative of conditions in low-land rivers. Velocity measure-
ments focused on H/hd = 1 and a mean velocity of 0.25 m/s.  
The patch shape significantly impacted the patch-scale drag (Fp) for H/hd = 1, with high-
density elongated patches exhibiting higher drag than the compact counterparts. The varia-
bility in Fp was lowered by scaling with the horizontal ground area of the patch, signifying 
the importance of the areal extent. The elongated patches showed steeper streamwise veloc-
ity gradients between vegetated and non-vegetated areas, which coincides with the higher 
Fp of this patch shape. Lateral velocity distribution showed high sensitivity to the patch 
position, affecting near-wall velocity and wake flow position. Low-density patches exhib-
ited high wake flow variance, indicating induced flow through the patch. Variance in Fp 
between patch densities corresponded to LAI differences and was reduced by scaling with 
leaf area, aligning with prior studies. 
High-density patches revealed a reconfiguration threshold (> 0.5 m/s) for H/hd = 1, sug-
gesting that the patchy structure influence plant characteristics. Understory grass contribu-
tion to bulk drag depended on relative submergence and velocity, with the highest contri-
bution at H/hd = 2 and low velocity, indicating it should not be neglected.   
The patches were arranged in a staggered pattern along the reach, where the approaching 
velocities between patches of the same stream side decreased by 14-21 % from upstream to 
downstream. In contrast, the approaching velocity increased between patches of opposite 
stream side, indicating that the staggered pattern confines flow paths. 
Future studies should explore a broader range of patch shapes and densities, as the 
patchy effect induces high variability in flow and drag, which is crucial for developing ef-
fective nature-based solutions and improving river management strategies. 
Keywords Hydrodynamics, riverine vegetation, patch-scale drag, flow field

1 Introduction 
 
1.1 Background 
 
Riparian environments are highly dominated by vegetation, which is intrinsically 
linked with flooding events, enabling new growth, removal, and restructuring of ge-
omorphic features (Naiman et al., 2005). In return, the vegetation also acts as a nat-
ural barrier to water flow, altering the mean flow velocity, introducing turbulence, 
and increasing flow resistance (Errico et al., 2018). However, traditional water man-
agement has viewed vegetation primarily as an obstacle to be removed to facilitate 
water transport (Errico et al., 2018; Gurnell, 2014).  
 
Despite these challenges, riparian vegetation offers numerous benefits, including im-
proving water quality and mitigating flooding in urban areas. Woody vegetation is a 
significant component of the riparian environment and can become a prominent fea-
ture once established (Naiman et al., 2005). The vegetation can be distinctively or-
ganized in patches of the same species or a mix (Schoelynck et al., 2012; Naiman et 
al., 2005). Many studies have focused on the patchy impact of aquatic vegetation 
patches (Afzalimehr et al., 2021; Savio et al., 2023) or rigid cylinders (White & Nepf, 
2008), which have highly different characteristics compared to flexible woody veg-
etation (Caroppi et al., 2021; Aberle & Järvelä, 2015; Aberle & Järvelä, 2013).   
 
Riverine vegetation has also been linked to increased biodiversity, making them cru-
cial indicators for riparian management strategies (Forio et al., 2020). Planning veg-
etated channels and riparian environments is considered part of the umbrella term 
Nature-based Solutions (NbS), which is increasingly adapted (Rowiński et al., 2018; 
Huttunen et al., 2024). The EU Water Framework Directive underscores the im-
portance of managing and enhancing aquatic ecosystems and riparian environments, 
requiring member states to develop and implement solutions to protect and improve 
these areas (Directive 2000/60/EC). Consequently, river restoration practices are 
evolving, integrating eco-hydraulics and ecological engineering insights to develop 
functional flow conditions (Nikora, 2010; Rowiński et al., 2022).  
 
Reliable estimates of vegetative flow resistance can significantly improve modeling 
results and thereby, the predictions of the performance of NbS, yet this area has been 
underrepresented in past research (Caroppi & Järvelä, 2022; Lightbody & Nepf, 
2006; Nepf et al., 2007). Vegetative resistance is identified as one of the most critical 
parameters in river morphology modeling (Västilä & Järvelä, 2018). Therefore, ad-
vancing modeling tools that bridge ecological functioning and engineering practices 
is essential. Vegetative resistance can be estimated from measurements of bulk flow 
and direct drag forces. Studies of vegetative flow resistance using simplified vegeta-
tion representation have provided valuable insight into basic flow structures and the 
importance of vegetative drag (White & Nepf, 2008). However, these simplified 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
structures often do not capture the complexity of natural vegetation, which can bend 
and adapt to varying flow conditions (Aberle & Järvelä, 2013). The bulk flow re-
sistance of rivers increases with additional vegetative drag forces, where foliage of 
woody vegetation has a significant contribution (Järvelä, 2002). The effect of woody 
vegetation patch shapes on flow resistance is gaining increased attention (Bae et al., 
2024; Ji et al., 2023) where the governing parameters are still to be found.  
 
Furthermore, the relative submergence influences flow resistance, which refers to 
the ratio of water level to the vegetation height (H/hc) (Aberle & Järvelä, 2013). Re-
search on woody vegetation has mainly focused on limited to emergent, H/hc < 1, 
and just-submerged conditions, H/hc ≈ 1 (Västilä & Järvelä, 2014; Västilä & Järvelä, 
2018). The flow field changes significantly as the water level exceeds plant height, 
where two layers of different velocities will develop (Sukhodolov & Sukhodolova, 
2010; Nepf, 2012).  
 
The density of vegetation is increasingly defined by the leaf area index (LAI) and 
used in vegetative flow resistance models (Järvelä, 2004; Västilä & Järvelä, 2014; 
Box et al., 2021; Box et al., 2022). The application of the parameter has much po-
tential as it can be estimated using remote sensing (Jalonen et al., 2015). There is a 
need for further research and validation for a broader range of LAI to develop more 
robust LAI-based hydraulic models (Box et al., 2022). Furthermore, natural vegeta-
tion density, size, and shape variability introduce uncertainties when applying plant 
characteristics in modeling (Vandenbruwaene et al., 2011).  
 
Vegetation alters flow velocity near the vegetation and in the main channel, which 
controls energy, momentum (Caroppi et al., 2019), and the transport of compounds 
(Västilä et al., 2022). Measuring flow velocity with adequate accuracy and resolution 
in experimental hydraulics is crucial. The drag forces of vegetation reduce the mo-
mentum of the flow, creating a wake region dominated by lower velocity and turbu-
lence, with approximately constant velocity behind the vegetation (Zong & Nepf, 
2011). The proximity of multiple vegetation patches also influences wake structures 
upstream and downstream of a patch (Meire et al., 2014; de Lima et al., 2015), which 
are crucial areas for particle settlement (Hu et al., 2018). The number of patches and 
geometric features influences turbulence and the following recirculation (Afzalimehr 
et al., 2021). However, patchy vegetation is also linked to increased flow outside the 
patch, which leads to erosion (Vandenbruwaene et al., 2011; Meire et al., 2014).   
 
The findings of this study will contribute to the scientific understanding of vegetative 
drag forces of woody vegetation patches and their impact on velocity distribution. 
Additionally, the development of a new measurement methodology will provide a 
valuable tool for future research on patchy vegetated flow.

1.2 Problem Statement, Motivation, and Research Objec-
tives 
 
Riparian environments, characterized by dense and often patchy woody vegetation, 
play a crucial role in river hydraulics by affecting flow resistance and velocity dis-
tributions. Despite the acknowledged significance of vegetative drag forces in shap-
ing these processes, a notable knowledge gap exists concerning the specific impacts 
of flexible woody vegetation patches with varying densities and shapes. This gap 
limits the ability to accurately create models and predict river flow dynamics, which 
is essential for effective river management, ecological restoration, and flood mitiga-
tion. 
 
Additionally, current methodologies for measuring direct vegetative drag forces of 
flexible patchy vegetation are limited, which presents an opportunity for developing 
a robust patch-scale measurement system that can be applied in a controlled labora-
tory setting. Furthermore, the influence of patchy flexible vegetation on flow char-
acteristics remains underexplored, particularly in how these vegetative structures al-
ter the velocity flow field in a laboratory setting. Two main objectives define the 
experimental work conducted in a laboratory flume.  
 
1. Quantify drag generated by flexible woody vegetation patches of different 
densities and shapes.  
Vegetative drag forces are a key element in understanding the impact on 
flow resistance. However, a knowledge gap exists on the drag exerted on 
natural woody patches of various shapes and densities.  
 
2. Describe how natural-like patchy vegetation influences the flow character-
istics, considering implications on experimental designs in a laboratory 
channel.   
An Acoustic Doppler Velocimeter (ADV) provides point-based measure-
ments of flow velocity used to investigate velocity distribution.  
 
A new force measurement system based on a vegetated bottom plate is developed 
and tested to fulfill the first objective. 
 
1.2.1 Scope and Limitations 
The study considers typical lowland conditions characterized by relatively low sub-
mergence levels. Just submerged to fully submerged conditions are investigated, 
H/hd = 1-2, where hd refers to the deflected height of vegetation patches (Nepf, 2011). 
The velocity conditions for the drag force measurements represent lowland flow re-
gimes with low mean flow velocities ranging from 0.05 to 0.7 m/s (Okhravi et al., 
2023; Okhravi et al., 2022). Velocity measurements are conducted for just-sub-
merged conditions (H/hd = 1) and a mean velocity of 0.25 m/s. 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
The vegetation used in the study is morphologically complex, resembling natural 
woody vegetation, and is represented in an idealized, repetitive pattern within the 
laboratory flume. The vegetation densities range from sparse to dense, with an LAI 
of 1.0 and 2.4. However, the idealized representation of vegetation patches does not 
fully capture the complexity of natural settings. Considerations of sediment and bank 
stability cannot be incorporated. Additionally, the range of shapes and densities stud-
ied is limited by the dimensions of the hydraulic flume, which limits the connection 
to the high variation of natural riverine environments.

2 Literature review 
 
Riparian environments, characterized by their distinctive vegetation patterns, are 
critical in maintaining ecological balance and influence hydraulic dynamics in river 
systems (Naiman et al., 2005). The interaction between vegetation and river flow has 
been the subject of extensive study due to its implications for flood management, 
water quality, and biodiversity (Västilä et al., 2021). Understanding the complexities 
of vegetative drag and its impact on flow resistance is essential for developing effec-
tive river management and restoration strategies (Rowiński et al., 2018).  
 
The study of vegetation in riparian environments has improved with contributions 
from both engineering and ecological perspectives. Historically, vegetation was con-
sidered solely a factor affecting hydraulic resistance, as it acts as an obstacle to water 
flow, necessitating its removal to optimize water transport in channels. However, as 
the understanding of eco-hydraulics advanced, the role of vegetation in enhancing 
ecosystem services has been increasingly acknowledged. The benefits of riparian 
vegetation in promoting biodiversity (Forio et al., 2020; Ismail et al., 2018), stabiliz-
ing banks (Gurnell, 2014; Kollmann et al., 1999), and improving water quality 
(Findlay, 1995) have gained increased attention (Rowiński et al., 2018).  
 
Previous experimental research has used idealized plants (rigid) and focused on fully 
developed flow through homogenously distributed canopies (Dupuis et al., 2017; 
White & Nepf, 2008). However, these conditions rarely reflect the characteristics of 
woody vegetation, considering the variation in morphology and biomechanics and 
growing conditions for different species (Västilä & Järvelä, 2018).  
 
 
2.1 Vegetation Properties 
 
The riparian environment consists of various types of vegetation, which differ be-
tween vegetation growing within the aquatic ecosystem and in the floodplain. Typi-
cally, aquatic vegetation is dominated by vascular plants and can be found to be 
completely submerged, partly submerged, or floating vegetation. Meanwhile, the riv-
erine vegetation is dominated by grasses, trees, bushes, and shrubs (Richardson et 
al., 2007). This vegetation type is commonly found along riverbanks and can stabi-
lize stream banks (Kollmann et al., 1999). As flow and environmental conditions 
change, the distribution and zonation of vegetation changes as well. For example, 
the frequency and magnitude of floods have been linked to the growth of vegetation 
(Nilsson & Svedmark, 2002; Naiman et al., 2005). Once vegetation is established, it 
can become a robust element, form islands (Kollmann et al., 1999), and impact the 
morphology of rivers (Van Dijk et al., 2013).  
 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
Plant morphology refers to the structure of the plant, which can be characterized 
through spatial parameters. Standard plant parameters include stem diameter d and 
canopy plant height, hc (Aberle & Järvelä, 2015). Some species have significant fo-
liage, which results in a plant width larger than the stem diameter. One-sided leaf 
and stem area, AL and AS, are key parameters for vegetation density (Västilä et al., 
2013; Jalonen & Järvelä, 2014) and are illustrated in Figure 1.  
 
Vegetation patches refer to plant communities growing in defined spaces and can be 
combinations of species that form irregular mosaics. In flow-vegetation impact stud-
ies, vegetation patches have commonly been identified as rhombus-shaped (Ji et al., 
2023; Västilä et al., 2022; Murray & Paola, 2003; Biggs et al., 2021; Schoelynck et 
al., 2012). A patch is typically characterized by the shape: length, width, and height; 
however, these parameters are subject to change for varying flow conditions (Bae et 
al., 2024). Prior studies have focused on the impact of aquatic vegetation 
(Afzalimehr et al., 2021; Savio et al., 2023; Siniscalchi et al., 2012; Cameron et al., 
2013), where canopy density is sufficiently parameterized by the stem diameter and 
spacing between vegetation (Nepf, 2011). However, this is insufficient for woody 
foliated plants, where the density must account for foliage (Aberle & Järvelä, 2015). 
The density is increasingly defined by LAI, which can be detected through large-
scale image analysis, making it easily applicable in river modeling (Jalonen et al., 
2015). LAI is determined by the ratio of leaf area (AL) to bed unit area (AB). AB is 
defined by the reach section, as illustrated in Figure 1, while the horizontal canopy 
area, AB,p, is defined as the area of the patch from plan view (Ji et al., 2023).

and volumetric blockage (BV) (Ji et al., 2023). 
 
A simulation study on the importance of structural connectivity of riparian environ-
ments found that vegetation coverage controls discharge patterns more than

hydrological connectivity (i.e., areas with attainable flow). However, at lower veloc-
ities, patch configurations and hydrological connectivity both had a high significance 
(Larsen et al., 2017). Wright et al. (2018) discovered through simulation of different 
vegetation patch sizes, density, and coverage, that the hydrological connectivity in-
creases when vegetation coverage is below 40-50 % compared to uniformly distrib-
uted vegetation. A vegetation cover above this results in preferential flow paths, 
where the velocity in unvegetated areas increases. However, these studies do not 
comment on the shape of the patches and spatial position. The effect of dynamic 
vegetation structures remains unclear, as the patches may alter spatially, in size, and 
density (Vandenbruwaene et al., 2011).  
 
The presence of woody vegetation has benefits for water quality, as a study of low 
vegetation cover of woody patches (volumetric coverage of 6-11 %), resulted in a 
reduction of peak concentration of solute (Västilä et al., 2022). Woody vegetation 
buffers along streams have been found to provide trade-offs for climate change mit-
igation and improving water quality (Cole et al., 2020). Furthermore, patches of 
woody vegetation have been found to enhance biodiversity and may play a crucial 
role in managing various stresses of central European streams (Forio et al., 2020).  
 
 
2.2 Flow Resistance of Vegetation 
 
Flow resistance can be estimated indirectly and directly, based on the applied 
method. Indirect measurements of the bulk energy loss can be used to calculate the 
Darcy-Weisbach friction factor f and Manning’s n, typical flow resistance measures. 
Direct measurements of forces exerted by vegetation can be used to estimate drag 
coefficients (Wilson et al., 2008; Schoneboom et al., 2008). The vegetative flow re-
sistance is the effect of drag produced by vegetation, which occurs because of vis-
cous drag along the object's surface and pressure drag from pressure differences sur-
rounding the object (Aberle & Järvelä, 2015). The common drag force equation is 
expressed as:

The total drag of vegetation, FD, is a combination of drag from foliage, FF, and stems, 
FS. The contribution of FF is significant in the total drag for many riparian plant 
species (Västilä & Järvelä, 2014; Aberle & Järvelä, 2013; Wilson et al., 2008). For 
typical floodplain species like willows, foliage accounts for 40 % of total drag (Ar-
manini et al., 2005). However, due to the complex interplay of biomechanical and 
bio-morphological properties, there is no direct relationship between the amount of 
foliage and the drag (Albayrak et al., 2014; Järvelä, 2004). Towing tank experiments 
have shown that foliage contribution is more influential at lower velocities, with 
FF/FD reaching a constant value at higher velocities (Jalonen & Järvelä, 2014). At 
low velocities, foliage drag (FF) can be overestimated using the simple relationship 
FF = FD – FS  (Jalonen & Järvelä, 2014). The original drag equation can be modified 
to accommodate the specific characteristics of vegetation (Järvelä, 2004):

The submergence has a significant impact on the flow field, as the vertical exchange 
reduces for higher submergence (H/hd > 1) (Nepf & Vivoni, 2000). The flow re-

vegetation with increasing submergence, where low mean flow conditions resulted 
in the highest difference due to vegetative reconfiguration (Box et al., 2021).

2.2.1 Patch Scale Drag and Flow Resistance 
The hydraulic resistance of patchy vegetation on reach-scale can be related to the 
blockage factors, BX  and BV, as illustrated and defined in Figure 1. Field data validate 
the relationship between hydraulic resistance and blockage factors for aquatic vege-
tation (Green, 2005; Nikora et al., 2008). Ji et al. (2023) found that even low-density 
patches significantly impact the bulk flow resistance. Specifically, a sparse patch 
with a volumetric blockage factor (BV) of 5 % resulted in 2-3 times the bulk flow 
resistance compared to unvegetated conditions, and this resistance increased fivefold 
when BV was increased to 10 %. This study highlights the importance of the patchy 
effect, but the impact of patch shape and density remains unclear. Bae et al. (2024) 
found that while the blockage factor of emergent artificial willow patches signifi-
cantly influences hydraulic resistance, canopy density is less significant. Addition-
ally, the study revealed that the spatial distribution of patches introduces variation in 
flow resistance as the hydraulic conditions of centerline patches differed signifi-
cantly from near-bank patches (Bae et al., 2024).  
Similarly, Savio et al. (2023) examined how the arrangement (aligned vs. staggered) 
and vegetative coverage of artificial aquatic grass patches influence hydraulic re-
sistance in a hydraulic flume. Their findings indicate that patch coverage signifi-
cantly affects hydraulic resistance, particularly at lower relative submergence. Nota-
bly, patches with a staggered arrangement led to increased hydraulic resistance, high-
lighting the need for further investigation into the spatial distributions.  
Tinoco & Cowen (2013) measured patch scale drag using a novel device designed 
for measuring canopy scale drag. The mechanics of the device derive from the com-
monly used shear plates (Park et al., 2019). The device, referred to as a drag plate, 
consists of a plate mounted on top of a load cell. Rigid cylinders was be fixed on the 
plate, where the measured force was used to estimate the drag coefficient:

of foliated patches. The patch flow blockage CDaD, where a is the frontal area per 
unit canopy, and D is the patch width, is largely affected by foliage for an emergent 
patch of willows. Caroppi et al. (2022) found that the patch blockage decreased by 
60 % because of drag-induced reconfiguration with increasing velocity, while in leaf-
less conditions, the blockage decreased by 45 %.  
 
Present research highlights the complexity of accurately estimating hydraulic re-
sistance in vegetated flows, particularly considering the variable characteristics of 
flexible, foliated vegetation. However, there is a need to bridge the gap between

simplified models and the intricate dynamics of natural vegetation patches to en-
hance the predictability and applicability of flow resistance models. 
 
 
2.3 The Vegetative Flow Field 
 
Vegetation influences the flow properties, such as bulk velocity, turbulence, disper-
sion, and vice versa, resulting in a highly complex, interdependent system. Field in-
vestigation has provided essential knowledge on how flow properties are altered by 
vegetation but has been primarily focused on aquatic plants (Biggs et al., 2021; Su-
khodolov & Sukhodolova, 2010; Cameron et al., 2013).  
 
Vegetation fragments the flow when comparing velocity measurements before and 
after plant growth. Flow affected by vegetation has been classified into three parts 
by Sukhodolov & Sukhodolova (2010): 1) free path, where the flow can become 
fully developed, 2) flow through a patch of vegetation, and 3) upstream blocked free 
path, where the shear layer of the upstream patch impacts the flow. The flow struc-
tures are presented in Figure 2. There is a definitive difference in the hydrodynamic 
processes based on the submergence of the vegetation. Figure 2 displays the velocity 
properties of submerged conditions, H/hc > 1, where hc is the canopy height. While 
for emergent cases, H/hc < 1, the flow properties are dominated by the wake created 
by the upstream vegetation (Aberle & Järvelä, 2015).  
 
 
 
Figure 2: The classification of flow paths of a vegetation patch from upstream to downstream, illus-
trating the streamwise velocity and shear stress. (Graphic from Sukhodolov & Sukhodolova (2010)).  
 
Flow above the vegetation will remain high, while flow passing through the porous 
patch will decrease significantly. The vegetation redistributes the momentum and 
introduces a shear layer, leading to turbulence (Sukhodolov & Sukhodolova, 2010). 
Bed shear and canopy drag also produce turbulence within the patch. The upstream 
blocked free path results from the shear layer formed in the wake of the vegetation 
(Nepf, 2012). The wake is a region of recirculation, followed by a von Karman vor-
tex street, a repeating pattern of swirling vortices caused by separated flow around a 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
blunt object (Chen et al., 2012). The mixing from the vortices allows quick recovery 
of velocity.  
 
Deposition of sediments is likely to occur in the wake region due to lower turbulence 
and velocity (Nepf, 2012; Yu et al., 2021; Schoelynck et al., 2012; Sohrabi et al., 
2023).  The wake length of vegetation, Lw, is much longer compared to one behind a 
solid object. The flow entering the patch (termed the bleed flow) ultimately delays 
the onset of the von Karman vortex, as it also makes up the wake region (Nepf, 2012).  
 
The flow structure between flexible uniformly distributed vegetation depends on 
Reynolds number (Re) and the gap length ratio, hc/Lg. Lower Re numbers result in 
skimming flow, which becomes recirculating flow with increasing Re. The skim-
ming flow produces little to no wake structure, while recirculation flow occurs when 
the shear layer from the canopy top separates and forms a recirculation cell (Folkard, 
2011).  
 
There is a significant difference between velocity fields of rigid objects and flexible 
foliated vegetation. Many papers have investigated rigid cylinders, which largely un-
derestimate canopy shear penetration and efficiency of lateral momentum transport 
when compared to flexible woody vegetation (Caroppi et al., 2021). Furthermore, 
foliage contributes to a stronger mixing of shear layers and higher velocity gradients 
between vegetated and non-vegetated areas compared to leafless conditions (Caroppi 
et al., 2019).  
 
2.3.1 Flow Properties of Patchy Structures 
 
The research area focused on understanding flow fields around vegetation patches is 
predominantly characterized using simplified patch structures by use of rigid cylin-
ders and cylindrical patch shapes (Hu et al., 2018; Zong & Nepf, 2011; Chen et al., 
2012; Taddei et al., 2016). These simplified models are widely employed due to their 
ease of analysis and ability to provide fundamental insights into flow-vegetation in-
teractions. However, they may not fully capture the complexity and variability of 
natural vegetation patches, which often include flexible and heterogeneous elements. 
 
Yu et al. (2021) investigated the impact of elongated simplified patches (stream-
wise), which showed that the wake region is related to the length blockage, CDaLp, 
where a is the frontal area per patch volume. As CDaLp increases, the wake region 
decreases until CDaLp>8, where von Karman vortices will be observed directly be-
hind the patch. The critical patch length can be found from this relation, Lc = 8/CDa, 
so a wake with deposition occurs for a patch length Lp<Lc. If the length is above Lc, 
erosion will occur behind the patch and disable the patch to grow longitudinally.  
 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
Foliage of an emergent patch of woody vegetation decreased the mean flow velocity 
and bed shear stress in the wake by 50 and 70 %, respectively, compared to non-
foliated conditions. The approaching velocity decreased by 5-10 %, and the exit flow 
velocity was 1.1-1.5 times lower between foliated and leafless conditions. The ve-
locity in the unvegetated part of the channel increased for the foliated conditions and 
occurred with a significant distance to the foliated patch, while for the leafless patch, 
it occurred parallel to the patch (Caroppi et al., 2022). The study highlights the im-
portance of foliage for considering seasonal effects and using proper representation 
of patches.  
 
The findings from studies of single patches should be carefully considered, as the 
flow field is intrinsically affected by the presence of other patches. Savio et al. (2023) 
argue that wake formation depends on the streamwise distance between patches. 
Afzalimehr et al. (2021) emphasized that the vertical recirculation along an upstream 
patch edge leads to increased TKE (turbulence kinetic energy) and Reynolds stress 
for the downstream patch.  
 
De Lima et al. (2015) found a similar relationship when patches (with diameter D) 
are in proximity by either lateral (L) or longitudinal (T) distance. When L/D > 5 or 
T/D > 1.5, the patches could be conceived as isolated patches, creating similar wakes. 
If patches were very close (T/D < 1.3), the wake of the patches would merge down-
stream and create a larger ideal growth area. At intermediate distances, the wake or 
area for possible growth would decrease. In this case, the wake of the upstream patch 
would decrease. Yamasaki et al. (2021) found a similar process occurrence, where 
conditions for deposition are increased along the centerline between proximal 
patches consisting of rigid cylinders, where the streamwise position remains a dom-
inating factor.  
 
The ratio between patch diameter and interpatch distance (D/d) of Spartina angelica 
(typical marsh plants) patches affects the flow in between. A ratio of D/d > 0.4-0.7 
implies accelerating flow, which interacts until D/d ≥ 6.7-10, where the accelerated 
flow between the patches becomes suppressed, and the patches will act as one (Van-
denbruwaene et al., 2011). The interpatch distance effect will likely be influenced by 
the species variations of morphology and biomechanics through the ability to divert 
flow and reconfigure but remains uncovered in current literature.

3 Methodology 
 
This study is structured around the experimental procedures, which consist of two 
parts: vegetative drag forces and velocity distributions. Section 3.1 describes the ex-
perimental conditions and the key vegetation characteristics. The following two sec-
tions describe the methodological approach, where Figure 3 illustrates the scale ap-
proach, vegetation properties, and flow conditions for each experiment.  
 
 
Figure 3: Diagram illustrating the experimental setup of the measurement procedures. Scale and 
flow conditions vary between the two procedures, while the patch properties considered remain the 
same and will be presented in detail in Section 3.1.1.  
 
The patch configurations differ between shapes A and B, corresponding to elongated 
and compact shapes. Two density conditions are investigated for shape A, high and 
low density, typical for riparian woody vegetation (Västilä & Järvelä, 2018). The 
detailed composition of the patches is introduced in Section 3.1.1. 
 
 
3.1 Experimental Setup 
 
The experimental runs were conducted in the hydraulic flume of the Environmental 
Hydraulics Lab of Aalto University. The flume is 20.3 m long, 0.6 m wide and 0.8 
m deep. The working station of the present study is from x = 5-12 m. The flume bed 
consists of a steel bottom with a 15 mm thick PVC plate on top to allow the fixing 
of vegetation. Nature-like vegetation is installed in holes within the PVC plate. The 
patchy vegetation setup along the flume is illustrated in Figure 4. A 35 mm plastic 
grass mat is fixed on the PVC plate.  
 
The x, y, and z axes refer to the longitudinal, lateral, and vertical directions, respec-
tively. The origin of the coordinate system is defined with x = 0 at the inlet cross-
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
section of the flume, positive going downstream. y = 0 is the center of the flume, 
with positive values to the stream left. z = 0 is defined at the top of the PVC plate, 
with positive values towards the water surface.  
 
The flume operates with clean-recirculated water using a centrifugal pumping sys-
tem. The water enters the flume from a reservoir, and before entering the flume, a 
dissipation plate and flow straightener minimize turbulence and surface instabilities 
caused by the inlet. The discharge is measured with a magnetic flow meter (0.5 % 
accuracy fs.), and a weir is located at the end of the flume, which is used to control 
the water level. The flume can be titled by an electric-driven tilting system, which is 
used to obtain quasi-uniform conditions. An underground box, used to place load 
sensors for drag measurements, is situated at x = 8.2 m (L 950 mm, W 230 mm, H 
407 mm). A false floor system is constructed for the box that does not obstruct flow 
within the flume.  
 
Drag forces were measured at varying velocity and at a relative submergence of 1-2, 
using two kinds of load cells (0.003-0.02 % accuracy fs.). They were connected to a 
LabVIEW program and measured for 60 s (20 Hz). The key flow parameters for the 
patch configurations are presented in Table 1-3, where Reh is the depth-based Reyn-
olds number calculated as Re = UmH/ν, where the ν  is the kinematic viscosity. Di-
mensionless numbers characterizing flow conditions are commonly used in hydrau-
lic disciplines. However, a characteristic reference of the area of the object, in this 
case, plant material, obstructing the flow path is needed. In vegetated conditions, the 
characteristic area of a plant will depend on flow conditions, and the specific ele-
ments (e.g., stem, branches, and leaves) of the plant change accordingly (Aberle & 
Järvelä, 2013). A depth-based Reynolds number is used in this study, to characterize 
the flow conditions, which are considered turbulent, as Reh > 104.

3.2 Patch Scale and Plant Scale Drag Measurements

the false floor in the flume, as shown in Figure 7. The patch scale drag is measured 
directly using a plate connected to a load sensor, a principle similar to shear plates

ground box. The plate is at the same elevation as the surrounding plates. A gap be-
tween the upstream and downstream plate ensures no other forces act upon it. How-

other plates installed in the flume, the plate has fixing holes for the vegetation and a

grass mat. The plate is placed 9 m downstream of the facility inlet, as seen from

3.3 Velocity Measurements  
 
The ADV Vectrino+ device uses the Doppler effect to measure the velocity of parti-
cles in water. This effect is based on the working principle of interaction between 
sound and particles. As the sound collides with a suspended particle, a wave is 
formed, which is detected and used to determine the flow velocity. The down-look-
ing probe has four receivers centered around a transmitter. It measures particle ve-
locity inside a sampling volume located 5 cm below the receiver, which makes the 
upper 5 cm of the water column unavailable for measurements. The Doppler 
backscattering method introduces Gaussian white noise, which can be caused by par-
ticles interfering within the sampling volume between pulses or turbulences occur-
ring at scales smaller than the measured volume (Jesson et al., 2015). The flow was 
seeded with air bubbles to ensure a high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). The recording 
time for each point was 120 s (200 Hz), which is enough to determine flow charac-
teristics, such as mean velocity (Dupuis et al., 2017).  
 
The raw ADV data was pre-filtered to discard data with SNR and correlation values 
below 15 dB and 70 %, respectively. The data was de-spiked using the software 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
MAJVSA (ver. 1.5.65), an open-source program providing filtering and multiple op-
tions for calculating flow and turbulence properties (Jesson et al., 2015). The phase-
space thresholding filter presented by Goring & Nikora (2002) was used to recognize 
spikes, and a 12-point polynomial was used to calculate replacement data. The phase-
space thresholding filter performed better at locating spikes in the data than the ve-
locity correlation filter (Jessen, 2013).  
 
For each patch configuration, three types of ADV velocity measurements are carried 
out: longitudinal and lateral transects and vertical profiles. The focus area surrounds 
the patch where patch-scale drag is measured, as depicted in Figure 10. Preliminary 
tests were conducted to understand the vertical velocity distribution, where a shear 
layer developed at a relative depth of z/H = 0.35-0.40 (H/hd ≈ 1). The lateral and 
longitudinal transects were measured at the normalized depth of 0.35.

5 Discussion 
 
5.1 Implications of Vegetation Patch Properties on Drag 
Forces and Velocity Distributions 
 
The study of patchy vegetation and its effects on flow dynamics have revealed com-
plex interactions between vegetation and water movement. The findings suggest that 
the patch shape (elongated and compact) and density (LAI = 1.0 and 2.4) signifi-
cantly influence drag forces. Meanwhile, the lateral velocity distribution is highly 
sensitive to the lateral position of the patches and patch shape. 
 
The vegetative patch scale drag showed that low-density patches followed an almost 
linear F-Um relationship for both just-submerged and H/hd ≈ 2 conditions. The linear 
relationship resembles what has been found in studies for both isolated flexible plants 
(Jalonen & Järvelä, 2014; Armanini et al., 2005; Wilson et al., 2008) and drag of 
uniformly distributed plants (Schoneboom et al., 2010). However, for high-density 
patches (LAI = 2.4) at just-submerged conditions, the drag increase was significantly 
lowered for Um > 0.5 m/s. This suggests a reconfiguration limit of the patches is 
reached at high velocity. Uniformly distributed vegetation with similar morphologi-
cal properties showed no reconfiguration limit for Um = 0.1-0.8 m/s (Box et al., 
2021). Other studies of single-plant drag found no distinct decrease in drag reported 
for higher velocities (Armanini et al., 2005; Wilson et al., 2008). The deflected height 
of foliated trees decreases linearly until a velocity of 1.0 m/s, which suggests a re-
configuration limit (Jalonen & Järvelä, 2014). The frontal projected area of vegeta-
tion depends on relative submergence, which has been reported to decrease for ve-
locities above 0.6 m/s at just-submerged conditions, which can account for some 
decrease in the drag of woody vegetation (Västilä et al., 2013). The minimal space 
between plants in the high-density patches could also limit the plant's ability to bend 
and the plants would lean on one another, causing a significant change in patch-scale 
drag.  
 
The patch-scaled drag normalized by the horizontal canopy area, Fp/Ab,p, resulted in 
a lower variability between patch shapes (16 %) compared to no scaling (29 %). Ab,p 
incorporates the effect of the horizontal area, which is significant for the volumetric 
blockage factor. However, for H/hd ≈ 2, the Fp variability between the patch shapes 
was low with no scaling (18 %), indicating that Fp is less influenced by patch shape 
at higher submergences. Higher submergence leads to a lower mixing (Nepf & Vi-
voni, 2000), which inflicts two separate flow paths between the vegetated layer and 
the non-vegetated above. Higher velocities are expected in the non-vegetated layer, 
while flow in the vegetated layer will be significantly lower and could explain the 
lower dependency of patch shape, but was not confirmed with velocity measure-
ments.  
 
==PAGE:49==
49 
 
The patch shape appears to have little impact on the longitudinal flow development, 
where shape B was found to decrease the streamwise velocity most effectively by 
21.0 % between patches on the same streamside, compared to 14-16 % for shape A. 
The patchy setup induced a preferential flow path along the center for all configura-
tions, which was most pronounced for the elongated shapes. The lateral velocity dis-
tribution of shape A revealed high velocity gradients between areas impacted by 
vegetation and non-vegetated areas. The preferential flow of AHD was 25-46 % 
higher than the velocity affected by vegetation. Meanwhile, the difference was 5-16 
% for BHD. The velocity distributions suggest that the longer patches increase the 
streamwise velocity of the preferential flow path. As the vegetative drag depends on 
the velocity term, the lateral velocity distributions corresponds well with the higher 
patch-scale drag of AHD. 
 
Schoneboom et al. (2010) found that the drag of plants arranged in a staggered pat-
tern was higher than in-line plants (1.2 times higher). In the present study, the elon-
gated patches were arranged in a staggered pattern (AHD), while the compact 
patches (BHD) were aligned in rows, as depicted in Figure 5. The patch-scale drag 
of AHD was 1.5 times higher than BHD at just-submerged conditions. The staggered 
pattern could be governing, but it is difficult to conclude definitively, as multiple 
factors influence the drag. Many studies have uncovered that the magnitude of flow 
paths is determined by the gap between vegetation (Folkard, 2011), patch diameter 
and interpatch distance (Vandenbruwaene et al., 2011), and lateral or longitudinal 
distance between patches (de Lima et al., 2015). Further research is recommended to 
conclude on the governing parameters leading to differences in patch-scale drag and 
lateral velocity distributions between patch shapes. The patch reconfiguration should 
also be investigated and parameterized to uncover the patch dependence on velocity. 
 
The sheltering effect of the patch was investigated by measuring the drag of a single 
plant (FSP) positioned at the downstream end of the patch (for the exact position, 
refer to Figure 5). The FSP/Fp ratio revealed an increased shelter effect with increas-
ing velocity. FSP/Fp of BHD was 68-83 % higher than AHD at the lowest velocity, 
suggesting that the BHD patch structure, with smallest longitudinal plant spacing, 
allowed a lower sheltering effect. This would contradict findings of Liu & Wang 
(2024), as they found that a smaller longitudinal spacing between rigid elements re-
sult in a higher sheltering effect. The difference is likely caused by the flexibility of 
the plants, as the single-plant drag can be increased by upstream plants leaning with 
the flow. However, the sheltering effect becomes similar between the patch shapes 
at higher velocities for both relative submergence conditions.  
 
The density of patches revealed a connection to the patch-scale drag, as the Fp of 
AHD was 2.4-2.6 times higher than ALD, resembling the difference in LAI. Previous 
studies have found Fp/AL is an appropriate reference measure for characteristic areas 
of woody vegetation (Västilä et al., 2013; Jalonen & Järvelä, 2014; Aberle & Järvelä, 
==PAGE:50==
50 
 
2013). Normalizing the patch-scale drag by the leaf area suggested that AL is an ap-
propriate scaling parameter to compare patches of various densities, as the variability 
in Fp/AL between high- and low-density drag was relatively low at 0.4-16 % for H/hd 
≈ 1 and 8-49 % for H/hd ≈ 2.  
 
The patch-scale drag was highly dependent on patch shape for just-submerged con-
ditions and less for H/hd ≈ 2. The friction factor of uniformly distributed woody veg-
etation decreased when H/hd increased from 1 to 2 (Box et al., 2021). Furthermore, 
the ratio of friction factors between H/hd ≈ 2 and H/hd ≈ 1 displayed a linear increase 
with velocity regardless of density. Similarly, the patch-scale drag ratio between the 
submergence conditions showed an overall increase with velocity, but the ratio also 
showed fluctuations and should be confirmed for a wider range of shapes and densi-
ties. 
 
The high-density patches of different shape exhibited χ varying from -0.8 to ≈ 0, 
which signified that the patch-scale drag increased approximately linearly with ve-
locity but showed significant error between modeled and measured forces, especially 
for the just-submerged cases. The low-density patches exhibited χ between -0.9 to -
0.5 and low RSS values, which signifies a closer linear force dependence on velocity. 
The drag coefficient was highly dependent on drag at low velocity (Um < 0.15 m/s), 
as CDχ decreased by 50 % when these were excluded. Due to the limit in reconfigu-
ration of the density patches at H/hd ≈ 1, it should be investigated further the velocity 
range of applying Equation 3. The patch-scale drag parameters are not appropriate 
to compare with single plant drag parameters.  
 
The contribution of understory grass to the bulk drag varied with submergence lev-
els, ranging from 2-17 % for H/hd ≈ 1, where the contribution generally increased 
with increasing velocity. Meanwhile, for H/hd ≈ 2, the contribution varied between 
5-24 % and was highest for the lowest velocity (0.1 m/s), while at higher velocities, 
the contribution remained the same. The highest contribution was found for the low-
density configuration, ALD. Box et al. (2021) found that the friction of understory 
grass contributes between 2-28 % to the bulk friction of woody vegetation uniformly 
distributed, which is in a similar range of the drag. However, the drag of understory 
grass is measured for conditions without the patchy woody vegetation, which does 
not produce a comparable flow field.  
 
The presence of vegetation patches may enhance erosion in the main channel (Van-
denbruwaene et al., 2011) while promoting sediment settling in the wake areas (Hu 
et al., 2018) and improve water quality (Västilä et al., 2022). The deposition leads to 
the elongation of patches over time (Chen et al., 2012; Zong & Nepf, 2011; Meire et 
al., 2014), increasing volumetric blockage and eventually resulting in a more uniform 
distribution of vegetation (Yamasaki et al., 2021). The studied patches all had a char-
acteristic wake feature, even though the low density allowed higher exit velocity and 
==PAGE:51==
51 
 
disturbed wake formation. However, this study only considered streamwise velocity 
distribution, while turbulent properties are essential to understanding the flow pat-
terns' momentum flux and underlying mechanisms (Caroppi et al., 2019).  
 
Even with significant advances in models predicting drag or friction of vegetation 
(Luhar & Nepf, 2013; Västilä & Järvelä, 2014) and an improved understanding of 
plant properties (Västilä & Järvelä, 2018; Järvelä, 2004), there is still much to dis-
cover, before definite generalizations can be achieved (Rowiński et al., 2022). The 
continued research in this area will help reduce uncertainties in modeling, which can 
affect flooding potentials while improving the knowledge of managing riverine en-
vironments. More effective river conservation, restoration, and management strate-
gies can be developed by deepening the understanding of these complex interactions, 
ultimately contributing to preserving these vital ecosystems (Rowiński et al., 2022; 
Rowiński et al., 2018). 
 
 
5.2 Experimental Challenges in Patchy Vegetation Studies 
 
The influence of nature-like patchy vegetation on flow characteristics has significant 
implications for experimental designs. As presented in the methodology chapter, the 
drag measured by the drag plate effectively reflects the density of the vegetation, as 
the drag of twelve plants uniformly distributed is approximately half for six plants. 
However, the patch-scale drag normalized by the leaf area was significantly higher 
than the values reported by Box et al. (2021) for similar leaf areas and uniformly 
distributed plants, which was measured with single load cells. However, patches of 
vegetation alter the flow structure differently compared to uniformly distributed veg-
etation, but the detailed difference is outside the scope of this project.  
 
The streamwise velocity distribution investigation revealed expected flow develop-
ment similar to those observed in other studies of patchy structures (e.g., Sukhodolov 
& Sukhodolova, 2010; Yu et al., 2021). However, the flow development largely de-
pended on the characteristics of patch configurations. The wake flow tended to be 
relatively short due to rapid re-acceleration. Yu et al. (2021) found that the length of 
the wake of emergent patches is related to the length-related flow blockage (CDaLp), 
where an increase in density or length results in a longer wake flow length. However, 
AHD had the highest estimated CDaLp but did not produce the longest wake. The 
wake flow is likely affected by multiple patch parameters, as an example the lateral 
location of the wake flow was influenced by patch shape and difference in lateral 
patch position (Figure 21). Västilä et al. (2022) found that alternating patches induce 
higher velocities in the non-vegetated parts of the channel and along the bank, as 
compared to centerline patches, which coincides with the significant differences in 
lateral velocity distributions of AHD.  
 
==PAGE:52==
52 
 
Furthermore, Bae et al. (2024) found that the flow resistance of a bank-side patch of 
woody vegetation was lower compared to a centerline patch for low flow velocity 
(Um = 0.3-0.5 m/s). This would suggest that the drag of stream right and left patches 
alter for AHD and ALD, highlighting the advantage of indirect flow resistance 
measures. This sensitivity to spatial patch position also highlights the need for care-
ful lateral patch placement in experimental setups. Bae et al. (2024) also provides 
essential reflections on how patchy structures are sensitive to morphodynamic dif-
ferences in channel geometry, heterogeneity in water levels and localized velocities, 
as many lowland rivers are characterized by non-uniform flow (Okhravi et al., 2023).  
 
Additionally, the flow paths generated by the staggered patchy structure along the 
reach (Figure 3) are confined by the stream side location, where the flow develops 
parallel. This becomes evident when comparing changes between upstream and 
downstream transects of the same and opposite stream sides. The longitudinal tran-
sects displayed a decrease in approaching velocity along the reach (Figure 18). 
Meanwhile, the lateral transects of alternating stream sides showed an increase in 
approaching velocity (Figure 19). The experimental design with patchy vegetation 
remains challenging, as the upstream flow paths are intrinsically linked with the pres-
ence of downstream patches (Afzalimehr et al., 2021).  
 
While laboratory channel experiments with patchy flexible vegetation offer valuable 
insights into flow characteristics that resemble field studies, the patchiness intro-
duces distinct flow structures, which should be considered when relating this to nat-
ural environments. Future studies on flow fields of vegetation patches could benefit 
from incorporating more diverse patch arrangements and variable channel geome-
tries, as these are intrinsically linked.

6 Conclusion 
 
Experimental flume investigations were used to investigate the hydraulic effects of 
patches of flexible woody vegetation, which are prominent features of riverine envi-
ronments. The vegetation alters the flow field in a higher degree, compared to aquatic 
vegetation, which has been the focus of prior studies. Two methods were applied, 
direct measures of drag forces at a range of velocities and just submerged conditions 
to low relative submergence (H/hd ≈ 1-2), and an An Acoustic Doppler Velocimeter 
(ADV) for investigation of streamwise velocity distribution at H/hd ≈ 1.  
 
A novel instrumentation was developed to measure patch-scale drag, revealing sig-
nificant variations in forces between patch shape and density. The patch shape is 
significant for just-submerged conditions, where the patch-scale drag of the elon-
gated, high-density patch was higher than the compact, high-density patch. The var-
iability in forces was significantly lowered by scaling with the horizontal canopy 
area, which signifies the importance of the patch’s spatial extend. This correlates 
with other studies of foliated woody vegetation patches, as the vegetative friction 
factor largely depends on blockage factors. 
 
The higher patch-scale drag of elongated patches, coincides with the velocity varia-
bility between areas impacted by vegetation and non-vegetated areas. The preferen-
tial flow path significantly increased and affected the approaching velocity. The lat-
eral velocity distribution revealed significant sensitivity to the lateral position of the 
patches, altering the magnitude of velocity near the flume wall and the wake position 
downstream of the patches. Higher velocities downstream of the low-density patches 
produced significant variance in the wake flow formation. 
 
Low- and high-density patch-scale drag resembled the difference in LAI (Leaf area 
index) independent of relative submergence. The drag variability between the den-
sity configurations was significantly lowered by scaling with AL, which confirms that 
variance in density is largely due to difference in foliage.  
 
The patch-scale drag decreased between just submerged to low submergence, which 
coincides with the relationship of vegetative friction factors and submergence of uni-
formly distributed woody vegetation. However, a reconfiguration limit of high-den-
sity patches was observed for just-submerged conditions at high velocities. Uni-
formly distributed vegetation does not display a similar threshold, which indicates 
that the high-density patchy structures play a significant role in reconfiguration.  
 
The drag of understory grass was found to contribute most for H/hd ≈ 2 at low veloc-
ity to the total bulk drag, which highlights that it should not be neglected as done in 
some prior studies.  
 
==PAGE:54==
54 
 
The staggered patch arrangement along the reach revealed alternating flow paths be-
tween stream sides. The velocity development between patches of the same stream 
side did not develop similarly to flow paths of different stream sides, which suggests 
that the flow paths are dictated by the patch arrangement, which is an important con-
sideration for experimental design.  
 
The significance of the patch shape for just submerged conditions highlights the im-
portance of expanding the knowledge of patchy structures of woody vegetation. Fur-
ther research on the patchy structures is recommended to understand the governing 
parameters of patch-scale drag and variance in velocity distributions. The findings 
emphasize the complex interactions between patchy vegetation and river hydraulic 
processes. Understanding these dynamics is essential for effective riverine planning 
and conservation in these vital ecosystems.
