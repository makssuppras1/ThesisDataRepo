Preface 
This thesis is submitted as one of the requirements for the degree Master of Fire Safety (MFS) at the 
Technical University of Denmark, DTU located at Kongens Lyngby. The project is credited for 15 ECTS points 
and was conducted from December the 01.12.2020 to April the 30.04.2021 under the supervision of 
Associate Professor Frank Markert. The project attempts to improve the fire safety of the nursing home - 
Plejehjem Gammelgaarden, according to the requirements of the FN-Curves dictated by the BR18 code. 
This is performed using both Classical and Dynamic Risk Assessment to stress test the soot yield limitations 
based on different kinds of polyurethane foam and active fire barriers located in an essential egress 
pathway.

Abstract 
In 2018 the handling of a project related to the fire documentation was privatized and is now in the hands 
of certified fire engineers related to the corresponding Fire Class depending on complexity. One of the 
reasons for this change was to streamline the design and control phase so the certifier is personally 
responsible for having built the fire safety according to the Bygningsreglement 2018 (BR18 Code). Before 
this change each Municipality had degrees of fire safety interpretations and was also able to give 
dispensations to building projects independently if they deemed it deemed it safe and secure. Several of 
these dispensations were given to nursing homes and over the last few years there has been several lethal 
fires in nursing homes which occurred as a direct result of these dispensations that deviated from the 
building code of said time. After a lethal fire in a nursing home in 2019 the Minister of Housing and Trafic - 
Ole Birk Olesen, has removed the possibility of giving dispensations and mandated the Municipalities to 
examine, whether their nursing homes live up to the legislation or not [1].  
The current case will be the nursing home – Gammelgården built in 1977, which received a dispensation 
from implementing sprinkling into the project which severely reduced safety level of the building as 
sprinkling is a major active fire barrier to control a potential fire [2]. Another concern is that the apartments 
are connected to an atrium which is their only escape way which in fire community is known as an egress 
pathway. This pathway contains flammable polyurethane foam couches which is a violation of the 
requirements of BR18, as the egress pathways according to BR18 only tolerates CMHR1 furniture in these 
locations.  
The thesis will introduce different solutions to satisfy the fire safety level of the building according to BR18. 
This safety level will be calculated and visualized by using FN-curves to create an acceptance line. The 
solutions involve 7 different kinds of polyurethane foam with different levels of theoretical soot yields. The 
polyurethane foam will be a mixture of CMHR and non-CMHR polyurethane foam in couches, which if 
mixed with the correct amount of active fire barriers could potentially allow for furniture options ignoring 
the soot yield criteria of BR18 – as i will prove equal or better fire safety levels.  
The traditional method of calculating risk is the Classical Risk assessment using conservative deterministic 
data input to facilitate a quantitative probability risk assessment. Another method is the Dynamic Risk 
assessment which simulates 5000 fire scenarios using dynamic variables to create the most realistic picture 
of a real fire scenario. Comparing these two methods allows me to pinpoint the exact limit of soot yields 
based on the given amount of active fire barriers installed for both the classical approach and the dynamic 
approach. 
The results show that it is possible to reach equal or better fire safety levels with several options satisfying 
either all polyurethane foam options or only CMHR options depending on the amount of active fire barriers

Abstrakt 
I 2018 blev byggesagsbehandling privatiseret og er nu i hænderne på certificerede brandingeniører som kan 
behandle den pågældende brandklasse alt efter kompleksiteten.  
Begrundelsen var at strømline design- og kontrolfasen, så den certificeringsansvarlige personligt har 
ansvaret for at byggeriet er projekteret og kontrolleret iht. BR18. Før denne ændring havde hver kommune 
sagsbehandling af brand og i den forekom det, at der blev givet dispensationer til diverse byggerier hvis 
man anså det forsvarligt. Flere af disse dispensationer blev givet til plejehjem, men der er løbende blevet 
dokumenteret dødsbrande fra plejehjem som en direkte følge af afvigelsen fra byggenormen. Efter en 
brand på et plejehjem i 2019 har ministeren fra Bolig og Trafikstyrelsen - Ole Birk Olesen fjernet 
muligheden for at give dispensationer iht. brandsikkerhed og har også krævet at kommunerne undersøger, 
hvorvidt alle plejehjem lever op til lovgivningen eller ej [1]. 
Jeg har valgt plejehjemmet Gammelgården fra 1977, som case study, da der under opførslen blev givet 
dispensation, så man undgik at skulle implementere sprinkling [23] . Gammelgården har et kraftigt 
reduceret sikkerhedsniveau, da sprinkling er en vigtig aktiv brandbarriere for at kontrollere en potentiel 
brand. En anden stor bekymring er at lejlighederne er forbundet med et atrium, som er eneste flugtvej ud 
til det fri.  Denne flugtvej (atriummet) indeholder brændbare sofaer i polyuretanskum, hvilket er en 
overtrædelse af kravene fra BR18, da flugtveje kun accepterer CMHR2-møbler. 
Specialet vil introducere forskellige løsninger til at tilfredsstille bygningens brandsikkerhedsniveau ifølge 
BR18. Dette sikkerhedsniveau beregnes og visualiseres ved hjælp af FN-kurver til at skabe en acceptlinje. De 
forskellige løsninger involvere 7 typer af polyuretanskum med forskellige niveauer af sod produktion. De 
forskellige typer af polyuretanskummet vil være en blanding af CMHR og non-CMHR-polyuretanskum i 
sofaer. Hvis sofaerne kombineres med den rigtige mængde aktive brandbarrierer, er det muligt at tillade 
møbler i flugtvejspassager som normalt ikke ville være tilladt under BR18.  
Den mest normale metode til beregning af risiko er en klassisk risikovurdering ved hjælp af konservativ 
deterministisk data input for at lave en kvantitativ sandsynlighedsrisikovurdering. En anden metode er den 
dynamiske risikovurdering, der simulerer 5000 brandscenarier ved hjælp af dynamiske variabler til at skabe 
det mest realistiske billede af branden. Ved at kombinere disse to metoder er det muligt at 
branddimensionere den præcise sikkerhedsgrænse for sodproduktion i lokalet alt efter mængde af aktive 
brandsikringstiltag iht. både den klassiske og dynamiske metode.  
Ud fra resultaterne kan vi se at det er muligt at eftervise samme eller bedre brandsikkerhedsniveau med 
løsninger som opfylder enten alle polyurethanskum møbler eller kun CMHR-godkendt møbler iht. 
mængden af aktive brandbarrierer implementeret i Gammelgaarden. Hvis både brandventilation og 
brandjalousi bliver implementeret i flugtvejspassagen (atriummet) kan alle versioner af møbler fra de 7 
alternativer anvendes. Det viser også at hvis FN-kurven var baseret på input fra den dynamiske simulering 
ville bygningen kunne tolerere et højere niveau af risici. Den dynamiske risikoanalyse trækker data fra flere 
kilder som derfor skaber et større spektrum af risici som atriummet ville kunne opleve. Derved danner den 
dynamiske risikoanalyse brandscenarier som afspejler større risici end visualiseret med den klassiske

1. 
Introduction  
1.1. Fire risk assessment 
The term fire risk assessment in Denmark is an estimation of risk to life from fires, mainly focusing on 
humans and farm animals. A probabilistic fire risk assessment looks at the probability of an unwanted fire 
scenario and explores the severity of the consequence of that outcome. A thorough fire risk assessment 
would look at all possible fire scenarios to occur at either the whole building or a certain part of the 
building that deviates from the prescriptive fire safety regulations [4]. Deviation normally happens for 
buildings that exceed the considerations and norms from the prescriptive fire safety regulations – like a 
skyscraper or an opera. The practical usage of a fire risk assessment in fire engineering community is to 
prove equal or better fire safety level than predetermined by the prescriptive fire safety regulations. The 
prescriptive fire safety regulations describe a minimum risk level which is more commonly known as the 
“FN-Curve” used to create an acceptance line. This minimum reference of safety reflects the inherent risk 
that any building naturally carries based on what type of building it is and how frequent fires occur in that 
type of building. The inherent risk can be determined using fire statistics reported in Denmark from the Fire

1.3. Fire barriers 
When making a fire risk assessment there are five major phases of a fire to consider such as: 1) Fire 
initiation, 2) Fire growth, 3) Smoke spread, 4) Evacuation of the occupants, 5) [4]. These five major phases 
have five Major Barriers to counteract the fire development. These major barriers can be further sub-
categorized into either Active or Passive fire barriers.  
Active fire barrier: Is a barrier that is activated by either a temperature sensor or a smoke 
detector. Its function Is never present in the building unless fire occurs. 
Passive Fire Barrier: Is an integral part of structural elements of the building such as walls, 
flooring or doors. The overall goal is containment and control of the fire by possessing a high 
temperature resistance before structural collapse. As it is an integral part of the building it 
does not require to be activated so the risk profile has fewer steps for failure compared to an 
active fire barrier.

1.4. Time-dependent modelling. 
As displayed from Table 2 we have a multitude of options of active fire barriers applicable to reduce RISK. 
The active fire barriers help reduce RISK by: 
1. Controlling the development and fire growth of a fire, while also limiting the spread to adjacent 
rooms.  
2. Facilitate quick and secure evacuation for the occupants and the fire brigade.  
Out of the active fire barriers mentioned in Table 2 it is specifically Barrier 1-3 that attempt to control the 
fire growth, development and spread and barrier 4-5 facilitate the evacuation process and by the assistance 
of the fire brigade. This means that that the efficiency of Barrier 1-3 should be longer than the amount of 
time it takes for people to evacuate throughout the egress pathways. If the effectiveness of the fire barrier 
is increased, then the probability of harmful fire scenarios to occur will be reduced. A similar logic applies to 
the addition of extra active fire barriers. The consequence of these fire scenarios can be determined using 
time-dependent modelling of fire dynamics and occupant evacuation patterns based on the given scenario. 
An example of how time-dependent modelling affects fire barriers could be this:

2.1. Tenability criteria 
Tenability is a term used to describe human ability to perform cognitive and motor skill functions, at an 
acceptable level during a fire. Tenability criteria dictates a threshold for smoke spread parameters exposure 
towards humans, such as temperature, CO, CO2 and soot concentration which in turn gives us an estimation 
of time available before compromised tenability. This estimation is known as Available Safe Egress Time 
(ASET) and usually given in seconds. The ASET is the outcome of the shortest time of our chosen tenability 
criteria, estimating the level of asphyxiant fire gases, visual obscuration, irritant fire gases and heat.  It is 
important that the ASET is higher than the Required Safety Egress Time (RSET). This relationship is known as 
Evacuation Safety Level (ESL) which gives an indication of consequence without active fire barriers. The 
formular can be seen from [2]

2.2. Available Safe Eggress Time (ASET) 
ASET is the calculation of time-dependent modelling using either analytical equations or fire simulation 
modelling of a fire, from its point of origin to its spread. The ASET is then calculated by using performance-
based criteria, such as the tenability limits mentioned in Table 5. Fire effluents, such as: soot yield, heat, 
radiation and visibility are all important parameters that rapidly will lead to untenable conditions, if no fire 
barrier is present, as discussed in previous chapters. An example could be a large room with a fire occurring 
next to the only escape door. Because of the volume of the room and the location of the fire, the tenability 
criteria for temperature will most likely be the chosen critical parameter. If the tenability criteria for the 
escape door is being met after 1 min and the room would be filled with smoke after 3 min, the temperature 
is our ruling tenability criteria controlling this scenario [11] [12] [13].  
In this report, ASET will be calculated using analytical equations to estimate the fire phenomena, which will 
be further explained in chapter 2.2.1 - 2.2.4.

fire, and therefore we will not be calculating the cumulative sum of all the gases released that combined 
give the most correct picture. However, FED is the correct method and includes all the gases and therefore, 
the theory and formulars will be quickly explained. 
The toxic smoke gases include asphyxiant toxicants and irritant toxicants. The parameters affected by 
asphyxiant toxicants are smoke height and visibility tenability, as these can cause upper-respiratory tract 
irritation and eye irritation, leading to immediate escape impairment or after a certain level of exposure 
may lead to carboxyhemoglobin (COHb) poisoning.

2.2.4. Radiation

periods it may be up to 10 kW/m2 but as long as it doesn’t exceed an exposure time of 2 seconds During the 
entire evacuation, the total radiation received cannot exceed 60 kW/m2 per person. Exceeding these 
amounts leads to a high probability that it would result in hyperthermia, body surface burns and respiratory 
tract burns and therefore lead to incapacitation [23].

2.3.1. Detection and alarm, 𝑡𝑑𝑎 
Fires can normally be detected by humans immediately if within close proximation of the fire. The ability of 
seeing, identifying smells, and hearing guarantee an early detection, if alert, before untenable conditions. 
However, if the occupants are not alert or do not possess sharp senses the detection of fire may not be 
immediate and therefore an automatic fire detection system is desirable. The nursing home contains 
people with dulled senses or severe dementia in sleeping conditions with limited available trained staff. 
Therefore, the detection time will be derived from datasets from PD:7974-6:2004 with an alarm system 
according to danish code [25]. 
2.3.2. Pre-movement time, 𝑡𝑟𝑑  
When alarmed the occupants will not immediately move which means that the pre-movement time,  𝑡𝑟𝑑, Is 
part of the following 3 steps [4]: 
1. Warning signal recognition  
2. Warning signal interpretation 
3. Pre-movement acting.  
Step 1 is the ability to receive or recognize the warning signal. The primary function of the warning signal is 
to reach all occupants for whom the warning signal is relevant. People who are at asleep or intoxicated 
might not hear the warning signal and therefore, time for recognition of the situation would be increased. 
Another factor to consider is that people can either be familiar or unfamiliar with the building, leading to an 
inability to recognize the warning signal, which increases the time for step 1. Trained staff and evacuation 
drills for both occupants and trained staff may help to reduce the evacuation time. The nursing home 
contains people with dementia and therefore, an alarm system is not desirable as it would cause confusion 
for the occupants and lead them to wander out of their rooms [25]. Therefore, the evacuation will be based 
on majority trained staff aiding the evacuation of the occupants and occupants within close proximity to 
the fire being able to self evacuate. The datasets will be derived from PD:7974-6:2004. 
Step 2 is the ability to interpret the signal and what level of danger, it should be categorized in. Direct 
contact with the fire or warnings from firefighters have the highest effectiveness on the correct 
interpretation of the warning and lead to immediate understanding [4]. If the warning system is simply, 
such as: traditional fire alarm, there is a high chance people do not respond quickly to it, as people have 
nowadays been de-sensitized to this type of alarm due to frequent fire drills, which ultimately leads to slow 
interpretation of the danger levels. If alarms are followed up with a voice message with specific instructions 
on the scenario, the interpretation will be faster, as it differentiates itself from a false alarm. As mentioned 
earlier, the alarm system will only notify the trained staff leading them to warn the occupants. This is 
considered less effective compared to the firefighters or direct contact to the fire [4], see Table 8.

Classical approach 
Case study description 
The nursing home is a type 6 building and was supposed to be constructed with sprinkling system back in 
1977 [2]. Since that time the shared atrium hallway, which facilitate the egress routes, have been reported 
to contain objects that reduce egress width and are flammable.  
To summarize the following deviations are: 
1. Type 6 buildings with a floor area above 1.000 m2 with shared egress routes should be installed 
with a sprinkling system to reduce, control, or quell a potential fire.  
2. Escape routes such as egress pathways should always be unblocked with minimum width of 1.3 
meter [2] to allow adequate space for evacuation and an acceptable fire load of maximum 50 
MJ/m² of the ground floor [28]. This fire load is equivalent to 2.5 kg wood pr. m2.  
 
The following project will be considered a complex project the nursing home is interested in not 
implementing the required sprinkling but still prove equal or better fire safety levels using alternative active 
fire barriers. Secondly we will stress test the atrium with different kinds of CMHR and non-CMHR couches 
to analyze the possibility of them using combustible couches. Therefore, it receives the highest level of fire 
class: Fire Class 4.

4. 
Dynamic Approach. 
The Dynamic Approach uses Discrete Event Simulation (DES) to simulate 5000 fires in the atrium.  This 
method is known as the Monte Carlo method which is a stochastic computation that repeats random 
sampling from a spectrum of deterministic datasets of problems, where the deterministic approach alone 
does not reflect reality [4] [36].  The validity of the Dynamic Approach relies on the fact that The Classical 
Approach is inherently static, as it uses deterministic input in static fault and event trees and therefore 
contain uncertainties. These uncertainties can be divided into two groups: Aleatory uncertainty and 
epistemic uncertainty [37]. 
• 
Aleatory uncertainty describes lack of the inherent variation in the reliability or effectiveness in the 
physical systems or the present environment located in the building. The physical systems for the 
nursing home would be the effectiveness and reliability of the active fire barriers. The aleatory 
uncertainty for the environment could be pre-movement times, movement speed, density at 
hallways for the residents and the trained staff or fire event characteristics such as:  fire location, 
ignition source or fire growth. 
• 
Epistemic uncertainty describes the lack of knowledge or data accessible to make a proper analysis 
or assumption about a given issue. The fire statistics are very scarce and not uniform across 
countries with similar risk acceptance levels. This means that data accessible to us today 
concerning active fire barriers might be based on the reliability of fire barriers constructed 30 years 
ago, according to building requirements of that timeline. The active fire barriers today are built at 
stricter codes and therefore should have a higher probability of working successfully, but until a fire 
has occurred and their data collected (if collected) the epistemic uncertainty will not have 
improved.   
These uncertainties are present in the Classical Approach because of the time-dependent input being 
applied as average deterministic values, from either: average failure rate of an active fire barrier based on 
its lifetime, average ignition source, average soot yields, average amount of trained staff or average 
distance in egress pathways etc. These simplifications are conservative and used to minimize the chance of 
underestimating risk levels. The method fails to captivate the dynamic relationship between the different 
variables and their stochastic influence on each other [38]. This can be further illustrated the following 
shortcomings: 
• 
The outcome of a quantitative risk assessment is based on a fire located inside 1 room. The 
statistical data provided to arrive to this quantitative result is based on a uniform chance of risk per 
m2, depending on what type of building it is. This is however highly inaccurate as each room does 
not have similar chances of risk but given the lack of more specific data, this is currently the dataset 
applied, as it is the only relatively complete dataset from the statistics. Additionally, research have 
shown that odds of fire occurring differ depending on what time of day it is [39]. 
==PAGE:48==
Andreas Bertram 
48 
 
• 
Other shortcomings are the evacuation process as it is intrinsically flawed. It is based upon the 
assumption of human being able to evacuate in a robotic manner, once alerted of the impending 
fire near their vicinity. The variables in RSET and their times (s) are based on scarce datasets, 
whereas most of it is from successful evacuation simulated from fire evacuation drills and not from 
real fire events with potential deaths occurring - as those are difficult to document. Not only does 
this provide too optimistic evacuation times, but it is also a deterministic dataset to a stochastic 
reality. This can be shown by the fact that some people during evacuation will respond very 
effectively and others will not. The deterministic output ignores individual characteristics such as: 
age, mobility, size, disabilities, and the fact that once people are alerted of a fire, they might 
perform actions that are counterproductive such as: get dressed, looking for items of value or seek 
out other family members. These individual parameters clash with the deterministic approach, as 
each of them have a stochastic influence on the RSET [40].  Newer data indicates that dwellings 
required evacuation times of 11 mins [41], whereas most RSET standards dictate 3-5 mins [40].  
• 
Another important parameter to consider is the base premises that RSET is based on. The first 
parameter of RSET is the pre-movement time. This parameter is first activated once the smoke 
detector has detected the fire and alarmed the occupants. There are two types of smoke detectors: 
ionization and photoelectric detectors and the assumption is that these detectors are equally 
effective, but studies have shown that they are neither equally effective, nor do they have a 
deterministic reliability of detecting a fire. This parameter is further varied by the type of fire that 
develops in the room which is another parameter ignored as we assume same type of fire growths

4.1. Determining fire growth 
One of the most important parameters influencing the ASET is the fire growth. When using the classical 
approach the prescriptive rules mandate a deterministic fire growth in nursing homes to be defined as 
medium fire growth, which provides an 𝛼 value of 0,12 kw/s. The deterministic approach is widely accepted 
and applied in fire engineering, but it has a cost as fire engineering might over- or under-dimension the 𝛼 
value which can be detrimental, as the fire growth is the most important parameter. The consequences are: 
• 
By using deterministic fire growth, the overall ASET will be larger than needed, which directly 
effects the RSET. At the same time, it might also lead to an increase in ASET, because the 
deterministic fire growth could be under-dimensioned compared to the actual fire scenario 
occurring. If the fire growth is under-dimensioned, certain active fire barriers, such as : Fire 
ventilation will perform poorly as they are specifically dimensioned according to the deterministic 
fire growth.  
• 
A more severe ASET means a more optimized RSET, which provides a higher cost of safety to 
counteract the ASET. This is more a cost-benefit effect if concerned about economical cost. 
The deterministic fire growth itself is based on deterministic models adjusted to experimental data 
obtained from controlled laboratory experiments. These controlled events produce a more controlled fire 
growth which would only be accurate for exact same scenarios. The fire is influenced by type of material 
burning, geometry, fire spread, surface boundaries and dimensions of potential ventilation openings in the 
surrounding space – and therefore creating a deterministic fire growth for a risk assessment is far from 
accurate as it contains so many variables making it very susceptible to aleatory and epistemic uncertainty 
[43] [44]. 
To minimize uncertainty and reduce likelihood of over-dimensioning the ASET the fire growth will be 
simulated using random distributed between a slow fire growth of 0,006 kw/s to a medium fire growth of 
0,012 kw/s to satisfy the BR18 requirement but at the same time creating a more realistic spectrum within 
the 5000 Monte Carlo simulations.

4.3.1. Reliability of fire ventilation 
There are multiple causes for failure of the fire ventilation system. This failure can be linked to either 
fabrication error from the factory or human error concerning faulty or lack or maintenance on the fire 
ventilation system [45]. The literature available is quite limited and the statistics of failure rates vary – 
therefore, the representation of the failure rates will be simulated using triangular distribution, whereas 
the minimum will be 67 %, the most frequent will be 90% and highest will be 95%.  
4.3.2. Reliability of sprinkling system 
The prescriptive requirements of the sprinkling system are based on operational reliability and 
performance reliability that provide the overall effectiveness of the sprinkling, when applied in a risk 
assessment. Statistics show that during a fire the sprinkling systems operational reliability is unavailable in 
5-10 of 100 cases [46]. Once it has activated the performance reliability of the sprinkling system has been 
recorded to control or reduce the fire with an effectiveness of up to 99.5%.

Dynamo 
To understand Dynamo and its possibilities, the user must first have knowledge of the software known as 
Revit, which is nowadays the most used software applied in the construction business. Revit is a Building 
Information Modelling (BIM) tool, applied to create 3D objects and coordinated the different fields relevant 
for the total completion of a building. Revit was the replacement of AutoCad which was the original 
software applied in the construction business but purely 2D based. Today the world is getting smarter and 
more digitalized and therefore more 3D oriented with a heavy demand for “smart solutions”. As the 3D 
software became the dominant software, a need rose for data management, data manipulation and 
simplification with a strong focus on automation, which resulted in Autodesk creating Dynamo in 2013.  
Dynamo is an open-source visual programming language which provides the engineers and architects a way 
to construct programmatic relationships using more approachable graphical interface compared to the 
more normal approach - which would be traditional “coding”. This means that the user can extract a large 
amount of data and apply mathematical modelling on the data extracted and facilitate automation. Before 
Dynamo, it was normal to export data to other software, such as: Excel or Rhino to perform data 
management and data manipulation but since Dynamo is a direct add-in to Revit, this process has now 
been simplified and Dynamo itself is linked directly into Revit. The direct link means that any data necessary 
for a risk assessment is possible to extract and the data itself auto-updates, as the shape of the model 
changes. An example would be an atrium with a volume of 150m3, where we conduct an initial risk 
assessment – then an updated version from the architect arrives and the risk assessment auto-updates 
based on the updated volume extracted from the live-feed. Dynamo gives the user a fundamentally new 
way of working with the geometric information and functions extremely well with function-based fire 
engineering as it relies heavily on the exact data of the building combined with the correct formulas.  
Programming in Dynamo consists of Nodes and Wires to create the visual programming. The nodes can 
represent Revit Elements like Model Lines, Model Families or Reference points but can also represent 
simple to complex mathematical functions, either provided by Dynamo visual programming options or 
written independently using Python language. The principle of the nodes is that on the left side, they 
absorb input from wires injected into the node and then on the right side of the node it releases output 
using another wire to send the data further down the pipeline, see Figure 13 below for the principle:

8. 
Conclusion 
It is possible to prove equal or better fire safety for the nursing home using both the Classical and Dynamic 
risk assessment for Plejecenter Gammelgaard. Fire ventilation and fire curtain are promising options that 
could be implemented, but with limitations on the available polyurethane foam options in the egress 
pathway (atrium). The initial results revealed a minimum theoretical soot yield of 0.115  yco [
g
g] from fire 
ventilation as the replacement fire barrier of sprinkling whereas the limit was 0.233 yco [
g
g] using a fire 
curtain. These numbers were reduced to 0.109 yco [
g
g] for the fire ventilation and 0.178 yco [
g
g] after the 
sensitivity study. This proved that it is possible to re-simulate the 5000 fire scenarios reaching an even more 
customized and precise level of soot, but it also revealed that other parameters in the 5000 fire scenarios 
require further sensitivity studies, as the 1st sensitivity analysis of fire ventilation concluded in the removal 
of 60 untenable fire scenarios as a result of a soot yield reduction of ≈0.009 yco [
g
g]. The sensitivity 
analysis also revealed that the initial results deviated by 5.17 % – 23.54 %, compared to the end results 
after the 1st and 2nd re-simulation in the sensitivity study.  
If applied as a sole replacement for the sprinkling, only a fire curtain allows the potential usage of non-
CMHR polyurethane foam materials. If both fire barriers are combined the egress pathway (atrium) can 
exceed the standard soot limit of BR18 and therefore tolerate any of the 7 kinds of polyurethane foam 
simulated. Using polyurethane foam with a theoretical soot yield up to 2.1 yco [
g
g] the consequence of the 
time-dependent modelling has drastically increased but by implementing 2 fire barriers, the probability of 
guaranteeing a functional fire barrier has also drastically improved and therefore the overall RISK is better 
than compared to the RISK level of BR18, using the standard fire barriers.   
There is a big difference in the acceptable risk between the classical and dynamic risk assessment. The 
dynamic risk assessment gives a more realistic picture of reality, as it contains less aleatory and epistemic 
uncertainty. From Solution 3 its visible that the classical risk assessment does not tolerable 590 cases of fire 
scenarios and therefore sets a more conservative risk level: whereas this solution is tolerated by the 
dynamic risk assessment because it allows a bigger spectrum of risk, since the method contains more 
literature, which is a more realistic representation of the actual risk of the given room.  
More literature available would strength the risk levels of the fire barriers – specifically the active barrier 
for curtain walls as this was very difficult to find. Another way to increase the confidence in the results is to 
increase the iterations of the Monte Carlo simulations.

9. 
Future works 
1. Multiple sensitivity analyses of parameters involved in the fire development are required to further 
strength the model.  
2. The dynamic risk assessment would be further strengthened by more literature, as several fire 
barriers had very limited data available and therefore increases the uncertainty in the results.  
3. The RSET should be dynamic, which would give a better representation if a real fire was compared 
to the current situation where RSET is modelled as a deterministic data input. Given the limited 
amount of people in this project this variable is less important but should still be more 
implemented into the code. 
4. The fire growth giving the acceptance line should have been modelled with sprinkling affecting the 
fire growth. This effect would have prolonged the ASET time which would have made it more 
difficult to live up to same or equal levels of fire safety.  
5. The dynamic probabilistic modelling is extremely complex and coded in Dynamo. This makes the 
accessibility for other people very limited, as it would be desirable that the model was simplified 
and therefore - more accessible.  
6. The model currently uses CO as our critical toxic gas parameter. FED / FEC is more desirable to use 
in the future as it calculates the cumulation of all the gases.
