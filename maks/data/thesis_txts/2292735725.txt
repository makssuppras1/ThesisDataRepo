Abstract
This Master Thesis presents the numerical reproduction of prior laboratory experiments
on a 1:60 version of the DTU 10MW wind turbine mounted on a tension leg platform
(TLP). The TLP wind turbine and the environmental conditions for the experimental
tests were Froude scaled and the rotor blades were redesigned to produce the scaled
thrust force. The dynamic response to wind and waves was measured in terms of motion
in the six degrees of freedom, nacelle accelerations and tension in the mooring lines.
The numerical model was implemented in Flex5. It featured the unsteady BEM method
for the rotor aerodynamics and the Morison equation for the TLP hydrodynamics,
with the option of Rainey nonlinear forcing model.
The model was calibrated and
validated through comparison of key features such as steady thrust curve, decay tests
in water and dynamic response to regular waves with and without wind. The response
to irregular waves was analyzed and compared to the tests, including the eﬀect of wind
and misalignment. The Rainey version of the model was coupled with fully nonlinear
wave kinematics generated in OceanWave3D to simulate the response to focused waves.
The comparison between test and simulation results showed a good match for surge and
an acceptable match for heave and nacelle fore-aft acceleration. The pitch measurements
were proven to be unrealistic. The nacelle side-side acceleration and the response in sway,
roll and yaw did not match, likely due to larger asymmetries in the laboratory setup than
in the simulation. The line tension was generally underpredicted by the numerical model,
presumably due to the smaller motion in roll and sway in simulations. The eﬀect of wind
and misalignment on the response was qualitatively similar in tests and simulations.
Finally, the simulation of extreme events with the linear and nonlinear versions of the
numerical model showed a certain correlation with the tests for the response in surge,
line tension and nacelle fore-aft acceleration. The diﬀerence in response between linear
and nonlinear simulations diverged as the incident wave grew larger.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Climate change and the depletion of fossil fuel resources are among the biggest problems
our world is facing today.
In addition, the current population growth rate and the
consequent increase in the energy demand require a shift in the energy model. Renewable
and clean energies, such as wind and solar, are more necessary than ever.
Wind turbines transform the kinetic energy contained in the wind into electricity through
a clean process with no emissions. They help to reduce the air pollution and to mitigate
the greenhouse eﬀect. They also diminish the need for energy imports and create jobs
and business opportunities in the area. In Denmark, the pioneer country in wind energy,
wind power produced 32.7% of the total electricity consumption in 2013 and 39.1% in
2014 [9]. Furthermore, the Danish government has commited to raise the share of wind
energy coverage to 50% by the year 2020 and to make Denmark free of fossil fuels by
the year 2050 [24].
Wind turbines can be installed on land or oﬀshore. The term oﬀshore, when used for
wind power, refers to any wind turbine or wind farm installed in the sea or in other
bodies of water, such as lakes or fjords. The ﬁrst oﬀshore wind farm was installed in
1991 in Vindeby (Denmark), with 11 wind turbines of 450 kW each. It was also Denmark
the one to build the ﬁrst large scale oﬀshore wind farm in 2002. This wind farm, built
by DONG Energy [7] and named Horns Rev I, is located oﬀthe coast of Esbjerg and
consists of 80 turbines of 2 MW each.
1.1
Why Oﬀshore Wind Energy?
Oﬀshore wind energy is still much more expensive than onshore (see Fig. 1.1 1). Some
key features of oﬀshore wind farms that have a direct impact on the higher cost are:
• The oﬀshore turbine suﬀers the combined loads from wind, waves and sea currents.
The stochastic nature of the sea, the variety of soil types and the scarce data
available for the validation of the numerical design tools contribute to the diﬃculty
to predict loads, which leads to oversizing of the substructures for safety reasons.
• Whereas the onshore turbine is directly attached to the foundation, a substructure
is needed to join the submarine foundation and the oﬀshore wind turbine.

• The oﬀshore wind farm, and specially the foundations, involves more diﬃculty

• All components (specially those submerged) must be treated to prevent corrosion.

Oﬀshore Concepts
Oﬀshore wind turbine systems are mainly classiﬁed by the water depth at the site they
are installed. For shallow water the turbines are bottom ﬁxed, i.e. connected to the
seabed by a relatively rigid structure (Fig. 1.3, left). For deeper sites, where the bottom
ﬁxed solution is not feasible, the wind turbine is mounted on a ﬂoating platform (Fig.
1.3, right) connected to the seabed by a mooring system. To the present day, bottom
ﬁxed structures are used for wind farms in the shallow waters of Northern Europe, being
the monopile the dominating concept. On the other hand, ﬂoating wind farms are not
a reality yet, although countries surrounded by deeper waters such as Norway, Spain,
Portugal, Japan or the United States would see ﬂoating wind turbines as a better option
once they become produced on an industrial scale [25].

Bottom Fixed Concepts
Bottom ﬁxed structures for oﬀshore wind turbines are mainly classiﬁed as:
• Gravity based: Designed for depths up to 25 m. The turbine is supported by a
concrete structure resting on the seabed.
• Monopile: For depths up to 45 m.
The turbine is mounted on a hollow steel
column, driven in the seabed up to 30 m.
• Tripod: Similar to the monopile, but connected to the seabed with three or four
legs. It can be used for depths up to 60 m.
• Jacket: They have three or four legs driven in the seabed and interconnected by a
lattice. They can be used for depths up to 70 m.

Floating Concepts
The dominating ﬂoating concepts are brieﬂy described below.
Spar Buoy
The spar buoy concept consists on a long, vertical ﬂoater with a large draft. It is self
stabilized by ballast, and therefore it can be towed to the desired location after assembly.
The structure is moored with catenary cables.
In 2009 the company Statoil installed the Hywind Demo [13] oﬀthe coast of Norway, a
2.3 MW wind turbine mounted on a spar buoy ﬂoater (Fig. 1.4). It became the ﬁrst full
scale ﬂoating wind turbine in the world. This demonstration project will serve Statoil
as a springboard to the construction of a ﬂoating wind farm in Scotland, composed of
ﬁve wind turbines of 6 MW each [13].
Figure 1.4: The Hywind Demo ﬂoating wind turbine installed by Statoil [13].
Barge
The barge consists on a platform with a large water plane area, which provides self
stability by buoyancy. As the spar buoy, the barge may also be towed after assembly
and is also moored with catenary lines, although this concept presents a much lower
draft than the spar buoy.
Although no full size barge prototypes exist to date, the French company IDEOL has
developed a barge platform named Damping Pool [14]. This concept is designed in the
shape of a square ring with a pool in the center, which contributes to the damping of
the structure to the ﬂoater motion (Fig. 1.5).

Semisubmersible
The semisubmersible is a structure normally made of three or four cylinders interconnected
by a lattice. It is also moored with catenary cables and self stabilized by ballast and
buoyancy, while having a medium draft. The semisubmersible also allows towing after

The WindFloat [26] is a tri-ﬂoater semisubmersible prototype installed oﬀthe coast of

Tension Leg Platform (TLP)
The tension leg platform (often abbreviated as TLP) includes a compact vertical ﬂoater
with a number of spokes at the bottom (normally three, four or ﬁve). The ﬂoater has
an excess of buoyancy, which is counteracted by tension in the mooring lines attached
to the end of the spokes. Unlike the other ﬂoating concepts described above, the TLP
is not self stabilized and therefore does not allow towing after assembly.
As it happens with the barge, full scale prototypes of the TLP do not exist at the
moment. The company Glosten has developed the PelaStar TLP [21], consisting of a
vertical ﬂoater attached to a ﬁve-spoke star-shaped spoke system (Fig. 1.7).

1.4
Motivation
The dynamic analysis of a ﬂoating wind turbine is a complex problem that involves
the wind turbine aerodynamics, the foundation hydrodynamics, the structural response
and the control system. The numerical modelling of such a problem requires the use of
aero-hydro-servo-elastic codes, numerical tools that can take into consideration all the
inputs mentioned before and provide a relatively reliable output. Nonetheless, numerical
models need to be validated with experimental data before they are completely trusted
and used in the wind industry.
Although some ﬂoating wind turbine concepts have already reached the demonstration
phase (spar buoy, semisubmersible), other concepts (barge, TLP) do not even count on
a full scale prototype that can be used to validate the corresponding numerical models.
Therefore, the validation of such numerical models can only be performed by using the
results of experimental tests on scaled models.
The work carried out by A. M. Hansen and R. Laugesen and reported in [16] extensively
describes the measured response of a scaled TLP wind turbine to diﬀerent combinations
of wind and wave loads. It is the goal of the present work to use this ﬁeld data to
validate a numerical model of the same TLP wind turbine and to reproduce the results
of some of the laboratory tests. The comparison will point out the similarities between
the experimental and numerical models, as well as potential weak points in both of
them. This acquired knowledge can be used as a guideline in the development of future
numerical models and experimental setups.
In the big picture, the validation of numerical models for ﬂoating wind turbines plays an
important role in the design of the ﬂoating foundations, adding reliability to the design
tools and conﬁdence to the sector of ﬂoating wind turbines, and ultimately pushing the
oﬀshore industry forward and bringing down the cost of oﬀshore wind energy.

CHAPTER 2
The TLP Wind Turbine
This Master Thesis focuses on the dynamic response of a wind turbine mounted on a
tension leg platform (TLP) subjected to wind and wave loads. Hence, a brief summary
of the geometry, degrees of freedom, loads and equations of motion for the TLP wind
turbine is given in this chapter.
2.1
Geometry and Degrees of Freedom
A TLP is a buoyant structure that consists on a ﬂoating body, a system of spokes1 and
a set of tendons, which attach each spoke to the seabed. The wind turbine is mounted
on top of the ﬂoating structure. Since the total buoyancy is larger than the total weight,
the static equilibrium is achieved by means of tension in the tendons.
A sketch of the TLP wind turbine used in [16] can be seen in Figs. 2.1 and 2.2. The wind
turbine with rotor diameter Drotor and tower with height htower and diameter Dtower is
mounted on the ﬂoating body, which in this case is composed of a partially submerged
transition part with diameter Dtp and a fully submerged ﬂoater with diameter Dfloater.
The portion of transition part above the water level, with height hfree, brings the hub
up to a height of hhub. The submerged portion of the transition part, with height hsub,
together with the main ﬂoater, with height hfloater, contributes to the buoyancy. The
ﬂoater is mounted on four spokes with length Lspoke and a 45 deg orientation measured
from the wind direction. Finally, each spoke is moored with a tendon of length Ltend
anchored to the seabed, which is located at a depth h.
The degrees of freedom for a TLP wind turbine are those of any ﬂoating structure, and
are also sketched in Figs. 2.1 and 2.2. Translations in x, y and z axes are labeled in
red (surge, sway, heave) while rotations about x, y and z axes correspond to green (roll,
pitch, yaw).
At this point it is worth mentioning that Flex5 models the turbine and foundation as
separate elements, linked through a force and a moment at the interface (orange dot in
Figs.2.1 and 2.2). Thus, for simplicity, the portion of transition piece above the water
level (labeled with height hfree) is considered as part of the tower in the numerical model.

3.1
Scaling Procedure
The correct scaling of wind turbines relies on the preservation of two non-dimensional
numbers, namely the Reynolds number Re and the tip speed ratio TSR. The Reynolds
number represents a ratio between inertia and viscous forces, and for a wind turbine
blade is given by:
Re = ρaV c
µa
(3.1)
Where ρa is the air density, V is the mean wind speed, µa is the air dynamic viscosity
and c is the blade chord. The tip speed ratio is a relation between the rotor speed and
the wind speed, namely:
TSR = ωR
V
(3.2)
Where R is the rotor radius and ω is the rotor speed. The most important non-dimensional
number for the scaling of oﬀshore structures is the Froude number Fr, a ratio between
inertia and gravitational forces:
Fr =
u
√gL
(3.3)
In this case u is the water velocity, g is the acceleration of gravity and L stands for the
characteristic length [2].
In an ideal downscaling of a ﬂoating wind turbine all three non-dimensional numbers
deﬁned above would be preserved. The preservation of the TSR is very feasible, since
lengths and velocities are relatively easy to downscale. The preservation of Fr can also
be achieved even though g is the same for the scaled and full size models.
On the
other hand, the preservation of Re implies a signiﬁcant reduction in the air viscosity

Given the dependency of the lift and drag coeﬃcients (CL and CD) on the Reynolds
number, if the rotor is downscaled geometrically the mismatch in Re number would

Experimental Setup
The laboratory tests described in [16] took place in the deep water wave basin at the DHI
facilities in Hørsholm, Denmark. A sketch of the wave basin is shown in Fig. 3.6, and
the experimental setup is sketched in Fig. 3.7. The wave basin is 20 m long, 30 m wide
and 3 m deep. The wave maker is able to generate waves with a heading angle between
0 and 30 deg. The wind generator is mounted on top of the wave maker. Opposite to
the wave maker a wave absorber dissipates the wave energy to reduce wave reﬂection
[16]. The data acquisition equipment located on the bridge receives the signals from the
instrumentation, brieﬂy summarized below:
• Diﬀerent wave gauges were installed in the basin, at the locations shown in Fig.
3.6. These gauges are able to measure the surface elevation at the given location.
• The line tension was measured using Z-gauges inserted as a link in each mooring
line. These devices have an accuracy of 1% of the measured value.
• A two-directional strain gauge was placed between the tower and the nacelle.
This gauge is able to measure the shear force between both components, which
is equivalent to the aerodynamic thrust on the rotor [16].
• Three single axis accelerometers were installed on the ﬂoater to measure the
acceleration of the foundation. In addition, two more accelerometers were placed
in the nacelle to measure the nacelle acceleration in the fore-aft and side-side
directions.

• The motion of the ﬂoater was measured using a Qualisys system, which employs a
set of white spheres attached to the structure and two infrared cameras that track
the motion of the individual spheres. The accuracy of the Qualisys system when
determining the position of each sphere is within 1 mm [16].
• The wind speed was measured using four TSI 8455 air velocity transducers (AVT)

3.3
Model Implementation in Flex5
The numerical model was implemented in Flex5, a modal based numerical tool developed
by S. Øye (DTU Wind Energy). Tools such as Flex5 are called aero-servo-elastic codes,
since they are able to simulate the behaviour of a wind turbine considering wind loads,
the structure dynamics and the control system. Flex5 uses the Runge-Kutta-Nystrom
scheme to solve the equations of motion and considers the wind turbine has 28 degrees of
freedom, 6 of which belong to the foundation [8]. This setup allows the implementation of
a ﬂoating foundation, turning the aero-servo-elastic code into an aero-hydro-servo-elastic
code able to take into account the wave loads and the dynamics of the ﬂoating structure.
For the present work an existing Flex5 model for the NREL 5MW reference wind turbine
mounted on a TLP was used. The model was ﬁrst developed by G. K. V. Ramachandran
as part of his Ph.D. Thesis [22] and later modiﬁed by M. Aubrion and M. Dupont for their
M.Sc. Thesis [8]. This Flex5 model was adapted to the downscaled TLP wind turbine
used in the laboratory tests described in [16], calibrated and validated as detailed in
Sections 3.4 and 3.5.

Thrust Curve
The rotor thrust is the most important aerodynamic parameter in the dynamics of the
TLP wind turbine, hence its correct downscaling is of utmost importance. Due to the
inability to preserve the Re number in the downscaling process the scaled rotor was
designed with longer airfoils [17]. The diﬀerent airfoils and the use of airfoil coeﬃcients
corresponding to a higher value of Re in Flex5 resulted in a mismatch between the
obtained thrust curve and the one downscaled from the DTU 10MW wind turbine [1].
For the present work and for the work described in [16] this mismatch in the thrust
curve was solved by modifying the blade pitch settings. For a set of wind speeds within
the operational range of the turbine the mean thrust was obtained and compared to
the thrust downscaled from the DTU 10MW turbine. Then, the pitch was modiﬁed
in order to obtain the reference thrust. This pitch diﬀerence never exceeded 2 deg in
tests or simulations [16]. The rotor speed was set to the downscaled reference value
in each case. A total of 22 simulations were run for the whole range of wind speeds,
considering the cases of no turbulence and the turbulence intensity of 5% reported in
[16]. The results are shown in Fig. 3.17, where the plots on the left side correspond
to the operational conditions (rotor speed and blade pitch) downscaled from the DTU
10MW turbine, which give a diﬀerent thrust in the numerical model. The right-side
plots show the necessary pitch settings in the numerical model to match the thrust.

4.2
Probabilistic Approach
The random nature of irregular waves and turbulent wind makes the comparison of time
series impractical. Instead, a probabilistic approach seems more suitable in this case.
The signals of interest for the present work are the motion of the TLP wind turbine in
the six degrees of freedom, the line tension in the tendons and the nacelle acceleration
in fore-aft and side-side directions. For each signal and each sea state the peaks were
identiﬁed and stored. Then they were sorted from minimum to maximum and assigned
an index i. The exceedance probability Pexc for each of the peaks was computed based on
its index i and the total number of peaks N, in a way that the largest value corresponded
to the lowest probability of being exceeded, namely:
Pexc = 1 −
i
N + 1
(4.1)
The exceedance probability was plotted against the response peaks in a semilogarithmic
scale. For the identiﬁcation of the response peaks the procedure detailed in [16] was used
in the form of a MATLAB script facilitated by A. M. Hansen and R. Laugesen. The
idea behind the method is that the extreme events should be uncorrelated. Hence, the
time series of incident wave is divided into individual waves, where each wave is deﬁned
between two zero-crossings of the surface elevation [16]. Then a peak is chosen as the
maximum value of the given signal within the time window deﬁned by the individual
wave.
It is well known from wave theory that the wave velocity is in phase with the surface
elevation, while the acceleration is oﬀset a quarter of a period. Since the ﬂow around the
ﬂoater is inertia dominated the hydrodynamic force is in phase with the wave acceleration.
It seems reasonable to assume the peaks in the surge (and in all the remaining modes
of motion) would take place shortly after the peak in wave acceleration [16]. Therefore
the period-wide time window where the maximum is seeked was delayed half a period
for the six modes of motion.
The tension in the front tendons will present its maximum at the same time as the
surge motion, therefore the time window was also shifted half a period forward when
seeking the maximum for front tendons and minimum for back tendons. The maximum

minimum, hence the window was delayed a full period in this case.
If the tower deﬂection is not signiﬁcant the nacelle acceleration is presumably in phase
with the ﬂoater acceleration, and then no shift was needed in the period-wide time
window when seeking the maximum for these signals.

CHAPTER 5
Model Adaptation for the
Analysis of Extreme Events
As mentioned in Appendix B linear wave theory is only valid for the cases where the
wave amplitude is very small compared to the water depth. In those cases, given also
the assumption of slender body, the coupling of linear wave theory with the Morison
equation for hydrodynamic forcing allows to neglect nonlinearities as well as the eﬀects
of radiation and diﬀraction. For the present work, all simulations to this point were run
under these assumptions.
As the wave amplitude increases this arrangement loses reliability and more advanced
models are needed.
Ultimately, the analysis of extreme events demands a diﬀerent
approach to the modelling of both the incident wave and the hydrodynamic forcing. For
the present work it was decided to simulate some of the extreme events reported in [16],
therefore a modiﬁcation of the Flex5 model including nonlinear wave kinematics and
nonlinear forcing was necessary. In this chapter a brief overview of this modiﬁcation is
given. All simulations for this section were run twice, using the linear and nonlinear
versions of the Flex5 model. The results from both simulations were compared to the
corresponding test data.
5.1

to irregular waves, in a way that they are composed of a sum of individual waves over

x0 and time t0, namely the focus point [8]. The surface elevation η(x, t) for a group of

5.3
Nonlinear Hydrodynamic Forcing
Under the assumption of slender body and linear wave theory the Morison equation is an
acceptable approach to the modelling of hydrodynamic forcing on bodies submerged in
an oscillatory ﬂow. These assumptions entails, among others, that the wave amplitude
is very small compared to the water depth. Hence, for the modelling of hydrodynamic
forcing due to focused waves a diﬀerent forcing model is necessary. The Flex5 model
developed by G. K. V. Ramachandran [22] initially employed the Morison forcing to
compute the hydrodynamic loads on the TLP. Later, an extension of the Flex5 model
that included the Rainey forcing was developed by H. Bredmose [3] and used by M.
Aubrion and M. Dupont [8].

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions and Future Work
This Master Thesis presents the numerical reproduction of laboratory experiments on
a 1:60 version of the DTU 10MW wind turbine mounted on a tension leg platform (TLP).
The experimental tests were carried out by A. M. Hansen and R. Laugesen at the Danish
Hydraulic Institute (DHI) as part of their Master Thesis [16] within the INNWIND.EU
project [15]. The foundation, wind turbine and environmental conditions were Froude
scaled and the rotor blades were redesigned to produce the scaled thrust force. The
TLP wind turbine was exposed to diﬀerent combinations of wind and wave loads and
its dynamic response was measured and analyzed. The analysis was made in terms of
motion in the six degrees of freedom, nacelle accelerations and tension in the mooring
lines.
The numerical tool used for the simulations is Flex5, an aero-hydro-servo-elastic code
able to simulate the response of a ﬂoating wind turbine taking into account wind and
wave loads, the structure dynamics and the control system. A Flex5 model of the NREL
5MW reference wind turbine mounted on a TLP was originally developed by G. K. V.
Ramachandran [22], employing the unsteady Blade Element Momentum (BEM) method
for the rotor aerodynamics and the Morison equation for the TLP hydrodynamics. The
model was later modiﬁed by H. Bredmose [3] to include the option of Rainey nonlinear
forcing model.
For the present work this full size numerical model was ﬁrst adapted to the downscaled
TLP wind turbine, and then calibrated to best mimic the behaviour of the scaled system
observed in the tests. The model validation was achieved through comparison of key
features such as steady thrust curve, decay tests in water and dynamic response to regular
waves with and without wind. Once validated, the numerical model was used to simulate
the response to irregular waves with and without wind. The eﬀect of misalignment was
also simulated and investigated for both regular and irregular waves. Finally, a set of
extreme events tested in the laboratory was reproduced by coupling the Flex5 model
with fully nonlinear wave kinematics generated from OceanWave3D and by switching
the hydrodynamic forcing from Morison to Rainey.
The comparison between tests and simulations revealed a satisfactory match in the
surge response for all cases. The heave motion presented an acceptable correlation for
the larger sea states, while for smaller sea states the measurements were too inﬂuenced
by uncertainty and no actual comparison could be made. The pitch measurements were
==PAGE:94==
78
6 Conclusions and Future Work
proven to be unrealistic, therefore the measured and simulated pitch were not coherent.
Nonetheless, the fore-aft nacelle acceleration displayed an acceptable match.
The motion in sway, roll and yaw as well as the nacelle side-side acceleration were largely
dominated by asymmetry in the laboratory tests. The simulations only contained a
response in these degrees of freedom when asymmetry was introduced in the numerical
model, by means of turbulent wind or misaligned wave. Nevertheless, and given the
higher degree of asymmetry in the laboratory setup, the test response in these degrees
of freedom always exceeded the corresponding response in the simulation.
The line tension was generally underpredicted by the numerical model. Given that the
roll and pitch motion play an important role on the line tension, the larger tension often
observed in the tests could have been linked to the aforementioned larger response in
roll and sway for the tests.
The eﬀect of wind on the behaviour of the TLP wind turbine was investigated, and in
both tests and simulations a certain degree of damping was observed, specially for pitch
motion. The cases with misalignment showed that the motion in sway, roll and yaw,
as well as the side-side nacelle acceleration, were magniﬁed when the incident wave was
misaligned. The eﬀect of wind and misalignment was qualitatively similar in tests and
simulations.
The simulation of extreme events with the linear and nonlinear versions of the Flex5
model was inﬂuenced by the diﬀerence in the incident wave between test and simulation.
The match in time series was not particularly good, although the analysis in the frequency
domain revealed an acceptable approximation for surge, line tension and nacelle fore-aft
acceleration. The uncertainty in heave and the unreliability of the pitch signal for tests
were translated into a worse match for these degrees of freedom. The diﬀerence in results
with linear and nonlinear numerical models was not so relevant for medium waves, while
for the most extreme wave the nonlinear version seemed to be closer to the experimental
results.
This observation is coherent with the incentive to implement the nonlinear
model in the ﬁrst place, since the nonlinear eﬀects become more signiﬁcant as the wave
becomes larger.
To summarize, the present work proves the ability of the given Flex5 model to reproduce
the behaviour of a TLP wind turbine in wind and waves with a certain degree of reliability.
Its limitations, however, include:
• The use of airfoil coeﬃcients for a single value of Re, which does not correspond
to the Re range for the scaled wind turbine.
• The lack of a control strategy that would allow the dynamic adaptation of the
operational parameters (rotor speed and blade pitch) to unsteady wind conditions.
==PAGE:95==
6 Conclusions and Future Work
79
• The simplistic modelling of the hydrodynamics around the TLP, which accounts
for the ﬂoater and transition part but neglects the hydrodynamic forces on spokes
and tendons.
• The modelling of hydrodynamic forcing with the Morison or Rainey equation,
which implies the assumption of slender body and therefore neglects radiation and
diﬀraction eﬀects.
Further investigations would focus on the improvement of the numerical model by:
• Implementing the use of airfoil coeﬃcients for diﬀerent values of Re number and
the interpolation of the correct coeﬃcients for each section of the blade.
• Using a control strategy coupled with the modelling of unsteady wind conditions
such as gusts or sudden changes in wind direction.
• Modelling the hydrodynamics on the remaining parts of the TLP such as spokes
and tendons.
• Including radiation and diﬀraction eﬀects by coupling the model with a numerical
tool such as WAMIT.
• Putting more eﬀort on the correct modelling of the yaw motion.
In conclusion, the knowledge and experience acquired through this project can be used in
the future for the development of new and more reliable numerical models, as well as the
corresponding experimental setups to validate them. The ultimate goal of driving down
the costs of oﬀshore wind energy could then be achieved by improving the reliability of
the design tools for ﬂoating wind turbines.

APPENDIX B

Loads
The oﬀshore wind turbine is also subjected to hydrodynamic loads, caused by the waves
the substructure is exposed to. Hydrodynamic loads are known to have a great inﬂuence
on the motion of a ﬂoating wind turbine. This chapter gives a summary of the theory
used to model waves and hydrodynamic forcing, based on [23], [3] and [19].
B.1
Airy Theory for Linear Waves
The theory for linear waves was developed by G. B. Airy in the 19th century. Linear
waves imply a set of assumptions, such as:
• The ﬂow is inviscid, incompressible, irrotational and planar.
• The wave amplitude is small compared to the water depth.
• The water depth is constant.
Since ﬂoating wind turbines are installed in deep water it seems acceptable to use linear
wave theory when modelling TLP wind turbines exposed to small waves.

B.2
Computation of Hydrodynamic Loads
When a body is subjected to wave loads the motion of the water around it is due to
three main causes: the incident wave, the waves generated by the motion of the body
(radiation) and the diﬀraction caused by the body.
When dealing with slender bodies in water, an approximation often made considers the
wave ﬁeld is not disturbed by the presence and motion of the submerged body. In other
words, the water velocities are solely derived from the incident wave potential whereas
radiation and diﬀraction eﬀects are neglected.
Hydrodynamic Forcing
In the most basic case the hydrodynamic forcing is computed using strip theory, i.e. the
submerged body is sliced into horizontal strips and the force is computed for each strip
and later integrated over the whole height. When computing the force in each of the
strips this method considers the body has inﬁnite length, and therefore does not account
for the fact that the water ﬂows under the structure.

motion is given by the combination of linear wave theory and the Morison equation
below. The force per length on a section of the submerged body with height dz is given

DTU Wind Energy is a department of the Technical University of Denmark with a unique integration of research, education, innovation and 
public/private sector consulting in the field of wind energy. Our activities develop new opportunities and technology for the global and Danish 
exploitation of wind energy. Research focuses on key technical-scientific fields, which are central for the development, innovation and use of wind 
energy and provides the basis for advanced education at the education. 
 
We have more than 240 staff members of which approximately 60 are PhD students. Research is conducted within nine research programmes 
organized into three main topics: Wind energy systems, Wind turbine technology and Basics for wind energy.
