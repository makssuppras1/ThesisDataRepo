Abstract
This Master’s thesis examines the design and modelling of turbomachinery for an energy storage plant.
Energy storage is a key part of modern energy systems: it stabilises electrical grids, increases the
penetration of renewable energy sources and reduces the environmental footprint of power generation.
Several technologies are commercially available oﬀering ancillary, integration and seasonal energy
storage services with high eﬃciencies. These include for example, batteries, hydro pumped storage
and hydrogen. An interesting low-cost alternative, free of geographical and material limitations, is the
Pumped Thermal Electricity Storage (PTES). The present study analyses a PTES system in which air
is compressed and expanded between a hot storage reservoir and a cold storage reservoir through a
conﬁguration of centrifugal compressors and radial turbines, depending on the energy demand from
the power grid. The round trip eﬃciency of this type of systems is signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by the
eﬃciency of the turbomachinery. Several studies concerning this storage technology are available in
the literature and great attention is devoted to the study of the eﬀectiveness of the storage reservoirs
and the inﬂuence of pressure losses, however the compression and expansion devices are generally
modelled assuming constant eﬃciencies. The current report presents a procedure for modelling the
charge and discharge cycles of a novel Pumped Thermal Electricity Storage system in which rotational
speed, pressure ratios and statistical eﬃciencies of radial turbomachinery are taken into account. The
results show that particular attention must be addressed to the turbines of the discharging cycle to
obtain a high overall eﬃciency, as well as the selection of optimal rotational speed when turbines and
compressors rotate on the same shaft. Furthermore, a parametric study conducted on the system
shows how a reduction in hot storage pressure increases the overall eﬃciency of the system, reaching
an optimal value of 53.52 % with a net power output of about 2 MW, considering the system to be
adiabatic and adhering to the thermodynamic limits of the cycle.

1
Introduction
This section presents ﬁrstly an introduction to the project and the case study of the thesis, secondly
a brief review of the literature on energy storage technologies, where the principle of operation and
the typical eﬃciencies of the main storage technologies commercially available are described. Finally,
a review of the literature on radial turbomachinery with particular regard to understanding the
correlations and concepts used in the development of the models described in the methodology section.
Scope and motivation of the thesis are also included.
1.1
Project background
This master thesis was carried out in collaboration with the company Stiesdal Storage Technologies
A/S. The subject of the project was the design and modelling of turbomachinery for a thermal energy
storage system developed by Stiesdal Storage Technologies A/S. Stiesdal Storage Technologies A/S
provided data regarding their system which was used for the models developed and the study of
turbomachinery.
The GridScale thermal energy storage solution proposed by Stiesdal Storage Technologies A/S is a
pumped thermal energy storage system collocated in the capacity range of 1 MWh and above, with a
release time between 10 hours and 10 days [1]. The storage system is based on the use of crushed basalt
rocks that store energy in the form of thermal energy. Energy is supplied by the grid in the form of
electricity when production from the power generation plants is high and there is a need to store energy.
Conversely, when production from the power stations is not suﬃcient to meet the demand, electricity
is requested from the grid and released from the storage facility. The process consists of a system of
compressors and turbines which compress and expand atmospheric air between a hot storage tank
ﬁlled with the rocky material and a cold storage. The cycle can be distinguished in two conﬁgurations:
a charging cycle and a discharging cycle. The previous consists of two centrifugal compressor stages
in series with a turbocharger, the latter include a high pressure radial turbine matched with a high
pressure centrifugal compressor on the same shaft and a low pressure radial turbine matched with a low
pressure centrifugal compressor on a diﬀerent shaft. In the charge conﬁguration, air is drawn in from
the cold storage tank and compressed by the ﬁrst two compressor stages. The two compressors are
powered by an electric motor which is powered by electricity from the grid. A third compressor further
compresses the air to the hot storage tank at a pressure of 8 bar and a temperature of 540◦C. The
third compressor is powered by a turbine which closes the loop by expanding air coming from the hot
storage back to the cold storage tank at -38◦C and 2 bar pressure. Previous to the expansion process a
heat exchanger decreases the air temperature and produces district heating hot water. In the discharge
conﬁguration, hot air at 540◦C is expanded through two turbine stages, the two turbines must produce
the necessary power to be converted into electricity through generators and fed into the grid and at
the same time drive the two compressors, connected on the same axis as the two respective turbines.
The loop is closed by re compression of the air from the cold storage to the hot one. The power rating
of the system is 2 MW. A schematic of the system is illustrated in Figure 1. In the scheme the charge
cycle is shown by the blue line, green is the discharge cycle, compressors are blue, turbines are red,
the hot storage tank is orange and the cold storage tank is blue. Figure 1 shows only two compressor
stages in the charge conﬁguration, however a third compressor is present as previously described.

1.2
Energy storage technologies review
The general concept of energy storage is to capture the energy produced in a given moment to use
it at another time later on. Charging is generally referred as the process of capturing energy, while
discharging is the process of releasing the energy. Energy storage is an important element of today’s
energy systems for several reasons: it allows grid stability, increases the penetration of renewable
energy sources, improves eﬃciency of energy systems and reduces the environmental impact of energy
generation [2]. To date, several technologies exist in the market with diﬀerent levels of maturity, which
can be classiﬁed according to storage capacity and release time. Figure 2 presents an overview of the
technologies currently available on a commercial scale. The Figure shows how the represented storage
technologies can release small amounts of energy from 1 Wh for few seconds, like in capacitors, up to
several TWh of energy in the form of chemical compounds for a period of up to one year.

Figure 2: Overview of existing storage technologies [3]
Capacitors and Superconducting coils are classiﬁed as Electrical Energy Storage (EES) systems, they
are capable of charging and discharging in extremely short times and they are used for applications
where large amount of power is needed for a relatively short time.
Flywheel Energy Storage (FES) is classiﬁed as mechanical energy storage, since electrical energy is
converted to rotational kinetic energy. Flywheels are made of a rotating mass which spins at high
rotational speeds, they are able to rotate at speeds up to tens of thousands of revolutions per minute
[4] and they are mainly used for power grid stability and frequency regulation [2].
Batteries use electrochemical processes to store energy and they are deployed in several applications
especially as ancillary services. Typical applications include peak shaving, load shifting, emergency
backup for commercial applications or in residential applications for self-consumption, oﬀ-grid homes,
and emergency backups [5]. The round trip eﬃciency of Battery Energy Storage is in the range of 60
to 90% depending on the technology and the operational cycle [6].
Pumped Hydroelectric Energy Storage (PHES) and Compressed Air Energy Storage (CAES) are
mechanical energy storage technologies since the previous uses the gravitational energy and the latter
uses pressure energy. PHES stores electrical energy by pumping water to a reservoir uphill using
oﬀ-peak electricity from the grid which will be used later in time by releasing the water downhill
to drive a hydraulic turbine and produce electricity, when energy is requested by the grid. PHES
constitutes more than 99% of the global large scale energy storage installation [2]. The conversion
eﬃciency of PHES is in the range of 65% to 85% [7], [8].
CAES systems are storage power plants in which compressed air is used to store energy. They are
used for grid control, such as the provision of balancing power: when more electricity is produced
than consumed, the excess energy is used to pump air under pressure into a storage facility, when
electricity is needed, the compressed air is used to produce electricity in a gas turbine. To date, only a
few storage power plants of this type are in operation worldwide: two of the best known commercial

which have a thermal round-trip eﬃciency of 42% and 52% respectively [9].
Eventually, among the storage technologies shown in Figure 2 there is Hydrogen which is a Chemical
Energy Storage (CES). CES includes all the processes where electrical energy is converted to produce
chemical compounds. Hydrogen is an energy carrier since it cannot be found in nature, but it has to
be derived from a primary energy source and can be produced through several processes, for example
reforming of hydrocarbons and hydrolysis of water. Hydrogen has a high energy density and several
investments are underway to date to include it in the national energy systems of several countries.
Since it is stored as a chemical it can be used as seasonal energy storage.
Technologies like PHES or CAES require special geology or topography, Batteries need high investment
costs and in some cases have a short operating lifespan. An interesting alternative is the Pumped
Thermal Electricity Storage (PTES). Figure 3 presents an overview of the basic working principle of
such technology. During the charging phase the PTES uses a heat pump to convert electrical energy
into thermal energy in a hot temperature tank and in a low temperature tank in the form of sensible
heat. When electrical energy is demanded from the grid the thermal energy is converted back through
an operating ﬂuid that is heated in the hot tank and feeds a heat engine that releases electrical energy
through an electric generator. The heat pump and heat engine processes generally occur through a
conﬁguration of compressors, turbines and heat exchangers. Examples of working ﬂuid used in studies
of this type of storage system were Carbon Dioxide [10], Air [11] or Argon [12]. Two of the main
advantage of PTES are: it is free of geographical constraints that characterise for example Pumped
Hydro storage and it is a scalable technology: the more tanks are used, the more heat can be stored.
Several studies have been conducted on this type of storage system, showing promising results in terms
of overall eﬃciency. Morandin et al. [13] gave in a thermoeconomic study of a PTES based on a
transcritical CO2 cycle, a maximum technical limit of 64% of turn round eﬃciency. Mercangoz et al.
[10] show in a thermodynamic analysis of a trans-critical CO2 cycle a limit of overall eﬃciency of 74%.
A PTES demonstration facility built in the United Kingdom at Newcastle University has a rated power
output of 150 kW, a storage capacity of 600 kWh and uses Argon as working ﬂuid. The achieved round
trip eﬃciency in 2019 was 65% [14].

Radial turbomachinery review
In this section, a brief overview of radial turbomachinery for compressible ﬂuids is presented. The
main concepts necessary to understand the equations and correlations of radial machines used in the
models developed for this thesis are explained.
Page 5 of 46
==PAGE:11==

Turbomachinery describes those devices in which energy is transferred either to, or from, a continuously
ﬂowing ﬂuid by the dynamic action of one or more moving blade rows [16]. The two main categories of
turbomachinery are: those that absorb energy to increase ﬂuid pressure (compressors) and those that
produce energy by expanding the ﬂuid from a high to a lower pressure (turbines). A row of rotating
blades is called rotor or impeller and the purpose of the rotor is to reduce or increase the total enthalpy
value of the ﬂuid ﬂowing between the blades depending on the purpose of the machine. In addition to
the rotor blades, a radial turbomachine usually has a row of ﬁxed, non-rotating blades called stator or
nozzle vane, which receive the inlet ﬂuid and direct it towards the inlet of the rotor in the case of a
turbine, or guides the ﬂuid at the outlet of the rotor to a collecting volute or a downstream component
in the case of a compressor. One row of rotor blades together with a row of stator vanes make up a
stage of the turbomachine, a multistage turbomachine is therefore made of multiple stages in series. A
radial compressor is also called centrifugal compressor because it takes advantage of the maximum
amount of centrifugal force that the impeller develops. Radial turbomachines are so called because
the ﬂow path occurs primarily in a plane perpendicular to the axis of rotation, as opposed to axial
machines in which the ﬂow path occurs mainly parallel to the axis of rotation. Figure 4 and 5 present
a typical centrifugal compressor and radial turbine geometry.

1.4
Motivation and scope of the thesis
On 12 December 2015, representatives of 195 countries of the world at the end of the 21st Conference
(COP21) of the countries that have signed the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate
Change (UNFCCC), signed in the French capital the treaty that provides as a long-term goal to contain
the increase in global temperature well below 2 ° C at the end of the century and pursue eﬀorts to
limit the increase to 1.5 ° C compared to pre-industrial levels. To do this, more and more electricity
from renewable energy sources will have to be used. I chose this topic and project because energy
storage is one of the missing pieces in the process of decarbonising the European and world’s electricity
grids and there is a lot of work to be done in this regard.
The scope of the thesis was to ﬁnd an optimal turbomachinery conﬁguration for the system developed
by Stiesdal Storage Technologies A/S, based on the statistical eﬃciencies of radial turbomachinery
found in the literature and having the data provided by Stiesdal Storage Technologies as starting data.
Diﬀerent conﬁgurations in terms of machine speed and thermodynamic parameters such as temperature
and pressure have been studied with the aim of achieving maximum round trip eﬃciency of the storage
system.
2
Methodology
In this section the equations, correlations and parameters used to establish an optimal conﬁguration
of turbomachinery in the storage system are presented and explained. The isentropic eﬃciencies of
radial turbines and centrifugal compressors were determined based on statistical correlations found
in the literature and then adjusted based on the thermodynamic conditions found in the cycle. The
statistical eﬃciencies of the compressors were calculated in a MATLAB [27] script and than transferred
to an automated procedure in Microsoft Excel [28]. Radial turbines’ eﬃciencies were also calculated
according to statistical values and correlations found in the literature. Thermophysical properties
of the working ﬂuid at all the stations of the system were found using CoolProp. CoolProp is a
thermophysical property database and wrapper for a selection of programming environments [29].
CoolProp was implemented through Microsoft Excel, were the models of both charge and discharge
cycles were set. A parametric analysis was then performed by changing the hot storage pressure and
ﬁnally a local sensitivity analysis was carried out by varying the isentropic eﬃciency of each individual
machine. Both charge and discharge cycle models were developed starting from the data provided in
Table 1 for the base case scenario. Several assumptions were made:
• No pressure losses throughout system,
• Adiabatic systems: no heat losses to the environment,
• Only internal energy losses in turbomachinery,
• Working ﬂuid: standard dry air,
• Constant air mass ﬂow rate,
• No inter-cooling between compression stages,
• The turbomachinery were modelled as single stage machines.

in several years of studies on radial type machines. In the following subsections the correlations which

2.2
Charge cycle
The charge cycle of the storage system was modelled with the turbomachinery conﬁguration shown in
Figure 14. According to this layout, air is heated in the cold storage unit and enter the ﬁrst compression
stage at point 1, then it is compressed through other two compression stages and delivered to the hot
storage tank at point 4 where it transfers thermal energy to the storage material at constant pressure.
The air is then cooled in a heat exchanger and is expanded in a turbine from point 5 to point 6. The
air enters the cold storage unit at very low temperature and the process starts again. The scope of the
Turbine is to expand and cool the air and drive Compressor 3, therefore to reduce the compression
work necessary to compress the air from the cold to the hot storage. The remaining work has to be
performed by Compressor 1 and 2 which are powered by an electric motor. Compressor 3 and the
Turbine spin at the same rotational speed on the same shaft, whereas Compressor 2 and 1 rotate on
another axle.

2.3
Discharge cycle
The discharge cycle of the storage system has a similar working principle to a Bryton-Joule cycle, where
the air is compressed by two compression stages, heated by an external heat source, in this case the
hot storage tank, and the turbines expand the air to ambient condition and produce power, however in
this system the air is expanded to the cold storage where it is cooled down to the cold storage design
temperature. The turbomachinery conﬁguration which performs the discharging process is presented
in Figure 15, where HP stands for high pressure and LP for low pressure. Point 4 is the inlet of the
HP Turbine where hot air comes in from the hot storage and point 6 is the outlet of the LP Turbine
where air is discharged to the cold storage tank, point 3 is the outlet of the HP Compressor where
air is pushed to the hot storage and point 1 is the inlet of the LP Compressor, where air is sucked
from the cold storage tank. HP Compressor and HP Turbine rotate on the same shaft, whereas LP
Compressor and LP Turbine spin on another shaft.

2.5
Overall storage eﬃciency
The overall performance of the storage system was evaluated by calculating the round trip eﬃciency.
The round trip eﬃciency is deﬁned as the ratio of the electricity output from the storage device
to the electricity input to the device during one charge/discharge cycle [2]. Since the system was
considered to be adiabatic with no losses towards the external environment from the storage tanks
and the compression and expansion devices, the deﬁnition takes only into account the internal energy
losses of compressors and turbines. Considering the same mass ﬂow rate for both charge and discharge
cycle, the same charging and discharging time, and the same temperature levels between charge and
discharge as described in the previous Section, the round trip eﬃciency can be determined as:
ηround−trip = Pdischarge
Pcharge
(115)
where Pdischarge is the net power output of the discharge cycle, determined by Equation 104 and Pcharge
is the power required by the charge cycle to compress the air from the cold storage tank to the hot
storage tank, as in Equation 59.
2.6
Parametric analysis
A parametric analysis of the entire charge/discharge cycle was carried out with the purpose of analysing
how the round trip eﬃciency of the storage system was aﬀected as the hot storage pressure changes
(p04 and p05 in the charge cycle). The analysis started with the base case, then the hot storage pressure
in the charge cycle was gradually decreased and the round trip eﬃciency calculated for each case. The
procedure ﬁrstly involved the establishment of the optimal point to minimise the power required by the
charge cycle according to the procedure described in Subsection 2.2, secondly the optimal discharge
cycle conﬁguration was determined through the procedure presented in the Subsection 2.3 and by
choosing few values of the HP and LP turbines speciﬁc speed so as to maximise the power output
and ﬁnally the round trip eﬃciency was calculated for the optimal conﬁgurations with equation 115.
One important parameter was checked for each scenario, such as the Turbine inlet temperature in the
charge cycle (T05). Since the inlet of the Turbine is connected to a heat exchanger that produce hot
water for the district heating grid, the Turbine inlet temperature should not be lower than the return
temperature of the water from the district heating. The case in which the turbine inlet temperature is
too close to the return temperature of the district heating water, constitutes the limit case.
2.7

A sensitivity analysis was performed to study how uncertainties in the output of the model, in this
case the round-trip eﬃciency, can be apportioned to diﬀerent sources of uncertainty in the model input.
The chosen model input was the isentropic eﬃciency of the turbomachinery and the output was the
round trip eﬃciency. The simulation was carried out on the base case scenario.

4
Discussion
Results from the storage charge and discharge models, the optimisation process, and the parametric
study were presented in Section 3. This section is devoted to discussion of the results and comparison
with other work in the literature and recommendation of future work. Some considerations regarding
the assumptions made in the models need to be discussed. First of all, the statistical correlation used
to calculate the isentropic eﬃciency of the turbines was a correlation based on total-to-static eﬃciency
values, this implies that the kinetic energy output to the turbine is not usefully utilised. For some
turbines, for example the turbine in the charging cycle, this assumption may be considered true since
the purpose of the turbine is to recover energy (in addition to expanding the ﬂuid at the pressure of the
cold tank), while for others such as the turbines in the discharge cycle it will in most cases be deemed
as a bad assumption. a bad assumption, especially for the HP turbine since the outlet energy of it
is equivalent to the inlet energy of the LP turbine. Whitﬁeld and Baines [20] mention in their book
that the diﬀerence can be considerable, a radial turbine might have an eﬃciency based on a stagnation
outlet pressure of 85% while one based on a static outlet pressure of 75%. Simulations based on a
steady-ﬂow mean-line model of a radial in-ﬂow turbine show total-to-total eﬃciency values of almost
90% while the corresponding total-to-static eﬃciency values are around 82% [18]. This assumption on
turbines eﬃciencies may have given a downwards estimate of the overall storage eﬃciency. Secondly,
it should be noted that the charging and discharging cycle times were not taken into account and
the same mass ﬂow rate was considered for both the charge and discharge cycle. By adjusting the
mass ﬂow rate of the discharge cycle, the net power output could be oﬀset to 2MW, thus the overall
eﬃciency had room for improvement. In addition, as explained in the Subsection 2.1.2, considering
the working ﬂuid as an ideal gas was ascertained to be a bad assumption, hence the calculation of
static pressures and temperatures at turbines outlets with the ideal gas assumption would have yielded
inaccurate results. Despite these drawbacks, the assumption of the correlation used for the turbine’s
eﬃciency is acceptable for the purpose of determining all the thermodynamic states of the cycle. The
calculations performed can be considered for a preliminary design of the turbomachinery, however
a detailed analysis of the turbomachinery with mean-line models or 3D ﬂuid dynamic analysis is
recommended to get a better estimate of the machine’s performance.
A further signiﬁcant assumption that may have inﬂuenced the overall eﬃciency of the plant is the
criterion of work distribution among the machines. The distribution of the work performed by the
turbomachinery can be based on diﬀerent criteria: by setting the pressure ratio of each machine, the
speciﬁc speed or the speciﬁc work. For Compressor 1 and Compressor 2 in the charge cycle the chosen
criteria was to have the same speciﬁc speed for both compressors. In this way the optimal polytropic
eﬃciency was assumed for both compressors, however the isentropic eﬃciency for a given polytropic
eﬃciency depends on the pressure ratio, that is to say the isentropic eﬃciency of Compressor 1 was
penalised due to uneven distribution of pressure ratio between the two compressors. As far as the
discharge cycle is concerned, the modelling criteria was chosen to have the same speciﬁc work among the
turbomachinery. A diﬀerent modelling approach of the discharge cycle was also investigated, where the
same pressure ratio was maintained among the machines. However, no signiﬁcant improvements were
found. Furthermore, the choice of having the same work distribution among the machines facilitated
the convergence of the discharge model and helped to have more consistent results. For instance, the
constraint of having the same speciﬁc work for both the HP and LP compressor, was applied. The

As mentioned in the Methodology section (2), several assumptions were made in the models created,
in particular that of having an adiabatic system, only internal energy losses to the turbomachines
were considered and pressure losses throughout the cycle were not taken into account. In reality, the
system is characterised by the above-mentioned sources of energy loss. White et al [12] carried out
a thermodynamic analysis of a PTES system. The working principle is equal to the one analysed
in this report, however with a simpler turbomachinery conﬁguration, since only one compressor and
one expander with a ﬁxed polytropic eﬃciency were deployed and two heat exchangers to reject heat
due to entropy generation. Argon was considered as working ﬂuid. In the study, major energy loss
sources were listed to be ﬁrstly compression and expansion losses, secondly thermal reservoir losses,
then electrical and mechanical losses, ﬁnally pressure losses. Electrical and mechanical losses due to
energy conversion through turbomachinery are cited to be around 10% or more in terms of round-trip
eﬃciency reduction. Pressure losses occur through piping, valves, diﬀusers of turbomachinery and at
the hot and cold storage, however these were found to have a small impact on the system. Thermal
energy losses to the reservoirs occur because the stored energy is degraded over time due to the ﬁnite
temperature diﬀerence with the external environment. McTigue et al [34] carried out a parametric
study and optimisation of the previously mentioned PTES system analysed by White et al [12]. The
system analysed in the study has a 16 MWh energy storage capacity at nominal conditions, with a 2
MW net power output. It is characterised by a pressure ratio between hot and cold reservoirs of 10,
a nominal hot storage temperature of 778 K (505◦C), a nominal cold storage temperature of 123 K
(-150◦C) and a mass ﬂow rate of 13.7 kg/s. After optimisation, the results show how the round-trip
eﬃciency mainly depends on the eﬃciency of the compression and expansion processes, while pressure
losses and heat transfer processes contribute only a few percentage points. The study states that
considering typical turbomachinery eﬃciencies, the overall round-trip eﬃciency of the system is unlikely
to exceed 50%, whereas considering reciprocating devices the round-trip eﬃciency would be less than
70% (including mechanical and electrical losses). As a comparison to the study case made by McTigue
et al, the case analysed in the parametric analysis in Section 3.4 where the hot storage pressure of the
charge cycle is 6 bar was considered. The hot storage pressure of the discharge cycle is increased by
3.12 bar, i.e. 9.12 bar in total (see Figure 31). The pressure ratio of the discharge cycle is therefore 9.12
for the present model, while in the McTigue et al [34] study it was 10 and the same between charge
and discharge cycles. The diﬀerence between hot and cold storage design temperatures in the model of
this thesis was 578 K, while in the study considered it was 655 K. The eﬃciency calculated with the
models of this thesis is 53.52 % (see Figure 33). The result is in line with the one produced by the
study by McTigue et al, however in the models developed in this thesis no pressure losses, heat losses
and mechanical or electrical losses were considered. By contrast, in the model analysed by McTigue et
al a constant polytropic eﬃciency equal to 0.9 for both machines was assumed, whereas in the models
developed in this thesis the turbomachinery eﬃciency was determined considering rotational speed and
pressure ratio. A performance and parametric study on a Brayton PTES system [35] demonstrated
how the round trip eﬃciency and the net power produced by the system increment as the nominal
hot storage temperature increases. This means that the performance of a PTES system is essentially
determined by the ratio of the highest temperature in the hot storage to the lowest temperature in
the cold storage [12], [14]. In the analysis carried out by Davenne and Peters [14], it is noted that the
eﬃciency of a PTES drops sharply when the eﬃciency of the turbines is reduced compared to the case
when the eﬃciency of the compressors is reduced, because the irreversibilities in the compressor lead to
a higher output temperature, namely high temperature useful heat that can be stored in hot storage,

thermodynamic analysis carried out on the system considered for this thesis and the comparison with
systems analysed in the literature, the following recommendations for Stiesdal Storage Technologies

5
Conclusion
A Pumped Thermal Electricity Storage system was analysed for this Master’s thesis project, with
particular regard to the modelling of the turbomachinery. The operating principle of the system was
that of a heat pump during the charge cycle and of a heat engine during the discharge cycle. The two
cycles occur thanks to two decoupled conﬁgurations of radial turbines and centrifugal compressors
compressing and expanding air between a hot storage reservoir with a maximum temperature of 540°C
and a cold storage reservoir with a minimum temperature of -38°C. The charging and discharging
cycles were modelled by taking into account the rotational speed of the turbomachines, the pressure
ratios and the statistical eﬃciencies found in the literature of radial turbomachinery. The system was
assumed to be adiabatic, with no pressure losses and only internal energy losses were considered. Both
charge and discharge cycles were optimised in terms of rotational speed and pressure ratios and the
optimum round trip eﬃciency was determined to be 43.52% in the base case scenario with a hot storage
pressure of 8 bar. A parametric study of the system demonstrated that the round-trip eﬃciency and
the net power produced by the plant increased as the hot storage pressure decreased in the charge
cycle, reaching a peak of 53.52% for the previous and of 1.98 MW for the latter, maintaining the same
temperature levels and the same mass ﬂow rate between the charge and discharge cycle. Through a
local sensitivity analysis, it was established that the most sensitive component of the system in terms
of round-trip eﬃciency variation was the low-pressure turbine of the discharge cycle. Increasing the
eﬃciency of the above-mentioned component by 2% yielded an increment of overall eﬃciency of 1.72%.
If the other machines in the discharge cycle were also optimised, they oﬀered a signiﬁcant increment in
round trip eﬃciency, with the low-pressure compressor, the high-pressure turbine and the high-pressure
compressor in order of importance. The results were then compared with studies in the literature and
found to be in accordance with the performance of similar storage systems.
