Preface
Offshore Wind energy is a promising source of energy production where the strong
offshore winds can be fully utilized to get clean and renewable source of energy. Mono
pile is still largely used a foundation type but in deeper waters multiple footing option
seems to better serve the purpose. Thus jacket structures with suction caissons tend
to be a viable option. For the dynamic analysis of the structure, dynamic impedance‚Äôs
need to be calculated first by taking the soil structure interaction in to effect.
The aim of this thesis is to analyze the dynamic response of the suction caissons
by using finite element computer program (Abaqus) where the soil foundation system is
model. The analysis is performed for a given frequency range where the interaction of
the caisson with the surrounding soil is analyzed. The surrounding soil is assumed to be
linear, visco-elastic mater with hysteratic type damping that is independant of frequency.
The surrounding soil is characterized to be finite soil that surrounds the caisson and the
far field soil is modeled as absorbing boundary to avoid and reflections.
Two different type of analysis are performed to obtain the dynamic impedance‚Äôs
where in the first case a unit displacement is applied to the caisson head where as, in the
other analysis a unit rotation is applied to get the impedance‚Äôs at the level of caisson
head. The comparison is made between analytical results obtained from modeling in
Abaqus with analytical solutions analyzed in Matlab. A series of parametric study is
done to account for the effect of different parameters on the dynamic impedance‚Äôs of the
suction caisson.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Human activity on planet earth is considered to be a major contributor towards global
warming which tends to overburden our atmosphere with unwanted gasses resulting in
ozone layer depletion. As a consequence, the temperature of the earth is increasing dras-
tically resulting in devastating effects on the environment, climate, and well being of its
inhabitants. Energy production from fossil fuels is one big contributor to this problem
and is being discussed everywhere at different forums and different suggestions are also
being made to consider more viable and environment friendly alternatives. Power gener-
ation from renewable energy resources rather than fossil fuel is gaining more popularity
due to its significance, public health benefits and minimal pollution issues [1].
Global energy demand tends to increase at the rate of 21% per anum until 2021.
There is an ongoing race throughout the world to fulfill the energy needs via renewable
energy sources leading to a quick development of offshore wind power; one of the most
favourable future contender. Wind power generation started onshore but factors like
greater space and consistent wind flow resulting into 40% higher energy output than
onshore, opened the door for offshore wind power generation. Since the first offshore
power plant was installed 25 years ago, the present turbine generation capacity is 15
times more as compared to its predecessor [2].
In recent years, Europe, in general, had a huge offshore wind power expansion with
3,230 wind turbines in 11 countries by the end of 2015. China is also speeding up its
wind power generation and had 246 offshore wind turbines as per 2014. Whereas USA
made its entry into offshore by developing its first ever offshore wind farm off the coast
of Rhode Island in 2016. With this positive trend and increased investment, there has
been a 60% decrease in the prices since 2000 [3].
Table 1.1 gives a brief overview of major offshore wind farm activity carried out in
Europe during 2015, where a total of 3,018.5 MW was connected to the main grid which
was 108% more as compared to 2014. Of these 3,018.5 MW, 86.1% contribution was
from the North Sea, 9.2% from Baltic Sea and 4.7% from the Irish Sea [3].

Motivation
Offshore wind power is relatively a new concept but it has a promising future because
of its effectiveness, latest advancement in technology and cost competitiveness due to
effective cost reductions. It is therefore, becoming a very efficient source of power gener-
ation and is gaining popularity around the world. Figure 1.1 best describes the average
offshore wind farm capacity, its potential and the average rotor diameter up to date
since the idea first emerged back in 1991 [2].

As offshore wind turbines (OWT) distance to the shore increases, so do the number
of challenges. These super giants thrive and operate in harsh marine environments for
decades and even some of the the favourable aspects like high offshore wind speeds might
tend to destabilize the structure. Besides that, turbine parts tend to increase in size to

As previously discussed, OWT¬īs are becoming quite a popular source of energy

A wind turbine from a distant view, might look like a simple structure but from the

suction caisson especially for jacket structures. Loading conditions in offshore environ-
ment is mostly dynamic and for this reason dynamic behavior of suction caissons is
studied.
1.2
Methodology
To evaluate the dynamic impedance functions of the suction caisson, finite element
computer program (Abaqus), for finite element modeling and analytical solutions will
be used. The dynamic impedance‚Äôs can be found from the analysis in the frequency
domain and the reason of doing so is to further used them in the dynamic structural
analysis.
Work on numerical modeling is started by doing a series of checks in order to make
sure that the numerical models work in the best possible way. To start with, a mesh
refinement study is done in order to make sure that there are no discrepancies/flaws
due to meshing. After gaining enough confidence, a validation of numerical modeling
approach is done by comparing it with analytical solutions and this is done to make sure
that the numerical modeling approach is accurate.
As there are no analytical solutions available for suction caissons, proposed solutions
for piles are to be used to investigate how far are the results of caissons from the results
of the piles and also if these solutions can still be used within certain approximation for
suction caissons.

1.3
Scope of work
After doing all the validation and in order to make sure that the numerical models give
accurate results, a series parametric study is done to investigate the effects of different
aspects like the effect ofslenderness ratio, Diameter effect and the height of the soil layer.
The effect of slenderness ratio is analyzed in order to observe the transition between
suction caisson and the pile behavior. Diameter effect is analyzed in order to see if this
parameter is normalized and changing diameter shows same or different behavior; as
in the current study, caisson diameter of 2 or 4 meters are investigated and sometimes
diameter can be 8 or 10 meters. Similarly, height of the soil layer is also analyzed in
order to check if this parameter can be normalized or not. in the current study, height
of the soil layer of 15 and 30 meters are investigated but in reality it can be any height,
smaller or larger than these chosen heights.
As mentioned earlier, dynamic impedance‚Äôs are first evaluated by using numerical
modeling approach. Later on, by using analytical formulations for floating piles pre-
sented by Latini et al. (2015) [4] in Matlab, analytical results are compared with the
numerical approach in order to find the closeness of the two methods of analysis [5].

2.1
General Introduction
Suction caissons or suction buckets have been used as foundation for offshore oil and gas
platforms over the years. Being comparatively cost effective and easy to install, they are
now used as foundations for offshore wind turbine structures (OWT). In its early days,
OWT¬īs were considered to be an expensive source of power generation when compared
to other sources like solar, biomas, Ownshore, coal etc.
but it is rapidly becoming
competitive with other power generation techniques. In order to make it a more cost
effective process, more and more research is being done to reduce the overall weight of
the wind turbines and its related structural components. This results in wind turbine
structures that are more flexible as well as more susceptible to dynamic excitations [6].
Most of the present offshore wind farms are in shallow waters; depth up to 20 meters.
In order to fully benefit and to get the maximum output from this renewable source of
energy production, wind turbines with higher generation capacity (bigger turbines) and
further away from shore (deep water wind farms in depths up to 40 meter and above) are
in pipeline. Monopile foundation is exclusively used as the foundation type for almost
all the onshore wind turbines which is relatively quite expensive and involves problems
like noise pollution produced from hammer hitting the pile while the foundation is being

2.2
Foundations for OWT¬īs
A suction caisson can be best described as a lid or cap of a bottle or and inverted bucket
that is embedded in the sea bed as an offshore structure foundation.
It is made of
steel and varies in skirt length and diameter as per design requirements. The complete
assembly consists of 3 or 4 suction buckets attached to a jacket structure which provides
the connecting point for the wind turbine tower. The whole assembly is made offshore
and is transported via ship to the installation point and with the help of a crane, it
is lifted and placed into the sea bed. This technique is best suited for seabeds with
soft clays or similar low strength soils and due to this factor, small embeddment is
automatically achieved due to the self weight of the whole jacket structure [8], [11].
Each suction bucket has a valve attached to the lid and once the penetration due
to self-weight stops, further embeddment is achieved by creating a pressure difference
with in the caisson skirt by opening the valves and pumping out all the water trapped
inside the caisson. Once the embeddment depth is achieved, the valves are closed and
the remaining gap between the caisson lid and the inner soil is filled by pumping a high
strength grout material. This provides some extra strength to the foundation system
to avoid pull out as the overhead structure undergoes heavy loading in stormy weather
conditions [11].
As mentioned above, the whole installation process is relatively very easy and as

As mentioned earlier, suction caissons have been quite extensively used as foundation
type for jacket structures in oil and gas industry. But the recent development of using this
type of foundation in OWT structures started a new discussion regarding their design
requirements. It is important to mention here that the loading patterns on offshore oil
and gas structures differ a lot as compared to the ones encountered by OWT. First and
the foremost difference is the depth at which these structures are located; OWT¬īs are
mainly in shallow waters as compared to oil and gas structures even though a lot of
research is going on to reach deeper waters (depth up 40 m and beyond) to increase the
generation capacity. Apart from that, OWT¬īs are lighter in weight as compared to oil

2.4 Dynamic Soil structure interaction

and gas platforms and the studies have shown that horizontal loads and the resulting
overturning moments are quite large in these structures and in some cases, approximately
60% of the total vertical load [15], [8].
When considering these horizontal loads, the major contribution is mainly from the
waves and the minor from the wind, but as the wind load has a quite large moment arm
as compared to the wave load that acts just a few meters above the foundation level,
its contribution to overturning moment is dominated. An OWT structure apart from
its foundation mainly consists of a tower, rotor and blades. When a turbine is fully
functional, it comes under the action of harmonic load from the rotor. The resulting
excitation frequency is referred to be the 1st resonance frequency which is 2.08 Hz, where
as the the 2nd resonance frequency for a three bladed turbine usually referred as blade
passing frequency is 6.25 Hz which is 3 times the 1st resonance frequency. So in order
to avoid the phenomena of resonance in the structure at these resonance frequencies,
deformation and stiffness properties of the foundation system are evaluated. As the
structure undergoes harmonic excitation, foundation system should also be designed for
fatigue criterion which is beyond the scope of this thesis [8], [6].
It is important to recognize that in the design of a turbine foundation, ultimate
capacity is not the dominant consideration but in fact, stiffness and performance under
fatigue loading is are the governing criterion‚Äôs and need to be addressed carefully [8],
[16].
2.4
Dynamic Soil structure interaction
Most of the structures that we see in our everyday life are categorized as structures
on the ground. Apart from that, some of these structures have a considerable portion
beneath the ground that can not be seen. OWT can be a good example as the foundation
(suction caisson in the present scenario) is embedded in the ground.
If a structure founded on a very stiff soil is subjected to an earthquake where the
soil has extremely high stiffness compared to the stiffness of the structure, the resultant
ground motion has no impact on the structure and is described as free-field motion. The
behaviour would be completely different if there is a layer of soft soil supporting the
structure and the dynamic response of the structure would result in deformation of the
soil. Such a process where response of the structure will influence the motion of the
soil and the response of the soil influencing the motion of the structure is described as
soil-structure interaction (SSI) [17].
The significance of SSI varies from structure to structure. A simplified and basic
description of this phenomena can be best described by a single degree of freedom
system (SDOF) mounted on rigid, massless, L-shaped foundation supported on elastic
soil where m is the mass of the structure, k being stiffness and c the damping coefficient.
If the material supporting is in accordance, the foundation will be able to rotate and
translate freely. The damping and stiffness parameters of soil and foundation assembly

gorized into two different phenomena:
‚ÄĘ Kinematic Interaction
‚ÄĘ Inertial Interaction
Kinematic Interaction:
In the case of an earthquake for a free field motion, displacements occur in vertical and
horizontal directions simultaneously. For embedded foundations, where the foundation
has very high stiffness as compared to the surrounding soil, it can not follow free-field
deformation patterns and thus its motion is influenced by kinematic interaction even if
the foundation has no mass. The idea is further elaborated in figure 2.4 where the mass
less rigid foundation does not follow the vertical varying horizontal free-field motion and
the motion of foundation is influenced by kinematic interaction.

same time despite the fact that the free-field motion being purely translational. For a
different frequency where the wavelength is smaller, rotation of the foundation can take
place. Similarly, the horizontally propagating waves can result in torsional vibration of
the foundation. The whole phenomenon is best described in figure 2.5 (a) and (b) below.

Mylonakis (2001):
In 2001, Mylonakis [19] presented an improved model of Novak (1974) [20] and discussed
its limitations. The simplicity of the model was preserved i.e, linear behaviour of the
surrounding soil and the shaft, but accounted for the normal and shear stresses acting on
the upper and lower face of the horizontal soil layer slice. These stresses were included
in the analysis by integrating the governing equations over the thickness of the soil layer
thus resulting into equations that are similar to the ones of the plain-strain model.
The study was based on large diameter cylindrical shafts surrounded by homogeneous
soil resting on a rigid base. The shaft head is subjected to dynamic loading; harmonic
vertical/horizontal displacement or rotation. The shaft is assumed to be long enough;
(L > 4d), so that the flexural deformations are dominant when shaft undergoes lateral
vibrations. The vertical shear stresses developed along the soil-shaft interface are con-
sidered to be negligible that come into effect due to lateral rocking vibrations. Following
limitations were identified by Mylonakis for the plain-strain model of Novak:
‚ÄĘ There is rapid decrease in stiffness when frequency is in the lower range and it
reaches zero when frequency is zero and thus the static stiffness of the model

3.1
Numerical Model
Numerical studies are computer-based techniques that take into account variational and
interpolation methods for modeling and then effectively solving boundary value problems
associated with distributed parameter vibration problems. The method used to solve
these problems is called finite element method that we in our everyday discussion refer
to as FEM. This method can analyse almost any structure or a part of it, that might
involve a very complicated or unusual geometry. The whole concept is based on an
approximation method where the structure under consideration is sub divided into a
number of small parts called finite elements and this division process is referred to as
discretization. This discretization process results in a grid-like pattern of the structure
usually known as finite element mesh and is made up of multiple elements. Each element
is connected to the neighboring element via endpoints that are called nodes and these
nodes are actually the points that define the boundary of an element.
==PAGE:40==
26
3 Methodology
Computer programs based on FEM are useful tools that are used to solve vibration
problems where equation of motion for each individual finite element is solved. These
solution are then combined in an assembly procedure that gives important properties
like mass and stiffness. The resulting displacement vectors thus give the mode shape of
the structure at each vibration mode and the frequency of interest for each vibration
mode is called natural frequency [22].
3D numerical models are designed in Abaqus to analyse the dynamic behavior of
suction caisson foundations surrounded by the homogeneous soil layer. The geometry
of the model is such that it consists of a suction caisson (which is a two part assembly
made up of a shell and a rigid cap) at the center of the axisymetric soil-foundation
model. The soil contained inside the caisson is defined as inner soil. All the surrounding
soil is characterized as outer soil which is an axisymmetric cylindrical half-space with a
hole at the top for the suction caisson and the inner soil. By using different partition
sets, this outer soil is further divided into the finite soil domain that surrounds the
caisson and the far field infinite soil domain that acts as a quite boundary in order to
avoid wave reflections. Different sets and surfaces are defined for the caisson, inner soil
and the outer soil and these predefined characteristics become useful in the definition of
boundary conditions.

and the inner soil are located at the axis of the model.

Element type:
As mentioned earlier, numerical model consist of three main parts and the type of
element for each part are listed below:
‚ÄĘ Suction caisson is made up of a shell with a rigid cap at the top. Both the shell and
the cap are discretized as S4 elements that is an abbreviation for shell elements
with 4 nodes.

of continuum finite elements have a linear shape function with 8 integration points.
‚ÄĘ Infinite soil domain serves the purpose of a quite boundary for the neighboring fi-
nite soil domain. In case of harmonic excitations, Rayleigh waves are generated and
these infinite elements being orthogonal to the direction of travelling waves have
tendency to absorb energy out of the finite element boundary and can be therefore
attributed as damping domain. The element are discretized as CIN3D8 which is an
abbreviation for 8 nodes 3D continuum infinite element with 8 integration points.

Parametric study
A series parametric study is done to investigate the effects of different aspects like the
effect ofslenderness ratio, Diameter effect and the height of the soil layer. The effect of

and the pile behavior. Diameter effect is analyzed in order to check if this parameter is

be 8 or 10 meters. Similarly, height of the soil layer is also analyzed in order to check if
this parameter can be normalized or not. In the current study, height of the soil layer of
15 and 30 meters are investigated but in reality it can be any height, smaller or larger
than these chosen heights.
The aim of the study is to investigate the dynamic response of suction caisson under-
going harmonic excitation for a given frequency range. In the parametric study, dynamic
impedance‚Äôs for d = 2m & d = 4m are investigated considering different dimensional

CHAPTER 4
Results & Discussion
The results of the parametric study are presented in this chapter and to start with,
reference case 2 is discussed in detail. Apart from that, all the three major aspects of
the parametric study are analyzed and different cases are compared to see the effect of
diameter, effect of slenderness ratio and the effect of the height of the soil layer on the
dynamic impedance of the suction caisson foundation where different dimensional and
non-dimensional parameters are also accounted for.
4.1
Reference Case/ Case 2
The reference case investigated in this chapter is case 2 from the parametric study table
3.4 as shown in the previous chapter. Due to the symmetry of the problem in hand, only
half of the soil-foundation system is modeled. The numerical model designed in Abaqus
consists of a suction caisson which is a two part foundation: a rigid cap (lid) at the top
attached to the flexible skirt where the thickness of rigid cap is five times the thickness
of skirt. The soil inside the caisson is defined as inner soil where as the surrounding
outer continuum soil consists of finite and infinite soil. The caisson shell and cap are
modeled as four node shell elements (S4), where as the inner and the surrounding finite
soil are modeled as eight node 3D continuum infinite elements (C3D8). Far field infinite
soil acts as quite boundary and it is modeled as eight nodes 3D continuum elements
(CIN3D8).
All the boundary conditions and interactions of the reference case 2 are similar to the
ones described in section 3.1 of the previous chapter where caisson, inner soil and the
outer soil are tied together to make sure that the displacement compatibility between
the three parts. The mesh characteristics of this model are similar to those of medium
mesh as previously shown in table 3.2 and the soil-foundation model has 587928 degrees
of freedom. Steady state linearized response of the model undergoing harmonic load
for a given frequency range (f = 0.009 ‚ąí15Hz), is performed. Two different types of
loads are applied; at first a unit horizontal displacement is applied and in the second

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
Suction caisson can prove to be a smart and economical foundation type that can be
used in the offshore wind industry for jacket structures. As a lot of work has been done
to analyse the static behavior of the suction caisson, an effort was made to analyse the
dynamic behavior of caisson using numerical and analytical solutions.
From the mesh refinements study, it is observed that the mesh type plays an im-
portant role in predicting the dynamic impedance‚Äôs of the suction caisson where a finer
mesh gives very good results but depending upon the geometry of the problem in hand
(as in the current case the soil domain has a diameter of 720 meters), using fine mesh
extends the computational times a lot. Considering this factor, a medium mesh was
used in the current study as the two mesh types predicted quite close results.
Some important conclusion were drawn from the parametric study are listed below:
‚ÄĘ It is observed that there is no effect of slenderness ratio on the translational stiffness
and damping component Ksu & 2ő∂su. For the coupling and rocking stiffness Ksu
& Ksőł, and their respective damping components 2ő∂sőł & 2ő∂mőł, no difference is
observed for of slenderness ratio 5 and above. For Hp/d ‚©ĺ5, slenderness ratio
shows no impact on the dynamic impedance‚Äôs.
‚ÄĘ Height of the soil layer shows an impact on the dynamic impedance‚Äô where for the
same slenderness ratio and reducing the height of the soil layer by half, a higher
drop in stiffness is observed for all the three stiffness components but for coupling
and rocking component the stiffness increases in the higher frequency range. For
all the three damping components, higher damping ratio is is observed for less soil
layer height. This means that reducing the soil layer reduces the stiffness but gives
a higher damping. Thus it is the height of the soil layer that governs the dynamic
response.
‚ÄĘ For the same dimensional parameters; height of the soil layer Hs and height of
the pile Hp, increasing the diameter (in our case from d = 2m to d = 4m) shows
a significant decrease in stiffness for the translational stiffness. For rocking and

coupling stiffness, stiffness increases in the higher frequency range. Increasing the
diameter increases the damping significantly. Thus, the diameter has an effect on
the dynamic response.
‚ÄĘ In the case of same non dimensional parameters (same Hp/d & Hs/d) diameter
effect shows no significant impact on the dynamic impedance‚Äô and thus its the non
dimensional parameters that effect the dynamic response.
‚ÄĘ In all the cases, its concluded that the rigidity of the caisson dominates the mode
shapes for the horizontal displacements at least for the 1st three eigen-frequencies
of the soil layer.
In the comparison of analytical vs. numerical, it is concluded that the two solution
do not converge and its seems that an effort needs to be done to improve the analytical
solution that were done in Matlab. Even though the difference in evaluation of static
stiffness at least for higher slenderness ratios is quite less, the dynamic impedance‚Äôs are
not in agreement for the two methods of analysis.
For the future work, work can be done to analyze the effect of other parameters
like Poisson‚Äôs ratio, varying shear wave velocity of the soil layer etc, can be studied to
understand the effect of these parameters in the dynamic impedance‚Äôs of the suction
caisson.
Lastly, it is advised to model the numerical models with absorbing (infinite) boundary
also at the bottom of the soil layer. This will minimize any chance of wave reflection
due to the corner effects and in this way the dynamic response of the suction caisson
can be analyzed as accurate as possible.
