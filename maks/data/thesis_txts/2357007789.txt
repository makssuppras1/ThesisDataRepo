Abstract
The design of advanced structures will often be limited by buckling problems, which
may force the architects to make undesired changes at a late stage of the design process.
This thesis attempts to address this issue using graphic statics.
It has been shown
by Akbarzadeh et al. (2014, 2015c) and Lee et al. (2016) that manipulation of the
force diagram, including subdivision of closed force polygons, can be effectively used
to modify the topology of the form diagram while preserving the static equilibrium of
the internal and external forces. This thesis demonstrates how controlled subdivision of
force diagrams can be used to address buckling concerns early in the design process.
A subdivision scheme similar to ’Barycentric Subdivision’ is used; a subdivision ver-
tex is inserted in a force diagram and connected to all the vertices of a closed force
polygon. As a result, new members emerge in the reciprocal form diagram and existing
members are shortened. The research highlights the degrees of freedom of the resulting
form diagram and respectively identifies important subdivision parameters. These can
be modified to establish a unique, determinate and reciprocal relationship between a sub-
divided force diagram and the corresponding form. This thesis applies the subdivision
methodology to compression/tension-combined form and force diagrams and presents
the results. In addition, an approach to subdivide the force diagram recursively and
compute the reciprocal form diagram is suggested.
A buckling adjusted load-path formula is used to evaluate the performance of the
resulting form. It is closely investigated how the mentioned subdivision parameters can
be chosen to effectively reduce the material requirement of the structure while taking
buckling into account.
The outcome of the thesis is implemented in a Grasshopper component package for
potential users. The components developed include modeling, analysis, and optimization
tools. The aim of the development of a computational component package is to provide
designers with insight and knowledge, without dictating design choices. This way, the
resulting structures can be explored freely and chosen rationally, and in a larger context,
ultimately narrow the gap between architects and engineers.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The building industry today faces the challenge of a gap between architects and engineers,
dividing them into being either designers or analysts respectively. The gap rears its head
when the vision of the architect clashes with contrasting concerns of the engineer; when
aesthetic ideas do not align with structural requirements. Even if the engineer ’can make
it work’ the project easily becomes a series of compromises, where neither the architect
nor the engineer is completely satisfied with the result (L. L. Beghini, A. Beghini, et al.
2014).
Vitruvius (c. 25 B.C.) wrote the book De Architectura and stated that good architec-
ture1 satisfies three virtues: strength (firmitas), function (utilitas) and beauty (venustas)
(L. L. Beghini, A. Beghini, et al. 2014; Vitruvius 1914). In the afterword of the book
Form and Forces: Designing Efficient, Expressive Structures Allen and Zalewski (2009,
pages 612-613) critically reflect over how some of the architecture of today is charac-
terized by a lack of accordance between these very three virtues. They further suggest
that ”Structures that are efficient tend to have this quality that some call beauty”. Ove
Arup (b. 1895, †1988), the founder of Arup Group, believed that ”when engineers and
quantity surveyors discuss aesthetics and architects study what cranes do we are on the
right road” (Emanuel 1980, page 45). Nervi (b. 1891, †1979) stated that ”a structure
that does not follow the simplest and most efficient schemes or a construction detail that
does not consider the specific characteristics of the material with which it is built will
only with great difficulty achieve good aesthetic expression” (Nervi 1965). Architecture
and engineering are indeed inseparable. A smooth route to a successful design, which
is both structurally efficient and aesthetically pleasing, requires that the gap between
architects and engineers is narrowed. In the following, examples of structures which to a
high degree succeed to combine architecture and engineering in a symbiotic relationship
are shown.
The Catalan architect Antonio Gaudi(b. 1852, †1926) explored the design of struc-
turally admissible masonry structures using that ”the form of a stable arch is that of an
inverted hanging chain”. With the use of wires and bags of sand, he was able to represent
the self-weight of a structure and explore design options in structural equilibrium. This
method was used to design Colonia Guell Chapel. The hanging chain model he used
is shown in Figure 1.1(a). The same approach was used to design the Sagrada Familia
Cathedral in Barcelona. Similar design methods would later be employed by Heinz Isler
(b. 1926, †2009) for the design of thin shells. (Addis 2007, pages 440-442).

1.1
Computational Design - Insight?
William F. Baker, a senior structural engineering partner at Skidmore, Owings & Merrill,
was invited to the Technical University of Denmark to give a special evening lecture
(Baker 2015). He brought attention to the development of computers and the possibilities
as well as challenges, with which they come. The ”challenges” engineers and artists like
Gaudi and Nervi encountered was the obvious lack of computational power, which they
unknowingly did not worry about. Today, however, the great challenge is to properly
harness and tame the vast amount of available computational power.
Based on a set of design rules and assessment criteria it has become possible to let
computers find ’optimal’ design solutions. Even though such algorithms may provide
structures which occasionally are both visually pleasing and efficient, the tools them-
selves are often ”black boxes, representing uninformed brute force and blindness” (Block,
Van Mele, and Rippmann 2016). Vividly Block, Van Mele, and Rippmann (2016) argue
that we ”instead of investing in further digital zookeepers to tame the the animal king-
doms of Rhino and Grasshopper2, we ought to throw some more evolved, logical creatures,
perhaps even humans, into the mix.”
William F. Baker brought up one of the key issues of today’s computational methods:

1.2
Graphic Statics
Graphic statics is a structural analysis method which was fundamental for structural
engineers in the late 19th century and early 20th century. Addis (2007) highlights the
importance of the method:”It would be difficult to overestimate the impact of graphic
statics on the worlds of structural engineering; it was certainly no less significant than
the impact of the computer in the late twentieth century.” Unfortunately, the method
lost popularity with the development of analytical tools based on maths. Recently the
==PAGE:24==
6
1 Introduction
method has however gained renewed attention (A. Beghini, L. L. Beghini, Schultz, et al.
2013).
The method uses that there is an explicit reciprocal(two-way) relationship between a
geometry of a structure and the internal distribution of forces. Graphic statics make use
of two diagrams: A form diagram and a force diagram, which, respectively, represents
the geometry and internal forces of a truss. For each line in the form diagram, there is a
corresponding parallel line in the force diagram which length represents the amplitude
of the internal force in said member of the form diagram. The longer a force line is, the
greater the amplitude of the force is.
The force distribution in a truss structure can with graphic statics be determined
with just a pen and a ruler (Allen and Zalewski 2009). In Figure 1.3 an early example
of reciprocal form and force diagrams is shown (Cremona 1872)4. The figure to the left
represent the geometry of a truss, and the figure to the right represent the amplitudes
of the internal forces of the structure.
Graphic statics creates a base for a better intuitive understanding of the relation
between form and forces. Wolfe (1921) introduces his book on graphic statics by stating
that ”graphical constructions often make it easier to understand how the stresses act and
how they may be analyzed... Therefore one of the best ways to teach analysis of stresses
is by the use of graphics... The better a man understands how the stresses follow through
a member or structure, the better his judgment will be”.
Many recent studies show that graphic statics in combination with computational
tools has great potential (Akbarzadeh et al. 2013; Baker et al. 2013; L. L. Beghini, Car-
rion, et al. 2014; Block 2009; Van Mele and Block 2014). This recent revival of graphic
statics is not due to a possibility or desire to compete with current structural analysis
tools. Instead, Graphic statics opens new opportunities for shaping and understanding
structures (Block, Fivet, et al. 2016).

1.2.1
Force diagram modification
The reciprocal relationship between form and forces allows to, not only modify a form
and find the corresponding forces but also to modify the size and distribution of forces
and obtain the corresponding form. Recent publications show how modification of force
diagram can be used as a design method (Akbarzadeh et al. 2014, 2015c; Lee et al. 2016).
A benefit of such methods is that it is guaranteed that the resulting forms are in static
equilibrium.
Akbarzadeh et al. (2014, 2015c) shows how it is possible to use subdivision of the
interior space of a force polygon as a design strategy to create sophisticated structurally
admissible designs. In Figure 1.4, examples of the structures resulting from such force
diagram modifications are shown. It is further suggested that this design method can
be used to address buckling issues actively.
1.3
Problem Statement
Optimizing a structure by reducing the required amount of material may in some cases
have direct economic benefits. But even more so, optimized solutions can serve as a
benchmark to which other solutions can be compared (Mazurek et al. 2011). Recent
papers have used graphic statics for such optimization and assessment (Baker et al.
2013; L. L. Beghini, Carrion, et al. 2014). However, buckling of compression members
is often only briefly discussed or not considered. This thesis will combine force diagram
modification with buckling-adjusted performance assessment and aims to answer the
question:
How can buckling issues in truss structures be addressed early in a design process
through graphic statics and subdivision of force diagrams?

1.4
Literature Review
Inspired by theorems on the equilibrium of polygonal (and polyhedral) frames by Rank-
ine (1858, 1864), Maxwell (1864, 1870) was the first to describe graphic statics and
reciprocality between form and force diagram. In the same era, Cremona (1890) and
Culmann (1864) both further developed the method. Wolfe (1921) wrote a practical
guide to the use of graphic statics and how different structural challenges can be tackled
with this method of analysis. Allen and Zalewski (1998) published one of the first newer
publications on the use of graphic statics which was later followed by the book ’Form
and Forces’(Allen and Zalewski 2009). With the book’s very comprehensive step-by-step
guide, it can be considered one of the key publications on graphic statics of today.
In recent years, many publications have shown the how graphic statics can be used in
a modern context. Based on graphic statics Block (2009), Block, DeJong, et al. (2006),
and Block and Ochsendorf (2007) developed thrust network analysis for the design of
compression-only funicular structures in three dimensions. Greenwold and Edward Allen
(2003) built a series of online interactive graphic statics applications and Van Mele
and Block (2014) describes a generalized approach to computational implementation of
graphic statics. A. Beghini, L. L. Beghini, Schultz, et al. (2013), Lee (2015), and Mueller
(2014) further shows how graphic statics can be used actively as a design tool.
Extending the theory from two dimensions, Akbarzadeh et al. (2013, 2015a,b, 2016)
are some of the major contributors to the development of graphic statics in three dimen-
sions. As it will be discussed later in this thesis, the development of 3D graphic statics
provides solutions for some of the weaknesses of 2D graphic statics.
Topology optimization is a widely researched field(L. L. Beghini, A. Beghini, et al.
2014; Sigmund 1997; Stromberg et al. 2011, 2012; Zhou and Rozvany 1991) where

1.5
Limitations
The research presented in this thesis considers only 2D graphic statics. This further
means that any out of plane buckling of compression members is not considered. The
only instability mechanism considered is flexural buckling. Local buckling of members
will be discussed in a qualitative, but not quantitative manner
When evaluating structures, only the ultimate limit state will be considered, meaning
that the serviceability limit state is ignored. Further, only a single load case is taken
into account for each presented structure.
The analysis presented will further assume that structures are infinitely stiff and
partial factors and other reduction factors are ignored.
These limitations and their
implications will be discussed throughout the thesis.
This thesis focuses only on the early stages of design, meaning that the structures
resulting from the presented method should be seen only as guidelines and not definitive
answers to the final structural solution.

CHAPTER 2
Trusses
This short chapter will briefly cover the basic theory and assumptions of truss structures.
The versatility of such structures is highlighted through a few examples, and lastly, the
structural determinacy of trusses is discussed.
2.1
Definition
Trusses are defined as structures consisting of straight members connected by joints that
permit rotation. Each member can only transfer axial loads, compression or tension. A
truss is in equilibrium when the sum of axial forces meeting in any node is zero. In this
thesis, the terms ”structures”, ”trusses” and ”frames” are used interchangeably (Krenk
and Høgsberg 2013, page 43). Further, only plane trusses are considered. It is assumed
that all examples throughout the thesis satisfy the following four idealizations (direct
quote from Connor and Faraji (2013, page 55)):
1. ”The loads and displacement restraints are applied only at the nodes.”
2. ”The members are connected with frictionless pins so that the members can rotate
freely and no moment exists at the ends.”
3. ”The stress due to the weight of the members is small in comparison to the stress
due to the applied loads.”
4. ”Each member is straight and is arranged such that its centroidal axis coincides
with the line connecting the nodal points.”
It is further assumed that all members have infinite stiffness.

and include the Eiffel Tower in Paris, France, The Tokyo Gate Bridge in Tokyo, Japan

where all compression members are isolated and do no touch each other but instead, are

A structure consists of a total of M members and N nodes and is supported by R ≥3
reactions (reaction forces must be non-concurrent). For each member M there is exactly
one unknown: The axial force. Each node provides two equilibrium equations. The
types of truss structures can be divided into three categories (Connor and Faraji 2013,
pages 57-58):
Statically Determinate The number of unknowns (reactions and axial forces of mem-
bers) corresponds exactly to the number of equilibrium equations.
M + R −2N = 0
(2.1)
Even though the above requirement is fulfilled, the layout of nodes and members
may still be such that the structure includes regions that are unstable.

concern requires further analysis and is covered by Calladine (1978) who shows

above requirement. McRobie et al. (2016), Mitchell et al. (2016), and Van Mele
and Block (2014) use the findings of Calladine (1978) in graphic statics, but the
approach in this thesis, will use the simple version as described by Connor and

2.4
Summary and Conclusion
Any member of a truss can carry only tension or compression. Further, a structure is
considered in internal equilibrium when the sum of forces in any node is zero. It has been
shown that a great variety of truss-like structures exist, highlighting the versatility of
such structures. Trusses can be divided into three groups: Indeterminate, determinate
and unstable. The difference between these groups will later be used in the analysis of
forms resulting from the subdivision of force diagrams.

Graphic Statics
By means of a diagram of forces for any arrangement of framework provided
for him, a person, quite incapable otherwise of taking out the stresses in
a moderately complicated structure, could get at these for any particular
modification of the arrangement with some assurance of accuracy.

Structural analysis is based on statics; how forces are being distributed in an object at
rest. Today most structural analysis is performed with mathematical calculations. For
truss structures, such methods include ’the method of joints’ and ’the method of sections’.
On the contrary, another branch of structural analysis uses graphics to determine the
relationship between form and forces. In Graphic Statics form and forces are described
by a reciprocal relation, meaning that if the forces are known the form can be derived,
and vice versa.
Graphic statics uses two diagrams: The form and force diagram, respectively describ-
ing the geometry and internal and external force distribution in a truss. In the form
diagram, each line describes a member and the length of the line naturally represent
the geometrical length. In the force diagram, a corresponding parallel line exists. The
length of this line represents the magnitude of the axial force carried by the correspond-
ing member (Allen and Zalewski 2009, page 36). Graphic statics makes it possible to
perform structural analysis with nothing but a pen and a ruler.
In this chapter, a brief summary of the history of graphic statics is provided, followed
by a description of the reciprocal relationship between form and force diagrams. A simple
notation technique that enhances the overview of the relation between diagrams will be
covered. It will be discussed how the relation between form and forces is not always
unique. Lastly, limitations of the method, used in the remaining part of the thesis, are
introduced.
3.1
History
In the paper A manual of applied mechanics, a theorem no longer than ten lines, Rankine
(1858) describes a geometrical representation of equilibrium of forces in members of 2D
polygonal frames. Rankine later extended the theorem to describe the equilibrium of
3D polyhedral frames (Rankine 1864).
Maxwell (1864, 1870) further expanded the
findings of Rankine to describe a reciprocal relationship between form and forces of
truss structures. Maxwell’s method involved drawing series of lines perpendicular to

the form line, exists in the force diagram.
Cremona (1872)worked with reciprocal form and force diagrams using series of paral-
lel lines instead of perpendicular lines. It must, however, be noted that Maxwell (1870)
was fully aware that for 2D frames any rotation of lines is possible, such that lines can
be drawn parallel or perpendicular (or any other angle), and many of his examples use
parallel lines. He even stated that ”It is often convenient to turn one of the figures round
in its own plane 90◦. Corresponding lines are then parallel to each other, and this is
sometimes more convenient in comparing the diagrams by the eye.” The reason Maxwell
had, for using sets of perpendicular lines, was that he considered 2D structures special
cases of 3D structures where lines of the form diagram are perpendicular to faces of force
polyhedra as described by Rankine (1858).
The method of drawing parallel lines became the standard way to work with graphic
statics. Same year as Maxwell described reciprocality between form and force diagrams,
Culmann (1864) finished the book Die Graphische Statik which also makes use of a graph-
ical representation of forces, however, his interest was mainly focused on the internal
forces of a beam (A. Beghini, L. L. Beghini, Schultz, et al. 2013).
Bow (1873) significantly simplified the procedure of drawing reciprocal diagrams by
introducing ’Bow’s notation’, which will be explained briefly in this chapter. Maxwell
(1876) later endorsed the method. Other contributors to graphic statics include Ritter
and Culmann (1888) and Lévy (1886). In the book Graphical Analysis: A text book on
Graphic Statics Wolfe (1921) shows how many advanced problems in structural design
can be addressed using graphic statics (Allen and Zalewski 2009; Baker et al. 2013; A.
Beghini, L. L. Beghini, Schultz, et al. 2013; Block, DeJong, et al. 2006; Lee 2015).

3.2
Reciprocal Diagrams and Duality
Maxwell (1864) describes reciprocal figures the following way: ”Reciprocal figures are
such that the properties of the first relative to the second are the same as those of the
second relative to the first.”. If a reciprocal relationship is unique it means the following:
If a diagram Γ is known another diagram ˆΓ can be created solely by the geometrical
information of Γ. If instead ˆΓ is known, Γ can be created solely from the information
provided by ˆΓ. The ’uniqueness’ of diagrams will be discussed later in this chapter.
Maxwell (1864) further refines the definition of reciprocal figures: ”Two plane figures
are reciprocal when they consist of an equal number of lines, so that corresponding lines
in the two figures are parallel, and corresponding lines which converge to a point in one
figure form a closed polygon in the other.” This can be summarized in 3 rules
• Each diagram must contain the same amount of lines.
• For each line in the one diagram, a parallel line exists in the other diagram.
• The diagrams are topological duals of each other. Each node of the one diagram
where a total of µ members meet correspond to a µ-sided polygon in the other
(Van Mele and Block 2014).
A node in the form diagram corresponds to a polygon in the force diagram. Each line
of the polygon corresponds to a force acting on the node. If the polygon is closed, the
sum of forces acting on the node is zero; the node is in equilibrium. This statement is
an essential part of graphic statics.

Bow’s Notation
Bow (1873) introduced Interval Notation, also known as Bow’s Notation, to make the
relation between form and force diagrams more intuitive. The notation has evolved since
1873. The notation in this thesis will be as in Form and Forces: Designing Efficient,
Expressive Structures by Allen and Zalewski (2009).
In graphic statics, every polygon of the one diagram corresponds to a node in the
other. Bow’s notation assigns an index to each area in the form diagram and assigns a
similar index to the corresponding node in the force diagram. The specific properties of
Bow’s Notation will be explained with an example. A form diagram and a force diagram
of a structure are shown in Figure 3.3.
First, the form diagram is considered. The open polygons/intervals between external
forces are denoted by upper case letters in clockwise order around the structure. The
internal intervals are closed polygons and are indicated with numbers.
A node in the form diagram is referred to by the neighboring intervals in clockwise
order around the node; for example the top node in Figure 3.3(a) is A-2-1-C-A or 2-1-C-
A-2 etc. It is not of importance what interval is listed first. The first interval is included
twice, as the first and last element.
A line in the form diagram is referred to by the neighboring intervals such that a
member A-2 has A and 2 as neighbor intervals. The members A-2 and 2-A are the same
member, but in relation to different nodes: 2-1-C-A-2 and B-2-A-B respectively.
In Figure 3.3(b) a force diagram is shown. The force diagram is the topological dual
of the form diagram. Each polygon, open or closed, of the form diagram corresponds
to a node in the force diagram. A node is referred to by the name of the corresponding
polygon in the form diagram. The nodes corresponding to open polygons of the form
diagram are denoted with lower case letters. For internal polygons of the form diagram,
the current notation does not distinguish between the form and force diagram. In the

3.4
External and Internal Force Polygons
A force diagram consists of a series of closed polygons. One of these polygons represents
the external forces and will be referred to as the External Force Polygon. All other
polygons describe how forces are distributed within the structure and will be referred to
as the Internal Force Polygons.
In the force diagram in Figure 3.3(b) the external forces are highlighted with dashed
lines. The closed polygon a-b-c-a is the external force polygon.
For a defined set of supports and applied loads, the external force polygon is inde-
pendent of the geometry of the structure. There are an infinite amount of structural
geometries that for such conditions are in equilibrium.
As long as all internal force
polygons are kept closed, and the external force polygon is kept intact, the layout and
topology of the internal force polygons can be changed as desired. Each layout will
result in a different form in equilibrium. (Akbarzadeh et al. 2014)
In the following chapters the External Force Polygon will also refer to the forces
keeping a considered part of a truss in equilibrium.

3.7
Limitations
In the remaining part of this thesis, it is required that no faces of the form diagram
overlap and that no lines of the form diagram intersect each other. Graphic statics does
not fail if these requirements are not satisfied and it does indeed allow for interesting
design processes (Lee 2015). However, Bow’s notation does fail in these cases making
the process much less intuitive.

3.8
Summary and Conclusions
Graphic statics use two diagram: The form diagram and force diagram, describing the
geometry and the force distribution of a truss, respectively. For each line in the force
diagram, a corresponding parallel line exists in the form diagram. The length of the
force line is proportional to the amplitude of internal the force in the corresponding
member of the form. Diagrams are each other’s dual, such that a node in each diagram
corresponds to a polygon in the other. For each node in the form diagram, there exists
a force polygon in the force diagram. If the force polygon is closed, the corresponding
node of the form is in equilibrium.
The understanding of the reciprocal relationship between diagrams can be enhanced
with the introduction of Bow’s interval notation: Each polygon in the form is given an
index, and the corresponding node in the force diagram is given the same index. The
relationship between form and forces is not necessarily unique even though two figures
are reciprocal. It was further shown that rotating a force diagram 180◦corresponds to
reversing the direction of all external and internal forces of a form diagram.
Graphic statics open up for two possibilities: Equilibrium of nodes in the form is
ensured simply by having all polygons of the force diagram closed. Hence, the method

CHAPTER 4
Subdivision
The reciprocal relationship between form and forces allows a designer to indirectly modify
a form by directly modifying the desired distribution of forces.
Changes in a force
diagram are directly reflected in the corresponding form diagram. Modifying a force
diagram while all force polygons are kept closed guarantees that the corresponding form
diagram is in both external and internal equilibrium (Akbarzadeh et al. 2014). As the
form is not subject to direct modification, such methods can lead to ”feasible designs
that are not biased towards known solutions or predefined typologies” (Lee et al. 2016).
Subdivision of force diagrams is an example of such ’force diagram modification’. The
method has been developed by Akbarzadeh et al. (2014, 2015c) who suggest that the
method ”can be used as a strategy in dealing with buckling in spatial funicular struc-
tures”. The buckling capacity of a compression member depends greatly on the length
of the member1. Hence, if the structural capacity of a truss member in compression is
governed by buckling, shortening the member will increase its strength significantly. As
the subdivision of force diagram can lead to shortening of members the potential of the
method as a way to address buckling is interesting.
To limit the scope of this thesis only one type of subdivision is considered. The type
will be referred to as Node-Insertion Subdivision: In a force diagram, a new node is
inserted. Every existing node of a desired closed force polygon is then connected to the
new node. A special case of node-insertion subdivision is when the new node is placed in
the barycenter of the force polygon. This case is called Barycentric Subdivision (Petersen
2015, page 185). In Figure 4.1 an example of subdivision of a force polygon is shown
along with the geometrical consequences of the form. The properties of node-insertion
subdivision will be discussed in this chapter.
First, the terminology for the analysis of subdivision of force diagrams is specified.
The explanation is combined with a general description of the geometrical consequences
of the subdivision. The degrees of freedom for such operation is then explored, followed
by an explanation of the different possible cases where subdivision of force diagrams
can be used. It is shown how an eligible subdivision point can be chosen and how its
location in the force diagram affects the force distribution and geometrical properties
of the resulting structure. A series of examples are provided, and it is shown how the
method can be used recursively to yield intricate structures in intriguing shapes. Lastly,
the stability and determinacy of resulting structures are discussed followed by a summary
of the findings.

• u: First location parameter of subdivision point in the force diagram.
• v: Second location parameter of subdivision point in the force diagram.
• s: Scale parameter for subdivision polygon in form diagram.

Recursive Subdivision
This section presents two different recursive subdivision algorithms and the geometrical
consequences of each algorithm is discussed.
This topic is included in the thesis to
highlight the extent of possibilities modification of force diagrams brings.
When a node of valency µ is subdivided µ new nodes emerge in the form diagram.

results in the emergence of new three-member nodes. The process is called recursive

4.9
Summary and Conclusions
Modifying a force diagram, while keeping all force polygons closed, is an indirect method
of modeling which guarantees that the corresponding form is in internal and external
==PAGE:72==
54
4 Subdivision
equilibrium. Subdividing a force polygon by inserting a subdivision point and connecting
it to every node of the force diagram can be used as a design method. The structures
resulting from subdivision are in equilibrium, but are unstable; some connections must be
fixed to ensure stability. Further, the resulting structures are characterized by shortening
of original members, and insertion of a subdivision polygon.
This very type of modification of force diagrams has exactly three degrees of freedom.
The selection of a subdivision node in the force diagram is determined by two parameters
u and v. The insertion of subdivision point in the force diagram results in the emergence
of a subdivision polygon in the form diagram, which scale is determined by a parameter
s in the form diagram.
It was determined that three different types of subdivision point exist
External point The subdivision point is outside the force polygon.
Internal clockwise point The point is inside the force polygon. Accessing the nodes
of the polygon in ascending order a →b →c →...→x →a is a clockwise rotation
from the perspective of the subdivision point.
Internal counter-clockwise point The point is inside the force polygon. Accessing
the nodes of the polygon in ascending order a →b →c →...→x →a is a counter-
clockwise rotation from the perspective of the subdivision point. This subdivision
point is never valid.
In the form diagram, three different types of initial situations were considered. The
difference between types is characterized by the force direction in the original members
meeting at the node considered for subdivision, and the angle between the original
members. The following summary considers the eligible subdivision point domain in the
force diagram and the resulting properties of the subdivision polygon. That a subdivision
point is valid means that the resulting subdivision polygon intersects the initial members
in their original domain while assuming they span from the center node to infinity.

Performance Assessment
Comparison of structures in regards to their performance requires a quantitative in-
dicator. Some performance indicators, such as the overall cost, are complex. Other
indicators, such as aesthetics, are hard to measure quantitatively. Too simple indica-
tors, such as the resulting size of support forces of a structure, may in most cases not be
adequate. Commonly used indicators for structural performance are stiffness (Stromberg
et al. 2011) and theoretic material volume requirement (L. L. Beghini, Carrion, et al.
2014). These measures do not take the complexity of a structure into account. Hence,
they do not necessarily provide an indicator for the actual cost of a structure.
Optimization of a structure may be carried out for both economic or aesthetic reasons
or simply to have a benchmark to which other solutions can be compared (Mazurek et al.
2011). A performance indicator such as material requirement can be considered a good
compromise between an indicator which is too simple and one which is too complex.
This chapter will show how the information provided by graphic statics can be used to
evaluate a truss structure regarding material usage while taking buckling into account.
First, a simple ’load path theorem’ by Maxwell is shown. The theorem uses the infor-
mation embedded in graphic statics to give an indicator of material volume requirement.
The theorem is then expanded to include buckling. The novel part of this chapter is a
simplification of the buckling adjusted load path theorem that keeps radius of members
constant. Lastly, an analysis of the load path formula is carried out through further
simplification.

problems in structures actively, and how buckling concerns can be implemented early in
a design process. The topics of Chapter 4 and 5 will be combined. The discussion will

6.1
Load Path as a Function of Subdivision
Parameters
It has been shown that node insertion subdivision of force diagrams has three degrees
of freedom. Two parameters u and v must be chosen for the selection of a subdivision
point in the force diagram, and a parameter s is chosen in the form diagram, to scale
the subdivision polygon.
The selection of three parameters results in a determinate
relationship between the geometry of the form and force diagram. A unique choice of
parameters corresponds to a unique form for which the buckling adjusted load path can
be calculated. In other words, the buckling adjusted load path is a function f of the
choice of parameters P = (u, v, s).

it is only necessary to set the value for one member. From the choice of one value of
s, all other values si can be calculated. This means that the exact value of s and its
impact on the final geometry greatly depends on the choice of the member to which s is
attached. This further means that there is no exact correlation between the actual value

Subdivision of Force Diagrams and Design
Methods
In the introduction of this thesis different design methods, summarized by Mueller (2014),
were presented. See Figure 1.2. In the following, it will be shown how these different
design methods can be used for subdivision of form diagram. The aim is to allow for a
free, efficient and rational choice subdivision parameters.
6.2.1
Guess and check
This method is simple: Draw a form and force diagram, subdivide the force diagram
and draw the subdivided form diagram.
The buckling adjusted load path for each

member can then be calculated to find the total buckling adjusted load path. In practical
situations, the length of members may be exported from CAD software to a spreadsheet
for calculations to speed up the process.
This method is very slow and does not provide any understanding of how a better
set of parameters can be chosen, or whether a chosen solution is good or bad; and even
more important, how good or bad it is. The following methods move beyond the limits
of this method.
6.2.2
Rapid Feedback
This method is similar to guess-and-check.
This method, however, implies that the
buckling adjusted load path is calculated in real time; when the designer modifies a
structure, the buckling adjusted load path is provided automatically at all times. This
allows to quickly check and compare a large array of solutions but without any deeper
understanding of what changes in parameters that improve the efficiency of the design
even further.
6.2.3
Computational optimization
Finding the parameters that yield the lowest buckling adjusted load path can often be of
interest as ”determination of the optimal solution can serve as a benchmark against which
the efficiency of other solutions can be compared” (Mazurek et al. 2011). As an example,
consider a structure A that is a result of an optimal choice of subdivision parameters,
and that has a total buckling adjusted load path of ϑ. If a structure B has a buckling
adjusted load path of 1.20ϑ then B can be expected to use 20% more material than A.
If structure B has characteristics, e.g., aesthetics, for which it is superior to structure A,
the extra amount of material may be considered negligible. However if the load path of B
is 10ϑ, the structure suddenly require ten times more material than strictly necessary. A
designer must then evaluate whether the advantages of the structure justify the excessive
extra material usage.

6.2.4
Guided design
Guided design as a method aims at introducing insight to the method of Rapid Feedback.
Assume that a set of parameters P are chosen and the buckling adjusted load path f(P )

is calculated (as in Rapid Feedback). To better understand the design space around the
chosen set of parameters the gradient ∇f(P ) can be calculated as in equation 6.8.
The magnitude and direction of the gradient provide the designer with valuable
information of how parameters can be most changed such that the buckling adjusted
load path is lowered. The magnitude of the gradient shows how sensitive the design is
to changes. The steeper the gradient is, the more the act of changing parameters will
affect the buckling adjusted load path.
Using the central differences scheme to calculate the gradient involves calculating the
load path for six different solutions. Compared to computational optimization and design
space understanding (next subsection), this method requires a very limited amount of
calculations and may be suitable, even for problems requiring a more significant amount
of computational power than subdivision of force diagrams.
6.2.5
Design Space Understanding
The last design method considered aims at better understanding the entire design space.
Essentially the idea is to calculate solutions over the entire spectrum of possible values
of P . This does, however, introduce several issues. P is defined in 3D-space. Efficiently
visualizing and navigating a 3D scalar field, such as f(P ), on a 2D screen is not consid-
ered desirable. Further, calculating a sufficient amount of values in 3 dimensions can be
computationally infeasible even for problems of little complexity. A grid with x different
values of u, v and s, respectively, results in x3 different values of P.
To reduce the complexity one parameter is set to be constant, and the design space
for the remaining parameters can be calculated. This allows for the visualization of a
2D scalar field. The visualization can provide the designer with an idea of the size of the
domain of P for which values of the buckling adjusted load path are acceptable. Further
it help the designer to understand the sensitivity of f (P ). By only calculating the solu-
tion space for two parameters much valuable information may however not be accessible.
For problems in even higher dimensions, this method may not be feasible. This method
is also computationally expensive. Hence, it is only feasible for low complexity problems,
such as subdivision of force diagrams.
6.3
Summary and Conclusions
The buckling adjusted load path is a function f of the three subdivision parameters. For
a given force diagram the optimal location (u, v) of a subdivision point is not unique,
as the relationship with the corresponding form diagram is not unique; different forms
with same force diagram have different optimal subdivision parameters. The geometrical
consequences of the choice of value for the scale parameter s, which is defined in the
form diagram, greatly depends on the selection of an arbitrary member.
The optimization algorithm Gradient Descent is proposed as a method to find the set
of parameters that yield the lowest buckling adjusted load path. Further, it is suggested
==PAGE:98==
80

to combine real-time calculation of the buckling adjusted load path in modeling tools,
with the determination of the gradient of f. The gradient can provide a designer with
insight as to how parameter values can be changed most efficiently and how sensitive f
is to changes. Lastly, it has been suggested to calculate entire regions of the design space
for better visualization and understanding of the effect of different parameter choices.

mentation of other components simpler. The component could be directly implemented
in the other components for a simpler interface, however, in some special cases, manual

This component is created to be able to find the choice of parameters (u, v, s) that
yield the smallest buckling adjusted load path. Further discussion of the topic is found in
Section 6.2.3 where both the motivation for implementation as well as the used formulas
can be found.
This component implements the gradient descent algorithm, which can be imagined
as balls that are being thrown at random locations of the 3D solution space of the
buckling adjusted load path function f(P ). The ’balls’ then roll, in the negative direction
of the gradient, to a local minimum.
Note: The actual implementation does not use line search, but instead a constant
step size (see Section 6.2.3.1). The use of line search was considered at too late a stage
of the writing of this thesis to be implemented. For the release of components at the
IASS conference in September 2017, the component will be updated to include line
search. As the optimization problem is not very computationally expensive, the reduced
computation time from using line search is not considered essential.

7.10
Summary and Conclusions
A total of 7 component for the parametric modeling software Grasshopper has been
developed. The component package allows users to use the theory of graphic statics,
subdivision of force diagrams and buckling adjusted load path evaluation. The aim of
the component package is to deliver insight to a potential user, and not dictate design.
The practical usage of the component package will be shown in the following chapter.
The components include the Synchronization component, which is used to sort the
data provided by the user. The Subdivide and Recursive Subdivision components per-
form normal and recursive subdivision, respectively, as suggested in Chapter 4. The
Evaluate Load Path component, provides the designer with both the regular and the
buckling adjusted load path value for a given structure. These components together
form a fast modeling environment where the subdivision can be performed easily, and
the performance of the structure can be evaluated quickly. The Design Guide component
calculates the gradient of the buckling adjusted load path for a given set of subdivision
parameters. The gradient provides the designer with a measure of what changes in pa-
rameter values that will increase the performance of the structure. The Find Optima
component try to find the optimal set of parameters which can serve as a benchmark
solution to which other solutions can be compared. The last component Solution Field
calculates the load path for a large amount of parameter value combinations, to effec-
tively visualize the size and location of parameter value domains that yield load path
values within a desired domain.

The potential of the research presented in this thesis is highlighted with the presentation
of two examples.
The examples shown in this chapter are inspired by two already
constructed structures:
• Stansted Airport by Foster+Partners
• Christina and John Markey Memorial Pedestrian Bridge by Rosales+Partners
The aim of this thesis is to show how subdivision of force diagrams can be used to
address buckling issues early in the design process. It will be shown how the methods
proposed in previous chapters can be used to design structures where the main design
drivers are ’the slenderness of members’ and ’a high level of design flexibility’.
The real structures are designed using local standards. The examples presented will,
however, be based on the Danish implementation of the Eurocodes. All dimensions are
pure estimates and do not necessarily represent the exact dimensions of the original
structures. The research presented in this thesis is aimed at the early design stages,
hence only rough estimates of loads will be presented.
Each section will highlight steps and thoughts of a design process using the findings
of previous chapters. It will be shown how the tools provided can be used to guide a
designer towards more efficient solutions without dictating the design.

8.3
Summary and Conclusions
Two examples have been shown. Both examples have highlighted how subdivision of
force diagrams can be used as a method to reduce the buckling adjusted load path of a
structure. However, in both cases, instability issues emerged as a result, which would
make it necessary to fix some connections. Further, the introduction of several new con-
nections increases the complexity of the structure. These factors make it inappropriate
to assume that the reduction of load path is directly proportional to a decrease in the
cost of construction.
From an architectural perspective, the primary driver in both examples was to reduce
the radius of compression members and to explore efficient structures freely. In both
examples, it was shown that the radius of members could be significantly reduced without
increasing the material volume requirement. The reduction is, however, theoretical and
the structures would have to be analyzed for all possible load cases which may change
the final structural requirements significantly.
The benefit of graphic statics did shine through, as the array of discovered structural
solutions all are in equilibrium for the main load case. One of the main aims was to
show how insight can be introduced in a design process and used actively, and how
computational tools can be used to guide a designer. By using computational tools that
provide insight and understanding of the solution space, it is possible to wisely generate
design solutions and rationally weigh e.g. aesthetics against a quantitative measure for
structural efficiency.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
This thesis has aimed to answer how buckling issues in truss structures can be addressed
early in a design process through graphic statics and subdivision of force diagrams. The
question has been answered in three parts:
Geometric relationship between subdivided force and form diagrams:
The force diagram modification method presented in this thesis is Node-Insertion Subdi-
vision, where a node is inserted in a closed polygon of the force diagram and connected
to all existing nodes of the polygon. The resulting form is characterized by the insertion
of a polygon of new members and shortening of the original members. Shortening of
members is essential to the method’s potential as a way to address buckling issues.
It was shown that node-insertion subdivision has exactly three degrees of freedom:
The location of a node in the force diagram and the scale of the subdivision polygon in
the form diagram. The findings of this research allow for subdivision of three types of
nodes:
Case 1 All initial members are in either compression or tension
Case 2 Initial members both in tension and in compression. No angle between any two
neighbor members is greater than 180◦
Case 3 Initial members in both tension and compression. An angle between two neigh-
bor members is greater than 180◦
The resulting form and force diagram has unique properties in each case. The dif-
ferences in properties include the eligible domain in which as subdivision point can be
placed in the force diagram. Further, they include the internal angles in the subdivision
polygon as well as the direction of forces in new members.
Quantitative assessment of buckling adjusted performance:
A buckling adjusted load path formula has been proposed. The load path of a structure
is directly proportional to the amount of the material required. Hence, the formula
can be used as an indicator for the material requirement of a structure. Further, the
formula assumes that members have a constant radius. This allows the designer to set
a predefined radius and evaluate structures based on the desired perceived slenderness
of members. The formulas are shown in formula 5.15 to 5.18.
The buckling adjusted load path, for the given assumptions, is directly proportional
to the amplitude of applied loads. Therefore, the ’shape’ of the solution space, for a given
==PAGE:156==
138
9 Conclusion
set of material parameters and desired radius, is independent of the amplitude of forces.
The amplitude of internal forces is used to determine the resulting wall thickness of cross
sections, which must be within a reasonable domain. Hence, the amplitude of forces
cannot be completely ignored. For small radius values, the load path is proportional
to the length cubed. The ordinary load path theorem, which does not include buckling
concerns, assigns equal importance to the length and internal force of a member. On
the contrary, when including buckling concerns, in the analysis of slender structures,
reduction of load path becomes a question of reducing the length of members much
more than a question of reducing the amplitude of internal forces.
Combining subdivision of force diagrams and buckling adjusted performance
assessment to guide design decisions:
The buckling adjusted load path can be seen as a function of the three subdivision
parameters, but even though two structures have the same force diagram, they may not
necessarily have the same optimal choice of subdivision parameters. Different methods
to efficiently, freely and rationally choose subdivision parameters have been proposed.
An optimization algorithm has been implemented as a computational tool to provide
the designer with a measure for the most efficient choice of subdivision parameters.
The solution serves as a benchmark against which other solutions can be compared. A
gradient based guide tool has been implemented to provide the designer with insight
as to how parameter values can be modified to lower the buckling adjusted load path.
Lastly, it was shown how the buckling adjusted load path can be calculated for numerous
combinations of subdivision parameters. This array of load path values can be visualized
to show both size and location of parameter value domains that yields solutions with
desirable load path values. These components are a part of a component package for
Grasshopper that has been developed to ease the use of subdivision of force polygons as
a design method.
Addressing buckling issues early in a design process using subdivision of force
diagrams:
It has been shown that buckling concerns can be included and addressed early in a
design process. This process utilizes that subdivision of force diagrams can produce
forms which consist of members that are shorter than those of the structures from which
they emerge. The method presented should not be seen as a method to solve buckling
issues of a structure with already desired topological and geometrical properties. Instead,
it can be used to explore new and more efficient layouts. By introducing insight and
design guidance, the design space can be explored freely and a solution chosen rationally.
Even though the theoretical findings of this thesis are promising, several issues must
still be addressed.
Subdivision of force diagrams results in unstable structures that
are more complex than the initial structure from which they emerge. Even though the
theoretical material requirement may be lowered, it does not necessarily mean that the
resulting structure is cheaper to construct. The structures emerging from this type of
force diagram modification are in statics equilibrium, but only for a single load case.

A thorough analysis must be carried out to ensure that the structures can carry all
necessary load cases. This may affect the requirement to material volume as well as the
possible slenderness of structures greatly. Another issue is that the analysis presented in
this thesis is constrained to 2D; any out-of-plane buckling is ignored. This assumption
is not feasible in real design situations. For increased usability, the methods should be
extended to 3D graphic statics.
9.1
Discussion
Subdivision of force diagrams is just one of many types of force diagram modification,
which all together form a toolbox that allows architects to explore shapes and topologies
of structures efficiently and freely. The major benefit is that the resulting structures are
in static equilibrium. However, as the method is an indirect way of modeling, it may be
difficult for a designer to realize a shape and topology that reflects the initial ideas and
concepts for a proposed project. Therefore the method of force diagram modification is
deemed more suitable for design exploration than for exact concept realization.
In the introduction of this thesis, the gap between engineers and architects was
discussed. By introducing engineering concerns early in the design process, performance
pitfalls can be spotted and avoided, and the final design may be less of a compromise
between the concerns of the engineer on the one side and the concerns of the architect
on the other. The methods presented involve both modeling techniques, analysis tools
and design guidance. It is believed that such combination of tools can help narrowing
the gap between the architects and engineers and allow efficiency of structures to be
implemented early in a design process. This can ideally be a way to compromise neither
aesthetics nor structural efficiency, but instead, let the two aspects work in symbiosis
with each another.
9.2
Novel Research
To the knowledge of the author, this is the first contribution to the field of graphic statics
that demonstrates that node-insertion subdivision of force diagrams has exactly three
degrees of freedom. It is further believed to be the first contribution to show how the
method can be used for structures with members in both tension and compression and
structures with two neighboring members meeting in a reflex angle. Furthermore, it is
the first publication to show a recursive subdivision algorithm within graphic statics that
guarantees to minimize the buckling adjusted load path with increasing recursive depth.
It is also believed that no other contribution has explored the eligible subdivision point
domain and how the choice of subdivision parameters affects the theoretical buckling
adjusted material requirement for a structure. Lastly, this is, to the knowledge of the
author, the first contribution that uses a buckling adjusted load path theorem that
==PAGE:158==

9 Conclusion
assumes a constant radius of members in combination with force diagram modification
as a method to design and evaluate structures.
9.3
Further Work
To be able to fully utilize the potential of the method several issues are still to be
addressed. The following topics are suggested for further research:
• The structures emerging from this method of design are unstable. It is to be shown,
how it efficiently can be determined what nodes in the resulting structures that
should be fixed and how sensitive a potential design is to varying load cases.
• This thesis have used a performance indicator that assumes all members to have
a circular cross section with constant radius. It is suggested to research how other
assumptions affect the performance evaluation of structures. Such assumptions
could be e.g. a constant relationship between the radius and thickness of members.
• Recursive subdivision has been briefly covered, but it is still to be shown how
a recursive algorithm can be optimized to most effectively reduce the buckling
adjusted load path of a structure.
• It is suggested that other force diagram modifications methods, such as insertion
of multiple nodes, controlled insertion of new lines and splitting and merging of
nodes, are researched thoroughly.
• The research should be expanded to 3D graphic statics where a package of mod-
eling tools that allow for all sorts of modification would be of great value.
In
such software, the performance evaluation presented in this thesis would still be
applicable.
