Abstract
In this thesis the influence of the geometrical and structural rotor blade properties on the
pitch bearing loads and deformations is investigated by developing a scalable finite element
rotor blade model in Ansys Classic and analyzing the impact of different blade designs. The
research aim of this thesis is to determine blade properties which are critical for the bear-
ing design, so these critical parameter can be considered by blade designer in future blade
designs to improve the performance of the pitch bearings. For this purpose the research
question is, which rotor blade parameter have a significant impact on the bearing and which
blade designs are favourable for the loads and deformations of the bearing.
To answer the research question, this thesis investigates several blade properties, including
the number and position of the shear webs, the width and arrangement of the spar caps, and
the overall bending stiffness of the blade. The author hypothesizes, that the shear web and
spar cap configurations of different blades have a significant influence on the rolling element
forces and deformations of the bearing. This assumption applies especially to flapwise loads.
For edgewise loads no significant influences are expected.
For the investigation many blades with different properties are needed. For this purpose,
this thesis presents one method of developing a scalable rotor blade model, which is used
to efficiently design many blades with different shear web and spar cap configurations, and
different bending stiffnesses. The different blades are then assembled to the bearing for the
parameter study. To perform the study as close as possible to reality, support structures like
a wind turbine rotor hub and a stiffener plate on the bearing are also considered.
The results reveal, that neither the shear web configuration, nor the spar cap configuration
have a considerable influence on the bearing loads and deformations. The results contradict
the hypotheses of the author and suggest, that it does not make sense to include the bear-
ing characteristics in the blade design process. However, to verify this conclusion, future
work is necessary, as some phenomena present in current blade model can not be explained
conclusively.

A wind turbine converts the kinetic energy in the wind to mechanical energy in the shaft
and finally electric energy in the generator. The maximum energy available in the wind is
obtained by

On the one hand this equation shows the importance of analyzing the wind resource for a
wind farm project as the available power increase with the wind speed cubed. On the other
hand, the rotor area is the only parameter that engineers can actively define to maximize
the available power and therefore maximize the performance capacity of the wind turbine
[19, 29].
Since the 1980s the average rotor diameter of newly installed on- and offshore wind tur-
bines worldwide has increased continuously. This trend is expected to continue as manu-
facturers today are developing turbines with more than 10 MW and rotor diameters of over
200 m [3, 13, 46].
The standard power controlling system for those modern horizontal-axis wind turbines of
the multi-megawatt classes is the rotor blade adjustment through a pitch system, Figure 1.1.
During operation, the lift and drag on the rotor blades can be controlled by varying the pitch
angle of each blade controlling power output and loads of the turbine. The rotating motion
of each blade along its longitudinal axis is enabled by a large pitch bearing (also called blade
bearing) connecting the rotor blade to the rotor hub [29].
With increasing blade length, the diameter of the blade root section also increases - some-
times up to 6 m - to ensure sufficient stiffness of the blade when mounted. Consequently,
the diameter of the pitch bearings also have to increase. As the height of the bearing and
the ring section width do not increase at the same rate to remain economical, large pitch
bearings are geometrically less stiff. Consequently, unfavourable operating conditions can
lead to premature failure of these bearings. Due to the difficult and costly replacement and
the high mass of the expensive bearings, damages to this component can be rated critical,
so detailed knowledge of the loading conditions of this critical component is necessary [24].
There is a general consent about the fact that surrounding structures influence the loads in
pitch bearings, but very little knowledge is existent on how and how much the loads change
for different characteristics of surrounding structures. The rotor blade of a wind turbine is
one such structure, which is found to have a considerable impact on the bearing loads and
deformations [48].

1.1. Research aim and objective
The results derived from this project are of high interest on an academic and industry level.
Currently, blade and pitch bearings are designed independently and the interaction between
the two is not part of the design process in either of them. The research aim is to determine
blade properties which are critical for the bearing design, so these critical parameter can be
considered in future interdisciplinary blade designs to improve the performance of the pitch
bearings.
Therefore, the research objective in this thesis is to quantify the influence of blade prop-
erties on the rolling element forces and deformations of the bearing rings by developing a
scalable finite element rotor blade model in Ansys Classic and analyzing the impact of dif-
ferent blade designs. The blade model is thereby used as a tool for this parameter analysis.
Therefore, one sub-goal is to design a scalable rotor blade model, including sensible bound-
ary conditions, an efficient design and a well designed interface to be able to connect the
blade to the bearing. The subsequent goal is to establish, which blade parameter have a
significant influence on the bearing loads and deformations.
From the research objective the main research question is derived: Which rotor blade
parameter have a significant impact on the bearing and which blade designs are favourable
for the loads and deformations of the bearing.

In order to answer the research question, the blade model will be fully parametric.
This
means, that all geometrical and structural blade properties can be adjusted by the user.
However, based on the literature study in chapter 2, not all parameter are expected to have a
significant impact on the bearing loads. Therefore, only the influence of following properties
is investigated in this project:
• The number of shear webs: The blade can either have one spar cap or two spar caps.
• The spanwise position of the shear webs: The shear webs either extend until the blade
root or end far before the blade root.
• The width of the spar caps: The blade can either have narrow spar caps or wide spar
caps.
• The design of the spar caps: The blade can either utilize a design with continuous spar
caps or the spar caps can be divided.
• The overall bending stiffness of the blade: The material thicknesses of the the blades
can vary leading to different bending stiffnesses of the blades.
The author hypothesizes, that the shear web and the spar caps configuration have a signif-
icant influence on the rolling element forces in the bearing. If two shear webs are used, the
author expects the rolling element contact forces to be distributed more evenly around the
circumference of the bearing under a flapwise load, compared to a blade with a single shear
web. The reason for this assumption is, that the forces are not concentrated in one shear
web but spread out over tow shear webs. For a shear web that ends far away from the blade
root, also a slightly improved load distribution in the bearing is expected. Furthermore, the
author assumes, that a blade design with wide spar caps or divided spar caps results in a
more even load distribution in the bearing, and thus reduces the peak forces in the bearing
for a flapwise load. This hypothesis is again based on the assumption, that wide spar caps
divide the loads over a larger area of the blade, so again the forces are spread out. For an
edgewise load, the author expects, that the shear webs and spar caps have no significant
influence on the bearing loads and deformations. Moreover, the author hypothesizes, that a
higher bending stiffness of the blade is beneficial for the bearing load and deformations for a
flapwise and edgewise load.
To illustrate the influence of the respective properties, a baseline design for a blade is
defined. The design of the baseline blade is inspired by the design of the IWT 7.5 MW reference
wind turbine of the IWES, which is described in section 2.4. The results of all variations of
the blade are then compared to the baseline model to assess the significance of the varied
parameter.
Validation or plausibilisation of the parametric blade is only performed on the baseline
blade because all other investigated blade models are only variations thereof. Thereby, de-
formation behavior, weight, position of the mass center of gravity, and eigenfrequencies are
compared to the validated blade model of the DTU 10 MW wind turbine. The results of the
baseline blade and the DTU 10 MW blade are compared by applying similarity rules which
describe the scaling of blade properties like mass and tip displacement with increasing or
decreasing blade length.
The parametric blade model is coded in the Ansys Parametric Design Language (APDL)
and all simulations are performed in Ansys Classic 19.3. For the parameter analysis, the
blade model is assembled to the validated bearing, hub and stiffener plate models, which are
provided by the Fraunhofer IWES.

2.1. Pitch bearings in wind turbines
While early horizontal axis wind turbines had rotor blades with fixed global pitch angles which
controlled the power by creating stall at the blades, modern wind turbines require blade
bearings to control the power by pitching the blades in and out of the wind. By adjusting the
angle of attack, not only the power can be controlled but also the load to the rotor blades can
be reduced. This leads to designs with lower weight which again reduces the loads to all parts
of the turbine [29]. New concepts like individual pitch control (IPC) adjust the pitch angle of
each rotor blade independently to account for different wind speeds in the rotor plane (for
example turbulence and wind shear). With IPC, single blades turn only a few degrees several
times during one rotor rotation to reduce the load on the turbine. Consequently, the amount
of material used in all parts of the turbine can be reduced which reduces the cost of energy
[15, 48], but the small and fast angle adjustments can lead to critical conditions in the blade
bearing and increase fatigue, which is discussed in section 2.1.2.

2.1.1. Pitch bearing concepts
The type of bearing used for the connection of blade and hub is called slewing bearing. Slew-
ing bearings are large rotational rolling element bearings that support heavy, but slow turning
or oscillating loads [31]. There are three main designs for pitch bearings:
• Bearings with spherical rolling elements called ball bearings
• Bearings with cylindrical rolling elements called roller bearings
• Configurations that utilize spherical and cylindrical rolling elements

(b) Roller bearing [5]
(c) Combination of cylindrical and spherical rolling

Modern wind turbines almost exclusively use double rowed four-point contact ball bearings
(short: 4P ball bearing), which is shown in Figure 2.2a. As the name suggests, the balls of
those bearings have four contact points with the raceway due to the different curvatures of

ball and raceway if unloaded. Because of the high bilateral pressure angles, the bearing is
suitable for high axial forces from changing directions [31]. However, under load almost all
rolling elements have a two-point contact. Due to the contact angle of approximately 45°,
the induced forces are resolved into axial and radial components. The resulting radial com-
ponent causes a bearing gap widening so the ball contact angle moves towards the raceway
edge. While normally the pressure in a ball-raceway contact is distributed elliptically, as the
contact angle moves towards the raceway edge, the pressure is concentrated in the truncated
area, Figure 2.3. This could then lead to plasticity in this particular area [44]. This damage
phenomenon is further discussed in section 2.1.2. Furthermore, as the turbines size and
therefor the blade geometries and diameters of the blade root increase, the slewing bearings
do also have to grow. Thereby, the thickness of the bearing needs to increase with the di-
ameter to ensure stability thus making the construction uneconomical [24]. Therefore, the
development of new bearing concepts is important for the future success of the wind turbine
industry.
An alternative concept is the design of a triple-row cylindrical roller bearing shown in Fig-
ure 2.2b. This type of bearing has two axial raceways to carry the high axial load components,
and one radial raceway to carry the radial load components. Consequently, gap widening is
greatly reduced and the pressures are always carried by the whole length of the raised rollers
which prevents pressure concentration. Consequently, the static and dynamic load carrying
capacity as well as the stiffness of the bearing is high compared to the four-point contact ball
bearing thus reducing material requirements and therefor saving costs and reducing weight
[5].
A second alternative concept is a bearing with a combination of cylindrical and spherical
rolling elements. This shown in Figure 2.2c. This bearing consists of two raceways with
spherical rolling elements with a support angle of 90° and one roller raceway.
With this
design, the axial forces and bending moments are carried by the two axial ball raceways and
the radial loads by the radial roller raceway. Because of the 90° contact angle the axial forces
are absorbed by the bearing structure and gap widening is prevented, analogous to the three
row roller bearing. As there is no significant change of the ball contact angle truncation is
prevented.
2.1.2. Failure modes of pitch bearings
Wind turbine pitch bearings are subjected to untypical operation conditions as small ampli-
tude oscillatory rotations are the dominant operation mode and the bearing is barely rotated
for a full rotation. Additionally, high combined loads are present in the bearing [45]. For
these reasons bearings are prone to several damage modes which are fatigue, edge loading,
core crushing, ring fractures, rotational wear, fretting and false brinelling [48].
Fatigue damage modes are relevant as large rotational movements, e.g. pitching from
production mode to idling, result in high rolling speeds of the rolling elements. Even though
blade bearings pitch with relatively low rotational speeds of typically 4 - 6 °/sec, the rolling
speeds are high due to the large diameters [47].
In a Hertzian contact, which describes
the stresses and deformations of two elastic curved bodies like rolling element and raceway,
the maximum stress occurs just below the raceway surface. While for through-hardened
bearings fatigue damage usually initiates here, the damage for surface-hardened bearings
can initiate just below the surface or at area where the surface hardness changes to the core
hardness. This kind of fatigue damage is called core crushing [43, 48].
Furthermore, the bearing rings are subjected to fatigue because of high dynamic bending
moments caused by aerodynamic and gravitational loading of the rotor blades.
The ring
fatigue can then result in ring fractures. Even though this damage mode is comparatively
unlikely, Stammler and Reuter [48] suggest to take the stiffness properties of the supporting
structures into account during the bearing design process to prevent such an event.
If the rings of the bearing are heavily deformed, the contact area between rolling elements
and raceway is shifted towards the edge of the raceway. As soon as the original contact area
extends beyond the raceway edge the original contact area is reduced and the remaining area
has to cope with the higher stresses. If the stress exceeds the maximum bearable load the

According to Schwack et al. [44] this type of damage is not respected properly in the DIN ISO
281 [26] for life time calculations of bearings as the standard only considers the mounted
contact angle. The actual contact angles under load, also referred to as free contact angles,
heavily depend on the stiffness of the connection parts. Schwack et al. further says, that the

Stammler and Reuter [48] and Bossanyi [15] present some work on the damage modes of
wind turbine pitch bearings. According to Stammler and Reuter the afore explained damage
modes can be evaluated during the design phase while this is not true for surface-initiated
wear like rotation wear, fretting and false brinelling. These damage modes occur if the lu-
bricant between the rolling elements and the raceway is pressed out of the contact. False
brinelling is a damage process, whereby oscillating loads at standstill of the bearing cause
micro-sliding movements in the hertzian contact area. During the relative motion of the two
bodies to one another, damage mechanisms like adhesion, abrasion and surface disruption
occur. This leads to rapidly decreased lifetime of the bearing [12, 25]. Possible reasons for
all surface-initiated damages are dynamic loading during standstill, very small pitch adjust-
ments or very slow rotations of the bearing. As already mentioned, normal pitch movements
lead to quite high rolling speeds for which reason rotational wear is normally not a dominant
damage mode. However, new control techniques like individual pitch control (IPC) can lead
to critical conditions in the rolling element and raceway contact.
Note, that current calculation methods described in the ISO 281 [26] are not suited for
such conditions. According to the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) no reliable
predictions for the lifetime of rotor blade bearings under non-elasto-hydrodynamic loads are
possible today [30]. This is confirmed by Stammler et al. [50], who further emphasizes the
importance of testing of pitch bearings under realistic conditions and loads.
Several publications investigate the importance of the surrounding structures and the influ-
ence of their stiffness on the loading of the rotor blade pitch bearing. Almost all modern wind
turbine rotor blades are manufactured out of composite materials due to the weight-saving
advantages of such materials [16]. Blades, however, have a relatively low stiffness compared
to steel components and therefore deform heavily under load. The structural shape of the
blade (spar caps, shear webs, aerodynamic profile) together with the anisotropic properties
of composite materials and the large deformations lead to a very unsymmetrical load distri-
bution at the blade root.
The hub usually is a large hollow body made of steel with a quite thin wall thickness
and can therefore not be considered as a rigid support. Indeed, the hub deformations of a
three bladed rotor are quite complex and therefore also have an impact on the loading of the

The importance of support structures is also emphasized by Stammler and Reuter [48].

Chen and Wen [22] agree with this opinion and transfer them to finite element simulations.
They compared the deformations of a bearing which is supported by a fully modelled hub,
a partial hub and a rigid support along the outer ring.
The results show that there are
significant differences in the bearing’s response for the different modelling assumption. The
maximum difference of the contact force in the model for the whole hub is more then twice

Furthermore, they suggest to model all three pitch bearings for the hub to represent the
stiffness of supporting structures accurately.
According to Stammler and Baust et al. [49] the outer load angle also has an impact on
the load magnitude and the relative load distribution of all rolling elements. This is due to
the varying stiffness along the circumference of the blade root flange.

The first common simplification is that rolling element cages, chamfers, fillets and other

In order to save calculation time, the rolling elements in bearings under static loading are
often modelled by non-linear traction-only springs instead of solid elements. Examples are
the work of Daide [24] and Aguirrebeitia [11]. The stiffness behavior of these springs is de-
scribed by a force-deformation curve derived from a Hertzian contact model. Furthermore, to
accurately represent the contact between rolling elements and raceway, rigid shell elements
are covering the approximated contact area. Note, that for ball bearings it is important to
locate the confluence points exactly in the curvature center to accurately simulate the con-
tact angle variation[11, 24]. This is illustrated in Figure 2.5. In roller type bearings this is
less important because there is no contact angle variation in these bearings [49]. Further-
more, Liu et al. [40] showed experimentally that the modelling of roller raceway contact with
Hertzian theory is valid by comparing simulation results with measurements.
2.2. Rotor blades in wind turbines
The rotor blades of a wind turbine extract the kinetic energy from the wind and transform
them into mechanical energy. The following sections mostly focus on the structural charac-
teristics of a blade, but section 2.2.4 discusses the aerodynamics of a blade.
2.2.1. Structural characteristics of rotor blades
A rotor blade of a wind turbine is mainly characterized by a split of functions into load carry-
ing components and aerodynamic components. The load carrying characteristics of a blade
are mainly determined by it’s internal structure, namely the spar caps and shear webs. The
aerodynamic properties of a blade are determined by it’s outer shell like structure which is
generated from airfoils. The main loads, acting on a blade can be divided into two categories:
Aerodynamic loads and gravitational loads.
The aerodynamic loads are the lift and drag
forces acting on the blade which are determined by the aerodynamic properties of the blade
(compare section 2.2.4, Figure 2.10a). The lift force on a blade makes the rotor rotate and is
the driving force for the energy production. The drag force usually acts in wind direction, so
in a right angle to the rotor plane. It has an static component, which permanently bends the
blade in flapwise direction, and a dynamic component resulting from the natural variations
in wind speeds, which causes fatigue. Gravitational loads are due to the mass of the blade
and mostly act in edgewise direction (in the rotor plane) during operation. The highest grav-
itational load occur if the blade is in the horizontal position. As the rotor turns, the gravity
force as seen by the blade changes it’s direction. Consequently, the gravity load is causing

high fatigue loads in the edgewise direction [17].
The forces determine the structural design of the blade. As the blade is an cantilever
like structure (clamped at the hub), the loads increase from tip to root. For this reason,
rotor blades are tapered along their longitudinal direction to economize with weight of the
material. The tapering is usually applied to the external shape and the thicknesses of shells,
shear webs and spar caps, to ensure the same material loading, i.e. maximum allowable
strain [17].

2.2.2. Composite materials of a blade
As mentioned earlier, wind turbine blades are mainly made out of composite materials, which
offer a high strength to weight ratio, good fatigue life and good overall stiffness. The low den-
sity of composite materials reduces the weight of the blade and therefore the gravitational
loads. The high stiffness is required to ensure optimal aerodynamic performance under high
loads and prevents extensive blade deflections. Good fatigue life is important to reduce ma-
terial degradation caused by the dynamic loads [17, 19].
Generally, composites consist of two main components: The very stiff and strong fibers
and the less strong and and rather flexible matrix. The matrix is needed to bind the fibers
together and maintain their positions to form a functional composite.
The stiffness and
strength of the fibers results from the fact, that fibers have far fewer defects then the bulk
material. Consequently, an assembly made of fibers will be much stronger compared to an
assembly of the bulk material with the same volume. Considering the above, it is obvious,
that the high strength of fibers is parallel to their fiber axis, while orthogonal to the fiber axis
the strength is negligible. This means, that a fiber assembly is highly anisotropic [18].
Usually wind turbine manufacturers prefer glass fiber composites over carbon fiber com-
posites due to the lower costs of glass fiber. However, in recent years some researcher inves-
tigated hybrid composite blades. Lin [39], for example, compared the structural performance
of hybrid rotor blades for different ratios of carbon and glass fiber. According to Lin replacing
75 % of the glass fiber in a blade with carbon fiber gives the best structural performance over
cost ratio. However, as this study was performed on a 7.5 m rotor blade it should only be
used as an indication for hybrid blade design, and further investigation for large rotor blades
is necessary.
The matrix is usually made of polymers, typically thermosets or thermoplastics. The most
used thermosets are polyester, venylester and epoxies which have a typical stiffness values
of 3-4 GPa and densities of 1.1-1.3 g/cm3. Thermoplastics have been increasingly under
developement in recent years due to their higher recycling potential, and similar stiffnesses
(1-3 GPa) and densities (0.9-1.4g/cm3) [17].
When creating a composite many combinations of fibers and matrix are possible. Besides
the respective properties of fibers and matrix, the most important parameters are the relative
proportion of fibers in the composite, and the orientation of the fibers. The amount of fibers
can be described with the fiber weight fraction 𝑊፟or, more commonly, with the fiber volume
fraction 𝑉፟. As an example, the stiffness of a composite can be calculated with
𝐸፜= 𝐸፟𝑉፟+ 𝐸፦𝑉፦
(2.1)
where 𝐸፜is the stiffness of the composite, 𝐸፟and 𝑉፟are the stiffness and volume fraction of
the fibers, and 𝐸፦and 𝑉፦are the stiffness and volume fraction of the matrix. In a perfect
composite with no porosity 𝑉፟+𝑉፦= 1. From Equation 2.1 it becomes obvious, that the fibers
are usually dominant for the stiffness of the composite [17]. Let us consider a typical glass
fiber-epoxy composite with 50% fiber volume, a Young’s modulus of the fibers of 𝐸፟= 72𝐺𝑃𝑎,
and an epoxy matrix with 𝐸፦= 3𝐺𝑃𝑎. This results in
𝐸፜= 𝐸፟𝑉፟+ 𝐸፦𝑉፦= 72𝐺𝑃𝑎⋅0.5 + 3𝐺𝑃𝑎⋅0.5 = 36𝐺𝑃𝑎+ 1.5𝐺𝑃𝑎= 37.5𝐺𝑃𝑎
where the fibers stiffness makes up 96 % of the overall composite stiffness.
As the highest stiffness and strength of a composite is achieved longitudinal to the fibers,
the main fiber direction is usually aligned with the main loading direction. For this reason,
the spar caps of a wind turbine blade consist mostly of an uni-axial laminate where the main
fiber direction is along the span of the blade. In the blade root section, however, loads from
many directions have to be taken by the material.
In order to ensure sufficient stiffness
and strength plies with different fiber directions are stacked on top of each other forming
a laminate. The elastic properties of this laminate are then determined by the individual
constituents and the thickness and fiber direction of the respective ply. These properties can
be derived with the rule of mixture and more advanced laminate calculation procedure [17].
These rules are applied and explained in section 4.1, where the material properties for the
blade model used in this thesis are derived.

For the skin and shear webs of rotor blades, sandwich layups are used. As these compo-
nents are loaded in shear and compression rather then tension (like the spar caps), they need
to have high buckling strength. Sandwich constructions are made of two (or more) facesheets
on the surface of the composite, and a light weight core. In rotor blades the core typically
consists of balsa wood or foam. By increasing the thickness of the core, the resistance to
buckling can be drastically increased, because the area moment of inertia increases with
the thickness cubed, and therefore the bending stiffness also increased rapidly. At the same
time, the added weight is small due to the low density of the core [36].
2.2.3. Modelling techniques of rotor blades
For the FE-modelling of rotor blades three different approaches are commonly used: The
design with beam elements, solid elements and shell elements.
In his work, Celso [34] compared the accuracy range of nonlinear beam elements with the
more researched shell elements. He illustrated that the beam model predicts very similar
responses compared to a shell element as long as no local buckling is present in the blade
and torsional effects are small. For cases where the blade experiences considerable twist the
results reveal a too high torsional stiffness leading to inaccuracies in the results.
According to Laird et al. [38], up to the time of publishing their paper in 2005, the common
approach for modelling a wind turbine blade was to use layered shell elements with the
nodes offset to the exterior surface of the blade. In their work, they compared the bending
and torsional response of a blade modelled with nodes offset to the outer surface of the
blade, and nodes placed at the thickness center of the shell element. A difficulty with the
latter approach is that for segments where the material is much thicker than the connecting
sections, the mid-thickness location has to be calculated for every element and the resulting
model is discontinuous. This is illustrated in Figure 2.8.
To simplify the investigation on bending and torsional response, Laird et al. performed
preliminary investigations with a cantilevered hollow cylinder. The advantage of this sim-
plification is, that there are analytical solutions for this type of geometry. The investigation
revealed that under bending the response of both models is sufficient, even for unfavourable
conditions like very small element aspect ratios or low number of elements. Poorly chosen
radius to material thickness ratios, however, can lead to significant differences of about 90
% if compared to the analytical solution. For torsional loads the results for the shell with
mid-nodes were quite sufficient while the results for offset nodes where highly inaccurate for
nearly all aspect ratios, number of elements and radius to thickness ratios. Furthermore, the
error increased for finer meshes which is completely contradictory to the general FE theory.
After ’an intensive interaction with ANSYS Inc.’ the authors concluded, that the results are
not due to an implementation problem of the FE theory in Ansys but a problem in the FE
theory itself. To verify this conclusion the results were reproduced in other FE-programs.

2.3. The finite element method
The finite element method is a method to solve field problems numerically. A field problem
requires the determination of the spatial distribution of one or more dependent variables,
which can be for example displacements or stresses of a plate. They are described by differ-
ential equations or integral expressions [23].
In order to solve such a problem, firstly, the nature of the problem has to be analyzed.
This involves i.e.
identifying the most important physical phenomena, if non-linearity is
involved, and what accuracy is required. Afterwards, the problem can be modelled. The
modelling process requires, that all information that are essential for the solution are repre-
sented within the model, but unimportant details are excluded. The geometrical model can
==PAGE:36==
18
2. Literature Review and Theoretical Background
then be translated into a mathematical model which is described by a set of differential equa-
tions and boundary conditions. The finite element analysis is applied to this mathematical
model. Note, that FEA is not the reality but a simulation, and that FEA is only applied to a
mathematical model. Therefore, an appropriate mathematical model is essential to produce
usable results which are in accordance with physical reality.
Figure 2.12: Method of transforming a structure into a FE-model [23]
The mathematical model is discretized by dividing it into a mesh of finite elements where
each element only describes a small portion of the finite element structure. It is important
to notice that the finite element method only approximates solutions because every field
quantity is only allowed to have a simple spatial variation [23]. The transformation from a
physical structure to an FE-model representation is illustrated in Figure 2.12.
Elements are connected by nodes. The finite element is solved for the unknowns at these
nodes and the solutions for the nodal quantities determine the spatial variation of the ele-
ment. Thus the solution for the entire structure is approximated element by element. Refin-
ing the mesh, so increasing the number of elements, can lead to a more exact solution, but
results in a more complex model which is computationally more expensive. Therefore, one
always has to ponder which accuracy is required [23, 37].
For solving a finite element problem, local stiffness and mass matrices have to be cal-
culated for each element. The mass and stiffness of each element depend on the material
properties that are associated with the element (for example density, Young’s modulus, Pois-
son ration), and the degrees of freedom of the element. The local element and mass matrices
are then assembled into the global matrices, and the boundary conditions are applied. This
way, the following general equation system can be set up:

2.3.1. The SHELL281 element
As mentioned in section 2.2.3, the aerodynamic structure of the blade is a shell like structure
and can therefore be represented by a 2D shell element. The chosen shell element formula-
tion is the SHELL281 element which is well suited for modelling thin or moderately thick shell
structures. SHELL281 is defined by 8 nodes, 4 mid-side nodes and 4 corner nodes, where
each node has six degrees of freedom. These are translations in all spatial directions and rota-
tions about the x-, y- and z-axis. The element is well suited for non-linear analysis, and most
importantly, supports the definition of composite layups and sandwich constructions. The
shell composite calculation is governed by the Mindlin-Reissner shell theory. The Mindlin-
Reissner shell theory is a refinement of the classical Kirchhoff plate theory and also takes
transverse shear deformations into account which is essential when the shells have a layup
of different composite materials. The transverse shear deformations are taken into account
by allowing the through the thickness-lines to rotate with respect to the shell mid-surface,
while keeping the through the thickness-lines straight. In other words, it is assumed, that
there is a variation of the displacement across the plate, but the thickness of the plate does
not change during the deformation. This is illustrated in Figure 2.13.
Figure 2.13: Shell element according to the Mindlin-Reissner shell theory [9]
The SHELL281 element allows two element shapes which are rectangular shape and triangu-
lar shape. Latter, however, is not recommended as it can lead to local stiffening, and should
be prevented in contact areas [9].
In Ansys, the layup definition and thickness of each layer is performed by assigning so
called section properties to the element. In this thesis, the multi-layer definition of Ansys is
used to define the sections, which allows specifying the thickness, material, orientation and
number of integration points through the thickness. Latter two, however, remain on default
as the material properties for different fiber orientations in the composite are pre-calculated
and saved as orthotropic materials (see section 4.1). For this element all keyoption setting
remained on the default value [9, 23].
2.3.2. The SOLID185 element
The SOLID185 element is a 3D solid element and is used for the modelling of the blade
root. The blade root is designed as a 3D structure to allow the implementation of bolt holes
such that the blade can be mounted by bolts to the bearing. The element type has eight
corner nodes with three degrees of freedom at each node (Translations in the nodal x, y
and z direction). This element type is recommended for modelling homogeneous massive
structures, and is also well suited for non-linear analysis. Furthermore, orthotropic material
properties can be assigned to the element. This is important as the blade root is mainly
modelled with orthotropic material properties.
Various element shapes are allowed for this element including the regular rectangular
solid shape, prism shape, tetrahedral shape and pyramid shape. The latter two, however,
are not recommended as they might lead to too high local stiffnesses. For this element all
keyoption setting remain on the default value [9].

Preliminary Investigation of a Shell-Solid
Contact
When modelling the rotor blade, the aerodynamic part of the blade modelled with shell ele-
ments, and the blade root modelled with solid elements, have to be connected with a contact.
This chapter discusses the configuration of such a shell-solid contact and the typical pitfalls
connected to it.
Contact definitions are apparent wherever components with different characteristics - for
example different element types or mesh densities - are connected, or where bodies generate
contact due to deformations of the respective bodies. Contacts can consider friction or they
can be stiff. In either case, one has to define a contact area with contact elements, and a
target area with target elements, which together build a contact pair. In a pair-based contact
the program only considers the interaction between the surfaces of these areas [10].
Nodes in contact
with each other
Nodes in contact
with each other
Figure 3.1: Exemplary demonstration of the pinball radius
In case of the shell-solid contact of the solid element blade root with the shell part of the blade,
the multipoint constraint (MPC) definition is used (Keyopt(2) = 2 of the contact element).
MPCs are used because in reality the contact between the shells and solid elements is not
existent but the blade is one single component. Consequently, friction, sliding or penetration
is not allowed in the contact area. This is ensured by using the MPC definition with the
always bonded keyoption setting (Keyopt(12) = 5). The main reason why a contact definition
is used to connect shell and solid elements is that both element types have different nodal
23
==PAGE:42==
24
3. Preliminary Investigation of a Shell-Solid Contact
degrees of freedom. The solid element only has three translational degrees of freedom, while
the shell element has three translational and three rotational degrees of freedom. To transfer
the reaction forces correctly, a contact definition is necessary. Furthermore, the parts usually
have different mesh densities. For this reason they do not share nodes.
To further define, between which contact and target nodes MPC equations are generated,
a so called pinball radius can be defined. If a constant pinball radius is defined, the contact
node only searches for target nodes in the specified radius around the node. Ansys then
only creates constraint equations between these nodes. The principle of the pinball radius is
illustrated in Figure 3.1.
In the following, the shell solid contact definition is illustrated with a simplified example.
The model consists of a plate which is partially modelled with solid elements and partially
modelled with shell elements. The two sections have the same isotropic material properties
and the same thickness, but a different mesh density. The plate is clamped on the solid side,
and loaded with a displacement loading on the shell side. The set-up is shown in Figure 3.2.
Solid elements are shown in grey and shell elements are shown in red.
Figure 3.2: Shell-solid assembly
In a shell-solid contact, the contact edge must be on the shell elements side and the target
surface must be on the solid element side [10]. Here, the contact is defined in exactly the
same manner as it is defined in the later blade model. The CONTA175 element type is used
to defined the contact edge and the TARGE170 element type is applied to the target surface.
The model uses the always bonded MPC definition and has a constant pinball radius corre-
sponding to the edge length of the shell elements. This pinball radius is chosen to ensure
contact detection but prevent an over-constrained contact. If the pinball radius is very large,
every contact node creates contact with every target node, which can lead to a wrong defor-
mation behavior of the structure. If the pinball radius is too small, no contact is detected,
which, again, can lead to a wrong deformation behavior.
As already mentioned, solid and shell elements have different degrees of freedom. This
is an important note that has to be considered in the contact design by constraining both
translational and rotational degrees of freedom for the shell-solid assembly. This can be done
by specifying the keyoption 5 of the target element to be Keyopt(5) = 4. With this setting all
degrees of freedom are constrained on both, the shell and the solid side, if an intersection is
found between the contact normal and the target surface. If no intersection is found, but a
pinball radius is specified, constraint equations are build anywhere inside the pinball region.
Figure 3.3 illustrates the importance of constraining all degrees of freedom. Note, that the
thickness plotting of the shell elements is turned off to improve visibility of the phenomenon.

Modelling of the Rotor Blade in Ansys
The first main task in this thesis is to design a scalable rotor blade model in Ansys for simple
modification of the blade properties. The parametric model of the rotor blade is written in the
Ansys Parametric Design Language (APDL) to spare the use of a third party program. How-
ever, it is convenient to create the input files containing i.a. information about the physical
shape and the laminate definitions of the blade, in Matlab or Python to prevent user errors
when writing the input files. The formatting of the input scripts is crucial and should not be
altered to ensure correct definition of all blade parameter in Ansys.
Firstly, this section describes the derivation of the material parameters used for the com-
posite layup of the blade.
Afterwards, the creation of the aerodynamic part of the blade
with shell elements is explained, and subsequently the creation of the blade root with solid
elements is described. Lastly, the baseline blade is validated, and differences between the
baseline blade and other blade designs are presented. Note, that all blade properties and il-
lustrations given in section 4.2 to 4.4 describe the baseline blade. The baseline blade serves
as a reference design for the parameter study performed in Chapter 5, and allows compara-
bility of the influence of different blade properties. Further note, that designing a whole new
rotor blade is extensive and requires a lot of experience, and is therefore beyond the scope of
this thesis. Alternatively, an existing blade design is used as an inspiration for the baseline
blade to also ensure structural integrity of the blade. The blade design that serves as an
inspiration is the IWT 7.5 MW reference wind turbine of the Fraunhofer IWES, short IWT7.5.
This blade design is chosen, because for this turbine a bearing model and a hub model are
available in Ansys.
To maintain readability of the APDL code it is split into several files which are also ex-
plained in the following chapters. Note, that only the main file Blade_v6.txt is meant to be
altered by the user for different blade designs. All other files, which in the following will be
called functions, are called in the main file and should not be changed.
4.1. Material parameter of the blade
This section presents the derivation of the material parameter for the baseline blade by means
of simple micro-mechanics equations (rule of mixture) and classical laminate theory.
The blade is mainly made of a glass fiber reinforced composite with different fiber ori-
entations. As core material for the sandwich structures of the blade, balsa wood is used,
and three different multi-directional plies are defined as materials. To define the composite
layups, the three composite materials and the balsa core material are stacked.

4.3. Modelling of the blade root
The blade root is modelled with SOLID185 elements to enable the connection of the blade
with bolts to the pitch bearing. The method used to create the blade root is fundamentally
different from the approach chosen for creating the shell part.
In the previous section, firstly, a geometric model defined by areas was created, and then
the areas were meshed to create the FE-model with nodes and elements. For the blade root,
the nodal positions are directly calculated from the input. For every node, the radial distance
𝑅, the angular position 𝜃, and the axial position z in the cylindrical coordinate system is
calculated, and every node has it’s own specific node number. The origin and orientation
of the z-axis of the cylindrical coordinate system coincides with the global Cartesian blade
coordinate system. When all nodes are defined, the nodes are connected by the elements,
which are mapped onto the nodes. The blade root cross section is shown in Figure 4.9.
Figure 4.9: Blade root cross section
The thickness of the skin illustrated in dark green is defined according to the laminate thick-
ness of the spar cap in the contact area. This is done, because for real blade designs the skin
of the blade root continues into the spar caps when the balsa wood core ends. The inserts
are made out of steel and are illustrated in grey. The part between the inserts, illustrated
in light green, is defined with the same material as the outer and inner laminate for all in-
vestigated blades, but can also be adjusted by the user to have different material properties
than the skin. Both, the inner and outer laminate and the material between the laminate is
tri-axial composite material. The thickness of the blade root at the flange is defined in the
input script, but for all blades used in this thesis, the total thickness is 11 cm. The blade
root core is illustrated in orange and consists of balsa wood. This core tailors towards the
shell part of the blade while the thickness of the laminate remains constant.
For the solid blade root the element coordinate systems also had to be adjusted. This was
done by making use of the cylindrical coordinate system in the origin of the global Cartesian
coordinate system. Modifying the element coordinate system to follow the cylindrical coordi-
nate system leads to an orientation, where the x-axis is normal to the laminates plane and
the z-axis is aligned with the global z-axis. Consequently, the previous material definition
for the shell elements are no longer valid as the orientations are different (x- and z-axis are
interchanged). Consequently, new materials have to be defined with the x- and z-axis prop-
erties interchanged to define the material directions correctly.
Now, the blade’s aerodynamic shell section and the blade root are defined, and are connected
by the shell-solid contact established in Chapter 3. The early testing of the model, however,
revealed convergence problems, which could be linked to the definition of the shell-solid
contact. To remedy the convergence issues, the mesh on the shell side was refined and the

These adjustments make the model computationally more expensive, but ensure convergence
for every blade configuration investigated in the parameter analysis.

4.5. Variations in the blade design
This section introduces the different blade designs that are investigated in the parameter
analysis. Thereby, three main blade configurations are distinguished, which differ in number
of shear webs and spar caps. Furthermore, some special cases are investigated, where i.e.
the shear webs end far before the blade root.
The blades designed for the parameter analysis do not necessarily represent realistic blade
designs and are extreme in some cases, but are treated like tools to investigate the influence
of the blade property on the bearing loads. However, all blade designs have approximately
the same bending stiffness and mass to be able to compare the influence of one parameter
rather then the influence of a stiffer or more flexible overall blade design. This is ensured by
investigating the mass, flapwise tip displacement, edgewise tip displacement and eigenfre-
quencies of every blade. The influence of the overall stiffness of a blade is also investigated in
the parameter analysis for a blade, which uses 30 % less material compared to the baseline
blade. All blade design are discussed below.

5.1. The influence of the shear webs
This section investigates, how the ball contact forces and contact angles change, if the num-
ber of shear webs in the blade changes. The analysis is performed for two different blades
and two load cases. The two blades are the baseline blade with two shear webs (Figure 4.14b,
page 44), and the basis blade with one shear web (Figure 4.16b, page 46). The two blades
only differ in the number of shear webs, all other parameter, i.e. width and thickness of the
spar caps, are identical.
Flapwise load case
Figure 5.4 on page 56 compares the results for the contact forces for the two blades for the
flapwise load case. In both cases, the results show the typical characteristic load distribution,
which is dominated by the blade’s load carrying structures (compare section 2.1.2, page 9).
Depending on the angular position, only one spring of each ball transmits a force. For an
angular position of 0° to 60° and 300° to 360° (push side of the bearing) only spring B carries a
load, while spring A is load free. The opposite is true for the pull side of the bearing between
100° and 260°, where only spring A is loaded. Furthermore, the rolling elements located
around the tipping point of the bearing at around 90° to 270° do not carry any load. This
is a consequence of the pure flapwise load case. For a load case with flapwise, edgewise

54

and radial loads no load free balls are existent. The higher amplitudes of the contact forces
on the push side of the bearing compared to the pull side of the bearing are a result of the
ovalization of the blade root shown in Figure 4.13a on page 43. There it can be see, that
under a flapwise load, the blade root tapers on the push side but flattens on the pull side.
Consequently, the forces on the push side are higher and spread over less rolling elements,
while on the pull side the contact forces are lower and spread over more rolling elements.
Moreover, for both blades, the loads are equally divided over the raceways of the bearing at
every angular position. An equally divided load is always desirable, because it prevents high
forces and therefore stress concentrations on one raceway.
The results for the two blades show no significant differences in the contact forces at any
angular position. The angular positions of the peak contact forces are exactly the same for
both blades (5° on the push side and 175° on the pull side). The difference between the
maximum contact forces at 5° is about 1%. On the pull side at 175° the difference in the
maximum contact force is even lower with less then 1%. The results suggest, that the num-
ber of shear webs has no important influence on the contact forces in the bearing.
Figure 5.5 on page 57 compares the change of the contact angles for the two blades for the
flapwise load case. Thereby, only the contact angles of loaded springs are of interest. For
this reason, Figure 5.5 shows the contact angles of unloaded springs as 0°. Note, that the
results for the contact angles always describe, how much the contact angles change from their
original position of ±45°, and do not give the absolute contact angle. For the baseline blade,
the contact angles increase by about 21° on the pull side of the bearing while they increase
by only about 9° on the push side. The change in contact angles generally corresponds to the
deformations of the inner ring of the bearing, so high changes in contact angles correspond
to high ring deformations. This is confirmed by Figure 5.2 on page 52. Here, it can be seen,
that the inner ring indeed tips about 2.4 mm at the top of the bearing, while it only moves
about -1.1 mm at the bottom. As already mentioned, this behavior is caused by the stiffness
of the hub.
The result for the basis blade with one shear web shows no significant difference to the
baseline blade in the contact angles at any angular position. In fact, the maximum contact
angles occur at the exact same angular positions and show no difference in magnitude. The
results suggest, that the number of shear webs has no important influence on the deforma-
tions of the bearing.
The results for the flapwise load are confirmed by investigating four other blades.
For a

the latter four blades.

In conclusion, the results presented in this section reveal, that the bearing’s rolling ele-

blade. Neither for the flapwise load case, nor for the edgewise load case any significant dif-
ferences in the contact forces and contact angles are observed for blades with one or two
shear webs. This is unexpected, and not in accordance with the hypothesis proposed in this
thesis. The author assumed, that the contact forces in the bearing are higher and more con-

For an edgewise load the author expected no significant differences between the blades with
one and two shear webs. This assumption is confirmed.
Furthermore, the author illustrated reasons for the general curve shapes of the contact
force and contact angles results. Unless there are significant differences, the reasons for
the curve shapes are the same for all upcoming results, and are not discussed again in the
following sections.

5.6. The influence of the thickness of the stiffener plate
The analysis of the influence of the stiffener plate on the bearing loads was originally not
intended to be part of this thesis. However, it was found to have a considerable influence on
the contact forces and contact angles, and could therefore be one source for the only minor
differences in the paramter analysis results for the different blades. Therefore included here.
To establish the influence of the stiffener plate, two extreme designs are compared. The first
design is a stiffener plate with a thickness of 1mm while the second design is a stiffness plate
with a thickness of 40mm.
Flapwise load case
Figure 5.29a on page 90 shows the contact forces for the stiffener plate with 40mm thickness
and the flapwise load case. The results show the typical characteristic load distribution which
is dominated by the blade’s load carrying structures, but opposed to the configuration with
the standard stiffener plate (25mm thickness), raceway 2 carries noticeably more load then
raceway 1. For the configuration with the standard stiffener plate, the contact forces on the
two raceways differed by maximally 3.1 kN, while they differ by 8.5 kN for the configuration
with the 40mm stiffness plate. As mentioned earlier, a smaller difference in the contact force
is favourable, as an evenly distributed force reduces stress peaks on one raceway.
Figure 5.29b shows the contact forces for the stiffness plate with 1mm thickness. Again,
the results show the typical characteristic load distribution, but unlike any other investigated
case the maximum contact forces are significantly higher on raceway 1 than on raceway 2
for the push side of the bearing. In fact, the maximum contact force on raceway 1 is 23%
higher than the force on raceway 2, while for all other investigated cases the contact forces
on raceway 2 were higher. This can be explained with the deformation behavior of the inner
ring of the bearing.

5.7. Discussion of the results
This section discusses the results of the parameter study. Therefore, to get more insight on
the influence of the blade properties, this section takes a step back, and analyses the stresses
in the blade root. This analysis is performed to investigate, at which point differences in the
results between the various blade designs occur, and where the influence of the different
shear web and spar cap designs possibly vanish.
The analysis is performed for the two
most different blades. The two blades investigated, are the stiff blade with one shear web
and narrow spar caps, and the stiff blade with two shear webs and wide spar caps. For
the analysis only the main stresses in the main fiber direction, so along the global z-axis,
are investigated. As a contact usually leads to local stress peeks in the element, the five
elements in the blade root closest to the shell-solid contact area are excluded. Furthermore,
Figure 5.33 only compares the stresses for the two blade designs up to the point in the blade
roots, where the massive tri-axial laminate begins and no balsa wood core is present anymore.

for the blade with one shear web and narrow spar caps (59.5 MPa) compared to the blade

spar cap configuration leads to a more even load distribution around the circumference of
the blade root, compared to narrow spar caps. The results support this assumption, but the
difference in the maximum stresses is smaller than expected.
The second thing that can be observed is, that as soon as the massive tri-axial laminate

derived in detail in this thesis and have realistic values. Furthermore, the thickness of the
blade root is determined by the required flange height to be able to connect the blade to the
bearing, and by the diameter of the inserts.
One starting point for future investigations might be a more detailed investigation of the
shell-solid contact between the aerodynamic part of the blade and the blade root. Therefore,
future works should take another step back and investigate in more detail the influence of
the shear webs and spar caps on the stresses and deformations of the shell section of the
blade. This way, the contact definition can be excluded as a source of error.
Only if the influence of blade properties on the blade root section are understood in detail,
the results for the parameter study performed on the bearing in this thesis, can be conclu-
sively verified.

==PAGE:115==
6
Final discussion and prospect
In this thesis, the influence of blade properties on the pitch bearing forces and deformations
was investigated with a scalable rotor blade FE-model. There is a general consent among re-
searchers, that the surrounding structures have an influence on the pitch bearing, but very
little knowledge is existent on how the bearing loads and deformations change for different
properties of surrounding structures.
In order to investigate the influence of different blade properties, many blade models with
different configurations had to be build. To create the models within a reasonable time, a
scalable rotor blade model was necessary. Chapter 4 of this thesis illustrated one way of
designing a scalable rotor blade model in the finite element software Ansys. Thereby, the
skin, shear webs and spar caps of the blade were modelled with 2D shell elements, while
the blade root was modelled with 3D solid element. This approach required the definition
of a shell-solid contact to connect the two element types, which was discussed in chapter
3. To plausible the chosen approach, the results for the baseline blade with two shear webs
and continuous spar caps, which was inspired by the IWT 7.5 MW reference wind turbine
blade of the Fraunhofer IWES, were compared to the results of the DTU 10 MW reference
wind turbine blade. To ensure comparability between the two blades, similarity rules were
applied.
The results showed good compliance, but the baseline blade was comparatively heavy and
stiff. The reason for this is the design height of the IWT 7.5 MW blade, which the baseline
blade was based on. However, the overall structural behavior of the developed baseline blade
model was rated as accurate. Consequently, at that point of the thesis, the general concept
of modelling a rotor blade with shell elements and a solid blade root, could be made plau-
sible. Afterwards, several blades with different shear web and spar cap configurations were
created, which were then used in the parameter analysis to investigate the influence of blade
properties on the bearing. Thereby, the global blade properties like deformation behavior,
bending stiffness and mass had to be similar for all blades to ensure comparability between
the blades.
The research aim of this thesis was to identify blade parameters which are critical for the
bearing. This was done in chapter 5 by means of a parameter analysis. For the parameter
analysis, firstly, the respective blade model was assembled to the model of a double-rowed
four-point-contact ball bearing provided by the Fraunhofer IWES. The bearing was stiffened
with a stiffener plate on the blade side, and connected to the model of a 1/3-hub on the
opposite side. With this configuration, the influence of blade properties can be investigated
with realistic surrounding conditions.
The results for the rolling element contact forces and contact angles of the bearing showed
no significant differences for the blades with different number of shear webs for a flapwise
load. Based in the results, the author concluded, that the number of shear webs in the blade
97
==PAGE:116==
98
6. Final discussion and prospect
has no important influence on the bearing. This is contradictory to the expectations of the
author. Consequently, the hypothesis, that a blade design with two shear webs leads to a
more even distribution of the rolling element contact forces compared to a blade design with
one shear web, is disproved.
Furthermore, the spanwise position of the shear webs has no important influence on the
bearing loads and deformations. The study showed, that for the bearing it does not matter, if
the shear webs extend until the blade root, or if they end far away from the blade root. This
result is again contradictory to the expectations of the author.
In summary, the shear web configuration of the blade has no important influence of the
bearing, neither for a flapwise load, nor for an edgewise load. While this results was expected
for the edgewise load case, the results are unexpected for the flapwise load case. The results
disprove the dependency of the bearing load and deformations on the shear web configura-
tion for a flapwise load.
Moreover, this thesis investigated the influence of the spar cap configuration on the pitch
bearing. The results for the contact forces show some differences for a blade with narrow
spar caps and a blade with wide spar caps. For the flapwise and the edgewise load case,
the maximum rolling element contact forces were slightly lower for a blade design with wide
spar caps compared to a blade with narrow spar caps. The reason for this phenomenon
is that the loads on the blade are spread over a larger portion of the circumference of the
blade root if the spar caps are wide. This result agrees with the hypothesis proposed by the
author. However, in relation to the extreme difference in spar cap width, the differences in
the contact forces were very small. To make matters worse, a blade with wide and thin spar
caps is prone to buckling under an edgewise load. To compensate for this, the blade designer
would have to increase the material thickness of the spar caps in a realistic blade design,
which would increase the weight of the blade. This would then require a stiffer design of the
whole drive train. The benefit of a slightly improved contact force distribution in the bearing
most likely does not outweigh the added material costs for the blade and drive train. Based
on the results, the bearing should not be considered in the spar cap design of the blade.
Furthermore, the bearing deformations show no significant dependency of the spar cap
configuration for a flapwise load or an edgewise load. For the flapwise load, this result is
unexpected and contradicts the hypothesis of the author.
For the blade with divided spar caps, the results show no significant differences in the
bearing loads and deformations compared to a blade with continuous spar caps, neither for
a flapwise load, nor for an edgewise load. For the flapwise load, this result is unexpected.
The results disprove the hypothesis of the author, that divided spar caps lead to a more even
force distribution in the bearing, similarly to a blade with wide spar caps. The results for the
edgewise load are according to the expectations.
The results for the blades with different shear web and spar cap configurations were con-
firmed by investigating the effect of shear webs and spar caps for blades that are less stiff
and utilize 30 % less material. For these flexible blades, the results showed the exact same
tendencies.
All in all, the results of the parameter analysis suggest, that neither the shear web design,
nor the spar cap design, are particularly critical for the bearing. Only extreme blade designs,
which are unfeasible for realistic blades caused small differences on the bearing forces. The
author assumed, that the stiffener plate could be one reason for the insignificance of the
blade properties on the bearing loads and deformations, and that without the stiffener plate
the influence of different shear web and spar cap configurations might have been more pro-
nounced. Therefore, the results with a very thin stiffener plate and a very thick stiffener
plate are compared. The results show, that the stiffener plate has a significant influence on
the bearing loads and deformations. However, the goal of this thesis was to investigate the
influence of different blade properties on the bearing with realistic surrounding structures,
which the stiffener plate is an essential part of. Furthermore, the results do not necessarily
mean, that the stiffener plate is the reason for the absence of clear differences in the results
==PAGE:117==
99
for different blade properties. To narrow down the reasons for the similarity of the results,
the author took a more detailed look at the blade itself, or more specific the blade root section.
The results for the stresses in the main fiber direction in the blade root revealed, that due to
the massive tri-axial composite layup in the blade root, the influence of different shear web
and spar cap designs vanishes entirely.
The results suggest, that the stiffener plate is not the main reason for the similarity of
the results, but rather the blade (model) itself. This result is doubtful and requires further
investigation in future works to verify or falsify the observations made in this thesis.
One source of error could be the shell-solid contact between the aerodynamic part of the
blade and the blade root. Future works should take another step back and investigate in
more detail the influence of the shear webs and spar caps on the stresses and deformations
before the blade root. This way the behavior of the shell section can be investigated in detail,
and the contact definition can be excluded as a source of error. Furthermore, future blade
model designs should utilize a more detailed definition of the areas with different composite
layups in the blade. In this thesis, the blade of 80m length was only split in 11 sections
along the blade span, which resulted in areas that are roughly 7 m long. This means, that
the layup of the blade is constant over 7m, while in reality the composite thickness changes
smoothly over 7m due to dropped or added plies. Even though, the author does not expect
significant changes in the response of the blade for a more detailed layup design, analysis
should be performed to investigate this assumption. Another investigation that is worth ex-
ploring in the future is a case, where multiple loads act on the blade. In this thesis, only
pure edgewise or pure flapwise loads were applied to the blades. If flapwise and edgewise
aerodynamic loads, and gravity loads act on the blade simultaneously, the coupling of the
forces might result in more pronounced effects of shear webs and spar caps. Only if future
investigations of the blade substantiate the observations made in the parameter analysis of
this thesis, the research question can be answered conclusively.
At the current point in time, however, all results suggest, that it does not make sense for
a blade manufacturer to consider the bearing in a blade design, as the shear webs and
spar caps do not cause significant differences in the bearing deformations and loads. The
for the bearing unfavourable loads, which are caused mostly by the spar caps (compare
subsection 2.1.2) for a flapwise load, are rather a general phenomenon of a blade, and can
not be greatly influenced by the blade designer.
Alternatively, the author suggests focusing future research on the development and anal-
ysis of alternative pitch bearing concepts. One alternative bearing concept is the triple-row
cylindrical roller bearing, which has two axial raceways and one radial raceway. The second
bearing concept is a bearing with two axial raceways and spherical rolling elements, and one
radial raceway with cylindrical rolling elements. Both bearing concepts are shown in section
2.1.1 on page 6. These new bearing concepts promise less deformation due to their higher
geometrical stiffness, and therefore reduce the change in contact angles. Consequently, the
bearings are less prone to edge loading. Furthermore, the contact area of the rolling elements
with the raceway is larger for the cylindrical rolling elements, which reduces the peak con-
tact forces. However, not only the bearing deformations and rolling element contact forces
determine the performance of a bearing, but also the tribological characteristics like lubri-
cation. To what extend the new pitch bearing concepts are an improvement over the current
state-of-the-art ball bearings has to be investigated in future works.
For bearing manufactures and researchers, the results of the parameter analysis have an-
other consequence. If the results of this thesis are confirmed in future works, no detailed
blade model is required to achieve the characteristic load distribution in the bearing. This is
an advantage, because instead of designing a blade for every bearing size, the blade model
can simply be scaled to any bearing size. The development of a scalable rotor blade model,
which is only depended on one scaling factor, is another future task. Furthermore, the shell
solid contact, which connects the aerodynamic shell of the blade with the blade root, requires
further investigation on future projects. In the current blade model the contact requires an

Results for an Assembly without Hub
Figure B.1 shows the contact forces of the balls in the bearing for an assembly without hub
under a flapwise load. Note, that this assembly uses the baseline blade. The maximum con-
tact forces are nearly equal on the push and pull side of the bearing. Figure B.2 shows the
contact angles. The maximum contact angles on the push and pull side of the bearing are
nearly equal which suggests, that the bearing deformations are about equal on the push and
pull side. Comparing the results to any assembly where the hub is considered, reveals, that
the deformations and contact forces are much more evenly distributed if no hub is present.
This shows the influence of the hub stiffness on the bearing deformation and contact forces
for a flapwise load.
Figure B.3 shows the contact forces of the balls in the bearing for an assembly without hub
under a edgewise load. Note, that this assembly uses the baseline blade. Note, that the
reason for the different curve shapes of Force A1 and Force A2 around 90° are a result of
the constraints on the outer ring of the bearing. However, the results also show the dip in
the contact forces and contact angles at around 90°, so this dip is most likely caused by
the blade and not by the hub. Figure B.4 shows the contact angles for an edgewise load.
Opposed to an assembly where the hub is considered the contact angles are higher on the
push side than on the pull side. Again, this shows the influence of the hub stiffness on the
bearing deformations for an edgewise load.
