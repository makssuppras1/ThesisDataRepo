Abstract
Filamentous fungi are a valuable source of bioactive secondary metabolites, many of which
have potential applications as natural dyes or drug candidates. A notable example is the TMC-
120 compound family produced by Aspergillus insuetus, which has demonstrated promising
anti-seizure activity. The purpose of this thesis is to explore the biosynthesis of secondary
metabolites in filamentous fungi, with a particular focus on the metabolic profiles of the fungal
strains Aspergillus insuetus IBT 28265 and A. calidoustus IBT 28268. Special attention is given
to the production of TMC-120 metabolites and their oxygenated analogs.
Dereplication was performed using HPLC-DAD-MS and data analysis through the open-source
database GNPS. Methods for the large-scale extraction and purification were developed to iso-
late an unknown secondary metabolite detected in A. insuetus, which was purified and sub-
jected to NMR analysis. Through 1D and 2D NMR techniques, the structure of the unknown
secondary metabolite was successfully obtained. Comparative studies revealed that A. insue-
tus consistently produced higher quantities of TMC-120 compounds across various cultivation
conditions, except the TMC-120 analogue Ustusorane B, which A. calidoustus was produced
in higher amounts when cultivated in MEA. Furthermore, A. calidoustus produced only the
nitrogen-based TMC-120 compounds in CYA, while exclusively producing the oxygen-based
analogs in PDA, suggesting that the nitrogen source availability plays a role in the metabolite
expression. Metabolites solely produced by each species were also identified. A. insuetus pro-
duced aurentinamine, which was absent in A. calidoustus, while the latter uniquely produced
(-)-Phenylahistin. These results indicate that, while both strains exhibit similar biosynthetic ac-
tivity, clear strain-specific metabolomic preferences are evident. Overall, this study concludes
that A. calidoustus does not serve as an alternative for superior production of TMC-120 deriva-
tives compared to A. insuetus.

Introduction
Fungi can be found everywhere, from soil, plants, and seas, even to be found in animals and
the human body. Fungi can be found in various forms, including yeasts, mushrooms, and toad-
stools. However, the type most relevant to this project is molds, also known as filamentous
fungi [1]. Fungi have an essential role in our ecosystem since they are responsible for breaking
down biological matter. Through this process, they release carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, and phos-
phor into the soil and atmosphere [2]. Spoilage of different materials comes from mold spores.
There are around 50 genera of fungi which is essential for spoilage. Each exhibits unique char-
acteristics and has specific environmental requirements for growth, including factors such as
material to grow on, humidity, pH, and temperature. The most relevant genera of fungi related
to spoilage are Penicillium, Fusarium, and Aspergillus [1].
Each unique fungi consist of different enzymes, which makes it possible for the mold to grow on
various materials. These enzymes are also responsible for enzyme-mediated reactions. This,
together with some regulated chemical reactions, is referred to as intermediary metabolism.
The multiple pathways involved in this process are collectively referred to as metabolic path-
ways. These metabolic pathways are essential for modifying carbohydrates, proteins, fats, and
nucleic acids into beneficial compounds for either growth, development, or reproduction within
the organism. This process is known as primary metabolism, and the compounds involved are
referred to as primary metabolites. However, fungi produce some unique organic compounds
not found in other parts of nature. These compounds are referred to as secondary metabolites
and can vary from one fungus to another. However, the benefits and roles of some secondary
metabolites in organisms remain unknown. Fungi have gained significant interest in various
research fields due to the organisms’ ability to synthesize unique chemical compounds that are
sometimes useful in everyday applications. Some areas where the use or interest of these
secondary metabolites is studied include mycotoxins, drug candidates, and dyes [1][3].
1.1
Mycotoxins
Mycotoxins are secondary metabolites produced by molds that can trigger toxic responses in
other organisms, even at low concentrations. In animals, these toxins may enter the body via.
Inhalation, ingestion, or skin contact [1]. The toxicity of a mycotoxin depends on its chemical
structure and concentration. The production of mycotoxins is a result of the mold trying to protect
4
==PAGE:7==
itself from other organisms, especially bacteria, which often thrive under the same conditions as
the molds. This results in a battle of resources between bacteria and the mold. Understanding
which types of molds produce harmful toxins is crucial for the safety of both humans and ani-
mals, especially given the role that certain molds play in the food industry, such as fermentation
and cheese production. There have been instances where outbreaks of mass mortality among
groups of domesticated animals have been linked to mycotoxin-contaminated feed. Therefore,
awareness and management of mycotoxin-producing molds are essential for protecting public
and animal health in both food production and consumption contexts [1].
1.2
Drug candidates
As mentioned before, molds can produce secondary metabolites to protect themselves from
bacteria. The substances can have anti-bacterial properties while simultaneously being safe for
human or animal consumption. One example of such a discovery was the metabolite penicillin
G produced by Penicillium chrysogenum. When working with the bacterium Staphylococcus au-
reus, Alexander Fleming found contamination of Penicillium chrysogenum inside the petri-dish.
He observed inhibition in the growth of the bacterium where the mold grew, thus discovering the
anti-bacterial properties of the metabolite penicillin G. [1] The secondary metabolites TMC-120A
and TMC-120C have been identified in the fungal species A. insuetus and A. calidoustus, which
are the fungi of primary focus in this project. These compounds have demonstrated potential
as anti-seizure agents for the treatment of epilepsy and will be discussed in greater detail later
in this thesis [4] [5].
1.3
Dyes
Filamentous fungi have also been shown to produce a variety of pigments as secondary metabo-
lites. This includes chemical classes of colors such as carotenoids, melanins, flavins, monascins,
violacin, and indigo [6]. The use of fungi-derived colors in food has been practiced for thousands
of years. In East Asia, dried fermented red rice powder is produced by fungal metabolism. To-
day, the use of filamentous fungi as a natural source for food coloring is an expanding field of
research driven by its lower production costs compared to synthetic methods. Additionally, cul-
tivating fungi requires simpler conditions than other natural pigment sources, making it a more
practical and efficient alternative [7]. However, concerns regarding co-produced mycotoxins
from filamentous fungi complicate the development process, requiring comprehensive toxicity
5
==PAGE:8==
studies to ensure the safe production of these pigments. Today, we can see filamentous fungi-
derived pigments in the market with products such as Arpink redTM from Penicillium oxalicum
and β-carotene from Blakeslea trispora [6].
2
Aim of this project
This project aims to explore the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites in the two filamentous
fungi Aspergillus insuetus and Aspergillus calidoustus. As part of this work, I will gain practi-
cal experience in the cultivation, extraction, and purification of filamentous fungi and their sec-
ondary metabolites. The metabolites will be analyzed using high-performance liquid chromatog-
raphy (HPLC) coupled with diode-array detection (DAD) and high-resolution mass spectrome-
try (HRMS). Structural elucidation of selected compounds will be performed through 1D and
2D nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy. Additionally, metabolomic tools such as
Global Natural Products Social Molecular Networking (GNPS) will be applied for dereplication
and comparison of the metabolites produced by the two fungal strains. Furthermore, A. insue-
tus has been reported to produce TMC-120 and related analogues, which have shown potential
anti-seizure activity. In this project, a comparative analysis of the fungal strains A. insuetus
and A. calidoustus will be conducted to assess whether the closely related A. calidoustus could
serve as an alternative producer of these bioactive metabolites.

Filamentous fungi
3.1
5th Kingdom
All living organisms are broadly categorized into five biological kingdoms: Animalia, Plantae,
Protista, Monera, and Fungi. While the animal and plant kingdoms are widely familiar, the
remaining three include less commonly discussed but equally important life forms. The Protista
kingdom comprises primitive eukaryotic organisms such as green algae. The Monera kingdom
consists of all prokaryotic microorganisms, primarily bacteria and archaea, and is characterized
by the absence of a true nucleus [8].
The fifth kingdom, fungi, is a diverse and ecologically significant group of eukaryotes. Unlike
protists, fungi are more complex and share some structural features with both animals and
plants. However, they are distinct from plants as they lack chlorophyll and are incapable of
6
==PAGE:9==
photosynthesis. In contrast to animals, fungi do not possess mouths or digestive tracts but
instead absorb nutrients through their hyphal structures [9].
Fungal cells are encased in rigid carbohydrate-rich cell walls and often form elongated, tubular
structures known as hyphae. These hyphae collectively create a dense, thread-like network
referred to as mycelium, which is responsible for nutrient uptake and environmental interaction
for the fungi [1]. An illustration of the mycelium on different scales can be seen in Figure 1.
Figure 1: Mycelium at different scales from left to right: A) Structure of a common mushroom
and its root system, B) the mycelium branched network, C) the hypha tubes, and D) the structure
for composition of each cell inside the hypha
A subset of fungi is known as filamentous fungi, named for their thread-like hyphal growth. While
not a formal taxonomic category, filamentous fungi are commonly found across several fungal
divisions, most notably Zygomycota and Ascomycota. The Ascomycota division, commonly
referred to as sac fungi, which is the largest division of fungi, defined by its reproductive strategy.
These fungi form spores inside specialized sac-like structures called ”asci”, which is the reason
for the division’s name [2].
The Ascomycota division encompasses a wide array of genera characterized by diverse mor-
phological and metabolic traits [10]. One particularly important genus is Aspergillus, known for
its relevance in biotechnology, food spoilage, and human health. Species from this genus are
frequently found on starchy food products, as well as in damp indoor environments such as
walls and insulation materials [1].
This thesis focuses on two Aspergillus species: A. calidoustus and A. insuetus. These species
have been isolated from environments such as indoor air, construction materials, and occa-
sionally from clinical settings where they may act as opportunistic pathogens [4]. Due to their
resilience in harsh conditions and potential to produce novel secondary metabolites, they serve
7
==PAGE:10==
as intriguing subjects for further study, particularly in the context of fungal metabolomics and
natural product discovery.
4
Natural product chemistry
Natural product chemistry is the study of compounds that are produced by living organisms.
These organisms include plants, bacteria, and, more importantly for this project, filamentous
fungi [11]. As mentioned earlier, Aspergillus is one of the more interesting genera of fungi
that has been studied a lot due to its widespread presence in different areas of the environ-
ment. They are also highly bioactive organisms capable of producing a wide range of sec-
ondary metabolites, even under harsh conditions, with some species remaining bioactive at
37oC [4][12]. This yields Aspergillus to produce both toxic and beneficial secondary metabo-
lites for animals and humans. An example of such metabolites is Ophiobolin analogous reported
from Marine-Derived Aspergillus insuets. Ophiobolins are sesterpenoid compounds that show
a wide span of bioactivity, including antifungal and antibacterial properties [13].
The types of secondary metabolites mainly produced by fungi can be classified into four classes:
polyketides, terpenes, non-ribosomal peptides (NRPs), and alkaloids [12]. The previously men-
tioned sesterterpenoid, such as ophiobolins, are examples of compounds that are structurally re-
lated to terpenes. The enzyme classes responsible for producing these metabolites are: polyke-
tide synthases (PKS), non-ribosomal peptide synthases (NRPS), terpene synthases (TC), and
dimethylallyl tryptophan synthases (DMATS) [12]. The different enzyme clusters can produce
these metabolites through unique biosynthetic reaction sequences [3]. The structure and bio-
chemical reaction mechanisms for producing some of these secondary metabolites will be ex-
plained further in the following subsections.
4.1
Polyketides
Polyketides are a group of highly diverse secondary metabolites that are grouped by their syn-
thetic pathways [3]. Some polyketide structures include polyenes, polyethers, polyphenols,
and more [12]. The diverse polyketide structure is derived from poly-β-keto chains of various
lengths. A series of Claisen reactions from the substrates acetoacetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA
produce these poly-β-keto chains [3]. The reaction Schemes for producing the poly-β-keto es-

4.3
Alkaloids
Alkaloids are used for a wide variety of natural products and are classified as compounds with
one or more nitrogen atoms in a heterocyclic ring [12]. Alkaloids can be grouped into four
different classes: alkaloids derived from amino acids, pruine alkaloids, aminated terpenes, and
polyketide alkaloids. A greater focus will be on the alkaloids from amino acids in this project.
The application of alkaloids in an organism is to act as a chemical defense system. They have,
however, also shown applications for medicinal use in humans.

In natural product research, various fungal strains produce complex biological extracts com-
prising a diverse range of secondary metabolites. To avoid the repeated isolation and structural
characterization of previously identified compounds, dereplication is used as a key strategy in
metabolomic studies. Dereplication involves the rapid identification of known metabolites by
comparing mass spectrometry data, including parent ion masses, adduct patterns, and, most
importantly, fragmentation patterns to established compound databases. Public platforms, such
as the Global Natural Products Social Molecular Networking (GNPS), further facilitate derepli-
cation by enabling molecular networking and spectral matches in available libraries [13]. The
following section outlines the analytical workflow used in metabolomic profiling and provides an
overview of the dereplication techniques employed in this study.

5
Analytical chemistry
5.1
Sample preparation
Before analyzing an organism or its metabolites, the route for preparing the analyte must be
considered to obtain a meaningful chemical analysis of the material of interest. The prepared
sample must be heterogeneous and represent the organism analyzed [16]. Samples obtained
from natural products are usually a complex mixture of chemical and biochemical substances.
These numerous compounds can interfere with an analysis or make the spectra from the an-
alytical instrument harder to interpret. Samples not prepared before being inserted into the
instrument can also contain substances contaminating the analytical instrument [17]. Sample
preparation is therefore the steps for converting a representative bulk of the organism into a
suitable sample for analysis [16].
Sampling for metabolomics research presents several challenges. During metabolite extraction,
the goal is to capture a precise ”snapshot” of the current metabolic state without allowing further
enzymatic activity that could alter metabolite levels. To achieve this, it is essential to rapidly and
effectively quench all metabolic activity. An ideal quenching method stops metabolomic activity
immediately while preserving the metabolites [18]. For quenching, e.g., fungal metabolomic
activity, cold methanol-water mixtures can be used with a methanol content of 40-60% [19].
Another way to enhance the extraction of metabolites from a sample is by introducing the sam-
ple to ultrasonication. Ultrasonication of samples can improve the release of compounds like
secondary metabolites from solid matrices and disrupt cell walls and membranes. This is es-
pecially useful since the method is easily reproducible and does not need high heat or harsh
chemicals to be successful.
After the extraction of metabolites from the solid matrix, further sample preparation techniques
can be used to obtain the desired compound(s). Here, techniques such as solid phase extrac-
tion (SPE) or liquid-liquid extraction can be utilized [16]. A small volume of chromatographic
stationary phase is used for a SPE to isolate or up-concentrate the desired analyte from a more
complex matrix. The stationary phase is loaded into a small column and can be tailored to the
specific needs of obtaining better separation. With modifications, preparing the column for ion-
exchange, normal phase, and reverse phase extractions, which is explained later in this thesis
under section 5.2. The extraction is carried out in the following steps, illustrated in Figure 7: a)
The column is conditioned with both the solid and the liquid phase, which includes rinsing the
14
==PAGE:17==
column with the used solvent system. b) The crude product is added to the column. c) Differ-
ent solutes are eluted depending on the interactions between the column and the compounds
(more interaction between the solid phase and analytes makes the analytes elute more slowly).
d) The desired analyte is eluted and obtained [16].
Figure 7: Setup for solid phase extraction
The other method for separating metabolites is liquid-liquid extraction. The principle of this
extraction is to have an aqueous layer and an organic liquid layer, which are immiscible with each
other. The setup for a liquid-liquid extraction is illustrated in Figure 8. The sample is dissolved in
the aqueous layer, followed by adding the organic layer. The sample is then shaken vigorously,
and an interaction between small droplets of both solutions occurs. In this interaction, the more
apolar analytes will transfer into the organic layer while the polar analytes stay in the aqueous
layer. The sample is then set to remain still, which allows the two layers to separate based on
the density of the solvents. Typically, the aqueous layer settles at the bottom, and the organic
layer is on top after separation. However, when organic solvents such as dichloromethane
or chloroform are used, the organic layer appears at the bottom due to their higher density
compared to water [17].
15
==PAGE:18==
Figure 8: Setup for liquid-liquid extraction
Choosing the right solvents is important for achieving a good extraction. For acidic and basic
analytes, the management of pH is vital for controlling the distribution of these analytes. By
reducing the pH of the aqueous layer below the pKa of acidic analytes, the analytes will be neu-
tralized and are therefore more susceptible to go into the organic layer. Likewise, increasing
the pH above the pKa of the basic analytes. The otherwise protonated analytes will be neu-
tralized and go into the organic layer. Picking the right organic solvent is also important for a
successful liquid-liquid extraction. The principle of ”like dissolves like” can be considered. In-
dicating that more apolar solvents are more prone to dissolve apolar analytes. An example of
some organic apolar solvents used for liquid-liquid extraction is shown in Table 2. Other factors,
such as the viscosity and volatility of the solvents, should also be considered. A less viscous
solvent may enhance interaction with the aqueous layer, improving analyte transfer. In contrast,
a more volatile organic solvent facilitates faster evaporation, thereby reducing the time required
to prepare the analytes for analysis [17].
5.2
Chromatography
Chromatography is the separation of different compounds in a sample based on their interac-
tions with the stationary and mobile phases. In gas chromatography (GC), the mobile phase

5.2.1
Gas chromatography
GC is used for separating volatile and semi-volatile compounds, with the setup for GC illustrated
in Figure 9. The setup consists of a carrier gas, a reduction valve for flow control, an injection
port, the capillary column, which is inside a column oven, and lastly, a detector [17].
Figure 9: Schematic for Gas Chromatography setup [20]
The carrier gas must be inert to avoid any deterioration of the stationary phase inside the column.
This leads to ”column bleeding”, producing elevated background signals and can contaminate
17
==PAGE:20==
the detector. The carrier gases used for GC are nitrogen, hydrogen, and helium. For injection,
the samples are dissolved in a volatile solvent. The solvent is then evaporated inside the injector
and injected into the column via a microsyringe. The column is a capillary tube made of fused
silica and coated with polyimide for mechanical support. There are three types of stationary
phases for the column: Wall-coated open tubular column, Porous-layer open tubular column,
and a packed column, illustrated in Figure 10 [17][16].
Figure 10: Different column types for Gas Chromatography; a) Wall-coated open tubular col-
umn, b) Porous-layer open tubular column, and c) Packed column
The wall-coated open columns are the most commonly used since they provide a higher res-
olution than the other two. One crucial factor for the retention and separation of components
in GC is the interactions between these components and the stationary phase. Therefore, the
choice of the stationary phase depends on the components you want to separate. Here, the
concept of ”like-dissolves-like” can be applied with a polar stationary phase for improved sepa-
ration of polar and apolar compounds. An example of some stationary phases with an increase
in polarity is illustrated in Figure 11 [17][16].

==PAGE:21==
Figure 11: Examples of stationary phases for Gas Chromatography with increasing polarity
The other essential factors for the retention time and separation are the temperature and pres-
sure inside the column. A controlled constant pressure is important for the consistency and
reproducibility of the chromatogram. When running a chromatogram, it is important to maintain
both upper and lower temperature limits to ensure stable and reliable separation. If the tem-
perature falls below the lower limit, there is a risk that compounds may tail or even become
retained on the column due to low volatility. The upper limit usually describes the limit at which
the stationary phase is stable. The compounds analyzed might also be unstable and break
down at temperatures above the higher limit. Gas chromatography can be run at two different
temperature methods. Isothermal (or constant) temperature, where the temperature is held at
a constant high temperature of around (150 ◦C). This method is usually used for low com-
plexity samples where compounds have a similar volatility. The other method is temperature
programming, where the temperature starts low and is then increased over time (usually from
around 30-280 ◦C). This initially results in a low vapor pressure, causing the compounds to be
more strongly retained on the column. As the temperature rises, the vapor pressure increases,
leading to progressively reduced retention of the compounds in the column over time. This re-
sults in the compounds giving sharp peaks when detected. After the column, the compounds
are detected with various types of detectors, including flame ionization, electron capture, and
photoionization, among others [17] [16].

5.2.3
Normal-phase chromatography
Normal-phase (NP) chromatography is an adsorption-based chromatographic method. Here,
the retention of analytes in the sample is based on the adsorption of set analytes to the stationary
phase, which consists of highly polar porous silica particles. The silica pores allow both the
mobile phase and analytes to enter the silica particles and for analytes to interact with the
stationary phase. The interactions responsible for retaining the analytes are based on hydrogen
bonding, dipole-dipole interactions, and ionic interactions [17]. Mobile phases often used for LC
can be seen in Table 3.

Running an NP chromatogram, the adsorption between the analytes and the stationary phase

typically starts as non-polar, with its polarity gradually increasing over time. Analytes with low
polarity and weak interactions with the stationary phase elute first, while more polar analytes
elute later in the chromatogram.
5.2.4
Reverse-phase chromatography
In reverse-phase (RP) chromatography, the polarity of the stationary and mobile phases is op-
posite to that in NP chromatography. The stationary phase is hydrophobic, while the mobile
phase is polar. The stationary phase is prepared by reacting the silanols on the surface of sil-
ica particles with an organic reagent, giving the silica particles a hydrophobic surface area [17].
Examples for stationary phases which are used for RP during this thesis are illustrated in Figure
13.

Figure 13: Examples of two stationary phases used for Reverse-phase Chromatography
In RP chromatography, analyte retention is based on the partitioning of compounds between
the stationary phase and the mobile phase. This process is primarily driven by hydrophobic
van der Waals interactions between the analytes and the hydrocarbon chains in the stationary
phase. The more polar compounds interact less with the stationary phase and will elute first.
The mobile phase systems used for RP are a mixture of water and an organic solvent misci-
ble in water. The most commonly used organic solvents are methanol and acetonitrile. The
column’s pressure and pH can also affect the separation and retention times of the analytes.
A methanol/water mixture is more viscous than an acetonitrile/water mixture. This can lead
to higher back pressure in the column and a lower resolution. Acetonitrile also has a stronger
eluent strength than methanol in RP chromatography, which can affect the efficiency of the chro-
matogram. Maintaining a stable pH is important for the consistent elution of specific analytes,
as changes in pH can alter their retention and cause them to elute more quickly. To prevent
this, buffers are often added to the mobile phases. One commonly used buffer for this purpose
is formic acid [17].
5.2.5
Thin-layer chromatography
Thin-layer chromatography (TLC) is a form of NP chromatography. Here, the stationary phase
consists of an alumina foil or glass plate coated with a thin layer of silica particles. The sample is
prepared by dissolving it in a volatile solvent, which is then spotted in a µL volume on the bottom
of the TLC plate. The TLC plate is then placed in a container. The container is prepared with
the mobile phase at the bottom. The mobile phase is a solvent system of volatile solvents. Two

examples of solvent systems for the mobile phase could be DCM/MeOH 95:5 or Heptane/ethyl
acetate 60:40. After placing the TLC plate inside the container, a lit is placed on top of the
container, and the mobile phase starts to move up the TLC plate via capillary forces. This
is a type of adsorption chromatography where the retention of compounds is based on their
adsorption to the stationary phase. When the mobile phase is close to the top of the TLC plate,
the plate is removed from the container, and the mobile phase is evaporated. The compounds
inside the sample can then be visualized via UV detection or by staining the TLC plate. An
example of a TLC experiment is illustrated in Figure 14.

5.3
Mass spectrometry
Mass spectrometry (MS) is an analytical method that measures the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) of
molecular ions. Here, the mass (m) is expressed in unified atomic ions (u) and z is a multiplier of
the elementary charge (e). The mass spectrum is obtained by accelerating the ions in an electric
field, followed by separation based on their m/z. If the charge of a molecule is ±1, the m/z value
on the spectrum equals the molecular mass. For a charge of ±2, the m/z is half the mass; for
±3, it is one-third of the mass, and so on. There are 3 essential factors for obtaining a mass
spectrum: 1) an ionization source for the molecules, 2) a mass to charge separator/analyzer,
and 3) a detector for the ions[16].
5.3.1
Ionization
Ionization is a crucial step for mass spectrometry. In this step, the molecules inside a sample
are ionized for multiple reasons. The ionization of sample molecules allows them to be manip-
ulated when they enter the mass analyzers. Furthermore, neutral molecules are invisible to the
detectors, which makes them impossible to measure and analyze without the ionization step.
25
==PAGE:28==
There are multiple ionization methods for LC, including Matrix-Assisted Laser Desorption-Ionization
(MALDI), Electron Impact (EI), Electrospray Ionization (ESI), and Atmospheric Pressure Chem-
ical Ionization (APCI). This section will focus on the ESI and APCI ionization methods.
Electrospray Ionization is illustrated in Figure 16a and is an ionization method where the
mobile phase from the LC column enters a small nebulizer. At the end of the nebulizer, the
liquid sample is funneled into a small needle where an electrical charge is applied to ionize the
molecules inside the sample. From the needle, the mobile phase is sprayed out of the nebulizer
using a nitrogen (N2) drying gas, forming small droplets. As the solvent evaporates from these
droplets, it is flushed away, while the sample ions are drawn into the mass spectrometer by an
electric field [16][17].
In Atmospheric Pressure Chemical Ionization, the solvent from the LC column enters the
nebulizer like with ESI. However, in this method, the solvent is evaporated by heating at the
end of the nebulizer. The sample molecules are then ionized by an electric corona discharge
formed at the end of the nebulizer needle. Like the ESI method, the mass analyzer picks up the
sample ions while a carrier gas flushes the mobile phase away[16][17].
Figure 16: Illustration for setups of: a) Electrospray Ionization in positive mode, and b) Atmo-
spheric Pressure Chemical Ionization [23]
Both ESI and APCI are considered soft ionization methods, meaning little to no fragmentation

phase analytes and ideal for analysis of small to medium MW analytes [18].

5.3.2
Mass spectrometers
After ionization, the different ions are separated based on their m/z values. The various in-
struments are called mass spectrometers, and each mass analyzer uses a unique method for
separating ions. The simplest analyzer is a quadrupole, often used as a precursor for other
mass analyzers. The quadrupole is shown in Figure 17, and comprises four parallel rods. A
constant voltage and a radio frequency oscillating voltage generate an electric field. This elec-
tric field allows only ions with specific m/z to reach the detectors. The other ions will collide
with the rods and not reach the detectors. The voltages can change rapidly, which allows a
wide range of m/z values to get the detector (full scan). They can also be adjusted to only scan
selected m/z ions (SIM mode) [17].

Figure 17: Setup for quadrupole [24]
Time-of-flight (TOF) mass spectrometry is a spectrometric method where a pulse inside a tube
accelerates ions. At the end of the tube, the ions are detected by a detector. The mass of the
ions can be detected based on their flight time inside the tube. This can be calculated since the
ions are accelerated with the same kinetic energy of 1
2 · mv2. Thus, the lighter ions will hit the
detector before the heavier ions[17]. An example of the structure of a TOF spectrometer can
be seen in Figure 18. The reflectron setup is to be observed on the right side of the figure. This
is installed to improve the resolution of the spectra. Overall, the advantages of TOF are its high
mass resolution, high acquisition rate of ions, and a mass accuracy of ∼2 ppm [16].

TOF-MS is usually coupled with a quadrupole for a Q-TOF setup. This has multiple applications,
such as enabling specific ions to be analyzed by tandem mass spectrometry (MS/MS), reducing
background noise from unwanted ions, and acting as an ion guide for the ions to enter the TOF
analyzer more efficiently [16].
Triple quadrupole (QqQ) is an analytical technique that is very effective for target mass anal-
ysis. The method is used for quantitative determination by SRM or multiple reaction monitoring
(MRM). The setup for QqQ is illustrated in Figure 19 and works as follows. The ions from the
sample are injected into the first quadrupole (Q1), where only precursor ions of a specific m/z
are passing through. The precursor ions then enter the collision cell (q2), reacting with N2 or
Ar, fragmenting the precursor ions into product ions. These fragment ions then enter the third
and last quadrupole (Q3). Q3 is set up like Q1 only to allow selected product ions to go through
the analyzer and enter the detector. QqQ is an analytical method with a very high sensitivity
since background ions are eliminated through the process. This also provides a high selectivity
of ions. The drawbacks of this method are a low resolution and lower selectivity when used for
metabolomics in full scan mode [16].

5.3.3
Detectors
After the mass spectrometer manipulates ions, they are directed into a detector to convert them
into electric signals. Detectors, which are often used in TOF or QqQ spectrometry, are electron
or photon multipliers. Here, an ion exiting the mass spectrometer strikes a dynode, releasing
secondary electrons. These electrons are accelerated to the next dynode, generating even
more electrons. This cascade continues through multiple dynodes until the electrons reach the
anode, where the resulting current is measured and converted into a detectable signal[16]. For
a photon multiplier, this concept is the same. However, the ions exiting the mass spectrometer
will hit a phosphorescent screen, emitting an electron and amplified by the electron multiplier
[18].
Diode Array Detection (DAD) is another way of detecting the ions exiting a mass spectrometer
and can be an extra step to obtain more information about the sample compounds. DAD is a
form of ultraviolet-visible (UV-Vis) spectrophotometric method. The setup for DAD is shown in
Figure 22 and works as follows.
31
==PAGE:34==
Figure 22: Schematic for Photodiode Array Detection
Light from a deuterium or tungsten lamp emits a broad spectrum of light through a flow cell
containing an eluent with the sample [29]. After hitting the sample, a slit is set up to capture the
light going through the sample and reflect it on a mirror. The light reflected by the mirror will
then hit a grating polychromator, which disperses the light into an array of photodiode detectors
that measure the light in the wavelengths 200 nm to 700 nm continuously [16]. The diodes
have a capacitor that will generate a current when light of the right energy hits them. Therefore,
a continuous readout of the current is present for the different wavelengths of light. When a
molecule absorbs light of a specific wavelength, the electrical current changes and gives a
signal in the resulting DAD chromatogram [16][29].
5.4
Data analysis
Data acquisition of the detected sample ions after LC-MS can be operated in different modes.
Full scan, selected ion monitoring (SIM), and selected reaction monitoring. In full scan mode,
mass spectra are recorded in a range of given m/z e.g. m/z 50-1000. This provides a broad
coverage of ions but has a lower sensitivity than the other modes [17]. In SIM, only specific ions
will be monitored, which is better for quantifying compounds but ignores other compounds from
the sample. SRM is very efficient for ultra-high precision quantification and targeted analysis.
Here, ions of specific transitions will be monitored. However, knowledge of the precursor ion
and fragment ions is needed to use this method.
The acquired data is stored in a raw data file, which can then be processed differently. For
mass analysis, the MS and MS/MS spectra look into the precursor and fragment ions. For
the chromatogram, the data processing modes can be Total ion chromatogram (TIC), extracted
32
==PAGE:35==
ion chromatogram (EIC), and base peak chromatogram (BPC). In TIC, the total ion current is
plotted as a function of the retention time. Total ion chromatograms give the full spectrum of
all ions registered by the mass detector. EIC is used to locate ions of a particular m/z value of
interest within the TIC. Here, a single chromatogram peak is extracted from the TIC, ignoring the
other peaks in the chromatogram [16]. Lastly, BPC provides a signal based on the maximum
intensity ion as a function of time [30]. This is similar to TIC, but with a more focused profile
for high-intensity ions, thus resulting in lower background noise than TIC. This can also make
BPCs easier to interpret when looking for targeted compounds.
5.4.1
Global Natural Products Social Molecular Networking
Dereplicating the metabolites produced in nature can result in a wide range of molecules for
untargeted MS analysis. This can make it challenging to annotate the different molecules ob-
tained in the mass spectrum. This includes the known metabolites reported in the literature,
the unknown analogues to known compounds, and new chemical compounds with no reported
spectra or relatives. A molecular networking method has been developed to improve the anno-
tation of the spectral compounds. Molecular networking is a computational strategy that helps
visualize and interpret MS’s data. Molecular networking provides a visual overview of the sam-
ples’ MS data and the relationship between different molecules. The chemical relationship is
established through the tandem mass (MS/MS) profiles for the molecules and comparing them
with each other [31].
This computational method has produced a platform for sharing data and knowledge of nat-
ural products. Global Natural Products Social Molecular Networking (GNPS) enables online
dereplication of natural product-based MS. MS/MS data is collected and compared with exist-
ing libraries in the network. This way, a sample’s MS/MS data will indicate hits with already
reported metabolites and analogues of the known metabolites. Those hits will be visualized as
notes, and the relation between each note is aligned as edges. Those relations could be things
such as adducts of the same molecule or analogues with a similar structure. The molecular
network process is shown in Figure 23. The edges connecting the notes on the left of the figure
are defined by a cosine scoring that determines the similarity of the spectra compared with a
score from 0 (no similarity) to 1 (identical). For further processing of GNPS data, third-party
visualization tools such as Cytoscape can be used [32].

5.5
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy is an essential technique for structural elu-
cidation based on the magnetic properties of the nuclei. Specific atomic nuclei have a property
called spin. The most common nuclei that possess this spin are 1H, 13C, 19F, and 31P. Atomic
nuclei with an odd mass or odd atomic number also possess a magnetic moment. When an
external magnetic field is applied to the nuclei, they can align either with (low energy) or against
(high energy) the field, as illustrated in Figure 24 [33]. This creates a difference in energy lev-
els known as Zeeman splitting. Specific radiations with a frequency in the radiofrequency (RF)
level are applied to the nuclei to excite them from a lower energy state to a higher one. After
excitation, the nuclei will relax back to the neutral state, thus emitting the RF energy back, which
is measured by the instrument and converted into readable data by Fourier transform [33].
Figure 24: Two spin states for a proton under the influence of an applied electrical field [34]
34
==PAGE:37==
Different local environments for a nucleus cause the nucleus to resonate at various frequencies,
which is called chemical shift. The protons are shielded by the electrons surrounding them,
which changes the chemical shift for the individual nuclei [35]. These variations can come
from, e.g., electron density, electronegativity of neighboring atoms, or pi-electron systems. On
an NMR spectrum, deshielded nuclei will appear more to the left on the spectrum (downfield),
while shielded atoms appear more to the right (upfield) [33]. As an example, nuclei for 1H close
to an electronegative OH group will be less shielded (deshielded) and will therefore appear more
downfield in the spectrum.
Another type of structural information, which is given by NMR spectroscopy, is spin-spin splitting
(also known as J-coupling measured in Hz). This phenomenon can be explained by the n+1
Rule. Each type of proton in the molecule can sense the other protons on neighboring carbons
[33]. Let’s take methyl ethyl ether as an example, Figure 25. Here, the lonely methyl group will
show up as a singlet on the spectra since no neighboring hydrogen atoms are present. Mean-
while, the CH3 on the ethyl group will appear as a triplet due to the two neighboring hydrogen
atoms, and the CH2 will appear as a quartet, following the n+1 Rule. The intensities of these
spectral splittings follow Pascal’s triangle, with the relative relations in intensities depicted in
Figure 26 [35].
Figure 25: Methyl ethyl ether
Figure 26: Pascal’s triangle [35]
NMR is also useful for the quantification of the protons present in a molecule. Measuring the
area under a peak will give a value that directly matches the number of protons present for that
peak. The relative height between these peaks is also to be seen on 1H NMR spectra. A singlet
for three hydrogen atoms will be three times larger than a singlet for one hydrogen atom [35].
This precision in quantification also makes it possible to detect impurities present in a sample.

There are multiple different NMR techniques which can be used for structural identification. I
will briefly explain the most common methods.
Common NMR Techniques For 1D NMR spectroscopy, the most commonly used techniques
are 1H NMR and 13C NMR.
• ¹H NMR – Proton NMR, used for analyzing hydrogen atoms in a molecule. Signals for this
spectrum usually appear in the range 0-12 ppm.
• ¹³C NMR – Carbon NMR, usually used to give an insight into the carbon skeleton for a
molecule.
13C has a low natural abundance, which makes the sensitivity for 13C NMR
spectra lower. To record a better spectrum, longer acquisition times are therefore needed
[33].
There are also 2D NMR techniques that combine two 1D NMR spectra to characterize interac-
tions within the molecule better.
• COSY – Correlation Spectroscopy, shows scalar couplings between protons in the molecule.
This technique uses two 1H NMR spectra.
• HSQC – Heteronuclear Single Quantum Coherence, this technique reveals direct corre-
lations between 1H atoms bonded to 13C atoms.
• HMBC – Heteronuclear Multiple Bond Correlation, shows long-range 1H-13C/15N cou-
plings (typically 2-3 bonds between the atoms).
• H2BC – Similar to HMBC NMR, however this technique only detects two bound heteronu-
clear couplings and is not able to see 1H-15N interactions.

6.4
Extraction
For the small-scale cultivation, agar plug extraction was performed. For each culture, a standard
screening of the plugs is taken from 1. the center of the colony 2. the middle of the colony and,
3. the end of the colony 27 and transferred to a 1.5 mL Eppendorf Safe-lock tube. 800 µL of
isopropanol:ethyl acetate (1:3 v/v) with 1% formic acid is then added to the tubes, and the lids are
closed. The tubes are then placed in an ultrasonic bath for 60 min and followed by centrifugation
at 12000 rpm for 3 min. After centrifugation, 750 µL of the supernatant is transferred into a new
1.5 mL Eppendorf Safe-lock tube, and the solvent is evaporated by nitrogen evaporation at
30 oC. After evaporation to complete dryness, 300 µL of methanol is added and the tubes are
placed in the ultrasonic bath for 20 min, followed by centrifugation at 12000 rpm for 3 min. After
centrifugation, 150 µL of the supernatant is carefully transferred from the top of the sample to a
labeled HPLC vial, ready for analysis.

Conclusion
In this thesis, one of the main objectives is to successfully cultivate and identify A. insuetus and
A. calidoustus was achieved. The confirmed growth of these strains enabled further investiga-
tion of their secondary metabolite profiles using HPLC-DAD-HRMS/MS for chemical profiling.
Dereplication of previously reported metabolites supported these identities whith the presence
of ophiobolin G and K, for both species and aurantiamine, which has been reported and ob-
served in A. insuetus.
One aim of this project was to see if the closely related species A. calidoustus is capable of
producing the TMC-120 compounds previously observed in A. insuetus. Dereplication analysis
revealed that A. insuetus shows a greater metabolomic activity related to these compounds,
except for ustusorane B, which A. Calidoustus has been shown to produce in large quantities
under certain environmental circumstances. Through this analysis, A. calidoustus was found
to produce specific compounds selectively depending on the growth medium. TMC-120–based
compounds were exclusively produced on CYA, whereas the oxygen-based analogues were
detected on PDA. This pattern suggests that the nitrogen source in the medium may play a
significant role in determining which compounds are biosynthetically expressed by this strain.
Lastly, an upscaling of cultivation and extraction was performed. From here, an unknown com-
pound with the exact mass of 637.3100 was successfully isolated and further analyzed by NMR
spectroscopy. The NMR spectroscopy confirmed the sample to be pure enough for structural
elucidation, and the compound structure was successfully found. However, looking up the struc-
ture in Reaxys, I found the compound a previously reported Aspertide C.
10
Outlook
If I were to repeat these experiments, I would cultivate the fungal strains again on rice-based me-
dia and try to obtain pure cultures, and generate a metabolomic profile specific to that medium.
Furthermore, improvements can be made during the purification steps. Before running the
Isolera chromatogram, further simplification of the sample might be necessary for better sep-
aration. Using a less loaded column would also minimize peak tailing and co-elution, yield-
ing purer fractions for subsequent semi-preparative HPLC purification and making the process
more efficient. With additional time, Fraction 3 obtained from the Isolera column could also be
85

further analyzed. Given the difference in molecular masses within this fraction, one potential
approach for separation could be by size-exclusion chromatography, followed by purification
using a semi-preparative column.
