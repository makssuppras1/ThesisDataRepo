Abstract 
Gamma delta T cells are highly conserved population of lymphocytes and can be found in all 
vertebrates. However, the frequency of γδ T cells varies greatly among different species. In human 
and mice, γδ T cells constitute a minor subset (approx. 1-5% in blood), while they constitute up to 
86% of total lymphocytes in pigs. Despite their high abundance, the knowledge about the biology of 
this prominent T cell subsets in pigs is partial.  
Based on this, the aim of this study was to investigate γδ T cells further, with focus on the magnitude 
and clonality of the γδ T cells and receptor, respectively. This was determined by whole blood 
samples from piglets and sows (age: 3-4 weeks and 1-2 years) from a Danish production herd at 
Askelygaard. The methods applied to accommodate the objectives was flow cytometry (FCM) and 
Next generation sequencing (NGS) platform Illumina MiSeq 300PE. For FCM the antibodies CD3, 
CD4, CD8 and Tcr1-N4 were applied to classify and asses the magnitude of γδ T cells.  
By applying of this methods, the results showed that no significant difference was observed in the 
magnitude of γδ T cells among the examined groups. Furthermore, the sequencing data analysis 
showed that cluster generation based on 100% similarity is not possible as none of the sequences 
obtained were 100% similar.  
By comparing the magnitude of γδ T cells in piglets and sows, the percentage difference was not 
significant different from each other. Furthermore, by comparing the cloned sequences the clones 
shared conservations in the variable and joining region, whereas they were highly variable in the 
diversity region. In addition the cloned sequences were clustered base on 100% similarity and showed 
that the sequences could not be cluster based on 100% similarity.

Resumé 
Gamma delta T-celler (γδ) er konserverede populationer af lymfocytter som findes i alle hvirveldyr, 
men frekvensen af disse varierer meget mellem forskellige arter. I menneske og mus udgør γδ T-
celler en lille delmængde (ca. 1-5% i blodet), mens de udgør op til 86% af de samlede lymfocytter i 
grise. På trods af deres tilstedeværelse er viden om denne prominente T-celle i svin mangelfuld. 
Derfor er formålet med dette projekt at yderligere undersøge γδ T-celler, med fokus på hhv. størrelsen 
og klonalitet af γδ T-celler og receptor. Dette blev bestemt ved undersøgelse af blodprøver fra 
smågrise og søer (alder: 3-4 uger og 1-2 år) fra en dansk produktion besætning på Askelygaard. De 
metoder, der anvendes til at imødekomme formålet, var flow cytometri (FCM) og Next generation 
sequencing (NGS) platform Illumina MiSeq 300PE. FCM antistofferne CD3, CD4, CD8 og Tcr1-N4 
blev anvendt til at klassificere og vurdere omfanget af γδ T-celler.  
Anvendelsen af disse metoder indikerede at ingen signifikant forskel blev observeret i fordelingen af 
γδ T-celler blandt de undersøgte grupper. Endvidere demonstrerede analysen af sekventerings data at 
cluster generation baseret på 100% lighed ikke er mulig, da ingen af de opnåede sekvenser var 100% 
ens. 
Sammenligne af størrelsen af γδ T-celler i smågrise og søer, indikerede ingen procentvise forskel 
mellem grupperne. Ved at sammenligne de klonede sekvenser blev der observeret konserverede 
områder i den variable og joining region, mens de var meget variabel i diversity region. Derudover, 
de klonede sekvenser grupperet på baggrund af 100% lighed viste at sekvenserne ikke kunne 
grupperes  baseret 100% similaritet.

1 Introduction 
To understand the biology of gamma delta (γδ) T cells in pigs, it is necessary to have a comprehensive 
knowledge of these and their T cell receptor (TCR) diversity. γδ T cells are a highly conserved 
population of lymphocytes throughout evolution and can be found in all vertebrates. As most 
knowledge about γδ T cell concerns γδ-low species, this study aimed to gain more knowledge of γδ 
T cells in γδ-high species such as pigs[1]. Current concepts of γδ T cells is that they act as “first line 
of defense”, “regulatory cells” or “bridge” between innate and adaptive immune responses[2]. Since 
the discovery of γδ T cells, the function of γδ T cell subset has been an enigma that is now being 
solved.  
1.1 Gamma delta T cells 
Animals can be divided into groups according to the magnitude of γδ T cells within their T cell 
population. Pig, cattle, sheep, chicken and rabbits belongs the γδ T cell “high” group, whereas human, 
mice, dogs and guinea pigs belong to the γδ T cell “low” group. Numerous studies have described the 
frequencies of γδ T cells in pigs ([3],[4],[5],[6]), and as it is shown by Stepanova et al., almost 30% 
of γδ T lymphocytes were found in the blood of 4-week-old pigs and the number increased with age, 
as almost 70% of these cells were found in blood of 6-month-old pigs[7]. The same was demonstrated 
by K. Stepanova and M. Sinkora, showing that γδ T cells were mainly enriched in the blood, spleen 
and lungs of piglets at age 2-6 weeks[8].  
Furthermore, as T lymphocytes in pigs and other species are defined by their expression of the cluster 
of differentiation 3 surface marker. These T lymphocytes can be further classified into subsets, 
according to the expression of either αβ or γδ TCR[9]. γδ T cells in pigs can phenotypically be 
identified by the monoclonal antibodies clone PGBL22A and PPT16. These mAbs recognizes an 
epitope on γδ TCR[10] and binds to CD3 molecules, only associated with the porcine γδ T cells[11]. 
The data available on the TCR-γ and TCR-δ chain repertoire is limited due to the lack of specific 
antibodies which recognize pig γδ T cell specific variable segments[12]. Even though this is true, 
numerous studies[13],[14],[15] have presented molecular data on the porcine TCR δ chain based on 
cDNA, suggesting that the δ chain is one of the most diverse antigenic receptors among those 
investigated so far. Previous studies performed by Uenishi et al. revealed that the genomic structure 
of the sequence encoding the joining (J) and constant (C) regions of the TCR α and TCR δ chain in 
porcine, was notable conserved with those of mice and humans[16]. Additionally, Holtmeire et al. 
have demonstrated that the TCR δ chain repertoire is polyclonal in young pigs and becomes 
increasingly oligoclonal and compartmentalized with age. Resembling humans and non-human 
primates[17]. 
Thus based on the expression of CD2 and CD8, γδ T cells can be classified into CD2-CD8-, CD2+CD8- 
and CD2+CD8+ subsets[8],[9]. Moreover, γδ T cells can in addition be classified according to the 
cluster of differentiation 4 (CD4) into three subsets: CD2+CD4-CD8-, CD2+CD4CD8low and CD2-
CD4-CD8-[9]. The different subsets are shown to differ in their homing characteristic, with subset 
CD2-CD8- dominating in the blood (circulation), while CD2+CD8+ and CD2+CD8- γδ T cells 
preferably reside in the lymphoid tissues (e.g. spleen and lymph nodes)[18],[19].

Furthermore, porcine γδ T cell mainly expresses the homodimer CD8αα, while a large subset of 
thymocytes and a minor intestinal lymph subset expresses the heterodimer CD8αβ[20].  
1.2 “null” T lymphocytes 
In 1970, a subset of lymphocytes in porcine referred to as “null” lymphocytes were discovered, due 
to the lack of both B cell and T cell markers (anti-Ig and E-rosette, respectively). Later on, by specific 
mAbs these cells were proven to be CD2 and CD8 negative. The “null” cells were however not 
Natural Killer cells (NK cells), as these cells are known to express CD2 and CD8. Subsequently these 
“null” cells were identified by an anti-sheep γδ TCR mAb 86D. Mab 86D recognizes a shared epitope 
among sheep, but only a subset of γδ T cells is recognized by this antibody. Moreover, it was seen 
that not all porcine γδ T cells have the “null” phenotype, since some of the subsets expressed CD2 
and CD8[21].  
1.2.1 T cell development and γδ TCR commitment 
The pig’s gestation period lasts for 114 days (3 months, 3 weeks and 3 days)[22]. In the beginning of 
the 114 days, the T lymphocytes begins to develop in the liver and continues development in the 
thymus at approximately second trimester and until birth[13]. Both αβ T cells and γδ T cells arise 
from common multipotent double negative (DN) precursors in the thymus[23]. Thus, around day 40 
of gestation (DG40) the T lymphocytes begins to develop and subsequently populating the periphery 
at DG45. At this time of development, the T lymphocytes do not express CD3, CD4 nor CD8 and are 
referred to as DN thymocytes. The DN thymocytes begins to express CD3 at DG40-50 and unlike 
DN thymocytes in human and mice, porcine DN thymocytes do not express CD25 during their 
development. The portion of γδ T cells decreases later in ontogeny (DG50-55) as αβ T cells expands 
and become the predominant CD3+ lymphocyte subset[13]. At DG60-70 DN T lymphocytes begins 
to express CD4 and CD8. A rapid proliferation of double positive (DP) (CD4+CD8+) thymocytes 
occurs at DG60-90 and becomes the predominant population of T lymphocytes.  
Furthermore, flow cytometric studies by Holtmeier et al.[13] have shown that γδ T cells are the 
earliest detectable T lymphocyte population in porcine, where they account for 3-5% of total T 
lymphocytes. Near the end of gestation (DG90-114), there is a rapid expansion of γδ T cells in the 
periphery, so γδ T cells in newborns and younger piglets account for about 20% of all peripheral T 
lymphocytes[13]. The reason for the rapid expansion of γδ T cells near the end of gestation is 
unknown, but it results in a higher ratio of γδ T cells in the blood. Since numerous thymocytes dies 
in the second and third trimester of gestation, positive and negative selection of T lymphocytes may 
occur in this period. Unlike human and mice, not much is known about positive and negative selection 
of porcine T lymphocytes. The T lymphocyte progenitors that enters the thymus during third trimester 
of gestation and after birth are produced from the bone marrow in contrast to the liver[9].  
Signal transduction by TCRs has shown to be critical for the development of αβ and γδ T cells. 
However, their role in lineage commitment remains poorly defined[24]. In the immune system, the 
two lineages differ during the course of chain rearrangement of the TCR, even though it takes place 
within the same precursor cells[25].

1.2.2 γδ TCR repertoire 
In all species, γδ T cells express a distinctive TCR, which has the capacity for highly diverse 
interactions. The TCR α and γ chains consists of constant (C), joining (J) and variable (V) regions, 
whereas β and δ chains consists of constant (C), diversity (D), J and V regions. The genes encoding 
the αβ and γδ TCR (TRA/TRD, TRB and TRG) are located in three distinct regions on the human 
chromosomes 14q11.2, 7q34 and 7p14, respectively and on the porcine chromosomes 7q15.3-q21, 
18q11.3-q12 and 9q21-22, respectively[26]. Studies in human and mice have revealed that the TRD 
gene, encoding the TCR δ chain is localized within the TRA locus encoding the TCR α chain. When 
the TRA locus is rearranged, the TRD locus is then excised from the genome[16]. The TCRs contain 
three complementarity-determining regions (CDRs) which is essential for antigen recognition. CDR1 
and CDR2 is encoded within the V segment, while CDR3 is encoded around the junction between 
the V and J segment in TRA and TRG, and V, D and J segment in TRB and TRD[27]. Based on this 
and the number of rearrangements, and the potential for N and P nucleotide (nt) additions, the γδ 
TCRs have the possibility for a major diverse repertoire[28],[9].  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The γδ T cells such as, αβ T cells and B cells uses V(D)J rearrangement to generate the diverse 
receptors to recognize different antigens[29]. The rearrangement of the TCR α and γ chains, involves 
V and J, while the TCR β and δ chains involves V, D, and J (Figure 1). Among α, β, γ, δ chains, the 
δ chain is special as it could be involved in more than two joining steps for rearrangement. This is 
seen in mice and humans, where three and four joining steps can occur, respectively[30]. The multiple 
joining steps provide a mechanism, which potentially can generate diversity at the V-D-J junctions, 
several orders of magnitude higher than that of other antigen receptors[30]. Even though γδ TCRs are 
comparable to αβ TCRs (e.g. structure and signal pathway), they recognize district ligands[28].

genes. Uniquely the locus encoding the δ chain is located within the α-chain locus. The three Dδ gene segments, four Jδ gene segments, 
and the single Cδ gene lie between the cluster of Vα gene segments and the cluster Jα gene segments. There are six Vδ gene segments 
in between the Vα gene segments. Of these six, five of them are shared with Vα and can be used by either locus. Bottom panel: The 
TCRγ locus resembles that of TCRβ, in having two C genes each with its own set of J gene segments. Rearrangement of the γ and δ
loci processes as for other TCR loci. But with the exception of TCRδ rearrangement, where two D gene segments can be used in the

1.2.3 Function of γδ T cells 
B cells and αβ T cells, are the major components of the adaptive immune system, whereas γδ T seems 
to contribute to both the innate and adaptive immune system as they appear to perform a countless of 
different functions[31]. γδ T cells are involved in the early immune response like other innate cells 
(e.g. neutrophils, NK cells, eosinophils, basophils) and activate adaptive immune cells[32]. 
Furthermore, γδ T cells can regulate the immune response by interactions with other cells, like 
presenting antigens for αβ T cells and provide help for B cells. Besides this, γδ T cells can produce 
different kinds of chemokines and cytokines, such as growth factor IGF-1, interferon-gamma (INF-
γ) and tumor factor-α[23]. In addition, γδ T cells have been reported to control CD8+ T cell expansion 
and regulate tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-α) secretion by these activated cells, secrete 
interleukin-10 (IL-10) and regulate the expansion and recruitment of monocytes and neutrophils for 
initiation of inflammatory response[33],[23]. Thereby, understanding how ligands are presented to γδ 
T cells is essential to comprehend the variety of functions carried out by γδ T cells. The exact ligands 
for γδ TCR recognition are still poorly disclosed, outside of mice and humans[28]. The recognition 
of antigens by γδ T cells is in a different manner than that of αβ T cells. Most αβ T cells recognize 
processed antigens in the form of peptide associated with the major histocompatibility complex 
(MHC). However no antigen processing and presentation requirements for ligand recognition by γδ 
T cells is known. It is therefore assumed that damaged tissues, cells and pathogens can be recognized 
by γδ T cells without any requirements for antigen degradation. This feature of γδ T cells will provide 
a greater flexibility in the response than that found in classical αβ T cell response[34].  
In addition to γδ TCR, γδ T cells also expresses other innate-type receptors that contribute to the fine-
tune sensing of microenvironment[28]. The receptors include NK receptors (e.g. NKG2D), scavenger 
receptors, integrin and pattern recognition receptors (PRR), such as toll like receptors (TLRs)[20]. 
These receptors permit the γδ T cells to respond to a variation of disease states[28]. 
 
1.3 Porcine as experimental model 
Why study the pig? Pigs are anatomically (e.g. body size, cardiovascular system, skin and urinary 
system) and functionally (e.g. gastrointestinal system and immune system) similar to humans[35] and 
furthermore have the same disease progression (e.g. metabolic and infectious). Thus, making pig the 
leading candidate for human xenotransplantation. In this regard, the pig has become an established 
as a research animal in some arears of pharmacological and biochemical research.  
However, there is a large amount of mouse models and less well-characterized pig model which can 
be sufficiently used to model the human immune system. Even so, it is difficult to argue solely for a 
swine model or mice model in particular based on human-mouse differences, since the status of the 
majority of immune-based mechanisms in pigs are limited or less well characterized[36].

1.4 Theory of methods 
1.4.1 Flow cytometry 
Flow cytometry is a particularly rapid technology which counts up to 10.000 particles per second and 
is an ideal method to examine cell subsets. FCM allows for simultaneously measures and analyse 
several physical characteristics of a single cell in a fluid stream through a beam of light. Using an 
optical-to-electronic coupling system it is possible to determine those characteristics, which in turn 
records how cells emits and scatter incident laser light. Forward-scattered light (FSC) and side-
scattered light (SSC) data, combined with staining pattern of each subpopulation can be used to count 
for relative percentage of total cell count.  As described, it is possible to measure FSC and SSC, which 
is proportional to the cell surface size and internal complexity or granularity, respectively. To identify 
a particular cell type based on the antigenic surface markers, it is possible to conjugate a fluorescent 
dye (fluorochrome) to a mAb[37]. Therefore, having a mixture of cells with different fluorochromes, 
can be used to distinguish and separate subpopulations.  
The fluorochromes used in this study were carefully selected based on excitation and emission 
wavelength and includes fluorescein (FITC), Brilliant Violet 421 (BV421), Peridinin chlorophyll-
Cyanine5.5 (PerCP-Cy5.5) and Alexa Flour 647 (AF647). 
1.4.2 Next generation sequencing 
Through the past 15 years, sequencing has gradually removed from classical Sanger sequencing 
towards Next Generation Sequencing (NGS)[38], has made it possible to sequence many samples at 
the same time with sample-specific barcodes or index sequences. This makes the NGS platform 
eligible for comprehensive studies with a large number of samples. NGS platforms permits for a 
variation of methods, allowing researchers to virtually ask any question related to the genome, 
transcriptome, or epigenome of any organism[39]. NGS gives the ability to generate enormous 
numbers of data cheap and simultaneously[40]. 
The Illumina MiSeq 300PE method was chosen in this study for the generation of data, based on low 
arrow rate compared to other sequencing technologies. 
1.4.2.1 
Illumina sequencing 
The Illumina platform (Illumina, San Diego, CA) range from the large HiSeq-system to the benchtop 
size MiSeq, offering a greater range of throughput[41]. Illumina MiSeq implements the Solexa 
technology (University of Cambridge), which is based on the principles behind sequencing by 
synthesis (SBS). The concept behind SBS applies the technology of four fluorescently labelled 
nucleotides to sequence tens of millions of clusters on a flow cell surface[42]. Illumina MiSeq 
sequencing concept is based on the same principles as the Genome Analyser II, which reads length 
between 35 and 150 base pair (bp)[39]. A major improvement in NGS technology (Illumina MiSeq) 
was the development of paired-end (PE) sequencing, which applied sequencing from both end of the 
DNA fragment in a sequencing library, and aligning the forward and reverse reads[39]. The method 
Illumina MiSeq is an integrated system, which includes bridge amplification, cluster generation, 
sequencing and data analysis[43].

are determined. Modified from: “An introduction to Next-Generation Sequencing Technology”[39] 
The workflow of Illumina MiSeq 300PE starts by the generation of a sequencing library. Following 
the library generation, samples containing DNA is bound to a solid phase (flow cell) by forward and 
reverse oligonucleotides. Complementary adaptors are then attached to the template at the 5’ and 3’ 
end, thus allowing them to bind the flow cell surface. The oligonucleotide adaptors bound to the flow 
cell surface do not solely act as capture molecules, but also as primers for initiation of bridge 
amplification. To enhance the sequencing signal from the flow cell, clusters are generated from single 
DNA strands, by solid phase bridge amplification. This bridge amplification results in up to 1000 
densely packed identical duplicates of the original flow cell bound DNA molecule (template). Using 
the reversible terminator chemistry, the sequencing is then performed and four oligonucleotides 
fluorescent-labelled are flushed over the flow cell surface simultaneously. The fluorescent-labelled 
oligonucleotides are designed to terminate the polymerization, in which only one nucleotide is added 
at each polymerization cycle. In order to facilitate multiplexing, adaptor sequences are following the 
DNA barcodes (index sequence) and this allows for identification of the sequences later on in the data

Objectives 
The objective of this study, was to assess the magnitude of γδ T cells and repertoire of the porcine 
TCR δ-chain by amplification of the variable, diversity and joining gene segments. Thus, cloning and 
sequencing of the porcine T cell receptor (TCR) Variable, diversity and joining genes (δ-chain) 
should contribute to the existing knowledge on γδ T cells role in the porcine immune system and 
response. To accommodate the objectives, two aims were formulated.  
Aims 
The first aim of this study is that it can be assumed that the distribution of γδ T cells is greater in 
piglet and increases in number with age. The second aim of this study it that it is assumed that T cell 
receptor δ-chain repertoire in young piglets is polyclonal and becomes increasingly restricted with 
age. 
The first part of this study will include a presentation of the magnitude of γδ T cells in piglets and 
sows. Two different ages groups will be examined with six pigs in each group. The data will be 
obtained by FCM by applying surface markers CD3, CD4, CD8 and Tcr1-N4, which enables the 
identification of γδ T cell.  
The second part of this study, will provide data on γδ T cell receptor clonality among the piglet and 
sows. The clonality data will focus on the δ-chain’s variable, diversity and joining gene segments. 
For the analysis of clonality, whole blood samples from twelve pigs will be obtained, processed and 
sequenced by Next generation sequencing (NGS) platform Illumina MiSeq 300PE.

2 Material and Methods 
The section includes material and method description to accommodate the objective described above. 
All procedures and experiments were carried out using related protocols.  
2.1 Animals  
A total of 24 pigs were tested to obtain information on γδ T cells and the receptor. The samples were 
collected from a Danish production herd at Askelygaard. The pigs from this farm were of breed 
Danish Landrace x Yorkshire (LY) and Danish Landrace x Yorkshire (LY) mixed with semen from 
100% Duroc (D) which leads to 50 % LY / 50 % D (LY/D) crossbreed. At time of collection the pigs 
were approx. 3-4 weeks (12 samples, breed: 50 % LY/50 % D) and 1-2 years (12 samples, Breed: 
100 % LY).  
Frozen cDNA samples available from four pigs were used to test the designed primers ability to 
capture any complementary DNA (cDNA). One of the four cDNA samples was from a Göttingen 
minipig (Ellegård, Dalmose, Denmark) and the three other cDNA samples were from pigs of the 
breed Danish Landrace x Pietrain (from a Belgian farm).  
2.2 Purification of PBMCs  
Purification of porcine peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) was carried out using Stem Cell 
Sep Mate. Whole blood samples from pigs, were drawn into a BD Vacutainer CPT Cell Preparation 
Tube with Sodium Heparin (BD Vacutainer®, ref. 368480). The blood was diluted 1:1 in sterile 
Phosphate-Buffered Saline (PBS) + 2% Fetal Calf Serum (FCS). 15 mL of lymphoprep™ (Axis-
Shield PoC AS) was added to 50 mL SepMate™-50 tubes (StemCell Technologies) and subsequently 
the diluted blood was carefully added on top. The tubes were centrifuged for 20 minutes at 1200xg 
with breaks on (ThermoScientific, SL40R). After centrifugation the top layer containing the PBMCs 
were poured into a fresh 50 ml falcon tube and washed twice with sterile PBS + 2 % FCS by topping 
up to 50 ml, followed by centrifugation for 8 minutes at 300xg. If there were still erythrocytes left 
after the purification step, the pellet was lysed with 5 ml lysis buffer (in house) for maximum three 
minutes and followed by three washes. The cells were then counted using a NucleoCounter® NC-
200™ (ChemoMetec A/S).  
2.3 Whole blood cells 
Whole blood samples from pigs, were drawn into a BD Vacutainer CPT Cell Preparation Tube with 
Sodium Heparin (BD Vacutainer®, ref. 368480). The tubes were subsequently rotated to ensure even 
distribution of cells. Afterwards 200 μl of whole blood (approx. 2 million leucocytes) was added to 
Fluorescence Activated Cell Sorting (FACS) tubes followed by addition of 1 ml lysis buffer (BD 
FACS™ Lysis Solution, Cat.no. 349202) in order to lyse the erythrocytes. Tubes were then vortexed

after which cells were ready for staining with specific antibodies.

2.5 Purification of RNA from porcine Whole Blood  
Whole blood samples of 2.5 ml, from 6 LY and 6 LY/D pigs, were collected in PAXgene Blood RNA 
tubes (PreAnalytiX Cat. No. 762165) containing a reagent, which stabilizes the RNA. The whole 
blood samples were stored at room temperature for 2-3 hours to ensure total lysis of blood cells, and 
were afterwards stored at -20 °C until use. RNA from porcine whole blood were purified using 
PAXgene® Blood RNA Kit (PreAnalytiX, Cat.no. 762174) according to the manufacturer’s 
instructions.  
Samples were first centrifuged for 10 minutes at 48000xg to gain pellet. Subsequently, the supernatant 
was discarded and the pellet washed in RNase-free water. After resuspension in optimization buffers 
(BR1 and BR2) along with 40 μl Proteinase K, the samples were incubation for 10 minutes at 55 °C 
with shake at 1350 rpm and then the lysates were transferred to PAXgene Shredder Spin Columns 
and centrifuged for 3 minutes at 15000xg. The flow-through fraction was transferred to fresh 1.5 ml 
tubes, without disrupting the pellet and ethanol was added (99.8 – 100 %) to adjust the binding 
conditions. The remaining solution were transferred to fresh PAXgene RNA Spin Column, and 
washed twice with buffer BR3 and BR4 to remove contaminants. In between washing steps, 70 μl 
RDD and 10 μl DNase I were added to remove trace of bound DNA. Lastly, the PAXgene RNA Spin 
Columns was transferred to new processing tubes and eluted in 40 μl BR5 elution buffer, followed 
by 5 minutes deactivation of DNase 1 at 65 °C. Before use, the eluted samples containing purified 
RNA were measured on NanoDrop ND-100 spectrophotometer (ThermoFisher Scientific). 
Subsequently the purified RNA samples were stored at -20 °C or -80 °C.  
2.6 cDNA synthesis from purified RNA whole blood samples 
cDNA synthesis of purified RNA was performed using Qiagen® QuantiTect® Reverse Transcription 
Kit (Qiagen, Cat.no. 205311). First, 500 ng/ml RNA templates were mixed with 1.5 μl Wipeout 
Buffer (0.8x Genomic DNA Eliminator Reaction components) and water to obtain a final volume of 
12.5 μl. The mix of RNA template, Wipeout Buffer and water was incubated for 2 minutes at 42 °C 
(lid 60 °C), to completely remove DNA. After removal of DNA, the samples were incubated with 
0.75 μl Reverse Transcriptase, 3.0 μl RT Buffer 5X, 0.75 μl RT Primer Mix and 1.5 μl water. 
Incubation of the mixtures were done for 15 minutes at 42 °C, followed by 3 minutes at 95 °C and 
subsequently stored at -20 °C for further process.

2.7 Primer design and validation 
For amplifications of Variable, Diversity and Joining (V(D)J) region of the δ-chain, primers were 
designed. Using the software CLC BIO workbench (Qiagen ®, version 9) and the database 
GenBank[44] (GenBank ID: D49560.1-D49584.1) sequences were aligned and assembled. The 
primers were designed based on conserved regions, in such way that they will submerge the variable 
region of the TCR δ-chain. The primers were designed as presented in Figure 3. Conserved regions 
around the variable region occurred near the region for signal peptide (SP) and the joining segment 
(JS). The primers were placed in relation to this and the amplicon would end up with a size of 
approximately 400-420 bp submerging the variable region of TCR δ-chain.  
Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) was carried out using AmpliTaq Gold® DNA Polymerase (Applied 
Biosystems, Cat.no. N8080246) and Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA) and were proven against known 
cDNA clones (Göttingen minipigs from Ellegård, Dalmose, Denmark and from a Belgian farm of the 
breed Danish Landrace x Pietrain). The PCR protocol used are shown in Appendix C and the program 
for cycles are in Appendix C.

2.8 PCR amplification of cDNA with barcode primer pairs 
PCR amplification were performed on cDNA samples, by targeting areas lining the variable gene 
segment of the TCR δ-chain, through the use of barcoded primer pairs listed in Appendix X, Table 
X. PCR was performed according to the protocol in Appendix C. A single PCR reaction were 
performed in a total volume of 50 μl containing, 5 μl cDNA template, 5 μl 1x PCR Buffer (Applied 
Biosystems, Cat.no. N8080246), 0.3 μl of AmpliTaq Gold polymerase (Applied Biosystems, Cat.no. 
N8080246), 5 μl of 10 mM dNTP (Qiagen, Cat.no. 201912), 5 μl of 20x Loading buffer (Sigma-

cycling condition was 10 minutes at 94 °C, 38 cycles of 96 °C in 20 seconds, 62 °C in 25 seconds, 72 
°C in 25 seconds, 72 °C in 3 minutes and hold at 10 °C until usage.  
After PCR amplification, 10 μl of PCR product were mixed with 10 μl water and analyzed on an E-
Gel Agarose Gel 2 % (Invitrogen). Visualization of correct size amplicon was done by comparison 
with a 50 bp DNA marker (Invitrogen, Cat.no. 10416-014).  
2.9 Sample preparation for NGS Illumina MiSeq 
The PCR amplified samples were purified, to insure a pure sample, using QIAquick® PCR 
Purification Kit (Qiagen Cat.no. 28106). Purification start with the pool of all PCR products for 
sequencing. After pooling, 5x volume binding buffer (PB buffer) were added to 1x volume of PCR 
reaction mix. To bind DNA, the samples were applied to the QIAquick column and centrifuged for 
30-60 seconds or until the entire sample has passed through the column. Subsequently, 750 μl wash 
buffer (PE Buffer) were added to each QIAquick column and centrifuge for 30-60 seconds and 
additionally 1 minute to remove residual wash buffer. Then the QIAquick column were placed in a 
new 1.5 mL tube and 40 μl of miliQ water was used to elute the sample during centrifugation for 1 
minute. The concentration of the eluted DNA was measured on NanoDrop ND-100 
spectrophotometer (ThermoFisher Scientific).  
2.9.1 Data processing 
Samples for Next Generation Sequencing (NGS) (Illumina MiSeq), included amplicons at 
approximately 400-420 nucleotides (nt) from six 3-4 weeks-old (LY/D) piglets and four 1-2-year-old 
(LY) sows. The pooled samples were send, at a concentration of 100 ng/μl, according to what was 
specified by the sequencing company (Eurofins Genomics).

Sequences from Illumina MiSeq platform was received in 2 separate fastq files containing read 1 and 
read 2. The sequences were processed with the BION-meta software, which was originally developed 
for 16S ribosomal analysis. The program runs seven consecutive steps, nevertheless only the first 
three steps was needed for the analysis of TCR δ-chain results. The first three steps were Pattern de-
multiplexing, Pairmate joining and Sequence cleaning. Pattern de-multiplexing: The sequence-reads 
were first de-multiplexed into files according to the barcodes, by matching a list of barcodes used. 
All files containing the same barcode was then gathered in one file corresponding to the pig sample. 
A quality check of sequence-reads was subsequently performed by checking barcodes length, by 
matching barcodes with primers, by checking sequences with their location on the Illumina MiSeq 
flowcell, and matching forward and reverse sequence, primers and barcodes. Sequence-reads which 
did not meet the criteria was discarded. Pairmate joining: The two generated files containing forward 
and reverse reads, was joined together with criteria allowing no gaps, a minimum overlap length of 
35 nt, and a maximum mismatch percentage of 80%. Sequence cleaning: The now joined sequence-
reads was run through a quality filter, which cleans and trims the sequence-reads at both ends by 
removing bases or sequences containing an overall quality of less than 80%. The remaining sequence-
reads was further checked for chimeras.  
After chimera check, sequences of each sample were separated into clusters corresponding to each 
pig, using the derep_fulllenght algorithm for the program USEARCH. Subsequently UCHIME 
algorithm was applied and afterwards the sequences were trimmed, with the criteria of clusters less 
than 10 reads discarded, and only the 50 largest clusters from each pig was kept. In the trimmed files 
to be analyzed, the barcodes and primers were removed. After the cleaning, de-multiplexing, joining 
and chimera check, the sequences was blasted against NCBI and a customized library for γδ TCR δ-
chains, assembled by NCBI homepage[44]. Furthermore, all unique cluster with minimum 10 reads

2.9.2 NGS and Statistical analysis 
For the investigation of significant difference in γδ T cells distribution among piglet and sows an un-
paired two-tailed t-test was performed. An un-paired two-tailed t-test was chosen due to two 
independent groups. This t-test was performed in the program GraphPad version 7. In addition, all 
visualization of results was performed in GraphPad 7 too.  
Fasta files of each sample, containing consensus sequence of the largest most abundant clusters were 
imported into CLC Sequence Viewer (version 7). Sequences were aligned in CLC Sequence Viewer, 
with default settings of Gap cost: Gap open cost 10, Gap extension 1, end Gap cost “As any other” 
and very accurate (slow).  
A multiple alignment was performed on the largest abundand cluster from each pig (output: fasta 
file). Subsequently the output fasta file was converted to nexus format by used of EMBOSS software. 
This was done due to the file format for PAUP*. In PAUP* the nexus file was loaded (>execute 
filename.nexus) and the distance matrix was generated (>showdist).

3 Results 
In this section a description of the obtained results are presented. The results include an evaluation of 
the γδ T cells distribution and the clonality of the TCR δ-chain.  
3.1 Titration of CD8αα antibody using PBMCs 
To evaluate the γδ T cell distribution in young piglets and sows, the expression of surface markers 
was assessed. PBMCs and whole blood cells were stained and analyzed by flow cytometry. PBMCs 
and whole blood samples consisted of LY/D piglets and LY sows, age 3-4 weeks and age 1-2 years, 
respectively. The first staining was performed on PBMCs and examined for expression of surface 
markers CD3, CD4, CD8 and Tcr1-N4. The staining controls applied throughout flow cytometry 
experiments were unstained cells and FMO control. Staining controls are important for experiments 
using flow cytometry, and they are particularly important for multicolor staining. This first staining 
showed an incorrect coloring of the cells and an improper separation of CD8αα and Tcr1-N4, 
probably due to technical errors in the laboratory (Appendix F, Figure 16 and Figure 17). Therefore, 
a titration was performed to achieve the correct concentration of CD8αα. The titrated concentrations 
examined were 10μg/ml, 5μg/ml, 2.5μg/ml and 1.25μg/ml. Anti-CD3 and anti-CD4 antibodies were 
included in this experiment since they are included in the staining panel for all staining and to assess 
their impact on CD8αα staining. The gating strategy used to examine CD8αα concentration was: 
single cells, lymphocytes, CD3+ cells and CD8+ cells. The gating strategy and FMO control are

4 Discussion 
4.1 γδ T cell occurrence 
In this study the aim was to investigate the occurrence of γδ T cells and subsequently the clonality of 
the T cell receptor in two populations (piglets and sows). In the view of this, it was decided to examine 
the expression of CD3, CD4, CD8 and Tcr1-N4 on T lymphocytes. Thus, based on the knowledge 
that pigs have an abundance of γδ T cells in their blood and that γδ T cells can be divided into 
subpopulations according to the expression of CD4 and CD8 (CD4+CD8+, CD4+CD8- and CD4-CD8-
), they were investigated. Therefore, to investigate the occurrence of γδ T cells and the subpopulations 
in piglets and sows, flow cytometry was applied. During the analysis of γδ T cells it was observed 
that these cells (γδ TCR+) constituted on average 39.3% ± 12.8 (piglets) and 30.3% ± 5.1 (sows) of 
CD3+ cells. Thus, to further evaluate on the magnitude of γδ T cell in the two groups, an un-paired 
two-tailed t-test was performed. This un-paired two-tailed t-test was chosen to see if there was a 
significant difference in the distribution between piglets and sows. This un-paired t-test showed that 
there was no significant difference between the magnitudes of γδ T cells in the two animal groups.  
The results gained from comparing the magnitude of γδ T cells and the age of pigs, established that 
there was no correlation between these two parameters, as would have been expected from 
examination of different studies. Nonetheless, by comparing our results to the literature, it became 
apparent that the time of sampling is important when looking at γδ T cell frequency. Talker et al. 
(2013)[5] reports that the number of γδ T cells increase from birth until week 19-25, where it reaches 
a plateau. The same observation has been demonstrated by Stepanova et al.[4] The prominent 
difference between Talker et al.[5], Stepanova et al. (2007)[4] and this study is that they assess the 
pigs γδ T cells population over a longer period of time and not only at two periods, which is the case 
for this study. Furthermore, Talker et al. also uses a different antibody (clone PTT16), which 
recognizes all γδ T cells regardless of the variable chain, which is different from Tcr1-N4, which 
recognizes a determinant on the constant region of the TCR δ-chain.   
4.2 Clonality of γδ T cell receptor 
As there was no significant difference in the magnitude of γδ T cells, among the examined pig, the 
clonality of the TCR δ-chain was investigated. As mentioned previously in the results, Illumine 
MiSeq was used to examine the clonality in the two age groups. Primers were designed to bind 
upstream of the variable gene and downstream of the diversity gene, as these genes are the variable 
part of the TCR δ-chain. The designed primer pair was further verified based on gel-electrophoresis 
(Figure 9) and the amplicons were send for sequencing.  On the basis of sequence analysis, it was 
observed that a cluster dominates within the sow samples, while the same was not observed for the 
piglets. A possible explanation for this could be that the piglet’s adaptive immune system (primarily 
αβ T cells) are not as developed and the more non-specific immune system (γδ T cells) therefore may 
be active in the beginning. As large clusters were generated for each pigs, an alignment of the most 
abundant clusters was performed. The alignment (Figure 12) showed that the majority of nucleotides 
within the V and J segments were conserved among all pigs, whilst the D segment was highly 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
variable. Thus, knowing that the CDR3 is encoded around the junction of V(D)J region and that it 
has variable features, might explain the observed variability within the D segment. Yet, this 
observation does not provide any indication about the clonality among all the clusters. Therefore, by 
comparing the cluster independent of pig, it was showen that none of the clusters could be grouped 
together with 100 % similarity. Thus indicating that there were no 100 % identical clones in the pigs.  
By examining publications regarding γδ TCR, it is obvious that the information on the γδ TCR in pigs 
are deficient, compared to publications on the clonality of the γδ TCR. For instance, Thome et al. 
(1993)[15] and Grimm et al. (1994)[14] examine the clonality of the δ-chain constant region in the 
blood of pigs. Furthermore, Yang et al. (1995)[30] and Holtmeier et al., (2002)[17] have investigated 
the clonality repertoire of TCR δ-chain in the tissue and liver of pigs. The latter publications have in 
contrast to this study, been able to group δ-chain variable clones into five groups based on 75% 
similarity. In order to gain similar results, the cloned sequences in this study would have to be 
regrouped into clusters based on 75% similarity. However, the regrouping of the clusters may not 
show the same, as Yang et al. and Holtmeier et al. have demonstrated cluster grouping in the liver 
and tissues, as there may be compartment differences.  
 
The findings acquired through this study might provide more knowledge within characterization of 
pig as a suitable model for analysis of γδ T cells magnitude, function (e.g. recognition of ligands) and 
localization in experimental research. This in turns can been applied to the existing knowledge on 
pigs as an animal model for human immunity.

5 Conclusion 
This study described the magnitude of γδ T cells in the blood of piglets and sows. As shown by 
comparing the magnitude of γδ T cells in piglets and sows, the percentage difference was not 
significant different from each other. This study further described the cloning and sequencing of a 
partial pig TCR δ-chain variable region. As shown by the comparing the cloned and sequenced genes, 
the clones shared conservations in the V and J region, whereas the D region the cloned sequences was 
highly variable. The conserved parts suggests that these regions have similar importance for the 
function of the γδ TCR in all pigs. Furthermore, this study demonstrated that the cloned sequences 
were clustered based on 100% similarity and showed that the sequences could not be cluster based 
this similarity. This suggesting that the clonality of partial V(D) J segments of δ-TCR in two groups 
of pigs were highly diverse.

6 Perspectives on future work 
The results obtained during this study have increased the knowledge on the magnitude of γδ T cells 
in two groups of pigs. For future work on this study, it would be interesting to examine the magnitude 
of γδ T cells in more than two age groups and if possible follow multiple pigs from the day of birth 
until adulthood (approx. 2 years) and observe the γδ T cell development. Furthermore, it would be 
interesting to examine the γδ T cell surface markers, to classify γδ T cells further. Moreover, it would 
also be interesting to e.g. sequence the whole part of the γδ TCR to gain more knowledge.
