Abstract
Nanolasers are promising components for improving future computer chips and making them more
energy-efficient, but their fundamental physics introduces noise, which motivates the investigation of
novel nanolaser systems. In this thesis, we examine a nanolaser system in which a single phonon
mode mediates emission into the cavity mode.
The system was analysed both with and without
interactions with its environment. It has been shown that phonon-mediated transitions can nullify the
effects of large detuning between the cavity and emitter in a nanolaser system, while also complicating
the dynamics, with three frequencies dominating the systemвАЩs evolution, leading to behaviour that
a number-state basis cannot account for. Despite these changes, the system reaches a steady state
in which lasing is possible at detunings where conventional nanolasers would fail. It was also shown
that it becomes challenging to reduce system dynamics to a rate-equation framework. These results
provide insight into a novel nanolaser system with complicated dynamics.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
The use of information and communication technologies, like the internet and computers, is instru-
mental to a lot of things in our lives. The downside of this extensive use is the enormous impact it has
on our climate, where information and communication technology is responsible for around 4% of the
worldвАЩs CO2 footprint [1]. Since there are no signs that the expansion of these technologies is slowing
down, the goal now becomes to optimize their energy efficiency. In the area of data transmission, this
has been achieved by switching to using fiber optic cables. Light has significantly lower loss compared
to the ohmic loss in copper cables, making fiber optic cables much more energy-efficient [2]. Computer
chips suffer from a lot of the same problems as communication using copper cables, with high loss in
the form of heat generation that is problematic not only to their energy efficiency, but also to their
performance, since excessive heat can affect a computer chipвАЩs performance [3].
A growing research field is exploring the continuation of the expansion seen in data transmission,
replacing electronics with equivalent systems that utilize light. One possibility is to replace some of
the electronic signals between cores on a chip with optical signals. This option is promising since most
of the energy loss in a computer chip comes from the internal communications and not the logical
operations [4]. Here, an electronic signal would be converted to light, which would then travel to
another part of the chip through a waveguide and be converted back into an electronic signal. To
actually see the benefit of this optical communication, the power consumption of each bit would need
to be very low [4]. A nanolaser has a very low lasing threshold compared to a standard laser, making
it consume a low amount of power while lasing, which makes it an ideal candidate for this application
[5].
Nanolasers consist of a tiny number of emitters, making the fundamental quantum noise of the system
have a big impact on their output. This becomes a problem if they are to be used to communicate
between cores since this communication has a stringent requirement on the Bit-Error-Ratio (BER)
[4]. One way to reduce the quantum noise is to increase the photon number of the light generated
by the nanolaser, which could make the nanolaser reach the BER requirement, but this also makes
the nanolaser use more energy. Another way to reduce the quantum noise is to change the physical
system in which the nanolaser operates. Some different approaches have been shown and theorized
to be effective. Machida et al. showed that it is possible to generate squeezed light by pumping a
semiconductor laser with a constant current [6], and M√Єrk et al. theorized that using a cavity that
exhibits extreme dielectric confinement would be able to generate intensity squeezed light [7]. From
this, we can see that to develop improved nanolasers, it is important to investigate new physical
systems and phenomena that may alter how the nanolaser operates.
A standard nanolaser system with a single emitter works as a coupled two-level-, and cavity system

Phonons can interact with the system of a nanolaser in different ways.
An example is the effect
called Brilliouin scattering, where light is able to scatter the periodic waves of phonons. It has been
shown that this effect is able to generate coherent phonons in an effect similar to lasing[14]. Brillouin
scattering is an effect where pump light directly interacts with phonons in a material, which can be
used in a nanolaser, but it is not what we want to describe in the thesis. Instead, we want to use the
effect that is also present in quantum dots, where phonons are generated and absorbed directly by the
two-level emitter system. This interaction can be described by the deformation potential interaction.
Here, the emitters and phonons in the system interact through the changing electron density in the
system.

We want to use this interaction to make lasing possible at high detunings. Specifically, we want to
describe a system where only a single phonon mode is available to interact strongly with the system.
Using a single phonon mode could give rise to new and interesting effects that donвАЩt occur in quantum
dots and nanolasers that interact with a phonon bath, such as stimulated phonon interactions or
phonon lasing.
Isolating a single phonon mode is not a new idea; it has been achieved in various optomechanical
applications, where phonon cavities, laser-like phonon generation, and increased interaction between
phonons and excitons have been theorized and realized. Some examples of a phonon cavity include
Mahboob et al.[16], showing that having two phonon modes will generate strain in a material, making
it possible to create a cavity for one of the phonon modes. Fainstein et al.[17] showed that a Bragg
reflector can contain both a photon and a phonon mode at the same frequency, which increased their
interaction strength.
Various forms of coherent phonon generation have also been demonstrated. Grudinin et al.[18] showed
coherent phonon generation with a threshold when coupling two mechanically tuned cavities to their
normal splitting. Xiong et al.[14] showed how coupling light and phonons together through Brillouin
scattering made it possible for the system to exhibit lasing of both the photon and phonon modes.
Chafatinos et al. have also shown that it is possible to couple optomechanical systems to polariton
Bose-Einstein condensates to generate coherent phonons in a laser-like behaviour. To further couple
our understanding of phonons to lasers and emitters, it has even been theorized by Spinnler et al.[19]
that the generation of single phonons can be possible by coupling emitters to mechanical resonators.
In addition, systems with increased coupling between phonons and photons have been demonstrated.
Shishkov et al.[20] theorized that the coupling to a polariton Bose-Einstein condensate can also sig-
nificantly increase the interaction strength between phonons and excitons. Burgwal et al.[21] showed
that it was possible to enhance the interaction strength between phonons and light by coupling light-
and vibrational modes in a photonic crystal beam.
In this project, we will combine all the effects described to simulate a nanolaser that only works by
phonon-induced transitions with a single phonon mode. In chapter two, we will analyse the system
through its Hamiltonian and describe how we will model the systemвАЩs interactions with the environ-
ment. The standard system parameters that will be used in the simulation of the system will also
be discussed. In chapter 3, we begin by describing the transient behaviour of the phonon-assisted
nanolaser system in the absence of environmental interactions to obtain an overview of the system
dynamics. We will then subsequently include environmental interactions to describe the full nanolaser
system. In chapter four, we discuss the steady state behaviour of the nanolaser system and analyse
how the different effects described in chapters two and three affect the laser properties. In chapter five,
we will comment on the effort of deriving the relevant rate equations that would be able to describe
the phonon-assisted laser. In chapter six, we will discuss the possibilities of creating a nanolaser that
works through the assistance of phonons.

CHAPTER4
Steady state system description
In this chapter, we will describe the steady-state behaviour of the phonon-assisted nanolaser. Through
this, we will attempt to understand what happens when the various parameters that define the system
are changed.
When describing a macroscopic laser, a standard method is to compare the input power to the output
power. This is usually done to see the clear threshold where the spontaneous emission of the system
is overcome by the stimulated emission. We can do something similar by finding the steady state
behaviour of the different populations as a function of the pump power.
This has been done in
fig. 4.1(A), the corresponding cavity-mode second order correlation function, g(2)(0), has been found
in fig. 4.1(B), and the phonon-stateвАЩs second order correlation function, g(2)
ќ≤ (0), has been found in
fig. 4.1(C). We see that, as described in chapter 1, there is no clear threshold. Instead, we can see
that the cavity-mode population increases while getting close to a second-order correlation function
of one. It then keeps increasing while staying around g(2)(0) = 1, which indicates lasing. It then
reaches a maximum, whereafter the second-order correlation function increases to two. Instead of
the standard threshold, we see three regimes of the pump power that are not that clearly defined.
At low pump powers, the low population of the cavity mode and a second-order correlation function
below one indicate that the system works like a low-photon-number emitter. At intermediate pump
rates, we enter a regime in which the cavity-mode population steadily increases and the second-order
correlation function hovers around 1, indicating that the system begins to behave like a laser. Lastly,
at high pump rates, the cavity-mode population begins to decrease, while the second-order correlation
function approaches 2, as we would normally observe in a thermal light source.
This is very similar to the behaviour of the comparable nanolaser system without the phonon inter-
action by Bundgaard et. al.[10], where we found our system parameters. Here, they also observe
these three regimes. They find that in the first regime, spontaneous emission dominates, in the second
regime, stimulated emission has a higher and higher effect, and in the last regime, the pump rate
becomes so high that it begins acting as a decoherence effect, decreasing the stimulated emission rate
and quenching the lasing.

frequency with, and also a peak that is slightly skewed from zero. Since we only have a peak at a
negative detuning and not one at a positive detuning, we can conclude that the process where phonons
need to be absorbed in a transition is not able to facilitate any meaningful build-up of the population
in the cavity mode and does not make anything close to lasing possible. We can also see this from the
corresponding second-order correlation function staying at two. When we do match the detuning to
a phonon frequency that matches the polaron shift and make use of the emission of phonons in the
transition process, we do see a peak similar to the one around zero detuning. We also observe that
the second-order correlation function approaches one, indicating lasing.
Another interesting effect is that even around zero detuning, where the phonons are not needed to
facilitate the transitions, they still seem to have an impact. We can compare the maximum cavity
population around zero detuning with and without phonons. This has been done in fig. 4.3. We
observe that near zero detuning, phonons have a decohering effect on the system, only worsening
lasing. We can also see that, compared to a system without the phonon interaction, there seems to
be an advantage to having a small detuning with the phonon interaction. We observe that generating
phonons in the system enables lasing at detunings that are not possible without them, but the process
produces less light in the cavity mode than without the phonon interaction and is not beneficial at
small detunings.

CHAPTER5
System rate equations
Part of this chapter is based on work carried out in a previous project[28]. The work has been expanded
to be of use in this thesis.
Up until now, we have used a system with a single emitter. We have done this in part to simplify the
dynamics and because the resource requirements to simulate a bigger system grow too large when the
full system dynamics are found. In a real nanolaser, you would usually see that the gain medium is
made up of a multitude of emitters, which makes it impractical or even impossible to find their full
quantum state solutions. A way to solve this is to derive the rate equations that describe the system.
As described in chapter 1, rate equations are differential equations that describe the transient be-
haviour of the different system populations. When deriving the rate equations, a lot of the system
correlations are removed. This is not a problem for bigger systems with a huge amount of emitters,
but for systems like nanolasers with an intermediate amount of emitters, this misses some crucial
dynamics in the system, like the quantum noise. This noise can be inserted phenomenologically in the
form of Langevin terms[9], or by reducing the rate equations to Markov chains[10]. This means that
if the rate equation is able to describe the main part of the dynamics, the lost effects can be added
on later.
To be able to describe systems with more than one emitter, we want to find the rate equations that
describe the phonon-assisted nanolaser.

limiting factor that limits the ability to make use of phonon-assisted transitions in nanolasers is the
necessary interaction strength.

CHAPTER7
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to investigate how a nanolaser system would operate if all light emitted into the
laser cavity mode required a phonon. We investigated whether lasing was possible when interactions
with a single phonon mode were required in transitions, and whether any fundamental dynamics of
the nanolaser system changed.
By analysing the system Hamiltonian, it was found that the phonon state that is natural in the system
is distinctly different from the number state basis the cavity-mode occupies in a standard emitter-
cavity system. Through this evidence, it was found that a coherent-state basis is more appropriate for
describing the phonon state. To get further insight into the workings of the phonon-assisted nanolaser,
the transient behavior of an emitter-cavity system that does not interact with its environment, but
with the same restrictions on its transitions, was analyzed. It was found that when a phonon needs to
be present in transitions, the system dynamics changes from being dominated by a single frequency, the
Rabi frequency, to being dominated by three frequencies that describe three processes. One frequency
was shown to govern discrete steps that involve phonons, which further mix the emitter and cavity
modes, consistent with our understanding of phonon involvement in transitions. Another frequency
was found to govern effective Rabi-oscillations that are able to occur when the phonon state assists
transitions. Both of these frequencies combine nicely with our understanding of the system, where
phonons mediate transitions and make the system resemble one without any detuning. The third
frequency, however, showed that the system dynamics are a lot more complicated. Here, it was found
that the phonon state oscillates between a coherent state and a mixed number state, which complicates
our understanding from the analysis of the Hamiltonian.
It was established that, similarly to a standard nanolaser, the phonon-assisted nanolaser also reaches
a steady state behaviour when it is being pumped and interacts with its environment. Using this, a
steady state analysis of the system was used to show that lasing is possible when phonons assist with
transitions in a nanolaser. The lasing output was shown to be severely reduced compared to a system
working with zero detuning and without phonons, but able to exist at a detuning where standard
lasing is not possible.
We were able to demonstrate different aspects characteristic of lasing in a
nanolaser, such as a second-order correlation function around one, indicating coherent light, linewidth
narrowing of the cavity-mode spectrum, and that there is no clear threshold power above which lasing
is clear. By analysing the spectrum, it was determined that the linewidth was between the Schawlow-
Townes limits, but around pump powers where the system is transitioning to lasing, and when the
system becomes quenched, the linewidth breaks the limit. At very low pump rates, we have also
found implications that the description of the cavity mode as a number state is not accurate, as the
cavity-mode spectrum here indicated a combination of the dressed state basis characteristic of Rabi
Page 38 of 67

==PAGE:49==
oscillations and a shifting central peak indicating a coherent state, as we also saw with the phonon
states.
To be able to describe bigger systems closely resembling real-world nanolaser systems, an attempt was
made to derive rate equations for the phonon-assisted nanolaser system. Two different approaches were
attempted, where the phonon state was either reduced to a number state or a coherent state. We were
able to show that both approaches eventually failed in being able to describe the system. This failure
to describe the system using rate equations seems to stem from our conclusion drawn from the analysis
of the systemвАЩs transient behaviour. Since the phonon state oscillates between a coherent state and a
mixed number state, when we try to reduce it to either, we miss some core dynamics of the system.
Taken together, these results show that phonon-assisted transitions in nanolasers fundamentally alter
both the transient and steady-state dynamics of nanolaser systems, in a way that cannot be captured
by rate equations derived using the standard approach.
The system and parameter space investigated in this thesis were limited by the rapidly increasing size
of the system Hilbert space that is necessary when including the additional subspace of the phonons.
This reduced the number of parameters available for simulating the system. More interesting dynamics
may lie hidden in high phonon populations that this thesis was unable to explore.
Overall, this thesis provides an overview of the properties of phonon-assisted dynamics in nanolasers
and gives an example of how changing the fundamental dynamics of nanolasers may reveal more
complicated and interesting dynamics.

the phonon frequency, ѕЙb, the effective Rabi frequency, вД¶1, and the amplitude oscillation frequency,
вД¶ќ≤. In the chapter, we concluded this from the transient behaviour of the system, but it also becomes

strength and the deformation potential interaction strength. To find the effective Rabi frequency, it

oscillation frequency, a fast Fourier transform was used to track the resulting peaks as in fig. C.1.

APPENDIXF
Simulation considerations
In all simulations in this project, the Julia package QuantumToolbox.jl[32] has been used. Quantum-
Toolbox.jl converts operators to the matrix form corresponding to a specified Hilbert space dimension.
Other than numerical noise from the solving algorithm, the only aspect of this approach that is not
exact is the size of said Hilbert space. Since our emitter is modeled as a two-level system, its Hilbert
space always has dimension 2. This means that the only free parameters in our simulation are the
sizes of the Hilbert spaces for the cavity mode and the phonon state. The population of the states
primarily determines the necessary dimensionality of the Hilbert spaces, and, as shown in fig. 4.1,
the populations can vary substantially with the system parameters. To ensure that Hilbert spaces
of appropriate size are used while keeping simulations economical, a convergence criterion has been
applied to all larger simulations throughout the project.
When simulating our system with master equations using QuantumToolbox.jl, the output is the system
density function in the number state basis. This means the diagonal is the distribution of populations
in our system. All quantum states we will simulate in this project are localized in their population.
This means we can check whether a Hilbert space is big enough by checking the density matrix at the
highest number of states simulated. Specifically, this has been done by doing a partial trace of the
density matrix and checking the element at the highest row and column, shown here:
