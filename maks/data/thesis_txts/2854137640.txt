Abstract
This thesis describes a design of 14-bit SAR ADC which operates at the sampling rate
of 500 kSPS. During the design process, a system-level model was created to support a
top-down design approach. The structure of the ADC includes a two-sided split capacitor
array DAC and an auto-zeroing comparator designed to help eliminate offset. In addition,
an input buffer circuit minimizes the effect of parasitic capacitance. A layout, with the
digital control logic included, occupies an area of 0.09 mm². Performance results show
an effective number of bits of 11.22 at a power consumption of 649 μW which results in
the FoM 544 fJ/sample.

Introduction
”Why do we need analog-to-digital converters when everything is already digital?” a friend
once asked me. We live in a world composed of analog units and analog-to-digital con-
verters are used in most applications that require converting information from the physical
world to a computing system. The key parameters of ADCs include resolution, speed,
power consumption and area. Each application requires different performance character-
istics.
One critical application area for ADCs is biomedicine. Low-power, compact ADCs are
commonly used with portable biomedical sensors that require continuous operation [1]
[2], a specific example is an ADC for heart rate monitors [3]. Many applications require
low conversion latency. High-speed ADCs [4] [5] are often used in TV receivers, digital
oscilloscopes, and wireless communication systems like GSM [6] or even faster in optical
communication systems [7].
High-resolution ADCs with medium speed are needed in audio systems such as micro-
phones or digital recorders [8] [9]. ADCs for hearing aids [10] are even more challenging
requiring additional low-power design. ADCs are also important components in video
systems with image sensors [11] [12] [13].
In the automotive sector, ADCs play a crucial role in control systems [14] and sensor
interfaces [15]. These applications demand not only low latency but also high reliability.
Another category is space and nuclear applications which require ADCs resistant against
radiation [16]. Similarly, industrial automation applications utilize ADCs in control systems
[17], where high resolution, speed, and robustness against harsh conditions are often
required.

2.1
Flash ADC
A flash ADC uses a large array of comparators to convert an analog input to a digital
output in a single step. Each comparator is connected to a unique reference voltage from
a resistive ladder network and all comparators operate simultaneously. When the input
voltage exceeds a comparator’s reference, it outputs a high signal, while comparators
with higher references remain low. This output is interpreted as a binary digital value that
directly represents the analog input.
Flash ADCs are ideal for high-speed applications because of their single-cycle conversion.

consumption and large area. That makes them suitable only for low resolutions.
2.2
Sigma-Delta ADC
Sigma-Delta ADCs use oversampling and noise-shaping techniques. Oversampling al-
lows operation at much higher rates than the Nyquist rate. Thanks to this, the noise
is distributed across a wider spectrum, making it easier to filter out. Meanwhile, noise
shaping is achieved through the Sigma-Delta loop, which pushes the quantization noise
outside the signal band. The main components can be seen in figure 2.2.
The converter consists of an anti-aliasing filter that removes high-frequency components
to prevent them from folding into the signal band during sampling which follows. Next,
the signal passes through a sigma-delta modulator which consists of a quantizer that
introduces quantization noise into the spectrum. Meanwhile, a negative feedback loop
is applied around the quantizer. This loop includes a filter that shapes the quantization
noise and pushes it outside the signal band. Finally, the high-frequency noise is removed
by a digital filter, and the signal is down-sampled to match the desired output rate.

The performance of a sigma-delta converter can be tuned by adjusting three parameters
[19]:
• Increasing the oversampling ratio enhances the SNR but demands a higher sam-
pling frequency to keep the signal bandwidth, which requires faster circuits and re-
sults in greater power consumption.
• Raising the order of the loop filter can suppress quantization noise at low frequen-
cies. However, higher-order filters may have stability issues. That has to be miti-
gated by using less aggressive noise-shaping functions, which might in turn reduce
quantization noise suppression. In some cases, these stability challenges can be
solved by using cascaded ADCs.
• Increasing the number of quantization bits in the converter reduces quantization er-
ror. It however introduces higher linearity requirements for the DAC in the feedback
loop.

input signal merged with a delayed version of the input signal that has passed through
the loop. Therefore, the error signal is a high-pass filtered version of the input. Due to
this, there are large signal swings in the filter [20]. Instead, a feedforward loop can be
used. In such a modulator, the output of the quantizer is not delayed compared to the

One example of such state-of-the-art ADC in 0.13 um technology is in [21]. It consists
of a 4th-order feedforward loop filter, OSR of 64 and a 33-level quantizer and achieves
SNDR of 118.5dB and FoM of 171.7 dB thanks to several attributes. It uses a class-B
resistor DAC for its low noise. The matching problem of the unit elements in the DAC
is solved with the common mode loop with chopped RCM resistors. This design also
uses non-overlap dynamic element matching. Normally, class-B DEM schemes have 3
states and therefore they need more than one flip-flop per unit element. That makes
them susceptible to inter-symbol interference (ISI) due to mismatches in the flip-flops.
The ISI causes high-frequency noise foldback. The non-overlap DEM improves the ISI
by reducing some transitions by adjusting the logic controlling the flip-flops. This design
also uses the final integrator functions as an integrate-and-hold stage for sampling of the
quantizer. Thanks to that the resistive DAC can be used instead of the capacitive DAC in
the quantizer which reduces the need for the high drive of the previous stage.
In [22], a dynamic zoom ADC is designed. It is an ADC which is made out of a 5b asyn-
chronous SAR ADC and a 2nd-order 1 bit ΔΣM. A dynamic zoom provides updating the
references for the ADC based on the conversion result of SAR in the previous cycle. This
enables the ADC to handle varying input signals. This configuration achieves an SNDR
of 118.1 dB within a 1 kHz bandwidth, while the FoM is 185.8 dB. Another example of
state-of-the-art ΔΣ ADC is designed in [23]. This ADC has an SNDR of 100.9 dB within a
24 kHz bandwidth and a FoM of 187.2 dB. In order to achieve such a noise-power tradeoff,
multiple OTAs are stacked in the first amplifier stage within 3rd order 1-bit ΔΣ topology.
2.3
Pipelined ADC
Pipelined ADCs convert an analog input signal in stages. Each stage of the pipeline
performs a partial analog-to-digital conversion on the input signal and typically generates
a few bits of the output. The residual analog error from each stage is then amplified and
passed to the next stage This approach allows multiple stages to work simultaneously on
different parts of the input signal.
The basic operation of a pipelined ADC is made of sampling of the input signal, processing
it through a sequence of stages, and combining the outputs. Each stage consists of a sub-
ADC, a DAC, and an amplifier. The sub-ADC quantizes the input signal, the DAC converts
this digital output back to an analog signal, and the amplifier scales the residual error for
the next stage. After processing through all stages, digital error correction logic combines
the bits from each stage, generating a high-resolution digital output. Pipelined ADCs

resolution.

SAR ADC
The SAR ADC uses a binary search algorithm to find a digital approximation of the signal
iteratively. Its block diagram is in figure 2.4. The input analog voltage is compared to the
output of a digital-to-analog converter. The input bits of the DAC are one by one adjusted
by the control logic from the most significant bit based on the comparison results. SAR
ADCs have a low power consumption, moderate speed and moderate resolution.

In recent years, SAR ADCs at medium speeds have often been used with noise shap-
ing to create high-resolution ADCs. One such example is in [24]. The designed SAR
ADC with 2nd-order noise-shaping achieves 99.1 dB SNDR, and its FoM is 179.4 dB.
Power consumption in this work is reduced by distributing the two integrations needed
for second-order shaping over time, eliminating the need for an explicit integrator. The
effect of capacitor mismatch is reduced by rotating the location of grouped unit elements.
Additionally, it uses a boosting logic that can, for a current output, reuse previous outputs
to set the CSAR voltages. As a result, the current sample requires a smaller charge flow
which leads to a significant improvement in the input impedance.
The SAR ADC with state-of-the-art accuracy in 0.18μm technology is designed in [25].
It achieves a FoM of 186 dB, an INL below 0.03 ppm, and an SNDR of 105.6 dB. It
uses zero-order dynamic element matching for MSB capacitors to reduce the effect of
capacitor mismatch. Furthermore, dithering is applied to the LSB segment to smooth out
quantization errors. In order to suppress offset and flicker noise, auto-zeroing is used and
the power consumption is minimized through power-down modes between conversions.
An ultra low power SAR ADC is designed in [26]. It has an FoM of 161 dB while the

SAR ADCs are commonly used in applications that require lower resolutions, typically
around ENOB 6-10, although they can achieve resolutions of up to 12-14 bits in some
designs. This would fulfill the lower bounds of the resolution requirements. However, at
14 bits, SAR ADCs often face challenges mainly with noise and linearity that can impact
performance. To achieve 14 bit and higher resolutions, SAR architectures are often ac-
companied by techniques like pipelining or noise shaping. On the other hand, Σ-Δ ADCs
are suitable for high-resolution applications and they often achieve 16 bits which is the
upper end of the resolution requirements.
However, while it would be useful to select a Σ-Δ ADC to achieve the desired resolution, it
would be challenging to achieve the required sampling speed of at least 200 kSPS since
Σ-Δ ADCs are usually designed for applications with lower speed. In addition, Σ-Δ ADCs
typically require higher power consumption at higher sampling rates due to their complex
oversampling and filtering stages, which would conflict with the 400 µA maximum power
requirement. In contrast, SAR ADCs are suitable for low power, high speed operation.
Ultimately, the choice is between a Σ-Δ ADC for its higher potential resolution or a SAR
ADC for its smaller area, lower power consumption and speed. Given the power con-
straints and the need to balance both resolution and sampling rate, the SAR ADC is the
more optimal choice. It offers sufficient resolution and high speed while remaining a low
power architecture with the potential to meet the power requirements.

3
Methodology
The SAR ADC was chosen for this project due to its low power consumption, high speed,
and compact area compared to other ADC architectures. Its main components are the
digital-to-analog converter, a comparator, and digital control logic.
The resolution of the DAC limits the resolution of the ADC. Its type and characteristics
mostly determine the ADC’s speed and area. A DAC is a major source of nonlinearity
because of component mismatch. Based on the type and component sizes, it affects
precision due to noise. The component sizes and selection of the switching type also
influence power consumption.
The comparator is usually the biggest contributor to power consumption. Reducing the
bandwidth of its amplifier stage can save power, though this might lengthen conversion
time. Additionally, the comparator’s noise and offset significantly impact ADC precision.
This chapter explores the specifics of each block of the SAR ADC that must be considered
for the design.
3.1
DAC
There are several different DAC architectures. A very common one is the resistor-string
DAC, which uses a series of resistors to create discrete voltage steps. It is simple, but
often slow and limited in resolution. Then there are R-2R ladder DACs, which use a
network of resistors to achieve better precision. Binary weighted DACs are faster and
more efficient, but they require a large number of precise resistors and consume a lot of
power for high resolution. Capacitive DACs have in comparison low power consumption
and they have the advantage of a built-in track/hold function.
3.1.1
Charge Redistribution
Charge redistribution in capacitor arrays is often used to convert a digital signal to an
analog signal. It consists of controlled switching of capacitors and redistribution of charge

3.2.1
Strong ARM Latch
The purpose of latch is to compare two input signals and output the sign bit of the dif-
ference. The Strong ARM latch is often used for its high input impedance, high speed
and low power consumption in SAR ADCs. It uses the regenerative feedback mechanism
to quickly amplify the small voltage difference between Vin+ and Vin−which leads to a
full-scale digital output where Vout+ and Vout−settle at opposite logic levels.
The schematic of the StrongARM Latch is shown in figure 3.6. This circuit operates in two
distinct phases controlled by the clock signal. When the CLK signal is low, the M7, M8
and M9 transistors are turned off, almost no current flows through the circuit and because
there is no voltage drop across transistors M5 and M6, the output nodes Vout+ and Vout−
are pulled up to the supply voltage VDD. This ensures that the output nodes are fully
precharged to a known state in preparation for the comparison phase. In the second
phase, CLK signal transitions to high and the current passing through transistors M1 and
M2 rises. Depending on the voltages Vin+ and Vin−, one of the transistors opens faster
than the other. If the voltage Vin+ is higher, the current flowing through transistor M5 rises
faster than in M6. Therefore, the voltage on Vout−decreases which leads to the closing of
the transistors M6 and M4 which further accelerates the charging of Vout+. That in return
supports falling of voltage Vout−through opening of the transistors M3 an M5.
The most critical parameters of the Strong ARM latch in SAR ADC are the input offset
needed to distinguish small voltage differences in ADCs with higher precision and the
speed of conversion.

3.4
Autozeroing
Autozeroing is one of the techniques to reduce the offset of the comparator. It works
by storing the charge corresponding to the offset on the capacitor in the first phase and
adding up the capacitor voltage to the resulting voltage in the second phase.
Figure 3.8a shows the principle of this operation [44]. When the clock signal is low, the
comparator inputs are connected to the reference voltage. Any non-idealities in the com-
parator, such as asymmetries in the layout, mismatches, or process variations, cause
one of the output signals to be larger than the other, resulting in the offset being stored
between the capacitors. In the second phase, when the comparator inputs are connected
to the input signal, the stored charge effectively cancels the offset.
Alternatively, the capacitor can be placed in front of the comparator in a circuit where
the outputs are shorted to the inputs during the precharge phase, as shown in figure
3.8b [45]. The amount of offset cancelled is limited by the size of the capacitor. However,
larger capacitors have a greater impact on the dynamic performance of the comparator. In
other implementations, an amplified difference between the outputs may be stored and so
thereby the offset is cancelled more effectively but with the use of additional components.

High Level Model
Selecting Total DAC Capacitance requires balancing several factors. A larger capaci-
tance reduces noise, reduces the effect of the parasitic capacitance of the comparator on
charge redistribution and improves matching accuracy. However, a smaller capacitance
is better for reducing power consumption, saving area, and increasing speed. The total
DAC capacitance is affected by the size of the unity capacitor and by the number of ca-
pacitors in the LSB part of the split capacitor array. The position of the split capacitor also
affects the non-linearity of the ADC.
In the created Matlab code, besides the quantization error, two non-idealities whose ef-
fect is largely dependent on the total capacitance of the capacitor array were taken into
account. Firstly, the mismatch of a specific MIM capacitor between metal4 and metaltop
layers with Pelgrom coefficient of capacitor mismatch 0.3 % µm was modelled accord-
ing to equations from section 3.1.5. Another factor considered in the Matlab model was
parasitic capacitance at the top capacitor plates whose effect on the ADC performance is
determined by eq. 3.26.
The parameters measured and compared in the model were SNDR, area, and non-linearity.
The area was determined from the area of the capacitor mentioned, which is 2.35 fF/μm²
under normal conditions. The SNDR is calculated by simulating the conversion of a si-
nusoidal input signal and using the sinad function. To retrieve the DNL, a linear signal
sweeping through the entire ADC range with steps LSB/100 was converted. DNL was
then determined by comparing the number of times each digital code appears in the ADC
output result with the ideal value - 100.
Based on the results of the parameter measurement, the value of the unity capacitor was
set to 250 fF and the number of bits in the LSB capacitor array to 6 (as can be seen in
figure 4.2).

Comparator Parameters
The test setup used to measure the offset is shown in figure 4.8b. A ramp signal was
applied to the comparator inputs, with the rising edge connected to the non-inverting input

at negative output transitioned from high to low was considered the offset voltage for each
iteration. A total of 40 iterations were conducted, and the results are shown in figure

iterations increases, the mean value tends toward zero, since the circuit is symmetrical.
The standard deviation of the offset was calculated to be 65.57 µV.
This is an improvement compared to the offset of the latch, which has offset with a stan-
dard deviation of 457 µV. However, the LSB of the 14-bit ADC with Vref = 1 V corresponds
to a voltage of 60 µV, meaning that the comparator’s offset still limits the overall precision
of the ADC.
The noise was measured using the test setup shown in figure 4.8a. A DC signal with
values from -0.6 mV to 0.6 mV, was applied to the comparator. These values were chosen
to be close to the expected noise level. A transient noise analysis was performed, during
which the noise caused incorrect comparison results multiple times in each iteration. To
eliminate the influence of conversion time dependency on the input value and ensure more
accurate measurements, registers were placed at the comparator’s outputs. The average
output value for each iteration corresponds to the input-referred noise of the comparator.
The scaled average values, mapped to the interval between -0.5 V and 0.5 V, are plotted
in figure 4.10. The average noise was measured to be 143 µV, indicating that the last two
bits of the 14 bit ADC might be sensitive to noise, potentially impacting the precision of
the conversion.

might be achieved by adding decoupling capacitance at the preamplifier’s output and at
the output of buffers or by increasing the sizes of certain components, mainly the differen-
tial pairs. However, both approaches would reduce bandwidth (without the input current
being increased), which would, in turn, increase offset. Overall, limited improvements can
be made regarding noise, apart from selecting some alternative low-noise architecture.
The settling time could be improved by reducing the unit capacitor size, which was initially
selected at 250fF based on system-level simulations to minimize capacitor mismatch.
Although decreasing the unit capacitance slightly could make the mismatch effect more
noticeable, it would improve settling time and potentially enhance overall performance. It
is now clear that the system-level design should have taken into account the settling time.
The resulting speed meets the required rate, although the requirements prefer a speed
of 1 MSPS. The speed is mainly limited by the settling time of the capacitors and by
the bandwidth of the preamplifier. Using a wide-band amplifier architecture could lead

to better results. With smaller unity capacitance, the clock period might be potentially
decreased, however this would involve trade-offs with power consumption.
The SAR architecture was chosen mainly due to power constraints, and in outcome, the
resulting power consumption meets the requirements.
Reducing unit capacitance could also help optimize the design area. The current design
exceeds the target area by about 4.5 times. However, meeting area requirements while
satisfying other design specifications is generally challenging.
In summary, reducing the unit capacitance could improve both the area and resolution
of the design. A significant reduction, however, would require some form of calibration
to correct the capacitor mismatch. Digital calibration is often implemented in SAR ADCs
designed with a smaller unit capacitance. Digital calibration introduces some overhead,
however, it is unavoidable in high-speed and high-precision designs. In the case of the
medium-speed ADC, which has been designed in this thesis, both methods: large capac-
itance without calibration and small unit capacitance with calibration, are possible.

7
Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to design and implement a low power ADC with perfor-
mance targets of ENOB at least 12, a speed range of 0.2 to 1 MSPS, a power consumption
of less than 720 μW and a compact area of up to 0.02 mm². The design process involved
selecting an optimal architecture, developing a system-level model and applying these
specifications to achieve an efficient design. Then circuit-level design and layout was
carried out together with a post-layout verification and parameter measurement.
After preliminary analysis comparing suitable architectures, SAR ADC was chosen. For
the design, a top-down approach was used, starting with a system-level model. Analysis
and system level contributed to choosing a type of DAC which was chosen to be a two-
sided split capacitor array. The system level has taken into account major nonidealities
and compared parameters such as SNDR or nonlinearity. It contributed to the sizing of
several components.
The design at the circuit level was done partly by theoretical analysis and partly with the
help of simulations. One of the main challenges was the need for a low offset of the
comparator, which was solved by using an auto-zeroing. Another limitation was the effect
of the parasitic capacitance of the preamplifier on the charge redistribution. In order to
minimise the parasitic effects, input buffers were implemented.
A common centroid topology was used for the capacitor array layout to ensure accurate
matching. The comparator layout was designed to achieve symmetry to minimise offset
and reduce parasitic capacitance. The digital control logic was implemented using an au-
tomated placement and routing tool which also helped optimising the power consumption.
The final layout, including digital control logic, occupies 0.09 mm². Post-layout results
in an ENOB of 11.22 and a power consumption of 544 fJ/sample at a sampling rate of
0.5 MSPS. Future improvements could be achieved by a more careful selection of the
unit capacitance, which could be optimized by incorporating capacitor settling time into
the system-level model.
