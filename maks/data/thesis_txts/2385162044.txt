ABSTRACT 
Abstract 
A significant part of the North Sea will be covered by offshore wind farms in the next decades. This 
study provides insight in the potential effects of offshore wind farms on the early life stages of six 
commercial flatfish species: sole (Solea solea), plaice (Pleuronectes platessa), turbot (Scophtalmus 
maximus), brill (Scophtalmus rhombus), flounder (Platichthys flesus) and dab (Limanda limanda). The 
output of a particle-tracking model (Larvae&Co) coupled with a 3D hydrodynamic model was used to 
analyse the connectivity patterns between wind farms and nursery areas in the North Sea over 10 year 
timeframe (1997-2006). Knowledge of how wind farms and nurseries are connected and what 
processes affect this dispersal is crucial as these individuals are subjected to different biological, 
environmental and hydrographical conditions and therefore act as an additional source of uncertainty 
in recruitment predictions. Offshore wind farms have shown a significant effect on connectivity and 
settlement. The consequences are however highly species and site-dependent. 
 
Key words 
Connectivity, IBM model, Larval transport, Marine renewable energy, Marine planning, North Sea

1 INTRODUCTION 
In recent years the pressure on the global seas and oceans has increased, due to intensified human 
activities and climate change (Halpern et al. 2008). Oceans are important elements of life on earth: 
they contribute to human food security, climate regulation, energy provision and the accommodation 
of several essential natural resources to humanity (Worm et al. 2006). This value doesn’t prevent the 
marine environment from being subjected to numerous pressures such as pollution, over-exploitation, 
climate change and declining biodiversity. Effective ocean management is required in order to mitigate 
anthropogenic effects, and to ensure the value of the ecosystem services provided. The potential of 
the ocean can only be realized when using sustainable approaches in decision-making, since ecosystem 
preservation and development of economic activities are inherently linked.  
Fishing, shipping, offshore wind and marine biotechnology are all examples of ocean related industries 
that are expected to growth in the near future (OECD 2016). The present contribution of ocean-based 
industries to the global economic output is significant: in 2010 this was 1.5 trillion USD which is 
approximately 2.5% of the world gross value added (GVA), and  predictions have suggested a doubling 
by 2030 of the contribution of the ocean economy to the global value. This is largely due to growth in 
sectors such as offshore wind, marine aquaculture and fish processing (OECD 2016).  
The general increase of human activities in marine areas, both coastal and offshore, together with the 
expansion of fisheries in the same areas, calls for integrated management measures of the marine 
environment. Maritime spatial planning (MSP) is regarded a way to support human activities in the 
marine environment, while accounting for the impacts they provide on the ecosystem (Halpern et al. 
2008; Douvere 2008; Foley 2013). Co-location of several activities in certain areas, as well of the 
exclusion of activities from sites can be considered (Jentoft & Knol 2013). 
In particular, offshore wind is one of the largest growing marine industrial sectors in the previous and 
next decades (OECD 2016). The North East Atlantic and Baltic Sea are especially affected by this growth 
as a result from the EU directive (EC 2009) which stipulates a use of 20% of the total energy from 
renewable energy sources by 2020. The increase in demand for renewables is motivated by the 
challenges of reducing carbon dioxide emissions and mitigating global warming. Additionally, use of 
renewable resources reduces risks caused by the displacement of harmful substance, radioactive 
pollution and the depletion of non-renewable resources. Due to the limited terrestrial space, wind 
farms are mostly implemented on offshore locations, with additional benefits the possibility to 
implement large windfarms, transport and construction of large units that is easier over water, 
produce higher energy per unit using the strong and more constant airflows above sea surface (Ewea 
2012). These offshore wind farms would cover a significant area in the North Sea and the Baltic Sea.   
According to the European Wind Energy Association (EWEA) at the end of 2016 there were 81 offshore 
wind farms (OWFs) – with a total capacity of 12,631 MW across 3,589 wind turbines – installed in 10 
European countries, and 71% of these farms is located in the North Sea (16.4% in the Irish Sea, 11.5% 
in the Baltic Sea) (EWEA 2017). The largest amount of offshore wind capacity is owned by the United 
Kingdom (40.8%) followed by Germany (32.5%), Denmark (10.1%) and the Netherlands (8.8%). The 
offshore wind industry is expected to grow up to a total capacity of 24.6 GW by 2020 (Figure 1).

The implementation of offshore wind farms affects the environment in various ways. Currently, there 
is little knowledge available on the effects of OWFs on the early life stages of flatfish. This study aims 
to provide insights in the potential effects caused by wind farms, and on what part of the settled flatfish 
larvae these effects would be of significance. The impacts caused by wind farms, including the impacts 
on the physical environment, will be explained in the following section. 
1.1 PHYSICAL EFFECTS 
Within a wind farm an average reduction of wind speed of 8-9 % has been measured immediately 
downstream of the wind turbine arrays (Christiansen & Hasager 2005), while the wind recovers within 
5 to 20 km distance from a wind turbine. Other potential effects of wind farms are changes in air 
pressure, air temperature, humidity as well as increases in water turbulence and partial blockage of 
flow by the turbines (Baidya Roy 2011; Rennau et al. 2012). Hydrographical changes take place within 
the wind turbine array, while only small changes occur up to 10 km outside the area. Reductions in 
wave height within OWFs have been shown, with subsequently reductions in suspended sediment and 
light extinction, as well as increases in net primary production and chlorophyll concentration, which 
suggests a general increase in ecosystem productivity (van der Molen et al. 2014). Within a wind farm 
spatial variability in changes of wave height occur related to the layout of the array. The effects 
decrease with increased distance between turbines. Similar results have been shown with regard to 
noise propagation. (van der Molen et al. 2014). 
1.2 FISH POPULATIONS AND THE IMPACT OF WIND FARMS 
The implementation of OWFs results, next to the physical impacts discussed in the previous section, 
also to environmental impacts. Several studies have highlighted the effects of wind farms during 
different phases i.e. the construction phase, the operational phase and the decommissioning phase 
(Petersen & Malm 2006; Bergström et al. 2014). The environmental effects of an operational wind farm 
can be both negative and positive. However, positive effects should be seen in perspective as

1.2.1 
Acoustic disturbance 
1.2.1.1 Construction 
The acoustic disturbance generated during construction is dependent on the foundation type used for 
the turbines (see also Text box I, page 4). The construction is mostly disturbing for marine mammals, 
due to the pile-driving that is necessary when constructing turbines with monopiles or jacket 
foundations. Marine mammals such as harbor porpoise have shown avoidance behavior due to the 
high sound levels (Richardson et al. 1995; Carstensen et al. 2006; Bailey et al. 2010; Brandt et al. 2011). 
This noise is also a cause of mortality and tissue damage in fish (Popper & Hastings 2009; Popper et al. 
2003; Nedwell & Howell 2004). 
Pile-driving is not required  to construct turbines based on gravity foundations, which has therefore a 
lower acoustic impact on the environment. The acoustic disturbance originates in this case from 
drilling, dredging and shipping. Fish and marine mammals will respond to this low intensity noise, but 
they are expected to return shortly after the disturbance has finished (Spiga et al. 2012; Scheidat et al. 
2011; Jensen et al. 2009).

1.2.1.2 Operation 
The noise generated by wind turbines ranges between 80 and 150 dB re 1mPa but differs with regard 
to the type of foundation; steel monopile foundations have shown to emit more underwater sound 
than jacket foundations, and the volume increases with wind speed (Norro & Degraer 2016). This 
sound can be heard by mammals, fish and certain crustaceans (Slabbekoorn et al. 2010; Popper & 
Hastings 2009; Carstensen et al. 2006). The vibrations are caused by the gearbox and generator of the 
wind turbines. Also, highly local vibrations through the sea floor might occur (Nedwell & Howell 2004). 
Additionally, frequency in ship traffic due to maintenance works might increase, which will enhance 
acoustic disturbance. The level of disturbance may vary between locations depending on the ambient 
noise present before the implementation of the OWF. There seems to be no clear negative effect of 
turbine-generated noise on marine species. However, the hearing abilities and processing of sound 
differ strongly between species, and many have not been studied yet. Fish can be sensitive to sound

used in Europe are steel monopiles (80.8%) and concrete caissons (7.5%), and the shape of these

The types of foundations mainly used for the construction of offshore wind farms in the North Sea 
are gravity foundations, monopile foundations, tripod foundations and jacket foundations. The 
type used depends on the conditions of the seabed and on the water depth.  
Gravity foundations are mostly used in water with a depth of maximum 30m. This type of 
foundations consists of concrete, ballasted with stones, gravel or sand. 
Monopile foundations are currently mostly used, preferably in water with a maximum depth of 
25m. The foundations consist of steel, and are driven into the seabed until a depth of maximum 30 
m is reached. 
Tripod foundations consist of three steel piles, welded together. This foundation type is mostly used 
in waters up to 35m. 
Jacket foundations are the most heavy and robust foundations, made of multiple steel piles and 
beams, and used in deeper waters (> 40m).

1.2.2 
Dredging 
Dredging is required during construction of the windmills, particularly when placing the gravity-based 
foundations and the cables. These activities cause sediment resuspension and release of sediment-
bound substances, increased sedimentations in the areas surrounding the dredging locations and 
temporary loss of these affected habitats. It also leads to an increase in turbidity, with negative effects 
on sensitive species and juvenile fish (Auld & Schubel 1978; Lake & Hinch 1999).  
In the locations where footings and scour protection are placed, all living organisms will be removed 
due to loss of habitat. The surface of the total OWF actually covered by turbines is a small proportion 
of the total concession because the large distance between windmills. It is approximately 7 times the 
size of the rotor blades between two turbines and may vary between wind farms.   
1.2.3 
Electromagnetic fields 
Electromagnetic fields are generated around the electric transmission cables (due to the magnetic field 
that is generated around the cables when they come in contact with moving water) (Gill et al. 2012). 
Many marine organisms can sense electromagnetic fields (Wiltschko & Wiltschko 1997). Cartilaginous 
fish are mostly affected, as they use electromagnetism for prey detection (Kalmijn 1982; Kimber et al. 
2011). Migrating fish such as eel use electromagnetism in their orientation (Tesch et al. 1992; 
Westerberg & Lagenfelt 2008). No significant effects have been shown for marine mammals or 
invertebrates. The electromagnetic fields related to the cables might, however, be mitigated by the 
improvement of the cable armor as well as the burial of the cables. 
1.2.4 
Habitat gain 
The construction of scour protection around the foundations of the wind turbines introduces hard 
substrates. Hence the OWF implementation in sandy or muddy areas increases habitat heterogeneity, 
and the turbines can act as a substrate for fouling organisms. Even though the introduction of hard 
substrates leads to disturbance of the original habitat, positive effects on diversity and density might 
occur - most importantly for hard substrate species.  
Increases in fish abundance have been observed in relation to the construction of artificial structures. 
These observations have been made mostly in relation to fish that prefer hard substrates and live in 
benthic areas such as two spot gobies (Gobius flavescens), sea bream (Diplodus spp.) and Goldsinney 
wrasse (Ctenolabrus rupestris)  (Ashley et al. 2014).  Several studies have indicated an increase in  
abundances of fish around OWFs (Bergström et al. 2013; Stenberg et al. 2015), but only in close 
proximity of the wind turbines, not on the full scale of the OWF. Limited evidence for the increase of 
certain commercially important fish species such as cod (Gadus morhua) and pollock (Pollachius 
pollachius) was shown at renewable energy sites. Catch per unit of effort (CPUE) values for pouting 
(Trisopterus luscus) and Atlantic cod was higher around OWFs, and these species showed dietary 
preferences for species associated with turbine foundations. These observations have been made 
shortly after OWF implementation and the observed patterns might change over time (Reubens et al. 
2011; Reubens et al. 2013). 
Even though increases in biomass have been detected, increases in biodiversity did not show an 
increase of similar strength (Wilhelmsson et al. 2006; Ashley et al. 2014). Construction of OWFs is 
expected to lead to a change in the relative proportion of different species groups. A decrease is 
expected of the dominant benthic species, followed by an increase in demersal species that are more 
strongly associated with hard substrates (Santos & Monteiro 1997).

However, the introduced hard substrates might generate unwanted effects by altering existing 
biodiversity patterns. Blue mussel (Mytilus edulis) and barnacles (Balanus improvis) have been 
observed in high densities around wind farms (Lindeboom et al. 2011; Andersson et al. 2010; 
Wilhelmsson & Malm 2008; Krone et al. 2013), as well as jellyfish (Janßen et al. 2013). The high 
abundance of blue mussels also affects other elements of food webs, and creates local increases in 
biodiversity. This is caused by their feed-backs such as increased ingestion of copepods and 
microplankton, egestion of fecal pellets as well as excretion of ammonium (Maar et al. 2009). 
The change in communities originating from the introduction of hard substrate depends on the original 
substrate. The type of material which is used affects the benthic community and succession patterns. 
An experiment assessing the recruitment and early succession of the benthic communities as well as 
the fish assemblages on steel and concrete pilings showed a difference between the most abundant 
epibenthic species and algae coverage of the steel and concrete pilings (Andersson et al. 2010). The 
final community composition depends on both the biodiversity in the area where the OWF is 
implemented, but also on the material of the construction (Petersen & Malm 2006). 
Yet, the hard substrates can facilitate the introduction of invasive species in certain areas due to the 
creation of stepping stones (Langhamer & Olivia 2012; Bulleri & Airoldi 2005; Page et al. 2008). Several 
introduced subtidal and intertidal species have been detected on the turbines of OWFs in the Belgian 
part of the North Sea (Kerckhof et al. 2016). OWFs are not expected to increase facilitation of subtidal 
invasive species in case large amounts of subtidal hard substrates are already present, for example in 
the North Sea. However, the risk of spreading of intertidal species will increase significantly when 
intertidal habitats are rare, which is the case in offshore areas (Kerckhof et al. 2016). 
 
1.2.5 
Fisheries exclusion 
Fisheries are not always restricted inside OWFs, but it might be restricted as safety measure. No 
international guidelines are currently implemented regarding safety of shipping and OWFs  (MSFD 
2008). Though fisheries are not officially restricted, fishermen have shown to be worried of losing gear 
due to obstacles in the seabed (e.g. scour protection, rock matressing to protect cables) or to the risk 
of vessel breakdown which could result in collision with turbines. Furthermore, the maintenance work 
of OWFs causes disruption due to closing areas from fishing. There are few examples of fisheries co-
existing within OWFs in the United Kingdom, with a small number of fishermen operating demersal 
trawling in cable-free corridors in between turbines (Gray et al. 2016). Potential co-existence of the 
renewable sector and the fisheries sector has been addressed, and indicated an inevitable 
displacement of fishing effort related to the expansion of renewable energy (de Groot et al. 2014). 
A reduction of fishing with towed gears would decrease disturbance of benthic communities and 
improve the recovery of previously disturbed communities (Wilhelmsson & Langhamer 2014; 
Lindeboom et al. 2011; van Deurs et al. 2011). Even though positive feed-backs on benthos can be 
expected, it has been shown to be difficult to disentangle these effects from other causes of increases 
in abundance such as habitat gain or changes in species composition. Macrobenthic communities have 
shown to differ significantly between locations far from and close to an OWF in the Belgian part of the 
North Sea. Higher abundances and diversity have been detected far from the OWF, with communities 
dominated by Bathyporeia elegans and Spiophanes Bombyx (Reubens et al. 2016). Studies addressing 
the effect of artificial reefs on fishing yield demonstrated potentially positive effects on the yield of 
target species at artificial reefs compared to surrounding reefs, as well as similar or greater catches at 
an artificial reef compared to surrounding fishing grounds. Additionally, restriction of certain types of 
fishing gear or through the implementation of no-take zones might result in spillover effects to the 
surrounding fishing grounds. On the other hand, allowing fisheries inside an OWF may cause increased 
mortality rates if the increase in aggregation of target species around the turbines lead to increased 
catches (Pickering & Whitmarsh 1997; Reubens et al. 2014; Polovina 1989).

As mobile commercial fishing gear such as trawls and dredges are already limited within OWFs, wind 
farms might act de-facto as a marine protected area (MPA). Hence it becomes a simple practical step 
towards full MPA designation. The Convention for the Protection of the Marine Environment of the 
North-East Atlantic (the OSPAR Convention) defined an MPA as “An area within the (OSPAR) maritime 
area for which protective, conservation, restorative or precautionary measures, consistent with 
international law have been instituted for the purpose of protecting and conserving species, habitats, 
ecosystems or ecological processes of the marine environment” (OSPAR 2006).   
Several studies have suggested the value of an OWF and it’s potential to act as an MPA. Fisheries are 
already (partly) excluded, there are fish aggregating devices and artificial reefs already present. This 
value is however hypothetical, and based on arguments and findings of MPA and artificial reef studies. 
Until now only limited evidence is available of OWFs that measure up to the requirements of an MPA. 
Moreover, this value is questioned assuming that the preservation of naturally occurring habitats is 
the main objective of the implementation of an MPA. 
Restriction of fisheries within the MSP might result in short-term higher costs for fisheries due to the 
restriction of fisheries activity (EC 2013). In the long term, however, spatial planning could generate 
an economic value for fisheries. Evaluation of MSP should identify the most energy-efficient and 
economical fishing areas, while evaluating the use of these sites from a social, bio-economic and 
governance perspective in order to avoid conflicting and cross-sector marine management (Jennings 
& Lee 2012). Avoidance of suboptimal strategies is required both from a societal and fisheries 
perspective, since fisheries are important components in coastal communities or with regard to food 
security of European countries (Lopes & Begossi 2011; Fabinyi et al. 2015; Crilly & Esteban 2013; Garcia 
& Rosenberg 2010). As fisheries are dependent on habitat and the fisheries resources are not equally 
distributed over space, spatial restrictions in an MSP context may lead to the closure of preferred 
fishing grounds or highly productive marine areas (Nielsen et al. 2014). If the restrictions are not placed 
according to their purpose, it could have severe consequences for fisheries and stocks (Weeratunge et 
al. 2014). When designing spatial constraints for fisheries, managers should be cautious to maintain 
viable fisheries. Strong adverse effects might occur unexpectedly if the closures are not specifically 
designed for the regulation of fisheries. Decisions of fishermen could change considerably after closure 
of an area, including stopping fishing, making the response of fishermen difficult to predict. Modelling 
approaches based on quantitative bioeconomic analyses, taking into account individual vessel 
decisions, can be used to assess the impact of new spatial plans on fisheries (Bastardie et al. 2014). 
There is a need for more knowledge of the environmental and economic effects of the OWFs in order 
to assess the potential to be managed as MPAs, and to identify possible knowledge gaps that may lead 
to suboptimal management decisions with regard to MSP. Within this objective knowledge of the 
effects on individual and community level is essential, more specifically from the perspective of 
ecologically motivated protection of sites  driven by the EU Marine Strategy Framework Directive 2008 
(MSFD) and the EC Habitats Directive 1992  (MSFD 2008; Council Directive 1992). 
Knowledge on this subject might be translated into other planning and management actions within 
the framework of the Common Fisheries Policy (CFP) and the Integrated Coastal Zone Management 
(ICMZ) recommendation 2002 (ICZM 2002). Potentially a management clash will occur between this 
environmental legislation and the targets that have been set regarding energy renewables, aiming for 
a 20% reduction of greenhouse gasses as well as 20% of the electricity production by renewable 
sources by 2020. These renewable sources cover primarily  wind and wave power – both having to be 
built in the marine environment with their related impacts.

1.3 LAGRANGIAN APPROACHES FOR MARINE CONNECTIVITY 
Currently strategies to select MPAs are based on biodiversity, using selection criteria such as species 
richness, endemism, or the intention to protect certain types of habitat. The fundamental aim of MPA 
design, however, is to maintain population persistence.  
Demographic connectivity is an essential process which affects the persistence and dynamics of 
spatially structured populations. A population can be defined as a group of individuals of the same 
species, inhabiting the same area. Traditionally populations are viewed as isolates, with the risk of 
extinction as a function of size. Small populations are more vulnerable to predation, parasitism, 
demographic and environmental stochasticity and inbreeding depression. Many populations are 
however connected through dispersal, and therefore part of larger networks of populations: 
metapopulations. Hence, connectivity can be defined as the exchange of individuals among and within 
marine populations. 
The classical metapopulation is a network of populations with similar dynamics, buffered against 
extinction due to dispersal i.e. by the arrival of immigrants. However, it is realistic to assume that there 
are differences in habitat quality of the different patches occupied by populations. In source patches 
resources are abundant, and more offspring is produced than necessary for the replacement of the 
patch – and the surplus offspring disperses to other patches. On the other hand, in sink patches 
resources are scarce. Populations in these patches are maintained because of the immigration of 
individuals from other patches. Source and sink populations should be discriminated, especially as 
sinks might contain higher abundances compared to sources due to immigration rates higher than the 
local mortality rate. In reality, populations fall in a continuum from sources to sinks, dependent on the 
local rates of immigration, emigration, birth and death (Figure 4). A population can only persist when 
births + immigration ≥ deaths + emigration, hence the persistence of a population is fundamentally 
linked to the degree of larval exchange and recruitment between populations and subpopulations – 
and therefore to connectivity. The quantification of this connectivity is therefore essential in the design 
of optimal networks of protected areas. 
 
Figure 4. The compensation axis with immigration (I), emigration (E), births (B) and deaths (D), in per capita rates (Thomas & 
Kunin 1999). 
The level at which a population is receiving individuals from other populations or is self-recruiting is an 
important determining factor governing population dynamics. The characteristics and life traits of the 
individuals settling in a new population might affect the existing individuals and even the future 
population in terms of survival and reproduction. On longer timescales the likelihood of the extinction 
of subpopulations will be affected by the degree of exchange among them.

Populations might range from fully open (i.e. large immigration and emigration rates) to entirely closed 
(i.e. no immigration or emigration). In order to investigate to what extent a marine population is open 
or closed, this might be conceptually viewed as the analysis of connectivity matrices (Cowen & 
Sponaugle 2009). A connectivity matrix indicates the degree to which subpopulations or nodes are 
connected to each other through dispersal, in this case by the dispersal of larvae. The nodes are 
indicated on the horizontal and vertical axis of the matrix. When a population is fully open, all 
subpopulations contribute equal numbers of particles to each other, which could be expected in case 
all particles were equally mixed and settled randomly. On the other hand, in a completely closed 
situation all particles would settle back in the area where they were released, i.e. 100% self-
recruitment. This would be the case if all populations or subpopulations were completely isolated.  
Many marine species, including flatfish, are characterized by small spores and larvae drifting over 
potentially long distances and sedentary or little moving adults (Thorson 1950). This dispersal over 
large spatial scales might lead to open populations with recruitment at least partially decoupled from 
the reproduction of local adult populations (Caley et al. 1996; Cowen 2000). Insights on population 
structure and connectivity could be obtained from empirical methods such as genetic markers or 
micro-chemical fingerprinting of fish otoliths, however only at limited spatial and temporal scale 
(Swearer 1999; Galindo et al. 2006). The quantification of the dispersal and connectivity of larvae is 
difficult because of their small size and the complex and vast fluid environment, leading to very low 
concentrations of propagules. Additionally differences in post-settlement survival increase the 
difficulty in measuring connectivity. The empirical results can therefore be complemented by 
connectivity estimates originating from biophysical models including ocean circulation and larval 
transport, such as for example individual-based models (IBMs) (Roberts 1997; Cowen & Sponaugle 
2009). These models allow for variation on individual scale due to larval reactions (e.g. stage duration 
or mortality) to the modeled environmental parameters (e.g. temperature) provided by physical 
models. Larval dispersal is affected by both biology (e.g. growth, development, offspring production 
and survival) and by physical processes (e.g. advection and diffusion). Additionally interactions occur 
between the physical properties of the environment and certain larval traits such as vertical migrating 
behavior. The drivers of the flow processes such as temperature, salinity and wind might however also 
affect nutrient availability, behavioral responses and physiological rates. Thus, connectivity patterns 
that have been assessed with these biological interactions included might predict quite different 
patterns of dispersal compared to patterns based on only physics (Savina et al. 2010). 
When using these IBMs (or Lagrangian transport models) with regional hydrodynamical models, 
spatially explicit connectivity matrices can be obtained allowing for the analysis of connectivity and 
dispersal. The variations in behavioral traits will affect the outcomes of the Lagrangian transport 
models.

1.4 FLATFISH IN THE NORTH SEA – A CASE STUDY 
In this study the output is analyzed of Lagrangian simulations of the dispersal of the early life stages of 
six flatfish species: brill (Scophthalmus rhombus), dab (Limanda limanda), flounder (Platichthys flesus), 
European plaice (Pleuronectes platessa),  sole (Solea solea) and turbot (Scophthalmus maximus). These 
species show differences in life traits regarding reproductive strategy (i.e. spawning ground and 
spawning period), physiology (i.e. stage duration and subsequent pelagic larval duration) and behavior 
(vertical migration). The study area covers a part of the North Sea, a temperate shelf sea with distinct 
characteristics (see Text box II). 
Plaice and sole are the main flatfish fisheries in the North Sea. Large effort reductions (of 
approximately 60%) of the beam- and otter-trawl fisheries took place in the years 2000-2012, with 
positive effects on the flatfish stocks (ICES 2016). The sole spawning stock biomass (SSB) has been 
increasing since 2012 due to a decrease in fishing mortality from 1997. The recruitment of sole has 
however been fluctuating since the early 1990s (ICES 2016). The plaice stock covering the North Sea 
and the Skagerrak increased over the past ten years, reaching a record high during the past five years. 
Plaice and sole are mainly targeted by demersal trawl fisheries whereas turbot, brill and flounder are 
mainly by-catch in mixed fisheries - dab is considered of low value and mainly discarded. Flounder, 
dab, turbot and brill act mainly as bycatch from the demersal fisheries for sole and plaice in the North 
Sea.  
Plaice, sole, flounder and dab are members of the Pleuronectidae family, the right-eyed flounders. Brill 
and turbot both belong to the Scophthalmidae family, a group of left-eyed flatfish. Plaice and  sole are 
the most important commercial flatfish species in European waters (ICES 2016).  For all species, 
spawning occurs offshore, from where the eggs and larvae are transported towards the nurseries in 
coastal areas. Nurseries are located in the inshore and coastal waters of the North Sea, with the 
Wadden Sea as most important nursery area (Kuipers 1977). As the juveniles grow older, they return 
back to deeper waters (Rijnsdorp et al. 1985; Bergman et al. 1989).

Text box II: The North Sea 
The North Sea is a temperate coastal shelf area with seasonal stratification occurring in the north, 
and a permanently thermally mixed water column in the south and the east. It can be defined as 
the region between the British Isles and the mainland of northwestern Europe. The North Sea is 
connected to the North of the Atlantic Ocean in between the Orkney and the Shetland Islands, 
and between the Shetland Islands and Norway. In the southwest it is connected to the Atlantic 
Ocean through the strait of Dover and the English Channel. In the East the North Sea is connected 
to the Baltic Sea through the Skagerrak and Kattegat. The northern part of the North Sea has a 
deep channel in the east and is strongly affected by inflow from the Atlantic Ocean. The southern 
part of the North Sea, the area assessed in this study, is influenced by strong river inputs and 
mixing.  
The North Sea is a highly productive area because of its shallowness, which allows the mixing of 
nutrients into the water column all year long. This causes high primary production, with positive 
feedbacks on the higher trophic levels – leading to high biodiversity and large abundances of fish. 
The North Sea fisheries are among others targeting cod, haddock, mackerel, herring, plaice and 
sole.

As the life cycle of these flatfish consists of a pelagic phase, which after metamorphosis is followed by 
a benthic phase, it is possible to use IBM modelling to assess dispersal and settlement in the early life 
stages. From the moment metamorphosis is reached and the larvae settle, they stay in the nursery 
areas where the juveniles move using selective tidal transport, to the adjacent shallow nursery areas 
(Rijnsdorp et al. 1985; Bergman et al. 1989; Ramzi et al. 2001). This behavior allows for simplified 
simulations while still making realistic assumptions with regard to connectivity. Additionally, it 
suggests that it is suitable to only know the nursery sector instead of the exact final position for the 
estimations of larval supply. 
The three ICES divisions that cover the study area (Central North Sea (ICES division IVb), Southern North 
Sea (ICES division IVc), Eastern English Channel (ICES division VIId)) are regarded as separate 
populations in this analysis (Figure 6, left). Connectivity between the spawning grounds located in the 
ICES divisions and the corresponding nurseries is assessed.  
The nursery areas are defined based on bathymetry and substrate characteristics. These nurseries are 
similar for all species, and have been defined as coastal areas with a maximum depth of 20 m, and 
substrate types ranging from mud to sand and from sand to mud (4-500 μm), according to the Folk 
classification (Kaskela et al. 2010). The nursery areas have been divided into seven areas: according to 
the national boundaries  (France, Belgium, The Netherlands, the German Bight) and three nurseries 
are geographically separated in the United Kingdom (Norfolk, the Thames estuary and Rye). The 
nursery area in the German Bight also covers part of the Danish coast. (Figure 6, right). Nurseries are 
also assembled with regard to their position within a management square: Norfolk and the German 
Bight are located in the Central North Sea, Belgium, the Netherlands and the Thames estuary are 
located in the Southern North Sea and France and the Rye are located in the Eastern English Channel.

In a first stage, a state-of-the-art analysis of connectivity of the six flatfish species in the North Sea has 
been performed. This analysis has been performed in order to obtain a better understanding of the 
spatio-temporal dynamics and how these differ among species. This allows for a better understanding 
of the influence of larval traits on the dispersal behavior, which provides useful knowledge with regard 
to the interpretation of the results when assessing the effects of wind farms. 
This is followed by an analysis of the proportion of larvae potentially affected by OWFs. Finally, two 
hypothetical scenarios investigating the effects of fisheries exclusion from wind farms have been 
assessed. In these scenarios, an increase in fecundity originating from wind farms as a result from 
fisheries restriction is hypothesized. Within this framework, an optimal network of MPAs has been 
calculated and compared to the current wind farm locations. 
Overall, this study is aiming to find answers to the following questions: 
I. 
How do the assessed species differ in their connectivity and dispersal patterns? 
II. 
What proportion of settlers is affected by OWFs? 
III. 
What would be the consequences on the survival probability and number of settlers of closing 
all wind farms to fisheries, assuming that closure of a wind farm would lead to an increase in 
fecundity? 
IV. 
How would this change the currently observed patterns in survival and connectivity between 
spawning and nursery grounds in the North Sea? 
These questions have been defined based on the general research question of this study: 
Does the presence of wind farms at present and in the future increase the resilience of flatfish larvae in 
the North Sea?  
2 METHODS 
2.1 NUMERICAL MODEL  
The fish larval transport model used results from the coupling between a 3D hydrodynamical model 
and an Individual-Based Model (IBM). The runs cover the period from 1997 to 2006. The simulations 
have been performed by coupling a 3D hydrodynamic model to a particle tracking model.  
2.1.1 
Hydrodynamic model 
The 3D hydrodynamic model NOS (North Sea), which is based on the COHERENS model (Luyten et al. 
1999). This model covers the area between 48.5° N and 57° N in latitude (with a resolution of 2,5’) and 
the area between 4° W  and  9°E in longitude (with a resolution of 5’) in a 157 by 205 horizontal grid – 
corresponding to the Eastern English Channel and the southern and central part of the North Sea. 
There are 20 σ-coordinate vertical layers. Boundaries are formed by the northern and western open 
boundaries (at 4° W  and 57° N) and by 14 rivers. For these rivers daily river discharges have been 
included based on their long-term average discharge over the period 1990-2006. The geographic 
implementation of the model as well as the 14 selected rivers are shown in figure 7. The hydrodynamic 
model is forced by weekly sea surface temperature (SST) data (Bundesamt für Seeschifffahrt und 
Hydrographie (Loewe 2003), on a 20 x 20 km grid and according to the grid resolution interpolated 
over space and time. The model is forced by 6 hourly wind and atmospheric pressure fields provided 
by the UK Meteorological Office in a spatially variable grid according to the available data. The 
resolutions of this spatially variable grid might range from 1.25° to 5° in longitude and 1.25° and 5° in 
latitude. Details about the model implementation can be found in (Savina et al. 2010) and (Lacroix et 
al. 2013).

2.1.2.2 Egg number 
Egg numbers have been calculated in multiple steps. The daily egg production has been estimated 
based on an egg distribution map. Subsequently the given numbers of eggs have been divided into 
multiple (3 for turbot; 4 for flounder, plaice, brill and dab; 6 for sole),  arbitrarily chosen classes. By 
multiplying the daily egg production by the duration of the entire spawning period (i.e. 100 days) for 
each grid cell the total number of eggs spawned during the entire spawning period can be calculated. 
The use of super-individuals allows to consider realistic egg numbers with a reduced number of 
particles. This weight differs between species: for sole each particle represents 1.9×106 eggs, for plaice 
4,8×106 eggs, for turbot 1.7×106 eggs, for brill 2.2×106 eggs, for flounder 1.3×106 eggs and for dab 
7.4×106 eggs. 
 
 
2.1.2.3 Spawning period 
The spawning period for sole has been defined based on water temperature. Sole has a temperature 
range for spawning between 7 °C and 12 °C (ICES 2005). The peak spawning date is defined as the first 
day when a temperature 10 °C is reached. For each grid cell this is based on the weekly SST, and 
subsequently averaged for each spawning ground (Lacroix et al. 2013 and ref therein). The spawning 
peak of flounder is based on the mean annual temperature, with higher temperatures leading to an 
earlier peak. For plaice and dab the spawning peak is based on latitude – an earlier spawning period 
has been shown for sole at lower latitudes in the North Sea (Rijnsdorp 1994). Probably this is due to a 
latitudinal temperature gradient. Due to a lack of information the spawning period for brill is the same 
for all years. 
A period of 100 days is centered around the peak spawning date, with spawning spread following a 
normal distribution (see figure 10 for the spawning period and spawning peak for each species). 
Further details on stage duration, mortality and vertical migrating behavior can be found in Appendix 
I.

2.2 SCENARIOS AND PARAMETRIZATION 
To assess the effect of the restriction of fisheries in wind farms on the dispersal and settlement success 
of flatfish larvae, two scenarios have been designed (Scenario 1 and Scenario 2).  
The regulation of fisheries activity within an offshore wind farm (OWF) in the North Sea is country 
dependent. Presently, no international guidelines are implemented regarding safety of shipping and 
OWFs, since the MSFD, the directive guiding this aspect of policy, is implemented on a national level 
(MSFD 2008). Within the North Sea, fisheries are restricted inside OWFs in Germany, Belgium and the 
Netherlands, and there is a safety zone of 500 m around the turbines. In Denmark and the United 
Kingdom the accessibility of wind farms are the responsibility of fishermen – fishing is allowed however 
with occasional restrictions. Often a minimum zone around the wind turbines of 50m is applied 
(Verhaeghe et al. 2011).  
To build the scenarios we however consider all wind farm areas restricted. The two scenarios differ 
with regard to the extension of the area that is considered impacted by OWF: Scenario 1 considers the 
present distribution of operational OWFs and Scenario 2 includes also the future development plans 
(Figure 11). Both scenarios have been compared to the baseline scenario, which is based on the original 
model output (Baseline).

3 RESULTS 
In this chapter firstly a state-of-the-art analysis of connectivity of the six flatfish species in the North 
Sea will be presented. This is followed by an analysis of the proportion of larvae potentially affected 
by OWFs. Lastly, two hypothetical scenarios investigating the effects of fisheries exclusion from wind 
farms have been assessed. In these scenarios, an increase in fecundity originating from wind farms is 
hypothesized as a result from fisheries restriction. Within this framework, an optimal network of 
marine protected areas is calculated and compared to the current wind farm locations.

Connectivity in the North Sea 
When assessing the patterns of exchange in the North Sea between the three areas (Figure 12), a

Species with a long pelagic larval duration (plaice, dab and flounder) show high exchange rates 
between the three different areas in the North Sea (as defined in section 1.4). For plaice and flounder, 
nurseries in the southern North Sea (Belgium, the Netherlands and the Thames estuary) receive 
individuals from the English Channel and the nurseries in the Central North Sea (Norfolk and the 
German Bight) receive inflow from the Southern North Sea, indicating a northward flow. For dab, the 
species characterized by the longest pelagic larval duration, nurseries in the Central North Sea receive 
individuals from the English Channel. Sole, brill and turbot, species with short larval duration, show 
lower exchange levels between areas. Turbot shows significant connection between the Central North 
Sea and the Southern North Sea. Brill is the species showing strongest local retention; it should 
however be noted that no spawning occurs in the EEC. The central part of the North Sea is mostly 
isolated for sole, there is low connectivity between the EEC and the Southern North Sea.

3.2 WIND FARMS AND THEIR EFFECTS ON LARVAL CONNECTIVITY 
3.2.1 
The contribution of wind farms to larval arrival 
Connectivity matrices between wind farms and nursery areas showed low connectivity for all species, 
on average 2% for plaice, 3% for sole and dab, 7% for flounder, 8% for turbot and 12% for brill  (Figure 
14). There is however variability in connectivity among years. The nursery areas that are most 
consistently connected to all operational and planned OWFs are the Netherlands and the German 
Bight, where OWFs are located close to the nurseries. With regard to differences among species, it 
should be noted that for brill the Thames estuary is well connected to spawning areas covered by wind

4 DISCUSSION 
In this study, the output of simulations of larval supply in coastal and estuarine nurseries for six species 
(plaice, dab, sole, turbot, brill and flounder) during a ten-year period (1997-2006) has been analyzed 
with regard to connectivity and the effect of OWFs.  
Overall two species groups have been defined; a summer spawning group with high mean encountered 
temperature, short pelagic larval duration and low dispersal, and a winter spawning group with low 
mean encountered temperature, long pelagic duration and high dispersal. A relatively short larval 
stage duration of e.g. sole or turbot results in moderate to low connectivity, and hence low 
connectivity exchange of larvae between populations. Inversely, relatively long larval stage duration 
of e.g. plaice or dab results in high connectivity, and high exchange of larvae between populations. 
The species assessed are differently affected by OWFs. Species with long pelagic larval duration and a 
large spawning area (place and dab) show little connection between OWFs and nurseries. Species with 
an intermediate size of the spawning area and OWFs located in sites with high spawning densities (brill 
and turbot), have relatively the highest proportion of inflow originating from OWFs. Coastal spawning 
species such as sole, or species with little overlap between the spawning area and OWFs (flounder), 
also receive little inflow in nursery grounds of areas covered by OWFs. The number of settlers 
originating from OWFs covers only a small percentage compared to the total yearly settlement for all 
species.  
Exclusion of fisheries from wind farms might lead to an increase up to 49% in settlement in certain 
nurseries (German Bight for dab). However, even though the total settlement increases, the survival 
probability areas might decrease due to an increased loss of individuals due to settlement in unsuitable 
areas. When defining optimal networks of MPAs, the sites are, regardless of the species characteristics, 
located in coastal areas close to nurseries. 
4.1 MODELLING OF FLATFISH DISPERSAL IN THE NORTH SEA 
The use of Lagrangian transport models contributes to the understanding of recruitment processes in 
marine populations, especially with regard to retention and transport. Eggs and larvae have different 
fates as they follow different trajectories in space. Hence, when assessing hypotheses related to 
transport between spawning and nursery grounds individual-based approaches are required. This 
==PAGE:47==
Influence of marine infrastructures on the population connectivity of flatfish in the North Sea 
37 
 
modelling approach inherently accounts for variation originating from local interactions between 
individuals and their resources, which is a key driver mechanism in ecology. The reliability of the 
outcomes of the model depends on the accuracy of the hydrodynamic model (Friedrichs et al. 2006) 
and the tracking algorithms. With regard to the particle-tracking, it is required to track sufficient 
particles in order to have their average ensemble resembling the true distributions of the transport 
process. Also, the detection of rare occurrences might prove to be difficult (Brickman & Smith 2002). 
The hydrodynamic model should resolve physical processes in a scale allowing for the expression of 
physical and biological processes. Biological processes will probably occur on scales smaller than the 
scale of the physical model (Helbig & Pepin 2002). 
This model excludes several sources in variability in larval survival related to trophic changes such as 
prey abundance and predation (Peck & Hufnagl 2012). Growth, which is in reality a function of the 
availability of food, is in this model based on the temperature field predicted by the hydrodynamic 
model. Temperature is considered a factor of high importance affecting vital rates of marine fish in 
early life-history, growth rates in particular. These growth rates can subsequently influence pelagic 
larval duration, the duration of exposure to size-dependent predation and the size of metamorphosis 
into juveniles (Miller et al. 1988; Bailey & Houde 1989; O’Connor et al. 2007). Furthermore, 
temperature can cause improved sensory and swimming performance, and therefore distribution 
patterns (Hewitt 1981).  
Even though Lagrangian transport models are an approximation of reality and several sources of 
variability are excluded, they are valuable to obtain insights in population structure and connectivity. 
Insights on these processes could be obtained from empirical methods such as genetic markers or 
micro-chemical fingerprinting of fish otoliths, however only at limited spatial and temporal scale 
(Swearer 1999; Galindo et al. 2006). The quantification of the dispersal and connectivity of larvae is 
difficult due to their small size and to the complex and vast fluid environment, leading to very low 
concentrations of propagules. Additionally differences in post-settlement survival increase the 
difficulty in measuring connectivity. The empirical results can therefore be complemented by 
connectivity estimates originating from Lagrangian transport models (Roberts 1997; Cowen & 
Sponaugle 2009). 
The use of this type of modelling with regard to flatfish is useful as their life cycle consists of a pelagic 
phase which ends when metamorphosis occurs, and is followed by a benthic phase. The main migration 
and dispersal of flatfish therefore occurs during the planktonic phase. After metamorphosis juveniles 
move, using selective tidal transport, to the adjacent shallow nursery areas (Rijnsdorp et al. 1985; 
Bergman et al. 1989; Ramzi et al. 2001). From the moment metamorphosis is reached and the larvae 
settle, they stay in the nursery areas.  
In this analysis two groups have been distinguished: a summer spawning group (sole, brill and turbot) 
with high mean encountered temperature, and a winter spawning group (plaice, dab and flounder) 
with low mean encountered temperatures. This difference is also reflected in the mean pelagic larval 
duration (PLD), with longer mean PLD for species spawning in winter due to the relationship between 
stage duration 𝐷 and encountered temperature 𝑇 (𝐷= 𝛼𝑇−𝛽). 
These observations can be explained by the different spawning characteristics of each species: species 
spawning early in the year encounter lower temperatures than species spawning in summer. 
Abundance of prey (e.g. dinoflagellates, copepods, tintinnids among others (Last 1978)) shows 
seasonality in temperate regions such as the North Sea (Colebrook 1980). A phytoplankton bloom 
occurs in spring due to increased light intensity, followed by a bloom of zooplankton due to increased 
food availability. The occurrence of these blooms show that individuals spawned early in the year have 
==PAGE:48==
Influence of marine infrastructures on the population connectivity of flatfish in the North Sea 
38 
 
less prey available. Additionally, development is slower due to lower metabolic rates in cooler 
temperatures – which is reflected in the stage duration and PLD (O’Connor et al. 2007).  
For coastal marine populations, longer duration of the planktonic larval stages allows for larger 
dispersal distances (Pringle et al. 2014). A relatively short larval stage duration of e.g. sole or turbot 
results in moderate to low connectivity, and hence low connectivity exchange of larvae between 
populations. Inversely, relatively long larval stage duration of e.g. plaice or dab results in high 
connectivity, and high connectivity exchange of larvae between populations. It is generally accepted 
that water from the Atlantic flows through the English Channel into the North Sea (Bailly du Bois & 
Dumas 2005), explaining the occurrence of particles spawned from the EEC settling in the Central North 
Sea, but not the opposite. 
Larval mortality has suggested to act more as a scaling factor, rather than a source of variability as 
variability in larval supply was not sensitive to changes in larval mortality (Rochette et al. 2012). This 
implicates that the relative variability in survival and the number of settling larvae can be used as an 
indicator for spatial patterns and the influence of hydrodynamics on larval recruitment success. 
The results on interannual variability in survival probability have indicated the highest mean probability 
of survival occurring in the Central North Sea for brill, sole and turbot, however also with highest 
variation (section 3.1.3). The highest mean survival probability of dab and flounder was observed in 
the Southern North Sea, but also with highest variation. Plaice is the only species showing highest 
mean survival probability in the EEC. These results can be explained with regard to the different 
configuration of spawning and nursery grounds. In the Central North Sea, for plaice, dab and flounder 
the spawning grounds are located offshore, and eggs and larvae have to be advected towards the 
coastal nursery grounds with variable chance of success. Spawning in the Southern North Sea and in 
the Eastern English Channel occurs much closer to the coastal nursery areas. Hence, when spawning 
in the English Channel relatively low numbers of larvae are lost due to metamorphosis outside a 
nursery area as spawning occurs already in coastal and shallow spawning grounds. These differences 
among populations with regard to distance and level of connection have also been shown in other 
studies for plaice and sole in the North Sea and winter flounder in the northwest Atlantic (Savina et al. 
2010; Bolle et al. 2009; Buckley et al. 2008). Other studies also found low exchange rates for sole 
between populations in larger spatial and temporal scale (Savina et al. 2016).  
 
4.2 WIND FARMS AND THEIR EFFECTS ON LARVAL CONNECTIVITY 
 
Wind farms affect a significant part of settlers in all species, ranging between 2% for plaice and 12% 
for brill. The definition of this percentage is important to take into account, as these individuals are 
subjected to different biological, environmental and hydrographical conditions. Therefore, they can be 
considered as an additional source in uncertainty for recruitment predictions. 
Brill and turbot show compared to the other species the highest proportion of settlers originating from 
wind farms (7.4% for brill and 6.6% for turbot respectively). Flounder shows the lowest number of 
settlers originating from wind farms (1.9%). Both sole and dab showed 2.1% inflow from wind farms, 
for plaice the inflow percentage is 2.2%. The high percentages for brill and flounder can be explained 
by the distance of the wind farms from the nurseries, and on the spawning behavior of the species. 
Species with high spawning densities in areas covered by wind farms close to coastal nurseries (e.g. 
brill and the Thames estuary, turbot and Norfolk) receive large inflow from OWFs. Plaice and dab, 
species with a large spawning in a large area, show low connectivity as the area covered by wind farms 
==PAGE:49==
Influence of marine infrastructures on the population connectivity of flatfish in the North Sea 
39 
 
is relatively small compared to the total spawning area. Sole spawns very close to the coast, and the 
spawning grounds show little overlap with OWFs. Even though for species such as brill and sole large 
interannual variability in the number of settlers can be observed, the proportion of settlers originating 
from OWFs remains relatively constant. 
There are however interannual variations shown in the connectivity between OWFs and nurseries, 
which can be very important in certain years (e.g. flounder in the Belgian nursery with an inflow of 
63.4% in 2001). This suggests that the hydrodynamic regime plays an important role in the connectivity 
between OWFs and nurseries, resulting in certain years with high inflow originating from OWFs (e.g. 
flounder and the Belgian nursery area). Overall, the level of connection between nursery areas and 
OWFs remains below 50%, with the connection between OWFs and the Belgian nursery from flounder 
as an exception. 
The low overall interannual variability in the number of settlers and the high proportions of inflow 
from OWFs in nurseries closely located to OWFs (e.g. The Netherlands, Thames estuary) might indicate 
that the main OWFs contributing larval flatfish are located in proximity of the nursery grounds.  
4.3 THE DEFINITION OF SCENARIOS 
The investigation of the scenarios has proved to be useful for the understanding of the processes that 
might occur in the future of the North Sea. Due to the restriction in fisheries increases in settlement in 
up to 49% have been shown, indicating that fisheries closure can have a highly significant effect on the 
total settlement of flatfish larvae. The increase in settlement occurred for all species in nurseries 
located closely to an area with high spawning density covered by OWFs. This was the case for brill, 
with in Scenario 1 a site with high spawning density covered by OWFs in the Thames estuary, leading 
to an increase in settlement of 28%, and similarly in Scenario 2 an increase of 29% was shown in the 
Netherlands. Turbot in Scenario 2 showed overlap between a site with high spawning density and high 
coverage of OWFs, leading to an increase in settlement in Norfolk and the Thames estuary of 30% and 
31% respectively. The most remarkable increase in settlement was however shown for dab, with 
settlement increasing 49% in Norfolk and 39% in the German Bight in Scenario 2. This is surprising, as 
a low proportion of inflow originating from OWFs was indicated for these nurseries. However, sites 
with high spawning densities are located close to these nurseries, and together with numerous OWFs 
in the same areas this leads to a very strong increase in settlement. 
Nonetheless, the increase in settlement does not necessarily coincide with an increase in the 
probability of survival; fisheries restriction also leads to increased larval loss due to settlement in 
unsuitable areas. In Scenario 2 the coverage of OWFs increases, resulting for all species in increased 
larval loss. This also implicates that the highest net positive effect, both with regard to total settlement 
and settlement success, is originating from OWFs located in coastal areas, close to nurseries and 
coinciding with areas of high spawning density. 
However, the scenarios proposed here are a simplistic approach to the effect of OWFs and several 
aspects are disregarded. Due to possible changes in the abundance of predators on adult flatfish such 
as harbor porpoise, or by shifts in food availability due to higher abundance of filter feeders in OWFs 
(see also section 1.2) predation and prey availability would also be affected by OWF implementation. 
A high abundance of filter feeders can cause changes in the phytoplankton community, resulting in 
shifts in zooplankton abundance – which is a food source for larval flatfish (Norén et al. 1999). 
Furthermore, other density-dependent effects could occur in both spawning as nursery grounds, that 
are not accounted for. There are however strong indications of increased biomass due to the presence 
==PAGE:50==
Influence of marine infrastructures on the population connectivity of flatfish in the North Sea 
40 
 
of hard structures (Rouse et al. 2017). This method has proved to be valuable and can be applied easily 
to more realistic scenarios from the moment more specific knowledge is available.  
By applying a scaling factor for the release from wind farms possible changes in dispersal patterns due 
to changes in spawning density are not accounted for. Nonetheless all results in this analysis have been 
based on super-individuals, allowing for a minimum number of particles in the model while covering 
for all dispersal patterns. Due to the small percentage of increase it can be argued that this method is 
still appropriate.  
4.4 THE DESIGN OF OPTIMAL NETWORKS OF MARINE PROTECTED AREAS AND THE EFFECT OF 
OFFSHORE WIND FARMS 
Marine population connectivity and the implications for spatial management has been brought 
forward by (Roberts 1997). Ever since, the spatial connections among different populations have been 
recognized as an important aspect in the design of networks of MPAs (Halpern & Warner 2003; 
Crowder et al. 2000; Gaines et al. 2003). In theoretical design of MPA networks this role of connectivity 
through larval exchange has been addressed (Gaines et al. 2003) and efforts have been made to obtain 
empirical data on potential larval dispersal (Palumbi 2015; Kinlan & Gaines 2003). In some cases adult 
movement has been considered as well (Gaines et al. 2007). Clearly, one single solution is unlikely 
given the wide range of marine habitats types, species and oceanographic environments. 
A method to identify a network of MPAs taking into account both spawning grounds and nursery areas 
has been proposed by (Jacobi & Jonsson 2011). This method is in particular suitable for flatfish as 
nursery areas and spawning grounds are different, but closely located to each other. The selection of 
the sites is based on the effect of protecting a specific site on the long-term growth of the 
metapopulation. Therefore, OWFs coinciding with protected sites gives an indication of the 
vulnerability of the metapopulation to OWF effects. The locations of the most important sites (i.e. the 
sites with highest effect on the metapopulation) are different among species, but for all species located 
in coastal areas. Only for plaice the main site in the network is located in an area coinciding with a 
OWF. It is interesting to note that the protected sites defined in this analysis do not correspond with 
the sites with highest mean survival probability (see also section 3.1 in Appendix III). Applying this 
method to the two scenarios showed changes in location and ranking for all species. Brill, sole and 
turbot showed in different scenarios a shift of the most important site in the network towards an area 
covered by OWFs.  
Overall, the selected sites differ among species and in function of the scenarios tested. OWFs have 
therefore a significant effect on connectivity. Changes in connectivity patterns due to the 
implementation of OWFs can result in shifts of important nodes in flatfish larval dispersal.  
This method i.e. optimal networks based on the donor times recipient value, has shown to perform 
better compared to optimal networks based on other selection criteria such as networks defined by 
the sink values alone (Figueira 2009), or the definition of networks based on the sites with low leakage 
or high local retention (Jacobi & Jonsson 2011). Additionally, the method used here has been suggested 
to be particularly suitable to measure the value of an individual site when abundances are low – which 
is the case for exploited populations (Ovaskainen & Hanski 2003). 
Plaice and sole however show an increase in spawning stock biomass (SSB) in recent years due to 
strong reductions in fishing mortality. However, only for plaice abundances have grown to sizes larger 
than before. For sole, as well as for brill, turbot, dab and flounder, increases have been shown without 
reaching earlier abundances (Figure 19).

4.5 MANAGEMENT IMPLICATIONS 
  
This study has shown the effects of OWFs on the connectivity and settlement of larval flatfish. The 
simulations of larval drift have proved to be useful to investigate variability in recruitment to early life 
history. Flatfish produce large numbers of eggs with variable and low survival probabilities showing 
survival rates up to 1/1000 within the period between spawning and metamorphosis (Le Pape & 
Bonhommeau 2015; Juanes 2007). Large variations in recruitment are at least partially due to the 
sensitivity of larval survival to the environmental conditions and hydrodynamics, and might explain the 
current lack in stock-recruitment relationships observed in many exploited fish species (Houde 2008; 
Cury et al. 2014; Vert-pre et al. 2013). Especially for flatfish the predictive power of the stock-
recruitment relationships are very weak, and this has shown to be partly linked to variability in the 
larval stages (Archambault et al. 2014).  
In this study the variability in larval survival rates and settlement success has been analyzed for six 
flatfish species with a large range in traits (i.e. PLD, spawning ground, spawning period and vertical 
migration) and abundances. The results provide more insight in the spatio-temporal variability of 
settlement of these species, which improves the knowledge on the population structure and dynamics 
(Wilber et al. 2013; Buckley et al. 2008).  
Furthermore, the total settlement affected by OWFs has been defined to determine settlement areas 
that are affected by the presence of OWFs. Although the precise effect of OWFs on the different life 
stages of flatfish is to date unclear, hypothetical scenarios have been investigated to determine the 
effects of OWFs on flatfish dispersal. Significant changes in connectivity and settlement have been 
shown, but also decreased survival rates of OWFs located in offshore regions. The effects are however 
highly species and site-dependent.  
4.6 SUGGESTIONS FOR IMPROVEMENT AND FURTHER WORK 
In this study, patterns in spatio-temporal variability of connectivity and settlement have been 
observed, but the sources of the variability have not specifically been identified. Even though the time 
period covered (10 years) is sufficient to assess interannual variability of connectivity (Berglund et al. 
==PAGE:52==
Influence of marine infrastructures on the population connectivity of flatfish in the North Sea 
42 
 
2012), running the model over a longer timespan would allow for more robust analyses of the general 
patterns in connectivity and dispersal.  
In further studies, it could be interesting to relate the encountered temperature in more detail to the 
distance travelled, to gain more insight in the sources of interannual variability and settlement success. 
The distance travelled by the particles in the model is not discussed in this study, but analyses on this 
topic can give a better understanding of the dispersal patterns.  
Furthermore, it would be interesting to further explore relations between survival probability and the 
North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO). These analyses could contribute to the understanding of the influence 
of hydrodynamics on interannual variability. NAO is a large-scale proxy for climatic variability caused 
by the fluctuations in the difference between the atmospheric pressure at sea level between the Arctic 
and the subtropical Atlantic. This NAO is an indicator for the changes in direction and mean wind speed. 
These changes in patterns of the westerly winds causes variation in the transport of heat and moisture, 
inducing variability in ocean currents, sea ice cover and temperature and heat content (Lifland 2003). 
Years with positive NOA conditions are characterized by a strong inflow from the North Atlantic into 
the North Sea, and higher SST. Negative winter NAO conditions are related to a reduced inflow of North 
Atlantic inflow and therefore reduced circulation in the North Sea during winter. The conditions in the 
North Sea become more continentally influenced with colder SST in winter (Pingree 2005). This NAO 
synthesizes however winter conditions, and is therefore only a suited measure for species with a 
spawning peak close to winter, as the larval drift for these species occurs in late winter and early spring. 
For summer spawning species with larval drift occurring during late spring and early summer, NAO 
would not accurately represent the climatological and hydrographical conditions (Savina et al. 2016). 
Previous studies also showed an effect of river flow on recruitment success at juvenile stages, but this 
is most likely due to the quality of the coastal and estuarine nursery habitats due to riverine inflow of 
organic matter (Savina et al. 2016; Le Pape et al. 2003).  Larval growth rates and length increases with 
temperature, as well as larval condition. However, experimental studies also showed settlement at 
smaller sizes of larvae subjected to colder conditions (Laurel et al. 2014). Mortality between 0 and 1 
group sole is however inversely related to winter temperature (Rijnsdorp et al. 1992), which might be 
caused by a better match between larvae and their food sources or by a decrease in predator 
abundance among others. 
In this study, larval growth in temperature-dependent. This however implicates that variations caused 
by food-dependent growth are not accounted for. This could be included by specific modelling of the 
bioenergetic processes and consumption allowing to directly link the environment and prey field to 
predict growth – an important factor in many recruitment hypotheses related to larval feeding 
(MacKenzie et al. 1994). However, when using this approach scales of the hydrographic forecasts are 
required to be very small. Moreover the processes governing the encounter between planktonic prey 
and predators are still investigated (Visser & Kiørboe 2006; Fiksen & MacKenzie 2002).  
Temperature is quite conservative, making this parameter less sensitive to variations occurring on 
scales below the scale of the grid of the hydrodynamic model. When using this approach possible risks 
are related to the temperature-dependent growth model: the variables related to this have to be 
parametrized correctly and applied to populations for which is has been developed (Folkvord 2005). 
Seasonal and year-to-year variability in prey abundance would allow for a better estimation of larval 
mortality as it addresses the match-mismatch between predator and prey. This coupling has been done 
for sprat (Sprattus sprattus L.) in the North Sea (Daewel et al. 2008).  
Predator interaction and abundance is also not accounted for in this model. The distribution and 
behavior of potential predators might not be driven by hydrography, and subsequently difficult to 
==PAGE:53==

43 
 
model. It has however been done by (Rose et al. 1999). When including predation, information is 
necessary on how the possible predators might change their behavior according to the distribution of 
larvae and eggs. Moreover, inclusion of additional prey in the model might be considered.  
Food-related and predation-related processes might be a function of spatially generated differences, 
and subsequently asking for individual-level approaches. In this case, variability in growth originates 
from spatial differences in temperature. However, variability can originate from other processes such 
as inherent differences in growth capacity among larvae – resulting in different growth patterns in 
similar environmental conditions. When regarding size-dependent sources of mortality, this variability 
among individuals might have important consequences not accounted for (Miller et al. 1988; Rice et 
al. 1993).  
With regard to the scenarios analyzed, possible reallocation schemes of fisheries due to restriction in 
the OWFs should be investigated further. The displacement of fisheries due to restrictions in OWFs has 
been studied by (Bastardie et al. 2014) and has proved to be a valuable tool. An analysis of the fishing 
pressure in the study area, for example by vessel monitoring system (VMS) data (J Witt 2017), would 
also have improved the scenario definition. Furthermore, no change in spawning distribution is 
assumed to occur due to implementation of OWFs in this study. However, due to changed and possibly 
favorable conditions in OWFs it might be possible that flatfish species will also use OWFs outside the 
current spawning grounds as spawning ground. The effects of OWFs on flatfish larvae are still not well 
understood, and further experimental analyses is needed to make realistic assumptions in order to 
define the scenarios.

1 APPENDIX I: INDIVIDUAL-BASED MODEL 
Details on the implementation and characteristics of the individual-based model are presented in the 
following section. All references used for the parametrization of this model can be found in (Barbut 
et al. In prep). Details on the spawning area, the egg number and the spawning period can be found 
in section 2.1.2.1 (Spawning area), 2.1.2.2 (Egg number) and 2.1.2.3 (Spawning period). 
1.1 LIFE STAGES 
The model covers four different life stages: eggs, yolk-sac larvae, first-feeding larvae and 
metamorphosing larvae (Lagardere 1999; Al-Maghazachi & Gibson 1984). The first-feeding larvae 
correspond to the larval stages 2 to 4a in Lagardère et al. (1999), metamorphosing larvae correspond 
approximately to the larval stages 4b to 5a.  
1.2 VERTICAL MIGRATION 
After the release of eggs in the bottom layer, vertical migration is species and stage-dependent. Sole, 
brill, turbot and dab perform after release an upward movement due to buoyancy-driven vertical 
migration. Certain species perform tidal vertical migration in specific life stages, with positive migration 
values during high tide and negative values during low tide. Details about migrating behavior can be 
found in table 4.

1.4 SETTLEMENT 
Larval settlement occurs at the end of the simulation, when the individuals have passed all larval 
stages. Survival of the larvae only occurs if metamorphosis is reached inside a defined nursery area, 
with suitable bathymetry and sediment characteristics.
