Abstract 
The purpose of this project is to investigate investment casting (IC) of complex geometries made 
from additive manufacturing (AM) using an engineering approach. The workflow in an AM aided 
IC process is investigated for dimensional accuracy, microstructure changes and corrosion 
resistance. A photopolymerization platform from EnvisionTEC tailored to produce small, high-
quality components is used in combination with a resin specialized for burnout processes to convert 
the computer aided design (CAD) part into a polymer part. The polymer part is embedded in SRS 
ceramic made for investment casting and subsequently burned out in an oven to produce a mould 
cavity which is casted in metal.  
The obtained microstructures through casting were examined for 99,9 % pure Sn, Zamak 5 Zn alloy 
and a AlCu4Ti Al-alloy at different casting mould temperatures. The corresponding surface 
roughness measurements were likewise made for the 3 metal alloys. The dimensional accuracy was 
measured for the polymer geometries as well as the Sn casted geometries, and the corrosion 
resistance was tested in a 1% NaCl solution for the AlCu4Ti. Furthermore casting defects were 
identified and the topic of sprue design and viscosity was shortly processed. 
The research showed remarkable differences in microstructures when casting at different mould 
temperatures: For Sn and Zn-alloy, the dendritic structure became larger containing less branches, 
and the roughness increased with increasing mould temperature. Noteworthy is that a mould 
temperature higher than the temperature of the Sn melt resulted in lower roughness than for mould 
temperatures equal to the melt temperature. The Al-alloy revealed severe coring and Al2Cu 
precipitations in the grain boundaries, increasing with increasing mould temperature, which lowered 
the corrosion resistance drastically. The Al-alloy likewise showed increased roughness with 
increased mould temperature. 
No quantitative pattern was found for the dimensional accuracy, though protrusions generally 
increased in size, whereas depressions decreased.  Expectedly the polymer parts were more precise 
than the castings.

CHAPTER 1  
1 Introduction 
Current IC processes are mainly based on know-how through trial and error without any 
engineering aspect or comprehension of how changes in the process steps affect the outcome [1]. Of 
the different methods, IC by AM is progressing to become one of the more used due to it being the 
more cost effective combination available while retaining the quality [2]. 
IC dates back more than 6000 years with the first investments being of e.g. bee’s wax or botanical 
items. The process is used for all sizes of products and is the main process for e.g. jewellery 
production [1]. 
AM comes in many varieties with DLP being on the more precise site [3]. Combining the two 
process types provides a process chain which is cheap, easy and with excellent outcome, which is 
why the combination is progressing in industry. AM is especially advantageous for mass 
customization production, but can be expanded for mass production. 
Depending on the equipment of choice, the combined process chain consists of 10-11 steps (Figure 
1.1).  As seen on Figure 1.2, the produced part itself goes through 8-9 steps, all in which choosing 
the right parameters in the process chain are crucial . As demonstrated on the figure, one erroneous 
step distorts the part continuously through production.

CHAPTER 2  
2 Brief info on IC by AM 
The process is based on the steps shown in figures Figure 1.1 and Figure 1.2. The desired geometry 
is first design as a computer aided design (CAD) model. This is followed by a corresponding 
support structure to prevent creep and ensure that the geometry continues to adhere to the build 
plate when building, as illustrated in Figure 1.3. The figure also shows the layout for a DLP printer, 
used for this project. Here, the part is built on the so-called build plate which is immersed in a resin 
bath. A light projection cures an entire layer at once, after which the built plate moves up to make 
room for a new layer [5]. As the entire part has been built, it is cleansed in an ultrasonic bath with 
isopropanol until all uncured resin is dissolved from the surface. Next, the part is post cured to 
enhance mechanical properties and ensure no uncured resin remains for safe handling prospectively. 
This is done at the cost of inevitably warpage as the part cures. Alternatively, the parts can be made 
by other printing methods in either polymer or wax. Wax is often used in IC, which is also often 
referred to as lost wax casting. 
The post cured model is attached to a rubber bottom and assembled with a flask used for the 
investing process as seen on Figure 2.1. Investing is when the powder and water is mixed to achieve 
a ceramic slurry called investment, which is poured into the flask to create a shell around the part. 
The flasks are then vacuumed to remove air bubbles and set to cure. After the investment has set, 
the flask is put into the oven for a burnout cycle. Here the polymer is burnt out and the investment 
sintered to achieve a castable mould. The mould is kept at the desired casting temperature and after 
casting and cooling, it is broken off to reveal the metal part. The sprue is cut off and the finishing 
touches such as assembling are done to achieve the final part [1].

3 Solution theory 
3.1 Limiting the process chain steps 
Optimizing production focuses on saving time and money while retaining quality, which based on 
Figure 1.2 often means having fewer steps in the process chain. Knowing there are several 
processes with fewer steps than AM-aided IC, one could ask: 
Why is IC better than processes with fewer chain steps when producing small complex metal 
structures? 
The first alternative to look at is machining. Even though machining can be done with various tools, 
there are severe limitations towards the complexity of the geometries. Even if the degree of 
complexity is achievable through machining, the material waste is to be considered. In creating 
complex geometries it is necessary to remove larger amounts of material in form of metal chips, 
whereas the IC process only has to remove the sprue. Therefore, the complexity of the geometry 
must always be considered when choosing process. The time and tooling costs are furthermore 
negative factors compared to IC. 
An option to look at as supplement is therefore selective laser sintering (SLS) and -melting (SLM) 
[5]. With SLS and SLM, one has the ability to create the complex structures without the same 
limitations and tool wear, since a laser is not worn the same was as e.g. a drill. Although the laser 
processes have their advantages, the finished part still needs post-treatment in terms of either 
machining, grinding or polishing. By machining one will only achieve a better surface on the 
outside which is not enough for e.g. corrosion resistance and fittings with small tolerances on 
complex geometries. On the other hand, grinding and polishing of such complex objects can be 
done with a high surface finish inside cavities as well but at the expense of kept tolerances. 
Furthermore the cost of laser sintering plus post-processing is much higher than using IC. 
Even though such complex objects cannot be made directly from metal, the process chain could still 
be reduced through creating a special mould:  
3.2 Rapid casting 
Printing the moulds would leave out all process steps regarding investing and burnout as well as 
lowering the curing time remarkably [6]. Furthermore it is easier to control shell thicknesses when

printing the mould instead of the geometry [6]. It is therefore noteworthy that this technique is not 
commonly used for smaller geometries: 
How come IC is favourable to the more cost effective rapid casting though printed moulds? 
Printed moulds have proven consistent with sand castings in terms of dimensional accuracy for 
larger geometries [7] but for smaller objects, IC has been proven superior [8]. This is partly due to 
printed moulds having a layer thickness of ~150-300 µm, whereas printed geometries invested have 
a layer thickness of ~25 µm [9] [3]. 
When working with a complex CAD model, the complexity makes it easier to visualise and modify 
a model of the desired geometry than a reversed model like a mould. If modifications are to be 
made after building, this is likewise easier to do on a copied geometry than inside a printed mould 
for such complex structures. 
Furthermore it takes less effort to modify the surface of a geometry by either chemical treatment or 
coatings than to coat the inside of a mould while keeping the tolerances.  This is especially practical 
when producing items where light reflecting is of importance. This could be highly reflective 
surfaces used in e.g. aerospace, or oppositely matte surfaces with low and diffuse reflectivity. 
In terms of shell thicknesses there are several options to control this however none of them are as 
efficient as printed moulds. Optimizing the thickness of the shells has a great effect on both 
production price, due to less used material used, and the metallurgy because of cooling rates. That 
being said, the method stuccoing by dipping is comparable to the printed moulds in terms of 
optimization of material use, however the process is more time consuming (up to several days) due 
to each shell having to dry before dipping [10]. Another more expensive but far faster method is to 
create the moulds by investing the geometry in flasks. The downside of using flasks is the large 
variations in shell thickness around the geometry, making the microstructure coarser. 
3.3 Master model design 
As a result of the above, a master model is necessary when mass producing small, complex metal 
geometries. As mentioned in the introduction, the main way of producing master models have for a 
long time been a master model in metal to mass produce wax patterns from. This cost a lot of 
money and makes small changes even more expensive due to the need of a new master model for 
each change, produced in metal. A far cheaper option is to produce the patterns directly, eliminating 
the need for an actual master model. This can be done through AM, either in wax or in casting resin. 
Is casting resin preferable to wax when producing casting patterns? 
Starting by looking at the best obtainable resolution, both wax and polymer lies around a layer size 
of ~25 µm. The build plates available are likewise approximately the same, meaning the end 
product will be of same quality and production rate, independent of the process chosen for model-
making.

As of support structures, it is possible to get printers for both polymer and wax capable of printing 
the support in a different, soluble material and thereby removing the support without indentations or 
other marks left on the patterns. The cost of the two printer types is likewise approximately the 
same [5]. 
On the encouraging side for wax patterns is the ability to hide printed layers – This can be done by 
lightly heating up the wax surface, whereas polymer patterns are in need of either a coating or a 
chemical etch. On the pro side of polymer patterns is the hardness and therefore handling of the 
patterns before investing. Wax is soft and therefore easy to cause indentations of while handling. 
This small difference is in addition the reason for choosing casting resins instead of wax, for a 
better chance of a flawless outcome. 
3.4 State of intent 
The aim of this project is to provide data for an IC process from an engineering point of view. The 
process chosen through the solution theory is based on additive manufactured complex geometries 
in casting resins invested in flasks in order to create metal castings. The process steps will be 
investigated as well as the metallurgical outcome for use of different metals and alloys.  
During the project, the Department of Mechanical Engineering, DTU wishes to answer the 
following research questions: 
- How much does the dimensional accuracy decrease with each step in the process chain for a 
given metal? 
- How are temperature-changes in the casting steps affecting the part surface, microstructure, 
roughness and filling degree?  
- How do changes in the microstructure affect corrosion resistance, and how can this be 
improved?

4 Pilot experiments for process optimization 
A process chain has been set up in order to be able to reproduce the IC workflow as it is commonly 
employed in the industry. A photo polymerization platform, the EnvisionTEC Perfactory MML, 
based upon digital light processing is used to produce the burnout part. This machine is tailored to 
produce small high-quality components and has a horizontal resolution of 15µm and a vertical 
resolution of 16 µm [3]. The polymer part I subsequently cast into an industrial ceramic for IC 
made by SRS [11]. This ceramic is based on Gypsum and is engineered for fine castings such as for 
jewellery manufacture. An oven from Nabertherm capable of 1000 °C has been used for both 
burning out the polymer, sintering the ceramic and producing the melt. A further description of 
these systems, as well as considerations regarding initial castings, test geometry (TG), sprue design 
and investment viscosity is available in the following sections.

4.2  Initial casting attempts 
While the design of a proper TG was ongoing, a few prints of an engine block and a ring was 
queued on the 3D printer to provide an initial idea of what could be expected from the first castings 
of the TG. All parts were printed with a primitive gating system as seen on Figure 4.3, as it was 
found in a preliminary project that creating the entire sprue in wax was not straightforward 
(Appendix D). The parts were stripped from the support structure and fixed to the bottom of a 
cardboard flask which would later be removed, as seen on Figure 4.34. These were subsequently 
invested and casted in Zamak 5 as described in the previous chapter. 
At casting it was seen how the mould succumbed to the heat and stress from the liquid metal, 
resulting in severe mould cracking (Figure 4.5). As a result, the TG castings were to be invested in 
steel flasks which would not be removed until casting was complete. From the castings it was seen 
that whereas some small features of the engine block could be casted, it was impossible to fill the 
entire mould cavity. This was on the other hand not the case for the rings, leading to the findings 
that sprue design had an essential role in filling the moulds. Figures Figure 4.6and Figure 4.67 show 
the results from two different sprues. 
Despite the misruns, the replicated surfaces were of excellent quality, with each individual printed 
layer being visible (Figure 1.1).The behaviour of the melt in these attempts lead to the design of the 
TG to be like seen in section 4.3.

4.3 Test geometry  
A TG was designed in order to test various aspects of the process chain with respect to the 
preservation of geometrical tolerances. Therefore a small geometry with a variety of features was 
designed. Each feature serves a distinct role for tracing changes throughout the process chain: 
Small features with sharp corners show how well the DLP printer performs. Depressions such as 
holes show how well ceramic fill cavities. Protrusions are used to determine if small features will be 
damaged during the casting of the polymer part into the ceramic as well as how well the different 
metals are at filling tight gaps. The TGs were designed focussing mainly on the AM process, not 
taking factors such as entrapped air when casting much into account. This was chosen because of 
the need to test both processes to their limit for a better chance of comparison. The smallest features 
were set to 0,25 mm and the total length of the geometry to 20 mm.

4.4 Sprue design 
Along with the mould/melt temperatures, the sprue design is essential for a proper filling and 
surface finish when casting. The sprues for the TG castings were therefore calculated and designed 
thrice for improvement. 
First, the design vas made using calculations for standard castings which are significantly larger 
than the small geometries in this project (Figure 4.11). The calculations are found in appendix E, 
and a simulation of the filling, carried out by the casting specialists at DTU, (appendix F) showed 
how the melt splashed inside the sprue, indicating that the equations cannot be used for castings this 
small.  
Furthermore, as seen on Figure 4.12, the effect of otherwise negligible factors such as surface 
tension suddenly comes into play. This meant for a redesign of the sprue, which started following 
the rules of friction and velocity but was redesign a third time, connecting the rules with practical 
knowledge: First of all, since this is a burnout process, there is complete design freedom, meaning 
that the curvature and L-shape of the sprue designed for parted moulds could be neglected. 
Normally the smaller the sprue, the better the flow, the small geometry size risks the sprue being 
clogged by oxides or larger impurities. 
An area of 9 mm2 was therefore chosen, creating a “mermaid tail” to angle the geometry 
downwards as seen on Figure 4.13 for a more uniform fill. The fangates were still calculated after 
the equations and found in appendix E, based on equations from the casting group at DTU. 
Simulations showed a slower but steadier fill as desired (Figure 4.14). An outlet/cooling channel 
was placed in the top as an attempt to avoid entrapped air, and a simulation (Figure 4.15) proved 
this helpful, although the design of the TG itself is not optimal for castings, leaving some bubbles 
unable to escape.

can be seen in Figure 4.16. Three different mixtures with the same ratio were made and tested

1. 10mL/25g mixture tested 1,5 minutes after vacuum 
2. 40mL/100g mixture tested 4 minutes after vacuum

4.6 Other experimental observations 
Several critical steps within the process chain were observed and will be described in the following. 
These relates to what can be considered as proper procedure when preparing a CAD geometry for 
IC, do’s and don’ts for moulding the ceramic investments, and for casting the metal parts. 
During CAD modelling, an equally time consuming and very important activity is the generation of 
support structures for the geometry. Any overhangs that the part and gating system may have need 
to be supported by a scaffold which can either be automatically or manually added to the geometry 
prior to printing. Though automatic support may be tempting to use in order to get the print 
executed fast, it may result in even longer time wasted trying to remove the support after printing. 
For parts with severe overhangs such as the TGs with sprues, the amount of resin used for support 
structure even exceeds the amount used for the actual parts (Figure 4.19).

Several support-generating programs are available, and the choice depends on the main function of 
the support: Is it just supposed to prevent creep and shrinkage, or is it meant to keep the part fixed 
to the build plate. For the rings printed in the initial attempts, the support was only providing creep 
prevention, meaning the support structure could be almost non-existent (Figure 4.20 left). Using the 
same program, Mesh Mixer, for creating support for the TGs resulted in failed castings, since the 
support could not hold the stresses from the print, tearing the upper part loose. Instead another 
software, Magics, was used to create a robust support structure optimized for the specific purpose 
(Figure 4.20 right). In principle the robust support structure could have been optimized further, but 
this was not chosen as focus point for this project. 
Ensuring a solid connection between the flask rubber bottom and the polymer part before investing 
is likewise an essential step. For this, melted wax was chosen due to its ability to mould easily 
while cooling. The procedure was time consuming and the wax was not too keen to adhere to the 
part surface, however it was still a better solution than simply placing the polymer part inside the 
rubber cavity. An alternative solution using glue was briefly discussed; however it was unsure 
whether ingredients from the glue could disturb the burnout process. Since wax is normally used for 
this, there would be no risk using this. 
Mentioned in chapter 4.2, using a metal flask was necessary to avoid expansion causing severe 
cracking of the mould. Even with the protective metal, several moulds still managed to crack to 
some extent. It was found that peaking inside the oven to check on the mould under cooling to 
casting temperature caused a temperature chock large enough to form cracks. Too long or too short 
curing time before burnout lead to too little or too much water in the investment, both being causes 
of cracking. Finding the balance was difficult, since the investment constantly varied in viscosity 
and therefore properties. The burnout process itself will be discussed further in chapter 5.1.2. 
Not only the viscosity but also the polymer print quality varied throughout the project. Several 
printer calibrations and replacement of parts were made with no luck in understanding these 
fluctuations. These fluctuations had significant impact on the part quality, as it will be discussed in 
the next chapter on accuracy.

 CHAPTER 5  
5 Accuracy and process defects 
As mentioned in the introduction and Figure 1.2, the process chain causes inevitable loss of 
dimensional accuracy throughout its steps. The CAD file was the initial design point, where the part 
dimensions were set. Converting the CAD file to a STL decreased roundness because of the 
triangular mesh, where a compromise had to be made between the step size of the triangles and the 
file size being small enough for the printer program to read. Printing of the polymer part was 
limited by the printer resolution and printing quality of which last mentioned proved unpredictable. 
The post curing warpage is unchangeable and challenging to measure, which is why this part was 
simply kept constant. Removal of the support resulted in indentations which are unavoidable when 
the support is printed in the same material as the part itself. Attempts were made to produce 
different support structures with less impact on the part, but optimizing this without easing the 
support function. Doing so would be an entire project itself and was therefore not investigated 
further. 
Investing naturally causes entrapment of air bubbles and the managing of this step is essential for 
the mould quality [12]. Mixing by hand was the only option available, but mechanical mixing 
would have been ideal as it was shown in the viscosity test where the viscosity proved inconsistent. 
If the air bubbles fail to emerge from the investment under vacuum, the trapped air risk expanding 
under burnout causing cracks. Furthermore bubbles risk adhering to the polymer resulting in 
bubbles and nodules on the final casting [12].  
The burnout process is dependent on the investing step, both in terms of expanding bubbles, but just 
as much how much of the water and powder has separated during vacuum [12]. For a production, 
the errors occurring during these two steps are not visible immediately, and can be difficult to 
distinguish from possible casting defects, since one cannot look directly inside the mould. 
Casting defects are one of the most crucial but also easiest things to improve. Casting defects 
include things such as bubbles, gas porosities, rough castings and incomplete fills. The quality of 
the castings obviously depends on the amount and degree of casting defects, but also the surface 
finish and microstructure contributes to the quality of the final product [1]. 
The post processing option always include removal of the sprue and in some cases the outlet, 
whereas heat treatments and polishing depend on  the chosen metal as well as surrounding 
environment and intended use.

5.1 Dimensional accuracy 
For the dimensional accuracy, two strategic points were chosen in order to establish the influence of 
the various steps in the process chain: The first being after support removal and the second after 
casting. The first point was chosen to be after removal of the support, since this is when all AM 
steps are complete. The second point was chosen due to it being the most robust step to investigate: 
If measuring on the mould cavity, this would be done either with the use of probes or light sensors. 
Since the mould is white, light sensors would not function adequately, and considering the porosity 
and fragility of the mould, using a probe could risk damaging the cavity walls. Since the only post 
processing used in this project was removal of the sprues, this would not affect the dimensional 
accuracy of the TG features. 
As an additional step, the moulds after burnout can be investigated qualitatively through CT-
scanning. In principle these could be measured quantitatively as well, though the outcome would be 
of high uncertainty. In a CT-scan, the cavity walls are not sharply defined but instead created as a 
variable boundary between the air and the investment, making it difficult to get exact measures. 
Instead the scan can be used to identify some defects though not all, once again because of the 
undefined edges.  
Both the polymer parts and the castings were measured on a DeMeet 220, each part being measured 
three times to compensate for the inaccuracy of the DeMeet. The moulds were kindly scanned by

5.2 Casting defects 
Gas porosities: All Sn castings contained gas porosities as seen on Figure 5.17and Figure 5.13.  
Suggestions for gas porosities points to either erroneous burnout or poor metal handling [12] [1], 
but the reason is most likely found elsewhere; all burnouts were executed the same, and since no 
porosities were found for Al or Zn castings, the cause cannot be inadequate burnout or air flow in 
the oven. The melting point for Sn is 232 °C, and it is recommended to cast at a temperature 
approximately 37,78 °C higher to ensure thorough melting [13]. The temperature can therefore not 
possible have been too high, nor can the metal be of too poor quality, since it was 100 % new metal 
and not scrap metal. Another cause beyond the standard defect causes must therefore be found, and 
two suggestions have been made: 
The most likely reason is due to the gas solubility of Sn. Neither Al nor Zn dissolves much oxygen 
in their liquid state due to the rapid oxide formation [14] [15]. Sn however does sharply increase its 
gas solubility with temperature, leading to release hereof when cooling [16]. The release of gasses 
when cooling obviously leads to entrapment of gas inside the mould and therefore gas porosities. 
The other reason could be organic material inside the mould being more reactive to Sn than the 
other two metals used. It is known that organic material is present in the moulds, however this has 
only been found at the inlet and not inside the mould cavity, which makes this reason less likely. 
Shrinkage cavities: All Sn castings contained shrinkage cavities as well in the form of sink marks, 
both on the inlet and on the TG as seen on Figure 5.178 and Figure 5.19 [17]. Sink marks occur 
when the metal does not solidify uniformly due to different thicknesses [18]. Therefore the marks 
appear where the parts have been thickest, since the solidification time is longer there. Most of the 
parts had sink marks on the backside of the geometry, just beneath the largest cone on the geometry, 
==PAGE:41==
5. Accuracy and process defects 
 
 
| 41 
which is consistent with sink marks appearing there the geometry is thickest. Despite this, some 
parts had a deep sink mark on the front of the geometry as seen on Figure 5.1921. This kind of 
shrinkage cavity appears when the solidified metal skin ruptures, exposing continuous liquid metal 
and creating a so-called hot spot [17]. The continuous liquid exposure keeps the spot warmer than 
the surrounding, and this will therefore be the spot where solidification takes place last, hence the 
shrinkage appear here. 
Having cooling channels incorporated on the geometry can help minimize the sink marks, since 
they ensure faster cooling of the thicker sections [17]. This can already be seen comparing Figure 
5.178 with Figure 5.21, where last mentioned has a larger depression on the backside. Alternatively, 
the geometry can be modified to compensate for shrinkage by adding additional thickness to where 
the shrinkage may appear [17]. This way, the extra thickness will compensate for the shrinkage, 
creating the desired shape. 
Air bubbles and nodules: These kinds of defects are a consequence of erroneous investing, where 
bubbles are left in the investment before curing [1]. Either insufficient mixing or a defect vacuum 
pump result in bubbles being unable to escape, leaving them as cavities alongside the geometry as 
seen on Figure 5.13. This particular part was the only one with air bubbles, and in this case they 
were caused by insufficient vacuum.  
Weld lines: The Figure 5.13 also displays weld lines at the sides of the towers. Such lines are 
caused by two cold metal fronts meet, and are in this case the result of splashing metal inside the 
towers when filling [19]. Therefore the weld lines are less noticeable on the other castings with the 
optimized sprue. Most commonly, weld lines are caused by improper sprue design or sprue 
placement, which in this case is the last mentioned [19]. The TG design was made to challenge both 
the printing process as well as the casting process without too complex sprues or gating systems, 
however the design at the same time makes these weld lines inevitable. To hide these, the part could 
be angled even more compared to the sprue, in order to minimize the splashing.  
Encapsulated and broken investment: Several of the castings have investment particles caught in 
the metal surface, as seen on Figure 5.212. For the sprues, this investment is stuck precisely where 
the CT-scanned moulds showed porosities in the cavity. Since it is at the bottom of the sprue 
beneath the TG, it indicates that investment broken off the TG has fallen to the bottom of the sprue, 
which is then encapsulated by the metal poured into the mould. The same phenomenon is observed 
on the castings where the towers are merged together. This was also seen on one of the CT-scans, 
suggesting that these investment gaps can be too brittle not to collapse. Whether this is due to the 
investment being weakened by separation or if any impact has caused it to disintegrate is unknown, 
however it is not a consistent problem, meaning the thickness is feasible. 
In the top of some parts, next to the air pushed out of the mould along the flow lines, a tiny bump is 
visible, as seen on Figure 5.13. This is most likely due to investment being torn off by the metal as 
the speed increases at the top, as it is seen in the video in appendix F-C. A way to improve this

AlCu4Ti 
As mentioned in Table 1, the main alloying elements in the AlCu4Ti are Cu, Mn, Ti and Fe. 
Iron is an element normally used to prevent die soldering, however the addition also modifies the 
Al-Si phase often found in aluminium alloys [21]. Just like Al-Si, the Al-Fe-Si increases tensile 
strength and hardness for wear resistance, but the phase itself is quite brittle. 
Manganese is used to reduce detrimental effects of especially the impurities Fe and Si. By adding 
Mn, the solubility of Fe and Si is reduced, and the morphology is changed from long needles to a 
“Chinese script” eutectic (alpha instead of beta phase), improving the ductility effectively.  
Copper is the most common alloying element for wear resistance, where copper adds additional 
strength due to Al2Cu precipitates forming through aging or precipitation hardening. The increase 
in strength is a compromise with ductility and especially corrosion resistance, which was 
investigated and discussed in the next chapter. 
The last major alloying element is titanium, which is used as a grain refiner. Aluminium alloys 
solidify through dendritic growth with an eutectic structure in the interdendritic areas, where Al2Cu 
is forming with the remaining aluminium. The dendritic network from wall to centre is reached only 
10 °C below the melting point (~660 °C) and at a fraction solid of 30 %. The remaining 70 % must 
therefore solidify in the interdendritic areas, and the volume contraction hereof puts severe 
requirements on the interdendritic flow, leading to porosities and occasionally hot cracking. By 
adding Ti (>0,15 %), the dendritic stems are reduced through a peritectic reaction mechanism 
(Figure 6.32. The peritectic reaction mechanism for grain refinement ) [22]. 
The Al alloy melt was 709 °C when used. Both the LT (49,9 °C plate) and HT (347 °C plate) drops 
showed a clear dendritic structure with an intermetallic phase of undissolved, eutectic Al2Cu [23]. 
The polished LT drop showed long intermetallic connections in the middle/sides (Figure 6.33), but 
smaller connections with less distance between at the top/bottom (Figure 6.334). This suggests 
==PAGE:53==
6. Microstructural defects and corrosion 
 
 
| 53 
faster cooling resulting in lesser dendritic formation as well as less precipitation of Al2Cu due to 
shorter working time. The top also showed more porosities which could be due to the before 
mentioned volume contraction. Throughout the sample, smaller round precipitates were present due 
to the sample having an overaged effect (Figure 6.35). These precipitates can either be Al2Cu as 
well or be Al20Mn3Cu as the other dark parts of the figure [22], but a scanning electron microscopy 
(SEM) analysis would have to be done in order to tell. If one looks carefully at the figure, a 
complex interdendritic eutectic structure close to the Al2Cu is visible [22]. 
The HT drop generally showed fewer, but wider intermetallic connections (Figure 6.356). This is 
due to the higher plate temperature causing slower cooling, leaving more time for dendritic and 
precipitation growth – the sample has the effect of overaging. The larger dendrites cause inferior 
interdendritic flow, leading to additional porosities, especially at the top and sides (Figure 6.37). 
The top and sides likewise has smaller dendrites and intermetallic area due to the faster cooling by 
air than the 347 °C hot plate. This also means the bottom of the drop clearly has the largest Al2Cu 
area, as seen on Figure 6.378. Just as for the LT drop, the HT one shows sign of Al20Mn3Cu , small, 
round precipitates and interdendritic eutectic structure.  
Etching the two drops revealed this eutectic structure as well as a classical example of coring 
(Figure 6.39). As seen on Figure 6.3940, Al and Cu are not very soluble in each other, and since 
equilibrium is difficult to establish, coring happens through the microsegregation at the dendrite 
formation (Figure 6.41 and Figure 6.412). 
Just as for the other metals, the lower mould temperature decreases the roughness as seen in figures 
Figure 6.43 and Figure 6.44. The roughness decrease likewise due to the smaller dendrites, however 
since the castings were misruns at low mould temperatures, such process parameter is not 
recommended.  The Cu-containing alloys are normally heat treated afterwards, casting in a warmer 
mould to quench and solution heat treat afterwards would be ideal. The roughness would then 
depend on an entirely different microstructure, which will be elaborated in the next chapter in 
conjunction with corrosion.

6.2 The microstructural effect on corrosion of AlCu4Ti 
As described in the previous chapter, heat has a significant effect on the achieved microstructure.        
Figure 6.45 shows the microstructure of the alloy before used for casting. Here, both the amount of 
Al2Cu as well as the precipitation in the grain boundaries is less severe. The eutectic phase is 
likewise smaller, leaving less cored dendrites than the casted samples. 
The amount of microsegregated copper plays an essential role in corrosion resistance. As seen on 
Figure 6.456, Cu is significantly nobler than Al. The two therefore acts as a galvanic cell in the 
alloy, and as described above, the more copper the less corrosion resistance. Two difficulties appear 
in the alloy microstructure; First of all, having the Al2Cu precipitates along the grain boundaries 
naturally leaves a Cu-depleted zone from diffusion next to it as seen on Figure 6.47. This means the 
depleted zone is acting as corroding anode in the galvanic cell, causing so-called grain boundary 
corrosion. The second thing is connected to the coring phenomena; here the middle of each dendrite 
is a Cu-depleted zone, causing it to corrode first. 
Pitting in aluminium alloys normally occur in the pH range of 4.5-9 (passivation zone) [26]. The 
shape of the pits depends on various factors such as the composition and quality of the oxide film. 
In chloride-containing solutions, corrosion promotes the formation of AlCl3, increasing the 
concentration with time. AlCl3 has a pH of 3.5, and its presence inside the pits leaves them no 
chance of passivating. The pits therefore continue to corrode until no oxygen or corrosive 
electrolyte can be transported to the bottom. If the middle of a dendrite starts to corrode, such a pit 
will occur, often continuing until the eutectic zone is reached, leaving the entire surface severely 
damaged. 
To test this in practice, polarization curves of the AlCu4Ti alloy was done for two samples with 
different microstructure: One casted in a 250 °C investment mould and one straight from the metal 
bar used for castings, which will here be referred to as non-casted. The samples were machined to 
==PAGE:58==
58 | 
 
6. Microstructural defects and corrosion 
fit the corrosion cell by being equal in size, and the datasets from the tests can be found in appendix 
L.  Both samples were immersed in an aqueous solution containing 1% NaCl, and an OCP 
measurement was run for 15 minutes before the cyclic sweep run from -50mV to 1200 mV at a 
sweep rate of 50 mV/min. The resulting polarization curves are displayed in figures Figure 6.48 and 
Figure 6.49. 
As expected, the OCP is higher for the casted sample containing more free copper, meaning this 
sample in principle corrodes slower (27,018 mm/year) than the non-casted one (34,543 mm/year). 
Shown in Figure 6.50 A) and B), the corrosion type however is much more damaging for the casted 
sample, and exactly as described above: The casted sample shows severe intergranular corrosion as 
well as heavy pitting in the middle of dendrites, consuming most of each grain (Figure 6.50 A) and 
Figure 6.51) [27]. The non-casted one however has a much smaller, more uniform pitting pattern 
and only few cases of intergranular corrosion (Figure 6.512). 
Consequently, the casted sample will fail before the non-casted one, due to the intergranular 
corrosion exposing more surface, loosening grains and ultimately causing them to break off. Then, 
new surface will be exposed, continuing the destructive corrosion process. 
Examining the casted sample further, it is noteworthy how the areas around porosities are not 
affected as much by corrosion (Figure 6.53 and Figure 6.534).  Comparing to the etched HT sample 
from the previous chapter (Figure 6.412) it is noticeable how the areas surrounding porosities have 
a high content of Cu. This explains how the areas are less prone to corrosion. Figure 6.412 also 
shows areas containing more Cu than others. The effect is seen in Figure 6.55, where one area is 
considerably less corroded.  
Even LOM photos from before the corrosion test shows evidence of how the two samples were to 
behave differently: Already on Figure 6.534, markings of the Al2Cu precipitates in the grain 
boundaries are visible, whereas Figure 6.556 shows only vague signs these, just as grain boundary 
corrosion only showed in few places afterwards (Figure 6.512). 
 
Therefore, it is advisable in castings to reduce the amount of Al2Cu precipitates. This can be done in 
several ways, including combining them for better results. First of all, faster cooling - preferably 
quenching - would help reduce the precipitation, especially at the grain boundaries. Solutions could 
be a thinner mould, e.g. by dipping, and/or immersing in cold liquid afterwards.  
Considering the copper is added to the alloy on purpose for strength, precipitation is not completely 
undesired, since the precipitates help stop grain boundary sliding in the aluminium FCC lattice. 
Hence, controlled growth hereof is favourable, and can be done through a heat treatment, such as 
T7 (solution heat treatment followed artificially aging for stabilization). 
The AlCu4Ti alloy is not designed for IC even though composition is close to casting alloys 201 
and 224. Therefore the heat treatment T7 could improve the microstructure for AlCu4Ti, just as for 
the two casting alloys. The T7 treatment creates fine precipitates both in grains as well as grain 
boundaries with no or little undissolved eutectic Al2Cu present [23]. The shrinkage cavities are 
contrary difficult to remove completely, but these can be minimized through the heat treatment as 
well.

CHAPTER 7  
7 Conclusion 
Prior to the project’s main focus, several pilot experiments were made to address potential 
challenges beforehand, gaining the following knowledge to use for successful castings: 
The pilot experiments showed replication quality good enough to reproduce the layers visible from 
the polymer part. Furthermore it was found how sprue design was essential for castings without 
misruns, and how the factors affecting the melt flow in sprues at larger castings are not the same as 
for small ICs. New considerations have to be made regarding e.g. surface tension in the smaller 
sprues. Since IC is not limited by mould assembly, there were no design restrictions, and a sprue 
was made combining casting knowledge, rules and common sense. The viscosity tests showed how 
mixing by hand caused constantly different behaviour of the investment. This was later linked as a 
possible reason for mould cracking, which turned out to be a consistent challenge. Ideally, a 
constant mixing by machine should make the viscosity identical for each batch and if not the reason 
is expected to be found in the composition of the investment powder. 
 
In the solution theory, several research questions were asked as a state of intent. The questions will 
now be addressed in the previously presented order: 
 
- How much does the dimensional accuracy decrease with each step in the process chain for a 
given metal? 
There was not found any quantifiable pattern for the dimensional accuracy for Sn. Although no 
pattern was found, it was still systematic enough to state a general behaviour: For the prints, holes 
smaller than 0,5 mm started to become problematic to replicate, especially when printing with 
attached sprues. It was likewise found that the used DLP was more accurate in the direction of the 
towers than the direction of the teeth. Therefore, the placement of the parts when printing affects the 
accuracy as well. Material build-up was continuously favoured, meaning that the printer’s attempt 
to build the material surround a depression is limited by the resolution and will therefore always be 
in favour of the polymer side, making depressions less accurate. As a consequence, protrusions 
were up to 7 % more accurate in dimensions than depressions. The same behaviour was found for 
the castings, where protrusions were up to 10 % more precise than depressions. Since only the 
horizontal orientations were investigated, further investigations are to be made for the vertical 
direction. 
Lastly for dimensional accuracy, the CT-scans showed how the burnout produce a fair replication 
but does affect the geometry in especially the sprue area and features >0,5 mm.  
==PAGE:64==
64 | 
 
7. Conclusion 
 
- How are temperature-changes in the casting steps affecting the part surface, microstructure, 
roughness and filling degree?  
It was shown that the correct parameters could eliminate most surface defects such as shrinkage 
cavities, gas porosities and weld lines. The temperature, microstructure and roughness were found 
to be closely connected; the warmer the mould, the bigger the grains, the rougher the surface and 
the more importance was assigned to the alloying elements. If no heat treatment is to be conducted 
after casting, the mould should be as cool as possible to ensure the best possible microstructure and 
roughness. If the mould is too cold, misruns will occur. It was found that a mould temperature 
higher than the melt temperature at casting resulted in a decrease in roughness for Sn. The reason 
for this is yet to be found and will need more tests objects as well as more microstructural 
investigations in order to find the answer.  
- How do changes in the microstructure affect corrosion resistance, and how can this be 
improved? 
Corrosion tests of AlCu4Ti showed how the distribution of copper affected the corrosion resistance 
tremendously. This was found to be caused by the galvanic coupling occurring because the nobility 
of Cu is higher than for Al. A coring effect in the grains combined with Al2Cu present at the grain 
boundaries promoted grain boundary corrosion and corrosion pitting in the Cu-depleted zones.  The 
warmer the mould when casting, the more coring and Al2Cu were present, meaning faster cooling 
could reduce corrosion. It was suggested to solution heat treat by standard T7, since literature 
indicates this as eliminating both severe coring and large GB Al2Cu precipitation. Instead, small 
precipitates would be found dispersed randomly throughout the metal, providing strength without 
reducing the corrosion resistance to the same extent.  
 
Through this project is has therefore been discovered how handling of the different materials and 
steps all have serious consequences for the final product. Sprues and geometry design is of 
importance in terms of filling degree and quality, and how the investment is mixed determines both 
the characteristics of the mould as well as the appearance of several casting defects. The 
dimensional accuracy has no numeric pattern, however protrusions generally appear larger and 
depressions smaller for each step. The temperature is of most importance for the microstructure, but 
whether to have a cooled or heated mould depends on the desired post treatments. That being said, 
having a heated mould would reduce production time and is preferable if a heat treatment is to be 
conducted afterwards. Lastly the microstructure turned out to have an essential effect on corrosion 
resistance; to ensure the best resistance, the nobility of the different compounds in the 
microstructure should be kept as equal as possible. For AlCu4Ti, the amount of Cu concentrated in 
the grains determined the corrosion resistance.

CHAPTER 8  
8 Future work 
Even though it was shown how most process defects could be evaded and how increase in geometry 
size was affected though the different steps, the research is far from finished. Several examined 
areas are in need of further investigation, as well as new areas explored. The following contains 
suggestions for future work which were not possible within the scope limit: 
 
Investigation of dimensional accuracy for each process step 
2 strategic points were chosen for examination of dimensional accuracy however the remaining 
steps should likewise be treated. More tests on the dimensional accuracy would also have to be 
done; not only for the Z direction which has yet to be measured, however also the X-Y directions 
when the printer is optimized for the exact purpose. More CT-scans of the moulds would make it 
possible to go into depth with setting the border between air and investment, making it possible to 
measure dimensions hereof fairly precise. How different metals possibly have different dimensional 
accuracy and how heat treatments hereof could likewise provide an effect would also be fitting to 
process. 
 
Different metals and post treatments 
Not only would the Al and Zn alloys have to be tested further, also alloys made specifically for IC 
should be tested. Quenching and heat treatments would be representative of an actual process chain 
and should therefore be completed alongside the castings for investigation of both microstructure 
and dimensional accuracy.  
 
Sprue design, TG and metal melt behaviour 
Since it was found how other factors influenced the metal flow in the sprue than for larger castings, 
it would be fitting to investigate these factors thoroughly. This would mean simulations and 
practical tests for e.g. surface tension, metal skin formation and oxide formation. The tests would 
conclude in new guidelines small sprues to follow for new designs. The TG would also need a 
redesign to prevent weld lines and to check the importance of depression and protrusion placement 
in terms of dimensional accuracy.  
 
==PAGE:66==

8. Future work 
Surface replication and roughness continued 
The issue of surface roughness was briefly considered, however a mix of surface roughness and 
surface replication is intended done thoroughly. Especially the phenomenon of lower surface 
roughness when the mould temperature exceeds the casting melt temperature is interesting to 
examine. The roughness of the polymer parts as well as the actual casted parts is to be measured for 
comparison. Attempts of examining the mould roughness would additionally be preferred to 
compare the process chain steps in the roughness area as well. 
Stress-strain analysis, hardness measurements and corrosion tests 
How well are the actual castings in terms of use? By performing both stress-strain analysis and 
hardness measurements on as-casted and heat treated parts respectively, one would get a 
quantitative aspect of the castings’ performances. New corrosion tests would have to be completed 
for heat treated materials, quenched and as-casted parts for comparison. Also salt spray tests for 
corrosion resistant metals would be suitable for observing the impact of mould temperature and 
processing. 
 
SEM and electron backscattered diffraction (EBSD) analyses 
Currently all data on intermetallic compounds and composition variations are based on assumptions 
made from literature. Even though these are assumed to be correct, performing EBSD analyses 
would verify these assumptions as well as be able to detect impurities currently not registered. 
Besides chemical composition and phase identification, a crystallographic analysis to detect the 
local orientation of grains and dislocations would support the hardness measurements intended as 
well as the polarized LOM photos.  
 
Investment powders and resins 
Currently only one investment powder and one resin type was used. Other types of both are 
available, and for the investment powders there even exist some claiming to be even better in terms 
of replication. Testing these to compare with the already used could help improving the quality of 
the parts as well as create a guideline for choosing the most cost effective materials for the desired 
quality. New viscosity tests would also have to be made, using a mechanical mixer to see whether 
this would make the viscosity more consistent for each sample. Even if using a mechanical mixer 
does not equalize the viscosity, having more data sets would help determining if there is any pattern 
at all. Determining the composition of the investment powder could help the understanding of the 
behaviour.  
For the cured moulds it would be convenient to measure the heat conductivity of the investment, to 
help predict the cooling rate as well as optimizing the burnout cycle. Testing the permeability 
hereof would help the understanding of mould-filling and –cracking.

APPENDIX A 
Main experimental setup 
The main setup is described in the following section due to continuous use of the methods through 
different experiments. The procedure for specific experiments will be described in the 
corresponding sections. 
Printing 
Most of the prints were made on an EnvisionTEC DLP printer at 700 mW/dm2, using the resin 
PIC100 2G specifically made for burnout processes. All prints were cleaned with isopropanol in an 
ultrasonic bath and cured in an Otoflash light polymerization chamber for 26.000 flashes, where the 
support structures were removed with a scalpel after the first 2.000 flashes. 
Attaching and mixing 
The printed geometries free from support were attached to the flask bottoms using melted paraffin 
wax. By using wax instead of e.g. glue, any unknown contamination in the burnout cycle was 
eliminated, since wax is already commonly used for the process. For most castings, a steel flask of 
both height and diameter of 100 mm was used. As mentioned in the solution theory, this could have 
been far smaller, but it was the only one available.  
The mixing procedure was done after recommendations from the investment powder company, 
SRS, in order to minimize variables for the project. Following the guidelines for a fine filling 
degree, the subsequent procedure was prosecuted: 
1. SRS CLASSIC™ investment powder poured into 22 °C tap water, ratio 100/40 g/mL 
2. Mixing by hand for 5 minutes before pouring into flasks 
3. 1-1.5 minutes under highest achievable vacuum, until bubble-free 
4. 2 hours of curing time  
5. Removal of flask rubber bottoms before burnout 
For some flasks at attempt was made to eliminate bubbles by gently tapping the flask on the table 
both before and after vacuum. This was done to prevent bubbles from adhering to the plastic 
surface.

resin made specifically for investment casting in order to produce a metal cast of a given geometry.

Qualitative microscope tests are used in order to compare the quality and resolution of the different 
process steps, and troubleshooting is done for each step in order to optimize the process and get a 
proper outcome.

Process Chain Study for Investment Casting 
 
 
 
 
 
 
1. 
Introduction 
The introduction states the outlines for the 
project: 
1.1   Objectives 
The purpose of this project is mainly to get 
comfortable with the investment casting 
process and equipment. The long-term aim is 
to produce an investment casted replica of a 
given geometry with a surface quality as close 
to the original computer aided design (CAD) 
file as possible. 
1.2   Definition of the project scope 
Additive manufacturing (AM) has a wide 
range of methods. In order to receive an 
additive manufactured geometry as close to 
the CAD file as possible, a printer with a 
resolution up to 16 µm. Likewise investment 
powder and metal is chosen as to receive a 
replica of highest quality.  
Since the project is the prelude to a master 
thesis, the project focuses entirely on making 
the process chain to perform with a successful 
casting each time.  
 
1.3   Research questions 
In order to organize the process of the study, a 
set of research questions were made:  
- Is it possible to make a casting with the 
given paraphernalia? 
- If possible; how can the process be 
improved? 
 
Since this is a preliminary study, the research 
questions are made solely to have a basis for 
whether or not the project ends successfully. 
 
 
 
2. 
Investment casting 
 
2.1   The process 
Investment casting – or lost wax casting - can 
be done in several ways: Using hand-made 
wax models, wax dipped rubber models, AM 
models or even objects from nature. In 
general, investment casting is done by 
creating a model in one material, investing it 
in a liquid slurry, burning out the material and 
casting with a selected metal [1].  
Since the project is based on AM as a tool for 
investment casting, the focus will be on this in 
the further description. An AM based 
investment casting consist of 10-11 steps: 
 
1 – Design 
2 – Printing the models 
3 – Removing the support structures 
(3.5 – Assembling the tree) 
4 – Investing the models 
5 – Burnout 
6 – Melting 
7 – Casting 
8 – Cooling 
9 – Cutting the cast pieces off the tree 
10 – Assembly and finishing of the geometry 
 
The design is made using a CAD system, 
which is custom for all types of investment 
casting. The models are then printed using a 
thermoplastic resin specifically designed for 
investment casting. The grounds for this will 
be explained in section 3.1. 
The 
printing 
jobs 
are 
be 
done 
with 
supportstructures 
to 
prevent 
creep 
and 
ensuring the model is attached to the printing 
plate while printing. An example of a model 
with support structures is seen on figure 2. 
These supports will naturally have to be 
removed before investing the models. 
Assembling the tree is not always a necessary 
step, depending on the models to be casted. If 
one is to cast several small models, it is by far 
easier to attach them to a tree as seen on 
figure 2 [1]. However with AM models, the 
models can I principle be printed directly on 
the tree, eliminating this step completely. 
How to build a tree and design the sprues is a 
long and complex story, which is why a 
description hereof will not be included. 
The next step is to invest the models – This 
means surrounding them with a liquid slurry 
that will become the moulds for casting. The

investment is made by mixing a calcium 
sulphate bonded powder (gypsum) with 
water. Other types of powder can be used as 
investment, however gypsum is the most 
common due to the low cost and easy removal 
after casting [1]. 
The invested models – flasks – are put to dry 
for a couple of hours before the burnout. All 
different investment powders have different 
recommendations for a burnout cycle due to 
different additives in the powders. The 
general purpose of the burnout cycle is to first 
heat enough to melt the thermopolymer to 
remove it from the flask, followed by heating 
in order to remove carbonaceous recidues left 
by the polymer. In order to ensure complete 
combustion of carbon, the temperature for this 
step needs to be over 690 °C, however 
temperatures over 750 °C will decompose the 
bondings 
in 
the 
gypsum, 
leaving 
a 
temperature of 730 °C as standard for almost 
all powders. 
After the burnout process, the temperature of 
the flasks will be lowered to the wanted 
casting temperature while the metal is being 
melted. 
Casting and cooling will then take place – 
Cooling either in air or quenching, depending 
on the wanted microstructure and properties. 
The casted models are then cut loose from the 
sprues, and if objects are to be assembled, this 
is done lastly.

2.2   Advantages and disadvantages 
Investment casting is a complex process, and 
yet at least 50 % of all jewellery is made 
using the process. This is because of the 
versatility: 
One 
can 
produce 
complex 
geometries on both a large or small scale in a 
relatively cheap way. The cost of a quality 
die, machining or forging is economically 
disadvantageous compared to investment 
casting. 
With this being said, the complexity provides 
some challenges. From a metallurgical 
perspective, step 1 to 8 in section 2.1 always 
has 
to 
take 
process 
parameters 
and 
metallurgical behaviour into account. The 
design of the model and sprue is crucial in 
terms of how the metal fills the mould cavity, 
how the investment and burnout is done 
reflects the surface quality and casting and 
cooling determines both the mechanical 
properties and the quality of the final product. 
This means all steps are connected, such as if 
one step is done improperly, the process will 
either be impossible to continue or will be of 
remarkably lower quality. In all of these steps, 
time is of the essence [1]. 
 
3. 
Additive manufacturing 
 
3.1   Printing in plastic 
The resins designed for investment casting 
have a lower burnout temperature and lower 
expansion 
coefficient 
than 
regular 
thermoplastic polymers. Printing can be done 
in several ways, however for this specific 
project the resin is a photopolymer, due to the 
light projection printer having a higher 
resolution than e.g. a fused deposition 
modelling printer. 
 
3.2   Advantages and disadvantages 
A 
standard 
investment 
casting 
process 
consists of 13 steps [1]. Some of these steps 
include making a master model to create a 
rubber model of. This rubber model is then 
used for creating several identical models of 
wax to use for investing. These are three long 
and complicated steps, where the slightest 
error will make the moulds/models of no use. 
This is not necessary with AM models, since 
the CAD file and printer will ensure identical 
models each time, making the mentioned 
steps redundant. On the negative side, AM 
models consist of layers, which will be visible 
on the final castings. Solutions proposed for 
solving this issue is to spray a thin coating of 
paint on top of the printed model to smoothen 
the surfaces [4].

5. 
Methods 
 
5.1   Modelmaking 
All models were made on an EnvisionTEC 
Perfactory printer using the resin listed above. 
Models were made with both incorporated 
sprues and with wax modelled sprues. The 
procedure is described in separate sections 
below.  
 
In order to examine the quality of the process 
steps, two different models were printed; a V8 
motor block and a hole-test geometry (figure 
3). The hole-test was a small flat-sided square 
with 5 holes in it. The holes were 0.9 mm 
deep and with a diameter of 1 mm, 0.8, 0.6, 
0.4 and 0.2 respectively. The printer was set 
to 25 µm in layer size – the lowest possible 
setting. 
 
The procedure for creating and removing 
support structure as well as curing and 
cleaning the models was the same 
independent of sprues. Supports were created 
manually in Magics, objects were cured for a 
total of 26.000 flashes with the support being 
removed after the first 2.000. The cleaning 
process before curing was done in an 
ultrasonic bath for 10+ minutes until clean of 
resin residues.

Separate cylinder sprues were made in 
paraffin wax with a diameter of

To facilitate the process, incorporated sprues

5.2   Investing 
The investments were made using paper cups 
as flask holders in order to ensure effortless 
removal after drying.  
Molten stearin was used to fasten the models 
inside the flasks. Without fastening, the 
model risked moving or tilting, making the 
investment unusable.  
 
5.2.1   Mixing and vacuuming 
Mixing was done following the SRS powder 
recommendations. The powder/water mixing 
ratio was 100g/40ml for fine filling, and an 
overview of amount vs. flask size from SRS is 
shown in table 1. As urged by SRS, the 
powder was mixed into the water by hand for 
5 minutes before being poured into the flasks. 
 
Just after filling, the flasks were put under 
vacuum for approximately 1 minute until no 
bubbles burst at the surface. It was made sure 
that each flasks did not spent more than two 
minutes under vacuum, since too long 
vacuum time would cause the water to 
separate from the powder/water mixture. The 
flasks were then left to dry – The first batches 
for approximately 18 hours and the last batch 
for 2 hours. The flasks where taken out of the 
holders and put into the cold oven.

5.2.2   Burnout 
The burnout cycle was set to follow 
recommendations from SRS as seen on figure 
4, however due to the oven being limited in 
its settings the flasks went through the 
burnout cycles and were then left to cool 
before re-heating and casting the next day. 
Later in the project it was found that the oven 
did not respond as though, meaning the urged 
sequence ended up being different from the 
executed one. This is described in detail in 
section 6.3.1. 
 
5.3   Casting 
As mentioned in section 4.1, the castings were 
made in zinc. Two of the batches were casted: 
For the first one the flasks were heated 
gradually over 1.5 hours to 400 °C and held 
for 1 hour before the casting. The zinc was at 
the meantime heated to 460 °C and 
subsequently poured into the heated flasks. 
For the second batch, the flasks were heated 
gradually to 600 °C over 3 hours and held for 
2 hours, and the zinc heated to 520 °C as an 
attempt to prevent misruns. Both batches were 
left at room temperature for several hours 
before breaking off the flask, exposing the 
casted models. 
 
A detailed guide of the methods including 
helpful hints is listed in appendix 2.

Results and discussion 
 
6.1   Replication quality 
Printing the different geometries went with 
ease. The set layer size of 25 µm fits properly 
with the outcome, as seen on figure 5. 
Scratches and unevenness is normal for the 
printed structures, meaning the outcome is as 
expected, stated by the supervisor. The holes 
were also proportional and circular as meant 
to be (figure 6) however the cleaning hereof 
had to be particularly thoroughly cleaned in 
order to remove all resin residue from the 
holes. In the first batch, this was not done 
properly, and some holes were therefore 
clogged with small amounts of resin residue 
which were then cured along with the models,

6.1.1   Support structures 
As seen on both figure 6 and 8, the models are 
distorted where the support structure was 
sitting. The balance between the amount of 
support and the ability to keep the object from 
creeping while printing is quite fine and has 
not been explored in depth for this project. 
Even though the removal of support structures 
was done carefully, the indentations were up 
to 700x430x250 µm in size. This means 
wherever the support structure is, there will 
be a severe loss of quality. Thus, this can be 
solved in two ways: One way is to use a 
different program for support structures, 
which either lessens the amount of support 
used or changes the amount of contact 
surface. Another way would be to do as in 
industry, where the model is made with 
additional plastic where the support is 
attached. This plastic is then grinded away 
before casting, leaving a surface of higher 
quality and a model in the wanted 
proportions. 
 
6.1.2   Sprue-making 
The two different ways of making sprues each 
had their advantages. The ones done by 
printing were by far the easiest. They limit the 
amount of work and ensure the same diameter 
throughout the sprue. The printed sprues also 
shortens the working time for mounting 
before casting, since the wax takes time to 
harden enough for the sprues to stay in place 
through the investing. A disadvantage for the 
printed sprues was the placement on the parts 
– In order to save time, the sprues were 
simply drawn on top of a flat surface on the 
heavy parts of the models instead of being 
sloped for the best casting possible (figure 3). 
Furthermore they were designed too small in 
diameter for a proper filling of the flasks 
while casting - resulting in misruns.

6.2   Investing 
Conducting the investment had to be done in 
a certain time span. If the investment was 
mixed for less than 5 minutes, errors such as 
bubbles and water marks could occur. If 
mixing or working time was longer than 6 
minutes, errors like fins and flashing could 
occur. Even though both too long and too 
short working time was tried, none of these 
problems occurred. The reason might have 
been that other mistakes were made, leading 
to a different outcome. 
In terms of vacuum, the same challenge of 
time span occurred: If put under vacuum for 
too long, water visibly segregated from the 
powder, lowering the quality of the 
investment. If put under vacuum for too little 
time bubbles were still trapped inside the 
flask, causing a risk for bubble formation in 
the castings as well as breakage under 
burnout. A vacuum time of approximately 1 
minute was found to be the most convenient. 
Additionally when being under vacuum, the 
investment seemed to rise and then collapse 
as the vacuum was reduced. One reason could 
be that more air was still trapped inside the 
flask, however no bubbles were visible and 
after more time the segregation of water and 
powder occurred, meaning the vacuum time 
was then too long. The reason therefore might 
be that the water starts boiling due to the low 
pressure, and expands as a consequence 
hereof.   
 
6.2.1   Troubleshooting 
Timing everything is crucial for achieving the 
highest possible investment. Thereby the most 
significant problems related to the investment 
could be eliminated, both in terms of mixing 
and vacuuming.

and several errors were found, but time was 
too short to see whether all causes were

An encouraging part of the otherwise lesser 
successful burnouts was the replication 
quality and burnout purity. 
Figure 10  shows how the details of less than

6.3.2   Trial and error 
The first error was the curing time. The guide 
from SRS said to have the flasks cure for at 
least 2 hours before burnout. Since the oven 
used was not programmable for the entire 
burnout process, the first flasks were set to 
dry overnight in order to start the burnout 
process the next day. This resulted in too 
much water evaporating from the investment, 
weakening them before the burnout and 
therefore cracking [1]. 
The error was fixed by leaving the flasks to 
cure for only 2 hours (less time could result in 
the opposite but with same result) and then 
starting the burnout, but still the flasks 
cracked. 
Two errors concerning the burnout process 
itself were then detected: 
After the burnout process, the flasks were 
cooled to room temperature.  
This was done because of limiting hours per 
day, when a burnout process took around 14 
hours to perform and the investing had to be 
done the same day 2 hours earlier.  
The casting was therefore postponed to the 
next day, leaving the opportunity to either 
cool the flasks to room temperature or keep 
them at casting temperature overnight. Due to 
environmental reasons, the cooling was 
chosen. 
Cooling the flasks as such was through online 
research found to cause cracking, which could 
be an explanation for the continuous cracking 
of the flasks. 
The other error was the settings of the oven. It 
was found that the oven operated differently 
than first believed. Due to the limited 
possibilities for programming, the oven was 
first set to heat to 230 °C over 1 hour and 
holding at that temperature for 3 hours.

Because the oven was not able to heat to more 
than 219 °C in 1 hour, the holding time was 
prolonged to 3 hours and 20 minutes. After 
this time, the oven was then again 
programmed to heat to 730 °C over 3.5 hours 
and held at the temperature for 4 hours before 
cooling to room temperature. The 
recommended burnout cycle is displayed as 
the green line and the believed cycle as the 
blue line on figure 15.  
By observing the oven it was found that this 
believed behaviour was not the case when 
going from 230 °C to 730 °C. The actual 
behaviour is illustrated as the red line on 
figure 15: Instead of using 3.5 hours to heat 
the missing 500 °C, the oven would 
understand the time as being from room 
temperature to 730 °C, and as it already was 
230 °C, one third of the time was cut off, 
heating the 500 °C in 2 hours and 18 minutes 
instead.  
This much faster heating could cause rapid 
expansion of the air inside the flask cavities, 
resulting in cracking of the flasks as it was 
seen for all batches. 
 
6.3.3   Troubleshooting 
 
Whether the cracks were caused by cooling 
after burnout, by the faster heating or by both 
is unknown, however since both are errors, 
both will have to be adjusted for further work. 
The heating error can be fixed by adding an 
extra hour to the program while the cooling 
error can be fixed by either leaving the oven 
at burnout temperature 730 °C overnight and 
then adjusting to casting temperature or to 
have an oven programmed to be able to run 
the entire burnout cycle in one step. 
The curing time error has already been fixed 
and will continue as being two hours. 
 
 
6.4   Casting 
All of the castings were misruns. The first 
batch was heated over 2 hours to 400 °C and 
held there for one hour before casting. The 
low holding time was once again due to time 
pressure and the flask did most likely not 
reach 400 °C throughout the flask.

6.4.2   Troubleshooting 
Some of the challenges should be easier when 
the flasks are not heated too rapid or cracked, 
however pouring speed and sprue size can be 
fixed rather quickly. These things combined 
should solve all known complications, 
resulting in a complete casting with higher 
surface quality. In terms of the temperatures, 
the higher temperatures for the second batch 
should result in better replication, and no 
signs of overheated metal or flask were 
present. 
It is known that top-filling casting is not the 
most efficient, which might also play a role. 
Other methods will therefore have to be tested 
in order to find the most efficient. How this 
will be done is described in the future work, 
section 8.2. 
It is likewise known that many gold smiths 
put their main focus on melting and casting,

7.  
Conclusion 
 
7.1   Answering the research questions 
It is possible to make a casting with the given 
paraphernalia however a casting without 
misrun is yet to be achieved. 
The project has revealed several challenges to 
be improved in future work: It was found that 
time is of the essence: Mixing time, curing 
time and the burnout times are all essential to 
get right in order to have a flask to cast in. 
Furthermore sprue size and pouring speed 
need to be adjusted to achieve a cast without 
misruns. 
When these things are in order, a detailed 
analysis of actual casting defects can be 
conducted. 
 
8. 
Future work 
 
Several suggestions for improvements have 
been made through this report. The future 
work is therefore based on implementing all 
proposals in order to optimize the various 
process chain steps. 
 
8.1   Optimizing the investment/burnout 
process 
Several destructive errors were detected, and 
the suggestions stated to fix these will be 
initiated in order to get a flask without cracks. 
New equipment has already been bought as an 
attempt to optimize this even further. 
 
8.2   Optimizing the casting process 
Currently the casting process has not been 
able to make a casting without any misrun. 
The errors identified will therefore be 
considered along with the proposed solutions 
in order to see whether or not everything can 
be solved. Different sprue placements will 
also be tested, in order to achieve the most 
efficient for castings without misruns. This 
includes top-filling and bottom-filling as well 
as sprues being angled instead of 
perpendicular on the models. 
 
8.3   Testing replica precision for other 
geometries 
The geometries tested have only been tested 
for layer visibility and for replication of holes 
of different sizes. Proper test geometries 
therefore have to be made in order to collect 
and compare precision data. 
 
8.4   Testing more powders 
The powder used for this project was a 
standard jewellery investment powder, but 
powders have been made for replicating in 
higher quality, advertised as the finest of 
surface quality. As soon as the process is up 
and running successfully, testing these 
powders for finer details and comparing the 
difference will be interesting.

10. Appendix 
Appendix 2 
Nadia’s guide to investment casting: 
Preparing the 3D object: 
1. Print the wanted object 
2. Clean the printed object with isopropanol in a ultrasonic bath, until no shiny areas are visible 
3. Blow dry the object gently until fully dry. Dry subsequently for 30 minutes in air. 
4. Cure: Start with 2x1000 flashes, resting 2 minutes in between. Remove support hereafter. 
Cure a total of 26.000 flashes. 
5. Make and attach a sprue* 
6. Put into flasks 
*Sprue size should be proportionate to the casting – 50-70 % of the area should be covered. Round 
sprues flared at the casting are the most effective. Attach sprue to the heaviest area of the casting as 
the heaviest areas will solidify last. For the main sprue always use a wax with a lower melting 
temperature than the wax patterns. This will allow the wax to flow out of the mould cavity without 
boiling inside the mould thus reducing mould erosion. 
Mixing the investment: 
1. For fine filling, use the powder/water ratio of 100/40 (see table 1).  
2. Put the water in a bowl* 
3. Add the powder to the water** 
4. Mix for 5-6 minutes. Always use 
5. Pour into the flasks*** 
6. Vacuum the flasks for 1 minute 
7. Leave to stand for at least 2 hours. Do not allow flasks to dry out, cover with a damp cloth 
if necessary. If the flask loses too much water before burn-out starts the investment walls 
may break, crack or weaken due to the powder drying out. Ideally the flask should be put 
into the oven on the same day as investing. 
8. Load into a cool furnace 
9. Always clean equipment after use. Check for rust in flasks. 
* Use demineralized water with a temperature of approximately 22 °C. 
** Store the powder in a cool dry area. Reseal the bag after use. 
***Allow enough space between the flask wall and the wax patterns for the investment to fill 
around (12,7mm min). This will reduce the risk of flashing. Leave enough room for a 25,4mm thick 
layer of investment at the top of the flask, this will eliminate blow outs. 
Burnout cycle: 
1. Start with a cool furnace with the flasks inside 
2. Heat to 230 °C in 1 hour 
3. Hold at 230 °C for 3 hours 
4. Heat to 730 °C by increasing 150 °C per hour (~3 hours) 
5. Hold at 730 °C for 4 hours 
6. Decrease to 630 °C or casting temperature (430-460 °C) 
7. Hold for at least 1 hour to ensure uniform temperature in the mould. DO NOT cool to room 
temperature after burnout!

Notes for zinc casting: 
Never use 100% scrap metal. It is recommended to use 50% old and 50% new metal. 
Do not overheat metal. Follow the recommended casting temperatures. 
Wait 15 to 20 minutes before quenching cans. Quenching before this could lead to cracking. 
Alter the speed on the casting machine to avoid the problem of non-fill. If the speed is not sufficient 
then the metal will freeze before it has a chance to fill all the cavities. 
Zinc melting point: 419,53 °C 
Recommended additional temperature to ensure all melted: +37,78 °C 
Zinc casting temperature: 457 °C 
Density near room temperature: 7.14 g/cm3 
Density near melting point: 6.57 g/cm3
