Abstract 
Four different thawing methods were used to access thaw rigor and quality on pre-
rigor frozen Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) which had been frozen for 5 months, first 
at -23°C for 2 months and later at -30°C for 3 months. The four different methods used 
were; (1) 9°C flowing water, (2) air thawing at 3°C, (3) air thawing at -5°C and then 
9°C, and (4) water with the starting temperature of 30°C and no water flow. Each 
method consisted of five fish and was conducted twice; once for analysis on lactic acid 
formation for an overview of the rigor mortis process, and once for assessing 
toughness, firmness, water holding capacity, and QIM by a sensory panel.  
Out of the 20 fish used for the lactic acid analysis 6 had a negative amount of lactic 
acid produced when comparing starting and final lactic acid content. It was suspected 
that the water used in three of the thawing methods (no. 1, 3 & 4) may have diluted 
the samples and given lower values. It is suggested estimating dry matter at all 
sampling times could give more reliable results.  
Results showed that out of the four methods, method (4) gave significantly higher 
results in water holding capacity, and scored the lowest with the sensory panel. Due 
to lack of comparative data it was not possible to state which method gave fillets with 
the most desirable firmness and toughness.

1 Introduction 
Marine fish stocks are the last major wild source we have for animal protein and human 
fish consumption has been rising at the average rate of 3.2% a year from 1961 to 2016, 
while population growth has been at 1.6%. With the over-exploitation of a large 
portion of the world’s fisheries resources, production from capture based fisheries 
hasn’t increased since the late 1980s (FAO, 2018). Consequently, there has been 
increased attention on both the general welfare of the fish and environment, and the 
value and quality increase of what is being caught. One aspect of this is the method 
used to catch the fish, which has mostly been controlled by factors such as fuel prices, 
fishing quotas, prices on frozen/fresh raw material, and historical aspects (Huse et al., 
2000) 
Seafood is a huge and important industry, especially for some of the Nordic countries, 
and the changes in value per kg can have detrimental effects for their fisheries. 
Throughout the value chain the product is constantly assessed for quality and freshness 
starting either when it is landed and sold to fish auctioneers or when it is processed on 
board of factory trawlers. The quality is repeatedly assessed further along the chain, 
but the chances of the quality increasing after the first assessment is improbable 
unlikely. Multiple studies on how post-harvest treatment e.g. different methods of 
bleeding, gutting, heading, and freezing effects the raw material quality have been 
conducted but still these methods are under constant evaluations and adjustments 
(Rotabakk et al., 2011; Karlsen et al., 2015).  
Commercial cod fisheries have played an important role in Greenland since the 
beginning of the 20th century, starting within the fjords and later offshore. They peaked 
in the 1960s with annual catches up to 400.000 t mainly consisting of offshore catches. 
From there on the catches started to decrease and in the 1980s the West Greenland cod 
stock collapsed completely and has yet to recover. It is assumed that the reasons for 
==PAGE:11==
 
2 
that are lower ocean temperature and decrease in the spawning stock. (ICES, 2017. 
Buch et al., 1994. Storr-Paulsen et al., 2004).  
Today there are four separate cod stocks in Greenland distinguished by their spawning 
areas: I) offshore West Greenland waters, II) West Greenland fjords, III) offshore East 
Greenland and Icelandic offshore waters and IV) inshore Icelandic waters 
(Therkildsen et al. 2013. ICES, 2017). Since 2012 the inshore West Greenland cod 
stock is managed separately from the other stocks and most of Greenlands cod fisheries 
are catching from that. Tagging experiments have shown that the inshore and offshore 
stocks are in general separated but that the coastal areas are mixing zones, particularly 
for juvenile cod. Cod captured in fjords and banks have been shown to be resident and 
re-captured in the same areas. However, cod captured in the coastal areas have been 
re-captured in all areas including the Icelandic waters. Due to this the inshore catch 
should be considered a mix of inshore cod, West Greenland offshore cod, and East 
Greenland/Icelandic offshore cod. In what proportions the stocks are mixing is not 
known and has yet to be quantified (ICES, 2017). 
The offshore West Greenland stock is at extreme low and is usually younger than 5 
years and has not started spawning; very rarely large older cods are caught in offshore 
West Greenland. The cod found in West Greenland offshore waters are thus considered 
to be of the East Greenland and Iceland offshore stocks. They migrate out of West 
Greenland offshore waters when they spawn (ICES, 2017).

1.1 Fish muscles 
Compared to many animals bred for food the fish structure is quite simple, consisting 
only of a few long muscles extending from head to tail. The fish muscle is however 
both rheologically and structurally complex, consisting of a mixture of solid and fluid 
components. The cod muscle has a metameric structure; it is divided by transverse 
sheets of connective tissue (myocommata) into roughly 50 segments (myotomes) 
corresponding in numbers to the fish vertebrae. The muscle fibers extend between two 
myocommatas with variable length depending on the placement of the muscle, with a 
max. length of around 12 mm, with a diameter of approx. 150 – 300 µm for a 95 cm 
cod 
(Love, 
1958. 
Dunajski, 
1979). 
The texture is affected by both chemical and biochemical composition of the fish, as 
well as handling of the fish both before and after slaughtering. The water, protein, and 
lipid composition is on average 78-83%, 15-19% and 0.1-0.9% respectively for 
Atlantic cod, but it depends on various factors such as size, maturity, sex, individual 
activity, anatomical location, and environment. In wild cod there is no significant 
difference in the composition of the right and left fillets (Huss, 1988. Dunajski, 1979).  
In non-fatty fish such as the cod the total “free water” is around 80% of the total water 
content. This free water and the muscles water holding capacity is an important factor 
in texture qualities of the fish fillets. The rest of the water is bound to sarcoplasmic 
proteins and is relatively constant through all postmortem changes. The proteins 
become partially coagulated during cooking but have insignificant effects on overall 
rheological qualities. Fish in good physical condition has a clear translucent and elastic 
flesh but during stressful situations, e.g. during spawning season, the raw flesh is milky 
white, watery, and feels soft due to lack of lipids (Love, 1964, 1975. Dunajski, 1979).

1.2 Postmortem changes 
Pre-slaughter stress affects the salt and water balance in marine species leading to 
elevated sodium, chloride and serum osmolarity levels as well as lower ATP energy 
levels for several hours after capture which results in water outflow (Wells et al., 1985; 
Eddy, 1981; Love, 1980; Cliff & Thurman, 1984). Stress affects the glycogen content, 
lactate levels, pH levels of the muscle, rigor evolvement and muscle firmness resulting 
in poorer quality of the raw material product (Skjervold et al., 2001). The biochemical 
changes that happen postmortem are dependent on the biological state of the fish 
before catch, the catching method, processing methods and cooling/freezing methods 
(Dunajski, 1979. Martinsdóttir & Magnússon, 2001. Arhcher et al. 2007).  
The textural changes post-mortem are due to changes in the physio-chemical state of 
the myofibrillar proteins, given that microbial spoilage are not causing spoilage as 
well. One of the main post-mortem changes is rigor mortis, when the muscles go from 
being soft and extensible to becoming tough, inextensible and contracted. Dark 
muscles can contract by up to 52% while white by 15%, as well as the fish can become 
deformed if not placed properly before rigor mortis sets. This can lead to problems 
during filleting and severe gaping of the muscles. It is important to make sure rigor 
mortis is fully finished at the end of the thawing and before cooking the fish. Cooking 
fish before rigor mortis takes place makes the process happen so quickly that toughness 
and loss of texture quality is inevitable (Love, 1964, 1975. Dunajski, 1979).  
The muscle remains soft while there is sufficient adenosine 5-triphosphate-Mg2+ 
(ATP) to prevent reaction between the actin and myosin filaments in the muscle. ATP 
has been used as an indicator of the rigor mortis process, where the depletion of ATP 
is directly related to the onset of rigor mortis (Bate-Smith & Bendall, 1947) as well as 
the amount of free Ca2+ and Mg2+ (figure 1) (Jeacocke, 1993). The amount of 
glycogen and creatine phosphate have been used to determine the amount of time 
needed for rigor mortis to set after death (Bate-Smith & Bendall, 1949. Bendall, 1951. 
Lawrie, 1953). ATP is resynthesized for some time post-mortem with phosphorylation 
of adenosine monophosphate (AMP) and glycolysis (Dunajski, 1979).

Glycolysis is the anaerobic degradation of glycogen to lactic acid. This causes the pH 
levels of the fish to go from neutral down to approx. 6.2-6.5. When the pH approaches 
the isoelectric value of the myofibrillar proteins, the negatively ionized myofibrillar 
protein molecules become neutralized causing a drop in the repelling forces between 
the unipolar groups and as such tightening of the protein structure. Low pH thus leads 
to increased cooking losses and an increase in the concentration of myofibrillar 
proteins which combined increases the toughness and dryness of the fish muscle 
(Dunajski, 1979). 
Gaping of the fillets is caused by contraction of the muscle cells during rigor mortis 
and the severity is dependent on the state of the muscles pre-rigor, i.e. if they are still 
attached to the skin and bones, temperature, energy available and pH of the muscle. If 
rigor mortis happens after filleting the muscles are not under tension and the muscles 
do not suffer great damage from gaping. before filleting the contraction disrupts the 
muscle cells leading to separation of muscle segments causing them to gape from each 
other. Gaping increases with increased temperature and with lower pH, so immediate 
chilling after catch is important. For fresh, ice stored muscles the toughness is highest 
when the pH is lowest and when rigor mortis is fully set. Weather the fish is cooked 
before, during, or after rigor mortis also affects the muscle toughness of the final 
product. If cooked before rigor mortis starts it will accelerate the process so the muscle

mortis the increased heat will partly decrease the muscle toughness. When cooked after 
rigor mortis cooking does not cause any significant changes in toughness as long as 
the fish is not spoiled (Dunajski, 1979). Different degrees of gaping in fillets from cod 
that was frozen headed and gutted (H/G), thawed and filleted can be seen in Figure 2

When filleted and cooked pre-rigor the muscles can contract by 30-50% of their length, 
but remain tender. When filleted pre-rigor but cooked post-rigor, fillets do not shorten 
as much if kept in cold storage since the low temperature slows down the rigor mortis 
process, so the shortening is not as extreme and partly reversible, but toughness is the 
same (Dunajski, 1979).  When fish is frozen pre-rigor there is a risk of thaw rigor 
happening when energy is left in the muscles. Thaw rigor usually happens if fish is 
thawed at high temperatures or cooked from a frozen state. This can cause the fish to 
become tough, stringy and suffer from high drip loss (Archer et al. 2007).  
1.3 Freezing and thawing 
Fish is a very perishable product and moving large amounts of fresh fish to markets 
worldwide can be problematic as well as the supply and demands are irregular. By 
freezing the fish, the storage time and shelf-life is greatly prolonged. Fish is either 
frozen H/G or filleted and cut down to consumer products. While freezing the fish 
extends it shelf-life and preserves its qualities, the method of freezing can be very 
detrimental for the final quality (Baygar et al., 2013).   
When frozen after rigor mortis the final product tends to loose quality and become 
deformed, dry and tough (Huss, 1988). By filleting and freezing the fish pre-rigor the 
risk of the fillets shortening and becoming deformed are decreased, as well as it 
decreases the total bacterial count of the thawed product (Martinsdóttir & Magnússon, 
2001). During frozen storage up to -20°C the fillets will continue to slowly go through 
the biochemical changes causing rigor mortis until the ATP reserves are finished 
(Martinsdóttir & Magnússon, 2001. Stroud, 1969). During thawing those changes will 
increase again when the glycolysis starts and ATP is synthesised again (Cappeln et al., 
1999). Fish frozen and stored at -40°C has shown to give better quality reservation 
compared to fish frozen and stored at -18°C (Nilsson, 1994. Becki, 2018). 
The thawing of the product is as important as the freezing and there is a large variety 
of industrial thawing methods being used to try and preserve the quality of the fish. 
However, it is important to state that quality lost during freezing cannot be gained back 
by using an optimal thawing method (Alizadeh et al., 2007).  
==PAGE:17==
 
8 
When frozen in blocks the fish is either thawed by using heat around the outer layers 
or by distributing heat within the inner layers of the block. Outer layer thawing is done 
with air thawing, thawing in water, vacuum thawing, and contact thawing. Thawing 
from the inner layers is done with high pressure, or electrical methods such as high 
voltage, dielectric, ultrasound, far-infrared, or with electrical resistance thawing 
(Backi, 2017). Here will only be looked at outer layer thawing with either air or water 
which are the most widely used industrial processes.  
Air thawing is done both with still air and with forced air streams. When thawing with 
still air, i.e. by placing the fish in a refrigerator or a room without any forced airstream, 
the process can be very long due to the low heat exchange. To speed up the process 
the fish can be placed individually or on a surface with air holes to increase the area in 
contact to the air. Separating fish such as H/G cod from frozen blocks can cause 
damage to the fish and is time consuming. When exposed to dry air for a long time the 
fish can get dry and there is a risk of lipid oxidation. This can be avoided by increasing 
the humidity in the thawing environment (Becki, 2018).  
By using forced air streams the thawing process becomes much faster and the risk of 
lipid oxidation decreases. To decrease the risk of drying fish is usually placed in a 
colder room and covered in ice at the final stages of thawing. This can be done with 
air blast thawing, where the fish blocks are placed in a room so that there is space all 
around and between them to let airstream flow evenly, or with impingement thawing 
where air is blown directly at the product (Becki, 2018).  
Water thawing relies on heat conduction between the water and the frozen fish. When 
comparing air thawing to water thawing on seafood products, water thawing has been 
found to be favorable in terms of quality reservation (Ersoy et al., 2008. Srinivasan et 
al., 1997. Dinçer et al, 2009. Becki, 2018). As seen in Figure 3 the fish thaws very 
quickly during the initial stages of thawing. At the start of the thawing the outer surface 
of the fish is glazed with ice, which has higher thermal conductivity than the water, 
and thus facilitates the thawing. After the surface glaze has melted the thawing rate 
decreases while the fish overcomes the latent heat stage (Figure 3). During this phase 
any cellular damage that has happened to the fish during handling, freezing or thawing 
==PAGE:18==
 
9 
results in the release of cellular components leading to drip and quality loss (Archer et 
al, 2008).  
 
Figure 3 The different stages of thawing and freezing  (Archer et al).  
Here is shown the fast thawing rate during the initial stages of freezing followed by a longer 
period of conversion of ice to water (latent heat stage). After the latent heat stage is passed the 
thawing rate increases again. Similarly, is shown that during freezing the conversion of water to 
ice takes the longest. 
Martinsdóttir & Magnússon (2001) did a quality trial on cod fillets frozen both pre- 
and post-rigor and air thawed at 0-1°C, 5°C, 8°C and 22°C. They concluded that the 
drip loss between these temperatures was not statistically significant nor were the 
results of fillets frozen pre- and post-rigor. They found a statistically significant 
difference in quality score of thawed fillets frozen pre- and post-rigor when frozen for 
less than 17 months. During the first 12 months, the longer the pre-rigor fillets were 
kept at a frozen state the lower their freshness scores (Torry scheme where 10 is fresh 
and 3 is spoiled) became, from 8.6 – 7.5. After 17 months of storage there was no 
significant quality difference between fillets frozen pre- and post-rigor, and the quality 
score went to 5.5.

1.4 Royal Greenland Nutaaq® cod 
Royal Greenland has recently invested in a new processing method for cod called 
Nutaaq®, which means “New” in Greenlandic. The fishing takes place between April 
and October each year when the larger cod enters the coastal areas. The cod is caught 
using net traps which cause a low environmental impact and causes low stress to the 
fish, compared to e.g. trawling, leading to higher glycogen content (Fraser et al. 1965). 
Due to it being a passive fishing method undersized and juvenile fish can be released 
alive back to the waters (FAO, 2001). The trap is transported to seawater pools and 
the cod is gently released from the traps to the pools without them leaving the water. 
The cod is transported using well-boats the cod is sorted by size and the undersized 
cod is released back to the ocean. The larger cod is transported in circulated sea water 
to the factory where it is stored in a stress-free environment for a minimum of one 
night to make sure the fish is stress free to try and get the best quality products. When 
the factory has the capacity to start the process the live cod is transported into the 
factory where it is stunned with electricity, slaughtered and bled out in cold water. The 
cod is frozen at the factory at -23°C H/G or filleted within 2 hours from when it first 
enters the factory to ensure they are frozen pre-rigor (Royal Greenland, 2018).

1.5 Aims of the study 
The aim of this study was to see if different thawing methods would affect the onset 
of rigor mortis during thawing and the end quality of Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) 
fillets produced with Royal Greenland Nutaaq® method. The cod was caught with a 
low stress method and frozen pre-rigor at -23°C for 2 months and further stored at -
30° for 3 months. It was speculated that the recommended fast thawing of frozen food 
could negatively affect the quality of the Nutaaq® cod products by inducing fast thaw-
rigor, and a slower cold temperature method would decrease the thaw-rigor and 
increase quality preservation. Four thawing methods were used to test that hypothesis, 
three using cold temperatures and one using hot water. Methods used were; (1) 9°C 
flowing water, (2) air thawing at 3°C, (3) air thawing at -5°C and then continued in 
9°C water, and (4) water with the starting temperature of 30°C and no water flow or 
circulation.  
To follow the rigor mortis process, production of lactic acid was measured using a 
high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC). For quality assessments fillets 
were assessed for water holding capacity and texture (toughness and firmness), and 
assessed by a sensory panel using Quality Index Method (QIM) scoring.

2 Materials & methods 
2.1 Raw material 
Cod (Gadus morhua) was provided by Royal Greenland from their processing method 
of Nutaaq® cod. Fishing for the Nutaaq® cod took place on October 25th 2017, using 
net traps. The cod was transported to large seawater pools where it was sorted by size, 
it was further transported in sea water to the factory by well-boat. At the factory the 
cod was placed in sea-water ponds for a minimum overnight, to minimize any stress 
before processing.  
The cod was brought into the factory using slow-moving barrier onto a belt where they 
are stunned, slaughtered, and bled out in ice water. The cod was cleaned, headed and 
gutted pre rigor mortis using one mechanical process. The processed product entered 
the -23°C freezer within a maximum of two hours after it was transported into the 
factory, where it was stored until transportation to DTU laboratories. 
2.2 Sampling 
Samples from the cod were taken from a restricted area of the fillet between the dorsal 
and pectoral fin. Each method (see further under Thawing) used 5 headed and gutted 
cods and every sampling was done in triplicate on the fillet of each cod, for each time 
point. While the fillets were frozen a blanking punch (10 mm) and a hammer were 
used to take a sample out of the frozen fillet. A surgical blade was used to cut out a 
sample after thawing started. Three samples were taken from each fish at each time 
point, placed in a plastic zip-lock bag and stored at -80°C. 
2.3 Temperature monitoring 
A Tinytag plus and plus 2 temperature probe (Gemini data loggers, UK) were placed 
vertically in the fillet at the center of the dorsal fin, of two fish, and at the bottom and 
top temperatures of the thawing area recorded for each method. The fish were 
numbered 1 and 5 and placed in the top and lower part of the thawing area. The probe 
recorded the temperature with an interval of 7 minutes and data handled with TinyTaq 
explora (Gemini data loggers, UK).

2.4 Thawing  
Four different thawing methods were conducted for comparison on two occasions, one 
for lactic acid determination using HPLC and the other for water holding capacity 
(WHC), sensory analysis, and firm- & toughness analysis. Dry matter was determined 
from all fish from both occasions. For HPLC the fish was thawed longer to track the 
formation of lactic acid. When thawing for WHC, sensory, and firm- & toughness the 
method was stopped when core temperature reached 2°C. At that point fish were 
filleted and stored in a vacuum sealed bag on ice for 1-3 days due to the different times 
it took for the thawing to finish. 
Method (1) was conducted using ~9°C running tap water. The cod was placed in a 
50 L plastic tub filled with tap water, and water let run with enough stream to produce 
circulation of the water until the core of the fillet had thawed (Figure 4). 
Method (2) was conducted using air thawing in a room at ~3°C. The cod was placed 
in a white plastic tray and stored approx. 1m above the floor surface until the core 
temperature of the fillet had thawed (Figure 5).  
Method (3) was conducted using a freezer at -5°C followed by cold tap water. The cod 
was placed in the freezer until the core temperature was approx. -5°C. After which 
they were put in a 50 L plastic tub with 9°C running tap water until the core of the 
fillet had thawed (Figure 6). 
Method (4) was conducted using hot tap water at approx. 30°C in a 50 L plastic tub 
until the core of the fillet had thawed. There was no circulation of the water or new

2.5 Sample extractions 
Samples of either approx. 1 or 2.5 g were homogenized in an ice cooled (0°C) 0.6M 
perchloric acid for 1 minute using Ultra Turrax homogenizer, and neutralized to a pH 
of 6.2-6.8 with 2,5M KHCO3 for a minimum of 1 hour on ice. The neutralized samples 
were transferred to 10 mL (1 g) or 25 mL (2.5 g) measurement beakers and filled to 
the mark with deionized water. Samples were then filtered through 4-7 μm filters 
(Whatman 595 ½) and stored in TVN cups at -18°C until further analysis. These 
extractions were used for determination of lactic acid. 
 
2.6 Determination of Lactic acid 
Lactate in the extract was analyzed by HPLC (Agilent technologies 1200 series) and 
separated on a BIORAD HPX-87H column. The conditions were: flow rate 0.6 ml 
min-1 at 50°C with an eluent of 0.008M H2SO4, run time 45 min and injection volume 
of 0.30 µl. UV absorbance was measured at 210 nm. Lactic acid content was expressed 
as 
ppm/g.  
Standards used were lactic acid (Sigma L1750) both undiluted and 10x dilution, in 
three different volumes (15, 30 & 45 µl).

2.9 Sensory Analysis 
Fillets from the right side of the cod were used for sensory analysis where the Quality 
Index Method (QIM) was used to assess the quality of the cod as described by Warm 
et al. (1998), excluding their gaping assessment. The assessed attributes were: texture, 
odour, colour, blood stains, and parasites on the scale of 0-3, 0-3, 0-3, 0-3, & 0-2. 
Every attribute weighed equally for the total QIM score which was found by 
summarizing the total QIM scores for each fillets and methods. 
Each fillet was given a random number between 0-200 (using www.random.org) and 
placed randomly on tables under synthetic day light and then estimated by a group of 
7 trained in-house personals.

4 Discussion 
The highest temperature in method (2) was 2°C and therefore that was the temperature 
used for comparison of time needed to thaw the samples. That was also used as the 
point where the thawing was stopped and the fish was filleted for the sensory, water 
holding and textural analysis. For fish used for the lactic acid determination the 
thawing methods were carried out longer to track the lactic acid formation and see if 
any changes could be observed when stored at the low temperatures for longer period 
of time and after the fish reached 2°C.   
The method for estimating lactic acid was recently adjusted at the university 
laboratories since it was discovered that organic acids tend to precipitate. Before 
putting the extraction into the HPLC vials each sample should be gently rotated so that 
the liquid part homogenizes without the material precipitation dissolving. With all 
samples here this was not applied, the actual amount of lactic acid is thus possibly 
higher than the values show. This applies for all results and as such does not affect the 
difference in increase/decrease of lactic acid between samplings.   
The two water thawing methods (no.1 & 4) took considerably shorter time compared 
to the two air thawing methods, and the two differed only of approx. 30 minutes. It 
took the temperature from method (1) approx. 3 hours to reach 0°C, staying at the 
latent heat stage for approx. 2 hours. In method (4) the fish was at 0°C within approx. 
1 hour and stayed at the latent heat stage for around 45 minutes. The water temperature 
stayed at 30°C for a limited time before it dropped down to temperatures that are 
usually used for industrial thawing of fish (12-25°C) (Archer et al.2008).  
Due to the fact that there is no other research that has been conducted exactly the same, 
comparison of data to those in other reports or papers is challenging. Ritchie (1927) 
concluded that during rigor the percentage of lactic acid in muscles of cod were 0.12% 
and rose to 0.16% after rigor mortis, on average, so increasing only by 0.04% or 400 
ppm. It is problematic to estimate what the lactic acid content of a resting fish is since 
as soon as it is caught, or here moved to the factory, it is in fact not in a resting state. 
Therefore, the initial lactic acid content might be different between individuals not 
==PAGE:41==
 
32 
only because of rigor mortis but because of their resistance before slaughter. This 
could partially explain the bigger difference and higher standard deviation within the 
starting concentration compared to the end concentration of lactic acid between 
individuals and methods (Tables 3 & 4).  
Having so few samples and individual differences, removing negative data and what 
could possibly be outliers was complicated. Only using fish with increase in lactic 
acid; method (1) had an average increase of 652 ppm (33%) in lactic acid, with 
standard deviation of 170. Method (2) the average increase was 585 ppm (35%), with 
a standard deviation of 541. Method (3) an average of 136 ppm (6%) increase in lactic 
acid, standard deviation of 105. Method (4) an average of 399 ppm (25%) increase in 
lactic acid, with standard deviation of 288. By removing outliers in attempt to lower 
the standard deviation the data got quite thin and in the end not considered 
representable as results indicating affects from the different thawing methods.  
Another explanation for the difference in initial lactic acid concentration is that the 
fish used in this experiment is from the actual processing at Royal Greenland. It is thus 
possible that some of the fish have taken longer to go through the processing and 
already been in rigor when frozen. Controls consisting of fish that were frozen post-
rigor, and fish frozen pre-rigor but only frozen for a limited amount of time would 
have made it easier to compare the initial and end values of lactic acid to estimate if 
each individual was frozen pre-rigor.  
The increase and then decrease in lactic acid between the second and fourth sampling 
in method (3), as shown in Figure 12, indicates that there might be something wrong 
with the sampling. The increase could be explained with the fact that ATP breaks 
down 
quite 
rapidly 
at  
-6°C. The fast breakdown is considered to be due to elevated Ca2+ when they are 
released from the sarcoplasmic reticulum at the latent thawing stage (Ma et al. 1992. 
Cappeln et al. 1999). The lactic acid content should remain high and not drop even 
though less ATP is formed and rigor mortis stops. A plausible reason for the decrease 
is the fact that after sampling at -5.7°C the fish was placed in 9°C water to finish the 
thawing process. Placing the fish in water, with several holes in the fillets after 
previous samplings, could have diluted the tissue for the next samplings. That could 
==PAGE:42==
 
33 
also explain the low increase and/or decrease of lactic acid when comparing the initial 
and final lactic acid content of the muscle for methods (1) and (4) (Table 5). Method 
(2) was the only method where increase in lactic acid was found in all samples when 
comparing first and final samples (Table 5). It had the highest dry matter in both 
occasions, and it was the only method where the fish were not placed in water during 
thawing.  
The average dry matter of the fish was lower in all methods when the fish was thawed 
for lactic acid analysis compared to when it was thawed for sensory analysis. In both 
cases fish from method (4) had the lowest amount of dry matter and as stated earlier 
fish from method (2) the highest. Fish from method (1) gave the second highest and 
fish from method (3) the second lowest dry matter amounts.   
It’s been stated that freezing fish for 8 weeks at -28°C or lower will avoid thaw-rigor 
as it will slowly happen until there isn’t enough ATP left to start new contractions 
(Martinsdóttir & Magnússon, 2001. Stroud, 1969. Archer et al., 2008). However, even 
if no ATP is left during freezing there can still be glycogen reserves left. During 
thawing the rigor mortis will start again when the glycolysis starts and more ATP is 
formed to start another contraction cycle as shown on Figure 1 (Cappeln et al., 1999). 
The Nutaaq® fish is caught with traps and should thus have glycogen reserves (Fraser 
et al, 1965) when it is slaughtered which can start glycolysis when thawed. 
Method (4) had significantly higher WHC compared to samples from the other 
methods which all had nonsignificant differences between them (Table 7). This could 
be due to the much quicker time it took fish in method (4) to reach and overpass the 
latent heat stage where cellular damage that has happened during the processing and 
freezing can negatively affect the quality of the fillet (Archer et al. 2007). The WHC 
calculations are based on the ability of the dry matter to bind to the water present in 
the muscle. Therefor even if the fish that was air thawed may have been drier than the 
fish thawed in water that would not have affected the results.  
No statistical difference was found in the sensory analysis between the methods, 
method (4) had the lowest score of 1.7 and method (1) the highest score of 3.1. Method 
(4) scored the lowest and method (2) the highest in the textural firmness and toughness 
==PAGE:43==
 
34 
analysis. Since finding comparable data was not possible due to different experiment 
set-ups, the ideal number for toughness and firmness isn’t known. Too low toughness 
and firmness can give an unpleasant texture while chewing, but having too tough and 
firm samples could also give an unpleasant texture in mouth. It is thus complicated to 
state based on this data if the low results for method (4) are more or less desirable than 
the highest results for method (2). Method (2) scored the highest in the texture part of 
the sensory panel with an average of 0.9 compared to method (4) that scored the lowest 
average in texture with 0.7. Method (4) had the most consistent results and fewest 
outliers in the textural measurements with 1 outer outlier in firmness and no outliers 
in toughness.  
From the PCA the samples from method (4) showed similarity to a cluster formation, 
but more samples must be used to obtain more reliable results. If a cluster formation 
is found from only one method out of the four it could indicate that that method gives 
a more consistent outcome. Product consistency is very important in branding and 
customer satisfaction and could therefore be a critical factor in the overall quality and 
marketing of the product (Aaker, 1996). 
The mixing of the different inshore and offshore cod stocks in Greenland should be 
taken under consideration for the good manufacturing practices (GMP) for the 
Nuttaq® cod. The possible differences in their protein synthesis, lipid storage, 
temperature preference, and migration behavior could affect the rigor mortis process 
and end quality. The stocks are continuously growing at different growth rates through 
their life so the constant formation of muscle fibers, as well as the size increase of 
those already present, is not taking place in the same amount of time. A weak decrease 
in firmness has been found with increased fiber size and lower fiber density (Johnston 
et al., 2000, 2004. Bugeon et al., 2003). The firmness could be different between the 
different cod stocks due to their different size and growth rate. The low firmness in 
the two fish removed from the average in method (1) could be explained with different 
genetics affecting the fish texture. Cod from Greenland has been found to have a 
relatively high pH compared to other cod stocks (Sørensen, unpublished data) which 
could indicate that fillets from those fish have less gaping compared to fish from other 
stocks using the same method (Love & Haq, 1970). 
==PAGE:44==
 
35 
Doing genetic identification of the fish during the experiment to see if there is any 
correlation with optimal thawing methods for different stocks could give new insight 
to the data. The method suitable to majority of the fish caught by Royal Greenland 
should then be used but for their GMP.

5 Conclusions 
Based on the results from the present study a thawing method that decreases the time 
cod stays at the latent heat stage is recommended when thawing pre-rigor frozen cod. 
Out of the four methods method (4), using 30°C water without further addition of 
water, gave the most desirable over-all outcome. It had the highest WHC, lowest QIM 
score, and more consistent textural results. This is contrary to what was initially 
believed at the start of the experiment, which was that when fillets are frozen pre-rigor 
the fast thawing method and use of hot water would increase thaw-rigor and decrease 
the final quality of fillets. Here the thawing methods were conducted for 5 fish at a 
time and further research on up-scaling for industrial thawing should be conducted. In 
this study the water temperature was calculated with a starting temperature of 30°C 
and final temperature of 5°C. Using higher starting temperatures could result in loss 
of quality, further research is needed regarding maximum temperatures to use for 
quick thawing of pre-rigor frozen cod.  
Using HPLC to determine lactic acid and the process of rigor mortis should be 
optimized by using a negative control from fillets frozen post-rigor. Samples should 
also be taken for dry matter analysis throughout the thawing method to make sure the 
water from the thawing does not affect the actual values.  
In this study the microbiology and gaping of the fillets was not assessed. Adding those 
two components to future studies is crucial for over-all quality assessment and the 
GMP.
