Abstract  
This study examines whether “green” methanol falls within the planetary boundaries for the 
shipping industry. As the industry seeks to reduce its CO2 impact, it identifies fuel as an area 
with considerable potential for improvement. As far as the author of this study can find, 
methanol has not been tested for its absolute sustainability in the shipping industry. To 
address this, the lifecycle assessment (LCA) method was employed to determine the burden 
of the fuels, allowing for the quantification of whether these burdens fall within the 
planetary boundaries through absolute environmental sustainability assessment (AESA). The 
findings of the AESA reveal that, at present, methanol with pilot fuel is not absolute 
sustainable, as five different types of “green” MeOH were tested in this study. Although it 
has a lower impact on climate change, it still exceeds the allocated safe operating space. 
There is also burden shifting when comparing methanol with Very Low Sulphur Fuel Oil 
(VLSFO) from fossil fuels. The AESA results obtained in this study are susceptible to the 
allocation principle used; therefore, it is not possible to determine whether methanol should 
be used in the future as an alternative fuel to fossil fuels in the shipping industry. It is 
therefore recommended that further studies investigate what would be required for 
methanol to be within the planetary boundaries and determine if it is attainable for the 
shipping industry and its partners to achieve.

1. Introduction 
Our planet today, is in the middle of a climate crisis and on the brink of an ecological 
collapse as the governments and industries are trying to lower their environmental impacts 
on the globe, so that we are within a safe operating space (EEA, 2024; MMMCZCS, 2025a). 
One of these industries is the shipping industry. The shipping industry today emits around 
3% of the global anthropogenic CO2 emissions (Malmgren et al., 2021; Roux et al., 20. The 
shipping industry considers the choice of fuel as an area, with a big potential for reducing 
CO2 emissions by changing the conventional fuels with new alternatives (MMMCZCS, 
2025a). One of the alternative fuels is “green” methanol also known as renewable methanol. 
Methanol is defined as a “green” alternative in Directive (EU) 2018/2001 if the feedstocks 
such as carbon and water in the production are sustainably sourced, and the electricity is 
considered renewable. A sustainable fuel meets the specific greenhouse gas (GHG) savings 
and sustainability requirements both established in the Directive (EC, 2023a, 2018; 
Methanol Institute, 2025a).   
Methanol, in general, is the smallest of the alcohols. It is a clear and toxic liquid at ambient 
temperature and pressure (Methanol Institute, 2025b; MMMCZCS, 2025b). This makes 
methanol similar to most existing fossil fuels as they are also liquids at ambient temperature 
and pressure. The similarity makes methanol favourable as an alternative fuel when it comes 
to both storage and handling as methanol will not require a lot of changes to present 
infrastructure as with some of the other alternatives, like hydrogen and ammonia 
(MMMCZCS, 2025b).  
To investigate if “green” methanol is a better alternative to conventional fuels, a life cycle 
assessment (LCA) can quantify the different impacts the fuels will have on the environment 
through the fuel’s whole life cycle (Hauschild et al., 2018). A review study by M. Roux et al. 
(2024) was checked for critical insights and gaps in several LCA studies for maritime fuels. In 
this study, 43 articles were reviewed and only 11 of them included methanol1. The few 
articles about methanol and other alternative fuels are problematic as the industry has a 
strong focus on alternative fuels (DFDS, 2025a, 2025b; Maersk, 2025). The low number of 
articles on methanol may relate to the maturity of the fuels for ship use (M. Roux et al., 
2024). In 2023 almost 95% of the total volume of the used fuels was fossil (DNV, 2024). The 
articles in M. Roux et al. (2024) concluded, that there was a reduction in the impact for 
climate change and that “green” methanol had the potential to be a pathway for reducing 
greenhouse gas in the shipping industry. 
One of the problems that M. Roux et al. (2024) identified was that a lot of the articles 
primarily focused only on the environmental impact category: climate change. By focusing 
solely on one impact category, the chances of burden shifting from one impact category to

another can increase without realizing it (Hauschild et al., 2018). Focusing on as many 
impact categories as possible is therefore vital, when working on enhancing the 
environmental impact of the shipping industry. The scientific community is not the only 
segment with a focus primary on climate change, also government bodies such as the EU 
and IMO (International Maritime Organisation) have increased their focus on this issue (EC, 
2018; IMO, 2025).  
One of the problems with a LCA is that it can only conclude if a result is more sustainable 
than something else, which makes it a relative sustainability assessment tool (Paulillo & 
Sanyé-Mengual, 2024). In a time where we need to know if something is actually 
sustainable, something more is necessary. A tool to do this assessment is the Absolute 
Environmental Sustainability Assessment (AESA) methodology. AESA considers the planetary 
boundaries and the safe operating space, first developed by Rockström et al. (2009) and 
later updated by Steffen et al. (2015) and Richardson et al. (2023). AESA combines the 
planetary boundaries with the burdens calculated in the LCA, making it possible to assess 
whether something is sustainable within the planet.  
When it comes to “green” methanol there are two overall ways to produce it: bio-methanol 
and e-methanol. In Bio-methanol the carbon feedstock comes from a biogenic source that is 
either made from biomass gasification or made from upgrading biogas (MMMCZCS, 2025b). 
The difference between biomass gasification and biogas upgrading is that upgrading biogas 
is produced through biological anaerobic digestion creating biogas. Biomass gasification is 
produced through a thermochemical reaction that produces synthesis gas (syngas). Another 
difference is that making the bio-methanol, syngas is needed. In biogas upgrading an extra 
step is needed by reforming the biogas to syngas (IEA Bioenergy, 2020), this is shown in 
Figure 1 in simplified format.  
 
Figure 1: Simplified “green” methanol production pathways  
The E-methanol is also known as electro-methanol. E-methanol is a Renewable Fuel of Non-
Biological Origin (RFNBO), which means that the carbon does not come from a biological 
==PAGE:10==
 
 
 
Page 3 
 
source (MMMCZCS, 2025c). The carbon can either come from direct air capture, or from 
capturing the carbon from other industries, like waste incineration and cement production 
plants. E-methanol is made with hydrogen that comes from Power-to-X. Both types of 
methanol have a lot of different pathways when it comes to feedstock and production.  
Regardless of whether methanol is renewable or conventional, it is used in other industries. 
Especially in the production of various chemicals, methanol is used as chemical feedstock to 
e.g. formaldehyde and olefins (Methanol Institute, 2025b). In 2023, 11% of the overall 
demand for methanol was for gasoline blending and combustion. This includes both 
shipping and road transport (Methanol Institute, 2025c). The shipping industry is not the 
only industry that are currently working on making a sustainable transition. It is therefore 
important that methanol is used in the most optimized way, to ensure that the limited 
resources are used correctly. The same applies to Power-to-X. Every industry is trying to 
move away from the use of fossil resources. The way we use the produced energy becomes 
more and more important, and finding the optimal areas for use, can affect what is emitted. 
The aim of this study is therefore:  
• To investigate if “green” methanol in shipping is better for the climate than 
conventional fuels. 
• To answer if the “green” methanol is also better for other impact categories than 
climate change or are there burden shifting for these categories. 
• Is “green” methanol within the safe operating space of the planetary boundaries? 
• Investigating how sensitive the conclusions of this study are as other industries than 
shipping industry are also using methanol.  
This master thesis is done in collaboration with DFDS, a Danish ferry company that transport 
both passengers and freight and is one of Europe’s leading ferry operators (DFDS, 2025c). 
DFDS is themselves in the middle of their own green transition and trying to lower their own 
environmental impact.

2. Goal definition  
The aim of this study is to investigate if specific methanol scenarios are within the safe 
operating space of the planetary boundaries. This is investigated in two parts; the first part is 
to make a comparative LCA (life cycle assessment) with the three scenarios: Bio-methanol, E-
methanol and the conventional fuel VLSFO (very low sulphur fuel oil). The second part is to 
combine the LCA results with the planetary boundaries using the method Absolute 
Environmental Sustainability Assessment (AESA). The reasons for performing the study are 
to evaluate if the methanol scenarios are absolute sustainable and to compare the methanol 
scenarios with VLSFO for all impact categories.  
As mentioned above “green” methanol has a lot of different feedstock and production 
pathways especially for bio-methanol. It has therefore been necessary to make specific 
scenarios for the comparison. Due to this fact it is important to emphasise, that the 
scenarios in this study does not conclude on whether all E-methanol or Bio-methanol 
pathways are sustainable or not. This study will only be able to give information on the 
sustainability of the specific scenarios.  
This study focuses on the temporal scope of the fuels that will be produced and used in 
2030. It will contain some limitations in the sense that some of the information in this study 
will be projections. It will therefore include some uncertainty. As this study include an 
absolute environmental sustainability assessment, the study will follow attributional 
modelling principle, as this is required in AESA. 
This study is a master thesis, and it will be made publicly available on the DTU literature 
database. If it should be disclosed to the public, it is recommended that it is has an external 
review by a neutral third party.  
3. Scope Definition 
3.1 Functional unit and reference flows 
For a fair comparison of the different maritime fuel types the functional unit is defined as: 
The average combustion of 1MJ of maritime fuel used in a maritime vessel with a two-stroke 
engine in Europe in 2030. As the engine is an internal combustion engine (ICE), the methanol 
cannot run effectively on its own and therefore it needs a pilot fuel. The reason for this is the 
low cetane number (Cheng et al., 2023). The energy content is also approximately half of 
other more traditional fuels, based on the fuels lower calorific value (LCV) (EC, 2023a, 2018). 
Both bio- and e-methanol have a LCV of 19.9 MJ/kg and two traditional fuels Very Low 
Sulphur Fuel Oil (VLSFO) and Marine Gas Oil (MGO) has an LCV of 41 MJ/kg and 42.7 MJ/kg 
respectively (EC, 2023a, 2018). In this study the pilot fuel chosen is VLSFO.

3.3 Modelling requirements  
Regarding geographical representativeness, it would always be best to get data from a 
specific location. In this study the fuels will be produced all over the world. It is not always 
possible to get country specific data, especially when using databases like EcoInvent, as a lot 
of processes in EcoInvent is defined by a region like Europe and not for a specific country. 
Another problem occurring is that often there is one process for Europe and one for the rest 
of the world. In a study where every scenario is a theoretical scenario, some assumptions 
will be simplified and therefore some of the data will not represent a country correctly. It 
can specially be a problem with the bio-methanol, as the use of water and fertilizer are very 
dependent on the location when producing biomass. To try and prevent this, it has been 
important to find data, which is close to the original location, and if the data has a large 
impact, then the sensitivity are tested. 
As the study focuses on near-future shipping industry in 2030, it is of high importance that 
the data used in the modelling are as new as possible. The shipping industry is in rapid 
development. The same applies to the electricity use and optimisation, as we are in a 
transition period where the share of feedstock is changing from less fossil to increased share 
of renewable. Power-to-X is also being optimised to use less energy to produce the 
hydrogen. Power-to-X also includes part of the technological representativeness. Currently, 
renewable fuels are not 100% mature in all parts of their life cycle. There are still areas 
where there are present solutions, but further development is needed as bio-methanol and 
e-methanol can be produced in multiple ways (MMMCZCS, 2025a). One of areas for 
development is the infrastructure at the ports to handle and bunker the fuels (MMMCZCS, 
2025c). Due to the sensitive character of production, the technologies used are important to 
state in the modelling. When it has not been possible to do this, are made clear in the 
modelling description.

To allocate the global carrying capacity to the fuels, three different allocation principles have 
been selected, each with its advantages and disadvantages. The three chosen are 
Grandfathering (GF), Economic Value (EV), and Sufficiency Lifestyles (SL). How the allocation 
principles are specifically calculated, can be seen in the LCI section 4.4. After allocating a 
share of safe operating space to the fuels, the fuels can be compared to the allocated 
carrying capacities. Thereafter will be possible determine if the fuels are within the 
planetary boundaries and therefor are sustainable in the sense of this scope of study.  
4. LCI and the carrying capacity allocation 
The ship route used in the study is based on an already established route for DFDS: 
Amsterdam-Newcastle (DFDS, 2025d). The ship is assumed to be a roll-on/roll-off passenger 
ferry (Ro-Pax ferry), based on what is already in use on the route (DFDS, 2025d). It is 
assumed that the ship has a 2- stroke internal combustion engine (ICE) with a compression 
ignition (CI) (Malmgren et al., 2021). The bunkering of the fuel is assumed will only happen in 
Amsterdam, as the infrastructure needed for methanol is limited and currently only in few 
ports and development for larger scale is needed (MMMCZCS, 2025b). Today the route uses 
around 40 tons of Heavy Fuel Oil equivalents (HFOe) per sailing and generates around 40500 
MJ per ton HFOe (EC, 2023b). DFDS expects that there will be an improvement of the fuel 
consumption of up to 10%-15% with new ships replacing the 35-years-old ships currently in 
use. DFDS's goal is to replace the ships by 2030, but there is potential for delays. The 
optimised fuel efficiency is a result from technical and operational energy efficiency 
initiatives incentivised by IMO climate regulations, such as EEDI, CII and EEXI2 (IMO, 2023).

4.2 E-methanol 
 The production of three different E-methanol (E-MeOH) scenarios is modelled in this study, 
and they all follow the same structure as Figure 4 as mentioned earlier. The scenarios are 
modelled as following: first is a production site in Denmark (DK) in Esbjerg, this scenario will 
be referred to as: E-MeOH DK. The second scenario is modelled from Spain (ES) in Tarragona 
and will be referred to as: E-MeOH ES. The last site for production of E-MeOH is Texas USA in 
Corpus Christi and will be referred to as: E-MeOH TX. Texas is chosen as Texas is the state in 
the USA that produces the most wind power (Conte, 2022). The differences between the 
three scenarios are the technologies used for carbon capture and how the carbon capture 
gets the electricity. The carbon capture method is in all three scenarios post-combustion 
CO2 capture (PCC) technology. The carbon capture method chosen for Denmark are from 
municipal solid waste incineration (MSWI), as Denmark in the year 2022 incinerated 22% of 
their municipal solid waste (Hornung Thorndahl, 2024). From MSWI there is a potential to 
capture and use the CO2 generated in waste incineration, as the CO2 is emitted with the fuel 
gas generated at the present (Bisinella et al., 2022). Spain was in 2022 the fourth largest 
producer of cement in Europe (USGS, 2025). Producing cement is CO2 intensive if the CO2 is 
not collected (Gallego Dávila et al., 2023) and therefore the carbon capture comes from a 
cement plant in Spain in the last scenario. In Texas in 2022 they consumed around 5.3 trillion 
MJ natural gas (EIA, 2024). It is therefore decided that the capture method is from power 
plant that uses natural gas combined cycle (NGCC). 
Figure 4 also included that the electricity for the electrolyses and the methanol synthesis is 
from renewable sources. In the base scenarios, it is assumed, that the electricity will come 
from wind turbines, more specifically from wind turbines >3 MW for all three scenarios. This

4.2.1 Carbon capture and use  
For all three scenarios of E-MeOH the carbon capture system is added to an existing plant, 
but the plants themselves are excluded in the models, only the carbon capture system is 
included, this is done to simplify the process. To perform the carbon capture three processes 
are needed. First process is to separate the CO2 in the post-combustion. After the CO2 is 
separated the next step is to compress it. At the last process CO2 finally needs to be 
dehydrated to remove excess water and then CO2 is ready for transportation. It is assumed 
that the capture capacity for all three plants is 0.5 million tonnes CO2 per year (mtpa) and 
that the carbon capture technology is operational for 15 years, based on Emborg et al. 
(2024).  
There are different technologies to capture the CO2 in post-combustion, like cryogenic 
separation, membranes and chemical absorption (Gallego Dávila et al., 2023). In this study 
the technologies used is chemical absorption with the use of MEA (monoethanolamine) as a 
solvent. When using MEA to capture CO2 there is a loss of the solvent as it degrades 
(Bisinella et al., 2021), and the loss varies between the different systems. For E-MeOH DK it 
is assumed that the loss is 0.1 kg MEA per ton CO2 captured based on Bisinella et al. (2021). 
For E-MeOH ES, it is 0.6 kg MEA based on Gallego Dávila et al. (2023). The natural gas power 
plant from Texas is based on Feron et al. (2020), more specifically the Shell Cansolv data 
where the consumption of MEA is 0.1 kg per ton CO2 captured. The amount of water used in 
the three scenarios are based on the data from Emborg et al. (2024). The values for the 
different scenarios can be seen in Table 9.

In this study the production of two different Bio-methanol (Bio-MeOH) scenarios is

produce MeOH in Sweden is wood biomass, as Sweden is one of the largest forested areas in 
the EU (EEA, 2023).

6. Interpretation and Discussion 
6.1 Energy source and demand for E-MeOH scenarios 
When it comes to the use of energy for the E-MeOH, the LCIA results showed that the 
electricity used for both the AEL and the CCUs are two of the largest contributors to most of 
the impact categories in the WtT. To better understand how electricity use affects the 
results, a sensitivity analysis has been conducted on three key parameters. The first key 
parameter is the type of renewable energy, the second parameter is the amount of 
electricity used in AEL, and last parameter is the amount of electricity used in the CCU. In 
the Scope and the LCI, it was mentioned that all three base scenarios for E-MeOH were 
wind-powered electricity chosen as the renewable electricity source for the AEL and 
methanol synthesis. In contrast, the electricity type for the CCU varies depending on the 
scenario. To test if there is a difference in using solar-powered electricity, three scenarios are 
tested against the base scenario, as shown in Figure 16. The reason to specifically test solar 
energy, compared to other renewable energy sources is, that it is one of the cheapest 
renewable energy sources to install (IRENA, 2024). It also is not limited by geographical 
restrictions in the same ways both hydropower and ocean energy are (Clarke et al., 2022).   
 
Figure 16: Illustration of the different scenarios tested in the sensitivity of renewable electricity type. CUU = Carbon Capture 
and use, AEL = Alkaline Electrolysis. 
Illustrated in Figure 17 are the results of sensitivity for E-MeOH DK; the figures of E-MeOH ES 
and E-MeOH TX can be found in Appendix 11. Regarding E-MeOH DK, there are minimal 
changes in some impact categories when comparing the base scenario with the other 
scenarios like acidification, particulate matter, eutrophication (marine and terrestrial) and 
photochemical ozone formation. All of these categories are not a large contributor to the 
MeOH production stages, as shown in the LCIA, and the results of the sensitivity analysis 
reflect this. Regarding climate change, the scenario involving total wind energy has the 
lowest impact at 25.8 g CO2-eq per MJ, representing a nearly 40% reduction from the base 
scenario. The total solar energy plant is nearly the same as the base scenario, and there is a 
25% increase with the scenarios that use solar energy, except for the CCU. The one impact 
category where total solar energy is a much better alternative is when it comes to resource 
==PAGE:49==
 
 
 
Page 42 
 
use for minerals and metals. There is a reduction of nearly 80% for both solar scenarios. It 
makes sense that wind turbines require a large amount of steel and other metals and 
minerals used in the construction.  
 
Figure 17: Sensitivity analysis for E-MeOH DK electricity type. Results are internal normalisation to the base scenario. Base 
scenario = 1. 
The one with the biggest difference from the base scenarios is with land use; both solar 
scenarios (total solar and solar except CCU) are over 7 times larger than the base scenario. 
One reason for the massive increase may be the amount of surface area needed to capture 
sufficient sunlight. It can also be affected depending on what kind of land is transformed 
when modelling the process.  
The sensitivity analysis of E-MeOH ES and E-MeOH TX reveals that many impact categories 
exhibit similarities to the sensitivity of E-MeOH DK. For E-MeOH ES, the categories where 
there are differences are Ionising radiation, Resource use (fossil), Ecotoxicity (freshwater) 
and Water use. The Ionising radiation decreases significantly when the grid is not used, 
falling by more than 90%. This makes sense, given the presence of nuclear power in the grid 
(Cruz and Arribas, 2023). It also explains the decline in fossil fuel resource use when solar 
and wind power are utilised, as the grid contains more than 25% gas and coal (Cruz and 
Arribas, 2023). The Land use for solar is only 5 times the base scenario, primarily because 
solar energy is already integrated into the grid (Cruz and Arribas, 2023). The last scenario, E-
MeOH TX, is very similar to the two other scenarios’ sensitivity analysis. Notably, E-MeOH TX 
==PAGE:50==
 
 
 
Page 43 
 
shows the most considerable reduction in climate change with total wind, exceeding 50% 
compared to the base scenarios. 
The total wind scenarios of the sensitivity analysis are the best performing when it comes to 
Climate change for all the E-MeOH scenarios. The E-MeOH scenarios have also been tested 
with AESA, and the tables with the results can be found in Appendix 11. Comparing the 
three total wind scenarios reveals, that E-MeOH DK is the best overall. Comparing the 
original E-MeOH, Bio-MeOH, and VLSFO scenarios with the Total wind results reveals that 
the Total wind results are the most effective when applying the allocation principle 
economic value, particularly in terms of climate change. With the other two allocation 
principles, Bio-MeOH SE still have the lowest impact, but the total wind results are still lower 
than the original E-MeOH scenarios when it comes to Climate change.  
In the EU’s REDII and REDIII, the use of renewable electricity to produce RFNBO can be 
calculated as zero GHG emission. To achieve zero GHG emissions from renewable electricity, 
it must comply with the rules set by the directive. As noted by EC (2023d, 2018), the 
production of fuels is directly connected to the production site. Although renewable 
electricity is considered zero emission for climate change for production of energy, does it 
not mean it has no impact on other categories. With wind energy one impact category that 
is increasing more, is the Resource use for minerals and metals. It is therefore important to 
be aware of how the different renewables affects the environment and not think that 
renewable energies have no emissions at all.   
In the EU, it will not be possible to account for captured emissions as avoided emissions 
from 2035 if the combustion of non-sustainable fuels is used for electricity generation (EC, 
2023d). This will mean that E-MeOH DK and E-MeOH TX will not be able to use their own 
generated electricity without the CO2 emissions that were previously considered recycled, as 
they cannot be. In theory, it is possible to connect a wind turbine to an MSWI and natural 
gas power plant. The question is whether it is realistic for energy producers (MSWI and 
power plants) to purchase renewable electricity to capture their CO2 and then sell both the 
CO2 and the electricity they produce themselves to the electricity grid. If following the waste 
hierarchy and only using MSWI as a last step, it is permitted to use MSWI for recycled carbon 
fuels in RED II and III, as it is then considered an unavoidable and unintentional consequence 
of waste removal (EC, 2023d). Burning the MSW solely to generate heat and electricity and 
not adhering to the waste hierarchy categorises it as a non-sustainable fuel in the EU, as it 
incinerates fossil resources such as plastic in mixed waste (EC, 2023d). E-MeOH DK can 
therefore still be used, but only MSWI is following the waste hierarchy and using as a last 
resort instead of landfilling the waste.  
As mentioned, the second and third sensitivity parameters, are the electricity use of the AEL 
and the reboiler duty from the CCU. The sensitivity parameters will be based on Smolinka et 
al. (2018) progressive and conservative projection for the AEL in 2030, that is, 47.1 kWh and 
52.4 kWh per kg hydrogen, respectively. The reboiler duty of the CCU will be based on the

units. The test is done to check if Impact World+ have the same tendencies as EF3.1 when it 
comes to land use. The Figure with the Impact World+ results for Land use can be found in 
Appendix 11. Notably, the characterised result with Impact World+ exhibits the same 
tendencies as EF3.1, as shown in Figure Land use. Bio-MeOH SE is with Impact World+

LCIA method the second highest, and the VLSFO DE has the lowest impact. This is the same

MeOH SE spike compared to the other scenarios.

humans use at a high level.

6.6 MeOH in relation to RED II and RED II 
For E-MeOH to be considered a green fuel in RED, it must be under the threshold for E-fuels, 
which is a 70% reduction from the EU-defined fossil reference at 94 g CO2-eq./MJ (EC, 2023c, 
2018). E-MeOH therefore needs to be below 28.2 g CO2-eq./MJ for it to be considered 
“green” in RED. In RED, they are examining the fuels separately; therefore, to determine if 
the E-MeOH is below the threshold in this study, it is only the E-MeOH emissions that are 
considered, not the emissions from the pilot fuel. In Section 5.1, the carbon footprint for 
only MeOH is mentioned for all scenarios. Here in this study the only E-MeOH scenario 
below the threshold is E-MeOH ES with 26.3 g CO2-eq./MJ. E-MeOH DK is less than one gram 
CO2-eq. over the threshold, and E-MeOH TX is nearly over 20 g CO2-eq. It is essential to note 
that this study does not adhere to the guidelines outlined in RED II and RED III, as the base 
scenarios do not account for zero emissions with renewable electricity production in 
determining the carbon footprint. However, if the study is to follow the zero emissions with 
renewable electricity production, the E-MeOH DK is 18.2 g CO2-eq./MJ, the E-MeOH ES is 
15.2 g CO2-eq./MJ, and the E-MeOH TX is 37.7 g CO2-eq./MJ. Of the base scenarios, it is 
therefore only E-MeOH TX that is not considered a “green” E-fuel. The higher footprint for E-
MeOH TX makes sense as it the CCU uses electricity only from only natural gas which is a 
fossil fuel. Whereas the other two scenarios do not have a 100% fossil electricity used for 
CCU.  
Biofuels in EU RED needs a reduction target of 65% below the threshold to be considered 
“green”, therefore does the Bio-MeOH in this study need to be below 32.9 g CO2-eq./MJ. 
Both of the Bio-MeOH scenarios are therefore considered “green” biofuels. When 
comparing both the E-MeOH and Bio-MeOH scenarios in this study with other studies and 
MAN Energy Solutions document, the carbon footprints for Bio-MeOH SE and Bio-MeOH CN 
are similar to what is already found in LCA studies (Megan Roux et al., 2024) (Roux et al., 
2024). On the other hand, the E-MeOH scenarios are higher on what is found in other 
studies, but this may be due to the choice of electricity for the CCU, as most would use 
renewable energy (Roux et al., 2024; Zahm, 2025).

sensitive to the choice of allocation principle (Bjørn et al., 2020; Ryberg et al., 2020).  All

favours a high price regardless of the harm (Ryberg et al., 2018). The disadvantage of

6.8 Recommendations  
6.8.1 Industry recommendation 
When it comes to recommendations for the shipping industry and its partners, there are 
some things that need to be taken into consideration. The first thing is that none of the 
MeOH scenarios tested in this study are within the safe operating space for climate change. 
All MeOH scenarios tested do reduce the impact, but more reductions are needed. The 
shipping industry and partners, therefore, need to push for improvements in the 
technologies used to produce the MeOH and, at the same time, also improve the 
effectiveness of the ship's engines so that less fuel is needed. It is recommended that the 
shipping industry and its partners not focus solely on climate change as the primary impact 
category but also consider others, as there is a shifting of burdens between impact 
categories when comparing different fuels with each other.  
With E-MeOH, it is essential to note that renewable energy does not necessarily result in 
zero emissions for climate change and other impact categories. It is also important to be 
aware that, depending on the type of renewable energy, there may be an increase in other 
impact categories that can push already critical categories to have an even higher 
environmental burden. 
When it comes to Bio-MeOH, it is recommended not to use wood shavings to produce Bio-
MeOH as the feedstock is thoroughly processed and, therefore, has a higher impact than 
other wood feedstocks. It is also recommended that the shipping industry and its partners 
clarify whether the residues used in RED II and Red III are more by-products than residues, 
as there will be a market for these residues in the future. At the same time, the shipping 
industry and its partners should try to prevent increasing the production of these residues 
and main products as the new market emerges prevent the use of unspoilt nature.  
This study recommends that there needs to be more transparency regarding the origin of 
the feedstocks used in MeOH production. The transparency includes being more critical of 
the feedstock's origin. This is in the context of certification of sustainable feedstock, where 
some certifications include process that the scientific community do not define as 
sustainable. Participation and transparency from the shipping industry and its partners can 
help prevent products from being greenwashed.

6.8.2 Recommendation for further studies 
When it comes to recommendations for further studies, there are four overall areas: E-
MeOH production, Bio-MeOH production, engine and ship types, optimised use of MeOH, 
and lastly, the use of AESA in shipping.  
For E-MeOH production, it would involve testing more types of E-MeOH scenarios. The 
testing includes different types of electrolysis technologies, CCU technologies, as well as 
testing various carbon sources, such as a biogas plant and biomass burning, where the 
carbon is biogenic from the outset. The assumptions made in this study indicate that none of 
the scenarios are sustainable across all impact categories, and burden shifting occurs by 
using MeOH instead of VLSFO.   
Another recommendation for further studies is to find the optimal ways to utilise methanol, 
as the shipping industry is not the only sector that uses methanol. To further investigate 
whether methanol is used more effectively in other industries, what can be done to optimise 
the use of already established fuels so that they are better for the environment? Similar to 
adding a filter on the engine to collect NOx particles or capturing CO2 on the ship, etc. 
When it comes to further studies for Bio-MOH, it would be recommended to try different 
biomass feedstocks, both of which are mentioned in RED II, as well as others that have the 
potential to be suitable for producing MeOH. Another part that would be good to test is the 
other method to produce MOH biogas upgrading, as in this study, biomass gasification was 
used to produce Bio-MeOH. 
It would also be recommended to test other “green” fuels, such as ammonia, to determine if 
they are truly sustainable. This is because some alternative “green” fuels might be more 
sustainable than MeOH, or they could face other challenges and impact other categories 
differently than MeOH.  
Lastly, it would be recommended to test what would be required to make MeOH absolute 
sustainable for climate change (prospective LCA). Is it technology that needs to be more 
energy-efficient, or is it about using less feedstock, or making the engine more efficient on 
less fuel? It would also be interesting to investigate what is needed to change regarding the 
other impact categories if MeOH is absolute sustainable for climate changes, and what could 
lower the impacts of these categories. 
7. Conclusion  
This study aims to assess whether “green” MeOH falls within the planetary boundaries using 
an absolute environmental sustainability assessment. This research compared five different 
MeOH scenarios, comprising two Bio-MeOH scenarios and three E-MeOH scenarios, with 
the conventional fuel VLSFO. The results showed that the MeOH scenarios have a lower 
impact on climate change, but it is shifting some of the burden into other impact categories. 
Overall, Bio-MeOH with wood residues performs as one of the best in most impact 
==PAGE:62==
 
 
 
Page 55 
 
categories. However, when the wood shavings are viewed as a by-product, it can significantly 
impact the results, ranging from one of the best to one of the worst, simply by adjusting the 
allocation of emissions to the wood shavings.  
When it comes to the production of E-MeOH, the electricity used in the production stages 
(carbon capture, electrolysis, and MeOH synthesis) has the most considerable impact on 
multiple impact categories; therefore, the choice of electricity is also important. As it can 
lower the impact on climate change, it can also considerably increase the impact on other 
categories.  
A limitation of the study is that it is difficult to argue for or against using MeOH in the 
shipping industry based on the results of the absolute environmental sustainability 
assessment, as there is a large variation in the impact over the three different allocated 
boundaries. Further research could therefore explore how much it would require, under 
different allocation principles, to achieve absolute sustainability (prospective LCA), possibly 
first for climate change but also other impact categories. Ultimately, recognising that at the 
moment, MeOH with pilot fuel (both Bio- and E-MeOH) is not absolute sustainable for the 
shipping industry, but could be essential for the industry in the future if there is future 
research to back up the claims.

7.8 Resource use, fossils 
In the Resource Use, Fossils impact category, all emissions across all scenarios originate from 
the WtT phase, as illustrated in Figure 28. Among the scenarios, the two VLSFO cases show 
the highest impact, while the Bio-MeOH SE scenarios exhibit the lowest. The dominant 
contributor in both VLSFO scenarios is the use of heavy fuel oil during the desulphurisation 
process, accounting for 94% of the impact in VLSFO CN and 96% in VLSFO DE. Notably, VLSFO 
DE has a 3% lower impact compared to VLSFO CN. 
As also shown in Figure 28, the WtT emissions from VLSFO contribute significantly to the 
total impact in the E-MeOH DK, Bio-MeOH SE, and Bio-MeOH CN scenarios. In E-MeOH DK, 
56% of the impact is attributed to pilot fuel production, while the remaining contribution 
comes primarily from the electricity used in the AEL. In Bio-MeOH SE, approximately 61% of 
the impact stems from VLSFO production, with the remaining 39% originating mainly from 
the shavings used in MeOH production. For the Bio-MeOH CN scenario, the impact is nearly 
evenly split, with 51% from WtT MeOH and 49% from WtT VLSFO. Here, the electricity used 
in methanol production is the main contributor to the WtT MeOH impact.  
In the remaining scenarios, E-MeOH ES and E-MeOH TX, the WtT emissions from MeOH 
production dominates, accounting for 75% and 79% of the total impact, respectively. In both 
cases, the primary source is the electricity used in the CCU process. Finally, the choice of 
pilot fuel has a minimal effect on this impact category across all MeOH scenarios, with 
differences remaining below 2%.
