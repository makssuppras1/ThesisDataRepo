Abstract
HOVMAND A/S is a medium sized company employing 70 people and having a
turnover of 108 Mio. DKK in 2022. The company is know for producing electrical
mobile lifters used in a range of industries such as industrial production or food
production. The lifters are structured in an architecture consisting of three core
modules:
1. Complete masts
2. Leg-sets
3. Tools
HOVMAND has three types of ES categories; ”Stretch”, ”Complex” and ”Projects”,
with the focus of this report being on ”Standard”, ”Stretch” and ”Complex” orders.
After collecting data from a variety of sources it was found that historically there
is claims on ≈9% and reported production errors on 8.5% of orders. Further it
was discovered that on deals the split of value between standard and ES approxi-
mately 50/50, however at component level ES only represents about 5% of value.
A total of 330 ES orders have been placed between 01/01-2022 and 22/02-2023,
with an average processing time of 6 hours.
The profit margin of standard components were found to be 64% (59% excluding
masts) whereas the profit margin of ES components is 41%, when considering
the processing time.
The findings above lead to the hypotheses that the way ES orders enter HOV-
MAND is conductive to errors/misunderstandings and that the master data is de-
ficient.
An investigation of the front end and back end was performed which lead to the
confirmation of the hypotheses above, finding errors on 90% of ES quotes and
70% of standard quotes on the front end. On the back end an error was found on
all investigated cases, with errors mainly being with revisions and BOM errors.
As described in the book ”Product Customization” by Lars Hvam, Niels Henrik
Mortensen and Jesper Riis, the larger the amount of errors the less efficient the
employees will be. With this motivation and the results found a number of im-
provements is suggested, the three main being:
1. Simplifying the modular structure
2. Implementing master models and drawings
3. Implementing modular ERP BOM’s
Implementing the suggested improvements is expected to reduce the amount of
BOM’s from 428 to either 81 or 56, halve the amount of claims, halve the amount
of ES processing time and reduce the component acquisitions spend by 5%. The
attributable savings is expected to be 3.415.000 DKK. The cost of implementation
is expected to be 1.250.000 DKK plus training of the sales representatives and
will take 1 year.

1
About Hovmand A/S
Hovmand A/S (mentioned as HOVMAND from now on) was founded in 1972 in a
small village near Sorø. The founders were Hans Christian and Bibi Hovmand with
the company starting out producing specially designed polyurethane machines.
After a number of years the company then known as H.C. Hovmand ApS diversi-
fied and started selling sack trucks and in 1995 the first electric lifter was launched.
In 1999 the part of the company focused on polyurethane was sold off, and focus
was changed to mobile lifting solutions [2]. In 2022 HOVMAND has grown to
reach a revenue of over 108 mio. DKK and employing 70 people [4].
1.1
Products and structure
As described previously HOVMAND produces mobile lifting units. The mobile lifter
is essentially a lifting column on wheels and a tool for lifting reels, boxes, wheels
and all other manner of objects. An example of a HOVMAND lifter is shown below
on figure 1.1. In this case it is a lifter of the IMPACT family, with a BOX-type leg
set and a fork for lifting boxes.
Figure 1.1: HOVMAND modular architecture

The lifters produced by HOVMAND come in a variety of sizes, heights and lifting
capacities, ranging from a lifting capacity of 60kg with the GO-lift and all the way
up to 300kg with the E300 series of lifters. HOVMAND claim that their lifters are
lightweight, resilient and highly customize-able, due to the modularity in the design
of the lifters. All lifter families are based on an product architecture consisting of
three core modules:
1. The Complete Mast
2. The Leg-set
3. The Tool
1.1.1
Masts
The mast is the module where the lifting force is created. In addition to a lifting
column that produces the lifting force, it also consists of a control box that houses
the batteries and the controls for the lifter and some tools. The final component
included in the mast is the handle, which is the part the user interacts with to push
the lifter and may also include buttons to operate the lifter. All together they are
refereed to as a ”complete mast” and is at the core of all mobile lifting units as all
other modules attach to the mast. The masts have two interfaces to other mod-
ules; one for the tool and one for the legs. In addition to fitting into the overall
module structure the mast itself is also modular such that different control boxes
or handles will fit onto the same lifting column.
The masts are available in set heights, usually a low (L), medium (M) and high
(H) and is the module that dictates which which leg-sets, tools and options are
available. Some masts are also available as a ”-3” variant that allows the mast to
control electrically operated tools. The masts are divided into the following fami-
lies:
IMPACT
The IMPACT family is the most popular lifter family that HOVMAND offers and
is responsible for the majority of sales in terms of both amount, income and rev-
enue. Is is available in the following series IMPACT 70, IMPACT 80, IMPACT 90,
IMPACT 130, IMPACT 200 and IMPACT 200-3, where the number indicates the
maximum load in kilograms. All IMPACT masts are also available in a variety of
heights. In addition HOVMAND offers several options, like anti-static masts, a
different handle or a swap-able battery pack. This means that the IMPACT series
has a total of 142 standard variations.
The IMPACT masts are made in aluminum and are well-suited in working in most
areas where tight cleaning regulations is not a significant factor. The tool interface
is shared with the INOX and IMPOX series.
INOX
The INOX family is the second most popular lifter family at HOVMAND, available
as the INOX 90, INOX 90-3, INOX 200 or INOX 200-3. The INOX series are all
made of stainless steel and are specifically designed with cleaning and hygiene
in mind. The INOX masts also available in anti-static versions and swap-able bat-
teries, but not with an alternative handle, yielding a total of 35 variations. The tool
interfaces are shared with the IMPACT and IMPOX series.

IMPOX
The IMPOX family of lifters is a small selection of masts available in aluminum.
The masts in this series is only the IMPOX 70, which is only available with stain-
less steel tools and legs. The only option for the IMPOX series is an anti-static
mast, yielding a total of 6 variations. Like the INOX series the IMPOX series is
meant for applications with significant demands for hygiene. The tool interfaces
are shared with the IMPACT and INOX series.
E300
The E300 lifter is the heavy duty offering from HOVMAND, capable of lifting loads
of up to 300kg. The E300 series is available as the E300 and E300-3. This series
has no special options as all masts are anti-static and have swap-able batteries,
this means that there is a total of 9 variations of the E300. The material for all E300
masts, tools and legs is stainless steel, making it ideal for applications where hy-
giene is a priority. Both the tool and leg interface is unique, meaning that tools
from other product families does not fit the E300 series of lifters.
GO-lifter
The GO-lifter is a single unique mast that fits the role of a cost-effective lifter. It is
meant for most light-duty applications and has a lifting capacity between 60 and
70 kilograms, depending on which of the two tools is chosen. The GO lifter is only
available with one type of leg-set and either a platform or a tool for plastic cans.
Both the tools and leg set are propriety and therefore only fits the GO lifter.
Compact
The Compact lifter is a single lifting column capable of lifting between 60 and
100kg depending on the tool used. The main unique selling point of the Compact
lifter is the small form factor. Like the GO-lifter, the Compact has unique tool and
leg-set interfaces, and there is no special options offered. The Compact lifter is
often sold as OEM.
1.1.2
Leg-sets
The leg-set is the module that allows the lifter to moved around so that it can be
used to lift a load. Further it also acts as a stabilizing factor to ensure that the lifter
doesn’t tip during operation. The leg-sets come in many variations, with different
lengths, widths, materials and some with adjustable widths. The leg-sets attach
directly to the masts and are also available in custom lengths and widths. The leg-
sets is one of the modules that get modified most often to fit customer demands.
1.1.3
Tools
The tool is the module that is attached to the mast and holds the load. As rule
of thumb the tools are categorised in two groups, complex tools and simple tools.
The simple tools are split into the following four categories:

1. Booms
2. Crane Arm
3. Forks
4. Platforms
The complex tools all have in common that they have moving parts. The complex
tools are:
1. Clamp and turn
2. Reel manipulators
Not all tools fit all masts, since the tool interface for all masts are not identical.
Further, specifically the tools made for the INOX family are meant for use in envi-

steel, unlike tools for other mast families.
While HOVMAND offers engineered solutions of all modules, the modules that
most often are engineered are the simple tools, more specifically the platforms
and booms (appendix A.1).
1.1.4
Options
In addition to the mentioned modules, HOVMAND offers a variety of options for
their lifters that in some way interacts with the interfaces one or more of the mod-
ules. Examples of options are extenders or quick tool change brackets. The
options may be exclusive to particular product families or they may be shared be-
tween some or all product families.
1.2
Order resolution types
Within HOVMAND incoming orders are split into four different groups:
1. Standard
2. Stretch
3. Complex
4. Project
The three categories ”Stretch”, ”Complex” and ”Projects” all include a non-standard
component and are as a group referred to as ”Engineered solutions” (ES) or ”En-
gineer to order”.
1.2.1
Standard orders
Standard orders are defined by the fact that all components on the sales order can
be is found in HOVMAND’s product catalogue, price list and product configurator.
Dealers and customers can make purchase orders for standard orders directly, or
through the sales department.
4

1.2.2
Stretch orders
A ”Stretch” order is characterized by the fact that one or more components are
non-standard thus are therefore not found in the product configurator or price list.
However within the price list for some components a range is given for certain
dimensions that can be customized by the customer, as well as a matching price.
If an order has one or more components that meets these demands, the order is
labeled ”Stretch”. Some dealers can place orders for ”stretch” directly, but cus-
tomers must order through the sales department.
1.2.3
Complex orders
Much like ”Stretch” orders, ”Complex” orders are characterized by the fact that
non-standard components are found on the sales order. However, unlike stretch
orders, ”Complex” orders, does not have predefined range of dimensions that
can be customized and the price is unique to each order. Therefore every time a
”Complex” order is offered the engineering department in operations is consulted
to discuss feasibility and price. Dealers and customers must contact the sales
department in order to place an order with ”Complex” components.
1.2.4
Project orders
The final order type is called ”Project” orders. They are defined by having a large
amount of complexity and representing significant value (+1 Mio. DKK). Projects
are an ES order type that unlike ”Stretch” and ”Complex” orders are not handled by
the Operations engineering team in the development phase. Instead these types
of orders are handled by the Project Sales department, and follows a separate
flow. Project Sales will also channel all communication with the customer, up
until the finished lifter is delivered. During the analysis, due to the separate flow
and comparatively low order share, projects have been largely neglected. In this
report the impact of ”project” type orders have largely been neglected due to the
comparatively low amount of project orders.

1.3
Company structure
Figure 1.2: Departments within Hovmand A/S
The HOVMAND organization consists of the main company with headquarters in
Køge, Denmark and a series of subsidiaries located in Germany, Switzerland and
France. The role of the subsidiaries is exclusively sales thus product develop-
ment and ES product processing is exclusively done at the Danish headquarters.
Figure 1.2 above illustrates the six main departments and how they relate to the
CEO and board of directors.
In addition to the subsidiaries, HOVMAND is also partnered with a selection of
other companies that has HOVMAND’s products in their portfolio, internally re-
ferred to as ”Dealers”.
Below is a brief description of all six departments, their key tasks and their relation
to the analysis.
1.3.1
Sales
The sales department is responsible for introducing customers to HOVMAND’s
product portfolio. Further it is important that the sales agents has to be able to
understand HOVMAND’ products and their unique sales points. The sales de-
partment is split into ”inside sales” and ”sales”, the difference being the type of
customers served. ”Inside sales” serves customers ”inside” HOVMAND’s circle
of influence, meaning dealers and subsidiaries whereas ”Sales” serves all other
customers. Depending on the complexity of the products requested by the cus-
tomer, the sales department can either offer on-site consultancy or discuss solu-
tions online. It is the sales department that creates offers for the customers, which
turns into sales orders if accepted.

One of the most prominent KPI’s of the sales department is the turnover of sales.
This is reinforced with a bonus-arrangement for the sales-agents, depending on
the amount sold.
The insight into this department has been provided by the Head of Sales, which
has extracted data regarding deal resolution time, industry and their win-rates.

Unit of measure
This variable is describes whether an item number is cut in a certain length or
counted in pieces. On the sales order components will always be counted in
pieces, but in the extracted it can be: ”Stück”, ”Pcs.”, or ”Stk.” (German, English

which, the language of the unit of measure has been used to determine the unit
of the price. It is assumed that if the unit of measure is ”Stk.” then the price will
always be in DKK, and if it’s ”Pcs.” or ”Stück” the price is in €. After converting all
prices to DKK, any prices that were obviously incorrect was then corrected man-
ually.
Quantity
A number that represents the quantity of items sold at a certain sales order line.
Used to calculate the value of each sales order line.

The price of which each item on the sales order was sold at. The price may be in

extracted, and where the unit price was missing in the sales order line, the item
number was found and the unit price was substituted. This parameter is used to
calculate the value of each sales order line.

Claims
Whenever a customer feels that their HOVMAND product does not live up to their
expectations, they have the ability to report back to HOVMAND. When HOVMAND
Efficiency Improvements in ETO companies

==PAGE:38==
receives such an inquiry it is registered as a claim in customer service. Following
registration customer service finds a resolution that both the customer and HOV-
MAND finds satisfying. Claims in HOVMAND are categorized into three main
categories:
2.5.1
Transport damage
A claim is categorized as transport damage if the product delivered to the customer
is of the correct typing and works correctly, but damages on the product are found
that was not present when the product was shipped. The reason why customers
might receive products with transport damages are mostly due to rough handling
by the courier. HOVMAND does some options to try to limit transport damages.
These include ensuring that the products themselves are robust to bumps and
knocks that might happen during transport and packaging the products such that
they are at a diminished risk of getting damaged during transport.
2.5.2
Functional error
A claim is labelled as having a functional error if the customer has received a prod-
uct that does not work as intended or at all. There can be multiple ways that this
might happen, including HOVMAND offering the wrong tool for the customer’s de-
mand or an ES solution being sent to the customer and not working as intended.
HOVMAND can minimize this type of claims by ensuring that the customer setup
and the HOVMAND product portfolio are both properly understood, such that the
correct product or product adjustments are sold to the customer.
2.5.3
Wrong deliveries
The third claim type is called ”Wrong delivery”, if the customer has received some-
thing on a order that does not match their expectations. An example could be that
the customer has requested that the tool of their lifter comes separate and not
mounted to the lifter. Another example could be if the customer receives another
component than requested or if a component (like a charger) is not included in
the packaging. Wrong deliveries can be cause by misunderstandings or lack of
information that means that important information is not shared with the neces-
sary employees.

4.3
Modular BOM’s in ERP
In HOVMAND currently the ERP and CAD systems are completely independent.
This means that every BOM within CAD has a manually created twin within the
ERP system. Further, all BOM’s in ERP are independent of each-other even if the
products are related. The consequence of this is a large amount of ERP BOM’s
that are manually created and updated whenever a product are revised. As all
the work regarding maintenance and updating BOM’s is done manually and re-
peatedly there is an increased risk of errors being created. It also means that if
a change is due for a whole product family, all family member BOM’s must be
updated independently.

4.3.1
What are modular BOM’s?
Instead of all BOM’s within the same product family being created from scratch
and independently, it is suggested to find the common quantity in each product
famliy and creating a BOM within ERP that contains it. The common quantity can
then be called by all members of that family. If this is implemented all BOM’s
that are part of a product family will contain the common quantity for the product
family, as well as the components unique to that specific variation. Modular BOM’s
can be implemented on all types of modules, but will be especially efficient on
mast families as the commonalities are plentiful between many mast variations.

Additional change recommendations
Other than simplifying the CAD models, using master drawings/models and using
modular BOM’s a number of other improvements are also recommended to im-

4.4.1
Location based job-cards
After consulting a number of production workers it was unveiled that the locations
of job-cards of any given component is not sorted according to their locations (ex-
amples in appendix A.15). This means that an inexperienced production worker
will have to go back and forth between shelves in the storage. Otherwise they
might go through the locations manually and try to plan a route to avoid repeat
visits to the same shelf. Even experienced production workers is expected to
have to make repeat visits to the same shelf in case of complicated products,
wasting time.
Therefore it is suggested to sort all job-cards according to the component entry
placement. This is expected to save time in the production and increase produc-
tion efficiency.
4.4.2
Remove errors in documentation
In order to have maximal productivity errors in documentation for ES and espe-
cially standard components should be without errors. It is especially important
on standard components since stretch parts and occasionally complex orders are
copied and changed version of standard components. Therefore errors on stan-
dard components will spread to ES components as well.
CAD drawings and models should be standardized and unambiguous. Further,
there should be no difference between the ERP BOM and CAD BOM.
It is suggested to start by reviewing the most sold standard products first such as
the IMPACT family of masts. Thereafter components that are often ”stretched”
such as forks, booms and platforms should be reviewed and finally the leg-sets.
If some ES solutions are sold repeatedly, these should then be reviewed.
4.4.3
ES solutions archive
After consulting a sales representative it was discovered that the sales team does
not have access to previously produced ES solutions. This is not a problem for
stretch orders, since they are just standard orders with custom dimensions, but
complex order may vary greatly. Since there is also a significantly larger devel-
opment cost of complex orders it would be beneficial to create a drawing library
that the sales department can also navigate in. This would ensure that resources
are not spent on developing new principles when a solution has already been de-
veloped beforehand.
4.4.4
Training sales department in product USP’s
It is recommended to ensure that the sales representatives has a good under-
standing of the USP’s of all products in HOVMAND’s portfolio. During the in-
vestigation of the ”front end” on case SO130436 it was discovered that a sales
representative sold a leg-set as a complex order (new wheels), even though the
wheels chosen by the customer was identical to the existing wheels. This lead to

a lot of wasted time for both the the sales representative and ES team. Training
of the sales representatives is expected to impact both the amount of processing
time for ES cases and the amount of functional error claims.
4.4.5
Revision control
In order to minimize the amount of BOM’s with different revisions in CAD and ERP
it is recommended to make the ERP-system able to read the active revision of any
given item in CAD. Making the two systems interdependent is expected to lower
the amount of errors with differing BOM’s and revisions as no human will be in-
volved in updating them.
4.4.6
Simplify or remove approval of stretch drawings
Stretch orders are defined as being standard items with custom dimension (within
a set interval) and a set price. However they still follow the same engineering
flow as complex orders, even though they are simpler than complex orders. That
means a drawing is made of the full lifter that then has to be approved by the
customer. This often takes time, with the average time between drawing sent and
approval is nine calendar days (appendix A.1). In order to reduce this time for
approval, stretch orders could follow a separate approval process. For example,
instead of creating approval drawings for the total lifter, the custom measurements
could just be marked on a master drawing and implemented, bypassing the need
for an approval. It is recommended to launch an investigation to decide the most
appropriate stretch approval process.
