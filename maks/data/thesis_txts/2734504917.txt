Abstract
The objective of this thesis is to analyse the feasibility and poten-
tial benefits of energy communities as precursors to Positive En-
ergy Districts. Using a quantitative methodological approach that
involves energy system analysis employing EnergyPRO, this study
emphasises the cooperative dynamics necessary between centralised
power plants and energy communities.
The integration of local
community-based heat pumps, alongside considerations of transi-
tioning to low-temperature district heating, is analysed with a focus
on its economic viability. Energy communities are explored as viable
solutions to address challenges in meeting rising generation demand,
ensuring capacity, and optimising overall system efficiency. Partic-
ularly in anticipation of changes in the energy market such as the
energy crisis, and expensive fuel and electricity pricing. The collab-
orative synergy between centralised and local production, coupled
with strategic storage, is analysed to show economic feasibility and
operational efficiency. This thesis contributes insights into sustain-
able urban development, offering specific findings such as flexibility,
particularly from the association of local PV electricity combined
with HP heat production and sustainability associated with ther-
mal storage. The findings of this thesis contribute to sustainable
urban development discussions, providing insight and opportunities
for future investigations into PEDs and energy communities.

This thesis was prepared at the Technical University of Denmark
(DTU) to fulfil the requirements for acquiring an MSc degree in
Sustainable Energy (Energy System Analysis). The primary objec-

module-based optimisation tool, this software cultivates an in-depth
analysis of the energy system, with a specific focus on the district

vant background knowledge and details related to the scenario de-
sign.

market dynamics impact feasibility.
Each chapter contributes to
an exploration of the characteristics within the chosen domain of

sustainable energy systems

Chapter 1
Introduction
As urbanisation intensifies, cities expand, and the demand for con-
sumer energy rises, the importance of sustainable and energy-efficient
urban development becomes more apparent.
A prominent solu-
tion especially in the European Union, is Positive Energy Districts
(PEDs). Simply put, PEDs are urban areas or communities where
collective energy generation exceeds consumption, resulting in a net-
positive impact on the energy grid. This framework builds upon and
advances key concepts like Nearly Zero Energy Buildings (NZEBs)
and Zero Energy Buildings (ZEBs), fostering a collaborative ap-
proach for broader-scale energy efficiency.
Facing climate change and the challenges caused by gradually in-
creasing urbanisation, Positive Energy Districts and energy commu-
nities appear as innovative solutions, seamlessly integrating energy-
efficient structures and renewable sources. This study seeks to anal-
yse the economic and operational implications of energy commu-
nities in connection to centralised power plants, emphasising co-
operative dynamics, economic viability, operational efficiency, and
the influential role of heat pump integration. The study addresses
necessary questions regarding the collaboration between centralised
and local energy production, storage capacities, and the objective of
an economically feasible and operationally efficient energy system.
Through a techno-economic approach, the goal of the study is to
provide insights into sustainable urban development within the con-
text of Positive Energy Districts, contributing to progressive urban
energy solutions.

Chapter 2
Theoretical Framework
This chapter introduces the theoretical framework, exploring the
underlying motivation that drives this research.
The theoretical
framework facilitates a deeper understanding of PEDs, the impor-
tance of knowledgeable choices and decision making and how the
Danish framework enables the work of PEDs- This chapter presents
Positive Energy Districts and details the transition process towards
sustainable housing in the urban environment.
2.1
Positive Energy Districts
In essence, Positive Energy Districts represent energy-efficient and
flexible urban areas or communities striving for net-zero energy con-
sumption. They actively manage an annual local or regional surplus
production of renewable energy [Eur22].
At its core, PEDs con-
centrate on cultivating collective energy-efficient housing, building
upon the principles of Nearly Zero Energy Buildings (NZEBs) and
Zero Energy Buildings (ZEBs) [Ber19]. In both the EU and Den-
mark, buildings account for approximately 40% of the total energy
consumption [DEA22].
Emphasising the concept of energy-efficient housing, PEDs recognise
the substantial energy savings potential offered by buildings. It is
highlighted that the scale of Nearly Zero Energy Buildings and Zero
Energy Buildings is too limited, and a larger collection of buildings
is required for optimising overall energy utilisation [Ber19].
Furthermore, the PED concept diverges from conventional urban
development models by heavily relying on the seamless integration
of renewable energy sources in sustainable urban living [ea23].
Unlike traditional urban development, PEDs adopt a collaborative

approach to both energy consumption and generation. This involves
encouraging synergy between interconnected buildings and promot-
ing energy sharing. District-wide energy planning takes into account
the collective needs of the community, ensuring a holistic and in-
terconnected energy system [ENSa]. Political incentives, subsidies,
and regulations play a pivotal role in enabling the PED framework
[RMJ23]. These policies are crucial for incentivising energy-efficient
construction, promoting the adoption of renewable energy, and sup-
porting sustainable urban planning in the form of energy-efficient
housing. Smart grid technologies are instrumental in PEDs, facil-
itating efficient energy distribution, storage, and management, en-
suring a stable and reliable energy supply in the community [Eur22].
PEDs recognise the significance of community engagement as a cor-
nerstone for success. Initiatives are underway to foster a sense of
shared responsibility among individuals and businesses [RMJ23]. By
actively participating in the energy ecosystem, individuals become
key contributors to the positive energy balance of the community.
This community-centric approach is essential for the long-term sus-
tainability, the success of PEDs and the potential expansion of pilot
projects as depicted in Figure 2.1.

2.2
Choice Awareness
In meeting the engagement requirements of PEDs, effective scientific
communication is necessary to provide knowledge. In this context,
awareness becomes the precursor to choice, and choice becomes the
catalyst for change. This section follows the similarities of Choice
Awareness Theory by H.Lund [Lun14] and the emphasis of the DTU
curriculum of Theory of Science [DTU]. While technological inno-
vation is undeniably crucial, the primary challenge does not solely
rely on the technology itself. Instead, the novelty of PEDs lies in
the transformation of services, management, policies, and infras-
tructural improvements [SSC].
Information serves as a valuable tool in creating awareness and high-
lighting the ongoing technological changes globally. It plays a pivotal
role as a guide for navigating the societal debate surrounding this
dilemma, emphasising the importance of informed choices and their
role in facilitating positive change.

2.2.1
Legal Framework - Challenges regarding Choice in
the Danish Market
Concerning choice awareness, initial steps towards integrating so-
lar photovoltaic (PV) is a widely recognised and popular choice for
local electricity production. It is favoured for its simplicity and con-
tributes to the positive efforts desired for Positive Energy Districts
and sustainable solutions. This section delves into some of the intri-
cate legal and regulatory frameworks that hinder the sharing factors
of energy communities.
The frameworks for installing local solar PV systems are intricate,
due to the necessity of dividing activities into separate companies.
Regions or individual municipalities have the flexibility to choose
the company structure for this purpose, provided that the munici-
pality is not held liable for the company’s actions.
The chosen company structure can be the municipality’s existing
utility company with limited liability or a newly established joint-
stock company (A/S) or public limited company (ApS), either fully
or partially owned by the municipality. Municipalities and regions
also have the option to collaborate with a jointly owned company
to establish and operate solar-powered systems across multiple mu-
nicipalities or regions [ENSb].
The complexity of laws and regulations poses a significant challenge
in the initial phases of designing Positive Energy Districts. Owner-
ship of two land registers by the same individual or business requires
each land register to be registered as a separate company. Conse-
quently, the output from a PV system cannot be transferred from

trade [ENSb].
As a response to these legal complexities, current PED prototypes
often involve the municipality or a cooperative entity serving as the
administrative party. This approach encourages a culture of con-

the collective effort to limit the rise in global temperatures to below
2°C preferably limiting it to 1.5°C. This commitment is illustrated
through emission pathways, providing a visual representation of how
emissions can and are expected to evolve towards 2100, as depicted
in Figure 2.4.

ent narratives and scenarios related to the future of energy systems
[oE].
Municipalities play a pivotal role in shaping the future energy sys-
tem, which is increasingly focused on local energy sources [UD].
Local energy planning authorities are entrusted with aligning local
initiatives with national and European energy goals. This includes

Strategic energy planning, surrounding long-term planning, scenario

In this thesis, strategic energy planning incorporates techno-economic

2.5.1
Electrification of the District Heating Sector
While biomass continues to be the dominant source of renewable
district heating, recent years have seen a growing contribution from
heat production through solar collectors and heat pumps, utilising
renewable electricity [DEA]. This shift aligns with the targets to
phase out CHP production based on gas as its fuel source.
The
trajectory also involves the continued deployment of wind power
and photovoltaic for electricity production, alongside the expanded
use of heat pumps for district heating [VEK].
As part of a broader national goal, Denmark is actively working to-
wards reducing dependency on fossil fuels, including coal, oil, and
gas-fired CHP plants [Ageb]. The ongoing transition involves re-
placing these fossil fuel-based plants with alternative technologies.
Notably, many centralised coal-fired plants have already been re-
configured to operate on biomass, reflecting the commitment to sus-
tainable energy practices [Ageb]. The collective heat supply in the
greater cities of Denmark is primarily facilitated by district heating
technology and regulated by the Heat Supply Act [DEA22]. In this
context, regions play a crucial role in taking charge of heat plan-
ning, grid expansion, and delineation of various forms of collective
heating.
To enhance the flexibility of the district heating/Combined Heat
and Power system, various technical measures can be implemented.
These measures include the integration of wind power and/or pho-
tovoltaic systems, utilising electric boilers, and incorporating heat
pumps. This approach allows DH plants to utilise excess electricity
for heating production, adding value to heating storage.
The implementation of these measures allows energy plants to opti-

2.5.2
Low-temperature district heating
In many Danish district heating networks, incentives have been di-
rected towards reducing flow temperatures, which typically range
around 80°C or higher, with return temperatures typically between
40-45°C [ENSa]. In specific cases, the flow temperatures can be as
low as 50/25°C [ENSa]. The energy production census from 2020
indicates a notable increase in the DH production share from fuel-
free sources, primarily attributed to the adoption of heat pumps and
electric boilers. Complemented by the growing use of solar heating,
geothermal heating, and the integration of surplus and waste heat
[DEA].
In district heating networks, distribution pipes often encounter sub-
stantial heat losses, reaching up to 20–25% of the total heat load, as
reported by Grundfos [Grua]. Following a similar rationale, it can be
assumed that by utilising low-temperature temperatures, the heat
loss could be reduced to an estimated range of 15-20% [Grua]. Ini-
tially, the high temperatures facilitate efficient heat transfer. Higher
forward temperatures were assumed to enable effective heat distribu-
tion. However, the municipality of Albertlunds has in collaboration
with Grundfoss, implemented their iGRID mixing system to reduce
heat losses in the pipes and save energy [Grub]. Adopting a 60/30°C
configuration finalised by 2026, the system has already proven a
cost-beneficial solution for the low-temperature strategy. Moreover,
the utility is also seeing the benefits of utilising renewable energy
sources and surplus heat from buildings, minimising heat losses and
making the overall system more efficient [Grub].
Waste heat from high-temperature production is an often-used heat

source for district heating networks in Denmark. Waste-to-energy
production and CHP plants are particularly effective solutions for
utilising waste heat [DEA]. The waste heat generated in these pro-
cesses can be harnessed without the need for heat pumps, as the
temperature levels are sufficient for the standard required network
temperatures.
Moreover, excess heat from various industrial sources, when com-

type of low thermal waste heat, when paired with heat pumps, of-

network [ENSa].
By optimising the district heating network to operate at lower tem-

tating the integration of a broader spectrum of heat sources. This
contributes to overall sustainability and aligns with the principles
of Positive Energy Districts.

==PAGE:47==
5.3 High and Low District Heating Temperature
17
Assumptions have been made to analyse the incorporation of en-
ergy communities in both a standard and low-temperature district
heating network, hence it is assumed as two identical models as dif-
ferent scenarios. The actual change to low temperatures will require
substantial infrastructure changes, investments and other economic
implementations [ENSa].
The model operates under the assumption that the return flow
within the district heating network can be recycled and reheated
to forward temperatures [DEA].
This assumption is rooted in a
semi-closed system, where the return temperature after the heat
has been utilised, circulates back within the system. Specifically,
the return temperature is intended to loop either back to the dis-
trict heating plant or through the deployment of local community
heat pumps [Grub]. The objective is to reheat the temperature back
to the levels used for district heating operations. This design em-
braces a cyclical heat exchange approach, to enhance the efficiency
and sustainability of the local district heating network.

Discussion and Future Research
This chapter engages in an exploration of the research findings, look-
ing into the potential impacts of the results and preliminary conclu-
sions. Additionally, insight, and reflection for understanding the eco-
nomic and operational dynamics within energy communities. The
designs and approaches are questioned regarding the final results.
9.1
Research Approach and Scenario Design
The methodology used in this study involves the development and
analysis of urban energy system scenarios using EnergyPRO. The
scenarios were designed to replicate the complexities and dynamics
in real-world energy systems to showcase the potential configura-
tions of Positive Energy Districts. By integrating a community with
local production such as heat pumps, storage systems, and renew-
able energy sources, the model aims to provide a comprehensive
perspective on feasible energy strategies. To ensure a coherent com-
parison among scenarios, the model was approached to facilitate a
clear understanding of the impacts of various energy configurations
and undertake the feasibility of the study.
Analysing the model from a socioeconomic standpoint incorporates
energy conservation measures, emphasising cost-effective strategies
for local demand. Considerations extend to heat savings, and deter-
mining the optimal scope of these measures is necessary for establish-
ing an economically viable environment for associated stakeholders.
This alignment between energy conservation and socioeconomic fac-
tors emphasises the strategic transition towards sustainable energy
systems.
A crucial factor of the discussion is the techno-economic and business-
==PAGE:77==
9.1 Research Approach and Scenario Design
29
economic synergies observed among Power-to-Heat technologies, lo-
cal storage, and low-temperature district heating. Optimising en-
ergy systems by integrating local production, particularly heat pumps
enhances the feasibility of the economic system. While showcasing
the potential for achieving both economic benefits and sustainability
in an energy community, the urban system be expanded to larger
geographical boundaries. This integration, especially within a com-
munity framework, significantly influences the utilisation, highlight-
ing the interconnected factors of operational income and technology
utilisation. However, it is essential to recognise that the financial
viability is dependent on certain conditions and assumptions made
in this study. Incentives have an increasingly vital role in balanc-
ing economic sustainability with social and business interests as the
market changes. Market participants will engage if there is an actual
contribution to lowering costs. In the case of the Danish framework,
district heating has to be non-profit. Therefore the cost of invest-
ments is based on socioeconomic costs.
Policies and incentives, such as the subsidy scheme, are key con-
tributors in incentivising the utilisation of heat pumps. However,
it is necessary to emphasise the need for a thorough approach to
changing policy landscapes. Shifts in market prices, subsidies and
taxes can substantially impact the economic feasibility of certain
technologies, necessitating adaptability in response to developing
regulatory environments.
A significant part of the study’s resilience is the focus of the sensi-
tivity analysis, examining the system’s response to changes in the
electricity spot market. The results revealed a bias from EnergyPRO
for the system to market fluctuations, particularly the reliance on
the CHP for electricity production under favourable spot market
conditions may not align with realistic scenarios for actual costs
and production.

9.2
System Resilience and Flexibility for Future
Research
The analysis brings forth the concept of system resilience and flex-
ibility, highlighting the differentiation between centralised energy
systems and the transition to diversified energy systems that come
with Positive Energy Districts. External factors, such as fuel prices,
electricity prices, and fuel availability, have a substantial influence
on all scenarios. The integration of PEDs and the sensitivity analy-
sis emphasises the system’s adaptability to change. This showcases
that a diversified system combining local electricity generation and
collaborative heat and energy production may experience fewer con-
sequences from external changes.
Upon reviewing the annualised costs of the energy systems, it be-
comes evident that both investment costs and operational costs play
key roles in enabling new energy systems.
Exploring the impact
of low-temperature district heating, adopting a lower temperature
range (60/30°C) in the district heating network will allow for en-
hanced energy efficiency, diminish heat losses, and ultimately system
heat savings. The system’s resilience is shown through its ability to
adjust operations in response to external influences, as demonstrated
in the variations of spot market and fuel costs. The utilisation factor
of the components, when analysed across all scenarios, emphasises
the importance of optimising the system toward flexibility to effi-
ciently meet demand. Notably, arguments can particularly be drawn
toward the flexibility in storage capacities, as well as considerations
of their strategic locations to minimise heat losses during transport.
Both contribute to an increase in efficiency and strategic planning
towards mitigating heat losses in the energy network.
Flexibility in the energy production strategy is an essential theme in
PEDs, contributing to increased operational income and providing
oversight in managing energy production and consumption.
As-
sumptions related to capacity and efficiencies should be acknowl-
edged, especially considering that investments in the energy com-
munity appear at a municipality level and require substantial up-
front financial commitments from all stakeholders. Active partici-
==PAGE:79==
9.2 System Resilience and Flexibility for Future Research
31
pation from all stakeholders is essential for the success of a sustain-
able energy community. This includes residents, local authorities,
businesses, and energy providers. Without widespread political in-
centives, awareness, and active involvement of all stakeholders, the
system’s success is at risk. Political incentives and choice awareness
are essential to encourage involvement and ensure everyone is well-
informed. Without collective commitment, the potential benefits of
the system may not be fully realised. It is a shared effort that re-
quires a common vision and continuous communication to promote
a sense of ownership among the community members. The inclusion
of photovoltaic modules introduces a dynamic element, and the re-
sults of HP and PV have demonstrated the financial and productive
advantages of local generation and utilisation patterns. To offset
the political challenges with photovoltaic modules, local authorities
or energy providers can take administrative charge to ease the tran-
sition for the residents.
The model, while providing an insightful overview, inherently sum-
marises energy conversion in EnergyPRO. In a real-world scenario,
the distribution of the heat pump and photovoltaic modules would
be allocated among multiple households and differ from the model’s
representation as a single large unit. Flexibility, as measured by
the availability of scrutinising individual demand and utilising low-
temperature district heating, can be further enhanced by allowing
the individual heating of the low-temperature district heating net-
work based on their heat demand. Smaller energy production units
when implemented gradually, offer a practical approach to increasing
the flexibility of the district heating grid. The inclusion of photo-
voltaic modules in the electricity grid provides coverage of the base
load in a similar fashion and is well combined with heat pumps.
Evaluating the economic metrics, the simple payback time showed
the investment viability with assumptions on investment costs based
on unit capacity. The financial benefits, however, extend to the po-
tential construction of the community functioning as a small smart
grid.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
Cooperative dynamics between the centralised power plant and en-
ergy community have shown to be beneficial for optimising resource
utilisation and resilience within an urban system.
Collaboration
can ensure efficient resource distribution, minimise heat losses, re-
duce waste, and enhance overall energy supply reliability. For in-
stance, the implementation of balanced production and storage has
resulted in a 12% reduction LCOH, responding effectively to fluctu-
ations in demand. Additionally, from a socioeconomic standpoint,
limiting biomass consumption which is a finite renewable resource.
Favourable results of local electricity production further advocate
prioritising electricity over biomass as a fuel source.
Heat pumps, especially in low-temperature district heating, showed
reduced operational costs and increased potential gain compared to
traditional sources. Local heat pumps, with a 21% utilisation rate,
and combined with PV modules for electricity generation, are a co-
operative and sustainable energy source. Retrofitting infrastructure
for heat pump integration is required to enable overall energy com-
munity feasibility. The integration has resulted in a 20% decrease
in operational costs for low-temperature district heating.
Energy communities have shown to be viable with a short payback
period (approximately 3.5 years) for a high capacity gain, effec-
tively challenging generation capacity and system efficiency. Viabil-
ity stems from collaborative approaches, such as the strategic util-
isation of localised storage capacities. The incorporation of energy
communities will require careful planning between centralised and
local stakeholders, requiring administrative usage of storage capaci-
ties for resource utilisation, contributing to the economic feasibility
of the energy system.
Collaborative efforts between centralised and local production, cou-
==PAGE:81==
33
pled with storage capacities, showed essential for achieving eco-
nomic feasibility and operational efficiency within energy communi-
ties. This collaboration can ensure a balanced and resilient energy
supply, leveraging both systems at peak demand. The use of local
storage capacities enhances the system’s flexibility during low and
peak demand. Results indicate a reduction in operational expenses
and an improvement in overall energy supply reliability through col-
laborative efforts.
In conclusion, urbanised areas capable of collaborating and relying
on local energy sources are advised to do so. The collaborative ap-
proach fosters resilience against future energy crises, contributing to
expense reduction for consumers and suppliers (up to 20%), emis-
sions reduction, and operational efficiency improvements.
