Abstract
Imagine a world where people work using their computers from anywhere; a beach resort, a
busy downtown co-working space or a cosy café. This thesis explores an exciting trend called
digital nomadism and analyses how this trend is shaping the IT industry and freelancing.
The primary objective of the study is to investigate the impact of digital nomadism on
individuals who have embraced this lifestyle. The study is supported by an exploration of
existing literature focused on various aspects of digital nomadism. This covers an in-depth
analysis of its versatile manifestations across individuals, particularly working within IT and
as freelancers.
The literature review is followed by an empirical investigation aimed at addressing gaps
identified in prior research. The focal point of this study is a cohort of 13 Digital Nomads,
who were interviewed to gain insights into their first-hand experiences as digital nomads.
These interviews focus on understanding the interplay between their work-life, lifestyle, and
the challenges they encounter. There is a particular focus on addressing the topic of
well-being throughout the thesis as this aspect was identified as a gap in current research on
digital nomadism.
In a nutshell, this thesis takes the reader on a journey into the world of digital nomadism. A
close look is taken at how it is changing the way people work remotely. By talking to actual
digital nomads and learning from their experiences, this research not only helps to
understand this trend but also gives useful tips for people and companies adapting to this
novel lifestyle.

I.
Introduction
 Digital Nomadism: Exploring a Modern Lifestyle
A report by the Global Business Travel Association found that the number of people who
correspond to the criteria of a digital nomads is expected to reach 1 billion by 2035 (“WYSE
Travel Confederation,” 2023). In recent years, digital nomadism has emerged as a prominent
lifestyle choice, revolutionising the traditional notions of work, travel, and personal
fulfilment. This global phenomenon refers to individuals who leverage technology to
maintain a location-independent lifestyle, allowing them to work remotely while crossing the
globe. Digital nomadism contains a diverse community of professionals, entrepreneurs, and
freelancers, each embracing the freedom and flexibility offered by modern technology. This
thesis aims to explore the flexible landscape of digital nomadism, supported by empirical data
and research papers, with a particular emphasis on the well-being effects and overall
well-being of these modern nomads.
1.1 The Rise of Digital Nomadism
The rise of digital nomadism can be attributed to several factors, including advancements in
telecommunications, the availability of high-speed internet, and the increasing popularity of
remote work. These technological developments have facilitated seamless communication
and collaboration across distances, making it feasible for individuals to work remotely while
maintaining connectivity with their teams and clients.
Yet, this context of new work practices depicted as increasingly flexible, agile, autonomous,
collaborative and entrepreneurial (Taskin et al., 2017) brings a growing desire for meaningful
experiences and personal growth and has fuelled the allure of Digital Nomadism as an
attractive alternative to the conventional 9-to-5 lifestyle, in the context of workplace
diversification and work flexibilisation, the figure of the nomad has been mobilised in
different ways (Näsänen, 2017). More recently, attention has been drawn to one particular
type of nomad, namely the digital nomad (referred to as DN) (Müller, 2016).

==PAGE:8==
In addition to the factors mentioned earlier, several other key drivers have contributed to the
increasing
popularity
of
remote
work.
Technological
advancements,
particularly
in
communication tools, cloud computing, and project management software, have made remote
collaboration efficient and productive. This has led to substantial cost savings for both
employees and employers, as remote workers can save on commuting expenses, work attire,
and sometimes even housing costs if they relocate to more affordable areas. Moreover,
remote work has gained prominence due to its positive impact on work-life balance, as it
provides employees with the flexibility to manage personal and family commitments
alongside their professional responsibilities. This increased flexibility leads to higher job
satisfaction and overall well-being, fostering a more motivated and engaged workforce
(Müller, 2016).
The COVID-19 pandemic served as an unexpected catalyst for the widespread adoption of
remote work (Leyens et al., 2022). As companies quickly implemented remote work policies
to ensure employee safety, they discovered that productivity remained high, if not improved,
in remote work settings (Möllmann & Marx, 2021). This experience showcased the viability
of digital remote work on a larger scale and accelerated its acceptance as a permanent work
arrangement in many sectors, turning into digital nomadism opportunities.
Furthermore, changing work culture and a shift in employer attitudes have played a
significant role in driving the popularity of remote work. Modern workplaces increasingly
prioritise outcomes and results over traditional notions of "face time" in the office, valuing
employee productivity and satisfaction above physical presence (Mirbabaie et al., 2022).
Digital nomadism offers a plethora of advantages, both for individuals and society as a
whole. By breaking free from the constraints of a fixed location, DNs can immerse
themselves in diverse cultures, build global networks, and gain invaluable experiences that
foster personal and professional development. Additionally, the ability to adapt schedules
with high flexibility led to increased productivity and the potential to provide services around
the clock, thereby benefiting businesses and clients alike.
8
==PAGE:9==
Despite its growth, digital nomadism comes with its set of challenges and limitations.
Practical issues such as reliable internet connectivity, visa regulations, and tax considerations
can create hurdles for DNs. Moreover, maintaining a work-life balance in constantly
changing environments can lead to feelings of isolation, stress, and burnout. Studies have
highlighted that DNs may struggle with feelings of rootlessness and the lack of a stable
support system, potentially impacting their mental well-being in the long run (Matt, 2022).
1.2 Problem Analysis: Well-Being as a Digital Nomad
Digital nomadism gives rise to crucial concerns regarding its potential impact on overall
well-being. The nomadic lifestyle, characterised by frequent travel and exposure to diverse
cultures, can undoubtedly enrich personal experiences. However, it may also lead to feelings
of loneliness, social disconnection, and emotional instability. Constantly adapting to new
environments and dealing with uncertainties can increase stress levels.
Moreover, the lack of clear boundaries between work and leisure spaces within the DN
lifestyle might foster workaholic tendencies, endangering the digital nomad's work-life
balance. Prolonged work hours and the pressure to prove productivity in unconventional
settings may result in burnout, reduced job satisfaction, and decreased creativity. The absence
of access to support and resources for maintaining well-being in remote locations can further
compound the problem.
It is essential to carefully consider and research the potential implications of digital
nomadism on well-being to ensure the promotion of a healthier and more sustainable lifestyle
for individuals pursuing this nomadic way of living (Näsänen, 2017).
1.2.1 Thesis Purpose
The purpose of the thesis is to investigate the discrepancy between the online representation
of DNs' lifestyles and their actual experiences.

Our literature review will delve into the evolution of the digital nomadism concept across
time and to pinpoint potential gaps in existing academic papers. The core objective of this

Literature Retrieval and Selection: The process of literature retrieval and selection follows
a meticulously structured approach. Initial searches are conducted in the chosen databases

publications for relevance to the research questions, making sure the topics of the literature
are linked to the context of digital nomadism and the effect on the corporate environment, as

alignment with the research objectives. This process is done several times to make sure to

In shaping the trajectory of this thesis, certain threads of the existing literature have been
intentionally omitted during the review process. In particular, a conscious decision has been
made to refrain from diving extensively into economic intricacies and related macro-level
analyses. This conscious exclusion comes from the central focus of the research, which
centres on a comprehensive exploration of the experiences and well-being aspects associated
with DN lifestyle.
Through the selection of this more refined scope, the primary intention is to facilitate a more
concentrated and finely detailed examination of the cultural factors and social dynamics that
envelop the subject matter. Also, studies with overlapping or replicating findings without
adding new insights were excluded to prevent redundancy, as well as research from
questionable or non-peer-reviewed sources were excluded to maintain the credibility and
quality of the literature review.
This strategic decision empowers the provision of a more precisely targeted exploration of
the well-being factors in motion, consequently contributing a distinctive perspective to the
ongoing discourse related to the research question.
Clustering Method: To derive meaningful insights from the extensive literature collection, a
clustering method is employed. Publications are grouped based on thematic similarities,
which emerge from the content analysis of the selected articles. This method facilitates the
identification of key trends, patterns, and gaps in the literature, enabling us to synthesise a
coherent narrative that addresses the research questions.

2.3 Definition of Digital Nomadism
“Both shaping and shaped by technological, economic and social facets, the world of work
has witnessed a wide array of changes.” (Aroles et al., 2019).
Digital Nomadism has emerged as a popular trend among professionals, enabling them to
work and travel simultaneously. This trend has been made possible due to advancements in
technology that allow remote work and the ability to work from any part of the world. The
structure of the labour markets is affected by this new work arrangement, becoming prevalent
(Sweet and Meiksins, 2013). This gives more flexible opportunities to a wider range of
people from multiple background areas, diversifying the amount of people who practise this
lifestyle.
There is a lack of common understanding of what ‘digital nomadism’ means (Hannonen,
2020). This lack of consensus creates challenges for policymakers, academics, and
researchers to study and regulate the lifestyle effectively. A useful definition was coined by
Makimoto and Manners (1997), the notion of digital nomadism encapsulates individuals
‘whose work does not tie them to any specific place (or to a specific itinerary), and who
therefore travel while working’ (Sutherland & Jarrahi, 2017).
While DNs and nomadic workers present some similarities, ‘what makes DNs distinct [from
nomadic workers] is their length of travel and decision not to have a home base’ (Hannonen,
2020). Importantly, digital nomadism lies at the intersection of three framings: as an
economic activity, as a cultural phenomenon, and as a new technology-enabled form of
working and organising (Wang et al., 2018). The prospect of combining work with travel and
exploration has appealed to a new generation of workers seeking to break away from the
confines of traditional office settings (a.Baruffati, 2023).

2.4 Context of Digital Nomadism Practice
Earlier on the diversification of job areas as DNs were mentioned, nonetheless as a very early
topic, as seeing this work style being adopted in most branches is yet a distant vision. Within
the last decade, an exhaustive acceptance of DN working models in the corporate sector
never really went beyond several IT companies offering remote work arrangements to
software developers, as highlighted by (Frick & Marx, 2021).
This shift can be attributed to the fact that software developers are often at the forefront of
new technological advancements. Additionally, there is a growing acceptance within
corporate circles of the notion that while software developers are expected to physically come
to work, their role requires a significantly higher amount of time spent in front of a computer
compared to other employees in the company (Frick & Marx, 2021).
Kong et al., highlights that the main obstacle to the successful integration of DNs into
corporate settings arise from a clash of values between these two domains. The challenges
stem from a mutual lack of comprehension of each other's preferences, inadequate execution
or adherence to their distinct institutional logics, and a misalignment of their underlying
worldviews (Frick & Marx, 2021).
The insights presented shed light on a compelling notion: the hurdles encountered in
effectively integrating DNs into corporate environments seem to extend beyond their
immediate context. These challenges, rooted in divergent values and understandings, push us
to contemplate a broader implication. It brings us to consider whether the distinctive clash of
values that obstruct the seamless fusion of DNs within corporations also contributes to the
apparent detachment observed between those with a freelancer or entrepreneurial mindset and
the traditional corporate realm.
The same factors that create challenges for smooth collaboration between digital nomads and
corporate structures could also be shaping why freelancers and entrepreneurs tend to prefer
working independently, fostering an environment where they naturally move away from
conventional corporate ideologies. This makes us wonder how these dynamics might be
quietly shaping broader ideas about work and careers.

As demonstrated by previous research, DNs actively pursue a dual objective: they seek both
flexibility and personal independence, while also endeavouring to establish a sense of

Prester et al. (Prester et al., 2019) offer an insight: a DN's work identity emerges through a
dynamic interplay between the desire for professional autonomy and the need to maintain a

2.5 Digital Nomadism as Serious Leisure
The DN lifestyle is often portrayed in the media as a leisure-driven dream life, the reality for
some may involve economic coping strategies due to low income and job insecurity,
especially among the Millennial generation (Thompson, 2018). The article explores key
concepts related to the DN lifestyle, focusing on balancing work and leisure. Stebbins' theory
of “serious leisure” (2001, 2007, 2008) expresses that DNs prioritise leisure considerations
over employment-based location. “Privilege and inequality” are examined to understand DN
demographics and their relative global privilege and its impact on social relationships.
Additionally, the idea of “community” is explored to analyse DNs’ social positioning in
contrast to family, friends, co-workers, peers, and locals (Thompson, 2018).
The study takes a critical view, considering the neoliberal context of the millennial
generation's downwardly mobile economic prospects. It also highlights the trade-offs of
prioritising leisure and location, such as social isolation, distance from loved ones, and
loneliness. Some DNs seek constructed communities in co-living/co-working spaces, creating
an insulated bubble-like existence that excludes local populations and cultural contexts
(Thompson, 2018).
18
==PAGE:19==
Beyond the superficial portrayal, there is an attempt made to shed light on the complexities
and challenges of the DN lifestyle associated with work-life balance and social dynamics.
Stebbins defines “serious leisure” as a distinction between mundane everyday leisure
activities and those taken to an elevated level with intense investments of time, money, and
seriousness. DNs exemplify this concept as they prioritise leisure and demonstrate unique
characteristics, making them an empirical example in the sociology of leisure studies.
In comparison, Blackshaw's concept of “devotional leisure” (2018) describes leisure pursuits
as the motor that sustains modern life and a basis for self-identity. DNs often define
themselves based on specific leisure interests and orientations, such as beach and surfing,
mountain climbing, or snowboarding, rather than their remote work type.
Despite claims of authenticity and embeddedness in travel patterns, DNs show little
distinction from traditional tourists. Kaplan (1996) highlights the inequalities and outcomes
of tourism, indicating that income from tourism often remains within multinational
corporations rather than benefiting local populations. DNs attempt to differentiate themselves
from tourists by claiming to “slow travel” or have a more committed travel lifestyle.
However, their location selection, bracketed existence, and interactions with similar
demographics make it challenging to distinguish them from traditional tourists or expats. This
leisure aspect of digital nomadism affects DNs’ connection to self-identity, as well as the
complexities of their travel practices and interactions with local populations.
2.6 Gig Economy
The current state of the gig economy has an impact on DNs and telecommuters, highlighting
the challenges they face in maintaining distinct boundaries between work, leisure, and family
life. The gig economy is celebrated in popular entrepreneurial literature as a flexible and
freedom-giving system that benefits both businesses and workers. Freelancers in the gig
economy have the freedom to control their schedules, while employers can avoid providing
overhead expenses like office space or healthcare. However, many aspiring DNs begin their
remote work in the gig economy, taking on piecemeal, one-time jobs provided by corporate
websites connecting freelance workers with employers (Thompson, 2018).
19
==PAGE:20==
The gig economy platforms operate on a rating system, where freelancers' future employment
opportunities are impacted by their performance reviews. This incentivizes freelancers to
work below market rate or even for free to accumulate more gigs and positive ratings on their
professional profiles. The lack of benefits in gig work, such as retirement and healthcare
coverage, means freelancers must cover their operational costs out of their income.
Schor and Attwood-Charles (2017) cite based on a survey that gig workers disproportionately
earn less than $30,000 annually. The gig economy workforce is divided between those with
full-time employment who take on extra gigs and those without full-time employment who
rely on gigs for their livelihood. DNs, unlike traditional telecommuters, often have distinct
boundaries between work, leisure, and family life. They are rarely concerned with
child-rearing, balancing work primarily with leisure and occasional visits with family and
friends. All these factors add an additional layer to the complexities of balancing work and
life in the context of remote working styles.
2.7 Privilege and Inequality
Aydogdu (2016) challenges the DN lifestyle for being tech- and phallocentric, overlooking
the importance of genuine connections with destinations and their people. The DN lifestyle
perpetuates
dominant
capitalist
logic,
prioritising male-biassed trends, gadgets, and
hyper-mobility. It embraces hyper-individuality, focusing on personal freedom, autonomy,
and control.
Digital nomadism celebrates entrepreneurship and capitalism, prioritising financial and
business interests, and personal lifestyle over contributing to location-based communities.
Alexander (2005) points out that privilege often breeds feelings of superiority and can lead to
a lack of accountability for the consequences of actions. DNs often choose destinations where
local economies have been impacted, potentially exacerbating the economic disparities. The
tourism-driven economy may encourage hospitality at the expense of local community
development, creating distances between locals and tourists that DNs tend to overlook.

==PAGE:21==
Their critical insights into the societal implications of the DN lifestyle, exploring its cultural
and economic impacts on both the travellers and the destinations they visit prompts a
reflection on the potential consequences of this lifestyle choice and the need to consider
broader ethical and social implications.
2.8 Well-being and Building Communities
Digital nomadism is a complex phenomenon influenced by various socio-economic and
cultural factors. The experiences of DNs are shaped by factors like race, gender, class, and
nationality, which all play a role in determining the opportunities and challenges they
encounter. These factors are examined and their contribution to the diverse experiences
within the DN community is addressed. It emphasises the importance of inclusivity,
promoting diversity, and addressing disparities among DNs to create a more equal and
supportive environment.
In addition to the individual experiences of DNs, it is important to consider the impact of
their lifestyle on local communities and economies. While DNs have the freedom to work
remotely from any location, their presence can have both positive and negative effects on the
places they call home. Therefore it is crucial to explore the social, economic, and cultural
implications of digital nomadism, shedding light on issues such as the rising cost of living,
tensions between residents and nomads, and the need for new policies and interventions to
mitigate conflicts and establish positive interactions.
Despite choosing a nomadic lifestyle and leaving behind their hometown, family, and friends,
for endless travel, DNs often experience feelings of isolation and loneliness. Their blogs and
online postings highlight these emotional challenges. Family and friends back home might
express shock at their lifestyle choice and find it difficult to relate to their experiences.
Additionally, DNs may not fully immerse themselves in the languages and customs of their
host countries, further contributing to their feelings of disconnectedness (Yuen Thompson,
2019).
21
==PAGE:22==
To combat these feelings of isolation, DNs seek out a sense of community with other
like-minded individuals. Online platforms and conferences become vital spaces for them to
connect with others sharing similar lifestyles. They aim to find friends and companions with
whom they can travel and share their experiences. Many nomads choose to reside in
co-living/co-working spaces, where they can interact with a cohort of individuals who share
their DN identity (Garrett et al., 2014).
Following, in this section the importance of community and social connections for DNs’
well-being are highlighted. The quest for like-minded individuals and the desire to find a

3.3.2 Analysis of Interviews with Digital Nomads
The qualitative data gathered from the interviews is evaluated through a thematic analysis,
inspired by the framework established by Braun & Clarke (2006). Their methodological
approach provides flexibility and accessibility, forming a pathway to understanding the
patterns within the data through a pattern-type approach.
There are some distinctive terminologies in the analysis that should be understood by the
reader. A fundamental unit in this context is the 'data item', describing individual pieces of
data, each contributing to the 'data set,' also known as the corpus. Furthermore, the 'data
extract' refers to a coded segment of data that was identified and extracted from a data item,
offering a smaller but more focused piece of information. Applied to our case, data items are
the interviews conducted with DNs, and data extracts express their individual opinions and
answers during those conversations.
This analytical method is characterised by the qualitative exploration of themes. Instead of
focusing on the quantity of a theme, its relevance is highlighted in the responses within the
broader context of the research questions.
Essentially, the thematic analysis, based on Braun & Clarke's model, serves as a flexible lens
to gain an understanding of the information received through the qualitative data. It offers a
deeper perspective, captures the core of the expressions of the participants, and structures
them into a coherent narrative.
The five phases as Braun & Clarke introduced them were utilised to evaluate the data from
the interviews are the following:
Phase 1: Familiarisation with the data
Phase 2: Generating initial codes
Phase 3: Searching for themes
Phase 4: Reviewing themes
Phase 5: Defining and naming themes

IV. Data Analysis
4.1 Preliminary phase
During the examination of data from interviews centred around the DN lifestyle, a methodical
approach was adopted, and the process was streamlined into three overarching phases to
make it more concise and easier to follow. This condensation was done by merging and
reorganising some of the steps from the original five-phase model proposed by Braun &
Clarke. Let us break down how our three phases align with their five phases:
Phase 1 - Data Preparation and Familiarization: This phase encompasses the first
steps of Braun & Clarke's method, which involve familiarising with the data and
preparing it for analysis. It includes tasks like data collection, transcription, and
reading through the data to gain a holistic understanding of the content.
Phase 2 - Coding and Theme Generation: In this phase, elements within Braun &
Clarke's phases 1 and 2 were combined. It involves coding the data, identifying
meaningful segments, and generating initial themes. This condensed phase combines
the initial data exploration and coding processes into one step.
Phase 3 - Theme Refinement and Finalization: Our third phase corresponds to
Braun & Clarke's phases 3, 4, and 5. Once initial themes are generated, they can be
refined and organised into a coherent structure. This phase involves reviewing,
consolidating, and finalising the themes to create a comprehensive analysis of the
data.
By condensing these steps, the aim was to create a more straightforward and approachable
method for researchers to follow. However, it is important to note that while certain steps
were combined, the essence and principles of the analysis remain consistent. Researchers
who are familiar with Braun & Clarke's original five-phase model can still see the parallels in
our condensed three-phase approach, and those who are new to the process can benefit from a
simplified yet effective way to conduct qualitative analysis.

chats, phones, and computers. Instead, they navigate specialised software that aligns with
their specific domains of work.

agenda” (Jan, 26yo, Freelancer, 5 years DN, June 2023).
“I get rid of this cost and only use the email [app] and Google Calendar to organise my

The critical distinction surfaces in their remarks: an environment that contrasts with the

step into a world where self-sufficiency in technology management is paramount. This
distinctive environment triggers a thought-provoking discussion on well-being management,
revealing the complex balance they must strike between their work, their nomadic lifestyle,
and the intricate choreography of their technological tools.
The idea that emerges from these dialogues is that technology is both an investment and a
necessity within this way of work. The DN landscape, intertwined with a constant evolution
of technology, requires a financial and cognitive commitment to stay ahead. In contrast to a
corporate context where technology management is often delegated, DNs stand as solo
navigators in a sea of ever-advancing digital tools. This duality [the investment and the
imperative] creates a unique dynamic that infuses their work life with a distinctive sense of
purpose and urgency.
Moreover, the 'Technology' category provides a window into the intimate nexus between

where technology becomes an inseparable companion. The omnipresence of technology
within their nomadic journey sparks a profound dialogue about how they manage the balance
between a tech-rich existence and the pursuit of holistic well-being.

V. Discussion and conclusion
Well-being Challenges Faced by Digital Nomads
This thesis contributes to the expanding field of research on digital nomadism by exploring
its impact in the IT and freelancing industries and the role of technology in facilitating this
lifestyle. By taking a technology entrepreneurship perspective, this thesis aims to provide a
more comprehensive understanding of the opportunities and challenges associated with
digital nomadism.
As the trend of digital nomadism continues to gain popularity, we want to understand its
potential effect on traditional forms of labour and the role of technology in enabling this
lifestyle. By shedding light on these issues, our thesis provides valuable insights for
entrepreneurs, policymakers, and other stakeholders in the labour market. Ultimately, this
research can help to shape the future of work and entrepreneurship in a more global and
digital age.
One main research question organised the overall thesis:
What are the experiences and interpretations in terms of well-being challenges
faced by digital nomads? What contributes to these factors?
Our research explains that DNs want to experience a new lifestyle coming from a need for
more freedom, novelty, and personal growth. The experiences of the DNs from our literature
review express the emotional issues of isolation and loneliness within their lifestyles. While
the freedom to explore different places is a key concept in their journey, the very nature of
constantly changing locations introduces a challenge in forming stable social connections. A
gap between the literature research and the interviews is visible, explaining that this feeling
of loneliness or isolation comes often because of the nature of their work as DNs.
53
==PAGE:54==
Yet, this lifestyle expresses that a perfect personal management of time is an absolute
necessity in order to perform work while travelling. Most of our interviewees explain that
they do not feel lonely, as they are constantly surrounded by travellers, and activities are not
missing, but rather that they actually observe a lack of empathy, or sensitivity from other
travellers that lacks to understand how they work, putting them into a separate box regarding
their time management.
The insights highlight the importance of adjusting to new situations and how relationships
can change quickly as a result. This reality accentuates the unique form of loneliness that
DNs experience, where they often find companionship during their travels but might feel
isolated during their working hours. These issues of struggling with isolation and loneliness
were often referenced by a number of our interviewees. Feeling isolated and lonely are
feelings that are present in almost every human, however, when living this unique lifestyle,
these effects can often strengthen negative emotions. They feel loneliness in the way they
work, as they are the only ones that can make their own work path, it is a constant ‘Do it
yourself’ lifestyle, resulting in relegating well-being to a second priority.
The DN lifestyle is synonymous with a flexible lifestyle, nonetheless it introduces challenges
in maintaining a good work-life balance. The absence of traditional office hours creates a
fluid work environment that often mixes work and personal life. Mixing up those two can
mess with having a regular schedule and clear limits. This can make it hard to switch off from
work-related tasks. The absence of a stable routine emerges as a key challenge for DNs. The
constant change in locations and work environments can disrupt well-established routines,
affecting sleep patterns, exercise habits, and overall mental well-being.
Keeping a work-life balance and having a stable routine were also often and strongly
expressed ideas by our interviewees. Multiple DNs we asked echoed that in order to maintain
a healthy work-life balance, they have to be creative and think out of the box in ways to
organise their schedules, save time, and practice self-control to avoid working at the expense
of enjoying free-time activities. Several people mentioned that they encountered periods in
their lives when working took over most of their time and did not have the skills to maintain a
balance. However, seemingly this is a skill that a number of the DNs ‘learned on the mission’
as they say it takes time and lived experience to be able to maintain a healthy work-life
balance and a stable routine.
54
==PAGE:55==
There is somewhat of a contradiction observed. These modern nomads understand the
importance of having a balance between work and personal life, but they struggle to actually
make it happen. The challenge here is that their lifestyle blurs the lines between when they
are working and when they are off the clock. They are driven by their passion, and this
passion often means work is happening all the time, fitting in with what they personally love
to do. It is a double-edged sword: while this approach can be not-so-great for their
well-being, it also presents an interesting angle. It makes one wonder whether merging work
and passion is a roadblock or a solution to a more satisfying life. This whole situation boils
down to a big question: Should one mix their passion and job, or should one keep them
separate to keep their well-being a priority?
Contributing Factors: Understanding the Complex Dynamics
DNs have to deal with constant change, leading to challenges in establishing stability and
routine. While the allure of exploration and diversity fuels their journey, the lack of stability
can also generate stress and feelings of rootlessness.
Job insecurity is another notable challenge faced by DNs within the gig economy. The
reliance on freelancing and remote work can lead to uncertain income streams and a lack of
benefits. This financial instability introduces stress and uncertainty into their lives.
The aspiration to explore new places is a driving force, but it can also introduce pressure to
constantly seek new opportunities and destinations. The desire to make the most of each
location can lead to overcomplication and potential burnout. This dynamic was highlighted
by Clem and Mumu, who emphasised that while well-being might not be a primary
consideration at the beginning, it becomes a concern as they adapt to the lifestyle and
understand their limits.
55
==PAGE:56==
The interviewed DNs often expressed that they had feelings of instability and rootlessness at
some point, or they still flare up occasionally depending on the environment they are in or the
personal struggles they are going through. A life that is constantly changing and encounters
situations that are out of the comfort zone for most people is seemingly an addictive element
for numerous DNs in our sample group. However, most of them agreed that one aspect that
they prefer to be stable is their job itself, as it strongly ties to the income generated, thus
enabling them to continue with this lifestyle.
Following, our sub-research question concerns more of the environment in which DNs
evolve:
How is technology enhancing the capacity for the practice of digital
nomadism, and what is the feasibility of working in this way?
Technology plays a central role in enabling the DN lifestyle. Our interviewees discussed the
need for tailored technological ecosystems that integrate with their work and exploration. The
importance of managing their own technological tools introduces a new type of challenge
compared to conventional office environments.
The analysis highlights the importance of self-motivation within the DN lifestyle. The lack of
traditional office structures requires a high degree of self-discipline to manage tasks and
maintain productivity. Some of the interviewees expressed struggling with self-motivation
occasionally, however, others reported boosted levels of motivation for the same reason,
which lies in the independent work style.
The use of technology also exposes DNs to social comparison. The presence of travel
influencers on social media platforms can lead to feelings of inadequacy and a distorted
perception of the lifestyle. A number of DNs echoed this idea and had to actively dissociate
from these feelings as it had a negative effect on their everyday lives. Feelings of lack of
authenticity mixed with high expectations and falsely observed peer pressure flared up
occasionally in some interviewees who also discussed how over time they were able to
handle and compartmentalise these feelings thanks to their evolving experiences.

==PAGE:57==
Limitations and Implications
Our last subquestion highlights our limitation in the context of digital nomadism :
What are the socio-economic and cultural dimensions of being a digital
nomad (e.g., gender, race, class, and values or cultural etiquettes), and how
can these disparities be addressed by identifying key challenges?
The study has given valuable insights into the growing research topic of digital nomadism,
yet the sample size of our interviewees, while diverse in terms of background, primarily
comprises individuals within IT and freelancing domains. This restricts the generalisation of
findings to the broader DN community. Additionally, the interviewees often expressed
positivity and minimal issues with well-being, leading to a potential discrepancy between the
narratives shared and the actual challenges they faced. The lack of legal protections and the
need for a larger, more versatile sample size are factors that future research should consider
addressing. Moreover, incorporating other research methods, such as surveys, could provide a
more comprehensive understanding of well-being challenges.
Our study's interviewees were mainly individuals from Western countries, with most of them
being white and holding master's degrees. However, it is important to acknowledge that this
demographic composition could potentially limit the generalisation of our findings,
representative survey studies could explore these factors deeper. One notable aspect that
emerged during our research was the significance of location choice among DNs. While
many DNs opt for affordable countries to sustain their lifestyle, a question arose concerning
the scenario of a DN choosing to work from a higher-cost region such as Scandinavia, where
salaries are generally higher.
57
==PAGE:58==
However, while promoting flexibility and personal freedom, digital nomadism gives rise to
ethical concerns that require thorough consideration. These concerns span a range of domains
including
tax evasion, the exploitation of low-income countries, and the potential
environmental repercussions. While the emphasis on cultivating responsible behaviour and
sustainable practices among DNs is commendable, the complexity of these ethical dilemmas
might require a broader exploration that delves into socio-cultural factors, economic
interactions, and the complexities of global policies. Consequently, a more comprehensive
investigation would be necessary to address the nature of these ethical concerns inherent to
the DN phenomenon.
Would this result in increased work demands? And therefore lower well-being management
and a loss of interest for DN lifestyle, as time to actually visit would be lower? This inquiry,
however, serves as a point of limitation within our study. Despite the prevalent demographic
trends observed, our findings may not be universally applicable, as our sample mainly
consisted of educated, affluent white individuals. Finally, it ought to be clarified that our
findings might not align with other studies in this field. Hence, it is necessary to maintain an
open and transparent perspective on our conclusions. In essence, our investigation does not
definitively resolve whether the chosen demographic and geographical factors directly dictate
work intensity among DNs.
