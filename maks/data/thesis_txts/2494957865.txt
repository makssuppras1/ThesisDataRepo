Abstract
In this report, it is sought to verify the buckling performance of
meta materials built of multiscale cell structures presented by
Thomsen et al. [2018].
The veriﬁcation includes buckling experiments and numerical
simulations. In the experiments, the meta material is repres-
ented by specimens consisting of only a few cells. The report
describes the processes of manufacturing test specimens suit-
able for buckling experiments, the execution and evaluation of
2D buckling experiments. Diﬀerent base materials and fabrica-
tion methods are explored in order to provide reliable specimens.
Diﬀerent test set up are developed to provide reliable test results.
Numerical models are set up by use of Comsol and the numer-
ical results are compared to the data found by experiments.
This report is compiled as part of the fulﬁlment of requirements
to obtain the Master Degree of Engineering Design and Applied
Mechanics.

Introduction
A lot of eﬀort has been put into development of artiﬁcial microstructures to obtain new
materials outperforming on diﬀerent material properties, among these the development
of lightweight structures with excellent strength has been of great interest. Recently, the
work presented by Thomsen et al. [2018] outlined an impressive potential of tailoring these
materials by utilising topology optimisation of the microstructure.
The work presented by Thomsen et al. [2018] includes optimisation of square, hexagonal
and triangular microstructures when exposed to diﬀerent macroscopic compressive load
cases including uniaxial, biaxial and shear load.
The microstructures are optimised to maximise the micro scale buckling strength and
several mathematical tools are utilised to keep track of the consequences of the macroscale
load case on the buckling instability of the microstructure.
In this thesis, it is pursued to explore the material properties of some of the metamaterials
presented by Thomsen et al. [2018] and to explore if the results being presented can be
veriﬁed by use of commercial software and experimental tests of physical specimens. The
thesis contains both an experimental and a theoretical part.
In the experimental part, the fabrication and tests of physical specimens will be described
and the obtained results will be presented.
In the theoretical part of the thesis, results obtained from numerical analyses performed by
use of Comsol will be presented and compared to experimental data.
For practical reasons, the experiments will be limited to uniaxial compression test of the
specimens. The specimens will be treated as models of a metamaterial even though the
number of cells will be very limited. The main focus of the experiments will be buckling
analyses.
From the diﬀerent cell types which were optimised, the square cell will be subject to both
the experiments and the numerical analyses.
Although the load case considered will be the uniaxial compression, the optimised version
of the square cell will be the one optimised for the biaxial load case, because the geometry
of this cell is assessed to be more simple compared to the geometry of the cell optimised
for uniaxial compression, and therefore easier to fabricate.

2.2
EPDM Sheet Specimen Fabricated by Water Jet Cutting
2.2.1
Prototype of EPDM Specimen
After the experiment with the specimens, 3D printed by Andy, it is sought to ﬁnd a fabric-
ation method which is more appropriate for fabrication of a larger number of specimens
and a material which will not perform plastic deformations or break when exposed to large
strains. The latter is desired in order to be able to reuse the specimens for more experiments.
It is decided to manufacture the next specimens by cutting them from a sheet of EPDM
rubber material. Laser cutting is deselected due to worries regarding the inﬂuence on the
material near the cutting edges of the specimens, caused by the heat from the process.
Water-jet cutting seems to contain interesting potential and it is decided to give it a try.
Initially, three specimens each consisting of one unit cell of the optimised structure and the
supports, are manufactured to get some knowledge of the capability of the tool and process.
The three specimens are scaled to have following edge lengths of the cell: 20 mm, 25 mm
and 30 mm. After an evaluation of the specimens, it will be decided which step to take
next.
By the ﬁrst impression, the work done by the machine seems to be relative ﬁne, even though
it is obvious that the cutting pattern is followed more precise at the 30 mm specimen.
The precision of the work is not evaluated by any measurement tools, the evaluation will
be visually combined with ﬁnger feeling. To support the visual evaluation, photos of the
specimens are processed in Matlab. It should be outlined that the Matlab processing of
photos taken by a mobile phone, does not provide any support to a quantitative evaluation
of the precision of the work done by the water cutting machine.
A photo of a part of the water jet cut 20 mm specimen is presented in ﬁgure ﬁg. 1a. Figures
of the edges of the specimens are obtained from the Matlab processing, the edges of the
20 mm specimen can be seen in ﬁgure ﬁg. 1b. Figure 1d illustrates the result when the
edges of the specimens and the edges of the model of the cell, presented in ﬁgure 1c are
combined in Matlab.
An evaluation of the specimens and the corresponding ﬁgures obtained from the Matlab
process leaves the impression that the 20 mm cell must be abandoned due to the level of
errors. The operator of the water cutting machine assesses that the quality of the work
might be increased if the edges of the model could be free of the staircases seen on ﬁgure
1c. He also mentions that the memory of the machine is challenged by the size of the ﬁle,
even though it is just one cell. Therefore it will not be possible to handle a full 5×5 cell
specimen.
It is decided to make a more smooth model of the optimised cell and scale it to 25 mm edge
length in order to minimise the impact on the specimen caused by the manufacturing errors.

2.2.2
Out of Plane Properties of EDPM Sheet
When fabricating specimens for a 2D test, it might also interesting to know something about
their out of plane properties, therefore the specimens are exposed to a simple compression
test, done by ﬁnger force.
When describing the test, the two sides of the specimen will be denoted the inlet side and
the outlet side referring to the direction of the water ﬂow during the manufacturing process.
When placing the specimen on a table, having the inlet side towards the table, and pressing
the supports by ﬁngers as illustrated in ﬁg. 2a the specimen is seen to buckle as expected
in a 2D test. After turning the outlet side towards the table, the test is repeated, now when
pressing the specimen it does not buckle in the 2D plane, instead, it buckles out of plane
as illustrated in ﬁg. 2b.
(a) In plane buckling
(b) Out of plane buckling
Figure 2 – Compression test of specimen by ﬁngers
The behaviour indicates that the specimen is stronger in the inlet side, which might be due
to manufacturing errors resulting in more material is taken away from the outlet side of
the specimen compared to the inlet side.
The water-jet from a water-jet cut machine is conical as illustrated in ﬁgure 3, based on
given material parameters, the machine is automatically adjusting the cutting angle to
compensate for this. When compensating correctly, it will have the right angle towards
the specimen and keeping the error in the waste material. based on measurements the
adjustment can be optimised by the operator, but in this case, due to the geometry and
size of the specimen, measuring is challenging. Another reason for the behaviour might be
anisotropic material properties. To distinguish between manufacturing errors and material
properties, more one cell specimens will be cut from both sides of the rubber plate.

2.4.1
Material and Fabrication Method
The viscoelastic properties can be avoided if the specimens are produced of silicone rubber.
The silicone is bought as a liquid two component material. The solidiﬁcation initiates
when the two components are mixed. The specimen will be cast in a 3D printed mould,
the best printing material for printing the mould is ABS but the available printers are only
allowed to print PLA, therefore the moulds are printed in PLA even though this material
has a tendency to delaminate.
Normally a mould should have slip angles to ensure the ability to get the specimen out
of the mould, in this case, symmetry of the specimens is important, therefore the moulds
must be designed without slip angles.
2.4.2
Reference Structure
It is relative simple to design and print the mould and do the casting of the reference
structure. The mould and the specimen can be seen in ﬁgure ﬁg. 6
The silicone rubber is a liquid two component product, mixing the two components initiates
the solidiﬁcation. By the mixing process and when the silicone is poured into the mould, air
bobbles might be trapped in the liquid material. In the mold, the air bobbles will naturally
seek upwards, therefore there is a risk of ending up with a higher concentration of air
bobbles in one side of the specimen, which will spoil the symmetry of the specimen.
To avoid this, the mould is knocked towards the table for a few minutes during the casting
process, to help the airbobbles out of the liquid. Figure 7 presents the mould of the reference
structure ﬁlled up with liquid silicone. The trace of the specimen can be seen as air bobbles
in the liquid. After solidiﬁcation, the mould is used as guide when the exceeding material
is cut away.

2.4.3
The Mould for the Optimised Structure
Designing the mold for the optimised structure is more challenging. The ability of the
printer to print the smallest details of the mould in a suﬃcient quality must be taken into
consideration when determining the cell size of the structure and the shape of the mould.
The mold will inevitable contain a large number of small gabs. To provide a high quality
of the specimen it is important to have the liquid silicone uniformly distributed to every
small gab of the mold without having air bubbles trapped.
Filling up the mould by Injection moulding performed by use of extremely high pressure
would by easy, but in this case having a breakable mould made of 3D printed PLA the
possibility of adding a pressure is quite limited.
It is sought to design the mould in a way that will provide the ability of shaking the air out
of the liquid after it is poured into the mould and then afterwards add a moderate pressure
by squishing the exceeding liquid out of the mould.
It is decided to design the mould in three parts, two of them can be seen in ﬁgure ﬁg. 8. The
part of the mould denoted "1" is the matrix which will be ﬁlled up with silicone, the part
8
==PAGE:14==
denoted "2" is holding all the tiny pins which will form the pattern of holes in the specimen,
Four pair of guides, one of them denoted "a" and "A" will ensure correct positioning of
the pattern of holes.
The geometry of each cell includes four tiny holes near centre of the cell, even though the
edge length of the cells is set to 41 mm, the pins which will provide these holes are to small
to be printed in a suﬃcient quality by the available print technology. instead both part one
and two of the mould are designed with holes to guide these pins which will be fabricated
from stainless steel rods having a diameter of 1 mm.

After designing the mould in Solidworks the design is saved as a STL ﬁle before printing.
Because a geometry saved as an STL ﬁle, is represented by a mesh of points connected
by straight lines, curves will always be slightly more narrow than the original ones. To
achieve the desired precision, the number of triangles must be set suﬃciently to ensure that

4.1.2
Approximation of Strains
The test structure and the supports are modelled with the same stiﬀness but due to the
diﬀerence in volume fraction, the supports are assumed to be much stiﬀer than the structure
to be tested.
Therefore the strains will be approximated by:
∆Htotal
H0(structure)
(4.1)
Where ∆Htotal is the change of height of the entire geometry and H0(structure) is the initial
height of the test structure.
25
==PAGE:31==
The risk of slightly over-estimating the strains of the structure by this approximation, espe-
cially for grids consisting of few cells, should be noted.
4.1.3
Imperfections
In most models of real problems, for which buckling analyses are relevant, couplings
between normal and bending forces are present, therefore linear buckling analyses will
only provide a crude estimate of the buckling load. A nonlinear analysis, in this context
a post-buckling analysis, will provide a much more precise estimate of the load-carrying
capacity of the structure as well as the mechanical properties of the structure before and
after buckling.
Never the less it is possible to build models without any couplings between normal and
bending forces e.g. a perfectly straight bar, exposed to an axis-symmetric load in the
normal direction. In this case, any load can be applied to the bar without ﬁnding a buckling
limit by a nonlinear analysis. To avoid modelling perfect symmetric structures exposed
to perfect symmetric load cases and ensure couplings between normal and bending forces
imperfections must be implemented in the model.
The most simple way to introduce an imperfection is to add an extra external force to the
model to ensure an asymmetric load case. Another possibility is adding a geometrical
imperfection, the latter one to imitate production errors.
The ﬁnest purpose of the imperfections is to enable the numerical model to imitate the
performance of the real physical model. Finding the right perturbations can in some cases be
quite challenging. In this project, geometrical imperfections will be added to the structure.
The imperfections will be obtained from one or more of the 8 ﬁrst mode shapes obtained
from a linear buckling analysis performed under prescribed displacement conditions.
After running the analysis, the node coordinates of the deformed meshes and the original
mesh is exported to Matlab. In Matlab, the mode shapes are normalised to a given value of
displacement of the nodes, after this the displacements of all the desired mode shapes are
added to the original coordinates and the new mixed mode is normalised again. A mesh ﬁle
with the new node coordinates is then imported to Comsol and used for the simulations.

4.1.5
Linear Buckling Analysis
Initially, a linear buckling analysis is performed. The linear buckling analysis is performed
by solving the general eigenvalue problem:
([A] + λ [B]) {φ} = {0}
(4.2)
The determinant of ([A] + λ [B]) must equal zero for equation 4.2 to have nontrivial solu-
tions.
26
==PAGE:32==
Written out the determinant forms a nth degree polynomial in λ where n x n is the dimen-
sion of the matrices A and B, the roots of the polynomial are the eigenvalues and {φ}
denotes the corresponding eigenvectors. In Finite element analyses, the eigenvalues are
not found by solving the polynomial, but by iterative methods [Cook et al., 2001]
The present linear analyses are performed by adding a prescribed displacement to a line of
the structure, therefore the outcome of the analyses will be the critical displacements of
this line, the corresponding critical loads are found by integrating the reaction forces along
the line.
For the present analyses the equation will be the following:
([K0] + λ [Kt]) {φ} = {0}
(4.3)
Where [K0] is the initial stiﬀness matrix.
In the initial step of the analysis a small displacement is added to the structure and the
corresponding tangential stiﬀness matrix [Kt] is found.
In the next step of the analysis, the right values of λ is found.
To satisfy the equation, the value of λ must be scaled [Kt] so the matrix obtained from the
sum of the two stiﬀness matrices becomes singular. To obtain the critical displacement,
the value of λ is multiplied by the value of the displacement applied to the structure in the
initial step of the analysis. {φ} denotes the corresponding mode shape.
4.1.6
Post Buckling Analysis
The linear buckling analysis can provide an estimate of the buckling strain and buckling
pressure, even though the analysed structure is having nonlinear properties.
By performing a post-buckling analysis, more detailed information are obtained on the
geometrical nonlinear properties of the structure when exposed to large deformations.
The post-buckling analyses introduce solution methods for solving the equation
[K] {D} = {R}
(4.4)
when [K] and {R} might be functions of {D}. The analysis is performed step wise. In the
present analysis, a prescribed displacement of the line marked red in ﬁg. 26 is chosen as the
controlling parameter. The direction of the displacement is indicated by the arrow seen on
the same ﬁgure. For each step of the analysis, the displacement of the line is increased and
the stiﬀness matrix and vectors of displacement and reaction forces are updated to fulﬁl
the equation.
For each step, the magnitude of the applied force F is obtained by summing up the Reaction
forces in y-direction along the line exposed to the prescribed displacement and setting R -
F = 0. The applied pressure is obtained by dividing the applied force by the area on which
the force is applied and is hence an average pressure.

4.1.10
Providing Geometrical Perturbations
In order to have well deﬁned perturbations the geometrical imperfections are provided from
the mode shapes obtained from a linear buckling analysis performed in Comsol.
The post processing of the mode shapes is performed by use of Matlab. The mesh of
each mode shape is compared to the original mesh to ﬁnd the Euclidean norm of the
displacement of each node, then the mode shape is normalised to a given maximum value
of the displacement of the nodes.
After the normalisation, the displacement of each node, caused by each of the selected
mode shapes, is added to the nodes of the original mesh.
After adding displacements from one or more mode shapes to the original mesh, the new

5
Discussion
5.1
Specimens Fabricated by EPDM
The experiments with the EPDM specimens is not providing information suitable for quant-
itative comparison of the performance of the two diﬀerent cell structures.
This is due to several factors, among these the signiﬁcant production errors of the optimised
structure and due to the fact that the wrong specimens of reference structure was delivered.
The observation of the reference structure buckling in a higher mode and then seeking
towards the ﬁrst buckling mode shape and corresponding pressure also indicates the need
for ﬁnding a more eﬃcient way to eliminate noise from friction.
5.2
Specimens 3D Printed by Damvig
The experiments with the 3D printed specimens is not providing information suitable for
quantitative comparison of the performance of the two diﬀerent cell structures.
The test is outlining a great need of avoiding materials holding viscoelastic properties when
producing the specimens. The test also, as seen in the experiments with EPDM, outlines
the need of ﬁnding a method to minimise friction as a noise factor.
5.3
New Ideas
5.3.1
Desired Properties of a New Material
Especially during tests of the 3D specimens the viscoelasticity has proved to be a signiﬁcant
factor of noise. When fabricating the next specimen, materials having this property should
be avoided. It is also sought to ﬁnd a material holding isotropic properties, therefore it
seems interesting to cast the specimens from silicone.
5.3.2
Friction
When exposed to compression during the test the specimens prefer to buckle "out of plane"
as illustrated in ﬁgure ﬁg. 43. The ﬁgure illustrates a reference structure but during tests
both structures have been seen to perform this out of plane buckling mode. In the test
set up the out of plane buckling has so far been controlled by the glass plates but by this
procedure friction is introduced. During tests, the specimens has been powdered with baby
powder to reduce the friction.
The impact of the friction is not known but as illustrated in ﬁgure ﬁg. 44 the buckling
modes of the optimised structure are mainly local, for the reference structure they are all
global. The ﬁrst buckling mode of the reference structure illustrated in ﬁgure ﬁg. 44a has
the largest amplitude at the location which is often seen to be the contact area between
the specimen and the glass plates, therefore it is likely that the friction has a much higher
inﬂuence on the buckling of the reference structure compared to the optimised one.
To avoid out of plane buckling without having friction, it could be a possibility to print the
specimen with a signiﬁcantly increased out of plane thickness and thereby ensure the ﬁrst
or more buckling modes to be in plane.

5.3.3
Dimensions of the Specimens
To ﬁnd an appropriate thickness of the specimen a 3D model of the reference structure is
set up in Comsol. From the model it looks like >18 mm is suﬃcient for a 5x5 cell specimen
(41 mm edge length) to ensure the three ﬁrst buckling modes to be in plane. Adding a little
safety factor will make it > 22 mm, which is doable. With this geometry it will be possible
to perform the test without guiding the specimen by the plates of glass and therefore also
without friction.
A 3D model of the optimised structure requires a number of elements beyond the limit of
the capacity of the available Comsol edition. As an easy work around, the equation of the
bending moment of a square cross section is used.

Since the optimised structure is expected to resist a pressure roughly four times higher than
the reference structure, the optimised structure should be cast with a thickness twice the
thickness of the reference structure.
5.4
The Silicone Specimens
Although improvements can still be made, casting the specimens of silicone material and
changing the approach when performing the 2D experiment has provided the most reliable
test results so far in this project. Changing the material model from linear elastic to hyper
elastic has provided the ability to perform a postbuckling analysis of the optimised structure.
Still when comparing the test results and the simulations several deviations between simu-
lations and experimental data are noted.
Before digging deeper into the comparison between simulations and data provided by tests
of the silicone specimens, presented in section 4.4, ﬁgure 42 the higher volume fraction
of the optimised specimen, described in section section 2.4.5 should be mentioned once
again. This deviation is without doubt responsible for a part of the superior performance
during test of this specimen. Having this said, the comparison provides more interesting
information to be interpreted. Simulations performed by use of both the linear material
model and the hyper elastic material model forecasts a geometric nonlinear behaviour of
the optimised geometry, the compliance of the geometry is seen to increase during the
compression. this is not veriﬁed during the test of the optimised specimen.
The reason may be other factors compensating for the expected increase of compliance
during the test, one of them can be strain hardening of the silicone material.
The local deformations of the optimised specimen becomes quite large during the test,
therefore strain hardening may be an important factor.
Strain hardening during the experiment can also be the explanation of the minor underes-
timation of the physical performance of the reference specimen seen in the simulations.
These underestimations are small, compared to the deviations seen for the optimised spe-
cimen, but the local deformations of the reference specimen during tests are also small
compared to the optimised specimen.
It may be possible to increase the coherence between simulations and experiments by
optimising the modelling of strain hardening of the material in the simulations.
During the experiments, when the optimised specimen is exposed to large deformations
and local buckling modes occur, some of the small gabs is seen to be closed when the
small beams collapse, when this happens, contact zones are established between the beams.
Including this phenomenon in the simulations may also be useful.
In the experiments, the optimised specimen is seen to outperform the reference specimen by
a factor six. It has already been mentioned that the load carrying capacity of an optimised
specimen having the right volume fraction will be expected to be lower, but must also be
mentioned again, that the loss of load carrying capacity seen in ﬁgure 42 appears when the
specimen is buckling out of plane. In other words, in experiments with silicone specimens
43
==PAGE:49==
a loss of load carrying capacity due to in plane buckling of the optimised specimen is not
seen yet.
5.5
General Observations
During experiments with the optimised structure, local buckling modes have been seen to
occur without aﬀecting the load carrying capacity of the structure, the load carrying capa-
city of a meta material having this cell structure seems to be connected to the appearance
of global buckling modes or global appearance of a large number of local modes.
The optimisation procedure presented by Thomsen et al. [2018] was carried out under
the assumption of an inﬁnite structure. A brieﬂy numerical examination of the size eﬀect
is presented in section 4.1.13 and 4.1.14. Even though it is only a brief examination, it
indicates the presence of size eﬀects, especially for structures holding a limited number of
cells of the reference geometry. All specimens produced for experiments, have so far been
holding a very limited number of cells. Until the size eﬀect is more thoroughly described,
it must be taken into consideration as an unknown factor of noise, when evaluating the
experiments. Especially if the intention is to validate the forecasts of the behaviour of a
meta material having a huge amount of sells.

Conclusion
In this project, topology optimised cell structures have been examined both by simulations
and by experiments. The cell structures are the outcome of an optimisation code presented
by Thomsen et al. [2018]. The aim of the project have been to gain more knowledge on
the behaviour of these structures.
During the fabrication, a lot of unforeseen challenges have been met. To ﬁnd a suitable
material and a suitable fabrication method turned out to be more challenging than expected.
This might indicate that mass fabrication of meta materials holding these cell structures is
still years ahead in the future. Nevertheless the ongoing development of 3D technology is
still the limit of what is possible. In this project, casting the specimens of silicone material
in a 3D printed mould, has provided the best quality of the specimens.
In the experiments, challenges with impact of friction on the test results was eliminated by
increasing the out of plane thickness the specimens to ensure in plane buckling and provide
the ability to execute the experiments without guidance of the specimens.
In the optimisation framework, the extend of the meta material is assumed inﬁnite. To ex-
amine the consequences of this assumption, size eﬀects have been explored by simulations
of structures holding diﬀerent numbers of cells. Size eﬀects for the optimised structure
was not found, for the initial structure the studies indicates that the size eﬀect is negligible
for structures holding more than roughly speaking 10x10 cells. Therefore simulations does
not give rise to worries concerning the consequences of the assumption.
Both the experiments and the simulations conﬁrms a large potential for increasing the
buckling strength of the meta material by optimising the geometry of the cell structure. In
this project the meta material have been represented by structures having a very limited
number of cells, and the structures have been exposed to uniaxial load case even though
the cells which have been the subject to the investigation are optimised for biaxial load
case. Despite this, overall a strong coherence between the forecasts of material properties
of the meta material by Thomsen et al. [2018] and the properties found by experiments
and simulations is found.
