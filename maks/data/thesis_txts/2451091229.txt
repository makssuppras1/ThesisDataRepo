Abstract
For critical listening spaces, a low modal density in the low frequency range presents a chal-
lenge, since isolated modes can introduce audible artifacts to the acoustic signal being per-
ceived by a listener. Under the assumption, that a frequency response with a high resemblance
to a straight line would minimize the unwanted eﬀects of a low modal count, in this project,
the application of gradient-based numerical optimization was explored in order to improve the
performance of rooms at low frequencies. More explicitly, shape optimization was applied to a
rectangular-box-shaped room, in order to deﬁne an optimized layout of the walls and a height
that would yield a more regular frequency response; whilst, through topology optimization,
an optimized distribution of an absorbing element along the walls of a room was sought. For
this second exploration, the study was limited to a simple study case only in 2 dimensions.
From the results, it was seen, that an improvement in terms of the regularity of the frequency
response can indeed be achieved using both methods, and the eﬀect of the changes that the
optimized designs introduce, is a modiﬁcation of the sound ﬁeld in a way that strong eﬀects
of constructive and destructive interference near the listener’s position are avoided. In the
development of this project, the degree of freedom for the optimization in the z-direction was
limited for the shape optimization, and non-existent for the topology optimization. Never-
theless, given the results found, applying shape optimization and topology optimization to
design rooms using full 3D models seems to have the potential to be a useful tool to design
rooms with improved performance for low frequency acoustics. Another natural extension
derived of this project, is to combine the two approaches to generate one design that is, ﬁrst
optimized in terms of its shape, and then, in terms of its absorbing material distribution.

Introduction
The eﬀect of individual acoustic modes in rooms, can have a large inﬂuence on the acoustic
signal being perceived by a listener, specially, in the frequency range in which the modal
density is low.
This phenomenon is thus more relevant in small rooms, as the ﬁrst non-
zero order modes will appear in frequencies within human’s hearing range. For this reason,
when designing spaces for critical listening; such as recording studios (both monitoring and
studio rooms), listening rooms, cinemas and broadcasting studios, it is desirable to ﬁnd a
room layout which minimizes the sound coloration introduced by isolated modes. A common
approach to solve this problem, is to ﬁnd a room’s geometry that will yield a convenient
modal distribution, and complement this with the installation of absorbing elements, such as
resonators, to help control the modes that are still isolated enough to create unwanted eﬀects
inside the room.
There are several previous works that aim to present guidelines for room’s designs, that would
minimize the subjective eﬀect of the room’s response at low frequencies [1–8]. The classical
approach taken by the existing works, is to design spaces as rectangular boxes with volume-
dependent ’ideal’ room aspect ratios (or ranges of room aspect ratios), that would fulﬁll an
objective criteria, that the corresponding author considers more suitable to minimize audible
artifacts introduced by modes. Proof of the popularity of this approach is the presence of
recommendations regarding room aspect ratios in documents such as the IEC TR 60268-13
standard [6], which presents guidelines to conduct listening tests on loudspeakers; and the
European Broadcasting Union’s Technical report 3276 [7], which presents recommendations
for listening conditions for the assessment of sound programme material.
More speciﬁcally, the works dedicated to solve the problem of coloration introduced by low
modal density in rooms can be divided into 2 categories, in terms of their approach to the
problem.
On the one hand, diﬀerent authors have presented works in which they deﬁne
the desirable aspect ratios (or ranges of desirable aspect ratios) under the assumption that
the coloration introduced by low modal density can be minimized, if the eigenfrequencies of
the room are conveniently distributed. The deﬁnition of what a ’convenient’ distribution is,
changes between authors; to name a few: Bolt’s [1] hypothesis was that good performance
for critical listening at low frequencies could be achieved if the natural frequencies of the
room were evenly spaced. Later, Gilford [2] argued that coloration on the signal introduced
by modes, was mainly due to axial modes, and in order to minimize unwanted eﬀects, the
desirable design should avoid separation between adjacent axial modes (or groups of axial
modes) which were greater than 20Hz. In turn, Bonello [4] proposed that the frequency range
of interest should be divided into third octave band intervals, and that within each band, there
should be the same amount, or more, natural frequencies than in the previous one; in addition
to the previous, there should not be degenerate modes, but in case they appear, it should be
in frequency bands for which the modal count is greater than 5. Finally, Walker [5] deﬁned
a room quality index to objectively rate the performance of a room at low frequencies; this
room quality index, was obtained by calculating the square of the diﬀerence between every
pair of subsequent eigenfrequencies, then summing all these squared diﬀerences, and dividing
the sum by the total amount of modes considered (i.e. the room quality index is the the mean
square spacing of the room modes). Following this criteria, a room with a small quality index
will have a good performance as a critical listening space.
On the other hand, other works have made use of another type objective criteria to deter-
mine the quality of a room for critical listening at low frequencies; this objective measure is

Nevertheless, all the aforementioned works limit the design guidelines to rooms with rectan-
gular box geometries. And, although this geometry has been widely studied, it can create
some acoustic challenges, among others, because the 3 sets of parallel boundary surfaces make
the space prone to ﬂutter echos, and can favor the appearance of degenerated modes. As an
alternative with more degree of freedom for the designs, Floody and Venegas [10] generated
an algorithm that would optimize the 2D shape of a room for low frequencies. Their code,
supported the design of polygonal rooms, and solved the optimization problem using a genetic
algorithm. The third dimension of the room was included assuming parallel boundary sur-
faces and applying separation of variables to solve the Helmholtz equation. Furthermore, their
objective function was slightly diﬀerent to the one used by Cox and D’Antonio [8], because
they calculated the Figure of Merit by measuring the deviations of the frequency response in
terms of loudness level to its average value. To calculate the loudness level they used a model
developed using an artiﬁcial neural network and Equal-loudness-level contours for pure tones.
As it can be seen, there is a lack of consensus between diﬀerent authors, with regards to
the objective criteria that a room needs to fulﬁll in order to have a good performance as a
critical listening space for low frequencies. Nevertheless, one essential aspect to consider when
selecting an appropriate objective measure for a rooms’ performance as a listening space, is its
correlation to subjective perception. Information like the threshold that sets the limits below
which, further improvement of the objective function will not be perceived by a listener, or the
diﬀerence limen, i.e. the minimum noticeable diﬀerence for human perception, give practical
meaning to the quantities given by the objective measure. Nevertheless, the study of the
subjective perception of modes in an acoustic signal is not a simple one, as it depends on the
decay time of the mode, its frequency, the type and reproduction level of the signal used for
the study, etc. [11–13].
Following this idea, a series of publications have been dedicated to characterize the subjective
perception of artifacts introduced by a poor modal distribution and its correlation to the
2
==PAGE:8==
common objective metrics to rate a room’s quality for low frequency acoustics. The main
remarks being: 1. Metrics, which are based on the distribution of the natural frequencies of
the room, do not correlate well with the perception of listeners, specially because they do not
take into account the modal coupling arising from source-receiver positions or the eﬀect of
damping which can vary for the diﬀerent modes [9, 14]. 2. There is not a clear relationship
between the magnitudes of the Figure Of Merit and a subjective rating, therefore a diﬀerence
limen for the Figure of Merit is not available. Nevertheless, in a study presented by Wankling
and Fazenda [9], the authors found that if the FOM score is used to classify the performance
of a number of rooms as ’bad’, ’medium’, ’good and ’best’, there is a good agreement with
the subjective classiﬁcation into the same categories. 3. In contrast, there appears to be a
better correlation between the subjective perception of isolated modes, and their modal decay
[13–15]. A specially relevant work in the latter sense, is the one presented by Fazenda et
al. [13], where they propose reference frequency-dependent modal decay times below which
the distortion introduced by the resonances of the room cannot be perceived anymore. The
frequency-dependent values they presented were for study cases in which they included modes
with frequencies of up to 63 Hz, 125 Hz and 250 Hz.
Given the previous, the aim of this Master’s thesis, is to explore the application of gradient-
based numerical optimization methods, in order to ﬁnd a convenient room layout, to mini-
mize the audible artifacts introduced in an acoustic signal, by the room’s irregular frequency
response at low frequencies. More speciﬁcally, two types of structural optimization method-
ologies were explored to solve the problem. The ﬁrst one, consisted on applying shape op-
timization, in order to ﬁnd a desirable room’s geometry; and the second one, consisted on
applying topology optimization to deﬁne a convenient distribution of absorbing material along
the walls of a room.
Through shape optimization, a modiﬁcation of the distribution of resonances across the fre-
quency range can be achieved, thus, potentially eliminating isolated modes. On the other
hand, by applying absorption over the boundaries of the room, an attenuation of the already
existing irregularities can be achieved, thus, minimizing the audible artifacts that they can
cause. Consequently, the more eﬃcient way to treat the problem seems to be, to devote some
eﬀort into ﬁnding a room’s shape that would minimize irregularities ﬁrst, so that attenuating
the remaining unwanted irregularities, becomes a simpler task. Based on the previous, for this
project, the bulk of the work was devoted to the shape optimization task, whilst the topology
optimization task was limited to a case study in 2 dimensions.
In the shape optimization implementation for this project, only the wall’s distribution, was
modiﬁed.
In contrast, for the z-direction, only the height of the room was changed, and
the ﬂoor and ceiling were assumed to be hard and parallel surfaces.
On the other hand,
due to time limitation, the topology optimization implementation done for this work only
supports 2-dimensional problems, therefore, this study was limited to the xy-plane, and for
the z-dimension, the room was assumed to extend inﬁnitely.
The remaining of this document, is divided into three sections. Each of the ﬁrst two, treats
one of the two optimization problems being solved, i.e. the shape and topology optimization
problems. In both chapters, the details of the optimization problems are presented, as well
as the details of the models implemented to solve the problems, the results, and the acous-
tical analysis of said results. In turn, the third chapter contains some brief conclusions and
discussions about future work that can be derived from this thesis.

Summary
In this part of the project, the objective was to ﬁnd a desirable geometry for a room, that
would yield a convenient modal distribution that minimizes the irregularities of the frequency
response at low frequencies, given a deﬁned sources-listener combination. This was achieved
by solving a gradient-based numerical shape optimization problem, in which, the position of a
number of corners in the xy-plane of the room was changed, as well as the height of the room.
In order to evaluate the performance of the rooms, the objective function deﬁned, measured
the deviations of the frequency response to the its best-ﬁt straight line.
Regarding the details of the solution method of the optimization problem, for the case studied
here, it seems like setting a limit on the magnitude of the change per iteration, that can be
introduced to the height of the room and the coordinates of the corners that have freedom
to move, has an inﬂuence on the eﬀectiveness of the minimization process. Moreover, the
ideal limit for the amount of change that can be introduced to these design variables seems
to depend on the amount of design variables deﬁned.
In addition to the previous, the designs generated, also changed when changing the param-
eters to estimate the gradient of the objective function with respect to the design variables.
Nevertheless, when inspecting the group of the best performing designs, it was seen that, in
all cases, at least one boundary had a shape that resembled a convex arc, which can serve the
purpose to minimize the eﬀective interference between the waves incoming to the wall, and
their reﬂection.
When analyzing the acoustic properties of the optimized rooms that yielded the best perfor-
mance in light of the objective function selected, it seems like the main improvement, consists
on reducing the magnitude of the drops in the sound pressure levels that arise when the inter-
action between several modes is such, that there is a low pressure region in the area around
the position of the listener. The reduction of this eﬀect, is achieved by avoiding nodal lines
of several modes to cross the output domain. Moreover, on the optimized designs, isolated
modes can still be perceived, but that is due, at least to some extend, to the fact that the
fairly small volume of the room under study (i.e. 99.61m3) physically limits its modal density,
so there is a limit on how much modal overlap can be achieved.
1.1
Optimization problem
Commonly, when applying numerical optimization to the design of an element, the objective
is to optimize its performance whilst satisfying a set of constraints. Said performance is quan-
tiﬁed using an objective function which, as well as the constraints, are selected according to
the problem that is being solved. Furthermore, numerical design optimization can be divided
into three main categories: size optimization, shape optimization and topology optimization.
Using an approach of the ﬁrst category, the size of the components of the element of interest is
optimized; parameters like length, thickness or radius of the components of the structure can
be optimized through this approach. In turn, the methods that correspond to the second cate-
gory, modify the shape of the boundaries of the problem in order to improve the performance.
4
==PAGE:10==
And lastly, through topology optimization methods, the aim is to ﬁnd the optimum material
distribution in a speciﬁed region of the domain (also referred to as the design domain) [16].
More speciﬁcally for gradient-based shape optimization, the methodology followed consists
on changing the shape of the domain in an iterative process, in which, the next state is cal-
culated based on the current state and the gradients of the objective function and the design
constraints with respect to the design variables; this design variables are usually parameters
that modify geometrical curves that describe the boundary [17]. In general, two of the main
elements that deﬁne the shape optimization method are the parametrization of the boundary,
which can be done e.g. by geometrical curves (such as lines, arcs and splines, among others
[18]) or more complicated functions (for examples see e.g. [19–22]); and the strategy imple-
mented to update the mesh in each iteration, in order to maintain the mesh quality since the
geometry of the domain is being changed. The deﬁnition of this latter strategy is specially
relevant when using ﬁnite element method to obtain the numerical solution.
Previous works on numerical shape optimization in acoustics, include a set of publications
devoted to optimize the shape of an acoustic horn, in order to minimize the reﬂected energy
at the horn’s outlet due to the impedance mismatch between the media inside the horn and
the free ﬁeld. In [20], the authors optimized the curvature of a horn which had ﬁxed aperture
radius values at the inlet and outlet. For this, they would change the positions of the mesh
nodes in the boundary, and make an interpolation using linear nodal basis functions, to
deﬁne the remaining of the domain frontier. To calculate the state ﬁeld in each iteration,
the authors used a ﬁnite element model, and to update the mesh every time the domain
boundaries were changed, they calculated the displacements of the inner points as a function
of the displacements of the boundary points by solving a boundary value problem described by
a Laplace equation through FEM. Then, in [21], Udawalpola et al. designed the acoustic horn
by optimizing the mouth aperture in the position of the element nodes in the boundary; the
deﬁnition of the boundary between these control points was made using a linear interpolation.
For the numerical solution, they used a boundary element model. Also, Bernalnd et al. [22]
presented a work in which they used the cutFEM approach to optimize the horn’s shape.
By using cutFEM, the boundary was deﬁned using a level-set function, and no re-meshing
was needed for the domain in each iteration; instead, a special treatment and a modiﬁed
integration scheme for the elements that were crossed by the level-set function was needed.
In addition to the previous works, Risby et al. [23] applied numerical shape optimization
to design the termination of an impedance tube, using sets of quarter length and Helmholtz
resonators. To model the acoustic ﬁeld, they made use of a Boundary Element model which
included viscous and thermal loses; and for the parametrization of the boundary, they used
cubic splines.
For this project, given that the element to optimize is a room, it was decided that the design
variables would be the x and y coordinates of a number of corners in the xy-plane, as well as
the height of the room. Furthermore, between the corners of the room, the boundary would be
described using straight lines, thus, representing walls consisting of straight surfaces, which,
for construction purposes are more convenient; and, in any case, if enough control points are
deﬁned, a good degree of freedom will be granted to the design space even with straight lines.
In turn, for every design state, the updating of the mesh was done by using the approach
proposed by Persson [24], in which, an initial mesh (in the case of this work, the mesh used
in the previous iteration) is iteratively modiﬁed by solving a static force equilibrium problem.
The principle is to regard the element edges as bars which have a force-displacement that
5
==PAGE:11==
depend on their length and their original length (a behaviour which is analogous to the
behaviour of springs), and include the external forces, as the boundary condition that moves
back to the domain any node that came outside in the previous iteration; the iterative process
ﬁnishes when there is no more signiﬁcant movement of the nodes. Emphasis should be made
on the fact that, the aforementioned force equilibrium problem, has nothing to do with the
actual acoustic problem being solved in this thesis, it is merely a strategy to update the mesh
used for the ﬁnite element discretization, every time the boundary of the domain is changed.

1.2
Method
1.2.1
Objective function
Deﬁning the right objective function in an optimization process, is instrumental to ﬁnding
solutions that will eﬀectively solve the problem being tackled. However, it does not seem to be
such a straight forward choice in this case, and proof of this, is the number of diﬀerent objective
criteria that has been proposed by diﬀerent authors (some of them brieﬂy explained in the
introduction section of this work), to evaluate the performance of rooms for critical listening
at low frequencies. Nevertheless, for this work, the Figure of Merit proposed in the work done
by Cox and D’Antonio [8], was used as the objective function to be minimized, since it has
been already used in a published work related to rooms’ shape optimization, and it takes into
account the source-receiver coupling, which has been shown to be of subjective importance
[13–15]. Furthermore, even though subjective studies suggest that a higher correlation could
be obtained between an objective metric based on the modal decay times, and the subjective
perception of artifacts caused by individual modes (see e.g. [13]), the fact that the purpose of
this part of the project, is to optimize the shape of the selected room, means that not much
variation in terms of the modal decay is expected, thus an objective function based on modal
decay would not be representative.
Given the previous, the optimization done in this work, consists on ﬁnding the shape of the
room, that minimizes the deviation of its frequency response at low frequencies, with respect
to an ’idealized’ (and unfeasible for a real application) response with a straight line shape

Implementation details for the solution of the optimization problem
As previously mentioned, the solution of the shape optimization problem in Eq. 1, was sought
following a gradient-based method, in which, a minimization of the objective function is done
through an iterative process, where every new state of the design is found based on the
previous state, and the gradient of the objective function and the constrains with respect to
the design variables for that previous state. More explicitly, to ﬁnd the solution, the interior
point algorithm of the fmincon function of Matlab’s optimization toolbox [29] was used.
In order to estimate the gradients of the objective function and the constraint with respect
to the design variables, the forward ﬁnite diﬀerence method was used. This choice was made,
given that the number of design variables for the problem was small, and thus, an estimation
of this kind would not make the computation process unfeasible. In Eq. 32, the expression
for the calculation of forward ﬁnite diﬀerences is shown, and it can be seen, that there is
one parameter that needs to be deﬁned for the calculation; namely, the ﬁnite diﬀerence step
(∆ξi). Consequently, a study was conducted to determine what would be the ideal step size.
In Appendix A, the results of this analysis are shown and discussed, and from these, it was
decided that the optimization would be run using a diﬀerent step size for the design variables
related to the coordinates of the control points in the xy-plane and the height of the room.
More explicitly, the optimization problem was solved using a value of 1x10−6 for the ﬁnite
diﬀerence step of the design variable deﬁning the height of the room, and 2 diﬀerent values
of the ﬁnite diﬀerence step for the coordinates of the control points, i.e. 1x10−3 and 5x10−3.
From this two diﬀerent values, diﬀerent solutions were found for each of the optimization
cases shown in Figure 7, in Section 1.3.1, the diﬀerent sets of results obtained corresponding

23

is that they have at least one boundary with a shape that resembles a convex arc, and in most
cases, this feature can be seen in the rear wall. Convex arcs are commonly used as scattering
surfaces, as the reﬂections generated by them have wave fronts resembling the ones from line
or point sources [32]; therefore, given that a mode of a room is composed by a number of waves
travelling in diﬀerent directions, then the tendency of the optimized shapes to include convex
boundaries, might help decrease the eﬀectiveness of the interference between an incoming
wave to the boundary with the convex surface, and its reﬂection. Two examples of the eﬀect
of the convex rear wall are presented in Section 1.3.2.
Another observation that can be made, is that, it might be worth including some additional
constraints on the design variables to avoid geometrical features that might make the space ill
distributed or the construction of the design unnecessarily challenging , and therefore, unlikely
to happen. An example of this, is the sharp corners in the rear wall shown in Sub-ﬁgures
14a and 15a, which could be avoided by including, e.g. a minimum distance between the
control points in one wall and the control points in the walls perpendicular to it. Another
thing that could be desirable to avoid, is rooms for which the ceiling is too high. Namely,
from the geometries selected, it could be seen that there are values for the height of the room,
that go as high as 4.4m; and, although a room of this height can be constructed, it can bring
unnecessary challenges for the construction that could discourage its implementation; in any
case, the maximum height value can be easily controlled by setting a lower maximum value
to the design variable related to it, than the one used in this work.

Given the ﬁrst general observation from Figure 17, i.e. that the natural frequencies of the
optimized rooms change with respect to the ones of the original room, a further analysis
regarding the eﬀect of this modal rearrangement was conducted. For this, two of the other
published objective criteria to evaluate the performance for rooms in the low frequency range
were used. Namely, the one proposed by Walker [5] was used to determine if a more uniform
distribution of the modes had been obtained, and the one proposed by Bonello [4] was used
to evaluate if a signiﬁcant diﬀerence can be observed in the modal density of the room per
third octave band (in the introduction section of this thesis, a short summary of the two latter
works is given). Of course, it is not expected a priori, that the optimized shapes are rated
better when evaluating them using the aforementioned criteria, as neither were represented
in the objective function; conversely, these alternative criteria are merely used to determine
if it happens to be that, the objective designs also present improvement in these aspects.
On the one hand, Walkers proposed room’s quality index, is the mean square spacing of the
room modes, for which, a lower score might indicate that there is less modal spacing, and
thus, a better performance for the room as a critical listening space is expected, as there
would potentially be less isolated modes. From Table 3, which shows the values of this room’s
quality index for all the rooms under study, it can be seen that, actually, it is the original
room which presents a better rating; although the diﬀerence between the lower and higher
score is of less than 1Hz.
Nevertheless, a visual inspection of the frequency responses of
the optimized rooms, shows that, in the response curve for the original room, three isolated
modes are clearly distinguishable (at 28Hz, 56.7Hz and 121.3Hz); whilst, in the case of the
optimized solutions for cases 2 and 3, 4 distinguishable isolated peaks can be seen (at 26.4Hz,
53.1Hz, 81Hz and 150.5Hz for the optimization case 2, and at 28.4, 58.3, 85.4 and 151.3Hz
for the optimization case 3), and in both cases, the second and third distinguishable peaks
actually correspond to the highest deviations of the frequency response curves with respect
to their corresponding best ﬁt lines. On the other hand, by inspecting the frequency response
of the room from the optimization case 1, a very distinguishable isolated peak can be seen
around 60Hz. All these previous observations, seem to go against the intention of ﬁnding a
design that minimizes the audible eﬀects of isolated modes at low frequencies in small rooms,
since subjective studies suggest that the main subjective problem with isolated modes, is
not so much the increase in the level they cause, but the slower decay rate of sound at this
frequencies [11, 12]. Nevertheless, this later observation does not represent a failure in the
shape optimization process, but instead suggest a limited ability of the objective function
selected for this work, to limit the appearance of isolated modes.

respect to their best-ﬁt line, and focusing only on comparing the responses of the optimized

mode shapes of the new designs was conducted. The relevance of this study, is supported
on the fact that, the sound ﬁeld, for any frequency can be described as a weighted sum
of the modes of the room (see Eqs. 18 and 29). For this, the ﬁrst 20 mode shapes with
variation only in the xy-plane for each room, were extracted. Then, given that the source

The aforementioned weighted mode shapes of each room, are shown in Appendix B. From the
results, it can be seen that, for the optimized designs, many modes show nodal planes which are
conveniently curved or placed in order to avoid crossing the output domain were the objective
function was being calculated. Some examples of this, include the modes corresponding to
89.87Hz, 129.25Hz and 154.68Hz for the optimized room from case 1 (see the top row of Figure
20); 80.88Hz, 114.91Hz and 120.29Hz for the optimized room for case 2 (see the middle-row
of Figure 20); and 52.66Hz, 85.29Hz and 113.48Hz for the optimized room for case 3 (see the
bottom plot of Figure 20). This can explain why, drops in the sound pressure level which are
as marked as the ones for the original room, cannot be seen. More speciﬁcally, in the regions
between the distinguishable resonances, where many modes can contribute to the sound ﬁeld
in comparable magnitudes, having a number of modes with a high pressure value within the
output domain, can help minimize the eﬀect of low pressure lines of other modes; or, if the
number of mode shapes with nodal lines within the output domain are very spaced between
each other, there will be intermediate frequency regions, were their eﬀect will not be observed
due to the frequncy separation. This discussion reinforces the idea that, when solving a shape
optimization problem, it is important to evaluate the objective function over an area of the
domain, and not only a single point, because otherwise, the solution will not be robust to
small spatial changes happening in the implementation process. Furthermore, it also helps
to explain why none of the deviations of the optimized results are as large as the largest
deviation of the original room and the increased regularity that the frequency responses of
the new designs show.
Additionally, a more general observation regarding the shapes of the modes of the rooms,
is that, whilst for the original room, the mode shapes show the well known structured dis-
tribution that characterizes the eigenfunctions of rectangular boxes, which causes the nodal
planes and anti-nodes of several modes to coincide; for the optimized designs, the coincidence
of minimum and maximum pressure values for diﬀerent modes is much less, especially in the
region where the output domain is located. Nevertheless, for clarifying purposes, it shown be
pointed out, that a common nodal plane is indeed shared by several modes in the optimized
designs, namely, the nodal line running parallel to the x-axis and crossing the middle of the
room. This consistent nodal plane for many modes is expected due to the symmetry of the
room with respect to it; nevertheless, given the sources distribution, and the fact that they
are in phase, a number of this modes are not excited with a high amplitude relative to the
amplitude of modes with other spatial characteristics (see e.g. the modes corresponding to
39.72Hz, 55.95Hz and 75.9Hz for the optimized room for case 1; the modes corresponding to
39.09Hz, 60.24Hz and 84.15Hz for the optimized room for case 2; and the modes corresponding
to 42.42Hz, 62.02Hz and 84.24Hz for the optimized room for case 3).

diﬀerent to the distribution seen near the front wall, thus suggesting a complicated eﬀect that
decreases the eﬀective interference between the waves travelling through the space. Proof of
this, is that not much axial (or mainly axial-driven) modes in the x-axis can be seen, and
the fact that the ﬁrst resonance peak in all the optimized designs, which is due to a mainly
axial-propagating mode, does not stand out in the curve as much as the ﬁrst mode of the
optimized design does.

1.4
Discussion
From the results obtained, it can be seen that even though the optimized rooms show a sig-
niﬁcant decrease in terms of the objective function, making a further analysis on the actual
frequency responses obtained, shows that, still some unwanted features in the frequency re-
sponse can be seen. More explicitly, some isolated modes can be seen in the new designs;
and, in fact, for the rooms generated by solving the optimization cases 2 and 3, these isolated
modes seem to have more weight in the overall irregularity of the frequency response. This
fact, reﬂects a disconnection between the objective function used for the shape optimization
in this work, and the aim to reduce the audible artifacts due to irregularities in the frequency
response of a room, since the slower decay rate of the sound ﬁeld at the eigenfrequencies,
plays an important part on the audibility of the artifacts, thus, isolated resonances should
be avoided. Nevertheless, the objective function used for this works does seem to present
advantages over alternative objective functions following the more classic approach of just fo-
cusing on ﬁnding a spacing between the natural frequencies that is convenient, since, as seen
here, not only the frequencies, but also the mode shapes of the room, have an inﬂuence the
irregularity of the frequency response on a region, e.g. if many of the modes share a common
anti-node point or nodal plane.
Furthermore, from the results, it can be seen that the optimized designs show an increase
in regularity in all the frequency range, which is not surprising given the objective function
used, but the audible eﬀect of minimizing small irregularities might not be as signiﬁcant
as decreasing the larger deviations.
One possible adjustment that could be made in this
regard, is to increase the power to which the deviations of the frequency response to the
ﬁtted curve are raised. This would put more emphasis into minimizing the largest deviations
through the optimization process; and, as can be seen, some of the deviations generated by the
distinguishably isolated modes from the optimized designs from cases 2 and 3 (see Sub-ﬁgures
17b and 16c), namely, at 53.1Hz, 81Hz and 150.5Hz for the optimization case 2, and at 58.3
and 85.4 for the optimization case 3, actually correspond to the largest deviations within the
frequency range of interest.
Of course, the evaluation of these results from the shape optimization process, is based on
objective criteria, that corresponds to educated hypotheses regarding the parameters that
aﬀect the listener’s perception of modal irregularities in a frequency response. Nevertheless,
weather or not the increased regularity that the frequency response of the optimized designs
show over the one of the original room and the change in the number of visually perceivable
isolated modes, is not so easily deﬁned given the vague connection that has been found, by
published studies, between the objective functions based on the modal content of a response,
and the subjective perception of artifacts introduced by it.
It is also worth mentioning, that the results obtained here, are dependant on the source and
receiver placement, as changing the source positions or the number of sources would result in
a diﬀerent excitation of the modes, and thus, a diﬀerent frequency response. On the other
hand, changing the listeners position to a point outside the output domain, would cause the
spectra of the acoustic signal being perceived by them, to change. In this respect, it is also
important to point out, that, given that the output domain was deﬁned only on an xy-plane,
the results presented here might be sensible to changes in the height of the room.

2
Optimization of the placement of absorbing material along
the walls of a small room, using topology optimization
Summary
In this section of the thesis, through the use of topology optimization, it was sought to optimize
the placement of a resonating absorber around the walls of a horizontal plane of a room that
was assumed to extend inﬁnitely in the z-direction, in order to decrease the irregularities of
its frequency response.
For the optimization, 4 frequencies were used, corresponding to the ones, for which the fre-
quency response of the room, at a given state showed the maximum deviations with respect
of its best ﬁt line; each within a diﬀerent sub-range of the frequency range of interest. To
assure that the frequencies for which the optimization was been carried out, were indeed
the ones corresponding to the largest irregularities of the frequency response, an update of
these frequencies was done every certain amount of iterations during the optimization pro-
cess. Even by conducting the topology optimization using this limited number of frequencies,
when comparing the frequency response for the room for which the material distribution had
been optimized, to the frequency response of a room with a commonly used distribution of
absorbing material, it was seen that an improvement was achieved.
Regarding the acoustic analysis of the optimized design, it was seen that the placement of the
absorbing material was such, that it coincided with the nodal lines of most of the mode shapes
that were eﬀectively excited given the source distribution inside the room. Moreover, it was
seen, that by introducing a change in impedance along the walls of the room, a diﬀraction eﬀect
was achieved, such that it would modify the sound ﬁeld to limit the eﬀects of constructive
and destructive interference of the waves travelling through the space, inside the region of
interest around the listener’s position.
2.1
Optimization problem
In this section, as mentioned previously, the aim was to ﬁnd an optimized distribution of an
absorbing element, more explicitly, a Helmholtz resonator, along the walls of a selected room,
which would minimize the irregularities of its frequency response at low frequencies. This
would be done by solving a topology optimization problem. Due to time limitation, the study
conducted in this part was limited to 2-dimensions. More explicitly, for this study case, a FE
model of the horizontal plane of the original room used for the shape optimization section
(see Section 1.2.2) was used, whilst the z-dimension was assumed to extend inﬁnitely.
Through the literature revision stage for this part of the project, it was found that the
number of published works, in which gradient-based topology optimization has been applied
for acoustic problems, is larger than its counterpart for applications of shape optimization
in acoustics. To name a few examples, it has been used to design silencers [33, 34], acoustic
horns [35], acoustic-structure interaction problems [36, 37], among others. In addition to the
previous, more related to this project, is the work done by Düring et al. [38], in which they
apply topology optimization, to minimize the sound pressure level over a region inside a room,
by distributing absorbing and reﬂecting materials along the walls and the ceiling of the room.
To model the sound ﬁeld, 2D and 3D FEM models were used. Furthermore, they applied

topology optimization to design an outdoor sound barrier. For both cases, the optimization
was done both, for a single frequency and for a range of frequencies.

region with material, and thus an absorbing region. Using this type of design variables, the

admittance value that is e.g. 0.5 times the admittance value (both for the real and imaginary
part).

2.3
Results and analysis
As mentioned in Section 2.2.1, for the solution of the topology optimization problem in this
chapter, the minimization of the square of the deviations of the frequency response with
respect to its best-ﬁt line, was done, using a limited amount of frequencies. More explicitly,
the frequency range of interest, was divided into 4 sub-ranges of equal size; and then, the
frequency for which the largest deviation was observed, within each of the frequency sub-
ranges, was selected for the optimization process. Then, after a deﬁned amount of iterations,
the complete frequency response was calculated, and the frequencies for which the largest
deviations appeared within each of the same frequency sub-ranges, were selected and the
optimization process continued using the new selected frequencies.
In order to determine
the best number of iterations for the frequencies update, the problem was solved in diﬀerent
occasions changing the value of this parameter. In Table 4 the values used for this parameter
can be seen.
Nevertheless, since in the iterative optimization process, the value of the surface fraction of
the walls that was covered by material would drop some times, a condition was included in
the code, so that, even if the number of iterations set for the frequencies update was reached,
if the amount of surface fraction was less than a minimum (0.27 to be more speciﬁc), the
update was not done, and the optimization process was continued until the minimum volume
fraction was achieved or the stop condition was reached. In the later case, the optimization
was concluded. The motivation behind including this extra condition to be fulﬁlled, was to
avoid that the update of the frequencies for the optimization was done in a state of the design
that causes a frequency response with large deviations with respect to its best-ﬁt line, not
due to the material layout, but instead, for the lack of it.
Furthermore, as mentioned in Section 2.2.4, it was deﬁned, that the design variable vector
should be composed with only ones and zeros (or values very close to ones and zeros); which at
the end, would indicate whether or not to place a Helmholtz resonator on a speciﬁc area of the
walls. Consequently, in order to force the topology optimization process to yield a solution for
which all the design variables were approximately zeros and ones, every optimization process
was started using a penalization value of 1 (see Section 2.2.4), and after a certain number of
iterations, the penalization number was raised by 1 unity. This penalization number raise was
repeated in intervals of iterations equal to the one used for the ﬁrst update, until the solution
converged, or until there was an update in the frequencies for which the optimization process
was conducted. In the later case, the optimization process was restarted using a penalization
value of 1 again. To determine the best number of iterations for updating the penalization
value, the optimization problem was run several times using 10, 15 and 20 iterations intervals
between updates of the penalization value.
In Table 4, the complete list of parameter settings used for the solution of the optimization
problem, can be seen. It should be noted that, as seen in the Table, one parameter deﬁnition
for the solution of the optimization process aﬀects the fulﬁllment of the aforementioned aim
to obtain a vector of design variables consisting only on ones and zeros. More explicitly, the
ﬁlter radius (see Section 2.2.5) was set to 3 times the maximum length of the elements of the
mesh (i.e. 0.636m), thus, introducing fairly large grey areas. Nevertheless, this value of ﬁlter
radius was selected because, on preliminary results, it was observed that this value would
yield more deﬁned black and white solutions by avoiding a lot of ﬂuctuation of the material
distribution across the space.

the discontinuity towards this area might not be a minor feature. In fact, as can be seen in
Figure 29, all the optimized designs generated show a discontinuity in the corner.

2.4
Discussion
Naturally, a sensible decision that aﬀects the eﬀect of absorbing material in reducing the
irregularities of the frequency response, is its selection.
Furthermore, when dealing with
absorbers of the resonant type, this selection might be more important, as they can have
marked frequency-dependant characteristics.
Therefore, it is expected that changing the
absorbing material to be used as an element to cover the room, would change the ﬁnal outcome
of the optimization process, if e.g. a resonator with a better performance around in the lower
end of the frequency range is used, or a combination of resonators tuned at diﬀerent resonant
frequencies is selected.

3
Conclusions and future work
In these work, it was shown, that an increase in the regularity of the frequency response of a
room can be achieved using numerical gradient-based optimization methods. More explicitly,
the cases studied here consisted on applying shape optimization to deﬁne a desirable room
geometry and topology optimization to deﬁne a desirable distribution of absorbing material.
In the ﬁrst chapter of this thesis, through shape optimization, a desirable layout of the walls
of the room, as well as its height were found, eﬀectively, achieving an improved regularity on
the frequency response, compared to one of a rectangular-box-shaped room with comparable
volume.
When solving the shape optimization problem, it was seen that limiting the amount of change
that can be introduced to the design variables had an inﬂuence on the result of the minimiza-
tion of the objective function. Furthermore, the best limit value for the amount of change on
the design variables per iteration was dependant on the amount of design variables deﬁned
for the problem.
Furthermore, by changing the parameters for the solution of the optimization problem, like
the estimation of the gradient of the objective function with respect to the design variables,
and the limit of the change that can be introduced to the design variables per iteration,
diﬀerent designs were obtained, nevertheless, they all showed at least one boundary that had
a convex shape, which might serve the purpose of introducing some scattering in the reﬂection
of incoming waves, thus limiting the eﬀectiveness of the interference between the incoming
and reﬂected waves on that surface.
In terms of the acoustic evaluation of the selected designs, it was seen that a change on the
value of the objective function between the 43% and the 48% was achieved. By examining
the sound ﬁeld of the selected designs, and describing it in terms of its eigenfunctions, it was
seen, that the increase in the regularity, is due to the fact that the new layouts show mode
shapes that avoid nodal planes crossing through the output domain. The later observation
shows that a regular frequency response not only depends on the modal distribution, but also
on the eigenfunctions that describe the sound ﬁeld. And thus, also proves the importance of
deﬁning a sensible sources and listener’s position when attempting to improve the regularity
of a frequency response of a room, as the result is dependent on the sources and receiver
placement, since a diﬀerent layout of sources will excite the modes in a diﬀerent way, thus
changing the frequency response’s shape; and, in the same way, a change on the listener’s
position that puts him outside the area for which the optimization is being conducted, will
cause that the spectra of the signal perceived by him to change. In this regard, the fact that
the output domain for this work was deﬁned as a horizontal plane, means that the obtained
results in this work can be sensible to changes in the height or ﬂoor and ceiling layout of the
room. Nevertheless, by extending the model for the sound ﬁeld to support a full 3D model,
the output domain can be deﬁned in 3 dimensions, thus, improving the robustness of the
results to changes in the ﬂoor and ceiling layouts.
In terms of the modal distribution, not much changes were observed between the optimized
and original rooms.
Nevertheless, a drawback that was observed from using the selected
objective function, which evaluated the performance of a room in terms of the regularity of
its objective function, is that a room that rates well in it, can even show a larger amount of
isolated modes in the frequency response. This goes against the ﬁnal goal that is sought when
optimizing rooms for critical listening at low frequencies.
53
==PAGE:59==
For the second part of this work, a control of the frequency response’s irregularities was
attempted by an seeking an optimal distribution of absorbing material along the walls of a
room. The case under study here, was limited to 2 dimensions, thus, the room in the z-
direction was assumed to extend inﬁnitely. Furthermore, the absorbent element deﬁned to be
changed in the room, was a Helmholtz resonator.
For the optimization only four frequencies were used, corresponding to the ones for which the
frequency response showed its largest deviation with respect to its bet ﬁt straight line. When
the best of the optimized designs was selected and its performance in terms of the objective
function was compared to the frequency response of a design with a generic absorbing material
placement, it was seen that the optimized designed also showed an improvement.
Furthermore, the eﬀect of optimizing the placement of absorbing material along the walls of a
room, seems to be similar to the eﬀect of optimizing the shape of the room, in the sense that
both modiﬁcations introduce a change in the sound ﬁeld distribution, such that interference
eﬀects that cause very large or very low values of sound pressure level, are minimized inside
the output domain.
In light of the results obtained by applying shape optimization and topology optimization on
problems that were no fully 3 dimensional, which proved that an eﬀective regularization eﬀect
on the frequency response can be achieved, it is expected that when applying this numerical
optimization methods to models that are fully 3 dimensional, a greater improvement would be
achieved, as a greater degree of freedom will be given to the designs. Thus, labeling gradient-
based numerical optimization methods as a a promising tool to design better performing
rooms as critical listening spaces at low frequencies.
Finally, there is a question still to solve regarding the subjective meaning of the changes
obtained in terms of the frequency responses’ irregularities. The lack of this information,
leaves uncertainty with respect to the practical signiﬁcance of the decrease in the objective
function value achieved by the rooms with the optimized layouts.

Appendix A. Analysis to determine the ﬁnite diﬀerence step
length for the numerical shape optimization
In order to determine a suitable forward ﬁnite diﬀerence step, to estimate the gradient of
the objective function with respect to the design variables in the shape optimization process
described in Section 1, an analysis was carried out to see the variation of the value of the
estimated partial derivatives of the objective function, with respect to each of the design
variables, when the step size of the forward ﬁnite diﬀerence calculation was changed.
According to Jamshid [18], the optimal size of the ﬁnite diﬀerence step can be diﬀerent for all
the design variables, and can vary for each optimization cycle. Therefore, for this analysis,
the evolution of the partial derivatives with respect to the step length, was calculated for 4
rooms with diﬀerent number of degrees of freedom and geometries. The plan view and control
points of each of the cases analyzed to determine the ﬁnite diﬀerence step, are shown in Figure
39; and the design variables in each case are the x and y coordinates of the control points
(identiﬁed with a non-primed number in the sketch), and the height of the room.
The left column of Figure 40, shows the evolution of the partial derivatives of the objective
function with respect to the design variables for the cases in Figure 39; note that the in the
y-axis, the values of the partial derivatives have been normalized. The ﬁrst observation that
can be done by inspecting the plots, is that the trends of the curves related to control points
(all the continuous lines) are very diﬀerent to the trend of the curve related to the height of
the room (the dashed line). Furthermore, since the error in the estimation is high at certain
ranges of the analysis, a more detailed look into a narrower range of the ﬁnite diﬀerence step
length is taken separately for the variables related to the control points (shown in the middle
column) and the height (shown in the right column).
From the middle and right columns of Figure 40, it can be seen that the behaviour of the
derivative with respect to the height is more stable when changing the step length, than
the derivatives with respect of the x and y coordinates of the corners of the room. Given
the previous, and the behaviour of the curves in the center and right columns of Figure 40,
it was determined that the step size for the partial derivative estimation of the objective
function with respect to the height, would be done using a step of 1x10−6, whilst for the ﬁnite
diﬀerence step length related to the corners of the room, two values were going to be used;
namely, 1x10−3 and 5x10−3.
Finally, in order to have an idea of the validity of the ﬁnite diﬀerence steps determined for
the solution of the Shape optimization problems, the same analysis of the evolution of the
partial derivative estimations with respect to the step length, was conducted to the optimized
geometries shown in Figure 16. The resulting plots are shown in Figure 41; and it can be
seen that there is a similar trend of the curves, as the ones observed in the initial analysis of
the ﬁnite diﬀerence estimation evolution shown in Figure 40. Nevertheless, in the Sub-ﬁgures
showing the evolution of the values of the gradient, related to the coordinates of the control
points of the room obtained in the optimization cases 2 and 3 (the Sub-ﬁgures in the middle
and bottom row and in the middle column of Figure 41), a more irregular behaviour can be
seen for the variables related to the x coordinates of the control points 2 and 3 (see Figure
7b) for the optimized room from case 2; and for the x coordinate of the control point 1 and
the x and y coordinates of the control point 3.
Given the previous, the evolution of the objective function for the optimization process of
