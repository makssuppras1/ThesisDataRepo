Abstract
Reynolds averaged Navier-Stokes(RANS) models are the most widely used numerical
models to simulate turbulent ﬂows. Among the RANS models most popular models
are two equation models respectively k-ω and k-ϵ. Among the these two models k-ω
model is well know for it performance under adverse pressure gradient but the model
fails to give a reasonable prediction of turbulence onset Reynolds number. This
problem was addressed by Wilcox. In order to improve the k-ω model Wilcox suggest
changes to the model. The k-ω model with the modiﬁcation suggested by Wilcox
is reﬀed as k-ω transition model. The goal of this study is to simulate transition to
turbulence using Wilcox k-ω transition model and studying the predictive accuracy
of the mode.
In this study oscillating wave boundary layer ﬂow is simulated using the k-ω tran-
sition model to study the transition region perdition of the model, results are in
good agreement with the experimental measurements. Then the simulation using
both k-ω transition model and k-ω model with out modiﬁcation were conducted
for ﬂow around a smooth cylinder in a steady current to study the performance of
the models in predicting ﬂow regimes and the drag crisis.The k-ω transition model
produced better results in predicting drag crisis compared to k-ω model with out
modiﬁcations.

Introduction
Taylor and von Karman(1937), deﬁned turbulence as "Turbulence is an irregular
motion which in general makes its appearance in ﬂuids ﬂows, when they ﬂow past
solid surfaces or even when neighboring streams of the same ﬂuid ﬂow past or over
one another". Some of the key elements of turbulence are that it occurs at very high
Reynolds number, and it occurs over a large range of length and time scales,and is
fully three-dimensional and time-dependent. Most ﬂuid ﬂows are turbulent it occurs
in all macroscopic scales throughout the known Universe from the circulatory and
respiratory systems of living creatures, to oceans and atmosphere. Theoretical anal-
ysis and prediction of turbulence has been, and still to this date is, the fundamental
problem of ﬂuid dynamics, particularly of computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD).
The major diﬃculty arises from the random nature of turbulence phenomena. Be-
cause of this random ﬂuctuations of various ﬂow properties it has been customary
to work with statistical approach. All engineering purposes are best served by using
the procedure introduced by Reynolds(1895) in which all quantities are expressed
as the sum of mean and ﬂuctuating parts. Then the time average of continuity
and Navier-Stokes equation is formed. The resulting averaged Navier-Strockes is
popularly know as Reynolds averaged Navier-Strocks equation(RANS). The non-
linearity of Navier-Strockes equation leads to appearance of momentum ﬂuxes that
act as apparent stresses through out the ﬂow these stress are popularly know as
Reynolds-stress. The Reynolds stress are previously unknown and deriving equa-
tions for these stresses results in equations with additional unknown quantities. This
issue of closure is know as closure problem. Closure problem is further elaborated
in Chapter2(Section 2.2) . The function of turbulence modeling is to devise approx-
imations for the unknown correlations in terms of ﬂow properties that are known so
that a suﬃcient number of equations exits to close the system of equation.
In past century of research on turbulence models has developed four main categories
of turbulence models :
1. Algebraic(Zero-Equation) Models
2. One-Equation Models
3. Two-Equation Models
4. Second-order closure Models
From above mentioned categories two-equation turbulence models are very widely
used as they oﬀer a good compromise between numerical eﬀort and computational
accuracy. When compared to Algebraic models and one equation models, two equa-
tion models provide wide rage of application from separated ﬂows to attached bound-
ary layer under adverse pressure gradient. Both one equation models and Algebraic

number. Prediction of transition to turbulence is really important for the engineer-
ing applications. To better understand the importance turbulence onset prediction
lets take an example of stalling angle prediction of aerofoil. If the model predicts
transition to turbulence ﬂow at lower Reynolds number then model will predict much
higher stalling angle at that Reynolds number and this will lead to over estimation
of the aerofoil performance.This is because the turbulent boundary layers separates
much later compared to laminar boundary layer under same adverse pressure gradi-
ent. Let us considered the most studied cases in history of ﬂuid ﬂows which is ﬂow
around a cylinder due to its numerous engineering applications like oﬀshore plat-
forms, underwater pipelines, bridges etc, due to prediction of turbulence transition
at lower Reynolds numbers all variations of k-ω models including k-ω SST [Yuce and
Ahmed Kareem, 2016] models fail to predict the sudden drop in drag coeﬃcients
at reasonable Reynolds number. The sudden drag drop for the ﬂow around the
cylinders happens in the range of 3 ∗105<Re<3.5 ∗106. Realizing the issue that the
k-ω models fails to predict reasonable good values for turbulence onset Reynolds
number David C.Wilcox proposed the transition model which is explained in detail
in section 2. In this study Wilcox’s (2006) transition model is incorporated into
OpenFoam to study the performance of the model.

turbulence onset prediction of the model.

1.1
Brief History of k-ω models
The very ﬁrst two-equation model was developed by Kolmogorov (1942).
Kol-
mogorov chose the kinetic energy of the turbulence as one of his turbulence pa-
rameters and modeled a diﬀerential equation governing its behavior. His second
parameter was dissipation per unit turbulence Kinetic energy, ω. There are two
note worthy feature of Kolmogorov’s model.
There is no analogy to production
term in the ω equation.This is because of Kolmogorov’s notion that ω is associated
with the smallest scale of turbulence and thus there is no interaction with the mean
motion. But this notion of kolmogorov was wrong as the large scale energy bearing
eddies will determine the time scale of turbulence which in turn will determine the
dissipation rate. Other noteworthy feature is that there was no molecular diﬀusion
term which restricts the application of this model to high Reynolds number ﬂows
and this model cannot be integrated through viscus sublayer.
Independent from Kolmogorov, Saﬀman(1970) developed a k-ω model. Saﬀman de-
scribed ω as "a frequency characteristic of turbulence decay process under its self
interaction". Saﬀman unlike Kolmogorov introduced the production term in the ω
equation. The most signiﬁcant insight Saﬀman had was to neglect the molecular
viscosity in the model equations. He formally represented molecular viscosity by
adding molecular diﬀusive to the model. Then boundary condition at the rigid wall
could be applied to wall itself. This laid the foundation for one of the most advan-
tages feature of the k-ω models that is the ease with which the equations can be
integrated through the viscous sublayer.
After these two models the most noteworthy model was Wilcox and Rubesin’s model
(1980) this model introduced a proportionality term in the omega equation which
limited the peak value of turbulent length scale. Which greatly improved the pre-
dictions for turbulent boundary layers. Similar to Saﬀman’s model the ω equation
was represented in terms of ω2. Building up from Wilcox-Rubesin model Wilcox
developed his own model which is known as Wilcox’s (1998) k-ω Model. The modi-
ﬁcation that Wilcox(1988) made improved the predictive accuracy of the model for
the ﬂow under adverse pressure gradient.The modiﬁcation Wilcox made was to write
the ω equation in terms of ω itself rather than in terms of ω2. Wilcox deﬁned ω as
ratio of ϵ to k, i.e, the rate of dissipation per unit turbulence kinetic energy.
Wilcox developed the k-ω transition model in 1994.Wilcox’s journal paper [Wilcox.,
1994] deﬁned turbulence Reynolds number and made changes to two closure coef-
ﬁcients in Wilcox’s(1998) model to improve its turbulence onset predictions . The
transition model is very similar to Wilcox k-ω 1998 model. Turbulence Reynolds
number controls the turbulence onset prediction of transition model. Wilcox’s 1998
and transition model have same fundamental equation. As Wilcox’s(1988) model
evolved the transition model was updated. The latest version of the Wilcox’s model
is know as Wilcox(2006) k-ω model. This model is also known as the k-ω standard
model. The most important additions to Wilcox’s-1998 model are addition of cross

goes lower than 5 then the turbulence onset prediction from k-ω transition model
become unrealistic.

initial boundary conditions. The key point here is that model behaviour is only
reliable as long as the criteria ωoδcr/Uo > 5 is satisﬁed.

predictions. All the data needed to compare the results are taken from the book
Hydrodynamics around the cylindrical structures by Sumers and Fredsøe [1999].
The drag crisis for the ﬂow around the cylinder is referred to the sudden drop
in drag which happens in the critical(lower transition) region (3 ∗105 < Re <
3.5105).At critical Reynolds number(Re = 3∗105) the boundary layer turns turbulent
at the ﬂow separation point. As a result, the shear layer eddies cause mixing of
the ﬂow in the boundary layer.This energizes the boundary layer and leads to its
reattachment.This leads to delay in separation which is associated with narrowing
of wake this leads to signiﬁcant reduction in the drag. Figure(6.14b),shows the drag
coeﬃcient values for a smooth cylinder as function of Reynolds number. The plot is
taken from Sumers and Fredsøe [1999] book, the plot is made from the experimental
data collection by Wieselsberger(1921) for 40 < Re < 5 ∗105 and the schewel(1983)
for Re > 105.

The ﬂow around the smooth cylinder is simulated in 2-dimensions. There are many
papers studying the simulation results sensitivity of domain size on the calculated
values of lift and drag coeﬃcients. For this study domain size recommended by
Guilherme Vaz et al[2012] is chosen because the simulation conducted in this study
are in the range of Reynolds numbers(105 < Re < 106) which are in interest of
this study. Figure(6.1) shows the domain dimensions, from the center of the circle
to the end of the domain in the ﬂow direction the domain size is 40 times the
diameter(40D),And in the rest of the direction from the center of the circle the
domain size is 15 times the diameter (15D).

7
Conclusion
The preset thesis focuses on simulating transition to turbulence using RANS models.
The RANS models Chosen to achieve this objective is k-ω transition model proposed
by Wilcox(2006). Wilcox’s k-ω transition models was incorporated into Open-source
CFD toolbox OpenFOAM (version 3.1) for conducting laminar to turbulence tran-
sition simulations. The model is validated against the experimental measured by
David R.Faruman et al(2010). The results show a good agreement with the ex-
perimental measurements, and the k-ω transition model showed improvement in
computing turbulence kinetic energy over k-ω standard model.
The Sensitivity study on k-ω transition model, showed that the turbulence onset
location predicted by model is not very sensitive to the initial values of turbulence
kinetic energy, and to the initial value of turbulence Reynolds number. The k-ω
transition model shows impressive performance in computing laminar to turbulence
transition for oscillating boundary layer ﬂows. The simulation results show that the
transition from laminar to turbulence does not happen abruptly which is concurrent
with the experimental measurements by Jesen et al. (1989). The results also show
that the rang of Reynolds numbers in which the laminar to turbulence transition
occurs reduced as the phase angle is reduced. But the k-ω transition model predicts
turbulence onset at a lower Reynolds number than the experimentally measured
values.
The last chapter of this study draws a comparison between the performance of the
k-ω transition models to the k-ω standard model in computing the ﬂow around a
smooth cylinder in a study current. The results are compared with the experimen-
tal observation and measurements documented in the book "Hydrodybamics around
cylindrical structures" by B.mutlu Sumer and Jørgen Fredsøe 1999. Both k-ω stan-
dard and transition models show good performance in computing the ﬂow regime in
the laminar region. The k-ω standard models predicts turbulence at the boundary
layer separation point at a very low Reynolds number which leads the model in pre-
dicting drag crisis at very low Reynolds numbers.The experimental measurements
show the drag crisis happens at Reynolds number in the region of Re = 3 ∗105,
whereas the standard model predicts the drag crisis in the region of Re = 5 ∗104.
The k-ω transition model shows improvement over k-ω standard model in predicting
the drag crisis region. The transition models predicts the drag crisis in the region of
4 ∗105 < Re < 9 ∗105 which is much closer to the experimental measurements. The
exact location where the k-ω transition model can be narrowed down by conducting
simulation at few more Reynolds number in between 4 ∗105 < Re < 9105.
