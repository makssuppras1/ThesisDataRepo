Summary English 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The aim of the thesis is to discover new loyalty channels between a company and its customers 
through the use of the newest self-learning algorithms in the form of service chatbots. 
 
To be clear, chatbots are not ‘fresh’ news – they have been around for many years but they have all 
been rather “dumb” and static until mid 2016 where self-learning algorithms had its breakthrough. 
 
The structure of the thesis is build upon a theoretical and empirical research which creates the 
foundation for the four different iterations with new perspectives on loyalty creation for the airline 
company SAS and its loyalty and bonus program, SAS Eurobonus.  
 
The tools used for searching, analysing and defining the outcome of this thesis have been the 
Elements of Value, UX prototyping combined with the Lean approach and User Story Mapping. 
 
The results, based on the four iterations, supported the hypothesis in the sense that it would be 
plausible for SAS and its customers to create a new loyalty channel through the use of a chatbot as 
the ‘bridge to close the gap’ between its customers. 
 
Finally, the thesis will gather the core findings in an outlook to future works presenting an action 
plan on how to implement a production ready chatbot. 
 
Overall the thesis shall be seen as exploratory iterative processes on how chatbots can be used to 
discover new loyalty channels that potentially could create value in untouched areas.

Chapter 1 
 
 
Introduction 
The purpose of the thesis is to seek new loyalty channels for value creation through the use of 
modern state of the art technology in the sense of a chatbot AI.  
The reason for choosing this area of study is due to the enormous potential that lies within self-
learning algorithms (a layer of Deep Learning) which is the newest break-through within the field 
of AI inspired by the artificial neural networks of the human brain. Even though this thesis will only 
scratch the surface of this field, it’s relevant to mention as it makes up the foundation of (newer) 
chatbots. In the author’s opinion, the new generation of chatbots with AI will affect the world in the 
same way that iOS-apps did somewhere back in 2006. 
 
Getting back to how humans interact with their surroundings, it is scientifically approved2 that in 
terms of how ‘home’ we feel (related to security, familiarisation) the better we perform in any given 
situation. 
 
“Perhaps surprisingly, the benefits of deep processing may not lie in the learning process itself. 
Instead, deep processing may influence subsequent events. More precisely, attention to meaning 
may help you by virtue of facilitating retrieval of the memory later on.”3 
 
This leads to how the human brain functions in terms of memorising and handling memories 
(recalling/suppressing memories), which is what humans use when facing new situations. To phrase 
it in another way: the more we know the better we perform or navigate in any situation.  
This could be interpreted as the quest for the thesis which is to: investigate if a chatbot can generate 
human emotions, more specifically provoke the emotion of loyalty, through modern AI-technology 
thus finding new loyalty channels between company and customer.  
 
The concept of ‘loyalty is ambiguous and thus seems important to dwell at. Especially when talking 
about loyalty channels like the well-known bonus programs initiated by the big corporations (e.g. 
Coop, SAS Eurobonus etc.). When you lookup “loyalty” this is the definition you get:  
 
“Loyalty can only occur between one or more living organisms that share the same cause or goal”4 
 
So as an extension to the above definition, the question is: can a person then be loyal to an 
organization or a company?

Current loyalty programs are (usually) to be considered as “non-human” and if we believe that 
loyalty is strictly an interpersonal matter and only occurs between two humans, then there is very 
little hope for the future of any loyalty program.  
Throughout the thesis this statement will be questioned and tested through several iterations of a 
build service chatbot that will serve as an ‘extended arm’ of a company in terms of human 
interaction.  
The thesis will especially have found inspiration from an IBM report [A4] A new chatbot for 
customer service on social media where it found that the interaction (customer support) between the 
company and its customers were distributed as 40% emotional and 60% information, which gives a 
perspective on what kind of support the customers demand.  
The thesis will also have found inspiration within the field of Cognitive Science where one 
distinguishes between “intentional” vs. “incidental” learning. This is very important in the sense 
that humans perform best in well-known and relaxed situations, which brings up the question; if a 
service chatbot can create an environment where the user will be exposed to “incidental” learning 
through the guidance of it and functions it may deliver. If so, a chatbot can function as an ‘extended 
arm’, bridging the corporate world between the customer’s well-known environment, or in other 
words: creating a channel which could, potentially, be the ‘missing’ link when it comes to creating 
true loyalty. Facebook’s Messenger platform is a channel that has been closest to succeed in 
bridging the gap between company and costumer through what appears to be a more ‘personal’ way 
of communicating. However, big corporations with a personal relationship to its customers still are, 
untouched land.  
In a solely technical perspective, finding new loyalty channels is about optimizing the general 
workflow to reach a greater user experience which, until now, haven’t been accomplished.

Methods 
The approach for the thesis has strived to be a floating lean-build process. This means that just 
enough information has been collected in order to talk, build and test for then to re-evaluate.  
For the scope of a master thesis it is important to move fairly fast, which is why the Lean Startup-
approach has been adapted for a build-measure-learn loop, which has always been at the back of my 
mind working as the main driver for fast progressing.  
The focal point for the thesis is to find value elements for loyalty creation and for this process 
Harvard Business Review released a paper called: The Elements of Value, containing theories that 
have been broadly applied to this thesis in terms of the investigations on value creation. 
Introduction to the Elements of Value Pyramid 
The Elements of Value was published in Harvard Business Review, September 2016 by Eric 
Almquist. It was developed to better understand value through the eyes of the customer; hereby 
measuring and delivering what consumers really want.  
 
The Elements of Value is based on the identified 30 elements (of value) divided into four different 
categories in a pyramid-shape.  
Starting form the bottom and up: Functional – “What does it do”, Emotional – “How does it feel”, 
Life Changing – “How does it change my life” and Social Impact – “What value to society”.   
The pyramid, as illustrated in image 2 below, has taken its form and dimensions from Marslow’s 
pyramid of needs, and is shaped in a 2-dimensional coordinate system. 
 
==PAGE:22==
 22 
 
Image 2 The Elements of Value, September 2016 Harvard Business Review 
When looking at the elements within the four categories they have been placed accordingly: 
“Inwardly-focused values” on the left (-x) and the “outwardly-focused values” placed on the right 
(+x). The elements are listed form the bottom up starting at a Functional level crawling up to the 
top to a “epic realization”. 
 
As mentioned, the initial entry point of the research was done from the hypothesis, that Marslow’s 
needs were never used the way it was supposed to, but more as an example of human needs, but not 
as an applied model.  
Furthermore, human needs and demands have shifted dramatically over a (short) course of the last 
20+ years. The following storyline simply illustrates this crucial point: how rapid development has 
managed to change our worldview and beliefs and hence our needs and demands:  
Not very long ago, there was a time where news papers were the only source for citizens to get 
information, and only once a day, but then came along the FM-radio and disrupted the market of 
information-channels as it could deliver the same news but instantly and with elements of 
entertainment. This was true until the television became everybody’s property still delivering the 
same news, but now in visuals. As we reached the early 90’s the World Wide Web met the masses 
introducing on-demand services, TV, news, shopping and entertainment anywhere anytime. Our 
needs and demands for instant personalisation were born and then everything was forever changed. 
From here, the rest is history. 
 
Companies that are doing well are companies that are playing at multiple elements at once, and 
particular from the top shelter of the pyramid. Tech-companies typically master the functional

Loyalty and Bonus programs 
In the following sections the concept of ‘Bonus and Loyalty-programs’ will be discussed in brief to 
understand the similarities and the differences. Loyalty and Bonus programs should be 
differentiated and treated as two separate elements with a common denominator of creating value 
for its users. If you look at Wikipedia for loyalty you will get this quote [L15]: 
 
Loyalty programs are structured marketing strategies designed by merchants to encourage customers 
to continue to shop at or use the services of businesses associated with each program…. 
marketing generally and in retailing more specifically, a loyalty card, rewards card, points card, 
advantage card, or club card is a plastic or paper card, visually similar to a credit card, debit card, or 
digital card that identifies the card holder as a member in a loyalty program. 
 
As stated in the quote the understanding of Loyalty is that it (hardly) cannot exist without a bonus 
program and vice versa. Throughout the thesis this will be proven to be false and that Loyalty and 
Bonus programs can and should be differentiated autonomously.  
In the following sections, a handful of well-known companies will be investigated and mapped 
according to the Elements of Value. The findings will be used to decide which direction the thesis 
will take, depending on where it seems like the most Elements of Value can be added. But first: 
how to distinguish the two concepts: 
Bonus Programs 
A bonus program is in its core a “thing” that a customer signs up for so that every time he purchases 
something, he will earn points with a fiat value which can be used for purchasing goods or services. 
This is also referred to as “Earn and Burn” concepts to ‘hook’ the customer and therefore force him 
to be loyal, as the points typically are useless outside the environment. 
 
The idea of a bonus program saw the light for the first time in the 80’s where American Airlines 
launched the worlds first Bonus and tier program. The more frequently a customer travelled the 
more points he would earn and the more “free” tickets he could get. Since then, bonus points have 
been used as a ‘symbol’ of how loyal a customer is: the more points, the more loyal. But the points 
do not necessarily reflect loyalty as each point will expire, typically, within 4 years of earning, and 
if you don’t have enough points, then there won’t be nothing to spend them on. This scenario is a 
problem, not just for the user, but for the companies as well. When a customer earns points these 
points will be equivalent to x dollars and it is by law a duty to freeze the equivalent dollars (to 
points) in a bank account until they are either used or have expired. In an interview with ÞÓRÐUR 
WALDORFF, IT and Loyalty Manager at Icelandair, he states that around 20% of all points expire, 
which is for no good for either parts! The rest is either spent or (for their Gold members only) 
“frozen” with no expiry date. Furthermore, Icelandair as a small company, have at any given time, 
around USD 22 million frozen at their account, for accounting, just waiting to be spent by the 
customers – then imagine the amounts big corporations holds. So this can be a costly affair, which 
is also why it is close to impossible for small shops to offer their customers this kind of service.

In recent years, a new sort of bonus program has seen the light of day. Witch is bonus and loyalty 
clubs that you buy into for a small or big annually membership fee. This means that one doesn’t 
earn the access to the tier-latter but simply buys the access to premium benefits. A good example of 
such a bonus program is the hotel alliance IGH where you, as a customer, have two options:   
 
• Enter the regular bonus system and move up the latter equivalent to your ‘usage’. 
• Buy into what they call the ‘Ambassadors Club’, which includes very lucrative benefits. 
 
On top of the buy-in option there is a next level that can only be achieved by invitation only (Royal 
Platinum Ambassador), where there is no annual fee and extreme benefits (for example available 
rooms at all times, personalized checkout hours, free minibar etc.). This can be seen as a 
omnichannel bonus and loyalty platform and is an very interesting business model in terms of 
adding value to both the business and the customer. 
Loyalty

consideration that it is highly doubtful that a human-being can feel loyalty towards a company or

Conversational Interface (CI) & Commerce 
The conversational interface (CI) was the original way of human computer interaction, before 
Apple and IBM took computer interaction to the next level with a graphical interface (GUI). Since 
then the main focus for development has been on the GUI and its interaction. But in 2016, at the 
Microsoft Build conference, CEO Satya Nadella predicted the future of Microsoft would lie in the 
field of “conversation as a platform” meaning that they should focus much less on the Office part 
and more on their AI’s Cortana, Tay and of course their Chatbot API: LUIS.  
 
"Bots are like new applications that you can converse with," said Nadella, adding that this system 
might include "personal assistants calling on bots on your behalf." [L13 Vincent] 
 
The same trend was seen at the Google I/O event, June 2016 where Sundar Pichai [L 14 Google 
I/O] announced the new ventures for Google. Amongst others, the main announced products were 
conversational chatbots through their Google Home Assistance (voice) and through their new Allo 
app (written), which will be discussed in the section about moving in to the customers pocket at 
page 24 which also remarks by the quote from Facebook’s guidelines for their Bot systems: 
 
 
“Messenger bots are conversational: interaction is an extension of the chat experience that's 
familiar to anyone with a mobile phone. It's easy to understand and simple to use. The thread 
provides a permanent, canonical record. And it can feel far more human than an app or website—
after all, what could be more human than a conversation?”6 
 
So what is a Conversational interface? A CI is any User Interface (UI) that pretends to be a “real” 
human through interacting (chatting). The fundamental idea is that the user should get the feeling of 
having a conversation with another human-being, and the best case scenario would be, if the user 
gets an idea of the chatbot having a personality. On the other hand, the worst-case scenario would 
be that the user simply sees the chatbot as a “dumb” robot only capable of interacting with pre-build 
answers.  
As we speak, there exists two basic kinds of CI’s: Written conversational (Chatbots) and Voice 
Assistants. A third option (pre to the 2017 chatbot) is the complete static one, where the user may 
only ask pre-well-defined questions and topics which has proven not to add any value.

and self-learning algorithms that are to transform our daily life in many ways, which is an exciting 
development which will be elaborated on it ‘Future work’ section.

Deep Learning 
For the purpose of this thesis it seems important to briefly take a moment to scratch the surface of 
Deep Learning in order to understand the real usage of chatbots. It’s a field that already has and will 
keep changing the way people communicate with organisations, and Deep Learning holds the 
potential to be the future rendezvous for customer vs. company value creation.  
 
So what is Deep Learning? According to Jeff Dean in a lecture on “Deep Learning for Building 
Intelligent Computer Systems” [L17]: 
 
Deep Learning is a subfield of machine learning concerned with algorithms inspired by the 
structure and function of the brain called artificial neural networks.  
 
Deep Learning is a top layer or maybe more precisely, a field of techniques that can be applied for 
self-learning algorithms. This sounds like machine learning, and it is to some extend, but machine 
learning is “dumb” and cannot learn the emotional context when responding. Around March 2016 
Google’s AlphaGo defeated the world’s best Go player, Lee Sedol. The match was won by 
Google’s machine learning 4-1.  
In Go there are hundreds of millions of playable moves which makes it the no.1 most difficult game 
to play, since it is build on intuition and “personal strategy”. Especially intuition is something 
machines do not have at this writing moment! The interesting thing about a machine’s learning 
curve is that it will never get exhausted. At AlphaGo’s birth it started out by learning millions of 
already played games – until the development team initiated AlphaGo to play against itself, where it 
played over 40 million matches over one night. 
 
The techniques that were applied with the new techniques for the AlphaGo, have later been applied 
to Google’s search and translate engine, which resulted in a drastic drop in the output’s uncertainty, 
from around 20% down to 0.5-1%. This happened overnight as Google implemented and applied 
the new Deep Learning techniques; so just imagine what will happen in a few years as users (us) 
help Google train their systems. This drastic decrease in flaws can be illustrated as shown in image

Within the field of Deep Learning many different techniques are applied, depending on which

NLP -in short 
When talking about training algorithms we talk about two ways of doing so: Supervised and 
Unsupervised. In basic terms, ‘supervised learning’ is when the dataset is provided -take for 
example the IBM report on “A new Chatbot for Customer Service on Social Media” [A4] (which is 
what this thesis aim to investigate) where they analyse and feed their chatbot with 1 million tweets 
that are directly context-based with emotional requests from the user (targeted) so that the chatbot 
would be able to identify the requests and gave a desired output. ‘Unsupervised’, on the other hand, 
is the opposite obviously, where no datasets have been provided, where the algorithm will, by itself, 
cluster the data into different classes (which could for example be Google’s search engine or even 
Watson perhaps, which has been trained for everything. 
 
The training process for training an NLP algorithm: 
 
• Clean the data to plain text without punctuations etc. 
• Tokenise the data and build a base “library” based on, for example, the 100K most used words in 
these types of conversations  
• Train the desired algorithm based on the training set. 
 
In IBM’s research they used Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)  (a recurrent neural network) in

Passive → Generative → Intuitive 
 
With the timeline in mind we see computers go from pure logic, to a phase of understanding where 
it uses a brute-force computing for best possibilities, for example to generate 3D models of cars 
based on end points and limitations, to the more intuitive form of responding and the self 
behavioural actions it can act. For the latter, we have Deep Learning and the field of self-learning 
algorithms to thank. A very interesting turning point was when a computer could determine on

Notifications 
Push notifications are something that has occurred over the past 6-8 years alongside the smartphone 
revolution. Suddenly it was possible for small and big developers to push whatever they wanted to 
whoever had downloaded their app, at any given time when feeling for it. The push notifications 
were meant as a tool to enhance the user experience and activate the users, but at some point it all 
turned around and today users are fed up by an overwhelming noisy channel based on badges and 
icons, auto-updates and the ultimately annoying factor: notifications on a closed screen. Meaning 
that we are now left with a noisy, random, and borderline abusive user experience where 
notifications have become a tactic to steal our attention rather than to improve our lives. This has 
led many users to turn off notifications on smartphones for the majority of their apps, which is not 
good for the developers and for the users, since they might miss out important stuff. A theory about 
the most effective notifications is that they contain topics related to: Finance, fear or have some sort 
of FOMO (fear of missing out). 
 
==PAGE:35==
 35 
So what does all this mean? It means that when we are talking about creating a ‘service chatbot’, 
which must include some sort of notifications, we have a responsibility to act responsible and only 
push when the user actually asked for it or if it’s strictly important (ex “time to check-in”). If this is 
not thought through we will end up with just another irritating ‘intruder’ and thus fail to create the 
desired loyalty, and even worse: neglecting the full potential of chatbots which potentially will end 
up as a irritating element that people will opt out. 
 
This leads us to think in a direction where a specific Notification API, or set of rules, will be 
needed: For example where one will never get notifications while in meetings, or places of 
importance but only by the current context. But when the user are on the go, in an Uber for 
example, or on the way to the airport or at other appropriate times he/she will get all the relevant 
information that needed from the whole palette of apps. This could potentially be job for AI 
algorithms to decide (based on the rule set placed by the Notification API). It could be the same 
Spotify uses the users personal taste in music versus the time of the day to suggest just the right 
kind of music for that specific hour. Why not apply techniques like this?  
A very impressive example is Slack’s decision map for deciding whether or not to send the user a 
notification so the user will only get them when he/she is in a situation where it’s appropriate, see 
Appendix A for the chart. 
 
All in all by creating a Chatbot through Facebook’s Messenger platform, both designers and 
developers hold great responsibility for creating an intelligent way of pushing messages and should 
be based on best practice AI and could take factors like Relevance, Urgency, location and earlier 
interests into account.

First Iteration 
The following section investigates a handful of SAS' Frequent Flyers' needs and demands for a 
smooth journey. The learning objectives or takeaways are based on field studies in the form of 
observations and one-to-one interviews with stakeholders. The “motivation” points are then 
translated into a User Story Map that will work as a “journey of demands" for the first 
potential Chatbot. In the end of the section a sub-conclusion will be presented.  
Hypothesis 
The hypothesis to be validated or verified in the first iteration is: 
  
• Does a frequent flyer have enough demands that he/she needs solved? 
• Will a potential use of Chatbots create any value for SAS? 
• If loyalty is linked with human interrelations, will a personal experience with a Chatbot 
(within its environment) then create true loyalty between SAS and its customers?  
• Will the Facebook Messenger platform work as a potential communication channel and 
can it fit into SAS’ existing infrastructure? 
 
It is within the scope of the first iteration to test the above in order to investigate if there is 
potential for using this kind of technology. 
Restrictions 
This iteration will mainly be looking at the top tier level of SAS Eurobonus, namely the 
Pandion members. This has been done for two reasons:  
1. They travel very often and have personal relations to other travelers.   
2. For a thesis like this speed is essential and therefore it is important to search in a segment 
that can give the most feedback and may be 100% comprehensive to all types of members. 
Method 
The process and approach for the first iteration has been to validate or invalidate the 
hypothesis. In order to take the necessary steps a User Story Map has been developed in 
collaboration with frequent flyers and SAS in order to pinpoint the “elements of pain”. The 
found pains have been used to map the requirements for the first build of a service Chatbot, 
which will be tested and evaluated in the Second Iteration.  
Lean development and User Experience

to waist fiat and most important our time the “human capital”. This approach has been used to

Third Iteration 
The following section is based on the outcome found in the second iteration and will 
investigate the “Product/Market Fit” as part of a potential Chatbot integration. The process is 
to update the function diagram to fit the Chatbot, setup a channel for flight information to 
push to the individual test users, and to test if the end goal of creating value and a 
“humanized” relationship has been established. Finally, a sub-conclusion will be drawn at the 
end of this iteration. 
 
Hypothesis 
• Will the added features found in the second iteration be enough to meet the basic 
needs in order to evaluate on value creation? 
• Will it be possible to “fake” the automatization by letting the author pretend to be a 
machine (by updating flight status etc. for each of the test members)? 
• If loyalty derives from human interactions – will a personal experience with a Chatbot 
(within its environment) then create a similar relationship between SAS and its 
customers? 
• Will a mixed reality (human versus computer) give the customer a feeling of a real 
connection with SAS and thereby implement value elements (emotional and 
informative) that can satisfy customer needs in any given situation?

its customers. Therefore, the first step is to update and re-build the Chatbot and release it, in 
order to get the users to test it. In order to make a “true to the case” – scenery, the author has 
been on standby to key in travel information in a spreadsheet of the results of the test group.

Conclusion 
The first build of a potential Chatbot has proven to trigger the expected feelings within the 
test group, why there is a strong belief that a chatbot can create true value and thus be a new 
loyalty channel. The scope of this thesis is not to build a production-ready chatbot in a true 
environment but rather to test the potential it could lead to.  
This has shown that the way airlines are interacting with their customers today are outdated, 
both seen from the costumer and SAS’ point of view where physical and human capital are 
wasted. Therefore it has been decided to move on in the search for more potential value 
creation within the SAS Eurobonus segment. This is based on the outcome of all three 
iterations on the fact that it doesn’t seem as if more Elements of Value can be added to the first 
scope. 
 
Before moving on, a short review of the hypothesis placed in the beginning of the third 
iteration will be verified: 
• The outcome of the three iterations has proven to meet the expectations that a service 
Chatbot can and will create and add immediate value to its users and to SAS. 
• It has shown that the process of “faking” the intelligence of the chatbot by letting a 
human (i.e. myself) do the work manually is sufficient and actually offers a deeper 
insight into the test persons' thoughts while on the go. 
• This mixed reality of a chatbot in combination with human customer support has 
shown great potential in satisfying the test persons' various needs for Emotional and 
informative information. 
• At this point, based on the functionalities of the chatbot and the timeframe wherein it 
has been tested, it still seems too inaccurate to conclude if a chatbot can deliver a 
trustworthy simulation of a human relation. Therefore, this question still remains open 
and will be investigated further in the fourth iteration.

Further considerations 
A kindly reminder of the purpose of the thesis which is to seek new loyalty channels by using 
modern chatbots. Therefore, as stated in the sub-conclusion of the third iteration, a new quest for 
further use of chatbots has been initiated. The iterations 1-3 have so far focused on a customer’s 
basic service needs and have turned out, more or less, as expected. It seems that the basic needs 
when using a chatbot as a service-channel, have been covered according to the Elements of Value, 
why it didn’t seem possible nor reasonable to squeeze more out of the concept-idea of an initial 
SAS Eurobonus service chatbot. This is why the next 4th, and last, iteration will search for potential 
in other related fields within the travel chain. A service chatbot can be a substitute for today’s lack 
of human capital, thus enhancing customer satisfaction and fast replies. Further, the goal is not to 
build production ready material, but rather to search, find, build and validate potential new channels 
for loyalty creation: to seek broad. 
Fourth iteration  
The motivation to amend the directions in fourth iteration, both originated from the above 
mentioned ‘further considerations’ in terms of searching for loyalty channels within other fields 
than the SAS Eurobonus and from a supervised session with Professor Michael Kai Petersen, where 
he raised a much valid question based on a personal situation he faced during a domestic flight in 
the states whit Delta Airlines. In the following, I’ll try to explain what happened: 
 
Sitting at the gate, everyone could see the airplane parked just outside the gate-windows waiting for 
someone to connect its doors to the gate. The ground crew was all at their posts but facing away 
from the airplane. 20 minutes passed slowly and no action had been taken. Still, 200 people were 
waiting patiently to board the airplane that they could only passively observe from the gate-
windows.  
More minutes passed, while absolutely no information was given -people starting to turn to each 
other asking if anybody knew what was happening. When a crew-member finally spoke, it was to 
inform that the airplane was overbooked and that some passengers had to be kicked-off for a later 
flight. Afterwards, the service and information delivered to the waiting passengers again vaporised 
and nothing happened.  
At some point a “random” (e.g. not a frequent flyer) passenger went up to the inactive crew and 
demanded a better treatment and at that moment, for the first time, the staff did something to 
change the situation and allocated all their focus onto this single woman. This only made things 
worse: the other passengers (especially the frequent flyers, thus considering themselves ‘loyal’ to 
Delta) got very upset, based on the overall lack of information, non-existing customer service and 
now the special treatment of someone less important. At some point during the chaos, the doors to 
the aircraft slit open and the ones who weren’t kicked-off or had left in anger, went onboard.  
==PAGE:70==
 70 
  
Delta’s biggest problem was to identify the most important travellers: the ones with high seniority 
and air-miles (another way of calculating ‘loyalty’) which usually counts their First -and Business 
Class customers. Unfortunately, no one, or no system, had any overview to solve the unfortunate 
situation that day, and the worst part is: Delta, undoubtedly, lost a bunch of ‘loyal customers’ that 
day. 
 
Imagine that Delta had owned a Service Chatbot, then they could easily had prioritised their 
customers and sent out differentiated information to the right people at the right time, and nothing 
would have lead to the unfortunate situation. These settings will happen now and then and they will 
only appear more often as the airlines start to outsource their ground staff - why the immediate 
thought would be that a personalised chatbot service would have many important aspects besides 
saving money, create value and enhance loyalty. 
 
If we look at image 24: “Travel Touch Points” it is clear that a lot of elements within the travel 
chain are still untouched. This strongly indicates that many undiscovered Value Elements could still 
be added to the travel chain for loyalty enhancement.

• To Create comfort  
o This stage is set to get the participants to feel good about being part of the workshop 
and to give them a feeling of purpose. (At this stage, it’s a good sign if the 
participants laugh and fall into a familiar mood). 
• Stage Discussion  
o To give the participants cues (prime them) on what outcome the facilitator expects, 
pointing them in a certain direction to set the mood. 
• Develop Together 
o Taking the developed discussion into consideration and put into perspective the 
findings for the next workshop (part n).

It is here important to mention that when working within a new field of technology, which are the 
chatbots, a project like this can get huge as ‘everything new’ always seems to have inexhaustible 
opportunities, which is why it was necessary to add some boundaries to this thesis.  
Therefore, it feel safe to say that it was not within the scope of this thesis to cover all parts of the 
travel chain and so it should be emphasised that many more opportunities lie ahead of the chatbots 
waiting to be investigated and mapped accordingly.  
And this is why a section on ‘Future Work’ is so great, giving the opportunity to touch on important 
extensions of the project which would otherwise have been too complex to include. 
Specifically, we will be looking into an action plan based on short - vs. long-term implementations 
and further chatbot “add-ons/elements” from new entry points / getting an introduction to the 
possibilities of a production-ready service chatbot. 
 
In the first three iterations, a chatbot was build in the Flowxo framework which, as mentioned 
earlier, is a brilliant tool for a quick prototype and is further fairly easy to get started with, but it has 
it has its limitations. The upside is that it lets you build custom web app with can be connected via 
webhooks. The work with Flowxo has been a proof of work process but in order to create a unique 
service where a chatbot can create the intended value, it will be necessary to build the service on a 
long-term sustainable self-learning algorithm.  
 
Before digging into road maps it will be necessary to raise an important question:  
 
How much does the current way of communicating and handling customer support cost versus how 
much will it cost to develop and integrate this system versus how much can we save on developing 
and implementing this new service environment?  
 
Short-term suggestions 
First step will be to implement the chatbot MVP as developed and tested in iteration 1 to 3 on a 
self-learning algorithm, then connect it to SAS’ existing user profiles and engines for ticket engines, 
airport statistics (ex. queuing-times at all touch points) and so on.  
A suggestion would be to look into Microsoft’s LUIS or Google’s Cloud Natural Language API, 
which seem the most promising services, offering easy access to the most well-developed Deep 
Learning algorithms available today.

Chapter 10 
 
 
 
Conclusion 
The world is shifting gear and consumer’s demands are continuously increasing alongside easy 
access to the super computers that we all carry in our pockets nowadays. As the age of technology 
matures and people are getting ever more comfortable with the possibilities it offers, consumers will 
(and are) be asking for automatisation and personalised services at an ever-increasing demand! 
In an era where Technology seems to offer endless possibilities to consumers, it can be difficult for 
companies to retain customers and even more so to establish a loyal relationship.  
There are quite many indicators that companies are at a desperate stage, where they would do 
almost anything to gain customers’ loyalty - resulting in an endless stream of  “dead” bonus 
programs containing nothing but “forced loyalty”. 
 
Backed up by a thorough research on the concept of ‘loyalty’ it seemed to be the inevitable truth 
that loyalty is an irrational medium which, in theory, can only occur between two living organisms 
that share the same goal or belief. 
However, as the project unfolded it quickly became evident that chatbots challenge this exact notion 
as they blur the lines between ‘being human’ and ‘acting human’ putting old truths to the test by 
asking within a modern context: “what exactly is human value’ and how do we create it?”.  
The possibilities that chatbots have given us, have proven to create a new platform that the 
consumers have never seen before, where companies, personal life and friends merge (see image 11 
– blurred lines). This opens up for new pathways of endless possibilities for value creation. Due to 
this same fact it was necessary to make limitations or boundaries for the thesis to avoid running off 
in various directions.  
Therefore, based on a market-analysis on current loyalty and bonus programs, the thesis chose a 
focal point around the SAS Eurobonus as this stood out as the most promising environment to test 
on the potentials of a chatbot and where it at first glance seemed to be missing the most Elements of 
Value. 
 
To better understand how the conclusion has been drawn, it is necessary to explain what happened 
during the iteration stages:  
The focus of the first iteration was to investigate and to accept or reject the hypothesis, that a 
chatbot potentially could create value in a travel situation. The approved hypothesis was then 
further backed up by user interviews and was thus the first step towards a chatbot prototype. 
 
The second iteration worked with the findings from the first iteration in order to form and create the 
first chatbot prototype and testing within a real environment. The feedback from the selected test 
group (the Pandion members) and from SAS, gave a clear indication of the fact that the findings in 
the first iteration was correct. The feedback was then translated into features that would support 
different scenarios in the travel chain [image 24]. 
 
The third iteration dealt with re-building the chatbot prototype and to make the features more ‘alive’ 
==PAGE:88==
 88 
to help engage the test persons at a higher level. This stage proved to involve a greater commitment 
from both the test persons and the author. This was based on the travel information that each test 
person handed in for the purpose of the thesis, and which was manually updated by the author (due 
to two reasopn: 1. It was not possibly to integrate the data directly with SAS’ servers 2. It would be 
to big and time consuming (hardcore coding has not been the turnaround for this thesis.)  
Furthermore, the outcome of the second build of the chatbot let to the discovery that the ‘MVP’ was 
found and that the travel chain’s value elements were saturated. Thus it didn’t seem plausible given 
the goal of the thesis (to search for new loyalty channels) to add further Elements of Value to this 
service. 
 
Therefore a fourth iteration with another approach was made. A four days workshop with eight 
participants build upon the theory behind the Double Diamond Model [image 27 and L22].  
Workshop 1 the ‘divergent’ stage was a discover phase where the participants came up with 
different (stereotypical) personas in a travel-related situation. 
Workshop 2, the ‘convergent’ stage -discussed and defined the findings from the first workshop. At 
this workshop, the participants were doing role-plays of the different personas while pin-pointing 
the scenarios that the personas could potentially face at the airport.  
Workshop 3 the ‘ divergent’ stage -determined the findings and scenarios where it seemed like the 
most value could be added. The participants created seven different scenarios from the four 
“situations” found during workshop 2.  
At the final workshop 4, everything was “assembled” at ‘convergent’ stage where the output were 
to deliver direct solutions to the different developed scenarios. 
Overall, the workshops were a great success as their results have verified that there indeed are many 
more areas within the field of chatbots to be worked on and looked at. 
 
Based on the iterations and the workshops described above, the thesis has taught us that today’s 
loyalty and bonus programs, for most parts, generate “forced” loyalty (as illustrated in image 6) and 
thus never will achieve the expected deep loyalty. This problem seems to derive from the fact that 
most of today’s loyalty and bonus programs are developed by marketing people with very limited 
technical understanding, needless to say that IT is the focal point for nearly all situations in our 
everyday-life and interaction. 
 
Then has the thesis proven that the use of a service chatbot will lead to a deeper relationship 
between the company and the customer and thereby initiate true loyalty?  
The answer to this is backed up by theoretical research and empirical data which have been 
collected, analysed and interpreted throughout the thesis, and the prompt answer to the question is: 
“The creation of a computerized human experience that creates a strong bond and therefore a 
shared goal – leads to greater loyalty”- which means ‘yes’ -but in theory, as this thesis has also 
shown that more technical knowledge and new innovative ways of thinking and perceiving our 
surrounding environments are needed (as implied by Deep Learning techniques) in order for the 
chatbot(s) to meet the demands of tomorrow’s customers and to be able to create the desperately 
wanted ‘unfair advantage’ in the form of a ‘channel’ that can, eventually, lead to customer loyalty 
on the same level as our loyalty to friends and family.  
 
It therefore seems safe to conclude that a service chatbot will become a new loyalty channel, and 
especially in a not to far future as Deep Learning algorithms mature to meet the customer’s 
emotional and informative requests. Furthermore, it will be extremely interesting to follow the 
maturing of Deep Learning technologies in the years to come and to see how corporations will use 
and unlock the potential that such a channel undoubtedly holds.
