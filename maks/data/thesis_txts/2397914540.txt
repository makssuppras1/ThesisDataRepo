Abstract
The current need for renewable energy sources is imminent due to the effects that emissions
from fossil fuels have on the climate and environment. A contribution to mitigation of
increased global warming from fossil oil use may be gasiﬁcation of wheat straw, and other
agricultural residues, on a low temperature circulating ﬂuidized bed (LT-CFB) gasiﬁer - in
combination with catalytic tar upgrading. This may prove to be an efﬁcient and ﬂexible
solution, that can produce high quality bio-oil, nutrient rich biochar, and utilize the producer
gas for heat and power production. This project seeks to gain knowledge for the future
development of this technology by examining essential operation conditions and analytical
methods. The following two central aspects of this solution are examined; 1) the effect of
reducing the pyrolysis temperature and biomass particle size on the yield and quality of the
tar, 2) producer gas quality with respect to the concentration of sulfur and chlorine, that
may cause problems in catalytic downstream processes.
Ten successful experiments were made on a 100 kW LT-CFB gasiﬁer with pyrolysis tem-
peratures between 688 and 568 °C and wheat straw particles in three size groups; 1-2 mm,
3-4 mm and 5-7 mm. Furthermore, a single experiment of testing partial oxidation on
the producer gas was made to investigate this, as a simple method for upgrading tar to
bio-oil. The producer gas was characterized by analyzing heating value, water content, O/C
ratio, tar composition, sulfur and chlorine concentration, and performing mass and energy
balances. Finally, different methods for characterizing tar and producer gas were compared.
The highest tar yield was 110 g tar / kg producer gas achieved by lowering the pyrolysis
temperature from approximately 660 ◦C to 580 ◦C which gave a two fold increase in yield.
It also favored the presence of oxygenated tar compounds, the formation of phenol’s over
PAH’s and increased the oxygen content by 3.5 fold (on a dry basis). The heating value
decreased with temperature, but despite that, the energy contribution from the tar increased
from 10 to 30 % of the total energy content in the producer gas. Introducing smaller particles
did not have the expected effect of increasing the tar yield. However, it reduced the accumu-
lation of char in the reactor signiﬁcantly. This is an advantage in continuous operation of
the LT-CFB gasiﬁer as severe accumulation was experienced as a result of increased fuel
feeding, which again was found to be the most suitable way to reduce pyrolysis temperature.
A comparison of methods for characterizing tar and producer gas resulted in three best
practice recommendations: 1) use the total tar condenser (system made by CHEC, Risø)
instead of the Petersen column for quantiﬁcation of tar, 2) use ofﬂine GC measurements that
includes the detection of C2 –C5 hydrocarbons for accurate measurements of the energy
content. The current continuous gas analyzer used, underestimated the energy content by
30 %; 3) use a bomb calorimeter (max. error = 0.85 %) for determining the heating value
instead of calculating it based on elemental analysis measurements (min. error = 6 %).
Sulfur and chlorine concentration was determined by measuring COS, H2S and Ch3Cl in
the dry producer gas. The highest concentrations were 133 mg S / kg dry producer gas and
340 mg Cl / kg dry producer gas. The content of COS was stable on 14-20 mg/kg. The H2S
measurements varied a lot and was inadequate due to experimental errors. This gave reason
to believe, that the sulfur concentration was even higher. Around 25-30 % of the chlorine in
the wheat straw was released and came in the form of Ch3Cl. It was also found to decrease

with increasing temperature, opposite of COS and H2S. Sulfur and chlorine concentrations
are found to be high enough to constitute a risk towards pollution of catalysts and process
equipment in downstream processing.

Sammenfatning
Behovet for vedvarende energikilder er på nuværende tidspunkt meget højt på grund af
de indvirkninger emissionerne fra fossile brændstoffer har på klima og miljø. En del af
løsningen til at begrænse øget global opvarmning, kan være forgasning af halm, eller andet
overskydende biomasse, på en lavtemperatur cirkulerende ﬂuidiseret ”bed” (LT-CFB) for-
gasser, der er kombineret med katalytisk opgradering af tjære til bio-olie. Dette kan vise sig
at være en effektiv og ﬂeksibel løsning, der kan producere højkvalitets bio-olie, næringsrigt
biokoks samt udnytte produktgassen til varme- og elproduktion. Dette projekt søger at
tilegne sig viden til brug for fremtidig udvikling af teknologien ved at undersøge væsentlige
driftsbetingelser af forgasseren samt analysemetoder til karakterisering af tjæren. Følgende
to centrale aspekter ønskes undersøgt; 1) effekten af at reducere pyrolysetemperaturen
og partikelstørrelsen af halmen på udbyttet og kvaliteten af tjæren, 2) produktgassens
kvalitet med hensyn til koncentrationen af svovl og klor, der er kendt for at kunne forårsage
problemer i katalytiske processer.
Ti vellykkede forsøg blev lavet på 100 kW LT-CFB forgasseren med pyrolysetemperaturer
imellem 688 og 568 ◦C samt med halmpartikler i tre størrelsesordener på 1-2 mm, 3-4 mm
og 5-7 mm. Der blev endvidere lavet et enkelt forsøg med at teste en partiel efter-oxidation
af produktgassen for at undersøge muligheden for at bruge det som en simpel metode til
at opgradere tjære til bio-olie. Karakterisering af produktgassen foregik ved at analysere
brændværdi, vandindhold, O/C forholdet, tjæresammensætning, svovl- og klorkoncentra-
tion samt at udføre masse- og energibalancer. Ydermere blev forskellige analysemetoder til
karakterisering af tjære og produktgassen sammenlignet.
Det højeste opnåede tjæreudbytte ved at sænke pyrolysetemperaturen fra ca. 660 ◦C til 580
◦C var 110 g tjære/kg produktgas, hvilket var en fordobling af udbyttet. Sænkningen af
temperaturen favoriserede også tilstedeværelsen af iltede tjæreforbindelser og dannelsen
af phenoler til fordel for PAH’ere samt øgede iltindholdet med en faktor 3,5. Brændvær-
dien faldt ved sænkning af pyrolysetemperaturen, men på trods af det steg den samlede
energimængde i produktgassen, der kom fra tjæren, fra 10 til 30%. Brugen af en reduceret
partikelstørrelse havde ikke det øgede tjæreudbytte, som var forventet. Det reducerede
dog imidlertid akkumuleringen af koks i koksreaktoren betydeligt, hvilket er en fordel ved
kontinuerlig drift af LT-CFB’en, da en egnet metode til at sænke pyrolysetemperaturen
er at forøge tilsætningen af halm samt fjerne noget af ”bed”materialet, hvilket fører til
øget akkumulering af koks i koksreaktoren. Forskellige analysemetoder til karakteriser-
ing af tjære og produktgas blev sammenlignet og resulterede følgende tre anbefalinger:
1) til måling af den totale mængde tjære i produktgassen bør total tjære kondensatoren
(opfundet på KT afdelingen, Risø) benyttes i stedet for at bruge Petersen kolonnen, 2)
for nøjagtige målinger energiindholdet i produktgassen bør ofﬂine GC målinger benyttes,
da det omfatter måling af C2 –C5 carbonhydrider. Den kontinuerlige gasanalyse, der på
nuværende tidspunkt benyttes, undervurderer energiindholdet i produktgassen med 30%; 3)
til bestemmelse af brændværdien bør et bombekalorimeter bruges, da det er mere nøjagtigt
(maks. fejl = 0,85%), i stedet for at beregne brændværdien på baggrund af analyser af
grundstofsammensætningen (min. fejl = 6%).
Svovl- og klorkoncentrationen blev bestemt ved måling af COS, H2S og CH3CI i den ikke

kondenserbare produktgas. De højeste koncentrationer var hhv. for svovl og klor på 133

målingerne varierede meget og vurderes utilstrækkelige på grund af mulige eksperimentelle
fejl. Der er derfor grund til at tro, at svovlkoncentrationen var endnu højere. Omkring

Introduction
The present and future development in climate change requires, that the energy from fossil
fuels are substituted by renewable energy sources. According to the World Energy Outlook
of 2017 (WEO 2017) from The International Energy Agency [1], the worlds energy demand
is projected to increase by 30% by 2040 if the current state of government policies remain
unchanged. The main reasons are growing population (up to 9 billion in 2040) and economic
growth (3.4% yearly). This creates a massive increase in energy demand, and makes it even
harder to move away from fossil fuels. But, the recent years’ investments and development in
renewable energy technologies has driven the prices down. Especially on wind energy, solar
energy and batteries. So the shift away from fossil fuels to renewable sources in electricity
production are expected to continue. Despite this development, oil production towards
2040 are projected to increase due to oil consumption in the transport sector. For future
perspectives, the ﬂeet of electric vehicles (EV’s) are projected to increase gradually. However,
the overall car ﬂeet is also expected to double, so that alone can not diminish the demand for
oil. Strong policies are required [1] and other solutions must be considered. Electriﬁcation
of the transport sector will be the largest part of the solution in the future, but there will still
be the need for substituting liquid fossil fuels in some areas, e.g. heavy transport, aviation
and ships, and petrochemicals in industry. Furthermore, the high penetration of ﬂuctuating
energy sources like wind and solar will require stable and reliable back-up solutions for
heat and power production in the future. For this, biomass is going to play a vital role.
Using thermochemical conversion of biomass for the production of renewable fuels, heat and
power is a promising way of reducing carbon dioxide emissions and will contribute to the
mitigation of increased global warming. Thermochemical processes includes combustion,
pyrolysis, gasiﬁcation and thermal liquefaction. Pyrolysis and gasiﬁcation are technologies
that offers ﬂexible solutions that are both cost effective and efﬁcient. Combustion is less
ﬂexible, because the outcome is limited to heat and power production. On the other hand, it
is a cheap and fully matured technology. Liquefaction offers the ability to produce liquid
fuels and chemicals from biomass, but requires high pressure and high tech equipment to
function. Pyrolysis and gasiﬁcation works at ambient conditions and offers a large ﬂexibility
by being able to utilize all products from the process.
Pyrolysis products includes liquid pyrolysis oil (tar), energy rich gas and char. Tar from
pyrolysis have multible applications that includes energy storage, upgrade to high quality
bio-oil for use in transport and extraction of chemicals used in industry, or for bio-materials.
Furthermore, the energy rich gas can be utilized in power and heat production and the
residual char can be used as fertilizer and soil improvement, because it is rich in minerals
and nutrients like e.g. phosphor, potassium, sulfur, magnesium and carbon. A technical
solution to meet these challenges may be gasiﬁcation of wheat straw and other agricultural
residues on a low temperature circulating ﬂuidized bed (LT-CFB) gasiﬁer in combination
with catalytic tar upgrading.

Purpose
The purpose of this thesis is to conduct an early-stage investigation of bio-oil production from
low temperature gasiﬁcation of straw. The investigation focus is on validation/modiﬁcation
of best practice methodology – analytical as well as operational, and includes: i) the re-
quirement for data and sampling, ii) ﬁrst tests of the inﬂuence of experimental conditions
(temperature, particle size) on bio-oil production and quality, and iii) examine the concentra-
tions of sulphur and chlorine in the producer gas to give knowledge for future experiments
on the production of high quality bio-oil, that includes the use of catalysts.
The main research question of the thesis is: What operational and analytical method should
be applied to optimize Low Temperature Circulating Fluidized Bed gasiﬁcation (LT-CFB) of
crushed straw pellets towards production of bio-oil?
This question is sought answered by addressing the following three sub-questions:
1. How well suited is reference operation of a Low Temperature Circulating Fluidized Bed
gasiﬁer for production of bio-oil, and how does a reduced fuel particle size distribution,
reduced pyrolysis reactor temperature and the inclusion of a partial oxidation process
affect the bio-oil yield and quality?
2. Are the currently applied in-house DTU method for tar quantiﬁcation, tar char-
acterization and chemical energy distribution in the volatile product adequate for
quantiﬁcation and optimization of tar productivity?
3. Do catalyst poisons Cl and S pose a challenge to future bio-tar production systems
with catalytic upgrading of the bio-tar quality?
Question 1 is answered in section 6.1 by investigating the process performance, gas compo-
sition and tar characteristics from a series of pilot scale experiments with tar production
conducted on a 100 kW LT-CFB converting crushed straw pellets.
Question 2 is answered in section 6.2 by conducting a thorough investigation of the currently
applied methods and the balance between method applicability and simplicity on one hand
and method adequacy and accuracy on the other.
Question 3 is answered in section 6.3 by analysis of Cl and S mass distribution among the
main products and by-products from the gasiﬁcation process.
Other aspects of the central question are addressed in a Perspectives section 8 and all sub
questions, as well as the main question, are answered in the ﬁnal conclusion.

Background
The major thermochemical processes for biomass conversion are pyrolysis, gasiﬁcation,
combustion and liquefaction, that each has advantages and disadvantages, but in general
offer a wide range of possibilities and ﬂexibility in product usage. An overview made by
A.V. Brigdewater and G.C.V. Peacocke [2] is presented in Figure 3.0.1.
The most commonly applied process is combustion, used for heat and power production.
When looking at the ﬁgure, the obvious advantage of using pyrolysis, gasiﬁcation and
liquefaction is that the products are fuel gas (syngas) and liquids, which carry many more
application possibilities other than heat and power production. Fuel gas can be synthesized
and liquids can be upgraded into methanol, diesel, gasoline, chemicals and more. What is
not shown in the ﬁgure is, that the char and ash residues from pyrolysis and gasiﬁcation
also can be utilized as fertilizer and soil improvement, and thus return valuable nutrients
back to the soil. Therefore, these technologies offer very ﬂexible solutions.
3.1
Gasiﬁcation
Gasiﬁcation is a thermal process with a series of chemical reactions taking place simultane-
ously. The overall process of biomass gasiﬁcation is considered very complex due to the
relatively complex composition of biomass. The thermal treatment of biomass is dependent
on temperature and gasiﬁcation medium (air, oxygen, steam) which contributes to different
outcomes of gas and tar species in the producer gas and solid products such as char, ash and
soot. Although biomass gradually decompose with increasing temperature, the gasiﬁcation
process can roughly be divided into four separate processes taking place in different zones
in the gasiﬁer: the drying zone, pyrolysis zone, partial combustion/oxidation zone and the
reduction zone [3].
3.1.1
Drying zone
The biomass used for gasiﬁcation typically has a moisture content ranging from 5 to 55%.
The majority of surface moisture and moisture inherent in the biomass will evaporate
in this zone [3]. Evaporation of water is an endothermic reaction, which thus requires
thermal energy transferred from other parts of the gasiﬁer, i.e. the partial oxidation zone,

or from external heat. A high moisture content therefore results in a reduced thermal
efﬁciency of the overall gasiﬁcation process [3] unless the heat is otherwise utilized, e.g. for
drying the biomass or for district heating [4]. The drying zone is between 100 and 300◦C.
The majority of moisture is released up to 200◦C. Physically bound moisture typically
evaporates up to 100◦C and here no signiﬁcant decomposition of the biomass happens. In
temperatures between 150 to 300◦C chemically bound water evaporates under the formation
of levoglucosan from glucose. Here two hydroxyl groups form an oxygen bridge inside the
glucose molecule, under the dehydration of water [5], [6].
3.1.2
Pyrolysis zone
At temperatures between 200-700◦C the biomass continues to dehydrate and the large
biomass polymers (cellulose, hemicelulose and lignin) vaporize into condensable liquids
(tar/bio-oil, water and large hydrocarbons) and non-condensable gases (light hydrocarbons
(C2 –C5), CO, H2, CH4 and CO2). The overall reaction can be described as ([3]):
CaHbOcNdSe + Heat −−→solid char + tar + gases (NH3 + N2 + H2S +
H2O + H2 + CH4 + C2H6 + CO + CO2, etc)

The tars at these temperatures are highly oxygenated and are a mix of organic acids,
carbohydrates, alcohols, ketones, phenols, guaiacols, syringols, furans, and mixed species,
also called primary tars. Especially acids, carbohydrates and furans are highly oxygenated
with O/C ratios up to 50% [7]. At this stage, most of the biomass is converted into volatiles
and smaller portions of char and ash. For example, a proximate analysis of wheat straw,
which is done with slow pyrolysis up to 600 ◦C, showed volatile, char and ash fractions of
70%, 25% and 5% of dw, respectively. Pyrolysis and the formation of tars/bio-oils, will be
described in detail in Section 3.4.

3.2
Reactor models
This section presents the overall speciﬁcations, advantages and downsides to different
gasiﬁcation reactor types. There are three overall types of gasiﬁers: Fixed bed, Fluidized
bed, and Entrained ﬂow. Entrained ﬂow gasiﬁers require a very small particle size and are
normally not used for biomass, due to the need of extensive pretreatment. It is used for coal
dust combustion/gasiﬁcation at large capacities (100-1000 MW) [8].
3.2.1
Fixed bed
In ﬁxed bed gasiﬁers, the biomass is fed from the top and moves downward as in a plug
ﬂow reactor. A ﬁxed bed is therefore sometimes referred to as a moving bed gasiﬁer. In
the bottom of the gasiﬁer, there is a ﬁxed bed, or grate to support the biomass. Fixed bed
reactors are distinguished by the direction of the airﬂow - updraft, downdraft and crossdraft.
In updraft gasiﬁers, the air is supplied in the bottom and exits at the top and ﬂows in
the opposite direction of the biomass. The heat is generated in the oxidation zone in the
bottom and travels upwards through the biomass. This means that most volatiles from
the drying and the pyrolysis zone is not thermally cracked or oxidized before exiting the
reactor. It is only the char fraction that reach the oxidation zone in the bottom of the reactor.

The heat from char oxidation is utilized very well in an updraft gasiﬁer for drying and
pyrolyzing the biomass and therefore the producer gas has a very low exit temperature
and a high energy efﬁciency. This makes updraft gasiﬁcation suitable for biomasses with a
high-moisture content [3], and is usually used in medium sized applications of 1-100 MW
[8]. The drawback is, that the producer gas (PG) contains high levels of tar and water and
can therefore not be used directly in e.g. a gas engine that requires tar free gas [8]. A good
example of a successful installation of an updraft gasiﬁer is in Harboør, Denmark (Figure
3.2.1). Here, a 3.5 MW (fuel input) updraft gasiﬁer has successfully been operated since
1993 running solely on woodchips with a total efﬁciency of 94% [9]. The bio-oil fraction is
effectively cleaned from the PG and stored for later use. For example on cold days where
extra heat production is required or in periods where the gasiﬁer is under maintenance [10].
In downdraft gasiﬁers, the airﬂow goes in the same direction as the biomass ﬂow. All
volatiles ﬂow through the gasiﬁcation and oxidation zone and is therefore oxidized or
cracked into non-condensable gases. This results in an almost tar free producer gas which
makes downdraft gasiﬁcation suitable for applications that require a very clean gas, e.g. in
combination with internal combustion engines for power and heat production [11] or in
solid oxide fuel cell applications [12]. Downdraft gasiﬁcation is usually found in small size
applications of 10 kW - 1 MW and the exit gas is high (900-1000◦C), which can take thermal
energy away from the process and lower efﬁciency [8] - if not designed correctly. A good

that need fast response to load changes[8].
3.2.2
Fluid bed
Fluidized bed reactors are widely used in biomass pyrolysis due to their very high heating
rates. In ﬂuid bed gasiﬁcation, very ﬁne sand particles are used as bed material and ﬂuidized
by aerating from nozzles in the bottom of the reactor. This gives the characteristics of a ﬂuid
which ensures uniform mixing of the biomass particles and a high mass transfer between
gas and solids. Sand also has a high thermal capacity which increase the heat transfer
and ensures an even distribution of heat in the reactor. The advantage of having a large
thermal inertia in the sand bed combined with the high level of mixing offers a wide range
of available fuel combinations or quality, which is very useful when gasifying biomass, that
often differentiates in quality and composition. Biomass also in general contains higher
amounts of ash and is more reactive than fossil fuels and is therefore sensitive to ash
agglomeration and sintering at high temperatures. The operating temperature range of

The three main types of ﬂuid bed gasiﬁers is the bubbling ﬂuidized-bed, the circulating
ﬂuidized bed and the twin ﬂuidized bed gasiﬁer. In a bubbling ﬂuidized bed (BFB), the
ﬂuidizing agent is supplied by nozzles or a perforated grate in the bottom of the reactor.
Biomass is fed into the ﬂuidized bed and instantly undergoes drying, pyrolysis, partial
oxidation and gasiﬁcation. The bubbles grows larger and moves up in the in the bed material
due to the combination of ﬂuidizing agent (air, steam or oxygen) and the release of volatiles
from the biomass. The residence time is relatively short and temperatures are normally
in the range of 700 to 900◦C [3]. The BFB gasiﬁer is usually equipped with a cyclone that
separates ash and char particles from the producer gas and recycles them back to the reactor.
The circulating ﬂuidized bed (CFB) functions similarly to the bubbling ﬂuid bed (Figure
3.2.3) but is operated with higher ﬂuidization velocities (3.5-5.5 m/s in CFB and 0.5-1.0 m/s
in BFB, [8]) and therefore has a much more turbulent ﬂuid bed. This means that hot sand
reaches the cyclone and is therefore separated and circulated back into the ﬂuid bed as with
the char and ash particles. Due to the higher turbulence, CFB’s are less prone to bed agglom-
eration and can therefore be operated at higher temperatures than BFB gasiﬁers (800-1000◦C).
Twin bed, or dual ﬂuidized bed gasiﬁcation (Figure 3.2.4) was made to avoid dilution of
the producer gas with nitrogen, that happens when air is used as the ﬂuidizing agent, by
making a system that separates combustion from gasiﬁcation. Dilution could be avoided by
using pure oxygen, but it is very expensive. A twin bed consists of separate combustion and
gasiﬁcation chambers, that is ﬂuidized with air and steam, respectively.

3.3
LT-CFB gasiﬁcation process
The low temperature circulating ﬂuid bed gasiﬁer system (LT-CFB) was originally developed
as a pretreatment of biomass for power and heat production, or for co-ﬁring in a power and
heat plant. Direct combustion of biomass can cause problems with cindering and agglom-
eration of ash particles, due to high temperatures that make the inorganic compounds in
the ash melt together. In cereal straw, the amount of KCl is high and it has a relatively low
melting point of 773◦C and it is therefore the biggest issue. The LT-CFB concept offers a
solution to the ash melting problem by having an efﬁcient process that keep the temperature
below the melting point of KCl.
The LT-CFB process consists of two steps; 1) pyrolysis of the biomass, and 2) gasiﬁcation
of char particles that heat the entire system. First, the biomass enters a pyrolysis reac-
tor (PR), where it is pyrolyzed. The product gas is a mixture of non-condensable gases
(H2, CO2, CO, CH4, and N2), condensable gases (tars), water/steam and solids (char and
inert sand particles). The solid fraction is separated in the primary cyclone (PC) that feeds
the char-reactor (CR). In the CR, char is oxidized by adding air, which is about 90% of the
total air added to the system. Char oxidation mainly occurs at temperatures above 600◦C
and reacts with CO2, H2O and H2 at 700-800◦C [6], namely the Boudouard, Water-gas and the
hydrogasiﬁcation reaction. The temperature in the pyrolysis reactor is mainly governed by
the hot sand circulating from the CR. The air added to the reactors, and the gas volume
expansion from char gasiﬁcation and biomass volatilization in the CR and PR, respectively,

3.4
Bio-oil
Bio-oil, pyrolysis oil and biocrude oil are all terms used about the brown/black viscous tar
fraction produced during pyrolysis of biomass. In other cases, the same terms are used for
tar that has been upgraded. Many deﬁnitions of tar can be found in literature of which
the common understanding is, that it is the product of pyrolysis that can be condensed
into liquid or aerosols at room temperatures [8]. A more speciﬁc deﬁnition is, that tar is
all molecules within a pyrolysis or gasiﬁcation producer gas with a molar weight higher
than Benzene (78 g/mol) [13]. This section describes the formation and characteristics of
tar/bio-oil and how the production can be optimized with different techniques like fast
pyrolysis (FP), catalytic fast pyrolysis (CFP) and more. In this thesis, tar will be used as a
term for the liquid pyrolysis products and bio-oil will be used as a term for the upgraded
tar.
3.4.1
Formation of tar
Tar is one of the products from thermal treatment of biomass. The majority of biomass are
lignocellulosic plant material (wood, agricultural waste and energy crops) and therefore the
most common biomass used for energy and materials. Lignocellulosic material primarily
consists of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin in different ratios, e.g. for cereal straws it
is normally 45-46% cellulose, 33-34% hemicellulose and 21% lignin [6]. When the large
biomass polymers are pyrolyzed, i.e. thermally treated without oxygen, they depolymerize
into volatile gases. The gases are divided into condensable or non-condensable gases1.

The non-condensable gases are low molecular weight compounds and mainly consist of
CO, H2, CO2 and CH4, and there is also a small, but not insigniﬁcant, amount of C2 –C5
hydrocarbons. The condensable volatile fraction (tar) is a complex mixture of hundreds of
different hydrocarbons and oxygenated hydrocarbons which undergo a certain development,
or maturation, that is temperature and time dependent and can be classiﬁed by primary,
secondary and tertiary processes, see Figure 3.4.1.
In the primary process, hemicellulose, cellulose and lignin volatilize at temperatures
between 200-600◦C and primary tars are formed. More than 90% of all hemicellulose and
cellulose degrade and leave a 10-20% char residue whereas lignin leaves around 40-50% char
residue [15]. Primary tars are characterized by being highly oxygenated and are a mix of
organic acids, carbohydrates, alcohols, ketones, phenols, guaiacols, syringols, furans, and
mixed oxygenated species. Especially acids, cabohydrates and furans are highly oxygenated
with O/C ratios up to 50% [7].
The secondary processes mainly occur at temperatures between 600-900◦C and/or at longer
residence times, see Figure 3.4.2. Here, primary tars further degrade and mature into
secondary tars and light hydrocarbons. Secondary tars are characterized by being mainly
phenols and aromatic hydrocarbons, and the oxygenates are drastically reduced. Also a
signiﬁcant fraction of nitrogen compounds occur, e.g. pyridines.
The tertiary tars start to form around 800◦C and consist almost exclusively of aromatics like
benzene, and polyaromatic hydrocarbons (PAH’s) like naphthalene, ﬂouranthene, pyrene
and many more. For an extensive list of primary, secondary and tertiary tars, see Table 5.2
in Ref. [6]. The tertiary processes also include soot/coke formation which can present a

3.4.2
Bio-oil characteristics
Bio-oil has many possible applications. It can be used directly as fuel in boilers or used to
produce fuels for transportation and bulk chemicals by upgrading the oil [18]. The partly
de-oxygenated bio-oil may be used as a feed for gasoline and diesel production in an oil
reﬁnery and thereby provide a source of renewable transportation fuels.
Bio-oil have a high water content and oygen content, which reduces the speciﬁc heating
value to about 40-50% of fossil fuel oil and reduces the ability to ignite. The water content
can vary a lot depending on the inherent water in the biomass and the severity of the
pyrolysis, i.e. the dehydration reactions [19]. Bio-oil generally contains a lot of organic acids
which gives a low pH and makes bio-oil corrosive to steel and aluminium (not stainless
steel) [19], which again makes it problematic in engines and furnaces. It also contains
char particles (solids) from the pyrolysis/gasiﬁcation process that can cause problems with
blocking and erosion when used in engines [19] as well as catalyze secondary reactions both
in the vapor and liquid phase [2]. Thus, char particles can actually contribute to the aging
process of bio-oil.
Aging of the bio-oil can be problematic. During storage, some of the oxygenated compounds
react and polymerize into very large molecules mainly via breakages of double bonds [19].
This increases viscosity and decrease volatility. The aging process is temperature dependent,
so the aging increases exponentially with temperature. The same change in viscosity that
happens in a year at 20 ◦C happens in one day at 80 ◦C [19].

3.5
Maximizing bio-oil yield and quality
3.5.1
Fast pyrolysis - Temperature and vapor residence time
Pyrolysis of biomass can be either slow, ﬂash or fast pyrolysis. Fast or ﬂash pyrolysis, is
characterized by very high heating rates, short vapor residence times and high tar yields. It
is a broad deﬁnition but generally, fast pyrolysis has very high heating rates (1,000-10,000
◦C s−1) and vapor residence times only a few seconds or less. Slow pyrolysis has low heating
rates (<10 ◦C s−1) and longer vapor and biomass residence times than fast pyrolysis. Flash
pyrolysis has heating rates between fast and slow pyrolysis [20]. In a study by Maggi and
Delmon [21] where tars from a fast pyrolysis process called rapid thermal processing (RTP)
and slow pyrolysis where compared. RTP had vapor residence times between 30 ms to 1.5 s
at 400-900 ◦C gave up to 80% liquid yield but had a heating value of only 21 MJ/kg and
contained 30% water. Slow pyrolysis with 15-30 min biomass residence time at 500 ◦C had a
much higher quality with a heating value of 25 MJ/kg and only 5% water, but it only gave
a 15-20% yield. High tar production with fast pyrolysis, not only requires a fast heating
rate in the reactor, but also rapid cooling and condensation of the producer gas to stop
the secondary tar processes. For most lignocellulosic biomass, the highest liquid yields are
found at temperatures between 500-520 ◦C and at a vapor residence time of about 1 second
[22]. In rare cases, the optimal temperature can be up to 600 ◦C [23]. Residence times up to
around 2 seconds can be tolerated without signiﬁcant decrease in liquid yield, but longer
periods will lead to signiﬁcant cracking of the tar [2].

3.5.2
Particle size
Generally, the application of small particles results in high tar yields. For larger particle
sizes (>0.5 cm) the tar yield is reduced signiﬁcantly, because the heating rate in the particle
are low, and secondary cracking reactions occur inside the particle, leading to a reduction of
the yield [24]. For larger particles, the optimal temperature will therefore be lower than for
small particles because secondary reactions are very temperature dependent. Many studies
have been made on very small particles (from <0.5 mm to 2 mm) and some of them [25], [26]
show higher yield with decreasing particle size (down to 0.59 and 0.34 mm, respectively)
while other studies showed and optimal particle size around 1 mm [23], [27], [28]. But, for
particles smaller than 2 mm, the difference in bio-oil yield is generally very small, only a
few percent.
3.5.3
Thermal Cracking and Partial oxidation
Thermal cracking and partial oxidation (POX) are, for the most part, used to reduce the
amount of tar, in the producer gas in order to make a clean gas. But, it might also be a
simple method for upgrading tar to quality bio-oil, because the oxygen content decreases
when primary tars undergo secondary reactions. The downside is of course, that the yield
decreases with temperature as explained below.
In the study of Wongchang et al. [29], the objective was to study yields and structural
changes of tar from thermal cracking. The pyrolysis temperature was 600◦C and yielded
38.63 g tar/Nm3. The thermal cracking resulted in yield reductions of approximately 32,
42, 65, 72, 86 and 90 %wt at 700, 800, 900, 1000, 1100 and 1200◦C, respectively. This, with
a constant residence time in the tar reformer. They found that, the average molar weight
increased with temperature and especially at higher temperatures, PAH’s where formed
and the amount of phenol and aromatic compounds decreased. The average molar weights
formed two distinct groups: A low MW group with approximately 150-200 g/mole and a
high MW group at 400-500 g/mole.
In an earlier study of Brandt and Henriksen [30], thermal cracking of tar in producer gas
was studied at 800, 900 and 1000◦C and partial oxidation was studied at excess air ratios
from 0-0.7. Partial oxidation showed signiﬁcant tar reduction compared to thermal crack-
ing. Excess air ratios >0.3 and 800◦C practically removed the tar (>90% reduction). With
conditions of an excess air ratio of 0.1 at 800 and 900◦C, approximately 35 and 45% of the
tar was removed, respectively. When only using thermal cracking, about 50%wt. of the tar
yield was decreased at 800◦C. Partial oxidation is very efﬁcient in reducing tar and external
heating is not required, due to exothermic oxidation reactions. Brandt and Henriksen did
not, however, analyze the structural changes of tar in this study.
Secondary tar reactions were studied more in detail by Morf et al. [31], i.e. the transformation
of primary tars to secondary and tertiary tars. The secondary reactions were studied at
temperatures of 500-1000◦C, at space time velocities of <0.2 seconds and without external
supply of oxygen. The tar yield decreased from about 40%wt. (of the biomass) to about 5%wt.
at 900-1000◦C and to about 20%wt. at 800◦C, so this reduction in tar yield is in line with
both Brandt and Henriksen [30] and Wongchang et al. [29]. Selected primary tar compounds
were analyzed. Guaiacol showed large reductions at temperatures of 600-800◦C and acetol
in the range of 700-900◦C. For acetic acid, concentrations remained stable at temperatures
14
==PAGE:26==
up to 900◦C. Selected secondary tar products were also analyzed which showed, that
phenol concentrations increased by a factor of 1.5 and ethyl-toluene increased dramatically
by a factor of 4. Tertiary tars (PAH’s) showed very low concentrations at temperatures
below 750◦C and thereafter increased signiﬁcantly. They encountered problems with soot
formation, which started to form at 800 ◦C and reached as high as 20%wt of the biomass.
3.5.4
Catalytic Fast pyrolysis with ZSM-5
The catalytic upgrading of tar proposed to work in combination with the LT-CFB process
is using the acidic zeolite catalyst ZSM-5. It has been studied extensively and is known to
effectively produce aromatics from pyrolysis vapors and reduce oxygen content and increase
the bio-oil quality signiﬁcantly [32]. Furthermore, metal promotion of the zeolite catalyst
has shown to further increase the production of aromatics and phenolic compounds and
thus enhance the bio-oil quality, for example by promoting the catalyst with molybdenum
[33] or by de-silication of the catalyst so it becomes aluminum rich [34]. Promoting the
catalyst with gallium, nickel, cerium, aluminum/iron and hydrogen (HZSM-5) have been
found promising for hydrocarbon production [32]. However, all studies report problems
with the short catalyst lifetimes, due to extensive coke formation that leads to inactivation
[32], [33], [35] which is the major problem in zeolite catalysis.
There are other options for catalytic upgrading of tar and non-catalytic upgrading. For
example is hydrodeoxygenation, using a Co–Mo2 catalyst and hydrogen under very high
pressure, considered a better alternative than using zeolite catalysts, due to better perfor-
mance. However, coke formation and expensive high pressure equipment complicates the
development of industrial application [35]. And for both zeolite catalysts and hydrodeoxy-
genation it applies, that further research are needed within this ﬁeld.
3.6
Catalyst poisoning
Sulfur and chloride are some of the elements in biomass, that can be damaging for catalytic
processing or potentially have an environmental impact if they end up in the producer
gas. The concentration of sulfur is relatively low in biomass, and therefore producer gas
from gasiﬁcation of biomass normally contain low concentrations of sulfur as well, a few
hundred ppm [36], [37]. However, sulfur is very poisonous to any metal catalysts [38],
especially hydrogen sulﬁde H2S which is very reactive and can, for example, deactivate
nickel coated surfaces at concentrations as low as 1 ppm [36] and platinum coated surfaces
at concentrations down to 10 ppm [38]. Sulfur mainly appear in the producer gas as either
carbonyl sulﬁde (COS) or H2S, where H2S is usually the dominant species with up to 95 %
of the sulfur occuring as H2S. Because no other sulfur compounds, besides COS, are formed
below 800 ◦C [37]. Sulfur, and also nitrogen species, are undesired in the producer gas,
because they are precursors for formation of SOx and NOx in the ﬂue gas after combustion
which has negative environmental impact in the form of acid rain and eutrophication,
respectively. Besides that, sulfur and nitrogen are often present as NH3 and H2S that both
are corrosive and thus damaging for equipment and machinery in downstream processing
and for example poisinouos to methanol synthesis and Fischer Tropsch fuel synthesis [39].
Chloride species in producer gas are mainly methyl chloride (MeCl, or CH3Cl) and hy-
drochloric acid (HCl). MeCl can sometimes act as precursor to HCl, which is very corrosive
15
==PAGE:27==
as well as poisonous towards catalysts and therefore unwanted in downstream processing
[40]. Concentrations as low as 0.5 ppm has been reported to be the tolerance level inside gas
turbines [41].

4.1
LT-CFB gasiﬁer campaign
The experimental campaign on the LT-CFB gasiﬁer was initially scheduled to be for two
weeks (10 workdays) between November 6th to November 17th. The ﬁrst week, the LT-CFB
was scheduled to run at normal conditions. The second week, the aim was to maximize the
tar production and thus running at lower temperatures and with smaller biomass particle
size. There was only little experience with running at low temperatures, and therefore the
risk of problems occurring was higher. The ﬁrst week was therefore meant to be used as
reference measurements.
The campaign, however, did not go entirely according to plan and was delayed due to several
reactor break downs, where the bed material agglomerated and had to be changed. But,
there were ﬁve successful runs that throughout this report will be referred to as Campaign
1. To ﬁnish the planned experiments, an extra week of LT-CFB runs was conducted from
November 27th to November 30th where another ﬁve successful runs were made. This will
be referred to as Campaign 2.

4.2.3
Experimental set-backs
The ﬁrst campaign was characterized by a number of experimental set backs. First of all, the
reactor had a minor gas leak. Secondly, the bed material agglomerated three times during
the ﬁrst two weeks, which resulted in involuntary reactor stops and necessitated, that the
reactor had to cool down and the bed material had to be changed.
Gas leak
The ﬁrst day went almost as planned except for a minor gas leak from inlet
pipe to the cyclone, which had to be repaired. The leak was most likely caused by the
sand-paper like effect that the circulating silica sand has on the pipes and it was probably
an undetected damage from previous experiments where the cool down from back then
caused the damaged area to grow into a leak.
First bed agglomeration
The second day went without problems until midday where the
temperature in the bottom of the char reactor started to increase a lot without any increase

and prevent circulation from stopping, but eventually it stopped. The failing circulation

Second bed agglomeration

hours of gasiﬁcation with a consumption of 194-223 kg wheat straw. This could suggest

4.3
Sampling
Here, the different types of sampling methods are described. The samples were solid
material like char, ash and bed material from the gasiﬁer, the secondary cyclone and the hot
gas ﬁlter. Bio-oil/tar and water in the producer gas was mainly collected via condensation
and the tar and water free producer gas was collected both for energy analysis and for S
and Cl analysis.
4.3.1
Reactor bed
Hot bed material was sampled directly from the CR via a drain valve. Prior to and after
sampling, the valve was cooled with water to prevent material damage. The bed material
was collected in metal trays and safety precautions were taken with heat insulating gloves,
full face safety mask and protective clothing. A blind sample was taken prior to the actual
sample to remove old bed material from the drain valve. The samples were immediately
brought outside to cool off in a water bath with a metal lid on to minimize char oxidation.
Char and ash from the SC were also sampled by using a designated drain valve. It was
sampled during periods of 10-15 minutes in the beginning and the end of the experiments.
Bed material from the MR and PR and char/ash from the HGF was sampled after the ﬁnal
shut down of the reactor in connection with emptying and cleaning of the gasiﬁer for all
solid material. During cool down, the whole system was purged with nitrogen to prevent
any char oxidation.
4.3.2
Producer Gas
Producer gas used for detection of sulfur and chloride compounds in the gas phase was
sampled in 300 mL gas pipettes. The pipettes were attached directly to producer gas outlet
valves without any tar and water removal. It was ﬂushed with producer gas for at least ﬁve
minutes to make sure no air residues were left inside.
The dry producer gas used to determine the energy content ofﬂine was sampled after the
total tar condenser in 5 Liter gas bags (Restek Corp. U.S.). Prior to sampling, the gas bags
were emptied with a vacuum pump.
4.3.3
Tar and water
Three types of tar sampling was made: 1) collection of the total amount of tars by simple
condensation - for total tar and water content of the producer gas, 2) tar collection in a
solvent before and after partial oxidation of producer gas with a Petersen Column, and 3)
solid phase adsorbtion (SPA).

Analytical
5.1
Proximate analysis
The content of moisture, volatile matter, and ﬁxed carbon of the wheat straw were analyzed
using a simultaneous STA 4491 Thermogravimetric Analyzer (NETZSCH, Germany). The
analysis was carried out under a nitrogen purge of 50 cm3 min−1 and ﬁrst heated to 105 ◦C
at a heating rate of 20 ◦C/min and a holding time of 5 min for the evaporation of water.
Secondly, it was further heated to 850 ◦C at a rate of 20 ◦C/min to determine the volatile
matter. Next, it was cooled down to 250 ◦C at 20 ◦C/min, still under a nitrogen purge.
Finally, the gas ﬂow was changed to air and the temperature increased to 850 ◦C at a heating
rate of 20 ◦C/min, for combustion of the ﬁxed carbon. The ash content was the residual
mass after combustion.
5.2
Particle size distribution
The particle size distribution of the crushed and milled wheat straw pellets were measured
on a total sample size of about 2 kg, taken from the top and middle of one of the big bags.
The biomass was sieved on a Retcsh RS 200 automatic sieve with sieve sizes of 10, 7.1, 5, 4,
3.15 and 2 mm for 10 minutes at an amplitude of 1 mm/"g".

Results and discussion
6.1
Optimization of tar production on the LT-CFB
This section describes and discusses the results of the tar production on the LT-CFB by
reviewing the effects of decreased pyrolysis temperature and particle size. It also describes
and discusses the tar quality of the produced heavy tar fractions by reviewing the results
from GC-MS and elemental analysis. Finally, the results from a single experiment with
partial oxidation is discussed with regards to yields and tar quality.
6.1.1
Effect of pyrolysis temperature and particle size on tar yield
Reduced temperature
The amount of condensed tar is presented in Figure 6.1.1 and shows
that there is a correlation between increasing tar concentration in the producer gas (heavy
fraction) and decreasing pyrolysis temperature. The best curve ﬁt is an exponential corre-
lation with R2 = 0.94. The increasing tar concentration was expected, because the general
tendency found in literature is, that the production of tar is temperature dependent and
will increase from around 250 ◦C up to 520 ◦C and afterwards it is decreasing due to tar
cracking caused by secondary reactions. The exponential regression (best ﬁt) of the heavy
tar fraction seen in Figure 6.1.1 shows, that the tar production does not have a linear relation
with temperature, but rather a bell shaped curve as also found in Salehi et al. [26] and Ate¸s
and I¸sıkda˘g [44]. There is also tendency of a small increase in the light tar fraction when
temperature decreased, where the best ﬁt is a linear regression.
In one of the experiments (Nov 29th), the concentration of both the light and heavy fraction
in the producer gas are extraordinary high, and amounts to 540 g/Nm3 and 134 g/Nm3,
respectively. On Figure 6.1.1, these measurements are not included in the trend line, as they
are considered outliers. The heavy tar concentration is about 35% higher compared to the
trend line. It could be the result of lower pyrolysis temperature, combined with reduced
particle size. In this experiment, the average pyrolysis temperature was signiﬁcantly reduced
(587 ◦C, Table 4.2.1) and the particle size of the wheat straw was also signiﬁcantly reduced
compared to the wheat straw in campaign 1 (see Table 4.2.1 or Figure 4.2.1). The effect of
reduced particle size will be further discussed later in this section. The high concentration
could also be the result of extensive overfeeding of the reactor and the removal of around
13 kg of bed material, prior to the time of sampling, which was done in this experiment
in order to drive the pyrolysis temperature down. The feed rate was 25.5 kg wstr/hr and
the AER ratio 0.18 on this day compared to the previous runs where it was between 19-22
kg/hr and 0.21-0.25, respectively. This have meant a large increase in the overall amount of
water in the system and increased the volatiles-to-air ratio, giving less diluted PG and thus
an increased tar concentration in the producer gas.
In Figure 6.1.2, the results show that the water content in the tar fractions correlate well
with temperature, especially the light fraction. The water content in the light fractions
increase with temperature, which is due to thermal cracking of primary tars by secondary
reactions. Primary tars contain more oxygen and are more likely to be water soluble. The
opposite tendency is seen in the heavy fraction, where the water content decrease when the
pyrolysis temperature increase. A reason could be, that the higher content of oxygenates at
low pyrolysis temperatures are likely to have the effect of emulsifying water and tar and

Other pyrolysis processes
Other current pilot scale processes show better performances of
producing tar/bio-oil than the LT-CFB. For example, the Bioliq pilot plant developed at the
Karlsruhe Institute of Technology has a bio-oil yield on wheat straw of 30%wt (product yield)
with a HHV of 23 MJ/kg and up to 60% energy yield from bio-oil. The maximum energy
yield from tar (heavy fraction) achieved in these experiments was 30 % and approximately
10 % wt. per kg wheat straw (Figure 6.1.4 and Figure 6.1.5, respectively). They use a CFB
gasiﬁer with external heating (methane) and char oxidation to keep sand at 600 C in the
riser and 500 C in the pyrolysis chamber. Furthermore, they use 1 mm sand particles so
char is entrained in the PG but not the sand, and can therefore be collected free of sand
(85% energy yield including the char fraction) [46]. Also other processes has shown better
bio-oil yields (Lurgi, BioBoost and Kornmayer) between 20-40% wt of the total products
with similar water contents and heating values [46]. However, some of the advantages of
the LT-CFB gasiﬁer are, that it does not require any external heating (in full scale) to be
efﬁcient, and it is very ﬂexible regarding fuel sources [47] and thus ﬂexible to enter a variety
of applications. The pyrolysis temperature has to be further decreased in the LT-CFB process
to achieve the same tar yields. However, it is a trade off, because higher tar yields will
increase the biochar production and therefore less energy will remain in the dry producer
gas, which could result in a dry producer gas with too low caloriﬁc value to run a gas
engine (producing power and heat), and thus wasting the energy. The important thing is to
ﬁnd a balance where all products (tar, char and dry producer gas) can be utilized.

6.1.2
Effect of pyrolysis temperature and particle size on tar quality
Change in tar composition
Although the pyrolysis temperature range tested in this project
is relatively low (570-690 ◦C), the results are as expected. The content of phenol’s and PAH’s
(naphthalene included) increase with increasing temperature, while the composition changes
so that the relative content of PAH’s increase with temperature while the content of phenol’s
decrease, see Figure 6.1.6.a and Figure 6.1.6.b, respectively. This is due to the effect of
secondary reactions that converts primary tars into secondary and tertiary tars. It is unclear
what caused the spike in produced phenol’s at 665 ◦C (Figure 6.1.6.a) because the operating
conditions were almost identical to the experiments at 659 and 663 ◦C. Note that only 5-20
% of the tar is quantiﬁed (Figure 6.1.6.a). The mass/charge (m/z) range of the GC-MS
method used is between 50 and 450. And for organic compounds, the charge is usually
one, so the m/z value is equal to the molecular mass of the detected species. Meaning that
species that are not quantiﬁed, are molecules with a molecular weight above or close to 450
g/mol. These compounds can be asphalthenes or aromatics that has polymerized into large
molecules.
Figure 6.1.7 show the change in tar composition with pyrolysis temperature. It is not to
say, that this is the exact composition, because only up to 20 % of the tars are quantiﬁed,
as before mentioned, which add up to around 60 % of the total area measured by the
GC-MS. Assuming, that the area on the chromatogram and the tar mass are correlated, then
approximately 30 % of the total amount of tar is quantiﬁed by the GC-MS. However, the
GC-MS analysis gives a good indication of what effect pyrolysis temperature has on the tar
composition.
Phenol’s are the most abundant compound measured. The content increases with temper-
ature up to around 650 ◦C where it decreases. This is an indication of how the primary,
secondary and tertiary reactions unfolds in the LT-CFB process. From Figure 3.4.2 we
know, that primary tars gradually decrease between 500 and 750 ◦C while the secondary tars
increase in the same rate. This correlates well with the results, because the relative amount of
phenol’s increase by 20 % while other oxygenate species decrease by 10 %. Also the relative
amounts of PAH-3’s and 4’s decrease. This suggests, that the higher PAH’s are simply not
35
==PAGE:47==
being formed and that the oxygenates are mainly converted into phenol’s. The contents of
N-aromatic’s and PAH-2’s (mainly naphthalene species) are stable in this temperature range
(570-650 ◦C), whereas the relative amount of aromatic’s (e.g. Indene and Benzene) increases
- probably also on the behalf of the oxygenate species. At temperatures above 650 ◦C, the
relative amount of phenol’s decrease while PAH-2 and 4’s increase. According to Figure
3.4.2, the PAH’s are formed at temperatures above 750 ◦C and primary tars should, at that
point, be totally annihilated or converted into secondary or tertiary species. Conversely,
the tertiary tars should not have been formed at the same temperatures as the primary
tars. However, the results from the LT-CFB shows, that the tars are not mutually exclusive
and at any pyrolysis temperature tested there is a mix of all types of tars. But this is not
uncommon in pyrolysis processes and especially not in ﬂuid bed gasiﬁcation. According
to Milne et al. [7] a mix of primary, secondary and tertiary tars indicates non-uniform
conditions in the process. The residence times, temperature and heating rates exerted on
the particles may vary and therefore result in primary tars "surviving" and tertiary tars
being formed unexpectedly. In ﬂuid bed gasiﬁcation, the physical forces from ﬂuidization
and difference in particle size can cause fast vaporization in some particles together with a
fast exit, while other particles remain longer and obtain an increased severity of the pyrolysis.
The measurement at the lowest temperature also had reduced particle size. It seems that
the formation of phenol’s are favored here on the expense of PAH formation. It is expected,
that less PAH’s are formed at this temperature, but the difference seem high compared
to the adjacent measurements. The smaller particles would most likely have the effect of
reducing the particle residence time in the reactor and furthermore reduce the difference
in heating rate between the particles. Thus create more uniform pyrolysis conditions that
would prevent the maturation of PAH’s. According to Milne et al. [7], having primary and
secondary tars instead of tertiary tars is an advantage if the purpose is to upgrade the tars to
bio-oil or other chemicals. Because PAH’s matured under more severe pyrolysis conditions
can be harder to convert into the desired product in downstream processes.
Change in O/C and H/C ratios
Element analyses of C, H, N, S and O (by difference) were
made on all tar fractions. The O/C and H/C ratios on a dry basis are presented in Figure
6.1.8. The O/C and H/C ratio are a good instrument for comparing bio-oil quality among
other processes or assessing the quality of your own process. Here, the results show that
higher temperatures decrease the O/C ratio and increase the H/C ratio. At 568 and 597
◦C, the particle size were reduced (1-2 mm) compared to the experiment at 587 ◦C, where
the average particle size were 3-4 mm (Figure 4.2.1). For the two experiments with the
small particle size, the H/C ratio is slightly higher while the O/C ratio is the same. This
suggests, that by reducing the particle size, the tar quality is increased. Thus providing a
better starting point prior to subsequent bio-oil upgrade. Furthermore, the O/C at 688 is
0.07 and it is 0.23 at 568 ◦C, which is a 3.5 times lower content of oxygen - suggesting that
thermal cracking of tars might be a simple solution for producing bio-oil with a low oxygen
content.

6.2.3
Measuring heating values
In this section, a method for estimating the heating value using an empirically determined
equation will be compared to bomb calorimeter measurements. The equation used (6.2.1)
is the correlation found by Channiwala and Parikh [50]. It was found by using 15 exiting
correlations and testing 50 fuel types including biomass, liquids, gas and coal. The contents
41
==PAGE:53==
of C, H, N, S, O and ash (in wt %) are used:
HHV = (349.1 × C + 1178.4 × H + 100.5 × S −103.4 × O −15.1 × N −21.1 × ASH) kJ/kg
(6.2.1)
Elemental analyses of CHNS-O were made on all tar fractions and used to calculate the
HHV of the tar fractions to evaluate the consistency of that against using a bomb calorimeter.
A calculation example is presented in Appendix B.2 and all BC measurements are presented
in Appendix A.3. The idea was, that if calculating the HHV using element analysis were
consistent with the results from the bomb calorimeter, then the element analysis could save
time and effort. Furthermore, the element analysis can be used for e.g. carbon balances as
well.
There are large deviations in using both methods. Three of the bomb calorimeter measure-
ments have large variations at temperatures 623, 624, and 597 ◦C) the measurements have
a c.v.3 of 15, 21 and 7%, respecitively. While the rest of the measurements are below 5
%. The variations are most likely due to inadequate mixing of the tars. The total amount
of tar available were small, so there was (for the most part) not enough tar to do more
than 2 replicates, this does however make the statistical evaluation of them inadequate.
The element analysis was also made with two replicates and the average of all standard
deviation (s.d.) for the elements were used. Here, 4 out of 8 measurements had c.v. between
6-9 % while the rest was below 5%. As seen on Figure 6.2.2, the heating values from the
bomb calorimeter measurements are generally higher than from the element analysis. The
bomb calorimeter measures 6 % higher, in average, than the element analysis with a range
of ± 10 %. The accuracy of the bomb calorimeter was determined by using Benzoic acid as a
standard, that had an average error of 0.84 %, Appendix A.3. Thus, the accuracy of the bomb
calorimeter are better than doing element analysis. The element analyzer is not necessarily
more inaccurate, but the sample sizes are very small (0.5-1.5 mg) compared to the bomb
calorimeter sample size (ca. 500 mg) , which requires a higher number of replicates to be
representative. Furthermore, for these small quantities even small amount of evaporation
can skewer the result.
The element analysis are adequate for quick estimates of the heating value. If used, it is
important that the chance of evaporation is eliminated and that the sample is completely
homogenized. If accuracy is important, then the best result is achieved by the bomb
calorimeter.

6.3.2
Behavior of chlorine and potassium
A mass balances on chlorine (Cl) and potassium (K) were made in the same manner as
the sulfur balance (Appendix B.6) and the results are presented in Table 6.3.2. The mass
balances are relatively good, compared to the sulfur balance. Only 19.3 % of the total K and
3.9 % of Cl was unaccounted for. The negative sign (-3.9 %) in Table 6.3.2 indicates that
more Cl has left the system than what came in. Silmilar values of methyl chloride (MeCl)
in the producer gas has been reported for previous LT-CFB runs. In six occasions of data
sampling on the LT-CFB and Pyroneer gasiﬁers, the MeCl concentration was between 90
and 134 ppmv at normal, or reference temperatures, i.e. at 650 ◦C in the PR and 730 ◦C
in the CR [40]. This stem reasonably well with the results in Figure 6.3.2 where the MeCl
concentration is between 50 and 110 ppmv at that temperature. The MeCl concentration
decreases with temperature from 250-300 ppmv around 570 ◦C to around 100 ppmv at 670
◦C. Although not very strong (R2 = 0.55), the declining tendency seems to be linear.
The majority of the chlorine released from biomass during pyrolysis is released as MeCl, as
the mass balance also indicates. The chlorine in MeCl is known to end up as HCl in the
gas phase at high temperatures, the thermal conversion of MeCl was studied by Rasmussen
[56] who found that the MeCl-Cl to HCL-Cl conversion is practically one to one. When
looking at the results in Figure 44 in Rasmussen [56] at gasiﬁcation conditions as the LT-CFB
gasiﬁer has (vapor residence time of 1.3 seconds in the pyrolysis reactor, see Appendix B.4),
the gasiﬁcation temperature needs to be higher than 750 ◦C for any conversion of MeCl to
happen and at 800 ◦C the conversion is only 5-8 %. Therefore, thermal conversion to HCl is
not cause of declining MeCl in the gas phase. The reason is that above 700 ◦C, the vapor
pressure of KCl increase signiﬁcantly and it starts gaseous KCl starts to be the favorable
form of K in the char and ash [57], which therefore changes the composition of Cl in the gas
to be a mixture of MeCl and gaseous KCl. However, the majority of K is retained in the ash
and char as mainly as K-silicates, and besides KCl also KOH, K2SO4 and K2CO3 is possible
[57]. This is also recognized from the mass balance in Table 6.3.2, 80.7 % of the total mass of
K is retained in the char/ash and bed material. But because all the chlorine are accounted
for as MeCl or retained in the bed, it is unclear what becomes of the remaining K. Some
fraction (not insigniﬁcant) of ﬁne particles ends up in the heavy tar fraction, which may
explain some of the loss of K. There is also the possibility, that some of the gaseous KCl
escape the reactor and make deposits in the pipes. Although the LT-CFB gasiﬁer is designed
so that the gaseous KCl that are bound to come from the char reactor (> 700 ◦C) is mixed
into the pyrolysis gas, and therefore cools down and condenses on to char and ash particles,
that then eventually will be caught in the cyclone, the hot gas ﬁlter or be accumulated in the
bed material.

Conclusion
Ten experimental runs was made on the 100 kW LT-CFB gasiﬁer which consisted of gasifying
wheat straw at pyrolysis temperatures between 568 to 688 ◦C and with different particle
sizes in order to evaluate the yield and quality of the tar and dry producer gas. Furthermore,
a single experiment of testing partial oxidation on the producer gas was also made to
investigate this as a method for upgrading tar to high quality bio-oil. The producer gas was
characterized by analyzing the tar yield and quality of the tars including; heating value,
water content, O/C ratio and content of phenol’s and PAH’s. Furthermore, the sulfur,
chlorine and energy content of the tar and water free producer gas was also evaluated.
The effect of reduced temperature and particle size on tar yield and quality
Reducing
the pyrolysis temperature from approximately 660 ◦C to 580 ◦C increased the tar yield
(heavy fraction) by more than 2 fold and the maximum tar yield achieved was 110 g tar/kg
wheat straw for the crushed pellets (3-5 mm particles) and 100 g tar/kg wheat straw for the
milled pellets (1-2 mm particles). The current temperature reduction favors the formation
of phenol’s and oxygenated tar species over the formation of poly aromatic hydrocarbons.
Furthermore, element analysis show that the molar O/C ratio increase from 0.07 to 0.23 (a
3.5 fold increase) at the current temperature reduction. Reducing temperature also increase
the water content (to around 30-36 % wt) and reduces the higher heating value from approx-
imately 30 MJ/kg tar to about 20 MJ/kg tar, on an as received basis. However, the higher
tar yield caused by the temperature reduction increase the contribution of tar to the total
energy composition in the producer gas from 10 to 30 %.
Although particle size reduction lead to a decrease in tar yield per kg straw, it counteracts
the accumulation of char in the bed material of the gasiﬁer. This is a problem at low
temperature running conditions that can cause a stop in ﬂuidization, but it can be mitigated
by additional fuel milling because the ﬁne char particles are entrained in the producer gas
and surpasses the primary cyclone, thus preventing accumulation in the gasiﬁer. Reducing
the particle size is also believed to favor the formation of phenolic compounds rather than
poly aromatic hydrocarbons because the smaller particles have a more uniform particle
size distribution leading to more uniform pyrolysis conditions and less severe pyrolysis.
Reduced particle size is recommended if the end goal is to use the LT-CFB process at lowered
pyrolysis temperatures in continuous operation for optimal bio-oil production.
Partial oxidation as a simple method for upgrading tar to bio-oil
Test results indicated
that introducing a partial oxidation step after the LT-CFB gasiﬁer could be a simple way of
upgrading tar to bio-oil by substantially decreasing the oxygen content. However, further
test results are needed in order to determine the efﬁciency and feasibility of the process.
Comparison of methods used for tar quantiﬁcation and tar quality analysis
For quanti-
fying tar yield in producer gas, the total tar condenser used in this project is recommended
as opposed to using the Petersen column. Because it measures much larger amount s than
the Petersen column and all tar and water that is condensable at 22 ◦C are collected. The
Petersen column does not measure the full amount, nor does it show any consistency in
measurement error. Therefore, it is not adequate to use for precise tar quantiﬁcation.
48
==PAGE:60==
The online gas analyzer used in this project is likewise not adequate to measure the true
composition and energy content of the non-condensable gases in the producer gas. It does
not measure light hydrocarbon gases (C2 –C6), which in average account for 33 % of the total
energy in the dry producer gas. It is therefore recommended, that the ofﬂine GC method
(used in this project) is used if precise mass and energy balances are required.
The determination of the higher heating value was evaluated using a bomb calorimeter for
direct measurements and data from elemental analysis for calculating the heating value. The
higher heating values from using the elemental analysis were 6 ± 4 % lower than using the
bomb calorimeter. The bomb calorimeter itself was evaluated against a standard (Benzoic
Acid) and had an error of 0.84 ±0.18 %. It is therefore more accurate to use the bomb
calorimeter, than calculating the heating value with data from elemental analysis.
Concentration of sulfur and chlorine in the dry producer gas
The contents of sulfur in
the dry producer gas (i.e. tar and water free producer gas) was found to be between 14
and 133 mg/kg. Small concentrations of carbonyl sulﬁde (COS) was consistently measured
at 13-18 ppmv (equal to 14-20 mg/kg dry producer gas), whereas hydrogen sulﬁde (H2S)
only was measured on a few occasions, hence the large differences in sulfur concentrations.
There is reason to believe, that H2S is present in the producer gas at all time, because some
studies also have revealed inconsistency in measuring H2S and others have determined that
H2S is the main contributor to sulfur in producer gas.
The amount of chlorine released into the gas phase is approximately 25-30 % wt. of the total
chlorine in the wheat straw. While the remaining resides in the ash. The concentration of
methyl chloride in the gas is temperature dependent, and were found to be in the range
of 204-292 ppmv, equal to 240-340 mg/kg dry producer gas at the current reductions in
pyrolysis temperatures.
Sulfur and chlorine concentrations are found to be high enough to constitute a risk towards
pollution of catalysts and process equipment in downstream processing and therefore
factors that must be considered in the case that downstream catalytic upgrading of the tar is
applied.

8
Perspectives
Originally the gasiﬁer was not designed to run at pyrolysis temperatures this low (< 650 ◦C)
and with particles sizes at 1-2 mm, but by experimentally optimizing the process conditions
to succeed with this, the LT-CFB concept could prove to be a very ﬂexible solution. A system
that would be able to gasify a wide variety of low quality biomass, for co-ﬁring at a power
plant or decentralized heat and power production on one hand, or produce bio-oil and
bio-char on the other hand, if needed. Controlling the pyrolysis temperature is essential to
control the tar production levels. This study has shown that it may be feasible to change
pyrolysis reactor temperature by adjustment of the feeding rate and bed level. Adjusting
feeding rate may lead to severe char accumulation, but this can be effectively mitigated
by increased fuel milling. Apparently, increased fuel milling may also beneﬁt oil quality.
In industrial application of the system, increased milling would be required in periods
with low electricity prices as these prices would favor a high bio-oil production over a high
electricity production. Oppositely, when electricity prices are high, less milling is required
as the system would focus production on heat and power. This match between ﬂuctuating
energy prices and milling requirements is a beneﬁt to the LT-CFB bioreﬁnery setup as it
increase the ﬂexibility in the production rate of oil, char, heat and power.
The upgrading of tar to high quality bio-oil is typically done by expensive and high
technology solutions that may have the prospects of being simpliﬁed by using partial
oxidation (POX). Because, although the POX experiment showed signiﬁcant reduction in
tar yield, it indicated improvement of the tar quality, i.e. reduction in oxygen content, and
therefore may be a cost-effective way to produce bio-oil. Development of this technique
could prove useful in combination with the ﬂexible LT-CFB process. The recommendation is
to test the process at temperatures between 650-750 ◦C, at air equivalence ratios ≤0.1 and
at prolonged vapor residence times in order to initiate secondary reactions of oxygenates
but at mild reaction conditions. Increasing the bio-oil quality by POX has the potential
drawback of reduced total oil quantity. However, the related increase in the energy content
in the non-condensible gas fraction may increase the feasibility of including a gas engine in
small scale application of the system.
