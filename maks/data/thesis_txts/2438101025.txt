Analysis of Quantum Communications Protocols in Aquatic Scenarios
by Silvia Tarantino
Exchanging information in the sea represents a challenging but yet necessary task nowadays.
The currently employed technologies to achieve communication in the maritime environment
exploit acoustic and low-frequency waves or optical wireless communication. However, these
techniques all suﬀer from security issues since none of them would provide an unconditionally
secure transfer of information in a post quantum world. An already available solution able
to manage this weakness, is quantum cryptography, which allows to achieve unconditional
secure communication thanks to the laws of quantum mechanics. Yet, the implementation of
quantum communications in aquatic scenario is still a relatively unexplored ﬁeld of research.
This project aims to provide a feasibility analysis of various quantum communications
protocols simulated for the sea water medium, by performing an evaluation of their secret key
rate when exploited in a realistic aquatic environment. Furthermore, three diﬀerent links are
considered, in order to cover the majority of the scenarios where secure communication must
be required. A careful study on the optical properties of sea water reveals that attenuation
of light varies considerably depending on the composition of suspended particles, which
characterize diﬀerent types of water. The secret key rate is evaluated for clear oceanic water,
where the minimum level of attenuation of light is registered.
The outcome of this analysis suggests that underwater quantum communication can be
implemented by adopting several protocols and in a point-to-point connection, in a link that
integrates a free-space optical wireless and an underwater point-to-point connection and in
a scenario where a beam of light is reﬂected on the sea surface to avoid potential physical
obstructions in the corresponding point-to-point link.
These results are of great interest from the scientiﬁc point of view of the rising re-
search ﬁeld of quantum technologies. Moreover, the implication of unconditional security
underwater as well as in free-space and optical ﬁber, suggests the employment of quantum
communication in the aquatic environment for defence and surveillance purposes.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Security in underwater communications is a very sensitive topic due to its great interest in
numerous scientiﬁc, industrial and military applications. In addition, the increasing number
of vehicles travelling on the sea surface or in the water medium itself, is requesting a channel
to exchange information which must be considered secure.
Quantum communication provides a solution to the problem of security thanks to some
fundamental axioms of quantum mechanics. However, the implementation of QC are limited
to the free-space and optical ﬁber channel, while only few introductory studies were conducted
for a maritime scenario.
The purpose of this project is, therefore, to provide a feasibility analysis for the perfor-
mance of this technology in seawater.
1.1
Motivations
The most widely adopted technique to send information, acoustic communications, has con-
siderably evolved in the past decades; however the limited bandwidth provided represents a
substantial limit for this technology.
Optical communication represents a solution for the expanding demand for a channel to
exchange information at higher data rates. Moreover, quantum communication is a powerful
resource for guaranteeing unconditional security for the transmitted information [1, 2, 3, 4,
5]. The feasibility of underwater optical wireless communications is a topic which has been
widely explored and analyzed [6]. In the same way, the problems and values of quantum
communications are well understood: information sent adopting quantum communication
protocols can be considered as unconditionally secure thanks to the principles of quantum
mechanics. Yet, these two ﬁelds have been rarely studied together as underwater quantum
communications.
The idea was introduced in [7] and the few examples of studies and experimental results
support the idea that quantum communication in the sea can be implemented successfully [8,
9, 10, 11, 12].
1.2
Project Content
The focus of this project is the creation of a comprehensive analysis about the potential
performances for diﬀerent types of quantum communication protocols for diﬀerent aquatic
scenarios.
The ﬁrst part of the project is focused on understanding the peculiarities of the underwater
channel, which means the optical eﬀects present in water such as scattering, beam attenuation,
beam wandering, turbulences etc. In addition, three diﬀerent types of channel are introduced:
line-of-sight (LOS), reﬂective non-line-of-sight (reﬂective NLOS) and air-water downlink.
==PAGE:18==
2
Chapter 1. Introduction
Second, the diﬀerent quantum communication protocols from the families of discrete-
variable, distributed-phase reference and continuous-variable, are studied and simulated with
Matlab software for a transmission in optical ﬁber. The protocols chosen use a single-photon
source, weak-coherent-pulses and decoy states for the DV family. Moreover, the diﬀerential-
phase-shift, coherent-one-way and diﬀerential-phase-time-shift protocols are also considered
for the DPR family as well as a CV protocol. The parameters used to assess the quality of the
performances, the secret key rate and quantum bit error rate, are introduced and, in particular,
the secret key rate is evaluated for each of the protocols described.
The third stage of the project aims to combine the ﬁst two parts indicated above, the un-
derwater channel and quantum communications. The parameter describing the optical eﬀects
caused by water, the attenuation coeﬃcient, is modeled and used to obtain the transmissivity
for the water link in the three diﬀerent scenarios described above. This transmission coeﬃ-
cient is then introduced in the model implemented for the quantum protocols deﬁned. Finally,
simulations on the ﬁnal channel model are performed and the results are compared between
the diﬀerent protocols. Each of the three diﬀerent types of link (LOS, reﬂective NLOS and
air-water) is tested in the simulation stage and the resulting secret key rates are displayed as a
function of the length of the channel.
1.3
Outline
The report is divided as follows. Chapter 2 provides the theoretical analysis of the optically
relevant properties of seawater. The composition of seawater is described into detail, in order
to list the particles that produce modiﬁcations in a beam of light travelling in the sea and the
parameters used to quantify the optical properties are deﬁned. Moreover, the reﬂectivity of
the ocean surface is studied, as well as the eﬀects of the wind on the slope distribution of
the surface. The attenuation is analyzed as a function of scattering and absorption, and from
it the losses for the channel are obtained. Some other sources of noise and losses are also
brieﬂy introduced.
The current technologies used in communications in the maritime environment are dis-
cussed in Chap. 3. Moreover, the three types of link listed above are described and their
transmissivities deﬁned.
Quantum communications represents the focus of Chap. 4: a brief introduction to the
strengths of this technology is presented, along with the main features of quantum mechanics
that determine unconditional security. Finally, the protocols considered for this project are
speciﬁed, in particular in terms of their secret key rates.
The core of the project is illustrated in Chap. 5: the method used to create the model
for the underwater QC system is outlined and the results of the Matlab simulations shown.
Furthermore, a brief discussion on the outcome of the simulation is presented.
The possible applications of this technology in the maritime scenario are proposed in
Chap. 6. In addition, some recommendations on the future steps for the actual realization of
the system are suggested.
Some ﬁnal conclusions are given in Chap. 7, along with a summary of the most interesting
results.

Physical and Optical Properties of Sea
Water
The mixture of organic and inorganic particles present in sea water produces considerable
optical eﬀects on a beam of light travelling in it [13]. A simple representation for the seawater
medium for transmission of light is illustrated in Fig. 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Basic Scheme of the transmission through the water medium.
In order to produce a good model of the sea water channel, the concentrations and types
of particles must be analyzed, in addition to the optical eﬀects in which they can interfere.
This chapter aims to give a complete description of the optical properties of sea water.
The ﬁrst section focuses on the compound of particles present and optically active in water
and is followed by a description of the quantities used to deﬁne these optical properties.
A brief analysis on the propagation of electromagnetic waves in seawater is also provided.
Moreover the deﬁnition of optical radiance is given and is followed by an analysis of the
surface of the water, focused on its reﬂectivity and the variations of the slope of it produced
by the wind. The two main optical properties of absorption and scattering are studied in detail
and used to deﬁne the total attenuation in sea water. Finally, some sources of optical noise,
such as ﬂuorescence, bioluminescence and background solar light are presented along with
the eﬀect of beam spreading. A ﬁnal way to describe the optical losses in sea water is given
as a conclusion to the analysis.
2.1
Composition of Sea Water
The constituents of the oceanic water are multiple and can be classiﬁed as dissolved or partic-
ulate, organic or inorganic and living or nonliving [13]. All the matter with dimension smaller
than 400 nm can be labeled as dissolved; an example can be given by local concentrations of
sea salt [14]. All that matter that can not be considered as dissolved is, therefore, classiﬁed
as mixture of particles.
In general, pure sea water is a composition of pure water and dissolved salts for an average
of about 35‰ of the water weight. In addition, another element of the compound of sea
water is represented by the mixture of dissolved organic matter which is the result of dead or
decaying organisms and cells. These matter tends to be brown or yellow in colour and, from

this characteristic, is called colored dissolved organic matter (CDOM), yellow substance or
gelbstoﬀ. Usually, CDOM is brought by rivers in the sea and, therefore, its concentrations
tend to be higher in coastal waters [14, 13].
A variety of organic and inorganic particles is also present in sea water and represents
the above-mentioned particulate matter [13]. The inorganic particles present in sea water
are usually residue of terrestrial rocks or soil brought to the water by the wind, eroded by a
river ﬂowing in the open sea or lifted from the sea bed by underwater currents. Generally,
these residues are a mixture of ground quartz sand, clay minerals and metal oxides and their
dimension is in a range that goes from 1 to tens of µm. On the other hand, sea water is
also a compound of organic particles. Viruses are a ﬁrst example of organic matter and can
be considered as “dissolved” since their dimension is usually between 20-250 nm, with a
concentration of 1012 to 1015 particles per m−3. A second type of organic matter is given
by colloids, which are non-living particles of dimension varying between 0.4 and 1 µm and
are usually present in water with a concentration of 1013 m−3. Furthermore, living bacteria
populate sea water in typical number concentrations of 1011 −1013 m−3 and with size range
usually between 0.2 and 1 µm. Another class of organic particles is given by phytoplankton,
which are microscopic plants of size between 1 to 200 µm, wandering usually close to the
sea surface, where sun light is abundant; in eﬀect, their photosynthesis process beneﬁts of
the solar light and the chlorophyll-a present in them plays a determinant role in the optical
properties of water, as will be described later. Other types of non-living organic detritus,
for example decomposing phytoplankton, can also be found in the composition of sea-water
along with living organisms of size greater than 100 µm, such as microscopic animals called
zooplankton or aggregates of particles.

2.2
Inherent Optical Properties
The eﬀects of the presence of these “optically active” particles are described by the inherent
optical properties (IOPs) of water, which mean the characteristics that depend only upon the
water medium itself [14]. On the contrary, the properties that depend both on the medium
and on the system used to measure the light beam are called apparent optical properties
(AOPs) [14]. For the purpose of this project, it is more interesting to focus of the IOPs, since
they describe the channel only, independently on the quality of the transceiver.
A narrow collimated beam of monochromatic light with spectral radiant power Pi(λ)
is subjected to three diﬀerent eﬀects when it propagates in water. A ﬁrst portion of Pi(λ)
is absorbed within water; in addition, a fraction is scattered out (assuming that only elastic
scattering occurs so that the wavelength of the scattered particles remains the same). Finally,

2.3
Propagation of electromagnetic waves in seawater
In order to describe the electromagnetic properties of water, it is useful to study the behaviour
of the electrical permittivity ϵ, the magnetic permeability µ and the electrical conductivity
σ.
On one side, µ is equal to its value for free space in vacuo, which corresponds to
µ0 = 4π × 10−7NA−2. On the other hand, the electrical permittivity and conductivity
both depend on the frequency ν of the propagating wave and, therefore, on its wavelength
λ. Seawater presents a very high conductivity caused by the presence of dissolved salts,
predominantly sodium (Na+) and chloride (Cl−) ions, which give to the water an average
salinity of 3.1 to 3.8%. [15]. This property of seawater produces a considerable amount of
losses when an electromagnetic wave propagates in it.

Sea surface analysis

it is also important to describe the phenomena that aﬀects the surface of the ocean.

can be approximated by not taking into account its imaginary part. This allows to use Snell’s
law when calculating the reﬂection coeﬃcients.

2.9
Fluorescence and Bioluminescence
Fluorescence represents the phenomenon of re-emission of light at lower frequencies from
an absorbing object that receive a beam of light. The intensity of the re-emitted light is
always considerably low, since it represents the 1 to 3 % of the intensity of the incident ray.
Three main components aﬀect the resulting ﬂuorescence emission spectrum: the ﬁrst one, is
given by the eﬀects of the yellow matter and represents a broad band in the spectrum; the
second one, which appears as a peak that varies according to the wavelength, is caused by the
Raman emission produced by H2O molecules. Finally, the last one is produced by the living
chlorophyll-a. Usually, the eﬀects of ﬂuorescence is an optical eﬀect that can be considered as
negligible for the measurements of attenuation and, only in case of highly ﬂuorescing waters,
it is recommended to use some blocking ﬁlters to decrease their impact.
On the other hand, bioluminescence is an emission of light originating from living
organisms and is, therefore, more signiﬁcant in region with high concentration of biomass
production. These type of emissions appears in form of ﬂashes of duration of around 10 ms
and its spectral distribution is usually broadband and focused in the blue-green window.

Chapter 3
Communications in Sea Water
The communication between the vehicles travelling in water or on the ocean surface, as well
as between sensors, buoys or submarines, represents a non trivial task, given the optical losses
present in the water medium and already studied in Sec. 2.
The technologies used for communication in the maritime environment are acoustic
waves, radio frequency (RF) waves and optical waves and can provide either a very long
communication distance or a higher data rate.
Furthermore, depending on the scenario where the exchange of information needs to be
achieved, various types of link can be necessary. The ﬁrst of the three links described for
the purpose of this project, is a point-to-point link called line-of-sight. Moreover, in case of
physical obstacles between transmitter and receiver, a second type of link, called reﬂective
non-line-of-sight, uses the sea surface as a mirror to transmit information. Finally, an hybrid
between a line-of-sight link and a free-space link in the atmosphere, allows the communication
between objects in the two mediums: water and air.
This chapter will provide a description of the techniques currently used in communication
underwater. Moreover the three diﬀerent types of link will be introduced and characterized
in terms of their transmissivities, in order to quantify the losses produced by seawater.
3.1
Current Technologies
The three diﬀerent technologies used for communication in the maritime environment are
acoustic waves, in the frequency band of 10-15 kHz, radio frequency (RF) waves, of 3 to 300
Hz or 3 to 30 kHz (extremely-low and very-low frequencies, respectively), and optical waves,
with a frequency band of 1012 −1015 Hz [6].
Most links in underwater networks adopt acoustic waves [6] since they can propagate
for considerable distances and are aﬀected by a relatively low level of absorption. In eﬀect,
acoustic waves can propagate for kilometers at a speed of 1500 m/s with an attenuation of
only between 0.1 and 4 dB/km, considerably lower compared to the RF or optical waves. In
addition, they do not require very large antennas on the receiver size, but can work eﬃciently
with antennas of 0.1 m of radius. The main drawback of this technology, however, is that the
distance-dependent bandwidth only provides a data rate of the order of Kbps.
RF waves for communication in water [6], usually refer to very low frequency (3-30 kHz),
also called VLF, or extremely low frequency (3-300 Hz), named ELF. Both of these two
communication techniques can partially solve the limitations of acoustic waves in terms of
data rate and transmission speed, since they enable a communication of the order of Mbps
at the speed of light (in water equal to 2.55 × 108 m/s). On the other hand, although, the
distance reachable using RF in water is quite limited to only a few tens of meters, because of
the higher level of attenuation (3.5-5 dB/m). In addition, both VLF and ELF communication
require bigger antennas on the receiver side and up to mW of power in terms of transmitted
wave.
==PAGE:40==
24

The last technology used for transmitting information in water is optical free-space wire-
less communication [6] and provides the best performances as regard to data rate, transmission
speed and eﬃciency. In eﬀect, with a transmitting-wave power of only a few watts, this type
of link guarantees a data rate of the order of Gbps at the speed of light and demands an antenna
on the receiver side with the same dimension as in case of acoustic communication. In this
last case, the main limitation is given by the attenuation in water, which can vary between
0.39 to 11 dB/m, depending on the turbidity of the sea, and limits the reachable distance to
a maximum of 100 meters.
The choice of the best technology varies considerably, depending of the scenario where it
needs to be realized. If, for example, the main requirement is a very long-distance communi-
cation, acoustic waves represent the best solution but narrow the quantity of information that
can be actually sent. On the other hand, if a very high data rate is requested, optical wireless
communication can be adopted, at the cost of a shorter maximum distance achievable.
3.2
Types of Channel
In order to take into account various situations where a communication channel is necessary,
it is important to include diﬀerent types of link and chose the preferable one. In this section,
three types of channel are proposed and their performances analyzed. The parameter called
transmissivity is introduced and calculated for each of the three links. The transmissivity t,
or transmittance, is a measure for the eﬀectiveness in the transmission of radiant energy and
is related to the optical thickness (deﬁned in Section 2.12) as
t = exp (−τ).
(3.1)

they describe the capture (generally called aperture) area of the receiver but also the geometry

The ﬁrst type of connection is a point-to-point underwater link, called line-of-sight. The
second one uses the diﬀerence between the refractive indices of air and water to totally reﬂect
the light and is called reﬂective non-line-of-sight. Finally, the last link is partially a line-of-
sight link in water and partially a wireless optical free-space link in the atmosphere and can
be addressed as water-air downlink.

Quantum Communications
A simple communication system is represented by a transmitter, in literature usually called
Alice, a receiver, usually identiﬁed as Bob, and a transmission channel.
The potential
eavesdropper, named Eve, is located among the channel and her aim is to steal the information
sent by Alice to Bob. The channel can not guarantee secrecy of the information transmitted,
although the encryption of the sent message provides a certain level of security.
Cryptography represents the solution for the requirement of a secure communication
between Alice an Bob since it conceals the original message in a way that only the legitimate
receiver Bob, who owns a special key, is able to recover the original message. The algorithms
that are used to encrypt the message are called cryptosystems and can be divided in two
families: symmetric (secret-key) and asymmetric (public-key [31]).
In the ﬁrst case, Alice and Bob own both the same key, which they need to exchange
through a secure channel before the communication begins, and use it to encrypt and decrypt
the information sent.
In the second case, the receiver owns a public and private key: the public key is available
to everyone, Alice included, so that everyone can send encrypted information to Bob, while
the private key belongs exclusively to Bob and is the only way to decrypt the message sent
by Alice. In particular, public-key cryptosystems, proposed by W. Diﬃe and M. Hellman in
1976 [31], adopt sets of trap-door one way function, which are one-way because of the fact
that they are easy to compute but their inverse is computationally hard to solve, and trap-doop
since, if some special information is provided, the hard computation becomes solvable in
polynomial time. This means that, by using the public key, Alice can easily encode the
message to send to Bob and that the legitimate receiver only owns the private key that allows
to “open the trap-door” and decode the message. The trap-door one way functions most
frequently used in asymmetric cryptosystems depend on the problems of factorization of
the product of two large prime numbers (for example is the RSA - Rivest Shamir Adleman
- cryptosystem [32]) and of the calculus of discrete logarithms (for example, in ElGamal
encryption systems [33]).
The major ﬂaw of this type of cryptography was shown by P. Shor [34] and is given by the
fact that the factorization problem can be solved in polynomial time with quantum computers;
moreover, with little modiﬁcations in Shor’s algorithm, also the discrete logarithm problem
can be solved easily. Actually, quantum computers can achieve a new mode of information
processing, which is a consequence of the property of superposition from quantum mechanics.
While in classical information theory a bit can be either a one, either a zero, the superposition
principle (which is stated in the next section) states that a qubit can be a one and a zero
at the same time. Since the processing in a classical computer is made through boolean
open gates applied to sequences of unique bits, if in a quantum computer the qubit can be
both a zero and a one, the results of the quantum-version of the boolean classical gates are
both the results both for the bit one and the bit zero. This allows a quantum computer to
perform for a system of N qubits a total of 2N resulting states [35]. If quantum computers
are used, most of the cryptography that makes communication exchange secure becomes
==PAGE:48==
32
Chapter 4. Quantum Communications
not eﬀective, because the high computational eﬀort necessary to solve the factorization and
discrete logarithm problems, would be reduced to a simple polynomial-time computation.
Quantum computers able to be used as a replacement for classical ones are still not available;
however, big companies such as IBM and Microsoft have speciﬁc research centers working
on their implementation [36, 37].
A technology that is already accessible and that solves the above-mentioned problem is
quantum communication. In this section a brief introduction on quantum communications is
given, along with the principles that make this technology unconditionally secure. The main
example of QC, quantum key distribution, is presented. Afterwards, the parameters used to
describe the performances of a QKD system are deﬁned. Finally, the three families of protocols
that implement QKD, discrete-variable, distributed-phase-reference and continuous-variable,
are presented.
4.1
Quantum Cryptography
Quantum communications provide unconditional security of the messages sent from Alice to
Bob and, therefore, it is commonly referred to as quantum cryptography. The ﬁrst example
of quantum cryptography was proposed in 1984 by C. Bennett and G. Brassard and is named,
after them, BB84 protocol [1]. The general setting for this protocol is given by a transmitter,
Alice, and a receiver, Bob, connected by two communication channel: a quantum channel,
used to exchange the quantum keys, and a classical channel, where the encrypted messages
are sent. The communication system described above is shown in Fig. 4.1. Both of the
Figure 4.1: Scheme for a typical BB84 QC system.
channels are not secure, which means that the eavesdropper Eve can manipulate the signals
sent through the quantum channel and listen to those travelling through the classical channel.
The security of quantum cryptography lays in some of the principles of quantum me-
chanics. For example, in order to steal the quantum keys, Eve needs to perform a generalized
form of measurement, which in general modify the original keys. Moreover, if Eve aims to
reproduce a perfect copy of the quantum keys, she would be prevented from succeeding as a
consequence of the no-cloning theorem [38].
4.1.1
Quantum Mechanics Features
The unit used for QC is the qubit, which corresponds to the classical bit but has diﬀerent
properties. Indeed, while a bit, which can be 0 or 1, has a value that is well deﬁned and
unequivocal, a qubit represents a quantum state, determined by two complex numbers α and

Fact 4.1.1 The quantum measurement process alters the state of the qubit: the eﬀect of the

gives as a consequence that if the outcome of the measurement of the linear superposition

This property is one of the main resources of QC, since the modiﬁcations of the qubits
introduced by the measurements of the eavesdropper Eve are easy to detect.
Moreover,

forbidden [40] as stated in Fact. 4.1.2.
Fact 4.1.2 The axioms of quantum mechanics forbid the creation of a perfect copy of any
quantum bit in an unknown state |φ⟩= α|0⟩+ β|0⟩.
If this was not true, Eve could easily clone the quantum states, perform measurements of
the originals and Alice and Bob would not be able to notice the tamper.
4.1.2
The BB84
The ﬁrst idea for a QKD protocol was given in 1984 by Bennett and Brassard [1]. Alice
and Bob agree on using the computational bases |0⟩and |1⟩and the complementary bases
of linear polarization, the diagonal bases | + 45⟩and | −45⟩, representing another way to
==PAGE:50==
34
Chapter 4. Quantum Communications
encode 0 and 1, respectively. Alice encodes in N photons some 0s, using either |0⟩or | + 45⟩,
and some 1s, in the bases |1⟩or | −45⟩. She then sends the N photons prepared in the four
mentioned bases to the receiver Bob through the quantum channel. When Bob measures the
received bases, he inevitably modiﬁes them, as explained above. At this point, Alice and Bob
share their N qubits series among the classical channel, compare the bases, and whenever
they do not match, discard the corresponding qubits. After this stage, called sifting, Alice and
Bob have approximately N
2 qubits each, since Bob’s measurements produce the modiﬁcation
of around a half of the original bases. These N
2 qubits list is called raw key. A random sample
of this raw key is shared among the quantum channel to estimate the error rate. In case of a
perfect channel and no tampering actions from Alice, this error rate is zero. Otherwise, the
errors introduced are represented by the qubits measured by Eve: if the information obtained
is too large, the attempt of communication should be abrupt, otherwise, the wrong qubits
must be corrected, in order to remove the information available to the eavesdropper. After
this ﬁnal stage, the transmitter and receiver own the same key, called secret key.
4.1.3
Secret Key Rate
In general, for a QKD protocol, Alice prepares N qubits and sends them to Bob. After the
sifting phase, where Alice and Bob discard some of the qubits, both of them own n ≤N
states, which represent the raw key. At this point, both the transmitter and the receiver correct
the symbol that do not match with the ones of the other party, by using some information
post-processing and ﬁnd themselves with a new key of length l ≤n, called secret key. The
asymptotic limit of key length, where N →∞, allows the deﬁnition of the secret fraction
r = lim
N→∞l/n.
(4.5)
The value of r can be quantiﬁed for a speciﬁc strategy of the eavesdropper Eve, called
intercept resend. In this case, after Eve measures the qubits, she prepares some new states
trying to guess what the original bases were. She will be able to obtain the values of the
original quantum states for 50% of the cases, however, by trying to reproduce the original
basis choices, she will introduce an error rate of approximately 25%. In this scenario, an
upper bound for r can be ﬁxed as [2]
r = max{[I(A : B) −IE], 0},

mutual information becomes I(A : B) = 1 −h(Q), where h is the binary entropy and Q the
quantum bit error rate (QBER). The BER in QC is called QBER and is deﬁned as

The secret fraction is extracted from the raw key rate by using some classical information post

the information that might be available to Eve at this point. The fraction of the key that is
removed is expressed as min(IEA, IEB), where IE is the information available to Eve from the
raw key of Alice and Bob.

4.2
QKD Protocols
Quantum Key Distribution protocols can be divided into three families: discrete-variable,
distributed-phase-reference and continuous-variable. The main diﬀerence between the ﬁrst
two consists in the technique chosen to encode the bits in the photons. Indeed, discrete-
variable protocols encode the bits as discrete variables, while diﬀerential-phase-reference
protocols use phase, time and space as degrees-of-freedom of the photons and observe the
phase coherence of subsequent pulses. For these two protocols, a photon-counting technique
need to be implemented in order to send and measure the bit carried by each photon. On the
other hand, continuous-variable protocols use Gaussian modulation of coherent state of light
sent by standard p-i-n photodiodes and measured by homodyne detection.
The analysis of the security parameters of the protocols is performed for collective at-
tacks [2], where Eve use the same strategy to attack the diﬀerent implementations for the
==PAGE:52==

communication channel, each one independently of all the others. Moreover, the eavesdrop-
per is provided of some quantum memory, where she can store the stolen information and use
it whenever she needs it.
A ﬁnal consideration is that the uncalibrate-device scenario is assumed for the following
discussion on the parameters deﬁned for the protocols. This assumption gives as a conse-
quence that all the losses and errors introduced by the devices and the transmission through
the channel are counted as errors produced by Eve.

Chapter 5
Underwater Quantum
Communications
After a brief presentation of the state-of-art in the research on underwater QC, this chapter aims
to give an overview of the method chosen to simulate the underwater quantum communications
links for the various QKD protocols. Moreover, the assumptions and choices of the parameters
will be stated and motivated. The secret key rate performance will be displayed for the diﬀerent
protocols as a function of the distance between the transmitter Alice and the receiver Bob, for
diﬀerent water types. Finally, a discussion on the results will be provided.
5.1
Background
In 2012, M. Lanzagorta ﬁrst proposed the idea of bringing the technology of free-space
quantum communications in the sea [7] by creating a feasibility analysis on the BB84 protocol
to implement point-to-point communication between a transmitter and receiver in water. His
analysis is focused on the performance of the channel on the water column above the transmitter
at about 100 m depth. This ﬁrst analysis created some interest in the topic and a few more
studies and experiments were made since then, in order to prove that quantum communications
could achieve promising results also in the diﬃcult medium of sea water.
The main focus for underwater quantum communications in the last few years has been on
the diﬀerent implementations for the BB84 protocol. For example, [9] proposes a feasibility
analysis of a QKD system in the maritime environment by, ﬁrst, investigating the properties of
absorption and scattering of seawater and, then, by implementing a Monte Carlo simulation
to obtain the attenuation of photons, the ﬁdelity of the scattered photons, the QBER and the
secret key rate. Even though attenuation in sea water is signiﬁcantly higher compared to the
atmosphere, as shown in Chapter 2, the results in [9] suggest that indeed some unconditional
security in underwater communication can be achieved.
Some experimental results shown in [8] support the previous simulations by proving that
polarization of the photons used to encode the quantum states can survive when travelling
in pure and sea water. The outcome of these experiments reveals that, regardless of the
compound of particles optically active in oceanic water, the eﬀects of polarization rotation
and depolarization are not excessively limiting and can be mitigated by spatially ﬁltering the
photons.
Moreover, an interesting analysis by H. Xu et al. [12] evaluates the performances of the
BB84 protocol for a free-space optical link where the light ﬁrst travels in the atmosphere in
downlink and then enters in water to reach a submerged receiver. The modiﬁcations in the
polarization of the photons are evaluated in particular in the transition between the air and
water medium.
A recent study from Bouchard et al. [11] investigates the eﬀects of turbulences on an
underwater quantum channel where quantum states are encoded in the spatial modes of
==PAGE:60==
44
Chapter 5. Underwater Quantum Communications
photons. The implementation chosen for this analysis is optical angular momentum (OAM)
of light, often indicated as twisted photons, in 2 and 3 dimensions and the experiment was
performed in an outdoor swimming pool. As expected, the presence of turbulences introduces
both errors and losses; however, a positive secret key rate was achieved using both 2 and 3
dimensions.
Finally, Y. Guo et al. [10] estimate the channel parameters for continuous-variable QKD
for a downlink transmitting from a satellite to a submarine in water. The assessment of the
parameters is obtained using Monte Carlo simulations. In order to have a comprehensive
analysis, various sources of noise or losses were introduced, such as background noise, wind
on the ocean surface and atmospheric turbulences. The outcome of the simulations shows a
positive secret key rate, and therefore a possible unconditional secure communication, over
this satellite-to-submarine channel.
5.2
Simulations
The previous works on the topic reveal some great potentials for QKD systems in the aquatic
scenario. However, a comprehensive feasibility analysis of various protocols from the three
families of discrete-variable, distributed-phase-reference and continuous-variable and a com-

Therefore, the aim of this project is to evaluate the performances of the protocol implementa-
tions described in Sec. 4.2 for the three links of LOS, reﬂective NLOS and air-water downlink,
illustrated in Sec. 3.2.

5.4
Discussion
The results from the simulations above agree with the recent studies and experiments described
in Sec. 5.1 and suggest that underwater quantum communications can be implemented in the
aquatic scenario, even considering the high attenuation typical of seawater. The simulated
scheme is assumed to be working in night-time, where the background light is minimized.
==PAGE:69==
5.4. Discussion
53
The LOS link provides the longest reachable distance and the best performances and the
outcome of the simulations appears relatively ﬂexible to changes of the attenuation coeﬃcient
and, therefore, of the oceanic possible water types. The reﬂective NLOS, which was analysed
here for the ﬁrst time for QC, bring an interesting solution in case of impossible point-to-point
connection between transmitter and receiver. Even though the losses are higher than in the
LOS link, mainly due to the reﬂection on the sea surface, a secret key can be extracted for
distances of several meters.
Introducing the eﬀects of the wind on the slope PDF of the sea surface was a necessary
requirement to create a more accurate description of a real maritime scenario. The losses
caused by the waves are considerable and can be clearly seen in the air-water downlink plots,
where they produce a drop in the secret key rate at the moment of impact with the sea surface.
Nevertheless, even in the proposed realistic scenario of light wind (4 m/s), a positive secret
key rate can be obtained for many of the protocols and also for diﬀerent water types.
Going a little more into detail, the reciprocal outcomes of the simulations for the diﬀerent
protocols reﬂect the behaviour shown by V. Scarani in [2]. In general, the single-photon
protocol presents the highest achievable secret key rate, but in reality its implementation is
quite challenging. The alternative implementation of the BB84 with decoy states is also quite
promising, along with the DPR protocols.
As proved by [8], transmission in sea water creates some eﬀects of depolarization, which,
however, can be signiﬁcantly reduced using some spacial ﬁltering. In addition, an interesting
argument could be that DPR protocols might provide a higher stability in water in the NLOS
and air-water downlink. In eﬀect, when the light reaches the sea surface, it incurs in some
additional depolarization eﬀect, which might destabilize the encoding on the quantum states;
on the contrary, DPR encoding relies on the coherence of the phases of consecutive pulses,
which would more likely remain constant during the impact with the sea surface.
An important consideration needs to be made on the reﬂective NLOS link. Total internal
reﬂection is achieved only when the angle of the transmitted beam is larger than the critical
angle θc, as already mentioned in Sec. 2.5. Indeed, if θ < θc a fraction of the transmitted
power is refracted outside the water. In this case, there might be some security risks given by
the fact that the eavesdropper Eve could steal that fraction of transmitted key.
Finally, it is important to notice that the implemented system represents an approximation
of the real aquatic scenario and, then, that a more accurate model can be provided only with a
continuous interaction between computer simulations and experiments and analyses in water.

Chapter 6
Applications and Future Work
The aim of this project is to simulate a communication system which guarantees an uncondi-
tionally secure exchange of information and to test its performances in diﬀerent scenarios in
order to prove the feasibility of this technology in the maritime environment.
This chapter will provide some examples for the applications of QC in the maritime
scenario and the recommended future steps for the true implementation of this technology.
6.1
Applications
Communication in the maritime environment represents an important resource both from a
scientiﬁc and an industrial point of view. On one side, the exchange of information between
sensors located on the seabed or in ﬂoating buoys is crucial for the monitoring of the changes
in the ocean environment and for research purposes.
On the other side, communication
between sensors and/or submarines is necessary for the surveillance of strategic geographic
areas with defense purposes. In addition, to protect these sensitive data a secure channel
must be provided. From a scientiﬁc point of view, proving the feasibility of underwater
unconditionally secure communications will increase the attention to the topic and attract
potential investors who might be interested in ﬁnancing this technology. On the other side,
the potential applications for defense purposes, submarines secure communication or sensitive
data exchange between sensors, reﬂect the industrial relevance of quantum key distribution in
the maritime environment.
Besides, the three types of link proposed are suitable for diﬀerent scenarios and cover
most of the situations where unconditionally secure communication is required in the maritime
environment. For example, the line-of-sight [28] link is a good solution for a submarine-to-
submarine communication, where tracking and pointing can be realized between the transmit-
ter and receiver. The reﬂective non-line-of-sight - NLOS [28] is suitable in case of obstacles
in the point-to-point link, such as banks of ﬁshes or in case of two sensors located at dif-
ferent depths on the seabed. By using the reﬂection of the light beam on the sea surface,
the signal can be redirected to the receiver, avoiding these obstructions. Finally, the last link
is partially a wireless optical free-space downlink in the atmosphere and partially a LOS in
water [41, 10, 12, 9]; by using this type of link ﬂying objects, drones or nanosatellite are able
to communicate securely with submarines or sensors located in water.
An additional interesting application for underwater quantum communications could be
as an encryption technique to protect the tagging signals of protected animals from potential
cyberpoaching attacks. The motivation behind this proposal is given by the fact that recently
many of this type of hacking attacks were reported by multiple newspapers and articles [42,

the tags of protected animals. The information recovered help poachers to identify the position
of these animals from protected species, thus, using quantum communications would prevent
poachers from stealing the GPS coordinates. For example, a possible implementation of

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to create a feasibility analysis for a QKD system in three diﬀerent
types of link in seawater, by implementing a model for diﬀerent quantum communication
protocols and estimating the achievable secret key rate.
In a the ﬁrst part of the project, a theoretical analysis was performed on the physical and
optical properties of sea water. A model for the channel in the three links, LOS, NLOS and
air-water downlink, was created and used to evaluate the channel transmissivities in each
case. The transmissivities decade exponentially depending on the attenuation coeﬃcient,
which represents the description of the eﬀects of scattering and water and which is strictly
dependent on the type of water. In addition, in order to make the model more faithful to the
real oceanic environment, the eﬀects of a light wind were added to the losses caused by the
attenuation.
The second stage of the project was focused on the real topic of this projects, an underwater
QKD system. An initial study of the equations describing the security parameters of the
protocols was completed; afterwards, the seven quantum communications protocols chosen
were implemented using Matlab software. The transmissivities obtained above were included
in the simulations, in order to give an estimation of the performances of the protocols in
terms of their secret key rate for a LOS, NLOS and air-water link. In particular, the reﬂective
NLOS was analyzed here for the ﬁrst time with some quantum communication protocols. The
results are obtained for a water type I, which represents the clearest oceanic water and for a
wavelength of the transmitted beam of 530 nm.
The outcome of the simulations indicates that a secret key rate can be extracted for most of
the protocols in the three links. However, the most promising performances were obtained for
a single-photon and a decoy-states protocol for the discrete-variable family and for DPS and
DPTS for distributed-phase reference. COW seems to achieve satisfactory values of secret
key rate in the condition of beam-splitter attack performed by the eavesdropper Eve; however,
in case of some more advanced attacks, the results decrease considerably. Weak-coherent
pulses and continuous-variable encoding does not produce satisfying secret key rates and in
the reﬂective NLOS and air-water link, do not allow to extract any positive secret keys.
If the LOS is considered, a positive secret key rate is achieved up to around 100 m; this
makes the point-to-point conﬁguration the one giving the best results, and therefore the one
most likely to be implemented. Moreover, in the reﬂective NLOS the maximum distance
obtainable for the described speciﬁcations, of 40 m, can be obtained for the single-photon
protocol; using other protocols, a positive secret key rate is given, as well, for around 25 m.
The simulations for the air-water downlink, which is the integration of a LOS link at the end
of a free-space wireless optical channel, produce, as well, reasonable results for up to 30 m
after travelling for 1000 km in the atmosphere.
Given, the promising results for the single-photon, decoy-states, DPS and DPTS protocols,
the simulation were performed also for increasing values of the attenuation coeﬃcient, in order
to include a wider range of oceanic water types. A positive secret key rate can be obtained in
==PAGE:76==
60
Chapter 7. Conclusion
most cases for ﬁve diﬀerent water types, even though for a higher attenuation coeﬃcient the
reachable distance is reduced substantially.
In conclusion, the outcome of this project is quite promising and suggests that quantum
communications can be indeed implemented even in an environment so hostile to the trans-
mission of light: sea water. The diﬀerent types of link proposed can be suitable for most of
the scenarios where a secure communication must be guaranteed. Some examples of appli-
cations are proposed to demonstrate the utility and ﬂexibility of this new technology, which
is attractive from a scientiﬁc, industrial and military point of view. In particular, the idea of
using quantum communications to encrypt the GPS signals sent by the tags of animals from
protected species, suggests an unusual and yet interesting new application for this technology.
