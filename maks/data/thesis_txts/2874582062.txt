Abstract 
 
This paper investigates the offshore wind farm layout optimization problem, trying to improve 
the Annual Energy Production (AEP) though a Genetic Algorithm. 
The algorithm is a mix of Random Search (RS) with Genetic Algorithm (GA). 
For the sake of time for the project, only 4 months, all the WT calculations (wind, power, annual 
energy production and wake effects) have been calculated using PyWake. 
PyWake is an open-source software, based in python that has libraries for wind calculations, wake 
effect, power generation, and cost models.  
The optimization model is based in improvements of the Wind Turbines (WT) locations, one by 
one. The model tries to improve the AEP of one WT, and once it gains an improvement in the 
total AEP, shifts to the next one, and the repeats the process. This process is done to the last WT. 
This is an Iteration(I). On each Generation (G), several Iterations are calculated. The number of 
Iterations is iter_num and the number of Generations is gen_nun. 
The model can deal both with the Horns Rev 1 wind farm in Denmark as well as for other 
locations and layouts. The user can select the location and original layout, the site, the number of 
iterations on every step and the successive generations in the algorithm calculations. 
The results are presented both:  
• Hornsrev1: this wind farm is complex, with 80 WT and no radical modifications in the 
layout can be done, as the farm was well studied, and the improvements should be slight 
• A smaller wind farm: another example is analyzed with more room to modify the WT with 
longer movements to achieve a new optimize solution, as the former is not optimized (the 
aim is to allow the model to look for smart solutions begging from scratch)  
 
Keywords: offshore wind farm; layout optimization; Jensen wake model; random search 
algorithm; genetic algorithm.

General approach to the problem 
 
The urgent need to mitigate climate change has propelled renewable energy to the forefront of 
global strategies. Among these, offshore wind energy stands out for its immense potential to deliver 
large-scale, clean electricity. Its significance is rooted in several critical advantages: 
• Superior Wind Resources: Offshore locations consistently exhibit higher and more stable 
wind speeds than onshore sites, resulting in enhanced energy production and a more reliable 
power supply. Better wind resources are present offshore. 
• Vast Development Potential: The expansive ocean provides ample space for large-scale 
wind farm deployment, effectively minimizing land-use conflicts. The land constraints are 
not present on the sea. Only power grid connectivity and its costs can be a barrier for the 
developments. 
• Strategic Proximity to Demand: Coastal regions, home to a substantial portion of the global 
population, often possess high energy demands. Offshore wind farms can be strategically 
positioned to directly supply these centers, reducing transmission losses and infrastructure 
costs. 
• Decarbonization Impact: Offshore wind power generation produces zero operational 
greenhouse gas emissions, significantly contributing to global decarbonization efforts. 
While construction and decommissioning involve emissions, the lifecycle footprint remains 
substantially lower than fossil fuel alternatives. Dismantling an offshore wind farm is easy, 
compared to the onshore farms. 
• Technological Advancements: Continuous innovation in turbine technology, including 
larger, more efficient models and the rapid development of floating offshore wind platforms, 
expands access to deeper, higher-yield wind resources. The WT dimensions are higher than 
in the onshore market. 
• Economic Catalysts: The offshore wind sector stimulates job creation and economic growth 
in coastal communities while driving innovation and fostering a thriving renewable energy 
industry.

Related work 
 
Optimizing wind farm layout for maximum efficiency is a complex, multi-faceted challenge, 
driving extensive research into computational optimization algorithms. Among the prevalent 
techniques, Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) has garnered significant attention. But this study 
focuses on Random Search (RS) and Genetic Algorithms (GA). 
 
DTU Wind and Energy Systems has significantly contributed to this field, with studies focusing 
on advanced optimization frameworks that integrate physics-based models with AI techniques 
to enhance layout design, particularly for offshore wind farms. These studies, often involving 
DTU personnel, have aimed to improve the accuracy and efficiency of optimization processes, 
considering factors like wake effects, electrical infrastructure, and cost models.

adaptive mechanisms, remembering and utilizing the information of a good move or accepting 
only movements that improve AEP, which means a ‘Random Move’ step that results in power 
increase. The procedure of the improved RS algorithm is shown in Algorithm 1 [1].

The Weibull distribution is a fundamental statistical tool in wind energy analysis, providing a robust 
model for characterizing wind speed variability. It is defined by two key parameters: the scale 
parameter (c), which relates to the average wind speed, and the shape parameter (k), which describes 
the distribution's form. In wind turbine calculations, the Weibull distribution is crucial for accurately 
estimating the frequency of different wind speeds at a given site. This information is then used to 
determine the wind resource potential, calculate the expected power output of wind turbines, and 
ultimately, assess the economic viability of wind farm projects. By providing a probabilistic 
representation of wind speed patterns, the Weibull distribution enables more reliable and precise wind 
energy assessments.

Optimization algorithms used in the project 
 
2.2.1 Random Search implications on the code 
There is a fitness function (fitness), that evaluates whether the turbines’ positions are adequate or not. If they 
are closer than 5 diameters from each other, a penalty is applied, to penalize the corresponding layout. 
 
# Fitness function 
def fitness(coords): 
    wt_x, wt_y = coords 
    penalty = 0 
    for i in range(WT_Num): 
        x1, y1 = wt_x[i], wt_y[i] 
        for j in range(i + 1, WT_Num): 
            x2, y2 = wt_x[j], wt_y[j] 
            distance = np.sqrt((x2 - x1)**2 + (y2 - y1)**2) 
            if distance <= 5 * 2 * WT_Rad: 
                penalty += -abs(distance - 5 * 2 * WT_Rad) * 100 
    return float(windFarmModel(wt_x, wt_y).aep().sum()) * 1e6 + penalty 
 
The code generates a first set of solutions through RS iterating a number of solutions, with slight movements 
on every WT position, ensuring that every new location gets a better AEP than the previous one. 
 
def generate_initial_population(initial_layout_x, initial_layout_y, 
population_size, border_params, iter_length): 
    population = [(list(initial_layout_x), list(initial_layout_y))] 
    for _ in range(population_size - 1): 
        new_x = [x + iter_length * 0.1 * np.random.uniform(-1, 1) for x in 
initial_layout_x] 
        new_y = [y + iter_length * 0.1 * np.random.uniform(-1, 1) for y in 
initial_layout_y] 
        center_x, center_y, radius = border_params 
        # Ensure new layouts are within the border (simplified for initial 
population) 
        valid_layout_x, valid_layout_y = [], [] 
        for x_i, y_i in zip(new_x, new_y): 
            if filter_points_inside_border(center_x, center_y, radius, (x_i, 
y_i)): 
                valid_layout_x.append(x_i) 
                valid_layout_y.append(y_i) 
            else: 
                # Fallback to original if outside (can be improved)

Improve_Hornsrev1_Layout 
 
The area where the WT locations can move is defined with the most external positions of the actual 
layout, adding 25 additional meters on x and y coordinates. 
In the beginning of the process, the maximum length of every new iteration, the number of Iterations 
(N) and the number of Generations (M) shall be fed in the interface by the user. 
With this model, the code is calculating N iterations to improve the actual solution. At the end of every 
step, the best solution is elected to be the seed of a new generation of improved solutions. Later, all 
these N solutions are ordered from minimum to maximum AEP. 
On a second step, a new generation is analyzed (N Generations are executed by the code). On each 
Generation, the best solution from the previous generation is used as a seed of new N solutions, 
generations with small variations on every WT position (80 turbines in the wind farm). A new set of 
N solutions is redefined. 
Again, all the N solutions on each Generation are ordered from minimum to maximum AEP, electing 
again the last solution, as a seed of the next Generation. 
On every Iteration, all the WT are reallocated; all of them. The approach of the code is to shift all the 
WT positions to get improvements on AEP, making a high number of new slightly modified solutions, 
searching for small improvements. 
To accelerate the process and avoid it being slow, a fitness function is used to evaluate solutions that 
will have best AEP. Although the wake effects are considered by the Py_wake model, the fitness 
function is penalizing solutions with distance between WT less than 5 time the WT Radius. 
The calculations need a huge amount of time, as 80 WT are worked out at the same time.

Due to the algorithm complexity, it this chapter the comparison between processing times with 
different number of cores on the computer. For these calculations a Macbook Air M3 16Gb RAM has 
been used. These processing time calculations and optimizations have been done only with Hornsrev1 
case, as the other two cases have not a high computing demand and it is not useful to focus on them. 
As we are comparing processing times, we are only calculating in this case only 2 Generations. This 
number is enough to compare the computer efficiency executing the code with the different 
alternatives. 
 
In this comparison, the number of iterations (iter_num) is calculated as a function of the number of 
cores. As we have used the Macbook Air M3, with 8 cores, iter_num is 8 for these comparisons. In 
the cases iter_num is established with 3 (Case 1 and 2) or 1 (Case 3). 
 
The original code on Case 3 has been analyzed. A multiprocessing calculation was implemented in 
the code and in the first steps it was expected that with an increased number of cores, the processing 
time would be reduced. This was not the case and in the former code, the multiprocessing memory 
needs were higher than the benefits the multiprocessing grated. It was surprising to realize that fact. 
 
It was clear that increasing the number of cores in the calculations via multiprocessing was not an 
obvious solution to make the code more efficient. Counterintuitively, the higher the number of cores, 
the lower the processing efficiency became and the higher the times needed were. 
 
A different approach was defined. First, it is not worth applying multiprocessing in processes that are 
simple, as the gains of the multiprocessing are less than the extra computing consumption. In this 
different approach, the following tasks were implemented: 
§ 
GetSolutions() and Getgensoltions() were unified in a single function, as they were doing 
almost the same 
§ 
Calculation of the border only once in the whole code 
§ 
Optimization of the distance between WT calculations, to avoid redundancies 
§ 
Avoiding redundant calculations, rearranging all the code 
§ 
Improving the genetic algorithm 
§ 
Changing the functions with highest computing consumption and rearranging them

Conclusions and outlook 
 
Conclusions 
This thesis successfully investigates the offshore wind farm layout optimization problem, 
demonstrating an effective approach to enhancing Annual Energy Production (AEP) by strategically 
relocating wind turbines (WTs). The study's primary objective was to develop and implement a robust 
optimization model capable of improving existing wind farm layouts or creating new, more efficient 
ones from scratch. The core of the methodology lies in a hybrid optimization algorithm that combines 
the principles of Random Search (RS) and Genetic Algorithms (GA). 
Key Findings 
The research presented in this thesis yielded several significant conclusions, validated through three 
distinct case studies: 
• 
Improved AEP through a Hybrid Algorithm: The developed algorithm, which iteratively 
adjusts the position of individual WTs and accepts only those movements that increase the 
total AEP, proved to be highly effective. The initial set of solutions is based on an RS 
algorithm, followed by a GA approach that uses the best solution from a previous generation 
as a seed for the next. 
• 
Case I: Optimization from Scratch: When applied to a small, non-optimized wind farm 
with 15 WTs in a triangular configuration, using 100 generations and 3 iterations, the 
model demonstrated its ability to dramatically improve the layout. The algorithm 
successfully moved the WTs to minimize wake effects, resulting in a substantial increase in 
AEP from 32.7402 GWh to 134.4021 GWh, an improvement of over 75%. 
• 
Case II: Impact of Boundary Conditions: The study on the same small wind farm with a 
circular border, also using 100 generations and 3 iterations, revealed that the size and shape 
of the optimization area significantly affect the outcome. A larger border allowed for a faster 
stabilization of the model and yielded a higher optimal AEP compared to the polygonal 
border in the first case. 
• 
Case III: Optimizing an Existing Layout: The model was tested on the well-studied Horns 
Rev 1 wind farm with 80 turbines, using 1 iteration and 40 iterations. This case showed 
that the algorithm is capable of fine-tuning already-optimized layouts. As anticipated, the 
improvements were marginal but still notable, demonstrating the model's precision. The AEP 
increased from a reference of 664.9653 GWh to a final value of 670.3601 GWh. This result 
confirms that the algorithm is capable of fine-tuning already-optimized layouts, which is 
crucial given the high costs associated with offshore installations. 
• 
Case IV: The Influence of Core Usage of the Multiprocessor: This case study explored the 
impact of computational resources on the optimization process, specifically using the Horns 
Rev 1 wind farm. The study found that running the model on multiple cores significantly 
reduced the processing time compared to using a single core, demonstrating the model's 
parallelization potential and its scalability for industrial-level computations.

Methodological Considerations and Implications 
The thesis successfully utilized the open-source software PyWake for all wind, power, and wake effect 
calculations, which was a practical choice given the four-month timeframe for the project. The 
modularity of PyWake, while a strength, also highlights the importance of transparently reporting the 
specific components used to ensure reproducibility. The use of other AI tools such as Google Gemini, 
ChatGPT, and CoPilot for assistance with simple Python tasks was also noted as a key factor in the 
project's efficiency. 
Limitations and Future Work 
While this study provides a robust and functional optimization tool, there are inherent limitations and 
areas for future research. The current model relies on a fixed number of iterations and generations, 
which could be improved with more dynamic convergence criteria. Additionally, the algorithm's 
greedy approach of only accepting AEP-increasing movements might lead to a local optimum. Future 
work could explore: 
• 
Advanced Algorithm Integration: Incorporating more sophisticated algorithms or further 
refining the hybrid GA-RS approach to better navigate the solution space and avoid local 
optima. 
• 
Multi-objective Optimization: Extending the model to include other optimization criteria 
beyond AEP, such as cost of infrastructure, environmental impact, or grid stability. 
• 
Scalability: Applying the model to larger and more complex wind farm layouts to assess its 
performance and computational efficiency at an industrial scale, particularly with parallel
