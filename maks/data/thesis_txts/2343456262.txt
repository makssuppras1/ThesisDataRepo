Abstract 
Product Development in Crowdfunding as a business model for entrepreneurs and early-stage start-ups 
lacking financial resources is a growing trend. It can act as a snappy, ubiquitous and low-risk funding 
tool to help organisations to kick-start their ventures, while testing their products/services on the 
market. Nonetheless, it is a relatively new phenomenon that has caught media and researchers’ 
attention due to its macroeconomic potential, and therefore, is still under the investigators’ 
microscopes. Current research efforts seem to focus on project-level related parameters and its 
relationship with project success without paying attention to the intrinsic characteristics of videos used 
in crowdfunding campaigns. This Thesis aims to investigate in the unexplored field of videography in 
crowdfunding by developing a statistical model, which is able to explain the correlation of a set of 
audio-visual parameters in terms of project success.  
 
To do so, this study starts with a thorough description of crowdfunding and its four main models. It is 
followed by an explanation of its potential benefits, both from an entrepreneurial and from a 
macroeconomic perspective.  
Next, a statement of the motives that led the author to choose this Thesis’ topic, together with an 
explanation of the scoping of the same, are provided. It follows the definition of the intended research 
questions to be answered and the formulated hypotheses to be tested, as well as a summary of the 
extant literature related to the topic. After this, a description of the followed methodology (web 
scraping, video analysis and statistical analysis) and an explanation of the chosen parameters are 
provided.  
Finally, the results of the overall investigation are being presented together with a discussion where the 
author explains the meaning of the statistical model’s output. The report closes with a set of 
conclusions, reflections and recommendations for future work. 
 
The results of this investigation confirm that video, as a way to introduce a product to potential 
consumers, is a key factor for achieving success in crowdfunding platforms. Furthermore, among all 
the studied parameters concerning the video qualities of a video campaign, the results show that 
showcasing the product, presenting scenes of the different uses of the product by using actors, and 
showing a 3D render of the product, can have a significant impact on project success likelihood.  
On the contrary, other visual elements that may appear to be linked with project success, such as public 
endorsement of celebrities, experts, or institutions, do not seem to have a significant correlation. These 
results can help entrepreneurs and start-ups to create or adjust their video-campaign strategies and align 
them with their budgets in order to concentrate their efforts on those parameters that seem to have a 
significant influence on project success.

Introduction 
What is crowdfunding? 
 
Crowdfunding (’kraʊdfʌndɪŋ): The practice of funding a project or venture by raising money from a 
large number of people who each contribute a relatively small amount, typically via the Internet.1 
 
 
Crowdfunding is nowhere near a new thing; the word itself might sound new, and needless to say, it is 
a huge trend in many social and economic circles that claim crowdfunding as the philosopher’s stone of 
new-age entrepreneurs. The fact is that crowdfunding is a new term for an old way of bringing 
resourceless projects to life. 
 
What do artists and scientists like Leonardo Da Vinci or Galileo Galilei, the Catholic Church and many 
forms of governments have in common? They all are (or have been) somehow crowdfunded. 
 
From the medieval and Renaissance ages in Europe through the feudal Japan, to certain southern 
kingdoms of Asia, patronage has been an ancient social institution created to develop technological or 
artistic projects in a time where the economical conjuncture did not allow technologists and artists to 
freely innovate. Consequently, aristocrats and wealthy individuals/groups were the ones with enough 
resources to invest in talent. Occasionally, these backers joined forces in order to fund more ambitious 
projects, in other terms, they grouped in a joint venture to develop a “jet-set” crowdfunded project. In 
connection, historical organisations like the Catholic Church and other religions are and have been 
crowdfunded by their parishioners by using a donation and/or alms fund system since ancient times.  
Nevertheless, crowdfunding’s definition does not implicitly specify whether the backers are willingly 
supporting a project or their contribution is actually imposed by a powerful entity. In that sense, it can 
be argued that taxation systems of many kinds of governments (locals and nationals) are, in fact, being 
crowdfunded. Here, taxpayers are the ones funding the government’s project portfolio (education, 
public health system, infrastructures, etc.). In return, contributors expect a set of social benefits.  
 
Either way, the principle of crowdfunding as a resource facilitator for projects, can be found in many 
social and economical models. However, what is really new in crowdfunding is the use of the Internet 
as a mean to provide easiness and freedom when selecting the projects to invest in. This is especially 
interesting in the case of marketers, entrepreneurs and established companies with focus on product 
development. Crowdfunding brings to these companies a unique tool to provide consumers with the 
power to decide what it needs to be developed and produced, this is, the purest form of market-pull for 
a B2C (Business-to-Consumer) market.

How it works? 
Crowdfunding has its own specific vocabulary when referring to the different stakeholders involved in 
project campaigns. In order to establish a common language when reading this document, the most 
important elements are being described below: 
 
• 
Project initiator, project creator or founder: The person or organisation that creates a 
crowdfunding project. 
• 
Backers or funders: The ones supporting the project with their own money. 
• 
Rewards or perks: The different outcomes that the backers can receive in exchange for their 
monetary contribution. These can be material (product or service) or immaterial (digital rewards 
like pictures, videos, e-books, podcasts, songs, etc.).  
 
There are different types of crowdfunding models that can be identified depending on what the 
backers/funders receive in return for their contribution (Belleflamme, Lambert and Schwienbacher, 
2014). The main four models are being described: 
 
• 
Equity-based. Founders trade a variable percentage of their company’s equity stakes for a given 
amount of capital. This form of crowdfunding is the natural evolution of the classic private 
investment-financing model Seed Funding (Business angels, Family, Friends and Fools, etc.). 
• 
Lending-based. Entrepreneurs can find an alternative mechanism to raise funds for their 
ventures, with lower lending interests but also with shorter timespans than traditional bank loans. 
It is also called peer-to-peer and peer-to-business loans. 
• 
Donation-based. The objective of this model can be either philanthropic or for sponsorship 
purposes. The backers do not expect any material reward for their contribution. It is also called 
patronage model. 
• 
Reward-based. A product or service is rewarded for those who backed the project. It can be 
seen as an early-bird e-commerce model, where the backers bet for the projects that they would 
like to bring to life. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Note: In this study, the author will be mainly focusing on the latest one: reward-based crowdfunding 
model. As it will be explained later, this model can act as a springboard platform for 
entrepreneurs/start-ups focusing on product development. From now on, this document will refer to the 
reward-based model when talking about crowdfunding in general, unless otherwise stated.

Crowdfunding’s operational principle is simple: A project initiator or founder creates an online 
campaign in a given crowdfunding platform by uploading a description, pictures and/or videos 
explaining the project/product. A goal, a deadline and a set of perks/rewards are then defined. During 
the timespan of the campaign, the backers or funders make an investment/donation/pre-purchasing of 
the project according to the available rewards. If the project reaches the goal once the time is over, the 
collected sum of money (which can be bigger than the initial goal) is then transferred to the project 
creator (after deducting platform’s fees). Some platforms allow their users to receive whatever funds 
they have collected, regardless of whether they have succeeded on reaching their goal or not.

Even that the traditional acceptance of crowdfunding (and the most obvious) for the general public is to 
act as a low-cost fund-raising platform for new ventures (Cunningham, 2012), the fact is that, in the 
right hands, crowdfunding can be used for many purposes: 
 
• 
Marketing catalyser: It is an effective low-cost tool to gather public interest attention before 
launching new products (Steinberg, 2012). Crowdfunding platforms have a strong backers 
community (Kickstarter has over 11 million registered backers, followed by Indiegogo with 
over 10 million registered backers). Furthermore, these platforms hold a strong position in 
terms of Social Media’s presence (Facebook, Twitter, etc.), which help to attract new potential 
customers. At the same time, the supporting backers become “network evangelists”, helping 
with the promotion of the campaign using their own Social Networks’ channels in their role of 
built-in marketing community (Hemer, 2011). 
• 
Community creator: By supporting a product that has not yet been produced, backers engage 
themselves in a tight relationship with project creators. Backers want the product to succeed as 
they have a personal interest in doing so. Therefore, they can also be incorporated into the 
product development phase, providing valuable insights, ideas or even skills to the project. If 
properly managed, this set of backers can turn into a valuable community of users, which in 
turn, can lead to new product propositions and create brand attachment.  
• 
Market tester and validator: It acts as an early-stage idea test and validation tool at almost 
zero costs and with few resources (a video pitch and a prototype can be enough to test the 
market) (Steinberg, 2012). 
• 
Start-up booster: Capital obtained from the pre-selling phase of a new product can be 
directly invested in R&D, production and marketing expenses (Steinberg, 2012), thus, 
accelerating time-to-market while keeping a cost-reduction approach.  
• 
Risk-reducer: In the case of unsuccessful campaigns, entrepreneurs can fall quickly without 
loosing much, rather than time and public image (Steinberg, 2012). 
 
At the expenses of the crowdfunding model’s success, a vast ecosystem of services relating to it has 
emerged. Its aim is to provide project initiators with the tools to increase the performance of their 
campaigns and to help manage their after-campaigns. Some examples follow below: 
 
• 
Dragoninnovation.com, Berkelysg.com, Hax.co: Companies that assist founders during the 
prototyping and/or production phases. 
• 
Backerkit.com: Company that helps gathering and managing data from backers (for logistics 
purposes). 
• 
Kicktraq.com: Online predictive analytics tools. 
• 
Backercamp.com: Marketing and PR firm with focus on crowdfunding-related companies. 
• 
Kickbooster.me: Company offering discounts to backers who share the project campaign.  
• 
Backerclub.co, Backershub.com: Organized communities of backers to get group discounts 
and pre-launching information.

Why is it important? 
Crowdfunding as a whole is a rapidly growing industry. According to the Crowdfunding Industry 
report (source: Massolution.com) crowdfunding reached 34 Billion USD fundraising volume globally 
in 2015 and it is projected to double this figure by 2016. It is also estimated that, around 2000 different 
crowdfunding platforms coexist nowadays. Each platform has a different approach: type of 
crowdfunding model, national/international market, policies, targeted customer segments, etc. There 
are platforms of all shapes and sizes: For artists, non-profit projects, musicians, real-states, money 
lending, etc.  
 
Looking through the regulatory lens, the JOBS act (Jumpstart Our Business Startups), a US federal law 
approved back in 2012, aims to regularize the crowdfunding industry in order to promote 
entrepreneurship. This is done by endowing companies with the legal mechanisms to fund themselves 
by attracting equity capital from the crowd (equity-based crowdfunding). By doing this, the US 
government recognises this economical phenomenon as a highly potential economy booster tool.  
By contrast, in Europe, significant regularisation approaches have been implemented, in this case, at a 
national level. In this sense, the European Union tried to gather a harmonizing directive by developing 
the Alternative Investment Fund Manager (AIFM) Directive 2011/61/EU (2011/61/EU Directive), 
which entered into force on July 2013. The directive establishes that AIMFs constituted in EU, (for 
instance: crowdfunding provision services), will need authorization to operate and will be subjected to 
detailed rules concerning transparency, remuneration, conduct of business and leverage limitations.  
In terms of reward-based crowdfunding model, little regularisation has been developed; instead, e-
commerce rules apply, which is widely used in nowadays-economic system. 
 
These governmental recognitions, together with crowdfunding’s economical trend prediction, make 
this phenomenon a potential macro and microeconomic tool that needs to be further explored, 
especially for entrepreneurs and SMEs. Traditional means for funding (Business Angels, banks, 
venture capital, etc.) do not allow a democratisation of the funding necessities for companies, as 
potential investors are usually limited in number due to geographical limitation. In this regard, equity-
based crowdfunding can be the panacea for many start-ups with funding needs: the investors’ market is 
now global and instantaneous. 
 
Nonetheless, the reward-based crowdfunding model also opens the door to a new way of doing 
business for product development companies. These organisations have now free access to a global 
market that can provide the necessary liquidity to kick-start the development of their products with 
very low financial risk as they only produce what they have already sold. This new business model has, 
furthermore, a positive impact on sustainability: Manufacturers do not have to produce more than what 
is exactly demanded, with the consequent saving on resources.

Motivation 
Since only 44% of campaigns are eventually successfully funded, it is very important for project 
creators to know their success probability as soon as possible, thus, being able to react accordingly 
(Etter, Grossglauser and Thiran, 2014). 
 
The main objective of this Thesis is to perform a statistical analysis over a set of projects launched in a 
large crowdfunding platform. The result aims to help project initiators, entrepreneurs, marketers, etc. to 
better estimate the likelihood of their projects becoming successful by selecting the factors that can 
better influence their campaigns. By doing this, this paper intends to contribute to the knowledge pool 
of research on business development, innovation, and entrepreneurship in the field of crowdfunding.  
 
The secondary objective is to help the author understand what crowdfunding is and how to use it to 
create a sustainable business model out of it. To do so, together with the present paper, a short self-
produced video follows: it documents the design of a business model intended for product development 
start-ups mainly focused on technological products.  
 
Research in the field of crowdfunding is firmly progressing, however, little has been studied on how 
videography can influence the likelihood of a project becoming successful. Current investigation of 
crowdfunding’s paradigm seems to focus on the relationship between social networks, media richness, 
the intrinsic parameters of the project itself (goal, timespan, amount of the rewards, etc.), and project 
success. By contrast, the study of video resources as a powerful tool for increasing project acceptance 
falls behind researchers’ agendas. The use of media in video format lowers the feeling of uncertainty 
and unsureness against project initiators (Egger, 2001, Fogg et al. 2001), this is, by rising trustiness 
over consumers in a very uncertain market. Nevertheless, very few studies have gone deeper into the 
effectiveness of the different visual resources available for crowdfunding projects. It is in this field 
where the author aims to explore the possibilities of videography as a trust-establisher and a marketing 
tool.

Scope 
The focus of this study is to analyse the reward-based crowdfunding model in terms of product 
development. To do so, the author has narrowed down the study scope by limiting the research to only 
one crowdfunding platform: Kickstarter.com. Due to time and technical limitations, it has not been 
possible to program web scraping scripts for more than one platform, as each one has its own specific 
configuration. Moreover, the author is mostly interested in Kickstarter as it is one of the current big 
players of this industry, and also because its restrictive policy “all-in or nothing”, where the founder 
needs to reach the goal before the deadline in order to keep the money. This crowdfunding policy is the 
most beneficial for companies testing new products in the market: In general, for a product 
development company launching a crowdfunding campaign for a given product, if a minimum amount 
of customers is not reached, it is not economically wise to be further developed. This campaign failure 
can show a weak product value for customers, a bad marketing campaign or a combination of both.    
 
In order to maintain focus on product development, a selection of Kickstarter’s categories had to be 
made. Kickstarter organises its projects in 15 main categories: Art, Comics, Crafts, Dance, Design, 
Fashion, Film & Video, Food, Games, Journalism, Music, Photography, Publishing, Technology, and 
Theater. The author has selected the projects contained only in the Kickstarter categories of 
Technology and Design. These groups are the ones mainly focused on tangible physical products, 
rather than artistic creations. Statistics show that Kickstarter (Kickstarter stats), which has pledged 
more than 2,4 Billion USD with 106.000 successfully-funded projects since it was launched back in 
2008, have Technology and Design as the 2nd and 3rd most backed categories, with 455 Million USD 
and 453 Million USD respectively, just after Games category (501 Million USD).

Theoretical background 
Crowdfunding’s related academic literature has traditionally focused on two main research lines: 
Intrinsic characteristics of crowdfunding projects and communication-related issues. 
 
Assessing the relationship between inherent parameters of the project itself has been the first step for 
developing predictive models of success in crowdfunding. For instance, the project description depth, 
the number of updates of the campaign (Xu et al., 2014), project description, the provision of videos or 
the requested amount of money (Mollick, 2014), have been studied, showing a strong correlation of 
these with campaign success. Meanwhile, Greenberg et al. (Greenberg, Hariharan, Gerber and Pardo, 
2013), proposed a Kickstarter predictive model based on attributes available at the launch of a 
campaign: funding goal, project category, number of rewards, length of the project, video presence, etc. 
Greenberg’s model reaches 68% accuracy on predicting project success.  
 
Later on, researchers have also focused on evaluating the impact of communication theory in 
crowdfunding. In this regard, many studies have performed analysis on how the different social media, 
communication channels, richness of the media, etc. can affect backers’ willingness to support a given 
project. Beier and Wagner (2015), observe a direct correlation between on-site communication richness 
and the success probability by developing a statistical analysis of a 740 projects dataset from a Swiss 
crowdfunding platform. The outcome suggests that projects with more media richness and more 
frequent information exchange with the backers have a bigger chance to become successful rather than 
off-site communication (through social media). It also advises that the use of video to showcase the 
project can raise purchase intention of potential customers, bringing a higher amount of donations. 
 
Similarly, Koch and Siering (2015) performed an investigation from a founder perspective on 
Kickstarter. Similar results were achieved: Impact of videos are highly relevant for project success 
(p<0.01), whereas the number of images showed a 10% level of significance. Moreover, researchers 
observed that founders who have been active in the platform prior to project launching (by backing 
other projects) showed a more positive impact on backers rather than the number of projects that 
funders had created in the past. Etter, Grossglauser and Thiran  (2014), perform a mixed analysis on 
both predictors based on the amount of money pledged, and predictors using social features. The 
resulting model achieved an accuracy of more than 76% when predicting project success on 
Kickstarter, only 4 hours after the launch of a campaign.  
 
Relating to communication theory, videography as a marketing tool designed to catch consumer 
attention and interest, has been widely studied since the irruption of TV commercials back in 1950’s. In 
nowadays’ society of audio-visual consumption, the use of video format in online marketing has 
revealed itself as an effective tool to impact the emotional responses of consumers. Marketers now 
have more channels to reach their audience with a lower marketing budget. 
==PAGE:15==
 
9 
In this evolving field, Park and Hopkins (1993), showed evidences that dynamic visual display of 
information is more effective than static forms of communications (image, text, etc.). Similarly, Jiang 
and Benasat (2007) find that websites with video-format content are perceived as more useful by 
visitors rather than picture-format only. They also argue that video format catches individuals’ 
attention due to dynamic scene changes and sound effects. By relating these studies to crowdfunding, it 
can be explained why visitors’ project acceptance is increased after a video-campaign presentation. The 
visitors’ familiarity or empathy towards the project content is boosted by the video presentation. It 
evokes trustiness towards project creators. If video is not present, visitors do not even try to gain the 
needed information to decide on whether funding the project or not, as they need to assure that the 
project creators are trustful and/or engaged towards the project (Koch and Siering, 2015). 
 
In a deeper level, marketers and researchers have tried to find how can an audio-visual design evoke 
the emotions, feelings, reactions, etc. that make the difference in terms of customer’s purchasing 
decision. In this sense, Teixeira, Wedel and Pieters (2011), find out that videos emphasizing surprise 
and joy can concentrate attention and retain viewers in a more effective way than other emotions.  
Hasson et al. (2008), describe the different techniques used to influence viewers by exerting 
considerable control over brain activity and eye movements. Results show that the use of certain 
directing styles, editing or movie content, can modulate viewers’ attention.  
 
Regarding to videography in crowdfunding, Liu et al. (2014) analyse the evoked emotions resulting 
from the watching of crowdfunding video campaigns as a whole, distinguishing between two types of 
video-campaign approaches: focus on campaign creators and their product-building journey, or focus 
on emphasizing customers’ utility and features. The results show that those campaigns emphasizing 
creators’ journey are more willing to draw higher donations, whereas those emphasizing product 
qualities are more likely to increase pre-order intentions. Nevertheless, it has not been possible to find 
any paper studying the effects of different visual elements on crowdfunding project success. 
 
To summarize, previous research followed two differentiated but adjacent crowdfunding’s fields of 
focus: communication theory and inherent project characteristics. However, even though both 
approaches are important to uncover the Holy Grail of crowdfunding’s success; it needs to be 
addressed from a holistic perspective. Consequently, this research aims to go into more depth in the 
videography branch, in order to contribute to the understanding of the crowdfunding phenomenon from 
a brand new approach.

Research questions 
Since video-format is a more digestible source of information for consumers, this is, inflicting a better 
emotional response than static information; backers can be better influenced by using more visual 
communications forms rather than just static descriptions (Koch and Siering, 2015). In this regard, the 
author hypothesizes that those projects containing a video (presenting the project or product) are more 
likely to succeed than those that do not have a video campaign and are solely based on static 
descriptions. 
 
Research Question 1: To what extent can a video campaign help a crowdfunding project to become 
successful?  
 
From here, a set of subquestions can be raised: 
Subquestion 1.1: What visual elements/resources included in a video campaign, can better influence 
the success probability of a crowdfunded project? 
Subquestion 1.2: What visual elements/resources included in a video campaign can have a negative 
impact on project success? 
 
Endorsement as a tool to influence buyers’ purchase decision by inflicting a psychological reaction 
towards certain social or technical entities (celebrities, experts, institutions…), which have an 
ostensibly better knowledge and/or expertise on the topic than consumers, has been widely used in 
advertisement. Consumers’ knowledge and/or expertise are usually limited. These expert endorsement 
entities (which consumers believe to have a better knowledge or expertise on the matter) 
subconsciously influence their purchasing decision. Hence, consumers try to minimize risk perception 
by transferring the responsibility of dealing with the technical aspects of the product by delegating it to 
recognized figures. Here, the author wants to test if there is any form of endorsement that can influence 
the consumers, this is, by boosting campaign’s effectiveness.  
 
Research Question 2: Can endorsement help a crowdfunded project to succeed? 
 
A set of subquestions concerning this topic follows: 
Subquestion 2.1: Which endorsement types relating to video campaigns can have a higher impact on 
project success? 
Subquestion 2.2: Can the crowdfunding platforms’ own endorsement influence on project success?

Methods 
In order to develop a robust predictive model of crowdfunding projects’ success rate, a set of valuable 
variables needed to be gathered and analysed. For that, three main methods were used: Web scraping, 
video analysis and statistical analysis. Web scraping and video analysis were used to provide relevant 
variables to feed the statistical model performed in the statistical analysis method. An explanation of all 
three methods is being presented in the following sections. 
Web scraping 
Web harvesting, web data extraction, or simply web scraping is a computational software technique 
developed to automatically extract and collect data from websites. In essence, it is a form of data 
mining process that allows the user to extract structured information from 3rd parties URLs when a 
public interface is not available.  
 
Kickstarter, like most of current crowdfunding platforms, does not allow a public indexation of its 
failed projects. As an organisation devoted to host crowdfunding projects, Kickstarter’s goal is to 
achieve the maximum number of successful projects as possible, mainly because Kickstarter gets a 5% 
fee of the total funds raised of every successful project (apart from a 3% + 0,20 USD per pledge on 
payment processment fees). It becomes clear why Kickstarter bases its marketing strategy on flaunting 
successful projects while trying to hide failures.  
Nevertheless, it is possible to access all the projects (successful and failed) if one has the exact project 
URL. To overcome this issue and in order to obtain a substantial sample size, the author developed a 
C# bot to automatically send a .JSON (JavaScript Object Notation) query to Kickstarter.com every 30 
minutes asking for Technology and Design projects that were about to finish. This script (named as 
ProjectCollector ) collected queries’ responses from the 12th of March to the 12th of April of 2016. The 
output was then stored in a .csv (Comma Separated Values) file containing:  
 
• 
UID: In order to keep score of the number of collected projects, each project was linked to a 
Unique Identification number starting from 1, and up to 1.000. 
• 
Project ID: Kickstarter own internal identification number per project. 
• 
Project Title: Title of the project. Named by its creator. 
•

The second method used to find more parameters to feed the statistical model was a manual video 
analysis.  
After gathering 1.000 projects’ data from the web scraping method, a list of 716 projects containing a 
video URL (384 projects did not have any video), resulted.  
Many methods have been developed in order to evaluate the impact of commercials on consumers: 
observations of reactions, interviews, content analysis, codification, etc.  
Kong, Zhao and Hu (2013), developed an electronic evaluation for assessing the impression index of 
video commercials by using EEG (Electroencephalography) activity. For this method, the author 
performed a manual video analysis by watching every campaign video and writing down the value of 
the studied variables in an excel sheet, this is, by observing the presence of certain parameters in the 
different video scenes.  
 
In order to find the most relevant factors for the study, a total of 22 variables were defined based on 
extant literature related to communication theory, marketing, advertisement, psychology, musicology, 
videography and neurocinematics.

Length: The first parameter is the actual length of the video in seconds. Even though any article 
was found linking the duration of a video advertisement with the impact on the viewers, the 
author wanted to know what is the average length of successful projects, and the average on 
failed/cancelled projects. Its purpose is to observe if there is any correlation between the length 
of the video and the amount of information that it transmits to the viewer. As the project success 
is strongly linked to the amount of trust that the product, project or project creator emanates 
towards the potential customer. The author’s hypothesis is that it can be predicted that longer 
videos have more time to earn the viewer’s trust until a certain length, were it starts to become 
boring for the audience. 
Values:

Music: It is well known that music is a powerful stimulus for affecting moods. Extensive 
research has been conducted in the field of marketing to decipher what parameters of music can 
affect consumers’ attention, purchasing intention, product recalling, etc.  
Based on Bruner’s (1990) article, the author decided to evaluate the two easiest variables to be 
rapidly measured in a given song: mode and tempo.  
 
o 
Mode: It refers to the arrangement of the scales played in a song. In contemporary Western 
music, two main modes exist: minor and major. In technical terms, a piece is in minor scale if 
the scale is only a half step between the second and the third tones compared to the major 
scale, where the difference between third and fourth note and between the seventh and the 
eighth note is half. A much simpler description would be that music in the major mode 
expresses more animated and positive feelings than music in the minor mode (Bruner, 1990).  
In a study concerning music in relation with commercials (Stout and Leckenby, 1988), it was 
found that mode is the music component with a higher impact on consumers. Respondents 
indicated that they made a more personal connection with advertisements where the music 
was using major or mixed modes rather than a minor mode. In this sense, ads with music in 
minor mode were less favourable to respondents and were rated as more irritating.  
In order to evaluate the impact of this parameter in relationship to crowdfunding projects, the 
author (based on his own music skills as a musician), assessed the mode of the different 
songs of the videos. 
Values: 
0 – Minor mode 
1 – Major mode 
2 – Mix of modes

Celebrity: A Celebrity can be defined as a recognizable person or authority that use this

and marketers use this to transfer celebrity’s image to the endorsed products. Some of the

Endorsement ads have a stronger memory trace (McCracken, 1989), they can help a product

Expert: An expert can be defined as a person who has a deep knowledge or expertise in a 
certain field. Experts have been widely used in advertising in order to inflict a persuasive 
effect on audience in terms of purchasing intention. This ‘expert power’, which is well 
documented, is based on the idea that people will believe someone’s opinion who is assumed 
to have an extensive knowledge in a given matter (French and Raven, 1960), as it enhances 
the believability of an advertisement mainly due to augmented source credibility (Maddux 
and Rogers, 1980). Hovland and Kelley, 1953 conducted a study to demonstrate that the 
attribution of a communication to either a prestigious or a non-prestigious source could 
influence the target’s evaluation of the communication. The results show that, in the same 
communication (a prestigious expert vs. an average communicator), the expert 
communication resulted to be more effective and influential (Fennis and Stroebe, 2010).  
Klucharev and Smidts (2008) worked on how expert power can modulate memory and 
attitude of consumers. The study shows that a higher purchase intention was found when an 
expert-celebrity presented products rather than when presented by a non-expert. It also 
demonstrates that viewers showed a better memory for objects presented by experts than by 
non-experts (experts increased the probability of an object recognition by 6,2%).  
Since certain uncertainty is involved in a product purchase scenario (due to the asymmetric 
information flow between buyer and seller), there is always a perceived risk for consumers

internalization (expert) works better in reducing consumers’ purchasing risk attitude for high

effect is magnified when the consumer has a high knowledge on the product’s field.

Social mimicry: Social mimicry refers to the act of representing everyday life scenes performed 
by actors while consuming the advertised product in different ways. These representations of 
personal situations aim to mimic different social groups. These can be easily identifiable by the 
watchers in order to create certain empathy or role-taking behaviour between the product or the 
project and the potential consumers, thus creating an emotional binding. By being mimicked, 
consumers create a feeling of rapport, affiliation and closeness towards the mimicker (Lakin et 
al., 2003).  
According to Solomon (1983), products have embedded symbolic qualities that are often 
determinants of product evaluation and adoption. This inherent symbolism is transferred to 
individuals, and in turn, individuals are evaluated and placed in a social context (to a significant 
==PAGE:29==
 
23 
degree) by the products surrounding them. This reflexive evaluation implies that product 
symbolism is used to assign meaning to others. Similarly, individuals also use it in order to 
create a social identity, as the consumer’s self-concept is based on others’ responses perceptions. 
Hence, the use of social mimicry in advertising can emphasize the product’s value by optimizing 
the performance of social roles. By this, it can contribute to the consumer’s self-evaluation 
projection, and lately, connecting the product with its social identity. 
Another use for these product’s utilisation representations is to help consumers to imagine a set 
of usage scenarios beyond the intended function. For instance, a video showing a music, 
photography, or sport device intended for professional use, can also represent alternative uses for 
other social groups not being directly related with the intended category, thus, creating new 
potential market segments.  
Values:

Warmth vs. competence: This parameter is based on the well-established Stereotype Content 
Model (Fiske, Cuddy, Glick and Xu, 2002). This model states that people’s perception of 
individuals or groups can be measured in two dimensions: Warmth, representing whether an 
individual or group has positive intentions towards an individual; and Competence, representing 
whether this person or group is able to execute these intentions. Recent research links this 
judgement with the one that consumers perform on brands. In this sense, consumers tend to 
identify with brands when these have a warm (vs. cold) personality (Stokburger-Sauer et al., 
2012) and a brand’s symbolic value. Linking this study with Liu et al., (2014)’s paper on video-
evoked perspective on crowdfunding platforms, it can be extrapolated that video campaigns 
emphasizing campaign creators and their project development journey can be identified as a 
warm organisation, whereas video campaigns where the emphasises is on customer’s utility and 
benefits of the project can be linked to a competent organisation. According to Liu et al. (2014) 
the videos showing the team’s journey are more likely to draw higher donations rather than the 
more competent, as they tend to increase pre-order intentions. (Liu et al., 2014).  
For this variable, the author’s intention was to test if a warm video can attract more backers than 
a competence video campaign, as the extant literature states.  
Values:

Statistical Analysis 
The final method used, and the one that provides tangible results is the statistical analysis. In order to 
determine the correlation of the given data, the author statistically analysed the resulting dataset using 
SPSS (IBM) software.  
 
A binary lineal regression was performed in order to find the parameters that have a positive influence 
on the dependent variable, this is, project status. Even though this variable can have 3 different values 
(successful, cancelled and failed), the author grouped its outcomes in two values: non-successful (0) 
and successful (1), with 71,5% of the sample valued as 0 and 28,5% valued as 1. By doing this, the 
dependent variable turns from a categorical to a binary variable, resulting in a more straightforward 
interpretation.   
 
Some of the variables were modelled to fit the analysis. It is the case of the social media account 
(SMAccount) variable, which becomes “1” if there is at least one social media link on the project 
description (Twitter, Facebook or Instagram), else, “0” if no social media network link is present. 
Funding goal and Pledged variables were converted to USD (using the market value of exchange rate 
on 21st June 2016) in order to unify the different currencies. Later, the Funding goal variable was 
transformed into its logarithmic notation (base e) to fit it with the dataset range.  
 
Lineal regression is a recurrent tool used in social science for modelling the correlation between a 
dependent variable and one or more independent variables or predictors. In this case, it is being used to 
quantify the relationship’s strength between the given data, providing us with a sufficiently robust 
model to test the proposed hypothesis. 
 
For this method, the author developed a 2-rounds regression analysis. In the first model, only the most 
important project-level variables were included in order to test its robustness. In the second model, the 
project-level variables were analysed together with the variables regarding video features. In order to 
ensure that the models fitted well, a Hosmer-Lemershow test was performed. It is a commonly tool 
used for assessing the goodness of fit of a logistic regression model.  
Results are being described in the next chapter.

Discussion 
The results of this empirical study clearly confirm the author’s hypothesis about the importance of 
video campaigning for achieving higher probabilities of project success by using video format as a 
window to efficiently attract potential funders rather than just using static communications channels 
(text/images). Answering to the first research question on “to what extent can video help a 
crowdfunding project to become successful”: from the given dataset, a total of 285 projects achieved 
success, from these, 262 contained a video campaign (92,28% of successful projects). Combining this 
with the regression analysis result of the video parameter (video campaign present/no present) 
correlated with success (β = 1,172, p < 0,01), it can be concluded that video campaign is one of the key 
success factors when crowdfunding a product.  
 
As it can be observed that even amateur-recorded video campaigns have a significant likelihood of 
success (31,30% of all successful projects containing video campaign were recorded by non-
professional directors/editors, table 11). With current low prices of video cameras, DSLRs (Reflex 
cameras) or even smartphones cameras, creating a video campaign is an inexpensive option for 
entrepreneurs and marketers to boost their project success’ likelihood.  
 
Referring to the subquestions 1.1 and 1.2, on “what visual elements / video resources can have a 
positive or negative impact on project success”: Among the different visual resources analysed, the 
product showcase seems to have a higher impact on project backers supporting a project. Even that 
showing the actual product you are trying to sell may seem an obvious choice, it is not the general case: 
from the acquired sample only 563 out of 716 projects (78,63%) containing a video campaign (table 
11) used this resource. It is therefore a simple lesson that project creators must learn: always showcase 
your product. 
 
Similarly, the Social Mimicry resource, this is, showing scenes of different actors using the product, 
have shown a significant impact on project success. In this case, the Social Mimicry scenes seem to be 
undervalued by project creators in the given dataset. As low as 195 out of 716 projects (27,23%) 
containing a video campaign have used this technique to attract customers, whereas 37,02% of these 
succeeded (table 11). It is clear that hiring actors to perform on a professional video is expensive for 
start-ups, but since the majority of these scenes are recorded without dialogues, the use of the project 
team or friends/family can be a low-cost solution. 
 
The last visual resource that seems to have a positive impact on project success is the use of 3D renders 
to showcase the product in a more technical way. It is also an inexpensive technique that can be used to 
make potential customers gain trust over the technical capabilities of the project team.  
On the other hand, the use of text in video campaigning as a visual resource to help viewers to capture 
more attention seems to have a negative effect towards success. It can be argued that viewers prefer 
cleaner videos, in terms of information saturation.  
==PAGE:42==
 
36 
In terms of the power of endorsement and to answer the second research question on if endorsement 
can help a project to succeed: The conducted research shows that the own-platform endorsement 
(Kickstarter endorsement) has a very strong influence on project success (β = 2,837, p > 0,01). For the 
given dataset (table 7), Kickstarter endorsed a total of 66 projects. 58 of which were successful 
(87,88%). The average percentage of pledged funds for projects not being endorsed by Kickstarter 
(both successful and non-successful) is 123%. By looking at the same parameter but only for the 
projects endorsed by the platform, this number achieves an astonishing 478%. The question to be raised 
is: Do these projects have success because Kickstarter endorses them, or does Kickstarter only endorse 
the “winning horses”, meaning, projects that they predict that will become really successful?  
It seems reasonable that Kickstarter has developed its own predictive model fed with more and richer 
data that the one developed here or the ones developed by the different researchers among the 
academic community studying the crowdfunding phenomenon; If so, Kickstarter (and its homonyms) 
possess the most accurate predictive model of project success.  
Having in mind that the platform gets a 5% share of every successful project, it would be naïve to think 
that this hypothetical predictor model is not being used to find potential successful projects (with 
estimated funding pledges rates long over 100%), and that Kickstarter helps boosting its marketing 
effect by using its platform and social media influence. 
Either way, from an entrepreneurial perspective, Kickstarter’s endorsement may look purely arbitrary 
and difficult to achieve, which in fact it is. Therefore, it must be seen as an early indicator for success. 
 
On the other hand, the different entities (celebrities, experts and institutions) appearing on video 
campaigns and publicly endorsing a given project do not show a significant contribution to project 
success. Curiously, the celebrity endorsement parameter seems to have a negative coefficient of 
correlation in terms of project success. This can be explained because the celebrities/institutions used in 
the sample are mainly known in their specific area/region, and therefore their “expert-power” is limited 
to the local audience. Nevertheless, using world-famous celebrities seems to have a powerful effect on 
crowdfunding campaigns. It is the case of the internationally known artist Neil Young and his 
Kickstarter campaign for a high quality audio portable device player, Pono, which pledged 6,2 Million 
USD from 18.220 backers in 2014. Luckily for entrepreneurs who cannot afford hiring worldwide 
renowned celebrities, the results show that this kind of endorsement is not significantly correlated with 
success. 
 
In terms of intervariable correlation, the Expert Endorsement seems to be the one most correlated with 
other visual resources: Celebrity Endorsement and Institution Endorsement. It can be argued that some 
celebrities are also known to be experts in a certain field, and therefore their endorsement can double 
the Expert Power’s influence on consumers. Continuing with the Pono’s example, besides being a 
celebrity, Neil Young can be recognized as an expert on music. Similarly, the Expert Endorsement and 
the Institution Endorsement seem to work well as a doublet. This can be explained because experts can 
refer to prestigious institutions were they are working or have worked in the past to emphasize their 
==PAGE:43==
 
37 
credentials and skills (an engineer who has worked in NASA, a programmer who has worked in 
Google or Microsoft, etc.).  
 
Last but not least, it is worth mentioning the correlation of the in-project variables and how these can 
be used in conjunction to boost their impact on project success. It is the case of the correlation between 
number of images and number of words, number of images and social media, and finally, number of 
images and number of rewards. As it can be seen, the images being presented on the project description 
have a straight correlation with the number of words. It has been proved that the amount of information 
has a positive influence on helpfulness of texts like product reviews (Mudambi and Schuff, 2010), as 
backers are better able to assess their supporting decision by having better information (Koch and 
Siering, 2015). In the given dataset, the average number of words for successful projects is 845,06 
words, against 482,92 words for failed projects (table 8). As it can be seen, the correlation between 
words and images is very strong: the bigger the description is (in words) the more images are needed in 
order to illustrate the description. Similarly, the higher is the number of rewards (and hence, different 
types of reward levels), the more images are needed to present the individual rewards. Lastly, the 
correlation with the Social Media account can be explained because these types of networks are 
commonly using graphical resources to attract viewers, and therefore, more images can illustrate the 
project description, also in the social networks.

Conclusions 
Entrepreneurs and start-ups focusing on product development have in crowdfunding a low-cost 
multifunctional tool for testing their products on the market while getting financed and marketing their 
products/brands, all this with minimal economical risk, in other words: the paradise of 
entrepreneurship. 
 
Nonetheless, crowdfunding is a relatively new phenomenon and the academic environment is still 
researching its hidden details. Since researchers seem to focus mainly on the project-level parameters 
in order to develop predictive models of success, little has been investigated on the use of videography 
as a crowdfunding’s key success factor. The aim of this study is to find out what are the parameters that 
can better influence the success likelihood of a crowdfunded project from a video perspective. After 
analysing a dataset of 1.000 projects by using web scraping tools, manual video analysis, and binary 
linear regression analysis method, the obtained results show a clear influence of video-format in terms 
of project success. Moreover, major visual resources have been identified and quantified (Product 
Showcase, Social Mimicry, 3D renders), as well as those resources that seem to have a negative effect 
on project success (Celebrity, Expert, and Institution Endorsements). 
 
According to the analysed impact of the main visual resources (most correlated with project success), 
every project creator designing a crowdfunding video campaign should always answer these three 
golden questions to its potential customers: “What is the product?” (Product showcase), “How it 
works?” (3D render), and “What to use it for?” (Social Mimicry). 
 
The reader should be aware of the limitations of the results of this study might be influenced by the 
specifics of the analysed platform (Kickstarter.com). Nonetheless, the main parameters selected for this 
study are usually present in other platforms allowing video campaigns.  
Managerial implications 
The results of this study provide support for the managerial attractiveness of product development 
companies operating in crowdfunding markets, especially for those with products/services focused on 
technology and design.  
The outcome of this study can be used to design or improve a given crowdfunding campaign by 
introducing a set of crucial parameters in the video campaign to help managers achieve better success 
rates while decreasing their campaign’s budget.  
Moreover, it provides with current trends of crowdfunding consumers, which can be used to target 
specific customer segments in the given crowdfunding platform.  
On the other hand, the self-produced video accompanying this paper provides the bases of a sustainable 
business model to be used by entrepreneurs aiming to develop profitable business with low financial 
risk in this promising new economic model, crowdfunding. 
==PAGE:45==
 
39 
Future work 
In order to get a better understanding of the individual factors related to crowdfunding video campaigns 
and their effects on its potential customers, the use of complementary observation’s methods could be 
further applied. For instance, by selecting subjective parameters like emotions, feelings, reactions 
towards specific video components that can be analysed by developing guided video-watching sessions 
where participants’ reactions can be monitored using electroencephalography, interviews, videotaping, 
etc. Its output can help to evidence the given statistical results developed in this Thesis from a more 
psychological approach.  
 
Another possibility would be to collect projects’ data from other crowdfunding platforms to see if there 
are significant users’ differences between platforms. It could be interesting to try to focus on national-
range platforms to better understand the relationship of the socio-cultural context of a given country 
towards the crowdfunding environment.  
 
Another possible further development would be to include the definition of more intrinsic parameters 
during the video analysis method. For instance, by proposing more variables to be tested: Gender, age, 
social context, body language… of the ones presenting the video; the spaces where the scenes are being 
recorded, the use of animals, babies, or other elements; the use of filters, colour analysis, light 
balance… from a more video-technical perspective; the transcription of the audio to be coded and 
analysed from a communication theory perspective, etc.
