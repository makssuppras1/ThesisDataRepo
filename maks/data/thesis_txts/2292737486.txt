Preface
This thesis is the result of a collaboration between Technical University of Den-
mark (DTU) and Haldor Topsøe A/S (HTAS) and progressed over a duration of
ﬁve months from the 1st of September 2014 to the 1st of February 2015, corre-
sponding to 30 ECTS points. Supervising the project were Associate Professor
Peter Fristrup (DTU) and co-supervisor Research Scientist Irantzu Sadaba Zu-
biri (HTAS). The work in this project was performed entirely at DTU with the
catalysts being supplied by HTAS.
The aim of this project was divided into four studies. The two studies looked
at the eﬀect of alkali metals and the eﬀect of heating rate on the conversion of
glucose to methyl lactate by β-Sn. A third study looked at the role of β-Sn in
retro-aldol cleavage. The ﬁnal study looked at the reaction kinetics of the triose
to methyl lactate conversion by tin, titanium and zirconium zeotypes.
The report is divided into three parts. The ﬁrst part will include an introduc-
tion to; the desired transition from oil to biomass (chapter 1), the applications of
zeolites and zeotypes with focus on catalysis and the β-Sn zeotype (chapter 2),
the catalytic reaction mechanism of biomass to methyl lactate (chapter 3), and
basic reaction kinetics (chapter 4). Leading to the aims of the project described
in detail in chapter 5.
Following this the second part of the report will present the development of
experimental methods (chapter 6), and go on to present the results obtained in
each part of this study and discuss their implications (chapter 7-9). Finally a
conclusion of the study will be presented in chapter 10.
The ﬁnal part of this report contains the appendices (denoted with letters e.g.
appendix A.2.2), hereunder the experimental procedures employed (appendix A)
and data from the analytical techniques (appendix B-C and attached CD-ROM).

Abstract
Expanding knowledge and understanding of an underlying process is the founda-
tion of an eﬃcient optimisation process. Therefore this project has attempted to
further understanding of a tin *BEA zeotype catalyst (Hereafter β-Sn) used for
the conversion of glucose to methyl lactate. In this project four diﬀerent aspects
of the catalytic process were looked into, they can be divided into two categories;
1. external eﬀects on the catalytic reaction with β-Sn and 2. β-Sn’s role in the
catalytic process.
External eﬀects on catalytic reaction with β-Sn
First of all in order to
further understand the eﬀect of alkali-metals and assess their inﬂuence on the
ability of the catalyst, a study of the reaction kinetics of the conversion of glucose
to methyl lactate was performed using in-situ NMR and is still ongoing. Initial
results from the study indicate little correlation between the rate of methyl lactate
formation and alkali content, but a large correlation between alkali-metal content
and glucose degradation. Thus so far indicating that the major eﬀect of alkali-
metals is on selectivity and not rate.
The second study attempted to assess if by-product formation was aﬀected by
the heating rate of the reaction. By optimising the yield of a reaction with a high
heating rate (0-170 ◦C over 2 min) and comparing it with one of a lower heating
rate (0-170 ◦C over 10 min), it was found that there is no notable change in yield
and therefore the heating process is not a signiﬁcant factor.
Comparison of Catalysts
The titanium *BEA zeotype catalyst (Hereafter β-
Ti), zirconium *BEA zeotype catalyst (Hereafter β-Zr) and β-Sn catalyst all ex-
hibit high conversion of six carbon sugars (97-99%) with varying yields (0-64%).
The third study determined the rate constants and activation energies of the di-
hydroxyacetone to pyruvaldehyde step for each catalyst and the pyruvaldehyde to
methyl lactate step for β-Sn, using a combination of ReactIR and a microwave
reactor combined with 1H-NMR analysis.
The study showed that the dihydroxyacetone to pyruvaldehyde step of the
reaction had a lower observed rate constant and a lower activation energy than
v
==PAGE:8==
the pyruvaldehyde to methyl lactate step, indicating that the reaction rate of the
latter step has a larger entropy contribution. Furthermore β-Ti150 and β-Zr150 had
almost equivalent observed rate constants which in both cases were much lower
than that of β-Sn150. However β-Zr150 had the largest activation energy and β-
Ti150 the smallest. The very low reaction rate of the β-Ti150 and β-Zr150 catalysts
may allow a kinetically more favourable step to dominate in reactions from six
carbon sugars explaining the high conversions with low yield obtained for these
two.
The fourth study used diacetone alcohol in a retro-aldol reaction as a model
compound to determine the catalyst role in the reaction. The reaction was con-
ducted with β-Sn200, a silicium *BEA zeotype, and no catalyst and showed that
almost no reaction occurred without the β-Sn200 catalyst present, indicating that
the acidity of the catalyst is vital in retro-aldol reaction of fructose.
Conclusion
In conclusion alkali metals eﬀect the selectivity of the β-Sn catalyst
not the reaction rate. The formation of by-products is unaﬀected by the heating
rate. The activation energy is not the limiting factor for the rate of reaction with
the β-Sn catalyst. The low reaction rate of β-Ti150 and β-Zr150 is likely detrimental
to their selectivity for the formation of methyl lactate. The tin active sites in the
β-Sn catalyst catalyse retro-aldol reaction.

be seen in ﬁgure 1.1, has almost doubled in the last 40 years and is forecast to

A ﬁnal and almost unavoidable downside to oil and other fossil fuels is their
environmental sustainability. One of the major concerns of recent years has been
emissions from combustion of fossil fuels, and the keyword on everyone’s agenda
is carbon dioxide. Figure 1.3 shows how the level of CO2 in the atmosphere is
growing with increasing haste. Despite constantly improving eﬃciency of power

1.2
Biomass as a Sustainable Resource
Biomass is organic material derived from living or recently living organisms. It is
carbon based and will typically fall into the categories of hydrocarbons or carbo-
hydrates. Asides from organic waste, vegetative biomass is of the greatest interest
in green chemistry, as it is the most eﬃciently produced source of biomass. Being
vastly abundant, biomass is easily attainable at low cost and is potentially CO2
neutral. Giving the development of biomass, as a resource, great potential.
Biomass in plants is formed by photosynthesis, by which carbon dioxide and
water react using sunlight as the energy source to form organic material. The

==PAGE:20==

resulting product is called lignocellulose and consist primarily of cellulose (glu-
cose polymer), hemicellulose (glucose/xylose polymer) and lignin (phenylpropane
polymer) (Hendriks and Zeeman, 2009).
Over 170 Pg (170 billion metric tons) of biomass is produced per year by
photosynthesis, of which 75% can be classiﬁed as carbohydrates, consisting in
largest part of glucose and xylose. Only 3-4% of this is utilised by humans (Corma
et al., 2007). This has created a growing interest for chemists to attempt to ﬁnd
new ways of utilising carbohydrates, often with focus on glucose.
Industrially, glucose is typically produced by fermentation of corn, a process in
which starch is hydrolysed enzymatically to glucose. By this process a single ton of
corn may produce 590 kg of glucose. If this was applied to the entire United States
annual corn production, roughly 250 billion tons, the United States would be able
to produce the carbon equivalent of 500 million barrels of oil, covering only two-
thirds of the annual consumption for chemical production. (Corma et al., 2007)
Another potential industrial source of glucose is woody biomass. Using wood
as a source of glucose would be considerably cheaper, increasing the added value
of all processes utilising glucose. However due to the robust nature of wood lig-
nocellulose, it requires pretreatment and current processes still lack the eﬃciency
needed to make this economically viable. An additional advantage of using woody
biomass is avoidance of the ethics concerning use of food sources for alternative
purposes. (Corma et al., 2007)

be recovered allowing for the aforementioned ﬂow-system. With the purpose of
ﬁnding a viable catalyst, heterogeneous catalysis has been intensely investigate for

1.2.1
The Terawatt Challenge
In general the greatest challenge faced when looking at alternatives to oil is not
only ﬁnding a working alternative but also an alternative which has the potential
to be scaled to such a degree that it can have a notable eﬀect on the market. The
aspect of scalability to compete with oil is commonly referred to as the terawatt
challenge.
The terawatt challenge is named so because any alternative to oil must be
able to be expanded to a terawatt scale to have any profound eﬀect (Vesborg
and Jaramillo, 2012). Currently fossil fuels account for 87% of the 16 terawatt
power consumed globally. With these ﬁgures it is obvious that this is a daunting
challenge, even if a new technology was able to achieve a one terawatt scale it
would still correspond to less than ten percent of oil’s energy impact.
Vesborg and Jaramillo (2012), have in their paper addressing the terawatt
challenge, researched the availability of many elements in relation to scalability.
A certain aspect of this is shown in ﬁgure 1.6 which shows the global annual
production of the elements of the periodic table. Roughly speaking, any of the 25
elements coloured dark green should be easily applicable at a terawatt scale. To
put this into perspective, a technology which uses one gram( or less) of the selected
element for every watt of power it is able to produce, should be able achieve an
energy production of at least one terawatt a year if the element production was
dedicated solely to that task.
Naturally this means that a higher power production per gram element is re-
quired if some of the less abundant materials should be implemented for this pur-
pose. This is an important aspect to include in any considerations with respect
to large scale energy or chemical production. When looking into catalysis such
a factor could mean that a less eﬀective catalyst may be employed industrially
because it’s more eﬃcient counterpart is inaccessible at a suﬃciently large scale.
In relation to the previous section on potential products from biomass produc-
tion of lactic acid is desired at large scale, but as a chemical commodity rather
than energy source. This slightly lowers the demand for scalability and depending
on the eﬃciency of the catalyst found, elements with over 33 kiloton production
a year should be prospective catalysts. Conforming with this, current research
in this catalytic ﬁeld has looked into silicon, aluminium and titanium, which all
reside in the megaton production range, as well as tin and zirconium which reside

2-hydroxypropanoic acid, a weak chiral organic acid found in many areas through-

by Fermi in a fermentation process that lay the grounds for its industrial produc-

1.3.1
Properties
Despite being a very small molecule lactic acid contains dual functionality, in the
form of a hydroxyl group and a carboxylic acid, giving the molecule high reaction
versatility. It is the simplest of the α-hydroxycarboxylic acids, and possesses a
single chiral centre. The chiral centre is very important for its applications as
only the L-isomer may be metabolised by humans. Combined with its chemical
uses this places demands for the production of lactic acid both in racemic and
enantiomerically pure form. (Corma et al., 2007)
The interest in lactic acid is also due to its biodegradability and general con-
sideration as a safe chemical, which allows for “free” utilisation in cosmetics, phar-
maceuticals, food products and similar areas with high human exposure and/or
areas where environmental consideration is important.
1.3.2
Production
Annually 350 thousand tons of lactic acid are produced globally and this is ex-
pected to grow by 12 to 15% annually. The majority of this production is done
by fermentation of glucose. The major drawback and thereby greatest challenge
of this process is the resulting recovery and puriﬁcation process. The recovery in-
volves three main steps; precipitation of lactic acid as calcium lactate, separation
of biological matter and restoration of lactic acid by addition of sulphuric acid.
After the recovery the crude lactic acid must then be puriﬁed. Alone the recovery
and puriﬁcation steps account for 50% of the production costs. The process pro-
duces a large amount of chemical waste (e.g. stoichiometric amounts of calcium
sulfate) making it environmentally liable. (Corma et al., 2007)
Recently activity in researching use of zeolites in the production of lactates
was initiated by Taarning et al. (2009) who demonstrated the formation of methyl
lactate, a derivative of lactic acid, from the trioses dihydroxyacetone and glycer-
aldehyde. Following activity in the ﬁeld resulted in the discovery that β-Sn could
be used for the synthesis of methyl lactate directly from C6-sugars with up to 64%
yield, opening the opportunity for a replacement of the current production method
(Holm et al., 2010).
1.3.3
Chemistry and Applications
Lactic acid is used as a chemical precursor in the production of multiple industrial
compounds and polymers in varying sizes, from propylene glycol to large acrylic
polymers. Their polymers are very useful for packaging, prosthetics and sutures,
due to them being biodegradable and biocompatible and are also widely used in
textile and pharmaceutical industries.

Zeolites and Zeo-types
Zeolites are a class of crystalline aluminosilicates ﬁrst categorised in 1756 by Axel
Cronstedt as a mineral group. Zeolites and zeotypes share the same framework
structure and are widely used as heterogeneous catalysts, and as previously men-
tioned have gained much interest in recent years for their ability to convert biomass
to useful commodity chemicals (Corma et al., 2007).
The name zeolite is derived from the Greek zeo, to boil, and lithos, stone,
and originates from Cronstedt’s discovery that when heated a hissing sound may
be heard as water is lost. The ability to “loose” water, and hence absorb water,
stems from the zeolite’s rigid framework. The framework contains well deﬁned
“channels” which intersect at “cages”, causing the structure to be porous. This
gives the solid a high surface area and internal volume hence the ability to absorb
water. Zeolites and zeotype cavities range in size from 0.2 nm to 2 nm making
them micro-porous, and allowing them to absorb a range of smaller molecules.
The ability to allow other molecules to pass through the structure is the basis
for many zeolite and zeotype applications, including absorbents for puriﬁcation,
molecular sieves for separation, drying and catalysis, cation exchange for water
softening and templates for nanoparticles. Zeolites represent a large part of the
chemical industry and are used abundantly in areas such as cat litter, catalysis for
petroleum puriﬁcation, and almost 80% of synthetic zeolites are used in detergents
as water softeners.
(Smart and Moore, 2012)

2.1
Zeolites
The discovery of amorphous aluminosilicates for catalysis was a great step forward
in heterogeneous catalysis. This was followed by the ﬁrst synthesis of acidic zeolite
catalysts at the end of the 1960s and resulted in a boom of catalyst production.
The zeolite catalysts were found to have many advantages over their amorphous
counterparts showing considerably higher selectivity and activity.
Zeolite synthesis is also much more reliable with a great increase in repro-
ducibility compared to amorphous aluminosilicates. This in turn gives a higher
reproducibility of the catalytic processes, an important parameter particularly in
industrial processes.
Zeolites also have the potential for much higher thermal and hydrothermal
stability in some cases being able to withstand temperatures of up to 900 ◦C. This
is particularly useful for hydrocracking where the reaction occurs at 650 ◦C and
regeneration of the catalyst is performed at 750 ◦C in steam.
Furthermore zeolites have a very high internal surface area compared to their
amorphous counterparts. An equivalent molecular amount of zeolite and amor-
phous aluminosilicate will allow the zeolite to contain 100 times more molecules.
Using an internal area also attributes to “concentrate” the reactants within the
zeolite, an eﬀect which greatly promotes bimolecular reactions. (Corma and Mar-
tinez, 1995; Smart and Moore, 2012)

2.1.2
Anions and Cations
As seen previously in formula 2.1 a zeolite contains two oxygen atoms for every
aluminium or silicon atom. For silicon with an oxidation state of IV this leads
to a neutral charge, however aluminium only has an oxidation state of III and
therefore the presence of aluminium in the framework leads to a negative charge.
The negative charge is balanced by metallic cations, these are not part of the
framework, but held electro-statically within the channels/cavities, resulting in the
framework of the zeolite being anionic. Naturally the diﬀerence between silicon
and aluminium inﬂuence the properties of the zeolite and therefore the ratio of the
two is of great importance.
A zeolite with a Si/Al ratio close to one is considered aluminium rich (e.g.
zeolite A and X). These types of zeolites are useful absorbent however the high
aluminium content makes them labile in acid as well as steam (water).
It is
generally observed that higher silicon content yields more stable zeolites. The most
stable naturally occurring zeolite is Siliceous which has a Si/Al ratio higher than
ﬁve. Attempting to create more stable zeolites, synthetic chemistry has managed
to produce zeolites with much greater Si/Al ratios than ﬁve, the greatest being
Silicalite which is almost pure silicon, resulting in an almost inﬁnite Si/Al ratio.
High contents of silicon naturally lead to fewer cations in the zeolite. This alters
the properties of the zeolite making it highly hydrophobic and instead giving it an
aﬃnity for hydrocarbons.
The metallic cations of a zeolite play an important role in the catalyst and as
they are held electrostatically within the zeolites channels/cavities, the cations are
easily exchangeable. The cations’ size, number, charge and position may alter the
zeolite channels and by exchanging the cations a desired eﬀect may be obtained.
Cations may block channels limiting the accessibility for other molecule, for ex-

2.1.3
Shape Selectivity
Zeolite catalysts have a very high degree of selectivity, which is an extremely useful
attribute. A large factor in the selectivity of zeolites comes in the form of shape-
selectivity. Shape-selectivity utilises the physical limitations of the zeolite channels
and cavities to obtain a selective product, this is similar to the sterical hindrance
observed in other areas of organic chemistry. The physical limitations of the zeolite
can come into eﬀect at diﬀerent stages of the reaction and is therefore categorised
into reactant, product or transition-state selectivity.
Reactant selectivity uses the same concept as molecular sieves in that only
molecules of a speciﬁc size or smaller may enter the zeolites cavities and thereby
gain access to the reactive sites within the zeolite. Figure 2.5 illustrates the eﬀect
by showing how a straight-chain alkane can enter the zeolite and react, whilst a
branched alkane is too large and is therefore unable to enter the cavity.

2.1.4
Electronic Selectivity
The pore size limitations of the zeolites or zeotype attribute to their catalytic
eﬀect as well as selectivity. The electrons in the zeolite/zeotype framework are not
localised around individual atoms, but posses a partially covalent character and
are partially delocalised through the bulk of the framework.
This forms a strong electric ﬁeld extending from the framework, which becomes
of great importance when the pore size of the zeolite/zeotype is close in size to
that of the contained molecules. In such a case electronic conﬁnement comes into
play and the electric ﬁeld may be able to polarize the contained molecule thus
potentially activating it for reaction.
When approaching the zeolite/zeotype framework the eﬀect of short range re-
pulsive interactions with the delocalised lattice electrons has a large eﬀect, causing
the density of the orbitals of the interacting molecule to fall. This eﬀect is so great
that the density of an orbital will approach zero as the distance to the lattice wall
approaches zero. The repulsion from the latices results in a contraction of the
molecules orbitals leading to a change in their energy level, which in itself can
preactivate the molecule. (Smart and Moore, 2012)

2.2
Zeotype Catalysis
In order to expand the types and thereby applications of zeolites, it was attempted
to replace the framework aluminium with other metal-ions. The resulting com-
pounds are referred to as zeotypes as they posses the same framework as that
of a zeolite, but the alumina is replaced by another metal such as tin (Corma
and Martinez, 1995). The ﬁrst successful synthesis of a zeotype was performed
by Taramasso et al. (1983), whom successfully prepared the zeotype titanium
silicate-1 (TS-1), which continued to be rapidly industrialised for the production
of diphenols only three years later in 1986 (Taramasso et al., 1983; Notari, 1993).
The introduction of a diﬀerent metal to form a zeotype does not aﬀect the
framework structure which means that a zeotype catalyst possesses all the struc-
tural advantages of zeolites; high temperature stability, shape selectivity and elec-
tronic selectivity. Zeotypes even use the same structural classiﬁcation as zeolites.
The profound diﬀerence between zeolites and zeotypes lies in their acidity, an es-
sential property for most of the catalytic reactions they are used for. (Smart and
Moore, 2012)
2.2.1
Acidity
A zeolite possesses predominantly Brønsted acid type acidity which is highly de-
pendent on the number of protons present. The number of protons is directly
correlated to the number of aluminium atoms in the catalyst. Therefore by regu-
lating the Si/Al ratio it is possible to also regulate the number of protons present.
It is interesting to note that the fewer protons and thereby acid sites present, the
more acidic the remaining sites become.
When aluminium is replaced with another metal to create a zeotype catalyst,
the acidic properties of the catalyst change profoundly. Replacing the aluminium
with metals such as tin, titanium or zirconium results in a change in electronic
conﬁguration essentially eliminating all Brønsted acid sites, instead the new metals
turn the zeotype into a Lewis acid, due to their electron acceptor properties.
Figure 2.8 illustrates the diﬀerence between a zeolite active site and a tin
zeotype active site, this clearly illustrates how the exchange of the negatively
charged alumina will also result in the removal of a proton and thereby Brønsted
acidity. It should however be noted that defects in the crystal lattice can result in
the formation Brønsted acid sites, however their occurrence will naturally be vastly
less abundant. Naturally such a considerable change is also replicated through a
change in catalytic properties of the catalyst. (Corma and Martinez, 1995; Tolborg
et al., 2013)

3.1
Isomerisation
The isomerisation ability of β-Sn, as seen in steps 1 and 3 of ﬁgure 3.1, has been
demonstrated on multiple occasions and is an essential property for reactions with
biomass (Taarning et al., 2009; Lew et al., 2012; Dapsens et al., 2014). As stated
in section 1.2 biomass is primarily composed of lignocellulose, which is to a large
extent comprised of glucose. In order to perform conversion to methyl lactate by
the pathway shown in ﬁgure 3.1, glucose must ﬁrst be isomerised to fructose.
Isomerisation can be viewed as a intramolecular Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley re-
duction and Oppenauer’s oxidation (MPVO reaction), which involves the reduc-
tion of a ketone or aldehyde and the simultaneous oxidation of an alcohol (Corma
et al., 2002). This reaction is catalysed by the presence of a Lewis acid, often in
the form of Lewis acid metal alkoxides. Lewis acid zeotypes were also found to be
catalytically active for this reaction, Van Der Waal et al. (1996) found that the
β-Ti zeotype had increased activity compared to the β-Al zeolite and hereafter
Corma et al. (2002) found that β-Sn is an even better catalyst, attributing it to
the increased Lewis acidity of the metal atom.

reaction mechanism in further detail than a simple comparison of yields. By using

be employed to gain further understanding of aspects such as the selectivity of a
reaction and the eﬀect of components such as catalysts.

methods require the removal of a sample for measurement, which could potentially
be an issue as the system is heterogeneous. It would require that the reaction was
performed under the assumption that the suspension is homogeneously dispersed
and therefore an equivalent amount of catalyst was removed with each sample.

eﬃcient. Furthermore by using an aluminium block, instead of a microwave, for
heating it would be possible to run up to nine samples simultaneously, versus one
for the microwave. With this it would be possible to run a large number of samples
thus increasing the accuracy of the result.
Initially the reaction proceeded as expected, however after attempting the re-

the solvent had been lost. It was proposed that the vial could not withstand the

therefore began to leak. This was tested by monitoring the mass of a vial contain-

Microwave and 1H-NMR
A substantial temperature increase was necessary to cause a large increase in reac-
tion rate. To accommodate this task it was decided that the reaction should be run
in a Biotage microwave reactor. When using microwave with accompanying vials
the setup can withstand up to 30 bar, an equivalent of methanol at approximately
180 ◦C. Initial testing of the microwave setup had shown that ﬂuctuations in pres-
sure at the limit of the microwaves capacity often lead to the system aborting
the reaction, therefore a slightly lower temperature was chosen in order to reduce
pressure ﬂuctuation.
A temperature range of 150-170 ◦C was selected. The limitations of the mi-
crowave come into account when testing samples, as sampling cannot be performed
whilst running, neither by sample extraction nor in-situ measurements. Due to
these limitations it was instead decided to run the samples for a short time so that
approximately 10% yield was obtained. Doing this allowed for the assumption that
the concentration was still in the linear range of the reaction proﬁle. Although
this introduced a new assumption, and thereby a possible source for error, it also
meant that only a single concentration was needed for discerning the rate of the
reaction.
The microwave is ideal for use with this method as it has a very short heat-
ing curve, which reduces any deviation that comes from the intermittent time
where reaction occurs but the desired reaction temperature has yet to be reached.
Together with 1H-NMR for analysis, which will be covered in section 6.3, this
procedure was used for testing of β-Ti150 and β-Zr150, of which the results will be
covered in the following chapter.

Reactions with Glucose
The reaction of glucose in the catalytic system is known to proceed at a much

ferent method was required to eﬃciently monitor a reaction with glucose as the
starting material. For this purpose three methods were proposed; ReactIR, mi-
crowave synthesis and in-situ NMR. Each method had profound advantages and
disadvantages which will be covered in the following sections.
6.2.1
ReactIR in Autoclave with Sample Extraction
ReactIR was selected as a possible method due to the ability to perform in-situ
measurements at high temperatures by use of an autoclave. The autoclave was
able to accommodate the 160 ◦C reaction temperature generally used for reactions

Furthermore the autoclave could be equipped with a sampling setup, allowing
for extraction of samples whilst the reaction was still running, a process which is

The greatest limitation of the IR-autoclave method lies in the scale required.

The catalyst was currently only synthesised on a small scale and therefore only

reactions without reducing the amount of catalyst considerably. A reduction of
catalyst would also require the amount of reactant to be reduced and as the amount
of solvent cannot be reduced, the overall concentration of all components would
fall.
ReactIR with an autoclave can overall be evaluated as a valid method of anal-
ysis, providing an abundant source of all reaction components. However, as that
was not the case in this project, using ReactIR with an autoclave was not ideal.
6.2.2
Microwave Vial in Microwave
A Biotage microwave reactor was suggested as another method. Like the autoclave,
the microwave setup has a high pressure tolerance, up to 30 bar, allowing the
reaction mixture to be heated up to 180 ◦C. As previously stated the limitations of
the microwave come into account when removing samples for testing. As sampling
cannot be performed whilst running, a separate reaction is required to obtain each
point in a concentration proﬁle. In addition to the high temperature attainable
with the microwave, it also has the advantage of a very short heating ramp.
From the initial experiments that were conducted using this method (procedure
A.2.3) and the aforementioned advantages and disadvantages, it was stipulated
that the use of the microwave is most ideal for experiments optimising ﬁnal yield
or discerning reaction rates based on the linear range of a concentration curve.

6.2.3
In-Situ NMR
The ﬁnal method proposed was in-situ NMR with the use of a sealed NMR pressure
tube (procedure A.2.5). This methods greatest strength and weakness was the very
small reaction scale (0.3 mL). The small scale allows for a very high concentration
of catalyst whilst using a very small overall amount, thus avoiding the problem
described previously for the autoclave.
This also means that the reaction can
be run at a lower temperature, by compensating with increasing the amount of
catalyst.
On the other hand the small scale causes deviations imposed by human error
to have increased inﬂuence, which could possibly be manifested as an increase
in the deviation of each sample. Although this is a practical issue that can be
reduced by employing a well deﬁned procedure and by experience, it is still a
factor to be considered when selecting the method. Furthermore when using a
volatile solvent such as methanol the vial must be pressurised in order to avoid
boiling. Fortunately the NMR pressure tubes have this capability.
The greatest advantage of NMR is that it is possible to discern many diﬀerent
structures in a complex mixture.
Providing that the correct technique is em-
ployed, an experienced analyst should be able to identify and quantify products,
by-products and in some cases reaction intermediates.

7.1.2
Multivariate Curve Resolution
In order to obtain a more accurate concentration curve it is possible, to discern
the concentration using the entire IR spectrum instead of only a few peaks. This
technique requires a standard spectrum for each of the compounds present in the
reaction, using each of these an algorithm attempts to subtract all the spectrum
adapting the magnitude of each, a technique known as multivariate curve resolu-
tion.
This process is performed using a series of matrices. The data received from an
experiment is compiled into a matrix X with the dimensions wavenumber × time
and the standard spectra recorded are compiled into a matrix S with dimensions
wavenumber × number of compounds, the values in the matrices are intensities
at the given point. These two matrices are correlated by a matrix C as shown in
equation 7.1, where T denotes the transposed matrix.

Kinetics of Lactic Acid
Rasrendra et al., 2011, performed a similar kinetic study for the reaction of dihy-
droxyacetone to lactic acid in water by a homogeneous aluminum chloride catalyst.
The study showed that ﬁrst reaction step (DHA to PA) had an activation energy
of 93±3 kJ/mol and the second step (PA to ML) had a activation energy of 68±3
kJ/mol, which is almost the opposite of what was found in this study.
69
==PAGE:84==

There are two notable diﬀerences between the reaction conditions of the two
studies which could give rise to the aforementioned diﬀerences. The ﬁrst is the
diﬀerence in choice of solvent, methanol versus water, this is a very signiﬁcant
factor as i results in two diﬀerent reaction products, methyl lactate and lactic
acid, respectively. The second is the catalyst which in both cases are Lewis acid
catalysts, however as covered previously tin has been found to be a much stronger
Lewis acid.
When observing the proposed reaction mechanism in ﬁgure 3.1 it appears that
the change of solvent can inﬂuence the reaction in a number of ways. First of
all if the loss of water from dihydroxyacetone to form pyruvaldehyde has some
equilibrium character, therefore a large abundance of water may inhibit reaction
thus increasing the activation energy of this step. The second aspect is attachment
of water/methanol followed by a hydride shift to form lactic acid/methyl lactate.
However a comparison of the activation energies in this step would be invalid as the
change in solvent results in two diﬀerent products and therefore transition states,
this means that the activation energies of the second step are for two diﬀerent
reactions rather than two diﬀerent pathways.
With concerns to the catalyst an increased Lewis acidity should better catalyse
the conversion of dihydroxyacetone to pyruvaldehyde. Based on this it would be
expected that the β-Sn catalyst has a higher catalytic eﬀect and thereby lower
activation energy as it contains a tin atom which is a stronger Lewis acid than
aluminium. This is in agreement with the diﬀerence in the activation energies for
the ﬁrst step, and as previously mentioned the second step cannot be compared.

8.2
Discussion of Temperature Selectivity
Contrary to what was expected the calculated optimum conditions did not produce
the highest reaction yield, according to table 8.2 the highest average reaction yield
was instead obtained from the reaction with 6.0 mg/L K2CO3.
If it is gone on to assume that the highest possible reaction yield under the
reaction conditions was indeed obtained, and the optimum potassium carbonate
content under the reaction conditions is instead 6.0 mg/L K2CO3, a comparison
of these experimental results can be made with those obtained by Tolborg et al.
(2015) using an autoclave. According to the autoclave experiments the desired
reaction temperature of 170 ◦C was obtained after approximately 10 minutes and
upon completion a methyl lactate yield of 53% was obtained. In comparison the
microwave studies were able to achieve a reaction temperature of 170 ◦C after only
2 minutes and obtained an optimal yield of 51.5%±0.4%.

==PAGE:97==

Despite the diﬀerence in heating rate between the two reaction process’ the
obtained yields diﬀer by only 1.5% and with possible deviations taken into account
the diﬀerence is even less. If it is also taken into consideration that the highest yield
obtained by the microwave experiments may not be from the optimum conditions
but only very close to, it would be reasonable to consider the yields from the two
experiments equivalent.
The equivalent yields indicate that the reactions selectivity towards methyl
lactate is not aﬀected by an increase in the heating rate. It would therefore also
be reasonable to assume that the formation by-products occurs with the same rate
relative to methyl lactate across the heating range. This result is in fact very
positive for the industrialisation of the reaction, as it eliminates the need for a
high heating rate to be included in the reactor design.

each containing 0 mg/L, 32.4 mg/L and 58.4 mg/L potassium carbonate respec-

9.2
Discussion of Retro-Aldol Studies
Table 9.1 shows that the reaction with the β-Sn catalyst results in a 16.0% yield
contrary to the other two experiments which achieve less than 0.5% yield. β-Sn
has therefore a 30 times greater yield than that of the other two reactions, clear
evidence of a catalytic eﬀect taking place in the β-Sn reaction.
Based on the literature covered in section 3.2, this is exactly what is expected.
The relatively low temperature the reaction is run at allows for a slow reaction
by thermal retro-aldol cleavage, only possible for β-hydroxy carbonyl compounds,
which explains the small yield detected in the two reactions without β-Sn. However
the Lewis acid properties of the tin atom in the β-Sn catalyst allows for reaction
by a pathway equivalent to the acid catalysed mechanism shown in ﬁgure 3.4.
Due to the very small yields produced by the reaction without catalyst and the
reaction with β-Si and the natural unrecordable deviations imposted on a reaction,
the two yields would generally be considered identical. However with the the very
low standard deviations showing a clear and substantial diﬀerence between the two
yields speculation into a possible diﬀerence would not be unfounded. Continuing
from this of the two reactions it is the reaction with β-Si which produces the lowest
yield. This indicates that rather than catalyse the reaction the structure of the
β-Si zeotype may inhibit reaction.
There could be two possible causes behind such an inhibitory eﬀect. First of
all it is necessary to assume that the diﬀusion of a molecule as large as diace-
tone alcohol through a zeotype structure is much slower than that of the smaller
solvent molecules. Such an assumption would mean that despite a small amount
of catalyst, relative to the total reaction volume, a substantial amount of the re-
actant is located within the catalyst framework. Assuming that no reaction can
occur when under the inﬂuence of the catalyst, then if approximately 25% of the
reactant is contained or aﬀected by the catalyst at any given time it would be the
equivalent of removing that amount from the reaction mixture and thus result in
the diﬀerence in yields seen in table 9.1.
The next consideration is, why should the β-Si structure inhibit reaction. One
possible reason is the anionic nature of the silicon framework. Thermal retro-aldol
reaction occurs by the mechanism shown in ﬁgure 3.4, which proceeds via a cyclic
intermediate where the hydrogen atom of the alcohol coordinates to both oxygen
atoms simultaneously.
In order to achieve such a conformation the carbonyl oxygen must come into
close vicinity of the hydrogen. If it is assumed that the molecule approaches a
negative ﬁeld, the electron rich carbonyl oxygen would most likely be repulsed,
while the electron deﬁcient hydrogen would be attracted. The overall eﬀect would
be an increase in the energy barrier for the conﬁguration required to perform
thermal retro-aldol cleavage. Considering the low yield achieved in the no catalyst
92
==PAGE:107==
CHAPTER 9. STUDY OF RETRO-ALDOL CLEAVAGE BY β-SN
experiment, a small negative eﬀect may be suﬃcient to inﬂuence the overall rate
noticeably.
Alternatively the inhibition could be a spatial eﬀect. Le Chˆatelier’s principal
states that an equilibrium (such as the aldol - retro-aldol reaction) proceeds in
the direction that minimises the eﬀect imposed by an outside inﬂuence. When
the amount of reactant inside the zeolite builds-up the molecules become more
conﬁned, an eﬀect similar to that of an increase in pressure. In order to minimise
the molecules spatial eﬀect the compound will prefer to remain as a singe molecule
rather than two separate. Once again such an eﬀect need not be very substantial
to be detected when the reaction already proceeds very slowly. This speculation
is only valid under the assumption that internal volume of the β-Si structure is
suﬃcient to hold a substantial amount of reactant which, considering the diﬀerence
in yield, should be approximately 25%.
The speculations on the eﬀect of the zeotype structure have a very large uncer-
tainty associated with them. However the current experiments clearly demonstrate
that retro-aldol cleavage is greatly catalysed by the Lewis acid properties of the
β-Sn catalyst.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
The studies covered in this project have given much material for thought thus far,
but are in most cases still ongoing in order to obtain the last substantial data
required for validation.
External eﬀects on catalytic reaction with β-Sn
The study of alkali-metals
eﬀect on the reaction kinetics of the catalytic reaction was performed using in-situ
NMR and is still ongoing. Initial results from the study indicate little correlation
between the rate of methyl lactate formation and alkali content, but a large correla-
tion between alkali-metal content and glucose degradation. Thus so far indicating
that the major eﬀect of alkali-metals is on selectivity and not rate. However no
conclusion can be made as to the eﬀect on activation energy or rate contents prior
to further research.
The study of the heating rates eﬀect on selectivity initiated by optimising
the alkali content. The highest achieved was 61.5%±0.4% obtained with 6 mg/L
potassium carbonate. When compared with the 53% achieved by autoclave it was
concluded that the heating rate had no substantial eﬀect on reaction selectivity.
Comparison of Catalysts
The kinetic studies of reactions from the triose in-
termediates to methyl lactate showed that the DHA-PA step of the reaction has
a lower observed rate constant and a lower activation energy than the PA-ML
step, indicating that the reaction rate of the latter step has a higher tempera-
ture dependency than the former, presumably as the result of a larger entropy
contribution.
Furthermore the results showed that β-Ti150 and β-Zr150 had almost equivalent
observed rate constants which in both cases were much lower than that of β-Sn150.
However β-Zr150 had the largest activation energy and β-Ti150 the smallest. These
results indicate that the very low reaction rate of the β-Ti150 and β-Zr150 catalysts,

allow a kinetically more favourable step to dominate in reactions from C6 sugars
resulting in high conversions with low yield.
Testing of DAA in a retro-aldol reaction with the β-Sn200 catalyst, β-Si and no
catalyst, showed that only almost no reaction occurred without the β-Sn200 catalyst
present. It can therefore be concluded that retro-aldol cleavage is catalysed by
the tin active sites in β-Sn. A ﬁnal conclusion will be drawn pending testing of
additional model compounds.

A.3.1
Synthesis of 4-Hydroxyhexan-2-one from Acetone
In a three-necked ﬂask, equipped with a stirring-bar, thermometer and dropping-

distilled oﬀand the residue distilled under reduced pressure (23 bar, 80 ◦C). The

The product was inseparable from a single by-product, present in large amounts.
Adapted from Bergmann and Resnik (1952)
A.3.2
Synthesis of 4-Hydroxyhexan-2-one from Isopropen-
yloxy Trimethylsilane
A round bottomed ﬂask, equipped with stirring-bar, was submerged into a dry-ice

cool. (Isopropenyloxy)trimethylsilane (1.0 g, 7.6 mmol) at −20 ◦C was transferred

The solution was then quenched with water (40 mL) and extracted with ether
(4x100 mL). The organic phases where dried with sodium sulphate and concen-
trated, yielding 519.2 mg of a yellow oil.
A.3.3

yloxy Trimethylsilane
A round bottomed ﬂask, equipped with stirring-bar, was submerged into a dry-ice

dried with sodium sulphate and concentrated. The product was puriﬁed by FCC
(ether/pentane 20-50% with 5% intervals of 200 mL solution, 50 mL fractions were
taken and the product appeared after fraction 21), yielding 143.3 mg (1.2 mmol)
of the desired product at 100% purity (by GC-MS).

A.4
β-Sn ( Si
Sn = 200) via Hydrothermal Synthesis
Procedure.
Synthesis:
The Sn-Beta zeolites were synthesized by Søren Tolborg using a mod-
iﬁed route described by Valencia and Corma, 1999.
In a typical synthesis procedure, 30.6 g of tetraethyl orthosilicate (TEOS,
Aldrich, 98%) was added to 33.1 g of tetraethylammonium hydroxide (TEAOH,
Sigma-Aldrich, 35% in water) under careful stirring, forming a two-phase solution.
After stirring ( 60 min) one phase is obtained and tin(IV) chloride pentahydrate
(SnCl4·5H2O, Aldrich, 98%) dissolved in 2.0 mL of demineralized water was added
drop wise. Stirring was maintained for several hours to allow ethanol formed from
the hydrolysis of TEOS to evaporate. Finally, 3.1 g hydroﬂuoric acid (HF, Fluka,
47-51%) in 1.6 g of demineralized water was added to the gel, yielding a solid with
the molar composition; 1.0Si:0.005Sn:0.02Cl−:0.55TEA+:0.55F−:7.5H2O.
All samples were then homogenized and transferred to a Teﬂon-container placed
in a stainless steel autoclave. This was then placed at 140◦C for 14 days. The
solid was recovered by ﬁltration and washed with demineralized water, followed
by drying overnight at 80◦C in air. The organic template contained within the
material was removed by heating the sample at 2◦C/min to 550◦C in static air
and maintaining this temperature for 6 h.
Analysis:
Powder X-ray diﬀraction (XRD) patterns of the calcined samples were
measured on an X’Pert diﬀractometer (Philips) using Cu-Kα radiation. Elemental
composition of the prepared materials was measured using inductively coupled
plasma atomic emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES) on a Perkin Elmer model Optima
3000 (Varian Vista). Surface area and pore volume measurements were performed
using multipoint N2 adsorption/desorption on an Autosorb automatic surface area
and pore size analyzer (Quantachrome Instruments). The total surface area of
the samples was obtained using the BET method and the micropore volume was
calculated by the t-plot method using the Autosorb3 software.

A.5
β-Sn ( Si
Sn = 125) via Post-Treatment Proce-
dure.
Note: This synthesis procedure was not used for the production of the β-Sn used
in this project.
Synthesis:
The Sn-Beta zeolites were synthesized by Søren Tolborg using a mod-
iﬁed route described by Hammond et al., 2012.
Commercial zeolite Beta (Zeolyst, Si/Al 12.5, ammonium form) is calcined
(550◦C for 6 h) to obtain the H form (dealuminated form) and treated with 10 g of
concentrated nitric acid (Sigma-Aldrich, ≥65%) per gram of zeolite beta powder for
12 h at 80◦C. The resulting solid is ﬁltered, washed with ample water and calcined
(550◦C for 6 h) to obtain the dealuminated Beta. This solid is impregnated by
incipient wetness methodology with a Sn/Si ratio of 125. For this purpose, tin(II)
chloride (0.128 g, Sigma-Aldrich, 98%) is dissolved in water (5.75 mL) and added
to the dealuminated Beta (5 g). After the impregnation process, the samples are
dried 12 h at 110◦C and calcined again (550◦C for 6 h).
Analysis:
Powder X-ray diﬀraction (XRD) patterns of the calcined samples were
measured on an X’Pert diﬀractometer (Philips) using Cu-Kα radiation. Elemental
composition of the prepared materials was measured using inductively coupled
plasma atomic emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES) on a Perkin Elmer model Optima
3000 (Varian Vista). Surface area and pore volume measurements were performed
using multipoint N2 adsorption/desorption on an Autosorb automatic surface area
and pore size analyzer (Quantachrome Instruments). The total surface area of
the samples was obtained using the BET method and the micropore volume was
calculated by the t-plot method using the Autosorb3 software.
