Abstract  
Future building design concerns more holistic perspectives related to sustainability seen in a 
wider range of objectives and criteria. The developed framework can be applied during different 
project stages and assist in the consideration of the sustainability issues through support of 
decision-making and communication with relevant stakeholders.  
 
This study investigates the use of Multi-Criteria-Decision-Making (MCDM) tools in the design 
process. MCDM provides strong decision making in domains where the selection of best 
alternative is highly complex, as with sustainable building design [1]. The MCMD tool which has 
been used is the Technique for Order of Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution (TOPSIS), to 
evaluate 8 different building designs for a case study in a holistically sustainable way.  
 
A decision- matrix was conducted for the community residential building, Fællesbyg Køge Kyst 
located south of Copenhagen. A set of 18 indicators were included regarding their relevance for 
the case study, grouped in 3 categories: Environmental, Economic and Social aspects. The 
indicators were weighted by their importance, done by the author of this study (simulating the 
weighting of an engineer), architects from Vandkunsten and the building client. Two additional 
weightings were conducted to test the sensitivity of TOPSIS. One with equal weighting and the 
other by the %-difference between the highest and lowest value of each indicator.  
 
The result of this study reveals that the decision-makers included in this analysis had different 
prioritizations. The engineer and architect had, in general, the same prioritization, where the social 
aspects were of highest importance. The building client, however, had prioritized the economic 
aspects highest. In general, there was not much difference between the ideal solution regarding 
the different weightings, however, the differences between the scenarios regarding the closeness 
to the ideal solution were significant, especially when assessing what choice was made in reality, 
and what choice seemed like the ideal solution.

1.1 Motivation  
This project originates from an interest in integrated design processes and decision-making and 
how to use engineering knowledge in the design phase, creating more comfortable and 
sustainable building design, resulting in better solutions and a more progressive building sector.  
Decision-making regarding the design within the building industry, often involves various criteria 
of different natures. Some are qualitative, others quantitative, some are objective, others 
subjective, but ultimately, they should all be aggregated and handled holistically to support the 
decisions. 
 
The global drive towards sustainable development has resulted in an increasing level of pressure 
on building developers and designers, to produce buildings with a significantly higher level of 
environmental performance. Preeminent amongst these is the energy performance. Current 
expectations of energy performance pose a definite challenge to designers, especially within the 
constraints of minimal fees and the time pressure of the modern development process. In this 
discussion, it happens often that qualitative values e.g. social quality is neglected. 
 
1.2 Concept of Sustainability in the Building Sector 
Sustainability is a concept that plays an increasingly important role in the building sector. In 
recent decades there has been a focus on reducing the overall emissions from buildings, which 
increases the complexity of the design process [2]. More specialized knowledge, a higher 
common understanding and more cooperation between the stakeholders are required.  
 
Architects and engineers are beginning to increase their focus on improving interdisciplinarity in 
favour of better holistic designs, but it is often unclear how to achieve that and not fall back on old 
habits. Design teams need simpler and intuitive means of decision-making and communication to 
others. The need for a systematic and dynamic decision-making tool is quite urgent. The rapid 
development of sustainable buildings, which is highly complex, will require more and more 
professions to be a part of the decision makings regarding the future development of buildings.  
 
One of the most important elements to achieve sustainable development in the building sector is 
the balanced utilization of technologies, innovation and political strategies [3]. Furthermore, the 
design and construction of buildings concerning the environment, must become an establishment 
and a regular practice and not only supplementary, as it can be easily underprioritized.

1.3 Sustainability Development Paradigm  
Sustainability development refers to a dynamic process of one opinion or another, which means 
there is no exact definition. All societies are evolving bypassing the time to become more superior 
or inferior [3]. Hence our goals including vision, ambition and technical feasibility are all subject to 
change.  
Sustainability can be described as incontestable development of society and economy on a long-
term basis, within the framework of the carrying inclusion of Earth’s ecosystems. Sustainability is 
currently not stated explicitly in the Danish Building Regulation (BR18) [4]. A voluntary 
sustainability class have been proposed, but not published yet. The idea behind a voluntary 
sustainability class in the Building Regulations is inspired by the voluntary low-energy classes in 
the BR and Building Class 2020. The voluntary low-energy classes and Building Class 2020 have 
been essential to the development of energy-efficient products and solutions. These have 
improved the Danish building sector by lowering the energy consumption considerably [4].  
 
Building rating tools and certifications have emerged as instruments for measuring and assessing 
the building performance more holistically. Building certification systems are a phenomenon, 
which has increased the awareness of sustainable buildings and their environmental footprint. 
Building assessment and certifications have especially been developed to correspond to the 
knowledge and competences of specific user groups such as architects, engineers and 
contractors. 
 
A great variety of design principles, methods and assessment tools for sustainable building 
design are currently available. There are several sustainable building certifications on the market, 
which focus on the performance of buildings. Most of them have that in common, that they have a 
reference model, which is usually an average value of multiple buildings with the same function. 
To sustainably certificate a building is voluntarily today.  
 
Certification systems offer the possibility to measure and compare the sustainable performance of 
buildings by applying a set of quantifiable criteria [5]. The certification scores make it possible to 
compare buildings certified under the same system. One of the most popular certifications in 
Denmark is Deutsches Gesellschaft für Nachhaltiges Bauen (DGNB) certification, which is used 
as an indicator of a building’s sustainable potential [5]. A DGNB certification is very 
comprehensive and as a result of this, can be very costly to conduct [6], and therefore rarely 
prioritized in smaller building scales [7].  
 
Due to greater awareness and stricter requirements for our buildings, the need for including 
comprehensive analyses and simulations of different building design scenarios is necessary. One 
of the main obstacles to measure and assess a building’s sustainable performance, in a single 
undisputed tendency, is that involved stakeholders have divergent interests. Some impose 
different demands on how the building should perform, and another obstacle is the fact that we 
build less with raw materials and more with products and technologies [8].

1.3.1 COP25 - The Role of the Building Sector 
Back in 2015, the United Nations (UN) agreed that by 2020, they would sharpen their national 
climate targets to limit global temperature rises to 1.5 and a maximum of 2 degrees Celsius [9]. 
This was a very ambitious agreement but has unfortunately not been realised as well as hoped 
[10]. According to the Danish Transportation, Construction and Housing Authority, buildings in 
Europe contribute to 20-30% of the most crucial damages made to the environment, and 30% of 
waste generated in Denmark is connected to the building sector [11].  
 
1.3.2 Integrated Design Process 
The design process has become more knowledge-heavy and technology has created new 
opportunities for more detailed technical insight. Especially BIM, parametric design software and 
advanced-simulation software have moved the opportunity for technical insight in the early design 
stages [12]. The benefits of introducing technically knowledge as a basis for design decisions in 
the early design phases, aids that necessary and preferable changes can be made while the cost 
of changing a design is still low. The practices of the integrated design are still an evolving 
process and is not a matter of course in Danish architectural and engineering firms yet, but it is 
under development [2].  
 
In recent decades there has been a focus on how knowledge of topics, such as energy 
consumption and indoor climate, can be introduced in the early design stages, and digital 
simulation tools have played an essential role in the reduction of the operating energy. In a typical 
design process, some elements are defined and very intricate to change. One of these elements 
is, for example, the site’s, energy - and heating supply. So, if the site has access to district 
heating another heating supply would not be considered. These elements are typically defined on 
a local plan. The different stakeholders have been mapped out in Figure 1.1 to get an overview.

For more clarification of the design process, the different stakeholders are involved in the process

building design concerns more holistic perspectives related to sustainability, covering a wider

Newer workflows should be a mean of improving and developing buildings, to meet the needs

1.4 Fællesbyg - Køge Kyst (Community Building) 
Fællesbyg Køge Kyst is a brand-new housing project, with the community in focus. Most of the 
apartments are sold as a project sale, where all members influence the design of the apartment 
and the common areas. The residents of Fællesbyg Køge Kyst get among other things, access to 
a common room with kitchen facilities and other common areas, like the use of rooftops. The 
moving in date is planned to be in the first quarter of 2021 [13]. The initiator of the project is Billige 
Boliger (Society for Cheap Housing), the urban development company Køge Kyst and 
Vandkunsten Architects.

The area around the train station in Køge has been under large development in the last few years 
and will continue for several years more. The area is cheaper to build on compared to inner 
Copenhagen, and due to the new rail system which completed summer of 2019, the travel time is 
around 25 minutes from Køge Station to Copenhagen Central Station [13].

In the beginning, Vandkunsten wanted to use CLT (Cross Laminated Timber) as the structural 
material, which was an idea the occupants were fond of. As more parties joined the project some 
disagreements arose about the choice of CLT. It was estimated that a building system of wood 
would give a saving of 80% of CO2 emissions [15], however, the contractors reported that they 
would not be able to build with CLT and still meet the budget, which led to choosing concrete 
instead.  
 
In this housing project there will be 45 different apartments, ranging between 65 -170 m2 
dwellings with 2-4 rooms located either on the ground floor or the 1st, 2nd or 3rd floor. All 
apartments are custom designed, in collaboration between the future occupants and the 
architects, so each apartment will have a unique interior. Most apartments attached to a parking 
lot in the basement. The range of different apartment types attracts different family constructions, 
as the building will contain families with small children, but also seniors. Prices for buying ranges 
from 2.028.000 - 4.930.000 DKK, which cover the expense for the apartment, a storage room and 
common facilities, which among other things includes a common room with kitchen, guest rooms 
with private bathroom and toilet, workshop and access to 4 large roof terraces [13].  
 
This case study is used to investigate if the decision of the design would have been different if a 
set of indicators chosen in this report were used.

1.5 Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM)  
Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM), is a mathematical approach, which can be applied to 
many complex decisions. It is mostly applicable to solve problems that are characterized as a 
choice among alternatives. It has all the characteristics of a useful decision support tool: It helps 
focusing on what is important, logical and consistent and simple to use [16].  At its core MCDM 
accomplishes the following: 
• 
Divides the decision into smaller, more understandable parts. 
• 
Integrates the parts to produce a meaningful solution. 
Depending on which type of complex decisions wanted to be solved, different MCMD tools can 
be used. Some of the most used MCMD tools is SMART, AHP and TOPSIS [1]. The nature of the 
recommendations of one of those methods depends on the problem being addressed: choosing, 
ranking or sorting. In this thesis, the focus has been on using TOPSIS to help decision-makers 
regarding building designs. The Technique for Order of Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution 
(TOPSIS) selects the alternative closest geometric distance to the ideal solution and farthest from 
the anti-ideal alternative [17]. The selection of this technique has been based on the transparency 
and ease of use. In this study, TOPSIS have been used for linear analysis of different scenarios, 
but it is also possible to use TOPSIS to analyse complex dynamic solutions. 
1.6 Research Question  
Currently, there has been a focus on reducing the overall emissions from the building industry 
and in general the building design, which increases the complexity of the design process. The 
research questions are the following: 
• 
Which sustainable indicators are important for the decision-makers in a building design 
process?  
• 
How to numerically evaluate sustainable qualities which are not exactly numerical during 
the building design processes?  
• 
And how to provide strong decision making during the process where the selection of the 
best design alternative is highly complex? 
1.6.1 Validation of the Problem 
If the diversity of tools and systems is caused by the many interests of different stakeholders and 
actors. It would be relevant to identify the largest user group and focus on making their definition 
of sustainability more synchronized, but still possible to customize. Using TOPSIS to help 
stakeholders in their decision-making would aid in sustainability becoming more universal, as to 
make it easier to apply compared to current certifications, which can be limiting. TOPSIS can be 
relevant for consultants in Denmark such as architects, engineers, contractors, etc. Nevertheless, 
this study focuses solely on the design practices of all mentioned parties.

2.1 Sustainability Approach  
Sustainability is a concept that plays an increasingly important role in the building industry today. 
It was in connection with the publication of the United Nations report Our Common Future in 1987 
(the Brundtland Report) that the concept of sustainability was introduced. It presents three 
dimensions of sustainability: the environmental, social, and economic aspects [18]. The modern 
philosophy of systematically thinking and theories underlines our broader goals and shows us the 
necessity of pursuing multiple goals at the same time.  
 
Systems utilizing complex design based on properties of development of dynamic networks, 
where the global functionality emerges, and elements interact, adapt, iterate and evolve over 
time, is a suitable holistic approach to develop practices for addressing the complexity of reality 
in building design process. 
 
2.2 Holistic Design 
Sustainability is not only achieved by measuring a single aspect but is about considering and 
weighting several different conditions at once such as environmental imprint, working conditions 
and finances. The most sustainable solution, in any given situation, will always be a holistic 
consideration. Sustainability indicators for a building project can be selected from various lists, 
introduced by the government, sector, and community.  
 
2.2.1 Indoor Climate in a Larger Perspective  
On average, a Dane spends around 62% of their time inside their private home [19]. That is 
almost 2/3 of our time, where we are exposed to negative and positive influences of our homes.  
But how large is the influence of our homes regarding poor health in general? The health issues 
regarding indoor climate are easiest to measure compared to, for example, psychological health 
issues.  
 
Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) and some particles in indoor air are harmful to our health. 
These substances are found anywhere in buildings, either in materials or as a result of the use of 
the building e.g. cooking. The health effects of VOCs and particles are asthma, cancer, 
cardiovascular diseases, hormone disorders, impaired fertility and many more [19].  
 
That the indoor climate in our building influences us to a great extent, becomes clear, when 
considering that there currently are more than 1.5 million Danes who suffer from some form of 
allergy mostly caused by poor indoor climate [20].  The expenses related to allergies are 
estimated at up to 290.000 annual sick days in Denmark, which ends up costing millions of DKK 
in a year [21]. All these workdays lost due to sickness are not necessarily associated with a poor 
==PAGE:23==
22/122 
indoor climate regarding VOCs, but it does show, in perspective, that improving the indoor 
climate has a noticeable positive effect. Not just for the individual occupant, but also on a societal 
level in general.  
 
The way the industry considers the issue about degassing and VOCs in buildings is by having a 
minimum ventilation rate to ensure those gassing compounds, including the CO2 we humans 
produce, are exhausted. In the case of new buildings, there is a quarantine period after the 
building is constructed, where the occupants aren’t allowed to move in. This quarantine period is 
to ensure a lower concentration of the particles, but the source of degassing isn’t only the building 
elements but also e.g. new furniture. This action can lower the concentrations but does not 
eliminate later effects.  
 
2.2.2 Socials Value 
Regardless of how good the home's indoor climate is on paper, the behaviour and habits of 
residents are of enormous importance regarding the actual indoor quality as it can change 
drastically [22]. This makes pre-occupancy evaluation, and in general arranging and considering 
the occupant in the design process, very relevant when wishing to achieve healthy and happy 
occupants.  
 
There are many social aspects of the design in this project. Some of these social values are of 
large importance for the building, however, not all of significance, regarding the design scenarios, 
were investigated as they would be equivalent.  
 
Housing and overall living patterns are always changing. A demographic conducted by Statistics 
Denmark (Danmarks Statistik) states that there are currently 37 different family forms in Denmark 
[23]. People live alone or in groups, and generally, all have a longer life expectancy than ever 
before. People change their way of living over time, and therefore rethinking our forms of housing 
in the future is necessary. The Fællesbyg building is designed with different type and sizes of 
apartments due to the customization. The future proof factor of the Fællesbyg project will be 
essential to investigate if the design scenarios change the layout of the building such as the 
limitation of transformation by the construction and/or ventilation regarding future scenarios 
implying the change of needs of the occupants and the changing surrounding environment.  
 
2.2.3 Life Cycle Costing strategy  
Life Cycle Costing (LCC) is based on a total cost principle of the building, including all relevant 
costs and revenues. The holistic approach must ensure that the quality and long-term 
consequences of the choice of solutions are assessed [24]. For most homeowners, housing is the 
largest financial investment of their life. During the lifetime of residential housing, there can be 
many unforeseen expenses, which can concern and stress the occupants.

2.3 Decision-Matrix: Indicators Included in TOPSIS  
The main goal of this report is to investigate if the mathematic model TOPSIS can be used in the 
decision making of early design phases, regarding a sustainable building design.  
 
A holistic approach was taken to get an effective overall result in this case. The chosen indicators 
vary a lot, however, chosen due to each describing an important aspect of the quality of a 
building. The decision matrix used in this study is including the 3 aspects of sustainability with 18 
indicators: It is prime that a starting point of indicators for TOPSIS is chosen at the beginning of a 
design process in relation with the building client, the design team and if possible, the occupants.

•

• 
Formation Potential of Tropospheric Ozone Photochemical Oxidants (POCP) 
•

2.4 Scenario Development  
The scenario developments are a fundamental decision, and therefore important to keep dynamic 
throughout the design stages since every other decision afterwards is going to depend on this.  
 
The scenarios were developed in a pragmatic sense. Studies by architectural studios in Denmark, 
have figured that when focusing on the climate perspective, the largest CO2 saving regarding 
LCA studies is obtained, when focusing on the primary constructions, such as bearing walls and 
slabs [25]. Which is why one of the main focuses in the scenarios’ development of this study, is 
regarding the structural elements. It is important that the scenarios are defined in detail and 
includes as many of the differences between the scenarios. Some of the scenarios are 
hypothetical and developed regarding what would be the most relevant for this specific case.  
 
In the first proposition, four scenarios were defined regarding the design process and the pre-
occupancy questionnaire. These 4 scenarios were developed to investigate the current design 
with a concrete structure and gypsum on the interior surfaces made by the architects. Calculation 
by MOE’s engineers showed trouble meeting the requirements regarding overheating (Appendix 
B01). Therefore, a modification of this scenario was considered, concrete without gypsum, to test 
if it would improve the indoor quality. These scenarios with concrete were compared to exposed 
and gypsum wrapped CLT.  
 
These four scenarios were after the second iteration doubled to 8. These additional scenarios 
were developed to investigate how a reduction of the window area could improve thermal 
comfort. There are of course many different alterations which could have been investigated, 
however normally when there are issues with thermal comfort, the following actions can be used 
to improve the indoor climate:  
• 
Use cooling  
• 
Add external solar shading  
• 
Reduce the window area  
Using cooling either in the ventilation system or as district cooling is very costly and will make it 
difficult to meet the BR18 regulation regarding energy consumption. Therefore, this action was 
neglected. Using external solar shading was also neglected, due to the harsh harbour climate 
and the cost of the elements. The only action left was a reduction of the window areas. This will 
change the architectural quality of the design but might help in making it easier to meet the 
restrictions regarding overheating in BR18. Therefore, a minimum reduction of the window area 
was investigated in this study to ensure that, especially the CLT scenarios, will meet the BR18 
requirements regarding overheating.

4.1.1 
Pre-Occupancy Evaluation 
To measure or clarify the social values there are these following methods to measure qualitative 
qualities: Interviews, observations and questionnaires [31]. In this project, the methods to assess 
the social values was done by a questionnaire, used as a pre-occupancy evaluation of the future 
occupants, and an interview with the chairman of the Køge Kyst Fællesbyg union.  
 
Pre-Occupancy Evaluation (PrOE) of buildings has been developing as a distinctive discipline for 
over 40 years and traditionally been used concerning energy, social values and architectural 
quality [32]. PrOE is one of the best tools to evaluate social sustainability and the corresponding 
indicators of the design phase [31]. To clarify the social sustainability in this case study, a PrOE 
was conducted through a questionnaire, to clarify what the occupants prioritized the most, 
throughout the project.  
 
4.2 Methodology of PrOE 
A questionnaire is a good tool to create data on social sustainability in an uncomplicated way. 
There are a few sources of error when creating a questionnaire, however, some of these can be 
avoided. One of the issues when conducting a questionnaire is if the formulation of the questions, 
is not objective enough, and thereby interferes with the results involuntarily. A way to decrease 
the uncertainty of the results is to analyse multiple questions, where the same content is 
presented in different ways.

5.2.1 
Indoor Environmental Qualities - Thermal Mass 
The thermal mass of a material describes the ability to absorb or release heat. Heavy materials 
such as concrete and stone take longer to heat, compared to lighter materials such as wood. 
Therefore, heavy materials will help stabilizing the indoor operative temperature.  
 
In buildings, thermal mass will store the passive solar heat coming in through the windows and 
release it during the night when the building gets cooled down, thus reducing the need for 
heating. And extensionally in the summer season, overheating problems will be reduced as a high 
thermal mass will take longer to heat up. Thermal mass in this situation is best applied over a 
large area rather than in large volumes or thicknesses, as to best store and release heat which is 
applied to the surface. 
 
Studies have shown that the heat accumulation in general increases with greater thickness of a 
material. The increased heat accumulation is close to proportional to the thickness of the material, 
resulting in that the effect of heat accumulation in a material is greatest in the first 5-10 cm of the 
thickness [36].  
 
In order to understand the physical effects of thermal mass, a short description of the related 
properties is stated in the following segments:  
 
Specific Heat Capacity of a material is the amount of heat needed to raise the 
temperature of 1 kg of the material by 1 K (or by 1 °C). A good insulator has a higher 
Specific Heat Capacity because it takes time to absorb more heat before it heats up 
(temperature rising) to transfer the heat [37]. 
 
Thermal Conductivity measures the ease of which heat can travel through a material 
by conduction. A good insulator will have a low thermal conductivity, as conduction 
is the main form of heat transfer through insulation [37].  
 
Density refers to the mass per. unit volume of material [kg/m3]. A high-density 
material maximizes the overall weight of the space it occupies. A low value leads to 
thermal diffusivity whereas a high density leads to a high thermal mass [37].

5.3.1 
Physical Properties of CLT  
Wood is an anisotropic material, which means its physical property differs when measured in 
different directions. The thermal mass of wood depends mainly on the type of wood and how the 
wood is cut. If the wood is cut along the fibres (end grain) the thermal conductivity is much 
higher, than if the wood is cut across the fibres (Figure 5.2). In general, the thermal conductivity 
differs ~50% depending on how the wood is cut for most tree species [37].  
 
  
Figure 5.2: Illustration of grains. 
Figure 5.3: Conventional assembly layup of 
cross-laminated timber [41]. 
 
Cross-laminated timber is made of several layers of wood boards glued crosswise together 
(Figure 5.3). The number of layers varies, however, there must be a minimum of three layers. This 
makes CLT very strong and stable for construction. The CLT market is quite new and is still under 
heavy development [42]. There are many different types of CLT, some glued together, while 
others use dowels to assemble the boards. In this study the most typically used CLT in Europe 
was chosen, using the method where the CLT boards are glued together. 
 
The size of the produced CLT-panels is essentially unlimited during the production, but 
transportability limits the size. The usage of CLT was introduced in Germany and Austria in 1990, 
and the demand has increased significantly over the last few years [42]. CLT can be used as a 
substitute for concrete and steel construction.

5.3.2 
Sources of Error 
It is impossible to simulate the real world with 100% accuracy. A few sources of errors were noted 
when simulating the indoor climate, as IDA ICE does not take the different resistances between 
the layers in the wall structure into account.  
 
There are different types of CLT some with properties as homogeneous or inhomogeneous layers, 
typically used in Europe as inhomogeneous. In IDA ICE, CLT is only possible to implement as a 
homogeneous layer. This simplification of the CLT element can influence the result of relative 
humidity and other comforts indicators, since the calculation of heat transfer is different. It is 
assumed that the influence is minimal when focusing on indoor temperature and energy 
consumption.  
 
To include additional indoor climate indicators, a secondary simulation of the hygrothermal 
properties could be done in external programs such as HEAT2 etc. HEAT2 is used for two-
dimensional transient and steady-state heat transfer. These kinds of simulations are usually used 
when there is a need for more accurate simulations and is rather time and data consuming and 
therefore left out in this study.

5.6 Discussion  
To investigate the concept and phenomenon of thermal mass and heating accumulation for the 
different scenarios, a close up of the operating temperatures have been conducted for all the 
scenarios (Figure 5.5). The following graph has been created to illustrate the thermal mass for one 
of the hottest days in Denmark (week 30 at the end of July). This is to investigate how high the 
temperature peeks are for the scenarios, when they occur and their duration.  
 
The graph shows the operative temperature in the same apartment for all scenarios. The graph 
shows that SC 02.02 with an exposed concrete inner surface is performing the best in the sense 
of thermal comfort. The temperature change is steadier and the difference between the highest 
and lowest temperature is the smallest, compared to other scenarios.  
 
SC 01.02 with exposed CLT elements is performing the worst in the sense of thermal comfort in 
general. But when investigated when these high temperatures occur for the concrete scenarios 
the highest temperature occurs from 8-12. However, the highest temperatures occur from 17-4 
o’clock for the scenarios with CLT. Which makes CLT constructions a perfect fit for office 
buildings, where the using hours is typically in-between 8-17 o’clock. But for a housing building, 
where it is assumed that the building is in use 24/7, a concrete structure is preferred, where the 
temperature differences are the lowest and the temperature is acceptably low in the evening and 
night, where it can be assumed that the house is fully the occupied.

window area. Which only fails in meeting the requirement for overheating hours > 28 °C, by 4.7

requirement for energy consumption. Showing that the use of CLT is not very far off, considering 
how close the results are to meeting the requirements.

6.1 Introduction  
The scenarios investigated in this LCA study is for hybrid CLT or concrete as the construction, 
with the original window size and with the reduction at 17% of the window area, with or without 
gypsum on the interior. 
 
Some assumptions have been made in order to conduct this LCA study. Some assumptions have 
been made to simplify the study since specific data have not been possible to find. Related to 
constructing with CLT there can occur waste product when customizing the elements at the site. 
This waste has not been included in this LCA study.  
 
In the process of finding a suitable EPD (Environmental Product Declaration) for CLT elements, 
many different EPDs were investigated. It was found that the difference between CLT produced in 
the US compared to Europe, has a much higher overall negative impact. Most of the EPDs were 
for a functional unit of 1 m3 of cross-laminated timber made of spruce, from cradle-to-grave and 
with a reference lifespan of 60 years. This lifespan is based on what there is proof of in reality. 
CLT elements as known today have only been monitored for 60 years, and therefore don’t have 
laboratory proof that the lifespan could be higher.  
 
When looking at SBi’s lifespan tables in SBi 2013:30, the lifespan for construction wood in external 
walls and as structural elements is 120 years [44]. To make the scenarios more comparable, the 
lifespan for CLT is assumed to be the same as for construction wood and construction material in 
general, at 120 years. Operating with the lifespan predicted for CLT, there will be a much higher 
advantage for CLT, compared to concrete. The reasoning for this is if the lifespan of the building 
is 120 years, and the construction elements only have a lifespan of 60 years, then all the 
construction material must be replaced when 60 years have passed. As known, wood is an 
effective material to bind and store carbon dioxide (CO2), so the production of the elements has a 
positive impact on the GWP. In effect, a lower lifespan of 60 years, will have a more positive 
impact on the GWP in the LCA. Concerning the different lifespans and practical knowledge, it was 
chosen to use a lifespan of 120 years for CLT. This assumption is more realistic and conservative; 
since a building rarely remains if the whole construction must be replaced. 
 
The functional unit (FU) in this case was defined as the whole building, with a few small 
exceptions e.g. the foundation. All scenarios have the same FU and the same lifespan at 120 
years.

6.1.1 
Goal & Scope  
Life cycle assessment is performed when assessing the overall environmental impact, in this case 
of different design scenarios for the same building case. This is done to compare the 
environmental imprint of the different scenarios, to determine which scenario is the most 
environmentally sustainable.  
 
The target audience for this analysis is the architects, whom in the early design stages want an 
evaluation of the different design proposals, to be able to make deliberate decisions. Since the 
detail level in the early design phase can be very minimal, it is necessary to make some 
assumptions and simplify the Inventory Analysis (LCI).  
 
The energy consumption used in this LCA is simulated for a corner apartment, which is assumed 
to be the worst-case situation regarding energy consumption and thermal comfort (Section 5. 
Thermal Comfort & Energy Consumption).  
6.1.2 
System Boundaries 
One of the commonly used LCA tools in the Danish building industry is LCAbyg, designed by SBi 
[35]. This is specially designed for buildings and uses the German library Ecoinvent and includes 
the processes stated in Figure 6.1 [35].   
 
LCAbyg is designed to be used in the early design phase, and gives the results of 7 environment 
impact categories, which are commonly used for buildings. The LCA method is based on the 
quantification of environmental impacts of a product throughout its life cycle, from "cradle to 
grave". In general terms, ISO 14040 standards establish the stages for LCA application [45].  
 
These life cycle stages are defined in the European standard EN 15978:2011 [46], that represents 
a methodological guide for the quantification of environmental impacts of buildings [46]. The 
system boundaries from DS/EN 15978: 2011 are used, with some exceptions, however possible to 
add on to the LCA further in Figure 6.1.  
==PAGE:64==
63/122 
Figure 6.1: System boundaries included in the LCA [47]. 
The rest of the modules are neglected due to lack of representative data, uncertainties, little 
significance or similarity between the environmental imprint of the processes, which does not 
make a difference when making a comparison [24].  
 
A4 (Transport) has not been included since it is assumed to have a minimal effect on the overall 
results, as both concrete and CLT can be produced within Europe, so the transportation will be 
similar. Considering the construction phase A5 includes many assumptions regarding how the 
planning phase is, which can be very complex to determine. If the constructors don’t plan the 
construction wisely, it can lead to concrete and CLT elements getting exposed to hazard weather. 
To continue the construction phase, it can be necessary to dry out the concrete elements, which 
can be very time and energy-consuming. The same phenomena can be seen in CLT 
constructions. If the CLT slabs get wet, it can be necessary to plane the wet surface of the slab 
more than usually needed when building with CLT, a process where you remove the upper layers 
of the wooden material. But both processes: The drying of concrete, and the additional planing of 
the CLT slabs, can be ignored if the construction is planned correctly and/or the construction site 
is covered so it isn’t exposed to the weather.  
 
B1 (Use), B2 (Repair), B3 (Maintenance) and have been neglected due to missing data. With a 
building lifespan at 120 years, it can be necessary to renovate (B4) the building, and this could 
have a large effect on the overall LCA results. However, it has not been possible to find sources 
on how to estimate a renovation of the structure in the future or predict the necessity of it, so this 
has been neglected. This will in most cases mean a completely new LCA study at that point in 
time.

6.2 Method - Inventory Analysis  
The data used for this study were extracted directly from the Revit model, and elements not drawn 
or included in the model were estimated and added later manually. To export the quantity of 
materials from Revit into Excel, the plugin Quantification - Buildingbook Extension was used.  
 
The exported material and components were used as input data in LCAbyg. To minimize the time 
spent finding specific EPDs, the ÖKOBAUDAT (version 2019-III from 29.05.2019) [48][49] 
database was used in cases where Ecoinvent didn’t have datasets.  
 
LCAbyg is structured so different building elements are collected in main categories. This can 
influence the result of the LCA since some materials lifespan will be affected depending on where 
the material is placed in the element. In general, materials exposed to harsh weather such as in 
harbour areas will have a shortened lifespan. It is assumed that the amount of concrete from the 
Revit model can be replaced in relation at 1:1 with CLT, as mentioned earlier. The CLT element 
used for scenario 01 and 03 is the same type and the data is provided by a Norwegian CLT EPD 
[50]. This specific CLT was used since it seemed to be the most plausible, as the place of 
production is in Europe: Jelgava in Latvia.  
 
The Revit model is quite detailed, but some material layers are as mentioned not defined. A 
manual check of these material layers was necessary, to ensure the amount of material was 
correct. The reinforcement is not drawn as well as the tile adhesive. Neither is the pumps needed 
to ensure the minimum ventilation rate, the floor heating or the solar panels e.g. These were 
assumed, based on the energy consumption MOE had made for SC 02.01 with a concrete 
structure.  
 
The drainage, sanitation system and electrical services such as wiring and sockets have not been 
considered in the LCA analyses, due to the lack of data on these matters. This simplification will 
not affect the LCA results as they will be similar in all scenarios. The assumption regarding the 
LCA study can be seen in Appendix C06.  
 
6.2.1 
Lifespan  
The lifespan of a building component depends in practice on various factors. Utilizing 
probabilistic models provides the opportunity for a more realistic description of the lifespan of a 
given building type. In SBI 2013:30 a definition of what defines the overall lifespan is described as 
following [44]:  
• 
Technical lifespan, including material quality, design, indoor and outdoor influences, 
conditions of use, maintenance, etc. 
• 
Functional lifespan, including social changes, technological development, changed 
lifestyle, urbanization, etc.

• 
Economic lifespan, including business cycle, interest rate, labour prices of building 
materials, material properties, design, etc. 
• 
Aesthetic lifespan, including lifestyle and fashion, etc.  
To determine the lifespan of the different building components and materials, a list of typical 
lifespans in Danish weather conditions was used [44]. This list was at first created for insurance 
companies but is now used as a guideline in the industry. 
   
As mentioned, the location of the building is near Køge harbour, which is a very harsh 
environment for the elements and materials exposed to the weather conditions. The elements and 
materials exposed the most are the same for all scenarios, such as windows and the façade 
cladding. In this case, the harsh harbour environment can be neglected, since those elements 
and materials exposed the most, are the same of all the scenarios, and won’t make a difference 
when comparing scenarios.

7.1 Introduction  
Qualities such as aesthetic, functional, environmental can be challenging to assess, if the lighting, 
acoustics, air and smells are uncomfortable and cause discomfort.  
 
A bad indoor climate is easy to relate to. It can be seen, heard and smelled, and it affects the 
people occupying the building. In the workplace, poor indoor climate costs a lot of money each 
year in reduced productivity and creativity of workers, and in homes, especially those who are 
sensitive, for example, kids. Whom are more vulnerable to the development of symptoms and 
diseases [52].   
 
Chemical substances released to the indoor climate can normally be referenced to as Volatile 
Organic Compounds (VOCs) and are important factors of Indoor Air Quality (IAQ). VOCs are 
emitted as gases from certain solids or liquids. VOCs include a variety of chemicals, some of 
which may have short- and long-term adverse health effects [53].  
 
Common indoor sources of VOCs include paint thinners, wood preservatives, adhesives, 
detergents and much more. One prominent irritant is formaldehyde, which is found in high 
concentrations in adhesives used to bind building materials such as plywood [53]. The VOCs 
from wooden elements are minimal compared with other materials. Formaldehyde can also 
evaporate from paint and chemicals used for impregnation.   
 
The VOCs emanating from a product dissipate over time as the chemicals vaporize. VOCs from 
paint dissipate quickly with most degassing occurring during the first 6 months after application. 
Other sources, such as particleboards may continue to degas for more than 20 years. A way to 
minimize the degassing into the indoor atmosphere is by sealing materials with a high VOC 
concentration. The EPA reports health risks from VOCs including eye, nose, and throat irritation, 
headaches and loss of coordination, nausea, damage to the liver, kidney (Figure 7.1) [53]. Some 
VOCs are suspected or known to cause cancer in humans.  
 
Figure 7.1: Illustration of typical health risks from VOCs.  
 
This is an important factor to consider in the design phase, especially when it is seen that VOCs 
have a high prioritization for the future occupants (Section 4. Pre-Occupancy/Questionnaire). 
Normally it is considered that most VOCs can be exhausted through a mechanical ventilation 
system. Since there is a relation between degassing from materials, and the rise of indoor 
temperature and the CO2 concentration.  
==PAGE:75==
74/122 
Other studies have found that levels of VOCs are on average 2 to 5 times higher indoors 
compared to the level outside. During and for several hours immediately after certain activities, 
such as paint stripping, the concentration level can increase to over 1.000 times of the 
background outdoor concentration [53]. This can have a high impact on our health and might not 
be possible to get rid of only by filtrating the air.  
In this study, the effects of VOCs from the scenarios are simulated for Human Health and 
Carcinogens. The exhaust ventilation has been neglected for this study since it is assumed to be 
the same in all scenarios and not possible to simulate in SimaPro.

8.1 Introduction to LCC 
This Life Cycle Costing (LCC) study aims to be able to compare the different scenarios and find 
the most beneficial scenario. LCC implies an overall assessment of the cost of construction and 
operation of a building in every cost aspect.  
 
The scheme thus settles the most optimal solution, as to not use too cheap construction costs and 
poor installations that leads to more expensive operation time. This will make the cheap 
construction the most expensive, seen over a longer period of time.  
 
The costs included in the section are those which are different for the scenarios: construction cost 
for the bearing elements, surface material and the different window areas. Using life-cycle 
assessment early in the planning process enables the optimization of building lifetime costs and 
profitability. By devising and evaluating different alternatives, it is possible to identify which 
parameters have the greatest influence, and thereby optimize the building's design, thus financial 
resources and the profitability of the entire building lifespan, can be improved as much as 
possible, as well as identifying the full worth of an element.  
 
8.2 Method and General Assumptions  
The life cycle costing analysis was made in LCCbyg, which is a software tool that calculates the 
total economy and produces a clear overview of lifetime costs for an entire building and individual 
building parts. There is no connection between the database in LCAbyg and LCCbyg, which 
means that the material quantities must be converted so they fit the description in LCCbyg. This 
conversion had to be made manually. Most of the prices were found in Nybyggeri - Bygningsdele 
2019 - Sigma Enterprise made by Molio. The LCC calculation for the amount can be seen in 
Appendix E01 - and the simulation files in Appendix E02.  
 
The period of consideration of the LCC calculation is 50 years, based on the DGNB system [55]. 
The lifespan of the building at 120 years could have been used as well, but typically there is a 
differentiation between the lifespan of the building and the period of consideration for an LCC 
calculation. The is due to the overall economy is considered by a shorter period than the LCA, 
because the discount rate means that spendings far in the future have a small effect on the 
present value [55]. If the full lifespan of the building at 120 years had been used as the period of 
consideration then uncertainties would have increased, since the calculation is simplified and 
does not consider different future scenarios either if, for example, maintenance cost would 
change throughout the lifecycle.   
 
The chosen calculation principle was using a discount rate and fixed prices, which is an interest 
rate that has been adjusted to remove the effect of expected or actual inflation. The general data

9.1 Introduction to Technique for Order Preference by

solution. It is built on the assumption of having “m” alternative solutions (referred to as scenarios

consists of linear algebra. Each indicator is given a certain weighting according to its importance. 
For each indicator it is also specified whether an indicator is preferred to be high or low, and 
hence referred to as a beneficial attribute or a negative attribute respectively.  
TOPSIS is a seven steps algorithm as shown below.

different scenarios/alternatives.  
Step 02: Normalize the decision matrix.  
 
Step 03: Calculate the weighted normalized decision 
matrix.  
Step 04: Determine the ideal and anti-ideal solution for 
each indicator.  
Step 05: Calculate the separation measures. 
 
Step 06: Calculate/ compute the relative closeness to 
the ideal solution.  
Step 07: Rank the preference order.

9.3 Results of TOPSIS Evaluation  
In the classical TOPSIS method, it is assumed that the ratings of alternatives and weights are 
represented by numerical data and the problem is solved by a single decision-maker. Complexity 
arises when there are more than one decision-maker. 
 
To make great use of TOPSIS and other MCMD tools, it is important to evaluate which type of data 
and indicators are available and of importance, to solve this multi-criteria decision. There are 
several different versions of TOPSIS available and different expansions, which can be helpful to 
solve multi-criteria decisions, where data is fuzzy or dynamic. In this specific case, the data 
collected and created was linear data as well as the indicators. However, the problem of 
choosing an appropriate method still exists. TOPSIS helps in defining a solution space regarding 
the indicators and their respective weighting, then the scenario closest to fulfil all the indicators, is 
crowned as the ideal solution, with other solutions ranked correspondingly. 
9.3.1 
Input-data in TOPSIS  
In the previous sections, the input data to the TOPSIS decision-matrix have been found.  
A decision matrix is a list of values in rows and columns that allows the decision-makers to 
systematically identify and analyse the information. The matrix allows visual comparison of 
possible solutions (referenced to as scenarios) by weighing their variables based on importance. 
Elements of a decision matrix show decisions based on certain decision indicators. The matrix is 
useful when looking at large masses of decision factors and assessing each factor's relative 
significance. 
 
The decision-matrix is created with generic input data and weighting. It is necessary when 
wanting to use MCMD in the early design processes and afterwards since the data is typically not 
accurate.  
 
Decision matrix techniques are used to define attributes, weigh them, and appropriately sum the 
weighted attributes to give holistic a relative ranking among all design alternatives. Note that, in 
practice, attributes are weighted as a numeric figure based on a prescribed ranking system for 
individual design alternatives. In some contexts, such as design-optimization, attributes are also 
called design objectives, which are to be maximized or minimized, or constraint functions, which 
must be kept within limits. In general, attributes are also referred to as design criteria or decision 
criteria as they are the evaluated and limiting factors of a design.

9.3.2 
Weighting of the Indicators 
The weighting of the different indicators has a large influence on the overall result. The weighting 
was obtained separately by the involved parties: the architects, building clients and the author of 
this study, simulated the weighting of an engineer. Another aspect which could be interesting is to 
see how the involved parts weight the indicators based their importance from their perspective. 
 
An optimum goal is to make the expert group get to a consensus about the weighting of the 
different indicators, so the results are as accurate as possible. The results of using TOPSIS must 
be done very holistically by the involvement of the indicators, but also the weighting of the 
indicators. There are different ways to conclude the weighting. One of the most frequently used is 
the centroid weighting system, where the weighing form the different parties is converted into a 
mean/average weighting. This can be practised so all the included parties have equal impact on 
the overall result/ideal solution. 
 
To ensure transparency in this study the ideal solution regarding the different weightings by the 
parties is illustrated separately. In this study, the classical TOPSIS method is investigated for a 
single decision-maker and 5 different weight sources have been considered. In this way, the 
different weighting conducted by different parties can be compared to each other.  
The result is found for 2 tests weightings and 3 different decision-makers: 
•

• 
W02. Weighting based on the difference between the max and min value for 
each indicator, based on all scenarios’ value.  
• 
W03. Weighting by the author, simulating an Engineer. 
•

•

•

sustainable scenario? 
•

In this section, a discussion of the sensitivity of TOPSIS concerning the variation of weightings 
was reported. The main objectives of this study assess the sensitivity of the TOPSIS method 
regarding the parameter weights and proving the feasibility and reliability of the TOPSIS method 
for this assessment of the best design solution. 
 
The weighting systems W01 - same weighting and W02- the difference in MAX/MIN value, are 
both conducted to analyse the sensitivity of TOPSIS. The ideal solution regarding W01, where all 
indicators have gotten the same weighing, comes to the same conclusion as the W02, W03, W04

switching between doing well and poorly. In this case, it ends up in equalizing the differences that 
are there, but this is a coincidence. This will not always be applicable if the number of indicators 
change or the weighting is given in favour of another aspect, then result will change as well.

10.1 Discussion 
The performance evaluation and ranking of modern building-design is a comprehensive process, 
in which multiple assessments and simulations are required to be considered simultaneously. 
There can be many uncertainties regarding analysing the early design phases. In most cases it is 
more of an assessment, rather than precise values as an authority project. It is important to use 
tools and methods which in the early design-stages are sufficient and flexible enough to 
accommodate a rapidly changing design process. 
 
10.2 Integrated Design  
Designing energy-efficient buildings with a good indoor environment involves elements of 
expertise deriving from multiple professions such as architects, civil, mechanical and electrical 
engineers [12]. With the current emphasis on sustainability, including building energy and indoor 
environment, design requirements from the involved parties have become more important in the 
early design stages. 
 
The current building regulation ensures a low common denominator, be stating restrictions for the 
indoor quality, which in some cases can function as an obstruction, as seen in this report. Some 
of the scenarios chosen as the ideal solution, were not able to meet the requirements in BR18. So, 
an otherwise innovative design solution, which excels at the indicators in TOPSIS, but might have 
been eliminated due to not meeting BR18 fully. This makes the regulation seem narrowminded 
and limiting in relation to an innovative design process.  
10.2.1 Thermal Comfort Requirements 
Concerning the larger windows and glass façade to ensure good natural daylight is the indoor 
temperature going to rise. This is especially an issue in the summer period and concerning global 
warming, it can be expected to rise every year. As mentioned, the way to ensure good thermal 
comfort stated BR18 is by having the possibility to open the windows and ensure natural 
ventilation and that the temperatures don’t exceed 27 °C with 100 hours and 28 °C with 25 hours 
for housing. But thermal comfort is much more diffuse and maybe it is too simplified in the Danish 
building regulation. In this study some of the scenarios didn’t meet the requirements over 
overheating hour by ~3 hours and would risk been eliminated due to that.  
 
To support this statement, an investigate of the current Norwegian building regulation (TEK17) 
have been conducted. Since it is assumed that the Norwegian weather is lookalike to the Danish 
climate. Where the requirement for thermal comfort is defined in a more dynamic way [61] 
compared with the Danish regulation.

10.2.2 VOCs & degassing  
In SimaPro it is not possible to simulate the effect of VOCs with the minimum mechanical exhaust 
ventilation in the apartment. The ventilation can be considered in USEtox, which is a scientific 
consensus model endorsed by the UNEP/SETAC Life Cycle Initiative for characterizing human 
and ecotoxicological impacts of chemicals [62]. An adjusted USEtox program has been 
developed to analyse VOCs from building materials in a volume (e.g. a room or apartment) with 
mechanical ventilation. This is however yet to be launched.  
 
Once a cocktail of different chemical substances is mixed, it can be difficult afterwards to 
determine which different ingredients have contributed. Individually, the compounds can be 
measured and tested, but once mixed it becomes difficult and comprehensive, because of the 
compounds interacting with each other.  
 
This is also the case when building a house, and various building materials and components are 
assembled crosswise. Even otherwise sustainable building materials can adversely affect the 
indoor climate. For example when maintaining wooden windows with linseed oil, both the wooden 
material and linseed oil are natural materials and not harmful for the indoor environment, but 
together they can be irritating or harmful for us humans [63]. Linseed oil is a natural product 
extracted from flax seeds.

10.3 Critic of the DGNB certification  
Deutches Gesellschaft für Nachhaltiges Bauen (DGNB) was developed in 2009 in Germany by 
the German government and the German Sustainable Building Council [7]. DGNB consists of 6 
main categories: 
 
Figure 10.1: Weighting factors of the DGNB categories from the 2020 international version [64]. 
 
Based on the weighting of the categories, a project can be certified either as bronze, silver, gold 
or diamond. It requires a minimum score in each category to obtain the certification, why a high 
average score is not qualifying. This can give unfair management of the project, just meeting the 
minimum score of e.g. bronze or being close to gold but not achieving it.  
 
The first 3 categories are each weighted 22.5% of the total score, and the Technical quality at 
15% and Process quality at 12.5%, lastly the Site quality takes up 5% [65]. The categories consist 
of subtopics, called criteria, which are weighed differently with a factor. The criteria are thus 
prioritized by their significance. But the categories with the same weighting do not have the same 
number of criteria. Which mathematically means it is more difficult to fulfil categories with fewer 
criteria compared to categories with more criteria. In other words, this means there is a higher 
probability to fulfil the requirements for categories like the environment, compared with the 
economic, since there are 11 criteria in the category of environment and only 4 in economic. 
10.3.1 Social Sustainability as a Qualitative Parameter  
Another issue is that some social indicators in DGNB are based on technical matters: the access 
to natural daylight and if there is view to nature. These indicators are in some matters technical 
not social indicators, and there is not enough to cover the social matters. To follow up this critic of 
==PAGE:109==
108/122 
the social indicators in the DGNB system, a case study done by Jan Johansson will be presented 
[66]:   
 
Økohuse 99, which is a project where two identical residential buildings who are performing good 
regarding LCA are sold to two different housing companies. The case studies are Ikast, 
Lærkehaven III in Lystrup (Case 1) and Grøndalsvænge in Copenhagen (Case 2). The cases are 
the following: Case 1, the housing company wants the occupants to write a personal application 
to get a residential, and case 2 where the company does not require anything, so the apartments 
is rented out randomly.  
 
The effect of these different approaches is that the building performs differently regarding social 
quality. The people who did not apply for this housing project with an application were not happy 
about the large glass façades and wanted more privacy, so they tended to block the windows 
internally all day. This led to a significant difference in the energy consumption, even though the 
buildings were identical. Those who had not applied for these building and blocked the windows 
did also use much more energy on heating and artificial lighting. None of these ways were the 
intention of the architect and engineers designing this housing project. The conclusion of this PhD 
project by Jan Johansson [66] is to apply the users' experiences in the development of future 
sustainable social housing, and that it is important to be realistic about funding and ambitions 
regarding sustainability, and how to modernise the communication.  
 
In DGNB matters, these two buildings will get the same score, since they have identical 
potentials. Which is what DGNB is measuring, not considering how the solution performs after it is 
built. The two buildings, in this case, are performing differently in sustainability matters, the 
occupants in the two buildings have different opinions on the large glass facades, which leads to 
large differences in the energy consumption and overall very different life quality.  
 
This critic does point out the matters of doing user involvement. The critic is not based on that the 
technical factors can’t be used as indicators for social sustainability, just that it is not enough to 
cover all the important qualitative social features.  
 
This finding points out the need to conduct a questionnaire for the occupants, so the social “soft 
values” can be covered as well as the social-technical values.  
10.4 Development of TOPSIS  
To provide the decision-maker with the ability to look into the future and to make the best possible decision, 
based on past and present information and future predictions, is the true goal of holistic decision-making 
[1].  
 
TOPSIS ensures innovation and rethinking of the design. It does not limit the designers and is not 
in favour of a limited design, unless this is desired. If a scenario is performing the best regarding 
highly prioritized indicators, but does not meet a requirement, then the scenario is not dismissed. 
==PAGE:110==
109/122 
It is possible then to either start modifying the scenario or involve the authorities into the decision 
process, either to get another weighting or a waiver for the ideal solution not meeting all 
requirements.  
10.4.1 Indicators Determined as Intervals  
The possibilities with MCMD as TOPSIS are endless and can be used to solve many theoretical 
and real-world problems. In the following, some of the issues regarding classical TOPSIS are 
stated and some of the known extensions which could be used to make TOPSIS a better decision-
making tool in the design phase.  
 
In most cases, determining the exact value of criteria and indicators is difficult and decision-
makers such as architects are usually more comfortable providing intervals to specify model input 
parameters. Especially regarding the design phase, where a detailed Revit model is often not 
accessible. Hence detailed evaluation with exact values as conducted in this study, is not always 
possible to achieve in the early design. But it is possible to include assessments and an interval 
of data formulated in a simple and intuitive way [67].  
 
Additionally, it is possible to consider an interval-based weighting system [67]. For example, to 
weight an indicator much higher if the value exceeds restrictions in current regulations. Another 
way could be if there is a party involved in the building design, which have a higher influence on 
the decision-making.  
 
The high flexibility of the TOPSIS concept can accommodate further extensions to make the best 
choices in various situations. In addition to interval-based input parameters, it is also possible to 
modify TOPSIS, to calculate the distance to the optimal solution for a group decision (weighting).   
Further research could induce the compiling of knowledge into a comprehensive workflow guide 
for building designs. This can be done by creating a generic TOPSIS model, where the only 
elements which are changing the scenarios.  
10.4.2 Integrated Sensitivity  
A study has scrutinized the standard decisions relevant to construction technology and 
management, and has found that deficiency of information is very often ignored [59]. The study 
showed that experts who belonged to those sectors, make use of unfavourable initial data, which 
were exaggerated and in case of need, corrected based on practical experience. However, the 
input data does not reflect the actual situation insufficiently. Acting in such a way, experts make 
allowable decisions, but most often these decisions are unfavourable. This investigation is 
especially important when using MCMD in the early design phase of a building.  
 
The conclusion of the study is to perform a sensitivity analysis of decision-making methods 
concerning the initial data, which may be not sufficiently accurate. This applies both to the values 
and weights of the criteria. The final decision should be provided alongside the results of a 
==PAGE:111==
110/122 
sensitivity analysis. For further studies, generic integrated sensitivity analysis as an extension to 
TOPSIS could be investigated. This could make a decision taken upon the results of TOPSIS 
much more accurate and valid. This could also intercept invalid scenarios regarding regulations 
(e.g. BR18) or other overshadowing factors or invalid combinations. 
 
10.4.3 Grey Relation Analysis (GRA) 
When doing a holistic assessment, there are some indicators which will affect each other when 
they are changed. These overlaps can be mapped through Grey Relation Analysis (GRA). This is 
a mathematical model which can be combined with the TOPSIS-matrix [17]. Combining GRA with 
TOPSIS can be used to solve multi-criteria decision making problems and to sort the alternatives, 
on an even more complex basis [17]. GRA is a method for analysing the relationship between 
discrete data sets. 
 
10.4.4 Monte Carlo Design Optimization  
What is the best approach to perform building simulations of scenarios as a mean to support 
decision-making and optimize building performance? Traditionally, the search for a compliant 
design is carried out in a manual one-at-a-time approach, where one design parameter is 
optimized before attention is shifted to the next parameter. In contrast, the Monte Carlo method 
makes it possible to combine many design parameters simultaneously and consider numerous 
input combinations [68]. For these simulations, design criteria can be applied to find a subset of 
high-performing solutions. The extent and shape of the design space and the possible solutions 
can be assessed by an uncertainty analysis. 
 
This helps decision-makers focus on the most important design parameters and their interactions, 
while insignificant parameters can be ignored. Monte Carlo simulations can be used to construct 
models from which millions of input combinations can be evaluated in an instant.  
The metamodels can enable rapid optimization or instant feedback on specific designs. There is 
already building performance simulation programs which facilitate Monte Carlo simulations, e.g. 
IDA ICE 4.8 (beta, ~2019), and BSim (2019) [68]. The results of this, could then be applied to a 
GRA and TOPSIS model, in order to find the most optimal solution, rendering most human design 
choices impartial or useless in comparison.

10.5.1 Absolute Sustainability  
In 2018, the IPCC defined a goal to keep the man-made temperature increase within 2°C globally, 
and it would require that the average CO2 emissions per person were decreased down to 3 
tonnes per person per year [69][70]. By comparison, an average Dane’s emission of CO2 is 
estimated at 19 tonnes annually [70]. Thus, a much higher ambition regarding the green transition 
is required, to slow down the effect of human impact on the environment. The limits of what the 
environment can absorb from man-made influences without permanent damage were quantified 
in 2009 under the concept of "planetary boundaries” [71]. This defines that humanity has a large 
influence of the development of Earth - including climate change, ocean acidification and 
biodiversity. If humanity can stay within this scope, one can talk about being within the limits of 
absolute sustainability. Currently, there are several ways to split and allocate safe operation 
space between our consumption segments (Figure 10.2).  
 
 
Figure 10.2: Overview of how to allocate a safe operating space of consumption segments [72]. 
 
One of the main questions of the sustainability discussion leads to: Who is responsible that we are 
achieving this goal? This is question is commonly asked by all political parties of every nation, 
especially those involved in the design of our buildings and cities. The conclusion must be that 
those involved in the building design must involve themselves so that the overall goal can be 
achieved. Since we know the planetary boundaries, could this be used as a reference. Absolute 
sustainability can be obtained by reducing the impacts from construction by 89%, energy 
consumption by 80% and the living area by 60% [73].

10.6 Conclusion & Recommendation  
This study addresses the goal of the building industry to communicate across disciplines to be 
able to solve the complex matter of sustainable building design. Early design decisions such as 
geometry, window/facade ratio, orientation, etc., are typically in the hands of architects. In a more 
holistic aspect, where qualitative values such as pre-occupancy evaluation and technical-
scientific simulations are used to back up the decision-making process.  
 
Using TOPSIS as a tool to help all the decision-makers in the design process to make decisions 
upon a larger list of indicators, is a step towards more holistically sustainable buildings. It became 
evident from the case study that TOPSIS has good potential for innovation regarding the design 
practices for the involved parties in a building-design process. TOPSIS can either stand-alone or 
in collaboration with certifications tool or current building regulation etc. As a result, the tool is 
universally applicable across most decision-making contexts within the building industry. Since it 
is customizable, in the sense that the indicators can be freely chosen.  
 
Based on the indicators for TOPSIS chosen in this study, the result reveals that there where 
different prioritization between the decision-makers included in this study W03 - engineer, W04 - 
Architect and W05 - Building client. The engineer and architect had in general the same 
prioritization, where the social aspects had the highest importance. Compared with the building 
client, which had prioritized the economic aspects highest. But in general, there was not much 
difference between the ideal solution regarding the different weightings.  
 
Ignoring that the requirements in BR18 are paramount, then the ideal solution regarding the 
engineer and architects is SC 03.02 of exposed CLT with reduced window area. Regarding the 
weighting of the building client, then the most ideal solution is SC 01.02 with exposed CLT and 
original window design. Besides the scenario chosen by the architect, none of the additionally 
investigated scenarios, in this study, met the requirements in BR18 fully.  
 
10.6.1 Operation of TOPSIS  
The quantitative MCMD methods are practical to use in decision support systems. Some of the 
advantages of TOPSIS methods are the simplicity, rationality, comprehensibility, good 
computational efficiency and ability to measure the relative performance for each scenario in a 
simple mathematical form in relation to the scenarios investigated.  
 
The ranking of scenarios in TOPSIS is based on the relative similarity to the ideal solution, which 
avoids the situation of having the same similarity index to both positive ideal and anti-ideal 
solutions. TOPSIS can also be used as a discussion or a process tool in the design phase. 
TOPSIS can also be used as a communication tool, so for example when the building client askes 
for a sustainable building, then they can be a part of defining what that means, by choosing 
indicators themselves.
