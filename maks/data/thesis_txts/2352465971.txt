Abstract 
This report is an investigation of salt frost scaling. Known theories describing this phenomenon are 
hydraulic pressure, microscopic ice lens growth, osmotic pressure and glue spalling. The theories 
have different explanations on the cause of salt frost scaling. Some of the theories’ statements are 
tested; this is done by conducting two freeze-thaw experiments based on the Technical 
Specification DS/CEN/TS 12390-9. The first experiment Part I: Freeze-thaw test with H2O and 
D2O as freezing medium is an investigation on the effects of capillary water, freezing mediums and 
salt concentrations. This is done by using both normal water and heavy water as capillary water 
and freezing mediums. Different salt concentrations is used in the mediums as well. Results 
indicates that the capillary water have an impact on the amount of scaling happening before the 
first renewing of freezing medium, in this case from temperature cycle 0-7. Furthermore, it is the 
heavy water and a salt concentration of 3 % resulting in the most salt frost damages. The second 
experiment Examination of boundary conditions is an investigation of the impact of supercooling 
during a freeze-thaw test. Supercooling cannot be avoided during tests, and a small increase in 
temperature will therefore occur at the point when supercooling ends. A series of freeze-thaw test 
are conducted with different amount of cooling power during the cooling process. Results show 
that supercooling have an impact on the scaling, the faster the increase in temperature is eliminated, 
the less salt frost scaling occurs.

1 Introduction 
Concrete is one of the most popular and used constructions materials today due to properties, 
durability, availability of materials and cost. As the amounts of casted concrete have increased 
during the last century, damages and errors have also appeared. Countless scientific work have 
been performed within the field, to clarify the mechanisms responsible for the problems. Today 
research of concrete have come far, however, there are still unanswered questions. It is important 
to investigate these questions, as it can have large impact on the concrete industry to minimize 
damages and errors, and optimize cost, properties and durability.  
In cold climates, damages on concrete exposed to water, de-icing salt and frost action is a common 
sight. The combination of water, salt and frost often results in salt frost scaling, where flakes of 
crumbled concrete surface leave aggregates exposed and increases the risk of ingress of damaging 
substances. The most optimal conditions for salt frost scaling to occur have been identified; 
however, there are still disagreements in regards to the explanation on why it happens. Scientists 
have during time suggested and investigated different theories but none of them have jet been 
accepted as the full truth.  
The four most popular theories, which seeks to explain the reason of salt frost scaling, are:  
 Hydraulic pressure (Powers 1945) 
 Frost heaving or microscopic ice lens growth (Collins 1944) 
 Osmotic pressure (Powers and Helmut 1953) 
 Glue spalling (Valenza and Scherer 2 2007) 
As it can be seen, the glue spall theory is a relatively new theory suggested in 2007, whereas the 
three others originates from the 1940s and 1950s. In this thesis, two salt frost scaling experiments 
will be conducted to investigate if the results can be explained by some of the theories. This can 
hopefully contribute to the research of explaining the mechanisms causing salt frost scaling.  
The report problem statement is clarified and explained in details at the end of Chapter 2. This 
structure has been chosen because theoretical knowledge is needed in the understanding of the 
statement.

2 Theory 
Relevant theories and definitions, in understanding the experiments, are clarified in this chapter, 
including: distinguishing between internal and external frost action damages in hardened concrete, 
salt frost scaling and different theories causing it, explanation on supercooling, defining liquids 
freezing points and lastly differences between normal water and heavy water.  
 
2.1 External and internal frost damage 
Frost action is caused by the weather, and is defined as repeatedly freezing and thawing periods 
(Mehta and Monteiro 2006), called freeze-thaw cycles. Frost action can also be created artificially 
by using, for instance, a freezing cabinet. These freeze-thaw cycles can damage the concrete. When 
working with concrete and frost, the difference between exposure during hardening and in the 
hardened state should be noticed. If the concrete have been casted correctly, frost damages in the 
period of hardening should not be a concern. Freeze-thaw cycles occur over long periods and 
cannot be controlled, and are therefore mainly a threat to the hardened concrete. Frost damage in 
hardened concrete can be identified as internal or external damage. These two types of damage can 
be present simultaneously  or one without the other (Valenza and Scherer 1 2007). Internal damage 
manifests itself as cracks inside the concrete. The internal frost damage causes reduction of 
concrete strength and stiffness, and thereby reduces durability. When working with hardened 
concrete structures, this type of damage can be fatal, because the extend of cracking is invisible at 
the surface. Contrary, external damage appears as visible detached flakes or small chips of concrete 
surface, in severe cases leading to exposed aggregates, an example can be seen in Figure 1. The 
flacking automatically affects the overall structure strength due to reduced cross section area. It 
can also result in ingress of moisture and aggressive substances causing internal damage and 
influencing the durability of the structure (Valenza and Scherer 1 2007). This damage is also known 
as salt frost scaling.

2.2 Salt frost scaling 
Salt frost scaling is associated with colder climates, with varying seasons of cold and warm 
weather. During the colder and snowy periods in these areas, salt is used for de-icing various 
surfaces such as roads, sidewalks, stairs, etc. to ensure safety. However, surfaces made of concrete 
can through several de-icings and freeze-thaw periods become damaged due to the salt and frozen 
water. The occurrence and severity of scaling depends on various conditions, such as availability 
of freezing medium, temperature, salt concentration and composition of the concrete, especially 
the air content. A freezing medium is required on the concrete surface for the scaling to develop; 
salt frost scaling is not possible without the presence of a medium. Outdoor, the medium generally 
consists of melted snow or rainwater that freezes. The de-icing salt mixes with the liquid medium, 
and a random salt concentration arises in the medium. Through lab experiments, a solute 
concentration of 3% salt (by weight), has proven to be the most damaging concentration in regards 
to salt frost scaling (Verbeck and Klieger 1957). Lab experience has also shown that damages 
increases when temperature decreases below -10 °C, and also when the time period of decreased 
temperature is increased (Valenza and Scherer 1 2007). Lastly, the air content is known to have a 
large influence on the severity of the frost action damages; if air is entrained into the concrete mix, 
the resistance to frost action improves (Powers 1954). Even though the most optimal conditions 
for salt frost scaling have been identified and acknowledged by scientist, the explanation on why 
it happens, are still debated. Different theories have been stated and outlined though the years; 
however, no final theory have yet been accepted.

2.3 Theories on the cause of frost damage 
The theories being covered in this section have been developed during time, and therefore certain 
similarities in the theories will occur due to improvement and effecting one another. The theories 
being covered are hydraulic pressure, frost heaving/microscopic ice lens growth, osmotic pressure 
and glue spalling 
2.3.1 Hydraulic pressure 
The theory on hydraulic pressure was developed by T.C. Powers, and is based on knowledge 
regarding expansion of water when transformed from liquid to ice. When water freezes it expands 
approximately 9%. This expansion is due to molecule structure and hydrogen bonds. Ice has a 
larger volume as the molecules are locked in hexagonal structures, opposed to the molecules more 
mobile and cluttered state seen in the liquid phase (Hansen 1995). Early on, it was believed that 
expansion of entrapped water in the concrete caused the frost action damages, due to lack of space 
when the water froze. However, Powers argued that this theory was based on the assumption of 
concrete being a closed system, with no or almost no empty air space available (Powers 1975). 
Concrete is not a closed system and enough space to resist the liquid-to-ice expansion will normally 
be present. Powers suggested that it was not the expansion itself that caused damages, but pressure 
created by the expansion. Ice crystals can emerge in either the concretes capillary pores or air voids, 
as sketched in Figure 2. 
When an ice crystal emerges in capillary pores, hydraulic pressure arises within the pores, forcing 
unfrozen water to flow away from the crystal as indicated in Figure 2 (a). In this scenario, air voids 
will provide escape boundaries for the water. However if the distance from the ice to the escape-
air-void exceeds a specific critical length, it is not possible for the concrete to withstand the 
pressure, and damage will occur. In scenario (b) in Figure 2, the ice crystal is emerging inside an 
empty air void, and in this case, no pressure will occur, as the ice crystal has enough space to grow. 
Figure 2: (a) Ice forming in capillary pores, causing hydraulic pressure to arise, forcing water to flow
towards air voids. (b) Ice forming inside an air void. (Mehta and Monteiro 2006)

The two scenarios support the importance of air entrainment in concrete. In this theory, the distance 
and the density of air voids determine whether the concrete is resistant to ice growth or not. 
2.3.2 Frost heaving – Microscopic ice lens growth 
Frost heaving is a phenomenon associated with soil mechanics; however, A.R. Collins discovered 
a possible connection to frost damages in concrete (Collins 1944). An investigation of low quality 
and highly porous concrete, casted during wartime lead to the theory. The theory is based on the 
varying pore sizes in concrete, and the water content within the pores. The freezing point of the 
water depends on the pore size. Smaller pores results in lower freezing points. The water within 
the largest pores will freeze first and the formed ice crystal will then draw water from the smaller 
pores filled with unfrozen water. This of cause implies that the larger and smaller pores are 
connected. The ice crystal will continue to grow as long as the smaller pores can supply the crystal 
with water. This scenario will cause ice layers to form inside the concrete parallel to the concrete 
surface. When all the water in a layer have been drawn into larger pores and transformed to ice 
nothing more will happen, until the cold front have reached a new depth with unfrozen water, where 
the process will repeat itself. Damage occur if the large pores are completely full of ice, and the 
water supply from the smaller pores have not yet run out, then the ice will push and crack the 
concrete so the growing can continue, as seen in Figure 3. Experiments have also shown that the 
ice layers will affect the overall strength of the concrete (Collins 1944). 
Collins proposed, based on the research, the following six conditions resulting in frost damages 
because of frost heaving (Collins 1944):  
1) Low strength concrete 
2) High porous concrete with small voids 
3) High moisture content inside the concrete 
4) Access to water either inside the concrete or outside on the remote side of freezing surface 
5) Temperature in the pores below the waters freezing point 
6) Freezing period should be long enough for the water to freeze and form the ice layers 
Condition 4) is notable, because this implies that the water does not need to be present on the 
surface being exposed to freezing. The water can be drawn and moved from the opposite side of 
the concrete, due to the pores. Therefore is it, for instance, important that a road is not build on a 
highly humid base, due to the risk of water transportation form the base, through the concrete, to 
the surface, where it causes scaling and damage.

Collins refereed to the theory as frost heaving, due to its origin as a soil mechanics phenomenon. 
However now the theory is commonly known as microscopic ice lens growth, when referring to it 
in relation to concrete. 
2.3.3 Osmotic pressure 
Osmotic pressure is a theory developed by Powers and Helmut in 1953 (Powers and Helmut 1953). 
When water with dissolved salt freezes, it is only the water being frozen. This causes the salt 
concentration around the new ice crystal to raise, which results in a local disequilibrium. 
Equilibrium is attempted to be reestablished, which causes water to flow towards the arising salt 
concentration to neutralise it (Powers 1975). This attempt to reestablish equilibrium will result in 
growth of ice as the water flow can supply the growth of the crystal. The growth of ice can, just as 
explained in 2.3.2 damage the concrete. The mechanisms of osmotic pressure and microscopic ice 
lens growth are similar to each other (Powers 1975), the only difference is the driving forces. In 
the theory of microscopic ice lens growth large ice crystals draw water from the smaller pores, 
whereas in osmotic pressure it is the local salt concentrations within the pores that drives the 
transport of water to the site of ice formation. 
2.3.4 Glue spalling 
The glue spall theory is a relatively new theory proposed by Valenza and Scherer in 2007 (Valenza 
and Scherer 2 2007). The theory exclusively focuses on external damage, and does not seek to 
explain internal frost action damages. Glue spalling is originally a glass decorating technique. The 
glass surface is sandblasted, and afterwards at high temperature covered with a layer of epoxy. As 
the temperature decreases, the epoxy contracts significantly compared to the glass, this causes the 
epoxy to rip the glass surface, due to stress difference, and in the end making it possible to remove 
small flakes of glass from the surface. The process is sketched in Figure 4. 
Valenza and Scherer proposed that the technique can be directly applied in the explanation of salt 
frost scaling, the concrete being the glass, and the frozen medium being the epoxy. For damage to 
unfold, salt is needed in the medium. When water with a specific salt concentration freezes, it is 
only the pure water that freezes, meaning that the salt concentration in the remaining unfrozen 
liquid rises, resulting in pockets filled with brine. The brine pockets are the weak spots where the 
cracks will occur. Salt concentration of 3% results in the most scaling, as ice formed from pure 
water have no weak spots, and concentrations higher than 3% have too many weak spots making 
the stress too small to rip apart the concrete.  The glue spall theory also states that the type of liquid

2.3.5 Comparison of the theories 
Hydraulic pressure, microscopic ice lens growth and osmotic pressure explains frost damages with 
offset in solutions inside the concrete, where the glue spall theory overlooks the effect of pore 
solution.  
It should also be noted that hydraulic pressure and microscopic ice lens growth only focuses on 
pure water, even though liquid inside concrete never only consists of pure water, which means they 
do not take the effect of salt into consideration. T.C. Powers argued against A.R. Collins theory on 
frost heaving (Terzaghi et al 1945), as he did not believed soil and concrete to be comparable due 
to the fact that soil is not a cohesive systems such as concrete. Powers participated in the 
development of both the hydraulic pressure and osmotic pressure theory, however he later on 
rejected hydraulic pressure and declared his belief in osmotic pressure (Powers 1975). Despite of 
this, hydraulic pressure is still today a theory being used in the explanation of salt frost scaling.

Experiments have proven that salt concentrations of 3% (by weight), result in the most severe salt 
frost scaling (Valenza and Scherer 1 2007). A solution of water containing 3% salt have a freezing 
point of approximately -2°C. However often when conducting frost action experiments 
measurements indicates lower freezing points (Powers and Helmut 1953) than predicted by 
calculations. The reason for this is supercooling. 
2.4.2 Supercooling 
Supercooling is a phenomenon occurring when liquid is cooled below the freezing point without 
being transformed into solid (Debenedetti and Stanley 2003), the state is unstable and only a small 
movement or contact with other particles can trigger the crystallization. The period between the 
liquids freezing point and the point of crystallization occurs because an amount of energy is needed 
to transform the liquid. When the transformation starts, energy is released and the temperature will 
rise a little.  
2.4.3 Normal water and heavy water 
The difference between normal and heavy water is found in the structure of the molecules. Water 
(H2O) consists of an oxygen atom that binds two hydrogen atoms. Heavy water (D2O) have the 
same structure but with two deuterium atoms instead of hydrogen. Deuterium is an isotope of 
hydrogen. Hydrogen nucleus only consist of one proton, where deuterium has both a proton and a 
neutron. The extra neutron affects the deuterium’s mass, and is therefore heavier than hydrogen, 
hence the name heavy water. The two molecules are sketched in Figure 5. 
The layout of water molecules is due to oxygens 6 electrons and hydrogens/deuterium 1 electron 
in the outer spheres. Oxygen seeks to have 8 electrons in the outer sphere and hydrogen/deuterium 
seeks 2, because all elements attempts to imitate noble gasses. Noble gasses have the maximal 
possible electrons in the outer sphere. Oxygen and hydrogen/deuterium therefore lend electrons to 
each other, by binding together as seen in Figure 5. The difference between H2O and D2O, two 
neutrons, have a large effect on the properties and characteristics when comparing the two. An 
extract of the difference in properties can be seen in Table 1 (Chaplin 2016).

2.5 Summary and problem statement 
In the paper regarding the glue spall theory (Valenza and Scherer 2 2007) Valenza and Scherer 
have 8 points, where they argue how the glue spall theory accounts for all the characteristics of salt 
frost scaling. Some of these statements will be tested in the first experiment. An important part of 
the glues spall theory is the deviation between external and internal damages. Glue spall only 
accounts for external damages, which is why Valenza and Scherer stats that the solution in the 
concretes pores have no effect on the salt scaling. They also states that the most optimal salt 
concentration of 3% is independent of the solute. These statements will be tested in the first 
experiment. Heavy water and normal water will be used in the investigation, due to the similarities 
in molecular structure, but differences in mass, density and freezing points. A detailed explanation 
on the experiment is stated below: 
 The first experiment, Part I: Freeze-thaw test with H2O and D2O as freezing medium, is 
based on the Technical Specification DS/CEN/TS 12390-9 (DS/CEN/TS12390-9 2006). 
However, modifications in regards to the capillary water and freezing medium will be 
introduced. Both normal water and heavy water will be used as capillary water, this will be 
combined with freezing mediums also consisting of both normal and heavy water, with 
different salt concentrations. This test setup makes it possible to investigate the effect of 
capillary water, salt concentrations and mediums. Even though heavy and normal water 
have the same molecular structure, there are notable differences in properties, for instance 
boiling point, freezing point and density. The difference in properties are believed to affect 
the outcome of result in this experiment, however it is not possible to predict how heavy 
water will react compared to normal water, because there are no available literature on the 
topic. 
An investigation on the effects of supercooling on freeze-thaw test will be the focus of the second 
experiment. It is almost impossible to avoid supercooling during a freeze-thaw test. It is wished to 
test how supercooling affects the outcome of a test. The experiment is described below: 
 The second experiment is designed to test the boundary conditions in the required 
temperature cycle stated in the Technical Specification DS/CEN/TS 12390-9. A mini freezer 
will be used to test and investigate the period of supercooling occurring during freezing. 
The mini freezer will be setup to run the freezing cycles with different power, to test how 
much influence the energy submission, from supercooling, and the afterwards 
reestablishment of temperature, have on salt frost scaling.

3.2 Method 
3.2.1 Test method 
The Technical Specification DS/CEN/TS 12390-9 consists of 3 test methods, whereas the Slab test 
(reference method) have been used for this freeze-thaw test. The method has been followed as 
accurate as possible. However, some deviation and changes have been implemented.  
Planned changes: 
 Shape of specimens: the test method is based on castings shaped as 150 mm cubes and 
afterwards cut into specimens of 150 x 150 x 50 mm. In the performed test, cylinders of 
diameter 150 mm and height 300 mm were casted and afterwards cut to specimens with 
height 50 mm.   
 Capillary water: the test surfaces should, according to DS/CEN/TS 12390-9, be covered 
with a layer of 3 mm de-ionised water for 3 days before the test starts. This procedure has 
been followed, however, two types of capillary water have been used instead of only one.  
 Freezing mediums: the freezing medium should consists of a water solution with 3% NaCl. 
However for the test 6 different freezing mediums have been used. 
 Cycles: DS/CEN/TS 12390-9 prescribe that scaled material from the surface should be 
collected after 7, 14, 28, 42 and 56 cycles. This has been changed due to lack of time, such 
that collection will be performed after 7, 14, 21 and 28 days.  
Unforeseen changes: 
 Age of specimens: instead of the specimens being 31 days old when put into the freezing 
cabinet, the specimens were 45 days old. All of the dates according to DS/CEN/TS 12390-
9 and the actual used dates can be seen in Appendix B. 
3.2.2 Casting process 
The needed amount (for 8 cylinders, 1 slump test and 1 air content measurement) of each concrete 
component are weighed out. All dry materials are mixed together in a concrete mixer for 1 minute, 
then water is added, and the mix is mixed for 3 minutes. The concrete are then poured into moulds 
while placed on a vibrating table. After casting, the moulds are stored horizontal in a climate 
chamber for 24 hours, before being demoulded, and stored in water bath for 7 days. The cylinders 
are then removed from the bath and placed in the climate chamber, with constant temperature of 
23 °C, until cutting.  
3.2.3 Preparation and naming of concrete specimens 
The preparation of the specimens has been done accordingly to DS/CEN/TS 12390-9. As 
mentioned, 8 cylinders were casted, even though only 6 are to be used for the test, the extra 
cylinders are in case of damage or errors. The cylinders are named with numbers 1 to 8 after 
demoulding. None of the cylinders had any visible errors, so random selection was used to identify 
the 6 test cylinders.

3.4 Discussion 
The results from section 3.3 will be discussed in this section. The uncertainties appearing in the 
test will be discussed first and then the actual results.  
3.4.1 Uncertainties 
Specimen heights: 
The average height of the specimens is 52.7 mm (Appendix F); this differs from the requirements 
of 50mm ± 2mm prescribed in DS/CEN/TS 12930-9. When looking at the measurements, it is 
seen that all heights are above 50mm, however 5 of them are within the requirements, whereas 7 
are too high. The saw used for cutting the specimens have a blade thickness between 4 and 5mm, 
this results in some inaccuracy when measuring specimens out on the cylinder and cutting them. 
However, the average of 52.7 mm only differs 0.7 mm form the prescribed requirements, which 
is a relatively small deviation compared to the overall height of the specimens.  
Absorbed capillary water: 
The volumes of absorbed capillary water are 5.73g for H2O and 6.45g D2O (Appendix F). This 
seems as a big difference; however, the difference in molar mass of H2O and D2O should be 
taken into account. H2O has a molar mass of 18.02 g/mol whereas D2O has a molar mass of 20.03 
g/mol. Based on this the following calculation is made to check the mass of D2O corresponding 
to 5.73g of H2O: 
20.03 ( 𝑔

6.40g is very close to the measured 6.45g of D2O. This means the same volume of capillary water 
has been absorbed, but due to the difference of molar mass D2O is heavier than H2O. This 
observation validates comparison between the two types of capillary waters, when later the effect 
of capillary water is discussed in section 3.4.4.  
In Appendix I calculations based on the volume of absorbed capillary water indicates that the 
migration depth is approximately 3.4 mm.  
Temperatures of freezing cabinet: 
The temperatures measured by the freezing cabinet fits the temperature requirements of 
DS/CEN/TS 12930-9. The freezing cabinet measures the temperature in the cabinet as an average 
between the top and bottom of the cabinet. A sensor in the cabinet is placed in a specimen’s freezing 
medium at the top and another one in the bottom. This arrangement will equalize temperature 
differences between top and bottom. The two sensors were placed in two specimens with 3% NaCl 
H2O as freezing medium, these two specimens were not part of the freeze-thaw test. The down part 
of the cabinets sensor setup, is that it will not detect if the sensors are measuring incorrect 
temperatures, as it only focuses on the average temperature being within the range of the desired 
temperatures. To check the temperature of the freezing cabinet the logger was used as a control. 
==PAGE:31==

The measurements conducted by the logger are plotted in Appendix J. These measurements 
indicates a disagreement between the freezing cabinet and logger, as the logger measurements are 
not within the limits. The disagreement lead to an error investigation where all sensors, both the 
cabinet’s and the logger’s, were calibrated. However, this did not clarify the difference, as none of 
the sensors deviated noticeable. Further investigation resulted in the conclusion that the cabinet’s 
two sensors have not been completely immersed into the freezing medium. The cabinet has 
therefore, due the average measuring setup, measured temperatures within the limits, but it has not 
been exclusively freezing medium temperatures. The logger has measured the correct freezing 
medium temperature. However, this should not cause any concerns as the results from the test only 
are compared internally. They have been exposed to the same conditions and are therefore 
comparable. 
3.4.2 Scaling results 
When comparing all 4 plots in Figure 11, both 0% and 7.6% concentration of NaCl seems very 
similar for all capillary/medium combinations. None of the 0% specimens have noticeable scaling. 
The 7.6% specimens have scaling within 0.0 - 0.2 kg/m2 after 28 cycles in all cases. It is at the 
concentration of 3% there seems to be a difference between the plots. In the period of cycle 0 to 7, 
no significant difference between the graphs of 3% can be identified based on the plots. After the 
7th cycle significant differences appears: 
 The scaling of Capillary H2O/ Medium 3% H2O follows the scaling pattern of the 7.6% 
throughout the rest of the test. This was not expected, as concentration of 3% should result 
in more scaling than 7.6% according to the theory. No obvious explanation of this have 
been detected during the test.  
 When looking at the case of Capillary D2O/Medium 3% H2O an accelerated scaling appears 
between cycle 7 and 14. Afterwards the graph level off.  
 In the case of Capillary H2O/Medium 3% D2O, a drastic change in scaling appears at the 
7th cycle. After the 7th cycle the scaling accelerates and throughout the rest of the test the 
scaling is linear. 
 Capillary D2O/Medium 3% D2O show the same tendency as Capillary H2O/Medium 3% 
D2O, but with more severe and accelerated scaling.  
Based on these observations, D2O seems to increase the salt frost damages compared to H2O. 
However the plots cannot be used in the determination of the effects of D2O as capillary water, 
because all 4 plots indicates that scaling between cycle 0-7 is similar. To investigate what happen 
in the first 7 cycles compared to the rest, new plots have been made.

Observation and photographs:  
The three scaling patterns pictured in Figure 12, Figure 13 and Figure 14 differs from each other.  
In Figure 12 all scaling happens at one side of the specimen. This type of scaling can be explained 
by the placement of the specimen. The layer of freezing medium is only 3 mm thick, which means 
a small skewness of the specimen can have an influence on the thickness of the medium. The 
specimens are during the test placed in buckets with insulation at the bottom and the sides. The 
insulation is soft and can easily be deformed by the much heavier specimens. In Figure 12 the 
specimen has been tilted a little during the test, which will cause damage due to a thicker freezing 
medium (Valenza and Scherer 2006) in this area of the surface.  
The damage on Figure 13 cannot be explained by a tilted placement, as the scaling appears as spots 
on the specimen’s surface. This type of damage can be due to unevenness and small damages at 
the surface before the test started. Small damages can for instance occur during the cutting process. 
When the specimens are cut the saw can damage the surface or make it uneven, as seen in Figure 
15. When first damage has appeared on the surface, this damage will continue to “grow” and 
become more severe during the freeze-thaw test.  
Figure 14 shows the specimen with most damage. The damage is homogeneous distributed all over 
the surface. This is a severe case of salt scaling, as aggregates are exposed.  
3.4.3 Future research 
If the test is to be repeated, as many specimens as possible should be used, such that there are 
several of every capillary/medium combination. This will make it easier to compare the results in 
case of errors or doubt. For example, Capillary H2O/Medium 3% H2O did not follow the expected 
scaling progress; however, with extra specimens comparison of results would make it easier to 
conclude if something is wrong with the scaling progress.  
Another experiment could be to investigate smaller concentration intervals, such that the curves on 
the concentration vs. scaled material plots became more accurate.

4.2 Method 
4.2.1 Test method 
This experiment is loosely based on the Technical Specification DS/CEN/TS 12390-9. The 
Technical Specification DS/CEN/TS 12390-9 have been used as guideline, because no 
Specification exists with the purpose of examine boundary conditions during cooling. DS/CEN/TS 
12390-9 have especially been used in the preparation process of the concrete specimens and when 
defining the temperatures for experiment.  
Deviations: 
 Shape of specimens: the original shape should be cubes. The cross section of the casting is 
sketched in Figure 20. The height of the casting is 200 mm, which is later cut into test 
specimens with height 50 mm. 
 Not the same casting: DS/CEN/TS 12390-9 states that 4 test cubes are required, this implies 
that the 4 cubes are from the same casting. This requirement is not fulfilled, because only 
one mould with the dimensions to fit the mini freezer exits. For every casting, 6 in total, 
new concrete was mixed, using the same mix design of cause. 
 No water bath: instead of being stored in water bath for a week after demoulding, the 
specimens were demoulded and stored in the climate chamber in sealed foil bags until use. 
The sealing is to avoid evaporation.  
 Age: the age of the test specimens differs a lot from DS/CEN/TS 12390-9; this is due to the 
capacity of the freezer and different casting days. The older the specimens are the smaller 
is the deviation of them not being casted the same day and exposed to freeze-thaw cycles 
at the same time. In Table 6 an overview is given of the specimens age from casted to being

4.2.2 Casting process 
Before every casting water content in the aggregates are determined. It is necessary to do this every 
time because the castings are conducted on different dates. The water content in the aggregates 
depends on the conditions within their storage boxes, refills have an effect, as the refilled material 
can be wetter or dryer than the already stored material. After determination of water content, the 
needed components for the casting are weighed off. The dry material are mixed together for 1 
minute, and then 3 minutes when water is added. It is important to be precise with the time, such 
that each casting is mixed as similar as possible. The concrete is then poured into the mould. The 
mould is specially designed to fit the dimensions of the mini freezer. The rounded sides can be 
removed when demoulding the casted concrete. The mould is pictured in Figure 21.

climate chamber until use.

4.2.4 Mini freezer 
The mini freezer consists of two components: the freezer and a computer used to control the freezer. 
The freezer is pictured in Figure 23. The cooling is performed by a Peltier element. When a Peltier 
element is supplied with electricity, a temperature difference is created between the two sides of 
the element. Heat is transferred to one side of the element, resulting in the opposite side becoming 
cold. The cold side is places in the freezing medium, but not touching the concrete surface, and 
thereby cools the medium. A fan have been installed on the heating side of the element to make 
sure it does not become too hot. Due to the thickness of the element, a too warm upper side will 
affect to temperature on the cold side. 
The freezer is connected to the computer. On the computer, the program Manikin is used to control 
the freezing process. The program needs information on the desired temperature cycle and power 
available for the Peltier element. The program is preprogramed before the experiment, and it needs 
information on the temperature and power for every hour in 1 cycle (1 cycle = 24 hours). An 
overview of the temperature cycle entered into the program is found in Appendix L. The power 
varies from specimen to specimen, however the chosen ones (12, 14, 16, 18 and 20 W) have been 
found by test running the freezer with the temperature cycle at different power. The test runs are 
clarified in Appendix M. The concrete specimen with AEA is tested with the power leaving the 
most damage on the other specimens, in this case 12 W.

4.4 Discussion 
The results from section 4.3 will be discussed in this section.  
4.4.1 Uncertainties 
Change in mass: 
The mass loss of the castings were measured to clarify that the castings did not lose mass during 
the long storing period. In Appendix N the average mass loss is stated to be 5.67 g. When looking 
at the total mass of the castings, which is over 2.6 kg, a mass loss of 5.67 g is very small. And all 
of the castings have lost mass, and none of them significantly compared to the average.  
Specimen height: 
When looking at the specimen height, also in Appendix N, the average height is 52.6 mm. This is 
0.6 mm more than the specified height of 50mm ± 2mm stated in DS/CEN/TS 12930-9. As 
discussed in section 3.4.1, the measuring and saw are accountable for this deviation.  
Temperature and power: 
The temperature and power measurements in Appendix O establish that the experiments have been 
running according to the intention. The two test with 12 W does not follow the temperature cycle; 
however, this was expected based of the test runs outlined in Appendix M.  
4.4.2 Temperature peaks and scaling 
Connection between peak results: 
There seems to be a connection between time of peak and the height of the peaks. The markers in 
Figure 26 and Figure 27 are placed in the same pattern. Late peaks results in higher peaks, this 
make sense because the peaks should not be able to go higher up than the mediums freezing point 
of approximately -1.96°C. Supercooling is as mentioned in section 2.4.2 the period from the 
freezing point until the peak appears, which is why the peaks should end at the temperature level 
of -1.96°C, as this is the temperature of crystallization. The slope of the temperature cycle increases 
the distance between the preprogrammed temperature cycle and -1.96°C as time passes, resulting 
in higher peaks.  
The connection between time of peak and peak height does not seems to have any connection with 
the amount of power being used.  
The length of peak, Table 8, however has a connection to the power. The higher power the smaller 
peak duration. This was expected, because when more power is available the faster the peak can 
be eliminated. 
Scaling:  
When the power is increased, a decreasing tendency is seen in scaled material in Figure 28. This 
highest case of power (20 W) have the lowest scaling. The length of the peaks could be accountable 
for the difference in scaled material, as the peak duration is shortened, when the power is increased.  
==PAGE:46==
DTU Civil Engineering 
 
 
38 
Note that the AEA specimen experiences zero scaling, even though it has been exposed to the most 
damaging power of 12 W, when comparing to the specimens with natural air content. This was 
expected as the specimen with AEA is frost resistant.  
The specimens exposed to 12 W are not fully comparable to the rest, as the specimens has not 
completely followed to temperature cycle, due to lack of power. The lack of power can contribute 
to a prolonged peak period, and thereby more scaling. However, the specimen has still been frozen 
and defrosted for the same period as the other specimens; the only difference is how low the 
temperature has been.  
4.4.3 Comparison of epoxy impregnated plane sections 
The UV photos supports the results from the plot of scaled material. Figure 29 of the specimen 
exposed to 14 W has significant more damages than the specimen exposed to 20 W. The green 
cracks in Figure 29 goes deeper into the concrete compared to Figure 30. Based on the photos it is 
not only the surface that has been damaged by the freeze-thaw cycle, as the damage propagate as 
cracks through the top of the concrete. It should be noted that the damages at the sides of both 
specimens were not caused by the freeze-thaw exposure, but from removal of the rubber sleeves 
before the specimens were epoxy impregnated.  
4.4.4 Future research  
As mentioned, the specimen of 12 W is questionable when comparing amount of scaled material. 
A solution could be to run a test on a new specimen, where it would be exposed to 12 W until end 
of peak, and then change the power to 14 W such that the temperatures would follow the 
temperature cycle. 
It could also be investigated how deep the damage would be on a UV photo, if the specimens were 
exposed to freeze-thaw cycles for more than 7 cycles. Mabey it could develop to severe internal 
damages, causing external damages to affect and trigger internal damage.

5 Common discussion of Part I and II  
It is difficult to compare the scaling results from the two experiments, because the conditions during 
the tests are different. The freezing cabinet have not completely followed the temperature cycle, 
the mini freezer has. In the mini freezer, the cooling element is directly in touch with the freezing 
medium. Whereas the freezing cabinet works by cooling the air inside the cabinet. Furthermore, 
the specimens in the cabinet are covered with a piece of plastic to prevent evaporation of the 
freezing medium. This plastic cover have an insulating effect because the air between the specimen 
and plastic is stagnant. These conditions can explain the difference in amount of scaling in the two 
experiments, the specimens in the mini freezer have more scaling after 7 cycles compared to the 
specimens in the cabinet.  
The results from the mini freezer establish the influence of power in relation to scaling. In 
DS/CEN/TS 12930-9 it is not stated how much power the freezing cabinet should use, only that it 
should run with a capacity such that the time-temperature curve presented can be followed for 
every position where a specimen is placed (DS/CEN/TS12390-9, 2006). Based on the results from 
the mini freezer it can be debated if this is a correct phrasing. The scaling and damage differs 
significantly when using a power of 14 W compared to 20 W, but both of them follows the 
temperature cycle stated in DS/CEN/TS 12930-9. This could also be the case when using a freezing 
cabinet, which would cause different results depending on the cabinet in use.  
Knowing that supercooling have an effect on the salt frost scaling it can be discussed whether the 
chosen setup of the freeze-thaw test in the freezing cabinet have been done correctly. The sensors 
that the cabinet navigated by were placed in specimens with freezing medium of H2O with a salt 
concentration of 3 % NaCl. However, not all of the freezing mediums used in the test had the same 
freezing point as H2O with 3% NaCl. This means that the freezing mediums with higher freezing 
points have experienced supercooling at an earlier stage. The freezing cabinet have not been able 
to eliminate these peaks, as it was navigating after a medium, which had not experienced 
supercooling yet. In Appendix I the logger measurements indicates that this scenario has happened 
during the test, as the orange and green graphs in the plots of cycle 0-7 and cycle 21-28, experience 
peaks earlier than the red graph of H2O with 3 % NaCl. This could explain why the specimens with 
3 % NaCl D2O have significant more scaling then the ones with H2O. D2O with 3% NaCl has a 
freezing point of 1.63 °C compared to the -1.96°C of H2O with 3 % NaCl. Another explanation 
==PAGE:48==

for the more scaling appearing on the D2O specimens could be because the medium have a higher 
freezing point and thereby have been frozen for a longer period than the H2O. This explanation fit 
well into the glue spall theory. The problem with the theory is that it cannot explain the difference 
in amount of scaling seen in the test with the mini freezer. The only condition being varied in this 
test was the power and the variation of power lead to a variation in scaling.

A specimen with 3 % NaCl dissolved in D2O could be tested in the mini freezer to investigate the 
scaling compared to the already obtained results from the mini freezer.

6 Conclusion 
A number of different conclusions can be made based on the outcomes of the two conducted tests. 
The capillary water seems to have an effect on the salt frost scaling within the first 7 cycles. This 
is concluded based on the specimens with D2O as capillary water, which had the highest amount 
of scaling before renewing of the freezing medium. The effect of the capillary water faded after the 
first renewing. It can be debated how much effect the capillary water actually has on the overall 
test period. 
Even though different mediums and salt concentrations were tested in Part I, overall it can be 
concluded that salt concentrations of 3% results in most damage. It is not the freezing point that 
determines the amount of damage, but the salt concentration, because the freezing point of 3% 
NaCl in D2O differs from 3% NaCl H2O. When looking at the combinations of salt and freezing 
medium the specimens with 3% NaCl in D2O had the most scaling. However, it cannot be 
concluded, if this is due to differences in the properties of H2O and D2O, or due to the freezing 
cabinet’s limitations regarding elimination of supercooling peaks. The freezing cabinet only 
navigates after one type of freezing medium, and in the test different mediums with different 
freezing points were used. 
In the investigation of supercooling, results shows that the duration of peaks have an impact on the 
amount of salt frost scaling. More power, and thereby faster elimination of peaks, results in less 
scaling. The Slab test (reference method) in DS/CEN/TS 12390-9 does not consider this, when 
describing the requirements of the freezing cabinets for the test. The lack of accuracy in this 
description can affect comparisons of freeze-thaw tests conducted with different cabinets.  
Lastly, it can be questioned if Valenza and Scherer’s statement in the glue spall theory regarding 
pore solution is true. They believe that the solution in the pores have no impact on the salt frost 
scaling. However, when looking at the results from the conducted tests, the pore solution seems to 
have an effect. After the first 7 cycles in the freezing cabinet, the specimens with D2O as capillary 
water had the most scaling. Furthermore, the glue spall theory cannot explain results obtained in 
relation to the effect of supercooling either.

Appendix D – Manual for CX-Thermo 
This appendix is a short manual concerning the freezing cabinet and computer program used to control 
the cabinet’s temperature. The freezing cabinet has been preprogramed, by the manufacturer to fit the 
temperature cycle outlined in the Technical Specification DS/CEN/TS 12390-9; however, it is possible 
to change this, using the computer program CX-Thermo. It should be noted, that this manual is based on 
the setup between computer and freezing cabinet available in building 118, room 47, at DTU. 
 
Using the freezing cabinet with preprogramed temperature period 
An on/off switch and controller display are located in the upper right corner of the freezing cabinet’s 
front. The switch is used when turning the cabinet on and off. The controller has various operations; 
however, it is recommended that it is only used to observe temperature and reset of temperature. The 
switch and display can be seen in Figure 3. 
 
When the freezing cabinet is turned on, the display automatically displays the measured temperature, 
PV, and the set point values, SV. SV is the preprogramed temperature which PV ideally should follow. 
It is possible to reset SV, this is done by pressing the two buttons, marked with green in Figure 3, at the 
same time, twice. At the first pressing, the compressor on top of the cabinet makes a click noise, and at 
the second pressing it starts working.  
At the end of a test or experiment, the freezing cabinet is shut off by pressing the on/off switch.

Appendix L – Temperature cycle of mini freezer 
When setting up the mini freezer, the temperature cycle needs to be entered into the program Manikin. 
The program requires the desired temperature for every hour; hence the Technical Specification 
DS/CEN/TS 12390-9 only provides upper and lower limits at 6 specified points, and a diagram where the 
points are connected. The temperature cycle for the mini freezer is placed in the middle of the limits, by 
finding the center between the points. Afterwards linear regressions are utilized to connect the new center 
points. Finally, the equation for each linear regression can be used for calculating the temperature levels 
for each hour. The upper limit, lower limit and calculated temperature cycle (Set value) can be seen in 
Figure 14. 
 
The calculated temperatures for every hour are listed in Table 46. This is the temperatures that have been 
entered into Manikin. The program’s temperature cycle is programed to start at hour 1.

Appendix M – Power needed for the mini freezer 
The computer program Manikin logs both temperature and power during the test and experiment. 
In Figure 15 below are the temperature cycles with power 10, 12 and 14 W pictured. The first test was 
with 10 W and then 12 W. It is seen that none of them have power enough to follow the limits stated in 
the DS/CEN/TS 12390-9. Another error is that they do not have enough cooling power to keep the 
programmed temperatures in the last part. This is because the cooling power when warming up was only 
set to 5 W to test how much power was needed to keep on track in that part of the cycle. The graph with 
14 W is within the limits. In this case the last part was set to 10 W, this is still not enough, because a 
small bump still appears. 
 
The power graphs for the 3 temperature cycles shows the same tendency, Figure 16. 10 and 12 W is not 
enough, which results in straight lines. It is also seen that it is a very short period of time the graph of 14 
W did not have enough power in the last part. To solve this problem the power is raised from 10 W to 12 
W in the last part of the cycle.
