Executive Summary
The report aims to evaluate the cost of production of e-jet fuel by minimizing the investment, ﬁxed
and variable operation and maintenance cost of plants/unit used to produce 10 Kton of e-jet fuel.
The optimization was performed using GAMS software and using CPLEX as a solver. The electric-
ity generating units considered were ﬁxed ground mounted solar, single axis tracking solar, double
axis tracking solar, offshore wind, onshore wind and gas turbines running on bio-methane. The main
feedstock was biogas from a nearby biogas facility. The electrolyzer considered are Proton Exchange
Membrane (PEM) due to their superior dynamic characterstics and fast response among different
electrolyzer types. In order to balance the ﬂuctuation in electricity, lithium Ion battery storage is also
considered. Morover, it was found that the biogas facility has a big biogas storage which can be used
and thus a ﬂexible operation of biogas input has been considered in the model. A hydrogen storage is
also considered so as to supply the hydrogen to downstream methanation plant when the electrolyzer
is not running at the required capacity. The production process selected to produce the e-jet fuel is
called the Fischer Tropsch process which takes syngas as input and produce e-jet fuel as output along
with other by-products such as gasoline, fuel gases and LPG. The Fischer Tropsch method for e-jet
fuel production was chosen because of its maturity, experience and ease of operating using feedstock
from multiple sources.
The syngas production mechanism chosen was based on using biogas as a feedstock and converting
it into CH4 by chemical methanation process by using green H2 produced using renewable energy
plants and subsequently converting CH4 using autothermal reforming to produce syngas. The net
cost of e-jet fuel was found to be 1.95 C/Kg using the mentioned production process. This cost is
based on the assumption that all the byproducts which are formed during the fuel production process
are sold in the market at appropriate prices. Biogas cost forms 30% of the total cost of production.
The favourable power plant according to the model is offshore wind, with almost 40% of the total
investment cost of the e-jet fuel plant. The sensitivity analysis of e-jet fuel was performed leading to
e-jet fuel cost range from 1.81 C/Kg to 2.06 C/Kg which is about 4.4 to 5 times the fossil jet fuel cost.
The investment cost ranged from 36% to 46.7% while the variable operation and maintenance cost
(which includes the biogas purchasing cost) ranged between 40.5% to 53.1% respectively. The e-jet
fuel cost range found using this study conﬁrms well with other similar studies conducted in academia.

Introduction
3.1
Need of Sustainable Jet fuel / Perspective
Anthropogenic activities have lead to signiﬁcant air pollution since the industrial revolution [12].
Aviation is one of the many sectors which contributes to this problem. As per International Air Trans-
port Association, commercial aviation is responsible for about 2-3% of global carbon emissions [13].
Aviation is also responsible for about 3.5% of effective radiative forcing, which gives an indication
of contribution by aviation in heating up the atmosphere [14]. For quantiﬁcation, domestic aviation
is counted in each country’s own carbon emissions, but emission from international aviation is not
counted in a country’s net emissions. Moreover, it is estimated that about 80% of CO2 emissions from
aviation sector are emitted from ﬂights of more than 1500 km, for which there is no practical alter-
native mode of transport [15]. In Denmark, domestic and international aviation in total accounted for
about 28% of equivalent CO2 emissions in the transport sector [16] with a fuel consumption (primary
energy) of 1.8 PJ and 36.5 PJ respectively in 2015 [17].
However, there has been a considerable willingness to reduce the impact by aviation sector on net
carbon emissions. In 2009, the aviation industry ﬁnalised a strategy for reducing the carbon emis-
sions whose main goals were to improve the average fuel efﬁciency by 1.5% every year from 2009
to 2020, have a carbon-neutral growth from 2020 and reduce the net aviation CO2 emissions by 50%
by 2050, relative to 2005 levels [13]. In 2016, governments adopted CORSIA, a scheme to offset
and reduce the carbon emissions from international aviation, starting from 2021 [18]. In this scheme,
2019 emissions from international emissions were recommended to be taken as a baseline, amounting
to about 600 million tons of CO2. Danish aviation sector has also sent encouraging signals for CO2
reduction. The industry’s goal is to reduce a minimum of 70% CO2 emissions caused due to domestic
ﬂights by 2030 as compared to 1990 [19]. Further, a minimum of 30% reduction in CO2 emissions
caused by international ﬂights is also envisaged by 2030 as compared to 2017. Finally, the Danish
aviation sector aims to be climate neutral by 2050.
The ambitious goals put forward by Danish aviation industry for CO2 emission reduction will need a
bunch of extra-ordinary measures. Some of the measures which are already implemented by interna-
tional aviation industry or are in the stage of research and development are listed below:
1. ASTM approved biogenic jet fuel production pathways using biomass or other organic raw
materials[20][21].
2. Flights operated using batteries [22].
3. Fuel efﬁciency measures [23]
4. Improved air trafﬁc management [23]
Bio-jet fuel as mentioned in point number 1 above are mainly derived from vegetable oils, animal
fats, biomass and other organic matter and can be blended with fossil jet fuel with varying level of
percentages depending upon the raw material [24]. According to IRENA, usage of these bio-fuels
does not require any modiﬁcations in existing jet engines. In 2011, American Society for Testing
and Materials certiﬁed the bio-jet fuel derived from hydro-processed Esters and Fatty Acids (HEFA
bio-jet), using oleo-chemical feed-stocks such as oil and fats. Jet fuel using Fischer Tropsch process
or thermal gasiﬁcation can be produced from a number of feedstocks including biogas, biomass, cap-
tured CO2 from air or ﬂue gases and hydrogen produced from electrolysis using industrial processes

However, it must also be mentioned that bio-fuels based on biomass doesn’t seem to be a long term

Electric aircrafts have been a topic of research lately. In 2018, Norwegian state owned airport author-

is based on the premise that electric batteries will offer sufﬁcient energy density within next 10-15

aircraft E-Fan [22]. In June 2020, an electric plane powered by a 750-horsepower electric motor and
supplied with energy by more than 2000 pounds of lithium-ion batteries ﬂew for over 28 minutes and
being dubbed as the largest electric plane yet, although having a room for only 9 passengers [27].
Proponents of the electric aircraft technology have re-stated the fact that the electric propulsion can
only be feasible for short-haul domestic ﬂights for a foreseeable future. The reason is energy-density
difference between commercially available Lithium-Ion batteries and fossil jet fuel.

3.3
Compatibility of Sustainable Jet fuel in current aircrafts
The renewable jet fuel to be discussed in this study is synthesized using renewable energy and is
considered to be carbon-neutral. This is because even-though the usage of renewable jet fuel will
lead to CO2 emissions, but during its production, carbon is absorbed through various mediums such
as atmosphere or biomass, making it carbon-neutral. However, in order to use renewable jet fuel in
current aircrafts, it must be ensured that the physical and chemical properties of renewable jet fuel are
almost identical to conventional jet fuel. The sustainable or hydro-carbon based renewable jet fuel
produced using methods such Fischer-Tropsch or biomass gasiﬁcation are quite similar in chemical
and physical properties to fossil jet fuel.
American Society for Testing and Materials, now called ASTM International sets pre-requisites such
as composition, volatility, ﬂuidity, combustion, corrosion, thermal stability, contaminants and addi-
tives of the jet fuels. In 1959 [29], ASTM International presented standard D1655 ’Standard Speciﬁ-
cation for Aviation Turbine Fuels’ for conventional jet fuels such as jet A and jet A1. In 2009, ASTM
International approved standard D7566 ’Standard Speciﬁcation for Aviation Turbine Fuel Containing
Synthesized Hydrocarbons’, a standard describing the jet fuel not derived from petroleum crude [30].
These synthesized molecules are referred to as “synthetic parafﬁnic kerosene” or SPK [30]. ASTM
International in D7566 gives details about the blending percentage of SPK and conventional jet fuel
and also explain the various production pathways that can be used to produce the SPK.
In September 2009, Fischer-Tropsch (FT) production pathway was approved by ASTM to produce
SPK. The FT SPK process can take virtually any carbonaceous raw material such as organic matter
in municipal solid waste (MSW), forest and agricultural residues, and turn them into transportation
fuels such as renewable diesel and SAF [30]. In the current standard D7566, SPK produced using
FT is allowed to be blended with conventional jet fuel to a maximum of 50% [30] and this mandate
certiﬁes the blend to be equivalent to conventional jet fuel, thus fulﬁlling D1655.
FT based jet fuels have some distinct advantages as compared to conventional jet fuels. They burn
cleaner in aircraft engines and generally produce approximately 2.4% less carbon dioxide, 50–90%
less particulate matter, and 100% less sulfur than conventional petroleum-based jet fuels while burn-
ing [31]. Superior thermal stability and remarkable low temperature properties of FT derived jet fuels
make them highly suitable for high altitude operation and low temperature startup. However, jet fuel
derived from FT process (referring to SPK) lack aromatic hydrocarbons that are useful to prevent
failure of aircraft engine seals. Hence, a required amount of aromatic hydrocarbons must be added to
SPK from FT process to solve this issue [32].

3.5
Method Overview
The methodology used to ﬁnd out the cost of sustainable jet fuel in this study will be based on mini-
mizing the total cost of production of a certain amount of jet fuel using linear optimization. The total
cost will be composed of investment cost of different plants, the cost of their ﬁxed operation, the cost
of their variable operation and the cost of feedstocks used to run the plant. Based on the selection of
a particular jet fuel production pathway, there could be different feedstocks and assets which needs
to be taken into account while making an investment. The selected jet fuel production pathway will
govern the total investments which needs to be made and the type of feedstocks required. Therefore,
the approved jet fuel production pathways by ASTM will be analysed brieﬂy and a ﬁnal pathway will
be chosen based upon its suitability in Danish context and its maturity.
After selecting a jet fuel production pathway, it is important to choose a suitable location which
not only enables selling of the produced jet fuel but also other downstream by-products in order to
have additional revenue streams. Morover, the location is also important as the jet fuel needs to be
produced using renewable energy and thus the selected location must be favorable for installing and
producing the renewable energy using technologies such as solar and wind. Hence, a location would
be chosen based upon several factors such as demand of jet fuel and by-products, availability of feed-
stock as well as high wind/solar conditions.
Lastly, it must also be decided beforehand if the jet fuel production plant should buy the electricity
from the grid i.e. should it be connected to the grid or should it be an off-grid plant running solely by
renewable energy power plants along with appropriate energy storages to take care of low wind/solar
conditions. However, this decision is very complex as it might be possible that sufﬁcient infrastructure
for grid connection might not be in place and would be extremely expensive to build that out. Hence,
several actors and inputs must be considered before this decision is made. Such inputs are out of
scope of this study and hence for simpliﬁcation an off-grid jet fuel production plant is considered in
this study.

Sustainable jet fuel is a term coined by aviation industry to denote a non-conventional (non-fossil
derived) aviation fuel [33]. This type of fuel can be produced from a number of non-fossil feedstocks
including biological resources i.e plant and animal materials or non-biological resources i.e munici-

the conventional aviation fuel and hence can blended with conventional jet fuel in differing percent-

have limited fresh-water requirements, their production should not compete with food production and

ASTM doesn’t guarantees that these pathways are sustainable. Therefore, before choosing any of the

In the Danish context, biomass including wood pellets, wood chips and straw are used extensively
for heat/power production and in the recent past, biomass has been replacing coal in combined heat
and power plants. As per a report by DEA [34], in 2018, wood accounted for 75% of solid biomass,
while biodegradable waste and straw accounted for 13% and 12%, respectively. More than half of
woody biomass used in Denmark is imported from abroad [34]. Similarly, biogas, which is produced
using animal manure, food waste and sewage sludge etc is also used for heat/power production. The
feedstock for biogas is collected from farmers, supermarkets and from municipalities collecting and
sorting the waste. The biogas plants are scattered all around Denmark, but they are connected through
a natural gas grid to which they feed bio-methane. The pathways 1, 2 and 5 mentioned in table 2 can
use biogas and woody biomass as feedstock for SAF production.
Although both biomass and biogas can be used for sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) production, this
study concentrates on producing SAF using biogas. This is because of two reasons. Firstly, as men-
tioned previously that Denmark imports about 50% of its woody biomass from other countries. There
is no data which can prove that the biomass is imported in a sustainable manner and doesn’t result
in any land use change in the countries from which it is imported. This induces an uncertainty on
whether the SAF produced using biomass will have reduced lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions or
not. Morover, there is no guarantee that biomass import can be increased proportionally if there is
an increased demand for SAF in Denmark and the production need to be scaled up, thus creating an-
other uncertainty on security of supply. Secondly, as the infrastructure for biogas distribution is quite
robust with almost all biogas plants connected to gas grid, location selection and scalability of SAF
production facility will not be restricted by a single biogas plant and thus SAF production facility can
be set up anywhere in Denmark, provided other metrics warrant the selection of a speciﬁc location.

input to the Fischer Tropsch reactor is syngas [32], which is a mixture of H2 and CO in the ratio

way number 5 in table 2 have production of an alcohol as the main intermediate step. The alcohol
can be methanol, ethanol or isomers of butanol [21]. The alcohol can then be converted into SAF
by dehydrating it into light oleﬁns, and then oligomerization to more heavier oleﬁns and subsequent
hydrogenation to parafﬁns in the range of jet-fuel [35] [21]. In the upcoming section, production of
syngas using different feedstocks will be discussed as syngas is the main input to Fischer Tropsch.

Reformation of upgraded biogas to syngas
Syngas production is one of the most critical as well as expensive step during production of SAF
through Gas to Liquid process [39]. As mentioned earlier, syngas is a mixture of CO and H2. The
ratio of H2 and CO is one of the determining factors of the output product yield after the Fischer
Tropsch synthesis [32]. In order to maximize the SAF yield, the ratio of H2:CO must be 2 [40], [32].
In this section, an introduction about the different reformation technologies will be given, listing their
advantages and disadvantages.
The three widely used reformation technologies are:
1. Steam Methane Reforming
2. Partial Oxidation
3. Autothermal Reforming
For Steam Methane Reforming (SMR), a process temperature of about 800-900 ◦C (lowest amongst
all reformer types) and a pressure of about 20-30 atm needs to be maintained [32]. The main chemical
reaction characterising this process are:
CH4 +H2O+δH −→CO+3H2,δH = +206KJ/Mol
(4)
CO+H2O+δH −→CO2 +H2,δH = +41KJ/Mol
(5)
In order to prevent formation of coke in the reactor tubes, a stream of excess steam is required, which
leads to a H2:CO ratio more than 5:1, which is suitable for ammonia and hydrogen production, but
not for Fischer Tropsch synthesis [32]. The H2:CO is then needs to be adjusted using the water gas
shift reaction (WGSR) 11. As WGSR involves feeding of CO2 in order to favour formation of CO,
this reformation technology is also suitable for feedstocks other than pure methane such as biogas
which has a CO2 content of about 35-40%. The SMR technology produces highest CO2 emissions
amongst all reformers.
In the Partial Oxidation (PO), also called as gasiﬁcation, methane is oxidised with an insufﬁcient
supply of oxygen at a temperature about 1300-1400 ◦C. This reaction produces a H2:CO ratio in the
range 1.6-1.9 and is exothermic [39]. PO produces low CO2 emissions however high temperature
heat recovery and product handling adds to process complexity.
CH4 +1/2O2 ←→CO+2H2,
(6)
The Autothermal reforming (ATR) is a combination of SMR and PO, running in a single reactor at
a temperature of about 950-1050 ◦C. The heat required for processing the Steam methane reforming
reaction is provided by the oxidation reaction and thus ATR doesn’t need any external heat source
[39]. The ATR produces a syngas ratio close to 2 which is favourable to FT synthesis for producing
jet fuel. It must be mentioned that for large scale FT applications, ATR appears to be more economical
than SMR and also produces low CO2 emissions [39]. The reactions characterising the autothermal
reformation of methane are mentioned below:
CH4 +1/2O2 ←→CO+2H2,∆H = −35.2KJ/mol
(7)
CH4 +H2O ←→CO+3H2,∆H = 206.1KJ/mol

ture. The relevant chemical reactions [32] and the general process steps in ﬁg 2 characterising a FT

catalysts are active to Water gas shift reaction which in turns supports to conduct FT synthesis using
a syngas which has high H2:CO ratio. Also, Co- based catalyst are more expensive than Fe-based

The FT synthesis is carried out in a reactor. The reactor technology used in past commercial FT
projects is either Fixed bed reactor or Slurry bed reactor or Fluidized bed reactor. As per Arno de
Klerk [39], the operating robustness of ﬁxed bed FT technology is much better than that of other two
reactors. Morover, the syngas cleaning requirements for ﬁxed bed reactors are less stringent and ﬁxed
bed technology can weather operating upsets in syngas production, cleaning and conditioning better
than either slurry bed or ﬂuidized bed technologies. However, it is not possible to replace the catalyst
online when it gets de-activated in the ﬁxed bed-reactor while this is possible in the other two reac-
tors. This means that Fixed bed reactors will have more downtime in their lifetime. More information
regarding the reactor technologies will be provided in Appendix.

2. Although HTFT syncrude contains more aromatics than LTFT syncrude, there is little differ-

zeolite based technology is employed.
3. Oligomerisation and hydrocracking technologies that are typically employed to adjust the LTFT
carbon number distribution to maximise jet fuel, introduce branching into the product that result
in jet fuel with low freezing point, which is must needed physical property.

exact oxygen consumption near the city of Esbjerg. In order to reduce the cost of produced jet fuel,
it must be ensured that the oxygen as a by-product can be sold in the market. Although, no speciﬁc
application has been identiﬁed near Esbjerg which uses oxygen, this study has assumed all the excess
oxygen can be sold in the market. This is due to the fact that the study has been conducted for the year
2030 and oxygen is one of the byproduct of water electrolysis as well. So, it is possible that industrial
applications for oxygen might be created in the future.

Data Inputs and Model Formulation
As mentioned in section 1.6, the main research question of this study is to ﬁnd out the cost of pro-
duction of e-jet fuel using Fischer Tropsch process in order to satisfy a given demand of jet fuel.
As described in section 2.4, the fuel production using Fischer Tropsch process consists of several
steps and inputs such as Biogas, electricity, hydrogen production, methanation, syngas production,
syncrude production and reﬁning. The methodology used in this study is to determine the optimal
capacities of power generating units, electrolyzer, methanation plant, syngas production unit and Fis-
cher Tropsch plant so as to minimize the total investment and operation costs subject to restriction
that the plant can be operated to fulﬁll the e-jet fuel demand as well as the mass and energy balances
are maintained. The main assumption is that the electricity input to the plant is taken from the renew-
able energy power plants such as offshore wind, onshore wind and solar. No electricity input from
the electricity grid has been assumed. In order to keep running the fuel plant during low electricity
production hours from renewable plants, a lithium ion battery backup is considered. In order to ensure
continuous operation of downstream plants, hydrogen gas storage have also been considered.
6.1
Time Horizon and E-jet Fuel Demand
The study has been performed considering that the investment decision for the plant will be made in
2030. Hence, all investment and operational cost related parameters have been assumed for 2030. The
optimization model will run in order to fulﬁll a speciﬁc demand of the output required. In this study,
the output is e-jet fuel. The production of e-jet fuel is limited by the quantity of biogas available as
biogas is the main feedstock for e-jet fuel production in this study. As mentioned in section 3.2, that
the maximum annual biogas input available is 5.13*10^8 MJ. This amount of biogas will be sufﬁcient
to produce 10^7 Kg of e-jet fuel. Hence, the demand which needs to be fulﬁlled in the optimization
model is 10 Ktons of e-jet fuel

Mathematical Model
As mentioned in previous sections, the main aim of this study is to ﬁnd out the cost of production of
e-jet fuel by minimizing the investment, operation and fuel cost in setting up different units to produce
the e-jet fuel. The investment cost is basically the ﬁxed cost paid for a certain capacity to be avail-
able through-out a long term planning time period or horizon. The variable operation cost is incurred
while operating the installed capacity of a unit in every time interval of the horizon. However, as the
planning horizon of the fuel production facility will be quite large (atleast 25-30 years) compared to
the time interval (which is usually around 1 hour), these large number of time intervals will result in
optimization solver taking a very long time to produce the results. In order to simplify and reduce
the model size, the investment costs are annualized by converting them into annual payments. Con-
sequently, the model can be restricted to 1 year. Therefore, in the present study, the model has been
optimized for 1 year by annualizing the investment costs.

Sensitivity Analysis
8.1
Description of the input variables
The sensitivity analysis will be performed on input factors which have the tendency to change the
results of the optimization model. In the previous section, two different scenarios were explained by
discussing the cost of e-jet fuel, investment cost and operation and maintenance cost. The scenario 2
gave a lower cost of e-jet fuel. As the main objective of this study is to ﬁnd out the cost of e-jet fuel,
and scenario 2 has given better results, therefore the scenario 2 will be considered as the reference for
the sensitivity analysis. The sensitivity analysis will be done in 3 cases by changing following inputs
in the base model:
1. Case:1 - Lower Biogas Price- The biogas price considered in the base model is equal to 57.2
C/MWh. However, this was the average cost of biogas with the cost range equal to 52 - 63.7
C/MWh [3]. Therefore, in case 1, the sensitivity analysis will be performed by taking lower
value of biogas price i.e 52 C/MWh. It is expected that with lower biogas cost, the e-jet fuel
cost will decrease as compared to the base model.
2. Case:2 - Higher minimum load operation of units - As the operation of units such as elec-
trolyzer, methanation, syngas and Fischer Tropsch are quite dynamic and they can operate at
variable loads, it is important to understand and see how the model will react if the operation
of these units is made more rigid, i.e the minimum load at which they were operating in the
base model is increased by a certain factor. In the base model, the electrolyzer, methanation
unit, syngas unit and Fischer Tropsch unit were assumed to operate at 20%, 20%, 30% and 30%
load respectively. In the case 2 of sensitivity analysis, these units are considered to operate at
30%, 30%, 40% and 40% respectively. The minimum load operation of power plants is left
unchanged.
3. Case:3 - Higher running efﬁciency of Gas turbine - Gas turbine takes bio-methane (normally
methane) as input in order to produce heat and electricity [60]. The efﬁciency of conversion of
gas to heat and electricity was considered as 50% in the base scenario. Now this efﬁciency is
assumed to increased to 70% in the case 3. this is because the efﬁciency of gas turbine lies in
the range 50% - 70% [60]. Hence, it would be interesting to ﬁnd the model results if the gas
turbines are operating at maximum efﬁciency.
The model results of all the 3 cases and the base case will be shown alongside in order to have a
better comparison. Only those results will be shown which had higher impact due to change in input
variables.

Discussion and Further Research
One of the main assumption that has been taken in this study is that all the byproducts of the e-jet fuel
production process will be able to be sold in the market. The main by-products are oxygen, excess
heat, side-products formed after Fischer Tropsch process such as Gasoline, fuel gas and LPG. The
chemical side-products might be fully sold in the market as they have high value and could be used in
transportation including cars, buses and trucks or even in industries or houses for speciﬁc application
and cooking respectively. However, product like excess heat which has the highest share of revenue
in the by-products are more difﬁcult to manage. This is because there must be well running district
heating grid to which the excess heat can be injected. Or there should be high industrial demand for
heat. But in summers, the heat demand would reduce and thereby all the excess heat could not be
sold in the market, thereby eroding the assumed revenue from selling heat. In order to take care of
this issue, there must be provision of seasonal heat storage such that excess heat generated during
summers can be used during the winter. However, seasonal heat storage will also require a capital
investment, thereby increasing the net e-jet fuel production cost. Hence, it must be veriﬁed if the ad-
ditional investment in the seasonal heat storage is beneﬁting to improve the e-jet fuel cost economics
or if it would be more better to just release the excess heat in the atmosphere.
Secondly, the model resolution has been taken to be 1 hour. This means that it was not possible
to take into account the ramping characterstics of different intermediate units such as electrolyzer,
methanation unit, syngas unit and Fischer Tropsch Unit as they have ramping constants in minutes.
Thus, in order to take ramping up/down constraints into consideration, the model resolution must be
reduced from 1 hour to 15 minutes or so depending on the ramping constants of most of the units.
Additionally , this model for the sake of faster optimization (reducing the time for solver to optimize)
has not considered the start-up and shut-down cost of different units. It is deﬁnite that for a certain
time duration the units/plants would be switched off for periodic/annual maintenance. Also, there
might be some instances when a plant would get shut down due to some failure. Hence, in a more
sophisticated model, the start-up and shut down constraints must be modelled to arrive at a more re-
alistic cost estimate.
Lastly, as this study was done considering that the jet fuel plant is not connected to the grid, it would be
interesting to see if the plant is connected to the grid and can import as well as export the electricity to
the grid, how would the e-jet fuel cost change and how would the capacities for different units/plants
would change. It is known that for a certain hours of the year the electricity cost is very high and
for a certain hours the electricity cost from the grid is lower. So, it would be interesting to see if the
e-jet fuel plant import the electricity from the grid during the low electricity price, how the system
dyanamics would change, Being given more time, i would deﬁnitely analyse all the cases mentioned
here.

Conclusion
The ﬁnal results of this study has indicated that the cost to produce 10 Ktons of e-jet fuel in a year
in Esbjerg location in Western Denmark using biogas as a feedstock and Fischer Tropsch as a pro-
duction process and the fuel plant not connected to the grid is 1.95 C/Kg. This e-jet fuel cost is 4.73
times higher than the fossil jet fuel cost. It must be mentioned that the cost of feedstock i.e. the
biogas forms almost 30% of the cost of producing e-jet fuel cost, which is the 2nd highest cost ele-
ment in the whole production process after the cost of investment in renewable energy power plants.
Also, as the location of e-jet fuel plant is decided in Esbjerg, close to the North sea, the optimization
model invested heavily in the offshore wind, simply because of high wind resource in the North sea.
Also, the model did not invested in the double tracking solar power plant. This could be because of
high investment cost of this technology and low energy to capacity ratio. Also, the model curtailed
a portion of generating electricity by solar/wind power plants in order to get the optimal capacities
of downstream units and thereby decrease the total cost of production. Morover, sensitivity analysis
was performed on three different factors namely decreasing the biogas cost, increasing the lower limit
on load of intermediate units and increasing the gas turbine fuel efﬁciency. The sensitivity analysis
resulted in a e-jet fuel cost of 1.81 C/Kg, 2.06 C/Kg and 1.85 C/Kg respectively for the three cases
mentioned.
This study was conducted in order to calculate the cost of production of e-jet fuel by an ASTM ap-
proved production pathway. Several pathways were discussed brieﬂy and it was ﬁnally decided that
Fischer Tropsch process with biogas as a feedstock is the most preferred pathway in Danish context,
simply because Danish energy system has a huge number of biogas plants scattered all over the coun-
try and the Fischer Tropsch process is one of the oldest gas to liquid fuel production process used
in the industry. The maturity and experience about this process overshadow other gas to liquid fuel
production processes. Morover, the main input to Fisher Tropsch process is syngas which can be
produced using a variety of feedstocks/processes such as biogas, biomass gasiﬁcation, chemical syn-
thesis of captured CO2 from point sources/air and H2. Thus, Fischer Tropsch process is not restricted
to the category of the feedstock.
Another important variable when deciding on investing in an e-jet fuel production plant is the loca-
tion of the plant. This is because there must be sufﬁcient demand for e-jet fuel close to the production
plant, otherwise the shipping cost will further increase the retail cost of e-jet fuel. The location is
also important because if the plant needs to be powered using green electricity, then there must be
adequate solar and wind resource available at that location. Additionally, the economics of the e-jet
fuel production plant can be improved in there is a market for selling the byproducts produced during
the production process such as excess heat, oxygen or other chemicals.
Carbon emissions have increased rapidly since industrialisation and air travel has become more and
more common for a wide range of world population and thus has lead to increase in carbon and other
harmful emissions due to domestic as well as international aviation. However, after the Paris Climate
agreement and other subsequent agreements by International Civil Aviation Organization and other
aviation stakeholders, there has been a willingness to reduce CO2 emissions due to aviation. There
has been a number of solution in the market, capable to reduce CO2 emissions in the aviation sector.
Methods such as use of electric batteries for short haul ﬂights, use of bio-fuels, use of improved air
trafﬁc management and other fuel efﬁciency measures have been tested in the recent past and a lot of
research is ongoing into new and innovative methods of reducing CO2 emissions. American Society
of Testing and Materials (ASTM) has laid out a set of jet fuel production methods which can be used
to produce bio-jet fuel. The jet fuel derived from these methods could be used as a drop-in fuel or be
mixed with the fossil fuel, without any large changes in the current aviation infrastucture.
