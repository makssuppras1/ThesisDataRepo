Abstract 
 
 
 
 
 
  
 
 
Abstract 
The importance of assessing thermal comfort in urban planning is increasing. The climate of urban areas 
is significantly different from the climate of natural areas. The built environment influences the urban 
climate to a huge extend. In order to determine to which extend the built environment is influencing the 
urban climate, different approaches can be chosen. Simulations require a lot of computational power 
and can be very time consuming. Measurements on site are time consuming as well and require a lot of 
work. This work investigates a different approach in form of data analysis for the determination of heat 
and cold stress for the assessment of climate comfort in open urban areas. The main focus is the Uni-
versal Thermal Climate Index (UTCI) and its potential for urban planning.  
The statistical analysis is focusing on measurements conducted by DTU students in 2018 and climate 
data measured at DTU. The mean radiant temperature will be estimated using an estimation of the globe 
temperature. The results of both estimations are compared to each other. The UTCI is calculated using 
both, measured and estimated mean radiant temperature.  
The estimation of the globe temperature proofed to be more or less accurate, depending on the cloud 
cover of the measurement day. A weak correlation between cloud cover and accuracy of globe temper-
ature estimation can be investigated. The accuracy of the globe temperature however has a huge influ-
ence on the calculation of the mean radiant temperature, as the equation used for the calculation is highly 
sensitive towards changes in globe temperature. As there is a high influence on the mean radiant tem-
perature, the calculation of the UTCI is also highly influenced by the accuracy of globe temperature 
estimation and therefore on the cloud cover.

Introduction

1 Introduction 
The importance of considering thermal comfort in urban planning is increasing. As stated by MUSCO 
ET. AL. 2016, urban planning has to consider the challenges of climate change, one of those challenges 
is an increasing air temperature and therefor an increasing thermal stress. According to the ROBERT 
KOCH INSTITUT 2019, thermal stress leads to an increasing amount of heat related diseases. from which 
especially elderly and children are affected. In addition to heat related diseases, an increasing tempera-
ture inside urban spaces leads to an increasing amount of energy use inside building through an increas-
ing indoor temperature as stated by BRITZE 2019.  
This leads to the need of introducing thermal stress into urban planning strategies. The influence of the 
urban climate on the human body can be visualized by the Universal Thermal Climate Index, which 
consists of four meteorological parameters, the air temperature, the relative humidity, the wind speed 
and the mean radiant temperature. There are several ways of obtaining the UTCI for an urban area, the 
first being on-site measurements with the use of heat stress trackers, the second being simulations. Both 
methodologies proofed to be time consuming or computationally expensive. Another methodology for 
the calculation of the UTCI in order to rate the climate of an urban area is needed, which is neither time 
consuming nor computationally expensive.  
This thesis tries to answer the question, if an integration of thermal stress into urban planning guidelines 
is possible, while focusing on the UTCI as a tool for urban planning. In order to investigate the usability 
of the UTCI in urban planning, a statistical analysis of climate data was conducted. This statistical anal-
ysis is based on on-site measurements in various urban settings in Copenhagen conducted by students 
of DTU in the year 2018 as well as climate data provided by the DTU climate station. Literature research 
is used to search for solutions regarding the integration of thermal stress in urban planning guidelines 
and the overall use of the UTCI.  
The UTCI estimation is based on an estimation of the globe temperature which is used for calculating 
the mean radiant temperature. An estimation of the mean radiant temperature is a difficult task, as it 
either requires measurement or simulation, as the calculation of the UTCI.  
This project’s goal is to propose on how to use the UTCI as an index in urban planning guidelines. In 
the following, this thesis tries to answer to what extent this is possible and how to use the UTCI as a 
tool and how to make the UTCI easier to use in practice.

2.1 
Urban Planning 
The quality of public spaces is dependent on various factors. The design of a high-quality urban space / 
environment requires intense planning. GEHL 2006 describes public spaces and their function. City 
space must be carefully designed to invite walking, cycling, and staying as stated by GEHL 2006. Anal-
ysis of historic pictures show, that the function of a public space changes throughout the years. Accord-
ing to GEHL 2006, the main function of public spaces around the 1900s are work related. Crowded 
streets with people with most of whom are using the city for daily activities. The use of these public 
spaces has changed in the 2000s. Necessary activities play a subordinate role. Recreational activities 
increased in importance in urban public spaces. The car claims more and more of the urban space starting 
from the 1950s in Denmark. The pressure of car traffic and functional planning in the 1960s triggered a 
counter-reaction to reclaim the city space as stated by GEHL 2006.  
Cities can be classified in four types, mentioned by GEHL 2006. The traditional city, where most activ-
ities are carried out on foot and the city is still used as a meeting place, a connection space or a market-
place. The invaded city is a city where a single function like traffic is dominant on the expense of other 
functions. An abandoned city is a city where car traffic dominates the entire public space and other 
functions no longer exist, public life is not possible in these cities. The fourth type is the reconquered 
city, where urban planning is used to reconquer urban spaces in order to make them usable for public 
life. 
According to GEHL 2006 City life in the 21st century can be divided into 10 Dimensions. The dimensions 
are transport, work sustainability, health, information and inspiration, recreation, social interaction, de-
mocracy and diversity, friendliness and feeling of security and room for the unexpected.  
GEHL 2006 points out, that for people to sit outside and enjoy life, they need to be comfortable with the 
thermal environment. To provide a satisfying urban environment is crucial and can be achieved through 
various actions. These actions are based on the individual setting and location of the public space, 
MUSCO ET. AL. 2016 points out that for some years, urban planning has changed in relation to challenges 
set by climate change. Topics like sustainability, mitigation, adaptation, renewable energy, low carbon 
transition, eco system approach and post disaster planning are now part of urban planning.

2.3 
Influences on Urban Climate 
An urban area influences the regional climate and air-quality to a high extend in contrast to undeveloped 
areas, as stated in KUTTLER 2004. Climates, influenced by built structures are called urban climates, in 
which the expression urban climate is the interaction between urban areas and climate, in which the 
climate is additionally influenced through anthropogenic trance substances and waste heat. According 
to HAGEN, STILES AND TRIMMEL 2010, distinctive between urban climate and climates in peripheral 
areas is the difference in temperature, wind, precipitation, and air pollution. According to KUTTLER

2004, an increasing heating of settlements can be observed, which is influencing health of humans, who 
are living in these. Due to an increasing urbanization, more people are affected by the negative conse-
quences of urban climates. The influence of a dense development on the temperature of an area can be 
observed all year, the highest influence however can be detected in dry conditions with low wind veloc-
ities. This effect can be explained through low convective heat transfer due to low wind velocities and 
low surface evaporation and therefor low latent heat transfer.  
Urban climates are divided into microclimates and mesoclimates, these can be distinguished by their 
size. The horizontal spread of mesoclimate vary between 20 and 200 km whereas the horizontal spread 
of microclimates is between 20 m and 20 km as stated in KUTTLER 2004. 
The formation of the urban climate is depending on numerous factors as stated by KUTTLER 2004. A 
high concentration of anthropogenic trace substances leads to diffuse solar radiation. The reflection of 
shortwave radiation is depending on the colour, structure, and geometry of buildings. Comparable values 
between urban areas and the countryside can be observed in absence of snow. Especially in Mediterra-
nean areas, a higher albedo can be observed due to the colour of buildings that are typical in these 
regions. KUTTLER 2004 elucidates that a higher absorption regarding longwave radiation can be detected 
in urban spaces, as a result of a higher absolute heat capacity. Reemission of infrared active gases and 
particles as well as a hotter city atmosphere with lower wind velocities are leading to an increased 
longwave counter radiation from the atmosphere above the city to the ground.  
HAGEN, STILES AND TRIMMEL 2010 designate the influences on the urban climate to solar radiation, 
wind, surface roughness, sealed surfaces, specific heat capacity, albedo, human heat production (process 
heat, eg. traffic or factories) and the sky view factor.  
The wind field inside a city can differ largely between city and rural areas. According to KUTTLER 2004 
and HAGEN, STILES AND TRIMMEL 2010, this has an enormous influence on the climate of urban spaces.

Thermal Comfort in Outdoor Areas 
According to VDI 3787-2:2008 climate and air quality must be considered in urban and regional plan-
ning. The regional level should be relevant for human health and wellbeing. The field of human biome-
teorology deals with the effects of weather conditions, climate and air quality on the human organism 
and therefor the beforehand described planning requirements fall within this field. The field of biome-
teorology can be divided into three specific areas as stated in VDI 3787-2:2008. The first area is about 
thermal factors which comprises meteorological elements of air temperature, air humidity, wind velocity 
and short as well as long wave radiation. The significance to human health is associated with the close 
linking of thermoregulation and circulatory regulation. This thesis will focus on this area of biometeor-
ology.  
The second area are air quality factors. In this area the air pollution through solid, liquid and gaseous 
anthropogenic or natural pollutants are assessed.  
The last area covers actinic factors which cover the visible and ultraviolet spectrum of solar radiation, 
which have a direct effect on human beings beyond thermal ones.  
2.4.1 
The Universal Thermal Climate Index (UTCI) 
According to KRÜGER 2021 the UTCI was launched in 2009 within the framework of the International 
Society of Biometeorology. The UTCI is used to rate the thermal comfort of humans in outdoor envi-
ronment and is based on the advanced multi-node model of thermoregulation by Fiala as stated by JEN-
DRITZKY, DE DEAR AND HAVENITH 2011. The Fiala model was validated intensively and was coupled 
with a state-of-the-art clothing model, which takes behavioural adaptation of clothing insulation into 
account. According to BLAZEJCZYK ET AL. 2013 the UTCI is defined as the air temperature of the Ref-
erence condition causing the same model response as actual conditions and is dependent on the air tem-
perature, the wind speed, the mean radiant temperature, and the water vapor pressure. This definition 
may be expressed using following mathematical equation 
UTCI = f(Tair; Tmrt; vair;vp) = Ta + Offset(Tair; Tmrt;vair; vp) 
 
(2.3)

Where Tair is the temperature of the ambient air in °C, Tmrt is the mean radiant temperature, vair is the 
wind speed measured at 10 m above ground in m/s and vp is the vapor pressure in m/s or expressed as 
relative humidity in %.  
In the Fiala model, the human organism is separated into two interacting systems as stated by to 
BLAZEJCZYK ET AL. 2013. The first system is the controlling active system, while the second is the 
controlling passive system. In this connection the passive system is a multi-segmental, multi-layered 
representation of the human body and inhabits information about the physiology and anatomy of it. The 
model is based on a body weight of 73.5 kg, a fat content of 14% and a dubious area of 1.86 m². Ac-
cording to FIALA 1998 the body is idealized as spherical and cylindrical elements with twelve body 
parts. Each body element is further divided into a core and a shell. The active system of the UTCI-Fiala 
model is predicting the thermoregulatory reaction of the nervous system as stated in BLAZEJCZYK ET 
AL. 2013. Therefore, the model implements e.g., shivering and sweating as well as suppression and 
elevation of the cutaneous blood flow, allowing a precise prediction on human thermal response.  
Furthermore, the UTCI-Fiala model is based on reference conditions as presented in BLAZEJCZYK ET 
AL. 2013. In order to convert climate impact to a single value, the UTCI reference conditions must be 
defined in terms of confirming to most people’s experience and applicable to the whole spectrum of 
climate zones. The non-meteorological variables metabolic rate MET and the thermal properties of 
clothing are of significant importance. The metabolic rate for the UTCI is defined at 2.3 MET, which 
represents a person walking with 4 km/h. The reference climate is decided to be 0.5 m/s at 10 m height, 
a mean radiant temperature equal to the ambient temperature and a vapor pressure representing a relative 
humidity of 50%.

Climate Related Impact on Human Health 
According to PANTAVOU 2021 a prolonged exposure to hot environments can cause adverse health ef-
fects ranging from thermal discomfort to heat disorders and heat strokes. The exposure of humans to 
heat will increase globally due to climate change.  
In general, heat illness or heat related morbidity occurs when the body of a human is unable to maintain 
or regulate the internal temperature in a range within certain boundaries, as stated by PANTAVOU 2021. 
The body can regulate its temperature through sweating and vasodilation (heat loss) or shivering (heat 
gain). In both cases, heat loss and heat gain, the thermoregulation is controlled by the brain.  
According to PANTAVOU 2021 environmental conditions can lead to the failure of thermoregulatory 
processes and lead to a faster heat production, than heat loss of the body. This phenomenon may lead to 
dangerous physiological responses or pathways leading to organ failure. 
The UTCI is used in studies related to health effects of heat stress. PANTAVOU 2021 used it to quantify 
the demographic information attributed to heat related morbidity of pedestrians.

The aim of this thesis is to integrate thermal stress into urban planning. To reduce computational time

midity, barometric pressure and bulb globe temperature. Based on these measured parameters, other 
parameters are calculated, like the mean radiant temperature using equation 2.7 or the UTCI.

3.1.3 
Statistical Analysis 
This project is about a statistical approach to the calculation of the UTCI. The calculated UTCI that was 
obtained through the urban environmental monitoring will be compared to the UTCI calculated using 
the equation 2.9 for the estimation of the globe temperature. The equation is using the solar radiation as 
one of its input parameters. The direct solar radiation measured on DTU campus will be used. The other 
input parameters are taken from the measurement of the specific location. At first the globe temperature 
of the time and day of measurement are calculated and compared to the globe temperatures that were 
measured. For the calculation of the globe temperature, the solar radiation measured by the DTU climate 
station is used, while the measured data of the urban environmental measurement for air temperature 
and relative humidity are use. In a second step, the mean radiant temperatures are calculated using equa-
tion 2.7 for the model situation and the measured data. Again, these are compared to each other. The 
last step is the calculation of the UTCI for both model and measurement. The UTCI is calculated using 
a MATLAB code which was obtained through the code of UTCI 2022 Again, the results of these calcu-
lations are compared to each other. The difference of the UTCI calculated through measured data and 
the model will be evaluated regarding behavioral observation and analysis conducted in as part of the 
Master thesis by BATSIOU 2021. 
In order to rate the effectiveness of the globe temperature model, the energy deficit for the solar radiation 
between clear sky conditions and measured solar radiation will be compared to the difference between 
measured and calculated globe temperature. The energy difference between clear sky model, which is 
representing the solar radiation, which reaches the ground without cloud cover and the measured solar 
radiation determines the cloud cover of the respective measurement day. A statement about the cloudi-
ness in relation to the accuracy of globe temperature estimation shall be made.  In order to calculate the 
clear sky condition the Haurwitz model as described by RENO ET. AL. 2012 will be used. The model 
consists of the equation

5 Discussion and Conclusion 
5.1 
Discussion 
The UTCI is an index consisting of various meteorological parameters, some of which are measurable 
and easily accessible like the air temperature, relative humidity and wind speed whereas one, the mean 
radiant temperature requires simulation or measurement. The estimation of the mean radiant temperature 
proofes to be difficult. As an approach, the estimation of the globe temperature through equation 2.9 
and the calculation of the mean radiant temperature through equation 2.7 can be sufficient for the esti-
mation of the UTCI. The approach which was used in order to prove the usability of mean radiant tem-
perature estimation is described in depth in chapter 3, the results of this estimation presented in chapter 
3 and chapter 4.  
The acceptability of the UTCI estimation based on the globe temperature model approach should be 
divided based on the cloud cover of the measurement day as the analysis showed a high dependency of 
the globe temperature estimation accuracy on the cloud cover. Therefore, this discussion will be divided 
into three paragraphs, the first representing clear sky conditions, the second representing day with fluc-
tuating cloud cover and the last one representing cloud overcast. A correction of Figure 4-42 needs to 
be done based on these categories. 
UTCI estimation for days with clear sky conditions 
The measurement days representing the clear sky conditions are the 3rd of June 2018 and the 7th of June 
2018. As it can be seen in Figure 4-18 and Figure 4-32 the solar radiation measured at that day is close 
to the clear sky model and only has a small calculated energy deficit. The energy deficit for these meas-
urement days ranges between 7.41 W/m² to 51.64 W/m². A mean energy deficit of 25 w/m² can be 
calculated. The difference between measured and estimated globe temperature has a mean value of 3.6 
°C and ranges within values between 0.13 and 5.42 °C. Important to mention is that the calculated energy 
deficit for the smallest globe temperature difference is 34.4 W/m² and therefore above the average. The 
highest difference between measurement and estimation has an energy deficit of 14.26 W/m² and there-
fore is below the average value. A mean UTCI based on measured data of 40.43 °C was calculated, 
while the UTCI based on the estimated globe temperature has a mean value of 31.9 °C, which results in 
a difference of 8.53 K.  
UTCI estimation for days with fluctuating cloud cover 
The estimation of the UTCI for days with a fluctuating cloud cover proofs to be more difficult than with 
clear sky conditions as it can be seen in Figure 3-10 and Figure 3-11. The measurement days represent-
ing fluctuating cloud cover are 13.04.2018, 23.04.2018, 29.04.2018, 27.05.2018, 14.06.2018 and the 
17.06.2018. These days were selected based on Figure 4-5, Figure 6-19, Figure 6-30, Figure 6-44, Figure

solar radiation. The mean deviation between measured and calculated globe temperature is 7.02 °C and

5.1.1 
Limitations of this work 
This thesis combines on-site measurements with meteorological measurements conducted at the DTU 
campus in Lyngby. The measurements were conducted in 2018 and consist of 9 measurements taken in 
various urban places in Copenhagen with varying cloud and climate conditions, starting at the end of 
winter and finishing at the beginning of summer (31.03.2018 – 17.06.2018). The measured solar radia-
tion on DTU campus might vary from the solar radiation during the measurement. The best example to 
show this is the measurement conducted on the 13.04.2018 in Nyhavn, where it can be observed that the 
sun is still shining during the measurement and the sky is clear (see Figure 4-7). But when looking at 
the measured solar radiation of that day and the time of the measurement in Figure 4-2 it can be seen 
that the solar radiation measured during the time of the measurement in Nyhavn is 0 W/m².  
When calculating the clear sky conditions after equation 3.1, the solar radiation curve needed to be 
moved in every case to fit with the measured data. Also, the simplicity of the used model is leading to 
some inaccuracy, as the measured data, especially during days with fluctuating cloud cover is exceeding 
the solar radiation calculated for the clear sky conditions. The results of the energy deficit calculations 
may be inaccurate due to the mentioned reasons. This applies especially for measurement days with 
fluctuating cloud cover. 
As the approach to calculate the globe temperature is based on a model, the calculated values might be 
inaccurate, as the model is based on measured data and as it can be seen in HAJIZADEH ET. AL. 2016 is 
correlating weakly.  
It is arguable to which extent humans are responding towards changes in UTCI values, as stated in 
BATSIOU 2021. The relation between occupation of public spaces and UTCI values is relatively weak, 
especially for heat stress.  
The observed sky conditions varied from the measured values for some of the measurements. A docu-
mentation of the sky conditions throughout the whole measurements was missing, hence a statement 
about the sky conditions during the full extent of the measurements could not be made.

Furthermore, the measurements used in this thesis were all conducted in four specific locations in Co-
penhagen in one year. This means, that these results only apply to the climate which is typical for Co-

5.2 
Conclusion and future work 
5.2.1 
Conclusion 
Urban planning guidelines lack the introduction of thermal stress. As it can be seen in Figure 2-5 at-
tempts have been made to integrate like the framework of wind comfort into urban planning. The exe-
cution however requires intense simulation for a whole year. In addition, this guideline only provides a 
solution for the city of London and is not universally transferable.  
The statistical analysis and the model used to estimate the globe temperature could be a tool for estimat-
ing the UTCI in a fast and accurate way. The accuracy of the estimation is however highly dependent 
on the cloud cover of the respective days. The model needs further improvement in order to prove itself 
as a tool for the UCTI estimation. 
In addition, there are huge uncertainties regarding this thesis, the used model for clear sky solar radiation 
estimation is a very simple model but sufficient for first estimation and improvement of the presented 
globe temperature estimation model.  
All in all, it can be said, that a statistical approach to the UTCI estimation, besides its limitations, can 
be a useful tool for urban planning guidelines and the integration of thermal stress into those. Further 
work on the estimation of the UTCI needs to be done as the following chapter will describe.  
5.2.2 
Future Work 
For future work, the relation between could cover and accuracy of globe temperature estimation and 
therefore UTCI estimation should be further investigated. The study should be expanded by more on-
site measurements, preferably covering a whole year with all the three cloud conditions for high and 
low climates. During the measurements, the sky conditions should be documented extensively. More 
measurement’s locations should be chosen that respect more urban settings than the four already repre-
sented settings. And extension of measurements to different climate zones would be interesting. 
The used model needs an adjustment to more accurately estimate the UTCI. The adjustment must be 
fitted to the cloud conditions and the season and climate of the location. Further investigations towards 
the effectiveness of the model must be made. Again, an investigation of the effectiveness on other cli-
mate zones would be interesting. A categorization of different urban settings and different climates 
would make it possible to deliver a framework for UTCI calculation that does not rely on measurement
