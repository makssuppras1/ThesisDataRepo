Abstract
Strong, high-frequency external excitation of a strongly nonlinear system can
have the eect of stiening the system, causing an increase in linear natural
frequency. This present work presents theoretical predictions and provides ex-
perimental testing of this eect known as external stiening. A 1-DOF system
is considered and the amplitude-frequency relation is approximated using a re-
cently proposed extended harmonic balance method. The method shows good
agreement with numerical simulations, but does not oer much insight, as simple
analytical expressions are not provided.
The amplitude-frequency relation is experimentally obtained, to which the
derived model is tted, resulting in an estimate of frequency shift due to the
high-frequency excitation. The frequency shift is also measured directly using
a dierent method. The estimated and measured frequency shift show to agree
quite well. Both approaches indicate that the frequency shift is quadratically
dependent on the level of high-frequency forcing, agreeing with analytical predic-
tions.
There is to the author's knowledge yet no published work, where the theoreti-
cal predictions of external stiening are experimentally and quantitatively tested,
which they are in this present work.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This thesis concerns analytical predictions and experimental testing of the ex-
ternal stiening eect (as opposed to the parametric stiening eect) of strong
high-frequency excitation (HFE) on a strongly nonlinear system. This includes
analysis of a generic strongly nonlinear 1-DOF system with external low-frequency
(LF) excitation superposed with strong external high-frequency (HF) excitation,
and laboratory experiments using a simple physical realisation of the system.
There is to the author's knowledge no published work which provides accurate
experimental results which quantitatively test analytical predictions of the exter-
nal stiening eect.
HFE can change a range of eective (or low frequency / average) system prop-
erties [1], but this work only addresses the external stiening eect. Stiening is
the eect of HFE changing the eective stiness, thereby actively changing im-
portant eective system properties such as resonance frequencies, buckling load,
stability and existence of equilibrium states - features which are important in
the design and troubleshooting in many engineering contexts, and also to the
understanding of observations of systems where HFE is present. Dierentiating
between external and parametric stiening is the distinction between stiening
caused by external and parametric forcing, respectively. In addition to strong
HFE, external stiening requires a strong nonlinearity, in contrary to parametric
stiening [2]. The forcing type of choice in this study is a simple time-harmonic
signal for both the LF and HF excitation, because it makes up a simple exam-
ple to study - this does not imply that external stiening is excitable by simple
harmonic signals only. These simple harmonic signals are however non-trivial to
realise in the laboratory due to the strong nonlinearity, which causes harmonic
distortion and thus higher harmonics to occur, if precautions are not taken.
Early studies of the eects of HFE deals with the change in equilibrium state of
a pendulum with fast vertically vibrating support by Stephenson [3] and Kapitza
[4].
Many theoretical studies of HFE eects have been carried out, e.g.
by
Tcherniak and Thomsen [5, 6], but especially by Blekhman, who outlines many
of the dierent eects of HFE and treats several specic examples thoroughly
[1, 7] using his Method of Direct Separation of Motions (MDSM) [8], which is a
central tool to derive the "slow" (or average/eective) dynamics when dealing
with systems with HFE.
1
==PAGE:10==
A study of the general eects of HFE for a considerably general class of me-
chanical systems by Thomsen considers three general eects. Among these is
stiening, for which well-dened formulae for the HFE eects are provided [2].
In a more recent theoretical study of HFE eects by Kremer [9], he uses the
term eect to dene a possible combination of variables which cause a change
in the eective properties of a system, i.e. eects in the Kremer-sense can be
understood as a class of excitation types. Kremer presents ve eects. Examples
of those are excitation dependent on position only and excitation dependent on
velocity and slow time. Thus, eects in the Kremer-sense concerns the causes
of the HFE induced vibrational phenomena, in contrary to the aforementioned
study by Thomsen, where eects are considered to be the observable eective
consequences of HFE, i.e. change in stiness or change in stability. Eects are
in this work used in the Thomsen-sense.
Experimental studies of the stiening eect includes an investigation of a
damped Mathieu system [10], a continuous elastic beam (realised using a piano
string) [11], an examination of stabilization of a buckled beam [12] and a study of
a monostable Dung oscillator [13], all of which reports to experimentally vali-
date the predicted stiening eect  however all these studies concern parametric
stiening.
There seems to be no published experimental observations to support the
theoretical predictions of the external stiening eect, as opposed to parametric
stiening. Thomsen suggests an experimental setup to observe external stien-
ing in the laboratory [14]. The suggested setup is used in in the MSc. thesis
work of Olesen [15], which the experimental results presented in the unpublished
manuscript by Thomsen et.
al [16] are based on.
Three dierent sources of
excitation are tested in Olesen's work: An electromagnetic shaker, a rotating un-
balance and a pneumatic exciter. With no active control, the shaker is unable
to deliver a simple harmonic forcing due to the nonlinearity as discussed. The
rotating unbalance can deliver a sucient level of excitation, but it delivers both
external and parametric forcing, which disqualies it from use in this context.
Likewise, the pneumatic exciter can deliver an adequate level of HFE, but the
setup lacks means to properly measure and control the force input, thus rendering
quantiable comparison between theory and experiment impossible.
Since the experimental work of Olesen was carried out, a shaker controller
with force feedback has been acquired for the experimental setup, with the intent
of enabling a simple harmonic, external forcing input.
The present work consists of both experimental and analytical work. The
experimental investigation of the external stiening eect consists of measuring
the amplitude-frequency relation in proximity of the rst resonance frequency
of the device under test (DUT) both with and without the presence external
HFE, looking for a shift in frequency of the response due to stiening.
The
backbone [17], which describes the amplitude of free, undamped oscillations near
the rst resonance frequency of the DUT as a function of the frequency of these
oscillations, is obtained via model parameter estimation with and without the
presence of strong HF excitation  subsequently looking for a shift in frequency
as a result of stiening. The analytical work consists of deriving the equation of

motion (EOM) of the DUT, justifying how strong nonlinearity can be realised in
the laboratory, deriving the LF eects of external HFE using MDSM and solving
the strongly nonlinear eective EOM by using an extended harmonic balance
method which has recently been proposed by Wu et al. [18].
Predictions of the eects of external HFE on strongly nonlinear systems are
derived and shown to agree with the literature. Experimental tests are carried
out, measuring the amplitude-frequency relation in the proximity of the lowest
resonance frequency of the DUT along with measurements of the linear natural
frequency. A clear frequency shift as a consequence of HFE is observed. A fre-
quency shift quadratic with the HFE magnitude is predicted, for small frequency
shifts, which the experimental results are shown to support.

predictions of HFE eects and an approximate solution to the averaged system.
Chapter 3 describes the experimental setup and the experimental procedures used

discusses these, and Chapter 5 concludes on the ndings.

Chapter 2
Mathematical Modelling and

The physical model describing the test setup is presented in the rst section of
this chapter. In the second section, the equation of motion (EOM) is derived
from the physical model using Newton's 2. law. The EOM containing HFE is
in third section transformed, such that only the eect of HFE remains, i.e. the
EOM governing the low-frequency dynamics is derived. The remaining analyses
presented in this thesis is based on the LF EOM. Section four presents an ex-
pression for the backbone of the system, which eciently describes basic dynamic
properties of a nonlinear system. In section ve, an approximate solution to the
LF EOM is derived and presented along with assessment of stability of solutions
and comparison between approximate analytical results and numerical results. In
section six, the impact of higher harmonic contributions in the input force are
assessed from numerical simulations.

The relationship between the frequency and amplitude for the free and un-
damped oscillations is of great importance, because it provides fundamental in-
sight in how the resonance frequency changes with oscillation amplitude, fre-

serves as the backbone of the corresponding forced response, i.e. the frequency re-

Eq. (2.21) is known as the homogeneous and undamped Dung equation,

Consequently, there are several
solution methods and methods to obtain the nonlinear frequency response of the
system. This includes methods like Method of Multiple Scales [19], Averaging [20],
the Lindstedt-Poincaré Method [19], to name a few. However, these methods only
apply to the weakly nonlinear case, rendering them inapplicable for Eq. (2.21), in
which the nonlinearity can be arbitrarily strong. Other approaches must therefore
be considered.
A method proposed by Burton & Hamdan [21] uses a time transformation
to obtain an approximation of the oscillation frequency expressed as an innite
series, i.e. the frequency may be determined to any desired degree of accuracy.
This method applies to all nonlinear restoration forces, so it can also be applied to
cases with e.g. quadratic or quintic nonlinearities. The general applicability along
with the option to get highly accurate approximations are the main advantages
of this method.

by He [22], in which the coecient of the deection term is expanded in a power
series along with the assumed solution to the equation. These additional variables
gained from the expansion makes it possible to cancel out secular terms by proper
selection of constant-values. The approximation accuracy depends on the order

3.1.1
Forcing
The test setup is an adaptation of a setup, which was developed in relation to
the work of Thomsen [14]. Prior to this present work, the test setup was used
by Olesen [15] for the same purpose as this present work. Olesen's work pointed
out two challenges in realising the external stiening eect experimentally: The
required level of HFE is high and the force input is in the original setting heav-
ily distorted, i.e. higher harmonics are large compared to the primary driving
frequency due to the strong nonlinearity. The method of which the forcing is
applied to the DUT must therefore address those challenges.
The most common way to excite systems, when a steady-state response is
of interest, is to use electromagnetic shakers. The excitation provided by the
shaker, is transferred to the DUT via e.g. a stinger or a wire. Ideally, the wire
or stinger will transmit the longitudinal force from the shaker to the DUT, while
decoupling the dynamics of the two.
Wires provide good dynamic isolation,
however, they require pretension in order to give a harmonic input, which is not
feasible for this test setup. A stinger is a long rod, typically made from metal
or plastic and provides, as opposed to a wire, compressive and bending stiness,
which means it does not require pretension. Stingers make a stronger coupling
between shaker and the DUT, i.e. the dynamic of the shaker can inuence the
dynamic of the DUT. Consequentially, decay experiments cannot be conducted
with the shaker mounted through a stinger, because one would also observe the
signicant damping introduced by the shaker itself. It is important to operate
away from resonance frequencies of the stinger, as failing to do so would obviously
contaminate the results.
An alternative method to apply the forcing directly to the DUT, is to transfer
it through the base of the DUT, i.e. base-excitation. An advantage of base-
excitation is that stiness and damping induced by the shaker does not directly
aect the dynamics of the DUT, which could enable direct measurement of the
backbone. Base-excitation might therefore be a good option, but it would require
the development of a whole new test setup, which is not within the scope of this
project.
An alternative shaker-DUT connection is magnets. Strong neodymium mag-
nets in repulsive conguration acts as a nonlinear spring. Even though there is
no direct contact between the DUT and the shaker, the two are still non-weakly
coupled, meaning that the shaker dynamics would probably not be negligible.
Thus, the magnet coupling introduces a nonlinearity and might not oer any
advantage over the stinger.
A method to suppress the higher harmonics in the input force is to implement
a force-control loop around the shaker. That is, to use a controller, which takes
the force transducer signal as input, to actively adjust the action of the shaker
to accomplish a simple harmonic force input. A considerable drawback of force-
control is that is does not operate well close to resonance, which is discussed
further in Sect. 3.2.
Besides using a shaker, available forcing methods include pneumatic exciters
and rotating unbalances. Both are tested in Olesen's work and the following is a
summary of his ndings: The rotating balance is not suitable since the unbalance

Chapter 5
Conclusions
Analytical predictions of the external stiening eect were presented and tested
in laboratory experiments. A mathematical model of the test setup with 1-DOF
was derived from Newton's 2nd law. From that, a model describing the low-
frequency (LF) dynamics of the system, i.e. the averaged system, was extracted
using the Method of Direct Separation of Motion. The obtained model for the
averaged system is a Dung equation with an arbitrarily weak or strong, cubic
nonlinearity. The model predicted the frequency shift being quadratic with the
amplitude of the high-frequency excitation (HFE) for small frequency shifts.
The amplitude-frequency relation of the averaged system was analytically ap-
proximated using a recently proposed extended harmonic balance (eHB) method.
The eHB method showed very good agreement with numerical simulations, pro-
vided the fundamental assumption, that the dominating frequency of the solution
being the same frequency as the driving frequency. However, the eHB method
does not provide much insight, as the method does not provide any simple analyt-
ical expressions for the amplitude-frequency relation. It should therefore be used
as a more computationally ecient alternative to ordinary numerical simulations
in cases where high accuracy is required.
Amplitude-frequency relations was measured in the laboratory for varying lev-
els of HFE, showing a clear frequency shift increasing with the level of HFE, in
a qualitative sense. The averaged system model was tted to the experimental
results, providing quantitative model parameter estimates, including the shift in
linear natural frequency. The frequency shift was shown to be well described
by a quadratic relationship to the HFE amplitude, agreeing with the theoretical
prediction for small frequency shifts. The estimated model parameters showed
an increasing relative nonlinearity with increasing HFE. This was not expected,
as the stiening eect was predicted to be accompanied by a straightening ef-
fect, making the system relatively less nonlinear with increasing HFE. A possible
explanation is, that the HFE-frequency used during laboratory tests were not
enough, thus violating a condition for the averaged system model to be valid.
The frequency shift was also measured directly using acceleration-feedback
control for the same levels of HFE. The test series suggested, similarly to the
amplitude-frequency relation tests, that the frequency shift depends quadratically
on the HFE amplitude.
45
==PAGE:54==
A weakness in the method used for obtaining the backbone curves, is that
they are obtained from parameter estimations, which are extracted from the
amplitude-frequency tests, i.e. the backbones are not measured directly. Like-
wise, it was not possible to obtain experimental amplitude-frequency data from
the overhang part of the amplitude-frequency curve. The amplitude-frequency
relation tests were conducted using force-feedback control, which combined with
the force drop-o phenomenon caused the shaker controller to become unstable
when approaching the resonance frequency, as the feedback-signal would vanish.
This weakens the credibility of especially the estimated damping and nonlinearity
parameters, as the part of the curve missing, is where those parameters have most
inuence, i.e. the estimated parameters cannot be expected to be robust. On the
other hand, the tted models appear overall to t the experimental data well,
and the estimated natural frequency and forcing parameters are not particularly
aected by the missing experimental data.
Two sets of frequency shifts for similar levels of HFE was presented. One set
was estimated via model parameter estimation, based on experimentally obtained
amplitude-frequency relations, the other set was measured directly by exploiting
the force drop-o phenomenon. Comparing the two sets of frequency shift esti-
mates, they are seen to agree quite well. The fact that they are obtained with
two largely independent methods while showing quite good agreement, is con-
sidered to strengthen the validity of two predictions presented: The HFE has a
stiening eect on the system considered and it increases quadratically with the
HFE amplitude for small frequency shifts.
The Dung equation is a widely used model, which is applied to dynamic
systems across many dierent technical elds and applications. In this present
work, the external stiening eect, in relation to a mechanical system modelled
by a Dung equation with a hardening nonlinearity, has been considered. In
order to fundamentally understand external stiening better, it would be rele-
vant to experimentally test a system with a softening nonlinearity, which could
be systems modelled as e.g. a pendulum or a transversely loaded, hinged-hinged
beam with one of the supports being allowed to move horizontally. Experimen-
tally investigating external stiening for multi-DOF systems, would drastically
increase the class of systems for which the eect has been tested.
The external stiening eect provides the potential to change the eective
stiness of a system by ipping a switch, which is arguably a powerful option in
many technical applications. A relatively large amount of energy is required for
the eect to be signicant. Thus, it is not feasible to use the eect on large-scale
or medium-scale structures, as the required power usage would be very large.
However, that limitation is probably not a concern on micro-scale, as inertia
forces there are considered small. This implies, that external stiening could be
exploited in MEMS (Micro Electro-Mechanical Systems) applications, including
micro sensors and micro actuators, which are highly used, e.g. in smart phones,
cars and various consumer electronics. It is therefore recommended to explore
the possibility of external stiening improving current MEMS devices or even
facilitating a new generation of MEMS devices.
