Abstract
A portable NMR spectrometer was developed which can account measure-
ments both for 1H and 13C thermal and hyper-polarized samples. A double
tuned resonance circuit was initially designed for that purpose, which was
showed its limitations compared to the single tuned circuit. Two single tuned
resonance circuits were also designed, one tuned at 1H and one tuned at 13C
resonance frequencies. Each circuit can be selected with the aid of a me-
chanical switch. An isolation box and a temperature control system were
additionally incorporated to the system, so that the magnets temperature
remains stable. Furthermore, ltering was applied to the system, so that
electromagnetic interference is suppressed, and hence, DC noise is removed.
Moreover, the sample tube was congured to ensure that a well dened sam-
ple volume can be inserted to the sample tube in a relatively short time.
Software congurations were held as well, for systematic noise removal and
dynamic range increase. Withal, modications to the coil design were held,
in order to increase system sensitivity.
Over and above, various thermal
sample measurements were performed, to qualify system performance as well
as to ensure proper parameter setting during data acquisition. Eventually,
the developed bench top NMR spectrometer was tested against 1H and 13C
hyper-polarized samples, indicating its capability to serve as hyper-polarized
sample control system.

Preface
A bench-top NMR spectrometer was developed, meant to serve as a control
system for hyper-polarised 1H and 13C samples.
The DTU Hyperpolarized Magnetic Resonance group, is working on de-
veloping new methods in hyperpolarization. Water (1H) and carbon (13C)
samples are typically investigated by the group, where the enhancement
factor  i.e. the ratio between the thermal and the hyperpolarized signal
strength  of each method shall be determined. Hence, the developed bench-
top NMR spectrometer can be employed for the enhancement factor deter-
mination.
Some aspects that were considered throughout the process of this project
include system reliability and sensitivity, as well as system rapidness. Ob-
viously, the developed system shall be able to yield accurate results in any
circumstances, on the grounds that hyperpolarized sample preparation is
generally costly, and therefore it is not preferable to be repeated. In addi-
tion, due to the relatively rapid hyperpolarized sample signal attenuation,
the bench-top NMR spectrometer shall be capable to provide a way of mini-
mizing the time between the hyperpolarized sample extraction and injection
to the system.

INTRODUCTION
Initially, the basic physical phenomena that are involved in an NMR exper-
iment are introduced. These include nuclear magnetization, magnetic res-
onance, excitation, relaxation, and precession. The NMR instrumentation
is briey described as well. The content of the rst chapter was written by
deriving information from [1], [2], [3].
Thereafter, the developed bench-top NMR system is introduced. In par-
ticular, the components that are part of the bench-top NMR system are
described, namely the magnet, the probe, and the spectrometer.
Furthermore, the whole system is characterized. Specically, the switch
insertion loss, the response of the system to RF signals, the noise level of
system components, as well as the ampliers noise gure are determined.
Characterization also includes measurements for input signal saturation spec-
ication and signal reproducibility determination.
Moreover, system congurations that took part through the system de-
velopment are explained in detail. Namely, electromagnetic interference sup-
pression for noise removal, sample tube conguration for rapid and accu-
rate sample injection, magnet thermal insulation and temperature control for
magnet temperature stabilization, probe congurations for dierent sample
measurements and SNR increase, coil congurations for sensitivity improve-
ment, and software modications for systematic noise removal and system
dynamic range increase.
Withal, the developed NMR spectrometer is tested against thermal and
hyperpolarized 1H and 13C samples. Measurements are also held to determine
the maximum system sensitivity as a function of sample position and volume
inside the magnet. Additionally, experiments are performed to determine
the 90° ip angle, as well as the signal to noise ratio as a function of scan
averages.
Eventually, some aspects concerning spectrometer development, that in
practice didn't considerably encountered, are mentioned. In addition, po-
tential congurations that might lead to further system improvement, are
discussed. Moreover, diculties that were came along hyperpolarized sam-
ple measurements are argued, and potential solutions to these diculties are
given.

1. THE NMR EXPERIMENT
What gives rise to the NMR experiment, is the intrinsic property that some
atoms seem to exhibit, called spin.
The spin of an atom indicates both
precession and direction. Microscopically, each atom in a sample precess at
a specic frequency, say Larmor frequency, and points at an arbitrary
direction. The Larmor frequency will depend on the gyromagnetic ratio,
say γ, of each atom, but also on any magnetic eld strength, that might
happen to be suciently close to it.
In the absence of a magnetic eld, atoms in a sample will still precess, but
they will also point in arbitrary directions through the sample space, resulting
in an almost zero total magnetization, say net or nuclear magnetization
(gure 1.1 (a)). When a magnetic eld is applied, their precession frequency
will be given as:
V = γ · B
(1.1)
, where γ, is the atom gyromagnetic ratio, and B is the applied magnetic
eld, i.e. static magnetic eld. Additionally, the atoms will tend to align
to the applied magnetic eld.
Microscopically, the alignment is insigni-
cant. Macroscopically though, the nuclear magnetization becomes signi-
cant, yielding a total magnetization (1.1 (b)) that is given by [2] as,
M0 = Nγ2ℏ2I(I + 1)B0/3kBTs
(1.2)
, where N is the number of spins at resonance per unit volume, ℏis the
Planck's constant, I is the spin quantum number, kB is the Boltzmann's
constant, and Ts is the sample temperature in Kelvin.

magnetic eld to a portion of nuclei, and thereby creating a net magnetiza-
tion described by 1.2, the magnetization can be ipped away from its axis

clear magnetic resonance experiment, and it's an important aspect of the

In addition, it was found necessary to insulate the magnet  so that it expe-

necessity of this modication can be explained by the magnet's magnetic
eld strength change, when it encounters small temperature variations. The
latter directly aects the nucleus precession frequency, which in turn alters
the NMR spectrum resonance peak.
To illustrate, the 1H precession frequency, for the current system set up,
was measured to be 12.44 MHz at ambient temperature (approximately 22
°C). In addition, the 1H gyromagnetic ratio is 42.576 MHz/T, which indi-
==PAGE:40==

cates that the magnet's magnetic eld strength is about 0.292 T, at the point
where the sample was placed. Furthermore, one degree temperature rise, cor-
responds to 0.0035% magnetic eld loss. Thus, the magnetic eld strength

rise, corresponding to ∼4.37 kHz frequency shift, for the 1H center frequency.
Similarly, considering that 13C gyromagnetic ratio is 10.705 MHz/T, its cen-
ter frequency will be shifted by 1.09 kHz, when the temperature changes by
one degree. The frequency shifts might appear relatively small, when they
are compared with NMR spectrometers equipped with strong magnets. Nev-
ertheless, they become more important when the signal evolution over time
is of particular interest, as in the case of hyper-polarized samples.

5.4.1
Hyperpolarized 1H sample measurement procedure
To illustrate the procedure of 1H hyperpolarized sample measurements using
the benchtop NMR, the hyperpolarized sample tube is initially degassed, by
employing a vacuum pump  degassing is employed to remove oxygen from
the NMR tube, which is regarded as a paramagnetic element, and hence it
shortens T1  . Afterwards, the hyperpolarized sample is examined by em-
ploying a relatively short amplication factor  0.1 is recommended to ensure
that the signal remains unsaturated on the grounds that the enhancement
factor is unknown before the experiment , while keeping the excitation pulse
time relatively short, i.e. 2-3 µs, so that signal saturation is avoided. There-
after, the experiment is repeated for dierent time instances, typically after
1 sec, up until the hyper-magnetization is completely decayed. Then, the
thermal sample is measured, by employing a relatively long RF pulse while
keeping the amplication factor to the maximum, i.e. 1. In order to compare
the sample before and after its decay, and hence compute its enhancement
factor, two corrections take place, one for using dierent amplication factor,
and one for employing dierent pulse time. When it comes to the ampli-
cation factor, table 4.1 is used as a reference for the correction, whilst for
dierent pulse time duration, gure 5.4 can be used. Further, the enhance-
ment factor and T1 is estimated using a 9.4 T NMR spectrometer as well, so
that bench-top NMR performance is determined  it should be noted that
the hyperpolarized sample is initially injected to the 9.4 T NMR spectrome-
ter and thereafter to the bench-top NMR, implying that it might be a time
delay of 3-4 seconds between the two sample injections .

6. DISCUSSION
Initially, it should be stated that not much emphasis was given on develop-
ing a system with a user friendly interface. Running experiments through
the command window might not be convenient for users unfamiliar with the
command prompt. Additionally, the data processing was held in Matlab®,
indicating that users unfamiliar with Matlab® might not be able to process
the data properly. However, the data provided by the bench-top NMR spec-
trometer can be manipulated by various NMR signal processing software.
In addition, its worth mentioning that there might be still some room of
improvement for the bench-top NMR spectrometer. One possible modica-
tion would be to isolate the ampliers from the power supply as they are
placed in the same box (i.e. see gure 2.6). Ferrite lters placement showed
that a signicant amount of noise was removed and EMI was suppressed.
Nevertheless, some of the wires might be close to the ampliers, and hence,
introducing some noise. With proper magnet isolation, noise level might be
further decreased. In addition, one might had chosen to stabilize the tem-
perature to a lower level, rather than setting it to 30 °C. That would result
in a stronger magnet magnetic eld strength and a higher coil quality factor.
That is, the system SNR would be, to some extent, increased. However, a
most sophisticated design should be considered in that case, since moisture
might be introduced to the magnet and the probe. Further, a stronger mag-
netic could be incorporated into the system, i.e. changing the magnet. On
one hand, a stronger magnet would increase the sample equilibrium magne-
tization, and therefore, the SNR [2]. On the other hand, as it was showed
in section 3.2, the noise level is commonly higher at lower frequencies. A
higher magnetic eld strength would increase both 13C and 1H precession
frequency, and as result, their spectrum might be less susceptible to noise
(probably only 13C spectrum). The coil quality factor might be increased at
higher frequencies as well, since a higher magnetic eld indicates that both
1H and 13C will precess at higher frequencies. Moreover, as it was discussed
in section 3.7, further amplication than necessary is generally undesired. A
lower total amplication would possibly increase the system dynamic range,
while forming a more compact amplication section overall. Withal, the coil
quality factor might be further increased. More detailed analysis concerning

skin and proximity eect shall be held so that an increase in the coil quality
factor is achieved. Last but not least, a pre-amplier can be incorporated to
the system so that transmitting signal is enhanced, and therefore 90° pulse

nal shall be taken into account, to avoid signal saturation and switch damage

Eventually, some practical considerations concerning hyperpolarized sam-

measurements. However, when the results are obtained involving the bench-
top NMR spectrometer are compared to that are obtained with the 9.4 T
NMR spectrometer, a divergence between the two instrument results was
observed. The divergence was observed between the two instruments can
be probably attributed to the bench-top NMR location  basically not the
location itself, but the path that the sample follows, in order to reach the
bench-top NMR spectrometer, which is obviously changing, as the magnet
location changes  . As it is suggested on the experiment described in sec-
tion 5.4.3, there is a dierence between the two instruments results whether
a hyperpolarized sample or a sample polarized at 9.4 T is employed. This
implies that the dierence between the two instruments results is not due to
the hyperpolarized sample nature. In addition, when the bench-top NMR
spectrometer position was changed (i.e.
when it was placed on the oor
of the lab), the dierence between the two instruments results, was further
increased, which is an indication that the measured enhancement factor is
inuenced by the sample path. Further, when the portable spectrometer was
relocated again, and placed closer to the 9.4 T magnet, the measured T1
between the two instruments almost coincide (it is reasonable to expect a
shorter T1 measurement when a weaker magnetic eld is employed), which
suggests that the hyperpolarization is inuenced by the sample path as well.
In order to determine whether the path that the sample follows inuences
its polarization, two experiments can be held. One would be to measure
the magnetic eld distribution through the laboratory space, since strong
static magnetic elds might exist in lab space and thereby inuence samples
polarization. Another trivial solution would be to repeat experiments while
changing magnet location. Obviously, a sample with a relatively long T1
polarized at 9.4 T could be used for the measurements, on the grounds that
both its inexpensive, compared to the hyperpolarized sample, and the same
sample can be used while changing magnet location.

7. CONCLUSION
In conclusion, it was shown how various system congurations can lead to a
more robust NMR spectrometer overall, as well as how in some cases, modi-
cations might lead to system stagnation or even degradation. In particular,
ferrite lters incorporation to the amplication box, removed noisy peaks
that they were appearing in the frequency domain especially at low frequen-
cies, and hence they were distorting the NMR spectrum. The sample tube
modication, was also aided in a more well dened sample volume injection
in a relatively short time. In addition, thermal insulation and temperature
monitoring, enabled the acquisition of long-term experiments, without the
inuence of temperature changes. Nevertheless, the system sensitivity was
reduced, due to the temperature rise, on the grounds that on one hand mag-
netic eld strength is inversely proportional to temperature, on the other
hand coil resistance is increases with temperature, and therefore coil quality
factor is reduced. Since the signal to noise ratio is directly proportional to
the magnetic eld strength and to the square root of coil quality factor [2],
then an SNR degradation is noticed. The reduction is not so signicant (i.e.
around 2-3 %), and that's why it was decided to proceed with magnet tem-
perature rise. Furthermore, nal probe set-up increased system sensitivity
by approximately a factor of 2.5. It should be noted though, that if the
mechanical switch had not been incorporated to the system, and the dou-
ble tuned resonance circuit had been employed instead, then the increase in
the SNR would be around 30% less for the 1H resonance peak. Moreover,
software modications, on one hand removed systematic noise, while on the
other hand, increased system dynamic range. Eventually, coil modication
showed that a better Q can be achieved. Nevertheless, a more detailed anal-
ysis on coil design shall be held, in order to obtain a coil that can practically
be incorporated to the system.
