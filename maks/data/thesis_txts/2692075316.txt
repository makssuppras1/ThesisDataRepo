1
Abstract
The movement of particles along a temperature gradient, known as thermophoresis, is an attractive
phenomenon to exploit for manipulation of nanoparticles. Despite decades of research, theoretical
understanding of thermophoresis remains incomplete. Nonetheless, recent studies show that the
method has potential in an array of novel applications where other optical methods fail. The goal
of this thesis is to investigate the possibility of using thermophoresis to improve drug delivery
in the human eye, which is particularly challenging. To achieve this goal, nanoparticles must be
moved on the centimeter scale. For this purpose, an optical setup for optothermal manipulation is
designed and tested. Using a near-IR laser to generate temperature changes in solution, a ﬂexible
optical setup with a variety of application methods for diﬀerent types of particles and systems is
built. The average temperature increases generated in a sample is measured and documented using
the ﬂuorescent compound Rubpy3+, yielding results in line with expectations from comparable
literature.
The competing theoretical approaches to describing thermophoresis are introduced
and comparisons between them are drawn. The optical setup is successfully used to manipulate
ﬂuorescent polystyrene microbeads of down to 600 nm, and quantiﬁable concentration changes
are observed for particles of 1 and 1.59 µm in diameter.
Whilst limited, the results indicate
compliance with some theoretical models and other similar research, supporting the legitimacy
of the measurements. Despite centimeter-scale manipulation not being demonstrated, the initial
steps towards this goal have been taken in this study. The results in this thesis do not rule out the
possibility, and suggestions for further research to reach a conclusive answer are made.

Introduction
In recent years, optothermal manipulation has gained traction as a group of methods for manipu-
lating particles on the nano-scale [1] [2] [3]. One of the phenomena that can be exploited for this
purpose is thermophoresis: the movement of particles along a thermal gradient. This method has
unique advantages, with good prospects for manipulation of biological systems [4] [5]. In this the-
sis, the ﬁrst steps towards developing a method to use thermophoresis for eye treatment are taken.
The end goal is to use a thermal gradient generated by a laser to move medicine to target areas in
the human eye. Throughout this thesis, the goal is to build an optical setup and demonstrate that
particles on the nano-scale can be moved using temperature gradients that are safe to apply in the
human eye. The optical setup used is described in detail, including calculations and measurements
to quantify optical parameters. It will also include the theoretical background for thermophoresis,
described by the equation:

The Structure of this Thesis
First, the theoretical background is covered in section 3.
This chapter includes considerations
for application in the human eye, an introduction to thermophoresis, a handful of theoretical

After this, the optical setup employed including key components and justiﬁcation for their choices

The chapter following in section 5 addresses the bleaching eﬀect of ﬂuorescent particles used in this

Following the theoretical approach, temperature

goal application.
In the last chapter, section 9, results from the diﬀerent measurements are analysed and compared.
Outlook and potential followup studies are discussed and suggested. Finally, a conclusion on the
work and results is drawn.

Theoretical Background
3.1
Biological Application Considerations
There is a multitude of considerations for medical use in treatment of the human eye. The ﬁrst is
how high of a temperature can be used. Higher temperature gradients cause a higher concentration
gradient and a higher force which means higher migration velocity. However, an arbitrary amount
of heat must not be applied to real systems, since protein denaturation, lesions, and other damages
could happen to the eye. It has been noted that instantaneous temperature increases of up to
34.8 ◦C should be safe in the human eye [6]. The critical temperature for hyperthermia seems
to be around 42-43 ◦C [7]. However, the steady-state temperature of the cornea is around 30
◦C [8] and therefore signiﬁcantly lower than average body temperature. Therefore it is hard to
determine what elevated temperature is permissible over longer periods of time, but a couple
of degrees Celsius should be acceptable. It has been suggested by Lin et al. [1] that a pulsed
femtosecond laser could pose an attractive way of using thermophoresis to move particles. From
the considerations described previously, it can be seen that applying larger thermal gradients is an
option as long as the exposure time is short, so for moving particles over large distances, a short
exposure time may be suﬃcient to move particles step by step. It has recently been demonstrated
that pulsed a femtosecond laser at 808 nm operating with 15 kW/cm2 can be used to trap and
release particles [5]. Another important consideration is what light is absorbed in the cornea. The
transmittance of the cornea has been studied and shows that wavelengths from around 450nm to
900nm are good choices[9], with an additional peak in transmittance around 1080 nm.
3.2
Thermophoresis
Thermophoresis, also known as the Soret eﬀect, thermodiﬀusion or thermal diﬀusion is the migra-
tion of particles in response to an applied temperature gradient[10]. Even though the fundamental
mechanisms of thermophoresis are still signiﬁcantly disputed, and a purely theoretical approach
that suﬃciently describes all instances of thermophoresis remains to be found, it can still be ap-
plied as an eﬀective tool. Whilst an array of optical tools exist for manipulation of small particles,
such as optical tweezers, plasmonic tweezers, optoelectric tweezers, electrophoresis, and others,
these all have limitations on what materials they can be used for.[1][11] Small particles are espe-
cially hard to control in the case where large force is unwanted due to the potential fragility of
structures. As such, thermophoresis oﬀers a compelling alternative for small soft-matter or tissue
samples. Speciﬁcally, it can ﬁnd application in fractionation of polymer solutions, microﬂuidics
of substances with similar physical properties, structuring of colloidal suspenses[10], manipulation
of biological cells[1], separation and characterization of polymers[12] and others. Despite the ap-
plications being manifold, there is a large variety of contradicting theories attempting to describe
the mechanisms of thermophoresis, especially the dependence on particle size and temperature are
heavily disputed. In the following sections, state-of-the-art theories of thermophoretic mechanisms
will be explored.

3.4.2
Temperature Field Approach by K. I. Morozov
In his paper [14], Morozov attempts to derive the Soret coeﬃcient purely theoretically. By treating
a colloidal particle as a spherically charged particle in an electrolyte and solving Poisson’s equation
a dimensionless potential is found. By taking the negative gradient of the potential in a system in

Scaling of the Soret coeﬃcient can theoretically be seen to be linear with particle radius in Moro-
zov’s [14] and Anderson’s model, whereas it can be seen to scale with R2 in the model by Bringuier.
Experimentally, Duhr and Braun [11] see a linear scaling with the diameter of Polystyrene beads
and the length of DNA molecules. This linearity is also observed for polystyrene colloids by Piazza
[20] and Putnam [18]. On the contrary, the results by Vigolo et al [21] are inconclusive but exhibit
behaviour more akin to scaling of R2 for microemulsions.

Obtaining the Soret Coeﬃcient from Experimental Results
Experimental methods require eﬀective ways of extracting the Soret coeﬃcient from measurements
taken on thermo-diﬀuse systems. A method used by Duhr and Braun [11] is ﬂuorescence imaging
of particles.
The intensity of the resulting ﬂuorescence imaging can then be processed, seeing
as the concentration of particles will be proportional to the intensity of the ﬂuorescence proﬁle.
This approach has also been used by Jiang et al. in their paper [22]. Asmari et al combined the
measurements of the initial concentration, with ﬂuorescent steady-state measurements to get a
reasonable approximation of the Soret coeﬃcient. They then proceeded to observe the diﬀusion
time after the system has cooled down to ﬁnd D, which means DT can also be computed [4]. In
a diﬀerent approach, in a paper by Pedersen et al. [23] DNA molecules were stretched by the use
of thermophoresis. By measuring the length change in the DNA, the force which is a function of
the Soret coeﬃcient can be modeled. As such, the Soret coeﬃcient can be computed. Another
approach to measuring the concentration in order to derive the Soret coeﬃcient is presented by
Piazza and Parola [20] using beam deﬂection. This allows for probing of unmarked particles as
long as they have a diﬀerent refractive index from the solvent. By focusing a beam on the sample
and heating it, the concentration changes which then causes a change in the beam deﬂection due
to a higher concentration of particles with a diﬀerent refractive index. This result can be exploited
to calculate the concentration increase, which is a function of this deﬂection. Using that, the Soret
coeﬃcient can then be written as:

Experimental Setup
4.1
Considerations
The goal is to create an optical setup with the available resources that can accurately measure the
migration of particles in response to a thermal gradient. As such, a laser wavelength with high
absorptivity in water is required to generate heat.
Figure 2: [24] Absorption spectra of light for diﬀerent temperatures of water. The thickest graph,
denoted as ”water”, is at room temperature.
As can be seen in ﬁgure 2 water absorption is strongest in the far UV and the IR regime. Duhr
and Braun [11] use a laser with a wavelength of 1480 nm. In this project, a 1064 nm laser was
used.
This laser is chosen because the laser and optical components such as ﬁlters and Light
Modulators is readily available, whereas purchasing a higher wavelength laser would be expensive
and may have compatibility issues with some of the other optical components. In addition, the
cornea has a high transmittance at 1064 nm[9]. This means that the wavelength is applicable for
the end goal of eye treatment. The disadvantage is the signiﬁcantly reduced absorptivity in water.
This has the consequence that a relatively higher power is required to obtain the same eﬀect. In
addition, a white light source for imaging and a camera that can be used both for visualisation
and ﬂuorescence measurements are needed. For ﬂuorescence measurements, an excitation laser is
required. In this project, stained polystyrene particles by Thermo Fisher and Duke Scientiﬁc are
used. Most sizes are readily available in green absorbing, red emitting dyes. For later parts of the
project, moving the sample or the laser relative to each other to perform sweeps could be required,
so the setup should take this into account. Filters for the ﬂuorescence measurements are also a
necessity.

4.4
Beam Proﬁle using Knife’s Edge Method
It is important to determine the beam’s structure since it determines how sharp the temperature
proﬁle created in the sample will be. This is crucial information since the temperature proﬁle
determines the speed at which particles can be moved using thermophoresis. It also determines the
peak temperature increase, which we see from equation 8 determines the steady-state concentration.
It is assumed that the beam is Gaussian.
4.4.1
Method
The knife’s edge method is a standard technique in Gaussian beam characterisation [25] and is used
to analyse the width of the beam in a selected plane. The core principle of the method is using a
power-meter to measure the beam at constant power. A sharp edge is then moved under the beam
with very small consistent steps. This results in a change of power as a function of position of the
Page 11 of 51
==PAGE:16==
edge as covered in the paper by Marcos A C de Ara´ujo et al [25]. An illustration of the method
can be seen in ﬁgure 6. Notably, in the measurements performed in here, the blade was moved
into the beam from far away, rather than starting under the beam and moving it out. This loss of
signal is then equivalent to the power of the laser in the given position, and this makes it possible
to estimate the beam proﬁle. This was then repeated in consistent intervals in the optical axis,
henceforth denoted as the z-axis, until the position with the shortest beam-width was found. This
information can then be used to estimate the beam width.
Figure 6: [25] Illustration of the knife’s edge method. As depicted, a blade is moved into the
laser beam in the imaging plane in small steps and the resulting power is measured in
the photodiode. This yields power as a function of blade position which can be used
to characterise the beam proﬁle.

5
Bleaching
5.1
Introduction
Bleaching poses a serious challenge when working with ﬂuorescence. When looking at samples
of ﬂuorescent polystyrene beads, using a speciﬁc intensity threshold to limit analysis to particles
in sharp focus is the easiest method. However, in the case where ﬂuorescence exposure reduces
the signal, this option may not be viable since later measurements will yield lower intensities for
similar particles due to bleaching. Another option is to use a high concentration of particles and
measuring the intensity change in the image to determine the change in concentration. Temperature
calibration measurements will also be performed. For these, the overall intensity is the parameter
of interest. An options for these measurements is to take very short measurements of the samples
at diﬀerent times to minimise the bleaching performed. However, in the case where bleaching is
signiﬁcant, this has the drawback that it is not possible to accurately correct for this. Instead, the
approach used is to continuously expose the sample but to measure a bleaching reference so that
the data can be corrected for bleaching.

Discussion
From the results seen in Table 2 and Figure 14 there seems to be a pretty steep drop oﬀin peak

over a long period of time from 60 to 120 minutes, only a minor change in peak intensity is observed.

although the signal at 120 minutes appears dimmer than at 15 minutes, particles can still be seen.
These results are intuitive, seeing as diﬀusion events should cause bleached particles to migrate
out of the image and unbleached particles to migrate in on a longer timescale. As long as the total
sample is signiﬁcantly larger than the measurement window, it seems reasonable that the intensity
trends towards an equilibrium where each particle experiences a certain amount of bleaching before
diﬀusing out of the image. The image here can be seen to be roughly 340x270 (µm)2, compared
to the total sample size of 1x1 (cm)2. Considering the depth of focus of 49.95 µm and sample
thickness of 250 µm, this means the imaged volume is approximately 1.8% of the total sample.
As such, it would take an extremely long time before signiﬁcant bleaching of the total sample is
achieved.
A concern that arose from these measurements was that the diﬀerence in intensity between out
of focus and sharply focused particles became much smaller. Although the intensity is lower, this
reduced diﬀerence means it becomes harder to analyse the image. However, such long exposure
times are unlikely to be of any interest for the purposes of this project. Nonetheless, exposure of
more than a couple of minutes should be avoided if bleaching is to be neglected during analysis.

6
Temperature Calibration
6.1
Introduction
When attempting to move particles using thermal gradients induced by lasers, it is crucial that the
temperature change induced is known. There is an array of ways to do this. Some authors depend
primarily on theoretical simulations or calculations of their experimental environment [1] [12] [32].
Others use thermocouples to measure the temperature [33]. It may be possible to use IR-cameras,
but this is not seen in the literature. However, since the samples used in this project can generally
be considered to be dilute, it is reasonable to assume that temperature changes through absorption
will be almost the same as for a sample consisting of just water. In this case, experimentally
observing the temperature changes in the system using Rubpy3+ as demonstrated by Pedersen et
al [23] should be a more accurate representation than a theoretical model. Nonetheless, estimating
the temperature proﬁle theoretically is still beneﬁcial and will act as a reference to validate the
experimental results.
6.2
Theoretical Approach
6.2.1
Introduction
It is useful to have some theoretical estimation of the temperature proﬁle to compare the exper-
imental measurements with for validation. The calculations performed here are not indicative of
the real system, but meant as a reference point. In addition to the assumptions described for the
speciﬁc examples provided, the presence of particles in the solution is neglected. The physical
properties of the glass surrounding the sample and how it aﬀects the system is also not considered.
6.2.2

niﬁcant heat. Being able to generate signiﬁcant temperature increases of a couple of Kelvin on a
small timescale is crucial if thermophoresis is to be used medically to move particles in the eye on
a cm scale. As an example, an idealised system can be described by a perfectly isolated rectangle
of water the size of the image used in previous measurements. The scenario is illustrated in ﬁgure

6.3
Temperature Reference Measurements
Before the temperature change from applying the laser can be found, a reference must ﬁrst be taken
for the Rubpy3+ at diﬀerent temperatures. For this purpose, the ﬂuorescence intensity of a sample
is measured at an array of temperatures and the relative intensity as a function of temperature
can be computed.
6.3.1
Materials and Methods
A sample was prepared by pipetting 23 µL 10 mM Rubpy3+ solution onto a gene frame and sealing
the sample using a cover slip. The sample was then mounted on top of the stage. Then the hotplate
required to control the temperature of the sample was fastened on top of the sample as seen in
ﬁgure 21. This was chosen due to the lower objective needing to be closer to the sample than the
top objective when focusing. The temperature was then set to a ﬁxed value. Before a measurement
is taken, the sample must have suﬃcient time to reach equilibrium since the heat driver overshoots
slightly upon heating, in addition to the heating sensor not being positioned on the sample but
rather on the attached hotplate. Therefore, the sample was left for at least 5 minutes after the
hotplate reached an equilibrium temperature to minimise errors. Then, the sample was recorded
for 1 second using the 450 nm laser at 15 frames per second, upon which the source was switched
oﬀto minimise bleaching. The sample was then moved to a new area and the process repeated.
This was done a total of three 3 times for 20, 25, 30, 35, and 40 ◦C, respectively. For each 1 second
measurement, the average intensities of the 15 individual frames were found, and these values were
then averaged. This yields three data sets with ﬁve points corresponding to the ﬁve temperatures.
For each sample, the relative intensity change is computed at the diﬀerent temperatures, and these
relative values are averaged. The experimental setup is shown in ﬁgure 21.

6.5
Estimating the Temperature Diﬀerence in the Image
6.5.1
Introduction
Unfortunately, the images obtained in the prior measurements that were used for the analysis
could not be used to ﬁnd the temperature diﬀerence in the image. One reason is that the focus
point of the laser saturated the center of the image despite the ﬁlter, resulting in the expected
temperature peak being eﬀectively blocked out in the image. In addition, attempts to measure
temperature gradients across the image yielded very small diﬀerences with the powers used in this
project. The gradients being relatively small, in addition to the fact that random ﬂuctuations
between images are very large, makes it very diﬃcult to estimate temperature diﬀerences. In an
attempt to quantify the diﬀerences, the image size is increased and a diﬀerent method is employed.
6.5.2
Materials and Methods
Using the 4x instead of 20x objective, images are taken as described in the previous section.
6.5.3
Results and Discussion
To measure temperature diﬀerences in the image, two points of reference in the ﬂuorescence image
are chosen, which consist of a small area. The ﬁrst point is at the very edge of the image and the
second is chosen outside the beam focus point. This choice was made since the brightness of the
picture is slightly aﬀected by the 1064 nm beam itself near the focus point. The distance from the
laser focus point the measurement area is roughly 264 µm. The average intensity of the area after
heating is compared to the initial average. After bleaching correction, the temperature change
can be estimated. This is done in both areas and the gradient between the two areas is estimated
by dividing the diﬀerence in the temperature change between the two with the distance between
them. This results in an estimate of the average temperature gradient 260-730 µm from the focus
point.

Thermophoresis in a Thin Liquid Layer
7.1
Introduction
In this experiment, a thin layer of liquid is heated using the 1064 nm laser to measure the Soret
Coeﬃcient. This was chosen in an attempt to draw a parallel to the results from Duhr and Braun
[11] where a similar system is used and the Soret coeﬃcient is measured. For this experiment, the
thickness of the samples is estimated using an optical microscope. After this, temperature calibra-
tion is performed again, similarly as seen in section 6. This is required because the sample is much
thinner and therefore irradiation will result in a diﬀerent temperature change. Once the system is
characterised, particles of diﬀerent sizes and temperatures are heated and the concentration change
is estimated to calculate the Soret coeﬃcients.
7.2
Materials and Methods
7.2.1
Measuring the Sample Thickness
A sample of water was prepared by adding 20 µL of solution with polystyrene beads to a cover
slip. Another cover slip was then put on top and pressed down gently until the entire surface was
covered. The sample was then sealed with tape and secured onto a microscope slider. Following
this, the sample was mounted on a Nikon eclipse T2 microscope and the microscope was focused
on the top side of the lower cover slip. Scanning the z-axis through the liquid layer until the
microscope was focused onto the lower side of the top cover slip yields a diﬀerence of z-position
that can be read and gives an estimate of the sample thickness. The same can then be done in
reverse. This procedure is repeated a couple of times to estimate uncertainty.
7.2.2
Temperature Calibration
A cover slide was placed on a microscope slide and 10 µL of 10 mM Rubpy3+ solution was pipetted
onto the center. Another cover slide was placed on top and any misalignment between the two
was gently corrected with a lab spatula. Each side of the cover slide sample was glued shut to a
microscope slide to avoid leakage and secure the sample. It was then left to dry for 15-30 minutes.
A sample is shown in ﬁgure 28. This was repeated 3 times. After drying a sample was mounted
on the stage and irradiated for 5 minutes with 2 minutes of pre-bleaching at 9.7, 17.2, and 80.9
mW, respectively.

Introduction

Materials and Method

8.3
Imaging using High Concentration Samples
8.3.1
Introduction
As pointed out in the previous section, the methodology must be changed to be able to gain any
meaningful results. In addition to the challenges previously outlined, the fact that no temperature
gradient could be documented using the 20x objective, as mentioned in section 6, indicates that
the image is too small. As such, the 4x objective was used instead, for which some temperature
diﬀerence was documented. This yields a signiﬁcantly larger image. To compensate for the loss
of signal the concentrations of particles has to be signiﬁcantly larger.
Another change that is
made is in response to observations during bleaching measurements of the polystyrene beads. It
was surprising that in comparison to the bleaching of Rubpy3+, where the bleaching is negligible
after a couple of minutes, a loss of signal is continually observed for the polystyrene beads. The
realisation was made that the signal loss could be due to sedimentation in the sample, rather than
bleaching, due to the polystyrene beads having a higher density than the solvent. This was also
noted by Braibanti et al [37], who chose to use heavy water to compensate for the diﬀerence in
density, noting that it should have negligible eﬀects on the thermophoretic eﬀect. As such, D2O
was incorporated to compensate for the weight and bleaching behaviour became more consistent.
8.3.2
Materials and Methods
The sample was prepared by suspending 1.59µm red ﬂuorescing polystyrene microbeads in Milli-Q
water and adding D2O (Heavy Water) at a ratio of 1.0577 H2O to 1 D2O. The solution was then
stirred using a stirrer. 23 µL of the solution was deposited in a gene frame and sealed. Imaging
was performed by irradiating the sample for 60 seconds at 80.9 mW which should result in a
temperature increase of close to 2 ◦C.
8.3.3
Results and Discussion

8.5
Temperature Dependence of the Soret Coeﬃcient
8.5.1
Introduction
As a follow-up to the two previous experiments and to be able to draw parallels to other literature,
observing in what way temperature impacts measurements is of signiﬁcant interest.
8.5.2
Materials and Methods
Samples are prepared equivalently to the previous measurements, except only particles of 1 and
1.59 µm were used. The hotplate was mounted on top of the sample stage and the samples were
taped onto the hotplate to ensure contact, as seen in ﬁgure 21. The samples were then measured
at 20, 30, and 40 ◦C, waiting for the hotplate to reach equilibrium and an additional 5-10 minutes
to ensure the sample was suﬃciently heated before measuring. The sample was then pre-bleached
to avoid the need for bleaching correction which was found to be very minimal for these large
particles. The samples were then, as previously, irradiated for 300 seconds with 40.28 mW. This
was repeated for both the 1 and 1.59 µm samples, respectively.
8.5.3
Results and Discussion
The results are plotted as relative concentration to the initial concentration calculated from the
intensity of the images near the laser focus point. The results are not corrected for bleaching
as it is negligible after the pre-exposure. As seen in ﬁgure 41 the trend of an initial increase in
concentration followed by a drop after some time is seen again. All of the samples excluding the
1.59 µm beads at 30 ◦C see a steep concentration increase for the ﬁrst 50 seconds or so. Both of the
measurements taken at 20 ◦C show little to no reduction of concentration even beyond this initial
increase. On the other hand, the 30 and 40 ◦C measurements for the 1 µm beads show signiﬁcant
loss of concentration after an initial peak is reached at 150 and 110 seconds, respectively. For the
1.59 µm sample at 30 seconds the expected behaviour of a strong concentration drop towards an
equilibrium is observed. Unfortunately it is an outlier in this data set. The 1.59 µm sample at
40 ◦C exhibits similar behaviour beyond the initial concentration increase. These results could
indicate that there is some unexpected factor causing the samples to increase in concentration
at the start of the measurement.
In addition, the measurements were inconsistent, with the
1.59 µm 30 ◦C sample being the only outlier that exhibits predicted behaviour similar to what
is seen in previous measurements. It is unreasonable to attempt to calculate Soret coeﬃcients
from these measurements, seeing as the results are uninterpretable. The recordings of the samples
exhibit particle transport that looks like convection, which could support the theory that signiﬁcant
convection is occurring in the samples, yielding the surprising results.

9
Discussion and Outlook
In this thesis, an optical setup that can be used to perform ﬂuorescence measurements of samples
whilst heating them with another laser source was designed and validated. This conﬁguration
proved beneﬁcial in demonstrating the possibility of optothermal manipulation of polystyrene mi-
crobeads in water using a 1064 nm laser. The setup contains a computer-controlled x-y stage,
which could be used to move samples relative to the laser beam, enabling investigation of ther-
mophoretic methods where particles are dragged over larger distances. This will be essential in
the process of upscaling to the centimetre scale, which is required for medical application in the
human eye. In tandem, thorough investigations into the distance that particles are moved using
the methods in this thesis is required. It can be inferred from images such as ﬁgure 37 that the
size scale of the movement is ≈100 µm, but this should not be seen as indicative of how far single
particles can be moved.
Another advantage of this setup is that the ﬂuorescence laser source can easily be changed, which
should be exploited for research of other types of particles.
Because although the polystyrene
beads used in this thesis serve as an excellent parallel to other literature, movement of particles
used in medicine may vary signiﬁcantly. This is especially true for non-spherical particles. On the
other hand, a downside of the system is the very large beam divergence found in ﬁgure 8. Whilst
the advantage is that steeper temperature gradients are possible, the beam is manually focused
by moving the lower objective. Especially in thinner samples, this means that human error can
signiﬁcantly impact the temperature gradients observed in the sample. It is recommended that a
beam with less divergence or a better method to focus the beam is developed. Beyond this, the
laser powers used in this thesis were relatively low, and signiﬁcantly higher temperature increases
should be permissible.
Bleaching measurements were performed both for polystyrene Beads and the ﬂuorescent compound
Rubpy3+ (Tris(2,2’-bipyridyl)dichlororuthenium(II) hexahydrate). Minimising bleaching so that
it must not be compensated for, is an option and other experimental methods may choose to do so
instead. However, the method of pre-bleaching for ≈2 minutes and compensating remaining images
for the bleaching eﬀect by measuring references proved itself to be eﬀective. This methodology
resulted in temperature/time curves that were in line with theoretical expectations, even for thin
samples that experienced very signiﬁcant bleaching.
The experimental temperature increase obtained for a speciﬁc laser power was found by using
Rubpy3+. Measuring the intensity of the compound at diﬀerent temperatures yielded a linear
relation, as seen in ﬁgure 22. A linear relation is expected, as observed by Filevich and Etchenique
[30]. For heating of samples using a 1064 nm laser, a declining slope towards a maximum was seen
in ﬁgure 23. However, even after 300 seconds a maximum is not reached. It is likely, that the
entire sample is continuously heated since the glass surrounding the solution has a signiﬁcantly
lower thermal conductivity than water. This could pose a problem and makes it more diﬃcult
to ascertain the actual temperature diﬀerence seen within the image plane. Attempts to measure
temperature gradients indicated very small gradients. Once again, higher powers at shorter expo-
sure times could combat this issue.
A simple theoretical model was created to indicate how temperature gradients generated in a
medium by a laser beam spreads throughout said medium.
A short calculation demonstrated
that temperatures of the required scale could be attainable using even a relatively low power of
80.9 mW. Using the thermal diﬀusion equation with a 3D Gaussian spot indicated that high peak
temperature increases can be obtained with a small laser spot, as seen in ﬁgure 19. Nonetheless,
these calculations are not indicative of the behaviour seen for a real Gaussian beam, which is only
Gaussian in two dimensions. Therefore, it would be very useful to generate simulations of the
temperature using the real parameters of the beam. This would give a better understanding of
what the real temperature proﬁle could look like and is especially useful since it proved diﬃcult to
experimentally determine the temperature gradient.
Although the peak temperature could not be measured experimentally, the theoretical calculation
of the peak temperature for a 3D Gaussian seen in ﬁgure 19 was still lower than what should be
permissible without risking protein denaturation in the human eye. As such, it should be possible
to use signiﬁcantly larger powers, potentially with a pulsed laser.
Page 48 of 51
==PAGE:53==
Experimentally, an average temperature increase of over 1 ◦C in the image plane was seen in less
than two seconds for higher powers. As such, it is reasonable to assume that even short pulses of
less than one second could produce large temperature gradients at higher powers.
Thermophoresis measurements for polystyrene microbeads yielded good results for a thin liquid
layer of ≈16 µm. It was observed that the microbeads experienced negligible bleaching, which
meant that they could be imaged for 5 minutes and analysed using particle counting without any
challenges. Unfortunately, no concentration change was observed for particles under 1 µm. It is
important to acknowledge that the temperature gradients used were likely small, and it is likely
that increasing these could make it possible to analyse the movement of smaller particles. However,
for larger particles of 1 and 1.59 µm signiﬁcant concentration reductions were measured at elevated
temperatures of 30 and 40 ◦C.
For thicker samples of 250 µm, behaviour was very inconsistent. Using particle counting was not
possible since having particles both in and out of focus meant that particles were occasionally
counted and occasionally not, independent of whether actual thermal movement occurred. This
meant that a 4x objective had to be used instead of 20x in conjunction with increasing the con-
centration to ≈0.5 µM. This made it harder to discern movement visibly and made the analysis
less accurate since the intensity was analysed instead of particle counting. Another issue with the
thicker samples was that some type of ﬂow that is not thermophoresis was observed. Said move-
ment looked like vortices, moving particles through the imaging plane and out of focus repeatedly
as seen in ﬁgure 37. It is worth noting, that it is not possible to ascertain that the transport
happening for any of the samples is truly thermophoresis. Other eﬀects, such as optical tweezing
or convection could be happening. This is especially true for larger particles and laser powers. A
possible cause for the thermal vortices is that the laser was not focused exactly into the image
plane. This could be a cause for nonuniform heating resulting in the observed ﬂow. For further
investigation, it is crucial to ﬁnd a way to avoid this.
At the start of the thesis, competing theoretical approaches to describing thermophoresis are pre-
sented. From the data obtained in this thesis, the scaling of the Soret coeﬃcient as a function
of particle size could not be determined. Whilst insuﬃcient data was obtained to conclusively
comment on the scaling as a function of temperature, the results seen for particles in a thin liquid
layer indicate a declining growth trending towards a maximum value. This is seen theoretically by
Bringuier and Anderson [18] and experimentally by Reichl et al [19] and therefore supports their
respective models.
10
Conclusion
Throughout this thesis, multiple methods have been designed which can be used to induce and
analyse optothermal movement of particles.
The optical system designed seen in ﬁgure 3 has
been demonstrated to be suﬃcient for these measurements and functions well as a starting point
or reference for further analysis.
The temperature increase of the system was analysed, using
the ﬂuorescent compound Rubpy3+ (Tris(2,2’-bipyridyl)dichlororuthenium(II) hexahydrate) and
a method to quantify and compensate for Bleaching was developed and employed. Attempts to
measure thermophoresis indicate that the Soret coeﬃcient scales with the temperature of the sys-
tem at a diminishing rate towards a maximum as seen in other literature [19] [18], although the very
limited amount of data makes this analysis inconclusive. The results demonstrate that optothermal
manipulations of particles on the µm scale is possible and limited measurements support that it is
also possible for smaller particles of 600 nm (see ﬁgure 35), using small changes in temperature of
about 2 ◦C. This combined with the prospect of using higher powers to generate large temperature
gradients in less than one second demonstrates the potential of using thermophoresis for optical
manipulation of medical particles in the human eye.
