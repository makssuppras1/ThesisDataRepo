Abstract
This project envisages the design, implementation and validation of a guidance system including collision
avoidance capabilities for an unmanned marine craft (SeaDrone). The SeaDrone is used for military drills by
the Royal Danish Naval Weapons School. The guidance system is based on waypoint navigation through a
path generated in real-time that accounts for obstacle information obtained from a smart sensor platform
detection system, and it ensures compliance with some of the international navigation regulations to prevent
collisions at sea. In order to follow the path and avoid obstacles, a novel guidance law is proposed, that
accounts for vehicle performance at high speed. The evaluation of the performance of the designed guidance
system though full scale experiments has been attempted, but the unfortunate occurrence of an actuator
breakdown and a "run ashore" accident during the two scheduled tests prevented the obtaining of acceptable
results. These episodes are discussed and analyzed, to improve testing procedures in further developments.

CHAPTER 1
Project Description
Solving the problem of guidance and collision avoidance is an important step towards obtaining an
autonomous vehicle. However, "autonomous vehicle" is a rather generic term: to align this thesis’ objectives
to the reader’s expectations, the project requirements will be described, then translated into actual tasks
that the SeaDrone should accomplish. An evaluation by means of vehicles autonomy levels supports this
discussion. Then, a brief description of the SeaDrone will be presented along with the challenges encountered.
Finally, the thesis’ contents will be outlined and its main contributions described.
1.1
Introduction
Autonomous: acting independently or having the freedom to do so.
Humans have an extraordinary capacity to understand their environment, learn, and make decisions
in a disparate range of situations. Our brain is capable of processing information from our senses in a
very sophisticated way. Where our senses lack perception, we use extensive tools to enhance our capacity
to perceive our surroundings. Machines, on the other hand, are very limited on this. Sea navigation is a
very complex activity that requires practice, skill, and ﬂexibility to handle a variety of situations and sea
conditions, and only men have the capacity to handle man-made vessels. Vessels can be equipped with
instruments to aid navigation, but some situations may be too dangerous for humans to handle.
The ﬁeld of unmanned surface vehicles (USV) is relatively young: the ﬁrst large USV were developed
during the second world war, while small ones appeared only during the ’80s. Most of the USV developed
until the ’90s were mostly remotely operated: direct human supervision was required at all times. The
term autonomous surface vessel (ASV) is more appropriate to underline autonomy from human operators.
The ﬁrst development of an ASV can be credited to the MIT Sea Grant college program in 1993 ([Manley,
1997]). The prototype "ARTEMIS" was based on a 1/17 scale replica of a ﬁshing trawler, equipped with
Global Position System (GPS), digital compass, and an onboard microprocessor that controlled the rudder
and throttle with a basic (Proportional Derivative) regulator. It was able to travel at low speed (1-1.5m/s)
with an endurance of 4 hours; missions were mostly hydrographic data collection. Today, ASVs can achieve
impressive level of autonomous endurance (the current record is held by the Liquid Robotics’ Wave Glider
with 14000 kilometres in one autonomous journey), high speeds, and long range obstacle detection (Elbit
System Silver Marlin can detect small boats up to 6km). Nowadays ASVs are typically used in missions of
exploration, rescue, defence, oceanographic/meteorological data collection and many others. Please refer to
[Bertram, 2008] and [Motwani, 2012] for comprehensive surveys on unmanned surface vehicles.
Autonomous vehicles capable of navigating and executing missions with little or no supervision by
humans pose a variety of challenges depending on the type of mission. In open sea, a vessel must be able to

1.2
Desired Autonomy Level
The SeaDrone (ﬁgure 1.1) is supposed to be deployed during favourable sea conditions in a limited and
known area nearby the Danish Forces Joint Target Team base. No ﬁxed obstacles (such as buoys or ﬁxed
structure) are in water, except for the pier. It is desired that the marine craft is able to navigate through a
set of waypoints (e.g. GPS coordinates) and be able to detect and avoid collisions with eventual unknown
obstacles such as buoys or small boats. Currently an operator on land visually supervises the drone and
he is able to take full control (via wireless link) over the marine craft when desired. The purpose of the
overall project is to achieve autonomous operation without direct visual contact with a human operator, and
to operate the drone at long ranges and in hostile environments.
The expected autonomous performance of a drone can be described by its "Autonomy Level", a value
obtained by means of metrics that assess the actual tasks that the drone is required to perform to complete its
mission. These metrics can be the level of human trust on the drone, or the mobility capabilities (reviewed
in [Frost et al., 2012]), and a comprehensive set is found in the Autonomous Control Level (ACL) chart
developed by researchers from the Air Force Research Laboratory [Clough, 2002]. The ACL describes the
autonomy level on a scale from 0 (Remote control) to 10 (Fully Autonomous). An adapted version from
[Kendoul, 2012] is presented brieﬂy in Table 1.1, and the SeaDrone autonomy level is selected on the basis
of desired speciﬁcation of Guidance, Navigation, Control, External system independence, Environmental
complexity, and Mission complexity (the last three speciﬁcations are lumped together in EEM for simplicity).
The level 4 represents the highest autonomy level for vehicles which do not cooperate with other
autonomous vehicles. In this project no distinction is made between the terms collision avoidance and hazard
avoidance. Table 1.1 contains many keywords that have now been introduced, such as Real-Time, Obstacle
Detection, Path Planning, and Trajectory Tracking.

Obstacle Detection
The onboard smart sensor based detection system, integrating a vision system with a
(car) radar, provides the capability to detect static and moving objects located on the water surface, frontally
with respect to the sea-drone attitude. The actual Obstacle Detection is dependent on an algorithm that
interprets information coming from the sensors and compute the size, position and eventual velocity of the
obstacles the drone should avoid.
Path Planning
Guidance is here dependent on an arbitrary (straight or curved) line that joints two
consecutive waypoints. The path is updated at any event such as when a potential collision with an obstacle
is detected or when a waypoint has been reached.
Trajectory Tracking
To make sure that the craft follows a generated path, the control system should
receive proper references for heading and speed. The terminology used in this thesis is the same as that used
in [Fossen et al., 2014] and [Skjetne, 2005], where the trajectory tracking problem requires the vehicle to
follow a path with given time constraints. When no time constraints are speciﬁed, the term Path Following
is used, which is the main problem that this thesis attempts to solve.
1.3
General Requirements
It is desired that the SeaDrone is capable of autonomous navigation and collision avoidance after received
information on waypoints. The current hardware allows both autonomous navigation and frontal obstacle
detection. To guarantee a safe distance for obstacle avoidance, a worst case scenario of maximum speed
combined with a detection of an unavoidable obstacle (too large to safely veer) is used. It is assumed

(1.1)

1.4
The SeaDrone
The SeaDrone is based on a one man watercraft SeaDoo GTX 215, powered by a 216hp engine that
drives a water-jet at the rear of the drone. By controlling deﬂection and strength of the water-jet, it is possible
to steer and move forwards or backwards. Veering is achieved by both water jet deﬂection and two small
outboard rudders hung on the stern. The combination of horizontal water jet deﬂection and the two small
rudders will be in the thesis referred together as rudder. Three servos have been mounted to achieve direct
control of throttle, rudder, and elevator (that respectively control jet strength, horizontal jet deﬂection, and
vertical jet deﬂection). An automotive radar and a low cost camera are the main sensors for frontal obstacle
detection, and are installed in a water-tight box on top of the drone. The main control unit is equipped with a
3-axis inertial measurement unit (IMU), a compass, and a global navigation satellite system (GNSS). The
current hardware was developed during earlier M.Sc. projects by [Svendsen and Holck, 2012], [Hermann,
2013] and enabled the drone to achieve the desired Autonomy Level with no further modiﬁcation. The reader
is advised to see appendix A for a complete description of the SeaDrone system.
1.5
Challenges
This project envisages the development and implementation of a guidance system that is the result
of techniques based on different topics and comes from very broad areas. Since little research relates to
high speed autonomous surface vehicles, the existing solutions and underlying assumptions have to be
critically evaluated, since some may not apply. As in any experimental thesis, many challenges come from
implementation issues. In fact, the current hardware and software consist of several subsystems developed by
different people. Partial communication between these subsystems was previously implemented, but to close
the control loop, a full bidirectional communication channel had to be developed. A deep understanding of
all the subsystems was required, and a large amount of time had to be put into developing a modular and
ﬂexible platform, to enable further improvements for later projects. Additionally, two separate sessions for
ﬁeld test were planned, but during the ﬁrst one no data could be collected due to a failure in the elevator servo.
The big challenge here is that only one chance was left to test the full system where several subsystems had
been modiﬁed and tested only in laboratory.

CHAPTER 2
Path Planning
Mission tasks such as navigation to a destination or patrolling an area, require the vehicle to follow a
certain path, that is a straight or curved line that the vehicle should follow to fulﬁl its mission. The level of
knowledge, the complexity of the environment, and the type of mission play an important role in choosing
the proper technique to generate a path. Countless techniques have been developed in literature due to the
variety of possible applications: a robot that has to navigate its way through an unknown labyrinth poses
different requirements with respects to a missile that has to strike a target. In addition if the objective is
to navigate from point A to point B, a desired path can be the shortest one or a longer one which reduces
fuel consumption or actuator wear and tear (like a driver who chooses to drive on a longer highway path
instead of going through a busy city center). The project requirements will be presented here, and some of the
common path planning techniques will be described. Details the of most suitable techniques are presented
along with motivations for the choice made.
2.1
Project Requirements
The requirements for this project include navigation based on waypoints (going from A to B, then towards
C after having reached B, and so on), while avoiding obstacles along the way. In addition in compliance
with the COLREGs, that is international regulations to avoid collisions with other marine craft. Common
techniques available in literature, regarding planar (bi-dimensional) navigation have been studied in relation
to the thesis’ objectives and a choice has been made based on the following criteria:
- Simplicity of implementation
- Computational burden
- Suitability for obstacle collision avoidance
It is desired that the overall technique should be relatively easy to test and implement in C code and will
provide ﬂexibility in handling unknown obstacles. The SeaDrone is a fast marine craft, therefore the
computational time for a new path should be small enough that the vehicle would not travel too far during
computation. At 30m/s it was deemed acceptable a computation time of 100ms, during which the craft
would travel 3m, so to have time and space to perform evasive manoeuvres when required.
Regarding the suitability for obstacle collision avoidance, in literature there are two distinct approaches
to collision avoidance: path re-planning, where the path is planned accordingly to obstacles and the craft
has to tightly follow the path, or reactive behaviour, where the craft performs evasive manoeuvres to avoid
obstacles, then returns to the originally planned path. In the path re-planning approach, the algorithm has

consecutive waypoints Pi = [xi, yi]T ∈R2 and Pi+1 = [xi+1, yi+1]T ∈R2 is:

several techniques exist in literature, such as Dubins paths ([Dubins, 1957]), clothoid curves, and Bezier
curves to cite some of the most common (see [Haugen, 2010] for a complete review).

Dubins ([Dubins, 1957]) proved that the shortest path (minimum time) between two conﬁgurations of a
craft (from a position with a given heading angle, to another position with possibly different heading angle)

are generated by circles inscribed in between the straight lines, as shown in ﬁgure (2.2(a)). This method is

to the simple path consisting by straight lines and circle arcs. It is also easy to use for human operators who

CHAPTER 2. PATH PLANNING
Consideration on the APF method
The APF method provides an elegant mathematical way to generate
curved paths. The possibility of having a clearly speciﬁed radius is a very positive quality of the APF that
ﬁts with collision avoidance requirements: as the SeaDrone detects obstacles, it also measures an uncertainty
in the estimation of their position. This uncertainty can be used to determine the size of the doublet. The
potential functions utilized in this project come from ﬂuid-dynamics and are not fully exempt from problems.
In fact, the doublets have the disadvantage that they must be oriented, since they are composed of a repulsive
area and an attractive area: this issue could be avoided by using different potential ﬁeld equations, e.g.
electric ﬁeld. Techniques to avoid local minima issues and the path planning exist in literature (e.g. [Zhu
et al., 2006]). Since the scope of this project is to implement a minimum viable solution, it has been decided
to use the Potential Flow method as described here, leaving path-planning improvements for future work.
2.6
Other Techniques
Optimization techniques
are an effective tool to generate the "best" (optimal) path according to given
constraints. Constraints on path, obstacles, and vehicle dynamics can be set to obtain a mathematical problem
which minimizes a chosen cost function. In rhis way different objectives can be taken into account, such as
minimize travel distance, or collision risk, or actuator wear and tear. These techniques show their strength
in complex environments and proved effective in collision avoidance settings [Fiorini and Shiller, 1996],
but due to the computational burden that optimization techniques bring along with them, and the need for a
simple solution, it has been decided not to further investigate optimal techniques.
Graph theory based techniques
are widely used in path planning: techniques such as A* search, Rapidly
Exploring Random Trees (RTT) and many others can handle very complex environments, provide optimal
solutions, and always provide a solution when available, unlike in the APF where additional checks are
necessary to verify that the path is valid. On the other hand, they are usually very expensive computationally,
although some attempts to apply RTTs in real-time have proven successful (see [Frazzoli et al., 2002], or
[Bruce and Veloso, 2003]).
2.7
Summary
Path planning is an active ﬁeld rich in research and techniques. In this chapter some techniques commonly
used in marine navigation were presented, from the simple Dubins paths, to the intuitive APF. Exploring
the full extent of path planning techniques in literature was out of scope of this thesis, as many techniques
are quite involved mathematically and provide little beneﬁt to the value of this project. The choice of the
technique to be used fell on the APF, that made it possible to satisfy the requirements for this project: it is a
relatively easy to implement, and provides an intuitive methodology to account for obstacles. Computing
time measurements show that a trajectory can be generated in under 50ms, which easily fulﬁlls the real-time
requirement, and even allows a margin for re-planning when unsuitable trajectories are generated.

CHAPTER 3
Guidance and Collision Avoidance
The approach taken in this project is to include the obstacles in the generation of the path: if a successful
guidance is achieved, the obstacles are avoided. The concept of guidance law will be described. Certain
situations may generate challenging paths that require rapid evasive manoeuvres which traditional guidance
laws are not designed to handle. Common guidance laws will be described, and since certain assumptions
do not hold for high-speed marine craft, a novel guidance law suitable to the needs of this project has been
proposed, and it will described and discussed. Finally, the collision avoidance behaviour of the SeaDrone
should comply with international navigation rules (COLREGs): a simple approach taking this into accounts
is developed and discussed.
3.1
Introduction
The path following problem at hand is a subset of the general path following problem. Speciﬁcally
it relates to under-actuated vehicles with non holonomic constraints. The SeaDrone is an under-actuated
vehicle because it is equipped with actuators that do not directly allow sideways movements on the XY-plane:
lateral movement is achieved only by moving forward while steering the rudder.
Holonomic constraints can be expressed as g(t, h) = 0, where t is time and h is a vector of parameters;
nonholonomic constraints are of the form of g(t, h, ˙h) = 0 that include constraints on the dynamics of the
system. In this last case, the problem of "path-following" takes the name of "dynamic path-following" or
"path-manoeuvring", where the dynamics of the system have to be controlled. The algorithms that make the
under-actuated vehicle to follow a given path, are called guidance laws.
The ultimate purpose of the guidance laws here presented is to minimize the distance between craft and

3.1.1
Actuator limitations
The guidance in between waypoints would generally be through straight lines, and the transition between
two straight lines when changing waypoints has several solutions (as discussed in chapter 2). In collision
avoidance, rapid manoeuvres are required to avoid impacts, which require sudden changes in direction and/or
in speed. When controlling the SeaDrone, the rudder does not instantly change its position due to both
physical actuator limitation, and rate limitations imposed by the controller for safety reasons. The SeaDrone
turning capabilities largely depend on the rudder capabilities, which are :
- Range of motion: ±15deg
- Time to swipe from full left to full right ≈1sec
The range of motion is further limited at ±5deg for speeds higher than 15m/s. The last limitation is often
neglected in literature studies: this can not be the case in this project since it plays an important role to
the real-world performance. When travelling at 30 m/s it is a very relevant delay that directly impacts on
collision avoidance capabilities.
Actuator rate limitations have a direct impact on the turning rate, however these limitations are often
neglected in maritime navigation, mostly because techniques are applied on relatively slow vessels that
should follow paths with mild variations. In high speed navigation with obstacle avoidance features, it is
necessary to act fast, usually operating close to the rudder saturation limits, both in rate and in amplitude.
3.1.2
Collision avoidance approach
Several approaches can be taken to avoid obstacles. In some studies ([Tobenkin et al., 2011], [Majumdar
and Tedrake, 2013] and [Spenko et al., 2006]), primitive manoeuvres are stored in a database and the
appropriate trajectory is selected in order to avoid incoming obstacles. In this kind of approach, the craft
executes an evasive manoeuvre, then it heads back to the original path. Other studies [Fiorini and Shiller,
1996] perform an online optimization to re-design the path to avoid the obstacles. Still others ([Gomes et al.,
2006], [Gomes et al., 2007]) developed a controller for an autonomous surface craft based on previews of
future path references.
==PAGE:37==
3.2. COMMON GUIDANCE LAWS

The strategy for collision avoidance selected in this project is based on the re-planning of the path,
following an Ωshaped trajectory to circle around the obstacle. Re-planning the path has a higher compu-
tational burden than selecting a primitive manoeuvre from a database. However, if only local re-planning
of the path in a neighbourhood of the obstacle is performed, then the required computational time can be
kept well within the real-time constraint. In this situation, it is of extreme importance that the craft does
not "overshoot" the line that encircles the obstacle, hence an accurate (and possibly conservative) path
following is desired. Another way to re-plan the path is by describing the obstacles as ellipses (by simply
re-formulating the doublets velocity ﬁeld), to generate paths with earlier turns. In this way the marine craft
would mildly glide around them, given enough space to manoeuvre. This may not be always possible, as a
mild change in trajectory is not enough to avoid an obstacle detected in the immediate vicinity of the craft.
Common guidance laws will be now investigated, and they will be evaluated also with respect to the
aforementioned project needs. For simplicity it is assumed that there are no sideways forces (e.g. due to
currents), hence the sideslip angle β = 0. When this is not the case, the sideslip angle can be estimated and
compensated as in [Pedersen and Fossen, 2012].
3.2
Common Guidance Laws
Three main guidance laws are found in the literature [Fossen, 2011], [Yanushevsky, 2006]: Line Of Sight
(LOS), Pure Pursuit (PP), Constant Bearing (CB). In marine guidance applications the LOS is deﬁned as
the vector that starts at the vessel position and passes through a point located on a path-tangential line at a
given look-ahead distance ∆> 0 ahead of the vessel projection on the (straight line) path. This deﬁnition
contrasts with that given in [Yanushevsky, 2006], where the LOS is deﬁned as "The line that starts at the
reference point (e.g. the missile) and passes through the objective of the guidance (the target)".
The LOS guidance law more common in the literature is the one that uses the former deﬁnition of the
LOS vector. The PP guidance law directs the craft towards a (moving) target with the objective to reach
the target, in case of missile guidance, or just follow it at a certain (variable) distance. In the latter case the
classic "Carrot Chasing" (CC) term is more appropriate to address the PP guidance law. In path following
applications, the CB guidance law is less relevant, since the objective is to follow a parallel track at a constant
distance from a moving target.
When following straight line paths a LOS guidance law with a ﬁxed look-ahead distance is generally
used (ﬁgure 3.1). The LOS guidance law for straight line paths it is simple and effective in converging to
the path by minimizing the cross-track error. To achieve this, the vessel’s course angle Ψ should converge
to the path angle αk. The convergence speed to the desired angle is often dependent on a relation between
cross-track error e and the look-ahead distance ∆so to obtain a less aggressive controller when the vessel is
on track. This solution is commonly implemented as the following LOS guidance law:

3.3.1
On the conservative or relaxed approaches
Two main approaches can be used when setting reference signals for heading and surge velocity :
- Conservative: the curvature taken as reference is max{κc, κp} where κc and κp are the curvatures
of the path corresponding to the craft projection and VTP projection on the path, respectively. This
approach produces a tight trajectory control along all the path.
- Relaxed: the curvature used as reference is κp. In this case the control is tighter in the ﬁrst half of the
manoeuvre, whether in the second half of the manoeuvre the curvature is generally lower ahead of the
craft.
The relaxed approach gives better qualitative performance in case of single-obstacles avoidance, since it is
not required for the craft to follow tightly the trajectory after the obstacle is cleared.

3.5
Collision Avoidance COLREG Compliant
The International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea (COLREGs) is a convention published
by the International Maritime Organization (IMO) in 1972 that describe the so-called "rules of the road",
that is navigation rules to prevent collisions between two or more vessels. It consists of 38 rules and 4
annexes that describe actions that vessels should perform in order to prevent collisions, and equipment that
the vessels should have such as lights and sound devices. The rules are generally written with few technical
speciﬁcations, since they are mostly subject to the understanding of the mariner in a given situation, who
holds full responsibility for avoiding collisions. In fact some actions in extreme cases can deviate from
the COLREGs, if deemed strictly necessary. Although applications of autonomous surface vehicles (ASV)
are growing fast, regulatory actions did not develop at the same pace and it will still take few years before
the legislation will be ﬁnalized. This lack of ad-hoc rules for ASV and the strong emphasis on human
interpretation poses signiﬁcant challenges for ASV that limit their deployment in trafﬁcked seas. Several
studies ([Benjamin et al., 2006], [Colito, 2007], [Loe, 2008], [Bibuli et al., 2009]) have tackled the problem
by determining which rules can be coded in an understandable way for a computer and deﬁne proper actions
to be taken during navigation, in order to comply with these rules.
Naturally, active sensors such as radar, lidar, camera, etc. have an important role in the capabilities of an
ASV: it is a prerequisite that the mariner should be always on the look-out around 360 degrees, to detect
if incoming vessels are signalling their actions via lights or sounds, and to understand which vessels (e.g.
ﬁshing boats) are not required to follow some of the rules. Generally, a vessel with limited capabilities in
terms of sensors and "intelligence" on board, will be limited to try to comply with a small subset of the
COLREGs.
In this project, due to the relatively narrow horizontal ﬁeld of view of the obstacle detection system, it
will not be possible to avoid collisions in all possible scenarios. The COLREG rules that can be implemented
in an algorithm, considering the current hardware (frontal detection only) are rule 13 - Overtaking, and rule
14 - Head-on. Additionally, rule 18 speciﬁes that power-driven vessels always have to give away to other
incoming vessels. In overtaking scenario, the vessel should keep away from the overtaken vessel, and this is
an underlying assumption of the obstacle avoidance controller. In the head-on scenario, an vessel coming
from the front, should be avoided by veering to starboard (the right side of the vessel). This feature can be
implemented without changing the overall strategy to avoid collisions, that is to generate a suitable trajectory
that the craft would follow to avert a potential collision. Furthermore, the craft behaviour should resemble as
close as possible what an human would do in such situation, to avoid confusing the incoming boat (assumed
to follow the same COLREG rules). It is assumed that the incoming boat follows a straight line path, which
can be estimated by the information received by the detection algorithm. A virtual obstacle is added to the
path planner, to generate a suitable trajectory that :
- makes the SeaDrone take action as soon as the detection takes place,
- makes sure that the SeaDrone veers to starboard.
The SeaDrone will lose sight of the incoming ship as soon as it veers enough. In this case one can choose if
the SeaDrone should keep the current path, or generate a new one with no obstacles, to resume the trajectory
towards the waypoint. For simplicity, the intersection between craft path and incoming boat path can be
taken as critical point (ﬁgure 3.16(a)). In the vicinity of this point a virtual obstacle can be placed in order to
generate a trajectory that veers to starboard (ﬁgure 3.16(b)). The algorithm works as follows :

3.6
Summary
This chapter has dealt with the solution of the path-following problem. The integrated approach "path-
following plus collision avoidance" focused on the guidance laws that generate heading references to keep
the craft on track. Common guidance laws have been evaluated and found to be lacking in performance
during high-speed manoeuvres scenarios. A novel guidance law has been proposed, that utilizes easy-to-
measure craft capabilities to provide suitable references, given a knowledge of the path ahead. Finally a
simple approach to satisfy navigation rules to prevent collisions at sea (COLREGs) has been introduced
and discussed. Not all the possible cases have been analysed, and the algorithm requires a better reﬁnement
to handle more realistic scenarios. Nevertheless, the objective to obtain a minimum viable solution for the
SeaDrone navigation system has been reached.
The algorithms for path planning and collision avoidance, have been developed and tested in Mat-
lab/Simulink (see appendices A and B for implementation details). Sea trials are the natural next step, to
validate the performance of the guidance and collision avoidance system in the real world.

The ﬁeld tests have been performed at the Danish Forces Joint Target Team base (ﬁgure 4.1), located in

Results

4.3
Accidents Description
Compromising accidents happened in two out of three days of ﬁeld tests. The two events will be ﬁrst
described with facts and contexts; then, the episodes will be analysed and supported by inﬂuential Human
Error theories; multiple concurrent causes will be identiﬁed, and ﬁnally, guidelines on how to perform
successful ﬁeld tests that would limit the occurrence of accidents will be provided.
First accident description
The ﬁrst ﬁeld test was held on February 3 2015. Despite being winter time
the sea conditions were good (ﬁgure 4.4). At the naval base was a rather hectic day and at the same time
most of the personnel was involved in training activities. To deploy the SeaDrone at sea, one person had to
physically be in water to remove the harness used to lift the drone. Due to a last minute contingency, one
operator could not join the procedure, leaving only the expert drone operator the task to be in water. In this
occasion the remote control was given to a non-expert operator.
As part of the procedure, the remote control is switched on to turn on the SeaDrone engine, in idle
position. At this moment, the SeaDrone was ﬂoating on water with a direction parallel to the rocky coastline.
Once the engine was turned on the following events happened within 3 to 5 seconds: the SeaDrone starts
moving forward at low speed, the operator in water tries to hold it in vain, the drone moves forward towards
some small rocks about 5 meters away, the RC operator steers the drone away, a small rock is hit, and the
SeaDrone moves towards open sea. A loss in the drone manoeuvrability halts the ﬁeld test: an hardware
failure is identiﬁed as a broken elevator servo.
The SeaDrone fail-safe feature
In order to later analyse the second accident, a description of the SeaDrone
fail-safe feature is needed. The fail-safe feature allows the remote operator to take full control over the drone
actuators by means of a switch located on the top left corner of the remote controller. The radio receiver on
the SeaDrone outputs 4 cables: fail-safe, rudder, throttle, and elevator. These cables are fed into the control
board (receiver side of the SLUGS, see appendix A). From the board, 3 cables are outputted towards the
drone actuators.
There are two possible conﬁgurations for the SeaDrone, that determine the source of the heading
reference: controller (automatic) or radio operator (manual). When the fail-safe switch is ON, the control
mode is ﬁxed on manual. When the fail-safe switch is OFF, the operator has full control until a valid request
to change control mode is received. These requests may come from the Ground Station Software (able to

4.4
When (Many) Things Go Wrong
4.4.1
Slips and mistakes
Errare humanum est: it is human to make errors, it was, and (most certainly) it will always be. Errors
alone, however, do not imply bad consequences: in fact, if one forgets to pull his car’s handbrake after
parking, the outcomes can range from nothing (car parked on ﬂat road) to a catastrophic disaster (possible
scenarios are left to the reader’s fantasy).
Norman [Norman, 1983] deﬁnes two classes of errors: mistakes and slips. "The division occurs at the
level of the intention: a person establishes an intention to act. If the intention is not appropriate, this is a
mistake. If the action is not what was intended, this is a slip". Both accidents began with a slip: it was
intended to keep the SeaDrone in position, but an involuntary error in carrying out the method, such as a
wrong Radio Command setting, led to a wrong outcome.
After the slip occurred, and the drone started moving forward, the two different operators in both cases
commanded the drone to steer. Here the intent is correct (avoid to hit the rocks), but the action is not (due to
scarcity of space to perform the maneuver), hence this classiﬁes as mistake.
Correct actions would have been: remotely switch off the SeaDrone (there is a switch for that), or reduce
speed and set the elevator to achieve idle or reverse movement.
4.4.2
Levels of human actions
According to Reason [Reason, 1990], human actions can be classiﬁed into three levels: :
- Skill-based: actions performed as a routine and highly automatic (e.g. an experienced car driver
shifting gears).
- Rule-based: actions performed as combination of conscious and unconscious processes, to determine
the response for known situations (e.g. a driver that has to avoid a pedestrian crossing the street).
- Knowledge-based: demanding actions that require concentration and conscious reasoning to choose
the appropriate response to an unfamiliar situation (e.g. a person who is learning how to drive).
With experience and learning processes, knowledge-based actions subsequently become rule-based actions
and ultimately skill-based actions. Slips fall under the Skill-based errors that happen "when our automatic
processes are interrupted" [Sternberg, 2011].
In the second accident, the fail-safe feature "OFF: full control until an external signal takes over steering"
was new enough to require a conscious level of attention when manoeuvring. Likewise, in the ﬁrst accident
the operator had little experience in manoeuvring the drone and reacting to situations. Hence, in both cases,
the required actions fell under the Knowledge-based category: with 3 seconds to react in a situation of risk,
there is an high chance of acting by instinct (steer away the vehicle), rather then reasoning to ﬁnd the best
action. Culture and emotions play a role in the picture, but these aspects will not be further analysed for the
sake of keeping a consistent analysis.
4.4.3
The Swiss cheese model of human error
Attributing the cause of an accident solely to a human action or a device malfunctioning is an approach
way too simplistic. All accidents, that can be the famous Three Miles Island, or plane crashes, or SeaDrone
hitting rocks, are the result of a build up and alignment of smaller errors that in combination with an hazard,
result in an accident.
==PAGE:60==

Reason [Reason, 1997] proposed a model that is commonly referred to as the "Swiss cheese model" of
system accidents (ﬁgure 4.7). This model does not provide quantiﬁable metrics of evaluation, but it is used to
describe the complexity of the contingencies leading to accidents. A given system, or situation, is composed
by successive layers of defences from hazards. Such defences can be good procedures, regular maintenance,
component redundancy, well-designed software, proper training, proper supervision, and so on. Gaps in
these layers (hence the analogy with the holes in the cheese) are caused by missing procedures, neglected
maintenance for some equipment, bugs in the software, lack of training, shortage of staff or supervision,
and so on. When these gaps align in certain combinations, the hazard "the SeaDrone is not behaving as
expected" will cause an accident "SeaDrone on the rocks".
In the ﬁrst accident, holes can be identiﬁes as: lack of staff/supervision, lack of operator training,
wrong orientation during deployment, wrong RC setting. In the second accident, holes can be identiﬁes as:
confusing fail-safe feature, lack of training with the modiﬁed control-switch, control command sent at the
wrong moment, gloves hitting the throttle lever, drone heading towards the beach. If any of these aligned
"gaps" was closed, the accidents would have been avoided.
4.5
What Now?
Now, as many gaps as possible need to be closed for the next ﬁeld tests. One critical issue to address,
is to reduce the cognitive load for the RC operator. A way to do it is to provide adequate training before
the ﬁeld test: a short session where the basic actions become automated (skill-based) for the operator. As
an example, if the craft does not seems to be controllable, immediately check if fail-safe switch is in ON
position, minimize throttle, and eventually shut down the drone; these actions may be repeated several times
to make sure they are executed promptly.
Additionally, the SeaDrone control-mode selector can be re-designed to make it less confusing regarding
the actual status of the drone: the fail-safe switch can be set such that ON means manual control, and OFF
means automatic control, with no intermediate checks.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusions
In this project a guidance and collision avoidance system for an unmanned high-speed marine craft
has been designed and implemented. The aim was to obtain a working solution to be implemented in the
real-system, and that it could be easily expandable. The project started with ambitious goals, some of which
could have not been achieved. This is due to the fact that a bi-directional communication channel between
SLUGS and SSP was absent and a solution to close the control loop had to be investigated and implemented.
The waypoint-based path planner based on artiﬁcial potential ﬁeld proved to be a fast and ﬂexible solution,
although some minor issues suggest that better ﬁeld functions should have been used. A novel guidance law
was proposed: based on measurable craft capabilities the heading reference from the guidance system takes
into account maneuverability limitations in performing high-speed evasive maneuvers to avoid obstacles. A
simple approach to comply with some COLREGs has been implemented, resulting in an intuitive algorithm
that very well blends with the current path planning strategy. Practical solutions have been preferred to
elaborate mathematical ones: because of that the reader may have found a lack of a consistent mathematical
framework supporting some decisions. Since the SeaDrone is not yet fully autonomous, the system has to
comprise also the human operator and the human-machine interface. The accidents happened during the sea
trials exposed how a careful design of the human-machine interface has to be an integrated part of future
development work.
5.1
Future Work
The SeaDrone is now enabled to navigate autonomously, however several tasks may need to be accom-
plished to further improve the system and obtain a reliable platform :
- The obstacle detection module developed by [Hermann, 2013] was not coded in the SeaDrone Software:
for actual real-time obstacle detection, this component is of primary importance.
- To improve the detection system a pitch-roll stabilizer may need to be installed: the servos have been
purchased and an Arduino board installed to control them. The servos would need to be physically
installed, and a stabilizing algorithm developed. As the roll-estimator based on the horizon detection
would be affected by the stabilizer, the attitude Kalman Filter may need to be adapted.
- A long range radio link would ﬁnally allow the use of only one software platform, the SeaDrone
Software on the SSP.

APPENDIX A
SeaDrone Platform
A.1
Modiﬁed SeaDoo GTX 215
The SeaDrone is based on a personal watercraft SeaDoo GTX 215, adapted by the Danish Forces Joint
Target Team to enable remote, unmanned, and autonomous operation. Some of the physical characteristics
of the platform are given in Table A.1.
Three servos are installed to control throttle, rudder, and impeller elevator. The throttle servo is controlled
in a continuous range and regulates the engine power. The rudder servo regulates the steering, and can be
controlled to its full extent ±15 deg where 0 deg is neutral position for forward movement without veering.
The impeller elevator ranges between 0 and 55 degrees and allows a water jet deﬂection of twice that angle. It
is assumed to keep the elevator at 0 degrees for forward movement, although is possible to move backwards
at reduced speed by setting the elevator at 55 degrees. The water jet in this case is deﬂected at twice the
angle (110 degrees), hence it points underwater towards the front. A more detailed description can be found
in [Theisen, 2013].
A.2
Radar and Camera System
The radar and camera system (ﬁgure A.1) have been installed by [Hermann, 2013]. The idea to have
two sensors for obstacle detection is to improve the overall robustness. The radar alone is capable to detect
obstacles in the way, but its small vertical ﬁeld of view can cause troubles when the SeaDrone oscillates due
to waves. The camera has a larger ﬁeld of view that can have obstacles on sight even during pitch oscillations
due to sea waves. Additionally, image processing techniques have been implemented to detect the horizon
and improve the SeaDrone roll and pitch estimates.

A.3.1
SLUGS
The Santa Cruz Low-cost UAV GNC System [Lizarraga, 2007] (SLUGS, ﬁgure A.4), was originally
designed to be used on Unmanned Aerial Vehicles. It is equipped with several sensors, of which GPS,
Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU), and magnetometer are utilized to obtain attitude and position estimation.
It comprises two separate Micro Controller Units (MCU): the Sensor MCU deals with acquisition and
ﬁltering of data from sensors; the Control MCU utilizes the data received by the Sensor MCU to compute
control signals, pilot servo motors, and send data to the telemetry link. The communication between the two
MCUs is established with an internal Serial Peripheral Interface.
The SLUGS is designed to allow programming of both MCUs independently using Mathworks Simulink
and the Real Time Workshop Embedded Coder. Open-source C libraries are written ad-hoc to manage the
data structures and prepare telemetry packages. Each MCU is equipped with 2 universal asynchronous
receiver/transmitter (UART), to exchange data using RS232 standard serial communication. One of the
Control MCU serial ports is used to send and receive data through telemetry link, and the other one is used
to exchange data with the Linux PC.
In this project, the Control MCU sends sensors information to the Linux PC and directly receives the
desired rudder angle from the control algorithm running on the Linux PC. The development idea behind
this logic is to move all the control algorithms into the Linux PC, leaving to the Control MCU only the
tasks to pilot actuators and send data. In this way the SeaDrone control system resides exclusively on the
more powerful PC where advanced features can be developed. The SLUGS is connected to a custom board
designed by [Lø wert and Christensen, 2010] that provide easy access to pins, serial ports and power supply.
A.3.2
Ground Station
The Ground Station is a laptop running Windows XP, equipped with a long-range radio communication
board designed to exchange data with the SLUGS. A "Ground Station Software" (GSS) with graphical
interface is installed and allows a real time visualization of the status of sensors, actuators and attitude
of the SeaDrone. Additionally, it is possible to give set-points and change the control mode. Since the
Ground Station Software is designed to communicate with the SLUGS’s Control MCU only, a Wi-Fi link to
