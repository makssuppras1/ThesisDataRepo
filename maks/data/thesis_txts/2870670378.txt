Abstract
This thesis investigates how airborne wind energy systems (AWES) can be integrated
into hybrid power plants (HPPs) to improve generation performance and system flexibility.
While AWES are typically studied in isolation, their potential role within hybrid systems
combining solar, wind, and battery storage has not been widely explored. This research
addresses that gap by modeling hybrid configurations that include AWES and evaluating
their performance across multiple European locations.
Using the HyDesign modeling tool, this study compares technology combinations such as
AWES-only, AWES + Wind, and AWES + Solar, and evaluates their output in three distinct
European locations. AWES were manually integrated into HyDesign via a validated static
power curve, with operation fixed at 200 meters due to wind data limitations. In addition
to location-based testing, sensitivity analyses were conducted to assess the impact of higher-
altitude operation and adjusted AWES power curves on system performance. A financial
assessment was also performed to examine how cost assumptions influence competitiveness,
including scenarios with reduced capital expenditure (CAPEX) and the addition of battery
storage.
Results show that AWES + Solar configurations offer the most complementary perfor-
mance, particularly in low-resource environments. AWES contribute more during non-solar
hours, while solar generation fills daytime gaps. Although AWES remained less competitive
than conventional wind turbines under current cost assumptions, cost parity was achiev-
able with reduced CAPEX. Battery storage enabled more flexible energy dispatch but had
limited effect on profitability. Overall, the findings indicate that AWES can improve the
performance and reliability of hybrid power plants when deployed in suitable environments
and paired with complementary technologies.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Context
As the world tries to reduce carbon emissions, wind and solar energy are playing an in-
creasingly central role in electricity generation. In recent years, both have advanced rapidly,
especially as costs continue to fall and as they become more commonly used around the
world. However, despite offering clean and abundant energy, these sources are heavily de-
pendent on weather conditions, with solar energy only available during the day and affected
by cloud cover, and wind energy varying significantly depending on wind speeds. These
resources are also highly location-dependent, with certain regions exhibiting stronger solar
or wind profiles than others [1]. These fluctuations make it harder to ensure a consistent
energy supply, especially as renewable energy begins to make up a larger share of the energy
mix.
To help with this problem, hybrid power plants (HPPs) have emerged as a promising
solution. These systems combine different energy sources, such as solar, wind, and batteries,
to balance out their strengths and weaknesses. Combining these technologies at a single site
can reduce overall costs by sharing infrastructure and simplifying grid interconnection [2].
Figure 1: Illustration of multiple airborne wind energy systems (AWES) [3].
A newer and relatively underexplored technology in this context is Airborne Wind En-
ergy (AWE). Unlike conventional wind turbines that rely on fixed towers, AWE systems
use tethered aircraft or kites to access higher-altitude wind resources, where conditions are
generally stronger and more stable [4]. Their operational flexibility, modular design, and
reduced material requirements make them well-suited for remote or temporary applications
where conventional infrastructure may be impractical.
Despite its potential, AWE’s role within hybrid power plants remains unclear. Many
Page 1 of 68
==PAGE:7==
studies look at AWE independently or compare it to traditional wind turbines, but few have
examined its integration with other renewable technologies in a real hybrid setup. Addi-
tionally, existing modeling tools do not adequately support airborne-specific factors, such as
variable flight altitudes, complicating the evaluation of AWES in hybrid configurations.
This thesis investigates how integrating AWES with solar, wind, and storage technolo-
gies can enhance the flexibility, reliability, and cost-effectiveness of hybrid energy systems.
1.2
Research Objectives and Questions
This study evaluates the integration of AWES into hybrid power plants and what kind
of technical and economic value they might bring. Compared to conventional wind turbines,
AWES can access stronger, high-altitude winds and require fewer materials, which may lead
to lower construction costs. These features suggest AWES could be a good fit for hybrid
energy systems, but there has been limited research into how well they actually work in that
context.
Moreover, this study explores how AWES could be combined with other renewable
energy sources, mainly solar, conventional wind, and battery storage, to enhance system
performance and reduce energy costs. It uses scenario-based modeling to test how different
setups perform under a range of environmental and operating conditions. It also looks at
whether AWES could help balance generation, reduce storage capacity requirements, and
make the system more flexible overall.
To guide the work, the following research questions are asked:
• How can AWES be effectively integrated into a hybrid power plant, and how well does
it complement established technologies such as wind and solar?
• How does the performance of a hybrid system vary across different geographic and
environmental contexts?
• In what ways can AWES performance improve by expanding its technical limits, such
as flying at higher altitudes or modifying key design parameters?
• What cost conditions would make AWES more competitive than conventional wind
turbines?
• How does the addition of storage influence the overall efficiency and economic value of
a hybrid system?

2
Problem analysis
2.1
Literature Review
Hybrid power plants (HPPs) that integrate renewable energy sources have attracted
growing attention in recent years, driven by global efforts to decarbonize the electricity
sector while maintaining reliability and affordability.
Within this space, Airborne Wind
Energy Systems (AWES) are gaining interest as a newer technology that could complement
more established renewable sources. This section reviews recent research on key technologies
relevant to hybrid power plants, including conventional wind turbines, AWES, solar PV,
battery storage, and market integration. It also points out the main gaps in current research
and explains why this study is relevant.
2.1.1
Hybrid Power Plants (HPP)
Hybrid power plants (HPPs) combine different energy sources such as wind, solar, diesel
generators, and battery storage to improve energy production and reliability. By integrating
multiple sources, hybrid systems can reduce the impact of weather changes, lower costs,
and improve grid stability [1, 2]. Some studies suggest that hybrid systems help lower the
Levelized Cost of Energy (LCoE) by making better use of available resources and reducing
the need for backup power [5].
Figure 2: Visual representation of a hybrid power plant integrating solar PV and wind
turbines [6].
The key idea behind hybrid power plants is to use different energy sources that comple-
ment each other to provide a more stable electricity supply. Solar power is strongest during
the middle of the day, while wind power often peaks in the evening and at night [5]. The
Page 4 of 68
==PAGE:10==
complementary generation profiles of wind and solar – typically producing power at different
times of the day – allow these systems to reduce the need for extensive storage capacity or
backup generators. When one source produces less energy, the other can often compensate,
contributing to a more balanced and resilient energy supply [5].
Hybrid power plants also benefit from seasonal differences in energy production. Wind
power is usually stronger in winter, while solar power is more available in summer. At night,
wind speeds at higher altitudes (200-1000 meters) often increase due to the formation of low-
level jets and greater wind shear, offering more stable wind resources for airborne systems.
During the day, the wind is more evenly spread out from the ground to higher altitudes
because of stronger air mixing near the surface [4]. These differences enhance the stability
of hybrid systems by mitigating the risk of energy shortfalls.
Battery storage plays a critical role by storing excess energy when production is high
and releasing it when demand is greater than supply [7]. This helps balance fluctuations and
ensures continuous power delivery, especially in systems with high shares of variable renew-
able energy. Research also shows that adding storage helps hybrid plants respond better to
market changes and increase profits [8].
Economic studies also show that HPPs can reduce energy costs compared to wind-only
or solar-only systems [2]. Optimization tools such as HyDesign are employed to determine
the most cost-effective combination of wind, solar, and battery storage resources [2].
Figure 3: Illustration of the seasonal complementarity between solar and wind energy re-
sources [9].
Despite their benefits, hybrid power plants face several challenges. Storing energy adds
to costs, making it essential to find the right balance between generation and storage. The
unpredictable nature of wind and solar generation makes it difficult to forecast supply and
match it with demand or market prices. Managing these fluctuations requires advanced
control systems and forecasting tools [10].
Page 5 of 68
==PAGE:11==
Research continues to improve the design and operation of hybrid power plants. New
forecasting models and optimization tools help operators better plan energy production. As
energy markets evolve, hybrid plants are expected to play a more prominent role in the
power system by providing a more stable and cost-effective supply of renewable energy.
2.1.2
Airborne Wind Energy Systems (AWES)
Airborne Wind Energy (AWE) refers to the generation of electricity using tethered
flying systems that capture high-altitude wind resources, where wind conditions are typically
stronger and more consistent than near ground level. There are two main types: Ground-
Gen systems, which pull a tether to drive a ground-based generator, and Fly-Gen systems,
which generate electricity onboard and transmit it to the ground via power cables in the
tether [11].
Figure 4: Examples of Airborne Wind Energy Systems (AWES) prototypes, including rigid-
wing, soft-wing, Fly-Gen, and Ground-Gen concepts. Source: Joshi et al. [12].
A key principle behind AWES is crosswind flight, first described by Loyd [13]. In this
mode, the kite flies across the wind rather than directly into it, increasing its speed through
the air and generating stronger aerodynamic forces. This results in significantly higher power
output compared to stationary flight, and most modern AWES designs rely on this principle
to improve efficiency.
The operation of a Ground-Gen AWES consists of two alternating phases. During the
reel-out phase, the system flies in a controlled crosswind pattern, pulling on the tether and
generating power as the cable unwinds from a drum connected to a generator. Once the
tether reaches its maximum length, the system enters the reel-in phase, adjusting its aerody-
namic profile to reduce tension, allowing the tether to be reeled back in with minimal energy
Page 6 of 68
==PAGE:12==
use. This cycle then repeats to maintain continuous power generation [12].
Fly-Gen systems operate differently by continuously producing electricity using onboard
generators and transmitting it to the ground through power cables in the tether, eliminating
the need for a reel-in phase. While this avoids energy losses from the retraction phase, it in-
troduces challenges related to airborne power transmission and increased system complexity
[11].
Figure 5: Working principle of a Ground-Gen AWES during the reel-out (power generation)
and reel-in (recovery) phases. Source: Joshi et al. [12].
Various companies and research groups have tested AWES prototypes to evaluate their
performance. Companies such as Kitepower and SkySails Power have demonstrated success-
ful power generation using repetitive crosswind flight patterns [12]. Studies on system scaling
highlight that larger AWES can deliver higher power, but also face increased aerodynamic
and structural challenges [14].
While AWES operate in the air, they still require surface area on the ground to ensure
safe flight and avoid interference with neighboring systems. Conservative estimates assume
spacing equal to the maximum tether length to prevent collisions and maintain stability [15].
Although land use is lower than for conventional wind turbines, these requirements can affect
system layout and scalability. Some studies suggest that coordinated control strategies could
reduce the spacing needed between units [15].
To improve AWES performance, researchers use modeling tools such as AWEbox [16].
These tools simulate flight paths and operational strategies to maximize power output. Other
models, like the multi-point kite model, represent the AWES as multiple connected mass
points to simulate structural flexibility and aerodynamic behavior [17]. These simulations
help refine system design and control strategies.
Environmental studies suggest that AWES could have a lower material footprint than
conventional wind turbines due to fewer structural components. Life-cycle assessments in-
Page 7 of 68
==PAGE:13==
dicate that these systems may offer advantages in sustainability [15]. Additionally, AWES
could have fewer visual and ground-level land-use impacts compared to conventional wind
farms, as they eliminate the need for tall towers and require only compact ground stations,
improving public acceptance [18].
A key challenge for AWES is optimizing the altitude at which they operate.
Wind
resource studies show that wind speeds generally increase with height, but tether length
and system control limit how high AWES can fly [4]. Accurate high-altitude wind data is
essential for optimizing AWES performance and ensuring consistent power generation.
2.1.3
Conventional Wind Turbines in Hybrid Systems
Conventional wind turbines are a key part of many hybrid power systems, often com-
bined with solar photovoltaic (PV) and battery storage to improve energy reliability and
reduce dependence on fossil fuels. Wind energy provides a stable source of electricity, partic-
ularly in regions where wind speeds are strong and consistent. Unlike solar power, which is
limited to daylight hours, wind turbines can generate electricity both day and night, making
them complementary to solar in a hybrid setup.
Studies have explored different ways to improve the performance of wind turbines in
hybrid systems. One approach is optimizing turbine design, including rotor diameter, blade
aerodynamics, and tower height, to maximize energy capture and reduce costs. Wake in-
teractions within wind farms also affect performance, as turbulence from one turbine can
reduce the efficiency of others. Research shows that coordinated control strategies, such as
yaw-based wake steering, can mitigate these effects[19].
While wind turbines provide reliable energy, they are limited by their fixed hub heights,
which restrict access to stronger and more stable high-altitude winds. Wind speed increases
with altitude due to lower surface friction, a phenomenon known as vertical wind shear[4].
However, conventional wind turbines cannot take full advantage of these high-altitude winds
since their towers are limited by structural constraints and cost considerations. This limi-
tation has led to interest in alternative technologies, such as airborne wind energy systems
(AWES), which are capable of operating at variable altitudes.
Comparative simulations
show that AWES can outperform traditional turbines in specific scenarios by adjusting their
operating altitude to capture stronger high-altitude winds, offering a key advantage in envi-
ronments with strong vertical wind shear[21].
Despite these differences, conventional wind turbines remain a mature and widely de-
ployed technology in hybrid power systems.
They are reliable, well-tested, and already
integrated into most energy markets, making them a trusted piece of hybrid system design.
Ongoing research continues to improve their efficiency, reliability, and compatibility with
other energy sources in hybrid configurations.

2.1.4
Solar Energy in Hybrid Systems
Solar photovoltaic (PV) systems play a key role in hybrid power plants by providing
electricity during the day, when demand is often high. Wind and solar power typically gen-
erate at different times, which helps smooth out the overall energy output and improves
system reliability [5].
The effectiveness of solar PV in hybrid systems depends on geographic location and sea-
sonal variations. Southern Europe receives higher solar irradiation, making PV a dominant
source of energy in these regions, while northern Europe benefits more from wind energy
due to stronger and more consistent wind speeds. The overall design of a hybrid system
considers these factors to optimize energy output and cost-effectiveness.
The integration of solar PV into hybrid power plants also brings economic benefits. Ad-
Page 9 of 68
==PAGE:15==
Figure 7: Global horizontal irradiation map of Europe, showing average solar resource avail-
ability [22].
vances in PV technology have made solar increasingly cost-effective, though this is not always
captured in operational studies. When combined with wind and storage, hybrid systems can
participate more effectively in energy markets and reduce their exposure to electricity price
fluctuations. Accurate forecasting and market-oriented optimization are essential for man-
aging hybrid systems and maximizing profitability [8].
Research continues to focus on improving forecasting models, control strategies, and
energy storage solutions to enhance the performance of solar PV in hybrid systems. With
ongoing advancements, hybrid power plants integrating solar PV, wind, and storage are
expected to play an increasingly significant role in future energy systems.
2.1.5
Battery Energy Storage Systems
Battery storage systems play a critical role in hybrid power plants by addressing the
mismatch between electricity generation and consumption.
Since solar and wind energy
production varies throughout the day and across seasons, batteries help store excess power
when generation exceeds demand and supply it when needed. This improves system stability,
Page 10 of 68
==PAGE:16==
reduces reliance on backup generators, and enhances self-consumption of renewable energy
[7].
Lithium-ion batteries are the most widely adopted storage solution in hybrid systems
due to their high energy density, efficiency, and significant cost reductions in recent years.
However, one of the main challenges is that batteries wear out over time. Their lifespan
depends on how often they are charged and discharged, how deep those cycles are, and the
temperature they operate in. To reduce lifecycle costs, researchers have developed battery
sizing strategies aimed at enhancing efficiency and prolonging lifespan by minimizing the
frequency of replacements [23].
Figure 8: Li-ion battery degradation process over 500 cycles at a 0.9◦C charge-discharge rate
[24].
Beyond lithium-ion technology, ongoing research explores alternative storage options
such as flow batteries and hybrid storage solutions. While lithium-ion remains the dominant
choice, flow batteries offer advantages in long-duration storage applications, where energy
needs to be stored for extended periods. Integrating battery storage into hybrid power plants
Page 11 of 68
==PAGE:17==
requires careful planning to balance costs, efficiency, and lifetime performance. Advanced
control strategies are being designed to optimize battery utilization in response to electricity
pricing, renewable energy forecasts, and system load conditions.
2.1.6
Market Integration of Hybrid Power Plants
As renewable energy penetration increases, hybrid power plants are becoming more
active in electricity markets by integrating generation, storage, and grid services. Due to their
ability to provide both stability and flexibility, these systems play a crucial role in managing
supply and demand, particularly in markets with a high share of variable renewable energy.
Hybrid plants can contribute to frequency regulation, peak demand support, and reserve
capacity, helping to stabilize the grid while generating revenue [8]. Their market participation
strategies depend on factors such as energy storage capacity, forecasting accuracy, and real-
time dispatch optimization.
Figure 9: Global weighted average of levelized costs of energy (LCoE) from various sources,
showing a significant decline in renewable energy costs between 2010 and 2019 [25].
The participation of hybrid plants in electricity markets involves multiple revenue streams.
These include selling electricity in spot markets, offering balancing services to grid operators,
and securing long-term power purchase agreements (PPAs). Spot market participation allows
Page 12 of 68
==PAGE:18==
hybrid plants to take advantage of price fluctuations by storing excess energy during periods
of low prices and discharging it when demand is high. However, increased penetration of re-
newable energy has led to periods of oversupply, driving down average market prices in what
is known as the "value drop" effect [10]. This phenomenon presents challenges for hybrid op-
erators, who must optimize bidding strategies and storage dispatch to maximize profitability.
PPAs are commonly used to mitigate revenue uncertainty by providing a fixed price
for electricity over a set period. These agreements are particularly relevant for hybrid sys-
tems that combine renewable energy with storage, as they ensure predictable revenues while
maintaining operational flexibility. Capacity markets and ancillary services offer additional
revenue opportunities, as hybrid plants can provide reserve power and grid support functions
that enhance system stability.
The integration of airborne wind energy systems (AWES) into hybrid plants introduces
new market opportunities, but also operational complexities. The capability of AWES to dy-
namically adjust their altitude to access stronger winds potentially allows them to optimize
power generation in response to real-time market conditions. This flexibility could provide
an advantage in responding to electricity price fluctuations, but it also requires advanced
forecasting and dispatch planning to ensure efficient market participation. However, chal-
lenges related to grid integration, such as regulatory compliance and power output stability,
must be addressed before hybrid systems with AWES can fully participate in electricity
markets.
2.2
Research Gaps and Relevance of this Study
The following points highlight critical research gaps and the specific contributions this
thesis makes in response. The gaps primarily relate to hybrid power plant (HPP) optimiza-
tion, high-altitude wind data uncertainty, AWES performance compared to conventional
wind turbines, and economic considerations.
2.2.1
Challenges in Hybrid Power Plant Optimization
Most existing research on hybrid power plants has concentrated on combinations of solar
photovoltaic (PV), conventional wind turbines, and battery storage. As a result, airborne
wind energy systems (AWES) have received little attention in hybrid optimization studies,
and are not currently supported by widely used tools such as HyDesign [2]. Most existing
models are built around fixed-height wind turbines, which limits their ability to account for
the altitude range that AWES can operate within. This raises open questions regarding the
optimal operation of AWES within a hybrid configuration. In addition, the economic role of
AWES in hybrid power plants remains poorly understood, as most cost assessments focus on
stand-alone AWES deployments. There is a lack of integrated studies that evaluate AWES
in combination with other technologies, such as batteries or grid infrastructure, within a
multi-technology system [26].
Page 13 of 68
==PAGE:19==
2.2.2
High-Altitude Wind Data Uncertainty
A major challenge in integrating AWES into hybrid systems is the limited availability
of accurate wind data above 200 meters. Traditional wind assessments often rely on ground-
based measurements and reanalysis datasets, which do not provide detailed, site-specific
information at higher altitudes [4]. This makes it harder to model and optimize AWES
performance, particularly in hybrid systems incorporating solar and battery technologies.
Recent research on wind shear and directional changes with height offers new ways to esti-
mate and predict wind behavior more accurately, helping to improve the control strategies
needed for efficient high-altitude operation [27].
2.2.3
AWES vs. Conventional Wind in Hybrid Systems
While AWES has been proposed as an alternative to conventional wind turbines, there
is limited comparative research on their relative benefits within hybrid power plants. The
advantages of AWES, such as higher energy density at altitude and lower material costs,
must be weighed against potential operational challenges like tether dynamics and system
reliability [12]. Existing models do not clearly define where and when AWES provides the
most benefit compared to traditional wind turbines. Further research is needed to identify the
optimal conditions under which AWES can be deployed in hybrid systems, and to evaluate
whether they are best suited to complement or replace conventional wind turbines [21].
2.2.4
Market and Economic Considerations
The economic case for AWES within hybrid power plants remains poorly defined. While
economic analyses of LCoE, CAPEX, and OPEX are well established for conventional wind
and solar, the cost structure of AWES in hybrid configurations remains underexplored [26].
Additionally, there is uncertainty about how AWES flexibility influences energy market par-
ticipation, particularly in balancing and capacity markets. Further investigation is needed
to assess whether AWES can help reduce curtailment and storage requirements by adjusting
their operating height to better match changing grid needs [8].

3
Methodology
This chapter describes the approach taken to evaluate how well a hybrid power plant
(HPP) performs when it includes airborne wind energy systems (AWES) alongside solar PV,
conventional wind turbines, and battery storage. The analysis is built around the open-
source modeling tool HyDesign [2], which is designed to help optimize system design and
operation with the goal of lowering costs and boosting overall efficiency.
The focus of the study is on understanding how these technologies work together, how
AWES complements or competes with other components, how energy is dispatched across
the system, and whether the configuration is economically viable. Several system configura-
tions are explored, including setups where AWES operates on its own, alongside conventional
wind turbines, or in combination with solar power. The analysis also looks at how well these
setups perform in different parts of Europe, taking into account local wind conditions and
resource availability. Finally, battery storage is included to see how it can help smooth out
power delivery and improve financial outcomes.
The methodology follows a structured workflow:
• Wind data collection
• Hybrid power plant modeling
• Airborne wind energy system modeling
• Cost and financial modeling
• Simulation Framework and Performance Evaluation
Each step is detailed in the following sections, describing data sources, modeling as-
sumptions, and performance evaluation criteria.
3.1
Wind Data Collection and Processing
The wind resource assessment in this study is based on ERA5 reanalysis data, provided
by the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) [4]. ERA5 is a
global climate dataset that assimilates ground-based and satellite observations into a numer-
ical weather model, offering consistent long-term meteorological data. The dataset provides
hourly wind speed and direction values at multiple altitudes, with a spatial resolution of
approximately 30 km.
For this study, ERA5 wind speed data were extracted for the year 2012, covering a full
annual cycle to capture seasonal variations. The dataset provides wind speeds at different
heights up to 200 meters, which aligns with the hub heights of conventional wind turbines.
Airborne wind energy systems (AWES) are capable of operating at higher altitudes where
Page 15 of 68
==PAGE:21==
wind speeds are typically stronger and more stable [4], however this study was limited to
data available up to 200 meters. Therefore, all wind-dependent components in this study
use the 200-meter wind speed dataset.
The ERA5 dataset includes wind speed and direction, enabling the derivation of power
curves for conventional wind turbines. However, ERA5 does not include power curve data
for AWES, which required an external source. To estimate AWES power output, a specific
power curve from Airborne Wind Europe [28] was used. This power curve defines the system’s
performance across different wind speeds, allowing for a realistic representation of its power
generation potential, as shown in Figure 10. Although the exact reference altitude at which
this power curve was measured is not disclosed in the source materials, the system can
operate at altitudes up to 750 meters. For consistency with available ERA5 data, the power
curve was adapted to wind speeds at 200 meters, as this represents the operational range for
the system under the constraints of this study.
Figure 10: SkySails Power Curve, verified by Windtest Grevenbroich GmbH. The plot shows
the power output as a function of wind speed [28].
Wind data were collected from multiple locations across Europe to analyze regional
differences in wind availability. The dataset’s hourly temporal resolution ensures that short-
term fluctuations are accurately captured, allowing for a realistic assessment of wind energy
intermittency.
3.2
Hybrid Power Plant Modeling
To model the hybrid power plant in this study, the open-source tool HyDesign [2] was
used. HyDesign is a simulation and optimization framework designed for hybrid power plants
Page 16 of 68
==PAGE:22==
that integrate multiple renewable energy sources within a shared grid connection. It enables
structured evaluation of system configurations by balancing technical performance, dispatch
strategies, and operational constraints. Its primary focus is on optimizing system sizing
while ensuring efficient energy management over the plant’s lifetime.
3.2.1
Optimization Framework
HyDesign employs a nested optimization framework that refines both the plant’s de-
sign and operational strategy. The outer optimization determines the best combination of
components by iteratively adjusting key design parameters such as wind turbine size, PV
capacity, and battery storage. Within each iteration, the inner optimization evaluates short-
term dispatch strategies, considering factors such as market conditions, grid constraints, and
battery operation to ensure stable system performance.
3.2.2
System Components and AWES Integration
HyDesign models the interaction of multiple renewable technologies, allowing for a com-
prehensive analysis of hybrid configurations. The core energy sources modeled include:
• Wind turbines: Conventional horizontal-axis wind turbines operating at configurable
hub heights, with power output determined using site-specific wind resource data. The
power curve for the conventional wind turbine in this study was generated using a
built-in lookup table provided by HyDesign. This table contains precomputed turbine
performance data across a range of wind speeds and specific power values. Based on
the defined turbine parameters – namely rotor diameter, rated power, and hub height –
the model computes the specific power and interpolates the corresponding power curve
from the lookup data.
• Solar PV: Fixed-tilt photovoltaic panels with configurable DC/AC ratios, modeled
using irradiance and temperature inputs.
• Battery storage: Lithium-ion battery systems designed for load shifting, peak shav-
ing, and revenue optimization, with degradation effects accounted for over time.
Airborne wind energy systems (AWES) were not originally included in HyDesign’s stan-
dard configuration. For this study, AWES was manually integrated using an external power
curve, operating at a fixed altitude of 200 meters.
This allows for a direct comparison
between AWES and conventional wind turbines within the hybrid power plant model.
3.2.3
Energy Management and Degradation Modeling
HyDesign incorporates energy management strategies to determine the most effective
dispatch of available power sources. The optimization framework ensures compliance with
grid constraints while maximizing renewable energy utilization and minimizing battery wear.
Short-term dispatch strategies involve balancing generation, storage, and grid interactions,

==PAGE:23==

Friis-Møller et al. [2].
while long-term adjustments account for operational uncertainties and component aging.
In addition, degradation modeling accounts for performance losses over time. Wind
turbines experience gradual efficiency reductions due to blade erosion and mechanical wear,
while solar PV degradation follows industry-standard linear loss rates. Battery degradation
is modeled using cycle counting and state-of-charge history, ensuring realistic lifetime pro-
jections.
For AWES, long-term degradation could not be modeled due to the limited availability
of historical performance data. However, the design philosophy behind AWES assumes more
frequent replacement of key components like kites and tethers. Unlike conventional systems
that rely on large, rigid structures with long service lives, AWES components are generally
smaller and more modular. This means they can be swapped out or upgraded more easily,
which could help balance out the uncertainty around long-term reliability and might even
offer some operational flexibility over time [15, 26].
3.2.4
Scenario Testing and Sensitivity Analysis
HyDesign enables scenario testing to evaluate system performance under varying con-
ditions. Simulations compare different plant configurations, including AWES integration,
conventional wind turbines, solar PV, and varying storage capacities. Sensitivity tests assess
the impact of grid constraints, variable market conditions, and resource availability. A full

discussion of the tested scenarios and results is provided in Chapter 4.
3.3
Airborne Wind Energy System Modeling
To understand how airborne wind energy systems (AWES) operate, we initially explored
the open-source simulation tool AWEbox [16]. This tool provided an initial understanding
of AWES power generation via crosswind flight and tether dynamics, offering detailed mod-
eling of aircraft dynamics, tether forces, and flight optimization strategies. However, due to
time constraints and the complexity of tuning the model, AWEbox was not used for final
simulations. Instead, we used a predefined AWES power curve published by Airborne Wind
Europe [28] (Figure 10), as referenced in Section 3.1.
AWES operation is based on crosswind flight cycles, which involve alternating tether
reel-out and reel-in phases. However, due to the complexity of AWES dynamics, such as
continuously changing flight angles and tether control (described in Section 2.1.2), these
behaviors were not modeled in this study. Instead, a simplified approach was used, relying
on the static power curve representation. The power generated by the system is largely
dependent on wind speed at different altitudes. Although AWES typically operate at higher
altitudes than conventional wind turbines, this study was limited to a 200-meter height due
to constraints in the ERA5 dataset.
The hybrid power plant model used in this study was developed with HyDesign, which
does not natively support airborne wind energy systems (AWES). While conventional wind
turbines in HyDesign rely on predefined power curves, AWES typically generates power dy-
namically based on real-time flight conditions, making direct integration more complex. To
include AWES, we manually incorporated power output data from the external power curve.
Therefore, in this setup, AWES was effectively treated like a conventional wind source,
using a static power curve and fixed-height wind data. The model did not simulate the flight
behavior or tether dynamics of the system, focusing instead on its energy output over time.
Moreover, as mentioned in Section 2.1.2, each AWES requires a certain amount of
surface area to operate safely, typically defined by the maximum tether length [15]. For
the 200-meter tether constraint applied in this study, this corresponds to a circular ground
footprint of approximately 0.126 km2 per unit. However, potential limitations in available
land area were not included as a constraint in this modeling setup.
3.4
Cost and Financial Modeling
To evaluate the economic feasibility of various hybrid power plant (HPP) configurations,
we used HyDesign. This tool integrates both capital expenditure (CAPEX) and operational
expenditure (OPEX) to optimize system design.
The cost model in HyDesign identifies
cost-efficient configurations by calculating key financial metrics such as the Levelized Cost
Page 19 of 68
==PAGE:25==
of Energy (LCoE), Net Present Value (NPV), and Internal Rate of Return (IRR). Addition-
ally, it factors in battery degradation and sensitivity to market fluctuations, such as energy
price changes [2], providing a comprehensive analysis of both initial investment costs and
long-term financial viability.
Although AWES were not initially included in the model, they were later incorporated
to assess their economic potential within hybrid systems. This analysis is detailed in Sec-
tions 4.5 and 4.6.
The key financial aspects considered include:
• CAPEX: Initial investment for system components (wind turbines, solar panels, bat-
teries).
• OPEX: Ongoing costs for operation, maintenance, and staff.
• LCoE: Average cost of electricity over the plant’s lifetime.
• NPV / IRR: Financial metrics assessing profitability and return on investment.
• Battery Degradation: Performance decline over time, impacting long-term costs.
3.5
Simulation Framework and Performance Evaluation
The hybrid power plant was evaluated through scenario-based simulations covering dif-
ferent system configurations, geographical locations, and modeling assumptions. These sim-
ulations aimed to understand how AWES can be effectively integrated into hybrid energy
systems, and how design choices influence technical and economic performance. A full dis-
cussion of the tested scenarios and results is presented in Chapter 4.
The simulation framework was designed to capture real-world dynamics by evaluating:
• Technology configurations: AWES alone, AWES + Wind Turbines, and AWES +
Solar PV.
• Location-based performance: Simulations were conducted for three representative
regions – Germany, France, and Denmark – each with distinct wind and solar resource
profiles.
• High-altitude and design enhancements: Sensitivity tests explored how AWES
performance changes when operating at higher altitudes or using modified power
curves.
• Financial viability: The simulations incorporated CAPEX, OPEX, and electricity
price data to evaluate the levelized cost of electricity and system profitability under
various assumptions.
Page 20 of 68
==PAGE:26==
• Role of battery storage: The effect of adding battery energy storage was analyzed
to understand its impact on power reliability and economic returns.
Time-series simulations were performed with hourly resolution to capture the opera-
tional dynamics of the hybrid power plant under seasonal variability, location-specific re-
source profiles, and fluctuating electricity prices. The following key performance indicators
(KPIs) were used to evaluate both technical and financial aspects of system performance
across the main scenarios:
• Annual Energy Production (AEP): Total yearly energy output from each config-
uration, used to compare system-level energy yield.
• Capacity Factor (AWES): Ratio of actual energy output to rated capacity, used to
assess AWES performance across locations.
• Power Source Contributions: The relative share of total generation provided by

periods of system inactivity or underperformance.
• Percentage of High-Output Hours: Share of time when the hybrid plant produced

• Revenue from Spot Market Sales: Total annual earnings based on hourly electric-

• Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCoE): Average cost per MWh produced, incorpo-

• Net Present Value (NPV): Present value of the system’s expected future cash flows.
• Internal Rate of Return (IRR): Discount rate at which the project breaks even
financially.
Additional scenario-specific evaluations were conducted to explore the break-even PPA

impact of modifying AWES cut-in behavior and operational altitude. These are presented

Summary of Findings
In summary, Section 4.1 compared three different technology setups at a location with
relatively weak wind and solar resources. AWES on their own showed frequent power drops
and could not provide a reliable supply without storage. Adding conventional wind helped
slightly, but since both technologies used similar wind resources at similar heights, the bene-

the day, the two technologies form a well-balanced hybrid system.

4.3
Performance Benefits of High-Altitude Operation for AWES
The results presented in Section 4.1.2 showed that airborne wind energy systems (AWES)
and conventional wind turbines exhibit similar power generation patterns when deployed
at similar altitudes. However, a key limitation of this analysis is the altitude constraint
imposed by the dataset, which only provides wind speed data up to 200 meters. While con-
ventional wind turbines are generally restricted to hub heights below this threshold, AWES
are designed to operate at higher altitudes. Studies indicate that AWES typically target
operational heights above 100 meters and up to 1000 meters or more, a range often referred
to as the mid-altitude regime [14]. At these heights, AWES are expected to harness stronger
and more stable winds, potentially improving their capacity factor and overall energy yield.
One of the main advantages of AWES over conventional wind turbines is their ability
to dynamically adjust altitude in response to changing wind conditions. Unlike traditional
Page 41 of 68
==PAGE:47==
wind turbines, which are fixed at a specific hub height, AWES can optimize power generation
by continuously seeking the most favorable wind speeds available within their operational
range. This altitude flexibility is particularly valuable in locations where wind shear effects
cause significant variations in wind speed with height.
4.3.1
Initial Performance Gains from Extrapolated Wind Speeds
To better understand how AWES might perform at higher altitudes, we carry out a
simplified analysis by estimating wind speeds at 500 meters. This should be seen as an
exploratory test case, aimed at understanding under what conditions AWES could benefit
from stronger winds at higher elevations. We use the same dataset from Germany that was
used in Section 4.1.2, which originally provided wind speeds only up to 200 meters. While
this extrapolation gives a useful first estimate, it should be treated with caution. As altitude
increases, wind becomes harder to model accurately due to factors such as lower air density
and transient events that are more frequent near the top of the atmosphere where AWES
typically operate [29]. These effects introduce uncertainties that simple extrapolation meth-
ods do not fully account for.
To estimate wind speeds at 500 meters, we use the power-law wind profile, which relates
wind speed to height using a wind shear exponent (α). Rather than relying on site-specific
measurements, we use a representative value based on regional observations reported in pre-
vious studies. These studies show that α commonly ranges between 0.3 and 0.5, depending
on wind speed, terrain, and atmospheric stability [30].
In particular, shear tends to in-
crease above 200 meters because AWES fly near the top of the atmospheric boundary layer
(ABL), where stable layers like the capping inversion are common and cause sharp wind
changes [30, 27]. Moreover, at heights above 100 meters, higher wind speeds are typically
associated with stronger shear, especially when the atmosphere is stable [30, 27]. Based on
these findings, we adopt a fixed value of α = 0.4 as a conservative and realistic estimate for
inland sites. While this simplifies the variation in α, it captures the average behavior ob-
served in multiple high-altitude measurement campaigns and provides a practical approach
for extrapolating wind speeds in the absence of local profiling data.

conditions. The current AWES power curve used in our simulations – based on data from
Airborne Wind Europe (Figure 12) – features a higher cut-in speed than conventional wind
turbines, limiting its ability to capture energy under mild wind conditions.
This performance gap can be partly attributed to the fact that AWES have not been
developed to the same extent as conventional wind turbines, which have benefited from
decades of intensive research, optimization, and large-scale deployment. As a result, there
is significant room for improvement in AWES design, particularly in areas such as aerody-
namics, flight control, and system responsiveness.

improves to the point where it can generate power at the same wind speeds as conventional

performance. Figure 28 compares the original AWES power curve (dashed red) with the

approximately 5 m/s to ∼3 m/s, matching that of the conventional wind turbine.
This
shift enables AWES to begin generating power at lower wind speeds, increasing its potential

4.5
Financial Assessment of Airborne Wind Integration in Hybrid
Plants
This section evaluates the economic performance of standalone AWES compared to
conventional wind turbines under realistic cost assumptions. Although the broader study
focuses on hybrid configurations, the financial analysis isolates 60 MW AWES-only systems
and wind-only systems to allow a fair, controlled comparison. The simulations are conducted
for the location in Denmark, selected based on the results of Section 4.2.3, where it was iden-
tified as the most promising location for AWES deployment, excluding the solar component
included in earlier hybrid configurations.
To structure this analysis, technology-specific cost assumptions are derived from the
HyDesign tool, which provides capital expenditure (CAPEX) and operational expenditure
(OPEX) parameters for conventional wind, solar, and battery systems. Since no standard-
ized cost references currently exist for airborne wind energy systems (AWES), we initially
assume that their cost structure mirrors that of conventional wind turbines. However, this
Page 51 of 68
==PAGE:57==
is likely a conservative assumption. Several recent studies suggest that AWES could achieve
significantly lower CAPEX, primarily due to the absence of towers and foundations, reduced
material usage, and simpler mechanical components [15, 26, 11]. Because AWES platforms
are designed with repeatable, streamlined components and can be scaled more easily in
production, they present promising opportunities for cost reductions in manufacturing and
deployment. That said, these savings may be offset by higher operational costs, depending
on the system design, particularly if components require more frequent replacement or if the
overall concept involves greater complexity [26]. These factors introduce a cost asymmetry
that should be carefully considered when evaluating the financial viability of AWES.
The following subsections explore two financial scenarios: a baseline case with identical
cost assumptions for AWES and wind, and a case where only CAPEX is reduced, reflecting
optimistic projections about cost reductions, to determine the level at which AWES become
economically competitive with conventional wind. Together, these cases offer a clear per-
spective on the cost benchmarks, at which AWES could become competitive within hybrid
power systems.

4.6
The Role of Battery Storage in Hybrid Power Plants
Battery storage plays a key role in hybrid power plants by mitigating renewable vari-
ability, reducing curtailment, and enabling energy arbitrage. By storing surplus energy and
releasing it during high-price periods, storage systems can improve revenue stability and
support more efficient grid interaction.
This section evaluates the economic impact of integrating battery storage into the AWES
+ Solar hybrid power plant configuration selected in Section 4.2.3. The baseline cost assump-
tions for AWES are taken from the reduced-CAPEX scenario developed in Section 4.5.3.
Three storage configurations are assessed: no storage (0 MW), moderate storage (60 MW),
and large-scale storage (120 MW).
The analysis is structured in two parts. First, a short-term simulation over one repre-
sentative month is used to explore how storage size affects system behavior, battery state-
of-charge (SOC), and daily revenue fluctuations. Then, a long-term economic evaluation
is performed over a 25-year period to assess cumulative revenue, levelized cost of energy
(LCoE), and net present value (NPV). Together, these analyses provide insight into the
trade-offs between added flexibility and increased capital costs when integrating battery
systems into hybrid power plants.

cators, including net present value (NPV), levelized cost of energy (LCoE), and total revenue.
A one-month simulation was performed to capture short-term system dynamics, with
power generation and battery state-of-charge (SOC) tracked across all scenarios. The results

5
Future Work
This thesis set out to explore how Airborne Wind Energy Systems (AWES) could be
integrated into hybrid power plants, alongside technologies like solar PV, conventional wind
turbines, and battery storage. The results showed that AWES can play a meaningful role
in such systems, especially when combined with solar power, thanks to their complementary
generation patterns. However, the work also uncovered several limitations, some of which
became the basis for exploratory tests that revealed where AWES performance could be
improved.
Some of the exploratory studies carried out in this thesis provided valuable insights into
where targeted improvements could make AWES more competitive in hybrid systems. The
high-altitude wind analysis, conducted in Section 4.3, showed that AWES performance im-
proves with increased operational height, particularly when altitude control is used to avoid
extreme wind conditions. This highlights the importance of real-time flight adjustment, sug-
gesting that future work should focus not only on better high-altitude wind data, but also on
modeling strategies that allow AWES to react dynamically to changing conditions. Similarly,
the shifted power curve scenario in Section 4.4 showed that lowering the cut-in speed can
significantly increase energy output, reduce zero-output periods, and improve overall system
utilization. While both studies were based on simplified assumptions, they point toward
specific areas, such as flight control, power electronics, and performance modeling, where
further research could help unlock more of AWES’s potential.
One of the main limitations in this study was the availability of wind data. Because
the ERA5 dataset only provides wind data up to 200 meters, we could not model AWES at
their full operational altitudes, which can exceed 1000 meters in some configurations [14].
As a result, AWES in our simulations were effectively constrained to the same wind resource
zone as conventional wind turbines, limiting their potential advantage. To better capture
the unique capabilities of AWES, future research should use high-resolution datasets or
atmospheric models that extend well beyond 200 meters. Wind behavior at these higher
altitudes is, on average, stronger and more consistent [30], but also exhibits greater short-
term complexity and unpredictability [27, 29], underscoring the need for further study into
high-altitude wind dynamics.
We had initially planned to use AWEbox to simulate the dynamic behavior of AWES
within the hybrid system. AWEbox is specifically designed to model flight trajectories, alti-
tude control, and energy generation in a realistic way [16]. However, due to time constraints
and the additional effort required to tune the model for reliable results, we were not able
to integrate it into our simulations. Instead, we used a verified power curve from Airborne
Wind Europe [28] as a static representation of AWES performance. This meant that in our
model, due to data limitations and the use of a static power curve, AWES in our simulations
operated at a fixed height, without real-time adjustments, despite that being one of its core
advantages. Future work could explore a more advanced integration of AWEbox, allowing
Page 64 of 68
==PAGE:70==
AWES to react to real-time system conditions and better reflect its dynamic capabilities.
On the economic side, we had access to detailed cost data from HyDesign for compo-
nents like solar PV, conventional wind turbines, and battery storage systems [2]. However,
similar data for AWES was not available. Because of this, we relied on general assumptions
to simulate how AWES might perform financially, and specifically looked at how changes in
CAPEX would affect their competitiveness compared to conventional wind turbines. That
said, AWES presents greater complexity in cost modeling. It consists of many individual
components, such as tethers, winches, kites, and control systems, and its operational costs
are structured differently, since some parts may need to be replaced more frequently than in
traditional wind turbines. For this reason, it is especially important to consider AWES not
just in isolation, but as part of a larger hybrid system. Understanding how their costs inter-
act with other components requires more detailed financial data. Future studies could focus
on building a more detailed and realistic financial model for AWES, taking these unique cost
characteristics into account. This would help better assess its long-term viability and how
it stacks up economically within hybrid energy systems.
Furthermore, this study did not take into account any constraints related to available
land area. As mentioned in Section 3.3, conservative estimates assume that each AWES re-
quires a circular ground footprint with a radius equal to its tether length. For the 200-meter
tether used in most of our calculations in Chapter 4, this corresponds to approximately
0.126 km2 per unit, resulting in over 63 km2 for a deployment of 500 AWES units. While
this is a substantial figure, it reflects a highly conservative assumption. Studies suggest that
required spacing could be significantly reduced through coordinated control strategies that
allow multiple AWES to operate in closer proximity without compromising safety [15]. Ad-
ditionally, if future technological improvements lead to higher power output per AWES unit,
fewer systems would be needed to meet the same energy demand, reducing further the overall
spatial footprint. Future work could investigate how to incorporate spatial constraints into
hybrid optimization tools and assess the impact of alternative layout strategies on overall
system performance.
Another area that could be explored is the geographic scope of AWES deployment. In
this study, we focused on just three land-based locations in Europe, but AWES could be
particularly interesting in more remote or temporary settings. Since these systems are rela-
tively easy to set up, take down, and relocate, they could be used in places where electricity
is only needed for a limited time, such as remote construction sites, disaster relief zones, or
island communities. Future studies could evaluate AWES integration with other technologies
beyond solar and wind. For example, hybrid systems that include wave energy, hydro, nu-
clear, or even diesel generators might change the role AWES plays and how complementary
it actually is in different setups.
In summary, while this thesis showed that AWES can be a valuable addition to hybrid
power plants, there are still many areas where more research is needed. Better wind data
at higher altitudes, improved integration of dynamic flight modeling tools like AWEbox,
Page 65 of 68
==PAGE:71==
and more detailed economic analysis would all help build a clearer picture of how AWES
could perform in real-world systems.
The exploratory results in this study also pointed
toward specific improvements, such as altitude control and lower cut-in speeds, that could
significantly boost AWES performance. Exploring new locations and different combinations
of technologies could also reveal use cases where AWES offers unique advantages. It is also
important to note that AWES remains an emerging technology, and many of the current
models and datasets are based on early-stage prototypes or downscaled systems. As a result,
the performance estimates used in this study may not fully capture the potential of AWES
at commercial scale. However, as seen with other renewable technologies, continued research
and development can significantly improve efficiency, reduce costs, and enhance integration,
paving the way for broader adoption in the future. Together, these steps could significantly
improve our understanding of the role AWES could play in future energy systems.

6
Conclusions
This thesis set out to explore how Airborne Wind Energy Systems (AWES) could be
integrated into hybrid power plants (HPPs), alongside solar PV, conventional wind turbines,
and battery storage. The goal was to assess both the technical performance and economic
feasibility of AWES in various hybrid configurations, using a simulation-based approach
supported by the HyDesign modeling framework. The project focused on comparing differ-
ent technology combinations across three European locations, and on evaluating the role of
AWES in improving system performance under real-world constraints.
In the first part of the results, we compared three configurations: standalone AWES,
AWES combined with conventional wind turbines, and AWES combined with solar. Among
these, the AWES + Solar setup emerged as the most balanced. The two technologies com-
plemented each other well across both daily and seasonal timescales, helping to reduce the
reliance on battery storage and leading to a more stable energy output. In contrast, the
combination of AWES and conventional wind turbines did not show much synergy. This
was largely because, in our model, both technologies were drawing from the same ERA5
reanalysis dataset, which did not include wind speed information above 200 meters. Since
AWES is designed to operate much higher, potentially reaching altitudes up to 1000 meters
or more [14], we could not fully capture its performance advantage within this setup.
The second part of the analysis explored how the AWES + Solar configuration per-
formed across three different locations: Germany, France, and Denmark, which had different
environmental conditions. While each location had its own characteristics, the results showed
that AWES tends to perform better in areas with steady wind availability, whereas its added
value is more limited in regions where conventional wind turbines already provide reliable
generation. Since both AWES and conventional wind turbines relied on the same limited
200-meter dataset in our model, they often behaved similarly. In contrast, solar followed a
very different pattern, creating more potential for complementarity. Under these modeling
constraints, pairing AWES with solar made more sense than combining it with conventional
wind turbines, as it helped spread generation across different times and reduce overall vari-
ability.
To get a sense of how AWES might perform under more favorable conditions, a simu-
lation was performed where wind speed data was extrapolated beyond the 200-meter limit.
While this was not based on actual high-altitude measurements, it gave us a rough idea of
the potential benefits. The results showed a clear boost in energy output, especially in places
with strong vertical wind shear. This suggests that if AWES could actually operate at those
higher altitudes, as it is designed to do, it could outperform fixed-height wind turbines in
certain situations. But without accurate wind data above 200 meters, it is hard to model
this with full confidence.
In another simulation, the standard AWES power curve was replaced with an improved
Page 67 of 68
==PAGE:73==
version that could start generating power at lower wind speeds. This was a way to explore
the potential impacts of technological improvements, for example, through better kite de-
sign or smarter control strategies. The results were promising, showing higher overall energy
generation across all locations. The findings suggest that even small technical improvements
could make a noticeable difference, especially in low-wind conditions.
From a financial perspective, this study did not attempt to build a complete economic
model for AWES, as detailed CAPEX and OPEX estimates are still lacking. Instead, we used
a simplified comparison to test how AWES would perform economically if it had the same
CAPEX as wind. In that baseline scenario, AWES generally underperformed due to their
lower energy yield, despite sharing the same cost assumptions as conventional wind turbines.
To explore under what conditions AWES could become more competitive, their CAPEX were
gradually reduced, while keeping all other factors constant. This helped identify the point
at which AWES would match conventional wind turbines in terms of profitability, assuming
there were no improvements in its energy generation. The outcome showed that AWES
would need to be significantly less expensive than wind turbines to become more profitable
under these conditions. While this was only a sensitivity analysis, it highlights the impor-
tance of developing realistic cost models tailored to the unique nature of AWES components,
such as kites, tethers, and ground-based systems, along with their specific operational and
maintenance needs.
The final part of the analysis looked at the role of battery storage in the AWES +
Solar hybrid configuration. Adding a battery generally improved technical performance by
increasing the annual energy production and total revenues, since it allowed more generated
energy to be stored and used when needed. However, this came at a cost. The inclusion
of the battery led to higher total system CAPEX, and over time, the need for battery re-
placements also increased operational costs. As a result, key economic indicators such as
NPV and LCoE actually worsened. This shows that while batteries can enhance system per-
formance from a technical perspective, they do not always make economic sense, especially
when electricity prices are low or when the additional storage capacity is not fully utilized.
Overall, this thesis showed that AWES can be a valuable addition to hybrid power
systems, especially when combined with solar energy. While the modeling was based on a
simplified power curve and a dataset that was limited to 200 meters, the results still high-
lighted how AWES could complement existing technologies, reduce reliance on storage, and
improve generation consistency. Although further work is needed to capture more realistic
system behavior, including access to high-altitude wind data, dynamic simulation tools, and
detailed cost models, this study adds to the growing understanding of where AWES fit within
hybrid energy systems. Rather than aiming to replace conventional wind turbines, AWES
offer an alternative in settings where portability, flexible deployment, or operation at higher
altitudes can enhance system performance.
