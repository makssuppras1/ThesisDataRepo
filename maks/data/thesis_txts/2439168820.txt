Abstract
The use of precast reinforced concrete elements enables fast and eﬃcient development of diﬀerent
types of structures. During construction, the precast elements are connected together in joints, in
such a way that the structural integrity of the structure is assured. The structural response of the
system is determined by the properties and performance of the connections. These constitute an
essential role in the assessment of the load carrying capacity and the deformation capacity of the
structure.
Structural connections can be categorized in many ways, depending on the elements that are joined,
the forces that are transmitted between elements or the materials that are used in the casting of the
joint. In the current work, the behavior of unreinforced casting joints or mortar-to-concrete joints
exposed to high level of conﬁnement is investigated. One relevant instance is represented by the
horizontal connection between the wall and the slab elements below, which are often constituted by
on-site grouted unreinforced casting joints.
The shear capacity of such a joint originates from the cohesion bond and from the interface friction
between the elements constituting the joint. The cohesion bond is governed by the chemical reaction
between the new and old concrete while the friction is governed by the conﬁnement pressure normal
to the joint. The conﬁnement pressure stems from e.g. self weight or external compression loads.
When combined horizontal and vertical loading is to be transferred, the local conﬁnement pressure
on parts of the joint may be very high.
For modeling purposes of the deformation capacity of the entire structure, a detailed knowledge of
the capacity and behaviour of the unreinforced joint stemming from friction is essential. To gain
knowledge of the behaviour, a detailed experimental program will be planned and executed on real
size connections, similar to typical joints used in current designs.
The experimental test setup will allow for combined compression and shear, while the use of rollers
will allow for unrestricted deformation during the test. The deformation characteristics will be
recorded with a digital image correlation technique (ARAMIS analysis) along with conventional
deformation measurements by linear variable displacement transducers. The inﬂuence of increasing
conﬁnement pressure up to 15 MPa will be one of the main focuses of the experiments. The interface
shear strength and the displacement capacity of the joints will be investigated. A comparison of
the experimentally determined interface shear strength with existing codes will be presented.

Introduction
1.1
Scope of work
The construction industry is experiencing an ever increasing pressure to renew itself. Increased
proﬁtability, eco friendliness, energy eﬃcient construction or low impact construction, are the main
concerns that have pushed the construction industry to new levels of technology. One construction
system that is capable of meeting the requirements imposed by the new regulations is the precast
building system. The advantages of using precast building systems are many and diverse.
One of the main advantages of using precast elements in construction is the increase in proﬁtability.
This is attained through lower construction costs and a decrease in construction time. Because of
the possibility to have fewer and even untrained workforce hired at the production facility, as well
as at the construction site, large amounts of capital can be saved. Using precast elements saves
expensive building materials, water and energy, all this while reducing the construction time to up
to ﬁve times, compared to the conventional in-situ method [2].
Using precast elements in construction is considered one of the most sustainable construction
methods, because during the production process of the precast elements, a minimum amount of
waste is created. Also, recycled precast elements can be reused in new constructions. The buildings
made of precast elements can be built in earthquake free as well as earthquake prone areas, can be
easily equipped to have low energy consumption and by prefabricating the elements in a factory,
the traditional noise and dust pollution, present on large construction sites is eliminated [2].
Last but not least, factory fabrication ensures high quality and precision which translates into an
increase of the quality of living conditions and into aesthetically appealing structures [2].
It would be a misunderstanding to assume that the precast building system is just a translation of the
in-situ cast technology into a number of precast components, which are assembled on the building
site in such manner that initial in-situ cast approach is achieved. The misunderstanding arises from
lack of insight into the design philosophy and the peculiar rules and aspects that are associated
with precast concrete design.
Adequate design and construction is obtained using appropriate
connections which provide the necessary attributes needed for the construction system to carry all
the loads during its lifespan [3].
In the precast building system, steel reinforcing bars, bolts, welds, grout and concrete are used in
order to join the precast elements in a mechanical way. Nonetheless, the main purpose of the joints
is not connect the precast elements together. The joints have to ensure the structural integrity of
the whole system, hence they posses a fundamental part in the completed building. The structural
response of the building, as well as the ﬂow of forces through the structure, will be inﬂuenced by
the properties and behaviour of the joints. Thus the main function of the structural joints is to

ensure the correct transfer of forces between the precast elements in order to achieve the designed
structural response of the loaded building system [3].
The precast building systems based on the implemented structural system and the structural
interaction can be categorized as [3]:
1. beam and column systems, consisting of beam elements, column elements and the adjacent
connection.
2. ﬂoor and roof systems, consisting of ﬂoor elements, roof elements and the adjacent connections.
3. bearing wall systems, consisting of interior wall elements, facade wall elements and adjacent

The types of connections presented in Fig.1.1 of the bearing wall system are [3]:
1. vertical joints between exterior and interior wall elements, point A.
2. horizontal joints between exterior and interior wall elements, point B.
3. horizontal joint between wall elements and the base slab or foundation, point C.

In this study, a detailed experimental investigation of unreinforced casting connections, i.e. concrete
to mortar, will be performed in order to determine the behaviour and shear capacity of the
connection under high levels of conﬁnement, Fig.1.2.
The unreinforced connection used in the experimental work will be constituted of two L-shaped
reinforced prefabricated concrete elements, joined by a layer of mortar. The concrete elements will
be precast in ﬁlm faced plywood, which is generally considered in the industry to yield a smooth
surface.
The inﬂuence of increasing and decreasing the conﬁnement will be studied. A wide range of conﬁning
pressures will be applied, i.e. from 0 MPa up to 14.5 MPa. The experimental investigation will
be done through the ”push-oﬀ” test method with the aim of determining the two components that
constitute the interface shear strength, i.e. cohesion bond and interface friction.

2.1
Failure criteria for concrete [1]
Coulomb’s frictional hypothesis suggests that failure often occurs along certain sliding planes or
yield planes. The resistance to sliding or yield is given by cohesion (chemical bond) and internal
friction (mechanical bond). The internal friction depends on the normal stress acting in the sliding
or yield plane.
It is generally accepted that Coulomb’s failure hypothesis ﬁts many material types, among which
concrete.
In order to ﬁt the experimental data, a limitation has been added to the frictional
hypothesis, namely a limit on the tensile stress. This extended hypothesis is called the modified
Coulomb criterion.
Based on this extended failure criterion, two failure modes are identiﬁed, namely the sliding failure
and the separation failure. The sliding failure represents failure parallel to the failure plane, while
the separation failure represents failure perpendicular to the failure plane. In a section, sliding
failure occurs when the shear stress, τ in the section exceeds the sliding resistance or the shear
strength given by the cohesion, c and the internal friction. The internal friction is deﬁned as a
friction coeﬃcient, µ times the normal stress acting in the section, σ. This means that the internal
friction is a percentage of the normal stress in the section.
Consequently, the sliding failure is
deﬁned in Eq.2.1 and Fig.2.1.
|τ| = c + µσ

where:
c is positive scalar, µ is positive scalar and σ is positive if stress is in compression.

resistance or the tensile strength of the section, fA. The separation failure is deﬁned in Fig.2.1.

2.2
Failure in construction joints [1]
Construction joints are joints in which new concrete is cast against a previously cast and already set
concrete surface. In previous tests performed on construction joints, it was observed by Johansen
[1], that the failure does not occur in any of the joined elements, but rather in the interface between
the two concrete surfaces with diﬀerent age. Consequently, the failure appears to be governed by
the shear strength of the interface.
Based on the Coulomb frictional hypothesis, the behaviour of the interface between two concrete
surfaces can be deﬁned using Eq.2.1. The roughness of the interface between two elements cast at
diﬀerent times is given by the formwork material used for casting and by the level of treatment of
the surface after casting, e.g. left ”as-cast”, deep grooved, longitudinal or transverse roughened,
indented [4]. According to [1], for surface roughness obtained by casting against steel, plastic and
plywood, an almost constant sliding direction can be deﬁned for a joint loaded in shear. The sliding
occurs at slope α with the shear force, as depicted in Fig.2.2, meaning that the small ”AB”-like
interfaces are to be climbed during the relative movement of the two elements constituting the joint.
Figure 2.2: Roughness dependent sliding failure in the direction of α [1]

shear capacity of a joint can be determined using Eq.2.2, [1].

The surface roughness at the interface between two elements inﬂuences the cohesion and the friction

from Eq.2.1 that the friction coeﬃcient µ = tanα. This show that the rougher the surface, i.e. the

the interface and thus the higher the cohesion contribution, c′, to the shear strength.
Without the application of any normal stress, σ, the shear strength of the interface, also denoted
as bond failure load, will be governed by the tensile strength of the bond and the cohesion [4].

2.3
Shear transfer mechanisms between concrete layers cast at
diﬀerent times
The shear resistance at the interface of concrete layers cast at diﬀerent times is given as complex
phenomenon where multiple eﬀects may occur simultaneously. The combination of the principal
mechanisms such as cohesion bond, friction or dowel action is complex and may be inﬂuenced by
the concrete type, the amount, quality and positioning of reinforcement or interface roughness.
Both the unreinforced and reinforced casting joints, can be deﬁned as the interaction of these
principal mechanisms. This is due to the inherent similarities existing between the two types of
casting joints. The only diﬀerence in the determination of the shear resistance of unreinforced and
reinforced casting joints is that for the reinforced joints, an additional friction mechanism has to be
considered, which develops along the reinforcement crossing the interface and the presence of the
dowel action. The following subsections present a short description of the above mentioned eﬀects
and their contribution to shear resistance.
2.3.1
Cohesion bond
This mechanism is represented by the binding forces that develop due to chemical reactions between
concrete layers cast at diﬀerent times. These chemical bonding forces are known as ”Van der Waals
forces”, the term being occasionally used to describe all intermolecular forces. ”Van der Waals
forces” combine attraction and repulsion forces between atoms, molecules, and diﬀerent surfaces,
as well as other intermolecular forces [5]. They are the weakest of the weak category of forces.
Nonetheless, provided a multitude of such forces are present, they may still provide signiﬁcant
strength [5].
Considering that the cohesion forces, also known as adhesion forces, are determined by attraction
of molecules, the cohesion bond is considered a rigid type of connection. The maximum value of
the cohesion is attained at a relative slip of maximum 0.05 mm [6].
2.3.2
Friction and Interface roughness
The friction mechanism appears with the development of the ﬁrst cracks which subsequently cause
a relative slip between interfaces. The friction force is determined by the magnitude of the normal
force or conﬁnement, that acts across the joint and the roughness of the interface. In order to
analyse the friction mechanism, one of the two friction models presented in section 2.4 may be used.
First, the shear friction model, advanced by Birkeland and Birkeland in section2.4.2 or secondly,
the aggregate interlock model, advanced by Walraven in section 2.4.5. The models are based on the
assumption that when two crack surfaces are forced to slide one relative to the other, a separation
will develop, which will determine the tensioning of the reinforcement. The tension stresses in the
reinforcement will cause a resisting compression across the cracked failure plane. Consequently, the
shear will be resisted by friction between the halves that determine the failure plane.
The interface roughness has the most signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the mobilisation process of the friction
mechanism.
One of the best known methods used to quantify the roughness parameters is the
SandPatchTest or SPT method. The method consists in pouring a certain amount of sand on a
surface and spreading the sand in a circle shaped area that has the thickness of one grain of sand.
The diameter of the circular area of sand is then measured and the roughness is then determined.
Recently, some new studies have emerged in which innovative techniques are employed to measure
the surface roughness. The most signiﬁcant contribution is attributed to J´ulio and Santos [7] whom
in their work have developed three diﬀerent methods to measure the interface roughness: PDI,
a method based on processing the digital image of surfaces, 2D-LRA, a two-dimensional surface

2.3.3
Dowel action
The dowel action represents a basic mechanism in the transfer of shear forces that develops when a
steel bar crossing a failure plane is subject to shear displacement. Depending on the steel class, the
thickness of the steel bar and the relative position of the bar compared to the element boundaries,
several distinct failure modes appear. When a strong steel bar is placed in a weak element or with
insuﬃcient concrete cover, the failure will be determined by splitting of the element On the other
hand, if there is suﬃcient cover on the reinforcing steel bar, the failure will occur by the development
of a plastic hinge in the steel bar due to bending as shown in Fig.2.3.
Figure 2.3: Representation of dowel action [8]
Due to the fact that the study in the current thesis is based on testing of unreinforced casting joints
between precast concrete elements, the eﬀect of dowel action is not present, and thus, it will be
disregarded from further discussions and analyses.

Previous studies
The interface shear transfer between concrete layers cast at diﬀerent times, have been subject to
numerous researches in the last 60 years. An outline of previous studies, that are most relevant
to the subject of this thesis will be presented in the following. Through extensive research works,
these laid the foundation and were essential in the development of the design models.

Hanson, 1960
In 1960, Hanson [9] presented a study which was performed on connections between precast and
in-situ cast concrete elements used in the construction of bridges. During these tests, the eﬀect of
roughness, cohesion, keys and shear reinforcement was considered. The author conducted a series
of 62 push-oﬀtests. In order to create a standard methodology of comparison between diﬀerent
contact surfaces, the stirrup reinforcement eﬀect was subtracted from all the curves where relevant.

shown in Fig.2.5.a, the external shear force V will generate a sliding motion which will be resisted

Mohamad et al., 2015
A recent study by Mohamad et al., [4], investigated the inﬂuence of ﬁve surface textures between
concrete elements on the cohesion and friction under varying normal stresses through push-oﬀ
tests. The aim was to determine the cohesion and friction by quantifying the roughness through
a roughness parameter, rather than through a qualitative evaluation of the surface texture, e.g.
smooth, grooved, indented etc.
The cohesion and friction were determined experimentally, applying Coulomb’s frictional hypothesis,
while the roughness was measured using a digital instrument. By correlating the pre-crack interface
shear strength (occurs just before break of the cohesion bond and is given by the sum of the

2.5.2
BS 8110-1 (1997)
In the British standard BS 8110-1 (1997) [19], several requirements for the design and detailing
of prefabricated, composite and in-situ cast concrete construction systems are presented. Various
design situations are considered depending on the stresses that act on the interface and design
recommendations are presented for each case.
In case of in-plane shear loading and with the design ultimate shear stress, vu not exceeding 0.23
MPa, it is not required to include shear reinforcement across the interface. However, very smooth
surfaces are required to be roughened.
In the case of concrete to concrete interfaces under compression, under all design circumstances, it
is not required to specify shear reinforcement across the interface. However, the interface is required
to be processed to present a rough ﬁnishing as long as the design ultimate shear stress, vu does not
exceed 0.45 MPa.

Experimental program
The experimental work described in the present thesis was carried out using the ”push-oﬀ” test
method on prefabricated reinforced concrete elements. Eleven (11) tests were performed for the
study.
These consisted of a pair of prefabricated reinforced concrete elements grouted together
using a layer of unreinforced mortar.
3.1
Test setup
The obtained joints were placed in the test frame and subject to normal and horizontal loads by
means of hydraulic actuators. Each actuator was equipped with a load cell in order to accurately
monitor the load during the test. A roller support was placed beneath the elements in order to
accommodate the development of interface slip and on one side in order to allow the development
of vertical displacements.
The rollers facilitated the movement of the test specimens in the test frame, but also allowed
unrestricted movement of the test specimens during the test. The schematic representation and the
actual test setup is shown in Fig.3.1 and Fig.3.2, where the vertical actuator is denoted with A, the
horizontal actuator is denoted with B, the horizontal sled is denoted with C while the vertical sled
is denoted with D.

3.4
Measurement methods
3.4.1
Linear variable displacement transducers (LVDT)
The linear variable displacement transducer (LVDT) (also referred to as linear variable diﬀerential
transformer) is a category of electrical transformers, used for measuring linear displacements or
positions. The LVDT is equipped with long slender coils which determines the output voltage to
be essentially linear over displacement, up to several several hundred millimetres long.
The biggest advantages of using LVDTs is that once these has been conﬁgured, any other movements
as for example the rotation of the axis besides the linear uniaxial motion of the core, will not inﬂuence
the measurements. Since they do not contain any electronics, even if the power is lost or switched
oﬀ, no positional information is lost and at restart the LVDT will show the exact same measurements
as before the power break.
Being frictionless devices, they are extremely reliable and have a virtually inﬁnite life-cycle. Based on
the above, the linear variable displacement transducers were chosen to be one of the two measuring
techniques utilized in this study.
In Fig.3.13, a schematic representation of the arrangement of the LVDTs on the specimens is
shown. In order to get more reliable results, two sets of LVDTs were used on each specimen. L1
and L2 measure the horizontal displacements, while T1 and T2 measure the transverse or vertical
displacements. L1 and T1 measure the movement of the top element, while L2 and T2 measure the
movement of the bottom element.

3.4.2
ARAMIS Digital Image Correlation Software
The ARAMIS software uses digital images recorded at constant or random intervals of time during
a deformation experiment, in order to determine the deformations and strains that occur on the
targeted surface over time. ARAMIS identiﬁes in the digital images taken by the camera, a surface
structure on the measured object and assigns coordinates to the pixels in the digital images.
In order to determine displacements and deformations of the targeted object, the ARAMIS software
compares the digital images with the ﬁrst image recorded, considering that the ﬁrst image exhibits
the undeformed state of the object.
Due to the fact that the interface which was measured had few characteristics, it was painted in
order to create a random speckle pattern also called a stochastic color spray pattern, Fig.3.14.
Figure 3.14: Stochastic pattern
These unique features are then tracked by creating a mesh of rectangular facets, each facet being
unique compared to its neighboring facet. The ARAMIS software tracks the deformation of these
rectangular facets, from one picture to another, thus being able to generate a displacement ﬁeld,
Fig.3.15.

3.4.3
Data analysis
The images that were recorded during each experiment were uploaded into the ARAMIS digital
image correlation software in order to be processed. First, a scale of measurements was deﬁned in
the software. This was done by deﬁning a known dimension from the ﬁrst picture taken in each
test. This picture included a measuring tool from which a segment of 1 cm was deﬁned into the
software as seen in Fig.3.16.
Figure 3.16: Scaling of measurements in ARAMIS
The second step was to deﬁne a surface on the elements which was going to be analyzed in ARAMIS.
The surface was chosen to be virtually the same size as the mortar area. On this surface two sets
of two points were deﬁned. These sets of two points coincided with the T1, L1 and T2, L2 sides of
the mortar interface as presented in Fig.3.13. Out of these two points that made up each set, one
point was placed on the top element, while the second was placed on the bottom element. Through
the ARAMIS software the relative horizontal and vertical distances between the two points was
recorded. The variation of these distances translated into the variation of displacement between the
two elements.
Both through LVDTs and ARAMIS, the negative vertical displacements are considered compression
displacements, due to the fact that the vertically mounted LVDTs are compressed, while through
ARAMIS the distance between the points decreases.

Vertical displacements
In the following, a detailed presentation of the similarities and diﬀerences observed between the
LVDT and ARAMIS measurements of the vertical displacement, during the experimental program

It was observed during some of the experiments that contrasting vertical
displacement measurements were obtained based on the two measuring systems.
Fig.4.3 and Fig.4.4 summarise the interface behaviour throughout the experiment. The ﬁrst column
summarises the behaviour before the interface shear capacity is reached (in case of experiments with
broken cohesion bond) or until the shear capacity is regained after the cohesion breakage (in case of
the experiments with cohesion bond). The second column summarises the behaviour following this
initial key event until the end of the experiment or interface failure. In case of experiments no. 1
and 2, due to the development of a crack quite early in the experiment, which induced a drop in the
shear capacity, the initial assessment is performed on the interval until this drop in shear capacity.
The sign convention adopted in the sketches is the following:
1. ”0” simbolises vertical displacements between -0.05 and +0.05 mmn, i.e. no or nearly no
vertical displacement;
2. ”-” simbolises increasing negative vertical displacements, i.e. increasing compression at the
interface;
3. ”+” simbolises increasing positive vertical displacements or decreasing compression at the
interface (which could lead to separation at the interface);
4. ”ct.” simbolises constant behaviour or no change in the vertical displacements in comparison
with an earlier phase of the experiment.
Based on the ﬁgures below, two variations of the vertical displacements can be readily identiﬁed:
diﬀerent displacement behaviour on the longitudinal direction, i.e. between the T1-T1 and T2-T2
sides and on the transverse direction, i.e. between T1-T2 ARAMIS and T1-T2 LVDT sides.
The diﬀerent displacement behavior on the longitudinal direction refers to one side of the mortar
interface being more compressed than the other, as seen in Fig.4.3 and Fig.4.4 for example for
experiments no. 2, 7, 9.
In experiments no. 2, 7 and 9 in the initial phase of the experiments, small compression displacements
are observed on the T1 side, combined with nearly no displacements or very small positive displacements
on the T2 side. As the experiments progress, the displacements measured on the T1 side increase
for experiments no. 2 and 9. This increase in displacement is combined with a relaxation of the
interface measured on the T2 side. In experiment no. 7 a decrease in the compression displacements
is measured on the T1 side, combined with increasing compression displacement on the T2 side. In
these experiments we can observe that the behavior of the interface from the initial phase is either
strengthened or evolves in opposite direction during the rest of the experiment.
These diﬀerences are believed to develop as the normal force becomes increasingly eccentric compared
to the center of gravity of the mortar interface due to increasing interface slip as seen in Fig.4.5. The
eccentricity of the normal force induces a moment which produces increasing compressive stresses
on one side of the mortar interface, while producing relaxation on the opposite side of the mortar
interface. Due to the nature of the push-oﬀtest in which high levels of interface slip are induced,
this eccentricity on the longitudinal direction is expected to occur in the majority of the tests, thus
is considered an inherent eccentricity of this test type.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
As it as been already mentioned, in the precast building system joints have to ensure the structural
integrity of the whole system, hence they posses a fundamental part in the completed building. The
structural response of the building, as well as the ﬂow of forces through the structure, are inﬂuenced
by the properties and behaviour of the joints. Thus the main function of the structural joints is to
ensure the correct transfer of forces between the precast elements in order to achieve the designed
structural response of the loaded building system.
In this sense an extensive experimental program was undertaken to investigate unreinforced casting
connections, i.e. concrete to mortar in order to determine the behaviour and shear capacity of
the connection under high levels of conﬁnement. The inﬂuence of increasing and decreasing the
conﬁnement is also studied while using a wide range of conﬁning pressures, i.e. from 0 MPa up
to 14.5 MPa. The unreinforced connection used in the experimental work is constituted of two
L-shaped reinforced prefabricated concrete elements, joined by a layer of mortar. The concrete
elements are precast in ﬁlm faced plywood, which is generally considered in the industry to yield a
smooth surface.
As it has been already presented the interface shear capacity increases linearly with the increasing
normal stress. The slope of the linear regression line is determined to be the friction coeﬃcient from
the Coulomb frictional hypothesis.
In section 5 the obtained average friction coeﬃcient was determined as 0.74 which is approximately
50% larger than the value prescribed in the current design codes for smooth or very smooth interfaces
and corresponds to the values prescribed for rough or indented surfaces. This diﬀerence is attributed
to the fact that the design codes due to safety reasons oﬀer generally lower values of the friction
coeﬃcient.
In this study it has been demonstrated that the frictional properties of an interface remain generally
constant even at high levels of conﬁnement, corresponding to 14.5 MPa or 40% of the compression
strength of the mortar. This ﬁnding is valid in the cases where the interface is not experiencing
deterioration due to local crushing of the mortar interface.
In some cases it can be observed that the shear capacity reduces with increasing levels of conﬁnement.
These are attributed to the increasing deterioration of the mortar interface, induced by local crushing
of the mortar. It has been observed that as the conﬁnement stress increases in comparison to the
compressive strength of the mortar, the probability of local crushing of the mortar to develop
increases.
As it has been already presented the interface shear capacity remains fairly constant even at high
levels of interface slip of 20 mm. This behavior is especially valid in the case of the experiments
==PAGE:76==
66
where the conﬁnement level reached was under 150 kN of 5 MPa. In the experiments where the
conﬁnement level was increased over this value, a decrease in the interface shear capacity can be
observed. Nonetheless from the observations throughout the experiments, the decrease in interface
shear capacity is not due to increasing interface slip but rather is attributed to the deterioration of
the interface due to crushing of the mortar.

Chapter 7
Future work
Throughout an extensive experimental program, such as the one undertaken for the current thesis,
it has been observed that several aspects of the experimental methodology used may inﬂuence the
results.
With the current design of the elements, a maximum of 14.5 MPa compressive stress was reached.
In order to resist the high compressive stresses developed by the setup a more sturdy design of the
elements needs to be found.
Due to the fact that any rotation or movement of the transducer supports can inﬂuence the
measurements and thus the experimental results, especially for the vertical transducers which
measure very small vertical displacements, a more sturdy way of placing and ﬁxing the transducers
on the elements needs to be found.
Through the current methodology used in the casting process, the cohesion bond was obtained only
in 4 tests out of 11. A clear reason being this phenomenon it is not known, but it is strongly believed
to be due to small contractions of the mortar as it cures. A solution could be to pour water on the
interface where new concrete or mortar will be cast in order to reduce these contractions.
As it has been presented in the chapter 4 at some of the experiments it has been discovered that
the surfaces that constituted the joint were not parallel. In order to to keep the surfaces of the
elements, in-between which the mortar will be cast, parallel to each other an optimisation of the
casting and clamping process is recommended.
Due to the fact that through the transportation and handling methods used, the interface can
deteriorate, e.g. the cohesion bond can be destroyed, it is suggested to improve the design of the
concrete elements. Steel hooks could be incorporated in the design of the concrete elements in order
to facilitate the use of the forklift and crane and thus facilitate the transportation from the casting
facility to the testing area.
