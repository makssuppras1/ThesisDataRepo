Abstract
Foci detection in cells is an important tool for investigating underlying biological
processes. This thesis investigates the application of Convolutional Neural Net-
works (CNNs) for the task of foci detection in cells. This thesis presents a set of
systematically designed CNN architectures with varying depth and complexity.
Training and testing of the networks are carried out in two experiments where
dierent representations of data is used. The results indicate that increasing the
depth and complexity of the network to some degree improves the performance.
That is, a limit is reached where increasing the depth of the network architecture
does not further improve the performance. Additionally, this thesis presents an
experiment where experts validate the predicted as well as the ground truth
label images in a fast and sucient way.
The results show that neither the
predictions nor the ground truth are sucient for a biological research study.
Nonetheless, this work demonstrate proof of concept of the application of CNNs
for the task of foci detection.

med Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) til anvendelse indenfor detektion

detektion af foci.

Introduction
Cells are the fundamental units of life and the study of cells provides essential
information about living organisms [6]. Through cell analysis scientists obtain
information about for example diseases and the impact of drugs. An important
tool within this eld is uorescence microscopy [33]. It is a technique that al-
lows researchers to visualise molecules within individual cells and hereby obtain
information about cellular mechanisms. Figure 1.1 shows a segment of an image
that is obtained by uorescence microscopy. Fluorescent biomolecules can be
visualised in dierent patterns within the cell, one of these patterns is foci. In
Figure 1.1 foci appear as small bright spots.
Figure 1.1: Image of cells obtained by uorescence microscopy.
==PAGE:18==

Introduction
Foci detection in cells is essential for data interpretation as it acts as an indi-
cator of underlying biological processes [32]. It allows researchers to perform a
quantitative analysis of foci which can provide information about mechanisms
and pathways, for example DNA damage (which can provide information about
diseases) and DNA repair (the process of which a cell identies and corrects
DNA damage) [23]. However, manual annotation of foci can be very time con-
suming and cumbersome. Hence, there exists a desire for an automated and
ecient annotation of foci. Furthermore, manual foci detection is aected by
human inconsistencies [23]. Inconsistencies in manual foci detection may cause
researchers to come up with dierent biological interpretations that is due to
human error and not biological dierences. Hence, an automated foci detection
algorithm will streamline the consistency of the results.
Foci detection consists in deciding whether a pixel in an image data is part of
a focus or not, hence, it is both detection and quantication. The problem can
also be referred to as pixel-wise classication or semantic segmentation. Foci
appear as small bright spots in the image. Several challenges arises from the
image data, as it contains a large variation in foci appearance. This study em-
ploys supervised machine learning, specically Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNNs), to address this problem.
CNNs are a class of deep learning methods within the eld of machine learning
[30]. They are designed to operate on data of multiple arrays which make them
ideal to process image data. The architecture of a CNN is composed of many
layers, hence, referred to as a deep network.
Essential for a network is the
convolutional layers. The convolutional layers perform image convolutions by
use of convolutional lters. These lters are learned through a training process of
the network. The popularity of CNNs has increased dramatically within the past
years with a breakthrough in 2012 where Krizhevsky et al. [28] constructed a
CNN that was used to win the ImageNet challenge [42]. The ImageNet challenge
consists of tasks such as classication, localization and detection. Among solving
problems within a broad variety of elds CNNs has recently been shown to
solve very dicult biomedical segmentation and detection problems (for example
[11, 41, 43, 51]). The main advantage of using CNNs in foci detection is that
there is no need for dening features of foci such as shape and range of intensity
in order to solve this task. The CNN learns automatically abstract features
from the data through a training process of the network.

A/S is a Danish company who develops, manufactures and sells high quality
instruments for cell counting and cell analysis [8]. Their instruments are used
within life science research, clinical diagnostics, pharmaceutical and food in-
dustries. They are currently developing new software to solve the task of foci
detection. This project originates from their desire to investigate whether deep

1.1
Fluorescence microscopy
Most mammalian cells are 10-100 µm, and hence, way beyond human vision [6].
Light microscopy is a technical eld that allows humans to obtain a detailed
view of the cells. There exist several dierent light microscopy techniques. The
one of interest in this project is uorescence microscopy.
The molecules of
interest are stained with uorophores, however, in some cases the uorophores
are naturally present in the molecules. The labelling is followed by illumination
of the specimen.
The uorophores absorb light with a specic spectrum of
wavelengths and then emit light of a longer wavelength [33]. In the absorption
state, energy from the photons is transferred to the molecules. The reverse is
happening in the emission state where energy stored in the molecules is emitted
as photons at a dierent wavelength. The emitted light is captured by a camera
in the microscope and a uorescent image is obtained.

1.3
Related work
Many previous attempts of foci detection have been published. CellProler was
launched in 2006 as the rst open source system designed for high-throughput
image analysis of cells [7]. It has a broad applicability and can conduct many
dierent types of analyses including foci detection. Despite good performance
CellProler is not ideal as it requires manual parameter tuning.
In 2009 Smal et al. [47] performed a quantitative comparison of nine dierent
foci detection algorithms [4, 5, 20, 24, 35, 38, 44, 48, 49, 50, 52]. Common for
all the methods is that the detection framework can be divided into three steps:
noise reduction, signal enhancement and signal threshold. Seven of the signal
enhancement methods are unsupervised while two are supervised. The methods
were both tested on synthetic data where ground truth data was available and on
real image data where manual annotations were used as ground truth. Overall
the algorithms with supervised signal enhancement performed better than the
unsupervised.
In 2010 Dzyubachyk et al. presented an automated analysis of time-lapse u-
orescence microscopy images [18]. Their analysis consisted of two parts: cell
analysis and foci analysis. The foci analysis involved nding foci markers, foci
segmentation and foci selection. The foci markers were found by a simple thresh-
old of intensity and then the number of false positives were reduced by applying
patch-based image reconstruction and depth correction of intensity. The size of
each focus was predicted from their respective intensity. The segmentation was
performed by energy minimisation that combines image- and smoothness-based
energies. The selection process in the third step was to reject false positives.
Overall, the performance of their method is comparable to manual analysis per-
formed by an expert. However, their method requires initial parameter settings.
Jucha et al. presented in 2010 the open source program FociCounter [25]. It
was developed to analyse foci from a specic, however, widely used protein.
The detection framework follows the three steps: noise reduction, signal en-
hancement where they apply a top-hat transform, and signal threshold. The
developers point out that the default parameters might be inappropriate so in
practise manual parameter tuning is needed.

Introduction
FindFoci is an open source foci detection algorithm presented by Herbert et
al. in 2014. It is a plug-in for the open source Java image processing program
ImageJ [19, 45]. The algorithm nds local maxima and expands these into peak
regions. Thereafter, a peak merge algorithm is applied to merge insignicant
peaks. This method requires a lot of parameters to be set. The software provides
an optimiser to identify the best combination of parameters in a supervised
setting. It is the users task to train the algorithm. The advantage is that the
user can train the algorithm for specic purposes, however, this also means that
the user need a training data set and has to go through a cumbersome training
stage. Additionally, Herbert et al. address the problem of human variation in
focus assignment. On the basis of an experiment with three participants they
show signicant inconsistencies in human focus assignment.
Three open source software tools for analysing foci were released in 2015: FoCo
[29], FociQuant [31] and the Focinator [37]. All three are likewise FindFoci build
on top of ImageJ. Both the Focinator and FoCo requires parameter tuning by
the user. The FociQuant has three options: fully automated, semi-automated
and manual segmentation.
However, the developers point out that the fully
automated segmentation suers from lack of precision.
In spite of the many attempts of automated foci detection there still exists a
desire for a fully automated, ecient and robust foci detector. While CNNs has
not yet been employed for this specic task, CNNs have successfully been used
to solve other detection and segmentation tasks that concern images of cells.
In 2015 Ronneberger et al. presented U-net, a deep network that showed very
good performance in various biomedical segmentation tasks [41]. The network
architecture consist of a contracting path and an expansive path that are fairly
symmetric. Additionally, they use skip connections from the contracting path
to the expansive path. One of the keys to success was their excessive use of
data augmentation. In addition to shift, rotation and gray value variants they
applied elastic deformations to simulate realistic deformations. Sadanandan et.
al address the issue of acquiring enough ground truth images for the task of
cell segmentation using CNNs [43]. They showed that by using automatically
generated ground truth segmentations based on uorescence image data to train
the CNN, they could obtain performance similar to manual annotations. Their
network architecture has likewise U-net an equal amount of down-sampling and
up-sampling layers. In addition, they make extensive use of skip connections.

Convolutional Neural
Networks
CNNs are models within the eld of supervised machine learning and can be
employed to solve a various number of tasks such as segmentation, classication
and recognition [30]. It is a method that automatically discovers multiple levels
of representation needed to solve the specic task, and thus, does not rely on
hand engineered features. CNNs are a special type of feed-forward neural net-
works which means that information only moves forward from the input layer,
through the hidden layers and to the output layer. This is in contrast to recur-
rent neural networks that include feedback connections for which information
are fed back into itself [21]. The following theory will be described in the context
of semantic segmentation.
2.1
Supervised learning
In supervised learning we use a training data set to learn a prediction model
[22]. This enables us to predict the outcome for new unseen data. It is called
supervised because we need a training set consisting of both images and their
corresponding ground truth segmentations to guide the learning process. The
process is also called learning by example. Figure 2.1 illustrates the process of
supervised learning.

Activation functions
CNNs perform non-linear mappings of the input. This non-linearity arises from
the non-linear activation functions. Without non-linear activation functions the
network could be formulated as a system of linear equations. This would be easy
to solve, however, the network would be limited in learning complex mappings.
Hence, we need non-linear activation functions to obtain a powerful network
that can model complicated data.
In order to enable gradient based optimization methods such as back-propagation
the activation function must be continuously dierentiable. There exists many
dierent activation functions such as Rectier Linear Unit (ReLU), sigmoid
and hyperbolic tangent, however, the most popular in recent years is ReLU. It
is dened by

ReLU overcomes this problem. Let us consider a deep network with sigmoid

tween 0 and 1
4. When performing back-propagation we might run into numerical
issues as many small values multiplied together results in a vanishing gradient.
The gradient of ReLU is either 0 for z < 0 or 1 for z ≥0, and hence, will not
vanish. However, if all input to ReLU is negative then the gradient will die and
block back-propagation. This is not a common event and can be overcome by
initialising weights with positive values and normalise input between 0 and 1
[26]. This problem did not occur in the experiments described in Chapter 3.

3.3
Experimental setup
The networks are implemented in Python [1] using the framework Keras [10]
on top of Tensorow [2].
The training process is carried out using either a
single Titan X or a GTX 1080ti GPU. The four networks are trained end-to-
end by minimising the cross-entropy loss between the probability maps and the
ground truth foci labels.
The optimisation is carried out by employing the
Adam optimiser [27] with hyper-parameters lr = 10−4, β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999
and ˆϵ = 10−8.
1 The values of the parameters β1, β2 and ˆϵ are chosen on the
basis of recommendations in [27]. The value of lr is chosen on the background of
initial tests. The networks trained for 250 epochs with no early stopping. The
size of the mini batches used for optimisation is one image. This is reasonable
because the cross-entropy loss is calculated for all pixels in an image, hence,
one image of size 1920 × 1440 pixels contains enough information to constitute
a mini-batch.
The function for random elastic deformation requires three input parameters
(code from [13]): α, σ, αaffine.
σ denes the width of the Gaussian kernel
which controls the smoothness of the displacement eld. α scales the intensities
of the displacement eld. αaffine controls the ane deformation. The range of
the parameters used for random elastic deformation were chosen on the basis
of small tests. The min and max values of the parameters were manually ad-
justed such that the algorithm produced deformations that resembles realistic
representations of cells. The input parameters were then drawn randomly from
a uniform distribution within these ranges.

3.3.4
Statistical comparison: ANOVA and Tukey's test
Even though the same images are used for network training the networks do
not produce the exact same results when repeating the experiments. This is
because the network weights are initialised randomly 3 and the training data is
randomly shued at each epoch. Due to this, both experiment A and B are
repeated three times, thus, the pixel-wise accuracy, IoU, precision and recall in
sections 4.1 to 4.2 are documented by the mean and standard deviation.
Initial results showed that within a single metric (pixel-wise accuracy, IoU, pre-
cision or recall) the distributions for each network occasionally overlap.
To
investigate if the means are statistically signicant a one-way ANOVA is car-
ried out. The signicance level is set to α = 0.05 and the null hypothesis is
formulated as
H0 :
two or more networks have the same mean
If p-value < 0.05 then the null hypothesis is rejected, meaning that, the dier-
ence between some means is statistically signicant. Subsequently, a Tukey's
test is carried out to nd the means that are signicantly dierent from each
other. The following null hypothesis is tested
H0 :
the means being compared are the same
If the Tukey condence limits for the pairwise comparisons do not overlap then
the null hypothesis is rejected. That is, the dierence between the two means
being compared is statistically signicant.
The one-way ANOVA test along with the Tukey's test make the following as-
sumptions [40]
1. The observations are independent within and among groups
2. The groups associated with each mean are normal distributed
3. There is equal within-group variance across the groups associated with
each mean
In the context of this project, a group is the set of trained networks that share
the same network architectures. We have four groups: atFociNet, midFociNet,
deepFociNet and veryDeepFociNet. Within each group we consider four types
of observations: pixel-wise accuracy, IoU, precision and recall. The statistical
comparisons are made within the four types of observations. Furthermore, the

3.4
Experiment: Validation by experts
It is highly relevant to examine whether the results are good enough from a
biological perspective, thus, an experiment is designed where experts validate
the results. The computations of the quantitative measures provide informa-
tion about the performance in relation to the ground truth. However, because
of errors in the ground truth further validation is needed in order to obtain
information about the quality of the label images.
3.4.1
Experimental design
The predictions from the best performing network based on the quantitative
measures in experiment A are used for this part. Three experts with a back-
ground in biology participate in the evaluation. Initially, the experts are given
the same instructions. The ground truth label image and the predicted label
image are pairwise presented to the experts along with the cell image and infor-
mation about the cell sample. The experts are each presented with the same 48
image pairs in random order. They are not informed which one is the ground
truth and which one is the prediction. For each image pair the experts should
state if one label image is better than the other or if they are equally good with
respect to the following criteria
1. Covering: How well the foci labels covers foci pixels in the cell image.
2. Separation: How well adjacent foci labels are separated.
The above criteria are formulated on the basis of initial individual interviews
with the experts. Furthermore, the experts are asked to asses if the label images
are good enough to use for making biological conclusions about the cell samples.
That is, they are asked to either accept or reject the label images.
3. Acceptance/rejection: overall assessment of the label image in regard to
applicability within research.

acceptance of the images the null hypothesis is formulated as
H0 :
An equal amount of predictions and ground are accepted
A rejection of this hypothesis means that there is a signicant dierence in the
number of predictions and ground truth that perform best. Likewise before,
this is a two-tailed test.

Within the eld of biology it is commonly known that humans annotate foci
dierently. This was also shown in [23] where three people were asked to man-
ually assign foci in 21 images. In this project the experts were not asked to
assign foci manually but instead they were asked to validate foci label images.
An experiment like the one presented in this project has to our knowledge not
been performed beforehand. Due to human variation in focus assignment it was
expected that there would be variation among the experts in the validation task.
5.5
Metrics for quantitative evaluation
One of the challenges in this project was to nd appropriate metrics to evaluate
the performance. Four metrics were chosen: pixel-wise accuracy, IoU, precision
and recall. Pixel-wise accuracy provided some information, however, due to the
very skewed classes it easily gave a distorted impression of the performance. As
described din section 3.3.3, IoU is commonly used to evaluate performance in
segmentation tasks. By focusing on the class of interest, the foci class, IoU gave
a very useful measure of performance. Precision and recall were primarily used
to examine the balance between false positives and false negatives.
In order to examine the results even further and investigate where the errors
occurred four dierent approaches were used: regular, adjusted, masked and
masked (wrt. the cell mask). These four measures provided information about
where the errors occurred. From a practical point of view it is important to
know where the errors occur. The false positives found outside the foci mask
are not critical errors as the cell/cell nucleus masks always are available. If the
networks nd foci outside the cell/cell nucleus then we simply apply the cell/cell
nucleus mask to remove these false positives.
One of the human challenges in foci detection is that it is often ambiguous
whether the border pixels are part of a focus or the background. This moti-
vates for automation of foci detection such that we can obtain consistent re-
sults. When performing supervised learning then ground truth label images are
needed. The label images used in this project are not perfect and the expert
validation showed that the quality of them depends on the expert evaluating.
This motivated for the measure adjusted that allows an error margin of one pixel
in regard to the ground truth label image. The size of the margin were based
on our experience with manual evaluation. On the basis of the ambiguity about
border pixels and if we only are interested in how well the foci labels covers foci
in the cell image then from a practical point of view the predictions are good if
adjusted is high.

5.6
Statistical comparison of means
One-Way ANOVA and Tukey's tests were used for comparison of performance
among networks. Because of the need of statistical tests for comparison, ANOVA
and Tukey's tests were used even though the assumptions are not fully met.
However, due to the robustness of the tests [14, 40] it is reasonable to use them
even though the assumptions are not fullled. The experiments were repeated
three times each, hence, the sample size for each group is three. This is a very
small sample size to perform statistical tests on. Initial tests showed the same
tendency as the results presented in sections 4.1.2 to 4.1.4 and 4.2.2 to 4.2.4
in regard to ranking of network performance. These tests included dropout,
weighting of loss function, testing on validation set, training for smaller number
of epochs (20, 100, 200).
The initial tests of the four network architectures
showed that the three samples are very representative for the outcome of re-
peated training along with testing. This slightly justify the use of the statistical
tests even though the sample size is very small. Due to the time consuming
training process it was not possible to repeat the experiments more times. The
repetitions of the network training was performed for the purpose of investigat-
ing the performance of dierent network sizes.
In the examination of expert validation a two-sample t-test were used. Three
experts participated in the validation, hence, the sample size is three. Again,
this is a very small sample size for statistical test. The answers from the experts
varied a lot which caused a very high standard deviation. At a rst glance the
means seem distinct, but due to the high variance the statistical tests did not
show that there is statistically dierence.
In spite of small sample size the
comparisons between the predictions and ground truth can be used as guidance.
Nonetheless, a sample size of three were enough to conrm that there is variation
among experts.
5.7
Data augmentation
Two classic methods were chosen for data augmentation, rotation and ipping,
as well as a method that make realistic deformations of cells, random elastic
deformation. Another method that was desired to use is adding noise to the
images, for example white noise. However, this was omitted in order to avoid
making the training time very long. Before adding noise as part of data aug-
mentation it should be investigated what types of noise that might appear in
the images.
==PAGE:83==
5.8 Network architecture
67
We observed that the performance improved after extending the size of the data
set by use of data augmentation. These results are not reported as it is a proven
fact that data augmentation improves the performance in deep learning.
In this project the data augmentation was performed prior to the network train-
ing. Alternatively, on-the-go data augmentation can be performed. This would
make sense for random data augmentation methods such as random elastic de-
formation and noise methods. Instead of applying these method multiple times
prior to the training in order to create a very large training set, less extensive
data augmentation can be performed in each epoch. This would reduce the size
of the training data that is fed to the network in each epoch.
5.8
Network architecture
The design of the FociNet architectures were inspired by U-net [41]. The rst
architecture designed was deepFociNet. Thereafter, the other networks were de-
signed as deeper and atter versions of deepFociNet. The depth of deepFociNet
were chosen on the basis of [36] and the hypothesis that when trying to nd
very small features the network does not have to be very deep. MidFociNet,
deepFociNet and veryDeepFociNet resembles U-net by the U-shaped and partly
symmetric architecture as well as the use skip connections. The choice of acti-
vation function (ReLU), pooling (max-pooling) and size of the lters (3×3) are
the same as for U-net. The upsampling layers dier. In U-net an upsampling
layer consist of upsampling followed by a 2 × 2 convolution. In FociNets the
upsampling layer consist in a transposed convolution. Besides 3 × 3 lters are
used in U-net it is also the currently most used lter size. Additionally, the
motivation for using a small lter size is that the features of interest are very
small.
5.9
Weighting of loss function
Table 4.2 and 4.3 for experiment A as well as Table 4.6 and 4.7 for experiment
B (i.e. the precision-recall tables) showed that all four networks have a larger
precision than recall. That is, there are more false negatives than false positives.
This is not surprising considering that the classes are very skewed. Early during
the project small experiments with weighting the loss function were carried
out. However, at that time the networks were only trained for smaller data
sets and between 10 and 20 epochs (i.e. the training was stopped long before
==PAGE:84==
68
Discussion
convergence was reached). These initial tests did not show a systematic pattern
in the imbalance between false positives and false negatives. On the basis of
these small initial test it was decided not to weight the loss function. However,
the results presented in sections 4.1.3 and 4.2.3 indicate that the loss function
should be weighted. In practice this can be done by creating weighted label
images. In this way the loss will get penalized more when the network predict
false negatives. There exist several ways of weighting the loss function to deal
with class imbalance. One of the classes can simply be weighted by a predened
factor. Alternatively, the weights can be calculated from the ratios between the
classes.
The primary goal was to nd the pixels that belong to a focus. However, it is
also desirable to have separated foci labels in order to make analysis of single
foci. Even though a pixel in the cell image seems to belong to a focus it is
desirable to label it as background if it is to separate two adjacent foci. The
results showed that the networks managed to separate adjacent foci labels fairly
well.
The separation of the foci the foci labels were only learned from the
training labels, however, it can be build into the model. Separation of adjacent
foci labels can be build into the model by weighting the loss function. This can
be done by creating weighted label images with high weights on the separation
borders likewise [41]. In this way the loss will get penalized a lot if predicting
false positives on the separation borders. The weighted label images can be
computed by use of dilation.
5.10
Evaluation time
The evaluation time of atFociNet, midFociNet and deepFociNet is 1 second
when evaluating them on a Titan X or a GTX 1080ti GPU, while it is 2 seconds
for veryDeepFociNet. The evaluation time has not been examined further in
this project. However, from a practical point of view it is desirable with an
evaluation time that is as short as possible. An evaluation time of 1 or 2 second
might not seem of much, however, if we do not have access to GPU and have
to run the computations on a CPU then the evaluation time will increase a lot
and we will most likely observe a signicant dierence in evaluation time of the
four networks.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This thesis have investigated the applications of CNNs for the task of foci de-
tection. Four systematically designed network architectures with varying depth
and complexities were implemented, trained and tested. They were named: at-
FociNet, midFociNet, deepFociNet and veryDeepFociNet. Data augmentation
was performed prior to the training process. The primary data augmentation
method was random elastic deformations which imitate realistic scenarios of
cells.
The networks were trained and tested for two dierent representations of data.
In the rst experiment the networks were trained and tested on the raw images
and in the second experiment the networks were trained and tested on images
for which a cell mask has been applied. In general both experiments showed
very similar performance, however, with a slight dierence in the impact of the
depth and complexity of the architectures. Both experiments showed that deep-
FociNet and veryDeepFociNet performed best with no statistically signicant
dierence in performance among the two architectures. This suggest an advan-
tage in increasing the depth and complexity of the architecture to some degree.
However, a limit was reached for which increasing the depth and complexity of
the architecture does not further improve the results. The overall best perform-
ing network is deepFociNet trained on the raw images. It obtained a pixel-wise
accuracy of 0.99614 and IoU of 0.6651.
==PAGE:86==
70
Conclusion
An experiment where three experts within the eld of biology validated the
predicted label images from deepFociNet along with the ground truth label
images was successfully designed and carried out. The results showed that the
ground truth label images were slightly better than the predicted label images,
however, the dierence was not statistically signicant. Additionally, the experts
assessed whether the label images were sucient for using in a biological research
study. Less than half of the predictions and ground truth label images were
accepted. Additionally, the results showed a large variance among the experts.
This emphasise the need for an automated and consistent method.
This study demonstrated proof of concept of the application of CNNs for the
task of foci detection. However, in order to obtain a usable model, high quality
ground truth label images are needed. Despite only a small training set and
limited data augmentation were used then for a suitable choice of architecture
it was possible to obtain predictions that are very close to the ground truth.

Chapter 7
Future work
This section outlines suggestions for future work.
Training data Acquire high-quality ground truth label images.
Data augmentation In addition to rotation, ipping and random elastic de-
formations data augmentation that imitate dierent noise levels should be per-
formed.
Weighted loss function Weighting of the loss function in order to obtain
an equal false positive and false negative rate. This includes testing dierent
weightings of the loss, for example global weights versus local weights. By global
weights we mean that the same weight is applied to all training images whereas
local weights are individual for each image in the training set. Additionally, the
weights can be set to a xed number or computed in accordance to the ratio
between the background class and the foci class.
Skip connections This project employed only long skip connections between
the contracting and expansive path. Both Drozdzal et al. [15] and Sadanandan
et al. [43] benet from using both long and short skip coneection. On the basis
of this it is relevant to investigate the impact of a more extensive use of skip
connections in the FociNet architectures.
