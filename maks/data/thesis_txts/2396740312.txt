ABSTRACT  
Today, buildings contain more and more combustible insulation materials in order to meet the 
increasing energy-performance demands. The combination of an increasing number of high-rises 
and combustible insulation increases the fire risk with potentially fatal consequences as seen with 
Grenfell Tower in London. A possible way to raise fire safety is by incorporating a firestop of an 
incombustible material in the insulation as a horizontal barrier, which help prevent or dampen the 
fire spread. Furthermore, a combustible material with a high fire classification may also be able to 
function as a firestop and dampen the fire spread. A literature review of different insulation ma-
terial was conducted. Furthermore, two initial tests on different combustible insulation such as 
expanded polystyrene (EPS), polyurethane (PUR) and polyisocyanurate (PIR) was conducted to 
find the thermal properties. A fire spread test and a heat of combustion test. Based on these find-
ings the combustible insulation material PIR were chosen to be further investigated as a possible 
firestop. Furthermore, stone wool was tested as a firestop so the combustible firestop could be 
compared with an incombustible one. Four firestop tests were conducted with PIR and stone wool 
functioning as firestop in the two different thicknesses 100 mm and 300 mm. The setup for the 
tests were a wall section consisting of a concrete backwall, EPS as combustible insulation, plaster-
board as final cladding and with stone wool or PIR as firestop. The results showed that all four 
firestops hindered the fire from spreading. However, the tests with PIR functioning as firestop 
showed problems with the fitting to the construction. This resulted in gabs between the construc-
tion and the firestop where heat and soot could travel to the EPS above the firestop.   
 
Keyword: External Fire Spread, Facade Fires, Prevention of Fire Spread, Insulation Materials, Fire-
stop.

1 INTRODUCTION  
A lot has happened and continue to happen within the building industry. One big reason for 
changes in the building industry is pollution and climate change. EU is targeting a reduction in 
greenhouse gas emissions of 20 per cent by 2020 (European Commission 2017). Buildings are ac-
countable for 40 per cent of the energy consumption and 36 per cent of CO2 emissions in the EU 
(European Comission 2016). Hence, obvious way to reduce total energy consumption and reach 
the emission target by 2020 is by reducing the energy consumption of buildings. The reduction of 
buildings energy consumption is primarily achieved by altering existing laws and national building 
regulations or introducing new ones. This has led to a continuously decrease in the allowed 
amount of energy consumption of buildings in Denmark through the last 50 years as shown on 
Figure 1. The development has forced the building industry to develop new materials and methods 
to build low energy buildings that complies with law and regulation.  
 
Figure 1 - Change in the Danish Building Regulations’ requirements for energy demand of single family houses 1961-
2020 (Danish Knowledge Centre for Energy Savings in Buildings 2016).  
This has resulted in new materials and combinations within facade solutions. Facades with new 
and more energy efficient insulation materials contributes to the reduction of energy consump-
tion but might also lead to issues with fire safety. Newer lighter insulation materials such as ex-
panded polystyrene (EPS), polyurethane (PUR) and polyisocyanurate (PIR) all have a low thermal 
conductivity which reduces the required thickness of the facades in order to comply with building 
regulations of energy consumption. However, they are all combustible. These newer insulation 
materials have grown in popularity and is now accountable for about 42 per cent of the insulation 
market in Europe (Calbraith 2013). 
In addition, a large urbanization is taking place throughout the world. Since 1950 the numbers of 
people in urban settlements has increased from 30 per cent to 53 per cent of the population in 
2014 and the urbanization is expected to continue in the future. By 2050 two-third of the world’s 
population is estimated to be living in urban settlements (United Nations Department of Economic 
and Social Affairs Population Division 2014). This urbanization has resulted in an increasing num-
ber of high-rises all over the world over the last decades (Illinois Institute Of Technology 2014). 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
 
11 
 
This tendency is also happening in Denmark where more people are moving to the big cities. Es-
pecially Copenhagen where the population is estimated to increase with 100.000 by 2027 
(Sørensen 2015) (Højer 2016). This has resulted in an increasing demand for housing in Copenha-
gen which has led to the construction of more high-rises (Sommerand & Thieden 2017).   
An increasing number of high-rises combined with combustible materials increases the fire risk 
and thereby the risk of people dying and property loss. The higher risk relates to both an increased 
evacuation time and reduced fire fighter accessibility as most firefighting lifts have a maximum 
height of 22m (Byggestyrelsen 2016). Buildings taller than 22m are required to install extra pre-
cautions.  
The combination of high-rise and combustible cladding had fatal consequences in the summer of 
2017 with the fire of Grenfell Tower in London where 71 lives were lost in the flames. Grenfell 
Tower had recently been renovated with 150 mm PIR insulation and a cladding consisting of two 
aluminum sheets and a polyethylene core. The cladding caught fire and spread to the entire build-
ing (BBC 2017) (Harper 2017).  
 
Figure 2 - Grenfell Tower fire (BBC 2017) 
 
1.1 Measures to reduce risk of fire spread 
In order to prevent fires like the one at Grenfell Tower different measures is taken in to account 
in Denmark. A fire in a multi-story building can spread to other rooms and floors both inside and 
outside the building. Inside the building fire and smoke usually spread through doors and other 
small openings between the elements or through the installations shafts in the building. In order 
to prevent or reduce the fire and smoke to spread through these openings, they are secured with 
a firestop of an incombustible material. The fire can also damage the structural elements and its 
load capacity, which is why a fire resistance classification of (R)EI60 or more is required. This clas-
sification means that the elements should be able to withhold its load bearing capacity (R), integ-
rity (E) and insulation (I) for 60 minutes when exposed to fire (Byggestyrelsen 2016). 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
Along with the structural requirements the building is also divided into different fire sections. The 
required size of the fire sections are based on the height of the building, the usage category (office, 
hotel or accommodation etc.) and whether an active fire safety system such as a sprinkler is in-
stalled (Byggestyrelsen 2016).   
As mentioned above the fire spread can also happen on the exterior of the building as was the 
case with Grenfell Tower. Figure 3 illustrates three ways of a facade catching fire; 1) from a fire at 
the neighboring building, 2) an external fire in front of the building or 3) by a room fire spreading 
through the window.  
 
Figure 3 - Three different facade fire scenarios. Neighboring building (left), container fire (middle), room fire (right) 
The fire can quickly spread to the entire facade and inside the building through openings in the 
facade. The ignition of the materials inside the building can happen either by radiation from the 
flames through the windows or if the windows breaks and the flames enters the room. 
Different measures for preventing this type of scenarios can be incorporated in the facade. One 
of the measures is the using of spandrels between the windows. This will reduce the temperature 
and radiation of the flames affecting the windows above. A spandrel of 1.2 m and a fire classifica-
tion of at least E30 is assumed to be enough (Byggestyrelsen 2016). Fire safe glass is another useful 
measure to prevent fire spread. When exposed to fire and high temperatures the fire safe glass 
foams up and becomes non-transparent. This results in a higher level of both insulation and re-
duction of radiative heat flux through the window (Machalická et al. 2016). Horizontal blockage 
such as balconies, canopies and cornices is also an effective measure to prevent vertical fire spread 
as it projects the flames away from the facade and has been proven very effective by several stud-
ies (Oleszkiewicz 1991) (Suzuki & Sekizawa 2003).  
Finally, if the facade is insulated with a combustible material the risk of fire spreading on the inside 
of the cladding is present. To prevent or slow down the fire from spreading through the combus-
tible insulation a firestop can be incorporated in the facade. As shown on Figure 4 the firestop is 
a layer of an incombustible material that creates a barrier. According to the Danish guideline for 
Building fire-safety a firestop should be present if the facade consist of insulation with a lower fire 
classification than D-s2,d2 (Byggestyrelsen 2016).

2 TECHNICAL BACKGROUND 
In order to investigate firestops several technical aspects should be considered. This includes fa-
cade systems, facade testing, classification of firestops and insulation types. These aspects are 
considered in this section to form the basis of this projects hypotheses presented in section 3 
below.   
 
2.1 Facade systems 
There exist several different façade systems of combustible insulation. The two most commonly 
used systems, External Thermal Insulation Composite System (ETICS) and Aluminum Composite 
Panels (ACP), are reviewed below.  
External Thermal Insulation Composite System 
ETICS is an external insulation facade solution which was first used in the early 1960s in central 
Europe. Its usage and popularity rose through decades due to both financial crises and oil crises, 
which made it more expensive to heat up buildings. Today ETICS is one of the most common meth-
ods to improve the energy performance of buildings (Künzel et al. 2006).  
ETICS is a system of different components which are added to a loadbearing structure such as a 
concrete or masonry wall. The system consists of a thermal insulation board, which could be any 
kind of rigid insulation material but an organic insulation material such as PUR, PIR or EPS is often 
used, as it is a quick, cheap and highly insulating product. The insulation is glued and then an-
chored to the load bearing wall. A base coat is then added to the insulation with some sort of 
reinforcement like a glass fiber mesh pressed into the coat. This is strengthening the coating and 
help prevent cracks in the surface. Last a finishing coat is added (Collina & Lignola 2010) (Malanho 
& Veiga n.d.). Figure 5 shows the different steps and layers in an ETICS wall system. The ETICS 
system is special compared to other systems as it is without any ventilation gap. The system can 
be used both when retrofitting old houses and as a facade solution for new buildings. 
 
Figure 5 - ETICS system (www.e-genius.at n.d.) 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
15 
 
Aluminum Composite Panels 
ACP is another system used for insulating and water proofing building facades. The system is a 
lightweight, rigid and durable product which consist of two aluminum face sheets bonded to a 
thin thermoplastic core such as PUR or PIR. The aluminum alloy face sheets are typical 0.50 mm 
thick and applied under high tension to the plastic core and then pressed together which makes 
highly resistant to delamination (Pine 1995).  
ACP was first invented in Germany in 1965 and was first produced in large scale in 1969. Over the 
decades and especially in the last few years, the system have grown more popular and is widely 
used in buildings exterior wall, curtain wall board, old building reconstruction and renovations 
(Zhao et al. 2017). Figure 6 shows a typical wall system with ACP. 
 
Figure 6 - ACP system (Jen Mills 2017) 
 
2.2 Facade testing 
The two types of facade systems reviewed above can be built with many combinations of different 
components. Before a facade element with an insulation material with a lower class than D-s2,d2 
can be used in Denmark, it has to pass a facade fire test (The Danish Transport and Construction 
Agency 2015). Fire testing facades is not only something that is done in Denmark. It is very com-
mon in many countries around the world. However, the methods for testing a facade differs from 
country to country. In Denmark, a facade is tested using the Swedish standard facade test SP fire 
105 which was the first fire test standard for facades in Europe. Other test standards are the British 
BS 8414-1 and the German DIN E 4102-20 which will be investigated further in this section.  
SP fire 105 
SP fire 105 standard is a method to determine the reaction to fire of materials and construction 
of external wall assemblies or facade cladding. The external wall assemblies or facade cladding is 
exposed to fire from a simulated apartment fire with flames emerging out through a window 
opening. The testing happens on a testing rig with space for an external wall of 4000 mm x 6000 
mm with two fictitious windows of 1510 mm x 1200 mm. An eave at the top of the rig is sticking 
500 mm out with a thermocouple measuring the temperature as seen on Figure 7. The simulated 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
room fire is created with 60 liters of heptane burning out of an opening of 3000 mm x 710 mm. 
The 60 liters of heptane correspond to a fire load of approximately 75 MJ/m2 total fire room sur-
face (SP Technical Research Institute of Sweden 1994). The fuel consumed in the SP method cor-
responds to a total energy release of 1830 MJ and the peak rate is approximately 2.0 MW 
(Anderson & Jansson 2013) 
 
Figure 7 - SP fire 105 setup 
 
For passing the SP fire 105 test a couple of criteria should be met. The temperature at the eave 
may not exceed 500°C for more than 2 minutes or 450°C for more than 10 minutes. No large pieces 
are allowed to fall down from the facade. The fire spread cannot reach further up than to the 
lower part of the window in the second story above the fire room. For general purpose, another 
criterion is that the heat flux into the center of the window in the first story above the fire room 
may not exceed 80 kW/m2.  
BS 8414-1 
The British standard BS 8414-1 is another method for testing external wall insulation systems ex-
posed to external fire. Where the SP fire 105 is a straight, the British standard BS 8414-1 differs as 
it is a L-shaped structure. One of the legs is forming the main test wall with a length of 2.6 m and 
the other leg forming the return wall with a length of 1.5 m and height of 8 m as shown on Figure 
8. A combustion chamber of 2 m x 2 m is placed at the base of the main wall to simulate a fully-
developed (post flashover) room fire, venting through a window. The heat source used in this test 
standard is a timber crib nominally measuring 1500 mm x 1000 mm in plane, and 1000 mm in 
height, constructed using softwood sticks nominally measuring 50 mm x 50 mm in cross-section 
1500 mm and 1000 mm in length. This heat source releases a nominal total energy release of 4500 
MJ over 30 min at the peak rate of (3 ± 0.5) MW (Bjegovic 2016). 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
17 
 
 
Figure 8 - BS 8414-1 setup 
In order to pass the BS 8414-1 test, the temperature 50 mm from the front, 5 m above combustion 
chamber, may not exceed 600 °C for a period of at least 30 seconds within 15 minutes of the start 
time. 
DIN E 4102-20 
The German standard DIN E 4102-20 is like the British standard a L-shaped structure. The struc-
tures first leg which is the main test wall has a length of 3 m where the second leg is 2 m long. The 
entire structure has a height of 5.5 m and a combustion chamber of 1 m x 1 m is placed at the 
base corner of the main test wall as seen on Figure 9. The heat in this standard is either a gas 
burner or a timber crib in the combustion chamber. The timber crib should be 500 mm x 500 mm 
in the plane and 480 mm high and made up by 40 mm x 40 mm softwood sticks. The nominal 
weight is 25 kg. If using a gas burner, it should have an output of 7.4 g/s + 24 m3/h air and only be 
turned on for 20 minutes. This gives a heat source of 320 kW which is about 10 times less than the 
heat source in BS 8414-1 of 3000 kW ± 500 kW (Pecur et al. 2015).  
 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
 
Figure 9 - DIN E 4102-20 setup 
Where the British standard BS 8414-1 only had one criterion the German DIN E 4102-20 have 
multiple criteria, which need to be met before passing the test. Firstly, there must be no fire dam-
age to the specimen 3.5 m or more above the fire chamber. Secondly, the temperature in and 
outside the facade system must not exceed 500 °C, 3.5 m or more above the fir chamber. Thirdly, 
no continuous flaming for more than 30 seconds, 3.5 m or more above the fire chamber, is al-
lowed. Fourthly, the flames may never reach the top of the top of the test specimen. Fifthly, the 
falling of burning droplets, burning and non-burning debris and lateral flame spread is not allowed 
for more than 90 seconds after the burners are turned off (Macdonald & Jones 2012).  
 
2.3 Classification of firestops 
A firestop is a fire protection system that seals the cracks and crevices of a space with fire proof 
material to keep a fire from spreading between rooms. This is a crucial step in ensuring fire and 
smoke from spreading through multiple spaces quickly. This will increase the chance of evacuating 
a building on fire without any loss of life and increase the time available for the fire fighters to 
come and get it under control. When looking at a firestop in a facade it should prevent or slow the 
fire from spreading both upwards and downwards. Upwards it should hinder or dampen the heat 
and flames from going through the firestop and ignite the material above it. Downwards the fire-
stop should prevent potential droplets from igniting the insulation below the firestop. The firestop 
has an extra function if it is part of an ETICS facade system. The firestop is delaying cracking of the 
render as it keeps the render and mesh in position longer time (Bjegovic et al. 2016).    
According to the Danish building regulation a firestop must be built in the facade if it is insulated 
with a flammable material (The Danish Transport and Construction Agency 2015). The firestop 
should have the classification of A2-s1, d0. Mineral wool is often used as firestop as it is classified 
A2-s1, d0, which means that it has no contribution to a fire (non-combustible), that it has a very 
limited smoke production and doesn’t creates droplets within 600s (European Committee For 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
 
19 
 
Standardization 2009). Other products with the same classification could be plasterboards but it 
is rarely used as firestop in the insulation as it doesn’t insulate that well and would create a ther-
mal bridge.  
The thickness of a firestop is not specified with a specific number in the Danish guidelines for 
Building fire-safety, but it is mentioned that a thickness as the deck or separating wall often would 
be sufficient. 
Looking outside of Denmark, other countries have similar rules about firestops when flammable 
insulation is used. In Germany firestops must be established if a building is insulated using an ETICS 
system with EPS. The specifics of the firestop are depending on the thickness of the EPS insulation. 
If the EPS is 100 mm or less no firestop is necessary. If the thickness of the EPS is between 100 and 
300 mm a firestop of minimum 200 mm mineral wool with a fire class of A2-s1, d0, a minimum 
density of 60kg/m3 and a melting point larger than 1000°C (Bundesverband Ausbau und Fassade 
2016). This is more specific than what the Danish guidelines for Building fire-safety suggest.  
 
2.4 Insulation types 
In order to investigate firestops of different material it is necessary to have knowledge about the 
behavior of different insulation materials exposed to high temperature. The following four sec-
tions describe the production of four common different insulation types used in the building in-
dustries. The behavior of each of the four insulation materials in fire and heat is also reviewed. 
The insulation materials investigated is expanded polystyrene (EPS), polyurethane (PUR) and pol-
yisocyanurate (PIR) which all are organic insulations materials. An investigation of the inorganic 
insulation material stone wool is also carried out.   
 
2.4.1 Expanded polystyrene 
Expanded polystyrene (EPS) is a thermoplastic material, lightweight and closed-cell rigid foam 
plastic and was first invented in Germany by the engineers of IG Farbenindustrie AG in 1950 
(Bozsaky 2011). It consists of approximately 98% air and 1.5-2% polymerized polystyrol which re-
sults in a low density of approximately 15-20 kg/m3 (Papadopoulos 2005) (Eps n.d.).  
The production of EPS 
The manufacturing of EPS consists of three primary stages. The first stage is to expand the poly-
styrene beads to form a hollow sphere. The beads get expanded from a diameter of 0.5 to 1.3 mm 
to approximately 40-50 times its original volume. The expansion takes place in an enclosed vessel 
called a pre-expander, where the beads are heated to approximately 100°C, while being stirred 
constantly. It is in this stage of the manufacturing process the density of the EPS is controlled. The 
longer the beads are pre-expanded the larger the diameter of the expanded bead gets and the 
density lowers and vice versa. Pentane is used as the propellant gas during the expansion process 
and in order to improve the fire-resistance of EPS it is mixed with a percentage of 5-7% of hexan-
bromcyclododecan which is a flame retarder (Papadopoulos 2005). The pre-expanded beads are 
then released into a bed dryer where all condensed steam moisture is dried from the surface. 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
The next stage is the maturing phase where the pre-expanded beads are stored in silos for ap-
proximately 24 hours in order to stabilize the beads.   
The final stage of the manufacturing process is the molding stage. The expanded and matured 
beads are pumped into a mold, where the beads are steamed in order to softening and expand 
the beads further. The constraining effect of the mold makes the softened beads fuse together at 
the right temperature. The fused blocks are then cooled under vacuum in order to remove the 
moisture from the blocks. When the cooling is done the blocks are ejected from the mold and can 
be cut in the right dimensions  (ACH Foam Technologies 2015) (Baker n.d.) (Anon 2017). 
 
Figure 10 - Expanded polystyrene (Anon n.d.) 
Thermal properties of EPS 
EPS is a thermoplastic material that is very flammable and produces a large quantity of black 
smoke when burning. It reacts to relatively low temperatures and begins melt which creates a risk 
of droplets during fire (European Manufacturers of EPS 2002). This results in a low fire class. EPS 
comes in the two different fire classes E and F, where F is a standard and the most typical one 
while the E class is a special fire-retardant product (Sundolitt n.d.).  
Different studies on how EPS reacts in different temperatures have been made and according to 
(Baker n.d.) EPS will begin to soften at a temperature of 90°C for short term temperatures and 
80°C for long term temperatures. Until it reaches a temperature of approximately 150°C, the EPS 
will shrink and return to its original density as a solid polystyrene. A further heating of the EPS will 
melt it into a liquid and when reaching above 200°C it will start turning into a gas. These gasses 
are in the risk of being ignited when the temperatures reach between 360 and 380°C and will self-
ignite at 500°C. This is close to what other sources indicates such as (European Manufacturers of 
EPS 2002) (Eps n.d.). According to (European Manufacturers of EPS 2002) (Eps n.d.) EPS will soften 
at a temperature of 100°C, which is a bit higher than the 80-90°C according to (Baker n.d.). If the 
EPS is exposed to fire and flames it will ignite at a temperature of 350°C for standard EPS and a 
temperature of 370°C for a fire retardant EPS (European Manufacturers of EPS 2002) (Eps n.d.). 
According to (European Manufacturers of EPS 2002) (Eps n.d.) standard EPS will self-ignite if it 
reaches a temperature of 450°C while the temperature is 50°C higher for fire-retardant EPS. Figure 
11 shows a schematic overview of the ignition temperature of EPS.  
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
21

Other studies and experiments shows that the heat of combustion of EPS is about 40-45 MJ/kg

When burning EPS a large and dense black smoke is created and results in carbon monoxide, mon-

Production of PUR 
PUR are a much more complex material than EPS and consist of very large polymers with repeating 
urethane linkage, produced by the reaction between isocyanate (-N=C=O) with compounds con-
taining at least two active hydrogen atoms (Chattopadhyay & Webster 2009). PUR might also be-
sides the urethane linkage, contain aliphatic and aromatic hydrocarbons, esters, amides, disubsti-
tuted urea, biuret, allophanate, isocyanurate, uretidione and carbodiimide groups (Singh & Jain 
2008). The many different groups that PUR can contain, create a large number of different PUR 
foams with different properties.  
The industrial production of PUR starts by having the raw materials separate in each tank where 
it is hold liquid. The right amount of each liquid raw material is then mixed with a typical ratio of 
one time polyol to two times diisocyanate.  
After the mixing, the raw materials are heated to a reactive level as they are pumped into pipes. 
In the pipes, the polymerization reaction occurs and the PUR is then formed before it reaches the 
end of the pipes where a dispenser head is placed. To produce rigid polyurethane foam insulation, 
the PUR foam is dispensed from the pipes onto some sort of baking paper.  
As the polyurethane foam is dispensed and mixed with carbon dioxide it causes a reaction that 
expands the polyurethane foam. The foam is then formed into the right size and geometry of a 
desired rigid polyurethane foam insulation board.   
Thermal properties of PUR 
Several experiments and test have been conducted in order to see how PUR behaves during fire 
and heating. Among some of these test (Jiao et al. 2013) have performed a thermogravimetry 
analysis (TGA) and differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) study of PUR in nitrogen atmosphere 
(N2) and air atmosphere with a heating rate of 10°C/min. These analyses showed a two-stage ther-
mal degradation in nitrogen atmosphere and a three-stage thermal degradation in air atmos-
phere. There is a mass loss of about 3% for both atmospheres between 100 and 190°C which is 
believed to be the evaporation of moisture. The second stage was found at 340°C for the nitrogen 
atmosphere and 314°C for air atmosphere and is seen as the main mass loss degradation stage. It

2.4.4 Stone wool 
Stone wool is a mineral wool product which means that it is an inorganic fibrous material. Stone 
wool is considered thermally stable as it can be used in very high temperatures up to 600°C and 
in some cases up to 1000°C (Karamanos et al. 2008). The fact that it is an inorganic fibrous material 
makes it an incombustible material and resolves in a fire class A, which is the highest fire resistance 
according to the European 13501-1 standard (Stec & Hull 2011). Stone wool was first invented by 
the American chemical engineer Charles Corydon Hall in 1897 (Bozsaky 2011). Stone wool can 
have a higher density than EPS, PUR and PIR but is often more flexible.  
Production of stone wool 
The main component of stone wool is volcanic rock. The process begins with preparing a mixture 
of raw material which typically consists of amphibolite and some additives such as limestone and 
various calcium oxides. The mixture can vary depending on the type of stone wool produced and 
the manufacturer. After the preparing of the mixture it is led into the top of a furnace oven which 
is heated to 1450-1520 °C which makes the mixture melt into a lava form. The melted mixture is 
led into a perforated spinning machine with microscopic holes. As the spinning machine have a 
high rotation speed (of approximately 700 rpm) the mixture is centrifuged and pressed through 
the microscopic holes in the spinning machine. As the mixture gets through the holes it cools to 
the ambient temperature and becomes solid and shapes as fibers with a diameter of 6-10µm 
(Bozsaky 2011). The next step is adding special resin which function as binder. The resin used in

3 HYPOTHESES 
Based on the technical review above it seems plausible that a firestop of stone wool can stop a 
fire from spreading. But what thickness should a firestop of stone wool have? Would stone wool 
with a density of about 30-45 kg/m3, which is the density of a typical insulation batt, be able to 
function as a firestop?  
Also, could another material such as PIR function as a firestop even though it has a lower fire 
classification than stone wool? PIR is much more compact than stone wool and thereby more air 
tight. Furthermore, it has a lower thermal conductivity than stone wool. Hence, heat transfers 
slower through it than stone wool and thereby dampens the temperature increase above the fire-
stop. However, PIR is a combustible material and have the risk of catching fire if the temperature 
reaches above 415°C which could add fuel to facade fire. 
In order to answer these questions this project will test firestops made of PIR and stone wool with 
a density of about 30-45 kg/m3 in an EPS insulation fire. Two tests for each material will be per-
formed with two different thicknesses of 100 mm and 300 mm.  
The hypothesis is that a firestop of 300 mm stone wool will be able to hinder the fire from spread-
ing. In Germany only 200 mm of stone wool is required as a firestop. However, the density re-
quirements are higher than 30-45 kg/m3. Hence the test of 300 mm stone wool. It is more ques-
tionable whether the test with 100 mm stone wool will be able to function as a firestop. It may 
however be able to dampen the fire spreading to a degree that increase the fire safety to a satis-
fying level. 
A firestop of 300 mm PIR is believed to be able to dampen the fire spread as it would take some 
time to burn through the firestop. Furthermore, the literature review showed that the material 
was difficult to ignite. Again, it is more questionable whether the 100 mm PIR firestop would func-
tion. This will be tested below.

4.1 Flame spread test 
The aim of the flame spread test is to determine the upward and downward flame spread on a 
vertical surface of different combustible insulation materials. A high flame spread of a given ma-
terial can have fatal consequences and is a very important component in the fire hazard assess-
ment (W. Leung 2015). The test will also give an indication of the flammability of the material. 
 
4.1.1 Theory of flame spread 
In order to obtain a flame spread an ignition of the material have to be performed. This is often 
modeled as a series of piloted ignitions, which makes the area adjacent to the flame front reaches 
a critical ignition temperature and ignite. This pyrolysis area is heated by gas phase convection 
from the flame itself, as well as from the incident heat flux from any external heat source.  
After the ignition of the material an investigation of the flame spread can be conducted. Flame 
spread is a process where the fire grows and the flame moves across the surface. Depending on 
the direction of flame travel it is divided into forced (horizontal) or buoyancy (vertical) driven flow. 
The speed of flame spread and how it develops depends on the gravitation and wind condition 
during the fire (Quintiere 1998).  
In this experiment two kinds of flame spread on a vertical surface will be tested. The tests depend 
on the direction and place of ignition. One test is the downward flame spread where the flame 
spread is occurring in the direction opposite to a directed flow of the environment and gravity 
(Quintiere 1988) (Hasemi 2016). This kind of flame spread leaves no flame extension over the 
unburnt area as the flame either is over the already unburned area or perpendicular to it. The 
scenario is shown on Figure 17.

4.2 Heat of combustion test 
The heat of combustion is the energy released when the material undergoes a complete combus-
tion. The heat of combustion can be used to quantify the performance of a material in a combus-
tion system. Heat of combustion can be investigated using different methods. The most common 
methods are by using a bomb calorimeter or by using a cone calorimeter. A bomb calorimeter is 
used for finding the total heat of combustion while the effective heat of combustion can be found 
using a cone calorimeter. The total heat of combustion gives a more conservative result as it is a 
result of a total combustion and usually gives a higher value. The effective heat of combustion 
usually gives a smaller and more realistic value as it is performed in normal air and often does not 
have a total combustion, which often is the case with a real fire.  
 
4.2.1 Experimental setup 
For investigating the heat of combustion of EPS, PUR and PIR a bomb calorimeter is used. A bomb 
calorimeter consists of a small metal vessel also called a bomb. The bomb is an airtight container 
with a crucible holder for the testing material and an ignition wire to ignite the content. The bomb 
is shown on Figure 20.

5.1.3 Evaluation of uncertainty of results 
During any experiment, different kinds of uncertainty or errors can occur which could influence 
the outcome of the tests. Some of these uncertainties which may have occurred when conducting 
the flame spread experiments, are listed below.  
The fact that it was conducted with a piloted flame from a lighter can be seen as an uncertainty. 
If the lighter is not removed or put out the exact same time the ignition happens, the flame spread 
rate can be increased by the heat from the lighter.  
The measurement of the position of the flame can also be recognized as an uncertainty. The po-
sition was conducted manually with a ruler next to the holder with the material. Even though the 
experiment was recorded on video and you have the chance to view it again and again it was 
difficult to see the exact position, as the number on the ruler wasn’t always that clear which could 
lead to an approximate position instead of a precise position.  
Last as the experiment was conducted indoor at the DTU fire lab, it had to be carried out under 
an exhaust hood in order to remove the poisoning fumes. The exhaust hood could possibly have 
affected the flame spread velocity. For the upward flame spread velocity the rate would have 
increased and for the downward flame spread the rate would have decreased because of the up 
driven wind aid from the exhaust hood.  
Some of these uncertainties would be eliminated or reduced by increasing the number of experi-
ments. The more experiments conducted would give a more correct result as it would statistical 
more precise. As the test was repeated three times for each material and all showed similar 
tendencies it is believed that he results are representative.

The fact that the four tests were carried out at an outdoor facility at four different times could be 
one of the reasons for the different fires. When making tests outdoor the weather will automati-
cally be a source of uncertainty and might have an influence on the results. If the tests were con-
ducted at an indoor facility the test conditions could easier be managed and uncertainty could be 
reduced. The four tests were performed over two days in December 2017, and thus different 
weather conditions for each test. Test one and two where performed on a day where it was quite 
windy and clouded. As the day went on and test two was being carried out it started to rain.  Test 
three and four were conducted on clear day with sun and with less wind compared to the first 
day. These small differences in weather conditions at each test could have been avoided if the 
==PAGE:54==
54 
 
testing took place at an indoor facility or if all four tests were performed at the same time. This 
would give results which would be more correct to compare.  
Another factor that could influence the test results is that the first test each day could have pre-
heated the concrete wall before the second test. Hence, in the second test the concrete would 
consume less of the energy from the fire, which instead would be affecting the firestop. However, 
a cooling time of 90 minutes between the two tests, when rebuilding the test setup, was present 
each day. This time is assumed to be enough to cool the wall. After the first test the concrete wall 
had a temperature of about 34 degrees according to the thermographic camera and about 50 
degrees after the third test.  
Even though the four tests are all carried out using the same test setup, small differences will 
undoubtedly occur between them. At the first test all of the materials were brand new and un-
damaged, while with the three other tests some of the materials were reused. After each test the 
first layer of plasterboard in the side and front plasterboard was replaced as it was too damaged 
from the previous fire. However, the second layer of plasterboard in the sides and the battens 
may have some damage which could resolve in cracks as seen in test three. However, looking at a 
real ETICS facade wall cracks would probably occur over time and the strength of the rendering 
would decrease. If a fire occurs in an older ETICS facade the fire would probably easier break 
through the rendering as it did in test three.   
Looking at how all the thermocouples were placed in the middle, more thermocouples placed in 
the sides would give a better picture of how much heat would travel between the firestop and the 
sides. This could help give a more clear answer regarding the amount of heat that travelled up the 
sides of the firestop in test two and whether it were close to ignite the EPS or if it was kept at 
melting level.  
If one compares the firestop test to the existing facade test standards like SP fire 105, BS 8414-1 
and DIN E 4201-4, it is seen that the amount of possible energy release is way lower. The total 
energy release of the EPS and thereby the amount of energy affecting the firestop in the four tests 
were calculated to be about 300 MJ. Looking at the British and Swedish standard the ignition fire 
had a total energy release of about 4500 MJ and 1830 MJ, which is way more than what is achieved 
during the tests in this project. However, a big difference between the tests is that the standards 
are simulating a room fire breaking out of a window affecting a total facade system and not just a 
firestop. The facade system is then affected outside of the cladding and eventually breaking so 
insulation caught fire. The situation simulated in this report is a case where the insulation already 
has caught fire and is spreading inside the cladding away from the ignition fire.  
The scale of the test is also a lot smaller than the test standard specimen. The Swedish standard 
SP fire 105 have a wall section of 4000 mm x 6000 mm and the British standard BS 8414-1 is 2600 
mm x 6000 mm above the fire chamber. If comparing the test setup to a real wall system where 
the firestop is supposed to be at each floor separation it is seen that the amount of burning EPS 
exposing the firestop in this project is a bit less than it could be. According to the Danish building 
regulation a room should have a minimum height of 2500 mm which this would be the height of 
the insulation below the firestop and thereby have a total energy release of about 430 MJ. The 
height of the insulation below the firestop in this project was 700 mm lower and had a total energy 
release of about 300 MJ.

6 CONCLUSION  
This project addresses the overall subject of preventing or dampen fire spread in combustible in-
sulation with a firestop. The problem has become topical as the use of combustible insulation have 
increased and several facade fires with the base of combustible insulation has occurred in recent 
time. The project focuses on possible types material and the thickness of the firestop.     
A literature study and two initial experiments - a flame spread and heat of combustion test - were 
conducted to investigate the properties of different insulation materials.  
The flame spread test was conducted on three combustible insulation materials; EPS, PUR and 
PIR. The test for EPS showed that the upward flame spread moves with higher velocity than the 
downward flame spread. This is in line with theory. The velocity of the upward flame spread was 
measured to 15 mm/s, while the downward test showed a velocity of 1.5 mm/s. The test for PUR 
and PIR failed as the materials did not ignite. In order to catch fire PUR and PIR needs to be ex-
posed to high temperatures over a longer period. The results indicate that both PUR and PIR could 
be used as possible firestops.  
The heat of combustion tests shows the potential release of energy when the materials combust. 
The test showed that EPS have a higher amount of energy release compared to both PUR and PIR. 
The energy release of EPS was measured to 44.94 MJ/kg while the energy release of PUR and PIR 
was measured to 28.70 MJ/kg and 27.62 MJ/kg respectively. The results are all in line with the 
literature review. This indicates that EPS could resolve in a larger fire than PUR and PIR when the 
materials have the same weight. However, the density of the tested EPS was half of the density of 
the tested PUR and PIR. Hence, the energy release of a m3 of each tested material would not be 
significantly different. 
Based on the literature review and the two initial tests firestop tests with PIR and stone wool was 
conducted. The two materials were tested with two different thicknesses of 100 mm and 300 mm.  
The Firestop tests were overall a success in the sense that all firestops prevented the fire from 
spreading to the EPS above the firestop. However, there were indications of possible issues with 
some of the firestops. 
The firestop test with 3 x 100 mm PIR as firestop showed a maximum temperature of about 50 
degrees 100 mm inside the firestop. As EPS shrinks at 100 degrees, flash ignites at 350 degrees 
and self-ignites at 450 degrees the result indicates that 100 mm PIR as a firestop might be enough. 
However, the test showed problems with the fitting of the material. It is difficult to fit the material 
so no gabs occur. Hence, soot was found on the EPS above the firestop. The maximum tempera-
ture above the firestop peaked at about 10 degrees and was not even close to affect the EPS.   
The firestop test with 100 mm PIR was exposed to a fire with a peak heat of 302 degrees. The 
maximum temperature above the firestop reached a maximum temperature of about 60 degrees 
which is 40 degrees below the softening temperature of EPS. The test showed the same problems 
with fitting of the material. As the thickness of the firestop only was 100 mm enough heat travelled 
through the gabs and melted some of the EPS above the firestop.  
==PAGE:56==
56 
 
The test with 3 x 100 mm stone wool acting as firestop showed a much quicker temperature in-
crease in the firestop than the test with PIR. The firestop was exposed to a peak temperature of 
about 343 degrees. The temperature above the firestop peaked around 45 degrees while the tem-
perature 100 mm inside the firestop reached close to 175 degrees. Since stone wool is more flex-
ible is was easier to fit the material so no gabs occurred. As a result, no soot or other visible signs 
was found on the EPS above the firestop.  
The test with 100 mm stone wool as firestop was exposed to fire that peaked around 246 degrees. 
The maximum temperature above the firestop was about 77 degrees which was low enough to 
hinder fire from spreading. Once again stone wool showed its ability to fit to the construction so 
no gabs occurred.  
To sum up, the overall impression is that all four firestops was functional. However, problems with 
the fitting of PIR resulted in heat and soot reaching the EPS above the firestop. If the PIR could be 
fitted without gabs, the material could be used as a possible firestop, at least when the fire only 
reaches the temperatures in the tests. The fire at each of the four tests acted very different from 
each other. It is a possibility that the firestop with 100 mm stone wool would fail if it was exposed 
to the same fire as the test with 300 mm stone wool, where the peak temperature was higher.
