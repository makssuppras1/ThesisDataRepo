ABSTRACT 
This work investigates deployment of a Cloud-Radio Access Network (C-RAN). C-RAN is a cloud computing 
based network architecture, approached for upgrading current network technologies and deploying future 
5G networks. The work introduces C-RAN and the benefits and possibilities it leads to. Decisions to consider 
and different deployment options are introduced and these leads to 4 steps of considerations when 
deploying C-RAN. This work has derived a method for ranking cells compared to each of their neighbor cells, 
for how well this combination of cells will benefit from implementing C-RAN. The method is derived based 
on what benefits a cell implementing C-RAN will achieve. These benefits include: better inter-cell corporation 
and resource sharing. Based on this, a pair of cells which will benefit the most from implementing C-RAN will 
have: Low inter-site distance, high interference, different peak times, high peak to average ratio and low 
average traffic. The cell ranking method is expanded to a pool ranking method, where cells are ranked based 
on how well they will benefit from being incorporated in a specific BBU pool. The work introduces a case 
study on TDC’s network where an area is evaluated. The work establishes roadmap using the cell ranking 
method, to determine which cells will benefit the most from implementing C-RAN. Results shows that when 
pooling the right cells together, chosen based on the cell ranking method, then a high multiplexing gain is 
achieved.

INTRODUCTION 
In 2016, 83% of all Danish homes owned at least one smartphone1. Smartphones have become a huge part 
of the daily life, and when the smartphone is not near many people feel like they are missing something 
essential. Smartphones are used as GPS when driving, for emails and as a piece of toy to play games, just to 
mention a few examples. But what really makes the smartphones very smart, is their wireless connection. A 
phone built to be carried around in the user’s pocket would not be very smart, if it should be connected to a 
wire to be able to perform a call or connect to the internet. This is one of the reasons why mobile networks 
are very important.  Whether the users are in Australia or at Vesterhavet, they want to catch Pokemons or 
check in on Facebook.  
The users want to be online everywhere, and the smartphones capabilities just increase this demand. A lot 
of apps, easing daily tasks, require a good connection to the internet or other networks. A good connection 
requires both coverage everywhere and enough capacity to serve all users. This good connection expected 
by the users is a huge challenge for the network providers to overcome. The demand for mobile network’s 
coverage and capacity keeps increasing. Figure 1 confirms this statement: 
 
Figure 1: Increasing amount of mobile and mobile broadband subscriptions, users are going mobile. [22] 
The Ericsson mobility report [22] states clearly that users want mobility. They wat to be able to perform a 
call or browse on the internet everywhere. The amount of mobile subscriptions is increasing. And the amount 
of mobile broadband subscriptions is steeply increasing, where the fixed broadband subscriptions are much 
lower and almost constant. When more users enter the network, more capacity is required for all users to 
achieve the good connection they want. This call for a challenge in the mobile networks as we know them. 
Mobile networks are necessary for the users to be mobile, meaning they are able to move around while using 
their smartphones. Mobile networks use the airborne radio frequency (RF) waves to exchange information 
between user’s entities (UEs) i.e. smartphones, and the antennas connected to the network. The mobile 
networks in Denmark are owned by three different network providers. These network providers have

be connected to a core network where the data processing is handled through a packet switched (PS) or

A traditional BS has its radio and baseband functions located in a server room on the location of the antenna 
mast. It uses an amplifier to amplify the signal from the server room to the antenna. The cable connecting 
the amplifier and the antenna is a coaxial cable. A coaxial cable can have a high loss and therefore the 
transmission power from the amplifier needs to increase by the cable loss. BSs are the most expensive part 
of a wireless network [1]. Operators continually increase the number of BSs as a result of the increasing 
demand from users. This leads to a rise in power consumption and a higher operational expenditure (OPEX) 
[25], this cost includes renting a place for the server room. Another disadvantage of the traditional network 
architecture is, that it has no central management, the BSs and their controllers make individual decisions. 
In a traditional RAN a certain BS provides a certain coverage and capacity to a certain area. And this is not an 
optimal solution because user traffic is non-uniform. Mobile networks enable the users to move around. This 
leads to differences in the traffic distribution, because in a certain area the number of users are not static 
and they do not use a static amount of traffic. Different types of areas have different densities of subscribers. 
In an urban area people live closely in apartment buildings. In a rural area, the subscribers will be widely 
distributed in houses with hundreds of meters in between. The urban area can be divided into residential 
and office areas, both with tight subscriber density, but the traffic is peaking at different times during the 
day. Usually users will move from residential areas to office areas in the morning and back again in the 
afternoon. This leads to the residential area having busy hours in the morning and evening, and in the office 
area the traffic is peaking during the day. This is called the tidal effect [2]. The tidal effect is illustrated in

The coverage of one antenna is limited no matter what type of area it covers. This is because radio waves 
attenuate. In the lower frequency bands, the attenuation is lower and in the higher frequency bands the 
attenuation is higher which limits the range of communication. In a RAN, the sites can be classified into 
macrocells and microcells. Generally, the macrocells are placed in the lower frequency bands, and the 
microcells adds extra capacity within a certain area of the macrocell. Microcells have limited coverage by 
being in the higher frequency bands but higher capacity within this limited area. This way the macrocells will

ensure the overall coverage of an area and the microcells will add extra capacity where it is needed. 
Macrocells have in general three to six sectors [14] each covered by at least one antenna, sectorized antennas 
with three sectors are the most common solution. For each sector, several RATs on different bands can be 
present. Microcells have usually one omnidirectional antenna, and operates usually on higher frequencies.

Figure 5: Macrocell with extra capacity provided by microcells. 
Figure 5 shows a macrocell where extra capacity is added in certain areas using microcells. Microcells can be 
used to enhance the bitrates where the users are. In UMTS and LTE all cells use the same frequency, this 
leads to increased interference, especially at cell edges. In future 5G networks it is proposed that the bitrates 
shall continue increasing, up to 10 Gbps [14]. To achieve these very high bitrates the distances of radio 
transmissions need to be decreased, because it is necessary to use higher frequency bands to gain enough 
available bandwidth. The cells are getting smaller, and the BSs are moving closer to the users. The higher 
amount of small cells in future networks, will lead to more cell edges and higher user mobility among these 
smaller cells. This will further increase the interference. To face this interference problem Coordinated 
Multipoint Transmission and reception (CoMP), Enhanced Inter Cell Interference Coordination (eICIC) and 
Radio Resource Management (RRM) can be used to improve the network’s spectrum efficiency. These will 
reduce inter-cell-interference, increase system capacity and improve the wireless resource usage [2]. These 
technologies are introduced for LTE-A networks [1].  
- 
RRM 
Radio Resource Management (RRM) handles the cells resource scheduling, by coordinating the radio 
resources across multiple cells gathered in a cluster. Clusters are formed based on feedback from UEs. 
It is very important to choose the optimal approach in a certain cluster which includes performance 
gain, backhaul capacity and scheduling. How complex the scheduling needs to be, depends on the 
speed of the moving users, number of users and number of cells in the cluster. The complexity can be 
reduced by limiting the number of cells within a cluster [2]. 
- 
eICIC 
Enhanced Inter Cell Interference Coordination (eICIC) is an enhancement of ICIC where UEs can report 
back to the BS if they experience strong interference on certain sub-carriers. Then the BS coordinates 
with the neighboring cell to not use these sub-carriers for that particular mobile. It prevents 
interference in the frequency domain. eICIC prevents interference in the time domain, using Almost 
Blank Sub-frames (ABS) to create an interference free time interval for transmissions. ABSs contains no 
==PAGE:14==
6 
 
data except essential signals for legacy support. The deployment of small cells to achieve extra 
capacity, can lead to increased interference between the different cells and thereby reduced 
throughput [1].  
- 
CoMP 
Coordinated MultiPoint (CoMP) transmission and reception is a way of turning the interference from 
other cells into a useful signal. CoMP groups several cells into a CoMP-set where they will cooperate on 
serving one user or a group of users. Based on feedback from the UE, the CoMP-set is then improving 
the Signal-to-Interference-plus-Noise Ratio (SINR) meaning the quality of the signal [1]. 
CoMP can be divided into three subcategories, with different levels of advancement [1]: 
 
Coordinated scheduling and beamforming (least advanced) 
In this case, one UE receives transmission from one BS (called the serving cell) while the 
remaining BSs in the CoMP set aid in avoiding interference. They do that by avoid using 
particular sub-carriers (coordinated scheduling) and/or beamforming antennas (coordinated 
beamforming) [1].  
 
Dynamic Cell selection (more advanced) 
In this case, the data transmitted to one particular UE, is made available to all cells in a CoMP 
set. The cells coordinate which BS should do the actual transmission. This is advantageous 
because UEs can move around and can then be served by the most optimal cell [1]. Both 
coordinated scheduling and beamforming and dynamic cell selection requires the same level of 
BS synchronization [1]. 
 
Joint Transmission (most advanced) 
In this case, the data to be transmitted is also available to all cells in the CoMP set. But here 
several cells are jointly and coherently transmitting to one UE. In single user joint transmission, 
several cells send the same information to one UE with exact timing to allow the signals to 
combine coherently at the receiver and thus achieving a SINR gain. A higher SINR leads to lower 
jitter and higher bitrates, because it is then possible to use higher order modulation and less 
capacity on error correcting codes. The disadvantage of joint transmission is, that it takes up 
resources in several cells, and it requires very accurate feedback from the cells [1].

1.1 CLOUD-RAN ARCHITECTURE 
Cloud-RAN (C-RAN) is a cloud computing based network architecture, approached for upgrading current 
network technologies and deploying future 5G networks. It is a new RAN architecture that require changes 
in the existing networks. The C-RAN architecture can be introduced in all types of RATs, like GSM, UMTS or 
LTE. The C-RAN architecture can be used to deploy a new network or replace the existing infrastructure to 
achieve many advantages.   
In a RAN the BS is responsible for communication between the users and the network. A BS have two main 
functions: baseband processing and radio functionalities [1]. The sub-functions of baseband processing are 
the L1, L2 and L3 functions, and the sub-functions for the radio functionalities are digital processing, 
frequency filtering and power amplification [1]. The idea behind C-RAN is to divide these BS functionalities 
into a Remote Radio Head (RRH) handling the radio functionalities and a BaseBand Unit (BBU) handling the 
baseband processing. This separation is illustrated in Figure 6:

Figure 8 illustrates the network architecture in C-RAN which is expanded by a fronthaul part between the 
BBU pool and RRHs. The RRHs transmit the RF signals via the antenna to UEs in the downlink (DL) direction 
or forward the signals from UEs to the BBU pool for further processing in the uplink (UL) direction. The RRHs 
and the BBU pool are connected by a fronthaul data transmission link. In the BBU pool, all the server capacity 
is centralized in one place. This gives the advantage that only one location needs to be rented, compared to 
BS where the server needs to have a location at each site. If there is available BBUs in the pool then it is easy 
to install a RRH and an antenna, this increases the scalability in the network. The BBU pool clusters the 
processing resources from multiple RRHs and this leads to a higher processing power because all processing 
servers are in the same room. This way a better corporation among the cells within the BBU pool can be 
achieved. The BBU pool create resiliency for the processing part, because if the BBU serving a certain RRH 
breaks down, then this RRH can be served by another BBU within the BBU pool.   
To increase the bitrates in future networks, the cells are getting smaller and moving closer to the users. This 
will further increase the dynamics of the traffic in a specific cell, because if users are moving they will stay in 
the same cell for a shorter time. The serving RRH changes dynamically when the user moves into a new cell, 
but the serving BBU is most likely still in the same BBU pool. Because the coverage of a BBU pool is larger 
than the coverage of a traditional BS [25]. The corporation among BBUs in a BBU pool ensure a better cell 
corporation and interference management because they can talk to each other at Gbps speeds [25]. Signaling 
traffic can be exchanged among BBUs with low latencies, this is an advantage for CoMP, eICIC and RRM. 
CoMP, eICIC and RRM reduce the interference and ensures an effective cell corporation for example by 
coordinating the radio resources across the BBU pool. CoMP, eICIC and RRM are important tools for future 
5G networks with increasing bitrates and decreasing cell ranges because these functions negate the 
interference problem which more and smaller cells leads to. The mechanisms eICIC and CoMP will also help 
to improve the network performance by reducing delay during intra-BBU Pool handover [1]. The fast 
responses in the BBU pool can give great advantages for example when covering a railway track or highway 
where the users are moving very fast [1]. The effective cell corporation avoids the latency of the x2 interface 
signals looping in the backhaul network. Because the BBUs are in one place and can communicates with each 
other, without needing to go through the backhaul network. This is illustrated in Figure 9:

During daytime, the peak traffic period only covers a small period of the whole day. The traffic load within a 
cell is fluctuating depending on the users’ movements. Cells have data traffic patterns with traffic peaks and 
a lower traffic load in the remaining time. Therefore, if the capacity planning is based on the peak traffic load

static cell deployment known from the traditional RAN architectures is not optimal [17]. A virtualized BBU

decide whether activation of an extra RRH is necessary or not [6]. At night when a BS is idle and most of the

1.2 VIRTUALIZATION 
The actual concept of C-RAN, as the name cloud implies, depends on a virtualization of the BBU pool to 
expand the network management. Virtualization defines a customized software operating system on top of 
the network. Virtualization of the BBU pool is a way to enable the dynamic resource allocation, which C-RAN 
benefits from. This virtualization requires high performance, real time, low power signal processing [1]. 
Several virtualization approaches exist as it is not yet fully developed, two approaches are Software Defined 
Networks (SDN) and Network Functions Virtualization (NFV). SDN divides the network into a physical data 
plane and an overall control plane. The purpose of this control plane is to manage the routing decisions in 
the network, to decide which path the packets should follow. NFV virtualizes the functions in the network. 
The different protocols are examples of network functions which can be virtualized. This means that running 
a protocol gets independent of the hardware – a function can be run on an individual computer with available 
capacity, no matter which or where it is located. NFV can use the SDN approach to create an automated 
framework integrating the functions in the underlying network. By integrating the underlying functions using 
a SDN controller, a fully connected end-to-end system is established [10]. 
The network virtualization appears as virtual functions within the BBU pool who share the same hardware to 
execute different functions. This is illustrated in Figure 11:

1.3 SUMMARY 
C-RAN is a cloud computing based network architecture, which can be used for upgrading current network 
technologies and deploying future 5G networks. C-RAN is a new RAN architecture that can be introduced for 
all types off RATs. C-RAN divides the traditional BS into a RRH handling the radio functionalities and a BBU 
handling the baseband processing. Several BBUs can be clustered in one BBU pool. This BBU pool gives C-
RAN advantages in interference management, user mobility and cell corporation. The BBU pool benefits from 
RRM, CoMP and eICIC because of the higher processing power and low latencies among the BBUs when they 
are gathered in one place. This effective cell corporation can be a huge advantage for future networks where 
cells are getting smaller and the user mobility will increase. In C-RAN, as the name cloud implies, several 
functions can be virtualized and moved to the “cloud”, which enables shared processing among the 
hardware. Virtual BBUs are software instances which realizes the base station functions, in such way that a 
virtual BBU has the entire same networking properties as a physical BS. These virtual BBUs can be gathered 
in a virtual pool, where it is possible for several virtual operators to share the environment. The main 
motivators for network virtualization are customization, resource utilization and resiliency.

2 CONDITIONS FOR CLOUD-RAN 
Telecom operators are showing increased interest in C-RAN and the promising features of cell corporation 
enabled by the BBU pool. However, introducing C-RAN in a network also induces some challenges. Thus, this 
chapter discusses opportunities and things to consider when deploying C-RAN. It will be attempted to 
compare different technologies and options using tables and figures. The purpose of this chapter is to 
evaluate different solutions for C-RAN deployment, and list the options in a way where they can be used for 
further evaluations in the following chapters. 
2.1 SITE CONFIGURATION 
C-RAN can be introduced in all types of RATs, like GSM, UMTS or LTE. When deploying C-RAN for different 
technologies in the same site it is necessary to have a RRH and two data transmission links (UL and DL), 
available for each technology at each sector. The total number of RRHs depends on the number of RATs 
present in the cell, because each RAT needs its own RRH [14] and antenna, or several antennas in case of 
MIMO. For example, a BS with three sectors and five RATs in each sector will need 15 RRHs [7]. The RRHs 
require power supply which needs to be available at the antenna location. The RRH can be directly connected 
to the antenna or they can be connected using a coax cable. The RRH specifications describe which RATs the 
individual RRHs support at which frequencies and number of antennas. 
The distance between a RHH and the BBU pool can span up to 40 km due to processing and propagation 
delay, because the HARQ procedure in LTE requires fast responses [5]. Maybe this distance can be enhanced 
in future 5G networks, if 5G networks will not be limited by HARQ.  
In a virtualized C-RAN every single BBU is a virtual machine, gathered with other BBUs in a datacenter called 
the BBU pool. Using this approach, additional processing power can be added as additional blades or server 
racks which makes it possible for BBUs to be dynamically instantiated and relocated according to load. The 
connection between BBUs must be reliable, have low latency, have high speed and support real-time 
transmission in the order of Gbps [1].  
Moving to a cloud based network requires a competence change from the network operators point of view. 
The network operators must become cloud service providers. Experienced cloud administrators are needed 
to configure, update and troubleshoot. The software is now the primary area of concern and cost, because it 
can be implemented on top of cheap generic computing and networking hardware [10]. The virtualization 
based on software requires implementation of a real-time processing algorithm [2]. Network operators need 
to be capable of selecting and integrating different virtualized solutions, or rely on their provider for a 
complete solution [10].

data types depending on the functionality split chosen. CS transmission creates a dedicated connection 
between the BBU and RRH, where the link is occupied all the time. PS transmission operates after the 
principle “send when you have something to send” which enables sharing of resources, but this also requires

CPRI has specified both an electrical and an optical transmission interface. Transmission of three distinct 
ﬂows is enabled over a single CPRI link, with the goal of covering all data transfer needs between the RRH 
and BBU. These three flows are user data, general control & management and synchronization. User data is

management plane [13]. Further, a frame check sequence shall be provided for bit error detection on L2 in 
the control & management plane [13].

2.4 DATA TRANSMISSION LINK TYPES 
The fronthaul network connecting the RRHs and BBU pool consists of a data transmission link. On this link, it 
is sufficient to provide high bitrates. The capacity in the fronthaul link is a matter of great importance when 
considering C-RAN deployment. The physical connections in the fronthaul network can be implemented using 
a fiber connection or other wired solutions, it can be wireless or it can be a mixture of wired and wireless 
solutions [5]. This section aims to provide an overview of different options for fronthaul transmission link 
deployment or upgrade. Different wired and wireless technologies and deployment options are presented 
for the purpose of getting an overview of some of the available solutions. 
2.4.1 
Technologies 
Examples of different technologies to be used for transmission between the RRH and BBU are listed. The 
technologies are divided into physical mediums and correlating L1/L2 technology. 
2.4.1.1 
Fiber 
Fiber have a large BW and is thereby the preferred option for fronthaul links in C-RAN [11]. Fiber can be 
difficult to deploy because a wire needs to be dig into the ground, and this can be a challenge for example in 
a residential area. The transmission delay for a fiber link is approximately 100 μs per 20 km [11]. The work in 
[5] shows that it is not economically feasible to deploy fiber in coverage areas larger than 20 km2. 
- 
WDM 
Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM) is a way to save fiber resources. Using WDM several signals 
are multiplexed into a single fiber using different wavelengths. Therefore, WDM can associate one 
wavelength channel pair (to cover both UL and DL) to each link. WDM needs management of the 
wavelength assignment, this can be achieved by implementing transceiver modules in the RRH and BBU 
ports [14]. This solution is beneficial for scenarios with limited fiber resources because 40-80 optical 
wavelengths can be transmitted in one fiber. Though upgrading from dark fiber to WDM can be costly 
[1]. If necessary, compression of the signal can save wavelength resources, but increases latency and 
jitter [14].   
 
CWDM 
Coarse WDM (CWDM) is a simple and cost effective solution with “passive” monitoring. CWDM 
can be a good option for short term fronthaul deployments. This solution is very reliable and 
has 18 channels with 20 nm channel spacing, this can be a limitation for larger cells with 10 or 
more RRHs because they need both UL and DL fibers. The optics color needs to be aligned 
which can cause extra management [7]. 
 
DWDM 
Dense WDM (DWDM) can be used for larger aggregate transport requirements [14] it is 
protocol- and bitrate-independent. This solution has colorless transceivers and a high number 
of available wavelengths. DWDM offers better spectral efficiency than CWDM. DWDM have 
typically 100 GHz or 200 GHz channel spacing. A pure DWDM fronthaul support high bitrates 
but the transmitter cost is very high, and it has strict temperature requirements [7]. 
- 
Ethernet  
Ethernet is a packet based L2 fronthaul solution. Ethernet provides speeds up to 100 Gbps by 
transmitting 25 Gbps over four wavelength channels [3]. Ethernet includes native OAM which is a great 
advantage for fault monitoring and maintaining. Ethernet switches have no synchronization among 
them, which is a challenge for C-RAN.  
 
Gb Ethernet 
Describes various technologies for transmitting Ethernet frames at 1 Gbps bitrate. 
==PAGE:39==
31 
 
 
10Gb Ethernet 
Describes various technologies for transmitting Ethernet frames at 10 Gbps bitrate. 
 
Carrier Ethernet  
Carrier Ethernet is an Ethernet based transport network solution offering carrier grade 
transport and traffic engineering providing quality of service [1]. 
- 
OTN  
Optical Transport Network (OTN) allows several tributaries on one wavelength. This solution is 
beneficial for scenarios with limited fiber resources because 40-80 optical wavelengths can be 
transmitted in one fiber. OTN efficiently supports both Ethernet and CPRI. CPRI can be transported over 
the low level optical channel data unit container. Though upgrading from dark fiber to OTN can be 
costly [1]. 
- 
PON 
Passive Optical Network (PON) has in general low bandwidth. Never standardizations such as NG-PON2 
offers 10 Gbps both UL and DL. In PON interfaces the TDMA solution requires a ranging time window 
when a new optical network unit is connected. PON interfaces also have issues with jitter [11]. 
2.4.1.2 
Wireless  
- 
MWR 
Micro Wave Radio (MWR) is a good solution when fiber is too hard to deploy. Typically, MWR solutions 
offer from 10 to 100 Mbps range. When radio waves propagate from transmitter to receiver, their 
intensity decreases. MWR can run over different L2 protocols: 
 
Hybrid MWR 
This is a dual TDM/Ethernet solution, approached for GSM/UMTS networks [23]. 
 
Packet MWR 
Packet MWR uses Ethernet for handling traffic flows, approached for HSPA and LTE networks 
[23]. 
- 
mmW 
Millimeter Wave (mmW) bands are located in the range from 30 to 300 GHz, here the available 
bandwidth is within 1 GHz, which makes it possible to achieve bitrates up to 100 Gbps [9]. The path loss 
at theses frequencies is very high (30 dB more at 60 GHz than at 2 GHz) and the signals are attenuated 
even further by atmospheric absorption, rain and leaves. Beamforming and spatial multiplexing from 
multi-element antenna arrays can compensate for the path loss [6]. 
- 
E-band MWR 
The E-band is located in the range from 70 to 80 GHz. The E-band is already lightly licensed and 
provisioned to allow up to 5 GHz of contiguous bandwidth [6]. It can provide capacity in the range of 
Gbps [1]. 
- 
Relaying 
In this solution the backhaul technology operates on the same frequency band and technology as the 
access link does. The range is up to a few km – like the access link, and so is also the interference. The 
capacity is within a few hundred Mbps. This solution can introduce more interference or reduce the 
bandwidth [3]. 
 
 
==PAGE:40==
32 
 
2.4.1.3 
MWR + fiber 
Here outlying BSs or microcells are connected to the nearest fiber connected C-RAN site using MWR, instead 
of being connected to the BBU pool directly. This solution can be beneficial if it is too hard to deploy fiber in 
the current area, and a fiber connected site is closer than the BBU pool is [5]. 
2.4.1.4 
Cobber 
- 
xDSL 
The Digital Subscriber Line (DSL) describes a variety of access technologies using cobber wires. xDSL 
technologies can offer bitrates between 8 Mbps (ADSL) and 100 Mbps (VDSL2 30a). These bitrates are 
low therefore, the xDSL solutions can be a candidate for small cells deployed by shops or companies for 
better indoor coverage, if the xDSL access is already available [3].  
- 
Ethernet 
Ethernet with bitrates up to 1 Gbps can run over cobber. 
2.4.1.5 
Unified fixed and mobile access  
This solution can provide both Passive Optical Network (PON) services and CPRI transmission on the same 
fiber. It is based on CWDM technology and combines a fixed broadband and mobile access network 
transmission with passive optical technology. This leads to an optical fiber supporting up to 14 different 
wavelengths, where the UL and DL channels are transmitted on different wavelengths, and CPRI can be 
transmitted on the remaining free wavelengths. This solution can be used for C-RAN deployment of indoor 
coverage [2].  
2.4.2 
Deployment  
- 
Dark fiber 
Dark fiber describes unused fibers which are already dig into the ground. Dark fiber is a beneficial 
solution because in this case the network provider has already deployed the fiber resources. These 
fibers can have been deployed previously when another fiber was deployed, because fibers are not 
particular expensive, but the deployment of fibers can be quite expensive! Therefore, network 
operators can deploy an extra fiber for future use, when they dig a hole in the ground to deploy the 
fiber they need at that particular time. As this solution is just a wire in the ground it includes no OAM, 
and it is probably not resilient depending on the deployment [1]. When choosing dark fibers, the 
operator needs to expect expenses for an upgrade to for example WDM. 
- 
Topology 
The fronthaul network can be deployed in different topologies; star, ring or mesh. The star topology 
connects the BBU pool and each RRH by a single link. The ring provides a more resilient solution where 
several RRHs are connected to the BBU pool in a ring structure, this makes each RRH connected in two 
ways. The mesh is a resilient solution where each RRH is connected to all other RRHs and the BBU pool, 
the mesh network can also be partially with less connections. A rule of thumb is: The more connections, 
the more equipment is needed, the more expensive will the deployment get. The operator need to 
weigh the network resiliency compared to deployment expenses when choosing the fronthaul 
topology.

2.6 SUMMARY 
When deploying a C-RAN network, several aspects must be considered. Among these aspects are: The 
functionality split, fronthaul interface and fronthaul link type. Where should the functional split between the 
BBU and RRH be? This work suggests three different options: split A with only the filter, ADC/DAC and 
up/down conversion in the RRH, split B which is a split between symbol-level and sample-level, and split C 
which is a split between bit-level and symbol-level. The fronthaul interface between the RRH and BBU can 
carry either CS traffic or PS traffic. CS traffic carries out the framing in regular intervals using a fixed-rate 
fronthaul interface such as CPRI or OBSAI. CPRI link rates have a constant bitrate, this results in a low 
utilization of the fronthaul link. Split A is beneficial for CS transmission. The PS option is beneficial for splits 
B and C. These solutions give great advantages in resource utilization because they benefit from the statistical 
multiplexing and dynamic BBU allocation. The statistical multiplexing gain can lead to fewer BBUs, because 
the same BBU can possibly process data from more than one RRH. PS loads can be carried by different 
transmission technologies, for example Ethernet. If RRHs supporting CPRI have already been deployed, and 
a PS solution is wanted, the CPRI protocol can be carried over a PS network using a gateway, but this solution 
adds more overhead.  
The physical link between the RRH and BBU can be wired or wireless, but the maximum distance between 
the BBU pool and a RRH is 40 km. Cobber technologies have in general low bitrates and low ranges. Fiber 
technologies have in general high bitrates and long ranges. The wireless technologies vary a lot in bitrates 
and range. Due to the higher attenuation of radio waves in higher bands, where higher bitrates can be 
achieved, the technologies in higher bands have low ranges.

3 CRITERIA FOR CLOUD-RAN DEPLOYMENT   
One of the main challenges a network operator faces, is to decide whether the introduction or expansion of 
C-RAN, despite its initial capital expenditure, is beneficial for the network operation and the business case 
on the longer term. This chapter evaluates, when deployment of C-RAN is a good decision, and if C-RAN is 
already deployed, what is then the consequence of adding new cells to the BBU pool. To determine this, 
important aspects to consider are the benefits C-RAN introduces to the network: 
 
Higher processing power  
 
Enhanced interference management 
 
Better user mobility between cells 
 
Adaptable to non-uniform traffic 
 
Statistical multiplexing gain 
 
Effective cell corporation 
 
Enhanced energy efficiency 
The above bullet points summarize the benefits already described in chapters 1 and 2. Implementing C-RAN 
will have the following consequences for a cell: It will get an effective corporation with its neighbor cells in 
the BBU pool, this leads to better user mobility between cells and lower interference. It will maybe get a high 
statistical multiplexing gain when sharing its resources with its neighbor cells and it will be adaptable to non-
uniform traffic. Implementing a C-RAN solution will make the biggest change, if the cells in the network to be 
upgraded, suffers from high interference and too many unused resources at certain times of the day. The 
reason to upgrade cells with low use of resources is, that these cells will benefit the most from implementing 
C-RAN, and thereby the network provider will achieve most benefits by upgrading these cells.

3.1 C-RAN DEPLOYMENT  
C-RAN can be deployed either as greenfield deployment or as upgrade of an existing network. In this work, 
greenfield deployment is seen as pure greenfield with no dark fibers or other network devices present in the 
area. This situation makes it possible, if the economy allows, to choose the most optimal solution from the 
beginning. In theory, it does not make sense to first deploy a temporary solution and then exchange it by 
time. When a network is getting upgraded to C-RAN, it is important to consider: Can we use what is already 
deployed? And what is the consequence if we use what is already deployed?  
Deployment of C-RAN can be divided into four steps: 1. Network deployment, 2. Separating the BBU and

3.1.1 
Step 0 – 1: Greenfield network deployment  
Only a greenfield network deployment will go from step 0 to step 1, as this section describes. In this step, 
there is no network and no current users.  
Greenfield deployment 
The first thing to consider could be the type of area. Are the odds that there will be a lot of users or just a 
few passing by rarely. C-RAN is most beneficial in urban areas, but has also advantages when covering a 
highway or train rails. A rural area might only need a normal BS because it will not achieve many benefits 
from C-RAN. It will though be very rare to find a total greenfield deployment area in Denmark.  
In this step, the network operator must decide what RATs should be present in the area and plan what 
frequencies to use. The location of the antennas and RRHs must also be determined in this step. The antenna 
mast needs to be positioned in a place, where it will cover the area optimally. This can be on a rooftop or on 
the top of a mast or hill. It should also be considered if any microcells are necessary in the current area. 
Maybe there is a mall needing extra capacity during the day or a group of apartment buildings, which needs 
extra capacity in the evening. Radio frequency planning is not within the scope of this work.  
3.1.2 
Step 1 – 2: Separation of RRH and BBU 
This step describes what to consider when moving from step 1 to step 2, where the BS is split up into a RRH 
and a BBU. Here it is necessary to determine what functionality split to use between the BBU and RRH. For a 
cell ready for future networks, it is recommended to follow the 3GPP specification. The specification on 
release 14/5G networks will be released in June 20174.  
Greenfield deployment 
For greenfield deployments, split B is recommended, if the decision must be made right now. This is because 
only split A and B have full use of CoMP functions, and the RRH is simple compared to split C, having less 
functions in the RRH and easy upgrade and maintenance. Split B is chosen rather than split A because of the 
lower and non-constant bitrate on the fronthaul link.  
If the decision on functionality split can wait a few months, it is recommended to wait and follow the 
recommendations in the 3GPP specification.  
Network upgrade 
For a network upgrade, it is assumed that all BSs are already deployed, and the current antenna mast 
locations and RATs will be reused. But the big question is: Which cells will be advantageous to include in C-
RAN? Which cells should be upgraded, starting by separating the RRH from the BBU, and which should not? 
The beginning of this chapter has already discussed what benefits a cell will gain from implementing C-RAN. 
From these benefits, it can be concluded that for C-RAN it is an advantage to have neighboring cells in the 
same BBU pool, because then C-RAN will reduce the interference and enhance the corporation and user 
mobility among these neighbor cells. These considerations have led to a cell ranking method which will be 
derived in section 3.4. If a cell gets a good ranking, it is prudent to split up the RRH and BBU and start the C-
RAN implementation.

3.2 FRONTHAUL AND BBU POOL ASSEMBLING 
The fronthaul link and the BBU pool locations are closely related. This is because when the fronthaul link 
reaches its limitations, then the traffic from the RRH cannot be carried any longer and it is necessary to insert 
a BBU to process the traffic. The BBU pool’s location should be a central place with room for the servers. For 
a network upgrade, the location can for example be estimated by looking at the bigger aggregation points in 
the previous backhaul. Because a lot of fibers are already leading to this place and it is therefore already 
centralized. Another thing to consider, for both network upgrade and greenfield deployment, is the range of 
the fronthaul links. CWDM can reach more than the maximum distance between the RRH and BBU which is 
40 km. If 40 km is the radius of the BBU pool, then it can cover an area of 5,026.5 km2. This range is far more 
than enough to cover all of Copenhagen which area is 179.5 km2 [30]. The area of Denmark is almost 43,000 
km2 [30], so in theory, as a thought experiment, Denmark could be covered by 9 BBU pools.  
3.2.1 
Fronthaul links  
The range and capacity of the fronthaul link is of great importance, depending on which functional split is 
used. When the distances and bitrates are too large for the current link type’s capabilities, a new BBU pool 
needs to be added to process the traffic. Fiber can carry large bitrates over long distances. If it is impossible 
to deploy fibers in the current area, then MWR can also offer a range longer than the maximum distance 
within C-RAN, which is a huge advantage when for example an isolated area needs coverage. If it is not 
possible to deploy fiber and high bitrates are required, then MWR in the E-band can be used. This solution 
can offer high bitrates but the range is limited to a few km. The problem for all wireless link types suggested 
is, when the bitrate increases, then the link’s range decreases. As calculated in chapter 2, the bitrates on the 
fronthaul link will increase dramatically in future 5G networks. Several hundred Gbps are demanded for split 
C and for splits A and B it is several thousand Gbps!

3.2.3 
Statistical multiplexing gain 
The best statistical multiplexing gain is achieved when most resources can be shared. Resources can be 
shared if they are used at different times or if they are very low. In this case, processing of data traffic is the 
resource to share. Two cells can share the same processing powers, if they use it at different times, or if they 
use just a bit of the total capacity all the time. By this is meant that for two cells to share the same BBU they 
need to have traffic peak at different times and they should both have a low average traffic. 
The optimal combination of cells in the pool can be determined by finding the best multiplexing gain. To find 
this several approaches needs to be tested, because the multiplexing gain is individual for each combination 
of cells. The calculation of statistical multiplexing gain is based on different peak times, because when pooling 
cells together the most optimal use of resources will be when cells are peaking at different times. The 
multiplexing gain explains the link utilization improvement when the resources are statistical multiplexed. 
The multiplexing gain is calculated as:

𝑀𝑎𝑥(∑𝑜𝑓𝑓𝑖𝑐𝑒 𝑙𝑜𝑎𝑑+ ∑𝑟𝑒𝑠𝑖𝑑𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑖𝑎𝑙 𝑙𝑜𝑎𝑑)
 
Equation 6 
This formula is calculated by dividing all cells in the current area into office and residential cells. Whether a 
cell is residential or office can be determined by looking at the cells peak time of day. The cells having their 
peak within a certain time range defined as “office hours” are office cells, and the remaining cells are 
residential cells. Due to the tidal effect, the users will be in certain areas (office) during the day, and move to 
other areas (residential) in the evening. The numerator is calculated by summarizing all traffic data for office 
cells within a certain period of time, finding the peak, and adding the same for the residential cells. The 
denominator is found by adding the sum of office data to the sum of residential data and finding the peak.   
From [1] it is recommended to serve 20-30% office cells and 70-80% residential cells in one BBU pool.

3.3 ENERGY EFFICIENCY CONSIDERATIONS 
Energy in mobile networks are in some countries mostly spent on air conditioning [1], this is not the case in 
Denmark. In Denmark, the primary source for reduced power consumption is saved processing resources. 
Processing resources can in a PS based network be saved using statistical multiplexing, because several cells 
can share the same resources, if they are not using all the available resources themselves. The number of 
BBUs can potentially be decreased due to statistical multiplexing, because less BBUs are potentially needed 
to serve the same number of cells. Thereby some of the BBUs can be switched off and energy resources are 
then saved. By this is meant, the better the statistical multiplexing gain achieved is the more energy resources 
can be saved, because the more BBUs can be switched off. 
Another way to save energy resources is using traffic management to switch off unused or very low utilized 
BBUs, for example during night [1]. In C-RAN traffic management in the BBU pool makes it possible to load 
balance traffic by switching BBUs and RRHs on and off, when they are not needed [1]. BBU resources can 
potentially be saved during the night, because if there a low average traffic, then less BBUs are needed to 
serve the same number of cells.  
Another option to consider is to save power by using low powered small cells instead of macrocells. Since C-
RAN benefits from eICIC and CoMP techniques for interference management, then a higher density of RRHs 
can be implemented in one area. Hence, smaller cells with lower transmission power can be deployed. Small 
cells reduce the energy used for signal transmission, which can reduce the RAN power consumption [25].

3.4.1 
Inter-site distance 
The distance between two sites will give an idea of how much traffic will move in and out of the cell. It can 
also give an idea of the interference level, because if the cells are close to each other the interference will 
probably be higher. Inter-site distance and users moving from cell to cell are illustrated in Figure 33: 
BBU Pool
 
Figure 33: The figure illustrates different inter-site distances and users moving between cells. 
If cells are smaller, the inter-site distance is smaller. Then there will be more traffic at cell edges and between 
cells when users are moving around. As cells are getting smaller in future networks, then the number of users 
moving from cell to cell will increase. C-RAN introduces a better user mobility between cells due to the 
effective cell corporation. This is the reason why cells with a low inter-site distance will benefit more from 
implementing C-RAN than cells with a high inter-site distance will. In the cell ranking method the inter-site 
distance is referred to as ISD. It is defined as the distance between cell A and cell B measured in meters. 
3.4.2 
Interference 
The interference defines how much “noise” the RF signal suffers from. The more interference, the harder it 
is to detect the actual signal. The amount of interference is for example based on the number of neighbor 
cells, the amount of traffic in the neighbor cells and the inter-site distance. These inputs will increase the 
interference, which is especially a problem at cell edges in RATs where the cells use the same frequency, like 
UMTS and LTE. C-RAN benefits from eICIC and CoMP which reduces interference. This is the reason why cells 
who suffer from high interference will benefit the most from implementing C-RAN. In the cell ranking method 
interference is referred to as I, and it defines the average interference within cell A.

3.4.5 
Peak times 
The peak time is the time of the day where the traffic in one cell is peaking. Figure 34 illustrates how resources 
can be shared if the cells have different peak times. If the cells peak at the same time, then they are not able 
to share the same resources unless the peak is very small. It is of great importance that two cells do not peak 
at the same time, if the maximum possible multiplexing gain should be achieved within the BBU pool. The 
peak times are calculated by finding the number of hours between the peak in two neighboring cells. This 
number can maximum be 12 if two cells are peaking at for example 08:00 and 20:00. If two cells are peaking 
at 08:00 and 08:00 the number will be 0 and they will not benefit from statistical multiplexing.  
In the cell ranking method, peak times are referred to as PT. It defines the number of hours between peak in 
cell A and peak in cell B.

The question is though, are all inputs equally important? Should all inputs have the same weight in the 
calculation? A high PA is important because it improves the resource usage when the traffic is not uniform. 
A high I is important because it can show how much the cell improves when introducing C-RAN because of 
CoMP and eICIC. A high PT is important because cells in the same pool should not peak at the same time to 
achieve the best multiplexing gain. A low AT is important because then more cells can share the same 
resources. A low ISD is important because this will increase the I and the need for cell corporation. Based on 
these assumptions, this work assesses I and PT to be the most important parameters. This assessment is 
based on, if these parameters are high, it will show at huge improvement in the network when deploying C-
RAN. Enhancing the values for I and PT makes the possibility for cells with the wanted values in these 
parameters, more likely to get a higher ranking. I.e. a cell with high peak time and interference are more 
likely to get a higher ranking, than a cell with high peak to average ratio and low average traffic.  
PT has already been categorized in 1 and 2, that makes it easy to weigh. Therefore, this number is just 
multiplied with a constant, and if the PT is good then the constant is doubled. The input I is enhanced by 
multiplying the constant with ½, because the numerical value should be low if the interference is high.

3.4.7 
Pool ranking 
When the first two cells are pooled together, then a BBU pool has been established. When a BBU pool is 
established, the remaining cells can be ranked after how much they will benefit from joining this pool. This 
will be referred to as the pool ranking method. This requires a few changes to the input values in the cell 
ranking method. In the cell ranking method, cell A is ranked with respect to cell B. In the pool ranking method 
cell A is ranked with respect to the BBU pool. The BBU pool’s input values are calculated by calculating the 
average value of the cells already incorporated in the pool. These values will be referred to as: The average 
value of peak to average ratio in the pool (𝑃𝐴𝑝𝑜𝑜𝑙), and the average traffic in the pool (𝐴𝑇𝑝𝑜𝑜𝑙). PT is 1 if 
another cell in the pool have the same peak time as cell A, or 2 if no other cells in the pool have the same 
peak time. Then the method will look as per Equation 16:

Equation 16 
The method derived in Equation 16 determines how much cell A will benefit from being incorporated in a 
BBU pool. The purpose of the pool ranking method is, that if a cell with a high ranking is incorporated in the 
BBU pool, then the statistical multiplexing gain will be enhanced. If a cell with a low ranking is incorporated 
in the BBU pool, then the statistical multiplexing gain will remain the same or be lower. 
The inter-site distance has not been taken into account in Equation 16. Because all cells with a range below 
40 km from the BBU pool should be able to join the pool, if they can get a transmission link connection from 
the RRH to that specific BBU pool.  
The most optimal number of BBU pools within an area can be found using linear programming, which is not 
the scope for this work. But the most optimal number of cells within a BBU pool can be found using the 
statistical multiplexing gain. Because when the cells are ranked, the ranking also determines when the cells 
should be implemented in the BBU pool. This way the multiplexing gain can be calculated for different 
amounts of cells in the pool, and the number of cells achieving the highest multiplexing gain, is the optimal 
number of cells within that particular BBU pool.

3.5 SUMMARY 
This chapter provides criteria for implementing C-RAN in either a greenfield deployment scenario or a 
network upgrade scenario. This chapter introduces four steps to follow for C-RAN deployment. Moving from 
step number 1 to step number 2 describes what actions to take when going from the classic BS setup to 
separating the RRH from the BBU. In this step the most important decision is whether C-RAN should be 
implemented in the current cell or not. For this purpose, a method has been developed one to rank the cells 
compared to their neighbor cells, based on how well they will benefit from implementing C-RAN. Another 
thing to consider is, what functional split to choose. This work recommends offhand the use of split B, 
because of its advantages related to CoMP and simple RRH. The third step clusters the BBUs in BBU pools. In 
this step, it is necessary to find the optimal combination of cells within the BBU pool, for this purpose the 
pool ranking method has been derived. To find the optimal number of cells incorporated in the BBU pool, the 
statistical multiplexing gain can be used as a guideline. Another thing to consider in this step, is the 
transmission link between the RRH and BBU. This work recommends the use of a PS fronthaul network. The 
link type depends partially on what is already available for the operator, but the solution providing highest 
bitrates and longest ranges is fiber. If fiber is not available another solution can be wireless links, MWR for 
long ranges or mmW for high bitrates. This step also considers the BBU pools, when locating the BBU pools 
it must be considered where it is possible to place a server room, but also where the fronthaul links can reach 
which should be within 40 km. The fourth step considers the BBU pool virtualization.   
This work does also consider the energy efficiency within C-RAN, which is mostly related to the use of a PS 
fronthaul and shared resources, or the exchange of macrocells with low power microcells.

4.4 ROADMAP TO CLOUD-RAN DEPLOYMENT 
This section will aim to create a roadmap for TDC to use for C-RAN deployment in the current case study area. 
The roadmap describes which cells should implement C-RAN first and the steps to follow. The roadmap is 
divided into three iterations, where each iteration can be deployed at different times, if outer circumstances 
do not allow the whole area to deploy C-RAN at the same time. Each iteration establishes a new BBU pool. 
In chapter 3, a method was derived to rank cells, based on how much they will benefit from upgrading to C-
RAN. The cell ranking method gives each cell a ranking for each one of its neighbor cells describing how much 
these two cells will benefit from joining a BBU pool together. The idea behind using this ranking to create a 
roadmap is, that the cells who will achieve the biggest advantages will get C-RAN deployed first. The primary 
benefits are resource sharing and interference management; hence the reason why peak times and 
interference are weighted higher than the other inputs in the cell ranking method. Equation 17 shows the 
cell ranking method with the numbers from TDC included:

4.5 DISCUSSION 
This work has introduced 4 steps of considerations for C-RAN deployment and derived a cell ranking method. 
The considerations and the cell ranking method have been tested on a real-life case study using TDC network 
information. Here it was proven that the steps introduced in chapter 3 can be used in a real-life network, 
because it is easy to find the network’s current step and then the remaining steps explains what to consider. 
This provides a good overview for the operator who wishes to deploy C-RAN either as greenfield deployment 
or as an upgrade of the existing network.  
The cell ranking method ranks cells based on the benefits they will achieve, when entering a BBU pool 
together with each of their neighbors. This method has proven successful based on the requirements listed 
in section 3.4. This is because cells, which will benefit the most from implementing C-RAN, achieved high cell 
rankings, and cells, which will not benefit that much from implementing C-RAN, achieved a lower ranking. 
The expansion of the cell ranking method, the pool ranking method, where cells were ranked with respect to 
a specific BBU pool, was a success because the multiplexing gain increased in the BBU pool, when using this 
method. The highest multiplexing gain was achieved in BBU pool AA, and was 2.03. All the three resulting 
BBU pools have an average multiplexing gain of 1.82. In section 4.2 the cells were distributed to the BBU 
pools only based on their peak times, the best multiplexing gain achieved here was 1.75 on average. The very 
high multiplexing gain achieved in the roadmap proves the efficiency and usability of the cell ranking method. 
When the BBU pool was assembled based on cell rankings the multiplexing gain increased. The chosen input 
and weighting of the input within the cell ranking method were useful inputs. The cell ranking method did 
not only prioritize the peak times but also the interference. These two inputs represent some of the benefits 
when deploying C-RAN using PS RRHs: Better sharing of resources and better interference management. 
Therefore, it seems very relevant to weight the correlation inputs, peak times and interference higher. Even 
though the cells were not only weighted by peak times but also interference, a higher multiplexing gain were 
still achieved compared to section  4.2. This concludes that the cell ranking method is very useful and 
relevant. The cells ranked highest will improve the resource sharing due to their different peak times, and 
they will achieve a lower interference when they implement C-RAN.  
An improvement of the method could be to weight the average traffic higher, maybe just for the second or 
third iteration of deployment, because it seems like the “left over” cells have a low average traffic, and a 
lower peak to average ratio, which gives them a low ranking. These cells will still be beneficial to pool 
together, because they can share resources when they all have a low average traffic. 
When the cells are incorporated in the BBU pools based on the cell ranking method then a lot of parameters 
are considered, and this makes it possible to find a much more optimal solution. First the best ranked cell 
combination is pooled together. Then the remaining cells are incorporated to this pool, based on their raking 
using the pool ranking method. The problem by using these methods is that all the cells benefitting most of 
each other are pooled together first, and then the remaining cells will be pooled together with those cells 
who are then the best option. This gives a very high multiplexing gain in the first pool, and then lower and 
lower multiplexing gains as all the best cells are taken. But does this matter? The average multiplexing gain 
when creating BBU pools based on the cell ranking method is higher than when they are pooled together 
only based on their peak times. And when creating a roadmap, it is important that the cells who will benefit 
the most from implementing C-RAN i.e. who have a lot of resources to share or suffers from high interference, 
will get C-RAN deployed first. 
This work has not derived a method to find the most optimal number of BBU pools within a certain area. But 
when the cells are ranked and the multiplexing gain calculated with a rising number of cells incorporated in 
==PAGE:89==
81 
 
the pool, then it is possible to find the optimal number of cells within that particular BBU pool. The locations 
of the BBU pools considered in this work are just suggestions. The author does not know whether the current 
locations have room and capacity for the BBU pool servers. This decision is left for the operator, to find a 
suitable place. Another suggestion could be to use cloud technologies to move the processing outside this 
case study area. Then a few BBUs are necessary inside the area to connect to the cloud and to handle time 
critical data. A big datacenter could be built in another location to handle all not time critical data processing. 
This could be an option for TDC, if there are not suitable locations for bigger server rooms in this very high 
populated area. But this solution requires a virtualization of the network.  
Another discussion relies on whether the cells having the lowest cell rankings should be incorporated in any 
pool, or they should remain in step 2. The answer will rely on whether the current low ranked cells will receive 
other benefits from C-RAN, faster processing within the BBU pool and better interference management could 
be advantages. Or maybe these cells will lower the resource sharing too much and it is more beneficial not 
to incorporate them in the BBU pool. It is not the case in the current case study area, but it could be a relevant 
consideration in other areas. 
Three different functional splits are proposed in this work. They all have pros and cons. Split A has a constant 
bitrate on the fronthaul link. It has all management centralized in the BBU pool. Split B supports a variable 
bitrate, but it can have some performance constraints with respect to the HARQ procedure. Split C also 
support a variable bitrate on the fronthaul link, but it has limitations on the UL CoMP scheme. Split B and C 
are preferred because they support a variable bitrate on the fronthaul link and thereby they can benefit from 
statistical multiplexing gain. This work recommends the use of split B, but does also note the weakness with 
respect to the HARQ procedure which must be evaluated by the operator before deployment.   
If TDC decides to keep the RHHs running CS CPRI, then the fronthaul bitrate from each RRH will be high. For 
RRHs handling RATs up to 16 QAM modulation and 2x2 MIMO, then CWDM can be used between each RRH 
and the aggregation point. After the aggregation point more capacity is needed, this could be for example 
using DWDM. If for example the RATs are expanded with higher order MIMO or higher modulation schemes, 
the fronthaul bitrates using CWDM are not big enough. Then it will be necessary to have another solution 
directly from the RRH for example 10 GB Ethernet or NG-PON2. 
Chapter 4 did also consider possibilities for TDC to save energy consumption when deploying C-RAN. The 
numbers, which these calculations are based on, are not very accurate, as it was not possible to achieve any 
information about the individual network element’s, i.e. the BBU, RFU and RRHs, energy consumption alone. 
Depending on how much energy it takes to switch on the BBUs in the morning, there might be energy savings 
in switching off low utilized BBUs during the night if the processing can be shared.  
The work in chapter 3 makes it possible for operators all over the world to evaluate their current network 
using the network deployment steps provided, and consider whether they will gain any benefits from 
introducing C-RAN using the cell ranking methods derived. All suggestions and recommendations provided 
in chapter 3 are universal, and can be used by any network operator, if he has access to the necessary 
information. The methods provided does not give a perfect solution, that can be achieved using a linear 
programming tool, but it gives a great overview of the current situation in the network.

CONCLUSION  
C-RAN is a cloud computing based network architecture, which can be used for upgrading current network 
technologies and deploying future 5G networks. C-RAN is a new RAN architecture that can be introduced for 
all types off RATs. C-RAN divides the traditional BS into a RRH handling the radio functionalities and a BBU 
handling the baseband processing. Several BBUs can be clustered in one BBU pool. This BBU pool gives C-
RAN advantages in interference management, user mobility and cell corporation. The BBU pool benefits from 
RRM, CoMP and eICIC because of the higher processing power and low latencies among the BBUs when they 
are gathered in one place. This effective cell corporation can be a huge advantage for future networks where 
cells are getting smaller and the user mobility will increase. In C-RAN, as the name cloud implies, several 
functions can be virtualized and moved to the “cloud”, which enables shared processing among the 
hardware. Virtual BBUs are software instances which realizes the base station functions, in such way that a 
virtual BBU has the entire same networking properties as a physical BS. These virtual BBUs can be gathered 
in a virtual pool, where it is possible for several virtual operators to share the environment. The main 
motivators for network virtualization are customization, resource utilization and resiliency. 
When deploying a C-RAN network several aspects must be considered, among these are: The functionality 
split, fronthaul interface and fronthaul link type. Where should the functional split between the BBU and RRH 
be. This work suggests three different options: Split A with only the filter, ADC/DAC and up/down conversion 
in the RRH. Split B with a symbol-level/Sample-level split. And split C with a bit-level and symbol-level split. 
The fronthaul interface between the RRH and BBU can carry either CS traffic or PS traffic. CS traffic carries 
out the framing in regular intervals using a fixed-rate fronthaul interface such as CPRI or OBSAI. CPRI link 
rates have a constant bitrate depending on RAT, this results in a low utilization of the fronthaul link. Split A 
is beneficial for CS transmission because it is the simplest solution. The PS options, split B and C, give great 
advantages in utilization because they benefit from the statistical multiplexing gain and dynamic BBU 
allocation. When considering the fronthaul transmission link there are two main options: Wired or wireless. 
Cobber technologies have in general low bitrates and low ranges. Fiber technologies have in general high 
bitrates and long ranges. The wireless technologies vary a lot in bitrates and range, but due to the higher 
attenuation of radio waves in higher bands where higher bitrates can be achieved, the technologies in higher 
bands have very low ranges.       
Criteria for implementing C-RAN in either a greenfield deployment scenario or a network upgrade scenario 
are provided in this work. This work introduces four steps to follow for C-RAN deployment. Moving from the 
first step to the second step describe what actions to take when going from the classic BS setup to separating 
the RRH from the BBU. In this step the most important decision is whether C-RAN should be implemented in 
the current cell or not. For this purpose, a method is derived to rank the cells compared to their neighbor 
cells for how well they will benefit from implementing C-RAN. Another thing to consider is what functional 
split to choose. This work recommends offhand the use of split B, because of its advantages related to CoMP 
and simple RRH. The third step clusters the BBUs in BBU pools. In this step, it is necessary to find the optimal 
combination of cells within the BBU pool, for this purpose the pool ranking method is derived. To find the 
optimal number of cells incorporated in the BBU pool, the statistical multiplexing gain is used as a guideline. 
Another thing to consider in this step is the transmission link between the RRH and BBU. This work 
recommends the use of a PS fronthaul network. The link type depends partially on what is already available 
for the operator, but the solution providing highest bitrates and longest ranges is fiber. If fiber is not available 
another solution can be wireless links, MWR for long ranges or mmW for high bitrates. This step also 
considers the BBU pools, when locating the BBU pools it must be considered where it is possible to place a 
server room but also where the fronthaul links can reach, which should be below 40 km. The fourth step

considers the BBU pool virtualization. BBU pool virtualization introduces more resiliency in C-RAN and creates 
and opportunity to achieve an even larger statistical multiplexing gain.     
To evaluate the methods derived in chapter 3, this work introduces a real-life case study investigating an area 
within TDC’s network. This area has 56 sites deployed already and is an area with a high population density. 
It is investigated how the current situation in the sites in this area is, in terms of peak times, peak to average 
ratios, average traffic, interference and how the sites are located compared to each other. The current 
deployment step is found, which is somewhere between step 1 and step 2, because some sites have deployed 
RRHs, some have partially and some have not. It is investigated how the composition of a BBU pool can be

deploy C-RAN in the case study area. The roadmap introduce three iterations of deployment, where the cell 
ranking and pool ranking methods are used to compose the BBU pools. The cell and pool ranking methods 
ensure that the cells which will benefit the most from incorporating C-RAN will have C-RAN deployed in the 
first iteration. The result is three BBU pools with respectively 16, 26 and 14 cells incorporated. The methods 
and deployment steps are evaluated, based on the resulting multiplexing gains in the BBU pools, and the 
input values from the cells chosen by the cell ranking method. The cell ranking method and pool ranking 
method chooses the right cells for higher rankings. The cell ranking method and pool ranking method
