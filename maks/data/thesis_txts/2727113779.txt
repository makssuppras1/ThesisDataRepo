Project Information: 
 
English title:  Real Option Analysis of CO2 storage project 
 
Description: 
The aim of this thesis is to develop a financial model that is designed to evaluate investment opportunities 
for an onshore geological CO2 storage project located in Denmark. The main part of this financial model will 
be devolved as a real option analysis. An investigation and selection of relevant parametrizes essential to 
the model will be subject to stochastic simulations (e.g Least Squares Monte Carlo-method) as part of 
modulating the many great economic insecurities in area of CO2 storage.  
As EU ETS (European Union Emissions Trading System) and implementation of a Danish CO2 tax most likely 
will have relatively large influence on revenue streams in a CO2 storage project, the thesis will contain a 
description and contextualization of relevant legislation in these areas and apply forecast of ETS-price to 
the financial model.

Abstract 
Focus on the development of Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) as a climate mitigation technology is 
increasingly relevant. The Danish government has formulated a strategy aiming to utilize the Danish 
subsurface for permanent carbon storage for both national and international emitters, in order to provide 
emission reductions and negative emissions of CO2. This calls for an assessment of the economic feasibility 
of developing relevant storage facilities and infrastructure in order to utilize the Danish subsurface for such 
a purpose. This paper provides an economic assessment of an onshore carbon storage project with a 30-
year operation time, located in Denmark, aiming at receiving relevant storage licenses in the upcoming 
tender process scheduled by the Danish Energy Agency to commence Q1 2024. Real Option Valuation 
(ROV) are applied in the form of Least Square Monte Carlo method (LSM). Including stochastic simulations 
of Europe Union Emission Trading System prices (EU-ETS) by Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM) and the 
development a carbon storage market by Logistic growth simulations in a domain relevant for permanent 
storage in geographical relevant locations to the storage project. The ROV is performed on multiple 
scenarios which all provide positive option values in the range of 187.3 to 1505.6 million €. For Mid-Case 
scenarios input variations effect on option value are performed. This shows that the variable cost of storing 
carbon has a large impact on option value. Also, increasing volatility applied to EU-ETS simulations effects 
option value in positive direction.

Background and introduction 
The Paris agreement aims to keep global mean temperature increase below 1.5/2.0 degrees. In addition, it 
is a set ambition in the European Fit of 55 (under the Green Deal Policy) to reduce EU greenhouse gas 
emissions with 55% by 2030 and reach climate neutrality by 2050. To reach the targets it is recognized that 
carbon capture and storage (CCS) will be a necessary climate mitigation measure. In this context the Danish 
government has a strategyA of utilizing the Danish subsurface for permanent geological carbon storage. If 
CCS is to play a role in combating climate change in the near future, it is relevant to explore the economic 
feasibility of private projects related to CCS. 
 
An essential part of the steps in CCS is the storage part. The Geological Survey of Denmark and 
Greenland (GEUS) estimates that there is a total carbon storage potential of up to 22 Giga ton in the Danish 
underground.B That is 500 to 1000 times the annual Danish emissions depending on counting methods.  
The three main steps in CCS - Capture, Transport and Storage of carbon - are not new technologies and 
have been applied for other purposes around the world for years e.g., to enhance oil recovery. Linking the 
three steps together in a large scale and in such a way that private companies are willing to invest and 
develop this value chain is where the innovation must take place. This in order to bring down the levelized 
cost of capturing, transporting and storing a ton of carbon. Thus, securing a cost-effective implementation 
of CCS. 
 
Where CCS can be applied 
CCS has the possibility to mitigate hard to abate emissions from industries where reductions of emissions 
cannot take place or have very high cost. E.g., steel production, cement production, oil refinery and more. 
CCS can be applied to the energy sector where retrofitting carbon capture hardware can aid in emission 
reduction in facilities where decommissioning are several years away. The time period to decommissioning 
is an important factor to consider, since payback times can be large in capture projects. Furthermore, it is 
possible to produce hydrogen out of natural gas and by including CCS will thus reduce production 
emissions. This process is referred to as blue hydrogenC. 
Bioenergy production, where biomass is utilized to produce energy, typically in biogas production or by 
combustion with carbon capture and storage (BECCS)1, and Direct Air Capture with Carbon Storage (DACCS) 
are also areas where CCS is applicable. The reason for the subdivision of CCS into BECCS/DACCS and non-
BECCS/DACCS has to do with the origin of the carbon and thus if the emission is counted as carbon-neutral 
or not. 
 
What are the economic drivers 
A large driver for the development of CCS in the EU member states is the EU-ETS.  
The sectors that are part of the EU-ETS or will be in the following years and have the technical possibility2 to 
apply carbon capture, can benefit from CCS as the need for European Union Allowances 
(EUA) will decrease and a cost saving will arise.  
As bioenergy production is not part of the EU-ETS the economic driver comes from elsewhere. This also 
applies for DAC. The incentive to develop BECCS and DACCS comes partly from the growing private market 
for Carbon Dioxide Removal certificate (CDR)D.  
Private corporations invest in CDR certificates in order to voluntary compensate for their carbon emissions. 
The most prominent so far are perhaps Microsoft that not only want to eliminate its current emissions, but 
additionally compensate for historic carbon emissionsE in part by investing in CDR. Typically companies like

Comparability in the CCS sector 
Different settings for the three main steps for CCS (Capture, Transport and Storage) can have a large 
influence on costs and risks associated with the induvial project. This means that it is hard to estimate a 
generic cost of each step in CCS that can apply to a wide number of different cases. The cost may vary 
significantly based on the specific case in consideration. 
As an example, a critical and energy intensive part of CCS is the capture process. Research and 
development in reducing the energy consumption and prevent degeneration of the absorber chemicals are 
critical for the CCS business case. Also, integrating the capture hardware in the production facility in 
questions can give significant reductions in energy consumption as excess process heat can be upgraded 
and utilized when boiling the carbon from the absorbent. Additionally, the distance between capture and 
storage site and the mode of transport has large impact on costs.  
 
Critique of CCS 
Critique of CCS technology and a largescale implementation as a climate mitigation tool is related to the 
following subjects. It is expensive compared to alternatives and it is energy intensive, which could increase 
emissions if based on fossil energy. There are risks of carbon leakage from the geological storage, and CCS 
could prolong the use of fossil fuels. It is out of scope here to elaborate on and evaluate this critique. 
 
Motivation, Problem statement & Research Questions 
 
Motivation 
Utilizing the Danish subsurface for carbon storage is a new possibility to mitigate climate change. By 
investigating the economic feasibility when restricting to an onshore project in a Danish context, and 
thereby excluding the use of more complex depleted oil and gas fields offshore, leads to the research 
question stated below. Many institutions like IPPC, IEA, EU etc. point to CCS both as a mitigation strategy 
and as way to create negative carbon emissions. Here the carbon storage is a vital part of the whole CCS 
value chain.  
 
If the captured carbon is not utilized, e.g., for producing different commodities, geological carbon storage is 
necessary for every capture project. Permanent and sufficiently large volume storage in a suitable

economic evaluation of such projects. 
 
Research Questions 
1. Is it economical feasible to develop a geological onshore carbon storage project in Denmark, 
including necessary infrastructure enabling the reception of carbon from both national and regional 
sources, with a project lifespan of 30 years. 
2. What is the maximum monetary resource that is economical feasible to apply, in order to obtain a 
storage license by winning a tender process.

Literature review  
This section describes relevant literature and provides an overview of the literature related to this paper.  
 
Longstaff and Schwartz (2001)I  
The least square Monte Carlo method (LSM) for option valuation proposed by Longstaff and Schwartz are 
originally applied in a financial option setting but have subsequently been utilized for real option settings. 
For the Longstaff and Schwartz’ valuation framework the assumption of no-arbitrage paradigm, hence the 
existence of a risk-neutral measure5 and the completeness of the market are applied, justifying discounting 
with risk-free rate. Longstaff and Schwartz apply the framework on a number of swaps and options 
including an American style put option in finite discrete time steps. The mathematical framework for least 
square regression and convergence hereof are elaborated and justified using L2 as a Hilbert space and the 
existence of countable orthonormal basis as basis function of the regression. Longstaff and Schwartz 
applies Laguerre polynomials as basis functions but argue that Hermite, Legendre, Chebyshev, Gegenbauer 
and Jacobi polynomial as basis functions can also produce accurate results. Comparison with other 
methods e.g., finite difference method is conducted. In general, Longstaff and Schwartz conclude that: 
“This approach is intuitive, accurate, easy to apply, and computationally efficient.” 
Longstaff and Schwartz is an important source in this paper as the methodology and methods applied here 
are based in large on the Least-Squares approach. 
 
Ahmadi and Bratvold (2022)J 
Ahmadi and Bratvold provide an extensive review of papers applying Option valuation and Real Option 
Valuation. Further, they apply Least Square Monte Carole approached to a simple case of oil production 
with embedded Real Options in the context of Sequential Decision-making framework. The aim is to 
investigate which impact the chosen regression basis functions for the least square regression has on the 
option value. Also, the impact of including/excluding simulations paths on which the regression is based 
and the effect this have on option value is performed. They conclude that in the case in question the choice 
of basis functions has little effect on the option value when simulation paths are deep out-of-the-money or 
deep in-the-money. The choice of simulation paths for regression has a large impact on option value. 
 
Abadie & Chamorro (2008)K 
Abadie & Chamorro assessed the economic feasibility of investing in carbon capture hardware on a coal-
fired power plant under stochastic uncertainties of EU-ETS prices and electricity prices. The assessment was 
completed as a ROV applying a binomial lattice approach. A conclusion was reached - subsidies or higher 
ETS prices had to be accommodated for feasibility to occur. 
 
Fuss et al. (2008)L  
Fuss et al. includes stochastic carbon- and electricity prices for a single electricity producer. Here the focus 
of the ROV is to assess the value from investing in a coal-fired power plant with or without carbon capture 
module, retrofitting the module and switching the module on and off, under the stochastic progress. 
Market uncertainties, a specific term defined in the article, is applied to both simulation of electricity and 
carbon prices, where political uncertainties, also a specific term in the article, associated with long term 
price direction is only applied to carbon prices. The valuation method is similar to that presented by 
Longstaff and Schwartz (2001), except no regression is applied for expected future values. A key finding is 
that producers facing market uncertainty tend to invest in carbon-saving technologies earlier compared to 
producers facing less uncertainties.

Zhu & Fan (2011)M  
Zue & Fan applies ROV with LSM method in order to evaluate an investment in a new thermal power 
generation with CCS to replace existing thermal power. The case under consideration is a compound 
investment option with four uncertainties as part of the model: investment in CCS technology deployment, 
thermal power generating cost, carbon price, and thermal power with CCS generating cost. The 
uncertainties are modulated by a stochastic process and linked via differential equations. In summary a 
simulation of the value of investing in a thermal power with CCS using LSM to evaluate, are put into context 
of the Chinese power sector performed in different scenarios. The scenarios include fixed and variable 
carbon tax implementation, governmental funded R&D subsidies schemes and subsidies schemes for the 
CCS value chain. Conclusions include that in order to reduce the high investment risk in CCS, R&D subsidies 
will have a large impact together with a variable carbon tax. 
 
Hauck & Hof (2017)N 
Hauck & Hof asses two real options. First option is for a single power producer under a cap-and-trade ETS, 
operating a new carbon capture ready power plant located in the vicinity of a recently depleted gas field. 
The option is to invest in capture and storage, with the related cost of capital, investment and increase in 
electricity consumption for carbon capture, but with cost reduction in emission allowances (EUA) and 
postponement of abandonment cost from gas production. Here the price of EUA is assumed to be 
stochastic following a geometric Brownian motion (GBM). 
The second option have the same setup as the first, but with the gas field still in production. Part of the 
option is to switch from producing gas to store carbon - as these two possibilities are assumed mutual 
exclusive. Here the revenue generated from sale of gas is included in the option valuation. The gas prices 
are also assumed to be stochastic following a GBM.  
The yearly volatility of the EUA prices in both options cases are simulated using the values of 0.1 and 0.12, 
with a starting price of 10€/ton CO2. 
This review focus on the first option (non-operating gas field), as this mostly resemblance the objective in 
this thesis. The option is evaluated as an American call option and the amount of emission reduction from 
CCS are assumed to be fixed. The method applied is similar to that develop by Longstaff and Schwartz 
(2001). One part of the result is that independent of the volatility of the EUA price the value of the option is 
increasing with increasing lifetime of the option – this is explained as a consequence of increasing EUA 
prices. Also, the threshold price for EUA, beyond which the option to invest is feasible, as a function of 
“time to exercise deadline”, is assed. Concluding that increasing time to deadline means decreasing 
threshold price, regardless of volatility of EUA. 
 
Rubin et al. (2015)O 
Rubin et al. aims for assessing costs for carbon capture and storage from new fossil-based power plants and 
compare the cost estimates with IPCC (2005) Special report on carbon dioxide capture and storage.  
 
Budinis et al. (2018)P 
Among other focus areas Budinis et al. estimate costs of carbon storage and draws upon Rubin et al. (2015) 
and ZEP - The Costs of CO2 Capture, Transport and StorageQ which has been updated in 2020. 
 
The literature summarized here applying ROV for financial feasibility assessment of CCS have focus on the 
emitter and the cost of capturing vs. the cost reduction of not emitting in an ETS or similar, and how 
climate policy or subsidy schemes can make carbon capture feasible. In this thesis a different focus will be 
applied on the CCS value chain. Focus is on a geological storage as the investment case, where the storage 
site will sell carbon storage at a price determined as a simple derivate of the EU-ETS price. It is assumed 
that the need for carbon storage will grow, and the growth will be simulated stochastically. Additionally, 
the ETS prices will be assumed stochastic. Methods by Hauck & Hof (2017) will be applied in order to

Case description and assumptions 
The subject of this analysis is the opportunity to submit an application to the Danish state regarding a 
license to explore an onshore geological carbon storage potential in the Danish subsurface, and 
subsequently obtain a license to store carbon in this location. 
The geological storage possibilities in this analysis are assumed to be a saline aquifer i.e., a geological 
formation only containing saltwater. This is in line with the forthcoming application round in Denmark for 
onshore carbon storage estimated by the Danish Energy Agency to start Q1 20246. It is out of scope to 
evaluate carbon storage in a nearly or fully depleted gas or oil reservoirs. To further explore this, see Hauck 
& Hof (2017). 
 
License to explore and license to store 
As part of the tender application to obtain the rights to explore a specific area of the Danish subsurface, a 
work program must be submitted that may include collecting 3D-seismic data and modelling, performing 
integrity analysis of geological structure, drilling test well etc. The recipient of the license to explore will 
conduct the specific work program and bear the costs hereofR. The cost associated with license application 
and the completion of the work program are in this paper denoted OP (Option Price). It is assumed that it is 
possible to execute the work program successfully, and that collected data supports the suitability for 
carbon storage in the area that is being explored. Further assumptions are that the total capacity and the 
yearly possible rate of carbon injection in the geological storage will have a sufficient size according to the 
scenarios described in subsequent sections. Once the work program is completed it is assumed that the 
license recipient will obtain exclusive rights to exploit the geological storage for permanent carbon storage 
for a 30-year period from first injection. The right is not an obligation. 
 
Project development 
In order to exploit the storage potential, the recipient of the license to store must invest in project 
development. This may include several injection wells, monitoring wells, compressor and injection site(s) 
facilities. It is assumed that the geological storage site is located adjacent to harbor facilities that are 
suitable for receiving carbon captured domestically and internationally transported by ship. In addition, all 
regulatory frameworks relevant for international carbon transport are assumed in placeS. Project 
development must then also include the cost of constructing a suitable harbor terminal, pipelines to the 
injection sight(s), intermediate storage tanks etc. 
The well(s) drilled as part of the work program are here negligible and will not be viewed as part of the 
possible yearly injection capacity. This means that no carbon can be stored before the development phase 
has taken place. Furthermore, it is assumed for simplicity that the development of the storage project is 
instantaneous, so as there is no delay from the time of deciding to develop and the storage project start to 
generate revenue.  
The cost of project development in order to reach the relevant yearly injection capacity, CAPEX of harbor 
facilities, onshore pipelines to injection site, injection- and monitoring wells, abandonment cost and cost of 
capital, are denoted by K. 
It is assumed that a change in cost of OP and K does not influence each other. E.g., offering a large work 
program versus offering a small work program have no effect on the cost of developing the project.

The carbon storage market and EU-ETS 
It is here assumed for simplicity that all carbon captured in a reasonable distance to Denmark is available 
for storage and that the emitters are part of the EU-ETS. This allows for at simple revenue modelling and to 
regard all captured carbon as a homogenous waist product. 
Further assumptions applied are that emitter participating in carbon capture do not receive any free EU 
Allowance (EUA). This assumption serves to ensure that the full cost reduction from avoided emissions in 
the EU-ETS can be utilized as payment for the value chain in CCS.  
National carbon tax, subsidy schemes or other support mechanisms are ignored for all parties in the CCS 
value chain modelled in this thesis.  
It is assumed that the price for storing carbon will be set on a spot-market where the price for storage is a 
simple derivative of the EU-ETS price. The price of storage in the model constitutes a fixed percentage of 
the price of EU-ETS price. The fixed percentage is denoted ETSrate. The difference between the EU-ETS price 
and the storage price constitute the value that is applied to service the part of the CCS value chain that are 
not in scope for the storage project i.e., all other cost, like the cost related to capture and transport.  
It is assumed that the actors in the CCS value chain including the storage provider are price takers, resulting 
in the fact that the development of the storage project do not influence the market size of carbon available 
for storage or the EU-ETS price development and vice versa. 
No correlations are assumed between the development of EU-ETS prices and market size of carbon 
available for storage.

Methodology 
Overall methodology 
The overall methodology applied to answer the research questions is Real Option Valuation (ROV). 
In this paper the Least Square Monte Carlo methodology (LSM) is applied and in large the methodology 
developed by Longstaff and Schwartz (2001) are adopted. The LSM methodology falls in to two main parts. 
1st part:  Stochastic simulation and estimation of the value of the underlying asset (Monte Carlo simulations 
and data analysis). 
2nd part: Evaluate when to exercise the option and calculate the option value (least square regression and 
optimal exercise strategy). 
 
The 1st part cannot be directly applied in the manner described by Longstaff and Schwartz (2001). This is a 
result of the fact that the underlying asset for Longstaff and Schwartz could be a stock, a warrant or some 
other kind of financial asset – and thus the value of the underlying asset can be simulated by a stochastic 
process, based on the historic price development and volatility of the asset in question. 
The underlying asset in this case i.e., a carbon storage facility including infrastructure and harbor terminal, 
has no historic value and thus it is not possible to extract price and volatility to utilize for a simulation. Since 
the business for storing carbon underground is in the early stages worldwide it is not possible to find similar 
facilities in operation that can be used as reference for evaluating the value of the underlying asset. In 
order to estimate the value of the underlying asset a method similar to that applied by Hauck & Hof (2017) 
is adopted.  
 
The 2nd part will be directly applied as Longstaff and Schwartz (2001) described. The details of both steps

Methodological choice 
ROV is often applied as an addition to a traditional discounted cash flow analysis (DCF) and in large also rely 
on applying parameters like NPV calculations and discounting in order to determine values of the 
underlying asset. Where DCF is restricted to scenarios, sensitivity analysis and simulations of input 
parameters, DCF lags the ability to consider the future decisions (or options) that managements will face. 
ROV is useful for investment decisions in a situation where great uncertainties are present. In addition, ROV 
aims to capture the value embedded in the fact that management have optionality in their future choices 
and that future uncertainties may be disclosed, either by external forces or by management taking 
measures to resolve the uncertainties. 
The specific methodology of Least Square Monte Carlo allows to incorporate uncertainties from multiple 
inputs (here market size and price development) as a stochastic process. For other forms of ROV e.g., 
Closed form or Lattice-based techniquesT the implementation of multiple uncertainties would quickly grow 
to become practically impossible to handle. Applying LSM makes it possible to extend the number of 
stochastic inputs to the model with relative ease, thus allow for a model that can be adopted and refined to 
the ongoing development of the carbon storage sector.  
Model and Method specifications 
 
The real option  
The option applied in the paper is both an initiation and a deferral option. 
Within the option lifetime of 5 years it is possible to initiate the storage project development by paying the 
option strike price, K or to defer the project development to a later time in the option lifetime. Once the 
option lifetime has expired the option has zero value, as the license to store is lost. The option style is a 
discrete American call option, as it is possible to exercise at any of the timesteps in the option lifetime. 
 
Model overview 
The EU-ETS prices and the carbon storage market are simulated in 420 discreet time-steps, each time-step 
correspond to 1 month, given a total simulation length of 35 years.  
Together with the carbonrate, the ETSrate and the variable cost of storage Cv the simulations of Market and 
EU-ETS are aggregated into 60 values, corresponding to a value for each month in a 5-year period.  
Each of the 60 values represent the estimation of the value of the underlying asset, one value for every 
month the option can be exercised. This aggregation will be described in detail below.

The simulation and the aggregation are performed in 10.000 parallel paths, given 10.000 different ways the

The expected value of exercising in the following month is estimated by applying least square regression

A series of months where it is more favorable to exercise immediately compared to waiting one month,

choosing the first of these months for each path.  
 
When all the earliest months of exercise has been identified the intrinsic value for the specific month is 
discounted applying the risk-free rate back to month zero. The paths where no exercise is favorable will 
have the discounted value of zero in time-step zero.

Optimal exercise strategy 
By the nature of a real option, it is only possible to exercise the specific option one time. For each path the 
option will be exercised the earliest time step where the option is in-the-money and in addition, that the 
expect value of waiting to exercise until the succeeding time step is less than the value of exercise 
immediately. 
 
Let E[n, t] be the expected value of exercising the option in path n = 1…10000 at time step t = 1..60 
For a fixed path n with option strike price K the optimal exercise time is given by: 
 
For t ∈ {1, …. ,59} 
Let A = {t | Vt,n - K ≥ 0 & S Vt,n - K ≥ E[n, t+1]} 
Inf(A) is the optimal exercise time step for path n 
 
If A = Ø then the option will not be exercised in any time step t = 1..59 of the path n. 
In the next section it is elaborated how to estimate E[n, t+1] 
The essence here is, that for all 60 timesteps, but the last one, the option is exercised if the option is in-the-
money and the expected value of exercising in the subsequent time step are smaller compared to 
exercising immediately. 
For the last time step t = 60 the optimal exercise strategy is to exercise if the option is in-the-money, as the 
expected value of waiting one timestep are zero.

Expected value of exercise in next time step 
Application of least square regression (LSR) gives an estimation of a conditional expectation function; Ft(x) 
for each time step t = 1..59 
For a fixed t, Ft(x) will be used to estimate E[n, t+1] for paths that are in-the-money in step t 
 
Starting from the time step index t = 59 and working backwards to t = 1.  
Take all values that are in-the-money as independent variable, X = (Vt,i … Vt,j)  
the set {i, … ,j} is a subset of {1, … ,10000}. 
 
Then for the same paths {i, … ,j} take the discounted intrinsic value of the succeeding time step as 
dependent variable Y = (ßi, … ßj). 
That is: 
ßm = max(Vt+1,m - K, 0)·e-r·dt for all m ∈ {i, … ,j} 
 
where r is risk-free rate, dt is the length of the time step.10 
 
Apply Least Square Regression in order to obtain best fitting function for X and Y. The function obtained for 
LSR is the conditional expectation function Ft 
 
Now for each Ft(Vt,m) for m ∈ {i, … ,j} the function value of Ft gives the expected value of exercising the 
option in the succeeding time step t+1 for that specific path. 
 
The following applies: 
 
For the paths that are in-the-money: E[m, t+1] = Ft(Vt,m) for m ∈ {i, … ,j} 
For the paths that are out-of-the-money: E[n, t+1] = 0 for n ∉ {i, … ,j} 
 
 
Along with the optimal exercise strategy described in the previous subsection a method describing how to 
decide for each path, in which time step to exercise the option has been elaborated.  
 
In order to estimate the option value, discount the intrinsic value for each path, in each time step t 
where the option is exercised using e-r·dt·t as discount factor. Include all paths where the option  
is not exercised anywhere in the full option lifetime, i.e., where the value is 0.  
Average these values to get the estimation for the option value.  
 
Basis functions for LSR 
Here the basis functions applied for the LSR are the Laguerre polynomials of max degree 2, as suggested by 
Longstaff and Schwartz. Ahmadi and Bratvold 2022J investigate the Impact of choice of bases function. 
 
To summarize:  
1. For all timesteps t = 1..59 and all paths, evaluate if exercising in t or t+1 gives highest value applying 
the expected value LSR algorithm described above. For timestep 60 exercise if option is in-the-
money.  
2. For all the possible exercised timesteps in each path, then exercise in the timestep with smalless 
index i.e., the earliest time the option is in-the-money. 
3. If a path is not exercised in any timestep, the option value in this path is zero.

In order to justify the choices for market size and market share and thereby calibration of the inputs to the 
model, an introduction is given into the context related to the current landscape for at Danish onshore 
storage project. This landscape is evolving at a rapid pace and a re-evaluation of market size and market 
share will most likely be needed for future use in the model developed for this paper.   
 
As an onshore storage provider in Denmark the market is based on the volume of carbon that can and will 
be captured and transported to a storage site located in Denmark. 
Since transport costs are a large part of the CCS value chain, distance from emitter to storage site are of 
relative importance. If all other parameters are equal, it must be assumed that for two competing storage 
sites the shortest transport distance provides competitive edge. The number of storage sites that are in 
operation or planning to operate in the relevant geographical area will have an impact on the market share. 
 
As of writing, Q2 2023, the Danish Energy Agency in collaboration with the Geological Survey of Denmark 
and Greenland (GEUS) are conducting seismic surveys as part of an initial scoping of the potential for 
preforming on- and nearshore carbon storage in DenmarkÆ. Eight on- and nearshore subsurface structures 
are being investigated and between zero and eight of the structures will be available in multiple tender 
processes awarding the winner of each tender the license to explore one of the eight potential storage 
sites. Together with the storage projects located in the North Sea12, these Danish on- and nearshore 
projects constitute the competitive landscape for the storage project in question. Since there is no 
indication of the capacity or the number of future on- and nearshore projects in Denmark, the evaluation of 
market share for the storage project (one of the eight on- and nearshore structure) must be based on 
assumptions. Storage projects are already in operation or have already obtained license to explore offshore 
carbon storage, both in saline aquifer and depleted oil and gas fields in the North Sea. Emitters with 
capture plans, which due to geographical locations, are most likely to utilize the North Sea storage sites will 
be excluded from the market size estimation. Thereby also excluding the North Sea storage projects from 
the estimation of division of market share between storage providers. 
 
Therefore, it is assumed that carbon captured in the following countries intended for storage will constitute 
the market size of the future onshore storage sites in Denmark - Sweden, Finland, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, 
Poland, part of Germany and Denmark. 
An equal division of the market will be assumed between the eight on- and nearshore storage possibilities 
that might be develop post the tender process described above. 
 
With these assumptions a market share estimate can be provided. i.e., 12.5% (one out of eight) market 
share for the storage project in question.

This give rise to a discussion of how the option value are produced. Imagine a scenario where no paths are 
exercised in the first month. But the value of the underlying asset, for all paths, grows to become larger 
than the strike price at some point in the option lifetime and result in an exercise as soon as the asset value 
is larger than the option strike price. The intrinsic value for the specific path in the timestep of exercise may 
be very small. This can lead to a scenario where all paths are exercised but the value of the option is very 
close to zero since the exercise is happening as soon as the asset value are a tiny bit larger than the strike 
price.  
The only mechanism of the LSM that can prevent an exercise from happening with a small intrinsic value is 
the expected value function E[n, t] detection if the value of waiting one time step after the positive intrinsic 
value is more favorable.  
In case an asset value grows sufficiently slowly from the time step where the intrinsic value becomes 
positive and to the succeeding time step, this result in an exercise in the specific path even though that the 
asset value might grow rapidly hereafter. The optimal time to exercise time for value maximation would 
not be to exercise the first time the option is in-the-money. The LSR only consider the expected value of 
waiting one time step to exercise and ignores alle following values 
An interesting research topic could be to investigate how an extension of the LSM method to take into 
account all future expected exercise values, how this can be justified and the possible effect on the option 
value. 
 
Furthermore, from figure 7 it looks like the value of the underlying asset is strictly increased for all paths in 
the 60 months timespan. Assuming this observation is true, exercising the option for all paths in the last 
time-step i.e., month number 60, would provide a higher option value. This is supported if the discounting 
effect on all intrinsic values in timestep 60 with the risk-free rate is smaller than the increase in option value 
form waiting to exercise to month 60. The discounting from month 60 only amounts to a discount factor of 
0.93315 
This opens for the discussion about the optimal exercise strategy. If the simulations performed as part of an 
ROV and the calculation of the asset value based on the simulations show, that the asset value will increase 
in the option lifetime and thus the option value will increase form waiting to exercise, why apply an 
exercise strategy dictating to exercise the option the first time it is in-the-money? 
The whole idea of using ROV is to deal with uncertainties and to take into account the possibility to act by

The reason for applying the exercise strategy of exercising the first possible time the option is in-the-money

Conclusion 
All 9 scenarios provide a positive value for the real option, under the assumptions listed in this paper.  
The answer to the first research question is: Yes - it is economical feasible to develop a geological onshore 
carbon storage project in Denmark including necessary infrastructure enabling the reception of carbon from 
both national and regional sources, with a project lifespan of 30 years – under the assumption applied in 
this paper. 
The answer to the second researched question: What is the maximum monetary resource that is 
economical feasible to apply in order to obtain a storage license by winning a tender process - is given in 
table 17. The answer depends on the scenario and lies in the range of 187.3 million € to 1505.6 million € 
with an average of 790.9 million € across all scenarios. 
 
An essential part of applying Real Option Valuation is the ability to provide a coherent and sufficient 
valuation of the value of the underlying asset in the full lifetime of the option. In this case it has turned out 
to be a difficult task to perform. An effort has been made to apply a close to market approach to this 
question using data from concrete project evaluations in the CCS value chain.

The market of storing carbon is still in the very early stages and are far from being a perfect market.  
There are not many buyers, and the buyers that are public about their plans are hesitating as they might 
not be willing to invest large CAPEX in projects with large sunk cost without an outlook for a CCS value 
chain taking form. The same goes for the sellers – it is the chicken and egg situation. 
Information is not free and is subject to large costs and large uncertainties. 
The participants in the market are not price takers and most likely will influence the price structure. 
It is relatively clear that CCS will play a major role in climate mitigation in the EU member countries, but 
how, and which business models will be profitable is still unclear and uncertain. 
 
Applying Least Square Monte Carlo methods in order to evaluate the economical feasibility of a carbon 
storage project is basically a sound approach as simulation allows to incorporate multiple uncertainties. 
Once the python model is built, it is relatively easy to extract how the different inputs effect the outputs. 
But no model is better than the applied data and the input data in this paper lags quality. During the 
research for the paper, it became clear that not a lot of data on cost and market development related to a 
storage project is available. I.e., the result of the analysis remain uncertain as long as sufficiently reliable 
data cannot be optioned. However, the conceptual model framework is now in place, and the model can be 
updated when better data become available.   
 
Limitations of this study 
It is questionable if this study can in fact be used to evaluate investment opportunities in carbon storage 
projects. A deeper and more detailed assessment of the cost estimates associate with the storage project 
and handling of carbon is top priority if developing the evaluation in this paper are to be performed. A 
continued surveillance and adaptation of how the market of carbon storage may develop is essential for 
this paper to remain relevant. The model produces as part of this paper can be utilized for specific storage 
project but need a in depth revision of the inputs on market and cost. The comparison between DFC NPV 
calculations from other sources may not be directly applicable.  
Perspectivation 
Even dough the results of all nine scenarios give a positive result, with an average number of paths 
resulting in exercising the option is 97.6%, the return over the 30-year project operation period is not great. 
Acknowledging that the cost-of-capital is included in the cost of exercising the option, it can be discussed if 
it is a large enough return for a project with CAPAX need in this size and the level of risk assonated with the 
CCS industry. 
However, there might be other reasons besides the return that leads to investing in carbon storage 
projects. Setting aside the climate mitigation potential from carbon storage, that can be argued both in a 
positive and negative direction for CCS technology – investing in and developing carbon storage can be 
viewed as a license to operate for specific industries in the future. E.g., for Oil and gas, Cement and steel, 
chemical industry and other industries. This might incentivize private companies in these industries to 
invest in the development of carbon storage projects with zero or small negative returns only to obtain the 
possibility to maintain their main business, as both legislators and customers demand may push hard for 
emission reduction.

Danish Carbon tax 
The Danish parliament has decided to implement a tax on emitting carbon. The tax will be stepwise 
implemented from 2025 to 2030 and will expand to sectors outside the current EU-ETS. 
Part of the revenue from the tax will be used to support carbon mitigation hereunder subsidies for CCS 
projects. With the implementation of the new carbon tax, earlier taxation on energy will be incorporated in 
the new carbon tax. 
 
There will be three categories of the carbon tax: 
 
1. Companies not covered by EU-ETS 
2. Companies that are covered by EU-ETS 
3. Companies working with mineralogical processes (cement, glass, mineral wool etc.)  
 
When the tax is fully implemented the companies not covered by EU-ETS will be faced with a tax of 750 
DKK per ton carbon.  
The companies that are covered by EU-ETS will pay 375 DKK per ton carbon on top of the price of the EUA.  
The companies working with mineralogical processes will face a 125 DKK per ton carbon on top of the price 
of the EUA. 
 
For the 2nd and 3rd category a lower limit will be set on the collected tax (tax + EUA). 
In case the EUA price will not develop as estimated (e.g., to 750 DKK by 2030) the 375 DKK and 125 DKK 
related to the two categories will be expanded to cover the gap, securing a lower tax limit of 1125 DKK and 
875 DKK per ton carbon.  
 
By allocating some of the revenue form the carbon tax for subsidies towards CCS projects in Denmark and 
by given emitters further tax incentives on top of EU-ETS to reduce emission, profitability of installing 
carbon capture on emitters will increase.  
 
Danish CCS subsidies 
As part of the “Roadmap for capture, transport and storage of CO2 – 2nd part”GG from December 2021 an 
almost unanimous Danish parliament decided to move forward in implementing CCUS subsidy schemes in 
order to drive the development of CCUS in Denmark that can contribute to national climate goals. 
This has amounted into two schemes with the overall titles of CCUS fund and NECCS fund.  
The CCUS fund is divided into two phases, each phase containing 8 billion DKK. 
 
The first phase could be awarded to a major emitter or a consortium of smaller emitters that would receive 
the funds of up to 815 million DKK per year for capturing, transporting and storing a minimum of 0.4 MTA 
of carbon form year 2025/2026 in a period of 20 years.  
==PAGE:37==
37 
 
There was no restriction on the origin of the captured carbon and could arise from all sources e.g., 
Cement production (fossil and mineralogical origin) 
Waste-to-energy (mixture of biogenic and fossil origin) 
Biomass-to-energy (biogenic origin) 
 
In the process for the second phase of the CCUS fund learnings form the first tender process are applied 
and will be offered in the years to come. The only public goal is reaching 0.5 MTA in this phase in 2030. 
 
The NECCS fund is directed at creating negative emissions of 0.5 MTA form 2025 originating form biogas 
upgrading, biomass-based power and heat, biogenic share of waste-to-energy and DACCS. 
The NECCS fund contains a total 2.5 billion DKK over an eight-year period. 
As of writing the Danish Energy Agency and the Danish ministry of climate, energy and utilities are 
developing conditions for the tender and engaging in a market dialog with possible applicants.  
Beside form exclusive focus on negative emissions the NECCS tender will likely differ from the CCUS fund in 
the fowling ways. The applicant can be any legal entity taking responsibility for the full value chain. There is 
no minimum requirement for the amount of carbon for the individual applicant i.e., the 0.5 MTA can be 
divides on to several applicants.

pool. The full scope of the contract is not publicly available, but some important details are provided. 
Ørsted will capture 0.43 MTA of carbon from the year 2026 on the two biomass-based power plants 
Asnæsværket (wood chip fired) and Avedøreværket (straw fired).  
According to Ørsted the carbon will be transported by truck from Avedøreværket near Copenhagen to 
Asnæsværket near the city of Kalundborg – later a pipe will be established in order to replace the truck 
transport. The carbon will be shipped from Kalundborg harbour to Northern Lights’ onshore import 
terminal in Norway, from where it will be transported by pipe to an offshore storage facility.  
Next to the subsidies provided by the Danish state, Ørsted has made a contract with the tech provider 
Microsoft regarding the sale of 2.76 million ton of carbon removal (CDR) in a 11-year period. The details 
and the monetary value of this agreement has not been public disclosed. According to the community 
driven platform CDR.fyi this is that largest contract ever made on CDR. 
 
This is a very interesting development in the CCS value chain.   
Since the minimum requirement in the 1st phase of the CCUS fund is 0.4 MTA and Microsoft has signed up 
for 2.76 MT in 11-years period (0.25 MTA in average per year) and the total amount of capture is 0.43 MTA, 
some part of the captured carbon must be funded both by the CCUS fund and by Microsoft. This may 
trigger double counting of the emission reductions, which could become a highly relevant research area to 
further explore as the market develops.
