Abstract
Leading edge erosion on wind turbine blades is a recent topic of concern in the research
field due to its negative impact on the Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE). It was noted that
the erosion has a serious impact on the AEP production of the turbine, and repairing these
damages is expensive and requires a lot of resources. Multiple studies have been conducted on
the structural effects of these damages, and categorization methods have been developed to
predict the extent of the damage. However, categorization of damages based on performance
deficit is yet to be explored. The impact of the leading edge roughness on performance has
been studied, but very few correlations exist for the impact of the roughness. This thesis
dives deeper into the reasons for the reduction in performance and studies the impact of the
roughness on the frictional and pressure forces acting on the surface. It also delves into the
impact of the roughness height on the boundary layer of the flow to explain the reasons for
the observed changes. Key findings demonstrate that the roughness influences the pressure
forces more than the frictional forces. The ratio of roughness height and boundary layer
really shows the reasons for the resulting losses. The overall lift of the airfoil is completely
dominated by the pressure forces, and the overall drag is affected by both friction and
pressure.

1
Introduction
The increasing demand for renewable energy sources to compensate for the reduction of
fossil fuels has led to intense developments in the wind energy sector. From increasing wind
turbine size to the development of multirotor turbines to improve production capacity, the
levelized cost of energy (LCOE) for wind turbines and farms is rapidly reducing. However,
with the first turbines erected approaching the end of their predicted life cycle, a drop in
annual energy production (AEP) of these turbines has been observed. The erosion of the
leading edge of the wind turbine blades has been discovered to be one of the main factors
that cause a decrease in the performance of these turbines [1]. It is currently under the
spotlight as existing turbines show an alarming drop in AEP as shown in Fig. 1.
Figure 1: Loss of AEP of offshore and onshore wind turbines computed using BEMT and
CFD for different scales of roughness on the blades [1]
Blade inspections showed significant damage on the leading edge of the blade due to prolonged
exposure to natural elements, such as rain, that cause serious damage to the blade. A case
of leading edge erosion is shown in Fig. 2. The root sections of the blade were observed
to be mostly intact with minor damage, while the rest of the blade showed damage that
progressively increased toward the tip of the blade. The worst case of erosion was observed
at the tips where the inner fiberglass structure of the turbines was exposed. It was determined
that different particles striking these blades at high speeds cause fractures that develop over
time into large patches of erosion. These surface deformations, if not fixed, can make the
blade unusable after a period of time and also increase the risk of turbine failure, which can
lead to expensive repairs.

With the continuous development in large-scale turbines with increased power outputs and
massive rotor sizes, the tip speed of these blades approaches Mach speeds.
This raises
alarming concerns about the structural integrity of these large-scale turbines. Moreover, the
location of the placed turbine plays an important role. Offshore turbines show more erosion
due to the corrosive nature of salt water that weakens the blades. These problems, coupled
with the cost of repairing the turbine blades, result in the demand for the development
of a categorization method that helps predict the period for the repair of the blades in a
cost-effective way[3, 2]. Existing methods are available for categorization based on the type,
extent, and progression rate of the damage. However, there are no methods for categorizing
based on the performance losses of the turbine.
1.1
Performance loss
Several techniques have been developed to determine the effect of these roughnesses on the
loss of AEP. To calculate the overall loss in turbine performance, some of these employ
blade element momentum theory, but this requires numerous assumptions [1]. Modeling the
damage of erosion on each element of the blade is highly complicated and very expensive.
Therefore, one of the most important areas of research is developing a general loss variable
that these theories could use. A detailed analysis of these performance losses of wind turbines
revealed that the roughness of the leading edge significantly alters the lift and drag curve of
the airfoil sections of the blade.
The lift coefficient (Cl) decreased with an increase in the roughness of the surface, and the
drag coefficient (Cd) increased, as shown in Fig. 3. However, it can also be observed that the
flow at a low angle of attack is not affected, as the first contact of the flow is over the roughness
patch, nullifying its effects. As the angle of attack increases, the stagnation points further
descend on the pressure side of the airfoil, leading to the formation of turbulent vortices
when encountering roughness elements [4, 5, 6]. This raised concern for blade designers that
if the maximum Cl of the airfoils becomes less than the design Cl of the blade, then the
blade will start to operate in stall conditions. This is a highly undesirable outcome, as it
suggests that a turbine with eroded blades is at a very high risk of failure. Also, with the
new large-scale turbines being more vulnerable to erosion, the design of these blades is forced
to be reduced as a precautionary measure.

In-depth research of the reasons for the loss revealed that the flow behavior at the wall
boundary was affected. Small variations in boundary layer levels resulted in changes in the
pressure and frictional forces acting on the surface of the airfoil. The effects of these changes
on flat plates and pipes have been studied extensively, with multiple models developed to
find correlations between roughness and flow behavior. However, in-depth studies have not
been conducted on the effect of these changes on the boundary layer of the flow on an airfoil.
Most studies on airfoils have been limited to finding the final performance loss of the eroded
airfoil. The reason is the extreme randomness of these roughness patches, making it hard
to find a common numerical parameter. This leaves a huge unexplored research field, which
this thesis focuses on.
1.2
Thesis objective
Various techniques have been developed to understand the performance of an eroded turbine.
However, none of this study explores the specifics of what occurs on the surface for each
erosion that is observed. The aim of this thesis is to establish a common ground between
the observed roughness and the performance figures of these airfoils. This would assist us in
estimating the impact that a specific kind of roughness would have on the turbine’s overall
performance. Calculation techniques must be developed to thoroughly investigate the forces
that act on each element. Using these approaches, four distinct studies have been performed.
These four studies are the following.
• Study the effect of the consequent pressure and frictional forces on the total performance
of the airfoil.
• Study the effect of roughness on the pressure and suction side of the airfoil.
• Study the contribution of roughness to the performance of the rest of the airfoil.
• To understand the effect of the roughness height on the boundary layer of the flow.

The data used for the studies come from fully turbulent 2D simulations performed on three
different airfoils with four different cases of roughness at different Reynolds numbers and

The thesis outlines the results from all these cases with the ultimate
objective of attempting to comprehend the significance of each case for the overall performance.

2
Roughness and flow
The aerodynamic and hydrodynamic effects of the roughness of a surface on the flow have
been a well-researched topic for a long time, with many comprehensive studies.
Hagen,
followed by Darcy, pioneered the research by investigating turbulent flows in pipes [7, 8].
They observed that the pressure change induced by the variations on the surface has an
effect on the drag forces acting on the flow. These variations on the surface, also termed
roughness, affect the flow boundary layer, resulting in instabilities in momentum transfer [9].
Multiple studies cover the effects of surface roughness on flow behavior and find a correlation
between the types of roughness patterns and the consequent losses in the flow.
2.1
Formation of surface roughness
Surface roughness are changes in the topography of the surface of a body formed as a
result of processes such as erosion, corrosion, and manufacturing processes.
The loss of
material from the top surface results in different textures and patterns on the surface that
lead to increased friction and pressure losses when induced to flow. These losses can affect
the performance and structural integrity of the body [9]. Multiple studies have shown the
change in the boundary layer due to roughness on flat surfaces, pipes, and airfoils. Variations
on the surface of a body can be classified based on the frequency of the height of the
variations. Low-frequency variations are called surface form, medium-frequency changes are
called waviness, and high-frequency changes are called roughness. High-frequency changes
are mostly irregular and hence cannot be defined on the basis of normal wave equations.
Hence, to quantify these changes, multiple methods have been developed and used [9].
The airfoils found in wind turbine blades, turbomachinery, and airplanes have shown significant
losses in aerodynamic performance due to small variations on the mm scale [10, 11]. Wind
turbine roughness can be created in multiple ways. From deformations in the manufacturing
process to the paint on the blades, these can reduce the performance of the blades. The
main problem explored in this thesis is leading edge roughness (LER) observed in turbine
blades. LER is a result of the sudden transfer of kinetic energy from a striking particle
on the leading edge of the blade. Particles such as raindrops and hail strike the blade and
create contact pressure on the protective layer of the blade, causing the blade surface to be
distorted. Eventually, after reaching a critical state, the damage begins in the form of a
very small crack that can be invisible to the eye. The weakened surface, when exposed to
more elements, results in crack propagation, and the trapped moisture within these cracks
weakens the composite structure further. Eventually, the damage reaches the putty layer of
the blade where the air bubble is trapped by the protective layer. The putty layer is made
of interlocking mesh grids that can trap these bubbles. If moisture reaches the gaps in the
blade, the damage can become severe and cause large fragments of the surface to break off
[2]. This process is represented in Fig. 4. Other forms of roughness on the leading edge
include the accumulation of snow, ice on the blades in cold conditions, and the accumulation
of salt and dust on offshore turbines. These types of roughness can be destructive to the
composite structure of the blades and can also be easily fixed at very low costs.

2.2
Flow over surfaces
To discuss the flow over an airfoil, the different methods developed to define the roughness
and flow over a flat surface.
Each patch of roughness created by erosion is stochastic
in nature, making it unique. This makes it very difficult to define a common ground to
categorize them. Throughout the years, many studies have been conducted with the goal of
finding a variable that defines the flow on a rough surface. Most of them extensively research
the flow very close to the surface, also called the boundary layer. The boundary layer of
an airfoil represents a thin layer in close vicinity to the wall where the tangential velocity is
less than the free stream velocity due to friction at the wall. The boundary layer develops
over time, from laminar flow at the surface to turbulent flow. A flow is said to be laminar
when viscous forces predominate, turbulent when pressure forces do, and transitional when
the forces are nearly equal during the middle stage. It is necessary to study the boundary
layer to understand the impact of roughness.
Transition in fluids is a very important concept, as even for smooth surfaces without any
roughness, transition occurs.
The reason for this is instabilities in the flow.
Roughness
on the surface is a major instability, due to which the transition may be initiated earlier.
Small vortices that form near the surface due to roughness can disrupt the laminar flow,
resulting in its breakdown. For clean airfoils, transition can occur through multiple methods
such as natural transition, bypass transition, and separation-induced transition. In natural
transition, instabilities like Tollmien-Schlichting waves gradually intensify before dissolving
into turbulence. Bypass transition, on the other hand, happens when significant levels of
external disturbances, such as surface roughness or free-stream turbulence, create a direct
shift to turbulence.
Separation-induced transition occurs when an unfavorable pressure
differential causes the flow to separate from a surface, causing instability and the creation of
vortices that lead to turbulence. The simulation of transition in airfoils is extremely difficult
to simulate and expensive; hence, this thesis focuses only on fully turbulent conditions.

Boundary layer for smooth and rough surfaces
The boundary layer of smooth and rough surfaces is described on a flat plate without any
pressure gradients. For a smooth surface, the fully turbulent boundary layer can be separated
into the inner layer and the outer layer.
The inner layer represents the section of the
flow from the surface that is affected by viscosity and other parameters, such as turbulent
eddies. The outer layer and the free stream are mostly independent of the viscosity but are
very susceptible to change because of the geometry. In summary, a small variation of the
roughness only affects the inner layer, and large changes will completely alter the flow over
the surface. The inner layer is further classified into three: the viscous sublayer, the buffer
layer, and the logarithmic layer. An example of a boundary layer profile is shown in Fig. 5.
Figure 5: Depiction of a boundary layer over smooth surfaces [9]
The viscous sublayer represents the flow closest to the surface, where the velocity of the flow
changes linearly with the distance from the wall. In this region, viscosity dominates the
flow and hence the viscous shear stress is much greater than the Reynolds shear stress. This
is represented in terms of the normalized tangential velocity (U +) and the normalized wall
distance (y+) as:
U + = y+
(1)

acts as a transitional layer between the viscous sub-layer and the logarithmic layer. The
logarithmic sublayer represents the section of the flow that follows the log law and can hence
be described by a log function. In this layer, the Reynold shear stress surpasses the viscous
shear stress. This is represented as:
U + = 1

(2)
Where κ is the Von Karman constant (κ ≈0.41). The logarithmic law is valid only for
y+ < 700.

the difference in this difference multiple theories such as the Law of the wake by Coles and
the Spalding law of the wall by Spalding were proposed. The law of the wake assumes the
existence of an additional wake function to the existing logarithmic equation. The only issue

2.3.5
Conclusion regarding statistical approaches
Statistical parameters are a widely used measure of roughness. The parameters mentioned
have uses to separate between different roughness. However, it fails to separate the type
of roughness that exists in real-world conditions. The best example of this case are the
variations found in eroded wind turbine blades.
2.3.6
Flow based approach
To generalize the shape and size of the roughness based on the flow over the surface, the
concept of sand grain roughness was presented by Nikuradse [15, 16]. The parameter called
the height of the roughness of the sand grain (k) was raised based on the diameter of a single
sand grain on the surface similar to a sandpaper, as shown in Fig. 10. However, in a real
case the roughness height of the sand grain cannot be applied directly, and therefore the
use of a length scale to represent the equivalent roughness of the surface was proposed [16].
This length scale is called the equivalent roughness of the sand grain (ks) and was calculated
based on similarities in flow over the surface for the two cases. ks is a widely used parameter
as a measure of small-scale roughness.

2.4
Research in leading edge eroded blades
All the parameters mentioned above assume that roughness is present on a flat surface.
Hence, applying these theories to curved surfaces, such as turbine blades, is not directly
possible. The erosion on the blades is different compared to a flat plate. The variations in
the surface of a flat plate are usually in microscopic scale and most changes are contained
within the viscous sublayer. Furthermore, the curvature of the blades changes pressure, that
is, the boundary layers over airfoils are subjected to large pressure gradients compared to
flat plates. The correlations discussed before work perfectly for a hydrodynamically smooth
flow. But the changes on highly eroded blades are very deep and have specific artifacts such
as forward-facing steps that have the ability to reverse the flow direction at that point. This
would mean that the pressure forces acting on the rough surface will exceed the viscous
force, creating a hydro-dynamically rough flow.
However, considering the fact that the
pressure is not uniform across the surface, finding a correlation between the flow and the
roughness is very complicated. Thus, most studies on eroded blades are limited to the overall
performance.
2.4.1

Multiple studies have been conducted on the loss of lift and drag of an airfoil due to the
presence of roughness. Sareen et al.[4] studied the DU 96-W-180 experimentally at three

showed that there was a 6 −500% increase in drag from light to heavy erosion. The lift had
a substantial decrease especially at lower Reynolds numbers. Gaudern [30] experimentally

performance of the GA (W) -1 airfoil with varying roughness sizes and distribution patterns.
The conclusions regarding the losses were the same, but using PIV it was discovered that
the staggered distribution of the roughness results in higher fluctuation in the wake flow
than aligned patterns.

Existing categorization methods
The different categorization methods that exist are purely focused on the visual aspects of
the blade. Since wind turbine blades are made up of multiple laminate layers, it is easier
to identify the extent of damage based on the exposed layer[33]. Other methods used for
classification include machine learning to study blade patterns and categorize them according
to the extent of damage. The classification of the leading edge images was carried out by
Best et al. [34] where they tried to use different machine learning algorithms and were able
to achieve an accuracy of 90% with one of the models. Another research by Aird et al. [35]
compared conventional machine learning algorithms with the pixel-by-pixel shadow ratio
algorithm to classify damage. They found that the conventional method provides better
results.
Extracting the roughness from the curved surface and using statistical parameters to characterize
the roughness is another method that can be used. This involves unfolding the leading edge
of the airfoils and could result in errors during the process. Hence, the classification of these
types of erosion is done manually, based on the height change and the pattern of erosion
observed. The issue with these methods of categorization is that they provide no information
regarding the performance figures of the blades.
They are great at showing structural
integrity, as these damages are categorized according to structural performance. However,
since there is no aerodynamic classification, it is necessary to establish a relationship between
roughness and aerodynamic loss.

the dominant force that acts on the section can help in understanding the effect of the
roughness. Although there are numerous other studies on the subject, this thesis is the first
to examine in greater detail whether separating the friction and pressure components aids
in estimating the impact of roughness. This can also give way to understanding the effect
of roughness on the boundary layer on an airfoil. The boundary layer is not uniform in an

Methodology
This thesis focuses on two different studies. The first study involves finding the effect of
friction and pressure forces acting on the airfoils on the total aerodynamic forces.
The
second study investigates more deeply the influence of roughness height on the boundary
layer of airfoils. This section describes the methods and data used to complete each study.
3.1
CFD results and data
The data used in this thesis are the results of CFD simulations performed on different types
of airfoil sections as part of the LERCat project, where three different airfoils of varying
thicknesses are studied [37]. The airfoils FFA-W3-211, NACA-63-618a1, and DU91-w2-250
shown in Fig. 12 were created with different configurations of roughness at the leading
edge. A spectral model generalizing the surface perturbations from leading edge erosion
and its application in CFD is used. The data used for the thesis consist of results from 2D

There are two major challenges involved in comparing and extracting the roughness of these
two surfaces. The first is to calculate the height of the roughness from the reference surface,
and the second challenge is to calculate the position of the specific roughness. To solve the
first challenge, a minimize function is used to find the smallest difference in height between

function for solving this issue helps in finding the normal distance from the eroded surface
because the shortest difference from a point to a curve represents the normal distance from
it. The second challenge of computing the position of the erosion is solved by calculating
the curved distance of the clean airfoil and assigning a distance value to each element of the
roughness. This data is then recentered to match the leading edge of the airfoil.

Boundary layer study
The final study involves understanding the effects of the roughness height on the boundary
layer, and the methodology defined before was applied. The data is used after correction of
errors in the boundary layer height data and extracting the roughness map from all three

help us learn what occurs to the flow. As discussed in the methodology, the boundary layer
height is compared to the roughness height. When the ratio exceeds one, the flow becomes
hydrodynamically rough flow. The higher the value of the ratio, the more unstable the flow
is around the airfoil. These crossings would result in the formation of turbulent eddies in

5
Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to find correlations between aerodynamic performance and
roughness. Many studies have been conducted on the overall results of losses due to roughness
present on the leading edge. Only a very few studies have taken into account the depth of
what happens due to the roughness on the surface of a wind turbine. It was also learned
that there are multiple methods for defining the flow over an eroded surface for flat plates.
However, none of these methods are directly applicable to wind turbine blades because the
surface is non-uniform and the roughness due to erosion is extremely random. Therefore, this
thesis explores multiple methodologies that can help in understanding the effect of leading
edge roughness of a wind turbine blade on aerodynamic performance.
5.1
Conclusions from the studies
In this thesis, four levels of roughness have been studied, each representing a type of erosion.
The data on the performance of these erosions were obtained from fully turbulent simulations
performed in CFD for different Reynolds numbers and angles of attack.
The following
conclusions were obtained from the first study.
• The first study on the contribution of pressure and friction forces to total performance
showed that airfoil lift was not affected by the frictional component, but airfoil drag
was significantly affected by pressure and frictional forces.
• Studying the changes due to the varying Reynolds number, it was observed that there
was a very small shift in the result.
• For thicker airfoils, it is observed that pressure forces dominate in the overall drag, but
for the thinner airfoils it seems that friction plays a very important role at low angles
of attacks.
• The reason for this was explained by the distribution of the roughness and the effect
of the roughness height on the boundary layer of the thick airfoil.
The second study involving the contribution from the pressure and suction sides of the airfoil
showed many differences between the three airfoil sections. The following conclusions were
obtained from the second study.
• Conclusions show that the suction side of the airfoil is the part most affected by
roughness.
• The extreme changes in performance due to the presence of a forward facing step was
very clear in the results obtained.
• The step change observed was studied and the forward facing step creates some instability
at that angle of attack.
Page 59 of 65
==PAGE:70==
Aerodynamic effects of leading-edge roughness
The sectional study of the airfoil showed great differences in the results when comparing the
effects of Reynolds numbers and significant differences when comparing the effects of the
geometry of the airfoil. The following conclusions were obtained from the third study.
• It was concluded that the leading edge of the airfoil does not affect the pressure forces
as much as it does the frictional forces.
• It was also drawn that a higher Reynolds number does not necessarily mean worse
losses.
The final study on the boundary layer explained the reasons for all these observed changes
in previous studies. The following conclusions were obtained from the final study.
• It was very conclusive that increasing the Reynolds number results in a lesser number
of roughness elements crossing the boundary layer height.
• This explains the lower aerodynamic losses from a higher Reynolds number results
• In general, this study proved that thicker airfoils are less susceptible to aerodynamic
performance losses due to erosion but still pose a large threat.
In the end, it could be concluded that the objective of the thesis was achieved. A really good
method to understand the effect of the roughness was to know the height of the boundary
layer and the height of the roughness. The results of the final method perfectly act as a
strong point for the other methods. All four methods helped us to understand what happens
to these forces as a result of erosion. They can be applied to the results from any simulations
or experiments of leading-edge eroded turbines to further help understand the problem.
5.2
Future work
Moving forward from this study, it would be amazing to test these theories in real cases of
erosion. More boundary layer data for the flow at the surface of these eroded airfoils could
help us understand whether these theories are perfectly valid for all situations. Studying
the transitional effects of these roughness cases is very important.
As discussed before,
large-scale roughness results in bypass transition, and observing the effect of the roughness
on the boundary layer is crucial. Furthermore, developing this theory into a 3D method to
apply to real cases of wind turbine blades would be the ideal method for categorizing these
erosions based on their aerodynamic performance. Using machine learning approaches to
help guide the conclusions of these results would be an excellent idea for future work.
