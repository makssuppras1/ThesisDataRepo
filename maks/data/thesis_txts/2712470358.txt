Abstract
Climate change is expected to cause more intense and extreme rain events
in the future, which, in combination with increasing urbanization, will create
new challenges for the environment and for everyone involved in its protection.
Among other issues, extreme rain events lead to the accumulation of different
pollutants into natural water bodies. In order to control for such accumulations,
the pollutant loads need to be quantified, monitored, and modelled. At the time
being, the pollutants load estimation is held back by lack of available water
quality data, as well as the lack of universal estimation methodology.
Throughout this project, an experimental method was created for approxi-
mating the pollutant loads and their dependency on the area of an urban land
use, that are discharged through combined sewer overflows (CSOs) during rain
events. The method consists of three major steps: i) analysing the urban land
use and land cover by creating a semi-automatic classification ii) estimating a
CSO catchment delineation by using a gravitational flow-based algorithm iii)
applying pollutant concentrations measured on an urban river to the areas and
discharged values calculated in the previous steps. The method combines different
open-source data into a succession of several geographic information system (GIS)
analysis. The newly created model is thus able to approximate the pollutant
loads based on terrain topography and different land uses. It was found that
increasing the road surface areas by 10% will have an influence of up to 8.5%
over the pollutant loads.
Simultaneously, different open and commercial data sources, for digital el-
evation model (DEM) data have been tested in parallel with the purpose of
delineating the CSOs catchments. The results were compared with official data
from Denmark and Switzerland. The results showed that even though the resolu-
tion difference between the two sets is considerable (30m for open source data vs.
0.4m for commercial data), the results are, sufficiently similar for allowing the
analysis to be done on the 30m resolution DEM. This finding increases the confi-
dence that an open source (in this case NASA) DEM can be used for catchment
delineations.
The conclusion is that the available open-source data can be used for calcu-
lating the dependency of certain pollutants to certain areas of different land uses,
but it is difficult to approximate how accurate the method is without complete
measurements: pollutant concentrations and discharge volume.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Climate change and urbanization are becoming an increasingly challenging problem
(Yang et al., 2013). The negative effects of climate change, in combination with an
acceleration of urbanization around the world contribute to degradation of water quality.
Moreover, climate change leads to an increasing occurrence and intensity of heavy
storms, which means that wet weather discharges (WWD) from a combined sewer
overflow (CSO) will also occur more often, with negative effects on the environment
(Owolabi et al., 2022). In addition to contributing to potential flood risk, the runoff
can wash up and spread pollutants into the environment (Wicke et al., 2021).
A significant part of the total pollution load that is discharged into environment is
carried by stormwater runoff from the impervious surfaces in urban areas. This makes
urban impervious areas an important source of micropollutants (MP), (Wicke et al.,
2021), independent of whether the MP come from building materials (Cu, Zn), paint
biocides (Burkhardt et al., 2012), or Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbon (PAH) from
roads, as a result of combustion process of vehicles (Wicke et al., 2021).
Quantifying pollutant loads from a WWD is important when considering the impact it
has over the natural waters (Vezzaro et al., 2015), yet running a monitoring system
becomes an expensive and time-consuming process, as WWD is influenced by the
unpredictability of its occurrence. Nevertheless, there are many different models that
are used for simulating the quantity and quality of the pollution produced by the
discharging CSOs, yet due to the multitude of variables, the calibration of such models
is difficult to obtain, leaving plenty of room for uncertainties (Dittmer et al., 2020).
This project aims at an estimation of pollutant loads from WWD by using multiple
open source data sets in order to contribute to establishment of a flexible model that
has potential to be applied independently of the geographical location, within certain
limitations. Two major data sets from two countries, i.e., Denmark and Switzerland,
will be used throughout this project, to verify the results:
• urban classification: Switzerland
• CSOs catchments, both size and borders: Switzerland and Denmark.
The quality of water discharged through a CSO during a rain event will be estimated.
Also, it will be assessed how the pollution sources influence the pollutant loads. There-
fore, in order to determine the origin of stormwater, the CSO catchment is delineated,
and its land use will be analysed. More precisely, the contaminants looked in this
project are part of PAH group. The reliability of the micropollutant load estimation in
the WWD is cross-checked by comparing the obtained results with data provided by
the Danish Environmental Agency.
The reason for choosing to use open source data for the analyses, is the scarcity of water
quality data in many places where the digitization of the public information system is
not developed yet, as traditional processes of water data sampling are tedious, expensive
and time consuming (Prasad et al., 2022). From this point of view, Denmark is one

of the most advanced countries in the world (Digitalizeringsstyrelsen, 2022), which
makes it suitable for comparing the results with governmental data provided by the
Danish Environmental Agency. By using open source data sets, the method could be
use for approximating the pollutant loads wherever such data does not exist, or to test
different scenarios, regarding rainfall, the position of a CSO, its catchment and land use.
1.2
Research questions
In this project, the following research questions will be addressed:
• Can satellite imagery be applied to develop a machine learning algorithm, that
can be universally applied to identify land use of urban areas?
• Can a realistic catchment of a CSO be estimated, using the available open-source
data?
• Are the results obtained with open source data comparable to the ones obtained
using higher resolution, commercial data?
• Do pollutant loads depend on the land use and land cover they are washed off
from? If so, how much?
The research questions presented above will be addressed in two stages. The first
part of the analysis focuses on the land use classification. All classification analyses
have been made on open source Sentinel-2 satellite images, from Copernicus Land
Monitoring Services. The robustness of the classification will be tested and discussed
in section 3.1, by using different methods and references: i) official Swiss land cover
classification and ii) SCP accuracy assessment method (Olofsson et al., 2014). The
second part of the geospatial analysis will be made for estimating the delineation and
area of a CSO catchment, for which the land use classification will be applied.

2.1.1
Sentinel-2 Data
NASA data sets have been in the past the main source for remote sensing data, however
new data sources have emerged and are now available from more than 1400 satellites
that orbit the Earth (Zhu et al., 2019). These new data sets can simulate a variety
of remote sensing algorithms and applications, from high spatial resolution of night
time light imagery (Li et al., 2019), to satellites that can map urban environments in
3D at global scale (C. Liu et al., 2019), to Sentinel mission from the European Earth
Monitoring Program, of European Space Agency (ESA).
Sentinel missions started in 2014 with the purpose to provide robust data sets for the
Copernicus programme. Sentinel-1 was launched in April 2014 and with its highly
developed RADAR system can see through clouds, rain and darkness, being used for
change detection and planning flood mitigation measures. Sentinel-2, with its single
multi spectral instrument provides a different perspective of the Earth surface, allowing
for monitoring in different spectral bands. They were followed by Sentinel-3, which
together with Sentinel-6 are tailored for monitoring the oceans and other open waters
and finally, Sentinel-4 and 5 are designed for monitoring the atmosphere (ESA, 2020,
www.esa.int).

Sentinel-2 is a polar-orbiting, multi spectral instrument (MSI), for Earth Observation
(EO) mission for land monitoring. With its 13 bands and a high resolution of 10m, it is
excellent for analysing land and water cover, imagery of vegetation, water ways, coastal
areas. At the same time, all data from the Copernicus programme is public and free of
charge, to encourage maximum use both in data-rich countries, but also in countries
where data base systems lack information, or are non existent. Thus, Sentinel-2 willl
be the go-to option for the satellite imagery for this project.
Out of the thirteen bands of the Sentinel-2 images, three of them: 1, 9 and 10 have
a very low spatial resolution, of ≈60m and low spectral characteristics (Gascon et
al., 2017) and thus, they cannot be used for the classification, Figure 2. The ten
bands that are downloaded will be level 2-A Sentinel-2 images, which are already
atmospherically corrected, i.e., the atmospheric effects are removed to reduce the
probability for erroneous results.

2.1.2
DEM: USGS Data vs. Danish Data
Delineating the CSOs’ catchments is not an easy and straight forward task, as the
catchments are often influenced among others, by the pipes system, roads system
and topography (Duque et al., 2022). As information about the road system, or pipe
system is not part of open source data in too many countries, the decision has been
made that the CSOs’ catchments will be calculated based only on the gravitational
flow, disregarding other aspects that might influence the delineation of a catchment,
such as any anthropogenic influences, i.e., road system, pipes system, administrative
boundaries.
For downloading digital elevation model (DEM) data from an open source, the Shuttle
Radar Topography Mission (SRTM) plugin has been used in QGIS. What SRTM plugin
does is to download data provided by NASA, as tiles. The DEM tiles of a specific
region will be automatically downloaded from the NASA website (NASA, 2022), after
creating an user account. The SRTM plugin offers the possibility of acquiring elevation
data at a global scale, with a highest resolution of 30m. One big drawback, though is
the fact that NASA does not provide data above 600N parallel, roughly the latitude of
Oslo and Helsinki.
Figure 3: SRTM data coverage (NASA, 2022). Capture from QGIS.
For validating the results obtained with data from NASA, the official Danish DEM,
provided by Dataforsyningen has been used, which has a much higher resolution, of
0.4m. In section 3.3.2, the results obtained with both data sets will be compared to
find out whether a higher resolution of DEM is sufficient for improving the accuracy of
catchment delineations.

2.2
Assumptions
In order for the experimental nature of the project to be tested, some assumptions will
need to be formulated and taken into consideration when interpreting the results.
• Classification: quantitative, rather than qualitative, meaning that the focus will
not necessary be on pin-pointing the location of the roads, but on the total
surface area covered by a certain land use, in relation to the total analyzed area.
• CSOs catchment: based on gravitational flow, rather than influenced by roads
and pipes system; information about roads and pipes system, administrative
boundaries are difficult, or impossible to find, depending a lot also by the area of
interest. To eliminate this bias, the CSOs catchments will be calculated solely
based on the gravitational flow, i.e., DEM.
• CSO discharge: based on average yearly precipitations; the available information
from GEUS (GEUS, 2022) about CSOs in Denmark and their discharge is in
yearly volume, even though in the precipitation data there are information about
each rain event.
• Pollutant loads will also be calculated in yearly volume, which is a direct conse-
quence of the assumption above.
• information about CSOs discharge for other couturiers is assumed to be available
from the Joint Research Center (JRC).
• Pollutants concentration, based on Berlin data; there is the risk that, depending
of area of interest, the pollutant loads to be evaluated to high, due to the nature
of starting concentrations i.e., measured on an urban river, in one of the biggest
cities of Europe.

2.3
Urban Land Use Classification
An urban land use classification can have anywhere between two, e.g., pervious and
impervious areas, to 35 - 40 classes, as it is the case for the Swiss classification, from
Swiss cadastral system (Cadastre.ch, 2021), Appendix B. As the purpose of this project
was to analyse the pollution originating from roads, the classification was made such
that the impervious surfaces from an urban area were divided into buildings and roads.
This way it was possible to extract the road surfaces from the total impervious area,
and connect the pollution with area covered by the roads.
Thus, the resulting classification has a number of four classes. As the classification is
based on the spectral reflectance of the surface, it is difficult to distinct between different
types of buildings e.g., industrial, public, residential buildings Figure 7. Therefore all
buildings will be classified under the same macro-class, i.e., Buildings.

2.3.1
SCP Plugin
Many earth surface features of interest can be identified, mapped, and studied on
the basis of their spectral characteristics. While some features of interest cannot be
spectrally separated, others have very similar spectral reflectance, which make it more
difficult when trying to separate different surfaces. Thus, it is important to understand
the spectral characteristics of the particular features.
The Semiautomatic Classification Plugin (SCP) (Congedo, 2021) is a free open source
plugin, developed by Luca Congedo, allowing for supervised classification of remote
sensing images, whether they are multi-band, or single band images. Additionally,
the plugin offers also tools for pre-processing and post-processing of the data and/or
of the results. SCP provides a supervised machine learning algorithm for Land Use
(LU) and Land Cover (LC) classification. Being supervised means that the algorithm
needs a training process, where it will recognize the special characteristics, i.e., spectral
reflectance of each label, name, type, ie., category and being able later on to recognize
all the categories together when shown just one image.
As explained in 2.1.1, the Sentinel-2 images consist in 13 separate bandes, of which
only 10 will be downloaded and processed. Seen one by one, the bands will appear
as highly pixelated (depending on the scale) black-and-white images, which would be
difficult, even impossible to work with. This can be fixed by changing the combination
of the spectral bands in channels, so that different characteristics can be emphasized.

As the number of classes of a training input for an urban classification can have a
high variability, depending on the purpose and the method of the classification, as well
as the data set used (Cai et al., 2019; Lu, 2006). The starting point for this project
will be a number of four classes, i.e., vegetation, roads, buildings and water. This
simplified approach helps to differentiate between permeable and impermeable surfaces,
i.e., finding the reduced factor and reduced area, which later on will be applied to each
catchment.
The process of creating the training input, together with classes and macro classes is
a tedious and highly sensitive one, especially in an urban area, due to relatively low
resolution of the Sentinel-2 images, in relation with the level of details in an urban
environment.
Each region of interest (ROI) that will be created for each of the four classes has a
specific wavelength, and a narrow range. It has been a long process of trials and testing
to find the balance between the number of ROIs, the accuracy of of the classification
and also the computation time. The conclusion was that the most efficient method is
to create relatively few (≈50) small polygons of similar dimensions, that are uniformly
distributed over the area. The training dataset has been evaluated to ensure that each
category is adequately represented, and every spectral reflectance is covered (Richards,
2022). The training data set was developed using manual identification of the four
classes and the information from this training data set was applied to the rest of the
areas.
The classification output is saved in a raster file, where each pixel has the value of one
of the four classes, plus an extra class -zero-, which is automatically categorized as "No
data".
Other methods of supervised and unsupervised classification, using different tools from
System for Automated Geoscientifc Analysis (SAGA) were tested, but failed. SAGA is
an open source GIS software, that can be used alone, or within QGIS. The two tested
methods are: i) Unsupervised classification, using K-means Clustering for Rasters and
ii) Supervised Classification for Rasters, which uses the same principle, with input of
training areas, as the SCP method. None of these methods gave a satisfactory result,
so the decision was taken to continue with the SCP method.

2.3.3
Classification algorithm
The SCP plugin provides the user with three classification algorithms, i.e., Minimum
Distance, Maximum Likelihood and Spectral Angle Mapping. The algorithms calculate
the spectral signature of each land cover classes, considering the value of pixels from
the ROIs, meaning that the image is classified by comparing the spectral characteristics
of each pixel to the mean spectral characteristics of each ROI, saved under Macroclass
ID, or Class ID (Congedo, 2014).
Spectral Angle Mapping algorithm has been proved to be the most efficient in such
a case (Y. Liu et al., 2019): the more traditional methods of classification, such as
Minimum Distance, or Maximum Likelihood have a higher efficiency as long as the
number of training inputs is large enough. Their accuracy will decrease significantly
when the the ratio between the number of training samples and the number of available
channels i.e., number of features, will decrease (Hughes phenomenon) (Hughes, 1968).
As the aim of this classification is to be applied universally, it is therefore desirable to
have as few highly particular features as possible, creating a limitation in the number
of ROIs used as input for the classification.

of an image pixel and the training spectral signature (Kruse et al., 1993), as:

2.4
CSOs catchment delineation
2.4.1
Theory
According to the definition, a catchment refers to the surface area for which all the
water drains to one outlet, also called drainage basin (Kwas and Menk, 2019). The
water can originate from rain, melting snow, glaciers and it will converge to a single
point at a lower elevation, which is usually the exit point of the basin where the waters
joint another water body, e.g., river, lake, sea, ocean, hence the gravitational flow-based
algorithm. DEM is a universal tool in the process of delineating a watershed, whether
the catchment is natural, or part of an urban area. However, in an urban area, the
pipes and drainage systems do not always follow the topography of the terrain and the
algorithm will not recognize the man-made features, such as pipes, roads, ditch (Chen
et al., 2011; Han and Hammond, 2006). This theory will be tested and the results will
be compared with data about urban CSOs catchments, where available.
2.4.2
Gravitational flow-based algorithm for catchment delineation
The algorithm for delineating a catchment that will be used throughout the project
is based on the gravitational flow and was developed for hydrological and water man-
agement applications, with the main purpose for delineating catchments for big river
basins (Kwas and Menk, 2019). However, throughout this project, the same algorithm
will be tested at a much larger scale, delineating catchments for CSOs, in urban areas.
That means that the two important aspects are i) DEM, for the gravitational flow
and ii) defining the outflow point. Therefore, any anthropogenic limitations, such as
road systems, pipe systems, or administrative boundaries, that would normally be
involved when designing a CSO catchment, will not be taken into consideration for
this project. All the described processes for hydrological analysis use algorithms from
SAGA (System for Automated Geoscientific Analysis) depository, in QGIS.
Regardless on the dataset that is used, i.e., USGS-NASA, or any other source for
acquiring the DEM, the algorithm for gravitational flow based for delineating the CSOs
catchments will be the same and it is presented in Figure 27 and explained below,
orange color meaning that the step is not mandatory in order for the algorithm to
work.

The first step is to download the DEM, which usually comes in tiles. Depending on the
country and location, data about DEM can be found from local sources, but more often
than not, it is not available due to high costs, or even political reasons. However, there
are sources for global open access data, in this case SRTM (Shuttle Radar Topography
Mission), provided by NASA and USGS (United States Geological Survey), with a
resolution of around 30m at the equator. Nevertheless, a higher resolution of the data
does not automatically mean a higher accuracy and these two terms should not be
confused. Even with a very high spatial resolution, the error per pixel can also be
very high, thus, the highest resolution is not necessary the best solution for every
purpose. The DEM tiles can be downloaded either from the USGS Earth Explorer
website (earthexplorer.usgs.gov), or directly in QGIS from SRTM Downloader plugin.
If a single DEM tile does not cover the whole study area, several tiles will be down-
loaded. Merging them represents the second step and the result will be the DEM
mosaic. No matter if one or more tiles have been downloaded, they will need to be
reprojected because the global datasets are usually in Geographic Coordinate System,
i.e., Latitude/Longitude and the unis in degrees. For DEM analysis, metric units
are normally used, therefore, the DEM tiles need to be reprojected to a Coordinate
Reference System (CRS), from the area of interest. Even though it does not seem
important, this is a crucial step, as the slope is calculated as:
slope = arctan ∆z
∆x
(4)
Where, z is elevation and x is distance.It is thus important that both z and x to have
the same units.
The next step is to subset the DEM, i.e., clipping the DEM. This step is necessary
because usually, the study area usually does not cover the whole mosaic, but only part
of it. Raster tiles are in general part of big datasets, which require heavy processing,
17 of 52
==PAGE:22==
so by subsetting the mosaic, the processing time and requirements will be decreased
considerably, avoiding this way a possible risk for the computer to run out of memory.
Interpolating the voids is an optional step, but which could have influence over the
accuracy of the results. Voids are pixels in the DEM, which have Nodata, due to
acquisition procedure, e.g., shaded areas, or snow covered areas. By interpolating these
areas, the surroundings information will be used to cover for the missing information.
The result will be as closest to reality as possible, but nevertheless, this procedure
represents a recreation of reality, and it is thus not 100% real.
The next step is filling the sinks and removing the spikes. Sinks shall not be mistaken
with voids. While voids are Nodata, sinks small depressions (artificial, or natural)
where water could get stuck. They could also be caused by the DEM creation process.
In other words, a sink is a cell which has no downstreams cells around it.
Figure 14: Grafic representation of a "sink"
Burning in the stream network is another optional step, but which could improve the
overall accuracy. It is used to force the flow direction algorithm to follow the real river,
but only as long as a river layer is already available, which makes this step not too
practical in places where public available data is hard to find, or it does not exist at all.
Calculating the flow direction is similar to the algorithm for calculating the slope, i.e., a
3x3 Kernel window will move over the whole surface and it will calculate the directions
to all the cells that are considered, but instead of returning the slope, in degrees, it
will assign the direction of the steepest slope Figure 15.

The method used in this project for deriving streams is the Strahler method. The
algorithm is based on the flow accumulation principle, as the precipitation that falls
within a catchment will flow to a stream link and towards the outlet and subsequently
will accumulate Figure 28a. The return result will be a raster file with values from 0 to
10, where the bigger the value, the bigger the river is.
Based on this ordering, a threshold can be set for defining what will be called a river, or
a stream. When delineating a river catchment, a threshold of 8 is sufficient for deriving
the biggest streams and rivers from that catchment, but for deriving catchments at a
much larger scale, e.g., CSO catchment, a much smaller threshold is necessary. For
the calibration of the model, a base map needs to be used, which usually is a satellite
image, or an existing map. After a succession of trials and errors, the conclusion was
that a threshold of 3 for the Strahler method is sufficient for deriving small catchments,
but at the same time big enough so it does not overrun the computing power.

higher reflectance than asphalt, they are often mistaken and mixed by classification
algorithms. Moreover, more often than not, these materials are also mistaken with
bare soil. There are several factors that influence the soil spectral reflectance, such as
moisture, roughness, composition. Each one of these factors can, independently or in
combination with others, make soil look similar to asphalt, or concrete. For example, a
higher moisture in the soil means a lower reflectance. At the same time, as materials,
such as asphalt and concrete age, their spectral reflectance will increase, bringing it
closer to the soil reflectance.

Outlook
This project started as an experimental approach for finding new methods of approx-
imating the amount of pollutants that is washed off from different land uses and
discharged through CSOs, during rain events so, it relies therefore on several assump-
tions.
The model created in this project and the preliminary results obtained are a starting
point towards the creation of a more accurate model. However, it is not possible at this
time to fully evaluate the accuracy of the results, due to lack of pollutant concentrations
data for the CSO discharges.
For future model development, the following aspects should be considered:
• Testing higher resolution satellite images for urban land use classification and
comparing the results to the ones obtained with Sentinel-2 images.
• Using anthropogenic limitations when delineating the CSO catchment.
• Having the CSO catchment borders from Denmark, as the simulated results are
similar to the official ones, would have been useful to help understanding how
the catchments are delineated and how the gravitational-based delineation differs
from the official one.
• Calculating the roads surface area and and finding their approximate location is
not sufficient for a precise evaluation of the pollutant loads, therefore taking into
account the traffic volume, as well, would increase the precision of the results.

5
Conclusions
The main focus throughout the project was on the GIS analysis, i.e., hydrographic and
topographic analysis, remote sensing analysis and how this relatively new tool can be
applied for environmental purposes. The overall preliminary results are showing that
open-source geodata data sets are an important and reliable tool for environmental
analysis.
After all the analysis, the conclusions regarding the research questions that started this
project are presented below:
• Satellite imagery can be be used for developing a machine learning algorithm
for urban areas, as long as the purpose of the classification is known in advance.
That means that some data sets might have too low resolution where high level
of detail is needed. The 10m resolution of Santinel-2 satellites might be too low
for highly detailed areas, or for a high number of classes. This is reflected also
by the qualitative (pixel) accuracy assessment, which is slightly lower than the
quantitative assessment. By quantitative assessment it is meant that the location
of a certain feature is not important, but only its surface area, in relation to the
total analyzed area.
• A realistic CSO catchment can be estimated by using the available open-source
data, i.e., DEM. However, the results differ significantly, depending on the
topography of the area. In areas that are relatively flat, such as Denmark, the
estimated catchment size is comparable to the officially-provided values, whereas
in more hilly, or mountainous areas, such as Switzerland the estimated catchments
differ significantly to the official ones. This leads to the conclusion that in order
to have a higher confidence in the results, the DEM alone is not sufficient and
more variables, e.g., road layers, sewer pipes system, boundaries need to be added
in the equation.
• Open-source 30m resolution DEM and commercial 0.4m resolution DEM return
similar results when used for simulating a catchment delineation, both at a large
scale, e.g., CSO catchment and at a smaller scale, e.g., river basin, confirming the
fact that the DEM provided by NASA and USGS can -for this purpose- be used
globally, i.e., as long as it is within the coverage limits of the satellite. Choosing
the discharge point, i.e., CSO for which the upslope area will be calculated proved
to be the most sensitive step, the Achilles’s heel in the process of catchment
delineation.
• The amount of pollutants considered to have the main source in road areas, i.e.,
PAH group, does indeed depend on the road surface area. The results show that
an increase of 10% in the road surface area lead to an increase of up to 8.5% in
the pollutant loads coming from a certain CSO catchment. This number will
fluctuate, depending on the ratio of road area to total area of the catchment.
