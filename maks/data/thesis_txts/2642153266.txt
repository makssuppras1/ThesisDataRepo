1 Abstract
The following thesis is numerically solving the 2D ﬂow over a cylinder by the use of
the URANS solver Ellipsys2D, with the turbulence model K-ω-SST proposed by Menter
(1994). The purpose of the project is to map out the ﬂow induced forces acting on a
cylinder at diﬀerent ﬂow regimes. Flows with three diﬀerent Reynolds numbers Re = 150,
Re = 1.7·106 and Re = 3.6·106 is investigated, with initially a ﬁxed cylinder and secondly
a cylinder under forced motion. The ﬂow over the ﬁxed cylinders at all three Reynolds
numbers is resolved accurately and the results obtained agrees with both experimental and
numerical references. For the cylinder under forced motion, the ﬂow is resolved accurately
at the laminar ﬂow of Re = 150, while the ﬂows of Re = 1.7 · 106 and Re = 3.6 · 106
shows discrepancies in comparison to the references, which is believed to be caused by
a combination of the lift coeﬃcients not being fully time or mesh independent, and the
setup for the reference being too diﬀerent.

3 Introduction
Harnessing the power of the wind has been known for centuries and was used by redirecting
the motion of the wind with extraction of work, fx. as sails on ships, or in ancient wind
turbines for food production, where the turbine rotated big stones to grind ﬂour. Modern
wind turbines is harnessing the power by converting it to electricity and providing it to
the consumer through cables. The modern wind turbines is basically a tower with a so
called nacelle mounted on the top. All the technology such as generator etc. is placed
in the nacelle, where the large rotor blades is mounted. The use of modern windturbnies
began in the in the late 19th century with the discovery of electricity and continued to
develop through the 20th century. With the oil crisis in the 1970s, power supply without
the use of fossil fuels became increasingly interesting and commercial power plants began
to develop (Pasqualetti (2004)). In the 1970s the danish wind turbine from Tvind was
build, with a construction very similar to the once known today. The wind turbine had
24m pitch-able blades and was 54m in height, which led to a production of 2MW, which
was considered a very large windturbine at the time. The size of commercial wind turbine
has increased very rapidly as illustrated in ﬁgure 1

(IEA)
The increasing size is due to the high costs of foundation and by the fact that the power
production increase by 4 if the radius of the blades is increased by 2. Since the ground
is slowing down the wind due to shear, higher wind speeds can be obtained by increasing
the height of the tower. The fact that the blades and the tower increased in size led to
an increase in the tower diameter, which for the 10 MW DTU reference wind turbine at
the nacelle is 5.5m Desmond et al. (2016). When assembling a wind turbine it has to

4 Theoretical background and literature survey
Flow over bluﬀbodies makes a high pressure upstream of the body and a low pressure
in the wake of the body. This will cause vortices to appear in the wake. When the ﬂow
velocity and therefore the turbulence intensity increases, instabilities in the ﬂuid closest
to the surface will cause one of the vortices to increase, and the smaller vortex will be shed
downstream, called vortex shedding. Vortex shedding in the wake of a cylinder, results
periodic force excitations and vibrations of the cylinder. This is known as the phenomena
vortex induced vibrations (VIV). The VIV is inﬂuencing a wide range of applications,
such as chimneys, bridges, marine cables and of course wind turbines. Throughout this
thesis the focus of VIV will be in connection to wind turbines.
4.1
Reynolds number
Initially an introduction to the dimensionless Reynolds numbers will be of the essence to
understand the following sections. The Reynolds number is the ratio between the ﬂuids
inertial forces to viscous forces and is deﬁning the turbulence intensity of the ﬂow. The
turbulence of the ﬂow can be deﬁned in diﬀerent stages as ﬂow regimes, and is based
on ranges of the Reynolds numbers which gives information about behavior of the ﬂuid.
Fluid ﬂows can be laminar or has increasing turbulence intensity, through a transition to
a fully turbulent state based on the magnitude of the Reynolds number. Fluid ﬂows has
diﬀerent characteristics based on certain parameters of the ﬂow. The Reynolds number
is deﬁned in equation 4.1
Re = ρ · U∞· D

cylinder and µ is the dynamic viscosity of the ﬂuid. Throughout this thesis the Reynolds
number will be imposed through the dynamic viscosity µ
4.2
Boundary layer
When a ﬂuid ﬂow is reaching a bluﬀbody the velocity of the ﬂow normal to the structure
goes to zero the closer to the surface it reaches. The pressure in this region will increase
due to the zero velocity. The ﬂuid changes direction going around the structure instead
of through it. The ﬂuid travelling tangential to the structure is slowed down due to the
shear of the surface, this is called the boundary layer. The tangential velocity of the
ﬂuid at the surface of the structure is zero, deﬁned as the no slip condition. The velocity
increases throughout the boundary layer until it reaches the free stream velocity when the
ﬂow is independent of the viscous forces. The particles of the ﬂuid at the surface has zero
velocity which aﬀects the next layer of particles and so on through the boundary layer,
which results in the development of a velocity proﬁle within the boundary layer.

Vortex shedding occurs when a ﬂow past a structure separates. When the Reynolds num-
ber (Re) is suﬃciently high, the vortices will start shedding in a certain frequency called
the shedding frequency, which is highly dependent on the Re of the ﬂow. The shedding
frequency of the ﬂow can be predicted through the non dimensionalized frequency the
Strouhal number, calculated as in equation 4.2
St = fs · D

4.5
Natural frequency and resonance
All structures has a frequency which it naturally vibrate at without the action of any
external forces. This frequency is called the natural frequency and is based on the stiﬀness
and the mass of the structure. If a structure is excited with an external periodic force
and the external frequency coincides with the natural frequency, the phenomena resonance
6
==PAGE:18==
4.6
Fluid structure interaction (FSI)
will occur, which can lead to excessive deﬂection and failure. When resonance occurs,
the amplitude of deﬂection is increasing and building up very rapidly until limited by
the damping of the system.
Resonance can occur in a big variety of applications for
example in rotating machines, where the frequency of the rotational speed coincides with
the natural frequency of a rotating shaft. The operational speed of the shaft is normally
designed to be diﬀerent from the natural frequency, however in some cases the natural
frequency is lower than the operational speed and the shaft has to go through a resonance
area when speeding up, which will increase the deﬂections in the shaft. The shaft has
to be able to endure these vibrations. In the same manner the windturbine tower has to
endure the vibrations it is excited with, since diﬀerent windspeeds cause diﬀerent shedding
frequencies. To limit the occurrence of ressonance, the tower has to be design in such a
way that the natural frequency is far away from the frequency of external forces such as
VIV. External forces such as wind or waves can induce periodic excitation of structures.
A classical example of wind induced vibrations which lead to a collapse of the structure,
is the Tacoma Narrows bridge in 1940 Rao (2017).
4.6
Fluid structure interaction (FSI)
Fluid structure interaction (FSI) is the interaction between a structure and a ﬂuid sur-
rounding the structure. The motion of the ﬂuid can cause deﬂection of the structure
which can be either steady, oscillating or a combination of both.
As stated in chapter 4.4 ﬂuid ﬂow over a cylindrical structure will start shedding with
a Re number exceeding 40.
When the shedding occurs, the structure will experience
an oscillatory interaction which will lead to vibrations in the structure. This is due to
the periodicity of the vortex shedding, since each vortex induces a lift and a drag on
the structure. Knowledge concerning the phenomena of FSI is essential, since vibrations
on a structure may weaken, or eventually lead to a fatigue failure. When the shedding
frequency gets close to the frequency of the cylinder motion, the shedding frequency syn-
chronizes with the cylinder motion frequency this is known as synchronization or "lock-in".
Synchronization or "lock-in" of the structural frequency and the shedding frequency causes
resonance, which will lead to an increase in the maximum deﬂection of the structure. All
vibrations is wearing and fatiguing a structure, which increases when the vibrations am-
plify. Eventually this can lead to premature fatigue failure. The complexity of the ﬂow
and FSI, makes VIV a widely studied subject, both numerical and experimental.
4.7
Investigating ﬂuid ﬂows
investigations of ﬂuid ﬂows has normally been studied through experiments in wind tun-
nels, but can also be studied numerically with the use of computational ﬂuid dynamics
(CFD). CFD is based on a set of equations called the Navier Stokes equations. These
equations is governed by the conservation of, mass momentum and energy, but is very
demanding computational wise to resolve. Diﬀerent approximations of the equations has
been made, to reduce the computational expense. A commonly used approximated solver
is the Reynolds Averaged Navier Stokes equations RANS, where the ﬂow is time averaged
and the turbulence is modelled through, so called turbulence models. Through the recent

4.8
Previous studies
The VIVs is, as mentioned, very dependent on the Re number of the ﬂow which makes
it desirable to map out the VIV for a wide span of Reynolds numbers, ranging from sub
critical to super critical ﬂows. Especially the sub critical low Reynolds number ﬂows for
high mass ratios has been investigated, where the boundary layer is fully laminar. Studies
concerning the fundamental of VIV has been carried out by Griﬃn and Ramberg (1982),
Bearman (1984), Parkinson (1989).
Griﬃn et al. (1973) studied the ﬂow over a cylinder at a Reynolds number ranging from
350 to 550 and from 550 to 900 with the purpose of predicting the vibrations in the lock-in
region.
Angelopoulos (2017) studied the lock-in numerically for a 2D cylinder in the subcritical
ﬂow regime, with a Re number of 200. Angelopoulos (2017) forced the motion of the cylin-
der with a 1-way-coupling and varied the frequency of the cylinder motion as a function
of the Strouhal number of the ﬁxed cylinder, which enabled him to determine the area
of lock-in. Furthermore he studied the eﬀect of increasing the amplitude of the cylinder
motion, with respect to the ﬂuid forces and the eﬀect of lock in.
Studies of Reynolds numbers ranging from 900-15.000 using SST k-ω model of Menter
(1994) was carried out for cylinders with low mass ratio by Guilmineau and Queutey
(2004) However, modern wind turbines also experiences ﬂows in both the transitional
ﬂow regime 3 · 105 <= Re <= 3 · 106 where the ﬂow transitions to being fully turbulent
in the boundary layer and in the wake, with a narrower wake and with the occurrence of
drag crisis. Furthermore modern wind turbines also experiences ﬂow in the supercritical
regime 3 · 106 <= Re, where the boundary layer is fully turbulent. Studies of VIV in
ﬂow regimes with Reynolds numbers of this magnitude is quite scarce. Experiments has
been carried out for these particular ﬂow regime, by Achenbach (1968) who studied the
total drag on the cylinder along with the separation point for diﬀerent Reynolds numbers
ranging from sub critical to super critical, with the focus in determining the transition in
between the regimes based on the shift in separation points. Jones et al. (1969) Exper-
imentally studied the drag and lift for both stationary and oscillating cylinders at very
high Reynolds numbers in the supercritical regime. He categorized the lift of the station-
ary cylinder into three regimes dependent on the Reynolds number, wide-band random,
8

narrow-band random and quasi periodic. For the oscillating cylinder Jones et al. (1969)
concluded that the periodicity of the lift destabilised the cylinder for frequencies below
the Strouhal frequency, but in the range above the Strouhal frequency the lift stabilised
the cylinder. Viré et al. (2020) numerically studied the VIV for a 2D circular cylinder
in the super critical regime. The CFD code was a hybrid code with a direct numerical

Experimental data was

SST K-ω model and the Detached eddy simulations (DES). The study concludes that
the improved K-ϵ model RKE-model is inaccurate at the upper branch of the oscillations
whereas the accuracy of Detached eddy simulations (DES) and the improved K-ω turbu-

to the higher computational expense of the DES compared to the SST-K-ω the SST K-ω
model stand out as the preferred model. Based on the conclusion of Khan and Ibrahim
(2019) the K −ω −SST turbulence model will be used in the present study.

Shedding modes
The vortex shedding acts in repeatable patterns based on the Re and the motion of
the cylinder. For ﬂows over a stationary cylinder the characteristic pattern called "Von
Karman vortex street" occurs. The cycles consists of two vortices with rotational direction
opposite to each other.
When including cylinder motion to the problem, a cycle is determined from the frequency
of the cylinder. Based on the Reynolds number, the frequency of the cylinder motion and
the amplitude of the cylinder displacement, diﬀerent patterns can occur as illustrated in

of vortices shed per cycle, which occurs for higher displacement amplitudes and higher
cylinder motion frequencies. Patterns "2P+S" is an asymmetric vortex pattern with a
single vortex shed in between each pair of vortices at each cycle, which is expected to
occur in the region of lock in for cylinder motions with high amplitudes. The "P" is single
pair of vortices, which is expected for low frequencies with high displacement. The Rest
of the patterns can be observed in ﬁgure 6, but isn’t expected to be observed within this

6 Mesh
The computational domain of the ﬂow over the cylinder is an O-shaped grid evolved from
the center of the cylinder. The grid will be oﬀthree diﬀerent reﬁnements going from
coarse to intermediate to ﬁne, to reduce the computational expense. The coarse grid
ensures the ﬂow is fully developed at a low cost, the intermediate mesh is a coupling to
the ﬁne mesh which ensures high computational accuracy of the study. The intermediate
mesh consist of 1/4 of the cells in the ﬁne mesh and the coarse mesh is 1/4 of the cells in
the intermediate mesh.
The domain is 110D (cylinder diameter) in diameter The ﬁrst cell height is based on
the Y + value. The cell size increase with the distance to the surface, which ensures high
accuracy in the area with the biggest velocity gradients. The cell dimension at the surface
of the cylinder is calculated for each Reynolds number, to ensure a Y + < 1 . The length of
the cells is based on the number of cells along the cylinder surface. Since vortex shedding
is expected to occur, large gradients in the wake of the cylinder must be present. The
mesh is therefore contracted in the wake, in such a way that a higher intensity of cells
is located downstream of the cylinder compared to upstream. To control the intensity
of the cells near the surface, a normal distribution of the cells has ensured that 90% is
located within the ﬁrst 10% of the mesh. The mesh is illustrated in ﬁgure 8 and 9, where

8 Cylinder under forced vibrations
In the following section, the ﬂow over a cylinder under forced vibration is investigated, to
study the forces acting on the cylinder in the area of lock-in. The Cylinder is excited with
a transverse periodic motion, with the non-dimensionalized forced frequency Stf. The
lock in frequency ratio Stf/St is the ratio between the non-dimensionalized frequency
and the Strouhal number. The simulations has been carried out for diﬀerent frequency
ratios ranging from 0.6 to 1.6, with the aim of determining the eﬀects of lock-in. The
simulations will be carried out in the three diﬀerent ﬂow regime, with the setup for the
converged solutions for the ﬁxed cylinder.

signal is present as would be expected. This underlines that the lock-in range is dependent
of both the motion amplitude and the motion frequency. As described by Jones et al.
(1969), decreased cylinder displacements results in a narrower lock-in bandwidth, while
increased cylinder motion is causing a wider bandwidth and the lock in shift towards the
lower frequencies. For this case it seems that the bandwidth is simply just too narrow to
lock any of the other frequencies in.

9 Conclusion
The research throughout this thesis aims to gain knowledge concerning the periodic forces
on a 2D cylinder, induced by the wind ﬂow. This has been carried out using the numer-
ical solver Elipsys 2D based on URANS equations with the K-ω-SST turbulence model.
Initially the cylinder was regarded as ﬁxed with the purpose of determining the periodic
forces at three diﬀerent ﬂow regimes.
For the subcritical ﬂow a converged solution was obtained through a time- and mesh
convergence study. The results was veriﬁed through comparison to several studies, both
numerical and experimental. The set up of the simulation allowed accurate prediction of
the shedding frequency, the point of separation, the drag coeﬃcient and the lift coeﬃcient
with very small discrepancies. The frequency of the drag coeﬃcient could be concluded
to have twice the frequency of the lift coeﬃcient. Furthermore it could be concluded that
the ﬂow indeed was laminar, since the solver allowed an equally accurate prediction of the
ﬂow without any turbulence models activated. The vortex shedding pattern in the wake
of the cylinder was accurately resolved as visualized through vorticity plots and velocity
ﬁelds and agreed that at Von Carman Street had occurred.
The ﬂow in the transitional regime of Re = 1.7 · 106 over a ﬁxed cylinder was initially
not resolved accurately in terms of convergence of the forces. This led to investigations
of very big mesh and very low timesteps, with huge computational expense without more
accurate results. Further investigation proved the importance of suﬃciently low conver-
gence criterias, along with a suﬃcient amount of inner iterations, for each timestep to
obtain accurate results. The convergence studies was based on the time averaged drag
coeﬃcient, while discrepancies of 12% was accepted for the lift coeﬃcient. In comparison
to experimental studies the time averaged drag coeﬃcient showed small discrepancies of
around 2.4% and in comparison to numerical references it showed a discrepancy of 6.3%.
The magnitude of the drag coeﬃcient in comparison to the subcritical case, shows evi-
dence of the drag crisis has occurred due to a more narrow wake. The shedding frequency
of the present simulation showed large discrepancies to the experimental data. While the
numerical reference predicted the same shedding frequency with small discrepancies. The
reason behind was thought to be caused be a perfectly smooth cylinder in the numeri-
cal setup, while the experimental studies had a higher roughness which agrees with the
theory of the Strouhal number. The pressure distribution along the cylinder surface was
predicted accurately, with some small discrepancies in the wake of the cylinder.
For the supercritical ﬂow over a ﬁxed cylinder, the drag coeﬃcient, the shedding frequency
and the separation point is spot on the numerical reference, while small discrepancies rel-
ative to the experimental data can be observed. The convergence study was once again
based on the drag coeﬃcient, and a discrepancy of 10% was accepted for the lift coeﬃ-
cient. The pressure distribution is resolved accurately in comparison to other numerical
studies, while small discrepancies can be observed in the wake of the cylinder. It can be
concluded that the present model is predicting the ﬂow with a satisfying accuracy.

For the cylinder under forced motion in the subcritical regime, lock-in has been observed
for a cylinder displacement amplitude of ymax = 0.25D, with the lock-in bandwidth rang-
ing from Stf/St = 0.80 to Stf/St = 1.05, as veriﬁed through numerical references. The
relative Clrms experiencing a large decrease in the area of lock-in, and increases with
increasing frequency ratios. The system is unstable through all frequency ratios ranging
from Stf/St = 0.74 to Stf/St = 1.4, with the highest instability at Stf/St = 1.05. The
ﬂow in the wake of the cylinder still forms a 2S pattern.
The ﬂow at Re = 1.7e6 over the moving cylinder is experiencing lock-in with a band-
width from Stf/St = 0.74 to Stf/St = 1.0. In comparison to the subcritical ﬂow, the
bandwidth has extended in the sub unity frequency ratio, while it is insecure whether
the bandwidth above unity has reduced or its simply due to the sample resolution. The
Clrms is experiencing increased lift due to cylinder motion in the lock in region, with
a tendency very similar to the reference of Viré et al. (2020) at much higher Reynolds
numbers. The tendency of the damping through the frequency ratios act as the reference
for higher Reynolds numbers. The setups in between the present study and the reference
is too diﬀerent to conclude whether the magnitude of the obtained results is accurate.
However it can be concluded that the behavior of the lock-in bandwidth, the lift coeﬃ-
cient and the damping is similar to the reference in the supercritical regime.
The ﬂow in the super critical regime at Re = 3.6e6 with the displacement amplitude of
ymax = 0.25D is experiencing a lock-in bandwidth from Stf/St = 0.74 to Stf/St = 1.0.
The lift is increasing in the lock-in, due to the cylinder motion. The system is stable
within the lock-in and unstable out of the lock-in. The magnitude of the lift shows large
discrepancies in the lift and damping, in comparison to the numerical study of Viré et al.
(2020), which is believed to be caused by not fully converged lift coeﬃcients in the con-
vergence study.
For the lower cylinder displacement amplitude of ymax = 0.025D, the lock-in bandwidth
has decreased to only include the frequency ratio at unity. The relative lift coeﬃcient is
resolved accurately below the frequency ratio of Stf/St = 0.9, but shows large discrep-
ancies at higher frequency ratios, which is believed to be caused by a combination of not
fully time and mesh independent lift coeﬃcients, along with growing diﬀerences in the
setups in between the present simulations and the references.
