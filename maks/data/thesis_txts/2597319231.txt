Abstract
Sand nourishment is a typically applied measure for coastal protection, especially in areas
such as Denmark & the Netherlands. Presently no clear guidelines exists regarding the
placement of nourishment, and with rising sea levels, knowledge regarding the long-term
behavior of shoreface nourishment becomes important to ﬁlls current gaps in research.
The original aim of this thesis is to study the inﬂuence of nourishment positioning in
relation to a quasi steady equilibrium proﬁle in an experimental setting. To achieve this,
an initially plane sloping beach is exposed to a set of irregular waves in a small scale wave
ﬂume (Hs = 0.15 cm & Tp = 1.6 s). These waves will develop the proﬁle until a quasi-
steady equilibrium proﬁle is reached after which the proﬁle will be nourished. Tested
nourishment designs involve: shoreward nourishment in trough of outer bar and seaward
nourishment adjacent to outer bar. The morphological development is directly compared
to a unnourished proﬁle. In addition, all proﬁles show erosive behavior as calculated sed-
iment transport rates show that all proﬁles loses sediment to the oﬀshore. Hydrodynamic
measurements reveal that the nourishment signiﬁcantly alters the nearshore wave hydro-
dynamics in terms of wave height, wave asymmetry and wave skewness. Furthermore, it is
seen that seaward nourishment signiﬁcantly reduces the shoreline erosion and eﬀectively
stops the shoreline from further retreating.
The second aim concerns developing an eﬃcient numerical model for simulating coastal
proﬁle evolution. The developed model is used to simulate erosion of plane sloping beaches
at two diﬀerent physical scales. Initially, the model proves to be able to simulate cross-
shore evolution at larger time scales at the expense of little computational eﬀort. The
hydrodynamic model is validated against detailed measurements of wave height variation
and undertow velocity proﬁles. Wave heights show excellent agreement, while the model
generally performs worse for the undertow velocity proﬁles, as it underestimates near-
bed velocity in the inner surf zone, while overestimating in outer surf. To some degree
performance is comparable with a non-stabilized k −ω turbulence closure model. The
morphological performance is tested for a set of erosive and accretive wave conditions. In
line with previous studies, the model performs reasonable well in the erosive test, with
Brier Skill Scores of fair & good. The model ability to predict morphology under accretive
conditions is less good, scoring a Brier Skill Score of fair & poor. Additionally a sensitivity
study testing a range of model parameters is conducted as the ﬁnal part of the study.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Due to the increasing development of coastal areas by humans, awareness of coastal pro-
tection has naturally become important. With rapidly growing population this area has
received increased amount of attention. This both in reference to protecting and poten-
tially saving human lives, but also to maintain balance from the sea in terms of progressing
coastal erosion which naturally pose a conﬂict with dense and more developed coastal ar-
eas. Correctly dealing with such conﬂicts is more important than ever, as the number
and severity of such conﬂicts will only increase due to climate change. Sea level rise caus-
ing increased shoreline erosion and hence a landward movement of the already retreating
shorelines. Increased storminess eﬀectively causing higher storm surges, higher and more
violent wave climates as well as a change in wave directions, are all important expected
changes in the coastal area due to climate changes (Mangor et al., 2017).
Coastal protection is not a new concept, with the ﬁrst well established dike-scheme in
Demark dating back to approximately year 1550 (Sørensen et al., 1996). The Danish
Coastal Authorities classiﬁes coastal protection measures as either soft (active) or hard
(passive) protection measures.
Nourishment can typically be viewed as a soft coastal
protection measure, as it does not involve structures, or have no permanent impact in
the environment. Sand nourishment is often used as a measure for coastal protection
as it has fewer negative side eﬀects compared to hard structures (Dean, 2003). Hard
structures like groins or detached breakwaters may inadvertently trap sand, causing a
sediment deﬁcit resulting in beach erosion downstream, referred to as lee-side erosion.
Nourishment may also be used in combination with structures.
Nourishment can be
considered as a natural way of mitigating coastal and shoreline erosion as it artiﬁcially
1

alters the sediment budget over a certain stretch with an added volume of sand. However,
since the cause of the initially caused erosion is not eliminated, erosion will continue in the
nourished beach. Hence by applying nourishment as a protection measure, it is understood
that the nourished sand is gradually being sacriﬁced. As such, nourishment requires a
continuous eﬀort in order to be considered an eﬃcient protection scheme. Nourishment
can be deployed in the form of backshore nourishment, beach nourishment or shoreface
nourishment also referred to as proﬁle nourishment by Dean (2003). The present study will
deal exclusively with a variety of types of shoreface nourishment. When positioned at the
shoreface, the nourishment indirectly protects the beach by acting as a feeder mechanism,
but also forcing waves to break earlier and increase energy dissipation, thereby reducing
the amount of wave energy that hits the beach, reducing shoreline erosion. The latter
is also referred to as the lee-side eﬀect.
Focused studies have then been carried out
contributing to knowledge regarding beach and shoreface nourishment, with the aim of
developing basic criteria for design of future nourishment schemes. A major role which
is still ongoing is to elucidate on criteria, in particular the necessity of repetition which
is naturally extremely important in areas with chronic/acute erosion. Due to the very
natural way of applying nourishment, the nourishment can often not be controlled or
restricted to a certain area. As such knowledge regarding the long-term behavior of the
nourished sediment is still to a large extent missing. In nourishment research there have
often been a tendency to completely separate the longshore and cross-shore eﬀects. This
is, as Dean (2003) writes, done both because the cross-shore developments are often seen
to occur on much shorter time scales than the longshore developments, and perhaps more
importantly for practical reasons.
Models that work well for longshore transport are
still not very accurate for cross-shore predictions, and even speciﬁc cross-shore models
struggle with accurately predicting cross-shore processes without tuning (Ruessink et al.,
2007). Even with state-of-the-art cross-shore models painting an accurate picture of the
physical processes involved in cross-shore transport is inherently diﬃcult, and speaks to
the uncertainty in drawing clear conclusions in a notorious complex environment. The
present study will follow in these footsteps, keeping this artiﬁcial divide, continue working
only in the cross-shore direction. Presently, no clear nourishment strategies exist. Only
rough guidelines surrounding the use of nourishment seem to be in place, some which states
that the nourished sand volume per meter coastline should be similar to the volume of
the main breaker bar (Walstra et al., 2010). Currently the Danish Coastal Authorities
nourishes the Danish North Sea coast with around 3.5 million m3/year, which translates

that in order to achieve sustainable protection (and more beautiful coasts!) soft protection
measures are to be preferred to hard solutions in the future.

1.2
Literature Study
Nourishment studies is a ﬁeld of ongoing research which is currently being approached
from a number of fronts; experimental studies, ﬁeld measurements campaigns and by
applying state-of-the-art numerical models. There have been many ﬁeld campaigns in-
vestigating the eﬀect of shoreface nourishment (e.g. Huisman et al., 2019; Grunnet and
Ruessink, 2005; van Duin et al., 2004). These have provided valuable insights, but are
still very case speciﬁc and furthermore, since these were ﬁeld measurements, the diﬀerence
between cross-shore eﬀects and longshore eﬀects could not be separated. van Duin et al.
(2004) investigated shoreface nourishment placed oﬀshore of the outer bar in combination
with beach nourishment behind the bar, at Egmond aan Zee, Netherlands which is consid-
ered an "erosion hotspot" and is supposedly very susceptible to rip currents. They found
that the shoreface nourishment caused the bar to grow and signiﬁcant onshore migration
was observed, and only after 2 years the system started to revert to its natural dynamic
equilibrium stage and lost all inﬂuence of the nourishment. However, investigating purely
in terms cross-shore eﬀects could naturally not be done. This was likewise concluded
by Huisman et al. (2019) who exclusively studied shoreface nourishment behavior at 19
diﬀerent locations along the Dutch coast. However, they also used a numerical model to
investigate initial sedimentation erosion rates, which predicted that cross-shore transport
makes up the largest contributor to ﬁrst year erosion rates of the nourishment. In order to
strictly separate the cross-shore and longshore eﬀects require either numerical modelling
or experimental studies, and even doing so might not be ﬂawless as experimental studies
have troubles of their own e.g. scaling issues. The amount of detailed papers regarding
nourishment studies is still very limited.
In an eﬀort to gain insight into predicting morphodynamic evolution of nourishment as
well as investigating primary transport mechanisms Luijendijk et al. (2017) did a mod-
elling study surrounding the unprecedented pilot project: The Sand Engine, Netherlands.
Using a process-based model, they achieved reasonable results when hindcasting the ini-
4
==PAGE:26==

tial morphological longshore response. They found amongst other that duration of storms
were more important than the respective maximum wave height. Grunnet et al. (2012) did
a case study involving long term morphological modelling of beach and shoreface nourish-
ment in Dunkirk, France. They used a mixture of detailed two-dimensional morphological
models and simple one-line models for the coastline evolution. 1-D models are especially
convenient for modelling of longer time scales in the order of O(days-weeks-years). A pre-
requisite to accurately model (long-term) nourishment behavior is the ability to accurately
capture the processes governing the development of the coastal proﬁle. Numerous authors
have since the 1980s developed deterministic models of cross-shore sediment transport,
however they all face troubles in later stages of simulation. (see Fredsøe and Deigaard,
1992, and references herein). A phase-resolved model based on Boussinesq equations for
surf zone conditions was used by Rakha et al. (1997) combined with a single-equation tur-
bulence model. They discussed, amongst other, that a single representative wave height
cannot be used when calculating sediment transport rates under irregular wave forcing.
Grunnet et al. (2004) used a state-of-the-art process-based model to hindcast morpho-
logical development. They found velocity skewness at nourishment resulted in onshore
transport, but morphodynamic predictability regarding nearshore bar migration and de-
velopment was found to be poor. Even so, the model seemingly performed reasonably
well in the longshore direction, reproducing lee and feeder eﬀects which have also been
observed in nature (e.g. van Duin et al., 2004). Ruessink et al. (2007) modelled cross-
shore bar behavior on time-scale of weeks, where they managed to capture and reproduce
important contributors to the cross-shore proﬁle development. This led them to predict
reasonable bar migration consistent with observations. However, heavy calibration was
needed and involved in particular the undertow and sediment transport model. More
recently Jacobsen and Fredsoe (2014a) utilized a state-of-the-art CFD model with a VOF
description of air/water interface for the hydrodynamics, to simulate in detail the short-
term behavior of the cross-shore redistribution of nourished sediment on a barred proﬁle.
They found that positioning nourished sand in the trough let to more loss of sand to the
oﬀshore than when the nourishment was positioned at the bar. However, for understand-
ing the long-term behavior of nourishment such models become practically worthless due
to the computational power required.
Walstra et al. (2010) (who’s setup arguably resemble mostly the one used in this study)
used the small scale Scheldt ﬂume at Deltares, Netherlands to study the eﬀects of various

shoreface nourishments designs exposed to irregular waves in what they deﬁned as erosive
and accretive wave conditions. They concluded that positioning sand in the trough of
the bar reduced beach erosion more than the case where the nourishment was positioned
seaward of the bar. In accordance with the, by Dean (2003), anticipated eﬀects, they also
found that the shoreface nourishment clearly altered the nearshore wave climate result-
ing in a reduction in the oﬀshore directed suspended sediment transport. Grasso et al.
(2011) likewise studied shoreface nourishment as well as beach nourishment in a small
scale setting, this time using light weight sediments (d50 = 602 −756µm; s = 1.19gcm−3).
In their experiments, they simulated nourished beach proﬁle response to a storm with

waves. They were able to get good similarity in Shields and Rouse numbers compared
to those in nature using the lightweight sediment. Grasso et al. (2011) concluded that
nourishment positioned at the beach face was more eﬃcient than nourishment positioned
at both bar crest and bar trough (shoreface), but that no clear distinction could be made
between the cases where the nourishment was placed on the trough and the crest, as both
contributed to slowing down shoreline retreat. Di Risio et al. (2010) considered both pro-
tected (by a submerged breakwater) and un-protected beach nourishment and concluded
that the combination of a hard structure with beach nourishment was most eﬃcient in
protecting the beach, as the submerged breakwater would change erosive conditions to
accretive. The limited amount of experimental studies makes it diﬃcult to formulate
clear nourishment guidelines. Considering shoreface nourishments there is, for instance,
diﬀerent opinions on where in the cross-shore proﬁle the nourished sand best should be
positioned. The few experimental studies make it diﬃcult to formulate clear nourishment
strategies. Considering shoreface nourishments there is, for instance, diﬀerent opinions
on where in the cross-shore proﬁle the nourished sand best should be positioned. Van
Rijn et al. (2011) stated that the nourishment is most eﬃcient when placed landwards of
the innermost bar crest and that troughs should always be ﬁlled.
In Dean (2003) the beaches are thought to revert to an equilibrium proﬁle determined by
the grain size alone and do so in a relative short time frame. Therefore any sand placed
on the shoreface, will be beneﬁcial, as it will shift the entire proﬁle, but in the long run
there will (in pure cross-shore terms) not be any diﬀerence in the proﬁles depending on
where the sand is placed. Dean (2003) equilibrium proﬁle ideas have some bearing, but
are also simplistic as natural beaches do have some cross-shore variation deviating from

the equilibrium shape (e.g. a breaker bar). Still, keeping the spirit of Dean (2003) ﬁndings
the cross-shore evolution of a shore face nourishment or even beach nourishment (if the
grain size is kept identical to the natural sand) could be predicted using the translational
model by Atkinson et al. (2018). In this study, they consider a proﬁle to be in equilibrium
with a certain wave climate, and their model can then be used to translate the entire
proﬁle due to a sea-level rise (or in this case a nourishment).
In reality beaches are retreating in many places, indicating that they are not in equilibrium
yet and furthermore the bars on a beach are not necessarily stationary. There is often
distinct diﬀerence between summer and winter proﬁles, and on a long term, at least in the
Netherlands, the bars migrate oﬀshore and eventually disappear, where after the inner
bar starts migrating oﬀshore and turns into the outer bar. Furthermore, if one believes
the idea of a "quasi" equilibrium (allowing for cyclic outer bar behavior as well as some
natural background erosion) ﬁeld measurements indicate that this quasi equilibrium proﬁle
shape is not reached right away, which indicate that nourishment strategies might have
an impact e.g. as in the study by van Duin et al. (2004).
1.3
Scope of present work
This study aims to investigate the inﬂuence of nourishment positioning in relation to a
steady or "quasi steady" equilibrium. In studying this, the thesis will also contribute to
the discussion of equilibrium and quasi steady equilibrium beach proﬁles. Can these be
disturbed by a nourishment?.
As a way to investigate the above research question, the study starts with an initial ﬁxed
slope, and subject this to a speciﬁc wave condition. This wave condition is designed to
produce a barred beach. From the initial ﬁxed slope a proﬁle is developed which can
be considered to be in "quasi equilibrium". At some point doing this development the
experiments are stopped, and the proﬁle at this point in time function as the ﬁrst initial

1.4
The mechanics of cross-shore sediment transport
Naturally, the cross-shore sediment transport is important, as it governs the erosion/de-
position patterns, which directly inﬂuences the coastal morphology and ultimately the
shape of the coastal proﬁle. In cross-shore sediment transport studies, the situation will
be considered to be completely two dimensional with zero mean cross-shore ﬂow, hence
zero cross-shore ﬂux. As this will represent what is encountered experimentally in the
wave ﬂume (Fredsøe and Deigaard, 1992).
Cross-shore sediment transport mechanisms can be split into two parts: outside and inside
the surf zone. In non-breaking waves (outside the surf zone), turbulence is restricted to
the boundary layer where the (bed) shear stress become important, and also acts as a
key parameter along with turbulence for bringing sediment into suspension (Fredsøe and
Deigaard, 1992). Fuhrman et al. (2009) used a RANS-model to systematically investigate
relative contribution to sediment transport for various processes (on a ﬂat bed). They
found that wave asymmetry and conventional streaming (boundary layer drift) was the
biggest contributor to onshore transport. This occurs due to larger bed shear stress in the
wave propagating direction (onshore motion), due to the non-linear relationship between

most important breaker types are: spilling breakers, plunging breakers and surging break-

Spilling breakers are characterized by the forward part of the wave top becoming unstable.
A mixture of water and air entrainment slowly grows while travelling with the wave as a
surface roller.
For plunging breaker, the crest of the wave moves forward and collapses down in the
trough in front of it, as a bore-like structured mass of water, generating a plume of turbu-
lence. This mass of water will generate a splash-up which continues the breaking process.
In the present study it applies that ξ0 = 0.2 initially, which indicates mostly spilling
breakers (Fredsøe and Deigaard, 1992). However, as the experiments progressed and the
bed proﬁle became more developed (change in slope), a shift was seen towards ξ0 ≈0.55
which indicates the waves begun to break more as plungers. This was also conﬁrmed
through visual observation.
Ting and Kirby (1994) experimentally studied undertow and turbulence under spilling
breakers and plunging breakers, in the surf zone in a wave ﬂume. They discussed cross-
shore sediment transport mechanisms qualitatively, based on the correlation between ﬂow
and turbulence levels. They discussed, that for plunging breakers, sediment in suspension,
would be directed shoreward as a result of the high turbulence which travels with the wave
top towards the shore. Conversely, for spilling breakers, turbulence is gradually spread
downwards by eddies originating from the surface roller after which it is transported
downwards. As such, the turbulence level is almost constant during one wave cycle, but
lower than for the plunging breaker. Subsequently, sediment in suspension would go in
the same direction as the undertow, causing predominantly oﬀshore transport.

2.4
Methodology
The initial bed slope of S = 1/25 acting as the initial condition, is subjected to the target
waves for a total period of 125 hours. The total period of wave exposure is not determined
beforehand, but is settled on based on the intermediate bed scans, as it is important to
have the proﬁle reach an equilibrium state before stopping the experiment. During this
time, the bed was scanned every 5 hour of wave exposure. To shorten the experiment
length of the following nourishment cases, one of these instants, which were set to be the
proﬁle after 75 hours, were chosen as the initial condition to be nourished. As such, the
scanned bed was smoothed and the bed was manually reshaped, or reshuﬄed, to what the
initial proﬁle looked like after 60 hours of wave exposure. This proﬁle was then subjected
to 15 hours of waves before the nourishment was applied to allow for formation of bed
forms as well as compacting of the reshuﬄed sand. The 15 hours run between the reshuf-
ﬂing and the addition of the nourished sand also allows for veriﬁcation of the approach, as
well as allowing for bed forms to be generated. As mentioned, the reshaping of the proﬁle
was done manually by scanning the bed every once in while after some sand had been
removed. This continued after a proﬁle with reasonable accuracy was achieved. There
was no pre-determined level of error tolerance, so the reshuﬄing just continued until a
reasonable ﬁt could be achieved by comparison. This is discussed at later stages.
The nourishment are simulated in the form manually added localized sediment deposits
located: (Case 5) shoreward nourishment in trough of outer bar, (Case 7) seaward nour-
ishment adjacent to outer bar.

2.5
Model scaling
As with any physical modeling, it involves laboratory and scale eﬀects. Scale eﬀects are
due to the reduction of magnitudes present at prototype scale to smaller scale, which
inherently exists, when scaling between small scale ﬂumes and prototype (≈ﬁeld con-
ditions).
In order to compare model results with prototype, it is important that all
dominant physical processes are of the same relative magnitude. For complete represen-
tation of the physical processes, similarity must be achieved in all important dimensionless
parameters. According to Kamphuis and Nairn (1985) the hydrodynamics and physics of
morphological studies depend on 8 independent parameters ranging from wave parame-
ters, sediment parameters, ﬂuid properties and ultimately gravity g. 8 parameters implies
that 5 non-dimensional parameters are required in order to achieve complete down scaling.
Atkinson et al. (2018) discussed, that experiments conducted in similar sized ﬂumes as
used in the present study, showed quantitatively same behavior in sediment transport rates
compared to larger scale ﬂumes. More importantly, they also showed same characteristic
beach behavior e.g. development of berms, breaker bars and troughs. These features
26
==PAGE:48==
CHAPTER 2. EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
are all observed in the present study, suggesting natural morphodynamics is captured to
a reasonable degree. This argument, can be applied to the current experimental study,
when qualitatively investigating equilibrium proﬁles in regards to sand nourishment.
Van Rijn et al. (2011) did a comprehensive study on scaling laws for beach and dune
erosion processes, in which they investigated beach proﬁle response in three diﬀerent sized-
ﬂumes, all with similar Froude number. They found good agreement in shoreline retreat
across all ﬂumes, however the use of same sediment led to diﬀerences in local beach slopes.
The aim of the study is to ensure morphology develops similarly to ﬁeld observations.
Froude scaling when modelling free surface ﬂows (waves) can be considered the most
important. As such, Froude similarity is favored, with a scale factor of 30 (λL = 30). This
leads to assuming gravitational eﬀects are the most important (dominant). Similarity in
Froude number, prevents simultaneously similarity in Reynolds number and hence this is
not achieved. Achieving similarity in the Shields parameter imposes the biggest scaling
issue of the present study. The Shields parameter govern the type of sediment transport
(bed load, sheet ﬂow or suspended load) and formation of bed forms (ripples or dunes).
The practical limitations associated with lack of down-scaling of grain size, is the very
large stabilising forces on the sediment particles. This explains the formation of vortex
ripples (due to low Shields parameters), which would otherwise not exist in nature.

3.2.1
Hamming ﬁlter
Due to the aforementioned noise contained in the signal, a data ﬁltering is applied in
order to achieve a viable signal for further processing. In the present study, a low-pass

presented in Smith et al. (1997). A Hamming ﬁlter is a bell-shaped ﬁlter characteristic
of a windowed-sinc ﬁlter. The underlying principle, is that frequencies below the cut-
oﬀfrequency, fc is passed with amplitude of unity, wheres frequencies exceeding fc is
completely blocked. In the present study a cut-oﬀfrequency of fc = 10 Hz was used.

Changes in the wave non-linearity supports this (Figure 4.6b and c). The velocity skew-

from WG 1 - 5. The acceleration skewness Ska, can be used to identify sawtooth-like
waves (asymmetric in shape). It reaches a minimum of Ska ≈−0.6 at the point of incipi-
ent breaking, suggesting a forward leaning wave shape. After breaking, it rapidly increases
to more or less the initial value.The resulting cross-shore proﬁle is the balance of various
oﬀshore and onshore sediment transport mechanisms, each with diﬀerent degrees of im-
portance with respect to the total ﬂux. As brieﬂy addressed in §3.2.5, wave non-linearities,
expressed by i.e. Skv and/or Ska, is one of the main components contributing to onshore
sediment ﬂuxes. Wave non-linearity becomes important when the wave shoals as which
point higher order harmonics become important and depending on their strength and
phase relative to the main harmonic will lead to either skewed or asymmetric (sawtooth-
like) waves. Wheres skewness is a direct consequence of non-linearity alone (addition
of higher order harmonics as in higher order Stokes theory), the sloping bed will result
in a phase shift creating additional asymmetry. As such, the changes in the sediment
transport can be discussed in relation to changes Skv and/or Ska. The possible increase
in sediment transport caused by Ska, can be attributed to the asymmetric free-stream
velocity, which will result in a smaller boundary layer in the positive half-cycle (onshore
motion), compared to the negative half-cycle (oﬀshore motion) where the boundary layer
will have plenty of time to grow in thickness. The smaller boundary layer under the crest
will result in a larger bed shear stress caused by the larger velocity gradients (recalling
τb ∼∂u/∂z) essentially shortening the distance for which the ﬂow has to accelerate from
zero (due to no-slip at sea bed) to free-stream. As the larger boundary layer during the
oﬀshore motion, essentially has the opposite eﬀect, the period-averaged bed shear stress
will be directed in the wave propagation direction, eﬀectively causing onshore transport.
A skewed wave, having high amount of Skv, will also result in a period-averaged bed
shear stress in the onshore direction. The bed shear stress under the raised & narrow
crest will be higher than the wide & ﬂat troughs, which due to the non-linear relationship

Chapter 5
Discussion
It is clear from the conducted experiments, that changes occur in the morphodynamic
processes related to nourishment, which governs the changes in the coastal proﬁle, is far
from obvious. Even when conducted in a seemingly controlled environment such as wave
ﬂume. Working in a an experimental ﬂume, can also have unwanted (and un-quantiﬁed)
eﬀects, which could inﬂuence the morphodynamical response (i.e. standing wave due to
wave reﬂection).
5.1
Quasi-equilibrium
The hypothesis of a cross-shore quasi steady equilibrium proﬁle is investigated in a ﬂume
setting. Present experiments show that as long as one is patient enough to run lengthy
experiments for given wave conditions, (quasi) equilibrium proﬁles can be obtained. Ja-
cobsen and Fredsoe (2014a) used the deﬁnition of quasi-equilibrium as the point in time,
at which the breaker bar keeps a more less constant water depth at the crest of the bar. In
C1 the migration deﬁnitely slows down towards the end of the experiments while the bar
at some point stops growing in height thus indicating a quasi-equilibrium stage. However,
due to the shorter run time it seems it does not reach it exactly, but is easy to imagine
had it run for additional 20 hours as in the other cases (see Figure 4.5a-b). The breaker
bar seizes to grow in height, when the hydrodynamics results in a local maximum in
sediment transport over the crest of the bar. Bar parameters for the nourishment cases
(see Figure 4.11), shows the same trend; bar settles in at a more or less ﬁxed location at
which point it stops growing in height. If the breaker bar is considered as proxy for the
65
==PAGE:87==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
equilibrium state, the drastic changes in the beginning aligns well with equilibrium theory,
as the beach right after nourishment is at a bigger disequilibrium. The small observed
diﬀerences are most likely attributed to the additional volume caused by nourishment.
When nourishing in the trough, the bar eventually redevelops similar to the bar in the
reference case. Bigger diﬀerences are observed for the seaward nourishment. The concept
of equilibrium proﬁles may also be viewed in terms of the entire proﬁle. The proﬁles have
for 70 hours of waves (t = 145 hr) after nourishment reached a quasi-static equilibrium.
Measured proﬁles for the two tested nourishment designs, shows signs of evolving towards
the same proﬁle. Given the additional volume which stems from the nourishment, the
proﬁle will diﬀer in some degree from C1; however, it seems the overall characteristics
of the proﬁle remains the same. In C7, some sediment is being transported seaward (as
a result of deepen trough) and deposited on top of the already placed nourishment, ef-
fectively extending the proﬁle. This also occurs in C5 but at a lot slower pace, and the
establishment of deposited sediment seaward of the bar crest is naturally not as devel-
oped. Much of this aligns with the cases investigated by Grasso et al. (2011), who added
consecutively added nourishment (in various types) after each simulated storm. Their
proﬁles were, in loose terms, also seen to revert back to the same; however, they did not
comment on it (see their Fig. 5). (It should be noted that obscure view of the breaker
bar when comparing Figure 4.11 and various subﬁgures in Figure 4.10, is related to the
bar deﬁnition adopted). In the region 16 < x < xs the end stage proﬁles qualitatively
match those predicted by eq. (2.1); In the sense that they are concave upwards. The
similarity in end stage proﬁles may be related to the waves. Eichentopf et al. (2019),
studied three diﬀerent initial beach morphologies exposed to the same set of bichromatic
waves (in a large-scale wave ﬂume). They found that all three proﬁles evolved towards
the same quasi static equilibrium, which is ultimately governed by the incident waves.
This obviously implies diﬀerences in the cross-shore sediment transport (due diﬀerences
in corresponding hydrodynamics). This is clearly also observed in the present study for
time-averaged quantities (see Figure 3.4).
5.2
Shoreline response
The expected function of nourishments, can typically be based on a lee eﬀect and/or
feeder mechanism. Typically, of the lee eﬀect is that the breaker bar acts as a ﬁlter,
promoting wave energy dissipation (from the breaking waves) and hence rapid decrease
66
==PAGE:88==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
in wave heights near the bar. The term lee eﬀect, refers to bar’s sheltering eﬀect for the
beach. The idea of the feeder mechanism, is that the artiﬁcially added sediment, reduces
the erosion most likely to occur in the nearshore area by transport of the nourished sand
through various processes (van Duin et al., 2004). The reduced shoreline erosion (around
10 cm ≈3 m in full-scale) in C7 (see Figure 4.14), as well as reduced oﬀshore directed
sediment transport (less stirring of sediment), can typically be linked to the lee eﬀect.
In the area shoreward of the nourishment (lee-side), oﬀshore sediment transport is de-
creased signiﬁcantly, actually tending to time averaged net transport shoreward (=overall
sediment increase). This is maybe explained by the onshore transport by more moderate
waves (non-breaking), with increasing wave non-linearities. This was also established by
Grunnet et al. (2004)(in model, in order to separate cross-shore and longshore eﬀects),
and can be considered as another characteristic feature of the lee eﬀect. The increase in
strength of the undertow which presumably follows from more pronounced wave breaking
at the outer bar seems to be of minor importance, given the resulting sediment transport
pattern. This is not supported data, as there were no active measurements of velocities
of any kind. Seemingly, some of the sediment transferred to the innersurf and the upper
beach face, most likely stems from the deepening of the bar-trough area as well as the
shoreward side of the nourishment. Evidently, the onshore transport is suﬃciently strong
to nourish the beach face directly, as the berm redevelops seaward of its initial position
(see Figure 4.10) up to as much as 10 cm in model scale. Shoreline erosion still occurs
with respect to time, but to a much lesser extent.
The performance of the trough nourishment is subpar relative to the seaward nourish-
ment, and apparently oﬀers almost insigniﬁcant protection in terms of shoreline erosion
relative to the unprotected proﬁle. This conﬂicts with measurements indicating more
reduced wave heights and increased wave non-linearities, which again are all character-
istic features of the lee eﬀect. The wave asymmetry generates onshore transport and
the smaller waves in the lee-side supposedly reduce the wave induced return ﬂow (under-
tow), hence reducing the oﬀshore transport. Poor performance may be because of large
local erosion happening in the nourished sediment. Placing nourishment in the trough,
apparently leads to an increase in the oﬀshore transport. It is worth noting, that the
trough nourishment surpasses the bar crest. This was because of the not very pronounced
trough, making it hard to nourish with the required amount of sand properly. Results
for this type of nourishments is in line with numerical results obtained by Jacobsen and
67
==PAGE:89==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
Fredsoe (2014a), whose bar trough is even more pronounced and explained it by an in-
crease in near-bed seaward velocity in the bar trough area. This contradicts the ﬁndings
of Walstra et al. (2010), who found that the largest reduction in erosion volume occurred
for nourishment located on top of the original breaker bar and covered the trough (which
they refer to as "high nourishment"); however, this is not supported by experimental data
from Grasso et al. (2011) which showed little diﬀerence between nourishing the crest or
the trough. Explanation for the discrepancies is unknown, things of inﬂuence may be bar
crest height or wave climate. Another possible explanation could be the importance of
when to nourish the initial proﬁle. This is in terms of the disequilibrium of the initial
proﬁle. In the present study, the signiﬁcant loss to the oﬀshore, happening for the trough
nourishment can maybe be explained by the nourished sediment being more accessible,
and hence more prone to transport.
Grasso et al. (2011) reports a steepen nearshore proﬁle, which may be a side eﬀect of
nourishment. This is also seen to occur in the present experiments, most noticeably in
the case of seaward nourishment (C7). For the trough nourishment, nothing signiﬁcant
happens. Grasso et al. (2011) explains this as a result of sediment sorting. This comes
as strong wave dissipation will reorganize sediment, such that ﬁner grains are essentially
carried to greater depths with smaller near bed velocities, while leaving the grains heavy
enough to avoid being continuously carried in suspension. Coarsening of the beach face
implies a steepen slope. This will eﬀectively also occur on the outer breaker bar. In the
present study, sediment grain samples were taken at various cross-shore locations (includ-
ing crest of outer bar and shoreline). Due to unforeseen circumstances surrounding the
pandemic these samples were unfortunately not analyzed. Some obviously larger grains
were observed visually at the shoreline; however, without sieve analysis these observations
remain inconclusive.
5.3
Migration
The interruption of the natural bar cycle is a common eﬀect traced to shoreface nourish-
ment. All proﬁles showed evidence of a migrating bar as well as migrating nourishment.
While all bar systems were initially dynamic, no bars showed signs of cyclic bar (changing
migration directions). The natural bar cycle refers to the oﬀshore migration occurring un-
der storm conditions (breaking further oﬀshore), while onshore migration typically takes
68
==PAGE:90==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
place in calmer weather conditions. In the present study both bars after nourishing imme-
diately followed the natural oﬀshore directed bar migration also displayed in the reference
case, and no eﬀect from nourishment was established in this regard, and can maybe be
explained by the ﬁxed wave conditions (wave time series looped every 30 min). In the ﬁeld
storm and calm wave conditions maybe associated with seasonal variation, but otherwise
there is obviously no ﬁxed wave conditions in the large for time frames typically in the
order of years O(years) used in cross-shore nourishment studies. Huisman et al. (2019)
experienced that shoreface nourishment would lead to onshore migration of nourishment
(opposite to the natural oﬀshore migration) and would last up to 4 to 5 years, after which
point it would revert to the natural bar cycle and again migrate oﬀshore. Such cyclic
behavior was also observed by van Duin et al. (2004), but on a shorter time frame. In
either case, the nourishment failed to migrate and merge with the berm/beach, as nour-
ishment simply started to disappear before reaching the shoreline. Ojeda et al. (2008)
also demonstrated onshore migration of the shoreface nourishment, and slowing of the
natural oﬀshore migration when nourishing in the seaward end of the active proﬁle. This
is as Huisman et al. (2019) states it, because placing a shoreface nourishment likely dis-
turbs the cross-shore sediment transport balance. Physical models with moveable beds,
seems to have diﬃculties capturing such cyclic bar behavior. The cases by Grasso et al.
(2011) showed some tendency to migrate onshore during the waning phases. However,
the timescales were much to short to view it in terms of quasi equilibrium states. Walstra
et al. (2010) also only observes one-way bar migration either onshore (oﬀshore) for ac-
cretive (erosive) wave conditions. Short experiment length also hindered any interaction
between nourishment and beach face. In the present experiment, the small ripples (which
were ﬁltered from the signal) prove to migrate very dynamically onshore/oﬀshore. These
could be to a large extent considered two-dimensional ripples (small changes in longshore
direction relative to cross-shore changes). This may also indicate that cyclic bar behavior
could be inﬂuenced by longshore eﬀects. Naturally these could not be investigated in
the present study, but it is deﬁnitely conceivable that interruption of longshore sediment
transport could be important. Walstra et al. (2010) concluded that their shoreface nour-
ishment had a lot of similarities with a submerged breakwater. This will reduce longshore
currents behind the nourishment due to wave breaking at the nourishment causing calmer
climate behind (lee eﬀect), causing upstream deposition and downstream erosion. Alter-
natively, the nourishment may also be exposed to some sort of diﬀused erosion at the
longshore edges due to gradients in the water level, at which point it may be transported
69
==PAGE:91==

away e.g. due to rip currents.
5.4
Future work
Based on the above considerations and what have been experienced in regards to the
present study, a few suggestions will be presented in the following. Naturally any future
work is not limited to any of these suggestions.
Obviously, the nourishment strategies (placement) is the essence of nourishment research,
and so new nourishment schemes could be derived and tested. However, this may also be
investigated in relation to diﬀerent initial conditions and/or wave conditions. As insin-
uated in the preceding sections, the disequilibrium of the initial proﬁle to be nourished
might be an inﬂuential factor in determining the eﬀectiveness of nourishment. This can
be viewed in terms of nourishing in the beginning or mid-winter season, i.e. following a
calmer accretive summer season. Other wave conditions might also be attempted. Instead
of constant wave spectrum, wave conditions consisting of erosive/accretive (corresponding
to winter and summer) could be attempted.
To obtain a more complete picture it of nourishment and its corresponding eﬀect on
coastal proﬁle morphology, the experiment could be repeated but while also taking de-
tailed measurements of e.g.
wave-induced velocities and sediment concentrations.
A
more representative investigation may be achieved using light-weight sediment as done by
Grasso et al. (2011), which beneﬁts are two-fold: ﬁrstly, it allows to combat scaling issues
and achieve similarity in maybe Shields parameter or Rouse parameter, secondly it be-
comes easier to (visually) observe the transport of the nourishment in the end-stage proﬁle
due to their artiﬁcial coloring (but it will not explain how it got there!). Alternatively,
the surrounding physical frames could be increased (e.g. water depth and wave heights);
however, this obviously poses practical issues as this would require bigger wave ﬂume.
Following in the footsteps of Jacobsen and Fredsoe (2014a), a numerical model of the
physical model may help to provide insight in understanding some of complex transport
mechanisms involved, but also contribute to data which could serve as a frame of reference.
Lessons were also learned related to the measuring campaign. Cross-shore proﬁle non-
uniformity were not quantiﬁed during the present study. This could be checked by running

multiple bed proﬁlers in diﬀerent lanes along the proﬁle. For reasons unknown, a large

Conclusion
This thesis aims to contribute and possibly broaden knowledge surrounding the concept of
equilibrium and quasi steady equilibrium proﬁles in relation to nourishment. This study
presents an experimental investigation into the positioning of shoreface nourishment and
its subsequent impact on coastal morphology, in a small scale wave ﬂume with a Froude
scaling of 30. Nourishment designs tested was (1) shoreward nourishment in trough of
outer bar, and (2) seaward nourishment adjacent to outer bar which were then compared
to a un-nourished proﬁle. It has been shown that as long as one is patient enough to run
lengthy experiments for given wave conditions physical modelling provides an alternative
in the investigation of quasi−equilibrium proﬁles. A qualitatively analysis of shoreface
nourishment in relation to the concept of quasi equilibrium proﬁles was done based on
proﬁle measurements, hydrodynamic measurements and calculated sediment transport
rates based on bed level changes. As a result of the present study the following conclusions
can be made.
1. The nourished beach proﬁles show signs of evolving towards the same (quasi steady

Model Description
In this chapter, a description of the numerical model is provided. The numerical model was
created due to a shift in attention of the thesis, and formally constitutes the 2nd part. The
rationale behind the inception of the model, is to rely on a parameterisation of various
relationships.
These parameterisations is typically derived from experimental results.
Unfortunately, the appearance of some free parameters is inevitable. These have, for the
most part, been assigned values presented in relevant literature instead of being assigned
arbitrary values and hereby acting as calibration parameters. This chapter is organized
as follows.
Brief description of model input is provided in §7.1.
The hydrodynamic
part of the model is shown implemented in §7.2, with the parameterization of the free-
stream signal shown in §7.3. The implemented sediment transport formulation is outlined
§7.4, with description of bed transport and suspended transport, with a description of
the morphology given in §7.5. Additional information about model implementation is
provided in §7.6.
7.1
Model Input
The model takes as main input, incident wave parameters (Hs & Tp; Hs and Tp being
the signiﬁcant wave height and peak period respectively), grain size d as well as an initial
proﬁle in terms of two vectors x and hb, which are the cross-shore discretization and bed
level respectively. The vectors should obey a Cartesian coordinate system, with x- and y-
axis located at the still water level with z-axis pointing vertically upwards. As a result,
x = 0 is located somewhere oﬀshore and increases shoreward. Water depth is measured
as positive.

7.2
Hydrodynamics
A precondition for a good representation of the sediment transport, a description of the
relevant hydrodynamics must be achieved. This is in terms of wave height, water motion
in the waves, turbulence level, bed shear stresses and if within the surf zone, the wave-
induced currents. This is naturally important, as it is well known that the most intense
sediment transport occurs in the surf zone (Fredsøe and Deigaard, 1992). The diﬀerent
hydrodynamics will be presented in the same order as implemented in the model.

7.4
Sediment transport
Fredsøe and Deigaard (1992) states, that it is common to split sediment transport into
three parts; however, in the present study, the sediment transport is limited to bed load
and suspended load.
Sediment which is considered as bed load, is part of the total
sediment transport which is in more or less continuous contact with seabed and is hence
driven by the bed shear acting on the seabed. The suspended load is, as opposed to the
bed load, being transported without continuous contact with seabed, which is the result
of ﬂuid turbulence. The formulation of the bed load and suspended load is detailed in the
following. It is worth noting that the approach used for both types of transport is limited
to a single grain size i.e. uniform sand.

repaired, which at times prove to be rather diﬃcult. This was typically encountered with
a inﬁnitely growing bar at which point the model would naturally stop.
7.6
Numerical model and implementation
The parameterized model outlined in the previous sections is implemented in the Matlab®
environment. All derivatives (x-derivatives) are computed using a standard ﬁnite diﬀer-
ence approximation. In computing the undertow velocity proﬁles, the model currently
make use of a linear stretched grid with 10,000 vertical grid points. Initially a logarith-
mically stretched grid (with far fewer grid points) was tested as this would have been
97
==PAGE:119==
CHAPTER 7. MODEL DESCRIPTION
preferable, but for reasons unknown this seem to cause some sort of numerical instability.
This problem was circumvented by applying a linear stretched grid with a large number
of grid points, as improved resolution would eventually approximate a logarithmically
stretched grid. 10,000 points were found to be more than suﬃcient in order to ensure that
the model converged. The temporal discretization of each wave period (hence free-stream
velocity signal) is based on 200 time increments (∆t = T/N, N = 200).
For the time integration of the Exner equation, given by eq. (7.68), the model employs
Matlabs built in ode45 function, which is the most basic and ﬁrst-attempt solver designed
for solving nonstiﬀsystems. The integrator is based on an explicit variable-step iterative
4th order Runge-Kutta (Dormand−Prince) method. Initial condition used for time inte-
gration obviously corresponds to the initial proﬁle. All simulations used a relative error
tolerance of 10−3.
Through input parameters Hs and Tp, the model calculates (based on linear theory)
various deep water quantities which are then used as initial values in various solution
procedures e.g. when solving dispersion relation everywhere or the system of equations
determining wave breaking. Accurately predicting erosion on the upper beach face is a
tough job for such a paramatarized model. Limitations will be modelling the highly com-
plicated wave dynamics associated with the swash zone. This is in particular the run-up
where depths eﬀectively are zero. As such, wave heights are initially all artiﬁcially set to
zero for the last grid point. The decrease in water depth which inevitably will hit zero,
will also pose problem when solving the dispersion relation across the entire model do-
main. This is circumvented by introducing a limiting depth hmin, beyond which nothing
is explicitly modelled. Naturally, it should be smaller as it otherwise would extend too
far, and hence avoid perfectly good waves to be modelled. Presently, the limiting water
depth is taken as hmin = 0.01 m (but can be freely adjusted). This implies that (parts
of) the swash zone is not explicitly. modelled. The model domain comprising the swash
zone is implemented as the region which is bounded seaward by the last grid point, for
which H < hαRdown, where αRdown = 0.9. While it is bounded landward by the ﬁrst grid
point located above the wave run-up. The instantaneous position of the shoreline is de-
termined by the intersection of water surface and sea bed. The sediment transport rates
is artiﬁcially decayed to zero at the end of the swash zone using a parabolic formulation.
With no waves "present" in the swash zone, this is largely what is governing the change

8.2.3
Prediction ability
One way of evaluating the morphodynamic modelling performance is by using the Brier
Skill Score (BSS) as proposed by van Rijn et al. (2003). The evaluation is based on the
ratio between the mean squared diﬀerence between predicted and observed value to that
baseline prediction and observation. In this case the baseline prediction is taken as the
initial proﬁle as also used in Van Rijn et al. (2011). Perfect prediction gives a BSS of 1,
wheres a baseline prediction results in a BSS of 0. If model is worse than the baseline
prediction the BSS will be negative. Apparently a limitation of the BSS is that it does not

Chapter 9
Conclusion
A simple numerical model have been developed in the well known Matlab® environ-
ment. It have been demonstrated that the developed model can simulate morphological
development of a coastal proﬁle on the time scale of O(34 hr) in around 260 seconds in
the most computational demanding case. The computed surface elevations and undertow
velocity proﬁles were validated against high resolution measurements. Computed surface
elevations were generally within acceptable levels compared to measurements, however
a slight missprediction in the break point were observed. Computed undertow velocity
proﬁles generally underestimated the velocity in the innersurf, while it overestimated the
velocity in the region immediately following wave breaking. This may be linked to the
aforementioned missprediction in breaking criterion. However, a good qualitatively match
was achieved in the velocity proﬁles due to the introduced eddy viscosity weight function.
The overestimation of the velocity in the outer surf, was comparable with a non-stabilized
k −ω turbulence model, which still illustrates the reasonable performance of the devel-
oped model, considering its simplicity. The model was used to simulate erosion of plane
sloping beaches exposed to irregular waves in two diﬀerent scales as those studied in the
EU-SANDS project. The model performed well in erosive wave conditions, correctly pre-
dicting oﬀshore bar migration. Waves tend to break too far seaward limiting the end-stage
migration. Predicted shoreline erosion was also within acceptable levels. The sensitivity
analysis showed, for reasons unknown, that the model was not always consistent in its
prediction across the two (Froude-scaled) ﬂumes, nonetheless the BSS for the erosive tests
was calculated to be 0.64 and 0.55, scoring Good and fair respectively. The upper beach
face was generally well predicted in accretive test, but lack of onshore sediment transport
resulted in lack of onshore bar migration. Overall performance for accretive tests were

less good; scoring a BSS of 0.38 (fair) and 0.14 (poor).
