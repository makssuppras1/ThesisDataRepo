Abstract
In this Master Thesis ﬂow in Vacuum Infusion Process is studied through a development of nu-
merical model and experimental campaign. Vacuum infusion is a manufacturing technique of
composites in which polymeric resin impregnates porous reinforcement and provides shear
stiffness after curing. Pressure equation accounting for reinforcement thickness variations
inherently occurring in the process is developed and solved both in infused and through-
thickness directions. Implementations based on 1D ﬁnite difference and 2D ﬁnite volume
schemes are shown. Experimental setup is developed featuring measurements of ﬂow front
position, pressure in the mold, laminate thickness and mass of infused ﬂuid during the pro-
cess. In total 8 experiments are conducted investigating infusion of unidirectional ﬁberglass
mat in transverse direction. Further, impact of gravity, through thickness ﬂow in the laminate,
effect of a peel ply and distribution medium on the process are also considered. The numeri-
cal models are checked against the experimental results and are found to present potential for
computing global parameters in the process like ﬁlling time and pressure but further experi-
mental work is needed to validate predictions of laminate thickness variations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter sketches the importance of infusion ﬂow modelling on the example of wind tur-
bine blades manufacturing. Vacuum infusion process is described in detail and necessary ter-
minology for understanding this report content is outlined. The chapter ends with a statement
of purpose and scope of this study.
1.1
On the way to larger precision
The average size of wind turbine blade has increased from 15 m to around 50 m in the last
30 years [1]. In 2016 the largest blades produced exceeded 80 m in length and 6 m in chord,
taking as an example the recent world record breaker - 88.4 m blade produced by LM Wind
Power. Advances in composite science allowed for production of larger rotors challenging
the e.g. fatigue, buckling or weight-related stresses connected with continuous upscaling of
blades. Large rotors in return allow to capture more energy what brings the cost of each kWh
electricity produced down.
For this process to be successful, the research in aerodynamics, structural and material engi-
neering must be accompanied by the development of manufacturing techniques that make it
possible to produce blades of such dimensions with quality and precision that ensure safe and
robust operation throughout the lifetime.
Wind turbine blades are one of numerous examples of composite structures where infusion
ﬂow technique is used in the production process. A polymeric resin that ﬂows through porous
reinforcement placed in the mold, impregnates it and provides shear stiffness of the compo-
nent after curing. Application of resin infusion can be also found in aerospace, civil, automo-
tive, marine industries [2] and others.

Accurate prediction of the ﬂow during infusion process is of large signiﬁcance. First, not fully
impregnated areas can create weak spots with larger risk of failure. Second, the speed of in-
fusion impacts the production time and therefore cost. Further, consumption of a resin and
therefore ﬁnal part weight distribution and dimensions have proven to be challenging to pre-
dict. Models of infusion ﬂow and experiments that validate them help to understand the
physics of the process and therefore build reliable tools that can support engineers in daily
work.
1.2
Fiberglass reinforced composites
Composite is a material that consist of at least two signiﬁcantly different components. It is
often the core material (e.g. wood foam) and binder matrix (e.g. resin) that keeps the core
in place. The advantage of composite materials are good fatigue properties and often high
speciﬁc strength.
Fibers constitute the reinforcement of the composite material in question of this thesis. The
commonly used type of ﬁbers in wind industry is glass ﬁber, therefore description will be
mainly focused on this particular material, however much of it also suits e.g. carbon ﬁbers.
There exist essentially two types of ﬁberglass mats. The ﬁrst type is a layer of randomly bonded
together ﬁbers. The next is non-crimp type of fabrics where parallel bundles of ﬁber yarns are
stitched together with stitching yarn. The bundles can cross each other at different directions
creating multi-axial ﬁber strands. The essential difference between these two types in terms
of ﬂow modeling is that the one length scale assumption is valid only for the random mat
while fabrics have in general multi-scale ﬂow (in large and small pores). This will be further
discussed in Chapter 2. Short description of commonly used ﬁberglass fabrics is available in
Appendix A, Table A.1.
Layers of different mats can be mixed together to shape composites of required longitudinal
and transverse properties. Moreover, the ﬁnal choice of layout impacts the nature of the ﬂow
during infusion. It happens therefore rarely that the ﬂow environment is isotropic. Also, due to
the variation during ﬁber production, differences in characteristics occur. Therefore param-
eters describing the fabrics and governing the ﬂow have statistical nature what sets limits on
accuracy of modeling in general.
During the infusion process resin ﬂows through ﬁber fabrics. The character of this ﬂow is usu-
ally referred to as a porous media ﬂow. Parameters that describe the ﬁber reinforcement and
govern the ﬂow through porous media are permeability, ﬁber volume fraction and porosity.

1.3
Resin
What bonds the ﬁbers together is a resin matrix. In wind industry typical resins are thermoset-
ting which means that after curing process they cannot be reformed. They are liquid at the
beginning to enable wetting of ﬁbers and become solid when cured. During the curing pro-
cess the polymer chains start to cross-link and form rigid three dimensional structures that
display good strength and hardness. This reaction is exothermic and heats up the resin. Resin
viscosity depends on temperature and solidiﬁcation process [3].
There are three main types of thermosetting resins used as a composite binder: polyester,
epoxy and vinyl ester. Polyester is perhaps the most common in wind blades manufacturing as
it cures fast at room temperatures and has a low cost [4]. It provides around one to few hours of
infusion time after which resin starts to gel and does not progress anymore. Nevertheless, dur-
ing the main part of the impregnation using polyester a constant viscosity approach is often
utilized [3]. Also at the same time isothermal character of the process is claimed.
Another important feature of the polyester resin is volumetric shrinkage of around 10% that
occurs during the curing process which makes it hard to achieve low tolerances. Lower shrink-
age is presented by epoxy resins which also have better mechanical properties but the curing
process is more complex and are considerably more expensive. Vinyl resin has similar proper-
ties to epoxy but it is cheaper and has a shorter curing time [4].
All the described resins are hazardous materials and can be very dangerous to human body.
While epoxy resin is mostly dangerous through the direct contact the polyester is also harmful
when inhaled. Therefore clean and closed processes like e.g. vacuum infusion are preferable
when working with resins.

1.4
Vacuum Infusion
Vacuum Infusion or VI 1 (also known as Vacuum Assisted Resin Transfer Molding VARTM, Vac-
uum Bag Molding VBM or Seemann Composites Resin Infusion Molding Process SCRIMP) is
a composite manufacturing technique that uses one-sided mold what brings the process cost
down, especially in case of very large components like a wind turbine blade or a boat hull.
The other side of the mold is replaced by a vacuum bag which is sealed to the solid mold.
A pressure difference between the atmosphere and vacuum inside the cavity provided by a
vacuum pump ensures that ﬁbers are compacted and the laminate follows the mold shape.
Moreover, the pressure difference is a driving force for resin ﬂow.
The drawback of this method is that the vacuum bag is ﬂexible and allows for some variations
of the reinforcement thickness during the process. This brings additional challenge to ﬂow
prediction as these thickness variations alter porosity, permeability and inﬂuence the precision
of a ﬁnal part. Variable thickness leads to nonuniform weight distribution what is important
for wind turbine blades as gravity and inertia forces are signiﬁcant design driver.
Figure 1.1 sketches a typical cycle of vacuum infusion. Once reinforcement (e.g. ﬁbers) is
cut for needed dimensions, it is placed in a mold and creates so called preform. As the mold
geometry is often curved (like it happens to be in case of blade cross-section, ref. Figure 1.1
where mold for blade shell is shown) the preform needs careful adjustments to ﬁt the mold
shape. This process is called draping and it is also important for ﬂow modelling as properties
of reinforcement are altered e.g. wrinkles and ﬁber misalignment can occur [3].
On the top of reinforcement the peel ply can be placed for later separation of a ready part from
vacuum bag and other consumables. The layer with high in-plane permeability called distri-
bution media (or material ﬂow layer) can be inserted between the peel ply and the vacuum
bag to speed up the ﬂow progression. If this is the case the ﬂow progresses faster within the
distribution media and then proceeds into the reinforcement in through-thickness direction.
At this stage inlet and outlet tubes are also placed. Depending on the part geometry various
strategies can be undertaken. This includes point or line infusion, infusion from many points
at the same time or sequentially. Vacuum pump can also be connected at one point or in
number of places as the outlet tube is also responsible for excess resin collection during so-
called bleeding stage. In case of the wind turbine blade infusion is performed along the blade
from the bottom of the mold and resin proceeds on both sides towards the leading and trialling
edge (ref. Figure 1.1). As ﬁbers are placed primarily along the blade, this signiﬁes importance
of laminate permeability in second (transverse) and third (through-thickness) direction.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
After the bag is sealed to the mold, vacuum is applied through the outlet tubes while inlets
are closed. This starts the pre-ﬁlling phase when the atmospheric pressure compresses the
preform making it tightly ﬁt to the mold. During this process the tightness of the bag is ensured
and desired level of vacuum is achieved. Choice of the vacuum level is driven by the aimed
ﬁber volume fraction of the composite. The ﬁber content determines stiffens, static and fatigue
strength properties. At this phase the pressure distribution inside the cavity is assumed to be
uniform and close to the vacuum level.
The ﬁlling phase starts when inlet tubes are open. Resin is sucked from a pot and infused into
the preform. Resin pressure at the inlet is around 1 bar and is a main driving force for the ﬂow.
However, gravity and capillary forces also affect the front progression [5, 6]. As resin reaches
subsequent positions along the mold the pressure there slowly increases and ﬂuid takes over
the load from atmosphere. This makes the preform to expand slightly and is one of the main
reasons of the aforementioned thickness variations. This problem is discussed in more detail
in Section 2.3 where compaction models are introduced. The ﬁlling stage lasts until the front
ﬂow reaches the end of the cavity.
Ususally, the front ﬂow reached the end of cavity the inlet gate is closed while the outlet tubes
remain open to draw out the excess of resin. Infusion is in post-ﬁlling phase. During that stage
the pressure and its gradient slowly decrease [2, 7, 8]. Ideally, a uniform pressure distribution
would eliminate unwanted thickness variations. However, usually resin starts to gel and freezes
pressure and thickness gradient that currently exist in the mold. It is therefore at this stage
when the component reaches its ﬁnal shape and properties. The time of gel point and curing
depend on the type of resin used, additions like hardener or catalyst and temperature.
After substantial period of time (at least few times the ﬁlling time) when the laminate is fully
cured, the vacuum bag can be unsealed. The part needs to be separated from all the consum-
ables and it is ready for use or further processing.

The purpose and the scope of this Thesis
This thesis aims on experimental validation of the state of the art infusion models that cur-
rently exist in literature. Understanding and discussion of physics during the infusion process
with reexamination of currently existing assumptions in the ﬁeld based on experimental evi-
dence are one of the main ambitions. The ideal model is understood here as containing suf-
ﬁcient representation of physics such that global ﬂow parameters can be accurately predicted
rather than accounting for all the effects. However, in particular large effort is put on proper

2.4
Capillary effects and multi-scale ﬂow
During vacuum infusion resin ﬂows in narrow spaces of pores and capillary effects are present.
These effects become the more signiﬁcant the smaller are pores. In general, non-crimp ﬁber
fabrics like e.g. unidirectional ﬁber mat have bimodal pore size distribution which means that
resin can ﬂow in large macro-pores between the tows and in small micro-pores within the tows.
This feature is often referred to as a dual- or multi-scale porous media [19, 24]. Naturally, the
signiﬁcance of capillary effects in micro-scale is larger.
It is a common practice to ignore capillary effects in the macro-pores ﬂow and include it when
impregnation of ﬁbers within the tows is modeled [3, 24, 25]. However some authors include
the capillary pressure in the macro ﬂow modelling by modifying the boundary condition at
the ﬂow front e.g. Sayre [6]. Even though it is a simpliﬁed approach, Sayre reported gaining
6 % points on accuracy of ﬁlling time prediction in his model with respect to measurements
of transverse impregnation in unidirectional glass ﬁber mat. Capillary pressure was found to
contribute with not negligible positive force on top of vacuum pressure gradient.
Multi-scale ﬂow is connected with occurring voids during the process. In most cases resin
ﬁrst ﬁlls the larger pores (in between tows) and than ﬂows into the small ones within the tows
[15, 24, 26, 27]. In such situation air can be trapped in the tows creating a micro-void. Therefore
uniform impregnation on both scales is preferable.
However, usually the rate of ﬁlling the tows is lower due to smaller permeability and an un-
saturated zone lags the main visually observed ﬂow front. Such not fully impregnated zone
introduces a number of complications to the modeling. First, it still absorbs the resin after the
front has passed what needs to be accounted for in mass conservation. Second, it is harder
to predict front progression accurately as resin ﬂows on two scales. Third, mentioned earlier
lubrication effect takes place within that zone.
Moreover, ﬂow in dual scale porous media violates length scale restrictions that were applied
on averaging volumes during derivation procedure of Darcy’s law (ref. Section 2.1). If char-
acteristic length scale for macro-ﬂow is taken to be distance between the ﬁber tows, then the
average ﬂow properties are very likely to change whenever internal of the tow is considered
which rises a question of proper choice of REV.
One way of dealing with unsaturated zone and multi-scale ﬂow is to solve the ﬂow (Darcy’s
law and continuity equation) on both scales, however that requires additional knowledge on
physical properties of the tows which is already hardly measured on the macro-level. Another
method is introduction of sink term in continuity equation that corresponds to tows ﬁlling
[15, 26]. However, still to model these sink terms some assumptions about the internal of ﬁber
tows are needed.

Finite Difference (FD) formulation is the oldest methods for solving partial differential equa-

introduction to the method can be found in [33]. It offers reasonable efﬁciency and is easy
to implement for simple geometry problems. However, it usually does not satisfy the mass
conservation unless the special care is taken.

3.1.4
Boundary conditions
There are two sets of boundary conditions for infusion process i.e. ﬁlling phase and post-
ﬁlling phase. Ideally, during ﬁlling the cavity is subjected to atmospheric pressure patm at the
inlet and chosen vacuum level at the ﬂow front pvac. This enforces two Dirichlet boundary
conditions on the pressure. While the ﬁrst one is ﬁxed to the inlet node, the second boundary
condition moves as the front progresses.
This was however not sufﬁcient to achieve a physical behaviour of the code. Whenever the
front was advanced the sudden change of pressure occurred and leveled out after some time.
To avoid that without penalizing computational performance by very ﬁne spatial discretization
the pressure in the front node was interpolated linearly between the desired outlet condition
and the pressure just before the front ﬂow

3.1.6
Numerical convergence and mass conservation
Due to the lack of analytical solution convergence is demonstrated towards the ﬁnest simu-
lated rational discretization both in time and space. This is done by ﬁxing ﬁrst the time step
and changing spatial step and then by changing time step length with ﬁxed number of space
nodes. Relative error in pressure solution with respect to the ﬁnest resolution was calculated
and plotted against the discretization in log-log scale. For the convergence analysis in space
the pressure proﬁle at the end of ﬁlling was chosen while for the time convergence the evolu-
tion of pressure at the ﬁxed point along the mold (25% of the domain). Additionally ﬁlling time
was plotted against the time discretization.
The results of convergence analysis are shown in Appendix D, Figure B.1. It was found found
that the scheme presents the rate of convergence near 2 in space and above 1 in time. In gen-
eral it should be possible to demonstrate the 2nd order convergence of the pressure ﬁeld in
time and space when using pure Crank-Nicolson method. However, in the following approach
there is a number of approximations that increase the truncation error. First, Adam-Bashforth
extrapolation was used. Second, the ﬁlling function which is sensitive to the choice of dis-
cretization both in space and time is responsible for moving the boundary condition and can
therefore also introduce an error. Still the analysis shows that the scheme is consistent and it
does converge. It is to be noted that the total ﬁlling time deviates by less then 0.5% for time step
of 2 s and 50 nodes with respect to the time step of 0.02 s what shows rather small inﬂuence of
how accurately temporal variations are resolved.

vacuum level pressure. Next, the outﬂowing ﬂuid through this boundary was monitored un-
til pressure dropped to the boundary level with tolerance 10−6 Pa. Difference in the laminate
area before and after the change of boundary condition was compared to integrated in time

3.1.7
Preliminary results
Validation of the model against experimental results is considered in Chapter 5, however be-
fore this is done, results from modeling are analysed in terms of their physical rationality and
if the model behaves as expected.
First, the impact of initial ﬁber volume fraction and stiffening index on the mold ﬁlling time
was analysed. Number of simulations was done when varying one of the parameters and hold-
ing other constant. To avoid resin gelling the viscosity was set constant to 300 mPa·s. The
results are shown in Figure 3.2.
Looking at Figure 3.2a it can be seen that for increasing initial ﬁber volume fraction (holding
thickness constant) the ﬁlling time increases. Increasing ﬁber volume fraction can be also un-
derstood as smaller stack height for the same surface density i.e. more compacted preform.
This is an intuitive result as one would expect the ﬁlling time to be longer for more compacted
reinforcement due to larger resistance. This tendency is a resultant of Kozeny-Carman equa-
tion which represents the change of permeability with respect to ﬁber volume fraction (Figure
A.1b).
In Figure 3.2b dependence of ﬁlling time on stiffening index shows that for more compliant
preform the ﬁlling time will be longer as for the same compaction pressure larger volume frac-
tions can be achieved (Figure A.1a). Accordingly the permeability will drop slowing down the
ﬂow as previously discussed. The sensitivity of ﬁlling time on stiffening index should be noted
as the plot has logarithmic y-axis.
Next, complete infusion process was simulated with parameters as summarized in Table 3.1.
Pressure evolution and corresponding thickness variations are analyzed (Figures 3.3a and 3.3b).
It can be seen that as expected pressure increases at sequential positions along the mold as the
ﬂow front proceeds. When resin arrives at certain node its compaction characteristic changes
to wet state and the thickness suddenly drops (Figure 3.3f). This feature could also be imple-
mented accounting for gradual change of compaction characteristic rather then discrete. As
the pressure starts to build up the preform expands.
The front ﬂow reaches the end of the cavity after around 130 min and post-ﬁlling phase starts.
Corresponding change of boundary conditions causes drop in the pressure. This is accompa-
nied by a decreases in the preform thickness as the atmospheric pressure loads the reinforce-
ment again. In this simulation the pressure equilibrium is never reached as the resin viscosity
reached limit value considered as a gel point which effectively means the end of ﬂow (Figure

3.2.4
Front ﬂow tracking
Due to two dimensional nature of the problem advancement of the ﬂow front is more complex.
To simplify the problem it is assumed that the ﬂow is always from left to right and from the top
to the bottom of the domain e.g. case of high permeability media on top of the laminate that is
being ﬁlled faster. The idea of ﬂow front tracking method is sketched in Figure 3.6. Information
about the front position is stored in a column vector with a length of number of cells in vertical
direction of the domain. Each row of a front vector corresponds to a vertical position in the
domain and value stored is a linear index (one number) that fully determines position of a
front cell in a 2D domain. Linear index allows directly reach the element of a matrix such as its
columns were stored on top of each other, as a vector.
All the eastern walls of a front cell are impermeable (red line ,Figure 3.6) until are ﬁlled. Vertical
transfer of mass is allowed in the saturated zone (all the ﬁlled cells) and between the front cells
if they are neighbouring with each other. To check that condition for loop pass is made through
the front vector that subtracts subsequent (in front vector) linear indices. If the difference be-
tween them is one, they are neighbours vertically. Otherwise, the difference is a multiplication
of total number of cells in vertical direction plus one and describes how far from each other
in horizontal direction are front cells of two neighbouring horizontal rows. This information is
used to lock mass transfer in vertical direction on a distance between these two front cells.

3.2.5
Boundary conditions
Similarly as in ﬁnite difference model, two sets of boundary conditions are imposed on the
pressure ﬁeld, during ﬁlling and post-ﬁlling phase. During ﬁlling all the cells beyond the front
and including the front line have prescribed Dirichlet boundary condition i.e. vacuum level.
The inlet is situated at most western wall. If distribution media is present the inlet is imposed
only on the walls that are contained within distribution media. This was found to represent
better experimental conditions and is close to a corner inﬂow. All other boundaries i.e. the rest
of western wall, top and bottom had implemented Neumann boundary condition meaning no
ﬂow through. During post-ﬁlling the inlet boundary condition is also changed to Neumann
i.e. the pressure derivative is zero. The details about implementation of particular boundary
conditions are given in Appendix B.4

3.2.6
Note on stability and code performance
The code was found to have quite narrow stability limits and sensitive to the choice of perme-
ability. Usually time step size was limited by the choice of permeability of distribution media
as there ﬂow proceeds the fastest. Especially through thickness permeability of distribution
media was a bottle neck taking into account very small height of the cells i.e. in order of 10−4m
for laminate of around 10 mm and 30 cells in y direction. For a better control of the code no
permeability model was included and large improvement was achieved by setting K22 perme-
ability of the distribution media to the same as laminate. This is rather a rough assumption
but in most of the cases permeability media constituted from 1 to few cells in vertical direction
and it could be argued that vertical mass transfer is governed by permeability of the laminate
and ﬂow from the distribution media to the ﬁber reinforcement is of the main importance.
Usually when the front reached half of the domain the pressure gradients were lower and it was
possible to switch the time step to higher value (by a factor of 10). This was found to be a easy
way to speed up the calculations.
Convergence test for subsequently ﬁner grid was done with respect to the ﬁnest simulated
grid which was 70 by 60 grid points. Rate of convergence obtained was around -0.8 This was
lower than expected but consistency of the code was veriﬁed. Result is available in Appendix
D, Figure B.2.
3.2.7
Preliminary Results for 2D Modeling
To check the performance of the code in 2D an infusion of 11 mm thick laminate with high per-
meability layer of 1 mm on top is simulated. Distribution medium stiffness index is set to low
value imitating low compressibility. The parameters used in the simulation are summarized in
Table 3.2. Front progression, saturation function, pressure and thickness traces are shown in
Figure 3.7.
In this simulation three top cells constituted permeability medium while the rest was the lam-
inate. It is seen that upper layers were ﬁlled much faster than the rest of the domain and the
code allows for the development of extended 2D front ﬂow. Change of the domain height due
to laminate compressibility can also be noted. Pressure is found to increase at different rates
along the laminate and thickness increase is now less regular as it is a sum of cells height at a
given position which are all compressible.
Even though the front ﬂow presented physical rationality, cells were overﬁlled in some re-
gions indicating problems with mass conservation of the scheme. Regions around the inlet
at the level of distribution medium-laminate interface were those where overﬁlling occurred
the most. Problem was decreasing with ﬁner time and space discretization.

Model 3: 1D Finite Volume with Equation of Motion
In this section a new model is developed where the preform is treated as dynamic mechanical
system that is allowed to move. Equation of motion for thickness degree of freedom is coupled
with ﬂow pressure solver built on Finite Volume scheme in 1D. However temporal changes in
vacuum infusion process are small, the motivation here is to replace power law that accounts
for laminate compaction characteristics with a model that is more suitable for representation
of solid-body interaction that takes place in the ﬁber reinforcement. Further beneﬁt of having
such a model would be a prediction of laminate response to relatively fast air ﬂow if one occurs
during the process e.g. during the inlet value opening (which can be numerous on a big struc-
ture) or leaks. However, this falls outside the scope of this thesis as multiphase ﬂow in porous
media is not simulated here.

of front velocity in time such to obtain the front displacement, in the ﬁnite volume formulation
the entire mass inﬂow to the cell is integrated in time what accounts for variable thickness and
porosity of the cell and their walls. These differences between the models (e.g. in ﬁlling time)
do not appear when the thickness of laminate is constant as simulated in the previous section.

4.2
Layup descriptions
Each layup was prepared in a similar manner. In all the measurements unidirectional ﬁber-
glass with area density 600 g/m2 was infused in transverse direction. Plies were cut for dimen-
sions 60x40 cm. Spiral tubes were used at the inlet and outlet. At the end of the preform peel
ply was used to prevent the total drainage of the laminate and under the inlet tube distribu-
tion media was used to provide uniform inﬂow conditions. An overlap region of around 2 cm
was aimed at both ends (except the tests where distribution media was placed over the entire
laminate up to the peel ply at the outlet).
A sketch of such setup is shown in Figure 4.3. Pressure and thickness measurements points can
be identiﬁed according to coordinate system of each setup which has an origin at the beginning
of distribution media on the inlet side as shown in the ﬁgure. Exact coordinates for all tests’
pressure and thickness measurements are available in Appendix C, Table C.1.
Epoxy resin and sunﬂower oil were used as infusion ﬂuid. The latter provides much lower
viscosity what speeds up the process and allows for infusion without distribution media which
can be assumed as one dimensional ﬂow. Same trials on epoxy resin showed that resin gels
before full saturation of the laminate.
The tests were chosen such to observe impact of different factors on the process: 1D generic
infusion on the plate plate, gravity impact, inﬂuence of distribution media on the ﬂow, change
of ﬂuid (oil/resin), stack height. Before each experiment oil was degassed under 90% vacuum
for at least 40 minutes and resin for 20 minutes. Besides, the laminate was compacted twice
(vacuum was released once) to provide fairly repeatable compaction state. In the case of the
vertical plate infusion, resin pot was lifted to the approximate level of inlet tube.

Chapter 6
Summary and Conclusions
Vacuum Infusion was shown to be a complex process consisting of many stages and factors
deciding about the quality and features of a ﬁnal part. Among them, the resin ﬂow is perhaps
one of the most important ones. Inclusion of all features in process ﬂow modeling is a very
challenging task and requires large number of parameters that may be difﬁcult to determine.
Therefore numerical simulations are limited by accuracy of the input data and statistical na-
ture of the reinforcement material in particular.
Physical phenomena in the process cross many disciplines as it usually is in ﬂuid-solid in-
teraction problems. The ﬂow was shown to be governed by Darcy’s law where permeability
of porous reinforcement is the main factor dependent among others on preform compaction
state and fabric saturation. Literature study revealed that capillary forces and multiscale ﬂow
present additional challenges, even in prediction of global parameters like ﬁlling time.
Even though the relation of the ﬂow to a height of a channel is well known in form of continuity
equation, the dependence of the channel height to the ﬂuid pressure in infusion process is
much more difﬁcult to ﬁnd and makes the problem complex and coupled. Suited for vacuum
infusion process mass conservation equation was shown and in conjunction with Darcy’s law
converted to a ﬂow governing equation that constituted an essential model of this thesis.
Pressure equation governing the ﬂow and accounting for compressible laminate is a parabolic
non-linear diffusion equation that has to be solved numerically. However, as process varies
slowly in time, 2nd order extrapolation of non-linear terms and having them explicitly calcu-
lated at the half time step in conjunction with Crank-Nicholson scheme were found to be a
robust solution.
One of the main challenges in building the code was moving boundary condition that governs
the time marching simulation in 1D dimensional model. As pressure function is discontinu-
ous in this point (like a moving shock-wave), pressure gradient needed for Darcy’s velocity has
71
==PAGE:82==
72
CHAPTER 6. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS
to be calculated with special care. In this thesis 4th order backward ﬁnite difference scheme
was used for Model 1. Nevertheless, instabilities were observed when sudden change of height
and permeability was simulated (e.g. a distribution medium or a peel ply). Further, interpola-
tion of pressure for the front cell based on the cell ﬁlling made the code additionally prone to
instabilities. If gradient reversed the sign at such discontinuity the boundary condition could
become negative. Therefore this feature should be reconsidered.
In 2D this problem becomes even more complicated and in general needs an interface-tracking
algorithm like e.g. volume of ﬂuid method. In this thesis a simpliﬁed method was used that
keeps track of cell indices that constitute the ﬂow front and pressure was stifﬂy set to vacuum
level in these cells. This was found to be sufﬁcient to reproduce front at the top and the bottom
of a laminate but did not satisfy the mass convergence as some cells, usually in the region of
distribution media were over-ﬁlled. Limitations were also encountered in stability of the code
e.g. locking through thickness permeability of distribution media to permeability of laminate.
In general further work is needed on the implementation of proposed schemes so the mass is
conserved on both global and local level. Mass conservation was observed to be ﬁner when
lower compressibility of the preform was simulated which may light the way to inconsistency
in the numerical scheme.
Experimental campaign conﬁrmed a few ﬁndings of other researchers but also raised ques-
tions about the validity of some assumptions. It was shown that the pressure proﬁle in vac-
uum infusion process is not linear. This was already argued e.g. by Correia et al. [14] and was
connected to compressibility of the laminate as a reason behind that non-linearity. It was like-
wise seen in the measurements of this thesis that laminate thickness varies in both space and
time during the process. This observation conﬁrms that Darcy’s law when combined with in-
compressible preform continuity equation cannot be expected to yield accurate results of e.g.
ﬁlling time.
However, it was shown by numerical analysis that inclusion of compressibility effects solely
will not yet lead to accurate prediction of pressure, its gradient and therefore ﬁlling time (Figure
5.1). Dependence of permeability on compaction of the laminate was found to decide about
the linearity of the pressure proﬁle. Yet matching the pressure gradient still did not allow to
predict the ﬁlling time accurately.
In some experiments (e.g. E1 or E3) front velocity further away from the inlet was found to
remain constant rather than decrease as in the simulation what suggests a missing parameter
in the analysis like e.g. capillary forces. It was already concluded by Sayre [6] that modifying
a boundary condition when infusing unidirectional ﬁber mat in transverse direction can in-
crease the accuracy of front time prediction. On the other hand, such 1D generic infusion is
==PAGE:83==
73
to a large degree an academic example whereas mentioned phenomenon was not observed
when distribution medium was used what corresponds more to an industrial application.
Further, for Darcy’s law to predict linear pressure proﬁle a constant permeability has to be also
assumed, except the incompressible media assumption (derivation available in Appendix A.3).
In this thesis only dependence of permeability on ﬁber volume fraction was considered while
it is also known to be a function of laminate saturation. This requires careful consideration
after Experiments 1-3 are analyzed where large discrepancy was found between measured and
simulated pressure traces during post-ﬁlling phase. Pressure traces appeared such as the per-
meability of laminate was comparable to the peel ply permeability and large gradient within
the preform was present through the most part of drainage phase. If the laminate permeabil-
ity was decreased to a level of the peel ply, ﬁlling time would not be reached. Therefore it is
deemed that permeability changed after the the laminate was fully saturated.
Gravity was observed to impact the front speed during the process, however the experiment
should be repeated to obtain more convincing results as total infusion time for horizontal (E1)
and vertical plate (E4), ﬂowing upwards, was almost the same whereas ﬂow downwards was
signiﬁcantly shorter i.e. by around 30%. Difference of much smaller magnitude were found
from calculations. Slight differences in pressure proﬁles between three experiment (E1,E3 and
E4) were found (Figure 4.10), however taking into account that hydro static pressure for oil
column of 0.6 m is around 0.06 bar the sought after variation is in order of accuracy of the
measurement (including pressure drop in gage’s tubing or occurring air leaks). Therefore it
cannot be concluded if these differences are due to gravity impact solely
When no distribution media was used in the experiment, no signiﬁcant time lag between the
bottom and top of the laminate was found. It can be therefore concluded that when the same
type plies are stacked ﬂow can be approximated as one dimensional (disregarding the varia-
tions in longitudinal direction of ﬁbers or race tracking effect). Signiﬁcant through thickness
ﬂow was however observed when diffusion media was applied. In particular cases of a thick
laminate with variable number of plies, this can lead to enclosure and trap the air inside the
laminate as a result of second front development (Experiment 5, 4.13).
Moreover, experiments with distribution media showed that it not only allows to saturate an
entire preform but also helps to achieve uniform pressure in the laminate during the post-
ﬁlling phase. After the inlet gate is closed, resin can easily ﬂow in high permeability layer from
regions of high pressure to lower pressure. Therefore, infusion process should be planned such
to provide time before resin gelling for the pressure to equalize. This in return should favor
uniform thickness and weight distribution of the ﬁnal part.
The tests have not delivered results that would clearly explain thickness variations during the
process. At some locations preform tends to expand at the front arrival (spring back effect)
==PAGE:84==
74
CHAPTER 6. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS
while in other it compresses due to lubrication by oil. This is opposite to research of e.g.
Simacek [23] and Yenilmez [20] where these correlations were evident.
The ﬁrst reason for this discrepancy can be measurement method. Laser measurements are
prone to surface ﬁnish which is in-homogeneous when unidirectional ﬁber mat is used, espe-
cially when combined with infusion media on top. In the last experiment that was conducted
(E5), small paper squares were added on top of the preform at the points of measurements and
slight improvement was achieved in smoothness of the signal (Figure 4.14) .
Conditions during the test were also not ideal due to complexity of the measurement system
e.g. number of vacuum connections that generated leaks. It was observed that small leaks and
therefore generated pressure increase cause detectable thickness variations of the laminate
whereas have smaller impact on the front progression (Experiments 6,7,8 Figure 4.16).
Air leakages that occurred in the measurement are deemed to be caused at the sensors side of
the pressure measurement path. Further, not only that connectors through vacuum bag were
sufﬁciently tight but they were also not found to disturb the ﬂow greatly. Even when spaced in
40 mm distance while having 16 mm diameter.
Another reason why thickness traces in most of the measurements were so inconsistent can be
behaviour of unidirectional ﬁber mat itself. In cited papers measurements were performed us-
ing homogeneous random ﬁber mat (CSM). Compaction characteristic of unidirectional ﬁber
mat may present large local variations depending how plies and ﬁber bundles in them are
stacked on top of each other.
If the last argument is the case it might be questionable if it is feasible to perform CFD analysis
if there is so much instability in the process itself and to account for it large number of hardly
measurable parameters would be needed. Then attention could be turned to simpler models
that do not involve numerical solution but utilize e.g. self-similarity of the process in time that
was also shown (Figure 4.8).
In the end it is noted, that measurements in this thesis were done on 10 plies stack with com-
pacted height of around 6 mm. It requires a lot of precision and and experience in measuring
variations in order of 0.1 mm that take place over a period of hours.
Model 1 showed good potential for predicting pressure in the ﬁlling phase if front velocity
could be accurately matched (Figure 5.3a). Thickness variations could be reproduced in some
cases (usually close to the inlet where spring back effect is well pronounced) but in general,
more experimental work is needed to check whether the developed models are sufﬁcient for
that purpose. It is however concluded that compaction characteristic of a laminate in a wet
state is needed and perhaps an individual experiment is needed to collect them. Using mea-
surement points from the infusion test itself was not found satisfactory for that purpose. Model
==PAGE:85==
75
1 is prepared to account for such wet compaction characteristic as it was shown in Chapter 3,
Figure 3.3.
Due to rather inconsistent thickness measurements it was not possible to evaluate fully Model
3 where dynamic equation of motion was used. Having both stiffness and damping term as
a parameters was found to give more ﬂexibility in ﬁtting pressure proﬁle and front position.
Mass matrix was ﬁlled with area density of the preform but its existence in general could be
questionable as accelerations in the process are negligible (for the same reason Navier-Stokes
equation was shown to be simpliﬁed). The model did not perform very well in terms of pres-
sure predictions. It could be because linear spring-damper system is not a a right representa-
tion for the preform which was shown to be rather governed by a power law relation.
In the end, 2D Finite Volume Model 3 was checked and showed good potential for predicting
top and bottom position of the front even though a simpliﬁed way for estimating permeability
was used. Such model could be used to ensure full in depth saturation of the laminate in the
real process.
Summarizing, this thesis contributes to the ﬁeld of understanding physics in vacuum infusion
process which in many aspects is still not well known and challenging to predict and control
leading to costly trail and error methods. Further work should collect stronger experimental
evidence for laminate thickness variations when infusing commonly used in industry fabrics.
In this thesis tests were made on a smaller scale but it is not certain that ideas developed at this
level will transfer to manufacturing processes of structures so large as wind turbine blade.
