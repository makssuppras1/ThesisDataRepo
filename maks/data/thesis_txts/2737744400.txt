Abstract 
This thesis explores the empowerment pathways of lower-income women entrepreneurs in Cape Town, 
South Africa. Through a combination of in-depth interviews with women entrepreneurs and brief 
analysis of support programs, this study identifies key motivations, challenges, and contributions these 
women make to their communities. The research reveals that the entrepreneurial mindset often begins 
in childhood, influenced by parental role models. As these women encounter high unemployment and 
job dissatisfaction, entrepreneurship emerges as a necessity-driven choice. Over time, personal initiative 
and the ability to seize opportunities transform these necessities into viable businesses. The journey is 
further enriched by a desire to address social issues and the ultimate achievement of personal fulfilment 
and empowerment. The study concludes that women entrepreneurs in Cape Town face a range of 
challenges, from social scepticism and gender biases to operational hurdles and the balancing act of 
family commitments. These challenges require both personal resilience and supportive external 
environments to overcome. Furthermore, the research highlights the significant impact of women 
entrepreneurs on their communities and families. Their entrepreneurial activities lead to personal 
empowerment, job creation, skill development, and a positive ripple effect on societal attitudes towards 
women's capabilities and entrepreneurship. The thesis emphasises the need for more accessible co-
working spaces, support networks, diverse representation in educational content, and comprehensive 
mentorship programs to alleviate the barriers faced by these women. While the study provides valuable 
insights, it acknowledges its limitations and calls for future research to delve deeper into systemic issues 
and explore a wider range of solutions. This thesis aims to spark interest and inspire further investigation 
into the empowerment of women entrepreneurs from smaller communities, advocating for continued 
efforts to create inclusive and supportive environments for their success.

Chapter 1: Introduction 
1.1 Background 
In the dynamic and often challenging economic environment of Cape Town, South Africa, women 
entrepreneurs emerge as pivotal agents of societal transformation and economic development. This 
thesis explores the unique role of these women, who operate against a backdrop of pervasive 
unemployment and stark economic disparities, identifying them not merely as business owners but as 
catalysts for social change and community development. 
Historically, entrepreneurship has offered a pathway out of poverty and a platform for economic 
autonomy, especially for those marginalised by the conventional job market. In Cape Town, where the 
effects of systemic unemployment are felt acutely, women entrepreneurs are increasingly recognised 
for their ability to innovate and drive sustainable growth within their communities.  
Despite their potential, these women face multifaceted barriers that stem not only from the socio-
economic conditions of the region but also from persistent gender biases that limit access to necessary 
resources such as funding, training, and support networks. The need to understand these barriers in 
depth is crucial; effective support for women entrepreneurs could lead to significant community and 
economic benefits across South Africa (Radebe & Smith, 2023). 
This research is grounded in qualitative methodologies, utilising in-depth interviews to explore the lived 
experiences, motivations, and aspirations of women entrepreneurs. By focusing on their personal stories 
and the broader implications of their work, this study seeks to offer insights that go beyond individual 
narratives to suggest policy interventions and community initiatives aimed at fostering an inclusive 
economic environment. 
Ultimately, this thesis aims to compile an analysis that not only highlights and explains the challenges 
faced by these women but also showcases the transformative potential of their entrepreneurship 
journeys. In doing so, it contributes to the discourse on gender equality and inclusive economic 
development, advocating for strategies that support women’s entrepreneurship as a cornerstone of 
sustainable growth and societal progress in South Africa. 
1.2 Problem statement 
In Cape Town, South Africa, the role of women entrepreneurs is becoming increasingly crucial in the 
socio-economic fabric of the city (Radebe & Smith, 2023). However, despite their potential to drive 
economic growth and community development, women entrepreneurs face a myriad of challenges that 
significantly hinder their efforts and impact. These challenges are multifaceted and deeply rooted in 
both systemic and cultural contexts (Radebe & Smith, 2023). 
==PAGE:8==
7 
 
Firstly, women entrepreneurs often struggle with access to critical resources such as financing, which 
is a fundamental barrier that affects their ability to start and grow their businesses. Findings indicate 
that women in South Africa have lower rates of access to capital compared to their male counterparts 
(Mastercard, 2022). This disparity is exacerbated by traditional banking systems and investment 
climates that often do not accommodate the unique challenges and needs of women-owned businesses, 
especially in less affluent areas. 
Secondly, educational and training opportunities specifically tailored to support women in 
entrepreneurship are lacking (Radebe & Smith, 2023). While general entrepreneurship programs exist, 
they do not always address the specific needs of women, such as balancing business development with 
familial responsibilities or overcoming specific market entry barriers in male-dominated industries. The 
absence of targeted educational support limits their potential for growth and innovation (Radebe & 
Smith, 2023). 
Additionally, cultural and societal norms present significant obstacles (Radebe & Smith, 2023). In many 
communities within Cape Town, prevailing gender roles can discourage or even stigmatise women's 
full participation in business and economic activities. These cultural expectations can restrict women's 
mobility, their decision-making autonomy, and ultimately, their ability to engage fully and effectively 
in the entrepreneurial ecosystem. 
The cumulative effect of these challenges not only stifles the economic potential of individual women 
entrepreneurs but also limits the broader economic and social development of the region. The 
underutilisation of a significant portion of the workforce in entrepreneurial activities contributes to 
ongoing cycles of poverty and economic disparity. This research seeks to delve into these issues, 
offering a comprehensive examination of the barriers faced by women entrepreneurs in Cape Town and 
exploring effective routes for fostering an environment that supports their success and empowerment.

Chapter 2: Literature review 
2.1 Motivations for women entrepreneurs 
2.1.1 Opportunity vs. Necessity 
Understanding the motivations that drive women to become entrepreneurs may be essential for 
designing effective support and training programs. It is possible for women’s entrepreneurial 
motivations to be broadly classified into necessity-driven and opportunity-driven entrepreneurship. 
Necessity-driven entrepreneurship is said to arise from economic needs, such as unemployment or 
underemployment, which may compel individuals to start businesses as a means of survival as they 
have no other way to make a means. In contrast, opportunity-driven entrepreneurship is mainly 
characterised by individuals who pursue business ventures to exploit market opportunities, gain 
independence, personal fulfilment, or improve their financial gains. 
The study "Entrepreneurship Driven By Opportunity and Necessity: Effects of Education, Gender and 
Occupation in MENA" (2018) examines these motivations within the MENA region, highlighting that 
necessity-driven entrepreneurship often stems from a lack of stable employment options. This research 
indicates that women with lower educational levels and unstable occupational backgrounds are more 
likely to engage in necessity-driven entrepreneurship. The authors also explain the push and the pull 
factors associated with female motivations to start a business. In terms of entrepreneurship, push factors 
are viewed as unfavourable conditions or circumstances that drive individuals to start a business while 
pull factors are conditions or circumstances that attract individuals to start a business (Nasiri & Hamelin, 
2018). Push factors are more commonly associated with necessity-driven entrepreneurship while pull 
factors are associated with opportunity-driven entrepreneurship (Nasiri & Hamelin, 2018). This study 
is relevant as it provides a comparative perspective on how socioeconomic factors influence women’s 
entrepreneurial motivations. It offers a foundation to understand similar dynamics in Cape Town, South 
Africa. 
Conversely, the study "Motivational Factors Influencing Women’s Entrepreneurship: A Case Study of 
Female Entrepreneurship in South Africa" (2015) provides insights into opportunity-driven motivations 
as well. However, it identifies that South African women are predominantly motivated by the desire for 
independence, freedom and filling a social need within their communities (Meyer & Landsberg, 2015). 
Per (Nasiri & Hamelin, 2018), these motivations align with opportunity-driven entrepreneurship, where 
women perceive business ownership as a pathway to achieve personal autonomy. This literature is 
pertinent for this research as it contextualises the multifaceted motivations of women entrepreneurship 
within the South African environment despite the drive of necessity. Furthermore, the authors highlight 
two main factors that influence one’s decision on whether to pursue entrepreneurship: perceived 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
opportunities and perceived capabilities (Meyer & Landsberg, 2015). According to the authors, 
perceived opportunities may relate to economic conditions or identified gaps in the market, but 
perceived capabilities could be an attribute that many females lack. This is because often women doubt 
that they have the required skills and experience to start a business and be successful (Meyer & 
Landsberg, 2015). Another factor the authors mention is fear of failure, and in the case of women, the 
fear is often more associated with fear of rejection from their communities due to potential failure. 
In the study "Are the Intentions to Entrepreneurship of Men and Women Shaped Differently? The Impact 
of Entrepreneurial Role-Model Exposure and Entrepreneurship Education" (2017), the authors 
(Entrialgo & Iglesias, 2017) add another dimension by emphasising the significant influence of role 
models and parental examples on women’s entrepreneurial intentions and early development. The study 
uses Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behaviour to explain how intentions are driven by attitudes toward 
behaviour, perceived behavioural control (PBC), and subjective norms.  
Attitude toward behaviour refers to the individual's positive or negative assessment of performing the 
behaviour. For entrepreneurship, this attitude is influenced by how desirable the individual finds starting 
a business. PBC signifies the perceived ease or difficulty of performing the behaviour. In 
entrepreneurship, this translates to the individual's confidence in their ability to start and manage a 
business. Subjective norms are the perceived social pressures to perform or not perform the behaviour. 
For potential entrepreneurs, it may include support (or lack thereof) from family, friends, and other 
external sources. Furthermore, (Entrialgo & Iglesias, 2017) highlight that parental role models have a 
more favourable influence on women's attitudes toward entrepreneurship compared to men, thus 
highlighting the importance of early exposure to entrepreneurial activities as well as the importance of 
female role models in motivating women’s perceived capabilities for starting entrepreneurship. 
The study "Teaching Personal Initiative Beats Traditional Training in Boosting Small Business in West 
Africa" (2017) connects these types of motivations by exploring how different training approaches can 
influence entrepreneurial activities. This research compares personal initiative training with traditional 
business training in Togo, demonstrating that the former significantly increases business profits and 
sustainability and has double the impact of traditional business training on small businesses (Campos, 
et al., 2017). Personal initiative training encourages a proactive, opportunity-seeking mindset, 
suggesting that such training can help women improve their perceived capabilities and opportunities to 
start entrepreneurship and help improve business practices for existing small businesses (Campos, et 
al., 2017). Notably, the authors’ results provide a middle ground between the “born with an 
entrepreneurial personality” and “made by learning specific entrepreneurial practices” viewpoints by 
showing that training can teach individuals to develop a mindset with entrepreneurial attributes, such 
as proactiveness, that are often assumed to be innate (Campos, et al., 2017). Ultimately the authors’ 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
results suggest the importance of having or developing an entrepreneurial mindset as a significant 
motivator to starting entrepreneurship for women. 
However, considering all the literature in this sub-heading, some gaps need to be addressed. Firstly, 
while existing studies highlight the importance of personal initiative and proactive behaviour, there is 
limited research on how these traits can be systematically developed among women entrepreneurs in 
different socio-economic contexts. The focus has largely been on West Africa, with less emphasis on 
other regions such as South Africa furthermore lower-income communities in Cape Town. Additionally, 
the interaction between necessity and opportunity motivations remains underexplored, particularly how 
one type of motivation can evolve into the other over time. Similarly, how necessity-driven 
entrepreneurship may have a more multifaceted nature by not only consisting of push factors as the 
primary motivation.  
This thesis aims to address these gaps by focusing on the experiences of women entrepreneurs in Cape 
Town, South Africa. By examining their motivations within this specific context, this thesis will explain 
how local socio-economic conditions influence entrepreneurial motivations. Furthermore, to explore 
how entrepreneurship education programs could be enhanced to encourage an entrepreneurial mindset 
to support women’s motivations to start their businesses. 
In summary, the motivations for women entrepreneurs are multifaceted, involving both necessity and 
opportunity-driven factors. Understanding these motivations is crucial for developing effective support 
systems. Programs that combine immediate support for necessity-driven entrepreneurs with long-term 
strategies that foster opportunity-driven motivations can significantly enhance the sustainability and 
impact of women-led businesses. Enhanced training modules focusing on personal initiative and 
proactive behaviours are particularly effective in facilitating this transition, ultimately contributing to 
broader economic empowerment and community development through enhanced motivations. This 
literature review sets the stage for further exploration in the findings and discussion chapters, where 
these themes will be examined in the context of women entrepreneurs in Cape Town, South Africa. 
2.2 Challenges and barriers of women entrepreneurs 
2.2.1 Internal vs external 
Identifying and understanding the challenges and barriers that women entrepreneurs face in South 
Africa is crucial for several reasons. First, it is well-known that entrepreneurship is a significant driver 
of economic growth and job creation. By uncovering the challenges and barriers that women 
entrepreneurs face, we can enhance their contribution to the economy and promote inclusive growth. 
Second, women entrepreneurs often bring unique perspectives and solutions to the marketplace, 
fostering diversity and resilience in the business environment. Lastly, addressing these challenges is 
essential for achieving gender equality and empowering women, thereby improving overall societal 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
well-being. Understanding the specific obstacles faced by women entrepreneurs allows policymakers 
and support organizations to develop targeted interventions that can help women overcome these 
barriers and succeed in their entrepreneurial endeavours. 
The literature on women entrepreneurs reveals a multitude of challenges and barriers women may face, 
which can be categorised into internal and external factors according to the literature. Understanding 
these distinctions is important for developing effective policies and interventions to support women 
entrepreneurs. This section synthesises insights from three similar studies. 
Internal challenges are challenges within the control of the entrepreneur, influenced by personal 
development, skills acquisition, and psychological factors (Panda, 2018). A prominent internal 
challenge is the lack of self-confidence and risk aversion. Many women entrepreneurs suffer from low 
self-esteem and imposter syndrome, where they doubt their capabilities and feel unworthy of their 
successes (Panda, 2018). This lack of confidence often leads to risk-averse behaviour, limiting the 
growth potential of their businesses (Panda, 2018). Additionally, insufficient education and training 
pose significant internal challenges. Many women entrepreneurs lack formal education and business 
training, which affects their ability to manage financial resources, market their products, and leverage 
technology effectively (Panda, 2018) (Chinomona & Maziriri, 2015). Without adequate training, 
women are less equipped to handle the complexities of running a business (Panda, 2018) (Chinomona 
& Maziriri, 2015) (Etim & Iwu, 2019). 
Work-family conflict is another critical internal challenge. Balancing entrepreneurial activities with 
family responsibilities is particularly strenuous for women, who often face societal expectations to 
prioritise their roles as mothers and caregivers. This conflict leads to stress and burnout, reducing their 
effectiveness as entrepreneurs (Panda, 2018). This struggle is compounded by subjective norms and 
traditional gender roles that further burden women with domestic responsibilities, making it difficult 
for them to fully commit to their entrepreneurial ventures. 
External challenges, on the other hand, are those outside the control of the entrepreneur, influenced by 
societal, economic, and political environments. Gender discrimination and societal norms significantly 
hinder women entrepreneurs. They often face biases and discrimination that manifest in various forms, 
such as difficulty accessing finance, limited support from societal networks, and stereotypes that 
undermine their credibility. In many cultures, patriarchal norms further restrict women's ability to 
engage in entrepreneurial activities, making it harder for them to break into and succeed in business 
(Panda, 2018) (Chinomona & Maziriri, 2015). 
Access to finance remains one of the most significant external barriers according to the three literatures 
mentioned (Panda, 2018) (Chinomona & Maziriri, 2015) (Etim & Iwu, 2019). Women entrepreneurs 
frequently struggle to obtain loans and investments due to gender biases within financial institutions, 
lack of collateral, and limited credit histories. This financial exclusion severely hampers their ability to 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
start and expand businesses. As (Panda, 2018) points out, financial constraints are exacerbated by 
systemic biases and the overall economic environment, which is often not conducive to supporting 
women-owned enterprises. 
The lack of infrastructural support and market access further exacerbates the challenges faced by 
women entrepreneurs (Etim & Iwu, 2019). Limited access to essential business infrastructure, including 
technology, market information, and supportive services, significantly affects their business operations 
(Etim & Iwu, 2019). Additionally, the scarcity of networking opportunities and consulting services 
hampers their ability to grow and reach new markets (Chinomona & Maziriri, 2015). Unfavourable 
political and economic environments also create substantial external challenges. Political instability, 
economic recessions, and complex regulatory frameworks pose significant challenges for women 
entrepreneurs. High tax rates and bureaucratic hurdles make it difficult for them to establish and sustain 
their businesses (Panda, 2018). 
A crucial aspect highlighted in the literature is the distinction between challenges and barriers. 
Challenges, while difficult, may have workarounds or solutions that can be implemented by the 
entrepreneur with the right resources and support (Chinomona & Maziriri, 2015). For example, self-
confidence issues can be addressed through mentorship and training programs, and work-family conflict 
can be managed through flexible work arrangements and supportive family policies. On the other hand, 
barriers are more rigid and prevent progression, such as systemic gender discrimination and lack of 
access to finance, which require broader societal and policy changes to overcome (Chinomona & 
Maziriri, 2015) (Panda, 2018). 
The interrelation between these challenges and barriers is also significant. For instance, the lack of 
access to finance, an external barrier, can exacerbate internal challenges such as low self-confidence 
and limited business skills. Addressing these barriers requires a holistic approach that includes policy 
interventions to improve access to finance, educational programs to enhance business skills, and societal 
changes to reduce gender biases (Chinomona & Maziriri, 2015) (Panda, 2018). Efforts to support 
women entrepreneurs should focus on both mitigating external barriers and empowering women to 
overcome internal challenges. This dual approach can create a more conducive environment for women 
entrepreneurs to thrive and contribute to economic development. 
Despite the comprehensive analysis provided by the literature used in this section, there are several gaps 
and limitations that need to be addressed. First, much of the research focuses on specific geographic 
regions, limiting the generalisability of the findings to other contexts. Second, there is a need for more 
longitudinal studies that track the progress and challenges of women entrepreneurs over time. Third, 
the current literature often lacks in-depth qualitative insights that can provide a more nuanced 
understanding of the personal and cultural dimensions of the challenges faced. Finally, there is limited 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
research on the effectiveness of various interventions designed to support women entrepreneurs, making 
it difficult to identify best practices and scalable solutions. 
In summary, women entrepreneurs face a complex array of internal and external challenges and barriers 
that hinder their business activities. Addressing these challenges and barriers requires a strategy that 
includes improving access to resources, providing targeted training and education, and fostering an 
inclusive societal and economic environment. By understanding and addressing both internal and 
external barriers, policymakers and support organizations can better support the growth and success of 
women entrepreneurs.  
2.3 Impact of women entrepreneurs on community and family 
2.3.1 Economic and social contributions 
The impact of women entrepreneurs on both the economic and social spheres of their communities is 
profound and multifaceted. This section delves into the specific contributions of women entrepreneurs, 
highlighting their influence on economic development, community well-being, and family support. 
According to the study “The Impact of Women Entrepreneurs on National Economic Development in 
South Africa” (2021), women entrepreneurs in South Africa significantly contribute to the national 
economy through the production of goods and services, employment generation, and tax contributions. 
Their engagement in small and medium-scale enterprises (SMEs) not only meets the demand for local 
goods but also propels economic growth by reducing unemployment and increasing GDP. This 
underscores the crucial role women entrepreneurs play in driving economic progress, particularly in 
developing regions (Ajani, Gamede, & Mkhasibe, 2021). 
The study "Developing Women Leaders through Entrepreneurship Education and Training" (2015) 
emphasises the transformative power of entrepreneurship education in equipping women with the skills 
needed to innovate and thrive in various business sectors. By fostering leadership and entrepreneurial 
capabilities, such educational initiatives enable women to launch and sustain successful businesses that

in enabling women to overcome barriers and expand their businesses, thereby fostering regional 
economic growth. This similar study illustrates how a well-supported entrepreneurial ecosystem can

Women entrepreneurs also play a pivotal role in social development. Their businesses often provide

2.5 Effectiveness of entrepreneurship education programs 
2.5.1 Evaluating program effectiveness 
The evaluation of entrepreneurship education programs is critical in understanding their impact on 
aspiring entrepreneurs, especially women. This section delves into various studies that assess the 
effectiveness of these programs, highlighting key findings and identifying gaps. 
The study, “Entrepreneurship education and training: can entrepreneurship be taught?” (2005) 
discusses a comprehensive overview of entrepreneurship education and training, questioning whether 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
entrepreneurship can be taught and how its effectiveness can be measured. (Henry & Leitch, 2005) 
argue that despite the proliferation of entrepreneurship programs, there is little uniformity in evaluation 
methods. This inconsistency complicates the assessment of whether these programs achieve their 
intended outcomes. The authors emphasise the importance of clearly defined objectives and measurable 
outcomes from the outset to ensure accurate evaluation. They also note the necessity for longitudinal 
studies to track the lasting effects of entrepreneurship education. 
Referring to the study by (Campos, et al., 2017) in which a randomised controlled trial was conducted 
in Togo to compare traditional business training with a psychology-based personal initiative training 
program. Their findings revealed that personal initiative training, which focuses on fostering a proactive 
mindset and entrepreneurial behaviours, significantly outperformed traditional business training in 
terms of firm profits and sustainability. This study is particularly relevant as it demonstrates that 
teaching a proactive entrepreneurial mindset can lead to substantial business improvements. These 
findings support the notion that alternative approaches to traditional business training can be more 
effective, especially for women who might face additional internal barriers that such proactive training 
could help overcome. 
The authors (Henry & Leitch, 2005) also discuss the art and science of entrepreneurship education, 
highlighting the need for both objective and subjective measures of program effectiveness. They suggest 
that evaluations should consider not only immediate outcomes, such as knowledge gain and business 
startup but also longer-term impacts like business growth, sustainability, and personal development of 
the entrepreneurs. This comprehensive approach to evaluation is crucial for understanding the 
effectiveness of entrepreneurship programs, especially in diverse contexts like those faced by women 
entrepreneurs in Cape Town. 
Furthermore (Henry & Leitch, 2005) elaborate on the evaluation of entrepreneurship programs, 
advocating for the use of control groups and longitudinal tracking to accurately assess program impacts. 
They highlight that many evaluations fail to consider the personal characteristics of participants and the 
voluntary nature of course enrolment, which can skew results. In addition, the study by (Campos, et al., 
2017) regarding non-traditional training approaches showed that personal initiative training led to 
significant increases in business profitability and growth compared to traditional training. This insight 
is particularly relevant for this thesis as it underscores the potential benefits of innovative training 
methods in empowering women entrepreneurs, who may benefit more from training that enhances their 
proactive and problem-solving capabilities or personal initiative. 
In summary, the literature in this section reveals significant gaps in the evaluation of entrepreneurship 
education programs, particularly regarding long-term impacts and the effectiveness of different training 
methodologies. This thesis aims to address these gaps by providing deeper insights into the sought-after 
support that women entrepreneurs need in their entrepreneurial endeavours.

Both studies underscore the importance of making entrepreneurship education programs accessible and

Chapter 3: Methodology 
3.1 Research design and methods 
The methodology of this study is designed to provide a deep understanding of the experiences and 
impacts of women entrepreneurs in Cape Town, South Africa. Employing a qualitative research design, 
this study prioritises the richness of personal experiences and contextual depth over statistical 
generalisability. The choice of qualitative methods is driven by the need to capture complex individual 
narratives and the nuanced socio-economic environments in which these entrepreneurs operate. 
3.1.1 Data collection methods 
The primary data collection method in this study involves in-depth, semi-structured interviews with 
women entrepreneurs from smaller communities. This technique is selected for its flexibility, allowing 
for the exploration of topics in depth and the emergence of new themes during the interview process. 
Each interview is structured around a guide that includes a series of open-ended questions designed to 
elicit detailed responses about the participants' entrepreneurial journey, challenges, motivations, and the 
impacts of their business activities on their communities. 
Interviews are conducted in settings where participants feel most comfortable, whether in their homes, 
at their places of business, or online via platforms like Zoom or Google Meet, depending on their 
preference. This flexibility ensures that participants are at ease, thereby facilitating more open and 
honest dialogue. 
3.1.2 Sampling strategy 
The study uses purposive sampling to select participants who meet specific criteria that align with the 
research objectives. Participants are women who have started and are actively managing their own 
businesses in Cape Town. They are selected to represent a variety of industries, business sizes, and 
stages of business development, to encapsulate a broad range of experiences and perspectives. 
Additional criteria for participant selection include: 
- 
The business must be at least one year old, ensuring that the entrepreneur has some experience 
navigating the initial challenges of business operation. 
 
- 
Women entrepreneurs from smaller communities who started their businesses out of necessity. 
3.1.3 Data Analysis 
Data analysis in this study is conducted manually to maintain a close connection with the data collected 
through interviews. After each interview, transcripts are created verbatim to ensure all details and 
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
nuances of the conversations are captured accurately. The thematic analysis process is deeply 
immersive, relying heavily on the researcher's engagement with both the interview environment and the 
transcription text. 
Themes are identified directly by the researcher. This approach allows for an organic development of 
themes based on the researcher's insights gained during the interviews and while reviewing the 
transcripts. This method ensures a highly personalised and reflective analysis, which is crucial for 
capturing the depth and complexity of the experiences shared by the participants. 
To aid in organising and connecting these themes, diagrams are used. These visual representations help 
in mapping out the relationships between different themes and in visualising the overall narrative 
structure of the data. This method not only aids in the analysis but also in the presentation of findings, 
allowing for a clearer depiction of how individual experiences interlink and contribute to the broader 
research questions. 
3.1.4 Ethical Considerations 
The ethical approach in this study is founded on principles of transparency, respect, and sensitivity 
towards the participants. From the outset, the study prioritises creating a comfortable and safe 
environment for all interviewees, ensuring that they feel respected and valued throughout the research 
process. 
Before each interview, participants are fully informed about the purpose of the study, the nature of their 
involvement, and the use of the information they provide. They are explicitly told that they have the 
right to withdraw from the study at any time without any consequence. This assurance aims to empower 
participants, giving them control over their participation and the information they choose to share. 
Informed consent is obtained from all participants, which includes permission to record the interviews. 
This process is handled with utmost care to maintain the integrity and confidentiality of the data 
collected. Additionally, participants are reassured that they have the complete freedom to refrain from 
answering any questions that they might find too personal or uncomfortable. This approach ensures that 
participation in the study is based on their continuous consent and comfort level, aligning with ethical 
research practices that prioritise participant welfare. 
All information collected during the study is treated with strict confidentiality. Identifiable information 
is anonymised in the study's outputs to protect the privacy of the participants and to maintain the trust 
placed in the research process.

3.3 Limitations of the Study 
While this study aims to provide valuable insights into the experiences and impacts of women 
entrepreneurs in Cape Town, there are several limitations that need to be acknowledged. 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
Sample size and diversity 
The study involves a relatively small number of participants due to the qualitative nature of the research. 
While in-depth interviews with a small group can provide rich and detailed data, the findings may not 
be generalisable to all women entrepreneurs in Cape Town or other regions. Additionally, while efforts 
were made to include participants from diverse backgrounds and industries, the sample may still not 
fully capture the wide range of experiences and perspectives present in the broader population. 
Self-reporting bias 
The data collected in this study relies heavily on self-reported information from the participants. This 
can introduce biases, as participants may present their experiences in a way that is influenced by 
personal perceptions, memory, and the desire to present themselves in a positive light. This can affect 
the accuracy and reliability of the information provided. 
Lack of quantitative data 
This study uses qualitative methods exclusively, focusing on the depth and richness of personal 
narratives. While this approach provides valuable insights, it does not allow for the statistical analysis 
or quantification of the findings. Consequently, the study may lack the ability to provide broader 
statistical trends or correlations that quantitative data could offer. 
Context-specific findings 
The findings of this study are context-specific to Cape Town, South Africa. The socio-economic, 
cultural, and political environment of Cape Town significantly influences the experiences of women 
entrepreneurs. Therefore, the findings may not be directly applicable or transferable to other regions or 
countries with different contexts. 
Researcher subjectivity 
As the researcher is deeply involved in data collection and analysis, there is a potential for subjective 
bias. Although every effort has been made to remain objective and systematic in the analysis, the 
researcher's interpretations and perspectives can influence the identification and presentation of themes. 
Temporal limitations 
The study captures a snapshot in time of the experiences of women entrepreneurs. However, the 
entrepreneurial landscape is dynamic, and factors influencing women entrepreneurs can change over 
time. The findings may not fully reflect future changes or trends in the entrepreneurial environment in 
Cape Town.

Despite these limitations, the study provides meaningful and in-depth insights into the lives of women 
entrepreneurs in Cape Town. Acknowledging these limitations helps to contextualise the findings and 
suggests areas for further research to build upon this work.

4.1 Motivational journey to entrepreneurship 
4.1.1 Parental example and entrepreneurial mindset 
The motivation for entrepreneurship among women in Cape Town is significantly influenced by their 
familial environments, particularly the entrepreneurial activities of parents. This formative exposure not 
only instils essential business skills but also shapes long-term motivations and aspirations. 
WE1 recalls her mother’s ventures into selling various goods as a means of survival, emphasising an 
early and intimate exposure to entrepreneurship. She states: 
“...working for myself is something I think I took from my mom. My mom was not employed for a long 
time and she wanted to try to make means and sell shoes, clothes, bags [...] When I was younger, I didn't 
know that it was something that I'm going to end up doing [too].” 
This reflection illustrates the subconscious absorption of entrepreneurial values. Her mother’s 
endeavours, born out of necessity, not only provided immediate financial support but also served as a 
living classroom for WE1. The realisation that these activities formed the bedrock of her own 
entrepreneurial motivations highlights the deep-seated impact of parental modelling. The linkage 
between her mother's adaptation to economic challenges and WE1's own entrepreneurial journey 
underscores a learned resilience and proactive approach to creating opportunities, which is a critical 
motivational factor for her entrepreneurship. 
WE2 provides a compelling narrative about growing up in a household where street vending was the 
primary income source. She describes: 
“I was born from a street seller [...] our home was just full of entrepreneurship lessons, even though it 
wasn't formal, like going to school and learning some theory and practice, but it was yet really practical, 
because that was the mean of our life, we had no source of income or no source of life, except through 
selling in the street.” 
This scenario points to the ingrained nature of entrepreneurship learned through direct engagement and 
observation. The practical application of these skills, witnessed daily, fostered an entrepreneurial 
mindset that was both necessity-driven and opportunity-oriented. This direct involvement not only 
==PAGE:28==
27 
 
equipped WE2 with essential skills but also instilled a perception of entrepreneurship as a viable and 
primary livelihood strategy. 
The positive association her parents made with entrepreneurship profoundly affected WE2's outlook. 
By observing her parents approach their entrepreneurial activities with resilience and creativity, she 
grew to see entrepreneurship not just as a means of survival but as a commendable and empowering 
pursuit. This positive symbolism attached to entrepreneurship by her parents enabled WE2 to view 
business ownership as a desirable and achievable goal. 
WE3 reflects on the influence of both her mother and brother, suggesting a broader familial impact on 
her entrepreneurial spirit. She notes: 
“...having seen my mother and my brother, I think I also had it in me, but maybe they kept that light

doing their business and how they love business. So, I can say the two of them, yeah, they had an impact 
on my entrepreneurial journey.”  
This testimony underscores the role of continuous exposure and emotional support in nurturing 
entrepreneurial interests. The 'light' metaphor illustrates the ongoing inspiration derived from observing 
loved ones who not only operate businesses but also exhibit passion for their endeavours. This 
emotional connection to entrepreneurship, reinforced by familial role models, significantly contributes 
to the formation of a business identity and the drive to pursue similar paths. 
Furthermore, WE3 reflects not only on the external influences of her family but also on her inherent

“...I think the entrepreneurial thing, one is born with it. It is not something that you pick up along the

WE3’s belief that she was naturally predisposed to entrepreneurship highlights an intrinsic motivation

a powerful synergy that propelled her towards her own business ventures. 
 
In essence, these narratives collectively reveal how entrepreneurial behaviours observed within the

4.1.2 Unemployment and work dissatisfaction 
Unemployment and work dissatisfaction are significant drivers that can be seen to compel women in

autonomy, a complex interplay of both push and pull factors. 
 
WE1 reflects a direct response to the high unemployment rates in South Africa, a classic push factor,

“In South Africa, there's a high rate of unemployment. People are unemployed, people are looking for

This decision underscores a proactive shift from passive job seeking to active creation of a livelihood,

motivational driver here extends beyond mere survival; it encapsulates a desire for autonomy and self-
determination, characteristics that lean towards opportunity-driven entrepreneurship within the 
framework of necessity. 
 
WE2’s narrative reveals a disillusionment with traditional academic paths, emphasising a misfit

“I was disqualified academically because that's the only thing that society always imposes on ourselves.

4.1.3 Personal initiative (opportunities and skill development) 
Personal initiative encapsulates the ability to self-start, proactively solve problems, and capitalise on 
opportunities. This trait is particularly vital in entrepreneurship, as it directly influences the perception 
and exploitation of opportunities, as well as the continuous development of relevant skills. The stories 
from these women entrepreneurs exemplify how personal initiative acts as both a learned and innate 
skill, cultivated through personal experiences and the necessity to adapt and innovate. 
 
W1's narrative is a prime example of recognizing and acting on an immediate opportunity, indicative 
of high personal initiative. She explains: 
 
“So since I was so passionate about it, I was like, I'm not going to give up. [...] I'm going to continue 
doing this... [...] every time I went to buy the cereals, I would pass some robots [traffic lights] [...] and 
[I thought] okay there's an opportunity and there's always traffic in the morning."  
 
W1’s ability to pivot her business strategy from selling breakfast cereals to leveraging high-traffic areas 
for sales demonstrates her adeptness at both identifying market gaps and adapting her business to better 
suit environmental conditions. This skill likely stems from both her intrinsic motivation and her 
experiences in trying multiple business ventures to sell food, showcasing how entrepreneurs often 
develop their capabilities through trial, error, and persistence. 
 
WE5 provides a poignant example of how adversity can lead to the honing of entrepreneurial skills:  
 
“[...] I got back on my feet and told myself I have to do something with this mountain as there is no 
path over or around [it].” 
 
Starting a business with what she had (a collection of craft supplies) illustrates her initiative to use 
available resources creatively. Her story reflects a significant aspect of Ajzen’s theory, which 
emphasises perceived behavioural control as a critical factor influencing behaviour. WE5’s belief in her 
ability to manipulate her resources effectively shows high perceived behavioural control, which Ajzen 
suggests will probably increase the likelihood of engaging in the behaviour. Which in this case is 
entrepreneurship.

4.2 Challenges in entrepreneurship 
4.2.1 Scepticism 
Scepticism towards women entrepreneurs’ manifests in various forms and significantly impacts their 
business operations. This scepticism, categorised into public-based, family-based, and community-
based types, often originates from external societal norms and gender biases. These external forces 
profoundly affect the entrepreneurs' Perceived Behavioural Control (PBC), a key component of Ajzen's 
Theory, which influences their confidence in successfully performing entrepreneurial behaviours. 
 
WE1, WE2 & WE3 mentioned how they faced public-based scepticism when establishing their 
businesses. WE1 stated:  
 
“Look, [...] people were laughing at me for standing at the robots [traffic lights] and selling 
sandwiches. They were laughing and saying how and why are you doing this? Why are you not at 
school? [...]. Sometimes, I won't lie, It would break my heart. I would cry. But at the end of the day [...] 
our stories are different. Some people are meant for this and some are meant for that.”  
 
This type of scepticism challenged her PBC by questioning her ability to succeed in a non-traditional 
environment, highlighting the need for strong self-belief and resilience to overcome public doubts. 
 
WE3 additionally mentioned dealing with family-based scepticism, especially from her husband, who 
doubts the viability of her business ventures:  
==PAGE:38==
37 
 
 
“People were very sceptical. Especially my husband, he is the one who would always tell me that, ‘Oh 
no it’s not going to work.’ but, because an entrepreneurial spirit is in me, I always push and start 
something.” 
This scepticism influences her internal assessment of her ability to execute and succeed, underscoring 
the importance of developing personal skills such as emotional resilience and self-efficacy to counteract 
the negative impacts of close relational doubts. Furthermore, WE3 advised to not take advice from 
people who do not possess an entrepreneurial mindset. 
 
WE5 encounters community-based scepticism in her small town, where entrenched views on women's 
roles and capabilities affect her business activities:  
 
“Many people in our town were doubting me as I didn't have a partner and in a small town like ours 
the women have this concept that a man makes you stronger or enables you to do more things with their 
help [...] they were doubting me because I wasn't rich and they thought it will not be possible to create 
and develop a sustainable business in such a small town that was basically just busy over holidays.”  
 
Her ability to persevere despite these community-based doubts demonstrates the critical role that self-
belief and resilience play in navigating and overcoming scepticism. 
 
The literature suggests that developing a strong sense of self-belief and resilience is crucial for women 
entrepreneurs, particularly in contexts like South Africa where societal norms can significantly impact 
entrepreneurial activities. These skills are not just useful for resisting scepticism but are essential for 
fostering a sense of control and empowerment that can drive business success. Enhancing these personal 
skills can help women entrepreneurs maintain their resolve and continue pushing forward, even when 
external forces seem overwhelmingly negative. 
4.2.2 Gender, societal and racial biases 
Gender, societal, and racial biases present formidable barriers for women entrepreneurs, especially in 
smaller communities in South Africa where traditional norms and economic constraints are deeply 
entrenched. These biases affect their access to financing, credibility in professional interactions, market 
engagement, and personal well-being, profoundly influencing their business operations and growth. 
 
WE1's experiences highlight the intersection of gender expectations and societal scepticism, where her 
entrepreneurial efforts are sexualized and trivialised, severely undermining her professional 
endeavours. She recounts:

WE3 and WE4 further illustrate the impact of gender biases on their entrepreneurial journeys. WE3

4.2.3 Family commitments 
Family commitments impose a significant challenge for women entrepreneurs, particularly in 
environments where traditional gender roles are rigorously upheld, and economic resources are

new designs, you have to go and sell if there's an international event, we have to go and display. [...] it

have all the time, you know, to design, to create, to produce and all of that.”

These narratives highlight the critical need for recognizing and addressing the unique challenges faced

4.2.4 Business-related challenges 
Business-related challenges are a significant barrier for women entrepreneurs, impacting their 
operations and sustainability. These challenges can stem from a combination of individual 
circumstances, market conditions, and structural inadequacies, which can be particularly pronounced 
for women from disadvantaged backgrounds or those operating in resource-limited environments. 
 
WE1 describes the emotional and physical exhaustion associated with her food business, which is a 
direct consequence of the high demands of food preparation and sales:

programs, to help mitigate these challenges.

The challenges these women face are not only traditional business challenges but are often intensified

4.4 Barriers to success 
4.4.1 Fear of failure and self-doubt 
Fear of failure and self-doubt are critical psychological barriers that not only influence women 
entrepreneurs' willingness to start a business but also impact their sustained engagement and success in 
entrepreneurial activities. According to Ajzen's Theory, PBC (an individual's perception of the ease or 
difficulty of performing the behaviour in question) plays a crucial role in determining whether they will 
==PAGE:50==
49 
 
engage in that behaviour. For entrepreneurs, this translates to their perceived capability to start and run 
a business successfully and the opportunities they believe are available to them. 
 
WE2 underscores the importance of resilience, a key component in strengthening PBC, as entrepreneurs 
must believe in their ability to overcome challenges and persist through setbacks: 
“It [what prevents people] is that uncertainty because they don't have that resilience. That's why I 
mentioned resilience at the beginning. If you want to become an entrepreneur, the number one thing 
you need to build is resilience, dedication, sacrifice, vision, innovation. All this you need to carry with 
you at the beginning. So, many people fear.” 
 
This resilience not only enhances perceived capabilities but also emboldens entrepreneurs to seize or 
create opportunities, essential underpinnings of entrepreneurial success. 
 
WE3 highlights how external feedback can significantly impact self-doubt, which in turn affects PBC: 
 
“...self doubt is also another downfall for us as women. Don’t ever doubt yourself. Don’t listen to other 
people because now most of the time, the people that you are sharing your ideas with are employees. 
People who are looking for jobs are not entrepreneurs. An entrepreneur, even if they are not selling 
anything, you speak to them and they say, "Oh wow, that is a great idea go for it!”" 
 
This reflects how perceptions formed by external influences can sway an entrepreneur's self-assessment 
of their capabilities and opportunities, impacting their entrepreneurial drive and actions. 
 
WE4’s comment on the fear of financial failure sheds light on how perceived risks can deter potential 
entrepreneurs: 
 
“...I do believe women are scared to start a business. The reason being financial failure. In terms of 
finance it’s a win or lose situation. Some don’t want to take a chance and they didn’t do research. In 
terms of failure, they are scared of being laughed at and being mocked at their downfall. Also, support 
from family and friends [whether they will get it or not].” 
 
Such fears directly influence PBC by making the task of launching and maintaining a business seem 
more daunting and risky. Additionally, channelling the influence of subjective norms from family which 
influence PBC and their attitude towards starting a business.

Finally, WE5 illustrates personal growth overcoming internal barriers, which is indicative of an increase 
in PBC: 
 
“Some of the biggest obstacles I've had to overcome was my shyness and I was very afraid to speak

“no” without hesitation. This is what I want or that's how I want it to be done.” 
 
This growth not only reflects increased self-confidence but also demonstrates enhanced perceived

In conclusion, addressing fear of failure and self-doubt is crucial for women entrepreneurs to enhance

4.4.2 Lack of representation 
Lack of representation is a significant barrier for women entrepreneurs, particularly those from minority

components of Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behaviour.

This narrative illustrates how the absence of diverse entrepreneurial models in formal education can 
diminish individuals' Perceived Behavioural Control, making them feel less capable and less likely to 
pursue entrepreneurial endeavours.

The lack of representation affects not only perceived capabilities but also attitudes towards

Efforts to address this barrier must focus on broadening the definition of entrepreneurship and

entrepreneurial activities and including success stories from various demographics, industries, and

In conclusion, enhancing representation is crucial for fostering a diverse and vibrant entrepreneurial 
community. Recognizing and supporting various forms of entrepreneurship can inspire more women to 
view themselves as potential entrepreneurs, thereby boosting their confidence, enhancing their 
perceived control over business outcomes, and ultimately leading to more dynamic and inclusive 
economic growth.

in Cape Town, contextualising these insights within the broader literature on women’s entrepreneurship. 
The key themes identified include motivations for entrepreneurship, socio-cultural and gender barriers, 
access to financial resources, the role of support systems, and the impact on economic development. 
This discussion aims to interpret these findings, highlight their significance, and offer recommendations 
for policy and practice.

and multifaceted process, rooted in a blend of early influences, socio-economic challenges, and personal 
aspirations. The motivations driving these women to entrepreneurship can be understood as a

entrepreneurial mindset. These early experiences align with Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behaviour,

As these women transitioned into adulthood, the harsh realities of the job market in South Africa became

entrepreneurship is a common theme in the literature, particularly in contexts with high economic 
instability. The lack of stable employment options forced these women to leverage their inherent skills 
and entrepreneurial inclinations to create their own livelihoods. 
 
Once they embarked on their entrepreneurial journeys, personal initiative and the ability to identify and 
exploit opportunities became critical. WE1's narrative about adapting her business strategy to suit high-

necessity-driven and opportunity-driven entrepreneurship.

significant motivator. Understanding the pervasive impact of high unemployment on society, they 
sought to create job opportunities and support their communities. WE1's commitment to creating jobs 
and WE2's efforts to empower less qualified individuals illustrate how their entrepreneurial activities 
extended beyond personal gain to encompass social contributions. This aligns with (Ajani, Gamede, & 
Mkhasibe, 2021), who emphasise the broader economic and social impact of women’s 
entrepreneurship. 
 
Ultimately, the entrepreneurial journey for these women culminates in personal fulfilment and

empowerment. 
 
In conclusion, the motivations driving women to entrepreneurship in Cape Town can be understood as

5.3 Research Question 2: What are the common challenges faced by 
women entrepreneurs in Cape Town, why do they occur, and how might 
they be addressed? 
 
The challenges faced by women entrepreneurs in Cape Town can be visualized as a funnel, where the 
broadest segment represents the initial scepticism they encounter, narrowing down through gender, 
societal, and racial barriers, and further tightening with business-related challenges, ultimately 
culminating in the balancing act of family commitments. This funnel model helps illustrate how these 
challenges shift from external to internal as women progress in their entrepreneurial journeys and 
provides insight into why these challenges occur. 
 
==PAGE:68==
67 
 
 
Figure 2: Women Entrepreneurship Challenge Funnel 
At the widest part of the funnel, scepticism is the first and most pervasive challenge that women face 
when they consider starting a business. This scepticism, often rooted in societal norms and gender 
biases, manifests as doubts about their capabilities and the viability of their business ideas. WE1, WE2, 
and WE3 recount experiences where they were laughed at, dismissed, or discouraged by both strangers 
and close family members. This scepticism is a significant hurdle during the idea phase of 
entrepreneurship and is primarily an external challenge, requiring internal skills such as resilience and 
strong personal initiative to combat. According to Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behaviour, overcoming 
this scepticism requires a strong PBC: the belief in one’s ability to perform the behaviour in question 
(Entrialgo & Iglesias, 2017). Women need to develop resilience and self-efficacy to counteract the 
negative feedback and scepticism they encounter. Encouragement from mentors and positive role 
models, as well as access to supportive networks, can help bolster their PBC, enabling them to persist 
despite initial doubts and criticisms. This scepticism occurs because of entrenched societal norms that 
undervalue women's entrepreneurial potential and perpetuate stereotypes about gender roles (Panda, 
2018). 
 
As women move beyond the idea phase and begin to establish their businesses, they encounter the 
second largest segment of the funnel: gender, societal, and racial barriers. These barriers are deeply 
ingrained in the fabric of society and significantly impact the early stages of business development. 
Women like WE1 and WE3 face gender biases that question their credibility and capabilities as business 
owners. Additionally, racial stereotypes, as highlighted by WE3, further compound these challenges, 
especially for black women who must navigate a landscape of preconceived notions about their work 
ethic and competence (Chinomona & Maziriri, 2015). These barriers are external but require a strong 
internal personal initiative to overcome. They occur due to long-standing patriarchal structures and 
==PAGE:69==
68 
 
systemic racism that marginalize women and people of colour (Panda, 2018). Addressing these 
challenges requires comprehensive societal change, including policies that promote gender and racial 
equity, education programs that raise awareness about these issues, and support systems that specifically 
cater to the needs of women entrepreneurs from diverse backgrounds (Birdthistle, Eversole, & Walo, 
2022). Creating inclusive business environments and advocating for equal opportunities are crucial 
steps towards mitigating these barriers. 
 
Narrowing further in the funnel, once women have managed to get their businesses off the ground, they 
face more nuanced business-related challenges. WE1, WE3, and WE4 describe struggles with 
operational inefficiencies, administrative burdens, and competitive disadvantages. For instance, WE1 
talks about the physical and emotional exhaustion from running a food business, while WE3 highlights 
the tedious nature of administrative tasks that detract from productive time. These challenges are 
internal as they directly affect the entrepreneur's day-to-day operations and well-being. However, 
external initiatives are needed to help combat these challenges. These business-related challenges occur 
because of limited access to resources, inadequate infrastructure, and insufficient training in business 
management (Panda, 2018). Solutions include providing targeted business training programs, 
improving access to financial resources, and creating supportive infrastructures such as affordable co-
working spaces and business hubs (Campos, et al., 2017). Government and community interventions 
play a crucial role in offering these resources and creating an ecosystem where women entrepreneurs 
can thrive. 
 
At the narrowest end of the funnel, the balancing act of family commitments presents a persistent 
challenge for women entrepreneurs. WE3 and WE4 discuss how managing business responsibilities 
alongside family duties can be overwhelming, particularly in cultures where women are expected to 
fulfill traditional caregiving roles. This challenge is internal, as it pertains to the personal and emotional 
juggling of work-life balance. However, it could also benefit from external initiatives such as promoting 
shared domestic responsibilities, offering flexible work arrangements, and providing childcare support. 
Additionally, societal attitudes towards gender roles need to shift to support women in balancing their 
professional and personal lives more effectively (Panda, 2018). These family commitments occur 
because of traditional gender roles that place the primary responsibility for caregiving on women, 
making it difficult to balance these duties with entrepreneurial activities (Panda, 2018). 
 
In conclusion, the funnel model aptly captures the progression of challenges faced by women 
entrepreneurs in Cape Town, starting with broad societal scepticism and narrowing down to specific 
business-related and familial pressures. As one moves down the funnel, the challenges shift from being 
primarily external to internal. Addressing these challenges requires a combination of personal 
resilience, supportive networks, policy interventions, and societal change. By understanding and 
==PAGE:70==
69 
 
tackling these barriers at each stage of the entrepreneurial journey, we can create a more inclusive and 
supportive environment for women entrepreneurs, enabling them to thrive and contribute meaningfully 
to their communities and the broader economy.

Chapter 6: Limitations 
While this research provides valuable insights into the experiences and challenges of women 
entrepreneurs in Cape Town, several limitations should be acknowledged. Firstly, the sample size of 
women entrepreneurs interviewed for this study was relatively small and limited to Cape Town. This 
may not fully capture the diversity of experiences and challenges faced by women entrepreneurs across 
different regions of South Africa or other countries. Future research with a larger and more diverse 
sample would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the issues at hand. 
Additionally, the data collected in this study relies heavily on self-reported experiences from the 
participants. While these narratives provide rich, qualitative insights, they are also subject to personal 
biases and may not fully represent objective truths. Participants might emphasize certain experiences 
over others, which could skew the findings. Cross-referencing these narratives with additional data 
sources or third-party observations could help mitigate this limitation. 
The study primarily focused on women who have already embarked on their entrepreneurial journeys 
and have experienced some level of success. This focus might overlook the experiences of women who 
attempted entrepreneurship but failed or those who have not yet started due to perceived barriers. 
Including perspectives from these groups could provide a more balanced view of the challenges and 
motivations related to women’s entrepreneurship. 
Furthermore, the research provides a snapshot of the current experiences of women entrepreneurs but 
lacks a longitudinal perspective. Long-term studies are needed to understand how the challenges and 
motivations of women entrepreneurs evolve over time and how their businesses grow and sustain in the 
long run. Longitudinal data would also help assess the lasting impact of entrepreneurship education 
programs and support initiatives. 
The study’s findings are specific to Cape Town and may not be generalizable to other regions with 
different economic, cultural, and social contexts. Women entrepreneurs in rural areas or other urban 
centers might face distinct challenges that were not captured in this research. Comparative studies across 
different regions would be beneficial to understand these contextual differences. 
Moreover, the study was conducted in a specific socio-economic period, and external factors such as 
economic downturns, policy changes, or global events (e.g., the COVID-19 pandemic) were not deeply 
explored. These factors can significantly influence entrepreneurial activities and the challenges faced 
by women entrepreneurs. Future research should consider these external influences to provide a more 
comprehensive analysis. 
==PAGE:78==
77 
 
While the qualitative approach provides in-depth insights, it limits the ability to generalize findings to 
a broader population. Integrating quantitative methods, such as surveys or statistical analysis, could 
enhance the robustness of the findings and support broader generalizations. 
The evaluation of entrepreneurship education programs was based on interviews and available 
literature, which might not encompass all the programs available or their full range of impacts. More 
extensive fieldwork, including direct observations and participation in these programs, would provide 
a more detailed assessment of their effectiveness. 
The analysis and interpretation of the data were conducted by the researcher, which introduces the 
potential for subjective bias. Although efforts were made to remain objective, the personal lens of the 
researcher might influence the conclusions drawn. Peer reviews and collaborative analysis could help 
mitigate this bias. 
Moreover, while the study sought to answer specific research questions and provide suggestions for 
addressing the challenges faced by women entrepreneurs, there may be limitations in the depth and 
breadth of the answers and recommendations provided. The findings and suggestions, while valuable, 
may lack the substance needed to address all facets of the complex issues at hand comprehensively. 
Further considerations and more in-depth research are required to fully understand and tackle these 
challenges. 
It is important to note that this study aims to gauge the topic and provide an initial understanding rather 
than offer definitive solutions. The primary goal is to spark interest and encourage further research and 
initiatives to drive deeper change. The findings should be viewed as a starting point for broader 
discussions and actions aimed at supporting women entrepreneurs and addressing the barriers they face. 
By highlighting these limitations, the study underscores the need for ongoing exploration and 
commitment to fostering a more inclusive and supportive entrepreneurial ecosystem for women. 
Addressing these limitations in future research will enhance the understanding of women’s 
entrepreneurship and inform the development of more effective support mechanisms. Despite these 
limitations, the current study contributes valuable knowledge to the field and provides a foundation for 
further exploration.

Chapter 7: Future research 
Building on the insights and limitations identified in this study, future research should aim to provide a 
more comprehensive understanding of the challenges and opportunities for women entrepreneurs, both 
in Cape Town and beyond. Several avenues for future research are recommended to address the gaps 
and deepen the knowledge in this area. 
Future studies should aim to include a larger and more diverse sample of women entrepreneurs. This 
should encompass a broader range of geographic locations, including rural areas, other urban centres, 
and different provinces within South Africa. A more diverse sample will help capture a wider array of 
experiences and challenges, providing a more holistic view of women’s entrepreneurship. 
To understand the long-term impact of entrepreneurship on women and their businesses, longitudinal 
studies are essential. These studies should track women entrepreneurs over several years to observe 
how their motivations, challenges, and business outcomes evolve. Longitudinal research will also help 
assess the long-term effectiveness of support programs and interventions. 
Future research should include women who have attempted entrepreneurship but failed, as well as those 
who aspire to become entrepreneurs but have not yet started. Understanding the barriers and reasons 
behind their decisions will provide a more balanced view and help identify critical support needs to 
prevent failure and encourage more women to start businesses.

the qualitative insights and enhance the generalizability of findings. Quantitative data can provide 
robust evidence on the prevalence and impact of various challenges and the effectiveness of different 
support measures.

observations and participation. Future research should assess the content, delivery methods, and long-
term impacts of these programs. This includes comparing different training approaches, such as 
traditional business training versus personal initiative training, to identify the most effective methods 
for empowering women entrepreneurs. 
Exploring the role of technology and digital platforms in supporting women entrepreneurs is crucial. 
Future research should investigate how digital tools can enhance access to resources, training, and 
markets, particularly for women in remote or underserved areas. Understanding the potential of 
technology to bridge gaps will inform the development of more effective support mechanisms. 
Investigating the impact of policy and structural interventions on women’s entrepreneurship is vital. 
Future studies should evaluate the effectiveness of existing policies and recommend new strategies to

Chapter 8: Conclusion 
This thesis set out to explore the motivations, challenges, societal contributions, and potential support 
mechanisms for women entrepreneurs in Cape Town. Through a combination of in-depth interviews 
and desk research, this study provides valuable insights into the entrepreneurial journeys of these 
women, highlighting both their resilience and the systemic barriers they face. 
The motivations driving women to entrepreneurship are complex and multifaceted, rooted in both 
necessity and aspiration. From an early age, many of the women interviewed were inspired by parental 
role models, which helped to instil an entrepreneurial mindset. This early influence is crucial in 
developing the perceived capabilities and confidence necessary to pursue entrepreneurship. As they 
entered the job market, high unemployment and job dissatisfaction served as significant push factors, 
compelling these women to seek alternative means of economic independence. Their personal initiative 
and the ability to identify and exploit opportunities played a crucial role in transforming their passions 
and skills into viable businesses. Moreover, their entrepreneurial endeavours often extend beyond 
personal gain, driven by a desire to address social issues and contribute to their communities, leading 
to personal fulfilment and empowerment. 
The challenges faced by lower-income women entrepreneurs in Cape Town can be visualized as a 
funnel, narrowing from broad societal scepticism to specific business-related and familial pressures. 
Scepticism is the first and most pervasive challenge, rooted in societal norms and gender biases. 
Overcoming this requires a strong sense of self-efficacy and support from mentors and networks. As 
women establish their businesses, they encounter gender, societal, and racial barriers that further 
complicate their entrepreneurial journey. These challenges require comprehensive societal change, 
supportive policies, and targeted educational programs. Once businesses are operational, more nuanced 
internal challenges arise, such as operational inefficiencies and administrative burdens. Addressing 
these issues necessitates targeted training, improved access to resources, and supportive infrastructure. 
Finally, balancing family commitments with business responsibilities remains a persistent challenge, 
underscoring the need for flexible work arrangements and shared domestic duties. 
Women entrepreneurs significantly contribute to the well-being of their communities and families. Their 
personal empowerment fosters greater self-esteem and societal roles, setting positive examples and 
nurturing a culture of resilience and self-reliance. At the family level, these women provide financial 
stability and serve as role models for their children, promoting a generational ripple effect. In their 
communities, they facilitate skill development, inspire others, and increase representation, which helps 
other women realize their perceived capabilities and opportunities. At the societal level, they contribute 
to job creation and economic growth, addressing high unemployment rates and fostering social stability.

To address the barriers faced by lower-income women entrepreneurs, current entrepreneurship 
education programs in South Africa need to evolve. A pyramid structure of these barriers shows that 
foundational issues such as lack of accessible co-working spaces and markets must be addressed first. 
This requires infrastructural and logistical support to create conducive environments for business 
operations. The next layer involves providing robust support networks and mentorship to foster 
relational and community-based support. Addressing societal and structural barriers, including lack of 
representation, is also crucial. This involves promoting gender and racial equity through inclusive 
policies and educational content. At the top of the pyramid, immediate and personal barriers such as 
fear of failure and self-doubt need to be tackled through proactive training programs that enhance self-
efficacy and personal initiative. 
The limitations of this study, including its small sample size and potential researcher bias, highlight the 
need for further research. Future studies should include larger and more diverse samples, longitudinal 
approaches, and a mix of quantitative and qualitative methods. Additionally, exploring the impact of 
external factors such as economic conditions and policy changes, and investigating the role of 
technology and digital platforms in supporting women entrepreneurs, will provide a more 
comprehensive understanding of this field. 
In conclusion, this thesis provides a foundational understanding of the experiences of lower-income 
women entrepreneurs in Cape Town, revealing both their resilience and the systemic barriers they face. 
By addressing these challenges through targeted support and policy interventions, we can create a more 
inclusive and supportive entrepreneurial ecosystem. This, in turn, will empower more women to achieve 
economic independence, contribute to community development, and drive broader societal change. The 
findings and recommendations of this study aim to spark interest and encourage further research and 
action to support women entrepreneurs in South Africa and beyond.
