Master Thesis
Abstract
This thesis investigates decision strategies for optimising Dynamic Dial-a-Ride Problems
(DDARPs), which is a core issue in demand-responsive transport (DRT) systems. The thesis
focuses on improving service quality by minimising the number of unserviced customer re-
quests and reducing excess ride times, while considering the operational complexity arising
from real-time demand.
A two-phase solution framework is developed, combining an of-
fline planning phase with an online insertion and re-optimisation phase, both using Adaptive
Large Neighbourhood Search (ALNS). The offline phase handles requests known before the
operational period, while the online phase dynamically inserts new requests as they arrive.
Two types of proactive decision strategies are designed and evaluated: Anticipation strate-
gies, which incorporate predicted future demand into the offline planning phase, and waiting
strategies, which relocate idle vehicles in the online phase. Instance data has been generated
inspired by real-life DRT systems and used to test the implemented solution framework and
decision strategies. Results showed that one of the proposed waiting strategies can reduce
the number of unserviced requests in highly dynamic problem types, while more static prob-
lem types will not benefit from applying idle vehicle relocation strategies. Furthermore, the
implemented anticipation strategies did not successfully decrease the number of unserviced
requests, but the work revealed important insights into the challenges of implementing such
strategies. In general, the work in this thesis revealed that the characteristics of the considered
DDARP greatly influence the efficiency of the considered decision strategies.

Introduction
Demand-responsive transport (DRT) systems play an important role in the context of modern transporta-
tion systems, especially in areas where conventional public transportation is not offered or beneficial,
whether due to low population density, limited infrastructure, or specific accessibility needs. Flexible,
on-demand services provide personalised, door-to-door transportation, dynamically adjusting routes and
schedules in real time based on customer requests. DRT is particularly critical for ensuring mobility for
vulnerable or underserviced populations, including the elderly, individuals with disabilities, and residents
in rural regions where fixed-route transit is economically or logistically infeasible.
As societies strive to promote inclusive and efficient transportation solutions, DRT systems must not only
meet user needs but also operate with high efficiency and cost-effectiveness. This requires the integration
of advanced planning algorithms and decision-support tools that can handle fluctuating demand and
optimise routing. In order to ensure that these kinds of services are offered, it is important to continuously
ensure their operational performance through data-driven optimisation and innovative service strategies.
1.1
Definition and Scope of Project
This thesis will consider the planning and scheduling aspect of a DRT service formulated as a dynamic
Dial-A-Ride problem (DDARP). The problem involves determining how to efficiently schedule and route
vehicles to pick up and drop off customers based on individual requests - some of these requests are
known before the operational period, and some occur during the operational period. The planning is
subject to constraints like time windows, maximum ride time durations, and vehicle capacities, while
allowing ride-sharing. An in-depth description of the considered DDARP can be found in Section 3. The
considered DDARP is inspired by practical systems such as Flextrafik, a real-world demand-responsive
transport service operated in Denmark. The methodology and results are generalisable and not limited
to this specific context.
A major difficulty when considering DDARPs is the exponential increase in computational time required
to find optimal routes as the number of requests grows. Hence, in this thesis, a two-phase heuristic
solution framework based on Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search (ALNS) is developed and used to
evaluate different decision strategies. The two phases consist of an offline planning phase, where routes
are constructed for the requests known in advance, and an online phase where the routes are re-planned
as new requests occur during the operational period. Another difficulty in the dynamic setting is to
balance current decision-making with future long-term effects while trying to ensure fewer rejections
of customers and shorter ride times for customers. This problem will be addressed by implementing
proactive strategies in the form of anticipation of expected future demand and strategic waiting of empty
vehicles. Both of these strategies require historic data, and this thesis will therefore also investigate some
simple data generation methods.
The primary objective in the planning process will be to minimise the number of customers that are not
serviced by the considered DDARP. As a secondary objective, the total ride time of passengers exceeding
their direct drive time is minimised. To address these objectives, the scope of this thesis is twofold:
1. To investigate the impact on the number of unserviced customers when incorporating waiting strate-
gies into the online phase, measured against a base solution method.

2. To investigate the impact on the number of unserviced customers when incorporating anticipation
strategies into the offline phase, measured against a base solution method.
Several simplifications are made in this thesis. These include operating with homogeneous vehicle fleets,
simplified customer requirements, and a focus on a single-day planning horizon. Furthermore, the focus
in this thesis is primarily on the route planning and scheduling aspects of the DDARP, and as a result,
supporting components such as instance generation and vehicle configuration have only been addressed
using relatively simple methods, even though extensive investigation could also have been done in these

1.2
Structure of the Report
To support these investigations, the thesis first introduces the concept of dynamic Dial-a-Ride Problems
(DDARPs) and reviews relevant literature in Section 2. As the Flextrafik system serves as the primary
inspiration, the specific characteristics of the corresponding DDARP are described in Section 3. This is
followed by a mathematical formulation of the problem in Section 4.
To evaluate the proposed solution approaches, two sets of problem instances are generated using data from
a comparable real-world system provided by Konsentra, as outlined in Section 5. The two-phase solution
framework, consisting of both an offline and an online phase, is presented in Section 7. A construction
heuristic and an Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search (ALNS) algorithm have been developed to solve
each phase and are detailed in Sections 6.2 and 6.3, respectively. This includes the development of a
tailored insertion heuristic to address the complexity of the problem.
The two-phase solution method will act as a base method, and the results of it are presented in Section
8. This base method will be extended by introducing proactive anticipation and waiting strategies in
Section 9 and 11, respectively. Both strategies are evaluated relative to the base method. The analysis of
the anticipation methods will focus on how to implement them in combination with ALNS in Section 10.
The analysis of the waiting strategies will focus on how problem characteristic affects the performance of
the strategy in Section 12. Finally, the overall findings are discussed and summarised in Section 13 and
14.

Litterature Review
2.1
The Dynamic Dial-A-Ride Problem
The Dial-A-Ride Problem (DARP) arose from the on-demand public transit service Dial-A-Ride in Ohio in
the 1970s, where customers phoned in their transport requests [Ho et al. (2018)]. In the DARP, customers
give requests containing a pick-up location and a drop-off location. In this context, the requests are often
defined as either being outbound or inbound. The outbound request is when the customer has to go from
home to a destination, and the inbound request is the other way. In case of an outbound request, the
customer furthermore specifies a desired arrival time, and for the inbound request, they specify a desired
pick-up time. The desired times are then converted into a time window with a specified width [Cordeau
(2006)]. In some DARPs, the customers can also specify how many people have to be transported and
if they have any special needs or criteria for the vehicle. Besides the requests from the customers, a
fleet size and specification for vehicles are defined for the problem. Lastly, an upper bound for the ride
time of a customer is imposed. The goal of the DARP is to create routes for the transportation of the
customers, satisfying a set of constraints. Multiple customers can be in a vehicle at the same time, giving
rise to ride-sharing [Deleplanque and Quilliot (2013)]. There are often two conflicting objectives present:
Quality of service and the minimisation of cost. Hence, the aim becomes to create these routes so that the
two objectives are balanced. The consideration of the human aspect in the DARP problem in the form
of both the maximum ride time constraint and the objective function makes it differ from many other
routing problems, such as the more commonly known Pick-up and Delivery Problem with Time Windows
(PDPTW) [Cordeau and Laporte (2007)]. Besides this, the two problems are similar, and literature and
solution methods for the PDPTW have also been used in this study as inspiration.
The DARP is a well-studied problem. Since the first DARP in the 1970s, various instances and variants
have been thoroughly studied, and multiple solution methods have been developed. In the following
sections, we will cover different characteristics that can be used to distinguish between different DARPs
and give an overview of the different solution methods that have been developed in the current literature.
2.2
Problem Characteristics
DARPs have been applied to many different areas, such as patient transportation [Schilde et al. (2011)],
public transportation in low-demand areas [Vansteenwegen et al. (2022)], and ride-sharing [Simonetto
et al. (2019)]. This literature study is primarily focused on on-demand/demand-responsive public trans-
portation.
A thorough review of demand-responsive public transportation systems can be found in
Vansteenwegen et al. (2022), where they categorise demand-responsive public transportation systems.
They define on-demand systems as systems where customers need to make a request in order to be ser-
viced. Demand-responsive systems can be on-demand systems, but are more generally defined as systems
that somehow react to demand, e.g., by using real-time forecasting. They further categorise DARPs into
static/dynamic, online/offline, and fully-flexible/semi-flexible problems. Static DARPs are the classic
problem known from most literature on DARPs, where all information about requests is known before-
hand, whereas Dynamic DARPs (DDARPs) cover the set of problems where some or all information
is received in real-time during the planning period. Ho et al. (2018) further categorises DARPs into
deterministic/stochastic DARPs, where stochastic DARPs have some degree of incomplete information.

The above elements are examples of different characteristics that influence the nature of the problem.
Different characteristics are relevant depending on the logistical context of the DARP. This can further
affect both the objective and constraints of the problem.
The constraints of the modelled problem
significantly influence the complexity and structure of the resulting DARP. E.g., time window constraints
may be soft or hard, very tight or very loose, and this will influence which methods are most efficient
and good at solving the problem. The Degree of Dynamism (DoD) is another important characteristic
in dynamic problems. The DoD describes the level of requests that are received dynamically, and it
affects both the solution and the design of solution methods for DDARPs [Wong et al. (2014)]. This
highlights the importance of studying the modelled problem and its characteristics before choosing a
solution method.
2.3
Solution Methods
DARPs can be solved in many different ways; this involves different solution methods, modelling tools,
and decision strategies. Many articles propose solution methods for solving static DARPs, while fewer
introduce methods to solve dynamic DARPs.
Broadly, solution methods for solving DARPs can be
categorised as exact methods or inexact methods. Exact methods aim to find optimal solutions using
different approaches, while inexact methods aim to find solutions that are good enough within a reasonable
time frame.
2.3.1
Exact methods
When considering exact methods to solve DARPs, mostly static DARPs are considered, as a dynamic
DARP quickly becomes too complex.
Cordeau (2006) proposes a branch-and-cut algorithm for the static Dial-a-Ride problem. The proposed
algorithm introduces new valid inequalities and a pre-processing step for reducing the problem size. Qu
and Bard (2015) proposes a branch-and-price-and-cut algorithm for solving heterogeneous Pick-up and
Delivery Problems with configurable vehicle capacity. They introduce a new set of subset-row inequalities
in the master problem and a labelling algorithm with enhanced dominance conditions in the pricing
subproblem. It is concluded in both Cordeau (2006) and Qu and Bard (2015) that their approaches are
suitable for small- to medium-sized instances, but cannot, by themselves, be used to solve large instances.
Generally, exact solution methods are not used to solve large problem instances, since DARPs are NP-
hard [Wong et al. (2014)]. This means that the problem cannot be solved in polynomial time, which
entails that for large problem instances, other methods are needed.
2.3.2
Inexact methods
Many inexact methods for solving DARPs have been proposed in the literature, either as hybrid solution
methods or purely heuristic methods. The proposed methods are often more tailored to the specific
version of DARPs that is studied, so it is harder to generalise the performance of these methods. In
general, heuristic and meta-heuristic methods tend to have a better performance if the methods are
tailored to the problem [Gendreau (2019)].
Static DARP
Multiple articles have presented successful implementations of inexact methods for solving the static

DARP. Here, we will mention some of them.
Paquette et al. (2013) proposes a multi-criteria tabu
search (TS) to balance cost and service criteria in the found solutions.
By integrating the reference
point method for multi-criteria optimisation into the tabu search mechanism, the approach produces a
Pareto-front of non-dominated solutions. These solutions can be used to analyse the trade-off between
cost and service criteria. Gschwind and Drexl (2019) proposes an ALNS procedure. They implement
a classic ALNS procedure as found in Ropke and Pisinger (2006) and add several removal operators.
They introduce a novelty constant-time procedure for checking the feasibility of insertions into a DARP
route. Belhaiza (2019) proposes a hybrid algorithm that uses elements of Genetic Algorithms (GA) in an
ALNS procedure. The proposed approach uses an extended ALNS procedure and employs an additional
diversification strategy by using genetic crossovers.
Dynamic DARP
Also, for the dynamic DARP, several successful inexact methods have been implemented. Schilde et al.
(2011) proposes 4 different methods to solve a dynamic stochastic DARP with expected return transport.
They propose a Variable Neighbourhood Search (VNS), a Stochastic Variable Neighbourhood Search (S-
VNS), a Multiple Plan Approach (MPA), and a Multiple Scenario Approach (MSA). They conclude that
under certain conditions, including stochastic information in the solution method, can greatly improve
the solution quality.
van Engelen et al. (2018) proposes a demand-anticipatory insertion algorithm.
The algorithm uses forecasting to determine expected demand when inserting requests into a partial
solution. They further incorporate empty vehicle re-routing into the algorithm. Bongiovanni et al. (2022)
proposes a two-phase solution approach that combines machine learning elements with a Local Search
(LS) procedure. The first phase employs a greedy insertion heuristic to feasibly insert a new customer
request into the partial solution. The second phase uses a machine learning-based Large Neighbourhood
Search (LNS), where a large data set of previously solved problems is used to predict which destroy-repair
methods should be used. The proposed method is found to, on average, outperform state-of-the-art data-
driven meta-heuristics.
This is a non-exhaustive list of proposed solution methods for the static and dynamic DARP. Many other
methods have been proposed in the literature. The reviews Ho et al. (2018), Zhang and Woensel (2023),
and Vansteenwegen et al. (2022) contain a more thorough overview of the existing literature.
2.4
Decision Strategies
Beyond the choice of solution method, various other elements can be incorporated into the problem and
solution method in order to achieve better results. We will denote some of these aspects using the overall
term: Decision strategies. In this study, the term will cover strategies for anticipation of future events
and waiting strategies for vehicles. These strategies will cover or overlap with other strategies such as
strategies for rejection and outsourcing of customer requests and reallocation of vehicles. In Zhang and
Woensel (2023), they give a comprehensive review of the existing literature related to the Dynamic Pick-up
and Delivery Problem (DPDP) and decision strategies. Decision strategies can generally be implemented
in a proactive and/or reactive way. Reactive decision strategies do not consider future events but solely
make decisions based on the current state of the problem. In contrast, proactive decision strategies also
consider expected future events, which can result in sometimes making seemingly suboptimal decisions
in the short term to achieve better outcomes in the long term. The proactive strategies can be important
in dynamic contexts, where the unpredictability adds an extra layer of complexity to the problem, which
5

makes it critical to be able to design routes with sufficient slack to accommodate future requests.
Some of the different decision strategies and related literature are briefly mentioned below.
2.4.1
Waiting strategies
In the DARP, situations can occur where a vehicle must wait at some location in between servicing
customers. Waiting strategies consider how to decide the best waiting location for vehicles in an idle
period. In Wong et al. (2014), they introduce three different reactive waiting strategies at customer
locations for vehicles: The Drive First (DF) strategy, The Wait First (WF), and The Modified Dynamic
Wait (MDW) strategy. They found that especially the last two methods showed promising results for
different aspects of the objective.
In Ritzinger et al. (2022), they introduce two different proactive waiting strategies that both incorporate
historic data to determine which customer locations to wait at: The Coverage Algorithm (CA) and The
Intensity Algorithm (IA). They conclude that for their problem, the IA performs best.
In Bent and Van Hentenryck (2007), they explore an alternative approach that looks into a relocation
strategy for the vehicles. Here, idle vehicles can move to strategic locations and wait instead of waiting
at customer locations. They conclude that this might be good when the objective is to serve as many
customers as possible, but that it might increase the total travel length and operating cost.
2.4.2
Anticipation strategies
Beyond the anticipation that is embedded in the proactive waiting strategies, one can furthermore imple-
ment direct anticipation strategies in order to improve the overall solution. In Deleplanque and Quilliot
(2013), they introduce an insertability metric that is designed to assess the feasibility of inserting future
requests into existing routes while still considering the given constraints. This is added to the normal
cost of inserting a customer into the route, hence both current and future impact is considered. In Rigas
et al. (2018), they use a different approach. Here, they develop an online scheduling algorithm that uses
a probability distribution derived from historical data to generate a set of expected future customers,
which are considered when making routes.
In Haferkamp and Ehmke (2022), they discuss anticipatory strategies regarding the rejection of customers.
They distinguished between myopic rejection, where requests are declined solely based on feasibility, and
anticipatory rejection, where requests are also evaluated based on their future impact on the system.
In Zhang and Woensel (2023), they mention that instead of rejecting customers, some DARPs use the
option of outsourcing customers to external providers instead.
This literature reveals that, despite extensive research on the static DARP, gaps remain in the literature
for the dynamic variant.
Since small changes to a problem can lead to substantial alterations in its
nature, many combinations of the problem characteristics are left to be investigated. We identified a gap
in the literature about how to construct insertion heuristics for problems similar to the considered DARP,
which will be targeted in this report. Furthermore, we will explore the combination of ALNS and various
waiting strategies and anticipation strategies for DDARP. This combination has, to our knowledge, not
been examined in depth before.

Problem Description
In this section, we will describe the problem that arises in the Flextrafik transportation service, which
serves as the inspiration to the dynamic Dial-A-Ride Problem examined in this report. The considered
DDARP acts as the foundation for the analysis and methods investigated. We will introduce the key
elements of the considered DDARP and define the relevant terminology of the problem and the corre-
sponding solution.
3.1
The Flextrafik Problem
Movia Flextrafik is a demand-responsive transportation service organisation operating in Region Hoved-
staden and Region Zealand in Denmark. The goal of Flextrafik is to ensure the mobility of all citizens
regardless of a person’s geographical, social or physical circumstances [Movia (2020)]. This is done by
providing a set of different transportation services. The general concept behind all of the services is a
transportation service where door-to-door routes are planned based on demand while allowing rideshar-
ing. Below, we will describe a simplification of all the services to use as inspiration for the DDARP we
propose. Many of the specifications can be found in Mahmood (2016).
When using the service, all customers must call in advance before they need to be picked up. They can
either request a pick-up time or a drop-off time. For a pick-up request, the planning system at Flextrafik
will then determine a pick-up time in some interval around the desired pick-up time. The customer is
immediately informed of this time. However, the vehicle is allowed to arrive a little before and a little
after the informed pick-up time. A similar process applies for a drop-off request. The allowance of time
windows gives more flexibility when designing the routes for the vehicles. If none of the Flextrafik vehicles
can service a customer, the customer will be outsourced to a taxi company, in compliance with the goal
of ensuring the mobility of all citizens. If a customer request has been accepted, it cannot be outsourced
later on. Furthermore, it is only possible to use the service during its operational hours. When a new
customer request appears, the planning system uses a short amount of time to determine the new routes,
considering available capacity and the service level of the customers. The service levels include service
within the defined time windows, and furthermore also a maximum ride time for the customers.
From this description, it is clear that the Flextrafik problem aligns with the description of a dial-a-ride
problem as described in Section 2.1. The problem is centred around the human aspect, which is also
reflected in the objectives. Besides the constraint that all customers have to be serviced, they aim to
meet all given service levels while also minimising the cost for the individual customer.
3.2
The Considered DDARP
Inspired by the Flextrafik Problem, we introduce the DDARP, which will serve as the basis for this
report. While the core concept of the DDARP is similar to the Flextrafik problem, we will specify relevant
elements and distinctions. The following description will act as the base problem, but alterations to some
of the characteristics will be introduced in later sections.
Operational period
In this project, the route planning for the DDARP service will only be considered over a single operational
period. This period is defined as the service hours 6:00 to 23:00 on a single day.

Dynamism
A degree of dynamism is introduced by assuming that customers who call in a significant amount of time
before the start of the operational period of the DDARP will be informed of their pick-up time at the
start of the day. Hence, these requests can be planned together, since the information is known before
the start of operation. Since the planning is not during operation, a larger time period is available to
find good routes for them instead of having a somewhat immediate response time. These requests will
be denoted as offline requests. Requests received during the operational period will be denoted as online
requests. The degree of dynamism is set to 40% as default.
Requests
It will be assumed that all requests are pick-up requests, and there will not be differentiation between
customers with regard to special needs and loads. Hence, a request consists of the transportation of a
single person and thereby only requires one seat in a vehicle. Furthermore, we assume a service time of
2 minutes for each customer when boarding and alighting the vehicle. A maximum ride time is defined
as 1.5 times the direct drive time between the pick-up and drop-off location. The customers have to call
in at least 2 hours before their desired pick-up time, and they can either call in during the operational
hours and be denoted as online requests, or they can call before the operational hours and be part of
the set of offline requests. In this project, we will consider services without any predefined recurrence of
requests. Requests that are not serviced by the DDARP solution are considered unserviced, and we do
not consider how these are handled. Depending on the transportation service, these requests might be
outsourced to other companies or completely rejected.
Time windows
When a customer makes a request, they are immediately informed of a scheduled pick-up time which
lies between 15 minutes before and 45 minutes after the desired pick-up time. The time window for the
pick-up time point is tightened upon informing the customers of their pick-up time, allowing vehicles to
arrive within 5 minutes before and 15 minutes after the informed pick-up time.
Vehicles
All vehicles are assumed to be identical, and all vehicles can service all customers, however, each vehicle
can contain at most 4 customers at a time. The vehicles are only active in their specified shift. There
are 4 different shifts covering the operational period. In this project, we have furthermore assumed that
vehicles can be idle while still having customers onboard. This should, however, be avoided as much as
possible. This will be discussed further in Section 6. Furthermore, this project will focus on how to plan
good routes with a given set of vehicles and will therefore not look in depth into what the best number of
vehicles is or how their depots or shifts should be distributed. This will be discussed further in Section 5.
Depots
A set of predefined depots will be defined across the operational geographical area. Each vehicle will be
assigned to a depot where they have to start and end their shift. A vehicle is allowed to return to its
depot multiple times.
Objective
In this project, the objective of the problem is to minimise the number of requests unserviced by the
DDARP service. As a secondary target, we aim at minimising the excess ride time of a customer in its
assigned route when compared to the direct drive time.

3.3
Definitions
Below, we define terms that are used in the description of the methodology or in future theory sections.
Request
A request consists of the need for transportation from one place to another. It contains a time and
location for both a pick-up and a drop-off of the customer. Furthermore, a request is defined as online
or offline. If it is an online request, a call time is also specified.
Instance
An instance consists of a number of both online and offline requests. The ratio between the two types of
requests is defined by the degree of dynamism. The instances should reflect customer requests occurring
in the DDARP service, considering a single operational period.
Activity
We define an activity as an element of a route. An activity is defined by a location and a feasible time
window for the start of service time at the activity. A customer request contains two activities: a pick-up
activity and a drop-off activity. Both request activities will be serviced by the same vehicle.
In addition to the request activities, a route can also contain depot activities and waiting activities. A
route will start and end at a depot activity. Waiting activities are used to pad the route, so that a vehicle
is always ”servicing” a waiting activity if it is not on-route to a customer or servicing a customer. When
servicing waiting activities, the vehicle is idle.
Activity Assignment
An activity assignment is the assignment of an activity to a vehicle. An activity assignment contains the
actual start and end of service time for the activity.
Route
A route is a sequence of activity assignments that are serviced by a vehicle. A route will always start
and end at the vehicle depot.
Vehicle Schedule
A vehicle schedule contains a vehicle and a route that is assigned to the vehicle. If the route is non-empty,
i.e. at least one request is serviced on the route, we say that the vehicle is active.
Solution
A solution S is a complete set of vehicle schedules for all vehicles in the fleet.
Request bank
A request bank U is the set of requests in a scenario that are not serviced in a solution S.

of m vehicles. Each customer request is defined as a request for transportation from a pick-up location

Instance Generation
To implement and test the solution methods described later, various problem instances have been created
for the proposed DDARP. The instances have been created based on data provided for a similar project
made in cooperation with the company Konsentra. The data from Konsentra was provided to us by our
supervisor, Evelien van der Hurk. Konsentra is an organisation responsible for public transportation in
certain regions of Oslo and Akershus, Norway. Similar to Flextrafik, they are responsible for offering
transportation options in rural areas and for people with special needs.
5.1
Preprocessing of Konsentra Data
According to Lantzenberg Dit Linval (2019), the requests from the Konsentra datasets can be split into
two different groups: Transportation of school children and transportation of exceptional requests. The
latter cases are requests that are different every day and are not related to any special event.
The provided data set consists of the following relevant elements:
• Request time: Time of either wanted pick-up or drop-off
• Coordinates: Coordinates for pick-up and drop-off location
• Name or address: Name or address of pick-up and drop-off location
Hence, several additional elements are needed, such as call times and service times, to generate problem
instances for the DDARP proposed in this thesis.
In order to use the Konsentra data to generate problem instances, we have filtered away some of the
requests to get the desired behaviour. Requests for schoolchildren’s transportation are often recurrent,
and the majority of the requests are connected to a particular time slot. This behaviour does not match
the expected behaviour in the proposed DDARP, as we do not know which type of recurring requests
would appear in, e.g., the Flextrafik problem or a similar problem - hence, these requests are filtered
away. The other group of requests, the exceptional ones, is similar to the description of the ones in the
proposed DDARP. Furthermore, requests outside of the defined available time windows of vehicles, as
described in Section 5.4, have been filtered away. This data will act as the basis for the generation of
the problem instances. After filtering the data from Konsentra, 379 requests are left. This is not a large
number of requests.
5.2
Generation of Distributions
To preserve some of the same characteristics from the data provided by Konsentra, all request features
have been sampled from distributions derived from this data. We have chosen to disregard potential
relations between different features in the data sets unless otherwise specified.
As described in Section 3, we have chosen to only work with pick-up requests to simplify the proposed
DDARP. The Konsentra datasets contain both pick-up and drop-off requests, however, we have used
all requests, regardless of their type, as a basis for the distributions described below. Furthermore, the
proposed DDARP contains both offline and online requests, i.e., some requests are known before the start
of the operational period, whereas others occur during the operational period. This distinction is not

made in the Konsentra data. Hence, we have chosen to differentiate between offline and online requests
by sampling their request time from different distributions.
We have used kernel density estimation (KDE) to derive the necessary distributions. Since the focus
of this thesis is not data generation, we do not describe the method but refer to KernelDensity and
Wikipedia.
Location distribution
A two-dimensional probability density function for the possible request locations has been derived from
the Konsentra data using KDE. The probability density function is defined on a given x- and y-range
where (x, y) corresponds to the coordinates of a location. The probability density function has been
smoothed to spread the probability, and the distribution has been shifted so that no coordinate pair has
0 probability. This distribution will be referred to as the L−distribution.
Request time distribution
Similarly, a one-dimensional probability density function for the request time of online and offline re-
quests has been generated. These will be referred to as the R+−distribution for the offline requests and
R−−distribution for the online requests.
As mentioned, as the Konsentra data did not distinguish between online and offline requests, we have
altered the request time distribution obtained from the Konsentra data to separately generate the R+−
and R−−distribution. Figure 1 shows the request time distribution from the Konsentra data. It reveals
that the datasets have two peaks during the day; a morning and afternoon peak.
We assume that
requests with an earlier request time are more likely to be known in advance. Hence, the first peak has
been enlarged in the R+−distribution by scaling it with a normal distribution. For the R−−distribution,
the second peak has been enlarged.
To not eliminate specific request times, both distributions have been shifted so that no request time has 0
probability in the defined interval. The distributions have furthermore been smoothed to ensure a bigger
variation in the request times.
Relationship between pick-up and drop-off location
To obtain realistic driving distances, a one-dimensional distribution for the direct distance between a pick-
up location and a drop-off location has been found using KDE. The distribution has been smoothed, and
the considered distance range has been extended to reduce the limitations imposed by the small dataset.
The driving distance distribution will be denoted D. The D−distribution will be used to find a drop-off
location based on a pick-up location. When a pick-up location is sampled from the L−distribution,
a driving distance d is drawn from the D−distribution. The drop-off location is then found from the
L-distribution while only considering locations that are d meters away ± some margin.

The instances have been created based on a specified degree of dynamism (DoD), which defines the ratio
of requests occurring dynamically. All instances contain requests in the same operational period, which is

Call times

will describe why we have chosen to generate instances of type II.
The instances of type II are generated with DoD=1.0, i.e., all requests in the instances arrive dynamically.
Additionally, the call time buffer is reduced, so that requests can call in up to 5 minutes before the
requested pick-up time. We add an additional bound on the call time, so that the call time is at most 15
minutes from the requested pick-up time. This forces all call times to be close to the request time. We
also modify the pick-up time window of requests, so that a pick-up activity can be serviced in the time
window [request time - 5 min, request time + 15 min], i.e., the request can be picked up in a 20-minute
time window. These modifications make instance type II more restricted than instance type I, and it is
also a much more dynamic instance type.

6
Solution Method
Due to the exponential computational time required by exact methods, a heuristic solution approach will
be used to solve the generated instances described in the previous sections. The heuristic solution method
aims at planning efficient vehicle schedules that contain as many requests as possible. The planning
consists of first constructing an initial solution using a construction heuristic, followed by optimisation
of the solution using Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search (ALNS). In the next sections, we describe
the used insertion heuristic, construction heuristic, and ALNS.
6.1
Insertion Heuristic
In order to construct feasible routes, we propose the following insertion heuristic. The heuristic attempts
to insert a request into an existing or empty route at a given pick-up and drop-off position. The heuristic
consists of two parts:
1. Feasibility check
2. Insertion
The feasibility check verifies whether a request i with pick-up activity pi and drop-off activity di can
feasibly be inserted into a route at a given pick-up position v and drop-off position w. The feasibility
check returns updated start-of-service times that the activities in the route would have if the request was
inserted into the route at these positions. This is returned only if all the constraints described in Section
4 are satisfied. The insertion step is used for the insertion of request activities into specified feasible
positions. It inserts the request activities into the route and updates the route accordingly. The insertion
step is not explained in detail, as the found start-of-service times from the feasibility check will be used
in the insertion step to update the route.
6.1.1
Feasibility check
The feasibility check initially checks if the vehicle that is servicing the route has the capacity for an
additional request between positions v and w. Additionally, it checks whether the activities before and
after positions v and w have time windows that are incompatible with pi and di. If the vehicle has free
capacity and the time windows are compatible, the feasibility check continues. It then checks whether pi
and di can be inserted such that the time windows for all existing activities in the route are still satisfied
and such that no request violates its maximum ride time. The full algorithm for the feasibility check will
be outlined in Algorithm 1.
The algorithm rebuilds a temporary route from scratch by attempting to insert the activities in the route
1-by-1 where the new start and end of service times are saved for each activity. The new pick-up and
drop-off activities are inserted at their respective positions. Each time a new activity is inserted in the
route, it is either inserted directly or the already inserted activities in the route are shifted forward or
backwards in time. If the activity we are currently attempting to insert is a drop-off activity, it is checked
if the maximum ride time of the request is still satisfied, if not, it returns false. After inserting a drop-off
activity, the corresponding pick-up and drop-off activities will, in future iterations, be shifted together.
Hence, the ride time will remain the same for inserted activity pairs. It is worth noting that the proposed
feasibility check does not guarantee that the insertion of a request is infeasible if it returns false, since the

6.3
Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search
Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search (ALNS) is a heuristic solution method first proposed in Ropke and
Pisinger (2006) to solve Pickup and Delivery Problems with Time Windows (PDPTWs). The method
is based on Large Neighbourhood Search (LNS) introduced in Shaw (1997). LNS for Vehicle Routing
Problems (VRPs) is a local search heuristic that starts with an initial solution. The initial solution is then
improved by iteratively removing and re-inserting requests in the solution, thus exploring neighbouring
solutions. In LNS, only one removal and reinsertion method is applied. ALNS extends LNS by applying
several removal and reinsertion methods, denoted as destroy and repair methods. In each iteration of
ALNS, a destroy and repair method is chosen based on its weights, which are updated throughout the
search based on their performance. By adaptively choosing a destroy and repair method in each Local
Search (LS) step, a large neighbourhood of solutions is evaluated.
The ALNS framework consists of several elements as described in Ropke and Pisinger (2006) and Pisinger
and Ropke (2007). At the master level, a Local Search Framework controls the acceptance of candidate
solutions, which are found by exploring a neighbourhood of the current solution. A number of destroy
and repair methods should be chosen as neighbourhood operators. A selection procedure and an adaptive
weight adjustment procedure control the selection of these methods in each iteration. In the next sections,
we describe each of these elements. We have chosen to implement an ALNS framework that closely follows

Destroy methods
A destroy method is a neighbourhood operator that removes parts of an existing solution. In Ropke
and Pisinger (2006), they describe three destroy methods: Random Removal, Worst Removal, and Shaw
Removal. We have chosen to implement the same three methods, and we describe them below.
In each destroy method, q requests are removed from the solution. q is chosen at random such that
pmin · nrequests ≤q ≤pmax · nrequests,
(22)
where pmin and pmax are, respectively, the minimum and maximum fraction of requests to remove, and
nrequests is the number of requests in the solution that are assigned to a route.
After removing a request from a solution, the vehicle schedule to which the request was assigned is
updated accordingly, such that the remaining activities have the correct start and end of service times. If
it is necessary, waiting activities are added to the vehicle schedule to ”pad” the route. A modification of
the feasibility check outlined in Algorithm 1 is used to ”rebuild” the vehicle schedules. Since the outlined
algorithm finds updated start of service times for all requests in a route, the procedure can be used to
find the correct service times when some requests have been removed from a route.
Random removal
Random removal is the simplest destroy method. q requests are chosen at random and removed from the
solution. The removed requests are added to the request bank of the solution. The cost and KPIs of the
vehicle schedule and solution are also updated.
Worst Removal
Worst removal is a destroy method where requests with a high cost are attempted to be removed. The
cost of request i in a solution S is defined as

there is no penalty for removing the request.
q requests are chosen according to the cost and removed from the solution. The selection is randomised,

Repair methods
In contrast to the destroy methods, a repair method is a neighbourhood operator that inserts requests

Basic Greedy Heuristic and Regret Heuristic. Again, we have chosen to implement the same methods,
and we describe them below.
For each repair method, every request in the request bank is tried to be inserted into the existing solution.
If this is not possible, then they will continue to be in the request bank, U. To check if an insertion is
feasible, the feasibility check outlined in Algorithm 1 is used.

Tuning of ALNS
Implementing any metaheuristic, including the considered ALNS, involves many parameters. Therefore,
achieving good performance from a metaheuristic typically requires extensive tuning, which can be rather
time-consuming. Since the scope of this thesis is not developing the best or fastest metaheuristic but
rather creating a functional one to use for further method analysis, we have based our choice of parameter
settings on the article of Ropke and Pisinger (2006). Only the time allocated to ALNS and the destroy
rate have been modified to better suit the considered DDARP. The DDARP is solved in two phases; the

described in Section 4 to evaluate the solutions found during ALNS. We have chosen α = 1000 as the
penalty for unserviced requests and β = 10 as the penalty for excess ride time.
To verify that the ALNS configuration makes sense for the considered DDARP, the algorithm has been
tested on a set of generated instances consisting of 20, 100, 300, and 500 requests, respectively. A further

7.1
Offline Phase
The offline phase of the two-phase solution framework is an initial planning phase.
In the proposed
DDARP, some requests are known before the beginning of the operational period. We refer to these
as offline requests. The offline planning aims to assign as many offline requests as possible to a vehicle
schedule. Hence, all known requests are passed to the offline planning, which then provides planned
schedules for all vehicles. The offline planning process begins by trying to insert all known requests using
the proposed simple construction heuristic, which is described in Section 6.2. The resulting schedules are
then optimised using Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search (Section 6.3). Additionally, as described in
Section 9, an anticipation strategy can be included in the offline planning phase.
34
==PAGE:43==
7.2
Online Phase
Master Thesis
7.1.1
Transition from offline phase to online phase
The offline phase results in a solution, where only the offline requests are planned. Some offline requests
might be unserviced, if it was not possible to place all requests in the available vehicle schedules. Any
offline requests that are unserviced in the offline phase are not passed to the online phase, as we assume
that they will have been outsourced or handled otherwise when the online planning period begins. Offline
requests that are assigned to a vehicle schedule will be assigned a start-of-service time, indicating when
the vehicle picks up the customer. Since we do not want to deviate too much from the planned pick-up
time, the customer is allowed to be picked up 5 minutes before and 15 minutes after the given time.
Hence, the pick-up time windows for planned requests are tightened, and their drop-off time windows are
correspondingly updated. This ensures that even if a request is serviced by another vehicle than it was
originally assigned to, the pick-up time of the request will be within a reasonable interval of the originally
assigned time.
7.2
Online Phase
The online phase of the proposed two-phase solution framework handles dynamic routing, which plans
the dynamically occurring requests. Requests that occur dynamically are referred to as online requests.
The online phase shares many elements with the offline phase, particularly the use of the same underlying
solution methods to insert requests.
The online phase solves the online problem, i.e., requests are inserted dynamically as time progresses,
and the current state of the system is dynamically evaluated. Each time a request occurs at a time t, the
current state of the solution is assessed. This means determining which activities in the current vehicle
schedules have been serviced at the given time, which are currently being serviced, and which will be
serviced in the future. We say that activities that are already serviced are in the driven schedules and
activities that are currently being serviced or will be serviced in the future are in the planned schedules.
Planned schedules are passed to the next state of the solution framework, while driven schedules are locked
and incorporated into the ”final” solution, which describes the complete solution after the termination
of the solution framework.
The assessment of the state of the solution is illustrated in Figure 10. The figure illustrates two vehicle
schedules, one for vehicle i and one for vehicle j. A request occurs at some time, illustrated by the blue
line. The red activities have already been serviced and will be part of the driven schedules. In the vehicle
schedule of vehicle i, the black activity currently being serviced cannot be modified, but will be part of
the planned schedule, so that the route continues from that activity. Likewise, for vehicle j, the black
activity cannot be modified, as vehicle j will have left the previous activity and be on-route to the black
activity. The green activities are future activities that can be modified.

7.2.1
Dynamic insertion of request
The proposed solution framework operates as an event-based simulation. Each event corresponds to the
arrival of a request at its call time. When an event occurs, the solution framework transitions to the next
state, and the planned and driven schedules are updated. When a request event occurs, the framework
attempts to insert it into the planned vehicle schedules. At the time of insertion, the request has a time
window for pick-up. However, as in the offline phase, once the request has been inserted into a planned
schedule, and the framework transitions to the next state, the time window for pick-up will be tightened,
and the time window for drop-off will be correspondingly updated.
Similarly to the offline phase, each request in the online phase is first attempted to be inserted into the
planned schedules by an insertion heuristic. The planned schedules are then optimised using ALNS,
which is described in Section 6.3. If the request that is attempted to be inserted cannot be assigned to
any vehicle, then it is unserviced, and it is not passed on to the next state of the solution framework.

Theory: Anticipation Strategy
In this section, we will investigate how historical data can be used to incorporate anticipation into the
solution method with the goal of obtaining better plans for the vehicles. As previously discussed, the
considered dynamic Dial-A-Ride problem contains a stochastic aspect in the form of the unknown requests
that appear during the day. Hence, requests have to be inserted into existing plans, which may or may
not have been planned flexibly enough so that they can easily accommodate new requests. The idea is
therefore to incorporate historical data to be able to foresee where and when new requests are likely to
appear. This foresight will be incorporated into the offline planning phase to obtain more robust routes.
The literature revealed two different approaches that showed promising results for incorporating antici-
pation strategies based on historical data. The first one was a study done by Schilde et al. (2011). Here,
they implemented a stochastic variable neighbourhood search (S-VNS) to solve a dynamic stochastic
dial-a-ride problem with expected return transports. A similar approach could be implemented for our
ALNS algorithm, where the search is guided by adding an extra step where expected online requests are
tried to be inserted. The cost should be modified to also include how many expected customers will be
rejected. The other study was done by Rigas et al. (2018). Here, multiple solutions are found by solving
for both known and expected online requests simultaneously. The best solution is then evaluated by
fixing the known offline requests and inserting a new set of expected requests. The evaluation is based
on the combined number of unserviced offline requests and the number of unserviced expected online
requests.
Due to the need for short response times in the DDARP when customers call in, and due to the fact
that an important aspect of ALNS is that the heuristic runs many iterations, it is essential that the
online algorithm is not too slow. The implementation of an anticipation strategy might introduce more
computational time, hence leading to less time for exploring the solution space. The implemented online
algorithm already does not reach a lot of iterations for the large instances in the Base method, therefore,
we have chosen to focus on implementing anticipation strategies in the offline phase. However, this should
also impact how good the routes will be in the online phase. We have chosen to implement a similar
approach as in Rigas et al. (2018).
The idea behind our implementation is that it should balance the tight nature of our insertion heuristic
by helping to spread out the solution by creating more space between requests, taking peak hours and
locations into account. This should hopefully create more space for future requests.
9.1
Anticipation Strategy
Due to a lack of real-life and historical data, expected online requests have been generated by drawing
from distributions derived from the Konsentra datasets as described in Section 5. The sampled expected
requests should represent and mimic online requests that will actually occur during the day. The an-
ticipation strategy will be a modification of the offline solution method described in Section 6.
The
modification consists of two steps: A solution generation step and an evaluation step, corresponding to
the two different loops found in Algorithm 4. These two steps will be described below, together with how
to handle potential waiting activities:

Step 1: Solution generation
A solution is generated based on both offline known requests and expected online requests using the
ALNS method described in the offline solution section. This solution is saved for later. All expected
requests are then removed from the solution, and waiting activities are inserted instead as described in
the next point. This is done to be able to insert new sets of expected requests using regret insertion
in the evaluation step. Hereby, from this step, the following two kinds of solutions are obtained: 1) An
initial solution S′i containing the known offline requests, expected online requests and waiting activities.
This solution is potentially being passed to the online phase. 2) A corresponding solution Si containing
only known offline requests and waiting activities to be used for evaluation in the evaluation step.
Handling of waiting activities
When the expected online requests have to be removed to obtain Si, they are replaced by waiting activities.
They will be inserted using a wait-first strategy, meaning that all waiting activities will be located at the
location of the first preceding non-waiting activity and wait there as long as possible before the vehicle
has to drive to the next activity. If there are multiple waiting activities next to each other, then all of
them will be merged into one waiting activity, and the vehicle will wait there as long as possible.
Step 2: Evaluation
To determine how good the solution Si from the solution generation step is at accommodating future
online requests, multiple sets of expected customers are generated. Each of these sets is attempted to be
inserted into the solution using regret insertion. An average performance is calculated from all of these
sets.
In the evaluation step, the solutions are still measured based on the number of rejected customers and
the amount of excess ride time - both for known and expected customers. However, we have let the cost
for not servicing known requests be higher than for not servicing expected requests. This can be seen
from a business perspective as a choice of how many expected requests we will let go to ensure the service
of a request that we know will appear.
Final selection and integration
These steps are performed a number of times to generate multiple different solutions, and the best solution
S∗is chosen based on the average performance obtained from the evaluation step.
In order to keep the idle periods in the routes created with the anticipation strategy, the expected requests
are kept during the online phase, but are again weighted less than the known requests. In this way, it
is ensured that the destroy and repair methods in ALNS do not destroy any potential benefits from the
anticipation strategy during the online phase.

10.4
Discussion
In general, the anticipation methods did not have the desired effect, especially for larger instances.
However, for the instances of size 20 and 100, better performance compared to the Base method was seen
for Anticipation Ib where forecasted requests are identical to online requests. Compared to the larger
instances, this probably helped the smaller instances because the routes are not as tightly packed, so
they have room to include both the expected and actual requests, as supported by the high number of
case 3. Since it is not the desired behaviour that both the expected and actual requests remain in the
solution, it might indicate some issues with the implementation of the solution method. This would have
to be addressed in order to get better performance. Rigas et al. (2018) implemented a similar anticipation
strategy for a different DDARP and only for instances up to 100 requests. They saw a small improvement
for these instances. Hence, the found results in this report do not contradict former found results, but
they might show an issue with the scalability and implementation of this method.
Due to the significantly higher number of unserviced requests for the larger instances for the implemented
anticipation methods when compared to the Base method, the experiments have primarily focused on
identifying elements within the anticipation strategy that affect the performance when combined with
ALNS. The experiments were only conducted on instances of size 300 to save computational time, while
assuming that the results would also be representative for instances of size 500. These showed that it
might be the increased size of requests that made it more difficult to solve. It was found that the following
elements helped increase the performance:
1. Increased ALNS runtime: The introduction of anticipation strategies increased the number of
requests in the offline phase and the number of activities in the online phase, hence increasing
the complexity of the problem. An increased amount of time allocated to ALNS seemed to be
beneficial. Hence, this is important to have in mind when working with problems that have real-life
time constraints. There can be a trade-off between performance improvement and the needed time.
It is, however, also important to notice that increased ALNS runtime might also benefit the Base

method.
2. Guided repair methods: Letting the repair methods prioritise to insert known requests before
expected requests seemed to be beneficial for increasing the performance of the anticipation method.
This might be because the chances of finding the right combination of requests to remove and insert
are too small in the original method. Hence, by inserting the known requests first, ALNS is guided to
begin its search from a solution that satisfies the actual demand rather than the expected demand.
3. Good forecasting: In this project, no interconnections between the components in the data
were considered when the data was generated. Hence, when forecasting requests, both locations
and times should be accurate. This has led to big variations in the expected requests and realised
online requests, hence the available slots created from the anticipation strategy might not have been
exploited in the optimal way they were initially intended. This emphasises the importance of having
good forecasting methods and the potential benefits of being able to exploit the interconnection
between data components.
The effect of adding/tuning the different elements was only tested on top of each other and not indi-
vidually, which could be done in order to test the degree of the effect. Nevertheless, these findings are
good to have in mind for future use and implementations of anticipation strategies. Other elements in
the anticipation method could also have been tuned, such as the amount of expected requests.
Even though the results did not look promising for the considered anticipation method for the larger
instances of the DDARP, considering other DDARPS or modifying the method could make an anticipation
strategy beneficial. We summarise the discussion and the results by suggesting the following four aspects
that could be next in line for investigation:
• Alternative instance: E.g. instances where decisions made in the offline phase are more crucial.
This could, for example, be instances with a higher degree of dynamism and/or less information
known in advance. We suggest the following two types of problem instances for further investiga-
tions:
1. A more dynamic instance (like Instance type II): This would be interesting since the informa-
tion is available later, hence the problem might be more difficult to solve, which might lead to
a bigger potential for improvement. This could be done by removing expected online requests
some time in advance of the current time in the simulation. For the currently implemented
code, this would, however, require sizeable changes, and due to a lack of time, this was not
done.
2. An instance with a more static online phase: In instances I and II, there is still some flexibility
to move the actual pick-up time for requests that have been given a pick-up time. If this were
no longer possible, then the decisions taken in the offline phase would be even more important,
and the anticipation strategy might have a bigger influence.
• Other solution method or modifications to the existing one: E.g. having a heuristic that
directly replaces expected requests with similar online requests during an event.
1. In our implementation of ALNS a Shaw removal heuristic is used as one of the destroy methods.
Assuming a good relatedness measure, Shaw removal should be able to identify and remove
matching expected and actual online requests. By further guiding the repair method to insert

Theory: Waiting Strategies
In this section, we will investigate how waiting strategies can be used to improve the route planning for
the studied DDARP. As described in Section 2, multiple waiting strategies exist, however, we have chosen
to focus on a proactive one.
As mentioned, the DDARP problem is a dynamic problem where some future requests are unknown. To
successfully serve these future requests, a vehicle has to be available within the correct time windows
and be sufficiently close to the pick-up location of a request. If no vehicle is available and sufficiently
close, it will not be possible to serve the considered request. To mitigate this problem, idle vehicles can,
proactively, be relocated. By proactively relocating vehicles, it is more likely that future demand will
appear close to an available vehicle. We investigate and implement two idle vehicle relocation strategies
in the following sections.
11.1
Vehicle Relocation
Vehicle relocation is performed in the following way in both of the presented idle vehicle relocation
strategies.
As described in Section 3.3, a vehicle route is a sequence of requests and waiting activities that are serviced
by the vehicle. The vehicle ends the route at its vehicle depot directly after serving the last activity in
the route. A vehicle k can arrive at its depot Dk before the end of the time window of availability [ak, bk].
To perform idle vehicle relocation, an end-of-route waiting activity is now introduced at the end of all
vehicle routes. The vehicle will always drive directly from an end-of-route waiting activity to its depot.
An example can be seen in Figure 19 where the waiting activity w2 is inserted between the last request
activity d3 in the route and the vehicle depot Dk.
As a default, the end-of-route waiting activity is located at the vehicle depot, such that a vehicle is active
until the end of availability bk but does not drive to any other location after the end-of-route. Vehicles can
be relocated by changing the location of the end-of-route waiting activity. We do not consider relocation
of any other waiting activities in the route, as we expect the route to be packed too tightly to gain
anything from relocating on-route waiting activities.

11.2
Relocation Strategy 1: Demand-based Idle Vehicle Relocation
In this section, we propose an idle vehicle relocation strategy that proactively relocates idle vehicles. The
strategy is inspired by the relocation strategies proposed in Iacobucci et al. (2022) and Ma et al. (2019).
Ma et al. (2019) formulate a ride-sharing problem with transit where passengers can use the ride-sharing
system as door-to-door transportation or as last-mile transportation to/from public transportation transit
stations.
They divide the operational area into zones in which vehicles can be relocated between to
balance the spatial distribution of vehicles. Each of these zones has a centroid at which idle vehicles
can be positioned. To prevent vehicle imbalance between zones, they, among other strategies, propose
a busiest zone idle vehicle relocation strategy where the fleet of vehicles is relocated to zones with the
expected highest demand. Relocation is performed at set relocation intervals, and vehicles are relocated
based on a mixed-integer linear model. In Ma et al. (2019), they assume that requests do not have any
time window constraints, so the problem is seen as a real-time operation.
Iacobucci et al. (2022) formulates a one-way car-sharing problem, where customers book a short-term
car rental.
The formulated problem expects rental requests to appear in real-time.
They propose a
modular multi-stage decision support system that handles fleet imbalance by relocating idle vehicles.
The operational area is divided into zones. Each zone is either a feeder zone with a surplus of vehicles
or a receiver zone with a deficit of vehicles.
The decision-support system consists of three modules:
prediction module, selection module, and schedule module. The prediction module predicts the maximum
inventory imbalance in each zone based on the current inventory levels and the prediction of expected
future demand in each zone. The selection module finds the number of vehicles to relocate between each
feeder/receiver pair based on the predicted inventory imbalance of the system. The selection module
looks into the future based on the set look-ahead horizon. The schedule module schedules the relocation
tasks that are found in the selection module. Vehicle relocation is performed at set relocation intervals.
We propose a 2-step idle vehicle relocation strategy.
In the first step, the predicted vehicle demand
over the entire operational period is estimated. In the second step, vehicles are relocated based on the
predicted vehicle imbalance in each grid cell, where vehicles are relocated to cells that have the highest
deficit of vehicle demand.
11.2.1
Step 1: Demand prediction
In the demand prediction step, the request demand is first predicted across the entire operational period,
and subsequently, the corresponding vehicle demand is predicted. Request demand is defined as a request
56
==PAGE:65==

for pick-up at a certain location at a certain time. The corresponding vehicle demand is the number of
vehicles that should be available to service the request demand.
As described in Section 5.4, the operational area is represented on a grid, where the centre of each cell
acts as a potential vehicle depot. The grid cells are used to discretise the operational area, and future
demand is predicted in each grid cell. Time is also discretised into periods c, each of fixed duration τ.
A time period c begins at time (c −1) · τ and ends at time c · τ. The operational period T is split into
nperiods time periods. The time periods correspond to the relocation intervals.
The granularity of the demand prediction depends on the number of grid cells ngrid × ngrid and the
duration τ of each time period. Large values of ngrid and small values of τ give a higher granularity.
The predicted vehicle demand is directly correlated to the predicted request demand in each grid cell in
each time period. Below, it is described how the predicted request and vehicle demand are determined.
Request demand prediction
To determine the predicted future request demand, historical data of request demand in the operational
area is used. nhist historic instances are used and each instance contains nrequests requests that occur in
the operational period T. It is assumed that the historic instances are all defined in the same operational
period and in the same operational area.
The historic demand is discretised into the defined grid cells and time periods. A demand of 1 corresponds
to a single request for pick-up at a location (latr, longr) at a time tr where tr is the requested pick-up
time of a request r. The request demand prediction is found by computing a simple average over the
discretised request demand in the historic instances. We denote the predicted average request demand
by ¯D where ¯D ∈Rnperiods×ngrid×ngrid.
Vehicle demand prediction
The predicted vehicle demand is found based on the predicted request demand ¯D and the parameter γ,
which is defined in Section 5.4.
To determine the predicted vehicle demand in a grid cell (i, j) in a period c, the predicted request demand
is considered in grid cell (i, j) over a look-ahead horizon [c, c + τl]. τl is the number of periods we look
ahead of the current period. The maximum demand in the cell (i, j) over the look-ahead horizon is
used to predict the vehicle demand. I.e., a worst-case instance is used, as we would rather over-estimate
than under-estimate demand. γ is used as a factor to determine the vehicle demand from the maximum
request demand. So, a predicted request demand of n results in a predicted vehicle demand of ⌈n · γ⌉.
The predicted vehicle demand should be integer values, so the found values are rounded up to the closest
integer value. We denote the predicted vehicle demand by ¯V where ¯V ∈Znperiods×ngrid×ngrid.

In Step 2, idle vehicles are relocated to grid cells where there is the largest predicted deficit of vehicles.
The predicted vehicle imbalance can be found in each grid cell, in each time period, by considering the
predicted vehicle demand found in step 1 and the current solution S.
Given a time period c, all vehicles in S that are active in c are found, and it is determined in which grid
cell they are in. Each grid cell is only counted once, regardless of whether the vehicle serves multiple
activities in the grid cell in the time period c. The predicted vehicle imbalance is found by considering

Relocation Strategy 2: Scoring-based Idle Vehicle Relocation
The idle vehicle relocation strategy presented in the previous section proved not to behave as intended,
as will be explained in Section 12.1.3. The idle vehicle relocation strategy presented in this section is
an attempt to design a vehicle relocation strategy that overcomes the issues found in the previously
presented relocation strategy.
We again propose a 2-step idle vehicle relocation strategy. In the first step, the predicted probability of
a request occurring at a certain location is determined. In the second step, vehicles are relocated to a
new waiting location based on a scoring system.

11.3.1
Step 1: Request location probability prediction
In contrast to the demand prediction presented in 11.2.1, we now only discretise request demand in space
and not in time. nhist historical instances are used and each instance contains nrequests requests. It is
again assumed that each instance occurs in the same operational period and the same operational area.
The pick-up locations of historic requests are discretised onto an ngrid × ngrid grid.
The predicted
probability of a request occurring in each grid cell is estimated by calculating the fraction of total historic
pick-ups that occur in each grid cell. We denote the predicted request location probability ¯P where
¯P ∈Rngrid×ngrid.
11.3.2
Step 2: Relocation scoring and relocation decision
In the proposed idle vehicle relocation strategy, the relocation decision is made based on a scoring system
and the predicted request location probability matrix ¯P. The scoring system tries to determine a new
waiting location for each vehicles that incentivise vehicles to wait at locations where there is a vehicle
deficit and a high probability of requests occurring. Additionally, the scoring system considers the drive
time between potential relocation locations and the location of the activity that is serviced directly before
the end-of-route waiting activity. It is not desired that any vehicles relocate to a waiting location that
requires a lot of driving, since this causes a lot of wasted time when relocating. The score calculation
can be found in Algorithm 8.
Vehicles are, as in strategy 1, relocated sequentially. To determine which waiting location each active
vehicle in a solution S should be relocated to, the matrix A of active vehicles pr. cell is determined as
presented in Section 11.2.2. The score is then calculated for each grid cell in the grid using both ¯P, A
and drive times. The new waiting location is chosen as the grid cell with the highest score.

Results: Waiting Strategies
In this section, two versions of DDARP instances are studied, instance type I and instance type II, as
described in Section 5. We explore the effect of applying Relocation strategy 1 (RS1) and Relocation
strategy 2 (RS2) to the instance types. When we apply a relocation strategy, we solve an instance using
the Base method extended with a relocation strategy. Each time a relocation event occurs in the solution
framework as described in Section 9, vehicles are relocated according to the strategy. Our main focus
is assessing the number of unserviced requests in the solution obtained with the Base method, RS1 and
RS2, since this has the most significant impact on the quality of the obtained solutions.
Across the results, the duration of a time period is set to τ = 30 minutes, and the operational period is
from 6:00 to 23:00. 20 historic request files are used for each instance size, and they are sampled from
the same distributions as the instances we solve. For instances of type I, the vehicle factor is γ = 0.5.
For instances of type II, the vehicle factor is γ = 0.7 as preliminary experiments showed there was not
enough flexibility in the solutions when γ = 0.5.

12.1.3
Conclusion and discussion
As previously discussed, instance type I is not well-suited for evaluating the effectiveness of relocation
strategies, since it is a near-static problem. Furthermore, RS1 also proves to be ineffective in such near-
66
==PAGE:75==
12.1
Results: Relocation Strategy 1
Master Thesis
static settings. Relocation decisions are made far in advance, since only end-of-route waiting activities
are relocated. In type I instances, this leads to multiple relocation events being based on very similar
information, since end-of-route waiting activities are often so far in the future that little new information
on the relevant time periods occurs between relocation events. As a consequence, the relocation decision
for the end-of-route waiting activity of a vehicle might alternate between two waiting locations at each
relocation event. The vehicle does not actually relocate back and forth, so it does not affect the solution,
but it further indicates that instance type I is not suited for applying relocation strategies.
We did not see the expected improvement in the solutions of instance type II, which we explained by a
significant increase in the empty relocation time and a smaller increase in the drive time to the nearest
idle vehicle in solutions found using RS1. These results indicate that not only does RS1 cause a large
amount of empty drive time, but it also does not successfully predict where we expect requests to appear.
An underlying issue is that the predicted request demand is, probably, quite uncertain. Despite utilising
the maximum along a look-ahead horizon, the predicted values remain very small.
The demand is
discretised both spatially and into time-periods, so especially for small instances, the historic request
demand is highly diluted.
The demand-uncertainty can be addressed by adjusting the length of the time period τ, the grid size
and the number of historic request files. Some preliminary experiments varying these parameters were
conducted, for instance type II, but did not show any promising results. We did not continue these
experiments, as RS1 will likely never improve the solution quality of either instance type, as explained
below.
We now make the point that RS1 expects there to be a correlation between the requested pick-up time
of a request and the requested pick-up location. However, our instances have been generated without
any relation between the requested pick-up time and the requested pick-up location. It is probable that
in real life, requests have some relation between pick-up times and pick-up locations, since there might
be patterns of where and when certain types of requests occur. E.g. pick-ups from schools or places of
work are likely to occur in the morning and afternoon peaks. Since we have not considered this in our
data generation, we cannot expect there to be any of these types of patterns in our data. As a result,
the selection of waiting locations becomes kind of arbitrary, since the historic pick-up locations within
a given time period represent a somewhat random distribution rather than meaningful spatial patterns.
In conclusion, Relocation strategy 1 is not suitable to apply to our instances, as we will not correctly
predict the location of future requests.
In contrast, the Base method makes better choices of waiting locations for idle vehicles. As was explained
in Section 5.4, the vehicle depots are sampled based on the location probability of requests. Therefore,
in the Base method, when vehicles are idle at their depot, they are positioned at a location where, across
time, a request is more likely to occur. This is an additional explanation as to why the Base method has
fewer unserviced requests than RS1, for instance type II. We have used this point and the above-presented
results to develop Relocation strategy 2.
In conclusion, the three main points from exploring the results of applying Relocation strategy 1 are
summarised below:
1. Instance type I will not benefit from applying relocation strategies

3. Relocation strategy 1 does not directly consider the drive time in relocation decisions, so a lot of

Conclusion and discussion
The results presented above show that applying Relocation strategy 2 can decrease the number of un-
serviced requests compared to the proposed Base method, particularly for larger instances. We partially
attribute the reduction in the number of unserviced vehicles to the decrease in empty relocation time, as
this is where we see the most significant reduction from the Base method to RS2. However, the decrease
cannot entirely explain the decrease in the number of unserviced requests, and we have not been able to
fully explain the performance improvement.
In the above sections, we have only considered the number of unserviced requests when measuring the
performance of the methods. We did not consider the excess ride time pr. request, as this is our secondary
objective. Furthermore, the excess ride time pr. request is very similar and very small across all methods,
indicating that, in general, there is not a lot of ride-sharing in the solutions. Tables of the excess ride
time pr. request can be seen in Appendix 16.5.2.
We saw that the average drive time to the nearest idle vehicle for requests is not considerably decreased
when applying RS2. Since idle vehicles wait at their depot in the Base method, they wait at locations
where requests are likely to appear, so it is not necessarily unexpected that the decrease in average drive
time to the nearest idle vehicle is not large. RS2 somehow still follows the same trend of letting vehicles
be idle at locations that are a comparable distance away from where requests appear. Since RS2 will
relocate vehicles to be idle at waiting locations that are near previously serviced requests, the waiting
locations also somehow follow the underlying distribution of request locations.
In general, RS2 shows potential to be further developed and refined. We now propose considerations for
further development:
Request location probability predictions
The method we have used to compute the expected location probability of pick-up activities is very
simple, it is an average probability across historic instances. To avoid too much bias from the chosen
historic instances, more sophisticated prediction and forecasting methods could be used.
Tuning period length and size of grid
The choice of period length, τ, and size of the grid, ngrid, influences the obtained solutions. The duration
between relocation events is important in a highly dynamic setting, as the state of a solution is almost
immediately changed, since a request will be serviced shortly after its call time, thus diverting the assigned
vehicle from its previous route. At each relocation event, updated information is used to relocate vehicles,
and it would be interesting to investigate the effect of varying τ. Especially for larger instances, where
requests occur at smaller intervals, changing the solution more between relocation events.
We did not choose a larger ngrid since the prediction of request location probabilities is quite simple, so
by increasing the granularity of the grid, the probability in each cell becomes more uncertain, i.e. we
choose to accumulate probability in a coarser grid to increase the certainty.
Alternative score
RS2 makes relocation decisions based on a score for each potential waiting location. As it is, the score
does not weigh the different factors which influence the waiting location decision. The probability of a
request occurring in a grid cell is divided by the number of active vehicles in the given cell and by the
drive time from the previous activity. Especially, the drive time can be large, so it dominates the score.
The waiting location choice will most often be made among neighbouring grid cells, even if the request

Lastly, we would suggest a deeper study into the differences in behaviour of the Base method and RS2,

Discussion
The core experiments and work of this thesis have been discussed in Sections 10.4, 12.1.3 and 12.2.3.
This section extends the discussion to the overall project.
Notes on chosen KPIs
In this study, we chose the number of unserviced requests as the primary objective, and the excess ride
time of serviced customers as the secondary objective. Both objectives reflect aspects of customer service,
capturing the focus of Dial-a-Ride Problems, where it is a central element. However, choosing the excess
ride time as a secondary objective caused a lot of unforeseen challenges. We did not find much literature
that considered this objective when proposing insertion heuristics.
The existing literature proposed
heuristics that would be too extensive to implement, considering the given time frame and main focus
of the thesis. As a result, we spent much time designing the proposed insertion heuristic. Furthermore,
the use of excess ride time as an objective had a big impact on our ALNS implementation. The excess
ride time objective does not allow for delta calculations, as the objective requires knowledge of the start
of service time for all requests in a route. This entails that for each possible insertion in a route, we
need to construct the route from scratch to find the resulting start of service times. This prevents the
use of early termination strategies in the repair methods and construction heuristic, which significantly
increases the computational time of ALNS. If we had chosen another metric, e.g. total driven distance,
insertions could be evaluated more efficiently by evaluating the delta distance increase of an insertion.
Additionally, the proposed DDARP does not consider any kind of operational cost in the objective.
In practical applications, operational costs, such as driven distance and driver salaries, would almost
certainly be factored into the planning of routes.
Mismatch in design of insertion heuristic and decision strategies
As described in Section 6.1, the insertion heuristic was generally designed to remove or minimise waiting
activities in routes. This choice was made early in the project, with the aim of improving the service
level. Unnecessary idle time in a route can increase the excess ride time of customers, which we wanted to
avoid. However, this design created several challenges when we later implemented the decision strategies.
Both the anticipation and waiting strategies rely on certain waiting activities being present in routes, but
as a default, the insertion heuristic would try to remove these waiting activities. As a result, we had to
implement several workarounds in the insertion heuristic to avoid removing waiting activities that were
placed as part of the decision strategies. This mismatch between the design of the insertion heuristic and
the logic of the decision strategies ended up being a recurring challenge throughout the project. In future
work, we would suggest aligning the insertion heuristic with the chosen decision strategies.
Considerations and notes on data generation
Throughout the project, the instance generation has presented several challenges. It has been difficult to
determine which characteristics to include in the generated instances. We received data from Konsentra,
but the data sets did not contain crucial elements, such as call times and descriptions of the vehicle fleet.
Additionally, the number of requests we used as a basis was very small, as we made the assumption that
school children’s transportation was not suited for the studied problem. Throughout the work presented
in this thesis, it has been difficult to recognise when our methods were ineffective or whether the generated
instances were wrongfully designed.
We furthermore had to make assumptions about the number of available vehicles for each instance size.
76
==PAGE:85==
Master Thesis
As shown in Section 8, the number of available vehicles significantly affects the number of unserviced
requests. Having access to real vehicle data would have allowed us to evaluate the quality of our solutions
under more realistic constraints and provided a stronger basis for comparison.
It is worth noting that the DDARP considered in this thesis is a simplified version of the real-world
problem. Only pick-up requests were considered, and all customers were assumed to be homogeneous
in terms of load and service time. While the current solution framework could be extended to support
heterogeneous customers, doing so would require further adjustments to the implementation.
Finally, Relocation strategy 1 and the anticipation strategies rely on a relationship between the pick-up
location of a request and its requested time. This relationship was not present in the generated instances
used in this thesis. For future work, we recommend generating data that includes such a pattern or
using real-life data where it naturally occurs. In general, we assume that proactive strategies are likely
to perform best when applied to problems where trends and patterns can be identified. If no trends or
patterns are present in a problem, it is difficult to anticipate future behaviour or occurrences of requests.
One pattern could be the difference in the characteristics of online and offline requests. In our instance
generation, we attempted to reflect this by using different distributions for the request times. However,
in practice, the location of requests may also vary depending on whether they are made in advance or
closer to the desired pick-up time.
Combining anticipation and waiting strategies
In this project, we did not have time to combine any of the proposed anticipation and waiting strategies.
The initial intention was to integrate the two types of strategies and investigate the combined effect on the
performance of the solution framework. However, since the initial decision strategies did not result in the
expected improvements, the focus was shifted towards developing and testing the strategies separately.
The anticipation strategies were included in the offline phase, and the waiting strategies were included in
the online phase of the solution framework, so the two types of strategies should complement each other,
and hopefully, lead to a reduction in the number of unserviced requests. Especially in the highly dynamic
instances used to evaluate the waiting strategies, applying an anticipation strategy in the offline phase
could have helped better accommodate future requests.
Based on the experiments and results from this thesis, it is clear that the design of decision strategies
should be highly dependent on the considered problem and desired behaviour of the planned routes.
Whether the main objective is service or cost-oriented will greatly influence which types of strategies
should be implemented. The effectiveness and relevance of decision strategies are highly dependent on
the operational context, as well as the characteristics and quality of available data.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated the use of proactive decision strategies, anticipation and waiting, in the context
of dynamic Dial-a-Ride Problems (DDARPs), with the objective of improving customer service levels.
The study includes a review of the DDARP literature and a formal problem description inspired by real-
world systems. This is followed by the development of a two-phase solution framework tailored to the
dynamic setting. A mathematical model was formulated to capture the key constraints and objectives of
the DDARP. Two sets of problem instances were generated using limited real-world data.
The core of the thesis was the design and implementation of the two-phase solution framework. The offline
phase plans routes for known (offline) requests while the online phase dynamically inserts new (online)
requests into routes as they arrive. Both phases are planned using Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search
(ALNS). A construction heuristic and a tailored insertion heuristic were developed as the basis of ALNS.
Within this solution framework, different variants of an anticipation strategy were applied in the offline
phase by inserting expected future requests into the initial plan. Two different waiting strategies were
implemented in the online phase, relocating idle vehicles to wait in better locations. Both strategy types
were tested against the base method of the two-phase solution framework.
While the implemented anticipation strategies did not lead to a significant reduction in the number of
unserviced requests, the work provided useful insights into the challenges of applying such strategies within
an ALNS framework. In particular, the interaction between anticipation and the solution framework
caused several issues that would need to be addressed in future implementations. The proposed waiting
strategies also did not show improvements when applied to the base problem, which proved to be too
static for relocation strategies to be effective. However, in highly dynamic instance types, we saw that
a scoring-based relocation strategy could reduce the number of unserviced requests. This suggests that
such strategies are beneficial, especially when request uncertainty is high and quick response times are
crucial. Overall, the results point to the importance of aligning decision strategies with the characteristics
of the problem and give a foundation for further work in more realistic operational settings. As a result
of having to generate data with limited prior knowledge and within time constraints, this project should
be viewed as a conceptual investigation into decision strategies. Despite these limitations, the findings
are still valuable, as they highlight a range of important considerations for implementing anticipation or
waiting strategies in an operational Demand-Responsive Transport system such as Flextrafik.
