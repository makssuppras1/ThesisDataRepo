Abstract
Modern wind turbines are often down regulated to optimise the performance of a wind
farm in terms of power gain and loads reduction. Wind turbine is a nonlinear system.
Controller parameters are designed based on a gain-scheduling mechanism, overcoming
the nonlinear aerodynamic properties of the turbine. Typically, the controller parame-
ters are well tuned based on the aerodynamic gain. However, in down regulation, the
aerodynamic properties vary, depending on the derating strategies. Most of existing stud-
ies investigate the load impact of down regulation but overlooked the control parameter.
Moreover, fatigue damage of turbine components is typically computed by running a rain-
ﬂow counting algorithm on the load signals of the components. This process is not linear,
thus, it is non-trivial for a fast wind farm control optimisation. To compensate these
limitations, this work will investigate the load impact of down-regulation strategies with
the re-tuned parameters and develop a surrogate model that will contribute in this fast
wind farm control optimisation.
In the ﬁrst part of the study, for three diﬀerent down regulation methods, the aero-servo-
elastic stability tool HAWCStab2 was used to calculate the control parameters of DTU
Wind Energy controller. For these new control parameters, the lifetime fatigue loads were
investigated. By comparing the results between the reference and the re-tuned control
parameters, the favorable control parameters were selected. For these control parameters
the eﬀect in lifetime fatigue and extreme loads of the down regulation strategies on DTU
10MW reference wind turbine, was analysed. Furthermore, a suggestion of the best down
regulation strategy for DTU 10MW wind turbine was made, and the eﬀect of this speciﬁc
down regulation suggestion in lifetime fatigue and extreme loads of a small wind farm was
evaluated.
In the second part of the study, several surrogate models representing the short term
damage equivalent loads and electrical power with respect to various wind conditions and
down regulation set-points were created with the help of Machine Learning. More specif-
ically, artiﬁcial neural network, linear regression and Gaussian process regression models
were chosen as the surrogate models. In conclusion, a statistical evaluation for perfor-
mance diﬀerences between the models was conducted, using the correlated t-test method
for cross-validation.

Introduction
1.1
Wind Turbine operation
Over the past twenty years, variable-speed pitch regulation is the most dominant control
system among the installed wind turbines. In this regulation, PI-regulators are used to
adjust rotor speed and electrical power through the collective pitch angle and generator
torque. As it is known, the power coeﬃcient Cp(λ, θ) is generally a function of the tip
speed ratio λ and pitch angle θ. By applying variable speed on a pitch-regulated rotor,
it is possible to operate the turbine at the optimum point where λ = λopt, θ = θopt and
Cp = Cmax
p
. For diﬀerent types of wind turbines, diﬀerent curves of power, rotational
speed and pitch angle, in respect with wind speed, can be obtained. For this study, the
10MW DTU wind turbine was selected to be the reference wind turbine. To gain a general
perspective about how a variable-speed pitch regulation can be implemented, DTU Wind
Energy controller was selected as the regulation tool.
The Basic DTU Wind Energy controller [1], which features both partial and full load
operation, typically works in three separated regions and these regions are shown in
Figure 1 and described below [2].
Figure 1 – Diﬀerent operating regions for DTU 10MW reference wind turbine.
• Strategy in Region 1: Keep pitch angle at optimum and use generator torque to
obtain optimal tip speed ratio in a torque equilibrium.
• Strategy in Region 2: Use generator torque and pitching to regulate rotor speed
to the rated speed.
• Strategy in Region 3: Regulate rotor speed by pitching and use generator torque
to regulate power either as constant power, or constant torque.

1.3
Down regulation control methods
In the nominal operation of a wind turbine, as it is shown in Figure 1, the rotational speed
has its rated value before power reaches its rated value. As a result, there is a region where
the rotational speed is constant and the goal in this region is to maximize the generated
power.
In the case of down regulation of a wind turbine, below a certain value, the
desired power output is reached, for a wind speed and rotational speed, below rated. A
control objective for a down regulation is to keep the power output constant. However,
there are many control strategies that show how to keep the power output constant and
how to choose the new operating point of the wind turbine.
The most common are:
maximum rotor speed Max-Ω[3], constant rotor speed Const-Ω[4] and minimum thrust
coeﬃcient Min-CT [5]. In this section, an analytical explanation of how each strategy can
be implemented is presented.

2.4
Discussion
From Figure 8 it is clear that the range of the load of the new gains is higher than the
reference gains. This will cause the lifetime fatigue load to have higher value for the new
control parameters than the reference. As already mentioned in Section 2.1 the transient
of the dynamic system is faster for the reference control parameters. In order to explain
this behaviour of the controller, and by using the new calculated PI gains (Table 1) from
HAWCStab2, a recalculation of the natural frequency in partial load operation was made.
For Region 2, the natural frequency for the reference and the new control parameters was
found ωΩ= 0.0394 Hz and ωΩ= 0.0498 Hz, respectively. This result, coincides with the
behaviour of the system in Figure 6, where the response of the new control parameters is
faster than the reference control parameters. It is known that higher natural frequency,
results in faster response of the system. When designing the wind turbine controller, it is
desired to choose smaller natural frequency of the controller than the lowest frequency of
the wind turbine system, which is usually the tower frequency.
In this case, the recalculated value of natural frequency for the new control parame-
ters was higher than the reference. As a result, the natural frequency for the new control
parameters is closer to the ﬁrst tower mode (0.25 Hz) and therefore, more oscillations,
bigger overshoots and higher lifetime fatigue loads occurred in steady and turbulent cases.
Further investigation should be done to improve the aero-servo-elastic stability tool when
applying diﬀerent down-regulation strategies.

3.1
Lifetime fatigue loads
Figure 9 shows the normalized lifetime fatigue loads for all the down regulation strategies
compared to normal operation. As it is shown, for Max-Ωthe lifetime fatigue loads are
reduced for YBroll and MBTor, whereas in the other load channels the diﬀerences are
negligible. For Const-Ωthe lifetime fatigue loads are reduced for BRﬂap, YBtilt, YBroll,
YByaw and MBtor, while BRedge, which is mainly driven by gravity, is not aﬀected. It
is important to be noticed that the fatigue load of TBSS has increased closely to 12%
compared to the normal operation.
For the last down regulation method Min-CT, a
decrease in BRﬂap, YBtilt and YByaw is observed. On the contrary, TBFA, TBSS and
MBtor are increased. More indubitable is the increase in TBSS lifetime fatigue load with
28% diﬀerence from the normal operation load. By looking at these diﬀerences in lifetime
fatigue loads in TBFA and TBSS moments, the time series and power spectra density
PSD at 10 m/s for both channels were plotted in Figure 10 and Figure 11. The time
series of both channels show that high oscillations occur in Min-CT down regulation in
comparison with the other down regulation strategies. At 10 m/s, the rotational speed for
Const-Ωand Min-CT down regulation is ω = 0.81 rad/s and ω = 0.725 rad/s respectively.
The ﬁrst tower frequency of DTU 10MW wind turbine is located at 0.25 Hz [2]. The
3P frequency of the wind turbine in normal operation is at 0.4 Hz. The 3P frequency
for Const-Ωand Min-CT down regulation is 0.38 and 0.346 Hz. As these frequencies are
closer to the ﬁrst tower frequency, the tower mode is excited and higher amplitudes, as it
is shown in Figure 10 and Figure 11, are observed. As previous studies have shown [7],
if 10MW DTU wind turbine was derated at more than 95% the oscillation amplitudes in
TBSS and TBFA and the lifetime fatigue loads are higher. Furthermore, the excited tower
side-to-side vibration couples with the drive train torsional mode, leading to higher drive
train torsional oscillation, which is closely related to the main bearing torsional moment
MBTor and the yaw bearing roll moment YBRoll. As a result, in these load channels, an
increase in the lifetime fatigue loads is observed.

3.3
Discussion and comparison of methods
The results show that the Min-CT and Const-Ωdown regulation strategies might result
in a sub-optimal performance or poor robustness due to the fact that there is possibility
of exciting the tower ﬁrst modes. DTU 10MW wind turbine is designed to operate at a
narrow rotor speed range of 6 to 9.6 rpm [2]. For these two down regulation strategies,
in order to avoid this phenomena, a rotor speed exclusion should be implemented in
control algorithm to avoid resonance [7]. For Max-Ωdown regulation strategy the results
show that the lifetime fatigue loads and extreme loads can only decrease. Furthermore,
since rotational speed is at its rated value, Max-Ωis favorable for inertial response of
the system. Since DTU 10MW wind turbine is designed to operate at narrow rotational
speeds, Max-Ωis the suggested down regulation strategy in this study.

Eﬀect of down regulation strategy on a wind farm
The use of down-regulation control strategies for wind turbines can potentially optimize
the wind farm performance. This section investigates the lifetime fatigue loads when oper-
ating turbines in a wind farm, using the aforementioned favorable control strategy, as well
as extreme loads and energy production. The control strategy was again, implemented

Dynamic Wake Meandering DWM model in HAWC2 is used so that the wake deﬁcits gen-
erated from the upstream turbines realistically represent the down regulation strategies,
as well as the interactions between wakes. The DWM model complex is constructed by
three namely essential models. These three models are: model of the quasi-steady wake
deﬁcit, a stochastic model of the down stream wake meandering process and ﬁnally, a
model of the added wake turbulence [12]. The model complex is illustrated in Figure 24.
4.1
Simulation and wind farm setup
In this analysis, again six diﬀerent turbulent cases were simulated with turbulence in-
tensity of Iref = 0.16. The wind turbine class was IA. The representative turbulence
intensity Irep was calculated from Equation 30. A simpliﬁed wind farm layout with two
turbines in a row was formulated, with a space distance that of four rotor diameters (4D)
in length. This wind farm represents a tight wind farm layout similar to the Lillgrund
wind farm. The down regulation strategy of the upstream wind turbine was Max-Ω, since
it was chosen as the favorable control strategy from previous fatigue and extreme load
analysis. The simulation time was 600 s for each wind speed and the wind speed range
was from 4 m/s to 26 m/s in 2 m/s increments. The ambient wind direction was set to
zero degrees, which means that yaw misalignment was not considered. Two cases were
simulated. In the ﬁrst case, the upstream wind turbine was down regulated to produce
95% of available power where the downstream wind turbine operated at 100% available
power. In the second case, both upstream and downstream wind turbines, operated at
100% available power.

For the calculation of lifetime fatigue loads, similar procedure was followed as in Section
2.3.Figure 14 shows that there are no major diﬀerences between the fatigue loads of the
ﬁrst and second case. The lifetime fatigue loads were normalized with respect to the loads
of the second case where the upstream and the downstream wind turbines were in normal
operation. It is known that wind turbines, in the wake of another turbine, have a higher
lifetime damage equivalent loads than they were designed [7]. In Figure 14 the lifetime
fatigue loads for the downstream wind turbines in both cases, are shown. It can be seen
that the lifetime fatigue loads in the ﬁrst case were slightly lower than the second case.
When the upstream wind turbine is down regulated, it produces a more uniform wind
ﬂow to the downstream wind turbine compared to the normal operation, where no down
regulation occurs. As a result, less lifetime fatigue loads are observed in the downstream
wind turbine in the ﬁrst case.

SURROGATE MODELS
5
Surrogate models
The aim of the present section is to deﬁne and demonstrate a simple procedure that maps
the outputs of the short-term damage equivalent loads Seq,ST and electrical power of a
wind turbine in a wind farm, in computationally eﬃcient surrogate models approxima-
tions. The resulting quick load assessment provides continuous, diﬀerentiable output and
can be used for applications such as wind turbine layout optimization, estimation of tur-
bine lifetime, and structural reliability analysis [13]. The scientiﬁc challenge of surrogate
modeling is the generation of a surrogate model that is as accurate as possible, using as
few simulation evaluations as possible. In order to develop an accurate surrogate model,
the use of Machine Learning algorithms is unavoidable. Given these points, three diﬀerent
types of surrogate models were developed for the representation of the short term DELs
for each load channel and three for the electrical power. More speciﬁcally, linear regres-
sion, artiﬁcial neural network and Gaussian process regression were the types of models
that were selected. The load channels that were used are: blade root ﬂapwise BRﬂap,
blade root edgewise BRedge, blade root torsional BRtor, tower bottom fore-aft TBFA,
tower bottom side-side TBSS, tower bottom torsional TBtor, yaw bearing tilt YBtilt, yaw
bearing roll YBroll, yaw bearing yaw YByaw, main bearing tilt MBtilt, main bearing yaw
MByaw, main bearing torsional MBtor moment. Thirty nine models were trained and
tested in total for the purpose of this study.
5.1
Simulations setup
For this part of the study, simulations were performed again with the use of DTU 10MW
reference turbine and the aero-elastic simulation code, HAWC2. Similar inputs with the

There are two potential issues in linear regression. If X contains many linearly depen-
dent rows or the number of features M is large relative to the number of the samples N,
the linear regression model can overﬁt. To avoid developing an overﬁtting model, a gen-

with λ = 0 which corresponds to Equation 43. This form of regularization term is com-

regression. In general, when the regularization λ is small, the models have high variance
and low bias. When λ is large, the models have low variance but high bias. As a rule,
varying λ to search for an optimal value of the generalization error will lead to better
models.
For the purpose of this study, the regularization constant was tweaked from

The ﬁnal model implemented in this study to predict the short term DELs for each load
channel and the electrical power is called Gaussian process regression model. A Gaussian
process is a random process in which any point x ∈Rd is assigned a random variable f(x)
and the joint distribution of a ﬁnite number of these variables p(f(x1), . . . , f(xN)) is itself

Kij = κ(xi, xj) is a positive deﬁnite kernel function or covariance function [17]. Given
these points, a Gaussian process is a distribution over functions whose shape is deﬁned by
a positive deﬁnite kernel function or covariance function K. In general terms, a kernel or
covariance function speciﬁes the statistical relationship between two points xi, xj in the
input space; that is, how markedly a change in the value of the Gaussian process at xi
correlates with a change in the Gaussian process at xj. If points xi and xj are considered
to be similar by the kernel or covariance, the function values at these points, f(xi) and
f(xj), can be expected to be similar too. For the purpose of this study, the squared
exponential kernel, also known as Gaussian kernel or RBF kernel was used:

then zA is better than zB, if the conﬁdence interval is all positive. In this speciﬁc study,
z describes the mean squared error diﬀerence between the two models.
By looking at Table 6 and Table 7, it can be derived that the Gaussian process regression
GPR model has the best performance. The conﬁdence intervals do not include zero and
therefore there is indication that diﬀerences occur between the tested models. Since the
conﬁdence interval for all the channels is positive and z = zA −zB describes the mean
square error, the error for GPR is lower than the other two models. Moreover, the p value
for all the channels is really small compared to α = 0.05 and as a result, the performance
of the model is unlikely to change. For the comparison of the other two models, it can be
seen that linear regression outperforms ANN since the conﬁdence interval for all channels
is positive. Like before, this behaviour is unlikely to change since the p values are really

CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
6
Conclusions and future work
This study was divided in two parts. In the ﬁrst part of the study, the results showed
that the lifetime fatigue loads for the new control parameters are higher than the ref-
erence control parameters, in most of the cases, for all down-regulation methods. This
deterioration of the system’s behaviour is due to diﬀerences in the natural frequency ωΩ
of the system. For the new control parameters the natural frequency was selected higher
than the reference and therefore closer to the tower frequency, which leads to higher life-
time fatigue loads. Further investigation must be done to the control tuning method of
aero-servo-elastic stability tool HAWCStab2, or changes in the implementation steps in
Appendix A in order to obtain better control parameters that will improve the overall
behaviour of the dynamic system.
Since the dynamic system’s performance deteriorated with the new control parameters,
the reference control parameters were selected as the favourable option to investigate the
eﬀect of the down regulation strategies in lifetime fatigue and extreme loads on DTU
10MW reference wind turbine. The results showed that Min-CT and Const-Ωdown regu-
lation strategies might result in a sub-optimal performance or poor robustness, due to the
fact that there is possibility of exciting the tower ﬁrst modes. For Max-Ωdown regula-
tion strategy, the results show that the lifetime fatigue loads and extreme loads can only
decrease. From a load analysis point of view, Max-Ωis the suggested down regulation
strategy for future down regulation use in DTU 10MW reference wind turbine.
The implementation of Max-Ωas a down regulation strategy in a small wind farm in
the upstream wind turbines, resulted in an overall decrease of 1.289% in the AEP of the
wind farm. On the other hand, a small decrease in the lifetime fatigue and extreme loads
in the upstream wind turbines, might compensate the loss in AEP by extending the life-
time of the wind farm.
In the second part of the study, the results showed that between several types of sur-
rogate models, the Gaussian process regression was the best ﬁtted model. As a matter of
fact, the Gaussian process regression model had the lowest value of normalized root mean
square error in all predicted targets. Moreover, the robustness of the model was veriﬁed
by testing the model to entirely new data. Further investigation and expansion of the
model is needed in order to include more input data and predict more complicated tasks.
More speciﬁcally, the surrogate model of this study take as input data only one down reg-
ulation strategy (Min-CT) and two rows of wind turbines in a distance of four diameters.
A potential expansion of the model would be to include all the down regulation methods
with four rows of wind turbines and with possible distances of four, ﬁve or six diameters
between them. In addition, more down regulation set-points can be inserted in the input
data. It is important to be mentioned that the Gaussian process regression method can
lose eﬃciency when the number of attributes exceeds a few dozens and a diﬀerent model
must be selected if the input data-set is large. In that case, with a big input data-set,
artiﬁcial neural networks is the suggested type of model that is expected to have high
performance in predicting this speciﬁc output parameters.
