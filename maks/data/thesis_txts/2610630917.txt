Abstract
As the demand of renewable energy is increasing new technologies emerge. O↵shore ﬂoat-
ing wind turbines (OFWTs) are a new and largely unexplored technology providing an
alternative to bottom-ﬁxed o↵shore wind turbines. This provides accessibility to deep-
water sites where bottom-ﬁxed turbines are no longer feasible. One of the many challenges
of OFWTs is that they exhibit rigid six degree of freedom motion as response to wind
and wave loading. This in turn leads to unsteady aerodynamic performance and wake
distortions. Furthermore, they reside in the turbulent atmospheric boundary adding to
the complexity of the system. In this work we seek to shed light on this unexplored topic
using the open source CFD tool OpenFOAM. This is accomplished by implementation and
validation of the neutral boundary layer and development of a new six degree of freedom
actuator disk method.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
A review of wind energy
Since the industrial era began in the 1800 vast amounts of green house gases have been
emitted into the atmosphere from the use of fossil fuels as an energy source. The heavy
emissions of greenhouse gases have resulted in climate change with widespread impacts
on ecosystems worldwide [35]. In the recent years climate change mitigation, adaption
and ﬁnance have been highly prioritized on the political agenda. As of February 2020
all 196 members of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UN-
FCCC) signed the Paris agreement [2]; a long term goal of keeping the average increase in
temperature below 2 °C. In 2014 the Intergovernmental Panel of Climate Change (IPCC)
appointed wind energy one of the best alternatives of renewable energy sources to mitigate
the risks associated climate changes.
Utilization of wind energy ﬁrst appeared around 600 AD as wind-mills used for irrigation
purposes and to grind grain [17]. When electricity came into use in the early 19th century
wind-mills became what we know today as wind turbines as the rotor were connected to
an electrical generator supported by the U.S. Marshall Plan in the aftermath of WWII.
One of the very early pioneers of wind energy is the Danish scientist and inventor Poul la
Cour who was among the ﬁrst to connect a wind-mill to an electric generator [14]. Poul
la Cour’s passion for wind energy also lead him to teach courses for ”wind electricians”
at Askov folk high school where one of his students, Johannes Juul, became of special im-
portance for the later development of wind turbines. The innovative Gedser wind turbine
(200kW) depicted in Fig. 1.1 was built by Johannes Juul in 1956-1957 for the electricity
company SEAS. The Gedser wind turbine was an utterly innovative and pioneering design
that made it the worlds most powerful turbine of its time. Amongst other innovative
features, the Gedser turbine had electro-mechanical yawing, controlled by stall-regulation
and had aerodynamic emergency tip-breaks invented by Johannes Juul (seen on the lower

1.3
Turbulence and turbulence modelling
Turbulent ﬂows as opposed to smooth laminar ﬂows are the stochastic and random motion
in a ﬂow of ﬂuids (gases and liquids). A laminar ﬂow will transition to turbulent when
the non-dimensional Reynolds number (Eq. 1.3.1) exceeds a critical value of Re ⇡2000.
Turbulent ﬂows are random in the sense that unavoidable and minute di↵erences in the
initial ﬂow state give rise to unique ﬂow characteristics and hence it is di↵erent in every
realization. They are stochastic since ﬂuctuations about the mean are varying irregularly
in both space and time in each of these realizations.
Around 1508-1513 Leonardo Da Vinci was one of the ﬁrst who depicted the 3-dimensional
nature of water ﬂows from observations (see Fig. 1.6). Turbulent ﬂows consist of coherent
structures referred to as eddies‡ existing in the velocity ﬁeld. Later this range of scales
was mathematically formalised by Kolmogorov in 1941 [23] which laid the foundation of
how turbulence is understood today.

The complex nature of turbulent ﬂows makes them mathematically intractable and hence
the study of turbulence is strongly rooted in use of observations. Nonetheless, the modern
era and advancing computer technology made it possible to study turbulence by means
of solving di↵erential equations numerically. Some of the ﬁrst numerical simulations are
attributed to Deardor↵[12] using a Large Eddy Simulation (LES∗) and Orszag and Pat-
terson [34] who made a Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS).†
In this study modelling is conducted by means of the Reynolds Averaged Navier–Stokes
approach as ﬁrst derived by Reynolds [40]. Both steady and unsteady Reynolds averaged
Navier–Stokes simulations are performed in this thesis referred to as RANS and URANS,
respectively. These equations are presented and brieﬂy discussed in section 1.4 along with
the closure problem of turbulence.
The highest ﬁdelity simulations method of the above mentioned varieties are the DNS
followed by LES (with hybrid methods in between) and then ﬁnally RANS. A comparative
study for RANS versus LES simulations for wind turbine wake studies in atmospheric
turbulence by [56] showed that generally LES is a factor 103 more expensive measured in
CPU hours. The larger expense for LES simulations arise since the eddies are explicitly
resolved for some speciﬁed part of the turbulence spectra.
In DNS the full spectra is
resolved and thus the cost is accordingly larger. The computational cost is related to the
Reynolds number which in turn is related to the range of scales as ⌘/` ⇠Re3/4, ⌘being
the smallest scales of turbulence. High Reynolds number ﬂows consists of a large range of
scales and is thus associated with larger computational cost. In the atmospheric boundary
layer the Reynolds number, Eq. (1.3.1) is typically on the order of Re ⇠107 −109 [60],
making it impossible to use DNS and very expensive to use LES. In RANS simulations
the whole spectrum of eddies is modelled which is a decrease in ﬁdelity level, however, at
the gain of a appreciably lower computational cost in terms of CPU time. This feature
makes RANS simulations a very feasible method to model atmospheric turbulence.

1.5
The atmospheric boundary layer
ricBoundary}
Wind turbines reside in the atmospheric boundary layer, which in general have very com-
plex, unsteady and in-homogeneous ﬂow. It is almost always turbulent and capped by
a temperature inversion aloft; it is a boundary to the free atmosphere where turbulence
cease to exists.
The ABL is the bottom layer of the troposphere where the air is in contact with the
surface of earth and hence inﬂuenced by it. Its depth (or thickness) ranges from tens of
meters in a stable stratiﬁed atmospheric boundary layer (SBL) to several kilometers in
the convective boundary layer (CBL). As depicted in Fig. 1.7 a diurnal cycle along with
surface properties§ governs the stability conditions of the ABL resulting in a CBL during
daytime when surface heating causes a positive heat ﬂux and vice versa at night when the
surface is cooler than the air. The ABL responds to such changes in a matter of an hour
or less (Roland [44]) and it is thus generally unsteady and in-homogeneous. In this work
however, we shall make the assumptions of quasi-steadiness and horizontally homogeneity
of the ﬂow. The assumptions are treated in detail later.

1.6
Wind turbine wakes
WindTurbineWakes}
When erecting multiple wind turbines, the inter-turbine spacing is important since the
kinetic energy in the wind extracted by upstream turbines in wind farms and produces
a momentum deﬁcit that will a↵ect downstream turbines from wake interaction. This in
turn leads to lower power production of up to as much as 20% [25]. Furthermore, upstream
wind turbines introduces additional turbulent kinetic energy in the ﬂow downstream which
causes higher fatigue loading on the downstream turbines [45]. A depiction of wind and
wind turbine wake is seen in Fig. 1.8.
Figure 1.8: A sketch of a wind turbine wake in the ABL by [45].
{fig:Sanderse
The near wake is a very complex region with vortices being shed of the blade tips and
convected downstream in circular motions. This region is largely dictated by the thrust
force exerted on the ﬂow by the wind turbine; a shear layer is formed from large velocity
gradients seen in ﬁg. 1.8. This region grows and thickens downstream due to turbulent
mixing between the low-speed wake ﬂow with the higher speed ambient ﬂow around it.
In the far wake region the atmospheric turbulence make the shear layer disintegrate into
smaller turbulence length scales with di↵usion processes dominating the wake recovery.
Further discussion of wind turbine wakes are found in [45].

The numerical investigations of this thesis is carried out in OpenFOAM [33]. It is an open

present work. OpenFOAM takes a ﬁnite volume approach to solve ﬂuid ﬂow problems in a
cell-centered grid. It can be broken down in three modules as pre-processing, solving and

2. Solving: Setting residual controls for the ﬁelds to be solved, setting relaxation factors,
choosing numerical schemes and choosing solving algorithms
3. Post-processing: Sampling and interpolation of ﬁeld solution data, visualizing data
and calculating properties of ﬁelds.

rithm by [9] is used for incompressible and steady state problems and the PIMPLE algorithm
which is a combination of the PISO by [20] and SIMPLE algorithms in OpenFOAM is used for
unsteady incompressible ﬂow problems.

Practical and numerical experimental setup
A 3-bladed model wind turbine (GWS/EP-6030x3) was placed in a wind-tunnel where the

z0 = 0.03 · 10−3m was used and corresponds to a friction velocity of u⇤= 0.106 m/s . The

Turbulence model test
In the present section several modiﬁed k-✏based models are tested. The reason for only
testing the ✏based models is that the NBL is modelled by boundary conditions based
on the Richards and Hoxey approach. This approach uses the standard k-✏[43] model
and explains how the turbulence model constants must be in equilibrium with the NBL
by satisfying the ✏-transport equations. The numerical setup as described in the earlier
sections remains ﬁxed in terms of grid resolution.
Two extensions to the standard k-✏are tested and were developed to improve the standard
closure model. One is based on Schwartz inequalities (details in [39]) namely the Realizable
k-✏and the other is based on re-normalization group theory and is formally known as RNG
k-✏described in [61].
The comparison of the turbulence models by mean stream-wise
velocity proﬁle u is shown in ﬁgure 3.4.
Due to the non-uniform incoming ﬂow ﬁeld, the wake is non-axisymmetric with a larger
momentum deﬁcit at and slightly above the hub height (in between the two black dashed
lines). Evidently the logarithmic inﬂow proﬁles is thus strongly a↵ecting the resulting
wake. A qualitative comparison of the velocity ﬁeld and turbulence intensity is shown in

upper tip of the rotor and it is therefore expected that the highest production of turbulent
kinetic energy is present in this region as also seen in ﬁg. 3.6.

turbine induction is extending further upstream than what is measured. The e↵ect is
also seen on the contour plot in ﬁg. 3.5. As expected the momentum deﬁcit is largest in

simulation of the present work under-predicts the momentum deﬁcit which is due to the
implementation of the actuator disk, where no tangential forces are applied, and hence no
rotation is occurring in the wake. In the near wake the e↵ects of rotation are important and

larger pressure does in turn lead to higher momentum deﬁcit because of decreased velocity.

3.5
Summary and conclusions of Part I
{sec:summaryV
This section concludes on the ﬁndings and pitfalls as discovered in the veriﬁcation study.
The main ﬁndings of this chapter are summarized:
The empty neutral boundary layer
• The implementation of the empty NBL was in good agreement with the analytical
expression of the log-law. The [19] using the commercial CFD codes Fluent and
CFX. The problem of decaying proﬁles downstream a long empty fetch was alleviated
following the Richards and Hoxey approach [43] with the implementation of the NBL
using the boundary conditions as listed in Table 3.1.
• The wall function approach gives discrepancies to the log-law proﬁles near the ground
since it is based on smooth wall types or sand-grain size roughness elements. The wall
boundary conditions should be revisited in accordance with atmospheric boundary
layer modelling.
However, for small roughness lengths the wall-functions should
perform reasonably.
Single ﬁxed wind turbine in the NBL
• An appropriate scaling of the force is needed if thrust and/or power coeﬃcients are
based on free-stream velocity when probing ﬂow velocity inside the induction region.
• It was shown that the k-✏model fails to predict the momentum deﬁcit in especially
the near wake region. This is attributed to its over-dissipative properties in regions
of large mean strain of the ﬂow. This e↵ect give rise to an increased rate of wake
recovery compared to the RNG and realizable k-✏EVMs. Both showed superior to
the standard version for modelling of wind turbine wakes. The RNG k-✏maintained
the wake deﬁcit slightly better than the realizable. However the TKE is generally
under-predicted in the wake.

Chapter 5
Conclusion and outlook
5.1
Conclusion
An open source CFD framework capable of modelling prescribed motion OFTWs in atmo-
spheric turbulence has been developed using OpenFOAM. The empty NBL was implemented
and validated with very good results compared to the log-law and available data. These
good results was obtained by following the recommendations of boundary conditions by
[43]. This NBL was used as the ﬂow regime for validating the built-in AD frame-work of
OpenFOAM against high ﬁdelity LES simulations and wind-tunnel measurements. In this
context three di↵erent EVM was tested: standard, realizable and RNG k-✏. The latter
showed the most promising results in prediction of the wake. A grid sensitivity study
for this turbulence model and NBL setup was conducted showing a required resolution
of at least D/16 in the axial plane and D/6 in the stream-wise direction of the wake do-
main. Based on the built-in AD method in OpenFOAM a 6-DoF AD method was developed
in previous work. This method was bench-marked against numerical experiments, but
turned out to be insuﬃcient for modelling of NBL ﬂows as it was based on a single prob-
ing point. Therefore, the AD-sixDoF Induction Method was developed and bench-marked
with promising results for surge motion in uniform ﬂow. It was found that a very ﬁne mesh
resolution is required around the AD when motion is applied. This was done as outlined
in Fig. 4.1 with good results. Afterwards a comparison between uniform and NBL inﬂow
indicated that peak-to-peak variation of the thrust and power highly dependent on the
shear proﬁle. The e↵ect was ampliﬁed e↵ect by increasing amplitude.
The developed framework with RNG k-✏, AD-sixDoF Induction Method and Unsteady
RANS simulations was used to study full scale double wake cases of NREL-5MW turbines
with a spacing of 5D. Three in-phase motions (surge, pitch and yaw) was applied and
compared to a case with bottom ﬁxed turbines with the following main ﬁndings:
• In case A1 (surge) and A2 (pitch) a relative increase in mean power and thrust for

5.2
Future work
In the double wake cases high ambient turbulent intensity of IH,1 = 8% was applied. The
e↵ect being a faster wake-recovery in general, but probably also a reduction in the e↵ects
of applied motions. It would be interesting to run the case for a low ambient turbulence
intensity to investigate the e↵ects and di↵erence with the studies conducted in this thesis.
Furthermore, while the module was validated for surge motion, further validation needs
to take place. With the current state of the AD-sixDoF Induction method framework, a
user-input time series of realistic motion for multiple DoFs simultaneously is also possible.
Hence, a realistic motion signal can be applied and the unsteady response in aerodynamic
behavior could the be validated against measurements. Only very few studies parametric
studies was conducted in the current work. However, with the mesh-recommendations
and suggested setup the simulations can be run in about less than one hour which allows
for computationally cheap parametric studies (at the cost of low ﬁdelity) simulations.
The unsteady wake dynamics arising from motion was clearly observed to most pronounced
at the position of the wind turbines. At the same time, the near wake region is largely
governed by an-isotropic turbulence, rotational e↵ects in the wake and rotor geometry. The
most adjacent improvement to make is to include a radial loading of the actuator disk. This
enhancement in ﬁdelity of the AD-sixDoF Induction method seem to be relatively cheap
compared to the gain. It can be achieved by applying a polynomial function as input to
