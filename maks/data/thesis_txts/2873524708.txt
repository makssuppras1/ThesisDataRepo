Summary
Students are currently one of the groups most affected by stress. The pressure they
face includes academic workload, financial diﬀiculties, social challenges, and personal
issues. Therefore, there is an urgent need for an app that can help them manage stress
effectively. This project aims to create a mobile application that helps university
students manage stress. With growing evidence showing that stress among students
is widespread and increasingly severe, this work aims to address the emotional and
psychological needs of students through user-centered design.
The design process followed the five stages of Design Thinking—empathize, de-
fine, ideate, prototype, and test—starting with a thorough understanding of stu-
dents’ stress sources and current coping behaviors. Insights were gathered through
interviews and questionnaires, which informed the creation of user personas and key
functional goals. A series of low- and high-fidelity prototypes were developed and
refined based on feedback from co-design workshops and usability tests. Based on
the results of student workshops and idea voting sessions, it was found that students
prefer emotion-focused strategies to cope with the negative feelings caused by stress.
Based on this insight, the design includes several key features: a support group sys-
tem tailored to different sources of stress, an Artificial Intelligence (AI) assistant that
helps match students with suitable groups, and an anonymous space for emotional
expression.
The final design demonstrates an iterative and user-centered process that led to an
empathetic solution. The app creates a safe and supportive space where students can
express their feelings, support one another, and manage stress effectively. Beyond the
product itself, this project also presents a clear example of how User Experience (UX)
design principles can be combined with mental health strategies. It highlights the
value of participatory design in developing tools that reflect the real experiences and
emotional needs of students.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Sixty percent of students report experiencing stress every day. One in five college
students states that they feel stressed constantly or most of the time. Over the past 30
years, stress levels among university students have risen by 30%. For most students,
stress has become a part of their lives, which tends to intensify as they move through
their academic journey [37].
Stress has recently received increasing attention, as numerous reports show a
growing number of students and employees experiencing anxiety, depression, and
even burnout [37, 20]. Studies have shown that chronic or uncontrolled stress can
lead to various physical and mental health issues, including high blood pressure,
weakened immune function, stomach problems, and mood disorders such as anxiety
and depression [8, 23, 3]. Especially in the post-pandemic era, stress-related problems
have gained more attention as reduced social interactions have intensified emotional
burdens [26].
Stress is a neutral, generally seen as the body’s reaction when external demands
exceed one’s capacity to cope.
It is a natural human response that helps us confront challenges and threats in our
lives. Everyone experiences stress to some extent [46].
Although stress can affect people of all ages, college students are particularly
vulnerable as they face multiple challenges, including academic pressures, financial
concerns, social diﬀiculties, and personal issues [12]. In campus life, high stress levels
can lead to negative consequences such as poor academic performance and decreased
mental well-being [29, 13]. More than half of university students report moderate to
severe stress, but only a small number of students seek professional help when facing
stress [37]. This statistic shows a gap in student support systems, showing the urgent
need for more accessible mental health resources.
However, existing traditional stress management solutions for university students
have significant limitations, such as diﬀiculty in scheduling appointments, long wait
times for psychological counseling, limited resources, and a lack of personalization.

1 Introduction
With the growing popularity and development of mobile Health (mHealth) [10], nu-
merous studies have explored tracking and intervention of stress using wearable de-
vices and self-report tools from clinical perspectives. For example, the StudentLife
project uses these tools to monitor students’ stress and anxiety, examining the rela-
tionships between students’ mental states, behaviors, and academic performance [39].
However, most of these approaches remain limited to stress detection and struggle to
address students’ real needs.
Additionally, various apps available on the market focus more directly on user
needs and help students cope with stress. These apps generally include three cat-
egories:
assessment apps that monitor stress or emotions and provide feedback;
problem-focused apps that aim to solve specific stressors, such as money or time
management; and emotion-focused apps promoting mindfulness or meditation. More
details can be found in the related work. Although these apps are somewhat effec-
tive in relieving certain types of stress, they also have significant limitations. For
instance, stress assessment apps often lack interaction and community support fea-
tures. Problem-focused apps are too broad, and the needs of these apps vary greatly
from person to person.
Emotion-focused apps are often not targeted enough and
provide some general suggestions.
Furthermore, they all often overlook a critical
need—the students’ desire to express their emotions in a safe, non-judgmental envi-
ronment. Besides, research and user studies show that many students are more likely
to seek peer-based support when facing stress [25]; they want a space to express
themselves. However, fear of being judged or being seen as weak often hinders their
willingness to share, especially on public social platforms.
To address these needs, this project proposes a design solution that combines peer
support and anonymous expression mechanisms. Instead of directly coping with dif-
ferent stressors, the project’s goal is to help students manage stress-related emotions
by providing an accessible, secure, and judgment-free emotional space. It aims to as-
sist students in expressing and releasing their emotions, finding comfort and support
through the shared experiences of others. The final design integrates an anonymous
wall and peer support, creating an empathetic and trustworthy emotional support
space for university students.

The previous section provided a brief overview of the project, highlighting that uni-
versity students are heavily affected by stress. It also explained how excessive stress
can affect students negatively.
This section focuses on the concept of stress, the main sources of stress among
students, what is the coping strategies, and the existing apps designed to manage

3.1
Definition and Source of University Stress
3.1.1
Stress Definition
Stress is a common phenomenon that includes psychological, physiological, behav-
ioral, and medical dimensions. Given the diverse contexts and research purposes, its
definition is complex and different.
From a psychological perspective, stress is a feeling of tension or pressure [41]. It
can be caused by external situations or internal perceptions, such as cognitive or emo-
tional factors. These perceptions lead to negative feelings like anxiety, discomfort, or
fear, which can make people feel stressed. Biologically, stress is the body’s response
to external challenges or environmental conditions. When faced with a stressor, mul-
tiple systems in the body respond.
For example, humans release two well-known
hormones during stressful situations: adrenaline and cortisol [42].
However, it is generally understood as:
A natural human response that prompts us to address challenges and threats in
our lives [46].
3.1.2
Stress Type
From the last section, the definition of stress has been introduced. This part discusses
how stress can be categorized based on its duration and whether its impact is positive
or negative.
According to the duration, stress is commonly categorized into two main types:
acute stress and chronic stress. Acute stress is typically short-term and often arises
==PAGE:16==
6

from temporary challenges that could result in some negative effects, such as anxiety
or tension. In contrast, chronic stress persists for longer periods. It is caused by
ongoing challenges and may lead to more serious effects on the body. Studies show
that chronic stress may disrupt hormonal balance, which can worsen existing health
conditions or lead to new problems [31]. Academic expectations and related pressures
among students are typically long-term in nature. Therefore, early intervention is
essential to reduce the potential harm caused by chronic stress.
In terms of its impact, stress can be classified as either positive or negative. Neg-
ative stress (distress) can lead to psychological and physiological diseases or even
burnout. But positive stress, or ”eustress”, can motivate and improve performance [4,
21]. For example, the stress from an upcoming presentation can push students to pre-
pare thoroughly, leading to better results. Therefore, it is essential to keep students
within a healthy range of perceived stress.
3.1.3
Stress Source among University Students
After having a basic understanding of stress, this chapter focuses on the sources of
stress specifically experienced by students.
Currently, the types of stressors encountered by students vary individually, but
it can be roughly divided into four categories: academic pressure, social pressure,
financial pressure, and personal issues [2]. These stressors make it diﬀicult for students
to maintain emotional stability throughout their academic journey.
Among them, academic pressure is widely recognized as the primary source of
student stress. Academic stress can be defined as the psychological pressure students
experience due to academic demands that exceed their coping abilities. Studies have
shown that key contributors to academic stress include heavy coursework, exams,
assignments, fear of failure, and uncertainty about the future [30].
Economic pressure is another key factor contributing to student stress. This type
of stress stems from financial diﬀiculties, such as high tuition fees, living costs, and
limited financial support. These challenges can intensify overall stress levels.
In addition, university students often experience social-related stress. This kind of
stress arises from interpersonal relationships and some social interactions. It may orig-
inate from various sources, including relationships, group work, family expectations,
and collaboration with others. Compared to local students, international students
tend to face more challenges, as they often lack suﬀicient social support and access
to information. As a result, they are more likely to experience loneliness and social
isolation.
Personal challenges also contribute to student stress, which vary widely from stu-
dent to student.
These may include relationship issues, sleep problems, language
barriers, or mental health conditions such as anxiety and depression.
Understanding the sources of student stress is essential for designing effective
support app.
By identifying the root causes, it becomes possible to address the
problems at their source and provide more targeted support.

3.2.2
Stress Management Approaches
After having a general understanding of how to measure stress, it is essential to ex-
plore how students cope with these challenges in daily life. Basically, students’ stress
management strategies can be categorized into two main types: problem-focused and
emotion-focused approaches [9].
3.2.2.1
Problem-Focused Coping Strategies
Problem-focused coping involves strategies aimed at directly managing or reducing the
sources of stress [6]. As previously discussed, academic workload, financial pressure,
and social issues are major stressors for students. Therefore, common problem-focused
strategies include time management, study planning, exam preparation skills, and
financial budgeting. These methods aim to resolve the issues that cause stress.
3.2.2.2
Emotion-Focused Coping Strategies
Emotion-focused coping is oriented toward reducing the negative emotion(distress),
such as anxiety, nervousness, or helplessness [24]. These strategies include meditation,
mindfulness, physical activity, and seeking emotional support. Emotional support be-
comes particularly important when the stressors cannot be directly controlled. More-
over, the study showed that students’ coping strategies were mainly emotion-focused,
and they were more inclined to adopt emotion-focused coping styles under high stress
levels [33]. This type of support is often found through peer communication, where
students talk, share feelings, and seek understanding from those in similar situations.
Therefore, peer support can be seen as a typical form of emotion-focused coping.
3.2.2.3
Peer Support as an Emotion-Focused Strategy
Peer support refers to the process of exchanging emotions, seeking help, and offering
encouragement between individuals in the same situation [34]. As an emotion-focused
coping strategy, peer support helps individuals manage negative emotions caused by
stress—such as anxiety, depression, or loneliness—rather than directly addressing the
source of stress.
Peer support can help students who feel isolated, particularly those facing high-
pressure environments such as academic demands or social adaptation.
Research
suggests that students who receive more peer support reflect stronger stress coping
abilities and achieve better academic performance [16].

3.3.2
Problem-Focused Skill Apps
This kind of application focuses on addressing specific sources of stress, such as time
management, sleep quality, and financial concerns. These apps do not aim to allevi-
ate stress directly; instead, they help users improve their ability to cope with daily
challenges, thereby reducing stress. Since the diversity and individual differences in
students’ stressors, a wide range of such applications has emerged. A few representa-
tive examples are discussed below.
Academic planning and time management tools (e.g., Google Calendar and Mi-
crosoft Teams) assist students in organizing their schedules, reducing procrastination,
and finishing deadlines, which can ease anxiety related to academic workload [14, 27].
Financial management apps (e.g., YNAB – You Need A Budget) help users plan their
daily spending more effectively, reducing uncertainty and stress caused by financial
problems [48]. Sleep tracking apps, often used with smart wearables, monitor sleep
quality and offer improvement suggestions, thus reducing the physical and mental
burden caused by fatigue or sleep disorders.
Although these applications are not explicitly designed for stress management,
they indirectly solve core stressors such as academic or financial challenges. However,
they often lack features that provide emotional support. Moreover, due to the com-
plexity and variety of stressors, it is diﬀicult to develop a problem-specific solution.
3.3.3
Emotion-Focused Peer Support Tools
This type of application primarily focuses on alleviating the emotional burden caused
by stress rather than solving the sources of stress [9]. These apps are not only designed
to manage stress-related negative emotions, but also help users cope with a broader
range of emotional diﬀiculties. As shown in Table 3.3 ,common examples such as
Calm and Headspace, which offer short, guided mindfulness and breathing exercises
to help students quickly regulate negative emotions such as anxiety and tension [7, 15].
These two apps are widely used among young people and are particularly effective for
managing short-term emotional discomfort, especially under high academic pressure.
Although such tools show effectiveness in emotional regulation, they are largely
individual-centered and pay less attention to students’ emotional needs of communi-
cating and empathizing with others. To fill this gap, some platforms have introduced
peer support features. For example, TalkLife offers an open community where users
can share their thoughts and receive responses from peers [36]. 7 Cups matches users
with volunteer listeners for one-on-one conversations, while Wysa uses AI chatbots
to guide users to express emotions [1, 47].
Despite their benefits, these emotion-focused and peer support tools still have
some limitations. Emotion-focused apps often lack interactive or ongoing feedback
mechanisms. Moreover, peer support functions on many platforms are not tailored
specifically to students, reducing their relevance and effectiveness for this target
group.

3.3.4
The Role of Anonymous Peer Support
While traditional peer support has shown a positive impact on supporting students’
mental health, many open social platforms—such as Reddit, online forums, or general
community apps—may cause new social stress. These include fears of being judged,
misunderstood, or compared to others. For students who are more sensitive or re-
luctant to express their emotions, such concerns may act as a barrier to emotional
disclosure, thereby limiting the effectiveness of peer support.
Studies suggest that anonymous environments can significantly reduce users’ psy-
chological concerns when expressing feelings. Anonymity has been shown to promote
openness and authenticity in emotional communication [5].
In light of this, several platforms have begun to emphasize the role of anonymity in
emotional expression and mental support [17]. For instance, TalkLife launched Talk-
Campus, a peer support platform specifically designed for students [35]. It allows
users to seek help anonymously within a global student community, thus reducing
the social anxiety associated with identity exposure. Similarly, Wysa highlights its
anonymous conversation mechanism, enabling users to access AI-guided support with-

out registration, thereby ensuring privacy and safety.
Building on these insights, this study incorporates an anonymous posting mecha-
nism. Compared to public posting systems, anonymous sharing lowers the threshold
for emotional expression among students. In an environment free from labels and
judgment, users are more likely to express their real feelings and seek inner support.
3.4
Summary
This chapter first gave a basic understanding of student stress by outlining its defini-
tions and common sources. It then explores various approaches to cope with stress,
including stress assessment, problem-focused coping strategies, and emotion-focused
coping strategies.
Subsequently, the chapter analyzes a range of existing stress management applica-
tions, which are generally divided into three categories: systems based on assessment
and feedback (such as Zen Space and StressOnCampus), skill based tools targeting
the root causes of stress (such as apps for time and financial management), and
emotion-driven platforms focusing on meditation or peer support (e.g., Calm, Wysa,
TalkLife).
Among these, peer support platforms are particularly favored by student popu-
lations, especially those including anonymity features, which help reduce expression
barriers and foster more open emotional expression. Such mechanisms have shown
promise in enhancing the effectiveness of mental support.
However, current plat-
forms still face challenges, including limited personalization and limited flexibility in
interaction.
Building on these insights, this study proposes an emotion-oriented peer support
approach that combines a stressor group with an anonymous emotional wall. The
goal is to create a app for students to express and share stress experiences.

CHAPTER 4
Methodology
After gaining a basic understanding of the problem and prior work, the next step was
to select an appropriate methodology. This chapter introduces a user-centered design
approach and explains how the design process was guided by the principles of Design
Thinking. It also provides a detailed description of the specific methods used at each
stage of the Design Thinking process.
4.1
UCD(User-centered design)
This study followed a UCD approach throughout the entire process to design an app
to help students manage stress.
User-centered design is a process that puts users’ needs, goals, and context at the
heart of design. It focuses on how users think, behave, and feel during every stage of
product development [45].
UCD was chosen because it allows for iterative design, based on real user needs.
This approach was particularly suitable for this project, as the goal was to help users
manage their stress. In this context, users’ preferences and emotional experiences are
important and must be taken seriously throughout the design.
4.2
Design Process Overview
The Design Thinking model was used as the main method to guide the design process.
It is a human-centered approach that focuses on understanding users’ needs and
experiences [18]. This model includes five stages: empathize, define, ideate, prototype,
and test, as shown in the Figure 4.1. It is important to note that this model is a
non-linear process and requires continuous iteration, which makes it suitable for UX
design.
To present the design process more clearly, this paper focuses on the final design
process. However, before the final one, an earlier attempt was made. This included

7.1
Reflection on Design Process
7.1.1
Addressing the Research Question
Research Question: How to use UX design methods to create an app
that effectively meets students’ stress management needs?
This question can be well answered in the Methodology section. The project aimed
to explore how UX methods can be used to design an app that meets students’ stress
management needs. The entire design process followed a user-centered approach and
was structured around the five stages of Design Thinking: empathize, define, ideate,
prototype, and test. At each stage, the real needs of students were the main focus,
which helped guide the development of the app.
Sub-question 1: What are the main sources of stress faced by university
students?
How do students currently cope with stress?
What negative
effects does negative stress have on students’ daily lives?
This sub-question was first explored through a review of existing literature. Prior
studies show that university students experience stress mainly from academic pressure,
financial concerns, social problems, and relationship issues. To verify if these findings
matched the experience of the target users, interviews and questionnaires were con-
ducted. These methods helped collect data on students’ current stress sources, coping
strategies, and the effects of negative stress on daily life. The results supported or
added to the findings from the earlier research.
Sub-question 2: What needs do students have for a stress management
app? Which of these needs should be addressed first? What features can
effectively meet these needs?
==PAGE:66==
56
7 Discussion
To explore students’ real needs for a stress management app, this research first
analyzed the results from interviews and questionnaires. Based on these findings, a
persona and user journey were created. The results showed that students had a wide
range of needs, mainly in three categories:
• Stress/mood Monitoring & Suggestion-Based Apps
• Problem-focused support
• Emotion-focused support
To decide which needs to be addressed first, matrix sorting was used to prioritize
them. Later, workshops with co-design methods were held to collect ideas directly
from users.
The study found that students often face multiple stress sources.
Their main
needs focused on two directions: one was to deal with academic pressure through
task management, and the other was to share feelings with peers who had similar
experiences and get advice from them.
Based on these insights, two low-fidelity
prototypes were created—one focusing on peer support, and the other on calendar
integration.
After a concept test and collecting feedback, the main need became clear:
Students want to be understood and get advice from their peers.
Therefore, this sub-question was answered through persona development, work-
shops (including matrix sorting, brainstorming, and idea voting), and early prototyp-
ing.
Sub-question 3: Do the features of the designed app truly help students
manage or reduce their stress?
This sub-question was explored through user feedback in the usability test, espe-
cially in the two follow-up questions.
• Q2: Did it feel helpful, supportive to stress?

• Q4: Would you use an app like this in real life?

them a space to express themselves. The mixed answers to Q4 showed a conditional
willingness to use the app—users would consider using it if it added more features.
Some participants were unsure about using such an app in real life because of

7.2
Design Impact
The app prototype designed in this project aims to support university students by
providing a space where they can interact and receive help from peers. Using a user-
centered design process, the final outcome reflects the real needs of students and aims
to create a warm, supportive environment.
One of the most significant impacts of this design is its response to the problem
of emotional isolation. During usability testing, several participants mentioned that
the app not only gave them a chance to express their feelings but also helped them
connect with others who had similar experiences.
Participant 19 provided a valuable insight:
“We have a good network—we use our friends. But it’s a really nice idea
for people who just moved here or don’t have a social support network.”
– Participant 19
This comment highlights the importance of this solution for international students,
newcomers, or those without a local support network. It can serve as an emotional
and cultural bridge, offering a safe, anonymous, and accessible space for those who
lack support systems.
In addition, the design encourages students to face stress more openly. Research
suggests that stress is neutral—it can act as a motivator for academic performance or
become a burden on mental health. In both the concept test and usability test, many
participants said they were happy to know that someone cared about their mental
state. Some also shared that reading the anonymous message wall gave them a sense
of connection and relief. These responses suggest that the app has the potential to
reduce the stigma around mental health issues among students.
On a broader level, this study shows how UX design can be used as a form of
emotional intervention in the academic background. It offers another strategy besides
professional mental health services— the power of peer support.
In summary, this design is not just a technical tool, but a form of social interven-
tion. It aims to build a warm and supportive space for university students through
empathy and help from others.

First, the sample size for data collection was relatively small. The interview phase
included 8 participants, 55 students completed the questionnaire, 6 students joined
the workshop, 9 participated in the concept testing, and 7 took part in the usability
testing. Therefore, the number of participants involved in each phase was limited.
Moreover, most of the participants were reached randomly from the university library
or Skylab, based on their interest. As a result, the study may not represent students
from diverse cultural backgrounds or different stress levels.
Use of a non-validated stress scale in Questionnaire
Second, there are limitations in the design of the initial questionnaire during the
empathize phase. The questions used were not based on a validated stress measure-
ment scale, such as the widely used PSS, which provides a more reliable and multi-
dimensional view of perceived stress. This choice was made due to time constraints
and the need for a quick and simple survey that participants could easily take part in.
Although the questionnaire was simple, it still offered helpful insights into students’
perceived levels of stress.
Also, there were some shortcomings when designing the questions and answers in
this questionnaire. This was because the questionnaire was created before conducting
the one-on-one interview. In theory, the questionnaire should be designed after ana-
lyzing the results of one-on-one interviews. As a result, some logical inconsistencies
occurred. For example, the options in the question ”What features would you expect
from a stress management app? (Select all that apply)” were created based on my
own assumptions, rather than summarized from the findings obtained from the user
needs in the one-on-one interview. Most of the options in the questionnaire do match
the feedback collected from the interviews. However, there is a possibility that some
needs were overlooked. Still, this does not affect the overall outcome of the design.
Constraints in workshop implementation
Another limitation is the eﬀiciency of the workshop. One week before the session,
recruitment began, and efforts were made to coordinate participant time. While eight
students initially agreed to participate, only six joined the workshop. Two of them
were recruited at the library information desk, two were classmates from the same
major, and the remaining three were invited by the researcher at the BDTU dormitory.
All participants were students with different cultural backgrounds.
Scheduling was quite challenging due to exam periods and the 3-week summer
course schedule, so the workshop was held online via Zoom on Friday at 6 p.m.
However, participants seemed tired that evening, and due to the remote meeting
and tight time pressure, many students lost focus on Friday night. As a result, it
seems like the generated ideas are not creative.
==PAGE:69==
7.4 Future Work
59
Technical limitations of the prototype
Lastly, there were technical limitations. Figma was used to build the prototype,
but many features could only be presented conceptually. During testing, it was often
necessary to explain that “this function is not yet available.” Some functions had
to be verbally explained as they were not yet designed completely.
Furthermore,
the prototype lacked the ability to support long-term tracking of user behavior or
intervention effects.
7.4
Future Work
Feature Expansion
Usability testing showed that users expect more from the app. Their feedback
mainly focused on three areas:
• First, the group chat could include link-sharing features, such as links to uni-
versity departments. This would help students get support for specific stress-
related issues.
• Second, the app could add simple stress assessment tools. Based on the results,
it could offer helpful suggestions or feedback.
• In addition, the features for anonymous posts could be improved to make it
clearer and more supportive.
Integration with Campus Services
In the future, the app could be connected with existing university support services,
such as counseling centers. For example, combining with the link-sharing idea above,
the app could allow one-click access to related and useful campus resources. This
would make the app more practical in a real university environment.
Further Feature Development and Technical Improvement
The current app is still in the early testing stage. More interactive details and
user testing can be added in the future. Based on user feedback, the app can be
improved step by step to enhance both its functions and user experience, helping it
move closer to becoming a real product.
Larger and More Diverse Sample
Future research should involve more participants from different cultural and aca-
demic backgrounds. A wider sample can make the findings more reliable and useful.
It also provides better data support for improving the app.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
This study aims to explore how to develop an app that could effectively support
university students in managing stress by using user-centered design methods. Fol-
lowing the five stages of design thinking (empathize, define, ideate, prototype, and
test (evaluate)), the research utilized interviews, questionnaires, workshops, and us-
ability tests to identify students’ sources of stress, their coping methods, and their
needs for a stress management app.
The findings show that students experience many types of stress, including school-
work, social relationships, and money problems. There are three common ways to
cope: tracking stress and getting feedback, solving problems directly, and focusing on
emotions. Insights from the workshop suggest that students tend to prefer emotion-
focused approaches. Based on these findings, a prototype app was developed with
anonymous posts and small group chats. The goal is to create an app to give students
an easy and flexible way to support each other.
During the testing, most users gave positive feedback to the app’s anonymous
feature, emotional expression space, and opportunities to connect with others. They
also expressed expectations for further improvements, such as clearer group structures,
integrated stress assessment tools, and connections with university support services.
Although the study has limitations in sample size and technical implementation, it
highlights the potential of UX-based methods in designing mental health interventions.
Future work may focus on improving the app’s interaction details, increasing sample
diversity, and continuously improving the system based on user testing at universities,
building a more effective student stress support system.
