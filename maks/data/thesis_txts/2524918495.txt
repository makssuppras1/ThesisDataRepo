Abstract 
I 
 
Abstract  
Remote Sensing (RS) geophysical model products from optical and thermal satellites, 
can efficiently monitor the spatiotemporal distribution of water cycle components such as 
evapotranspiration (ET), in very large areas. Although products could assist in the mitigation 
of climate change problems especially in arid regions, they suffer from data loss mainly because 
of cloudiness. The main data loss problem, is that clouds are blocking the incoming solar 
radiation, decreasing the ET values of the surface, compared to the ET in a sunny day. This 
study, aims to assess the performance of different imputation methods in a spatiotemporal ET 
dataset from Spain. The used imputation method, Historical Average which is based on sunny 
days, was overestimating the imputed ET values. The tested imputation algorithms, ranged 
from naïve methods to time-series (TS), machine learning (ML), matrix completion (MC) and 
tensor decomposition (TD). The study area was the Majadas Oak Savanna in Extremadura. 
Tests were conducted, in a small homogeneous and a larger more heterogeneous area in 
simulated, compressed and the real dataset. Comparisons were done to ET from Eddy 
Covariance (EC) flux towers. It was shown that Tucker decomposition (TD method) improved 
the results of the basic imputation methods, because it takes into account the spatiotemporal 
patterns simultaneously. The highest of spatial and a low temporal rank, were required to yield 
the optimal results for Tucker. The MC method robust principal component analysis (RPCA), 
yielded the best results, because it uses more parameters than Tucker, which however provided 
more robustness. MC methods were not able to gap-fill completely empty time-frames, because 
they require spatial information, but TS methods were not affected. The results in the small 
homogeneous area were better than in the larger heterogeneous, due to the increased 
phenological and computational complexity. 
Based on the previous outcomes, a combination of methods was proposed. The area had 
to be small and homogeneous, because the idea was that the observed values that were adjacent 
to the missing in a semi-sparse time-frame, should be similarly affected by the cloud existence. 
The semi-sparse time-frames would be imputed with RPCA and the completely sparse time-
frames with the TS method, moving average (MA). The proposed algorithm was named RPCA-
MA and the addition of Tucker on top of MA was also evaluated, forming RPCA-[MA-Tucker]. 
It outperformed Historical Average in the compressed tensor and also in the real data, especially 
in spring, summer and autumn, when vegetation is fully grown and the sunny days have high 
ET. The proposed method also caught the EC ET declining trend successfully. Tucker improved 
the results in the EC ET comparison. The method has a positive potential in gap-filling of 
geophysical datasets. However, further investigation is required in different land use types.

Preface 
II 
 
Preface 
The current Master Thesis was conducted as the final project of the MSc degree in 
Environmental Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). It lasted from 26 
of August to 26 of February and accounts for 35 ECTS credits (European Credit Transfer 
System). The thesis was carried out under the supervision of Associate Professor Monica Garcia 
from the department of Environmental Engineering (DTU Environment) and Assistant 
Professor Andreas Baum from the department of Applied Mathematics and Computer Science 
(DTU Compute). Additionally, Postdoc Gorka Mendiguren Gonzalez from DTU Environment 
was co-supervisor. 
The subject of the thesis is the performance comparison of machine learning methods for 
missing data imputation of a remote sensing spatio-temporal evapotranspiration dataset in 
Spain. It is part of the FORWARD European project.

1. Introduction 
Several studies have proved that regions located in the Mediterranean Basin such as the 
Iberian Peninsula, are immensely sensitive to changes in the climate. In these areas, the 
consequences of global warming are already obvious and more conspicuous that in the rest of 
the Europe (Giorgi & Lionello, 2008; IPCC, 2007). Moreover, it is predicted that over the next 
50 years, the availability of water resources is going to be reduced due to climate warming, 
causing a further intensification of the global water cycle, including evapotranspiration 
(Alcamo et al., 2010). 
The term evapotranspiration (ET) is formed by the evaporation and plant transpiration 
from the land and ocean surface of Earth to the atmosphere. Evaporation incorporates the water 
that moves to the air from sources such as soil, canopy intersections and waterbodies. The 
vapored water from vegetation that moves to the air through stomata in the leaves of plants, 
constitutes transpiration. The ET rate is usually measured in mm per unit time. The latent heat 
flux (λE), is the energetic equivalent of evapotranspiration and has a unit of W/m2. When a 
surface is evaporating, heat is transferred to the air and it is stored in water vapor as latent heat 
(the temperature is kept constant in this process) (Bonan, 2008). Accounting approximately two 
thirds of the precipitation on land, ET is considered as a very important part of the water cycle 
(Vicente-Serrano et al., 2014). Especially in arid zones, ET consumes more than 90% of the 
annual precipitation (Glenn, Huete, Nagler, Hirschboeck, & Brown, 2007). In this climate 
zones, carbon and water cycles are very closely interconnected (García et al., 2013) and ET 
controls the equilibrium between the recharge and discharge of the aquifers (Last et al., 2004). 
Moreover, in dryland regions of the Iberian Peninsula, approximately 80% of the annual water 
consumption is due to irrigation demands (Ramos-Merchante & Prenda, 2018). Additionally, 
remote sensing (RS) data from the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) 
covering a period of the last 30 years, has shown that one-third of the global vegetated area is 
greening (increase of Leaf Area Index (LAI)) (Zeng et al., 2018). The main drivers in this 
drastic augmentation, are human intervention through land use management and the increased 
rate of photosynthesis in plants. The increased photosynthesis results from the growing levels 
of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere (CO2 fertilization) (Zeng et al., 2018). Although the 
impacts of this phenomenon have not been fully studied yet and more research is required, there 
is evidence that in arid areas, the greening decreases the soil moisture through its synergy with 
the atmospheric cycle (Zeng et al., 2018). Due to greening, an upward global trend is observed 
in ET records and if these conditions remain unrestricted, there is a further risk of drought 
related perturbations (Zhang et al., 2015).

For these reasons, more precise estimations of ET spatially and temporally, are highly 
important and will assist to improve irrigation planning, agricultural, watershed and aquifer 
management, meteorological predictions, hydrological modelling and comprehend land 
surface-atmosphere interactions (Yuan et al., 2010; Zhao et al., 2013).  
 
1.1 Evapotranspiration Estimation Methods 
and their Constraints 
One of the traditional in-field methods to estimate the ET is the Eddy Covariance (EC) 
technique. Proposed by Swinbank (1951), it is a micro-meteorological technique that offers the 
ability to measure vertical turbulent fluxes within atmospheric boundary layers. In other words, 
the gas exchanges between soil, vegetation and the atmosphere in an ecosystem are estimated. 
The wind and scalar (carbon dioxide, temperature, water vapor etc.) atmospheric data series are 
analyzed and values of fluxes are yielded. The idea behind the method, is that the scalars are 
transferred by the turbulent eddies, which are part of the movement of air flow above and 
through vegetation. The eddies are carrying the scalars in a vertical movement from a lower to 
a higher height and vice versa. In order to obtain measurements, tower stations are placed in a 
field study area (Figure 1). They are equipped with various sensors such as the anemometer, 
which measures the wind speed and direction and the infrared gas analyzer, which measures 
gas concentrations in the air. The flux density of each scalar is obtained, through the calculation 
of the covariance of fluctuations in the vertical wind velocity and the scalar concentration 
(Shunlin Liang, 2012).  
Although the method has been acknowledged for its efficiency (Friend et al., 2007) and 
can be used as benchmark validation to RS ET models from satellites (García et al., 2013), it is 
restricted by its difficulty to expand easily in larger scales. Each tower covers an area of only 
100-2000 m2 (Shunlin Liang, 2012) and cannot be placed in heterogeneous areas in terms of 
land use. Nevertheless, flux tower networks are expanding the last 20 years in more than 500 
stations all over the world, which belong to various projects such as FLUXNET or Ameriflux 
(Shunlin Liang, 2012).

As demonstrated in the case of greening, RS has been proved to be a highly accurate and 
practical method to monitor the spatial and temporal distribution of ET through optical and 
thermal satellite images (Zeng et al., 2018). On the other hand, one of the main constraints of 
RS products, is the lack of information that is introduced when obtaining these data. The main 
reasons that are causing this, are cloudy weather conditions or malfunctions in the satellite 
sensors. It is estimated that the global cloud fraction at all times, is around 67% and the cloud 
fraction over land is approximately 55% (King, Platnick, Menzel, Ackerman, & Hubanks, 
2013). Additionally, as an example, three-quarters of the detectors (in band 6) of the Aqua 
MODIS are ineffective (L. Wang et al., 2006). As a result, the urge to reconstruct the missing 
information of satellite images is on high demand. Multispectral satellite data is usually highly 
dimensional, being composed by two spatial dimensions, a temporal dimension and another 
dimension for each spectral band that is included in the analysis. This multidimensionality of 
satellite datasets creates difficulties concerning the management and interpretation of the data, 
as well as the computational requirements which can be solved by dimensionality reduction 
techniques (Burger & Gowen, 2011). Additionally, from multispectral satellite datasets, other 
spatiotemporal products such as ET can be derived with the usage of models that are based on 
proved equations.

1.2 Missing Data Mechanisms and Imputation  
Three are the usual problems related to missing data: loss of efficiency, increase of 
complication in data handling/analysis and bias generated by differences between missing and 
complete data (Farhangfar, Kurgan, & Pedrycz, 2007). According to Rubin (1976), every data 
point has some likelihood of being missing. There are three different types of missing data 
mechanisms: missing completely at random (MCAR), missing at random (MAR) and not 
missing at random (NMAR) (Rubin, 1976).  MCAR means that there is no relationship between 
whether a data value is missing and any values in the data set, missing or observed. In other 
words, the probability of a value to be missing is the same for all cases. If the probability of 
being missing is the same only within groups defined by the observed data, then the data are 
MAR. MAR is more general and more realistic than MCAR. Modern missing data methods 
generally start from the MAR assumption. Lastly, in the case of NMAR, there is a relationship 
between the propensity of a value to be missing and the missing values. 
In order to tackle with the problem of missing data there are two approaches: ignore the 
missing values or impute them. According to Farhangfar et al. (2007), the first approach is 
suggested only when a small amount of data is missing but still the risk of missing valuable 
information exists. For that reason, the proposition of imputation techniques is flourishing the 
recent years. Traditional methods use statistics and rely on simple algorithms such as mean 
imputation. However, statistical methods fail to capture the daily fluctuations of the highly 
dynamic water cycle components such as ET (S. Wang, Garcia, Ibrom, & Bauer-Gottwein, 
2019). On the other hand, the increasing computational capacity provided the capability to 
handle very large datasets and more complex algorithms. This gave rise to the usage of a new 
family of imputation methods which implement Machine Learning (ML) and Tensor 
decomposition (TD) techniques.

1.3 Literature Review 
Several scientific publications have demonstrated that TD methods can be used for 
missing data inpainting. Tomasi & Bro (2005), used PARAFAC (a TD algorithm) for 
chemometrics data reconstruction. Liu et al (2013), estimated missing values for visual data 
with TD algorithms. Asif et al., (2016), applied matrix and tensor based methods for missing 
data estimation in large traffic networks. Concerning RS and satellite image information, most 
of the studies focus on using TD methods to reconstruct the missing information caused by 
clouds or other technical issues by combining spatial, temporal and spectral methods in order 
to correct the reflectance values from the different spectral bands (Ji, Yokoya, Zhu, & Huang, 
2018). In that way, the satellite images are totally reconstructed and their products can be 
derived afterwards, without missing values. These methods are very effective but also 
complicated, as more than 3 dimensions of tensor datasets are being used. Specifically about 
RS model products (spatio-temporal geophysical datasets) such as soil moisture, temperature, 
ET etc., that have missing values, G. Wang, Garcia, Liu, Jeu, & Dolman, (2012) used a 
penalized Least Square Method to fill in gaps of soil moisture product derived from satellite 
images. Li, Yang, Yu, Hu, & Yuan (2018), applied the TD method Canonic Polyadic Weighted 
Optimization (CP-WOPT) to gap-fill geopotential height and air temperature datasets. The 
method provided better results than the more traditional method Spatiotemporal Kriging. 
Giannopoulos, Savvaki, Tsagkatakis, & Tsakalides (2017), implemented the matrix completion 
method, Augmented Lagrange Multipliers and the tensor completion method Low-Rank Tensor 
Completion for temperature data imputation. According to Zscheischler & Mahecha (2012), 
the method Singular Spectrum Analysis, which is based on Singular Value Decomposition 
(SVD), is a popular method for geophysical dataset gap-filling. Specifically for ET datasets, 
Pasupa (2013), used the expectation maximization (EM) algorithm to impute a reference ET 
dataset. Alavi, Warland, & Berg (2006), evaluated the time-series imputation method Kalman 
filtering to interpolate monthly and annual ET datasets with promising results.

1.4 Objectives 
The aim of the current thesis is to assess the reconstruction of missing data from a 
spatiotemporal daily actual ET dataset in Spain and provide a suggestion of a method that yields 
better results than the currently used imputation method.  
To achieve the overall objective, the following steps have been defined: 
 Assess several imputation methods in synthetic and real-tensor datasets, gap-
filling artificial and real missing data respectively.  
 Assess the effect of high or low spatial heterogeneity in the performance of the 
ML imputation methods. 
 Assess the effect in the performance of the imputation methods with the 
combination of a Tensor Decomposition (TD) method. 
 Propose an imputation algorithm and a strategy that has to be followed (ideal 
size and homogeneity of the area).  
The thesis offers an important addition to the existing literature that deal with the 
evaluation of ML and TD methods in geophysical datasets reconstruction. Moreover, in the 
proposed imputation algorithm and strategy, the spatial and temporal patterns of the dataset 
were unfolded and the effect of clouds to the ET of a surface, was taken into account. 
Additionally, by the completion of the RS ET dataset of Spain, the results of other researches 
that use the specific ET dataset could be improved.

too intensely. Then, the community of Valencia is considered the driest part of Spain. Further 
south, there is Andalucía. Here, some low pressures enter the Mediterranean Sea through the 
Strait of Gibraltar. This region is greener with some occasional rains. On the Atlantic side, the

high resolution climatic data and from the MODIS satellite data. The algorithm that was used 
to create the dataset was based in the Priestley-Taylor Jet Propulsion Laboratory (PT-JPL) 
originally developed by Fisher, Tu, & Baldocchi, (2008). This algorithm, originates from the

(Fisher et al., 2008). In Priestley-Taylor, these parameters are replaced by the α constant factor 
which usually holds a number of 1.26. However, the Priestley-Taylor equation describes the 
potential ET which is defined as the amount of evaporation that would occur if sufficient water 
sources were available (Priestley & Taylor, 1972). Fisher et al., (2008), modified the Priestley-

conditions where water deficit exists). The α constant was kept at 1.26 so that the Priestley-
Taylor equation as a potential ET equation, remains intact as it was originally designed and 
confirmed. The potential λE, was scaled down to actual λE by applying plant physiological 
limitations and soil evaporation partitioning. García et al. (2013), further modified the model 
(PT-JPL-Thermal) by using thermal remote sensing data, in order to derive the soil moisture 
constraint. It was validated with EC flux site data benchmarking. Moyano et al. (2018), applied

not consider limitations due to soil moisture restriction (overestimation in comparison to actual 
ET), the general monthly-seasonal spatial trend was followed (Figure 100 in the Appendix).

Most of the missing data in the MODIS ET dataset were happening due to cloud coverage 
(Mu, Zhao, & Running, 2011). Furthermore, in a cloudy day the λE values of an area are usually 
lower than in a neighboring sunny day. The incoming radiation is blocked and the Earth’s 
surface cannot evapotranspire at its maximum potential. This means, that there is a relationship 
between the missing and observed values of neighboring days, with the missing being usually 
lower than the observed. The exact difference cannot be estimated since it depends on many 
factors, such as seasonality and cloud density. On the other hand, the clouds over an area are 
occurring randomly. For these reasons, the missing values of the RS ET dataset could be 
characterized as MAR. 
The currently used imputation method of the ET RS MODIS dataset is the Historical 
Average. As there were available data that spaned over 15 years, in order to impute for example 
the missing day 9 of july in 2017, all the observed 9ths of July in the previous years were 
averaged. The problem with this method was that the missing values -which happened due to 
cloudiness- were interpolated with the average of observed values in non-cloudy days. This 
caused an overestimation between the imputed values and the ground truth EC ET in the north 
and south tower. For this reason, other imputation methods that could overcome this limitation 
had to be tested. The following sections, include the matrix and tensor operations as well as the 
ML algorithms that were investigated in this study. 
 
3.1 Tensor Basics 
The 3-dimensional ET RS MODIS dataset could be characterized as a 3-mode tensor. A 
tensor is a multidimensional array. The number of the dimensions of a tensor is called mode or 
order or way (Acar & Yener, 2009). Tensors generalize the vector and matrix concepts to higher 
dimensions. Therefore, a scalar could be characterized as a 0-way tensor, a vector as a 1-way 
tensor and a matrix as a 2-way tensor (Figure 11). Tensors of order three or higher, are called 
higher-order tensors. To simplify things and since the specific dataset of the current project 
consists of a 3-mode tensor, the examples of higher-order tensors will be given with a 3-way 
tensor Xi j k, where i’s represent tensor rows, j’s columns and k’s tubes.

The fibers of a tensor are the higher-order analogue of matrix rows and columns. To

frontal by X: : k (Figure 12). The frontal slices in the current case, correspond to the daily time-

3.2 Imputation Methods 
There exist several types of imputation methods that differ in complexity. A method 
could be single, meaning that only one value is imputed, or iterative, if it is following a 
repetitive process to reach the optimal result. Additionally, some methods take into account the 
temporal evolution (time-series), others only the spatial features and others simultaneously find 
the spatio-temporal patterns of a dataset. A range of algorithms were used that covered a wide 
variety of types and complexity. The software and packages that were used in the thesis are 
presented in Appendix I. The Python language was mainly used in this study. 
 
3.2.1 Mean Imputation  
The Mean imputation is one of the most naïve methods. It is a single imputation method, 
where the average of the available values in the dataset is calculated and used to gap-fill the 
missing values. Although it is a popular method due to its simplicity, it was used as a benchmark 
method as it suffers from three basic problems (Allison, 2011): 
 
It reduces the variance of the imputed variables. 
 
It shrinks standard errors, which invalidates most hypothesis tests and the calculation 
of confidence interval. 
 
It does not preserve relationships between variables such as correlations. 
==PAGE:40==
Imputation Algorithms, Tensor Operations and Decompositions 
24 
 
3.2.2 Last Observation Carried Forward (LOCF) 
LOCF is considered a naïve time-series method, where a missing value is replaced by 
the last observed value. When multiple values are missing in succession, the method searches 
for the last observed value. However, if there are many missing values in a row, the resulting 
time-series might look too artificial, with the same value being repeated for several time-points. 
It can be applied with confidence in cases where there is certainty what the missing values 
should be, for example, for administrative variables in longitudinal data. The method has long 
been used in clinical trials (Woolley, Cardoni, & Goethe, 2009).  
 
3.2.3 Moving Average (MA) 
MA is a more sophisticated time-series method. It is widely used for imputation in 
several applications such as meteorological datasets (Flores, Tito, & Centty, 2019). MA is also 
referred as moving mean, rolling mean or rolling average. Each NaN value is replaced by the 
mean of its adjacent values from both sides. Usually, the desired window size can be defined 
by a number k.  
A k=2 was set, which in the specific algorithm meant that 2 values are taken into account 
from each side, past and future. The used algorithm also incorporated a semi-adaptive window 
size in case of long NaN gaps. Whenever there were less than 2 non-NaN values available in 
the window, the window size incrementally increased, until at least 2 observed values were 
included. Furthermore, the Exponential Weighted Moving Average (EWMA) version was 
chosen, where weighting factors were used, which decreased exponentially. The observations 
directly next to a central NaN value i, had weight 1/2^1, the observations one value further 
away (i-2, i+2) had weight 1/2^2, the next (i-3, i+3) had weight 1/2^3, etc. In that way, the 
closest days to a missing value were taken more into account than the more distant ones. The 
algorithm was used only in the proposed method as a replacement to LOCF. EWMA has been 
also used in imputation. For example, Haghighati & Hassan (2018) imputed missing data from 
control chart pattern recognizer (CCPR) with EWMA.

3.2.4 k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) 
kNN is basically a supervised classification ML algorithm (Figure 15), firstly introduced 
by statisticians Evelyn Fix and Joseph Hodges, in an unpublished US Air Force School of 
Aviation Medicine report in 1951 (Silverman & Jones, 1989). The algorithm can also be used 
for imputation of missing data (Beretta & Santaniello, 2016). The method is similar to MA but 
in a spatial concept. k neighbors are chosen, based on some distance measure and their average 
is used as an imputation estimate. The method requires the selection of the number of nearest 
neighbors, and a distance metric.  
 
Figure 15: A visual example of the k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) classification algorithm. The k value 
defines the distance measure that the neighboring values should satisfy in order to be included in the 
classification scheme. Retrieved from: Link. 
 
In the current project, the Euclidean distance was used. The used algorithm was first 
imputing the missing values with the mean and then it constructed a k-d tree (a space-
partitioning data structure for organizing points in a k-dimensional space) to compute nearest 
neighbors. After finding ‘k’ nearest neighbors, it was calculating the weighted average. The k 
value of the algorithm was chosen to be 5. A disadvantage of kNN, is that the computational 
cost is quite high especially in large datasets, since the distance of each query instance to all 
samples has to be computed. This cost could be reduced with an indexing method such as the 
k-d tree. 
 
3.2.5 Expectation-Maximization (EM) 
The expectation-maximization (EM) is an unsupervised clustering algorithm (Dempster, 
Laird, & Rubin, 1977). It is an iterative method used to find maximum likelihood estimates of 
parameters in statistical models, where the model depends on unobserved latent variables. The 
EM process switches between two steps, the expectation (E) step and the maximization (M)

the parameters that maximize the log likelihood of the complete data. These parameter 
estimates are then used to determine the distribution of the latent variables in the next E step.

Z a set of missing values, and θ a vector of unknown parameters. With a likelihood function    
L(θ; Χ, Ζ) = p(X, Z|θ), the maximum likelihood estimate of the unknown parameters is 
determined by maximizing the marginal likelihood of the observed data:

The EM algorithm is very popular in missing data imputation, since the E-step and M-

on the gaussianity of the data and the randomness in time of the missing values. For these 
reasons, it can get stuck into local optima (Kondrashov & Ghil, 2006). 
 In the current used project, the code of the EM algorithm was creating a Gaussian 
distribution from the mean and standard deviation from the observed values (E step) of each 
column of the matrix, and imputed the missing values with random samples from the 
distribution (M step). The process was repeated until the likelihood did not change at least 10% 
and had repeated at least 5 times. The maximum EM loops were chosen to be 10000. Two EM 
types were used, a Spatial and a Temporal by transposing the sparse matrix accordingly. Since 
the algorithm was using each column separately, by keeping the matrix un-transposed, only the 
time-series of a pixel were taken into account providing a Temporal EM type. By transposing 
the matrix, the algorithm was making calculations based on the pixels of each time-frame (day), 
providing a Spatial EM type (Figure 14).

3.2.6 Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) 
Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), is a linear algebra operation. Any matrix M = m 
x n whose entries come from the field K, could be factorized such as M = U x Σ x V*, where U 
is an m x m unitary matrix, Σ is a m x n diagonal matrix with non-negative real numbers on the 
diagonal and V*, a n x n unitary transposed V matrix. If M is real, U and V=V* are real 
orthonormal matrices (U x U*= I, V x V*= I) (Figure 16). The diagonal entries Σi, i of Σ, are 
known as the singular values in descending order. The number of non-zero singular values is 
equal to the rank of M. The columns of U and the columns of V are called the left-singular 
vectors and right-singular vectors of M, respectively. 
 
Figure 16: Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of a matrix M = m x n where, M = U x Σ x V*. U is an 
m x m unitary matrix, Σ is a m x n diagonal matrix with non-negative real numbers on the diagonal 
and V*, a n x n unitary transposed V matrix. U and V=V* are real orthonormal matrices (U x U*= I,   
V x V*= I). Retrieved from: Link. 
 
By applying SVD in a matrix, the patterns of the data can be unfolded. For this reason, 
SVD has a wide range of applications such as pattern recognition or signal processing. For 
example, it has been used by Netflix to predict user ratings for films, based on previous reviews 
(Paterek, 2007). The algorithm can be used also for missing data imputation, if a low-rank

3.2.7 Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) 
To describe the Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA), firstly the traditional 
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) should be mentioned. PCA is a dimensionality-reduction 
method, often used in large datasets for easier interpretation. PCA replaces original variables 
with new variables, called principal components, which are orthogonal (i.e. they have zero 
covariations) and have variances (called eigenvalues) in decreasing order. PCA, finds in the 
data space, the dimensions (directions) with the largest variance out of the overall variance 
(Figure 17). As a result, high accuracy is sacrificed in the expense of simplicity.

3.3 Tensor Decomposition (TD) Methods 
The SVD concept of a matrix decomposition, can be expanded to higher dimensions and 
applied into higher-order tensors. This type of decomposition is called Tensor Decomposition 
(TD). TD includes a group of algorithms that have gained a wide attention in the field of ML 
and data mining (Kolda & Bader, 2009; Rabanser & Günnemann, 2017). With the usage of TD, 
the spatio-temporal patterns can be recognized simultaneously. It has been proved that the 
quantitative spatio-temporal data analysis with TD methods, can yield trustworthy results 
(Henretty, Baskaran, Ezick, Bruns-smith, & Simon, 2017). Moreover, TD algorithms are vastly 
==PAGE:47==
Imputation Algorithms, Tensor Operations and Decompositions 
31 
 
used for missing data imputation. Since their implementation is so extensive in this field with 
distinct challenges, the application of TD in missing data imputation gradually forms the field 
of Tensor Completion (TC), with TD being an intermediate process in the whole procedure 
(Song, Ge, Caverlee, & Hu, 2019). The most fundamental TD algorithms are the 
CANDECOMP/PARAFAC (CP) Decomposition and the Tucker Decomposition.

3.3.3 Tensor Completion (TC) Strategies 
In order to apply a TD algorithm in a sparse tensor and attempt to impute the missing 
data, some preprocessing must be made. The Tucker Decomposition algorithm cannot 
decompose the tensor if there are missing values. There are 2 ways to overcome this problem.  
The first approach, requires to impute the sparse tensor and then apply the 
decomposition. In the second method, which is called marginalization, the missing values are 
ignored and the model is built only based on the observed part. This is achieved by using a 
mask weighted tensor, where the observed values are given the number 1 and the missing the 
number zero. The marginalization method is often found in gradient-based optimizations or 
probabilistic methods. As an example, Acar, Dunlavy, Kolda, & Mørup in 2011 developed a 
CP-WOPT (CP-Weighted OPTimization) algorithm which was optimized based on a first-order 
gradient and it was proved to be both effective and highly scalable with different missing ratios. 
The marginalization methods are considered more robust than imputation when the missing 
ratio is large, but are hard to be optimized (Song et al., 2019).  
The imputation method was chosen for the implementation of Tucker Decomposition for 
the following reasons. Firstly, it was not possible to find marginalization methods for Tucker 
in the Python language. Secondly, the missing ratio of the dataset for 2017 was relatively low 
(34.43%) allowing the imputation method to still be effective. Thirdly, a sub-objective was to 
assess the performance of Tucker Decomposition on top of each imputation method.  
The most widely used imputation method before the application of decomposition is EM 
(Song et al., 2019). However, in this case almost all of the previously mentioned imputation 
methods were tested. The followed process was that at first, the tensor was unfolded in order to 
apply the imputation method. Then, the imputed matrix was folded again back to a tensor. The 
imputed tensor was decomposed with Tucker Decomposition in a preferably low rank in order 
to capture most of the pattern information and consider the rest as noise. As a result, the imputed 
values were affected by the observed and vice versa. The reconstructed tensor had all of its 
entries altered, but only the ones that were located in the position of the imputed were kept. In

3.4 Comparison of Matrix and Tensor Methods 
The CP decomposition model due to its super-diagonal core tensor, could be considered 
as a restricted version of Tucker (Papalexakis et al., 2016). Therefore, it allows for less degrees 
of freedom. Two-way methods such as PCA or RPCA provide even more degrees of freedom. 
This is happening because as a multi-linear array unfolds into a matrix, the variables of the 
unfolded modes get mixed up, so that the effect of one variable is not associated with one, but 
many elements of a loading vector. When a model uses more degrees of freedom, it can be 
considered as more complex and flexible, providing better fit than a simpler and restricted 
model (Bro, 1997). On the other hand, two-way models are prone to use the higher degrees of 
freedom to model noise and result in overfitting. The reason for the usage of multi-way methods 
such as Tucker, is not to achieve a better fit, but more robust and interpretable models than 2-
way methods (Bro, & Kiers, 2003). The increased number of parameters in two-way methods 
can be seen with an example. If PCA is applied to a I x J x K array, the 3-way tensor will unfold 
to a matrix I x JK (example in Figure 22). An D-component PCA solution would result in 
D(I+JxK) parameters (scores and loadings). A Tucker model with the same number of 
components in each mode, would have D(I+J+K)+D3 parameters and CP,  D(I+J+K) 
parameters (Bro, 1997).

4.1 Methodology 
In this type of study, the missing values were spread along the whole dataset. For this 
reason, the division of the dataset to a training and a test set in order to compare the imputation 
methods and validate the results, was not possible. Even if this option was available, it is 
suspected that the trained model would have been biased. The model would have been trained 
with data only from sunny days, whereas the missing values were happening in cloudy days, 
where the λE is lower. The patterns of the dataset had to be uncovered with unsupervised 
methods, but still comparisons had to be done.  
To achieve this, at first the study area in Majadas Oak Savanna was chosen. If the whole 
area of Spain (834x1115x365) was attempted to be imputed at once, the procedure would not 
only be extremely computationally expensive, but also there wouldn’t be the capability to 
control the improvement of the results by tuning the model parameters.  At first, the land use 
conditions had to be as homogeneous as possible, to test the algorithms under the simplest 
conditions. Moreover, the area should be small enough for computational efficiency and at the 
same time should be large enough, so that the results could be meaningful. Additionally, the 
study area should include the two pixels where the north and south EC flux towers were located.  
After the area choice, three different tests were conducted, gradually imitating more 
realistic conditions for the algorithms. In the first test, as it can be seen in the workflow of this 
Chapter (Figure 23), simulated tensors were created with repeated time-frames from the area.

4.2.3.1 Temporal Rank Approximation of Tucker 
Decomposition for each Imputation Method 
Choosing the appropriate temporal rank, apart from the missing data percentage, depends 
on how many independent spatial changes exist over time. For example, in an area that a city 
is located next to a forest, the two land surfaces will evapotranspire differently throughout the 
year. The ET of a forest will be dynamic over time, but the ET of the city will be much more 
stable. For this reason, two time-mode loadings would be needed for the accurate description 
of the patterns. In this project so far, since the area is relatively homogeneous, everything is 
more or less changing together and a very small time rank could be used. However, if many 
independent phenological changing patterns exist, the temporal rank should be increased, 
otherwise the error would become larger. The complexity of the system would not be properly 
described from the existing parameters with a very low rank. That is one of the reasons why 
the whole area of Spain was not chosen for decomposition, but instead smaller regions 
separately. The entire peninsula would have so many independent changing land use patterns, 
==PAGE:68==
Evaluation of the Imputation Methods in a Small, Homogeneous Area 
52 
 
that would require a very high temporal rank, to avoid the error increase. On the other hand, 
with a lower temporal rank, the most important information is kept and the rest is considered 
as noise improving the results of the basic imputation algorithms. It would not be possible to 
use this trick in such a large area. 
In Figure 41, the temporal rank approximation for the LOCF Tucker algorithm is 
presented. It is observed that Tucker improved the RRMSE of LOCF even by using only the 
least ranks for each dimension (1, 1, 1). The best ranks are (5, 13, 8) for 1-30 % of missing data, 
(2, 13, 8) for 30-75% and (1, 13, 8) for >75%. This was happening for the same reason (degrees 
of freedom) as it was explained about the SVD rank approximation in the previous section 
(page 50), but this time for Tucker Decomposition. The rank (2, 13, 8) was chosen, as it yielded 
the most robust results.  
 
Figure 41: Temporal rank performance comparison (RRMSE) of LOCF-Tucker imputation algorithm for 
different percentages of missing data in the compressed tensor (213x13x8). LOCF: Last Observation 
Carried Forward. 
 
It is also evident that by increasing the temporal rank, the RRMSE gradually increased 
and when the maximum number of temporal mode loadings was used (213), LOCF-Tucker 
perfectly converged with LOCF. This proves the fact that, when all of the ranks in the Tucker 
Decomposition are used, the reconstructed tensor is identical to the tensor before the 
decomposition, because all of the reconstruction information is available. On the other hand, as 
it was already stated, by using less time-mode loadings than the maximum to describe the 
temporal distribution for the entire tensor, the most important information is kept.

4.3 Conclusion 
The assessment of the imputation methods showed that in the compressed tensor, RPCA 
performed better than Tucker Decomposition in low missing ratios and vice versa. A MC 
method (RPCA), is more complex, because it contains more parameters and degrees of freedom 
than a TD method (Tucker) which provides robustness. The TD method Tucker combined with 
the imputation algorithms, improved their performance. The highest spatial and a low temporal 
rank provided the optimal results. More temporal ranks i.e. parameters of Tucker 
Decomposition, yielded better results in low missing ratios and vice versa. In the real dataset, 
it was seen that Tucker generally homogenizes an imputed time-frame, which is not preferable 
if the area is heterogeneous. MC methods (RPCA, SVD) could fill semi-sparse time-frames 
with accuracy. They were taking into account the observed values of a semi-sparse time-frame, 
which had lower λE because of the cloud presence. The methods could not gap-fill completely 
empty time-frames. However, time-series methods are based on temporal information and can 
fill empty time-frames. Finally, it is more safe to compare the imputed RS ET time-series to 
EC ET time-series in the two towers qualitatively than quantitatively. The RS model did not 
always converge well to the EC ET trends, resulting in metric numbers that are misleading. 
Although Historical Average vs EC ET had a similar RRMSE to the observed RS vs EC ET, 
the declining trend of EC in cloudy days was not followed by Historical Average.

5.3 Creation of the Compressed Data 
The main difficulty for the creation of the compressed dataset, this time in a larger area, 
was the existence of two pixels that were NaNs at every time-point. These two pixels did not 
allow the arrangement of dense time-frames, since the compressed tensor should be consisted 
of pixels that did not contain NaN values. As it was already stated in chapter 2.2, there were 
1.28% of missing data at all times inside the region of Spain. This was happening because the 
model did not run into city areas and also because there were pixels that their reflectance could 
not be captured by the sensors. In that specific case, these two pixels belonged to the water 
body of the river. In order to create the compressed tensor, a high number was applied (9999) 
to all the time-points of the pixels which was afterwards removed. As a result, the created 
compressed tensor had two pixels with NaNs at every time-point. Since the area was larger, it 
was more likely for a time-frame to contain a cloud which led to missing data. For this reason, 
less time-frames were included in the compressed tensor, which had dimensions 30x40x168, 
as opposed to 13x8x213 for the smaller tensor. Artificial missing data in different percentages 
were also added to the compressed tensor. 
==PAGE:94==
Implementation in a Larger Area 
78 
 
Not all of the imputation algorithms were applied to the larger tensor. Mean and 
Temporal EM did not yield sufficient results in the small tensor, so they were abandoned. kNN 
was also not used, because it was too time consuming for the larger area. As a result, the applied 
algorithms were LOCF, Spatial EM, SVD along with their Tucker Decompositions and RPCA. 
A rank approximation was also conducted by increasing gradually the temporal rank. 
The existence of the NaNs in the two pixels created also problems with the application 
of the algorithms, since the RRMSE, R2 and Tucker Decomposition results, should not be 
affected. For this reason, an implementation method was created.  
For LOCF, as the algorithm could not function if there were NaN pixels at every time-
point (the algorithm would not have any time-series input), at first the pixels were given again 
the number 9999. Then, LOCF was applied for the rest missing data and the RRMSE and R2, 
were calculated without taking into account the 9999 values, since these values were considered 
as original. Afterwards, the 9999s became NaNs again and were filled with the kNN algorithm 
(k=5). This was done because the next step was to apply the Tucker Decomposition and since 
Tucker is rearranging the whole tensor, it should not be affected by a high number such as 9999. 
Lastly, the RRMSE and R2 for LOCF-Tucker were calculated again without taking into account 
these pixels, by masking them as original.  
Historical Average did not contain any original values for the two pixels from the 
previous years to impute. The values were gap-filled with kNN and were not taken into account 
in the metrics calculations. 
For SVD, the two pixels could not be filled and were given only zero values. The zeroes 
were again turned into NaNs and gap-filled with kNN. The gap-filled values with kNN were 
not taken into account in the metrics calculations. 
Lastly, Spatial EM and RPCA could successfully impute the pixels (they were only 
affected by completely missing time-frames), but again these pixels were not included in the 
metrics calculations.

5.5 Conclusion 
This Chapter, mainly contained the assessment of the effect of high or low spatial 
heterogeneity in the performance of the imputation methods. The methods performed better in 
a small, homogeneous area compared to a larger, heterogeneous area, because of the increased 
complexity of the latter. Furthermore, the imputation in a larger area was more computationally 
heavy and time consuming, causing some algorithms like kNN to be too expensive to apply. 
Concerning the TD effect on the methods, the Tucker Decomposition required more ranks to

6 Proposed Method 
Based on the results from the tests in the previous chapters, it was decided to propose a 
combination of methods for the imputation of the ET RS dataset of Spain. The idea behind the 
proposed method was supported on a series of conclusions.  
Firstly, the algorithms were performing better in smaller and more homogeneous areas. 
Additionally, none of the applied methods alone could provide the best results and at the same 
time was capable of gap-filling the whole tensor. The MC methods such as SVD and RPCA, 
could not impute completely missing time-frames. However, these methods were taking into 
account the reduced λΕ of the observed values in semi-sparse time-frames due to cloudiness, 
gap-filling the missing data accordingly. On the other hand, a time-series method such as LOCF 
could gap-fill the whole dataset, but in semi-sparse time-frames, it was not so effective as the 
MC methods. Furthermore, another drawback of the specific time-series method LOCF, was 
that since it was imputing a time-point with the value of a previous day, if many time-frames 
were missing, the result was to impute the same value in a row. This created an artificial looking 
time-series, which did not correspond to the dynamic fluctuations of ET. Moreover, when there 
were many missing time-frames in a row, neither Tucker Decomposition was able to provide 
fluctuations, as it was changing the imputed time-frames in exactly the same way. 
 
6.1 Methodology  
For the previously mentioned reasons, it was decided to gap-fill the whole dataset of 
Spain by using many small, homogeneous areas. The time-frames that were not completely 
sparse would be imputed with a MC method, in order to take into account the values of the 
available surrounding pixels (page 62). In a small homogeneous region, the missing pixels 
should have similar values as their neighboring available pixels. Compared to SVD, RPCA was 
the most precise and for this reason, this algorithm was chosen.  
The time-frames that were completely missing would be imputed with a time-series 
method. On the other hand, a more complex time-series method than LOCF should be applied 
in order to avoid the repetitive gap-filled values. For that reason, the moving average (MA) 
time-series method was chosen to be used. As it was already stated in Chapter 3, the exponential 
average was used in the MA method. The observations that were located closer to the missing 
value, were given a higher weight for the calculation of the average. 
==PAGE:102==
Proposed Method 
86 
 
The imputation process would be: Apply RPCA to the sparse tensor → Only semi-sparse 
time-frames are imputed and the completely sparse time-frames now have the zero value 
because of RPCA → Make zeros again Nan → Apply MA to the whole tensor to fill the 
completely sparse time-frames. 
Afterwards, it had to be tested if a combination with Tucker Decomposition was yielding 
better results. The name of the proposed imputation method is RPCA-MA from the abbreviation 
of each separate algorithm. With the addition of Tucker, the method becomes RPCA-[MA-
Tucker]. The brackets symbolize that Tucker was implemented only to the imputed values with 
MA and not with RPCA. The Historical Average, RPCA-MA and RPCA-[MA-Tucker] were 
compared in a compressed tensor, where again missing data was added in different percentages, 
as it will be shown in the next section. Lastly, the proposed algorithm was implemented in the 
real dataset and compared to the ground truth EC ET. The workflow of this chapter is 
demonstrated in Figure 87.

Random, EC: Eddy Covariance. 
 
An important problem was that all of the code processing in this project was done in the 
Python programming language, which did not contain sufficient time-series imputation 
packages, as opposed to the R programming language. The imputeTS R package was decided 
to be used for the MA imputation. The trial of running the R package through Python (with the 
rpy2 package) was not successful, so a more practical method was used. To avoid spending 
time rewriting all of the previous code again in R, all of the work until the RPCA imputation 
was conducted in Python and saved in a .csv file as a sparse matrix. Then in R, the .csv file was 
loaded and imputed with the moving average method, saving the results in a new complete

matrix in a .csv file. The file was loaded in Python in order to continue the whole process, such 
as the Tucker Decomposition and the calculation of RRMSE. This procedure was repeated 
manually when the algorithm was applied in different percentages of missing data. 
 
6.1.1 Application in the Compressed Data 
At first, it had to be tested if the MA method yields better results than LOCF in a small 
compressed tensor (13x8x213), for different missing ratios. Additionally, before applying the 
RPCA-MA method to the real dataset, it had to be confirmed that the algorithm was yielding 
better results than the Historical Average in a controlled environment, were the imputed values 
could be compared to the observed. For this reason, again, the same small compressed tensor 
(13x8x213) was used. On the other hand, this time the artificial missing data that would be 
introduced in the dataset, should also include completely missing time-frames. If only partially 
empty time-frames were used, all of them would have been successfully gap-filled by RPCA. 
As a result, artificial missing data were added at different percentages (20%, 37.5, 56% and 
79%), mixing the pixel-wise and time-frame-wise types throughout the tensor.  
 
6.1.2 Application in the Real Dataset 
RPCA-MA and RPCA-[MA-Tucker] were implemented to impute the small real tensor 
(13x8x365). The results were interpreted qualitatively by visualizing the spatial and temporal 
profiles of the imputations. Comparisons were done to Historical Average too. Furthermore, 
the ground truth from the north and south EC flux towers were used for comparison to the gap-
filled RS values.

6.3 Conclusion 
The gap-filling method RPCA-MA, was proposed combining two imputation methods. 
Tucker was also assessed in combination with the imputed values from MA (RPCA-[MA-
Tucker]). The chosen area has to be small and homogeneous in order to take advantage of the 
cloud influence to the observed values that were adjacent to the missing. The proposed method 
outperformed the Historical Average in the compressed tensor. In the real data, the method 
==PAGE:114==
Proposed Method 
98 
 
imputed significantly lower values than Historical Average in the spring, summer and autumn 
seasons. In the winter, Historical Average imputed lower values than the proposed method in 
some days, because the vegetation was still growing and the was no significant difference in 
the λΕ of a sunny and a cloudy day. RPCA-MA and RPCA-[MA-Tucker] followed well the EC 
ET trend. The Tucker addition improved the RRMSE, R and R2 of RPCA-MA. However, with 
Tucker the imputation method becomes more complex. Further investigation is needed in 
different types of land use, because the methods were tested only in the Majadas Oak Savanna 
region, where the vegetation is very specific.

7 General Conclusion 
In this study, an assessment of several imputation algorithms in a spatiotemporal sparse 
RS ET dataset of Spain, was carried out. The missing data were created mainly because of the 
existence of clouds. Clouds were also blocking the incoming solar radiation, decreasing the real 
λΕ values of a surface, compared to the λΕ of a sunny day. The currently used imputation 
method is the Historical Average. This method, overestimated the imputed λΕ values because 
it was based on sunny days for gap-filling. The explored methods were the Historical Average, 
Mean imputation, LOCF, MA, kNN, SVD, the Tucker Decomposition after each imputation 
method and RPCA. The chosen study area was Majadas Oak Savanna in Extremadura. 
At first, three types of tests were conducted in a small, homogeneous area. The first two 
tests were applied in simulated and compressed tensors, where artificial missing data (MCAR) 
were added. An assessment of the combination of Tucker Decomposition with each method 
was also conducted. In the third test, the imputation algorithms gap-filled the missing values of 
a real tensor (MAR). Comparisons were done to the EC ET in two flux towers.  In the 
compressed data, the best algorithm was RPCA, because as a MC method, it was using more 
parameters, providing more degrees of freedom and a more complex model. Tucker however, 
provided robustness because as a TC method, it was taking the spatiotemporal patterns into 
account simultaneously, simplifying the model. Tucker improved the imputation performance 
for all the imputation algorithms.  The optimal Tucker rank would require low temporal and the 
highest spatial ranks. In low missing ratios, more time-mode loadings were yielding better 
results and vice versa. Less temporal ranks provided robustness in high missing ratios, as they 
restricted the degrees of freedom. In the real data, RPCA could not fill completely-sparse time-
frames. Time-Series methods were able to impute completely sparse time-frames because they 
were not based on spatial information. Historical Average performed better in the winter when 
the vegetation was still growing and the λE of a sunny was similar to a cloudy day. The 
algorithms were also applied to a larger, less homogeneous area, in a compressed tensor. More 
Tucker ranks were required to provide the optimal results, which were worse than in a small 
area, because the phenological and computational complexity were increased.  
A combined method was proposed with the name RPCA-MA. RPCA would gap-fill the 
semi-sparse time-frames and MA the completely sparse. The addition of Tucker to the gap-
filled values only from MA was investigated, forming RPCA-[MA-Tucker]. The area should 
be small and homogeneous. The reason was that, it is suspected that the observed values that 
are adjacent to the missing in a semi-sparse time-frame, are also affected by the cloud presence 
and their λE is similarly reduced, given that the area is homogeneous. The tests of the proposed 
==PAGE:116==
General Conclusion 
100 
 
method RPCA-MA, showed that it significantly outperformed the Historical Average in a 
compressed tensor (MCAR). In the real tensor (MAR), the method could significantly reduce 
the overestimated λΕ values of Historical Average in spring, summer and autumn. Compared 
to the EC ET time-series, the numbers of the RPCA-MA successfully caught the EC trend. 
Furthermore, the addition of Tucker, improved the results for RPCA-MA. The proposed 
method is promising for geophysical data gap-filling, but more investigation is required in 
various land use types.

8 Limitations and Future Work 
In the current thesis, the Python language was mainly used. However, after extensive 
investigation, it was realized that the language lacked in missing data imputation packages, as 
opposed for example to the R language. In fact, most of the imputation packages in Python 
were translated from R. On the other hand, the commands of Python for data preparation were 
very fast and convenient. As an example, the author could not find trustworthy Tensor 
Decomposition packages that support missing values in Python (marginalization method). The 
R language has a wide variety of imputation libraries and the MA imputation was used from 
the imputeTS R package. However, the transition from the R language to Python and vice versa 
was time-consuming. There exist packages such as the Python package rpy2, that runs R from 
a Python environment and the R package Reticulate, that runs Python in an R interface. 
However, their application was complicated and the encountered problems could not be solved 
in the period of this thesis. For this reason, in order to take advantage of the positive aspects 
from both languages, the usage of these interoperating libraries could be further explored. In 
that way, an easier accessed code would be created and more imputation algorithms from R 
libraries could be applied and studied. 
Additionally, after investigating that the method provides satisfactory results in several 
land use types, it could be attempted to impute the whole RS ET dataset of Spain. This process 
would require the usage of High-Performance Computing Cluster (HPCC), that could handle 
the high memory computational demands of the calculations.

Appendix I 
Software that were Used in the Project  
 
For the sake of being able to explore the RS ET dataset in a fast way, the Panoply 
Software was used. Panoply is a desktop application created by NASA that interactively plots 
georeferenced and other data encoded in the NetCDF, HDF and GRIB file formats. 
The ArcMap Software was used to create the maps of the communities, land use and 
digital elevation model of Spain. Additionally, with the same software the basemaps and the 
land use maps of the chosen test area in Majadas Oak Savanna were created. ArcMap is the 
main component of Esri's ArcGIS suite of geospatial processing programs, and is used primarily 
to view, edit, create, and analyze geospatial data (Esri, 1987). 
The Python open source interpreted programming language was mainly used in this 
project. The Python version 3.7 was used in the Spyder 4 IDE which functions within the 
Anaconda Framework. Bellow, the most used external Python packages are listed: 
 
Pandas: It provides data structures and operations for manipulating numerical tables 
and time series (McKinney & Team, 2015). It was mostly used to read and analyze the 
EC flux tower (.csv) dataset. 
 
Numpy: It adds support for large, multi-dimensional arrays and matrices, along with a 
large collection of high-level mathematical functions to operate on these arrays (Klein, 
2014). This package was used extensively in this project, in procedures ranging from 
the preparation of the RS ET dataset to problem solution. 
 
Xarray: It introduces labels in the form of dimensions, coordinates and attributes on 
top of raw Numpy-like arrays (Hoyer & Hamman, 2017). It was used to read the 
NetCDF RS dataset and to provide time-series labeling when Numpy was incapable. 
 
Matplotlib: A Python 2D plotting library which produces quality figures in a variety of 
hardcopy formats and interactive environments across platforms (Hunter, 2007). The 
main plotting module in the project. 
 
Seaborn: Another data visualization library which is based on Matplotlib (Waskom, 
2018). It was used for heatmap plotting and for scatter plots.  
 
Impyute: A missing data imputation package with ML and TS algorithms. The ML 
algorithms that were used were kNN and EM and the TS algorithm was LOCF.  
==PAGE:124==
Appendix I 
108 
 
 
Fancyimpute: Another missing data imputation package with several MC algorithms. 
It was inspired by the softImpute imputation module for the R programming language 
(Hastie, Mazumder, Lee, & Zadeh, 2015). The Iterative SVD algorithm was used.  
 
Tensorly: A tensor methods Application Programming Interface (API) with tensor 
algebra, regression and decomposition algorithms (Kossaifi, Panagakis, Anandkumar, 
& Pantic, 2019). The RPCA algorithm was used for imputation and the Tucker and 
Non Negative Tucker algorithms were used for decomposition. 
The package imputeTS was used from the R programming language (Moritz & Bartz-
Beielstein, 2017). ImputeTS is a time-series imputation package that offers several imputation 
functions and missing data plots. The moving average (MA) algorithm was used from it.
