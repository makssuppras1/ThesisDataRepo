Abstract
In many areas of Denmark, clayey tills with heterogeneous features have formed the
aquitard overlaying the primary aquifer where the contaminants caused by surﬁcial leakage
and spillage are often retained. For the purpose of risk assessment and remediation of
contaminated sites, predictive work for quantifying the contaminant discharge in such
heterogeneous systems is needed. This study was to quantify the contaminant discharge
and understand the process of contaminant transport in a heterogeneous clayey-till aquifer
system by the approach of numerical modeling. An active industrial site at Industrivej,
Lynge located in the central part of the Capital Region of Denmark was relied on for the
case study. The study site has been identiﬁed with contamination of trichloroethylene
(TCE) and apparent sand lens heterogeneity in the clayey till.
A comprehensive understanding of geological and hydrogeological properties at the
site constituted the preliminary preparation for the numerical modeling. Crucial site-
speciﬁc properties including local geology, soil characteristics, hydraulic conductivity and
contaminant source distribution were fully integrated and utilized for model setup. Spatial
interpolation method and computer-aided design have supported the interpretation and
visualisation of contaminant source area and geological heterogeneity in the modeling
process. In this study, ﬂow and solute transport models in 2D and 3D space were
established respectively. Forward simulation was conducted to evaluate the contaminant
mass discharge and the mass storage in the till. Contaminant leaching into the aquifer
was observed to form a plume and cause pollution to groundwater in reality, and this
process was also simulated in the study, providing a reﬂection on the historical plume
development.
Results from 2D model yielded a contaminant mass of 0.27 kg/m, and by considering
a maximum contaminated length of 60 m at the site, a maximum of 16.2 kg can be
estimated as the entire mass storage. Mass discharge into the aquifer was simulated
between 0.3∼4 g/m/yr (max. 18∼240 g/yr) with an inﬁltration range of 20∼150 mm/yr.
Depletion period of removing 90% of initial mass storage could range between 80∼500
years in 2D model with an inﬁltration range of 20∼150 mm/yr. 3D model showed that
the total mass storage at the site was around 6 kg and a mass discharge of 5 g/yr with
an inﬁltration rate of 75 mm/yr, resulting in a depletion period between 90∼200 years by
considering only sand lenses or only clay matrix existing in the till. Overall, 2D model
results have shown good agreement with previous investigations while 3D model results
appeared to have underestimated the contamination status at the site.
The simulations of plume development in the aquifer showed that after a leaching
period of 50 years the 100 m downstream concentration proﬁles become comparable with
the current measurements. Compared to an industrial history of 50∼60 years at the site,
the simulation was able to reﬂect the possible process of contaminant transport in the

past.
With respect to the inﬂuence of sand lenses, it was found from 2D model simulations
that the horizontal contaminant spread in the till was hardly aﬀected by the existence of
sand lenses. But lower inﬁltration rate could lead to stronger horizontal contaminant
spread in the long run. In contrast, the inﬂuence from inﬁltration rate was found to have
importance inﬂuence on the ﬂow and contaminant transport, superior to the inﬂuence of
anisotropic conductivity.
Finally, the limitations and possible improvements on the study were discussed.
The needs for reliable inﬁltration rate and accurate delineation of the source area were
highlighted.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Groundwater constitutes the largest reservoir of freshwater in the world, accounting for
over 97% of all freshwater available on earth (excluding glaciers and ice caps) (Euro-
pean Commission, 2008). Groundwater is an important resource of drinking water for
inhabitants, and also for the use in industrial and agricultural activities. In addition
to the human-related utilities, groundwater has its considerate environmental value,
functioning as an important link of the hydrological cycle through the maintenance of
wetlands and river ﬂows (European Commission, 2008). Deterioration of groundwater
quality not only aﬀects the practical use but meanwhile may cause direct impact on other
ecosystems (European Commission, 2008). The European Commission has made water
protection one of its priorities and issued the EU Water Framework Directive (WFD) in
2000 to regulate the water and environmental resources with the purpose of reaching
good status for all EU’s surface- and groundwater bodies. The WFD establishes the
basis for international action to conserve, protect and improve water bodies’ quality and
their sustainable use in all member states (Vázquez-Suñé et al., 2006).
By 2014, more than 35,000 Danish properties and sites have been classiﬁed as either
contaminated or potentially contaminated (Danish Environmental Protection Agency,
2014) in Denmark.
Much soil is contaminated due to former land use.
Industrial
and trade activities have over time resulted in a large number of contaminated sites
(COWI, 2001). Inappropriate handling of chemical substances and oil-products has
caused their spillage and leakage to the soil and even groundwater environment (COWI,
2001). Contaminants leaching from old landﬁlls/industrial sites have become a big
concern. In Denmark, the regions have the public responsibility for reduction of risks
from contamination in soil. The Capital Region of Denmark (2018) has deﬁned an overall
goal to reduce contamination and conduct risk assessments in the regional areas where
80% of drinking water is abstracted, by the end of 2024. Contamination with chlorinated
solvents is a subject of high priority for the Danish government due to the serious threat
to groundwater quality.
For planning and regulatory purposes in groundwater protection, predictive work is
necessary to be performed at contaminated sites to assess leaching from soils and the
evolution of groundwater quality for the purpose of risk assessment and remediation
of contaminated sites. Field experiments are costly, time consuming and sometimes
impossible to conduct due to the inaccessibility of the plume below the ground surface,
or due to unclear knowledge of historical placement of contaminant source. As a result,
numerical modeling has been extensively used in the study of contaminant transport
(Pinder & Gray, 2013; Anderson et al., 2015) and served as a very eﬀective way in
dealing with these types of pollutant problems. Numerical model is promising to solve
practical problems with heterogeneous domain and boundaries by means of excellent

From previous studies, contaminants have been observed to migrate through pref-

tills such as fractures and macropores where advective ﬂow can become eminent. In

been paid on the preferential ﬂow caused by fractures and macropores in aquitards.
Embedded sand lenses are another geological feature, occurring in the shallow portion of
till, which have been observed in many clayey till investigations (Krüger, 1979; Lesemann
et al., 2010), Sand lenses consist of materials with coarse grain sizes (e.g. sand and gravel)

sand lenses have elongated, anisotropic shapes with horizontal extents ranging from few

of ﬂow and solutes from surface, the conductive paths caused by sand lenses could be
more moderate. Sand lens heterogeneity and its hydraulic property can lead to a change
of ﬂow direction and cause a wider spread of contamination in horizontal directions.
Existing studies on contamination transport modeling have paid less attention to clayey
till types with embedded sand lenses, especially lack of ﬁeld-scale studies. Therefore it
is meaningful to have an in-depth knowledge and understanding of the ﬂow and solute
transport pattern in site-speciﬁc clayey till system, which constitutes the initial of the
project.
The project studies a contaminated site at Industrivej, Lynge located in the central

about 6 km away. The area’s groundwater aquifer is overlain by the aquitard of clayey
till, which extends from the top of the aquifer to the ground surface. The heterogeneity of

distinct fractures have been observed. The site has been occupied for industrial business

contamination in soil and groundwater underneath the site with concentrations exceeding
the threshold limit. Chemicals leaching from sparse storage and production activities
are expected to act as point sources. Chlorinated solvents, primarily TCE has been
identiﬁed as the main contaminants in the subsurface, and therefore TCE is selected as

Background research
2.1
Geology of clayey tills
Much of the soil contamination that represents a source of leaching to groundwater in
North America and Europe is found in low-permeability clayey till (Jørgensen et al.,
2004). Till or glacial till is a term that refers to unsorted glacial sediments, and clayey
till thus refers to clay-rich glacial deposits.
Glacial deposits are found in large parts of the Northern hemisphere including
Scandinavia where the ice shields covered the land during the late Pleistocene glaciations
(Houmark-Nielsen, 2003). The formation of glacial deposits is a result of a complex
interplay of glaciotectonic processes. The deposition of heterogeneous material, as well as
the diﬀerent patterns and degrees of deformation, have resulted in the unique and complex
geology in glacial landscapes (Kessler, 2012). The material deposited by ice is unsorted or
mixed in size, and therefore the contents of glacial till vary from clay to mixtures of clay,
sand, gravel, and boulders. Because deposition and deformation are recurrent processes,
glacial till is formed by sediment successions rather than homogeneous sedimentary units,
which may cause diﬀerent layers in the till (Larsen & Piotrowski, 2003).
One signiﬁcant structural heterogeneity in tills is the embedded sand deposits which
have been observed in numerous till studies (Krüger, 1979; Lesemann et al., 2010). The
sand deposits are collectively referred to as sand lenses, which are typical deformation
structures in glacial sediments. These lenses of coarse material occur at multiple spatial
scales and vary drastically in geometry and composition (Kessler et al., 2012). Most lenses
are comprised by ﬁne, medium or coarse sand, sometimes mixed with ﬁne gravel. The
grain composition of sand lenses is variable, due to the diﬀerent origin and glaciotectonic
ﬁngerprints. Sand lenses are observed at many till sites and show multiple spatial scales
and variation, characterized by elongated, anisotropic shapes with horizontal extents
ranging from few decimeters to tens of meter (Kessler et al., 2012). The elongated
geometry and hydraulic property of sand lenses may cause a change of ﬂow direction and
lead to a wider spread of contamination in horizontal directions.
2.2
Inﬁltration groundwater recharge
Inﬁltration is the ﬂow of water from above ground into the subsurface and is one of
the main processes determining groundwater recharge (Ferré & Warrick, 2005). Some
water that inﬁltrates into the subsurface will remain in the shallow soil layer, moving
vertically and horizontally through the soil and subsurface material. Some of the water
may inﬁltrate deeper, recharging groundwater aquifer (US geological survey, 2020). The
==PAGE:24==
6
2 Background research
greatest factor controlling inﬁltration is atmospheric precipitation, and other factors
include slope discharge, irrigation, river runoﬀ, soil characteristics and saturation, land
cover and evapotranspiration (Kovalevsky, 2010). The value of inﬁltration groundwater
recharge can be usually determined by the following water balance equation:
W = P −E −R ± S
(2.1)
Where W is inﬁltration; P is precipitation; E is evapotranspiration; R is surface
runoﬀand S is change of moisture storage in the aeration zone (unsaturated zone).
In diﬀerent regions, the proportion of precipitation contributing to groundwater
recharge can vary from 5% to 40% of annual total precipitation or 20% to 28% of eﬀective
precipitation (Kovalevsky, 2010).
Evapotranspiration is another important factor aﬀecting the inﬁltration recharge. At
shallow depth (2∼3 m) evapotranspiration normally exceeds inﬁltration and groundwater
discharge will occur in such locations (Kovalevsky, 2010). With increasing depth of
groundwater table, the intensity of evapotranspiration decreases and at a certain depth
will be balanced and then prevailed by inﬁltration (Kovalevsky, 2010).
At depths
exceeding 5∼7 m, inﬁltration recharge in porous media can be considered constant for
practical assessment (Kovalevsky, 2010).
Inﬁltration recharge has preferential pathways in soil and rocks due to ﬁssures, cracks,
macropores and soil fauna, and also will concentrate in surface depressions (Kovalevsky,
2010; Ferré & Warrick, 2005). In the latter case, the water volume puddled in the
depressions may exceed ﬁeld capacity resulting in water that percolates down to recharge
groundwater so that the water level rises under such depressions during recharge periods
(Mitchell & Jones Jr, 1978).
2.3
Groundwater ﬂow and solute transport
The transport of solutes in subsurface environments has been studied with great intensity
since the last century as a result of growing concern about water quality and pollution
(Dagan, 1987). The ﬂow of water through a porous media is governed by laws of physics
and can be described by diﬀerential equations (Fetter, 2001). The water can act as a
carrier of solved contaminants and the rate at which a solute moves through a porous
media is determined by several mechanisms. These mechanisms often act simultaneously
on the solute and may include such processes as convection, diﬀusion and dispersion,
retardation and degradation (Fetter, 2008). Due to no evident degradation has been
observed at the study site, the solute transport concerned in this study is determined
by the four processes: convection, diﬀusion and dispersion and retardation. In essence,
the method to describe solute transport is based on partial diﬀerential equations (Fetter,
2008). This section will introduce the groundwater ﬂow and transport processes and
their governing equations.

2.4
Numerical modeling
2.4.1
Inclusion of heterogeneity
In clayey till, hydraulic properties are highly dependent on the geology, heterogeneous
features and soil composition (Kessler, 2012). The clay matrix usually has a high density
and low permeability and thus causes a slow solute diﬀusion process (Christiansen et
al., 2010; Manoli et al., 2010). The existence of high-permeability sand lenses can create
preferential ﬂow paths and enhance the ﬂow particle transport within the till. The
diﬀerence of characteristic hydraulic conductivities between clay matrix and conductive
sand lenses can reach 4∼6 orders of magnitude (Kessler, 2012).
In the study of ﬂow and transport model, challenges exist in the degree of geological
heterogeneity included in the model (Kessler, 2012). A lot of studies have focused on
the solute transport in fractured tills, where the methods used to represent fractures
include to consider equivalent porous media (Jørgensen et al., 2004) or to consider
double-permeability models (Berkowitz, 2002) or to consider more complex transport
processes (Chambon et al., 2010). Sand lens heterogeneity can be accounted for ﬂow
and transport model as separate geological formation in contrast to the clay matrix

(Kessler, 2012). To model a system with heterogeneous sand lenses, the inclusion of
site-speciﬁc geology can rely on the investigated geological proﬁles while the lack of
three-dimensional excavation often limits the research to two-dimensional space. For the
development of a ﬂow or transport model in three dimensions, constructing a particular
3D geological model has been explored by many studies. Based on high-resolution 2D
geological data, stochastic algorithms were explored to simulate the spatial correlation
of geological features such as sand lenses to obtain a complete heterogeneous geological
scene (Høyer et al., 2019; Kessler et al., 2013; Huysmans & Dassargues, 2005).
2.4.2
Modeling software
In this study, the software "COMSOL Multiphysics" has been used for modeling. When
solving the models, COMSOL Multiphysics uses the proven ﬁnite element method (FEM)
(COMSOL, 1998). The FEM is a general numerical method for solving both ordinary
and partial diﬀerential equations in science and engineering (Engsig-Karup, 2017).
The FEM is based on a variational method of approximation of the PDE and is
well-suited to deal with problems with complex geometries (Engsig-Karup, 2017). In
general, to solve a problem, the FEM divides a global domain into sub-domains, referred
as to ﬁnite elements, where a set of local approximations is constructed which can
be patched together to form a global solution to the problem (Engsig-Karup, 2017).
The subdivision of the global domain is achieved by speciﬁc discretization in the space
dimensions, which is implemented by the construction of a mesh of the object.
The software runs the ﬁnite element analysis together with adaptive meshing (if
selected) and error control using a variety of numerical solvers (COMSOL, 1998).
In this study, building a model in COMSOL consists of the following steps:
• Deﬁne the model geometry, which means the particular 2D/3D clayey-till aquifer
geological proﬁle in our case;
• Deﬁne the relevant variables, which includes a series of particular hydrogeological
parameters such as hydraulic conductivity, porosity and so on;
• Specify physics setting, which refers to deﬁne the equation to be solved, boundary
conditions and initial conditions;
• Create meshes, which enables the discretization of the geometry model into small
units of simple shapes, referred to as mesh elements;
• Compute the solution, which can be achieved through various built-in solvers in
the software either the default solver or self-selected solver;
• Processing and analyzing results, which includes deriving values and tables and
creating suitable graphs to suit the needs of the study.

Estimating hydraulic conductivity from grain size distribution analysis has been
studied by hydrogeologists for over one century and a variety of predictive formulas were
proposed. However for a given sample the prediction can vary by an order of magnitude
between various methods which causes a diﬃculty to identify the best method (Devlin,
2015). Thus it is supposed to be beneﬁcial to include various methods for a better
estimation.
Devlin (2015) have comprehended the major existing estimation methods and de-
veloped an Excel-based tool ’HydrogeoSieveXL’ worksheet to facilitate the estimation
of hydraulic conductivity and as well the comparison of results from diﬀerent methods.
This study utilized the worksheet ’HydrogeoSieveXL’ to perform the calculation. In
the worksheet, the grain size distribution curve of each soil sample was used as input
and a total of 15 methods can be applied to estimate K value for the sample. The
validity of these formulas depends on the characteristics of each grain curve, and can be
automatically determined by the worksheet.
After completing the computational work in the sheet, the valid formulas’ results
were collected to calculate the geometric mean and the result was used as the estimated

4.3.4
Data classiﬁcation based on geological formations
Plenty of information about soil properties and hydraulic conductivity has been obtained
from the existing datasets and as well from new results obtained in this study. Three
types of samples and measurements were classiﬁed by geological formations for the
purpose of site characterisation and they included the soil samples with foc, the soil
samples with clay content, and measured/estimated hydraulic conductivity.
Based on the documented investigation of site geology, the clayey till in the subsurface
was generally composed of the clay matrix and the high permeability phase of embedded
sand/silt lenses, where a major embedded sand layer had been observed in several
transects, dividing the clay matrix into two layers with diﬀerent consolidation status. In
the study, the clayey till was divided into three parts: upper clay cover layer (referred
to as upper cover), sand lenses, and lower clay cover layer (referred to as lower cover).
Beneath the clayey till was the transition zone with higher permeability which was
underlain by the deeper aquifer. A chalk layer existed below the meltwater aquifer.
The soil samples were classiﬁed according to the ﬁve geological formations including
the three formations in clayey till, the transition zone and the shallow depth of aquifer.
The measurements and estimations of hydraulic conductivity were classiﬁed in the similar
way but with more subdivision in the deep aquifer, because a zone with high permeability
was observed in the middle of the aquifer in previous investigations (NIRAS, 2019). As a
result, the deep aquifer was naturally divided into three layers referred to as L1∼L3 in
the following, and a total of seven geological formations were applied for the classiﬁcation
of hydraulic conductivity.
After the measured/estimated data were classiﬁed, for each geological formation, the
average value of foc and clay content were used to calculated the coeﬃcient Kd. The
geometric average of the valid values of estimated hydraulic conductivity were obtained
for each formation.

5.3
Delineating source area
Delineation of the source area was based on the ﬁeld measurements of discrete soil samples.
Considering that the soil samples are discrete and cannot represent the complete scene of
source area, creating a continuous source area from discrete sampling points was required
for the transport model.
Spatial interpolation is a common technique for creating continuous surface from
discrete sampling points. The technique uses discrete points with known values to
estimate values at unknown locations to create a continuous ﬁeld covering the sampling
area (QGIS, 2020). By connecting interpolation points with the same values, contour lines
can be drawn. A variety of spatial interpolation methods exist, for example Triagulated
Irregular Network (TIN), Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW), cubic splines, natural
neighbor interpolation, etc. Due to ease of implementation, TIN interpolation was used
in the study.
TIN is a vector-based means to create a surface by triangulating a set of sample points
(Esri, 2020). To do this, circumcircles around selected sample points are created and their
intersections are connected to a network of non overlapping and as compact as possible
triangles (see ﬁgure 5.3) (QGIS, 2020). This process is called Delaunay triangulation.
Then interpolation for each line in the TIN will be performed and the simplest method
is linear interpolation which is also frequently used in drawing contour lines manually.

(QGIS, 2020)

6.1.2
Soil characteristics
Soil samples were collected in the three campaigns (DTU-2018a-soil, DTU-2018b-soil
and DTU-2020-soil) mainly from the clayey till and the shallow depth of aquifer. Total
organic carbon content and clay content are two analyzed soil characteristics. These
soil samples were classiﬁed into diﬀerent geological units according to their depth and
geological descriptions.
6.1.2.1
Clay content and total organic carbon
Results of classiﬁed organic carbon content and clay content are shown in ﬁgure 6.1 in
which all samples collected from previous investigations have been plotted. Quantitative
results including the average values and standard deviation can be found in Appendix
C. The clay content in most of the discrete samples is below 15%, except for several
high-clay samples (20∼35%) in the upper cover. Most of the soil samples in the transition
zone and deep aquifer appear to have lower clay content (<5%) compared to samples
from the till. Organic carbon content in the three units of the clayey till has shown large
ranges, and most of soil samples in the clay till have values below 0.25% where the lower
clay cover appears to have higher foc values (>0.15%). The upper cover and sand lenses
generally have the organic carbon content below 0.15%. In contrast, the deep aquifer
appears to have lower foc (<0.1% or 0.05%). Few samples exist in the transition zone
and the observed foc can be found between the lower cover and the aquifer.

largely by its mass proportion of ﬁne particle, and the storage of organic carbon depend
at least partially on speciﬁc surface area of soil clay mineral (Yang et al., 2016). It was
expected that the soil with high clay contents also exhibits high level of organic carbon
content, while this trend is not observed in the classiﬁed results, except for the deep
aquifer where both contents had the lowest level.

6.1.3.2
Hydraulic head versus precipitation
The groundwater ﬂow model is assumed in steady state in this study, which means the
ﬂow ﬁeld is relatively stable. Transient ﬂow model is used when dynamics of the ﬂow
ﬁeld are important. Apparent ﬂow dynamics can be caused by human activities such
as water abstraction. In addition, groundwater level also exhibits natural ﬂuctuation
due to the varying recharge rate spatially and temporally. Precipitation is one important
factor to determine inﬁltration recharge. A series of hydraulic measurements have been
performed at the study site since 2017. In ﬁgure 6.6, measured hydraulic heads between
2017 and 2020 in three boreholes BA2, BA12 and BA13 have been displayed, in which
two screens exist for each borehole, respectively in the sand lenses (embedded in the clay
==PAGE:66==

cover) and in the deep aquifer. Precipitation data between 2018 and 2020 are plotted
against with hydraulic measurements and a moving average trend line is shown.
Figure 6.6: Temporal change of hydraulic heads in sand lenses (screen 2) and deep aquifer
(screen 1) at 3 boreholes against which monthly precipitation is plotted.
From the two-year precipitation pattern in ﬁgure 6.6, it is observed that rainfall is
usually weak during the period of April-June, especially in the year 2018 which had
a very dry period. Intense precipitation usually happens during August-October, and
March has also been observed with more rainfall. The annual precipitation in 2018 and
2019 is 471 and 813 mm/year respectively.
For each borehole, screen 1 is located in the deep aquifer and screen 2 is located in
the embedded sand lenses. The hydraulic head measured in sand lenses is higher than
the aquifer, and thus causes a downward vertical gradient through the clay cover. In
comparison to the monthly precipitation in the big catchment, in the deep aquifer, the
highest water level often appears in the lowest-precipitation period, which is between
April to May. The high water level is possibly related to the delayed impact of the prior
wet period, during which low evapotranspiration rate is likely to exist because of low
==PAGE:67==

land temperature and short sunshine time, as shown in Appendix G. The lowest water
level appears in wet periods especially in 2019 autumn, at the end of dry period. In
contrast, measured hydraulic heads in the sand lenses do not show apparent correlation
with the precipitation, and also exhibit larger amplitude of ﬂuctuation.
The possible reason for the observed phenomenon could be the regional precipitation
is linked to the overall recharge rate in a larger area. The water level in the sand lenses
could be more aﬀected by the varying recharge rate at a local scale, which is usually more
aﬀected by the strong evapotranspiration rate at shallow depth. Groundwater recharge
for the aquifer could be more controlled by the regional precipitation and less aﬀected by
the evapotranspiration due to its great depth.

According to Darcy’s Law, the recharge rate can be estimated as the product of
vertical gradient and vertical hydraulic conductivity. Taking the measurement in June
6, 2018 as an example, the vertical gradient observed in the six boreholes is between
0.4∼0.55. Hydraulic conductivity in the lower clay layer has been summarized in table 6.5,
in a range between 5.3 · 10−8 ∼6.4 · 10−6 m/s. By assuming an anisotropy ratio Kh/Kv
of 10, the inﬁltration rate can be estimated to have a range of 67∼11100 mm/yr. On the
other hand, annual precipitation during 2018∼2020 has a range between 471∼813 mm/yr
calculated from the monthly data, causing a upper limit to the possible inﬁltration rate.
Therefore, the inﬁltrate rate can be roughly estimated between 67∼810 mm/yr.

were virtually removed in the model. This was achieved by treating all sand lenses in the
clayey till as part of the lower clay layer and assigning the same local variables (Kh, Kd
and porosity). Mass discharge and mass storage were simulated for the two scenarios
and the results are shown in ﬁgure 6.24.

values of Kd and porosity in the original sand lenses were adjusted to the same level as the
clay matrix (the lower clay cover) while the aqueous concentration is still the same, thus
the total mass storage becomes higher in consequence of high soil sorption. In the two
scenarios, both vertical mass ﬂux and mass storage in the clayey till have shown similar
changing trend as time increases, and in particular no visible diﬀerence of vertical ﬂux
could be observed during the starting simulation period. As time increases, the scenario

From the literature, sand lens is expected to facilitate contamination transport in
horizontal direction and thus spread the contamination in consequence of its normally
large longitudinal dimension. To evaluate its inﬂuence on the spread of contamination
area, simulations were performed to quantify the amount of contaminant in two areas in

2D model performance

Limitations and future research
6.6.1
Delineation of source area
This study used TIN interpolation to represent the source area. The major limitation
of this method lies in the triangulation process only allows interpolation for the area
within the collected sample points and is not suitable for extrapolation beyond the area
==PAGE:99==
6.6 Limitations and future research
81
with sample points. See ﬁgure below. Thus for sample points with high concentration
distributed in the center, TIN is considered suitable to be applied. But if high con-
centration is more distributed on the outer points, TIN may not be able to reﬂect the
practical continuous source area but forms the interpolated area with high concentration
at the corner or border. In the 2D model, this problem has been mostly avoided because
the 2D source area is well surrounded by sampling points with zero contamination.
However in 3D model, the limitation could be more evident, and from the delineated 3D
source area in ﬁgure 6.10, the problematic pattern can be observed at some depths, for
example 35.5∼34.5 mDVR90. In such cases, the amount of mass calculated based on
TIN could be lower than the reality, which is could be one important reason for the low
estimated mass storage in 3D model in contrast to the results from manual estimation
and geostatistical modeling. Given that the application of interpolation depends on the
quality of sampling points and no ubiquitous method could be relied on to all cases, To
obtain good interpolation results which can match the speciﬁc case, it is worth trying
diﬀerent methods and compare to ﬁnd the optimal method.
In addition to the TIN interpolation, there are a variety of spatial interpolation
methods, such as Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW), cubic splines, natural neighbor
interpolation, etc. For example, in the IDW method, the sample points are weighted
during interpolation such that the inﬂuence of one point relative to another declines with
distance from the unknown point to be interpolated (QGIS, 2020). Figure 6.32 shows

6.6.2
Delineation of 3D geological heterogeneity
The study relied on possible hydraulic contact to estimate the spatial connection of sand
lenses in 3D space with the assistance of 3D CAD software. However, the uncertainty of
estimated hydraulic contact was not considered in the study. Whether from the long-time
hydraulic measurements at the site (see Appendix F) or 3D simulations, the hydraulic
head in these screens show some changing trend against time in reality, or changing trend
against inﬁltration rate in simulations, but generally the diﬀerences between them are
not eminent, causing a diﬃculty to distinguish the screens with hydraulic contact from
screens without contact. Thus the reliability of the speculated sand lens connectivity
could be aﬀected. For further research, multiple scenarios with diﬀerent degrees of
connectivity can be proposed. Geostatistical simulation is also promising to learn the
heterogeneity of sand lenses.
On the other hand, for the study of contaminant transport modeling, it is worth
considering how to include geological heterogeneity in an eﬃcient and eﬀective way. It is
recommended to perform pretests for scenarios with low to high sand lens connectivity
which exhibits simple geometry, through which the necessity of including sand lens
heterogeneity could be assessed. Of course, this requires the pretests to be appropriately
designed.
6.6.3
Groundwater recharge estimation
A reliable estimation of inﬁltration rate for groundwater recharge is of high importance for
the assessment of site-speciﬁc ﬂow and contaminant transport. The known information
about groundwater recharge at the study site is limited and unclear. Previous groundwater
modeling studies (∼20 years ago) indicates the recharge can range from <20 mm/yr to
higher level of <150 mm/yr, and in this study the range of 20∼150 mm/yr was relied on.
Besides a simple calculation based on vertical gradient and hydraulic conductivity was
made and an inﬁltration range of 67∼810 mm/yr was estimated. In order to obtain a
more accurate picture about the status of contaminant transport and have a more reliable
risk assessment for the contaminated site, it is worth conducting investigations to acquire
better knowledge of local groundwater recharge. Various methods to estimate groundwater
recharge are available, and techniques based on groundwater level ﬂuctuations are amongst
the most commonly applied (Scanlon et al., 2002). The main assumption is that if the
rainfall reaches the water table, the amount of rise is mainly a function of the eﬀective
porosity (speciﬁc yield) of the strata, which become saturated. If the actual ﬂuctuation
in groundwater level is ∂h/∂t [L/T], and the eﬀective porosity of the strata is φ [-], the
recharge can be calculated as φ · ∂h/∂t [L/T] in the case without drainage (Scanlon et
al., 2002; Semere, 2003). The major challenge of this method exists in estimating the
average rise in water table over the extent of the aquifer and deﬁning representative
values of the eﬀective porosity.
The chloride mass-balance (CMB) technique is another tool in estimating groundwater
recharge in regions where measurement of hydrological parameters are not reliable. Chlo-
ride has conservative nature and the availability for large-quantity measurements. Input
==PAGE:101==
6.6 Limitations and future research
83
of chloride to groundwater comes mainly from rainfall. By analyzing its concentration in
available rainfall samples and considering concentration in saturated zone or unsaturated
zone, regional or localized recharge can be respectively estimated (Semere, 2003).
6.6.4
Model setup
The Dirichlet boundary applied in the 2D/3D ﬂow model has a potential to be improved.
In the model domain, the vertical upstream and downstream borders in the aquifer were
set as constant through the entire thickness of aquifer. Based on previous investigations,
the vertical gradient in the aquifer is minor and has relatively high uncertainty (between
<0∼0.025) (NIRAS, 2019). If consider the dominant horizontal ﬂow in the aquifer and
its vertical gradient in contrast to the clayey till, such a setup with ﬁxed head boundary
is justiﬁed. But the ignored vertical gradient could have the potential to inﬂuence
contaminant diving in the deep aquifer, which has been simulated at a more shallow
depth in 2D model in comparison to the site measurement. Therefore, it is possible to
improve the model performance by applying the Dirichlet boundary with small vertical
gradient in the aquifer.
Another limitation needs to be notice is that the source area included in the model is
based on the investigations in recent years from 2015 to 2020, which has been 50∼60
years since the industrial site was initially contaminated in 1962 (or 1970 for hall 3).
For the purpose of evaluating mass discharge in current status or performing prediction,
including such a source area is appropriate. In our model, no contamination existed
in the aquifer at the beginning, and as simulation time increases, the phenomenon of
source depletion disables the prediction of future plume development. Promising further
research for the plume prediction include coupling a well-developed plume to the model
with the current source area and then continuing forward simulation. On the other hand,
reverse simulation to track contamination history is also promising with the combined
plume and source area.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This study have characterized the geological and hydrogeological properties of the sub-
surface environment at a particular contaminated site and investigated the numerical
modeling method to quantify contaminant mass discharge and contaminant plume de-
velopment in the clayey-till aquifer system. The complex local geology characterized by
sand lens heterogeneity was included in the 2D and 3D models in which the migration of
contaminant in the current state of the source area was forward simulated.
The local geology underneath the study site was categorized into a variety of units
with distinguished geological and hydrogeological properties. The soil composition in the
clayey till generally exhibited higher clay content (<15%) and organic carbon content
(<0.25%) than the soil in deep aquifer (clay<5%, foc<0.10%). The two soil characteristics
showed large measurement ranges in these geological units, and no signiﬁcant relationship
was found between them. To quantify the eﬀect of soil sorption, Kd values were estimated
by four published formulas which did not show good predictions for soil samples. A
linear regression equation as a function of organic carbon content was found to provide
good predictions of Kd values and also exhibited statistical signiﬁcance. Hydraulic
conductivity K values were estimated from grain size distribution curves by using a
published excel-based tool. The previous and ongoing investigations showed that the
clayey till generally had lower hydraulic conductivity (10−10 ∼10−7 m/s) than the deep
aquifer (10−7 ∼10−5 m/s) while K values in the clay matrix and sand lenses appeared
to have high degree of overlapping. A series of hydraulic measurements were analyzed
and compared with the regional precipitation, revealing that the groundwater recharge
in the deep aquifer could be more aﬀected by regional precipitation and the recharge
in the till could be aﬀected by the mutual eﬀect of multiple factors. In addition, the
eﬀective groundwater recharge rate was estimated between 67∼810 mm/yr by analyzing
the observed vertical gradients and the hydraulic conductivity in the clay cover.
Geological and hydrogeological properties determined from site characterisation were
applied in the numerical model and the source area delineated by TIN interpolation
method was included into 2D and 3D models. The 2D model simulations showed that
the vertical ﬂow in the till had a residence time between 20∼30 years and it took 17∼34
years for the horizontal ﬂow to reach 100 m downstream. With the inﬁltration rate
ranging from 20 mm/yr to 150 mm/yr, mass ﬂux was simulated between 0.3∼4 g/m/yr
and a range of 18∼240 g/m was estimated for the entire site by assuming a contaminated
thickness of 60 m. The mass storage was simulated to be 0.27 kg/m and a total of 16.2 kg
was estimated for the entire site. The depletion period of removing 90% of mass storage
was found in the interval of 6∼590 years with an inﬁltration range of 20∼150 mm/yr.
==PAGE:104==
86
7 Conclusion
The simulations of plume development in the aquifer showed that after a leaching period
of 50 years the 100 m downstream concentration proﬁles become comparable with the
current measurements, indicating the history of plume development. With respect to
the inﬂuence of sand lenses, it was found from 2D model simulations that the horizontal
contaminant spread in the till was hardly aﬀected by the existence of sand lenses. But
lower inﬁltration rate could lead to stronger horizontal contaminant spread in the long
run. In contrast, the inﬂuence from inﬁltration rate was found to have importance
inﬂuence on the ﬂow and contaminant transport, superior to the inﬂuence of anisotropic
conductivity.
In 3D model, estimation of sand lens connectivity in 3D space was based on the
observed hydraulic contacts between screens in sand lenses. However the simulated hy-
draulic results did not show as good performance as 2D model. Preliminary exploration
carried out in 3D transport model revealed a contaminant mass discharge of 5 g/year over
the entire site with an inﬁltration rate of 75 mm/yr. The depletion period of removing
90% of the initial mass could vary between 90∼200 years by considering only sand lenses
or only clay matrix existing in the till. The simulated results of mass discharge and mass
storage appear to be lower than previous investigations. The inaccurate 3D source area
included in the model could be one reason. Besides, the complex geometry of the 3D
sand lenses and source area posed diﬃculties in constructing high-quality meshes, which
was essential for achieving accurate numerical calculations. It was diﬃcult to solve this
problem well within the time limit of the study but it is promising to improve 3D model
performance via technical troubleshooting in the future.
For further improvements of the current modeling work, source area delineation
especially in 3D space and determination of local inﬁltration recharge rate should be
two focuses. On the other hand, by coupling the source area with the simulated current
state plume, it is possible to predict the future development of contaminant plume in the
aquifer. Meanwhile, the historical development of contaminant plume can be tracked by
performing reverse simulation.
