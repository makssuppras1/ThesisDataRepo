Abstract
A US based company "Texas Wind Tower" is currently in the process of examining the
possibility to produce 140+ meters high wind towers. The motivation for such high towers
is that a higher tower can support a larger wind turbine and thus, more energy can be
extracted. For such high towers, optimal design is crucial in order to increase tower sti-
ness, consequently lowering stresses with minimum usage of material possible. To study
the design possibilities, the topology optimization method is used.
Topology optimization is a systematic numerical approach used to obtain an optimized
structure. The method of topology optimization can be described as a distribution of
a given amount of material in a specied design domain, which is subjected to certain
loading, boundary conditions and constraints. This domain can then be optimized to
maximize specied objectives, for example stiness.
In this thesis, a topology optimization code for wind tower is to be devised. First, dif-
ferent aspects of the optimized designs are analyzed, including rotor weight loading, yaw
moment and tower self-weight, while narrowing down the many options of optimization
parameters. Subsequently, overturning moment and wind pressure are added, and the ob-
tained wind tower designs are analyzed. Volume constraint for the optimization process
is applied.
The implementation is based on the parallel framework for topology optimization using
PETSc which allows for very ne discretizations needed to resolve the wanted length
scales of the structure.
After analyzing numerous optimized wind tower designs, the main tendency was ob-
served; the optimization process produced dierent structural features only if there was
not enough material to wrap a design into a closed shell. When given more material to
be used for the optimization process, the design became more and more wrapped into a
closed shell, rather than using the extra material to produce new structural features.

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Texas Wind Tower Co.1 is a company located in Texas, USA that specializes in techno-
logical development of wind turbines. The company is currently examining the possibility
to produce 140+ meters high wind turbine. In this regard, professor Niels Aage was con-
tacted by Texas Wind Tower Co. representative John Buttles, after he read the famous
article in Nature journal on topology optimization of Boeing 777 wing [1]. Mr. But-
tles wanted to investigate possibilities of wind turbine tower designs by using methods
presented in the article. These possibilities I study in my Master thesis.
Figure 1.1: Current and future wind turbine size. Courtesy of Texas Wind Tower Co.
There are many reasons for building taller wind turbines, however two main reasons
can be highlighted.
First, a taller tower can support a larger wind turbine head with larger wing span, thus
more energy can be extracted. According to Texas Wind Tower Co. estimate, 3-9 times
more energy could be generated with taller wind turbines compared to the current ones.
Second, U.S. locations where the wind speed is generally low and thus not suitable for
current wind turbines might very well be utilized by the taller wind turbines, as the
wind speed increases with altitude. The orange color in gure 1.2 depicts areas where
wind energy lacks behind in US, mostly due to too low wind speeds, but could be very
well exploited by the new taller wind turbines.
More wind energy generation would
consequently mean cost-eective, low-carbon electricity for millions of Americans, job
growth etc.

Taller wind towers bring many fascinating possibilities and benets, however they also
introduce lots of new engineering challenges. Larger wind turbine head and greater rotor
torque transfer greater loads on the wind turbine tower. Furthermore, taller wind tower
itself means greater self-weight load. Hence, with taller and more powerful wind turbine,
the wind tower will be subjected to loads of magnitude, never dealt with before. Yet,
the wind tower has to be designed such that it withstands these enormous loads and is
stiest with minimum usage of material to keep expenses as low as possible. The clas-
sical designing methods might provide a solution, but it does not necessarily mean that
the wind tower design will be optimized in terms of highest stiness while keeping the
material usage as low as possible.
Topology optimization method, on the other hand, is a systematic numerical approach
used to obtain an optimized structure, in our case an optimized wind tower. The method
of topology optimization can be described as a distribution of a given amount of material
in a specied design domain, which is subjected to certain loading, boundary conditions
and constraints. This domain can then be optimized to maximize specied objectives, for
example stiness. Topology optimization is the newest technique in the eld of structural
optimization. Bendsøe and Sigmund [2] presents three categories of structural optimiza-
tion: sizing optimization of truss structure, shape optimization and topology optimization,
gure 1.3. In all these optimizations, the stiest structure is wanted to be obtained, often

referred as the minimum compliance problem.
Figure 1.3: Three categories of structural optimization: a) Sizing optimization of truss
structure, b) shape optimization and c) topology optimization
In sizing optimization of truss structure, 1.3 a), the total volume of trusses is xed,
and the cross section area of individual trusses is increased or decreased, providing an
optimized structure in terms of stiness.
In shape optimization 1.3 b), the shape of
holes is optimized in order to provide stiest structure. Here, number as well as area of
holes are kept xed. Finally, 1.3 c) is a classical topology optimization problem, with
initial domain, load, boundary conditions and prescribed volume fraction, from where an
optimized structure is achieved. The topology optimization will be employed in studying
possibilities of the wind turbine tower designs.
1.2
Parallel computing in PETSc using C++
Topology optimization algorithm, depending on problem size, might require lots of compu-
tational resources. For a 2D topology optimization problems, a single PC might be enough
to run the optimization program and obtain results within a reasonable time. However, for
a 3D topology optimization even with quite coarse discretizations, the parallel computing
is a necessity. Parallel computing, also known as high performance computing (HPC) en-
ables to obtain an optimized structure with very ne discretizations within hours or days,
which could take years on a single PC. In parallel computing, the optimization program
is submitted to a cluster, where the program runs on hundreds or even thousands of cores
simultaneously.
Aage et al. [3] has developed a parallel topology optimization code for smallest com-
pliance problem in C++ PETSc (Portable and Extendable Toolkit for Scientic Comput-
ing) framework. The code is available at www.topopt.dtu.dk/PETSc. I have adapted this
code to the wind turbine tower optimization by modifying and extending it. My main
contributions to the code are stated as follows. Implementation of:
• topology optimization for multiple load cases

• 11 design loads
• various Young's modulus interpolation schemes
• continuous penalization scheme
• passive elements, with some code segments provided by Niels Aage
• stress calculator
Three dierent DTU's cluster systems were changed as the time went by, in order to run
optimization problems with ever ner discretizations. Starting with a cluster that has 100
cores, then a cluster with 1280 and nally a cluster with 3200 number of cores available
for the user.

1.3
Material: UHPC concrete
The material considered for an optimized wind tower is a type of concrete, called Ultra-
High Performance Concrete (UHPC). UHPC refers to a relatively new class of advanced
cementitious composite materials whose mechanical and durability properties far surpass
those of conventional concrete [4]. This class of concrete has been demonstrated to facil-
itate solutions that address specic problems in the U.S. such as highway bridge infras-
tructure. Initial material development research on UHPC began more than two decades
ago. First structural deployments began in the late 1990s. First eld deployments in the
U.S. highway transportation infrastructure began in 2006.
The UHPC concrete has higher strength properties and is stier while having approx-
imately same mass density compared to a classical concrete such as Portland Cement
Concrete (PCC). The values of relevant mechanical properties are presented in table
1.1.UHPC has also smaller minimum feature thickness; with the UHPC concrete it is pos-
sible to form structural features of thickness less than 25 mm whereas PCC has a thickness
range of 25 mm−35 mm [5]. However, UHPC is about twenty times more expensive com-
pared to PCC, respectively $130 m3 and $6.5 m3. The given prices can depend on the
supplier etc.

2
Finite element discretization
In this chapter, a nite element (FE) model of the wind turbine tower is prepared. This
includes design domains, boundary conditions and design loads. For all illustrations with
nite elements, number of elements (will be also referred as resolution) nx × ny × nz =
96 × 96 × 2048 is used.
2.1
FE settings
In the FEA the bilinear quadrilateral elements (Q4) are used, based on Cook et al.[6].
All elements are set to be cubic (wel × wel × wel) to avoid giving benet to any direction.
Generalized Minimal Residual method (GMRES) solver was originally used for FEA.
However, later on it was substituted by Flexible Conjugate Gradient method (FCG) as it
showed faster time per iteration and less iterations, especially for problems with passive
elements, which are introduced in the next section. Maximum of 200 solver iterations and
maximum relative tolerance of 10−6 showed to be an appropriate choice.
2.2
Design domains and symmetry
To get an idea how the optimized design changes depending on the initial design domain,
three optimization domains are considered: rectangular, cylindrical and conical, shown in
gure 2.1.

The advantages of that are many.
Not only the FE
problem becomes four times smaller, but it was observed that the number of solver itera-
tions decreased at least twice. Also, when modelling 1/4 domain, a perfectly symmetrical
structure across the symmetry planes is obtained, whereas for the full domain model, the
numerical noise can aect the symmetry to such an extent that antisymmetric designs
occur. Since load disturbances/imperfections are not a part of the model, it is preferred
to avoid these symmetry deviations.
In order to discretize cylindrical and conical domains, gure 2.1 (b) and (c), the use
of passive elements is made. The explanation goes as follows:
In the topology optimization program, the rectangular design domain is rst dened
and discretized with some number of elements.
Each element is assigned an initial
stiness Ei = ¯˜xiE0, where E0 is the Young's modulus of UHPC material and ¯˜xi is
an elements density, initially dierent from zero, but a unity at maximum.

3.1

task of topology optimization algorithm is to provide an optimized wind turbine tower
design in terms highest stiness or in other words lowest compliance. The topology op-

optimized for such loading scenario. Hence, for the tower optimization, MLC formulation
will be used where the design is optimized taking into consideration all possible loading
scenarios also referred as load cases. Mathematically, this topology optimization problem

4.2.2
Load weighting study
In this section, the inuence of dierent weighting, α on the optimized designs is studied.
The following weightings will be studied:
56
==PAGE:58==
α = [1, 0, 0] , [0, 1, 0] , [1, 0, 1] , [0, 1, 1] , [10, 1, 1] , [1, 10, 1] and [1,1,10].
Weighting α = [1, 0, 0] means for instance that only design load 0 (rotor weight force) is
applied, while loads 2 and 3 (yaw moment and self-weight) are not present. Weighting
α = [1, 1, 10] on the other hand indicates that all three design loads are considered, but
load 3 is given ten times more signicance compared to the other two loads. The reason
weighting α = [0, 0, 1] is not considered is that it includes only self-weight. The problem
with self-weight alone is that it has the optimum solution when no material is left, since
no material means no load and thus zero compliance. To avoid this issue, a lower bound
of volume constraint could be implemented, preventing the solution with no material.
However it is not done here, hence weighting α = [0, 0, 1] is omitted from the analysis.

advantages of UHPC material is namely the ability to form very thin structural features.
By having a simple closed and thick shell this advantage of UHPC is left unexploited.

Conclusions and future work
6.1
Conclusions
In this thesis, topology optimization method is used in order to study design possibilities
for wind turbine towers of 160 m height. In order to achieve high nite element discretiza-
tion resolution, High Performance Computing (HPC) has been used. The material the
optimized towers are planned to be made of a type of concrete, called Ultra High Per-
formance Concrete (UHPC), which mechanical properties surpass those of conventional
concrete and ability to form robust structural features below 25 mm thickness.
In the rst part of the thesis, rotor head weight, yaw moment and tower's self-weight de-
sign loads were considered for the wind tower design optimization and three design domain
were considered: rectangular, cylindrical and conical. In order to choose an appropriate
stiness interpolation scheme due to the added self-weight, three schemes: SIMP, RAMP
and modied SIMP each with constant and continuous penalization were employed to
produce optimized wind tower designs. It was found that modied SIMP with constant
penalization produced the best designs for all three design domains in terms of best de-
sign denition combined with highest stiness. Comparing the optimized designs using
rectangular, cylindrical and conical design domains, it was decided to proceed only with
the conical design domain, as the optimized design obtained showed high stiness and
were similar to ordinary wind towers, yet optimized.
Increasing FE discretization resolution, the structural features of the optimized design be-
came tinier, more complex and diverse. However, it was also observed that with increasing
resolution, more and more shell was forming on the outside of the design and a complex
bottom, resembling some sort of foundation was establishing. When more material was
allowed to be used for the design, a fully closed shell has formed and lots of material was
placed in the bottom of the design, making the bottom fully solid. Designs with higher
volume fraction did not change much with higher resolution. It was found that the closed
shell feature was caused by the yaw moment load.
In the second part of the thesis, overturning load and wind pressure were added. The
foundation seen before have disappeared and the all material from the foundation was put
into the shell, either closing it fully for low volume fractions or increasing its thickness for
higher volume fractions. It was discussed that due to the way wind load is implemented,
it forces the shell to be fully closed, so both yaw moment and wind load contribute to
closed shell developing.
A wind tower design that has a closed shell is a necessity in order to generate a wake
region behind the wind tower, which is approved by the US energy department. However,
the optimized designs did not show to include any features besides the closed shell, which
is a disappointed. One of advantages of UHPC material is that it can form thin structural
features, hence this advantage would not be exploited if the proposed solid shell towers
77
==PAGE:79==
should be manufactured.
Finally, in the time assigned for this thesis, the nal optimized wind tower design was not
proposed, however, many observations and discoveries were made which should be used
as a stepping stone for the future work, if the pursue of an optimized wind tower design
using topology optimization should continue.
6.2
Future work
There are numerous extensions that could be added if the work should be continued. Here
some of the most important are presented and the importance/signicance of them comes
in more or less the following order.
Stresses in the optimized wind tower were unfortunately not calculated as there appeared
technical problems on the cluster the last days. So, stresses have to be calculated, as
stress constraint in addition to volume constraint might be needed as well.
There were technical issues when running the code with higher FE resolution. The prob-
lem size becomes very large, but nothing that is unheard before for HPC. It is necessary
to obtain higher resolutions as it might be that structural features are not forming due
to still too low resolution.
Adding a foundation to the model could have an impact on the design of the wind tower
and thus it should be implemented as a next step. The easiest way of doing that would
be to make the lower portion of the design domain solid to some height. The solid portion
of the tower could resemble sort of foundation. If seeing big changes in the wind tower
design, when this sort of foundation is added, then the next step would be to model a
more sophisticated foundation that mirrors one in reality.
If increasing resolution would not produce structural features anyway, the inll formula-
tion, proposed by Wu et al. [21] could be implemented. This formulation enforces for-
mation of bone-like porous structures, hence using this formulation more features should
emerge in the optimized tower design.
Figure 6.1: Optimized half MBB-beam beam using (a) classical (as for wind tower) and
(b) inll topology optimization formulation. Structures produced by own code.
The foundation itself could be optimized, applying topology optimization. The proce-
dure is described by Bendsøe [2] in section 2.7. Then both wind tower and its foundation
could be optimized.
