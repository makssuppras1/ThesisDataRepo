Summary (English)
The goal for this thesis is to investigate the change in dynamic behaviour of
a platform when a tank is present on the structure. In the thesis, a platform
model in scale 1:100 is used in the experimental study. The model represent
two platforms, where one of the platform is considered. A tank with changing
content is placed on the platform to observe the changing behaviour of the modal
properties.
The thesis starts by introducing the project background and deﬁnition before in-
troducing relevant theory from operational modal analysis(OMA). Theory such
as correlation function(CF), spectral density(SD) and identiﬁcations are pre-
sented. The identiﬁcation of the modal properties are performed in both time-
and frequency domain. Further, the setup for the model and description of each
test are presented.
The results looks at the modal properties of the platform from analytical so-
lution, FE model and experimental results, where the natural frequencies are
compared. All methods examine the platform without and with a tank with
changing liquid content. From the research, it is concluded that adding a water
tank to the platform will reduce the natural frequency of the structure, but
there will not be introduced additional modes due to the changing water.

Abstract
Oﬀshore platforms are often subjected to one of more tanks placed on the plat-
form deck with changing liquid content. The identiﬁcation of the modal proper-
ties is important for structural health monitoring, model updating and control.
Therefore, knowledge of how the modal properties of the platform are changing
is useful in conducting an accurate dynamic response analysis.
The following research is based on experimental data from a small platform
model in the scale of 1:100, where the data are recorded from accelerometers.
It is investigated how the modal properties of a platform will be aﬀected by
adding a tank with changing liquid content. The tank is studied in the range
from empty to full tank. The experimental results are validated from analytical-
and ﬁnite element model results.
The modal properties of the platform are identiﬁed from the time domain au-
toregressive methods and the frequency domain poly-reference Least Squares
Complex Frequency-Domain method.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Oﬀshore platforms are subjected to rough environments, where the dynamic
characteristic of the platform is unknown. [1] An increased problem, is that
many oﬀshore platforms are reaching their design lifetime, and the structural
health state is unknown. If the structural health state of the platforms could
be projected and used to increase the service lifetime, the owners would have a
signiﬁcant savings. Accurate structural health monitoring can minimise the cost
of maximising recovery from existing oil and gas ﬁeld. The dynamic response
of the structure can be monitored by measuring systems and be used to observe
the modal properties. [8]
Tanks, equipment, cranes etc. are placed on platforms, and will contribute to
the measurements. For this reason, experimental and analytical investigation of
a platform model in a rough scale 1:100 is undertaken to observe the dynamic
characteristics of a platform when a water tank is placed on the structure.
The water tank is observed in the range between empty and full tank. The
thesis investigates how a tank may inﬂuence the modal results obtained from
monitoring of the platform.
The experiment consist of two platforms with divergent cross-sections, one small
platform 30x30 cm and one large platform 40x40 cm, however only the small
platform is considered in this thesis. The platforms are excited with pressurised
air blowing randomly to the model to simulate random weather response. Ac-

The experimental study is compared with analytical solution and a FE model in

the poly-Reference Least Squares Complex Frequency-domain (pLSCF).

1.0.2
Project Deﬁnition
How is the dynamic behaviour of a platform inﬂuenced by a tank placed on top
of the unit when the tank is being emptied and ﬁlled.
The aim for the master thesis is to investigate the inﬂuence on the modal prop-
erties when a tank is placed on the platform deck. The tank is analysed in the
range from a full to an empty tank, and question such as how the dynamics of
the structure is changed, and what is the eﬀect of having diﬀerent tank sizes
and the tank placed at diﬀerent location are answered.

Chapter 2
Operational Model Analysis
(OMA)
Operational model analysis (OMA) is a method that studies the dynamic char-
acteristics of the system, such as the natural frequency, damping factors and
mode shapes. [6] Normally the dynamic response of a structure is said to follow
the rules of modal superposition meaning that the response can be described
as a linear combination of the mode shapes and the modal coordinates.[7] The
data are prossessed in matlab where functions related to the book Introduction
to Operational Modal Analysis [3] are used, and the functions are referred to.
2.1
Filtering
A ﬁlter is applied to the experimental data to exclude unwanted frequency
signals. From ﬁgure 2.1, the spectral density of the singular values of the spectral
matrix from the platform is illustrated. The unwanted frequency signal can be
detected from the plot, and hence a fast Fourier transform (FFT) ﬁlter is used
on the experimental results. The matlab function fftfilt1.m in toolbox A7 [3]
is used for ﬁltering the data, where the function uses a Hanning window with
50% overlap.

2.1.1
FFT Filtering
The data from the experiment have been tested with a sampling frequency of
2560 Hz for the preliminary tests and 1652 Hz for the tank tests. These data
are resampled to a smaller frequency in order to have the Nyquist frequency in
the range 10 % to 90 % of the sampling frequency. The data are down sampled
by a Ndec factor equal to 32, with the function fftfilt1.m [3], using decimate
as the optional argument. The FFT ﬁlter is based on the following principles:
[3]
• Using the Fourier transform to transfer the accelerometer data to fre-
quency domain.
• In the frequency domain, deﬁne a window W(ω) which is comparable to
the preferred shape of the ampliﬁcation function |H(ω)|.
• Multiply the window with the Fourier transform.
• Transfer the data back to the time domain.
A second ﬁlter is applied to the data, a band-pass ﬁlter. This is used in the same
fftfilt1.m function, however with bandpass as the optional argument.[3] The

2.4
Identiﬁcation
Two approaches are used to identify the mode shapes for the platforms, the time
domain and the frequency domain. In time domain the physical information for
the structure is extracted from the correlation function, while in the frequency
domain the physical information is extracted from the half spectra. The time
domain identiﬁcation look at the free responses over the full timespan of the
data. [3]
2.4.1
Time Domain Identiﬁcation
The time domain is performed by using the autoregressive (AR) model, which
is similar to the poly reference technique, where it is assumed that the CF is
free decay. The time domain identiﬁcation use the matlab function id_tdpr.m
from toolbox A7. [3] The identiﬁcation uses the full CF matrix transposed, and
the number of auto regressive coeﬃcient matrices (na) is chosen to 2, to ﬁnd
the ﬁrst six modes of the platform.
2.4.2
Frequency Domain Identiﬁcation
The frequency domain identiﬁcation is based on the poly-Reference Least Squares
Complex Frequency-domain (pLSCF) method also commercially known as PolyMax-
method. [2] The pLSCF identiﬁcation uses the data from the half spectra. The
advantage with this method is the speed and that it provides a clear stabiliza-
tion diagrams. However, it is not always accurate when estimating the highly

7.1
Analytical Results
The platform have a uniform cross section and it is therefore expected that
the ﬁrst two modes are the same, which is conﬁrmed from the results listed in
table 6.1 to 6.3. The water tank is calculated by adding an additional mass to
the plate, and from the results it is seen the natural frequency of the ﬁrst two
modes, with a tank, is reduced compared to the plate without a tank. This is
expected as an increase of the plate masse will reduce the natural frequency.
The torsional frequency is not aﬀected by an increase of the platform weight.

remove unwanted disturbances.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
In this master thesis, an investigating on how the addition of a water tank, with
moving liquid, will inﬂuence the modal properties are conducted. The research
question is investigated experimentally and veriﬁed from analytical solution and
FE model, where there are obtained good agreement between the results.
A small platform model is considered, where identiﬁcation from time-and fre-
quency domains are used for identifying the natural frequency, mode shapes
and damping ratios. From the results, it is concluded that the water tank did
not change the spectral density matrix and there are not introduced additional
modes due to the tank. It can be concluded that the tank act as a tuned mass
damper on the platform. When the platform is excited, the water in the tank
will resonance out of phase. Another observation, is that the natural frequencies
for the transversed modes of the platform is reduced with an increase water in
the tank. This change in frequency is due to the additional mass being added
to the platform. The torsional mode is not changing when a tank is added to
the platform.
The study conducted in this master thesis helps to understand how the modal
properties of a platform will change if there is a liquid tank on the platform.
However, there are also many other variables that may aﬀect the modal proper-
ties obtained from a platform, such as a bridge between two platforms, moving
equipment/cranes etc. For further research it could be suggested to investigate
==PAGE:62==
48
Conclusion
the problem in a water tank to obtain an accurate overview of the behaviour.
This thesis is based on a model where the platform is excited with air, when in
real life there will be other changing variable such as water, contribution to the
response of the platform.

T =

Where, T is the duration of the recording in s, ζ is the modal damping factor
and f is the model frequency in Hz. The lowest natural frequency from the
experiment is 8.3Hz and the damping ratio (ζ) is 0.007, which gives a time
duration for at least 170s. For the tank experiment the time duration is set to
210s, which is acceptable for the time series.
