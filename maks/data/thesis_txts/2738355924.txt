Abstract
The development of the IEC standard 61400­15­2 for uncertainty in Wind Resource Assessment
presents significant potential for enhancing Uncertainty Quantification (UQ) in specific models
and workflows, such as the ”industry standard” software Wind Atlas Analysis and Application
Program (WAsP). This thesis explores the combined effect of relevant uncertainties and examines
how the energy yield of a project is sensitive to various sets of input conditions. Initially, a review
of Uncertainty Quantification theory and current industrial procedures is conducted, along with the
identification of key uncertainty components in scientific programs that model and measure reality.
The analysis carried out employs PyWAsP to perform Monte Carlo simulations, with the primary
goal of investigating sensitivities and uncertainties within an idealized version of the vertical ex­
trapolation model. This work culminates in a comprehensive application that can be readily used
and integrated into PyWAsP for quantifying and analyzing uncertainty under different scenarios.
This application enables the measurement of uncertainty in the model’s outputs for various types of
onshore projects, given a specific combination of input uncertainties. By addressing these aspects,
the thesis aims to provide a deeper understanding of the uncertainties inherent in wind resource
assessments and offers methodological improvements for their quantification, ultimately leading
to more accurate and reliable energy yield assessments.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
It is undoubted that the wind energy industry has grown and improved to unforeseen levels in
the past two decades. Certainly, this has led to the formulation of compelling and convincing
statements in annual wind energy reports, such as the assertion ”Onshore wind is a proven, mature
technology” which are usually found in International Energy Agency (IEA) reports [1]. Likewise,
the CEO of the Global Wind Energy Council expressed the following in the 2023 GWEC report:
”The wind industry is no longer the hobby sector of forty years ago. Our technology is resilient
and mature...” [2]. Although the technology is truly advanced, the wind sector is broad and there
is a perception that such statements often refer to certain parts of the industry like wind turbines
themselves, their control, or supply chains, yet what about the underlying physics that has enabled
this technology to expand to present levels? These have remained the same, and they are still a
source of uncertainty in the early stages of a wind farm project.
In 2011, the European Wind Energy Association (EWEA) conducted a comparative case study
exercise. The objective was to assess the accuracy of wind speed and energy yield predictions for a
specific wind farm. The exercise involved 37 organisations representing diverse areas of expertise,
all capable of conducting aWind Resource Assessment (WRA). The aim was to compare different
result metrics, including long­term predictions and energy yield measurements, such as the gross,
net and potential Annual Energy Production (AEP).
The outcome from the comparison exercise exposed high variability in results that are critical for
wind farm projects, meaning that consensus between the different organizations was rather low.
The full conference proceeding [3] showcases how through the different steps of the chain towards
calculating the potential energy yield, variability increases rapidly. Focusing on the AEP metrics,
Figure 1.1 shows a standard box­plot results. Concerns must arise when looking at the range of
these results, particularly those such as the P90 (AEP with a 90% confidence of exceeding that
amount of production), as this is often a value presented to investors in order to make predictions
on the project profitability. The results of the P90 range from 73 to 99 GWh/y (on a wind farm that
is just 28 MW rated) and the coefficient of variation ( CV = σ/µ) between the results of all the
organizations was found to be 6.2%.
Figure 1.1: Distribution of predicted energy yields of the case study wind farm. [3]
1
==PAGE:13==
Having such dispersion in these type of results that are intended to be consistent and that have
an utter importance in the erection of a wind farm is not a good sign and translate into a lack
of standard methods and best practices to account for uncertainty in what is already considered
a ”mature industry”. Ultimately, raises the more than often hard­to­swallow question of: ”Can I
trust my results?” and ”To what extent can I trust them?”.
The latter, leads directly to recent studies pointing out the under­performance of wind farms with
respect to their Energy Yield Assessment (EYA), like the Wind Energy Prediction Bias in European
Onshore Wind Projects report made by Hendrickson Renewables [4], which found an average bias
of ­8.9% on net AEP over 77 projects studied. These, have a negative impact in the reputation
of the sector, as it rises doubts on the accuracy of the EYAs which are performed as part of the
WRA, usually with software program tools such as Wind Atlas Analysis and Application Program
(WAsP).
The process of carrying out a WRA relies heavily on understanding the micro­meteorology and
terrain of a wind farm site. Otherwise, incorrect assumptions or poor estimates of the uncertainty
of results output from the software tool may be made. Moreover, this also requires having certain
knowledge on the physics being applied in the program´s algorithms or at least understanding the
process that input data is undertaking over the different stages towards the output results. Unfortu­
nately, programs such as WAsP are incredibly complex and the degree of its validity under a large
spectrum of cases is still not well known.
This leads to the motivation for this master’s thesis. A deeper comprehension of the WAsP pro­
gram, particularly through its Python iteration, ”PyWAsP,” can provide useful insights in terms
of Uncertainty Quantification (UQ) for this key piece of software in the field of Wind Resource
Assessment. Ultimately being able to predict EYAs with more fidelity and veracity as we will
achieve a deeper understanding of the intricacies of the models being used.
1.2
Problem formulation
According to our motivation, we are driven to some obvious questions that are central when as­
sessing uncertainty. These are the same questions as presented in [5], which are:
• In what quantities do we have interest?
• Upon what data do these depend?
• How do the quantities (data) ‘behave’?
• How do our models use these quantities?
While considering these questions, one quickly realizes that the problem, or more precisely the
”challenge”, of Uncertainty Quantification in software programs that model and measure reality,
is to correctly integrate the diverse components that UQ involves. Figure 1.2 presents the afore­
mentioned components and illustrates the pillars on which this thesis is built.
Knowledge in the field of wind energy is necessary, as the assumptions of the model, its physics,
and the quantities relevant to the study need to be well understood. Moreover, a significant part
of Uncertainty Quantification derives from the field of statistics, which defines how uncertainty
is handled. Lastly, skilled use of programming applications is essential. Firstly, the model itself
is written in lines of code, utilizing various programming languages. Secondly, the latest methods
and algorithms in the field of uncertainty analysis heavily rely on numerical techniques.
2
==PAGE:14==
Figure 1.2: What is UQ adapted from [6]
By navigating the different fields of engineering, statistics and programming, we will seek to an­
swer different questions that may arise in the building of this thesis such as:
• How can we establish a reliable and direct method of assessing uncertainty in PyWAsP?
• Which statistics and probability libraries exist and can be implemented in the python version
of the WAsP program?
• How does the studied model’s uncertainty affect an energy yield study ?
1.3
Objectives
In reality, performing a UQ study of a program like WAsP involves individually investigating the
different models used to obtain a particular set of Quantities of Interest (QoI)s. It is evident that
AEP is one of the most important QoIs that a user can output from a program like WAsP. However,
to arrive at this metric, several combined models need to be used. Figure 1.3 illustrates, via a
chart diagram, how these different models integrate. Due to the complexity of this study, we will
focus our investigation on one of the most important sub­models that comprise the wind resource
segment of WAsP, which is the vertical extrapolation (VE) model. This will be achieved using
PyWAsP, the Python API of the software.
Figure 1.3: Model structure for typical AEP estimation [7].
3
==PAGE:15==
The objectives that this thesis proposes are therefore the following:
1. Study the effect of different atmospheric, surface, and terrain conditions on the performance
of the vertical extrapolation model, using uncertainty as a measure of reliability.
2. Perform a sensitivity analysis of the most important inputs and parameters of the model to
determine which variables generate the most uncertainty.
3. Quantify the uncertainty of the model and ultimately assess the impact of this uncertainty on
an energy yield metric such as AEP or capacity factor.
1.4
Limitations
Firstly, time constraints limit the scope of this study. Thus, in this master’s thesis, we will focus
only in one specific component of the WRA model, despite our initial intent to broaden the UQ
study.
Second, for a UQ study framework to be fully complete, it must be accompanied by a verification
and validation analysis that examines the model’s form. This requires measurement data, which is
not available to us. Consequently, we cannot complete this aspect of the framework.
Lastly, this thesis assumes that the input variables studied are independent of one another. This
assumption restricts the study to a less general analysis.
1.5
Structure
The chapter 1 introduces the motivation behind the study, presents the central problem, and de­
fines the objectives and limitations of the work. Essentially, the importance of understanding and
managing uncertainty in wind resource assessment is highlighted.
The chapter 2 provides a complete theoretical framework and review of existing literature on un­
certainty quantification and statistical methods to handle it. The theory and methods presented
here are later utilized to develop the application that will study uncertainty in PyWAsP.
The chapter 3 methodology, describes the tools and techniques used to conduct the uncertainty
and sensitivity analysis. It details the sampling and simulation methods, specifies the vertical
extrapolation model, and concludes by presenting the set of case scenarios to be studied. This
chapter explains these aspects, first for a local method of assessing uncertainty, and subsequently
for a global approach.
In chapter 4 the results obtained from the simulations and analyses are presented. General trends
observed are discussed, along with the model’s sensitivity to different input variables and condi­
tions. Ultimately selected research questions are answered.
The chapter 5 summarizes the main findings of the study and discusses the implications of these
results for the field of wind resource assessment.
The chapter 6 suggests possible directions for future studies, identifying areas where the research

Theoretical framework
2.1
An introduction to uncertainty quantification
This section will be devoted to an examination of the terms inherent to Uncertainty Quantification,
which can be shortly summarized to be “the science of identifying, quantifying, and reducing
uncertainties associated with models, numerical algorithms, experiments, and predicted outcomes”
[8]. Ultimately, its objective is to characterise models in terms of what is known, what is not known,
and to which extent, in order to gain a more comprehensive understanding of what is being being
modelled [6].
In order to introduce the field of Uncertainty Quantification, it is convenient to begin by defining
a set of basic terms that will assist the reader in following the arguments presented in this report.
It has been observed that the terminology used in this field can sometimes be confusing, thus
a general overview of the different terms will be provided with the aim of grouping them into
concrete definitions.
To begin, it is critical to understand the difference between uncertainty and error. As given by
GUM [9], ”Uncertainty means doubt, and thus uncertainty of measurement means doubt about
the validity of the result of a measurement”. Because there are no distinct terms to differentiate the
broad idea of uncertainty from specific metrics that quantify this concept, the term “uncertainty”
may also be employed in such cases. That is why, for example, standard deviation of a variable
sample could sometimes be referred as uncertainty of that variable. Another way of referring to
uncertainty is ”unknown bias”.
The term error is used to indicate the difference between a measured value and the true value
(given that we know the true value of the measurement), in this sense, error could also be re­
ported as ”known bias”, as it is known with exactitude how far from the actual true value we lie.
The aforementioned nicely sets another important distinction between the definitions of accuracy
which refers to how close a measurement is to the true or accepted value and precision which de­
scribes how close measurements of the same item are to each other. Given a precise measurement,
the error would be reflected by the distance to the target, i.e the accuracy of that measurement. In
many cases, errors cannot be known, but they can be modelled as uncertain variables.
Having clarified the distinction between error and uncertainty, we are now able to explore uncer­
tainty more thoroughly, including its various forms. The term, uncertainty, remains somewhat
ambiguous because it encompasses both the absence of knowledge and the stochastic nature (in­
herent randomness) of a variable [10]. From these two ideas, we derive the following definitions.
• Aleatoric uncertainty: also referred as residual variability as it cannot be reduced since it
is inherent in the reality being modelled. It is purely physical / natural variability caused by
the stochastic behaviour of a variable. An example of it would be the turbulent nature of a
fluid (e.g wind). This type of uncertainty can be characterized by the Probability Density
Function (PDF) of the true variable.
• Epistemic uncertainty: is the type of uncertainty which can be reduced since it originates
from the lack of knowledge or imperfections. For instance, every measurement inherently
includes some degree of epistemic uncertainty as in practice it is impossible to have a com­
pletely perfect measure of reality. High quality materials, perfect assembly/manufacturing,
thorough calibration, etc, will result in a narrower tolerance of the measurement device, but
to some extent there will still be some. Since tolerance is not probability weighted, this type
of uncertainty can be represented by a uniform PDF.
5
==PAGE:17==
In this thesis, we will frequently refer to variables that are uncertain or ”random”, by this, the reader
should bear in mind that we mean that the variable is modeled as a stochastic variable, unless oth­
erwise stated. Although no direct measurements will be taken, we will be working with variables
that result from such actions. Therefore, it is also beneficial to provide the GUM definitions on
types of measurement uncertainties. Sometimes, measurements such as those of wind speed, can­
not be categorised as purely aleatoric or epistemic; they are a mixture of both. This is why in GUM
[9] proposes two types of assessment of measurement uncertainty:
• Type A uncertainty: is a method of evaluation of uncertainty by the statistical analysis
of series of observations. It basically represents the component of uncertainty that can be
estimated from multiple independent repetitions of the measurement using the ”experimen­
tal standard deviation of the mean”1 (i.e the variation between the calculated mean of the
population and one which is considered known, or accepted as accurate.)
• Type B uncertainty: method of evaluation of uncertainty by means other than the statistical
analysis of series of observations. This represents the epistemic uncertainty in measurements
due to instrument or process. It can be estimated by assuming a PDF for a variable. This
comes from scientific judgement based on all of the available information on the possible
variability of the variable.
Having established the types of uncertainties that exist and the types of methods that can be em­
ployed to assess it, we can now turn our attention to the sources of uncertainty. It has already been
established that the field of UQ is a complex and multidisciplinary one. However, considering that
the uncertainty under consideration is that of a wind resource model, the objective of this study
becomes examination of the modelling of applied wind and atmospheric physics. Consequently, it
is necessary to identify the various sources of uncertainty that may affect the outcome of the mod­
elling process. Mark Kelly and Juan Pablo Murcia define the following most common sources of
uncertainty[10]:
• Physical sources of uncertainty need to be considered when handling variables which present
a stochastic behaviour. Plenty of variables that serve as input to the wind resource models
have this characteristic behaviour, wind is of course the most common example, but temper­
ature, pressure, etc also present themselves as random variables during over a measurement
campaign of any time.
• Measurement uncertainty, we have already mentioned that measurements are at some de­
gree somewhat imperfect and that depending on the variable being measured, these could
comprise both epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty. If measurements of a variable are going
to be used as inputs, the epistemic uncertainty needs to be added to the aleatoric uncertainty
of such variable to fully model its total uncertain contribution to the model. In order to do
this, type A and type B uncertainties need to be characterized and then added together to
have the full statistical characterization of the measurement uncertainty.
• Statistical, moreover, the sample size of the input variables matters. Whether it is measure­
ments or model evaluations of a variable, these are both finite and therefore we are limited
to an estimation of true statistical moments. This is a epistemic type of uncertainty since it
can be reduced by using immensely large data sets.

2.2
A review on statistical methods to handle uncertainty
In the preceding section, we were introduced to the field of uncertainty quantification. In this
section, we will explore the various probabilistic methods and programming tools that can be em­
ployed to address, propagate, and quantify uncertainty. The initial step will be to examine the
uncertainty associated with the inputs to our model. Next the different methods of uncertainty
propagation will be investigated. Finally, we will conclude our discussion by examining the field
of sensitivity analysis.
2.2.1
Input Uncertainty
In simulation studies, the representation of uncertainty in the input environment is known as input
modeling. This involves the choice of probability distributions to depict the input processes. When
these processes consist of sequences of independent random variables with the same distributions,
the task is straightforward. Yet, it can be the case that other systems appear to have input processes
that are dependent and multivariate, then the task becomes more convoluted [13].
In this thesis, the work developed will be conducted on the assumption that the inputs to the system
are independent from one another. The validity of this assumption will be discussed in greater detail
in the methodology section. Nevertheless, the following section will provide the reader with useful
resources to combat both cases.
Parametric probability density functions and how to fit them to measurements.
Parametric probability density functions PDFs are commonly employed to represent variables that
are uncertain. The designation ”parametric PDF” is derived from the fact that these distributions are
characterized by analytical expressions that are functions of hyper­parameters, which are typically
location, scale or shape. The most common parametric distribution is the Normal or Gaussian
distribution, which has a location parameter and a scale parameter that coincides with its statistical
first and second moments, namely the mean and the variance of the distribution. Nevertheless,
there are other common parametric distributions, such as the Uniform, the LogNormal, and the
Weibull. The probability density functions PDFs and notations for these distributions can be found
in Table A.1.
In many cases, the inputs to a model may be derived from measurements, that is, from observations
of the actual physical process under consideration. A relatively large number of observations will
result in a histogram that can be relied upon. The histogram may then be fitted with a distribution in
order to identify the hyper­parameters of the parametric PDF that best fits the independent sample.
A number of different methods exist, with the most common ones being the following:
Firstly, the method of moments, this is applicable when the distribution has closed­form expres­
sions relating statistical moments to distribution parameters. In such cases, the distribution param­
eters can be estimated from the sample moments by solving the system of equations that defines
the aforementioned expressions iteratively. A good example of a distribution which is unable to
used for this method is Cauchy. Secondly, the maximum likelihood estimation approach. This
method maximizes the likelihood that a set of parameters represents a population of the observed
data. Commonly, in this method, a log­transformation is used such that the maximum likelihood
is found by minimizing the negative log­likelihood. Finally, we may always rely on least squares
fitting. This entails fitting a function to the empirical CDF of the sample by minimising the least­
squared errors. There are other abstractions of these methods, as well as other methods for fitting
non­parametric distributions. However, the latter will not be covered in this work. For further
information on this topic, please refer to the following: [14] or [15]. More information relating
the first 2 methods can be find in subsection A.1.2. Moreover, it should be noted that the fitting of
distribution is an approximation to a histogram of recorded values, which introduces a degree of
numerical approximation error.

==PAGE:22==
The most common joint distribution.
In the majority of cases, scientific models incorporate a multitude of inputs. In this context, the
term ”distribution” is used in a broad sense to refer to a set of random variables represented by
a set of distributions and a relationship structure between them. This is in contrast to the more
traditional interpretation of distribution as a single univariate probability density function.
One of the most prevalent parametric multivariate distribution is the multivariate normal, X ∼
N(µ, C), where µ is the vector of means and C is the covariance matrix 3. This distribution is
employed in multitude of uncertainty quantification methodologies despite not always being the
most accurate in certain occasions. The reason is, it is the easiest and most manageable distribution,
as it characterises the distribution of multiple independent normal variables and the correlations
between them. The drawback of this composed distribution is that only allows to capture linear
correlations between variables. It is also uni­modal, meaning there is a single maximum) [16]. In
this thesis, there will be no dependency (not linear nor non­linear) in the input variables such that
a multivariate Gaussian would work fine.
Other types of multivariate distributions
It is not uncommon the need to construct joint distributions with non­normal marginals and with
a dependency structure that is not constrained to a mere linear relationship. In such instances,
the objective is to employ iso­probabilistic transformations to transform the correlated random
variables with diverse distributions into a set of independent variables within a standard normal
space. This will provide solutions for the estimation of probabilities using the multi­normal CDF
and the generation of random samples. It is necessary that iso­probabilistic transformations be
reversible, allowing the transition from a vector of dependent variables to a vector of independent
and identically distributed normal variables and vice versa. The most common transformations
are: the Rossenblatt transformation, Cholesky decomposition and Nataf transformation [17].
In order to construct input models with fully specified PDFs, several approaches exist. Different
techniques shall be employed for specific cases. A brief overview of the aforementioned tech­
niques and their respective applications shall be provided: Firstly, the conditional distributions
methods may be employed if the distribution is not explicitly known. These type of methods fac­
torises the joint distribution into a set of conditional and marginal distributions, which facilitates
the generation of samples with the desired properties. These type of methods make possible to
utilize the broad techniques available to generate uni­variate problems. Within this methodology,
two approaches that are attractive from the point of view of generating a random vector of variables
distribution are Rossenblatt (1952) [18] and Arnold et al. (2001) [19]. Secondly, another branch
of methods which require the joint distribution to be explicitly given can be identified. The most
used approach in such case is the method of copulas. A family of distributions can be represented
by their marginals and their copula, which represents the kind of relationship structure the family
holds. A copula may be thought of as a function that maps points in the unit hypercube to val­
ues in the unit interval. the copula can be determined empirically or can be chosen from a set of
pre­existing parametric families (one viable option is still the construction under an independent
copula, this method has been used in this work and will be latter commented on the methodology
chapter 3). A disadvantage of copulas is that is affected by ”the curse of dimensionality” whereas
as an advantage is that the copula remains invariant under strictly increasing transformations of
component variables which simplifies the vector generation. It should be noted that there are other
methods, which tackle the same objective, that will not be covered here, particularly since many
treat the topic of non­parametric distributions. For a complete overview, please refer to [14].

Having established the methodology for fitting distributions derived from observed data and to the
construction of multivariate distributions, the attention is turned to cases where there is no access

2.2.2
Propagation of uncertainty
Sampling techniques
The initial step in propagating uncertainty is to implement a well­defined sampling strategy and
have a design of experiments (DoE). As the term implies, random sampling entails the random
selection of values from a variable in accordance with its Probability Density Function. A robust
sampling strategy must address the issue of high dimensionality inherent in models, which presents
the challenge of determining the optimal approach to cover the operation space domain. A variety
of sampling methods can be employed to generate a sample within the unitary space.
Sampling techniques + DoE can be broadly categorised into two main groups. The first one is
called structured designs and as the name suggests, results in the points of the DoE forming a
regular grid. One advantage of this approach is that the sampling can be readily scaled up in both
the number of dimensions (factors) and the number of levels per dimension. However, the number
of experimental points increases exponentially with the number of factors, which is known as “the
curse of dimensionality”. This makes it less effective when having many dimensions.
Unstructured designs create irregular grids and are much more robust and not so sensitive to number
of dimensions. Within this group, several sampling techniques that generate samples in the unitary
hypercube 4 exist. A concise overview of the most significant of these methods is provided below.

Propagation through the model
The objective of uncertainty propagation is actually to find the PDF of the Quantities of Interests,
(i.e the model outputs which are of interest). There are several methods of uncertainty propagation.
Next, we will outline these methods and explain the most important ones in detail.
• Analytical techniques: in simple cases, uncertainty propagation can be performed ana­
lytically via the convolutions function. This method does not require a sampling strategy.
However, in a scientific programming software, it is highly unlikely that an analytical ex­
pression for the convolution operation can be found. Consequently, this method can only be
applied in very simple cases (e.g model can be represented as a linear combination of a few
inputs/parameters which are independent and identically distributed with well­known PDF).
• Model simulations: the predecessor of evaluating uncertainty via model simulations is the
Monte Carlo (MC) simulations method. The concept is straightforward, it consists in obtain­

2.2.3
Sensitivity analysis ­ using Sobol Indices
Once the propagation of uncertainty has been successfully completed, it is possible to calculate
other interesting metrics in addition to the descriptive statistics of the QoIs. This is where Sen­
sitivity Anlaysis (SA) plays a crucial role, as it allows to gain insight of which uncertain inputs
driving the variability of the outputs and in which way. Consequently, Sensitivity Anlaysis be­
comes a fundamental step of uncertainty quantification in scientific programming, as it enables
18
==PAGE:30==
the identification of variables that are required to be considered uncertain and those that can be
neglected.
In recent times the field of Sensitivity Anlaysis (as an intrinsic part of ”uncertainty analysis” [UQ +
SA]) has seen a significant growth. [30] Presents a brief overview of the vast number of approaches
that can be used when performing SA. Moreover all of them can be broadly categorized in 2 big
groups. These are ”local” vs ”global” sensitivity methods.
In general, the evolution of this field has been characterized by the tensions between local and
global perspectives and the desire for practical applicability [31]. However, in this thesis, only
global sensitivity analysis will be addressed. The reason is that local sensitivity consists in applying
a derivative­based approach which objective is finding the gradient of the model output with respect
to its inputs around a specific design point. Thus, making this type of approach unwarranted when
the model is non­linear or when the model inputs have large uncertainties [32].
Within global sensitivity, focus will be directed to variance­based methods, since this is the ap­
proach that will used in this work. Variance decomposition consists in decomposing the total vari­
ance of the ouput/s of a model into the contribution shares of the inputs. A variance­based method
is useful when the model is complex and inputs have many interactions between each other, as
the model itself can be treated as a black­box [33] (i.e it is a model free method, only input and
output samples are needed). Moreover, with this type of approach, the influence of the full range
of variation of each input factor can be captured.
The most famous method to measure the relative importance of an input in explaining output vari­
ance is Sobol. Considering a random input vector X = (X1, X2, ..., Xd) and output Y (to derive
the explanation a single output will be considered, however Sobol can easily be generalized for
multi­variable outputs), Sobol indices can be used to measure the influence of X to the output Y ,
considering the part of the variance of Y produced by each input. The basis of this theory is a
combination of the recurrent use of the Bayes theorem (conditional probability), MC simulations
and the one­at­a­time approach. Assumption of independent inputs variable is still present.

Software packages to handle uncertainty
To end this section 2.2 on methods to handle uncertainty, a brief overview of the current state
of the art on the software packages available to perform uncertainty analysis will be given. In
addition, it should not be overlooked that modern publications such the one from J. Roy and L.
Oberkampf on designing a framework for uncertainty analysis in scientific computing [11]; have
helped immensely in the development of this field, while other authors such as Andrea Saltelli in
his newest book ”The politics of modelling” has put the spotlight of sensitivity analysis and the
use of models in the popular public.
Before the computer era, sensitivity analysis was limited to manual and analytical techniques
based in local­sensitivity, however with the improvement of computational power, community
researches began to exploit the uncertainty analysis on complex models which involved many
input parameters. The first breakthrough was the adoption of the ”random” sampling strategies
which allowed for the exploration of a vast range of input combinations. With further comput­
ing resources improving, in the 1990’s Sobol is is able to introduce its innovative variance­based
approach. Towards the end of this decade, new uncertainty analysis communities emerged which
turned global­sensitivity as their primary focus, which involved that the fields of sampling and
propagation evolved in parallel. First softwares started to appear, mainly thanks to the interest
from commissions and institutions.
Despite the accelerated popularity that the field of UQ and SA was experiencing, only a handful of
software packages saw light through­out 1990 to 2010. It is past 2010 when the increasing com­
plexity of models made researches develop new tools to deal with computational expense. These
new methods involved the construction of surrogate models that could serve as an approximation
to the original process, but which still could yield with confidence the metrics out of an uncer­
tainty and sensitivity analysis. A great reference book that combines what has been found and that
provides information about softwares in the field of UQ is [35]. Moreover, to ease the timeline
visualization of this topic, [31] produced Figure 2.5 and Figure 2.6. In the later, the explosive
appearance of software packages post 2010 (specially for Python language) can be noted, which
coincides with the outbreak of PCE as a method of performing SA.

3.2.3
Sampling strategy and uncertainty propagation
In subsection 2.2.2 different types of DoEs were reviewed and stated to be the cornerstone of a
good sampling strategy. Sampling efficiently is key in this context given the necessity to work
with multiple dimensions. The objective is to utilise the minimum number of samples to cover
the greatest possible volume of hyper­space. When referring to propagation of uncertainty, this is
essentially just a vast number of model instantiations. Nonetheless, running such great quantity
of simulations is computationally expensive, thus the generated input samples need to optimize
how they cover the hyper­space to ensure that the desired behaviour is achieved at the lowest cost
possible.
To fulfill this objective, unless otherwise stated, the sampling strategy used to generate the samples
of the random input vector X is a LHS strategy. In OpenTURNS, samples are easily generated by
first using the random weighted DoE LHSExperiment(distribution, Nsamples) and then
calling the method .generate(). In the latter, distribution is the multivariate joint distribution
of the input space and Nsamples is the number of samples that will be generated.
The joint distribution π of the random input vector is easily constructed in OpenTURNS with the
method of copulas (which is the default in this library). The copula represents the part of the joint
CDF which is not described by the marginal distributions, that is, it models the dependence struc­
ture of the input variables. However, in this work, we will assume that all inputs are independent
and therefore we will make use of the independent copula. The other argument that is needed to
construct a multivariate distribution is its marginal distributions. These are given by the PDFs of
the input variables, which are the shown in Table 3.1 and Table 3.2.
After a sample of size N of inputs is generated, N simulations, each with a different set of values in
the random input vector and same parameter value, can be run. Since the VE model is deterministic,
the simulations will output N different values of the direct outputs Aout, Kout and the indirect
outputs Uratio and CF.

3.2.4
Sobol Experiment using Saltelli algorithm
OpenTURNS offers extensive methods to perform Sensitivity Anlaysis of a model using Sobol in­
dices. In subsection 2.2.3 theory behind this type of variance based SA was reviewed and in this
subsection, its practice will be demonstrated. It should be noted that the Sobol indices only provide
a measure of the share of influence of each individual input to output variance.
It should also be recalled that in the examples shown in this report, Sobol indices SA is being
performed from a UQ point of view. This implies that the relative importance of each input’s
uncertainty in influencing the output’s uncertainty is being evaluated, rather than the sensitivity of
the model to changes in each input.
It has been already mentioned that variance­based methods are ”model free”; meaning that the
model can be treated as a ”black­box”, with the focus solely on the input and output samples that
enter and exit it. Indeed, in OT different class constructors can be employed to perform this task.
However, all of them require the same arguments, namely input and output samples and a size. In
this work, the Saltelli algorithm constructor has been employed, and a step­by­step explanation of
its use will be provided ahead.
The class constructor used to compute the first and total Sobol index is SaltelliSensitivity
Algorithm(inputDesign, outputDesign, N). This class requires a specific structure for the
outputDesign and inputDesign, which needs to be generated using the DoE: SobolIndices
Experiment(distribution, N). The reason is that the input sample needs to follow the ”pick­
freeze” estimator method as explained in subsection 2.2.3. In short, the latter class will split the
DoE of the given joint distribution in 2 samples of the same size N, A and B. Their columns, then,
get mixed in order to produce a very large sample, matrix C. The latter has size N(2+d), where d
is the dimension of the input random vector. As it can be seen, the sample will grow significantly
by adding a larger number of dimensions.
Figure 3.4 shows the Sobol indices computed both for benchmark case 1 and benchmark case 2,
both having the same input hyper­space, but different operational point. A size of N = 25000 has
been used to create the SobolIndicesExperiment. This class offers the setting of the sampling
method used in the experiment, thus this was set to LHS. the total size of simulations run for this
size is therefore: 25000(2 + 4) = 1.55.
Lastly, it should be noted, that in this type of experiment, both ln(Z0meso) and Z0meso can be
used to generate the input sample and this will have no impact in the efficacy or computation
time of the program. Furthermore, since the relative importance of the uncertainty is what is being
measured, the Sobol index for the roughness length variable will be the same either with or without
the transformation, as the marginal distribution is essentially the same.

3.2.5
Model reduction: AGDL, BGDL
A powerful application of Sobol Indices SA is its use for model reduction. It is often the case
within large scale softwares, that functions admit a vast amount of inputs. Working with high
dimensionality brings complexity to any type of model and it can be a nuisance time­wise both
computationally and when performing visualization of the results.
This subsection presents an example on how Sobol indices could be employed to ”remove” non­
influential parameters from the uncertainty analysis in the VE model. It should be remarked that
the removal of variables in this context is merely a matter of fixing input variables at their nom­
inal values, not the elimination of that variable from the model.The rationale behind it is that an
uncertain input that does not contribute to the variation in the outputs of the model (i.e model’s
uncertainty is not sensible to the uncertain behaviour of such input) can be disregarded from the
uncertainty propagation point of view.
In a method such as the SaltelliSensitivityAlgorithm, the exclusion of input factors from
the input hyper­space serves a great purpose in reducing both the number of samples required to
adequately cover that space and the computational time it takes to achieve the output samples. It
should be remembered that the value N is multiplied by the number of dimensions in order to
generate the inputDesign sample.

3.3
Fitting a surrogate model
In subsection 2.2.2 the PCE as an analytical approximation of a physical model was reviewed.
Furthermore, it was noted that this type of approach is interesting when running a large number of
simulations is computationally expensive. In reference to Figure 3.3b, it was suspected that 105
samples may be insufficient to accurately elucidate the variance of the outputs. To put things into
perspective a large simulation of 106 model realizations takes approximately 3 hours to conclude.
Should we wish to reassess the UQ analysis again at another pair of heights, this would require an
additional 3 hours, as the parameters that affect the output sample generated by the model would
need to be changed. It is therefore convenient to attempt to approximate our model via a Polynomial
Chaos Expansion fit.
OpenTURNS library offers a class that creates Polynomial Chaos Expansion based on an input and
output sample of the physical model. It is necessary to reiterate that the propagation of uncer­
tainty of a set of inputs through the model is being performed. Therefore the surrogate model
merely replicates the behaviour of the physical model for values that lie within the joint distribu­
33
==PAGE:45==
tion of interest. In other words, the surrogate model is just a polynomial replica of the propagation
of uncertainty step, rather than an exact representation of the model itself. A discussion on the
methodology for fitting a PCE to the complete physical model and its subsequent application will
be presented in chapter 6.
In the examples provided in this subsection, the class constructor used has been: FunctionalChaos
Algorithm, which uses the following arguments: (inputSample, outputSample, distribution,
adaptiveStrategy, projectionStrategy). A detailed explanation in the methodology used
to build the aforementioned constructor will be provided ahead.
FunctionalChaos needs to be ”trained” with an input sample, which is generated from a given
joint distribution and its subsequent output sample. In this example, the multivariate joint distri­
bution remains as given by Table 3.1 and Table 3.2. Here, focus should be directed to the con­
venience of having all input marginals follow a normal distributions. Why is this the case? A
review of the polynomial basis functions given in Table 2.1 shows that Log­Normal does not have
a formal closed form. Meanwhile, Normal distribution has the Hermite polynomial, which one of
the simplest forms. Does this imply that a log­normal distribution cannot be employed to gener­
ate samples in the hyper­space? The answer is both yes and no. There is another constructor of
FunctionalChaos that does not necessarily require a distribution to be fed into it. Instead, it can
obtain the necessary information based on the input sample itself. Nevertheless, it is possible to
find the following note in OT API documentation: For example, the Log­Normal distribution has
orthonormal polynomials of arbitrary degree but the projection onto the functional space gener­
ated by these polynomials may converge to a function that differs significantly from the function
being projected. In light of this consideration, the simple transformation proposed appears to be a
reliable approach since the original distribution proposed for the roughness length variable remains
essentially unchanged.
After providing the input and output samples, the next step in the construction of the PCE is to build
the set of orthonormal univariate polynomial families. This is done through the distribution
argument, as the orthonormal basis polynomials will be constructed directly from it. In this work
all of the varaibles’ uncertainty are represented with Hermite polynomials in the orthonormal basis.
In orther to get these forms, StandardDistributionPolynomialFactory class is used, along­
side with LinearEnumerateFunction, which is used to create a one­to­one mapping between an
integer j and a multi­index Nn
X.
Once the aforementioned steps have been completed, it is necessary to select a truncation strat­
egy and an evaluation strategy for the approximation of the coefficients. For the first one, OT
FixedStrategy has been employed. This simply consists in selecting the first P, elements of the
polynomial chaos basis, the latter being ordered according to the given EnumerateFunction. In
the present case, for a given natural integer p which represents the total polynomial degree, the
number of coefficients is equal to: P = (nX + p)!/(nX!p!), where nX is the number of input
dimensions. In regard to the evaluation strategy, a least squares regression has been employed.At
this point, the algorithm for the FunctionalChaos can be constructed and executed.
Once the meta­model has been achieved, OT provides a class to validate it. In order to achieve
this, the meta­model parametric function and an input and output test samples are required. The
generation of the test samples is achieved through the implementation of a new sampling process
from the joint distribution. The output of the aforementioned validation method is presented in
Figure 3.6. It can be observed that a PCE of order 2 appears to be the most suitable for achieving
a solution.

3.4
The global method of assessing uncertainty in PyWasp
In the two preceding sections, we described the methodology for uncertainty propagation, SA
using Sobol and PCE fit on the surface response of the PyWAsP VE model. This analysis used
a specific joint distribution to model input uncertainty and a particular operational point which
defined the pair of heights, Zmeas and Zpred, at which wind VE is performed. It was demonstrated,
that indeed the the PCE approach successfully solves computational time issues. However, a new
complication arise. While this setup allows for rapid analysis in a specific case scenario, modifying
the joint distribution to assess different input uncertainties or changing the heights for the VE entails
repeating the PCE fit. This repetition, though computationally manageable, becomes cumbersome
when processing and structuring multiple data sets. If our goal is to study how the uncertainty of
an output increases or decreases in relation to distinct case scenarios, a methodology that would
facilitate the repetition of model calls was required.
In this context, we propose the following approach. Figure 3.9 and Figure 3.10 support this idea
graphically.
1. Construct a multivariate distribution where each marginal follows uniform distribution rep­
resenting the domain of the input variable (i.e, the possible values that the variable can take).
This allows the generation of a large input sample (∼106), which can then be propagated
to produce an equally large output sample. The joint distribution is a uniform multivariate
because, indeed, the selection of any point can be equally probable. These X and Y sam­
ples capture all the possible combination of input values and their respective outputs. The
multivariate uniform is represented in Figure 3.9 as the rectangular pieces. In theory, any
number of uncertain inputs could be added to the analysis without compromising computa­
tional time, however, in practice a new issue appears. With higher dimensionality a larger
sample size needs to be used to densely cover the hyper­space volume. This limitation will
be addressed later.
2. Construct a local joint distribution that models the inherent uncertainty of each input. This
step remains the same as the one that has been followed in the previous sections. It is im­
portant to recall that for the PCE to work, the uncertainty of the input is modelled as a joint
distribution of independently and uniformly distributed variables. For each global input
marginal, different­sized normal distributions are drawn in Figure 3.9. This illustrates that
any normal distribution with a mean and spread of our choosing can be constructed to model
the uncertainty of a given input.
3. Evaluate sample X points generated from the joint global distribution at the multivariate
local distribution built in the previous step. Essentially, the probability density value of a
point laying under the local joint distribution is being computed. From now on those values
will be called weights. The weights from the evaluation of the global input sample under
the benchmark joint distribution are displayed in Figure 3.10. As expected, most of the
weights have a value of ≈0. The non­zero weights are then used as an extra argument
in FunctionalChaosAlgorithm to make the PCE fit to the model response of a specific
combination of inputs uncertainty.
By following the steps described above, one will arrive at the following class constructor: Functional
Chaos Algorithm(X_global_sample, W, Y_global_sample, Local Distribution, adaptive
Strategy, projectionStrategy). Here, the global samples X and Y have been masked to
remove zero­valued weights, as represented by the points below the red horizontal line in Fig­
ure 3.10. It is crucial to remove these values because the PCE algorithm does not accept close to
zero weight values. However, it is acceptable to retain low­weighted points since they will not
influence the fit.

Introduction to the results
In this chapter, the results for each scenario outlined in Table 3.7 are presented through a series of
plots. The only outputs under analysis are the ratio of extrapolated to observed wind speed (Uratio)
and the capacity factor (CF), since these are the most relevant and combine both Weibull A and K
outputs in their forms. For each scenario, the results are depicted across three distinct sets of plots.
• The first set displays the mean of the output variable alongside with error bars which repre­
sent the standard deviation.
• The second set of plots illustrates the coefficient of variation (C.O.V = σ/µ) for each output
variable. This represents the relative standard uncertainty of the output resulting from the
propagation of the input uncertainties through the model.
• The third and final set of plots presents the Sobol indices of each input variable for both of
the outputs being analysed. This is used to quantify the contribution from each input to the
output uncertainty.

For readers unfamiliar with the roughness length variable (Z0) and the temperature scale mean
(Toff), as well as their effects on vertical extrapolation, a brief explanation is provided below.
• The roughness length is a parameter that represents the height of surface inhomogeneities
affecting wind flow. A higher value of this variable, is indicative of increased surface drag.
This implies that the ground’s roughnessis perceived more strongly. Consequently, this
means that achieving a certain wind speed at a higher Z0 requires a greater height com­
pared to a lower Z0 .
• The temperature scale mean is a means of characterizing atmospheric stability. For a com­
plete description the reader is directed to [37]. In summary, stable conditions, (negative
temperature variation, i.e. the terrain surface is colder than its surroundings) result in strati­
fied, non­mixed layers. This facilitates the increase of Weibull A with height. Conversely,
an unstable atmosphere results in turbulent mixed layers due to the air being pushed up­
wards. This hinders a rapid increase Weibull A with height. The standard deviation of the
temperature scale (Trms) plays a much smaller role as will be shown in the results since its
impact is primarily on the Weibull K parameter.

4.3
Discussion on the general trends with respect to the operation
point of the model
The discussion of the results will begin with an examination of the different trends, mainly in un­
certainty, relative to changes in geometric mean and height ratio at which the vertical extrapolation
is performed.
• Wind speed ratio output:
In the output Uratio, it is observed in all cases, that a one­step increase in the ratio (R) leads to
a greater increase in relative standard uncertainty compared to a one­step increase in geometric
mean (GM). This indicates that uncertainty rises more significantly when the extrapolated to
observed wind speeds are separated by a greater distance than when performing the VE at a higher
altitude. Additionally, it can be stated that within a given GM the uncertainty of Uratio increases
linearly with a logarithmic increase of the ratio, as the data points are stretched similarly for each
ratio level. It is important to note that the ratio steps increase logarithmically.
Interestingly, increasing or decreasing the value of GM varies the rise in uncertainty that takes
place with the ratio. For instance, in plots of cases 1, 2, 5, and 6, the higher the extrapolation is
made from the ground, the greater the uncertainty grows with the ratio. Being the operational
cases with high GM and high R the ones that produce the most uncertainty. However, in cases
3 and 7, uncertainty grows the most with the ratio when near the ground. In cases 4 and 8 a mix
between both effects takes place. These results suggest that a low value of Z0 causes uncertainty
to increase more with the ratio at higher altitudes from the ground.
• Capacity factor output:
The determination of the relative importance of the ratio (R) and the geometric mean (GM) as
driving factors in terms of uncertainty for the output variable CF is more complex than for the
previous output. When the ratio is fixed at a low value, almost no variation in uncertainty is
observed with respect to GM. However, when the ratio is fixed at a high value, increasing the
GM results in a significant increase in uncertainty.
Upon fixing the GM and varying the ratio, it becomes evident that the resulting trends are highly
non­linear, resulting in complex patterns. With a closer examination of these patterns, it can be
seen that in plots of case 1 and 2, a linear increase of uncertainty with the logarithmic increase of
ratio is present, with the exception of ratios that are either very short or very long. Moreover, an
increase in the GM will make the uncertainty even more sensible to changes in ratio. Highest
uncertainty values are found at large values of both GM and R.
In plots of cases 3 and 4, we witness a downside U­pattern in relative uncertainty. This interesting
behavior has a rational explanation. At high ratios (R > 3), the ratio of extrapolated to observed
wind speed, exceeds 1.3 for all GM values. This simply means that for a a given Ain, the output
Aout will be considerably large. A PWC with large Weibull A enables a wind turbine to operate
in rated power most of the time. The plots that show the mean of the simulated CFs support this,
demonstrating that at these ratios, the capacity factor exceeds 55% in all points. When operating
at rated power the power output remains constant, thereby making this output less uncertain.
However, plots 3 and 4 exhibit different behaviors when increasing the GM, since plot 4 shows
a more linear response but is also much more sensitive to changes in this variable. Still a large
value of both GM and R produce the most uncertainty.
An unexpected phenomenon occurs in plots 5, 7, and 8, where the relative uncertainty of CF
steadily decreases while operating at higher ratios. This behavior, although requires further in­
vestigation, can be explained with the following. At low ratios, the standard deviation remains
relatively constant, but the mean increases, causing the relative mean to decrease. At higher ra­
tios, Aout increases to values where rated power is generated, leading to the previously explained
57
==PAGE:69==
effect of reduced uncertainty. This trend persists or even intensifies at higher ratios. This can be
attributed to the fact that variable Ain is the most significant factor in explaining output variance
in this cases; since the only way to have lower uncertainty in CF while having larger uncertainty
in Ain is by operating at rated power or zero­power. Still, this behavior is particularly interesting
as it is peculiarly more pronounced in plots of cases 5 and 7, where the atmosphere is slightly
stable, compared to plot 8, where it is very stable.
Finally, plot 6 exhibits the most complex behaviour, combining the effects observed in plots of
cases 3, 4, 5, 7, and 8. Operating at low GM values will almost not modify the standard deviation,
but the mean is still increased, thus the relative uncertainty decreases. At higher altitudes, the
wind speed ratio does not exceed 1.4 until an R ∼3, 4. At these points, the turbine operates at
rated power, leading to a decrease in uncertainty.
4.4
Discussion of the research questions
• Do group B scenarios consistently exhibit more uncertainty?
It can indeed be observed that all scenarios of group B) consistently produce more uncertainty
that their equivalent counterparts of group A), but only in the capacity factor CF output. The
plots of Uratio remain unaffected by the increase in σAin. The reason why only CF is affected by
this change in the uncertainty of one of the input variables can be understood by examining the
Sobol indices. It can be noticed that for all scenarios, the input Ain does not contribute to Uratio
variance, as its Sobol index is always 0. In contrast, in the CF output, Ain already had some
relevance in group A scenarios.
This increment in CF uncertainty raises both the minimum and maximum relative uncertainty
values observed in the plots of the 4 different scenarios. This is especially true for case that
goes from 1 to 4 and the one from 3 to 7 (the slightly unstable atmosphere cases), where relative
uncertainty increases considerably after this change, even though the mean remains the same.
• Which scenario produces the most uncertainty?
Based on the evidence presented, the cases with a very stable atmosphere produce consistently
greater uncertainty than the slightly stable ones. This is due to the fact that in these cases, Toff
becomes the most dominant variable in explained variance, both in Uratio and in CF outputs. This
is consistent with the explanation given at the beginning of this chapter, as very stable conditions
enable a fast grow of Weibull A with height. We could examine again the global sensitivity
picture Figure 3.11a, (although it only represents the operational point 65), to see that within this
region of the input space of Toff, the model is very sensitive to changes in this variable. This is
especially true, when the VE is performed high above the ground (large GM), as Z0 becomes
less relevant. It is there when uncertainty peaks.
To differentiate now whether the scenario with low Z0 or high Z0, coupled with a very stable
atmosphere, contributes the most to uncertainty, we need to examine this variable, as it still plays
a role. So it is actually low Z0 cases that produce more uncertainty as in case 2 and 6 produce
more uncertainty than cases 4 and 8 in all operation points. But again this can only be said for
output CF.
The reason why this occurs is because in cases 4 and 8, Z0 has a larger impact at low GM
and low R than its counterparts 2 and 6, where Toff remains relatively relevant throughout all
operational points. The spread of Toff appears to generate more uncertainty than the spread of
Z0. Consequently, as the mean temperature scale loses importance relative to the roughness
length, scenarios with large Z0 values exhibit slightly less uncertainty than those that us smaller
values of Z0.
To conclude, we can state that scenario 6, characterized by a high value of σAin, a low value of
58
==PAGE:70==
Z0 and a very stable atmosphere, stands out as the scenario generating the most uncertainty in
its outputs. This combination creates a perfect recipe for uncertainty.
• How can uncertainty be explained with Sobol indices
The information provided by the Sobol indices is valuable way for identifying the most important
sources of uncertainty. In our analysis, 4 distinct input variables are considered, indicating that
there are four potential sources of uncertainty.
The first noticeable observation is that the input variable Trms does not play a significant role in
generating uncertainty in any of the presented case scenarios. Given this, we can move on to a
more detailed explanation of the different cases, starting with group A:
In case 1, for the Uratio variable, Toff is consistently the most relevant variable except for the first
three GM values. Regarding output CF, the input Toff also dominates but only in ↑GM, ↑R,
points. In the opposite points, Ain generates most of the uncertainty. Notably, at a GM = 20
and high ratio values, Z0 becomes increasingly relevant. This indicates that when performing
VE at a point like 65 (from 50m to 130m), Toff explains all the variability in Uratio; while for CF,
the variability is distributed evenly between Ain and Toff. This is because we are far from the
ground, where Z0 is not so relevant anymore. Conversely at a point like 7 (from 10m to 40m),
Z0 variability generates a decent amount of uncertainty in both outputs.
In case 2, the effects of a very stable atmosphere result in Toff accounting for nearly all the
uncertainty. The only exception is at GM = 20, R = 1.2 for the CF output.
In case 3, Z0 gains much more relevance compared to case 1, which had the same atmospheric
stability but a smoother surface. Relevant Sobol index values are shared evenly between Z0
and Toff for Uratio. The same applies to CF, except at for very low extrapolation ratios, where
interestingly Ain becomes the most relevant.
Case 4, Sobol indices are basically the same as in case 2, with a very slightly increase of impor­
tance of Z0.
Comparing the Sobol indices of group A) to the similar cases in group B), we observe that only
the CF Sobol values change. As expected, the trends seen in group A are maintained, but with
a significant increase in the importance of Ain.This variable becomes one of the most important
factors in explaining variance for most standard operational points where VE is performed. This
result is not surprising, as the power curve depends on wind speed raised to the cube, and wind
speed is greatly influenced by Ain.
To conclude the discussion of the results, it should be noted that in real projects, achieving low
uncertainty in the measured wind speed is common, making the results of group A cases more rel­
evant to applied studies. Additionally, these types of 2D plots can be used as tabulated information
of mean and variance at different operational points for a given combination of input uncertainties.
However, these results are only valid for an idealized case of vertical extrapolation, and horizontal
extrapolation is not modelled.
Lastly, it should be said that the fact that Trms has not been relevant in either of these cases does not
exclude it from being relevant in other scenarios. It has been found that the only times that Trms
becomes relevant is when the stability of the atmosphere is unstable, i.e. Toff > 0. This is unlikely
in most parts of the world where wind farms are located. However, the results for such a case are
displayed in the appendix subsection A.2.6.

Conclusions
This thesis has focused on the quantification and analysis of uncertainties in wind resource assess­
ment modules used in PyWAsP. To succeed in this work, first a comprehensive understanding of
the context and foundation of this research was established through an extensive literature review.
This review provided a solid base of theoretical and methodological knowledge in the field of
Uncertainty Quantification and Sensitivity Anlaysis. The insights gained from it have been instru­
mental in guiding the later analyses and model evaluations presented, ensuring that the research
was both grounded and robust.
The literature review chapter not only clarified the current state of knowledge, but also identified
gaps and opportunities for future research. One the one hand, the most significant weakness iden­
tified is the impossibility of completing the full process presented in Figure 2.1. This incompletion
is due to the unavailability of measurements, which are needed to validate the model at the differ­
ent points analyzed. As a result, the full UQ framework as presented by [11] could not be fully
completed and part of the uncertainty in the model will never be known. On the other hand, the
review of advanced statistical methods and modern software tools has highlighted the potential for
further innovations in this field. These innovations pave the way for more effective management
of uncertainties in wind energy projects that make use of scientific programming tools for their
analyses.
The methodology chapter has thoroughly outlined the procedures employed to sample from a dis­
tribution, propagate the uncertainty of a set of inputs though the model, and finally quantify the
uncertainty of the output simulations. This is followed by an explanation of how a PCE surrogate
model could be fitted to such type of process, effectively reducing the lengthy time previously
needed. This sequence reflects the actual process followed in this work, which involved build­
ing upon incremental milestones that were eventually achieved. These efforts culminate in the
successful integration of all these processes into a global method for quantifying and analyzing
uncertainty in PyWAsP, which allows for an easier and quicker way of making these analyses.
Regarding the process mentioned above, it is important to acknowledge that a selection of inputs
based on their a priori perceived relevance had to be made to define the study. This implied ruling
out other variables of the model that may well had a relevant impact in the uncertainty of the
outputs. Additionally, the model itself has other intricacies whose sensitivities and uncertainties
could not be known, including, for example, the numerical methods used to solve the differential
equations. Moreover, the model reduction applied to AGDL and BGDL was only proved at 2
points and for a determined combination of inputs uncertainty, these tried to be as representative
as possible but is still a limitation to consider.
Finally, the application of the developed method was demonstrated to effectively quantify and
analyze uncertainty in the PyWAsP vertical extrapolation model with minimal computational time
for a selection of interesting case studies. From the results, it was discovered that the temperature
scale variable of the new WAsP stability method significantly impacts the uncertainty in key project
metrics. These analyses demonstrated that uncertainty is generated in various ways depending on
the terrain and stability configurations. Ultimately, it was proven that high uncertainty in the mean
temperature scale variable during very stable atmospheric conditions over homogeneous terrain
generates the greatest uncertainty in capacity factor. Additionally, in this setting, attempting to
extrapolate at a height ratio greater than 2 will significantly increase the uncertainty. This is a
common and relevant setting in standard wind resource assessment campaigns and should be noted.

==PAGE:72==
One of the greatest achievements of this work, however, is not the findings made upon the results
but the creation of the application which makes the study itself possible. The true power of this
method lies in the ability to study any possible combination of input uncertainties with almost
zero waiting time. This opens a new door in the field of Wind Resource Assessment applied to
wind farm planning and development, providing a solution that could be potentially implemented
in a future version of PyWAsP. This method has the potential to become a standard approach for
addressing the repetitive tasks associated with sensitivities and uncertainties in projects.

Future work
Most of the time spent in this work was used assimilating how to make use of the stand­alone
PyWAsP VE model and integrating it with the OpenTURNS library methods. This integration has
been the most time­consuming task, leaving limited opportunity to explore other components of
WRA, such as the flow model, the IBL model, and long­term correction. Future investigations
should focus on these components to provide a comprehensive overview of the WRA model’s
uncertainty. This would ensure a more direct and accurate quantification of uncertainty. Addi­
tionally, a more thorough investigation and selection of input variables is necessary, although this
could derive in a time­consuming process.
Another important future task is to compare the analytical curves of uncertainty in vertical extrap­
olation published by Mark Kelly and Ib Troen in [38], with the results obtained from numerical
brute­force simulations using the methods presented in this thesis. This will therefore harmonize
numerical and analytical investigations.
Furthermore, additional sensitivity analysis focusing on the global operation of the model should
be conducted. This would involve a better understanding of how the model’s outputs react to
changes across the entire input domain. The PCE approach can be used to save time in this analysis.
By feeding the global input and output samples into the PCE algorithm along with the uniform
distributions used for extracting those samples, a fast understanding of the system’s non­linearities
could be achieved. Uni­variable graphs of output to input variables could be generated by fixing
all variables except the one of interest, since a polynomial model has been generated by the PCE.
This type of study, although seems fairly simple, in practice will require careful sample selection
from parameters that become new variables, such as the observed and predicted heights.
Overall, expanding the scope of analysis and refining the methodologies will enhance the robust­
ness and applicability of this work. Which has the potential to become part of the uncertainty
quantification framework in wind resource assessment applications of PyWAsP.
