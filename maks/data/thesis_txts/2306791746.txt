ABSTRACT 
 
Identification of B-cell epitopes is likely to play an important role in future development of vaccines 
and in immunotherapy. Presently the experimental methods used to identify B-cell epitopes are both 
time consuming and expensive and the development of a reliable tool for predicting epitopes would 
be of considerable interest to the industry and in research.  
Most existing methods rely only on antigen features for identification of B-cell epitopes, but in 
practice these methods perform poorly [1] since they do not take the antibodies into account. In a 
study by Sela-Culang et. al. [2], it was shown that utilizing both antibody and antigen features can 
improve B-cell epitope predictions.  
In this study, we use a similar method to predict antibody-specific B-cell epitopes. Two different 
prediction methods were made to predict antibody-antigen interactions; one uses a Random Forest 
and the other a Neural Network. Of these two, the Random Forest model has the highest 
performance. 
Finally, we demonstrate that the predictions from the Random Forest model can be used as restraints 
in a rigid-body docking protocol producing a highly significant increase in the predictive performance 
for B cell epitopes when compared to other state-of-the-art structure-based prediction methods.

RESUMÉ 
Identifikation af B-celleepitoper spiller en vigtig rolle i den fremtidige udvikling af vacciner samt 
indenfor immunterapi. De eksperimentelle metoder der anvendes i dag, er både tidskrævende og 
dyre, og udviklingen af et pålideligt værktøj til at forudsige epitoper har stor interesse for industri og 
forskning. 
De fleste eksisterende fremgangsmåder bruger kun egenskaber fra antigenen til at identificere B-
celleepitoper. I praksis er disse metoder generelt ikke særlig gode[1], da de ikke tager antistoffet med 
i betragtning. I en undersøgelse foretaget af Sela-Culang et. al. [2], blev det vist, at man ved at udnytte 
både antistof- og antigen egenskaber kan forbedre B-celleepitop forudsigelserne. 
I denne undersøgelse anvendte vi en lignende metode til at forudsige antistof-specifikke B-
celleepitoper. To forskellige machine-learning metoder blev brugt til at forudsige antistof-antigen 
interaktioner. De to brugte metoder var Random Forest og Neurale Netværk. Af disse to havde 
Random Forest modellen den højeste ydeevne. 
Endelig har vi vist, at forudsigelserne fra Random Forest modelen kan bruges som bibetingelser i en 
rigid-body docking protokol. Fra dette ser vi en signifikant stigning i metodens evne til at forudsige B-
celleepitoper i forhold til de bedste nutidige struktur-baserede metoder.

INTRODUCTION AND MOTIVATION 
For counteracting infections the immune system has evolved a very specific method for the 
identification of foreign molecules found in both bacteria and viruses. These foreign molecules are 
generally very complex and currently we are not able to consistently predict how they interact with 
the immune system.  If we could do this it would have a profound impact on vaccine development. 
In this thesis, we sought to investigate how foreign molecules, also known as antigens, bind to 
antibodies from the immune system. Previously, methods have attempted to do this by looking at the 
antigen alone, but recent studies have shown that in order to make useful predictions it is crucial to 
utilize information about the antibody as well [2]. The specific part of the antigen that the antibody 
binds to is called a B-cell epitope. 
The existing methods for B-cell identification primarily use the antigen sequence as input [3][4][5]. 
These methods are best at identifying single continuous stretches of amino acids, called linear 
epitopes. But around 90% of all B-cell epitopes consist of small segments that are far apart in the 
sequence and only brought together by the folding of the protein [6][7]. Such epitopes are called 
discontinuous epitopes and identification of these is difficult since they are virtually indistinguishable 
from other antigen surface residues [1]. However, it has been shown that the amino acid composition 
found in antibody-antigen interfaces differs from the amino acid composition found in other protein-
protein interfaces [8]. Therefore, it might be possible to train a machine learning algorithm to learn 
whether or not a given set of antibody binding residues are likely to bind a certain epitope.   
The scope of this project is to present a prediction method utilizing both the antibody and the antigen 
to identify B-cell epitopes on the antigen surface.   
For each antibody-antigen structure, we identified all possible interactions between all residues found 
in the antibody binding site and all the residues found in the antigen. Each of these interactions was 
then marked as either positive if the antibody-antigen pair was in close contact, and as negative if the 
pair was not in contact. This was done in order to produce numerical data used to train a machine-
learning algorithm to learn which interactions are in contact and which are not.  
To produce the best predictions, we trained two different algorithms, Random Forest and Neural 
Network, and found that the Random Forest produces the best results. We then optimized the 
predictions from the Random Forest using two different structural-based methods. The first 
optimization method uses the antigen structure to do structural smoothing of the predictions while 
the second optimization method uses the pairwise prediction produced by the Random Forest model 
to guide a docking process between an antibody and an antigen. We present the performance of 
these methods and compare them with DiscoTope, a prediction tool utilizing the antigen structure to 
predict B-cell epitopes.

2 THEORY 
2.1 The adaptive system 
In the adaptive immune system there are two major types of cells, T-cells and B-cells. Both are 
produced in the bone marrow and are commonly known as lymphocytes. Once produced, the B-cells 
remain in the bone marrow to develop and mature, while the T-cells migrate to the thymus for their 
maturation. During the maturation both cell types undergo negative selection where cells that 
recognize self-antigens are eliminated to make sure that the immune system will only attack intruding 
pathogens. After maturation both types of lymphocytes enter the bloodstream as naïve T-cells and B-
cells.  
 
 
Figure 1: Under an invasion the pathogen is engulfed by an antigen presenting cell (APC) and 
presented to the T-cells on a major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecule. Only if the T-cell 
receptor (TCR) recognizes the presented antigen is the T cell activated by an APC cytokine release. 
Activated T-cells proliferate and differentiate into T helper (TH) cells. The TH cell can then activate B-
cells but only if the antigen presented by the B-cell is recognized by the TCR on the TH cell. If the TH cell 
registers the presented antigen, it activates the B-cell by releasing cytokines and the activated B-cell 
proliferates.  
 
Under an invasion, the naïve T-cells become activated when an antigen presenting cell (APC) presents 
an antigen that can be recognized by the T-cell receptors found on the surface of the naïve T-cell. 
Activated T-cells proliferate into T helper (HT) cells which are needed for the activation of B-cells (see 
Figure 1).  
The activation of a B-cell begins when the B-cell binds an antigen via its B-cell receptor (BCR). Antigens 
are usually peptides, polysaccharides, or lipids from intruding pathogens such as bacteria and viruses. 
The B-cell can recognize specific antigens from an intruding pathogen by its BCR and once recognized 
the pathogen is engulfed by the B-cell. The engulfed pathogen is degraded and the specific antigen is 
presented to a T-helper cell on a MHC molecule on the surface of the B-cell.

Helper T-cells registers the presented antigen from the pathogen and activates the B-cell by releasing 
cytokines matching the type of antigen presented. The cytokine profile released by the helper T-cell 
determines which type of antibody will be produced by the B-cell. Although the BCR found on this be 
B-cell has sufficiently high affinity to allow activation, the affinity is not high enough to facilitate an 
effective immune response. The activate B-cell moves to the lymph node where they will form a 
germinal center (GC) and start to proliferate.

To produce antibodies with high antibody affinity, the B-cells undergo a micro evolutionary process 
involving mutation and selection (See Figure 2). In the nucleus of the activated B-cell an enzyme called 
Activation Induced-cytidine Deaminase (AID) induces random point mutations in the variable region 
of the antibody genes, thereby changing the affinity of the antibody. The growing population of B-
cells expresses these hypermutated genes producing antibodies with a range of affinities for the 
target antigen.  
The different B-cells are then selected based on their antibody affinity for the pathogenic antigen 
presented by the follicular dendritic cells. Only B-cells with high affinity antibodies have the ability to 
bind the antigen presented by the follicular dendritic cells. A high affinity interaction induces the B-
cell to spread on the surface of the follicular dendritic cell, capturing more and more antigen. The B-
cell then contract stripping the antigen from the follicular dendritic cell. This captured antigen is then 
degraded and presented to T-cells which return the chemical signal required for cell survival. The B-
cells with a low affinity fail to capture antigen and therefore do not receive this signal and so undergo 
apoptosis.  
Only the B-cells with the highest antibody affinity remain in the variable population after this 
competitive selection. In each generation AID induces random point mutations in the variable region 
of the antibody genes and in each generation only cells expressing antibodies with high affinities 
toward the pathogenic antigen survive. This ensures that the antibody affinity for each generation 
increases over time.  
Hypermutation and selection acting on a large population over a number of generations produces 
dramatic evolutionary effects as only the most fit cells contribute genetic material to the next 
generation. The B-cells which have survived this competition can then differentiate to plasma cells 
which produce massive amounts of highly specific antibodies.  
2.2 Antibody structure  
Antibodies, also known as immunoglobulins, are Y-shaped proteins produced by B-cells and as 
mentioned above these antibodies play a vital role in the clearance of foreign pathogens [10]. In order 
to elicit the right immune response the antibody needs to bind its target antigen with a high affinity 
and it needs to elicit the right immune response recruiting other immune cells and molecules. 
Each antibody molecule is composed of two identical heavy chains and two identical light chains. Each 
of the heavy chains is comprised of four immunoglobulin domains. One variable, named VH and three 
constant, named CH1, CH2, and CH3, and each of the light chains is composed of two immunoglobulin 
domains, one variable named VL and one constant named CL [10] (see Figure 3).  
The antibody can be further divided into three fragments with two identical fragments containing the 
antigen binding site (Fab fragment) and one fragment containing the tail region of the antibody (Fc 
fragment). The Fab fragment is involved in binding of a specific antigen from the intruding pathogen. 
==PAGE:12==

The residues involved in binding of the antigen are usually referred to as a paratope and are found in 
the variable regions of the antibody. The Fc fragment activates the immune system by interacting 
with cell surface receptors called Fc receptors and proteins of the complement system [11][12] (see 
Figure 3).  
 
Figure 3: Structural overview of an antibody showing the location of different fragments, chains and

2.2.1 Complementarity determining regions (CDRs) 
Located within each of the variable regions is six hypervariable loops, three found in the heavy chain 
and three found in the light chain (see Figure 4). These hypervariable loops are commonly referred to 
as the complementarity determining regions (CDRs) because the surface they form is complementary 
to the surface of the antigen to which they bind [10]. It has been a common practice to identify the 
CDR as the antigen binding site, but it is now known that not all residues found in the CDR are 
involved in binding. Furthermore it has been shown that about 20% of the residues involved in 
antigen binding fall outside the CDRs [14] and that these residues play an important role in antigen 
binding. One of the reasons why previous methods fail to identify the right epitopes is that they 
include residues that do not contribute to binding and exclude residues that do.    
==PAGE:13==
6 
 
Although CDR loops are hypervariable it has been shown that they adopt only a limited number of 
conformations, which are given by key positions in the sequence and by the length of the 
hypervariable loop [15][16].

2.3 Antigen  
An antigen is any small molecule from intruding pathogens, such as bacteria and viruses, which 
provokes an immune response to produce antibodies. Antigens are usually 
peptides, polysaccharides or lipids. Each antibody is specifically produced to match the antigen 
allowing a precise identification of the antigen. 
2.4 Epitopes and paratopes 
The association between an antibody and an antigen involves many non-covalent interactions 
between the epitope (the set of antigen residues involved in binding) and the paratopes (the set of 
antibody residues involved in binding) [10]. A single example of an antibody-antigen complex is shown 
in Figure 5, where both the epitope and the paratope are highlighted.

A paratope is the part of an antibody which recognizes and binds an antigen. A general paratope 
consists of 15-22 amino acids but only 2-5 of these contribute to the binding energy [17].  
As mentioned above many of the paratope residues are found in the hypervariable loops, but even 
though the mutation rate in these loops are high some types of amino acids are more prevalent than 
others [8]. Most abundant amino acid types found in the paratope is tyrosine, tryptophan and 
phenylalanine [18]. Especially tyrosine plays a major role in antigen binding [19][11].  
The part of an antigen recognized by an antibody is called an epitope and the residues found in the 
epitope are usually found surface-exposed [6][20].  
Epitopes are generally grouped into two types. Continuous and discontinuous epitopes [21] (see 
Figure 6). Discontinuous epitopes are composed of discontinuous sections of the antigen's amino 
acid sequence brought into close proximity by the folding of the amino acid sequence, whereas 
continuous epitopes are composed of a single linear stretch of amino acids. As mentioned above 
around 90% of epitopes are discontinuous [22].

2.4.1 Epitope predictions  
It has been shown that the amino-acid composition of epitopes is essentially indistinguishable from 
other surface-exposed residues not found in the epitope [23][1]. Furthermore it has been shown that 
most of the antigen surface may be part of an epitope and that the location of the epitope depends 
on the antibody. In Figure 7 we show the binding of three different antibodies to the HEL protein. 
From this figure we see that the most of the HEL surface is in contact with an antibody. This makes 
the prediction of epitopes difficult since an epitope for one antibody is not the same as an epitope for 
another antibody. It has therefore been suggested to predict epitopes for a certain antibody 
[10][24][2]. By doing this the method will obtain general features for the interaction between the 
antigen and the antibody rather than obtaining only general features for the antigen.

2.6 Protein-Protein docking 
The main task of protein-protein docking is to predict the three dimensional structure of a protein-
protein complex by using separate protein structures (see simple overview in Figure 9). The docking 
algorithm is a structure-based program which searches the conformational space of two interacting 
proteins to identify structures that are most likely to occur in nature [25] by using a score function to 
find the protein-protein complex with the lowest energy.   
 
 
Figure 9: Simple overview of docking. Given two separate protein structures the docking algorithm 
tries to identify the biologically relevant complex.   
2.6.1 Docking algorithm  
The RosettaDock algorithm is a Monte Carlo based algorithm used for protein-protein docking.  
The method employs a low-resolution and a high- resolution [25] (see Figure 10). For each input 
multiple potential output conformations called “decoys” are generated and ranked using an energy 
function.  
The input for the docking algorithm is the structures of two different proteins (eg. an antibody and an 
antigen). Random initial locations and orientations of the two molecules are chosen. After this the low 
resolution stage begins. In this stage each amino acid is represented by a single pseudo atom, called a 
centroid. The low resolution stage includes 500 Monte Carlo based rigid body moves. For each of 
these moves one of the partners is randomly translated and rotated and the move is either accepted 
or rejected based on the change in energy using the Metropolis Criterion [25].

rejected. If ∆𝐸< 0 then the random Monte Carlo move is always accepted, but if ∆𝐸> 0 then the 
move is only accepted occasionally. In the Metropolis Criterion the probability of accepting a move is

algorithm. The output is a structure containing a potential complex of the two partners and this is 
then ranked using a score function.

3.1 Dataset 
In this section the dataset is introduced as well as a detailed description of how the training and test 
sets were defined.    
3.1.1 Creating a non-redundant dataset 
The dataset was obtained by searching the Protein Data Bank (PDB) [30] for antibody-antigen 
complexes using LYRA [28] to identify lymphocyte receptor chains. For each antibody-antigen 
complex, the antigen was defined as any non-antibody protein chain within 4Å of any antibody 
residue in the complementarity determining region (CDR). To obtain a good dataset, only structures 
with a resolution below 3Å were included. Structures with an antigen smaller than 60 amino acids or 
with non-canonical amino acids involved in binding were also removed. Additionally structures 
containing multiple antibodies were split into separate structure files with their interacting antigen.  
This resulted in a dataset with 665 redundant antibody-antigen complexes. The sequences from these 
antibody-antigen complexes were then clustered using CD-HIT (Cluster Database at High Identity with 
Tolerance) [31] with a cutoff on both antibody (>90% ID) and antigen sequence (>70% ID). The final 
non-redundant dataset contained 155 antibody-antigen clusters and is the one used in this study.  
==PAGE:20==

3.1.2 Redefined CDR  
It is conventionally assumed that the antibody binding site only contain residues found within the 
complementarity determining regions (CDRs) [32], but it has been shown that about 20% of the 
residues involved in binding fall outside the CDRs and that these make a significant energetic 
contribution to antigen binding [14][10].  
We therefore redefined our CDRs by calculating the binding frequency of each antibody residue using 
only residues shown to bind in more than 5 structures in our dataset. This resulted in a redefined CDR 
with 79 residues among which 33 residues were found in the light chain and 46 residues were found 
in the heavy chain. The frequencies of the redefined CDR are shown in Figure 11.

3.1.3 Predicting B-cell epitopes utilizing antibody information    
The main purpose of this project is to investigate if it is possible to improve the prediction of 
discontinuous B-cell epitopes by including antibody information. To do this, we first aimed at 
developing a model for prediction of interactions between antigen and antibody residues. For each

3.1.4 Definition of B-cell epitopes 
Each residue in our dataset was annotated as either epitope or non-epitope (for the antigen) and 
either paratope or non-paratope (for the antibody), dependent on whether the distance between the 
antibody and the antigen residue was below 4Å or above 6Å. All interactions with distances between 
4Å and 6Å were removed, as they could potentially be either an epitope-paratope pair or a non-
epitope-paratope pair. The threshold of 4Å was chosen since it has been argued to give the best 
results [22]. 
3.1.5 Included features 
The training set used to train both the Neural Network and the Random Forest includes all the 
interactions between each of the residues found in the redefined CDR and all antigen residues. For 
each interacting residue pair the following features were included in the dataset:

For the Neural Network all features with large values were normalized to ensure that all data had 
similar magnitude. This was done since it has been shown that  input  data  normalization  with  
certain  criteria  prior  to  training process  is  crucial  to  obtain  good  results [37]. 
3.1.6 Defining the training and test set 
In order to get the best performance it is important that the machine learning tool learns the 
difference between positive and negative antibody-antigen residue pairs. For this purpose a number 
of different training sets were created to test which of these would give us the best predictions.  
To define the best training set we first looked at the relationship between the positive and negative 
interactions. As shown in the results the relationship between the positive and the negative 
interactions are very disproportionate since the number of positive interaction is very small compared 
to the number of negative interactions. We therefore decided to change the negative to positive ratio 
in the training set. This analysis was performed by Martin Closter Jespersen using Random Forest and 
the investigated positive to negative ratios were 1:1, 1:5, 1:10, 1:20, 1:50, and 1:100. From this 
analysis we found that the best performance was obtained using the 1:10 ratio and this ratio was 
therefore used in the final training set. The ratio in the test set was not changed and contains the

typical usage situation rather than the specific dataset used for training.

3.2 Neural Network and Random Forest 
To produce the best predictions two different prediction methods were used namely Neural Network 
and Random Forest. The performance of the Neural Network was investigated in this project while 
the performance of the Random Forest was investigated in a parallel study performed by Martin 
Closter Jespersen. For each of the models the training set and the test set was divided into five 
subgroups which were then used to do a 5-fold cross-validation with the Random Forest and a 5-fold 
nested cross-validation with the Neural Network, using the training set to train the models and the 
test set to evaluate the performance of these models.

3.2.2 Random Forest 
The Random Forest was implemented in Python by Martin Closter Jespersen using the 
RandomForestRegressor from sklearn [39] using 1000 trees.   
3.2.3 Model output  
Both the Neural Network and the Random Forest model produce pairwise prediction scores between 
each of the residues found in the redefined CDR and all the antigen residues of a specific antigen-
antibody complex. The output of both models is a score indicating how likely the interaction is to be 
an epitope-paratope pair. This score is from now on referred to as the interaction score. If the 
interaction score is high the interaction is likely to be a positive pair and if the interaction score is low 
the interaction is likely to be a negative pair.  
From the interaction score we then calculated a condensed antigen and an antibody score. The 
condensed antigen score is defined for each antigen residue as the highest of the interaction scores 
for that specific antigen residue and it is used to evaluate how likely this antigen residue is to be an 
epitope. The antibody score is defined similarly for each antibody residues as the highest of the 
interaction scores for that specific antibody residue and is used to evaluate if a single antibody 
residue is likely to be a paratope.    
Using the interaction score, the antigen score and the antibody score, we evaluate the performance 
of the machine learning model by calculating the area under the receiver operating characteristics 
curve (AUC), the AUC10%, the positive predictive value (PPV) and the true positive rate (TPR). These 
performance values were calculated for each structure and the average of these where used to 
evaluate the performance of the method. The AUC was calculated as described in [40] and the 
AUC10% was calculated by integrating the area under the ROC curve up to a false positive rate of 
10%. The PPV indicates how many of our predictions are true positives and is defined as

3.2.4 Feature importance 
For the final model the importance of the different features was evaluated and the feature space was 
later reduced to make sure that only important features were included in the dataset. Feature 
importance was performed by sequentially including features listed in section 3.1.5.       
To find the most important features in the Neural Network a 5 fold nested cross-validation was 
performed using only the training set. In each round of the nested cross-validation, one partition was 
used as the validation set and the remaining four partitions were used to do a 4 fold cross-validation 
creating four models using one partition as test set and the remaining three for training.  From these 
four models the overall test performance was calculated by concatenating the predictions from the 4 
tests and then calculating the AUC. This 5 fold nested cross-validation resulted in 5 predictions, one 
for each of the 5 partitions.  
Using the method described above we investigated how different features influenced the 
performance of the Neural Network. Among the investigated features were the antibody, antigen, 
RSA and secondary structure for the neighboring residues. After this we narrowed down the feature 
space by adding features, sequentially picking only the features with the best performance and 
selecting only features that improved the performance significantly.

3.2.5 Comparison of Neural Network and Random Forest 
The predictions from the Neural Network and the predictions from the Random Forest were 
compared using the AUC, AUC10%, PPV50 and TPR50 for each of the individual models. It was here 
found that the Random Forest outperformed the Neural Network and we therefore decided to 
proceed with the Random Forest predictions. These predictions will from now on be referred to as the 
initial Random Forest predictions.  
3.2.6 Refinement  
In a parallel study performed by Martin Closter Jespersen it was shown that the predictions of the 
Random Forest could be significantly improved by introducing a refinement step, and these refined 
results were therefore used in this study.  
The predictions from the refinement is an ensemble of two different methods, one using singular 
value decomposition to remove background specific noise and another using sequential smoothing of 
the initial Random Forest predictions. These refined predictions will from now on be referred to as 
the refined Random Forest predictions.   
3.3 Optimization methods   
To improve the B-cell epitope predictions produced by the initial Random Forest two different 
methods were used: Structural smoothing and guided docking. In both of these optimization models 
the structure of the antigen is needed and in the guided docking both the antigen and the antibody 
structure is needed.  
3.3.1 Structural smoothing 
The main idea in this method is to modify the predictions from the initial Random Forest by assigning 
the average prediction of the neighboring residues to each antigen residue. By doing this we hope to 
downscale residues with high scores if found in an area with many low scoring residues, and to 
upscale residues with low predictions if found in an area with many high scoring residues.  
Since epitopes are usually found on the antigen surface we only include residues with a RSA above 0.2 
in this analysis, since these are found on the protein surface [41]. All residues with an RSA below 0.2 
were excluded. For each of the antigen residues included in the dataset the closest neighboring 
residues within a specific distance were assigned to a patch and an average of these antigen scores 
was calculated and assigned to the residue in the center of the patch.  
To find the optimal size of the patch the different patch radii were used ranging from 0 to 40 Å. For 
each patch radius the AUC and the AUC10% from each antigen structure in the dataset were 
calculated and the mean of all these were then used to evaluate the final performance of the model.

3.3.2 Guided docking 
The main goal of the docking procedure is to investigate if it is possible to improve our B-cell epitope 
predictions, by performing global antibody-antigen docking using the predictions from the refined 
Random Forest.  
For each antibody-antigen structure in our dataset, a global docking procedure was performed using 
the RosettaDock protocol [26]. RosettaDock allows the addition of a constraint file to include 
constraints to the Rosetta score function. In Rosetta each constraint consists of two parts. The type of 
interaction being measured and the function used to define how the measured value is transformed. 
The different types of interactions and the functions are listed on the Rosetta webpage1. In this study 
two different docking protocols were prepared to evaluate whether or not docking can be used to 
improving our B-cell epitope predictions.

docking can be improved in a naïve way by using the residues found in the CDR as constraints. In this 
protocol each docking attempt should minimize the distance between the residues found in the CDR 
to any given antigen residue. This is done using the Rosetta interaction type called SiteConstraint. The 
SiteConstraint minimizes the distance between a single residue from one chain to all residues from an 
opposing chain. The constraint function used in this protocol is a standard Rosetta function,

3.3.3 Docking protocol 
To prepare the structures for docking, each antibody-antigen complex was cleaned to remove all 
water molecules and ambiguous residues. The antibody heavy and light chain are named H and L, 
respectively and all antigen chains are combined into one chain object named A. This allows us to 
define the antibody heavy and light chain as one partner and the antigen chain as the other partner.  
Prior to docking both docking partners were optimized using the prepack protocol [26]. Running the 
prepack protocol ensures that all side chains have a low energy conformation which is essential for 
scoring the output structures.  
The prepacked structures were then used as an input to the RosettaDock protocol [26] and 500 
decoys where produced for each input structure. The two partners from the starting structure were 
then randomly moved apart using an incorporated function –dock_pert. As mentioned above another 
input to the RosettaDock protocol is the constraint file that allows us to add docking constraints to 
specific residues. The output of the docking protocol is a score file containing the score and RMSD 
from each structure and a metafile containing a PDB file for all generated decoys.  
==PAGE:30==
23 
 
In both protocols described above the docking partners were defined as the antibody heavy and light 
chain and the antigen chain, and additionally side-chain rotamers were added using the two build in 
options –ex1 and –ex2 as these are recommended as default values.  
3.3.4 Evaluation criteria for docking  
For each antibody-antigen structure the decoy with the lowest score was extracted from the metafile 
and both the lowest score and the equivalent root mean square deviation (RMSD) from this decoy 
was used to evaluate the docking. The RMSD is a measure of the average distance between all the Cα-
atoms in the decoy compared to the input structure and it is used to assess the similarity between the 
input structure and the decoy. The decoys are sorted by their Rosetta scores, ranging from highest to 
lowest. The decoy with the lowest score was considered as the best decoy.  
To further compare the results from the docking protocol with the refined Random Forest predictions, 
the PDB file of the decoy with the lowest score was extracted for each antibody-antigen complex. This 
PDB file was then used to define the predicted epitope residues from the docking. Here antigen 
residues within 4Å of any residue found in the redefined CDR were defined as predicted epitopes and 
antigen residues not in contact were defined as non-epitopes. The performance of the docking was 
then evaluated by using PPV and TPR for all residues predicted as epitopes for each antibody-antigen 
complex.

4 RESULTS 
Identifying B cell epitopes is difficult, since the location of the epitope depends on the antibody [10]. 
We have therefore developed a method to identify B-cell epitopes for a given antibody utilizing both 
antibody and antigen features. For this purpose two different machine learning programs were used. 
This study mainly focused on training a Neural Network while a parallel study focused on training a 
Random Forest. By comparing the methods it was found that the Random Forest model had the best 
performance and the Random Forest predictions were therefore used in further optimizations. In this 
study two different optimization methods were used both. The first optimization approach included 
structural smoothing of the predictions from the Random Forest and the second optimization method 
included a global antibody-antigen docking.

4.2 Pairwise prediction model  
A Neural Network algorithm was trained to identify B-cell epitopes utilizing both antigen and antibody 
features. The output form this Neural Network is a score between each of the residues found in the 
redefined CDR and each of the residues found in the antigen, and this score indicates how likely the 
interaction is to be an epitope-paratope pair. 
The performance of the model was evaluated using the area under the receiver operating 
characteristics curve (AUC), the AUC 10%, the positive predictive value (PPV) and the true positive 
rate (TPR). For each structure the performance was calculated and an average of these where used as 
the final performance of the model. To evaluate the performance of each antibody or antigen residue, 
only the highest of the predicted interaction was used. As shown in the analysis above the average 
epitope size was 17 and the average paratope size was 19. These values were therefore used as 
threshold in the PPV and TPR of the antibody and antigen predictions. When evaluating all 
interactions the PPV and TPR threshold was set to 50.

To investigate the influence of the different features we first analyzed the effect the number of

remaining four partitions were used to do a 4 fold cross-validation creating four models, using one 
partition as test set and the remaining three for training.  This 5 fold nested cross-validation resulted 
in 5 predictions, one for each of the 5 partitions. This setup has some degree of overfitting as the test 
set was used for both early stopping and feature selection, but this is unlikely to have a major effect 
as the feature space is small and the data set very large.

RSA and secondary structure window size does not increase the performance of the Neural Network.

4.6 Global protein-protein docking  
Antibody-antigen complexes have been shown to exhibit a high degree of shape complementarity at 
their interfacing surfaces [8][19][10]. In this method we therefore want to combine the 
conformational matching of the antibody-antigen structures with the prediction from the refined 
Random Forest to identify epitopes. We here present a global docking protocol where the predictions 
from the Random Forest are used to guide the antibody-antigen structures toward a better solution 
than those obtained from standard docking.     
For the docking to work there needs to be a correlation between the prediction from the refined 
Random Forest and the distance between the two residues to which the prediction belongs.  
Figure 22 shows a histogram over the average distance between all the interaction in the dataset and 
their corresponding prediction. The predictions were divided into a series of intervals according to 
their prediction values and the average of all distances in a specific interval was calculated. From this 
==PAGE:40==

plot we see a clear correlation between the prediction and the distance which indicates that residue 
pairs with a high prediction are also located close to each other in space.

When docking we want to include only the highest predictions since these are believed to contain a 
large percentage of the true interactions, but in order to include only the best predictions we have to 
select a threshold for which predictions to include.  
Figure 23 shows the distribution of the positive and negative predictions and we can use this to find a 
good threshold. In this histogram the red columns indicate the distribution of the true negative 
predictions and the gray columns indicate the distribution of the true positive predictions. We see 
that the true positive predictions are higher than the true negative predictions. From this plot we 
decided to set the threshold to 0.81 since almost all predictions above 0.81 are true positives. Using 
the top predictions from each structure it should therefore be possible to guide the docking of the 
antibody and the antigen structure toward the real solution of the complex and hopefully find the 
right location of the B-cell epitope.

The main goal of the docking procedure is to see if it is possible to improve our B-cell epitope 
predictions by performing global antibody-antigen docking using the pairwise predictions from the 
refined Random Forest method. As shown above the top prediction from each antibody-antigen 
structure holds a vast majority of the true paratope-epitope pairs and the distance between these top 
predictions are relatively low. The main idea in this docking protocol is to include the pairwise 
predictions in a constraint function. This constraint function penalizes a given conformation if it 
contains many antibody-antigen pairs which are close in space but has low interaction scores and if 
the conformation contains many pairs that are far apart and have high interaction scores (this 
constraint function is shown in Materials and Methods). 
As mentioned in the theory it is known that the antigen usually binds to residues found in the CDR.  
Using these antibody residues it is possible to define a score function that forces the antigen to bind 
only the CDR, thereby removing many solutions where the antigen binds to the frame work. It is 
relatively easy to identify the CDR region on any given antibody structure without any prior 
knowledge about binding and we therefore use the results from the protocol with the CDR as 
constraints and used this as a background to the protocol with the predictions from the refined 
Random Forest.  
For both docking protocols we generated 500 decoys for each antibody-antigen complex in the 
dataset, and out of these the decoy with the lowest score was used as the final output.  
Using each of these we can make a plot showing the RMSD between the native structure and the final 
output from the docking protocol using either the CDR or the predictions.         
 
 
Figure 24: RMSD as a function of the antibody-antigen complexes found in the dataset. Gray dots

indicate that the results are from the docking protocol using the predictions from the refined Random 
Forest.     
 
From Figure 24 we see that using the predictions from the refined Random Forest in general 
decreases the RMSD, indicating that these predictions guides the docking of the antibody and the 
antigen toward a better solution.

5 DISCUSSION 
Identifying B-cell epitopes is a difficult task mainly because virtually any residue on the antigen 
surface may be an epitope for a specific antibody [20][6]. Most existing methods use only the antigen 
to identify residues in the epitope, but using this approach is problematic since a specific epitope 
residue might be either a true negative or a false negative depending on the antibody. We therefore 
suggested that the epitope predictions should be antibody-specific and we successfully demonstrated 
that this leads to better epitope predictions.   
The presented docking protocol represents an initial platform which can be further improved by 
adding a step where all decoys are clustered to find the most representative decoy. Likewise adding a 
refinement step at the end of docking could also improve the docking result.  
When studying the structures obtained from docking, what is usually observed is that high scoring 
residues can be found in both ends of the antigen. When this is the case, our current method will try 
to optimize the solution by minimizing the distance to both of these two ends and the final antibody-
antigen solution will therefore place the antibody somewhere in between the two ends of the 
antigen. This might be avoided by introducing a final relaxation step where the predictions are no 
longer used as constraints, allowing the docking program to find the solution that minimizes the 
energy of the complex.   
When evaluating the docking results we use the PPV and the TPR for all residues predicted as 
epitopes, and this might not be the best way to evaluate the docking as it is hard to compare the 
docking results with other presented methods. The reason they cannot in principle be directly 
compared is that the two methods use different thresholds.  
For a better comparison one could recalculate the PPV and the TPR of all presented methods using 
only residues predicted as epitopes by the docking model. By doing this the different methods could 
be compared using the same approach to evaluate the performance. Another approach could be to 
use the best N decoys from the docking protocol to find how often a specific antigen residue is 
predicted as an epitope. For residues in the antibody-antigen structure we would then get N 
predictions and an average of these would be used as the final prediction for this specific residue. 
Using this approach we could then use the PPV17 and the TPR17.  
For future studies it would be interesting to compare the docking results with other antibody-specific 
docking protocols, like ClusPro [18] or EpiPred [42]. From this we hope to see that the docking 
protocol presented in this study will outperform the other methods.   
Lastly, our protocol might be improved by removing irrelevant interactions that our program is 
currently considering as epitopes. It has been shown that only 20% of the residues found to be in 
close contact with the antibody actually contribute to the binding and it might therefore be 
interesting to find a better way to identify the residues taking part in the antibody-antigen 
==PAGE:45==
38 
 
interaction. In this project we defined an antibody-antigen pair to be positive if the two residues are 
within 4Å of each other, but this might be too simplistic as it allows residues which do not actually 
contribute to the binding energy to be included in the positive antibody-antigen pairs. We therefore 
expect to be able to improve performance by using only specific interactions believed to contribute to 
binding, such as hydrophobic, hydrophilic and charged residues.

6 CONCLUSION 
 
We set out to develop a method for predicting B-cell epitopes utilizing both the antibody and the 
antigen. This was done using both Neural Network and Random Forest and we showed that the 
Random Forest outperformed the Neural Network with a 40% larger PPV50.  
We then showed that it is possible to further improve the performance of these initial Random Forest 
predictions by introducing a refinement step that removed the position specific background signal. It 
was shown that this refinement step improved the performance of the model, increasing the PPV17 
by 86%. 
Using these refined predictions we further improved the performance with a global docking protocol 
where the refined predictions were used to guide the docking of the antibody and the antigen. This 
showed further improvement compared to the refined Random Forest with a 23% increase in the 
performance. 
All three methods, the initial Random Forest, the refined Random Forest, and the docking, were all 
compared with DiscoTope 2.0, a state-of-the-art prediction tools for B-cell identification. We showed 
that both the refined Random Forest and the docking were significantly better than Discotope 2.0 
outperforming this with a 58% and a 95% better PPV performance, respectively. 
Moving forward, we hope to further improve the predictive power of the docking by introducing 
additional refinement and optimizations steps.
