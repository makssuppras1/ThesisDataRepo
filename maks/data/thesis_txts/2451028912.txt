A B S T R A C T
This thesis presents a study on the use of bidirectional scattering distribution functions
(BSDF) in daylight simulations, for later use in a comparison with practical measurements.
The objective of the project was to perform a sensitivity analysis on the impact of varying
BSDF resolutions on a range of daylight metrics, taking into account both luminance- and
illuminance-based simulations.
Based on ﬁndings in the literature and previous studies, shading systems representative for
the Danish market was sourced, an ofﬁce was modelled from the MIT reference ofﬁce and a
setup suitable for daylight simulations was prepared. Three daylight metrics, DA, cDA and
UDI were found in previous studies to be capable of comparing the resolution of BSDFs.
The tool genBSDF was used for generation of BSDFs of different resolution, and daylight
simulation methods using Radiance were created as basis for the daylight analyses.
A simulation study was performed on 7 shading systems for both annual and point-in-time
climate-based circumstances. Comparing real life measurements with simulations were
done in the ﬁeld measurements, where glare metrics were used for analysis of visual com-
fort in a small ofﬁce.
One of the main ﬁndings of the simulation study was the small effect varying BSDF resolu-
tion have on daylight metrics in annual climate-based simulations. The investigation on the
impact of varying BSDF resolution on point-in-time climate-based simulations showed that
there are noticeable differences at times when the solar angle is at a level that allow high
penetration of direct light, while the differences are minor at times when the penetration of
direct light of a shading system is at a low level.

1
P R O J E C T D E F I N I T I O N
Of the global ﬁnal energy consumption, 36% is consumed by buildings and the building
construction sectors, while they are responsible for 40% of the CO2 emissions, either di-
rect and indirect (GlobalABC, 2018). One of the main contributors of the total energy use
of a building, is the electrical lightning. It is estimated that the electricity for lightning ac-
counts for approximately 15% of the power consumption globally, and 5% of greenhouse
gas emissions (U4E, 2017). One way to reduce the energy use for electrical lightning would
be to let in as much daylight as possible. However, during the warmer periods of the
year, this would in return increase the energy use for cooling of the building due to the
increased solar radiation that will enter the building. Nonetheless, solar radiation is bene-
ﬁcial in the heating season, providing a free source of heating the building. Solar shading
devices are often used to block the direct sunlight and solar radiation in the cooling season,
while allowing it to penetrate the glazing in the heating season. A solar shading device can
achieve an optimal balance between the penetrative daylight and the thermal conditions
in the room, and thus a balance between the energy use for electrical lighting and cooling.
These complex fenestration systems (CFS) have a large potential of reducing energy use. In
US commercial and residential buildings alone, the energy use is expected to be reduced
with approximately 1.055 EJ/yr in 2030 (Farese et al., 2012).
The indoor climate and optical environment is not only deﬁned by the buildings energy use
and the amount of daylight entering the rooms. Designing settings that are comfortable and
healthy demands a focus on several parameters such as: the thermal, optical, visual and
acoustic environment. To reduce the use of artiﬁcial lighting in non-domestic buildings,
daylight have been found to be a very effective method. Not only does it improve the
buildings energy efﬁciency, it has also been found to be important for the people using
the building since the daylight makes users able to see both the tasks and the space well
(Christoffersen, 2000). In the Danish Building Regulation code (BR18) the requirements
that needs to be taken into account when designing with daylight in mind, is described:
2
==PAGE:18==
P R O J E C T D E F I N I T I O N
3
”Buildings must have light conditions that ensure that no risk will occur to the health,
safety and comfort of people. Sufﬁcient daylight and view of the surroundings must
be ensured as well as sufﬁcient electric lighting with due consideration of the use.”
(Statens Byggeforskningsinstitut, 2018)
To ensure that this criteria is met, the code further states ﬁve points for optimal utilization
of daylight in designing and planning of buildings (Statens Byggeforskningsinstitut, 2018).
1. Daylight must be utilised as a source of light to the extent it is possible.
2. Unnecessary energy consumption should be avoided.
3. Unnecessary transfer of heat to the rooms should be avoided.
4. Nuisance from direct sunlight can be avoided.
5. Blinding nuisance must be avoided.
However, reaching a holistic and optimal solution for both energy use and human comfort
that would include all the recommended points proves to be a great challenge in the design-
ing and planning of the building. More speciﬁcally, achieving points regarding the usage
of CFS that would guarantee a visually comfortable and glare-free environment while de-
livering maximum amount of daylight inside are unclear with fewer guidelines on how to
document or handle.
To achieve conditions that meets the points made in BR18, simulation software and tools can
be utilized to assist the decision making processes in building planning. Using such tools
buildings can be investigated via simulation software, and it is therefore of importance
that the simulation software can produce results that are as close to real life as possible.
CFS modelling methods are therefore becoming increasingly important to characterize the
directional transfer of solar and daylight ﬂux in the window systems (McNeil et al., 2013).
These modelling methods are under constant development to achieve the most accurate
results. In lightning simulations, the ﬂux transfer of light through a CFS can be described
by a bidirectional scattering distribution function (BSDF). The BSDF acts as a proxy for the
geometry of the CFS and uses pre-calculated data of the light’s interaction with the shading
system. One of the latest adoptions is the use of high-resolution variable BSDFs.

1.1 T H E S I S S TAT E M E N T

1.1
T H E S I S S TAT E M E N T
A large number of consulting companies and architects are specialized in advising cos-
tumers about daylight, optical environment and visual comfort on the basis of simulation
software and tools. The choice of software, and therefore the method, varies making it dif-
ﬁcult comparing and evaluating the results. Furthermore, the duration of simulations can
be very lengthy, slowing down the workﬂow and preventing better solutions to be tested,
making it very costly for both consultants and costumers.
There is no industry-standard concerning daylight modelling tools, constituting big differ-
ences in how the tools and applications are developed and what they are capable of. Newer
software, available on desktop computers and laptops, have made it possible to render
complex lighting scenes to look realistic, but without providing illuminance values. This
method can not be used to accurately predict lighting conditions and can lead to wrong
design decisions being made. The Unix software available to researchers, like Radiance de-
veloped by Greg Ward in 1985, can accurately predict illumination levels by tracing direct,
reﬂected light from their point of termination to the eye of the observer (Eastman, 2011).
Designing daylit spaces using this accurate reproduction of illumination levels, is the best
way of making informed decisions.
For the more realistic simulation software accurate data on geometry, materials and weather
is needed to predict accurate lighting situations. The better and higher resolution data used,
the more realistic the simulation is expected to be. The production and collecting of higher
resolution data can be time consuming and present itself as expensive and a challenge for
consultants. Resolution of the data and how it affects the simulation results, is thus crucial
to reach an optimal simulation setup for minimized computational time. With such infor-
mation at hand, accurate results can be obtained with higher precision that demonstrate the
realistic behavior of the building components.
1.2
A I M O F T H E S I S
Considering the importance of realistic simulation results with fewer undersecretaries for
building design and planning, this study sets out to investigate the effect of varying BSDF
resolutions on several daylight metrics used for evaluating illumination levels and visual
comfort, both on a annual basis and for speciﬁc hours of the year. The main objectives in
thesis will be:
What is the optimal resolution for different shading devices?
==PAGE:20==
1.2 A I M O F T H E S I S
5
How are different lighting metrics affected by the different resolutions?
How the existing glare metrics are affected by the resolutions?
How does the results compare to the real life measurements?
The study of this topic rises a number of questions that have been addressed in order to
reach the objectives of the project. These questions are chronologically as follow:
1. Which types of solar shading devices are the most used and relevant in the Danish

2. Which daylight metrics are relevant for comparison of the BSDF resolution and which
metrics can asses the glare in daylit spaces?
3. How are BSDF data generated and used in simulations of daylit spaces?
4. What simulation methods supports the use of BSDF data and how are they performed?
5. What is HDR imaging and how is it performed?
6. How can simulations on glare be compared to physical measurements done with HDR
imaging in real life settings?
These questions should be answered in order to complete an adequate solution to the main

2.1
S H A D I N G S Y S T E M S
The transparent parts of buildings are of huge importance regarding design and perfor-
mance. The role of an installed shading system is to improve thermal and visual comfort.
At the same time the shading system can affect the users’ visual contact with external sur-
roundings and make it difﬁcult to get information on the time and weather conditions (Tsan-
grassoulis, 2015). According to Kuhn (2017) shading systems or solar control systems can
be described by two multidimensional spaces; the design space and the evaluation space.
The design space relates to the design parameters such as colours, dimensions and surface
properties. The evaluation space relates to the performance of the system, i.e. if the design
of the system is able to satisfy the functional and aesthetic requirements.
2.1.1
Classes of shading systems
A light transmitting window technology that have integrated a non-transparent layer or a
layer with switching properties is said to be a complex fenestration system (CFS) (Bueno et al.,
2015). This could include insulating or constructional transparent elements, but is mainly
used to describe shading devices, such as blinds, roller shutters or electrochromic glazing.
The different shading systems have many various purposes and abilities, and an overview
of the principles behind the shading systems is needed.
9
==PAGE:25==
2.1 S H A D I N G S Y S T E M S
10
A method for dividing shading systems into classes is by looking at their angular selectivity.
This is the description of the systems’ ability to block direct radiation from the sky, whilst
letting the diffuse part of the radiation into the occupied space. Shading systems with a
high degree of angular selectivity count louvers, blinds and overhangs. However, a range
of shading systems have a reduced angular selectivy, meaning the performance is indepen-
dent from the sun’s angle of incidence (Tsangrassoulis, 2015). In this selection of systems
we ﬁnd screens, curtains and diffusing layers in the window glass.
Another classiﬁcation of shading systems is the placement of the device in regards to the
glazing. In this case three options are possible. An external installation, installation between
layers of glazing in the window or internal. Kuhn also introduces the placement or position
as a branch of design parameters in the design space. According to Tsangrassoulis (2015)
shading systems installed externally exhibit lower values of solar heat gain coefﬁcients than
systems installed internally. Shading systems most likely to be ﬁnd installed external are
louvers, blinds and perforated metal sheets (Tsangrassoulis, 2015).
Kischkoweit-Lopin (2002) presents an overview of shading systems using both diffuse radi-
ation and direct sunlight. It is established that venetian blinds are mostly found in temper-
ate climates on vertical windows, and fully available on the market. Venetian blinds con-
sists of slats, that come in a range of different designs, deﬁning the shading systems’ proper-
ties. Venetian blinds are therefore available in many different shapes and sizes (Kuhn, 2017).
It is also stated that perforated shading systems are found in temperate climates. They are
also fully available on the market, but comes with some dependencies. The glare protection
in these kinds of shading systems are highly dependent on the use or parameters of the sys-
tems. Mainini et al. (2014) states that their effectiveness to ﬁlter daylight and reduce solar
gains depends on their geometry, texture, and application.
Both shading systems have been investigated in previous studies on their properties’ effect
on daylight assessments and bi-directional measurements. A virtual venetian blind was
modelled and compared with a physical version in (Andersen et al., 2005). In the study
bi-directional transmittance distribution function (BTDF) measurements were performed
on a venetian blind with a goniophotometer, followed by a comparison with ray-tracing
results gathered using a virtual model of the venetian blind. A close agreement between the
simulations and the measurement was concluded and the BTDF assessment method was
validated. Also the expanded metal sheets have been used in studies about their spectral
optical properties. In (Mainini et al., 2014) metal mesh grids and perforated sheets are
investigated. BSDFs were created virtually for different types of metal mesh grids and solar
and light properties were evaluated on large samples using a spectrophotometer.

Luminance-based metrics
In DS/EN-12665:2018 (2018) the term visual comfort is deﬁned as: ”subjective condition
of visual well-being induced by the luminous environment”. It is characterized by the in-
teraction between the physiology of the human eye, the quantities and the distribution of
light (Carlucci et al., 2015). Adequate levels of light granting occupants the possibility to
accomplish their task would be a way of describing good visual comfort, but to further
investigate the subject, the daylight metrics presented in subsection 2.2.1 can be used to
check for lower or higher levels of light causing discomfort. The visual discomfort can be
described using the phenomenon, glare, presented in Rea et al. (2000) as: ”the sensation pro-
duced by luminance within the visual ﬁeld that is sufﬁciently greater than the luminance to
which the eyes are adapted to cause annoyance, discomfort or loss in visual performance
and visibility”. The term, glare, is divided into two types: one is the disability glare and
the other is discomfort glare. Disability glare occurs when there is a excessive amount of
light reaching the occupant’s eyes or a too wide range of luminance in a visual ﬁeld. When
this type of glare is present occupants experience an immediate reduction in their ability to
perform visual tasks (Osterhaus, 2005). Similar to disability glare, veiling glare also changes
the luminance contrast of the retinal image due to luminous reﬂections from surfaces that
changes the contrast of the task presented to the user. Discomfort glare is caused by the
excessive contrast between illuminated and dark parts of the visual ﬁeld (Carlucci et al.,
2015). Contrary to disability glare, discomfort glare may not be noticed by occupants when

2005).

3.3
H E M I S P H E R I C A L G E O M E T RY
A lightsource in daylight simulations is deﬁned as the sky. It is therefore necessary to pro-
duce the right sky to get results that are close or similar to conditions that could be expected
in a real situation. The sky is therefore recreated in the simulations to be as precise and close
to the one experienced by occupants in a daylit space. This section will explain the concept
of the representation of skies in daylight simulations, the luminance distribution of different
skytypes and how the division of a sky affects the radiation through the CFS.
The original Tregenza subdivision of the sky dome consists of 151 circular patches of iden-
tical shape and area that combined covers 70% of the total surface of the sky hemisphere.
The subdivision scheme was created as a scanning pattern for scanning luminance meters
to record the brightness of the sky (Tregenza, 1987). Another version of the sky subdivision
is also described by Tregenza (1987), in which the number of circular patches are reduced in
two bands, resulting in 145 patches. This provided 3-way symmetry but the total coverage
was reduced to 68%. The sky subdivision has since been developed further. Mardaljevic
(2000) proposed a continuous sky subdivision covering the whole hemisphere based on the
Tregenza subdivision scheme of 145 patches. The continuous subdivision consist of trape-
zoidal patches except the patch at zenith, which is a circular patch. The trapezoidal patches
are arranged so that their centers matches the centers of the circular patches of the Tregenza
sky. More accurate calculations can be acquired by discretizing the sky dome. Each patch
can be subdivided to create a ﬁner mesh for the sky, however, the drawback is a higher
simulation runtime.
Klems (1994a, 1994b) developed a method to calculate the overall transmittance matrix of
a CFS. It was developed to predict the solar heat gain of a CFS, however, the method is
applicable for predicting the daylight through a CFS as well. Beforehand, the solar heat gain
would be calculated using calorimetric measurements, but with the matrix layer calculation
method it could be calculated from a smaller set of measurements. Both hemispheres, in-
going and outgoing, are divided into a number of patches, which is called a basis. The
default Klems basis consist of 145 patches. in which there are 145 patches entering and 145
patches exiting. The hemisphere is divided into nine ranges deﬁned by an angular range θ
between the incidence plane and its normal. Each range is subdivided into varying number
of elements based on an angular range φ. The output from the ﬁrst layer of the CFS is used
as input to the following layer. This procedure repeats itself until the total radiation through
the CFS is computed.

Skytypes
The amount and types of light that a CFS is exposed to, depends on the position of the sun
and view of the sky. This is a factor that changes rapidly and various daylight situations
can therefore be experienced during the year. These situations ranges from overcast and
cloudy to complete clear skies. To create a universal basis for the luminance distribution
and a method for sky luminance calculations, the standard ISO 15469 was produced. This
standard deﬁnes the relative luminance at any point in the sky as symmetrical about the
solar meridian and as functions of the angular distance, Zs, between the sun and the zenith.
It also describes the relative luminance as smooth continuous functions that are depend-
ing on the angle, χ, between the sky element and the sun, and on the angle, Z, between
the sky element and the zenith. The two functions are: the relative scattering indicatrix,
f (χ), and the luminance gradation between horizon and zenith, ϕ(Z). The simplest sky
model, describes a sky with constant brightness. It is the CIE Standard Overcast Sky and

3.4.2
Fixed-resolution BSDF
The BSDF data is packed in a data ﬁle of the format of XML. It consists of basic information
of the window system, including name and manufacturer. Different attributes are described
in the XML ﬁle including wavelength (visible or solar), direction (transmission front, trans-
mission back, reﬂection front, reﬂection back), and ﬁnally the angle basis (full Klems, half
Klems, quarter Klems, Tensor Tree).
The scattering data is deﬁned as matrices for the front and back reﬂection and transmis-
sion. The most common angle basis is full Klems based on a BSDF resolution of 145 patches
which yields a 145x145 matrix or 21025 data values. The angle basis deﬁnes 145 incoming
and outgoing directions. The patches of the ingoing and outgoing hemispheres are num-
bered starting at the zenith of each hemisphere. The numbering continues for each band in
the direction of the horizon. The hemisphere is divided into 9 bands, each with a different
numbers of azimuth divisions, see Table 28. The patches have roughly the same projected
solid angle. The numbering of the ingoing and outgoing hemispheres makes use of a coordi-
nate axis reversal, meaning that the starting number (except 1) of each band in the outgoing
hemisphere is placed at 180◦along azimuth relative to the starting number of the incident
hemisphere.

3.4.3
Variable-resolution BSDF
The possibility to create tensor tree BSDFs was introduced in Radiance 4.1. A varying BSDF
resolution provide a higher resolution, where the distribution is non-uniform, or so called
peaks. Where the distribution is smooth, the resolution will be lower. The tensor tree BSDS
will thus only have a higher resolution where it matters. This is highly beneﬁcial since it
allows high resolution BSDFs but in a more efﬁcient way than the high resolution discretiza-
tion of the Klems angle basis.
3.5
M AT R I X B A S E D S I M U L AT I O N S
Daylight simulations in Radiance consist of individual steps that are combined into several
matrices. The ﬁnal results are found by matrix multiplication. Several methods for daylight
simulations can be applied. The common matrix for all methods is the S-matrix which is
the sky vector or matrix. This section will cover three simulation methods. These are:
• The Two-Phase Method (Daylight Coefﬁcient Method)
• The Three-Phase Method
==PAGE:46==
3.6 T H E T W O- P H A S E M E T H O D ( D AY L I G H T C O E F F I C I E N T S)
31
• The Five-Phase Method
Prior to the introduction of the methods, common matrices are introduced:
• View matrix, V: The relation of ﬂux transfer from sensor points or a view point to the
surface of the glazing or CFS.
• Transmission matrix, T: The relation of ﬂux transfer from ingoing to outgoing patches
of the CFS.
• Daylight matrix, D: The relation of ﬂux transfer from the surface of the glazing or CFS
to the sky.
3.6
T H E T W O- P H A S E M E T H O D ( D AY L I G H T C O E F F I C I E N T S)
The daylight coefﬁcient method calculates the daylight in a sensor point by the sky and
geometry of the scene. In short, this can be reduced to matrix format as:
E = CdcS
(20)
where:
Cds is the Daylight Coefﬁcient Matrix
S is the sky vector or matrix
The daylight coefﬁcient for a single sensor point is calculated by the contribution of each
patch in the discretized sky. Summation of these will result in the total illuminance in
the sensor point. Assuming a Tregenza sky of 145 patches, Cds is then a matrix with 145
values for each measurement point. The calculation is not dependent on whether a sky
vector (point-in-time) or sky matrix (annual) is used. The only difference is the resulting
illuminance ﬁle which will show the illuminance for each point for a single time step or for
the 8760 hours of the year.
The Two-Phase Method is most suitable for less geometrical complex scenes, mainly with
the glazing and solar shading in mind. For CFSs, the Three-Phase and Five-Phase Methods
are more suitable.
3.7
T H E T H R E E- P H A S E M E T H O D
The Three-Phase Method differs from the Two-Phase Method by a matrix, T, which is a pre-
calculated matrix describing the light scatter of the CFS, i.e. it is not calculated in the actual

erated by genBSDF. The Three-Phase Method is based on three independent phases that
can be expressed in matrices as:
E = VTDS
(21)
where:
V is the view matrix
T is the transmission matrix
D is the daylight matrix

Since the matrices are independent, the Three-Phase Method is suitable for parametric stud-
ies of different shading systems as V and D can be reused once they are calculated.
Common for the Two-Phase and Three-Phase Method is that the sun position is approxi-
mated over 3-4 patches of the discretized sky, which means that the sun size is overesti-
mated. The accuracy can be increased by increasing the sky discretization. Though it is
more accurate, the sun is still approximated over 3-4 patches, but since the patches reduces
in size with a higher discretization, the overestimation of the sun is smaller. Another ap-
proach to increase the accuracy of the direct sun contribution was developed. This method
is used in the Five-Phase Method.

T H E F I V E- P H A S E M E T H O D
The Five-Phase Method consists of the Three-Phase Method and two additional phases. The
equation of the Five-Phase Method is thus an extension of Equation 21 in which two phases
are added expressed as:
E = VTDS −VdTDdSd + CdsSsun
(22)
where:
V is the view matrix
T is the transmission matrix
D is the daylight matrix
S is the sky vector or matrix
Vd is the direct view matrix
Dd is the direct daylight matrix
Sd is the direct sky vector or matrix
Cds is the direct sun coefﬁcient matrix
Ssun is the direct sun vector or matrix
The subtracted part of the equation, VdTDdSd, is basically the direct contribution of the
Three-Phase Method, meaning that only the diffuse contribution of the Three-Phase Method
is used. The direct contribution is added as the last phase of the equation, CdsSsun. For better
accuracy of the direct contribution, the actual sun position is approximated to the nearest
sun location. The sun locations are created at the centre of each patch of the discretized
sky. The used sky should thus be of higher discretization to maximize the more accurate

approximation of the sun location, otherwise one might as well use the Three-Phase Method
since the calcuation of Cds is time consuming.

detailed components is presented in section 5.4.2 and 5.4.3.

After the shading system choices have been made, the next step is to create an input for the
BSDF generation. For the generation of BSDF data, the chosen shading systems have to be
modelled into geometry that can be handled by the program, genBSDF.
5.3.1
Geometry
For the modelling of the shading geometry, the CAD software Rhinoceros 3D (Rhino) was
used. Rhino creates geometry on the basis of the mathematical model, NURBS and the
mathematically presentation of curves and free from surfaces (Rhino3D, 2012). The visual
scripting language, Grasshopper, is an add-on that runs within Rhino. With the use of
open source components users can generate algorithms used for parametric modelling of
ex. structures or energy consumption.
The main advantage in using Rhino/Grasshopper for the geometry creation is that it can be
converted to a .rad ﬁle using a component from Honeybee[+]. Honeybee is a open source
Grasshopper component, using Radiance as a analysis engine for daylight calculations de-
veloped by Ladybug Tools LCC, founded by Mostapha Roudsari and Chris Mackey (La-
dybug Tools LCC, 2012). Together with the component, Ladybug, it is possible to do both
annual and time step daylight simulations, including daylight glare. Honeybee[+] is a fur-
ther development of the Honeybee platform which is built upon Radiance-based workﬂows
and methods (Roudsari, Mostapha, 2017).
Rotation of geometry
Since the geometry is used as input in genBSDF, it must be created according to the LBNL
WINDOW coordinate system, which differs from coordinate system in Radiance. WIN-
DOW’s coordinate system uses +Z as inside, i.e. the room, while +Y is up. The whole

may risk not being raytraced. To ensure that the geometry can be used as a valid input, it
can easily be translated and rotated in Grasshopper, if it is not already drawn according to
the WINDOW coordinate system. Alternatively, one can place the geometry correctly in Ra-
diance by using the xform command, however, Rhino/Grasshopper is more user friendly.

5.3.2
genBSDF
Once the .rad ﬁle with geometry and material properties is written in Grasshopper, it can
be used as input in genBSDF. Before running genBSDF, two Radiance programs, getbbox
and objview, can be used to validate that the .rad ﬁle is correct. Though they are optional
to use, they are free of computation time and are quick and easy to use.
getbbox
The program getbbox is a very simple tool that takes a .rad ﬁle as input, and retrieves the
minimum and maximum coordinates in x, y and z for the bounding box of the geometry
described in the .rad ﬁle. By calling getbbox with the .rad ﬁle written in Grasshopper,
zmin should be negative and zmax should be zero or negative. The x and y coordinates are
of less interest. If zmax goes beyond Z=0 the .rad ﬁle must be corrected with xform when
running genBSDF, or the geometry must be corrected in Rhino/Grasshopper.
objview
The program objview is used to render objects interactively. However, since few light
sources are added, the rendering time is very low. The program creates an octree of the
input .rad ﬁle, and calls the program rvu which is an interactive viewer. If the default view
is not satisfying, a different view can be set in the interactive viewer. This program is used
to check if the written ﬁle in Grasshopper is correct, i.e. that no geometry is missing.

5.4 D AY L I G H T S I M U L AT I O N S
50
Parameters
The parameters used in generation of the BSDF data are important to ensure symmetry in
the scattering distribution since the shading systems are symmetrical. Too few sample rays
will not give a proper representation of the system since the probability of randomness is
higher with fewer samples rays. All data sets are generated using the same parameters.
The common parameters are: -c 4000 -r ’-ab 7 -ad 5000’. Each shading system have unique
parameters that relate to the sampling area of the geometry.
Resolution
Unless modiﬁed, the standard Radiance distribution only supports the full Klems resolu-
tion. Discretization of the Klems angle basis can be made, though it requires modiﬁcation
of the code in the programs. No parameters or input is needed to specify the use of full
Klems resolution. Creating a tensor tree BSDF requires input, either speciﬁed as a rank 3 or
4 tensor tree followed by a number, n, denoting the maximum resolution of the tensor tree.
The resolution follows the formula 2nx2n. This means that n = 6 corresponds to a maxi-
mum resolution of 64x64 or 4096 regions. While increasing the number by one will double
the numbers multiplied by each other, an increase of one will quadruple the number of
regions, i.e. if n = 7 the maximum resolution is 128x128 or 16384 regions.
5.3.3
BSDFViewer
Visualization of the BSDF dataset can be performed in BDSFViewer, which is a tool for in-
teractive viewing of BSDF xml ﬁles. The user can choose an incident hemisphere patch and
see the outgoing distribution. Both transmission and reﬂection for the front and back is vi-
sualized, if the loaded ﬁle have such data. It is difﬁcult to say if the distribution of the BSDF
is correct without validating it against real life measurements, however, with BSDFViewer
it is possible to explore the distribution and judge if it makes sense. If somethings seems
odd, it is likely due to the parameters, geometry or materials used in genBSDF.

8.1
A N N U A L C L I M AT E- B A S E D S I M U L AT I O N S
In the following chapter, results from the annual based simulations will be presented. The
results have been visualized in both graphs and data charts from the Grasshopper compo-
nents, Ladybug and Honeybee. Furthermore the difference of the results gathered by using
the Klems, TT3, TT4 and TT5 BSDFs compared to the ones found by using TT6 BSDFs will
be presented by the use of the four errors of deviation: Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Mean
Bias Error (MBE), Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) and Normalized Root Mean Square Er-
ror (NRMSE).
The results found in the annual based simulations have been found to be very similar and
the visual representations of the results have therefore not been to much use and will not
be shown in this chapter. The visualized parts of the results is instead shown in Appendix
A.
The errors of deviation for one shading systems will be presented on a single page, with a
graph and table for each daylight metric. Finally, a comparison of the shading systems will
be included. This comparison is only done using MAE and NRMSE since these two mea-
surements shows the error in the same scale as the measured data and presents normalized
values facilitating comparisons respectivetly.

D I S C U S S I O N
10.1
W O R K F L O W
The change from an all Grasshopper/Ladybug/Honeybee based workﬂow to a workﬂow
based on Radiance could might have been avoided. A number of forums and mailing lists
were searched for answers to the problems, and though examples of successful Grasshopper-
workﬂows were found, no solutions to our speciﬁc problem could be located. Due to al-
ready performed research on Radiance and genBSDF, knowledge of Unix and the use of
bash-ﬁles was obtained and a change to a workﬂow based on daylight simulations done
using only Radiance was a possibility for gaining valuable knowledge and know-how. Fur-
thermore, the increased ﬂexibility and possibilities of using Radiance helped lowering the
simulation times due to the possibility to use more than one core and made it possible to
increase simulation parameters and reach a result with better accuracy.
The change of workﬂow late in the process challenged the structure and workﬂow of the
thesis and resulted in an increased workload that might have ensued a lowered number of
results. This change of workﬂow could have been decided earlier if other solutions wasn’t
thoughtfully examined ﬁrst. A faster decision would have improved the chances of limiting
the impact on the thesis.
10.2
S I M U L AT I O N S T U D Y
10.2.1
Climate-based annual simulations
The investigation of BSDF resolutions’ effect on daylight metrics indicated only small vari-
ations during the annual study. For all shading systems the highest error of deviation for
DA300 between TT6 and any other BSDF were found to be 0.76 (MAE), -0.62 (MBE), 0.98
(RMSE) and 0.04 (NRMSE). For cDA300, 0.53 (MAE), -0.43 (MBE), 0.69 (RMSE) and 0.01

Climate-based point-in-time simulations
For the climate-based results on February 6th at 12:00 the errors suggest that TT5 is closest

Visually, on February 6th at 12:00 the results are nearing that of TT6 for increasing BSDF
resolution. On November 27th at 11:00 the pattern is different. Here, the tensor tree BSDFs
are nearing TT6, while the results of the Klems BSDF show a very different result. Finally,
on December 29th at 12:00 the results appear more like TT6, but deep in the room TT3 show
more illuminance than TT6 while TT5 show less illuminance. The visual graphs might
indicate that the result of the BSDFs does not always follow the same ranking ordered by
furthest from TT6 to nearest TT6.
While the results are nearing those of TT6 for increasing BSDF resolution on February 6th at
12:00, we see that Klems and TT3 heavily overestimates the illuminance compared to TT6
for Shade 3, 4A and 4B. For Shade 1A, also taken at this hour of the year, Klems and TT3
seem to be more alike TT6 in illuminance levels. At this hour of the year the solar angle

To get a better understanding of the differences for each hour of the year, annual maps
showing the MAE were created. Looking at the annual MAE for the illuminance results
(Figures 8.24 through 8.30), the general trend is that the shading types that allow the most
penetration of direct daylight, also has higher values of MAE. This is best exempliﬁed by
shading systems 3, 4A and 4B, in which higher values of MAE occur at a signiﬁcant higher
frequency. This makes sense since the BSDFs are used to calculate the direct daylight in
the Five-Phase Method. It is thus not surprising that the errors are more visible the more
direct daylight penetration the shading system allow. Another trend is that for all shading
systems, the values of MAE of the different BSDFs compared to TT6 can be ranked in order
from highest to lowest; TT3, Klems, TT4, TT5. This ranking follows the order of the BSDF
resolution from lowest to highest.
The venetian blinds have a more distinct period of the year in which higher values of MAE
occur because of their cut-off angles and geometry compared to the mesh shading systems.
For the venetian blinds there are close to none hours in the months April through Septem-
ber in which the MAE values reach a signiﬁcant level. It is only for Shade 1A, the most
penetrative of the venetian blinds, where the MAE reaches values that are visible in the
annual plots in this period, however, this is mostly for TT3, and otherwise towards the end
of September. Shade 2A resembles 1A, the difference being that errors are less magniﬁed
for 2A. The angled venetian blinds both show lower values compared to those of their non-
angled variants which is to be expected. Shade 2A, with a cut-off angle as low as 13◦, show
very few instances of noticeable MAE values since the solar altitude goes below the cut-off
angle only for a limited period from end of November to mid of January. Summarized,
what we can gather from the annual maps of the MAE is that for shading systems with a
higher penetration of direct daylight, the errors will be larger. Furthermore, the BSDFs can
be ranked in order from highest to lowest (MAE); TT3, Klems, TT4, TT5.

C O N C L U S I O N
The investigation of shading systems present on the Danish market and in previous studies
established seven types of fenestration systems that would be suitable for tests and evalu-
ation of BSDF resolution and lighting metrics. Venetian blinds, expanded metal and micro
perforated metal sheets were used for generation of BSDF data and evaluation of three an-
nual daylight metrics.
For annual based daylight evaluation, shading systems with the largest surface area and
shading coefﬁcient showed the best ﬁt with the BSDF of highest resolution. This applies
to venetian blinds with smaller cut-off angles or/and larger dimensions, including retrolux
systems which directs the light towards the ceiling. The errors of deviation were nearly
identical for all BSDFs during the annual based simulations, while the generation time
showed great differences which suggest that using the Klems BSDF would be the best solu-
tion for combining resolution and time saving.
Findings from the comparison of lighting metrics showed that the use of various resolution
BSDFs had minimal to no effect on the daylight metrics, DA, cDA and UDI during annual
simulation studies. Thereby proving results from other studies describing BSDFs limited
effect on annual daylight metrics. The study of illuminance values from the point-in-time
simulations clearly demonstrated that in worst case scenarios TT5 was the best ﬁt to the
highest resolution, TT6. It was also noted during the comparison that the TT3 resolution
was furthest from a ﬁt with TT6. For the point-in-time study the BSDF resolutions can be
placed in order from worst to best, with TT3 being the worst ﬁt, Klems, TT4 and TT5 being
the best ﬁt.
The investigation of glare metrics revealed that at all measurement points the highest values
of DGP and UGR were found using the highest resolution BSDF, TT6. TT5 predominantly
resulted in the second highest values and the study demonstrated that higher resolution
BSDFs ensues higher values of glare metrics.
Although the number of comparisons between simulations and real life measurements were
limited, the study found nearly identical values of glare metrics with the highest difference
of DGP being 4% and for UGR 6.7%. The study also indicated that the difference between

with the difference being 2.5% at measurement point 2 and 1.75% at measurement point

With only three cases for comparison these have to be examined further for clariﬁcation.
It can be concluded that the resolution of BSDFs is not affecting the annual daylight sim-
ulation in such a way, that extra time should be spent generating higher resolution data
expecting more precise results. For point-in-time simulations, differences between BSDF
resolutions are quite distinctive and in cases where time allows it, the highest resolution
BSDF should be generated and applied. Simulation of visual comfort can be done with
reasonable correlation with real life measurement under circumstances with low levels of
direct light.

F U T U R E W O R K
For the purpose of comparing the results of the illuminance-based simulations on an equal
basis, the same parameters were chosen for all simulations. Mainly the ambient divisions
were of interest in the project, however, doubling the ambient divisions also doubles the
computation time. The TT6 BSDF deﬁned the minimum ambient divisions but it would be
interesting to see how a lowered number of ambient divisions for the simulations with the
other BSDFs would compare to the results of TT6. It has already been shown that there
are differences for the climate-based point-in-time simulations, which would likely be the
case of simulations with lowered ambient divisions, but the question is whether the annual
metrics would show the same tendency, i.e. the differences between the results of varying
BSDFs on annual metrics are insigniﬁcant.
To give a comprehensive evaluation of the comparison between real life measurements and
simulations using BSDFs, a greater number of measurements under different conditions
needs to be done. The difference in glare metrics when simulating for worst case scenarios
indicates a possibility of large differences between real life measurements and simulations.
Producing simulated HDR images using the recommended size of 800x800 would also be
interesting to implement in a comparison.

B
P R O J E C T E D T H E S I S W O R K F L O W
The following sections describes the projected workﬂow of the simulations.
B.1
D AY L I G H T S I M U L AT I O N S
The daylight simulations are made in Grasshopper. The Ladybug Tools components works
as a front end to Radiance. New developments allow application of the Five-Phase method
including BSDF support using Honeybee[+]. Within Honeybee[+] it is also possible to ex-
tract several daylight metrics, since these are made as components.
Since Grasshopper is a visual scripting language, the Radiance workﬂow can be set up
much like the workﬂow one would use in for example a bash script containing all the Ra-
diance commands. Every component in Grasshopper receives input and presents output
much like the Radiance programs. By combining these components it is possible to string
together a daylight simulation using Radiance as the engine.
Step 1: The geometry of the caseroom is split into its different parts allowing materials
properties to be applied. Each material is named and given a value of diffuse reﬂection.
Specularity and roughness are left at zero. Finally, the geometry and Radiance materials are
combined.
Step 2a: An epw weather ﬁle is loaded. It is converted to wea format by epw2wea. The
output is used to generate a skymatrix by gendaytmtx. The sky density is chosen by a
number n starting at 1. This is the number that follows -m in gendaymtx, deﬁning the
sky resolution following the Reinhart patch subdivision. The default option 1 yields the
Tregenza sky of 145 patches plus one patch for the ground.
Step 2b: There are no Radiance processes in this step. Based on the ﬂoor surface an analysis
grid is created. The analysis area is offset by 0.5 m, creating a excluded boundary layer
near the walls. The grid size and height is set. From the grid size, the analysis grid is
approximated, i.e. a size of 0.2 m will be not be exactly 0.2 m but likely within ± 0.02 m.
154
==PAGE:170==
B.1 D AY L I G H T S I M U L AT I O N S
155
Step 2c: A BSDF material is created by linking the desired BSDF xml. This material is then
applied to the surface of the glazing from step 1. The BSDF material and surface is turned
into a window group. Window groups are usually useful to get the contribution from each
window group. Since there is only one window in the caseroom, this is irrelevant.
Step 2d: The simulation method is chosen among point-in-time, daylight coefﬁcient, 3-
phase, and 5-phase. Radiance parameters for the view matrix and daylight matrix are typed
using the regular Radiance syntax.
Step 3: The output from step 2a, 2b, and 2d are used as input in step 3. It takes the input
and prepares it for the analysis in an analysis recipe. The analysis type can also be chosen
among illuminance, radiation, and luminance. The view matrix and daylight matrix can
be reused if earlier simulations have been run. This is highly beneﬁcial since it reduces the
run time by a large margin. For the purpose of comparing the run time from scratch, the
matrices will not be reused.
Step 4: This is where the simulation is started. It takes the analysis recipe from step 3 along
with the output from step 1 and 2c, i.e. the geometry, materials, and window groups. The
simulation ﬁles are saved to a folder by the users’ own choice.
Step 5: The output from step 4 is used to gather the data. Honeybee[+] provides compo-
nents for this purpose, making it possible to get point-in-time and annual daylight metrics
rather quickly. The data output can be speciﬁed by a speciﬁc day and time for point-in-time
or by a schedule for the annual daylight metrics.
Step 6: The results are visualised. The visualisation can be performed with a high level of
customisation. It takes the analysis mesh from step 2a and the desired results from step 5.
The legend and color gradient can be deﬁned by a gradient library or by custom colors. The
numerical values can also be overlaid on the colored mesh.
B.1.1
Simulation run time
In Grasshopper, the processing time for each component can be viewed. In this case, the
run time would be for the component runRadiance. However, this is the run time for the
component, and does not show the true run time of the Radiance related code. The compo-
nent run time is longer than that of the Radiance related code. Honeybee[+] writes a batch
ﬁle in runRadiance with all the Radiance code required to run the simulation. In order
to get the true run time, this component has been modiﬁed. After the batch ﬁle has been
written, it will be opened and certain code will be appended at speciﬁc places in the batch
ﬁle. The code are divided into two parts. One before the Radiance related code, in which
the start time will be saved, and one after the Radiace related code, in which the end time
will be saved. The start time, end time, and difference between the two, i.e. the run time in
==PAGE:171==
B.1 D AY L I G H T S I M U L AT I O N S
156
seconds, is saved to a ﬁle called runtime.txt.
echo Start time = %TIME% >> runtime.txt
set HH=%TIME:∼0,2%
set MM=%TIME:∼3,2%
set /a MM=%MM% * 60
set /a HH=%HH% * 3600

set /a start=%HH% + %MM% + %SS%
RADIANCE RELATED CODE
echo End time = %TIME% >> runtime.txt
set HH=%TIME:∼0,2%
set MM=%TIME:∼3,2%

set /a HH=%HH% * 3600

B A C K G R O U N D
Besides computional simulations of daylight and lighting, it is also possible to make man-
ual surveys of the lighting situation at a given moment. The approach used for the manual
measurements includes the concept of ’high dynamic range imaging’ (HDRI). This mea-
surement protocol will feature a description of HDRI, the materials needed for such a mea-
surement and how to obtain HDR images.
1.1
L D R A N D H D R I M A G I N G
In Figure 1 the luminance values that can be displayed on CRT and LDR monitors are
shown, compared to the luminance values visible to the human eye. By using HDRI, it is
possible to store and edit all colours and brightness levels in the visible spectrum.
Figure 1: Real-world luminance values compared to the values displayed on CRT and LDR
monitors.
160
==PAGE:176==
1.2 P H O TO G R A P H Y S E T T I N G S
2
The traditional imaging method called low dynamic range (LDR) makes use of the immedi-
ate conversion to JPEG/MPEG ﬁle formats, which are constrained by the restricted colour
gamut and small ranges of luminance and contrast captured. Pixels in the JPEG format are
represented in the YCrCb colour space by three integers between 0 and 256. In Figure 2 it is
shown that this colour space is limited in relation to the visual colour gamut.
Figure 2: The visual colour gamut compared to the YCrCb colour space.
The formats used for LDR imaging (JPEG, MPEG, etc..) are considered to be device-referred,
due to the coupling of their capabilities with the devices they are displayed on. HDRI is,
contrary to LDR, capturing the real photometric ranges of luminance or radiance values
found in the depicted scene. Thereby being device independent and being able to deliver
high-quality content only affected by the display options. This method of image represen-
tation is called a scene-referred representation.
1.2
P H O TO G R A P H Y S E T T I N G S
For the manual measurements the settings of the photographic lens and sensor have to be
adjusted or calibrated to store the correct representations of the colours in the visible range.
These settings includes the Aperture, Shutter speed and Exposure Value.
1.2.1
Aperture
In digital photography, aperture is the ability to control the amount of light reaching the
sensor. Changing the size of the aperture will expose the sensor for, either less or more
light, depending on whether the aperture is made smaller or larger.
161
==PAGE:177==
1.2 P H O TO G R A P H Y S E T T I N G S
3
To describe the aperture of the lens a number named the ’f-number’ or ’f-stops’ is used. This
number is the ratio of focal length to the effective aperture diameter. The lower the ’f-stop’,
the larger is the aperture opening allowing light into the sensor. A diagram of apertures
sizes and their corresponding ’f-stops’ can be seen in Figure 3.
Figure 3: A diagram of changing apertures or f-stops.
1.2.2
Shutter speed
Just as the aperture, shutter speed is also a setting deciding the amount of light reaching the
cameras sensor.
The shutter speed is the amount of time that the sensor is exposed to light. It opens the
aperture for the amount of time chosen in the camera settings. In Figure 4 different shutter
speeds are listed from slowest to fastest. This exposure time is proportional to the amount
of light that reaches the sensor.
Figure 4: Shutter speeds available on digital cameras.
162
==PAGE:178==
1.3 C A L I B R AT I O N
4
The values listed in Figure 3 and Figure 4 are all standardized in a 2:1 scale. This means
changing the aperture or shutter one step would either double or halve the amount of light
reaching the sensor.
1.2.3
Exposure value
The combination of aperture and shutter speed is represented by a number called the, ex-
posure value (EV). The scale in Figure 5 shows the exposure values used to indicate what
combinations yield the same exposure.
Figure 5: The scale of Exposure Values.
The EV scale is also the one used when building the HDR image. For each step on the EV
scale, a picture is taken for later use in the HDR software.
ISO
Besides the EV, another setting used for getting correctly exposed pictures is the ISO. ISO
is the sensitivity of the camera sensor. The lower the ISO the less sensitive the sensor is to
light. All settings on the camera is made at an ISO of 100.
1.3
C A L I B R AT I O N
The absolute calibration made by the manufactures should guarantee reliable measure-
ments of the luminance distribution in the ﬁeld of view. Using response functions the cam-
era can be calibrated by relating pixel values in the RGB scale (0-256) to radiance levels in
the scene in view (Wm−2sr1). The calibration should be derived from a scene with both dark
and bright areas, where the pixel values are no less than 20 and no higher than 200. These
response functions results in 3 response curves (Figure 6) that corresponds to each gamut in
the RGB scale. The resulting derivation from the response curves is a polynomial that can

==PAGE:179==
1.3 C A L I B R AT I O N
5
be used to calibrate the camera. For each camera these functions and resulting polynomials
are different and the calibration is individual.
Figure 6: Response curves for the RGB colour gamut.

the exposure value to each pixel, while the comand pfilt resizes the image to a resolution
lower than 800x800 pixels. After these steps the Evalglare tool is able to work with the HDR
image.
