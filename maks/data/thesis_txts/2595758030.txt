Abstract
With increasing shares of renewable energy sources in the European energy system there
is an acute need for accurate modelling approaches that can capture and describe the
spatiotemporal dependencies in large-scale wind generation.
This Master’s Thesis applies the reanalysis approach to model the variability in onshore
wind generation. The thesis presents a method for estimating the wake losses that occur
within a wind power plant using machine learning surrogates (ML). Two diﬀerent ML
surrogates are evaluated: a random forest regressor (RF) and an artiﬁcial neural network
(ANN). The best performing surrogate is implemented in Correlations in Renewable
Energy Sources (CorRES), a large-scale energy simulations tool. The wake loss implemen-
tation of CorRES (WL model) is validated on measured time series for several European
countries in the years 2015 and 2016. An in-depth analysis of Germany, the Netherlands
and Spain is conducted, while an evaluation of Denmark, France, Italy, Ireland, Portugal
and Sweden is conducted in brief.
The results show that WL model follows the measured data closely for Ireland, the
Netherlands, Portugal and Sweden. For these countries the simulated capacity factor
(CF) is with -3 to 7% of the measured CF. For Denmark, France, Germany and Italy
the relative diﬀerence between the measured and simulated CFs is 19-44%. Plotting
the residuals between the measured and simulated time series against wind speed and
turbulence intensity (TI) shows that the WL model is unbiased towards these variables
in Netherlands and Spain. For Germany the residuals show a clear correlation with both
wind speed and TI, indicating that the WL model captures the eﬀects of wind speed and
TI in some countries, but not all.
It is concluded that the ANN is superior to the RF at predicting wake losses. Through
large-scale energy simulations it is concluded that the WL model outperforms the baseline
model for all evaluated countries and years except for Sweden in 2016. It is also concluded
that for the WL model simulations the distributional shape, standard deviation and
90th percentile are closer to the measured TS than the baseline model for Germany, the
Netherlands and Spain.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The combined installed capacity of onshore and oﬀshore wind power plants (WPPs) in
Europe was 205 GW in 2019. In practice this resulted in 15% of the electricity demand
being covered by wind energy (Komusanac, Brindley, and Fraile, 2020). In 2030 onshore
wind energy alone is projected to cover up to 17 % of the total European electricity
demand, whereas wind and solar energy combined may supply up to 40% of the demand
(Dalla Riva, Hethey, and Vitina, 2017). Both wind and solar energy varies according to
environmental conditions, meaning that it is diﬃcult to predict the amount of energy
being produced from these sources. This poses a challenge for energy planners and for
the transmission system operators (TSOs) who dispatches the diﬀerent power plants so
the inﬂexible electricity demand is met. To improve the ability to operate the grid and
plan for future investments representative power system modelling tools are needed. The
transmission grids in the European countries are very interconnected. Therefore, it is
sensible to perform large-scale energy simulations of the variable electricity generation
for several or all European countries simultaneously.
One way to evaluate whether a large-scale energy system modelling tool is representative
of measured data is to compare the capacity factors (CFs) of the two. The CF indicates
how much of the installed capacity in an area has been utilized during a period. A
comparison of the simulated and measured values gives a rough indication of how well a
model is performing. Another indicator for large-scale energy simulations are temporal
and spatial correlations.
1.2
Motivation of the project
A popular approach for large-scale energy system modelling is reanalysis methods,
which can capture the temporal and spatial dependencies in large-scale wind generation
(Gonzalez-Aparicio et al., 2017; Staﬀell and Pfenninger, 2016). One reanalysis method is
the Weather Research and Forecasting model (WRF) (Skamarock et al., 2013). However
using reanalysis methods directly tend to result in overestimated CFs. To solve this
problem Ellmann, 2019 presented a method for downscaling the WRF wind speeds based
on the Global Wind Atlas (GWA) version 2 (GWA, 2020). The method proved to be
sensible and increased the overall performance of the modelling framework. However,
since the work of Ellmann, 2019 a newer version of GWA (version 3) has become
available. Dörenkämper et al., 2020 compares GWA version 3 to WRF and a large
range of historic measurements. Here it is concluded that at the varying heights (and

this the signiﬁcance of the downscaling method presented in Ellmann, 2019 is reduced.
However, the overestimation of CFs when using WRF directly persist. One possible

plants (WPPs).
1.3
Aim of the project
This project aims at developing a machine learning (ML) based wake loss prediction
surrogate, The surrogate is implemented in the large-scale energy system simulation
tool: Correlations in Renewable Energy Sources (CorRES) (Koivisto, Das, et al., 2019).
Two diﬀerent ML surrogates are tested: a random forest regressor (RF) and an artiﬁcial
neural network (ANN). The best performing surrogate is implemented in CorRES. Using
CorRES large-scale onshore wind energy simulations are performed on several European
countries and validated on measured data.
The learning objectives of the study are:
• Understand the input data needed to perform large-scale energy simulations of
WPPs in Europe
• Understand how to simulate and validate time series of onshore wind power pro-
duction with respect to country speciﬁc aggregated values
• Be familiar with the assumptions and general methodology needed to produce
WPP power curves with state of the art wake models
• Estimate country speciﬁc WPP power densities used to estimate WPP areas.
• Explain the assumptions and limitations of the used machine learning regression
models
1.4
Thesis structure
The thesis is structured as follows: Chapter 2 contains a literature review of diﬀerent
methods for large-scale energy simulations. The chapter also include a literature study on
wake modelling techniques and on diﬀerent machine learning algorithms. Both chapters
3 and 4 include a methodology, a result and a discussion section. Chapter 3 present the
development of the ML surrogates used for wake loss prediction and the validation of
these. Chapter 4 evaluates the wake loss implementation of CorRES with large-scale
energy simulations of a range of European countries. Overall conclusions are presented
in chapter 5.

CHAPTER 2
Literature review
2.1
Large-scale energy simulations
There are several approaches for modelling the energy generation from WPPs for large-
scale energy simulations. One way to distinguish the approaches is by the type of input
data used, in general three types are used: Mesoscale, synthetic and microscale data.
2.1.1
Mesoscale data
Mesoscale data used for large-scale energy simulations usually consists of reanalysis data
generated from historical observations. Multiple reanalysis products are available eg.:
MERRA, MERRA2 and ERA5. MERRA and MERRA2 are produced by the National
Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) and has a 50x50km spatial resolution
and comes with 1h temporal resolution (Bosilovich, Lucchesi, and Suarez, 2016). ERA5
is produced by the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF).
It has a spatial resolution of 30x30km and an hourly temporal resolution (Copernicus
Climate Change Service (C3S), 2017). MERRA, MERRA2 and ERA5 can be used as
input for the Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) model, which can downscale the
data to a ﬁner spatial resolution (for instance 10x10km) (Hahmann et al., 2010).
Nuño et al., 2018 uses the WRF model to downscale mesoscale data for simulating wind
and solar PV generation over Europe. This is done using aggregated power curves for
all WPPs within an area. The results show that the model was able to capture the
inter-annual and intra-annual variability in the power generation from WPPs. Marinelli
et al., 2015 also use WRF data but has a focus on simulating the correlation between
wind and solar PV generation. This is done using the same time series as input for both
wind and solar PV simulations, however a correlation factor of -23 % is obtained from
the simulated data as opposed to -13 % in the historic data.
Staﬀell and Pfenninger, 2016 proposes to use scaled MERRA data directly and introduces
a method to estimate the scaling parameters to match historic CFs. The method gives a
good ﬁt for country wise power production, but only one scaling parameter is used per
country for the entire time series. This makes the method diﬃcult to use for other spatial
and temporal resolutions. Furthermore, it is diﬃcult to asses the impact of an increased
share of variable energy in future scenarios because this increase might necessitate a
changed scaling parameter.
Koivisto, Maule, et al., 2018 uses WRF data to simulate large-scale wind generation.
To make up for missing technical information regarding the diﬀerent WPPs a linear

regression method is introduced. In order for the simulated wind generation to match
historic CFs the WRF inputs requires calibration. Plakas, 2019 proposes to use a random
forest algorithm to estimate unknown hub heights and wind turbine types, hereby building
on the work presented in Koivisto, Maule, et al., 2018.
2.1.2
Synthetic data
Synthetic data can be stochastically generated based on vector autoregressive methods
to match spatial and temporal autocorrelations and cross-correlations (Sørensen, Hansen,
and Rosas, 2002).
To make the short-term variability in wind speed more realistic Koivisto, Das, et al.,
2019 combines mesoscale data generated through WRF with synthetic data, allowing
intra-hour simulations. The paper demonstrates 3 simulation scenarios which beneﬁts
from having realistic intra-hour variability.
Ekström et al., 2017 uses a Monte Carlo-based vector autoregressive model to generate
time series of wind speeds and solar irradiance. The approach captures spatial and
temporal correlations over the simulated area. The generated time series is used to assess
the variability of diﬀerent shares in wind power and solar PV generation and shows good
correlation when compared to historic values.
In Sørensen, Hansen, and Rosas, 2002 a synthetic wind speed model is developed to
study the impact of WPPs on the power system that they are connected to. The results
are in good agreement with measured data. Sørensen, Cutululis, et al., 2007 presents
a wind power ﬂuctuation model which is used to assess the ramping characteristics of
a WPP at diﬀerent power levels as well as the need for reserves due to ﬂuctuations in
the generation. From the modelled ﬂuctuations it is concluded that WPPs leads to an
increased need for ramping and reserve capacities in the power system.
2.1.3
Microscale data
Microscale data may be obtained from local weather stations and can therefore consist of
data with a high temporal resolution. Local weather data is not necessarily measured at
the wanted height or exact location. Therefore, both horizontal and vertical extrapolations
may be needed, adding extra uncertainty on the data. Another application of microscale
data is for ﬂow modelling, where local site parameters are estimated to describe the wind
conditions. For instance the Global Wind Atlas (GWA, 2020) estimates Weibull A and k
parameters for binned wind directions.
González-Aparicio et al., 2017 introduces a method for combing mesoscale data with
microscale parameters. This is done by scaling the mesoscale data to match local Weibull
A and k parameters in GWA. This increases the short-term variability in wind speed
and matches better with historic production values compared to using mesoscale data by
itself.

2.2 Wake modelling

Kiviluoma et al., 2016 uses historical power production measurements from several WPPs

2.2
Wake modelling
The change in wind ﬂow that occurs in the wake of a wind turbine alters the ﬂow that
downwind turbines experience. This impacts the overall power production of a WPP
and aﬀects the loads on individual turbines. Diﬀerent wake modelling approaches can
be used based on the speciﬁc area of interest: Göçmen et al., 2016 gives a thorough
overview of diﬀerent wake models developed at DTU, while Porté-Agel, Bastankhah,
and Shamsoddin, 2020 gives a more general overview of diﬀerent wake models and their
perspectives.
The wake modelling performed in this work is done through PyWake where three wake
model are available: Jensen, Bastankhah Gaussian and FUGA (PyWake, 2020). These
three models are presented in the following along with the dynamic wake meandering
(DWM) model and computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) based wake models.

2.2.3
Fuga
In Søren, Berg, and M. B. Nielsen, 2011 Fuga is presented as a fast engineering tool based
on linearised RANS equations. Fuga constructs a velocity ﬁeld behind a turbine using a
system of look-up tables and then uses linear summation to evaluate multiple wake cases.
This makes the model computationally fast and gives robust results, especially for sites
with homogeneous terrain.
Ott and M. Nielsen, 2014 presents an updated version of Fuga. The updates include an
atmospheric stability parameter and the ability to evaluate several types of turbines at
the same time, which makes it possible to predict wake interaction between WPPs. The
report shows that Fuga is good at predicting annual energy production (AEP) values,
especially for oﬀshore WPPs.
2.2.4
Dynamic wake meandering model
In Keck et al., 2015 the DWM model is evaluated. The model consists of 3 components:
Meandering of the wake, rotor induced turbulence and velocity deﬁcit behind the rotor.
This causes the model to need a highly resolved turbulence ﬁeld for it to function well.
The DWM model shows good performance when tested on LES simulations and predicts
both power production and turbine loads.
Reinwardt et al., 2018 compares the performance of the DWM model to the Jensen
model for predicting wind speed and power production downstream of a wind turbine.
Here the DWM model is superior. The study also show that the loads predicted by the
DWM model are in good correspondence with the measured values.
2.2.5
Computational ﬂuid dynamics
Computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) are used extensively within wake modelling. Two
popular approaches are RANS and LES. In RANS all turbulence scales are parametrised.
In LES only the smallest scales need to be parametrised, while larger and more energetic
scales can be solved explicitly. An advantage of RANS compared to LES is that it can
produce useful results at lower computational costs. However, LES can yield very accurate
simulations of the turbulent ﬂow around a wind turbine or within a WPP. Sanderse,
Van Der Pijl, and Koren, 2011 and Cabezón, Migoya, and Crespo, 2011 presents reviews
of diﬀerent RANS techniques used for wake modelling, while Mehta et al., 2014 gives a
review of LES models used for WPP aerodynamics.
2.2.6
Discussion and further use
The wake modelling in this project is done for multiple WPPs at various environmental
conditions (eg.: wind speeds, wind directions and turbulence intensities). Therefore,
the used wake model needs to be both computationally eﬃcient and provide relatively
accurate results at diﬀerent terrain and conditions. The dynamic wake meandering
model along with the CFD based wake models are all computationally demanding. The

2.3
Machine learning
Machine learning (ML) can generally be seen as a collection of methods that study
pattern recognition in data. ML can be split in two categories: supervised learning and
unsupervised learning. The main diﬀerence being that for supervised learning the output
data is included in the training data, while in unsupervised learning the training data
does not include any information on the output data. Therefore, the goal of unsupervised
learning is to infer the structure present within the input data. Supervised learning can
be split into classiﬁcation and regression problems. In a classiﬁcation problem the aim
is typically to map the input data according to certain output labels. In a regression
problem the aim is to predict the value of a continuous output (Nelli, 2018).
Within the ﬁeld of supervised learning many models are available eg.: response surface
modelling, polynomial chaos expansion (PCE), kriging, random forest (RF) and artiﬁcial
neural networks (ANN). In the following a mostly philosophical introduction is given to
the latter two, which are the ML models used in this study. Moreover, a few use cases
for the two models are highlighted here, while Marugán et al., 2018 presents a survey of
uses for ML within wind energy systems. This involves power and load forecasting, fault
detection and control optimization.
2.3.1
Random forest
Decision trees are essentially the building blocks of a RF algorithm. It can be visualized
as a series of yes/no questions concerning the values of several input variables. Based on
the answers to the questions an output value is predicted. In a classiﬁcation problem the
output value will typically be an integer, while it can be any real number in a regression
problem. Figure 2.1 shows a sketch of a simple decision tree. Here the terminal nodes
represent the output value, while the input variables (c1 and c2) are evaluated at the
root and internal nodes. If a series of decision trees with diﬀerent questions is used, the
ﬁnal output will typically be the mean output of the trees (Berk, 2016).
When using decision trees in ML over-ﬁtting can be a problem. This occurs because the
decision trees are ﬂexible and can branch out indeﬁnitely to follow data very closely. If
this is the case the architecture of the tree will vary with the training data used, giving
it a high variance. If a decision tree is not allowed to branch out it can become inﬂexible,
which means that the tree tends to simplify the problem, giving it a high bias.
==PAGE:24==

Figure 2.1: Schematic of a simple decision tree (ﬁgure: Berk, 2016).
In RF two randomizing concepts are used to generate decision trees. The ﬁrst concept is
known as bagging (bootstrap aggregating) and was ﬁrst introduced in Breiman, 2001.
The basic idea is that each tree in a RF learns from a random subsample of data. This
reduces the overall variance of the forest, while maintaining the bias. The second concept
is known as random subset and was ﬁrst introduced in Ho, 1995. The idea of the random
subset method is that each node in the decision tree should only consider a certain
number of random input features. Combining the two concepts ensures that the decision
trees in a RF are trained on slightly diﬀerent sets of input data and input features.
Clifton, Kilcher, et al., 2013 uses RF to predict the power generation of a wind tur-
bine using 3 diﬀerent environmental inputs. The study ﬁnds that using RF results
in approximately three times more accurate predictions than using a power curve. In
Clifton, Daniels, and Lehning, 2013 RF is used to generate site speciﬁc power generation
predictions in a mountain pass. The study shows that a wind turbine operating in the
pass could have 10% higher power generation than the same turbine operating in a less
turbulent environment.
2.3.2
Artiﬁcial neural network
An ANN can be seen as a representation of the nervous system, which consists of neurons
that communicate with each other through certain connections. ANNs ﬁrst appeared in
the 1970’s, however due to the lack of computational power they did not prove useful then.
In recent years signiﬁcant progress has been made for ANNs as a result of the advances
within computer technology (Zaccone, 2017). In recent times, ANNs has proved useful
in the wind energy industry, especially for forecasting and fault detection applications
(Marugán et al., 2018).
ANNs are built on the idea of computational graphs. A computational graph consists of
nodes and directed connections. The node can correspond to an operation or a variable,

decides how the information is passed (Michelucci, 2018). Figure 2.2 show an example of
a computational graph where an input variable (xi) is used to generate an output variable

which returns the input value unchanged (f(z) = I(z) = z). However, diﬀerent activation
functions are available with sigmoid, hyperbolic tangent (tanh) and rectiﬁed linear unit
(relu) being some of the more popular ones (Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville, 2016).

In order for an ANN to learn a cost function must be deﬁned, which is minimized (or

is minimized (or maximized) by changing the weights and biases iteratively for each
neuron. Examples of cost functions are mean squared error (MSE) and mean absolute
error (MAE). To minimize the cost function an optimization algorithm must be chosen,
examples are ADAM, RMSprop and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). Each can be
given a learning rate indicating how much the change in weights and bias can vary per
iteration.

2.3.4
Validation
Validating the performance of a ML model is important to fully understand how well
it performs and to compare it against other models. Diﬀerent approaches are available,
with train/test split and k-fold cross-validation being some of the more popular ones (Liu
and Özsu, 2018). Train/test split is the most simple validation method. Here the data is
split into a training set and a test set. This enables the model to be tested on a set of
data that it has not seen before. If the training data is not representative of the test
data this method can lead to poor model performance. Instead a k-fold cross validation
can be used, where the model is trained and tested on k diﬀerent combinations of the
data as seen in ﬁgure 2.4. A clear advantage of this approach is that all data is used for
both training and validation, giving an indication of how robust the architecture of the
model is.

2.3.5
Limitations of machine learning
ML algorithms are very dependent on the amount and quality of data used for training.
Especially ANNs require large amounts of data to function optimally. Moreover, it is
important that the data is representative for the problem, and that it is not erroneous. In

well on out of sample data. Therefore, it is important to construct the training data
to represent all ranges of the used features. An alternative to ML is traditional linear
regression, which can be used for extrapolation.

CHAPTER 3
Wake modelling
This chapter aims at describing the process used to train and validate the ML surrogates
used for wake loss (WL) predictions. Section 3.1 presents the methods used, while section
3.2 contains the validation of the ML surrogates. Finally section 3.3 presents a discussion
on both methodology and results.
3.1
Methodology
This section presents the methodology used to develop, train and validate the ML
surrogates. Sections 3.1.1 and 3.1.2 presents the assumptions and methodology used
to create the training and validation datasets respectively. Section 3.1.3 presents the
methods behind the used thrust coeﬃcient (CT) and power curve predictor. Section
3.1.4 presents the methods used to build the ML surrogates and how the ﬁnal surrogate
architectures are chosen.
3.1.1
Design of experiment for generic WPP training set
To generate a generic WL model an experiment is created aiming to represent both
current and future WPPs. To describe the WPPs four parameters are chosen to generate
generic WPPs: rotor diameter (RD), turbine rated power (RPturbine), WPP rated power
(RPWPP) and WPP area (AWPP). Moreover, a distribution of wake expansion coeﬃcients
(kw) is also included to account for geographical diﬀerences in turbulence intensity. All
ﬁve parameters are chosen to follow either trapezoidal or uniform distributions:

Agency, 2016. The trapezoidal distribution chosen for RD ensures that the density of
samples is higher for the current plants than for future plants. The distribution of
RPturbine follows the rotor diameter since Arotor = π(RD/2)2, moreover the distribution

Using the distributions described above 1000 generic plants are sampled using the Latin
hypercube sampling method (Mckay, Beckman, and Conover, 2000). For each generic
WPP a circular layout is generated using a node peddling algorithm, which allows
the turbines to repel each other with a force proportional to the distance between the
neighbouring turbine, essentially maximising the spacing between the turbines (Hines,
2020). For each WPP 10 diﬀerent layouts are generated using diﬀerent seeds.
Having the features of each WPP it is necessary to estimate CT and power curves,
as described in section 3.1.3. When the CT and power curves are speciﬁed, the wake
losses are calculated for each WPP using the Basthankhah Gaussian wake model, as
implemented in PyWake (PyWake, 2020). This is done for 20 evenly distributed wind
directions and for wind speeds from 4-16 m/s with a 0.5 m/s increment, resulting in a
database of WL curves at diﬀerent layouts, wind speeds and wind directions. When the
database is complete the WL curves are averaged over the 20 wind directions and the 10
layouts, smoothing out the wind direction dependencies and minimizing the seed to seed
uncertainty in the layout generator. This results in a WL curve corresponding to wind
speeds from 4-16 m/s for each generic WPP. The WPP features (and wake expansion
coeﬃcients) and WL curves are then used as inputs and outputs respectively to train
two ML surrogates as described in section 3.1.4.
3.1.2
Design of WPP validation set
In order to evaluate the performance of the ML surrogates a validation dataset is created.
Here 500 random WPPs are drawn from a WPP database covering all WPPs in Europe
(The Wind Power, 2018). For each WPP RD, RPturbine and RPplant are extracted directly
from the database, while the plant areas are estimated using equation 3.4 and a wake
expansion coeﬃcient is sampled using equation 3.5. Having extracted the WPP features
a WL curve is computed for each WPP following the method described in section 3.1.1.
The training and validation sets are presented in section 3.2.1 along with the WPP
database.

3.1.4.1
Hyper parameter tuning
To ﬁnd the best performing architectures of the two ML surrogates a hyper parameter
tuning is conducted. For the tuning process a basic grid searching algorithm is imple-
mented. Because the grid search only evaluates a number of predeﬁned architectural
combinations it is limited in it’s search domain and can be computationally expensive.
Therefore the grid search is supplemented with a hyper parameter optimization algorithm
as implemented in KerasTuner (Keras Tuner, 2020). The optimizer searches within a
range of predeﬁned variables to ﬁnd the best conﬁguration. This allows for an expanded
search domain and increased ﬂexibility. For both grid search and optimizer a 10-fold
cross validation is performed on the training data described in section 3.1.1.
For the ANN diﬀerent loss and optimizer functions were tested. However, mean absolute
error and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) proved to be superior. For the SGD an
exponentially decaying learning rate was used. To ﬁnd the best performing architecture
of the ANN various number of dense layers and number of neurons in the layers were
tested. Moreover diﬀerent numbers of dropout layers were tested along with individual
dropout rates and diﬀerent batch sizes. For all dense layers a relu activation was used
since this was performing the best. A set seed is used within the tuning process to
ensure an equal starting point for all architectures. For the hyper parameter tuning 100
epochs are used to train the ANN within each fold. Having found the best performing
architecture this is trained for 1000 epochs.

Discussion

3.3.1
CT and power curve predictor
In the database of CT and power curves there is only 3 turbines with a rated power >
7 MW present. When these turbines are in the test fold during the 10-fold validation
process it is diﬃcult for the model to make an accurate prediction of the CT and power
curves for these turbines. This is seen in ﬁgure 3.4(d), where the predicted power is
often too high at high wind speeds. However, the model that is used for further work is
trained on all turbines, meaning that the model should be able to make more accurate
predictions for powers above 7 MW.
The largest turbine in the CT and power curve database has a rated power of 12MW,
which is lower than the turbines included in the training data This can cause the model
to not provide accurate predictions for these turbines. Even so, it was not possible to
include any larger turbines in the database as they are purely theoretical at this time.
The PCA may help smooth the predicted CT and power curves, so that they are at least
representable in shape.
3.3.2
WL predictions
3.3.2.1
Hyperparameter tuning
Figures 3.5 and 3.6 highlight the limitations of both hyper parameter tuning methods.
When using the grid search relatively few hyper parameters are evaluated, meaning that
the best architecture may not be found. On the other hand, the optimizer, especially for
the ANN surrogate, does not manage to ﬁnd the global optimum at every run. Therefore,
the optimizer still need numerous trials in order to ﬁnd the best architecture, causing it
to be computationally expensive. For the ANN the optimized architectures are slightly
better than the grid search, however for the RF surrogate the optimized architectures
seem to perform as well as the grid searched architectures.
When comparing the range of the standard deviations of the two surrogates it is clear
that the RF has less variation, while the ANN is more aﬀected by the architecture and
has more variation. This indicates that relatively good performance can be provided by
the RF surrogate if it is tuned with a small range of variables, while a more thorough
tuning process is needed for the ANN.
3.3.2.2
Performance of the chosen architectures
Comparing the performances of the RF and ANN surrogates, it is obvious that the ANN
is performing better. Especially when looking at the overall standard deviation and

Within the scope of large energy simulations entire countries (or regions) may be modelled

If the standard deviation of the residuals is directly interpreted as the uncertainty of
the WL curve predictions, then there is an uncertainty of 1.6% (for the ANN surrogate).
The surrogate is implemented in a large-scale energy simulation tool, which is associated
with diﬀerent uncertainties, eg.: the exact coordinates of 54 % of the WPPs are known,
while the rest are approximated (The Wind Power, 2018). Other examples of uncertain
inputs are: hub height and turbine model, which are also estimated in some cases. In
this context an additional uncertainty of 1.6% may not have a signiﬁcant impact on the
overall uncertainty of the large-scale energy simulations.
An uncertainty of 1.6% indicates how good the surrogate is at replicating the validation
data. But, the validation data is also uncertain. For instance the predicted CT and
power curves are uncertain. Moreover, the WL curves in the validation data are based
on a wake model so these are also uncertain. Therefore, an uncertainty of 1.6% does not
necessarily represent the full uncertainty of the surrogate model.

CHAPTER 4
Large-scale energy simulations
While chapter 3 describes how a ML based generic wake loss predictor is trained and
validated, this chapter aims at describing how the predictor is implemented and used
in large-scale energy simulations. Moreover, the eﬀect of the WL predictor is validated
with regards to historical data for several European countries.
4.1
CorRES outline
Correlations in Renewable Energy Sources (CorRES) is a simulation tool developed
at DTU Wind Energy. It is capable of simulating historic and future scenarios for
both wind and solar energy generation (Koivisto, Das, et al., 2019). CorRES uses a
combination of WRF reanalysis data and stochastic ﬂuctuation simulations to represent
temporal dependencies in the energy generation. The WRF data consists of an hourly
meteorological time series (TS) on a 10 km × 10 km grid.
Based on the WRF data CorRES simulates a TS for every location of interest by
interpolating horizontally and vertically to the exact location and height of interest
(eg. WPP coordinates and hub height). Having simulated a TS for each location the
power generation is computed. In the case of WPPs this is done by interpolating the
simulated wind speeds to a power curve matching the turbine in the WPP. If wake losses
are included the WL predictor (from chapter 3) is used to generate a WL factor curve
corresponding to wind speeds from 4-16 m/s, which is then multiplied with the WPP
power curve.
4.2
Methodology
This section aims at describing the methodology used to evaluate the WL predictors
eﬀect in CorRES. In section 4.2.1 a method used to aggregate WPPs based on the
inter-distances is presented, while section 4.2.2 presents the data used to validate the
CorRES simulations and the process behind it.
4.2.1
WPP aggregation
The wake of a WPP inﬂuences the power generation of nearby WPPs. Therefore, it makes
sense to model such interacting WPPs together. In CorRES all WPPs are modelled
individually, meaning that the wake losses between interacting WPPs are not considered.
==PAGE:46==
32
4 Large-scale energy simulations
To ensure that the wake losses between interacting WPPs are included in the simulation,
the WPP database is aggregated.
The aggregation is done using an agglomerative clustering algorithm implemented in
scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011). The algorithm evaluates the distances between all
the WPPs and aggregates them if they are within a chosen threshold. When more than
two WPPs are within the threshold the mean of the distances is used. If the clustered
WPPs do not share turbine model, the most occurring turbine model weighted by the
installed capacity is chosen to represent the aggregated WPP, while an average weighted
by the installed capacity is used for hub height, rotor diameter and turbine rated power.
In the used WPP database (The Wind Power, 2018) one set of coordinates is available
for each WPP. Therefore, for a WPP with multiple turbines the actual location of the
outermost turbine is unknown. In practice the outermost turbines can be close to a
nearby WPP even though the coordinates of the two WPPs are not close. To ensure
that wake losses between such WPPs are modelled, WPPs within a distance of 2km are
aggregated.
4.2.2
Validation against measured data
The WL predictor implemented in CorRES uses diﬀerent WPP features and a wake
expansion coeﬃcient to predict a WL curve. While the WPP features are based on
the WPP database, the wake expansion coeﬃcient is estimated as follows: the yearly
mean turbulence intensity (TI) is computed at each WPP location using equation 2.5
(as suggested by Peña, Réthoré, and Laan, 2016). From the mean TI a WPP speciﬁc
wake expansion coeﬃcient is computed through the linear relation in equation 2.12 (as
reported in Niayifar and Porté-Agel, 2016).
To evaluate the performance of the WL implementation in CorRES (WL model) the
simulations are compared to measured TS of the onshore wind production for several
European countries. For the Netherlands the measured data is acquired from ENTSO-E,
2020 while the data for the remaining countries is from Open Power System Data, 2019.
For Germany grid curtailment is included as a ratio between the total wind generation
and the total wind curtailment, as reported in Joos and Staﬀell, 2018. The total installed
capacities are acquired from IRENA, 2020, while yearly total production numbers are
from IRENA, 2019. Within this work all the TS are normalized by the total installed
capacity, corresponding to the speciﬁc countries and years.
The WL model is used to simulate a TS of onshore wind production for 2015 and 2016.
A similar CorRES simulation without including wake losses (baseline) is also performed.
Within each year the WPPs that have a commissioning date later than January 1 are
excluded in the simulation. WPPs without a commissioning date are also excluded. The
missing values from the historic TS are replicated in the simulated TS from CorRES
while a WPP unavailability of 3% is assumed. Both CorRES simulations are compared
to measured hourly TS of the respective years

4.4
Discussion
This section aims at discussing the presented methods and results.
Here diﬀerent
subsections are presented, each containing a discussion on a particular subject. Section
4.4.1 discuss the used aggregation method, while section 4.4.2 discuss the measured data
along with the WPP database and the WPP area estimation. Section 4.4.3 discuss the
used WRF data. Section 4.4.4 discuss correlations between WPP features aggregated
per country and the performance of the WL model. Section 4.4.5 presents a discussion
on the dependencies of the WL model.
4.4.1
WPP aggregation
For a WPP consisting of a small number of turbines the used threshold distance of 2
km may be unreasonable. Depending on the size of the turbines and the atmospheric
conditions the wake can have dissipated within the 2 km. Another option is that wake
from the small WPP will only inﬂuence the WPPs at a relatively narrow bin of wind
directions. However, the current methodology does not distinguish by the number or size
of the turbines.
For each WPP only one set of coordinates are available which can be anywhere within
the WPP. In a WPP with several turbines the outermost turbine can be relatively far
from the provided coordinates. The outermost turbines can therefore be closer than 2 km
to a neighbouring WPP, in which case the wakes of the WPPs are deﬁnitely interacting,
in which case the current aggregation is sensible.
A relatively simple addition to the current method is to weigh the distances between
multiple WPPs by the installed capacities. This would ensure that the centre of the
aggregated WPP corresponds to the centre of the wake generation. Currently a simple
mean is used, meaning that the capacity of each WPP (and the signiﬁcance of its wake)
is not included. Because the current aggregation method does not distinguish by the
installed capacity of each WPP it may have aggregated too many WPPs. In this case
the WL predictions will be too high for some of the aggregated WPPs.
Ideally a more advanced aggregation method should be used to ensure that the WPP
aggregations are valid for all sizes of WPPs. This could for instance be based on the
estimated WPP areas. Using the radius of the WPP area a wake inﬂuence distance of 4-5
WPP radii could be assumed. If the wake inﬂuence distances intersect then the WPPs
should be aggregated. A more advanced aggregation method was not implemented in
this work due to time constraints, but should be implemented at a future stage.
4.4.2
Measured data
While the WL model enables an accurate TS simulation of some countries (Ireland, the
Netherlands, Portugal and Spain), this is not the case for all countries. For Denmark,
France and Germany the simulated CFs are 23-49% higher than the measured CFs.

Even though the measured TS of Germany for the two years generally seem low, it
matches well with the total yearly production values. This is also the case for the TS of
Denmark and France. So the measured TS being wrong does not provide an explanation
for the overestimation of CFs for Denmark, France and Germany.
Looking at the installed capacities reported by IRENA and included in CorRES (table
4.3), there are relatively large diﬀerences for many of the simulated countries. While
some WPPs are simply not in the database others are excluded because of eg. a missing
commissioning year. Especially for the Netherlands, Spain and Sweden large diﬀerences
in the installed capacities were found. At this stage it not known whether the missing
data is structural or random. If the missing data is randomly distributed amongst the
WPPs the impact on the simulations may not be signiﬁcant. However, if the missing
data is structurally distributed within a country and eg. an entire region is missing, this
could impact the simulations very signiﬁcantly.
Plakas, 2019 presents a method for estimating missing WPP features. For future research
a similar method should be implemented to estimate the missing values in the database.
This would decrease the relative diﬀerences between the installed capacities.
4.4.2.1
Estimating WPP areas
The estimation of the WPP areas should ideally be based on actual values reported in
literature. However, this was not feasible for this project.
Portugal and Sweden are the only countries where ρ = 10MW/km2 gives the best ﬁt to
the measured CF. This may be because of the high roughness in these countries which
make the wake dissolve faster. But it is also a possibility that the turbines are actually
spaced further apart in Portugal and Sweden (compared to other countries), in which
case using ρ = 10MW/km2 is sensible.
For the countries where the WL model is overestimating the CF it is tempting to increase
ρ. However, for a WPP consisting of Vestas V90 2MW turbines with a square layout
the turbine spacing is approximately 3 rotor diameters (assuming ρ = 60MW/km2).
In practice when two small WPPs are located within the 2km threshold distance and
aggregated they are therefore modelled as if they are spaced 3 rotor diameters apart.
This may not be sensible, while increasing ρ further would only worsen this problem.
4.4.3
Scaling with GWA
In Ellmann, 2019 a method for downscaling WRF wind speeds using GWA version 2
is presented. Here it is concluded that on average the WRF wind speeds to be scaled
more at a height of 50m than at a height of 100m. However, Dörenkämper et al., 2020
presents a comparison of WRF to GWA version 3 and to a wide range a measurement
masts. Here it is shown that at the varying heights (and locations) of the measurement
masts WRF is more unbiased than GWA. This reduces the argument of scaling wind
speeds based on GWA.
==PAGE:62==
48

The TS generated through WRF is supplied at a relatively coarse resolution of 10km ×
10km, causing the roughness of the landscape to be averaged within each block. At low
hub heights the wind speed is very dependant on the roughness of the landscape, while
this dependency becomes less pronounced as the hub height increases. That the wind
speeds are more inﬂuenced by the roughness at low heights can explain why Ellmann, 2019
is scaling the wind speeds at 50m more than those at 100m. However, from Dörenkämper
et al., 2020 it is not clear if this tendency is present when comparing the GWA version
3 to WRF at a height of 50m. If the wind speeds generated through WRF needs to
be scaled at a height of 50m this could explain why the simulated CFs are too high in
Denmark, where the weighted hub height is 60m.
For future research a method similar to Ellmann, 2019 should be applied to assess if
there is a basis for downscaling the WRF wind speeds using GWA version 3.

4.4.5
Residuals
Results show that the wind speed and TI dependencies in the WL Model are working
well in the Netherlands and Spain. However, the WL model is not performing well in
Denmark, Germany and France and the reason for this is unclear. One explanation is
that the WL model would beneﬁt from more dependencies. To assess this ﬁgure 4.9 show
the residuals between the measured and simulated TS against the inverse Obukhov length
(stability) averaged by the installed capacity. The ﬁgure includes Denmark, Germany,
France, the Netherlands and Spain.
In ﬁgure 4.9(a) the residuals of Denmark are shown against the stability. Here there is a
tendency for higher residuals at neutral conditions (±0.02), moreover the residuals also
tend to be high at stable conditions (> 0.02), where wake losses are generally higher.
Figure 4.9(b) show the residuals of France against stability. Here the residuals are centred
around 0 at unstable conditions (< −0.02), while they deviate from 0 at neutral and
stable conditions. This tendency is also apparent in ﬁgure 4.9(c) showing the residuals of
Germany against the stability, although the increase in the residuals at neutral conditions
is more evident for Germany.
Figure 4.9(d) show the residuals of the Netherlands as a function of the stability. The
mean of the residuals seem to be uncorrelated with the stability, however there is a
larger spread in the residuals at neutral conditions. This spread could be due to the
large variations in TI at neutral conditions. Because the current WL model assumes an
averaged TI over the year, these variations are not captured.
Figure 4.9(e) show the residuals of Spain against the stability. For Spain there is a small
increase in the residuals at neutral conditions.
There is a clear correlation between wake losses and stability that is not captured with
the current WL model. Moreover, based on ﬁgure 4.9 it seems like the current WL model
is not capturing the temporal variations in TI. For future research both stability and TI
should be included in the WL model and modelled as a TS. This should decrease the
spread of the residuals at neutral conditions and reduce the model overestimation at
stable conditions.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
5.1
Wake modelling
Two machine learning (ML) surrogates are developed to predict wake losses as a function
of diﬀerent wind power plant (WPP) features and a turbulence intensity (TI) dependant
wake expansion coeﬃcient. The surrogates, an artiﬁcial neural network (ANN) and a
random forest regressor (RF), are validated on a sample of current European WPPs.
The validation of the surrogates show that the ANN is signiﬁcantly better at predicting
wake loss (WL) curves than the RF. The standard deviation of the residuals between the
actual and predicted values show a correlation with both turbine rated power and rotor
diameter. There is also a clear correlation between the standard deviation and the wake
expansion coeﬃcient, while the performance of the surrogates are uncorrelated with the
number of turbines in the WPP.
5.2
Large-scale energy simulations
The ML surrogate is implemented in Correlations in Renewable Energy Sources (CorRES)
(Koivisto, Das, et al., 2019) and validated on onshore wind generation time series of
several European countries for 2015 and 2016. For the validation CorRES including wake
losses (WL model) is also compared to CorRES without wake losses (baseline model).
It is concluded that WL model provides better results than the baseline model for all
the evaluated countries and years except Sweden in 2016. For Ireland, the Netherlands,
Portugal and Spain the WL model replicates the measured CF within a relative diﬀerence
of −3 to 7%, while the baseline model has a relative diﬀerence of 6 −25% for the same
countries. For Denmark, Germany, France and Italy the relative diﬀerences in the CFs
are 19 −44% with the WL model, compared to 40 −71% with the baseline model. It
is also shown that the distributional shape, standard deviation and 90th percentile of
the WL model are closer to the measured TS than the baseline model for Germany, the
Netherlands and Spain.
The residuals between the measured and simulated TS plotted against wind speed and
turbulence intensity (TI) show that the WL model is able to capture the variation in
wake losses that happens as a function of the wind speed and TI in Netherlands and
Spain. However, the residuals of Germany plotted against wind speed and TI show that
the WL model does not capture the variations in wake losses here.
