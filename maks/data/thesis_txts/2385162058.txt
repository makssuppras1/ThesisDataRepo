Chapter 1
Introduction
In the current society data is everywhere and is begin collecting in rapidly in-
creasing rates. Most of the data is never used and when it used it is often used
dierently then originally intended. In this thesis I will develop predictive ma-
chine learning models for detecting companies with high probability of needing
correcting in their periodical income and expenses reports based on aggregate
data provided by the Danish tax authorities.
1.1
The problem
The problem that the Danish tax authorities face is that the receive self re-
ported income and expense reports from Danish companies and based on this
they have to either decide to take a company out for manual control or to accept
the income and expense report and pay or charge the dierence in the tax the
such pay and the tax the actuarially paid. Due to budget constraints taking all
companies out for control is not possible. So a method of during better selec-
tion is very valuable. Both choosing companies with the highest probability of
errors is important as well as choosing the companies with the highest expected
adjustments. In this thesis I will focus at hitting the highest accuracy given it
was the problem I was presented with.

Chapter 2
Predictive machine learning
Predictive machine learning is the discipline of using known data to estimate the
outcome of unknown or not realized variable(s) using machine learning models.
In the following chapter I will introduce this paradime, by introducing the
modeling culture, how to evaluate a model, the bias/variance trade-o and
approaches to solve it. Either Bayesian or by using multiple models and en-
sembling. Finally based on this I will discribe the model testing and evaluation
framework I will use for this problem based on these principals.
2.1
Algorithmic modeling
Algorithmic modeling dened in Leo Briemans article "statistical modeling -
two cultures" is the approach where evaluation is based on generalization er-
ror. This is in contrast to the standard statistical framework (data modeling
culture) where model evaluation is evaluated on goodness-of-t and residuals
being Gausian. In the standard statistical framework models are highly speci-
ed and the entirety of the data generating process parametrized typically with
a low number of parameters. These are white-box approaches seeking to nd
the instance of the prestated model that ts the data the best. The typical
questions answered is: "what paramaters are signicant in there contribution

2.2
The testing framework
In predictive machine learning the underlying the goal is to be able to generalize
to unseen data. In order to test the models capabilities to do this I use a training,
validation and test framework. With the following methodology:
• Split the data into a 80 pct. training data and 20 pct.1 testing.
• Split the training data into two evenly sized folds and train a model on
each of these and generate validation prediction on the fold not being
trained on. Repeat 3 times.
• For the average prediction of these generate cross-entropy, AUC and pre-
diction errors
• For all setting in a class nd the setting with the highest validation AUC
• Report the test results on the average of the predictions and the average
test results on the predictions

2.6
Ensembling
The alternative generic variance reducing approach is to form a prediction out
of a combination of many models.
If models are not perfectly correlated a
combination of them will have lower variance. It is also common sense that
combinations of expects can be better than any single expert.
Thereby the
motivation for ensembling commities of models. How to form these commities
can be split into three main categories bagging (e.g random forest), boosting
(e.g. gradient boosting) and stacking.
2.6.1
Bagging
In bagging (or bootstrap aggregation) one makes many independent estimators
and form a prediction by simply taking an average of their prediction. Each
of these models are obtained by taking a sample of the original training set
with replacement. Thereby each estimator have a dierent dataset to t and
will generate dierent predictions.
The weights of the each observation will
approximately follow a poison(1)-distribution and therefore exp(−1) = 0.378
will have weight 0. A commen generalization which I use in this thesis is to
make the subsampling rate a hyperparameter.
The strength of a bagging algorithm can be explained the following way. The
overall error can be composed into bias and variance. Bagging reduces the error
by taking many models and average their results and if the variance term is
dawn somewhat independently then the average of the models are better. If
the models are completely independent the variance term will disappear, while
with perfectly correlated models adding extra models give no benet, since they
all give the same answer. The reasons models are correlated are they use some
of the same data to make their predictions they will have to use some of the
same structure in data to form low bias predictions. So it is imposible to make
the models completely independent without making the models so weak that
that the performance drops. So a good bagging algorithm make an ensemble
of models balancing maximal uncorrelatedness and minimal bias. Thereby the
objective of a algorithm used as a contribution to a backing diers from the
standard machine learning algorithm since it does not need to be as robust to
variance. This means that high variance models as decision tree (section 6.1)
and neural networks are obvious candidates for bagging. But as will be showed
bags of gradient boosting ensembles can also yield great benets.

Including randomness to the tree construction decorelates the tress and reduces

• Maximal depth of each tree
• Minimal leaf size and minimal size of node to allow split
• Number of nodes trained

7.1.1.3
Pruning
To prune a tree is in the context of decision trees to convert an internal node
with two leaf children to a leaf by collapsing the two children. Many of the
beforehand described hyperparameters (tree reaches maximal number of leaf,
no splits yields any gains, maximal depth is reached, etc.) can be viewed as
preforming pruning since they stop leaf construction. This is eqaulant to expand
the node and afterwards pruning it therefore often refered to as prepruning.
Pre-pruning is computationally ecient since all nodes constructed are used.
But it also has its limitation since a leaf without protable splits can have
children with protable splits. This is best illustrated by an example:
• X is two uncorrelated binary variables,x1, x2
• y = XOR(x1, x2)
Here splitting on either x1, x2 will not give improvement other then the improve-
ment due to sampling bias. So with many stopping criteria the tree construction
will stop here. While the next level splits will yield perfect separation in both
of their children. So by during pre-pruning one are at risk of not modeling vari-
able interactions. Therefor rst constructing a large tree and then afterwards
prune is desirable. There is o course no reason to construct nodes below the
maximal depth or with fewer samples in then the minimum number of samples
per leaf, so here pre-pruning can be preformed without loss of performance. But
for minimum gain criteria post-pruning is desirable. This is implemented in the
xgboost and not in the scikit-learn implementation. A good argument against
post-pruning is that a benecial split on top of many unbenecial splits will
leave many children of the unbenecial splits behind potentially eecting the
model performance.

A.0.1
Optimization methods
Optimizing a large series of parameters that interacts with each other is a dif-
cult task. It is inherently non-convex1 and even nding a local minimum is
dicult. An analytical solution is for obvious reasons impossible, so one needs
to resort to numerical optimization. Here various types of gradient decent algo-
rithms are used and for all practical applications each update are only preformed
looking at a small subset of the data, called a minibatch. Thereby fewer evalu-
ations/gradient calculations are needed and training can be done much faster.
Furthermore various combinations of momentum2 and second order derivatives3
are used to speed up convergence faster. In my implimentations I have used
Adaptive Moment Estimation Method (ADAM) and Nesterow momentum. Nes-
terow momentum is momentum method using a one step look-ahead when up-
dating. ADAM is a second order momentum method using second order statis-
tics to determined an amplier of the momentum update that is individual to
all weights.
In practice nding the actual minimum of the loss is seldomly desirable since we
are not interested in the best t of the training data but the best generalization
error. Here early stopping is often preformed, so when the validation error has
increase for some time training is stopped.
Another way to combat large dierences in the derivatives scale is batchnormal-
ization. Batchnormalization is simply to normalize the output of each neuron
to zero mean and unit variance for the batch after or before the nonlinearity is
applied during training. Thereby the scale dierences of gradients cannot accu-
mulate over layers leading to diverging gradients either towards zero (vanishing
gradients) or towards +/-innity (exploiting gradients). This is often important
when training deep neural network where gradients due to the chain rule is a
product of a chain of sub gradients making it more unstable th longer the chain
is. My results of using batch normalization did not yield improvements for this
specic dataset. So it where not used.
