Summary
In this report, damage limit states of a cylindrical reinforced concrete silo are proposed,
based on a multi-record, non-linear incremental dynamic analysis (IDA) [33]. As a result
of an in-depth literature review, typical earthquake-related damages of silos are identi-
fied, and the shortcomings of current design codes are highlighted. Furthermore, articles
discussing the seismic behaviour of silos are summarised, and a theoretical background
is provided as a framework for the report. Utilising the conclusions of the literature
review, a numerical model is created in the commercial software ABAQUS, using the
following constitutive models: hypoplasticity for the filling material; and concrete dam-
aged plasticity in case of the reinforced concrete wall. The numerical model is rigorously
validated in terms of benchmarking against reliable results found in literature.
The
typical seismic response of the silo model is then presented by means of a time-history
analysis. Results as displacement response, base shear, dynamic pressure and damages
are presented and discussed. Thereafter, twelve dynamic capacity curves of base shear
over displacement are created by running 118 time-history analyses based on a carefully
selected and scaled set of strong ground motions. The capacity curves are thoroughly
analysed, and five damage states (i.e., None, Slight, Moderate, Extensive and Complete)
are established in terms of maximum top displacement.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Industrial facilities are critically important to society as they represent essential assets
in a country’s economy. Silos are a specific type of common industrial facilities, designed
to contain and store goods, such as wheat, cement, sand, and other valuable materials.
The undisturbed operation of silos during their design lifetime is of high importance to
society. Silo damage or collapse can cause wastage of the contained material, as well as
put human life at risk. Since there is a demand for building silos all around the world, it
is inevitable to construct them in hazardous environment. Several occasions have been
reported over the past years, when silos were severely damaged or even collapsed due
to catastrophic earthquakes [2, 4, 32]. Consequently, the design of earthquake resistant
silos is crucially relevant in order to avoid structural failure, and secure functionality
over the entire lifespan.
In case a performance based design approach is adopted, damage limit states have
to be readily available during the design process. On the other hand, it is also often
necessary to quantify damages based on straight-forward damage criteria. Moreover,
damage limit states provide an essential background for conducting a fragility analysis
of silos. However, to the author’s best knowledge, only limited amount of research is
available on the seismic performance limits of silo structures as per this date. Therefore,
it is essential to establish a clear overview of the damage states of such structures and
quantify them. The aim of the current report is to carry out a multi-record incremental
dynamic analysis (IDA) [33], and identify the damage states of a typical cylindrical
reinforced concrete silo.
By means of a multi-record IDA, it is possible to obtain capacity curves (i.e., IDA
curves) by processing the results of a large number of time-history analyses. The IDA
curves provide the necessary information to identify different damage states. It is vital
however, to capture the structure’s dynamic behaviour during the analysis in the most
realistic way possible. As a consequence, advanced, highly non-linear constitutive laws
are to be applied in a numerical model, both in terms of contained material and reinforced
concrete structure. Furthermore, the numerical model requires rigorous validation to
ensure the reliability of the obtained dynamic response. Apart from the accuracy of
the numerical model, the dynamic response is heavily influenced by the characteristics
of the chosen strong ground motions in the time-history analyses.
Therefore, a set
of earthquake records is to be carefully selected and scaled to cover a wide range of
responses. As a result, a truly representative overview of the seismic performance is
obtained that serves as a valid basis for deriving the corresponding damage states.

CHAPTER 2
Literature review
The following chapter presents a thorough literature review in order to provide a frame-
work for the current report. Typical silo damages and failures due to past earthquakes
are reviewed as a starting point, followed by an overview of the provisions of current
seismic design codes of silos and their shortcomings discussed in scientific literature. At
last, research papers on the seismic behaviour of silos are summarized.
2.1
Typical silo damages
In order to evaluate seismic performance, the common failure modes of silos due to
earthquakes are to be identified at the beginning. Several reports have been published
over the years that discuss typical silo damages caused by strong seismic motions.
Looking at the relationship between earthquake characteristics and silo damages, it
is clear that the most significant effect is caused by the horizontal ground accelerations.
The vertical component of a ground motion usually has a small effect on the relatively
heavy silo. The most typical consequence of the horizontal ground motion is the non-
uniform stress distribution at the base due to bending moment.
These stresses can
be significantly larger than the ones caused by gravity loads. Moreover, the contained
material can oscillate due to seismic motions and cause damages in the upper part of
the silo. The oscillation of the material introduces additional dynamic pressure on the
silo walls [4].
Local failures often lead to catastrophic collapse of the entire structure, due to both
the lack of structural redundancy of cylindrical shells, and the inability to redistribute
stresses through alternative load paths. Silo collapse can cause among others, contam-
ination of the contained material, loss of material, clean-up an replacement costs and
most importantly injury or loss of life [4]. The most common failure type is observed as
elephant foot damages. In this respect, reinforced concrete silos suffer the same type of
damage that steel tanks do [25]. As an example, severe silo damages are reported due
to the Zemmouri earthquake in Algeria in 2003 [2]. The silo complex was constructed in
the 1970s and consisted of five batteries with 66 m high and 8.5 m diameter silos. The
most damaged battery happened to be the one that was almost full at the time of the
earthquake. The ones that were nearly or half empty suffered less damages. As it is seen
in Figure 2.1, severe concrete crushing, rebar fractures and buckling, and partial sliding
of the external concrete shell were observed. In addition, the quality of the concrete and
casting process seemed to be inadequately mastered during construction [2].

2.2
Design codes
Codes of practice on the design of earthquake resistant silos are vague and only contain
general requirements. The seismic design of silos is commonly performed on the basis of
identifying an effective mass which interacts with the silo walls under seismic excitation,
i.e., which horizontally pushes on the silo walls [27].
ACI 313 [1] prescribes that 80 % of the mass of the contained bulk material is to be
taken into account as effective mass, when conducting a seismic analysis. The centroid
of the effective mass shall coincide with the center of gravity of the actual volume. Apart
from this requirement, there are no specific guidelines mentioned, only a reference to
general building codes. Eurocode 8-4 [31] provides two methods on the seismic design of
silos. The less accurate method is identical to the one proposed by ACI [1]. The slightly
more refined approach introduces additional normal pressure exerted on the wall by the
dynamically excited filling material. The corresponding effective mass can be obtained
by integrating the pressure distribution, which results in a range of 75 ÷ 95 % of the
total mass [27]. The additional normal pressure on the wall is defined as

verification of the above analytical method is presented in [28], where similar conclusions
are drawn as mentioned before, i.e., Eurocode 8-4 is judged too conservative.
2.3
Seismic analysis of silos
Numerous research papers have been published in recent years that discuss the seismic
behaviour of silos. Most of them put a large emphasise on modelling the contained
material, and the interaction between silo wall and material as realistically as possible.
In most cases, this interaction is modelled as a simple Coulomb friction interface. As for

the contained material itself, hypoplasticity is highly favoured over other constitutive
models.
A general way of modelling silos for seismic analysis is presented in [35]. The proposed
general numerical model consists of hypoplastic granular material, frictional contact
interface and shell elements. Two actual models are used as examples, a steel and a
reinforced concrete silo respectively. The models are validated against results from both
experiments and theoretical static analysis.
Both material-silo and soil-structure interaction are taken into account in [5]. A three
degree-of-freedom analytical model is proposed and compared to a numerical model. It
is concluded that the analytical results show good agreement with the numerical model.
A similar system without soil-structure interaction is investigated in [15]. An analytical
model is once again proposed that, in general, seems to show a good match with the
numerical model. The effect of wall flexibility on the dynamic material pressure, base
shear and overturning moment is analysed in [16], with the conclusion that wall flexibility
has a large influence on the outcome of the numerical analysis. All of these three articles
[5, 15, 16] use fully elastic numerical models, both in terms of contained material and
silo structure. The reason for which is not made completely clear, however it seems that
the intention is to achieve better compliance with the proposed analytical methods.
Two numerical models are created in [19], with and without granular material-silo
interaction. The constitutive model of the concrete wall is concrete damaged plasticity
in both cases. In case of the model without granular material-silo interaction, 80 % of
the granular material is assumed as effective mass and distributed evenly on the silo
wall. As for the other model, hypoplastic granular material is modelled with simple
frictional wall contact as mentioned earlier. It is concluded that by accounting for the
material-silo interaction, the expected damages in the silo become considerably lower
than those indicated by not modelling the interaction. Similarly, hypoplasticity is chosen
for modelling the granular material inside the silo in [20]. The silo structure in this case
consists of a cylindrical steel shell and assumed to behave elastically.
The dynamic behaviour of granular material silos is discussed in [10]. The numerical
simulations take into account soil-structure, as well as bulk material-silo, interaction.
The latter is modelled using frictional contact and hypoplastic material. The results
are compared to those obtained from a numerical analysis based on the provisions of
Eurocode. It is concluded that the Eurocode provisions for slender silos are quite good
while for squat silos a reduction of the active mass would be in order. It should be noted
however that this comparison is based on European Pre-norms (prEN) published prior
to Eurocode 8-4 [31].
Once again, hypoplastic material model is chosen in [11] in order to investigate the
nonlinear dynamic behaviour of granular materials in a base excited steel silo. Results
with loosely and densely packed granular materials are presented in terms of displace-
ments. It is concluded among others that the hoop and vertical stresses in the steel silo
wall are higher for loose packing of the granular material than for dense packing.
Seismic vulnerability assessment of a reinforced concrete silo is carried out in [7] based
on results from an IDA. The numerical model in principle complies with the one described
in [35]. Hypoplastic granular material is considered with frictional granular material-wall

interaction. As for the reinforced concrete structure, damaged plasticity model is used.
In the IDA, maximum element drift ratio is selected as the demand parameter, whereas

The major shortcoming of this damage state definition is that only one out of several IDA
curves is selected arbitrarily for derivation. This means that the limit states only reflect
the damages caused by the specific seismic motion corresponding to this IDA curve,
and do not include the effect of different strong ground motions. Since the individual
IDA curves differ significantly, reliable limit states cannot be determined based on one
specific curve. Furthermore, PGA is adopted as an intensity parameter when deriving
the damage states.
PGA does not necessarily reflect the severity of the structure’s
response, since motions with the same PGA can induce various responses.

CHAPTER 3
Theory
The fundamental theoretical background of the current report is presented in the follow-
ing chapter. First, incremental dynamic analysis is introduced and thoroughly explained,
followed by an overview of the main principles of the constitutive models, hypoplasticity
and concrete damaged plasticity. Based on the literature review presented in Section
2.3, these two models are found to be the most accurate with regards to the numerical
modelling of reinforced concrete silos subjected to seismic excitation.
3.1
Incremental dynamic analysis
In principle, incremental dynamic analysis (as described in [33]) involves the seismic exci-
tation of a structural model by one or more strong ground motions, each scaled to various
levels of intensity, thus producing one or more dynamic capacity curves. Consequently,
IDA concludes in an accurate estimation of the structure’s seismic performance.
In practice, a full time history analysis is carried out for each scaling level of inten-
sity, for every ground motion in question. The absolute maxima of dynamic response
parameters (i.e., moment-curvature or base shear-displacement) corresponding to each
time-history analysis are extracted and plotted. The procedure is illustrated in Figure
3.1, where V is the base-shear and d is the top displacement. Choosing the values of
dynamic parameters can require further discussion, as often the maxima do not occur
simultaneously (e.g., the maximum base shear may occur at a different time instant than
the maximum displacement). In general however, it is more conservative to choose the
absolute maxima [22].
Figure 3.1: Illustration of a single record IDA [22].
==PAGE:18==
10
3 Theory
Compared to the conventional non-linear static pushover analysis, IDA proves to be
superior in several aspects. Static pushover neglects dynamic effects and focuses only on
the strain energy of the structure during monotonic loading. It neglects other sources of
energy mainly associated with dynamic components of forces such as kinetic and viscous
damping energy. Moreover, the progressive changes in the modal properties during non-
linear yielding and cracking in the structure are not taken into account during a pushover.
This phenomenon leads to period elongation and different spectral amplifications [22].
Furthermore, an important element of the uncertainty related to the seismic response
of structures that can be captured in a multi record IDA, is the random variability in
the ground-motion prediction [34]. Naturally, there is a major drawback to IDA when
compared to static pushover. In terms of computational time and post-processing of the
results, IDA is heavily demanding. A static analysis is often solved numerically in tens
of time steps, whereas a dynamic analysis can easily take thousands of increments [22].
The IDA curve produced in the aforementioned way is an ideal tool to investigate
the seismic performance of a structure. A straightforward method to derive performance
limit states based on an IDA curve is presented in [34]. The main principle is that the
damages are attributed to the lateral stiffness degradation of the structure. The lateral
stiffness is taken as the slope of the IDA curve, and the stiffness degradation can be
visualised by plotting the derivative of the curve. Thus, the damage limit states can
be defined as distinctive points in the derivative plot. For example, the first limit state
is chosen where the first sudden drop in the derivative plot is observed. This point
corresponds to the first yielding in the structure and therefore understood as the limit
state between no and slight damage. The procedure is illustrated in Figure 3.2, with the
limit states indicated as dsi.

3.2
Hypoplastic constitutive model
Hypoplasticity is an incrementally non-linear constitutive theory of cohesionless granular
materials.
The early version of hypoplasticity was created by Kolymbas [13] but in
most cases von Wolffersdorff’s interpretation [37] is referred to as the basic one. The
constitutive law describes the stress rate as a function of stress, strain rate and void
ratio. It is assumed that grains are aggregated to a simple granular skeleton with the
following properties described in [21].
• The state of a granular material is fully characterized by the effective stress and
the void ratio.
• Grains are permanent (i.e., they keep their size and form during deformations).
• Deformation of the granular skeleton is due to grain rearrangements. Compression,
abrasion and crushing of the grains are negligible.
• Surface effects (i.e., capillarity, cemented bridges, osmotic pressures) are absent.
• The deformation under homogeneous boundary conditions is homogeneous (with-
out shear localization).
• Three pressure dependent limiting void ratios can be distinguished (ec, ei and ed).
• Change of the limiting void ratios with the mean pressure is related to the granular
hardness hs, which represents a reference, pressure independent stiffness of the soil.
Eight material constants are required in the basic hypoplastic model.
They are
related to the geometric and material properties of grains and can be determined from
standard index tests. The definition of the parameters are as follows. Φc is the critical
angle of friction, ec0, ei0 and ed0 are the critical, maximum and minimum void ratios
at zero pressure, hs is the granular hardness, n is an exponent taking into account the
pressure-sensitivity of a grain skeleton, and α and β are exponents derived from triaxial
compression test. Figure 3.3 illustrates the relation between mean pressure, ps, and the
limiting void ratios; the grey zones denote inadmissible states for simple grain skeletons.
In case the void ratio is above the upper bound (e > ei), either macro-voids are present
or the grain contacts are opened, i.e., the grain skeleton could not exist any more. On

The most significant shortcoming of the basic hypoplastic model is that it is unable
to simulate small load amplitudes in case of cycling loading. Thus, the deformations
accumulate unrealistically, causing the so-called ratcheting effect [36]. In order to over-
come this issue, a new state variable, an intergranular strain is introduced. Intergranular
strain can be understood as a macroscopic measure of micro-deformations of the inter-
face layer between grains. In this manner, not only the deformation of the granular
skeleton due to grain rearrangement is considered, but also the deformation of the inter-
face layer [9, 21]. A straightforward, one dimensional interpretation of the intergranular
strain is illustrated in Figure 3.4 with two grains and a shaded interface area. D is
the strain rate and δ is the intergranular strain with a maximum value R. The initial
state of the grains with D = 0 is shown in Figure 3.4a. An arbitrary value of the strain
rate (e.g., D = −1) is hereafter chosen to initiate deformation. During the deformation,
the intergranular strain reaches R (Figure 3.4b), which cannot be surpassed by further
stretching, thus the interface remains deformed while the grains are sliding. This sliding
corresponds to the rearrangement of grains. After a reversal of D (Figure 3.4c), the
deformation concentrates in the interface alone, until δ passes the original zero value
(Figure 3.4d) and finally reaches R on the opposite side (Figure 3.4e) [21].

There are five additional parameters in connection with the intergranular strain

3.3
Concrete damaged plasticity
Concrete damaged plasticity (CDP) is a continuum, plasticity based, damage model used
by several researchers [7, 19] to accurately model reinforced concrete silo structures. Two
failure mechanisms are assumed in the model, tensile cracking and compressive crushing
respectively. Both are described by damage variables, dt in tension and dc in compression,
as a function of plastic strain. The damage variables characterize the degradation of
the elastic stiffness and take values from zero to one (zero represents no damage while
one represents total loss of strength).
CDP is designed for structures subjected to
dynamic loading, since it allows stiffness recovery during cyclic loading reversals. The
experimental observation in most quasi-brittle materials, including concrete, is that the
compressive stiffness is recovered upon crack closure as the load changes from tension
to compression. On the other hand, the tensile stiffness is not recovered as the load
changes from compression to tension once crushing micro-cracks have developed. The
equations below define the uniaxial compressive and tensile stresses as functions of the
respective damage variables [29].

CHAPTER 5
Model validation
In order to guarantee the validity of the numerical model, several verification schemes
are carried out and presented in the following chapter. The validation schemes typically
consist of benchmarking, i.e., comparing the results of numerical analyses to those found
in literature and deemed correct. The validation is successful if the comparison yields
closely matched results. A step-by-step procedure is followed; starting the validation
with a simplified, elastic model, and concluding with the final model presented in Chapter
4. First, a modal analysis is conducted to validate the modal characteristics of a fully
elastic model. Secondly, the material-structure interaction is validated through static
analyses with both elastic and hypoplastic filling material. Lastly, the dynamic response
of the final model setup is verified by means of time history analysis.

Validation of modal characteristics
A fully elastic silo model, with three different geometries, is investigated in [20], with
regards to free vibration. It is assumed that both the contained material and the silo
structure are elastic, and the material is constrained to the structure. Frictional contact
behaviour, with allowed separation upon tension, cannot be taken into account in the
modal analysis due to its non-linear properties. The bulk material is divided into four
layers with increasing moduli of elasticity towards the bottom. Table 5.1 shows the

The numerical model shows very close agreement with Janssen’s theory. The small
differences are due to the fact that the analytical method is based on Mohr-Coulomb

distribution around the base is attributed to two reasons. It is partly caused by the
deformation of the wall that is shown in Figure 5.5. Even though the wall is expected to
displace outwards, and (5.2) is formulated using the active pressure coefficient, Janssen’s
approach still doesn’t consider the specific mechanical properties of the wall, such as

larger deformations, and therefore the pressure is reduced in horizontal direction. It
is observed that the deformation around the base deviates from the initial linear part,
and suddenly drops as it reaches the fixation at the base. This behaviour results in
disturbance of the pressure distribution at the base. On the other hand, the deviation
in the pressure distribution around the base is also attributed to the fact that the base
of the silo is accurately modelled in the numerical approach, and frictional interaction
is present between base and filling material. On the contrary, the base configuration is
completely neglected in the analytical solution. Therefore, it is expected that the two
methods show larger deviation around the base.

5.4
Validation of dynamic response
The dynamic pressure, exerted on the silo wall by the granular material under seismic
excitation, can be calculated according to (2.2), suggested by Eurocode 8-4 [31]. Several
researchers have compared this pressure distribution to results from time-history analysis
of numerical models [11, 20]. Even though all of these papers reveal significant differences
in the results, this comparison can still be used to provide an indication of the validity
of the numerical model.
However, it must be noted that the two methods are not
expected to be in close agreement, therefore the Eurocode approach merely serves as a
conservative indication of the magnitude of dynamic pressure along the silo wall.
In order to calculate ∆ph,so, it is assumed that α(z) is constant along the height, and
determined based on the response spectrum of the specific earthquake motion and the
period of the first translational mode of the silo. Since the modal analysis can only be
carried out with elastic properties, the eigenfrequencies of the non-linear model cannot
be determined precisely. This introduces some level of uncertainty in determining α(z).
To eliminate this uncertainty, a synthetic seismic motion is generated, with a response
spectrum that has a plateau around the assumed period. In this way, it is ensured that
the right α(z) value is obtained, even though only the approximate period is known.
The synthetically generated earthquake accelerogram is plotted in Figure 5.10 and the
5% damped response spectrum in Figure 5.11. The modal analysis follows the same
principles as described in Section 5.1, and the elastic moduli of the filling material layers
are interpolated between the values of Model 1 and 2 in Table 5.2. The geometry of the
silo is given in Table 4.1. The first translational mode is shown in Figure 5.12, along
with the corresponding eigenfrequency, f = 5.23 Hz, and period, T = 0.19 s. In Figure
5.11, it is found that α(z) = 0.6, approximately.
The dynamic pressure distributions are computed for the final model configuration
described in Chapter 4. The maximum dynamic pressure in the numerical model is
obtained by integrating the pressure distribution along the height for each time step, and
selecting the one having the largest resultant. As such, the derived pressure distribution
occurs in one particular time instant of the strong ground motion. The obtained pressure
distribution is plotted in Figure 5.13a. Additionally, as presented in [11, 20], the envelope
of the maxima at each node along the height is also computed and plotted in 5.13b.
However, it must be noted that these maxima do not occur at the same time instant
and they are merely presented to gain better compliance with Eurocode provisions.
In both Figure 5.13a and 5.13b, the results of the numerical analysis are in a com-
parable range to that of Eurcode. Based on previous studies [11, 20], the difference is
not larger than expected, therefore the agreement between the two methods is deemed
acceptable.

CHAPTER 7
Incremental dynamic
analysis
In the following chapter, an incremental dynamic analysis is conducted, in order to define
the damage limit states of the silo model presented in Chapter 4 under seismic loading.
A large number of time-history analyses are carried out based on a carefully selected
and scaled set of strong ground motions. The absolute maxima of the selected response
parameters of each analysis are plotted to create the IDA curves. The damage limit
states are thereafter established based on the assessment of the IDA curves.
7.1
Strong ground motions
The set of strong ground motions applied in the time-history analyses are selected along
the following principles.
• Severe earthquakes are chosen with moment magnitudes, M > 6.
• Near-source motions are avoided. The distance from the epicenter is kept above
or around 20 km.
• In terms of frequency content, it is ensured that the set contains diverse motions
with Tm = 0.22 ÷ 1.48 s. Thus, guaranteed that a wide range of responses are
induced during the time-history analyses.
• The site classification, according to the definitions of NEHRP [3], corresponds to
very dense soil and soft rock (C), or stiff soil (D).
The basic characteristics of the selected twelve seismic motions are listed in Table
7.1, and the corresponding accelerograms and response spectra are found in Figure A.1
and A.2 in Appendix A.1 and A.2.
7.2

placement at the top of the silo wall, δ. The outputs of each time-history analysis are

The twelve ground motions in Table 7.1 are scaled in accordance with the following
principles. As a starting point, all accelerograms are scaled to four basic levels of PGA =

scaling is applied in regions where linear behaviour is assumed. Moreover, the seismic
motions are only scaled up to a PGA corresponding to the displacement response around
the expected collapse limit. In this manner, an adaptive scaling is applied, as opposed
to a uniform one with even increments of PGA. As a consequence, significant amount of
computational time is saved without compromising on the quality of the obtained results.
In total, 118 time-history analyses are run, each taking about five hours to complete,
providing on average ten pairs of response parameters per IDA curve.

curves share the same initial elastic linear region until the first yielding occurs. This
phenomenon is typical to any structural model with initially linearly elastic elements
[33]. The elastic slope is followed by a second hardening phase that ends at the final
yielding zone, whereafter a plateau-like section is reached.
As non-linear behaviour
takes over after the initial elastic region, a large scattering is observed between the

commonly observed that a structure produces dissimilar responses when subjected to

7.3
Damage states
Structural damage varies from None to Complete as a continuous function of deforma-
tions, however for practical reasons, generalized states are commonly used to describe
structural damage [8]. The damage state definitions of HAZUS [8] are adopted as fol-
lows: None; Slight; Moderate; Extensive; and Complete. The general description of these
damage states are only available for building structures, however the main principles are
also applicable in case of silos.
The limit states are defined as maximum top displacements corresponding to the
onset of damages of different severity. The characteristics of the IDA curves with their
distinctive regions, supported by results from the numerical analyses, serve as a basis
for determining δmax at each limit state. The considerations taken during the process
are thoroughly described in the following subsections, corresponding to each one of the
damage states.
It must be noted that the damage states could be determined by means of advanced
regression analysis. In this manner, an ”average” IDA curve could be approximated
over the individual IDA curves in Figure 7.1. This curve could then be the basis of
a derivative plot, and the damage states could be derived as explained in Section 3.1.
Given the time limitations of this study, the regression analysis could not be carried out,
however it is in the author’s best intention to do so within the framework of a follow-up
publication.
7.3.1
None
The structure is assumed to remain entirely undamaged throughout the initial elastic
part of the IDA curves, before the onset of tensile cracking. Therefore, as proposed
in Section 3.1, δmax at the limit state between None and Slight damage corresponds to
the first yielding of the structure, the initiation of tensile cracking. Hysteresis plots
are ideal tools to determine whether the structure has surpassed its elastic state during
the analysis or not.
Moreover, the initiation of tensile cracking can be captured by
contour plots of dt. Figure 7.2 presents results from the first point of the IDA curve of
NORTHR/TUJ352 (i.e., the time-history analysis of the first scaling level on the IDA
curve, highlighted in Figure 7.2b) in terms of hysteresis and contour plots. As seen in
Figure 7.2a, the structure behaves elastically. Furthermore, no tension or compression
damages are observed in Figure 7.2c and 7.2d. Consequently, the structure remains
undamaged, with a maximum top displacement of δmax = 0.11 cm. On the contrary, in
Figure 7.3, results from the second point of the same IDA curve indicate some level of
tensile cracking while δmax = 0.32 cm. Relatively small plastic deformations are seen in
Figure 7.3a, accompanied by tension damages in Figure 7.3c. After processing the results
from the initial elastic slope of all IDA curves, it seems clear that the onset of tensile
cracking occurs at a maximum top displacement, δmax = 0.25 cm. The aforementioned
results are found in Appendix A.4.

7.3.3
Moderate damage
Concrete spalling initiates in a few locations in Moderate damage state, but as its severity
increases and it spreads around the bottom of the wall, the structural damage becomes
Extensive. The second hardening slope of the IDA curves ends at the final yielding zone.
The final yielding of the structure is attributed to the spreading of compressive stiffness
degradation at the bottom of the wall, induced by the crushing of concrete. Therefore,
the final yielding point is identified as the limit state between Moderate and Extensive
damage. In order to determine the maximum top displacement corresponding to this
limit state, results from all IDA curves are taken into consideration. Thus, a value of
δmax can be selected that characterises final yielding in case of all twelve seismic motions.
It must be noted that not all IDA curves have a clear final yielding point, therefore it may
prove challenging to determine a specific yield displacement in every case. Nevertheless,
it is still possible to approximate δmax corresponding to final yielding in an average
sense. Figure 7.5 presents contour plots of dc in three cases where pronounced yielding
zones are observed on the IDA curves. Final yielding is observed at varying maximum
displacements within a range of 4.5 ÷ 9.2 cm. Assessing the yielding characteristics of
all IDA curves in Figure A.3 in Appendix A.3, δmax = 7.5 cm can be selected as an
appropriate average yield displacement.

7.3.4
Extensive damage
Severe concrete spalling is expected around most of the bottom of the silo wall in Ex-
tensive damage state. Severe spalling is characterised by the maximum value of the
compression damage variable (dc = 0.950), corresponding to εc ≥1.44% (see Figure 4.2).
A slightly higher compressive strain is suggested in [14] as a threshold for unrepairable
spalling, therefore εc ≥1.44% is considered as an adequate strain limit for severe spalling.
The limiting maximum top displacement between Extensive and Complete damage states
is defined in the following section.
7.3.5
Complete damage
Complete damage indicates that the structure has collapsed or is in imminent danger of
collapse. As such, severe concrete spalling takes place in the entire bottom of the silo
wall, and a plateau or descending slope of the IDA curves is reached (i.e., increasing
δmax with constant or decreasing Vmax). Severe crushing of the concrete at the entire
wall bottom corresponds to the actual elephant-foot-like collapse mechanism of silos due
to real earthquake events, discussed in Section 2.1.
Contour plots of dc are presented in Figures 7.6 and 7.7, along with δmax and the
deformed shapes of the silo structure. The results correspond to the last three, high-
lighted points of the IDA curves of SFERN/PEL180 and VICT/CHI102. The contour
plots present the evolution of compression damages at the bottom with increasing top
displacement, whereas the deformed shapes indicate the plastic deformations induced by
the seismic excitations. The limit state can be defined as the displacement corresponding
to the onset of severe compression damages in the entire bottom of the silo wall. This
displacement appears to be δmax = 20 cm that typically corresponds to the plateau or
descending part of the IDA curves. Figures A.31 - A.42 in Appendix A.6 provide more
validity to the established limit state, as they present the contour plots of dc, and the
maximum top displacements for the highlighted points on the IDA curves, in case of all
twelve ground motions.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion & Further work
In the current report, an incremental dynamic analysis was conducted with the objective
of defining the damage limit states of a cylindrical reinforced concrete silo containing
granular material. The following are concluded based on the results presented in the
previous chapters.
• Hypoplasticity, extended with the intergranular strain approach, was implemented
as a constitutive model for the granular material in the silo. Compared to using
an elastic material, the computational time significantly increases when introduc-
ing hypoplasticity as a user-defined subroutine (UMAT) to ABAQUS. Nevertheless,
precise modelling of the dynamic pressure, exerted on the silo wall by the contained
material, is essential if an accurate seismic response is to be obtained. Therefore,
hypoplasticity with the intergranular strain extension is deemed an ideal constitu-
tive law to model the behaviour of granular materials in silos during IDA.
• An incremental dynamic analysis was carried out, and capacity curves were plot-
ted in terms of base shear and top displacement. A distinct disadvantage of IDA,
compared to a static pushover analysis, is the exceedingly high computational cost.
On the other hand, dynamic effects, changes in the modal properties, and the vari-
ability of seismic motions can be taken into account during an IDA. Consequently,
IDA is judged as an appropriate method to derive the capacity curves of reinforced
concrete silos.
• Damage limit states were defined based on the IDA results. It is concluded that
the characteristics of the IDA curves provide a good indication of the silo’s damage
states; and IDA curves, supported by results of individual time-history analyses,
serve as a solid basis for identifying the corresponding limit states.
• The response parameter, maximum top displacement, proved to be a suitable
measure to quantify damages, even in case of the non-slender silo in question.
Additionally, the current report provides several opportunities to further investigate
the seismic performance of silos. It is in the author’s best intention to conduct further
research on the following topics, and present the results within the framework of a
follow-up publication.
• Openings are known to be critical locations for earthquake-related damages in si-
los. Moreover, the reinforcement design of the concrete structure has a significant
==PAGE:56==
48

influence on the seismic performance. Consequently, the configuration of the nu-
merical model presented in Chapter 4 could be modified with additional openings
and a more detailed reinforcement design.
• As mentioned in Section 3.1, an important element of the uncertainty related to
the seismic response of structures that can be captured in a multi record IDA, is
the random variability in the ground-motion prediction [34]. Even though, the
multi-record IDA presented in Chapter 7 is based on twelve, carefully selected
strong ground motions, including more accelerograms in the analysis could provide
additional validity to the established damage states.
• As previously noted in Section 7.2, an advanced regression analysis could be carried
out over the scatter plot of all IDA points. Thus, the damage limit states could
be determined in accordance to the procedure explained in Section 3.1.
• The established limit states serve as an essential framework for further research on
the fragility of silos. To the author’s best knowledge, fragility analysis of silos is
a seldom discussed topic in literature, despite the fact that the results of such an
analysis provide a straightforward, easy-to-understand assessment of the seismic
vulnerability of silos.
