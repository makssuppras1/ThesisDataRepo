Chapter 1
Introduction
Metal additive manufacturing is an ever-increasing area of interest because of its ”po-
tential to revolutionize the global manufacturing and logistic landscape” [2]. However,
a few challenges are still hindering the adaption of these techniques. This includes the
distortion of products. Distortion is an important phenomenon to understand and con-
trol, because it impacts cost-drivers such as product design eﬀort and support structure
usage.
Distortion is an inherent challenge in additive manufacturing. It refers to the straining
of the part from the desired geometry, both under and after the manufacturing. For
example, when printing the bracket seen in ﬁgure 1.1 the result might be slightly dif-
ferent, as seen in ﬁgure 1.2. These strains are caused by the release of residual stress,
as the print is separated from the base-plate. The focus of this thesis work has been on
modelling these strains in an eﬃcient manner, using the Inherent Strain Method (ISM).
The ISM is an eﬃcient tool for calculating the residual stress in welded parts, but has
also been applied in prediction of the distortion of parts made by additive manufacturing
[3–6].

Eﬃcient prediction of distortion of AM parts will open a wide range of possibilites. In
essence, an accurate prediciton of the strains in AM parts will reduce manufacturing
time, reduce material waste and increase the quality of the manufactured parts. This
can for example be achieved through the possibility to:
• Predicting the deformations of parts
• Determining what geometries are feasible to manufacture.
• Selecting the printing scheme that minimizes the critical deformations.
• Compromising between the required printing time and deformations.
• Optimizing the use of support-structure to minimize post-processing.
The main motivation for developing an eﬃcent method for predicting the distortion of
parts made by additive manufacturing is that topology optimization will be feasible.
Topology optimization is a form of structural optimization in which no topological fea-
tures, such as holes or the shape of faces, needs to be speciﬁed on beforehand. This means
that the algorithm will be free to create and destroy the features in the search for an
optimal structure, based on the given optimality criterion. This versitiliy resonates with
the design freedom that additive manufacturing implies and therefore renders topology
optimization a desirable tool for optimizing parts to be made by additive manufacturing.
Topology optimization is computationally heavy as the model used in the structural cal-
culations has to be evaluated for each change in the design parameters. Hence, topology
optimization is only feasible if the structural model is eﬃcient.
Through this introductory chapter the reader will be introduced to the inherent strain
method (ISM) and the assumptions needed to apply it to additive manufaturing (AM).
Subsequently, the scope of the project and the authur’s contributions are cleariﬁed.
Finally, the reader will be presented an overview of the research.
1.1
Context/Background
Additive Manufacturing is, as deﬁned by ASTM, ”a process of joining materials to
make objects from 3D model data, usually layer upon layer” [7]. Because of the joining,
or fusion, process, parts made by additive manufacturing experience complex thermal
exposure [2]. This thermal exposure is cyclic as the part is manufactured by depositing
layer by layer. Such history of thermal gradients can result in signiﬁcant residual stresses
that causes the part to warp. This distortion is refered to as the deﬂections induced by

Additive manufacturing of metal parts is, by its nature, similar to multipass welding;
joining and solidifying strip by strip, layer by layer. Hence, most work on modelling
the distiortion of AM metal parts has been based on methods for predicting welding
distortion. The most accurate approach to model the distortion of welds is by developing
full-scale thermal elastic-plastic models that seek to include all the physics in the welding
process. This however, is computationally expensive for larger structures. To reduce
the computational cost of predicting weld distortion the inherent strain method is often
used.
1.1.1
The inherent strain method for welding
Yukio Ueda developed the inherent strain method as an experimental/analytical method
for determining the residual stress in welds [8]. The main property of the inherent strain
method is that the total strain and the residual stresses can be expressed as a linear
function of the non-elastic* strains in the weld, called inherent strain, ϵ∗. In other words,
the inherent strain can be seen as a parameter for the total strain, ϵ, and the residual
stress, σ, related through linear functions g and h.
ϵ = g(ϵ∗)
(1.1)
σ = h(ϵ∗)
(1.2)
Because the inherent strain only exist within the weld, not in the rest of the structue,
this means that by obtaining the inherent strain for one weld only, the distortion of
any structure containing a similar weld can be predicted. That is, similar in geometry
and thermal history. This is especially useful in eﬃciently predicting the distortion of a
large, complex structure, such as the one in ﬁgure 1.5. Suﬃciently far from the edges,
the inherent striain in this weld is constant along the weld. Hence, the inherent strain

1.1.2
The inherent strain method for Additive Manufacturing
As for welds, the most accurate approach to predict the distortion of parts made by
additive manufacturing is by developing full-scale thermal elastic-plastic models that
seek to include all the physics in the fusion and annhealing process []. This however,
is very computationally expensive, because of the complex thermal history. For weakly
coupled models, most eﬀort is spent on solving the transient thermal ﬁeld [], but the
non-linear elastic-plastic problem can also be a bottleneck.
It has therefore, in recent work, been sought to apply the inherent strain to additive
manufacturing. Promising results has been found for as good as direct applications of
the inherent strain method. Liang et al.[6] predicting the distortion of an oblong block
and comparing to a thermo-mechanical simulation, yielding fairly good accuracy. Liang
et al. also proposed a modiﬁcation in the calculation of the inherent strain, yielding
even more accurate results.
A great challenge in the application of the ISM to AM is that there are many variables.
It is, for example, possible to use many diﬀerent scan patterns, dictating the path of
**the weld arc or laser beam** in a layer, an example of this is seen in ﬁgure 1.7.
Furthermore, layers of various print paths can be combined to make up the printing
scheme of a stucture, as seen in ﬁgure 1.8. Moreover, the geometry of AM parts can

To counter this challenge, Keller made the simplifying assumption that the inherent
strain is uniform throughout an entire AM structure, regardless of the geometry [4, 5].
By doing so, he could accurately predict the distortion of beams made by AM with
various print schemes, while only performing a single thermo elastic-plastic simulation
of a ”scanned island”. Moreover, the resulting inherent strain can be rotated to ﬁt all
scan patterns. This can be seen in ﬁgure 1.7, considering the red and blue cubes to be

Theoretical Background
This chapter explains the underlying theoretical concepts used in the modelling work
of this thesis. Firstly(initially), the concept of inherent strains is presented formally.

2.1
Inherent Strain
Inherent strains, {ϵ∗}, as deﬁned in Ueda’s initial publication [8], are dislocations pro-
ducing the residual stresses in a free body[8]. For thermo elastic-plastic processes, such
as welding, these include residual plastic strain {ϵp}, residual thermal strain {ϵthermal}
and phase transformation induced strain {ϵphase} [12]. The sum of these strains make
up the inherent strain in a body, as described by eq.2.1.
{ϵ∗} = {ϵp} + {ϵthermal} + {ϵphase}
(2.1)
Concurrently in the welding process, the residual stresses, produced by the inherent
strain(s), does in turn induce elastic strain(s) {ϵe} in the body. Consequently, the total
strain in a body, {ϵ}, is the sum of the elastic strain and the inherent strain, as given in
eq.2.2.
{ϵ} = {ϵe} + {ϵ∗}
(2.2)
From solid mechanics we have that the elastic stress, σ, in a body is related to the elastic
strain through the constitutive matrix [C][13].
Hence, the deﬁnition of the inherent

{σ} = [C] ({ϵ} −{ϵ∗})
(2.3)
where {ϵ∗} determines {ϵ}, which in turn determines {σ}, as will described in the fol-
lowing. Hence, σ is the residual stress corresponding to the inherent strain {ϵ∗}.

In this chapter the most theoretically rigid approach to applying the inherent strain

Accumulating Layers
Accumulating layers (AL) is the approach that best mimics the process of additive
manufacturing. AL is based on the assumptions taken when considering the inherent
strains found by analysis of the ”scanned island” to be representative for the entire
structure. Hence, the approach is similar to the one used by Keller [4][5]. Initially all
layers are deactivated, i.e. they are not included in the assembly of the stiﬀness matrix
or have a very small stiﬀness. Then, each layer is activated and the appropriate inherent
strain force is applied to that layer. All layers previously activated remain activated,
having the stiﬀness of the material. However, inherent strain is only applied to the most
recently activated layer.

Mesh update
The AL approach to ISM for AM consist of a series of sequentially evaluated quasi-
static linear elastic equations for displacement. This means that the geometry of layer
j is dependent on the displacement induced by the inherent strain in layer j −1. This
is accounted for by adding the displacement of layer j −1 to the nodal coordinates
used to assemble the stiﬀness matrix for layer j. When using a mesh with deactivated
elements, with stiﬀness of E × 10−9, the displacement is arbitrary for elements not yet
activated. To counter this, the mesh update is strictly deﬁned to only include nodes
related to layers 1 to j when updating based on {D}j−1, the displacement induced by
applied inherent strain in layer j. To illustrate this please consider the three meshes in
ﬁgure 3.4, showing the AL approach step by step for a cube with three layers.

3.2.3
Convergence
The two most relevant sources of error are the modelling error and discretization error.
The modelling error represents that which is part of the physical system , but is not
included in the mathematical model.
It is the result of all the simpliﬁcations made
in deﬁning the model. The discretization error on the other hand is introduced when
formulating the mathematical model in the form of a ﬁnite element model.
3.2.3.1
Discretization error
It is beneﬁcial to understand the impact discretization has on the modelling approach
in order to verify implementations and be aware of whether the solution is greater or
smaller than the exact solution. Also, convergence with respect to the discretization also
points to that the mathematical model is stable, however it does not say anything about
the accuracy of the mathematical model.
The discretization error are the diﬀerence
between the mathematical model and the ﬁnite element (FE) model. It would be zero
if there was inﬁnitely number of degrees of freedom. Hence, the exact solution of the
mathematical model can be estimated through a convergence study, investigating the
inﬂuence of the element size. A convergence study would give the experimenter the
convergence rate and the possibility of extrapolating to estimate the exact solution.
==PAGE:33==
23
For standard structural FEA using 8-node box elements the convergence rate of the
displacement ﬁeld with respect to the mesh discretization is on the order of the char-
acteristic length squared, O(h2) [13]. The AL approach is a combination of standard
structural FE analyses. However, the order of the convergence will be that of the FE
analysis with the highest order of convergence. Hence, the order of convergence of the
AL approach with respect to mesh discretisation is O(h2).
In FE modelling of additive manufacturing discretization error can be divided into two
categories, mesh discretization and layer discretization. Both types will be investigated
in the following.
3.2.3.2
Mesh Convergence
Mesh convergence is here deﬁned as the impact of the mesh discretization, regardless of
the layer discretization. Hence, the study is conducted with several mesh reﬁnements,
all evaluated with 16 layers.
The coarsest mesh used had one element in z-direction per layer. In other words, it had
nelx × nely × nelz = 96 × 12 × 16 elements. Moreover, the reﬁnements were uniform,
such that the number of element in each direction stayed proportional for all meshes.
When looking at the mesh discretization, the characteristic length is usually the average
side length of the elements in the relevant region. Here, structured meshes with close-
to-cubic elements are used, hence the mean side length of the elements is used when
calculating the order of convergence of the mesh discretization error.
Figure 3.7 shows the displacement sampled at the end of the beam structure for several

above, on the order O(h2). Therefore, h2 is used as the horizontal axis. From the ﬁgure
it can be concluded that the displacement indeed converges proportionally with h2, for
suﬃciently ﬁne meshes.

3.3.1
Strain Parameter Dependence
The methods for estimating the strain parameters are based on two assumptions, a linear
regression and an assumption about the correlation between the strain parameters and
displacement.
The ﬁrst assumption of the methods is the linear regression based on results generated
by an ISM approach, such as the AL approach developed above. This implies that the
estimated strain parameters are not general parameter of the physical system. Further-
more, the estimated inherent strain is dependent on the mesh used in generating the
data for the regression. For these reasons, this technique does not estimate the physical
inherent strain, the strain parameter estimated here is rather a numerical, mathematical
and physical parameter. In other words, the estimated strain parameter is a function of
discretization, model choice, and printing technique.
The second assumption is the assumption that the displacement result of the (mathemat-
ical/discretized/IS)* model in any direction is uncorrelated with the strain parameter for
the z-direction ϵ33, normal to the printing-plane. This is an assumption about the model
used in predicting the displacement, and the eﬀect of ϵ33 on the real print*(system) is
in some sense irrelevant* (to this reasoning)*. Making this assumption, the regression
model for the displacement in the z-direction dz can be based on ϵ11 and ϵ22 only.
Furthermore, it is assumed that there is no mesh update. And ﬁnally it is assumed that

Chapter 4
A Fast Approach to ISM for AM
Because computational eﬃciency was the reason for applying ISM to AM, ways of re-
ducing the computational cost even more than in the AL approach was investigated.
The approach developed is called the decoupled layers (DL) approach. In this chapter,
the strains parameters related to the DL approach are estimated estimated in the same
manner as for the AL approach, in chapter 3. The same thing goes for the validation
as well. The results obtained are compared to Keller’s measurements and simulation
results, as well as the measurements of the beam structures printed for this project.
The decoupled layers (DL) strategy regards all the layers to be subjected to ﬁxed bound-
ary conditions on their bottom planes. This way, the calculation of the residual stresses
resulting from the inherent strain force in each layer is much cheaper as it only includes
the layer with the applied strain. The reduction in computational cost, compared to
the AL approach, becomes greater as the number of layers increases. In this strategy,
as well as in AL, the residual forces of all layers are summed. However, the summed
residual force is here distributed throughout the ﬁnished part. The two-step procedure
of the DL strategy is illustrated in ﬁgures 4.1 to 4.4 and formulated below:
Strategy: DL - Decoupled Layers
1.
Do n-times, for each layer:
i) Calculate the displacement of the activated layer, due to the inherent strain
force in that layer. Fixed boundary conditions are used at the bottom of the
layer.
ii) Calculate the residual force
2.
Calculate the displacement due to the sum of all the residuals.

4.0.2
Mesh update
Similar to the AL approach, the DL approach to ISM for AM consist of a series of
sequentially for displacement. This means that the geometry of layer j is dependent on
the displacement induced by the inherent strain in layer j −1. This is accounted for by
adding the displacement of layer j −1 to the nodal coordinates used to assemble the
stiﬀness matrix for layer j. Because the layers are evaluated separately, the displacement
of the remaining layer has to be deﬁned. Heuristically, it is deﬁned so that the mesh
==PAGE:57==
47
update in layer j + 1 is a linear interpolation between the displacements at the top of
layer j and the unchanged location of the top of layer j + 1, just as in the AL approach.
This seeks to capture that the top a newly printed plane without inherent strain would
have the same coordinates as in the ideal geometry
4.0.2.1
Neglecting mesh update
Neglecting the mesh update has the same implications for the DL approach as the AL
approach. It requires that the change in stiﬀness due to intermediate distortions are
small.
And, the beneﬁt of neglecting the mesh update is that the system becomes
embarrassingly parallel.

Mesh Convergence
The study of mesh convergence was conducted with several mesh reﬁnements, all evalu-

all meshes.
When looking at the mesh discretization, the characteristic length is usually the average
side length of the elements in the relevant region. Here, structured meshes with close-
to-cubic elements are used, hence the mean side length of the elements is used when
calculating the order of convergence of the mesh discretization error.
