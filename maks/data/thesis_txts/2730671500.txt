Abstract
This thesis aims to enhance an existing XMM-Newton data analysis pipeline, initially
designed for point sources, with a focus on the Low Mass X-Ray Binary (LMXB)
system EXO 0748-676. The upgraded pipeline successfully extended its capabilities
to other instruments on XMM-Newton, utilizing calibration and standard software,
producing spectra, light curves, and images.
Simultaneously, modern thermonuclear burst theory was applied to archival XMM-
Newton data from 2003. It was done so specifically with the intent of examining
the unique characteristics of EXO 0748-676—a thermo nuclear bursting in relation
to dipping, eclipsing within a binary system.
Three separate thermonuclear bursts were investigated, and multiple different mod-
els where applied to accommodate for the different circumstantial nature for each
burst analyzed The first burst, following a dip, led to the testing of two persistence
modeling methods, with one proving particularly effective. The second burst during
a deep dip posed challenges in constraining burst models. The third burst, when
modeled, raised concerns about potential underestimates due to its association with
a dual system.
The findings supported initial observations, and allowed for the similar results results.
New estimates of flux and mass accretion rate were derived by applying a model
accounting for enhanced accretion during thermonuclear bursts.
In conclusion, this thesis set out to achieve its objectives like advancing the XMM-
Newton data analysis pipeline, and deepening the understanding of EXO 0748-676’s
physics through the application of modern thermonuclear burst theory. As it has
been possible to recreate results from prior observations, it can be said with some
degree of confidence that this thesis successfully achieved the prior mentioned ob-
jectives.
Future work is suggested to refine burst models under varied circumstances, building
upon this study’s foundation, as the technology and methods advance to make new
findings.

Introduction
In the fast evolving field of X- and -ray physics, the study of Low Mass X-Ray Bi-
naries (LMXBs), is an extraordinary phenomenon that calls for explorative analysis
and physics. These systems consist of a compact object, typically a neutron star or a
black hole, in a binary system with a low-mass companion star. These systems emit
X-rays as the compact object accretes matter from its companion star, making them
observable with X-ray telescopes. This thesis takes particular focus on themonuclar
bursts, under varies circumstantial events such as dipping, eclipsing, etc.
Since
it’s discovery in 1985, the LMXB EXO 0748-676 has undergoes a different physical
process associated with these interesting systems. It is a fascinating example of a
LMXB and has since it’s discovery in 1985 contributed to our understanding of the
extreme physical processes that occur in LMXB systems, as well as the behavior of
neutron stars and their interaction with their companion. This work aim to design,
implement, and improve the previously developed pipeline for fast processing of
XMM-Newton data of point sources, utilizing traditional methods that allow users
to modify them easily, leading to greater eﬀiciency, in reducing XMM-Newton ODF
files. This work will aim to implement new features and techniques to address the
issues identified in the previous report and, to improve the pipeline’s eﬀiciency and
accuracy. The purpose of this thesis is to utilize the pipeline for fast processing for
examining historical data from the Low Mass X-Ray Binary (LMXB) EXO 0748-
676, comprising of a Neutron Star (NS), with the objective of reproducing previously
reported published results, to assess the eﬀicacy and quality of the pipeline. The
development of an effective data analysis pipeline for LMXRB data will contribute
to a better understanding of compact objects, and the accretion process and will
facilitate the analysis of large datasets from current and future X-ray missions.

2.1
Neutron stars in Binary systems
At the end of its life, a star will leave the main sequence, as it exhausts its supply
of fusible elements. The hydrostatic balance between pressure and gravity becomes
unstable and can lead to a core collapse. Particularly massive stars, corresponding
to a mass of M > 8 −10M⊙, can end their lives in a supernova explosion and the
core will collapse to nuclear density, as protons and electrons form into neutrons
and neutrinos. If the star is massive enough, the pressure created by the degenerate
neutrons may not be suﬀicient to stop the core collapse and consequently leave a
black hole behind. However, if the pressure remains stable, the explosion will leave
behind an exposed core consisting almost purely of neutrons - aNeutron Star (NS).
[1] These compact objects are associated with very strong gravitational fields and
small radii. They are a source of high energy emission, which can be observed from
large distances, making them ideal to use as a standard candle. [2]
Approximately 50% of stars in our galaxy greater than 1M⊙are part of a multisys-
tem, and as a consequence, neutron stars can be observed to be a part of a binary
system with a low-mass companion star. These systems are a part of a class called
Low Mass X-ray Binary Systems comprised of a neutron star or a black hole as the
primary object, and a low-mass companion star of 1M⊙.
These systems are known to be a source of high-energy radiation, as the compact
object draws matter from its companion. For LMXB systems, the mass transport
mechanism is via Roche lobe overflow, where mass is transported through the inner
Lagrange point between the gravitationally bound objects.
[2] As the matter is
drawn towards the companion (or donor star), it will swirl around the compact
object and accumulate, creating an accretion disc (AD). As the matter falls in from
the outer disk to the inner disc, the disc becomes very hot due to high gravity, giving
rise to a soft blackbody radiation in low X-ray ranges.[1] Because the companion
star is the late-stage low-mass star, the matter that is accreted usually consists of
helium and hydrogen, but usually consists of other elements associated with stellar
composition. The accretion disc is, however, not the only source of high-energy
radiation. If the surface of the neutron star is visible to the observer (low inclination),
there is also blackbody emission originating from the boundary layer where the disc
meets the neutron star, blackbody emission from the neutron stars surface. However,
if the source is observed close to the orbital plane (high inclination), the neutron star
is only observed through a hot corona above and below the disc, emitting scattered
X-ray.[1] In this case, the photons originating from the NS surface, can either interact
with the neutron star atmosphere, or the accretion disc can obscure or interact with
the photons.

2.1.1
Dips and Eclipses
As shown in figure 2.1, the photon energy and the amount of photons that can be
observed from such systems, vastly depend on the viewing angle or inclination. If the
LMXB system is observed at higher inclinations (i > 60◦) part of the accretion disc
can as a consequence be obscured by the accretion disc and/or the donor star, as the
donor star and companion star orbit around each other. This can give rise to orbital
modulations.[3] If the viewing angle is parallel with the orbital plan (80◦< i < 90◦),
the compact object is not visible, but only scattered X-rays from wind or corona can
be seen. As the system orbits, the flux may experience slight modulation when the
donor passes our line of sight. If the viewing angle 60◦< i < 80◦the accretion disc
becomes visible, and at 60◦≈i the neutron star also becomes more visible. This is
the regime which gives rise to dipping and eclipsing.
Dipping occurs in LMXB systems when the accretion steam from the donor star
reaches the disc, and the stream penetrates the accretion disc to smaller accretion
radii. This steam impact region becomes a large region in the accretion disc which
has more absorbing matter (larger column density), consequently obscuring the X-
rays. This gives rise to a periodic dipping when observing the flux as a function of
time, with a period corresponding to the orbital modulation of the system. Dipping
LMXB can occur at an inclination 60◦< i < 80◦, but at a slightly higher inclination
75◦< i < 80◦, eclipsing can occur. If only the top-part of the donor star eclipses the
accretion disk, this can lead to a dip. But if the accretion disk is entirely eclipsed by
the donor star, then this will cause a total eclipse. Dipping and eclipsing behavior
in LMXB systems can be complex because the geometry of these systems can be
hard to estimate. Variations in light curves can exhibit a superposition of various
periodic phases. The most prominent dipping is due to the mass stream impact, but
other dipping behavior can also arise due to additional structures in the accretion
disk. [4]

2.2
EXO 0748-676: An overview
EXO 0748-676 is an X-ray binary consisting of a neutron star, which undergoes dips,
eclipses, and Type-I burst. It is a well-known burster and has exhibited a variety
of type-I bursts, including PRE-bursts, double, and triple bursts.[13][7].It was first
discovered in EXOSAT in 1985[14] and its donor star UY Vol. This source was
found to have a high inclination in the range ≈75 −83◦, as it exhibits periodic
eclipses and dips in the X-ray regime.
As is the case for many high-inclination
LMXB systems, restricting the inclination is a challenge. Knight et al. (2022)[15]

periodic behavior, as it is both a dipper and displays eclipses with an orbital period
of 3.82hr.[18][13] Furthermore, the source has been shown to exhibit a Quasi-Periodic
Oscillation (QPO) at 552 Hz, during a Type-I burst, which is thought to originate

of discovery and remained active until Sept. 2008 when it went into quiescence.

emission lines were detected, possibly arising due to a pulsar wind interacting with
the donor star.

Chapter 6. Conclusion and Future Work
6
Conclusion and Future Work
The purpose of this thesis had two objectives. The first objective was to improve
an exiting XMM-Newton data analysis pipeline, developed for point sources, with
a particular focus on the Low Mass X-Ray Binary (LMXB) system EXO 0748-
676.
This thesis focus on modern thermonuclear burst theory, and apply it to
archival XMM-Newton data on the this X-ray binary system. The second objective
of this thesis is to apply modern thermonuclear burst theory and the effect linked
to accretion processes, with the aim of investigating the physics involved in LMXB-
systems. It can be concluded that it was possible to develop the exiting pipeline to
accommodate for the other instruments on XMM-Newton, as it prior to this work
only worked with the MOS instrument. The pipeline developed is able to callibrate
and run the standard XMM-Newton software, create spectras, light curves, and
images.
It can be concluded that it was possible to further apply modern thermonuclear
burst- and accretion theory, which has not been done before on the chosen archival
data from 2003. The LMXB-system considered in this thesis is a dipping, eclipsing
and bursting binary, giving rise to many physical phenomena, that simplistic burst
theory cannot always resolve.
It can be concluded that it was possible to recreate previous results, but at the
same time making new estimates of flux and mass accretion rate, as a consequence
of applying a model which aims to take into account the enhanced accretion which
can occur during a thermonuclear burst.
This thesis investigated three thermonuclear bursts under very different circum-
stances: Burst #1 occurred right after a dipping, and two methods were tested for
modelling the persistence are proposed - one of which worked. The second burst #2
occurred during a deep dip, and it was not possible to constrain the burst models.
The third burst, was possible to model, but may be subject to underestimates as is
one part of 2 systems.
The findings of this thesis supports the results from when it was first observed.
There are many complex phenomena occurring in accreting LMXB systems, and
especially those with high inclination. In conclusion, this thesis successfully achieved
its dual objectives of enhancing the existing XMM-Newton data analysis pipeline
and applying modern thermonuclear burst theory in order to investigate the physics
of the LMXB system EXO 0748-676. Future work may delve deeper into refining
burst models under varied circumstances, building upon the foundation laid by this
study.
