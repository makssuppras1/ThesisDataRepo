Abstract 
This thesis explores how sustainable urban mobility can be measured in order to monitor progress 
towards achieving desirable performance. For this purpose, the concept of sustainable urban mobility is 
defined based on a broad literature review. Four basic domains and several elements in each one of them 
were identified able to describe sustainable urban mobility in a holistic way; urban space, transport service 
and modes, passability and effects. Indicators for sustainable urban mobility were classified according to 
these domains which were afterwards prioritized based on the perspective of selected stakeholders 
experienced with the transport sector. The result of this prioritization was a short list of the most 
appropriate indicators representing sustainable urban mobility. Copenhagen municipality is selected as 
the case study for the data analysis and assessment of these indicators. With the scope to communicate 
sustainable urban mobility, a single index is developed able to show performance at a glance. Alternative 
methods as a part of the index development are discussed and finally a conclusion is drawn.

1. Introduction 
Many modern cities today suffer from the extensive impacts of industrialization which prevailed during 
the second half of the 20th century. During that period the dominant priority was economic growth in 
order to overcome the disasters of war and meet the growing consumption needs of the population. At 
that time the accumulation of the population in urban areas took place in high rates. Starting from the 
beginning of the 20th century urban population accounted for 15% of the global population whereas in 
1990 this proportion reached 40% of the total population. Today, more than half of the world’s population 
live in cities while this proportion is expected to be 66% by the year 2050 (United Nations, 2014). It is 
essential to improve urban life as more and more people tend to live in cities. 
At the same period around 1960 the invasion of the motorcars dominated the lifestyle and became a 
major contributor to the shape cities have today. People could now “move” faster, more and at greater 
distances. Initially perceived as a symbol of freedom, motorcars were more and more owned by people. 
It is quite impressive that in 1993 the annual rate of growth for cars was greater than the growth rate of 
the global population.

For years, urban planning was oriented towards giving more space to cars and less space to people. The 
impacts of this development are clearly visible today in modern cities. Congestion, air pollution, noise 
emissions, energy overconsumption, degradation of landscape, safety and health issues, social 
inequalities and even low quality of urban life are some of the impacts and characteristics of a car-oriented 
city with high shares of motorized vehicles. As Fred Kent said, "If you plan cities for cars and traffic, you 
get cars and traffic. If you plan for people and places, you get people and places". Transportation 
nowadays account globally for one quarter of the CO2 emissions, half of the oil use and almost half of the 
air pollution in urban areas (Sperling, 2015). 
According to Benfield and Replogle (2002) our nation’s environmental quality will be sustainable only if 
we pursue transportation in a sustainable way. Transportation, especially the use of motorized vehicles, 
contributes substantially to a wide range of environmental problems, including energy waste, global 
warming, degradation of air and water, noise, ecosystem loss and fragmentation, and degradation of the 
landscape.  
Apart from environmental conditions, transportation systems have significant impacts on social and 
economic conditions too. This can be more clear looking into an example; air pollution or road accidents 
affect the health of the human population which then has an impact on the wealth and productivity of 
the society and consequently on the economic accounts of the government due to hospital treatment and 
loss in workforce.  Transportation is a part of everyday life and a component of a series of relations within 
communities. Improvements at this sector affect the quality of life, environmental state and economic 
figures as well. 
Transportation is a fundamental part of urban forms and the sustainability of a city is highly dependent 
on its transportation patterns and traffic conditions. In the bigger picture, there can be no sustainable 
development without sustainable transportation. Transportation has proven to be a major contribution 
to sustainability and thus in order to achieve sustainable development, transportation has to be 
considered too.  
Sustainable transport is nowadays the focus of several plans and policies and the goal for many 
communities. The only way of knowing whether progress is being made towards sustainable development 
is to measure how we are doing based on existing and prior performance and to use this information to 
consider what changes should be made towards achieving sustainability goals. It is essential to measure, 
predict and monitor such impacts using appropriate indicators, in order to prepare for the future or reach 
==PAGE:16==
3 
 
specified targets; “indicators are essential tools to transform the notion of sustainability into reality” 
(Gudmundsson et. al). 
Urban mobility is an essential component of urban sustainability a term which appears in several 
definitions of sustainable transportation. Achieving sustainable urban mobility is a vital step in the overall 
improvement of the urban environment, the maintenance of the economic vitality of cities and reducing 
negative impacts on health (Fudge, 1996). 
More efficient mobility can benefit both citizens and stakeholders. Considering that the average European 
household spends €500 on transportation each month, mobility is a more attractive market than ever 
(cited in The Climate Group et al., 2011). Mobility is a concern for authorities and politicians and there is 
often a need to communicate figures in the decision making process. A clear and easy to understand set 
of indicators can be a very useful tool in policy making and implementation of initiatives. Moreover, when 
economic matters are involved it is often easier for initiatives to take place using clear figures and 
indicators.  
The purpose of this report is to highlight the need of obtaining solid tools for measuring and monitoring 
current trends of transportation patterns towards achieving sustainability. In particular, it is sought how 
green mobility can be expressed in a single index. The goal is the development of an index which will 
include key aspects of sustainable mobility and will be able to measure a city’s performance compared to 
latest years with respect to desirable goals and targets. 
For this purpose, it is firstly researched what would be the ideal sustainable mobility at an urban context, 
the factors affecting mobility and possible indicators that can measure it. The most appropriate 
framework it is then sought based on which the mobility index will be developed. The goal is to conclude 
on a final list of indicators that can measure sustainable urban mobility, a study which will be conducted 
for Copenhagen municipality. One of the challenges of this study is to prioritize the several indicators 
measuring mobility and as a final step to seek how several aspects of sustainable urban mobility can be 
expressed in a single index. The problem statement is then taking the form of the following question: 
How can sustainable urban mobility be measured in order to monitor progress towards achieving goals? 
Several research questions are raised for which an answer will be sought throughout this report: 
 
What are the factors shaping sustainable urban mobility? 
 
Which set of indicators best describe sustainable urban mobility? 
==PAGE:17==
4 
 
 
How to communicate sustainable urban mobility into a single index? 
Involvement of stakeholders may contribute to the selection process and prioritization of indicators.  A 
part of the research concerns also the development of a quantitative sustainable index methodology. The 
index should be clear and easy to understand as it can serve as a tool for demonstrating the performance 
to stakeholders and politicians and also be able to use it over the years and change of data. The approach 
will be qualitative but also quantitative as the framework, after developed, will be applied in a real 
context. 
Methodology 
The methodology followed in this report for the development of the sustainable urban mobility index is 
based on four main steps as illustrated in the following Figure 2: 
 
Theoretical background on green mobility. As a first step, “sustainable urban mobility” is defined 
as well as the components and factors accounting for it based on the existing relevant literature.  
The main domains describing sustainable urban mobility are presented as a compilation and result 
of the existing research on the subject. 
 
Framework for the development of the mobility index; Investigation on existing frameworks 
developing sustainable indicators will lead to the framework upon which the formation of the 
mobility index will be based. The chosen framework is then described including boundaries and 
definition of criteria. 
 
Analysis and prioritization; this step requires the completion of the two previous steps and 
includes the process under which the final list of indicators will be formed. Prioritization of the 
indicators involves the application of the chosen criteria and the creation of a questionnaire 
addressed to relevant stakeholders.  
 
Application of the framework to Copenhagen municipality; the framework developed will be 
applied for Copenhagen municipality. This includes the collection of the necessary data for the 
chosen indicators, the results and the evaluation of the municipality’s performance. In the end, 
there will be an attempt for aggregating the indicators into a single index as well as a discussion 
on the results and chosen framework. 
 
These steps are not independent from each other and not necessarily implemented in this order during 
this whole process; several interplays occur among them. For example, the first step could be a part of 
==PAGE:18==
5 
 
the second meaning that the definition of mobility is also a part of the chosen framework. However, it 
was preferred that the definition of the research topic to be stated at the very beginning. Moreover, each 
step is necessary in order for the next step to take place. Some steps also require to look back or forth for 
their completion. An iteration process is taking place until the point when the final list of sustainable urban 
mobility indicators is formed. During the whole process, new information at several stages may change 
the list initially developed.

2. The concept of sustainable urban mobility 
When it comes to measuring sustainable mobility it is quite fruitless to do without knowing what does 
sustainable mobility stands for. For this purpose, an attempt is being made to conclude on a definition of 
sustainable urban mobility as a synthesis and inspiration from the relevant literature on mobility. In the 
end, an operational definition is needed because the concept must be in principle observable and 
measurable. 
The approach to the definition of mobility depends on the context it belongs to. Mobility is hereby defined 
within the context of an urban environment under the perspective of developed countries. It is also 
targeted to people although mobility can also refer to movement of goods. The intention is to define 
mobility in order to find indicators that can describe it and be able to measure and compare the 
performance of an urban area among years. Finally, mobility is oriented towards achieving urban 
sustainability and thus it is researched what are the factors, indicators and definition describing 
sustainable urban mobility. 
Etymology 
Linguistically speaking, it is interesting to know that the -ity suffix of a noun usually means the quality, 
state or condition of being what the adjective describes (Dictionary.com, 2015). The word “mobility” 
therefore implies the quality, state or condition of being “mobile”. The origin of the word mobile derives 
from Latin mobilis which means movable, easy to move; loose, not firm. Mobile can be defined as “capable 
of moving or being moved” (Merriam-Webster, 2003) or “having freedom of movement” (Dictionary.com, 
2015) or “movable”. Etymologically, “mobility” is used among others to indicate capacity for motion, 
activity or speed.  
Potentiality 
It is broadly agreed among the relevant literature that mobility expresses a possibility or else potentiality. 
According to Berge et al. (1992) mobility is “the potential to move around and not the movement itself”. 
Kronbak (1997) and COWI (1998) also define mobility as the capability to be moved, depending on the 
easiness with which individuals can move around (cited in DTU Transport, 2014). Sager (2005) implies that 
potentiality is an inherent part of mobility which then defines as the ease with which a person can move 
==PAGE:21==
8 
 
through space (cited in Martens et al., 2012). Mobility is also referred as the possibility of being able to 
“move about” (DTU Transport, 2014). 
Easiness 
Another element often mentioned when describing mobility is the ease of movement. Mobility is related 
with how easy is to travel. The key aspects of “easy” could be fast, cheap, safe and comfortable, since 
these parameters are the most common obstacles for our potential mobility (DTU Transport, 2014). A 
comprehensive approach on the definition of mobility was made by the Department of Transport of 
Technical University of Denmark (2014) where mobility was defined as “an expression for how fast or far 
you can move geographically by the use of the transport system in a safe, comfortable and affordable 
way”. It is also stressed that priority should be given to the “fast” attribute. This definition summarizes 
many of the characteristics of mobility found in the literature. 
With a more simple view on mobility Litman (2008) refers to it as a physical movement measured by trips, 
distance and speed with possible indicators to be the average traffic speed or congestion delay. The 
author claims that all else being equal, increased mobility increases accessibility; the more and faster 
people can travel the more destinations they can reach. However, this approach does not incorporate any 
sustainable aspect in it.

described in order to set the boundaries and clarify the differences among these terms.

Returning to mobility, sustainable mobility should not relate with the physical movement itself; traveling 
more does not necessarily mean that mobility is increasing in a sustainable way. Towards supporting 
sustainable urban mobility it is essential to develop measures to reduce demand for travel rather than 
continuing to emphasize on measures which seek to increase the number of trips or minimize travel time 
(Fudge, 1996). The challenge is to satisfy the same demand for travel having less negative impacts of the 
transport which implies more effective way of moving around. This may refer to several aspects like the 
infrastructure utilization, shifting on greener modes or greener technologies.  
It is a fact though that when mobility is higher people tend to move around more, so the potential to 
move leads to actual movement in the end. This points out the importance of how people move around, 
for example with which modes of transport. It is a matter of evaluating an increase in the quality of trips 
and not the absolute number of trips itself. 
Fast trips versus congestion 
A lot of focus is given on moving faster as a determinant component of better mobility. Historically higher 
travel speed and increasing wealth (and consequently car-ownership) has continuously raised mobility 
and generated more traffic. Today, too much traffic compared to infrastructure capacity, named 
congestion, increasingly limits our mobility. Congestion often constrains urban-peak vehicle travel, 
limiting mobility and thus accessibility. However, efforts to increase automobile traffic speeds and 
volumes can reduce other forms of accessibility by constraining pedestrian travel and stimulating more 
dispersed, automobile-oriented development patterns (Litman, 2008). 
The “faster” component can be also translated as decreasing travel time or distance and consequently 
increased speed. Traveling is often regarded as a “must do” process, a necessity or a quite unpleasant 
experience during the day which is mandatory in order reach certain activities. A traveller would like to 
have shorter or faster trips to make this “process” last less. What if commuting was the most interesting 
part of the day? Then maybe a traveler would be more interested about the reliability or punctuality of 
the trip or even the quality of the trip or the safety instead. In the end, it has also been observed that a 
great part of the time savings resulting from an improvement in transport services is used again for 
traveling.  
==PAGE:24==
11 
 
At the same time, economic constraints clearly also play an important role. After all, it is practically free 
to bike or walk, but our access to faster modes of transport can be more valuable to us and be sometimes 
limited by additional costs. But the importance of the price will depend on the individual financial capacity 
(DTU Transport, 2014) which implies a necessity for a fairly priced transport system. 
It is questionable whether or not reducing congestion will lead to higher mobility, when this implies 
improving infrastructure in favour of cars for example by widening roads. Less congestion should not 
attract more trips but on the contrary, actions to reduce trips may have as a result less congestion and 
higher level of service during a trip. A contradiction also exists among transport policies about whether 
increasing or decreasing congestion is desirable. It seems like an optimal level of congestion is required, 
in order to keep the balance between the level of service for the road users and the environmental and 
safety issues related with slower traffic (Banister, 2008).  
This brings us to the vicious circle of congestion (Figure 55). When traffic exceeds the limits of the 
infrastructure capacity the solution is often to increase the capacity by creating new infrastructure. This 
has as a consequence for new trips to arise as people can travel more for the same amount of time as 
previously. A great proportion of these time savings is again used for new trips often to destinations that 
are further away. The impact will be to have additional traffic, that means more trips and consequently 
more congestion which will restrict mobility again. The more the capacity the more the traffic will be. In 
a behavioural manner, if congestion is a common state at specific road segments then people may switch 
to alternative ways of traveling like public transport, cycling or even walking.

A solution to congestion can be a more optimal use of the existing infrastructure and the urban space as 
a whole. Urban space is limited and thus valuable, so we have to make the more efficient use out of it. 
More efficient use of existing infrastructure can lead to the same benefits as if creating new. Ciuffini (1995) 
argues that what matters is to regain the spatial equilibrium that has been lost between the different 
modes of transport; to take back the space we gave to cars so generously and distribute it to walking and 
cycling again. The excess of car-mobility nowadays and the lack of alternative options causes in fact non-
mobility (Ciuffini, 1995). The orientation should be in increasing mobility for alternative modes of 
transport, as space is not enough to host this disproportional and ineffective way of space utilization cars 
require (Figure 66). This also implies that we have to increase personal mobility without increasing vehicle 
mobility, for example with higher vehicle occupancy (Litman, 2008).

At an urban scale the increase of mobility due to a car does not improve accessibility (as result of mobility) 
but rather worsens it; it disrupts overall viability, compromises economic life, and degrades the quality of 
life and the environment. Pedestrianism is a measure of quality of life and in this direction if services are 
getting closer, are more mixed and dense then more people will walk (Ciuffini, 1995).  
The “sustainable” part of mobility is well summarized by Banister (2008) who concluded that “the 
sustainable mobility approach requires actions to reduce the need to travel, to encourage modal shift, to 
reduce trip lengths and to encourage greater efficiency in the transport system”. A similar point of view 
is presented by Holden et al. (2013) who claim that in order to achieve high mobility three approaches 
can be adopted; travel more efficiently, travel differently and travel less: 
==PAGE:26==
13 
 
1. Reduce the need to travel – substitution. This measure is about reducing trips by replacing them 
with a non-travel activity or substituted with the help of Information and Communication 
Technologies (ICT).  
2. Encourage modal shift. Encourage and increase walking, cycling and the use of public transport 
instead of cars. This measure also includes reallocation of space from cars to greener modes of 
transport and more effective use of the available capacity or released space through demand 
management. Here new mobility services like bike-sharing, car-sharing or ride-sharing are crucial. 
3. Reduce trip lengths. This action relates to land-use policy measures and more efficient use of 
urban space as mentioned previously, with increasing densities, concentration and mixed land-
use development. Transit oriented development (TOD), car-free development and city design are 
some of the practices which can lead in distance reduction regarding trips. 
4. Greater efficiency in the transport system which implies among others that the energy 
consumption for transport should be reduced covering the same or more travel distance (Holden 
et. al, 2013).  
The 3 dimensions of sustainable mobility 
In the context of sustainable development and the three pillars supporting it, Zegras (2005) defines 
sustainable mobility as the one that “maintains the capability to provide non-declining accessibility in 
time” and recognizes mobility as a throughput. The author argues that sustainable mobility can be thought 
of as a balancing act between the desire to expand accessibility, which builds human capital, and the need 
to maintain capital stocks which implies the scarcity of resources as well. “A more sustainable mobility 
system provides more welfare (utility) per unit of throughput (capital drain)”. Here accessibility represents 
the welfare or else utility that people derive from the transportation and land use system, and capability 
implies a minimum level at least similar to the one we enjoy today (Zegras 2005). 
Ciuffini (1995) locates mobility into the three-pillar framework and proposes to measure the quality of 
mobility by three different costs: social costs meaning the cost of equity, environmental cost by means of 
environmental damage and generalized costs like costs driven by congestion and other direct costs. As an 
additional dimension to those, he proposes the urban scale, according to which the indicators should be 
defined. 
Mobility poses strong social implications as well. It creates value for the society in terms of the ability to 
reach activities. Therefore mobility shouldn’t be a privilege but a right independent of social status or 
==PAGE:27==
14 
 
geographic circumstances. Ciuffini (1995) defines mobility as a characteristic of people, of social 
categories, and of the society in general that depends on the lifestyle of a population and its aspirations. 
Any action affecting mobility therefore interferes with economic and social activities, and must be 
measured carefully in relation to those factors. The author also points out the importance of public 
perception for identifying solutions that are "acceptable" to a society. 
The availability or un-availability of transport shapes people’s life opportunities (Lucas, 2006). Ιt 
determines whether a person can take advantage of education and health services, can access job markets 
(Ihlanfeldt and Sjoquist, 1998; Ong and Blumenberg, 1998) and thus advance economically, can keep in 
contact with friends or family, or whether she or he can enjoy leisure and recreational facilities (Frank et 
al., 2006) (cited in Martens et. al., 2012). 
High mobility is attractive because it gives options for activities which may result in a more vibrant 
economy. The challenge is to have the least impact on the environment at the same time. Higher mobility 
and not necessarily car-mobility can bring growth which is something that stakeholders have to realize 
too. 
Sperling (2015) points out that we need a more robust transportation system with more choices and 
options of transport. He claims that new mobility options can capture 30% of passenger travel and 
increase transit ridership by providing among others first mile – last mile service. Such mobility options 
supported by ITS, for instance mobile apps or online services, include car-pooling, mode sharing, dynamic 
ride sharing or smart paratransit.  
European Environment Agency (2013) relates urban transport within the prism of quality of life, air 
pollution and climate change mitigation.  It is argued that quality of life is affected by noise, road accidents, 
traffic congestion, social inequalities and land take associated with transport. The report also stresses the 
underlying factors determining urban passenger transport patterns to be the size, density and design of 
urban forms, the quality and provision of transport infrastructure, socio-economic factors and costs of 
transport. Options to minimize impacts include avoiding the need to travel by land use and planning (i.e. 
compact cities, mixed land uses, urban realm design), information and communications technology, 
consolidating supply and demand or access management. Supporting modal shift, improving modal 
efficiency and with sustainable urban mobility plans transportation patterns may improve.

3. Framework and indicators of sustainable 
urban mobility 
How is it possible to find the appropriate indicators that best describe sustainable urban mobility? Is there 
an ideal framework, and how feasible is to use it in practice? 
Frameworks and indicators are essential in order to achieve sustainable transport. There is a need to gain 
knowledge and measure such a complex concept. In addition, it is essential to select relevant information 
which can capture the most important issues to be measured. Even if an element cannot be expressed 
directly, approximate variables can give an indication of the situation. Frameworks are useful for 
organizing sustainability indicators in some logical fashion. The current framework incorporates 
conceptual, operational and utilization aspects as different but connected parts of it (Cornet & 
Gudmundsson, forthcoming). 
In order to represent and measure sustainable mobility a set of measures is required as opposed to a 
single one. Frameworks are needed to compose the proper set of indicators, setting the basis for this 
process of sustainable urban mobility indicators construction. There are various ways to frame indicators 
or else structure a framework and define domains of measurement. Such frameworks include for instance 
the triple bottom line approach comprising of the environment, economy and social dimension, the DPSIR 
framework or other frameworks based on rationally defined themes of measurement.  
The DPSIR framework  
The following Figure 8 illustrates the DPSIR framework as an example of a causal framework. Mobility is 
presented under the DPSIR framework, a theory driven framework used by the European Environment 
Agency. 
The logic of this DPSIR framework can be described as follows: 
 
Driving forces are underlying factors influencing a variety of relevant variables, deriving from 
social and economic development. Driving force indicators, used alone, are not very responsive 
to change e.g. the number of cars per inhabitant. 
 
Pressure indicators describe the variables which directly cause or may cause environmental 
problems. Pressure indicators should be responsive and point directly at the causes of problems 
==PAGE:31==
18 
 
e.g. CO2 emissions or noise caused by road traffic, space required for cars, waste produced by 
scrap cars.  
 
State indicators show the current condition of the environment. State indicators can serve to 
make a first assessment of the current situation and are often too slow e.g. the noise levels near 
main roads, global mean temperature.  
 
Impact indicators describe the resulting effects of changes of state. Impact indicators react even 
slower than state indicators because it is usually too late for action when the impacts are 
observed. The main purpose of impact indicators is to demonstrate DPSIR patterns and cause-
effect relations e.g. mortality due to noise-induced heart attacks, number of people starving due 
to climate-change induced crop losses.  
 
Response indicators demonstrate the efforts of society to solve the problems. Response 
indicators are very fast, since they monitor the measures which are intended to make the slow 
socio-economic system move e.g. cars with catalytic converters, maximum allowed noise levels 
for cars, price levels of gasoline. 
 
Bell and Morse (2008) present DPSIR indicators across time showing that response measures can 
contribute during a project while pressure is continuously affecting the state, creating impacts along the 
way. The authors relate sustainability to four domains of practice and the use of sustainability indicators 
as well; what is, what could be, delivering a project and the deliverer of the project who is instituting

==PAGE:32==

sustainability. The state is defined by “what is and what could be”, the pressure and response by “the 
deliverer” and the drivers and impact by the “deliverable”. 
It is well understood that DPSIR framework is dynamic in the sense that indicators interrelate and the 
variables that they may represent can affect or shape others, creating the need of a reexamination when 
looking the DPSIR framework as a whole. According to the purpose of the framework, different kind of 
indicators in DPSIR categories, can be selected which will reflect the respective need. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The three pillar framework 
The three pillar or else triple bottom line framework is domain based, incorporating all of the three 
dimensions of sustainability; economy, society and environment. These three dimensions of sustainability 
can be considered as the basis of the framework and indicators for the assessment of a sustainable 
transportation system can be assigned to those.

choice of transport modes or efficiency of transport systems, affects both the economy and the society. 
In the same way, a balanced regional development can bring prosperity which will then have an impact 
on the population as well. Moreover, a change in one dimension may have an effect on the others. The

3.1 The framework for sustainable urban mobility 
Each framework has competitive advantages and disadvantages. A superior or catholic logic may not exist 
however a starting and reference point is necessary. The structure of the current framework was chosen 
and formulated to fit with the theory and important aspects on sustainable urban mobility. The current 
indicator framework is theory driven based on the concept of sustainable urban mobility as this was 
defined under the 4 basic domains identified. It was concluded that this structure was more appropriate 
for the specific topic compared to other frameworks. The framework will set up the basis for developing 
the set of indicators able to measure sustainable urban mobility.  
3.1.1 Purpose, boundaries and approach 
The purpose of the framework and set of indicators researched hereby is to present a review of the 
current situation, to measure how the current mobility pattern is performing. The intention of this 
framework derived from the necessity to achieve sustainable mobility, the importance of which has 
already been stressed. It is crucial that we measure the current performance to know where we are 
heading and to change direction if necessary. The framework is mainly addressed to stakeholders and 
experts in the transport policy field or people involved in the achievement of sustainable mobility goals. 
The methodology used here for identifying sustainable urban mobility indicators follows a top-down 
approach. This means that the set of indicators is a result of a research of what is to be measured; the 
theoretical background, the meaning and factors interacting with the essence under research. The

definition of the concept of sustainable urban mobility helps to diagnose problems and can lead to the

The conceptual dimension of the framework is often omitted while is rather essential; in order to measure 
sustainable urban mobility we have to know first what it means. At this point, it should be defined what 
is to be measured with indicators, in this case Sustainable Urban Mobility as it was described before. For 
more transparency, a definition of sustainable urban mobility was developed as a result of a literature 
review on the topic (see Chapter 2 for detailed analysis on the concept). Reminding here the definition 
developed; sustainable urban mobility is supported by a well-designed and utilized urban space, with 
efficient performance of transport service and modes, satisfying the needs of users while minimizing 
negative effects. 
Within the goal of achieving sustainable urban mobility the boundaries of the framework can be set. Bell 
and Morse (2008) stress the importance of space and time to be addressed in the attempt to measure 
sustainability issues. The purpose of this framework is to monitor sustainable urban mobility in a 
developed urban environment able to measure and monitor progress across time. The “advanced” level 
of development, targeted also to the transport system, plays an important role here because critical 
aspects for a developing society may not be important for a developed one; investments on new road 
infrastructure is more crucial for a civilization that lacks in road network. The urban aspect is also good to 
bear in mind as it differs from the regional level or even national where the indicators will reflect a 
different scale and range of impacts.  
Even when restricted to a city or municipality where the spatial boundaries are clear, the system is still 
complex as it is not independent, interacting with the nearby areas and the rural environment. Sustainable 
urban mobility is influenced and depends on external factors and travel patterns occurring outside of a 
specified area like a municipality. 
The set of indicators should be able to measure progress over time which means that data should be 
collected in a frequent rate and be available to the interest parties. This can be at least in a yearly basis or 
less if data is unavailable for certain indicators. Throughout a specific time period an indicator could 
change values such as that the attribute measured could be more, less or not sustainable at all. So, a 
reference point in time is needed to measure progress and in the scope of this framework to compare 
performance between years. Different indicators may require more or less frequent measurement 
depending on the necessity or the actual ability to measure it; different components of sustainable urban 
==PAGE:35==
22 
 
mobility may best be measured in different time frames. However, in such a framework, all indicators 
should be measured in the same time scale in order to obtain consistency.  
In addition, the framework entails both intra and intergenerational time scale regarding the impact 
assessment which depends on each indicator; CO2 emissions refer to longer term impacts compared to 
particulate matter pollution.  
This context can lead to the determination of criteria for the selection of the indicators. The criteria were 
sought among those most frequently used in the relevant literature (Maclaren, 1996; Marsden et al., 
2005; Castillo and Pitfield, 2010; Joumard and Gudmundsson, 2010) and selected as those to be more 
appropriate for the purpose of this framework: 
 Relevance with the concept 
 Ability to measure what is defined – validity 
 Clear in interpretation – easy to communicate 
 Acceptable by the stakeholders 
 Availability and quality of data 
 Adaptive to change over time 
 Able to form an index – limited in number 
Among these criteria there is at least one for each of the three basic categories of criteria as defined also 
by Joumard and Gudmundsson (2010) referring to representation, operation and application.  According 
to these categories, the indicators should accurately represent an impact, be operational in the sense that 
it is practical to monitor and be applicable and useful for decision making applications. Some criteria were 
not included here since they were not relevant. The criterion regarding “ethical concerns” is not applicable 
in this framework as the indicators are not related to fundamental human rights, morals or beliefs. 
Irrelevant were also considered the criteria “comparable with other indicators” or “attractive to the 
media”, with the latter to be more a concern of frameworks addressed to media. In addition, a shorter list 
of criteria makes it easier in practice as well. 
Some of the criteria finally selected are fundamental for sustainability reporting and others very much 
related to the specific current purpose. The criterion “limited in number” is specifically for composing a 
whole set of indicators to index, although contrasting with the need to be comprehensive. The indicators 
should be also easy to communicate as they are addressed to stakeholders involved in the decision making 
==PAGE:36==
23 
 
process where simplicity is often necessary. The relevance with the concept is also a prerequisite for an 
indicator to be selected as well as the availability of data, criteria often difficult to fulfil in practice. 
The success of the framework will depend among others on the available data, the goals of the particular 
community, or even the different interests among stakeholders. Compromises are often needed and the 
framework will therefore adapt to the practical obstacles that may arise. In order for the framework to be 
used “there needs to be a compromise between technical excellence and a form of presentation that 
allows for easy assimilation” (Bell and Morse, 2008) which points out the importance of the utilization 
dimension of the framework.

various indicators were incorporated in the four areas of sustainable urban mobility identified; urban 
space, transport service and modes, passability and effects (Figure 7). This process allowed for a re-
examination of the concept of sustainable urban mobility as well, meaning that indicators added a value 
in terms of some additional elements not identified in the theory of the concept.

sustainable transportation or mobility conducted in recent years.

however it was not included in the list since ecosystems rarely exist in urban areas.

Some indicators were also inconsistent with the criterion referring to the ability of measurement or easy 
to understand. Urban fragmentation was one of them, for which further explanation was not given by the 
author, and it was considered as a hard indicator to measure. A further research on the specific indicator 
gave however more light. Ortega et al. (2014) defined fragmentation in a transport system as the lack of 
connectivity between places mainly due to infrastructure, reflecting easiness for pedestrian movement. 
Another similar term is the barrier effect defined as the limitation or delays of non-motorized travel 
caused by roads which depends on the speed, traffic volume, width of the road and crossings as well as 
on the need to cross the street depending on the intensity of the land use close to the road (Barfod et al., 
2012). 
It has to be mentioned that several elements can be expressed by more than one indicator. For example, 
congestion can be expressed by hours lost in queues, travel time or speed variability, lane occupancy rates 
or qualitatively through user satisfaction surveys reflecting acceptable levels of travel time and delays. 
Moreover, the list of indicators gathered contains overlaps which will be clarified further on when defining 
the final list of the appropriate indicators. A description of the main issues under each one of the four 
domains presented in Table 2 follows below. 
Domain 1: Urban Space 
This domain refers to the synergy of urban and transportation planning in the sense that urban planning 
can shape transportation patterns and vice versa. Recalling the land-use transport feedback cycle (Figure 
3), the organization and structure of urban areas determines the location of activities and consequently 
influences transport demand. This domain consists of urban design characteristics like density or mix of 
land uses, density of population and density of transportation network. In addition, accessibility related 
to activities and means of transport as an end result of mobility, as well as utilization of urban space is 
included here. It has to be noted that accessibility to means of transport can be interpreted in terms of 
distance (proximity), ownership or even availability of transport mode sharing services. Accessibility is 
here referring to the proximity to transportation services, which can be transport modes or network. For 
instance, this can be indicated by the distance from main roads, cycle paths, pedestrian area or public 
transport stops and stations. The urban space domain reflects among others the potentiality and 
availability as an inherent part of mobility. 
Domain 2: Transport service and modes 
This field takes into account how transport services are performing including different modes of urban 
land transport. It is about trends regarding volume and energy consumption in modal split, capacity 
==PAGE:43==
30 
 
utilization both of infrastructure and occupancy rates of vehicles, and transport costs reflecting 
attractiveness of different modes. A comparison between modes can be included here between private 
and public modes or motorized versus non-motorized transportation reflecting efficiency as well as 
attractiveness in terms of cost. In the same manner, transport volume should be assessed in modal split, 
as an increase of volume may indicate positive or negative evolution depending on which mode it refers 
to. Trip reduction as a measure was found to be essential which can be represented by the substitution 
of need to travel. Intelligent transport systems (ITS) in supply of services like traffic management can be 
also a characteristic of transport services. 
Domain 3: Passability 
This domain reflects how users perceive their experience on transport services including level of service 
characteristics. It is human oriented stressing among others the social aspect of mobility. It represents 
elements reflecting easiness as a part of mobility and user-friendliness which can refer to ITS user 
applications like travel cards. This area includes level of service characteristics like speed and travel time, 
congestion or delay. As with other attributes, congestion is not only related to the quality of transport 
services provided to users, but also with energy consumption, pollution, noise and cost. It is however 
considered here to be more of a level of service-related element connected to variability in travel times. 
Moreover, amenities for people with restricted mobility and quality of services, for example reliability or 
punctuality, are included in this domain. Prices and cost of transport services, for example as a share of 
total income, reflects affordability by users although is a transport mode characteristic if measured as a 
net amount. In addition, measuring transport prices per mode for the same trip allows for a comparison 
of the competitive advantage of different modes which may also reflect their attractiveness which is again 
a transport mode characteristic included in “Transport service and modes” domain. At specific cases, like 
with transport prices, it is a matter of defining the appropriate indicator.  
Domain 4: Effects 
Effects relate mostly with negative impacts of mobility. This category includes noise, air pollution and 
accidents in transport sector, degradation of landscape and consumption of natural resources with energy 
consumption as a primary input. Here, energy consumption is evaluating treatment of natural resources 
and not mode efficiency as noted before. Air pollution is further categorized to human related and climate 
impact emissions.

The method chosen which will lead to the prioritization of the first list of indicators presented in the 
previous Chapter (Table 2) is based on a survey addressed to people with experience with the transport 
sector. 
Involving participation to such a procedure has the strength of transparency providing a more integrated 
view on which are the most important indicators. In practice, this procedure involves complexity in 
matters such as how stakeholders can be represented out of a broad group or how to avoid capturing 
issues by powerful interests (Bell, 2008). Stakeholder analysis is often used for understanding the 
dynamics and guiding policy measures whereas the scope here is to incorporate different opinions from 
stakeholders which are involved in such matters. 
Stakeholders are people or organizations who either stand to be affected by the project or could influence 
the project’s success. They may be winners or losers, have a say or not in the decision-making process, 
users of results or participants in the process (The World Bank Group, 2001). It is expected that individuals 
and groups will have different perception of project goals and purposes and may well have a significantly 
different view on what constitutes sustainability. Outside of the current scope, a system-level 
collaboration between all stakeholders of the mobility ecosystem is crucial in order to come up with 
innovative and integrated models leading to sustainability practices (Van Audenhove et al., 2014). 
It is quite important to take into account different opinions from relevant stakeholders who may have an 
interest or influence on the research topic. Each stakeholder has different power and possible influence. 
One could say that the opinion of stakeholders who are placed higher in the power hierarchy should 
matter more, as their influence will be greater. In fact, it is often not the case that people with authority 
take the right decisions, partly because of inherent interests or absence of the necessary knowledge and 
awareness on the problem. Assigning weights to the different stakeholder groups is not an objective task 
and thus difficult to apply. It is hereby assumed that all the stakeholder groups have an equal importance. 
Nonetheless, it is up to the user’s will to assign different weights when applying the current framework 
and come up with a different list of the relevant and important indicators to assess. 
==PAGE:45==
32 
 
The process for selecting the most important indicators includes the construction of a questionnaire which 
is to be answered by the relevant stakeholders identified. The scope of the questionnaire is not to choose 
the best decision for a new project that participatory process often involves but to give importance and 
prioritize the different indicators. It is a matter of having the best tools, reflected by the most appropriate 
indicators, for measuring current performance and progress for the sake of pursuing sustainable mobility. 
The ultimate objective is to be used as a basis and give light for improvement or changes. 
4.1 Selected stakeholders 
Four main groups of stakeholders were identified plus one virtual stakeholder representing the future 
generation’s perspective. The stakeholders were identified based on their relevance to transportation 
issues and power or influence to related decisions (Figure 11). The purpose of the questionnaire required 
knowledge in transportation and the participants were selected accordingly.  
It was for example considered not so relevant to include end users who, although experiencing transport 
services, may not judge the importance of indicators to be measured in a rational way. This kind of survey 
addressed to citizens also entailed obstacles such as the language, time limitations as well as the risk of 
having answers of people who are not familiar with the topic and relevant terms. If the case was to 
evaluate the performance of transport service, the opinion of the citizens would matter more. It was 
therefore considered that the relevant stakeholders are the following: 
a) Higher education, university or research community and consultants. This group has a high level of 
knowledge regarding transportation issues. It relates more to background education, than kind of 
interests. In this group are also included academic experts in consultancy firms. Students in transportation 
education were excluded from this group in order to target people with more experience in the field.  The 
degree of influence and importance of this stakeholder group is questionable. When it comes to 
consultants the degree of influence can be high but the degree of importance is relatively low in terms of 
losing or gaining from a decision or project in this field. 
b) Transport authorities have a high degree of influence and importance as well. This stakeholder may 
take or affect decisions and the results are very probable to have an impact on them. Their interests 
related to transport policies are therefore great. 
==PAGE:46==
33 
 
c) Transport service providers are responsible for the operation or supply of transport services and most 
commonly they don’t affect decisions in the same degree as transport authorities which however may 
stand an obstacle or be beneficial for them. 
d) Non-governmental organizations (NGO’s) are selected and divided into business sector, green 
organizations and organizations involved with social matters. It is a group covering a broad range of 
different perspectives which may be contradicting. Although not an attribute for all NGO’s, actions of this 
group can affect decision making processes and they can, to a certain degree gain or lose from some 
decisions. Some of the NGO’s also indirectly support citizens’ perspective e.g. organizations involved with 
social matters or environmental concerns. 
e) Future generations is an iconic stakeholder representing the needs and perspective of upcoming 
generations with a focus on issues often neglected when powerful interests are involved. This virtual 
stakeholder doesn’t have the ability to affect policy processes but they stand to lose or gain significantly 
from them as it can be seen as the group representing all of the rest stakeholder groups. The estimation 
of their perspective is elaborated later on.

4.2 The questionnaire 
For the questionnaire the elements for prioritization were selected from the initial list of indicators 
presented in Table 2 in the previous Chapter. Not all the elements were included in detail but the most 
representative from each domain and category which were also easy to understand and communicate 
complying with some of the criteria defined. In addition, some of the elements could be translated directly 
to indicators and some others were flexible in terms of interpretation or choice of different indicators to 
be expressed by. Similarly to the initial list of indicators, the questionnaire included some indicators that 
were overlapping; this could prove some consistency among the answers, meaning that overlapping 
indicators should receive the same level of importance, but on the other hand it could be misleading for 
the participant as well. 
The participants were approached by e-mail and could choose either to fill in the questionnaire in a 
Microsoft Word Document and return it back via e-mail or follow a link to an online survey with the exact 
same content (Appendix I). The questionnaire was only sent to stakeholders in Copenhagen area and it 
was both in Danish and English. Out of the 130 questionnaires sent in total, 42 responded validly out of 
which 7 were returned as a word file and the rest 35 were completed online. Out of the total responses 
31 were from academics, 6 from NGO’s (2 from the business sector and 4 from green organizations), 3 
from Transport authorities and 2 from Transport service providers. In addition, 7 participants were asked 
to represent future generation’s perspective out of which 6 responded back. Incomplete answers were 
not included in the analysis.

For the assessment of the importance of the different elements it was used a typical ordinal scale; very 
important, important, moderately important, of little importance and unimportant plus the “I don’t know” 
choice of answer. The values of the chosen scale express an order or else sequence. The distance between 
the scales can be perceived differently by each participant or even the distance could be unequal among 
all the scales. For example, the difference between “of little importance” and “moderately important” 
could not be the same as the one between “important” and “very important”. However, for the purpose 
of this research the scale will be considered to be equidistant but will be also assessed more thoroughly 
checking dispersion of answers for different elements. 
In order to prioritize the different elements a weight of 4, 3, 2, 1 and 0 is given to the very important, 
important, moderately important, of little importance and unimportant answers respectively while “I 
don’t know answers” were excluded. This method is an estimation of the ranking among the elements 
and more responses and a greater range for the scale would have perhaps generated more complete

4.3.4 Future generations’ perspective  
The last major stakeholder group consists of the view and perspective of future generations. This group, 
as a sustainability advocate, represents a strong viewpoint on the longer-term impacts and benefits 
regarding sustainability. This “virtual” stakeholder should also have a say on the importance of mobility 
indicators taking into consideration its interests. If we consider the “sustainable” perspective of the 
indicators, future generation’s perspective has a crucial role towards this direction. In addition, the 
impacts of the transport sector in particular, are very often long running.  
The importance of this virtual stakeholder is easily perceived if we recall the commonly agreed definition 
on sustainable development also known as the Brundtland definition which states that “sustainable 
development is development that meets the need of the present generation, without compromising the 
ability of future generations to meet their own needs” (World Commission on Environment and 
Development, 1987). More specifically about sustainable transport, the perspective of future generations 
is also stressed in the definition of a sustainable transport system developed by the European Council 
(2001) which is the one that:  
• 
allows the basic access and development needs of individuals, companies and societies to be met 
safely and in a manner consistent with human and ecosystem health, and promotes equity within 
and between successive generations; 
• 
is affordable, operates fairly and efficiently, offers choice of transport modes, and supports a 
competitive economy, as well as balanced regional development; 
• 
limits emissions and waste within the planet's ability to absorb them, uses renewable resources 
at or below their rates of generation and uses non-renewable resources at or below the rates of 
development of renewable substitutes, while minimising the impact on the use of land and the 
generation of noise. 
Narrowing down a bit more, the assessment can be also based on the definition on sustainable urban 
mobility as it was formulated in Chapter 2.  
In order to “model” the future generation’s view on the importance of different elements describing 
sustainable urban mobility, it is sought how the desired future should look like for this stakeholder. If we 
envision a certain desired future scenario, we can apply backcasting and determine how this future can 
be attained (Holmberg & Robèrt, 2000). The environment provides our society with essential services that 
sustain human society. Successive future generations should be able to use these services as well as we 
==PAGE:55==
42 
 
do. The perspective of future generations would stress longer-term impacts like climate change, 
biodiversity loss, resource overconsumption or social equity. Although most impacts pose long-term 
implications as well. 
Adequate transport service is of crucial importance for society. Transport is strongly related to basic 
values, like the freedom to move, which may have an impact on the accessibility to goods and services 
necessary for the human well-being. As a consequence, this access can improve the welfare of the society 
as well. In addition, long term negative implications on communities and nature relate to land take from 
transport infrastructure, degradation of landscape, but also barrier effects for people and animals.  
Long-term impacts can be viewed from 30 years up to 100 years or more corresponding to at least 3 
generations. For transport, these impacts relate to among others, infrastructure, energy use such as fossil 
fuels and other non- renewable resources, and climate change (Van Wee, 2010). Overconsumption of 
non-renewable resources may leave future generations with fewer possibilities to fulfill their needs. Hood 
(2003) argues that “climate change raises issues of equity and justice because it is expected that climate 
change will not affect all people or all countries the same” (cited in Van Wee, 2010). From an 
intergenerational perspective, it is important to know what the current generation would leave for the 
future, not only in terms of non-renewable resources but also man-made resources, such as buildings and 
transport infrastructure, electricity or water facilities. 
A strong sustainability point of view is obtained when elements of the environmental, social, and 
economic dimensions are not in isolation from each other; the economy exists within society, or is a 
product of social interaction, and both society and the economy depend upon the environment. This 
implies that if human activity exceeds the carrying capacity of the environment, this outcome must affect 
social well-being and the economy (Gudmundsson et al.). 
The Natural Step organization has set four sustainability principles of success which can preserve a 
sustainable society (Figure 15). These principles derive from the respective four system conditions 
necessary for sustainability. Targeted to transportation the four principles can presented as follows: 
1. Concentrations of substances extracted from the Earth’s crust must not systematically increase in 
nature; transport relies on fossil fuels over than 90% with oil as the primary fuel source. Metals 
like aluminum and steel are widely used for infrastructure and vehicles. 
==PAGE:56==
43 
 
2. Concentrations of substances produced by society must not systematically increase in nature; the 
burning of fossil fuels is a major source for greenhouse gas emissions. Synthetic materials, plastic, 
toxics and new compounds are used or produced for the function of the transport system. 
3. Nature must not be subject to systematically increasing degradation by physical means; transport 
infrastructure degrades and fragments ecosystems, occupies land, guides urban development and 
pollutes air and water.  
4. In society, people are not subject to conditions that systematically undermine their capacity to 
meet their needs; air pollution and road accidents from transport have serious impacts on human 
health. High fares or unequal access or choice to transport services can lead to injustice and non-
fulfillment of certain human needs. 
In the research entitled “Planetary boundaries: Guiding human development on a changing planet” there 
are identified 9 planetary boundaries within which humanity can operate safely; climate change, novel 
entities, stratospheric ozone depletion, atmospheric aerosol loading, ocean acidification, biochemical 
flows, global freshwater use, land system change and biosphere integrity. It is estimated that two of them 
are beyond the zone of uncertainty meaning that high risk is involved: genetic diversity as a part of 
biosphere integrity and both phosphorus and nitrogen consisting biochemical flows. In addition, land 
system change and climate change have also exceeded the safe planetary boundaries resulting in only 
three that humanity has not transgressed. The last three planetary boundaries are not yet quantified 
(Steffen, et al., 2015). Transport sector relates to several of them like climate change, land take, chemical 
pollution or even biodiversity loss and water pollution.

It was commonly agreed that impacts like air pollution, safety and renewable energy consumption

4.4 Remarks 
The approach to the participants of the questionnaire was through e-mail which made it less time 
consuming for getting the desirable responses. Personal interviews could lead to a more thorough and 
qualitative assessment which was however not applied due to time limitations and possible difficulties 
with the difference in spoken language. 
After the circulation of the questionnaire it was understood that a different context and structure of the 
questionnaire could have different results. The scale of importance that stakeholders were asked to give 
to the different indicators was limited to 5 possible answers, so the responses were pretty dense and 
concentrated which made it difficult to distinguish and evaluate the different levels of importance across 
indicators. The questions regarding the importance that the participant would give to different 
stakeholders for selecting and assessing sustainable urban mobility indicators (Question 4) as well as the 
importance of it in achieving sustainability (Question 3) could have been left out as they were not directly 
to the scope of the questionnaire. Additional questions and respective time could be the reason for 
incomplete questionnaires or even mislead the participant from the scope of its contribution. 
Moreover, some of the indicators were not clearly defined at that time while others created confusion 
because of overlapping issues across indicators. The latter issue was however on purpose in order to select 
among similar indicators the most appropriate one.

5.2.2 Proximity to public transport 
Proximity to public transport is measured by distance from home to the closest transit stop or station. 
This distance indicates how close is the walking time to the station or stop to the “acceptable” levels. 
When talking about trips by public transportation, it is a challenge how first and last-kilometer transfers 
can be improved as a part of the door-to-door service. Not being able to serve this first or last kilometer, 
a trip with public transport is often neglected over other modes of transport e.g. car.  
Ciuffini (1995) argues that a tolerable walking distance to public transport is 15 minutes which is around 
1 km of walking. This distance however depends on the type of public transport and the conditions or 
environment of the walking distance. People will walk further if the transit service is of good quality, for 
example fast or frequent, which points out further distances for trains stations over bus stops. A common 
rule of thumb says that people are willing to walk around 400 m to a local transit stop (bus) and about 1 
km to a rapid transit stop (train). This rule is adapted for the evaluation of this indicator. 
In 2014, 88.3% of people living in Copenhagen were up to 1 km away from a train or Metro station whereas 
none was more than 3 km far with only very few (0.3%) living 2-3 km away from a station. According to 
TU data this was the highest share of people living within 1 km from a station since 2007.

5.3 Domain 2: Transport service and modes 
This domain is represented by two indicators; energy consumption per mode and passenger transport 
volume per mode expressed by trips. The intention is to describe the performance of different modes of 
transport in terms of efficiency and share in the modal split. 
5.3.1 Energy consumption per mode 
Energy consumption across modes indicates efficiency of modes and with this respect points out the 
greener modes of transport. As total energy consumption is indicated by CO2 emissions for each mode 
and the respective traffic volume (indicator presented later in Effects domain), here only modal cross 
comparison is made upon the energy efficiency calculated per passenger kilometers which is the energy 
for one kilometer traveled by one person. Passenger kilometers may provide the best cross modal 
comparison accounting for the different capacity of each mode as well. The number of passengers or trips 
alone could mislead the judgment regarding energy efficiency across modes. 
The energy consumption for the main modes of urban land passenger transport, namely car, train, metro 
and bus are calculated using the 2015 version of TEMA tool developed by COWI for the Ministry of 
Transport of Denmark. The software has several possible options to specify for each mode of transport 
such as distance traveled, fuel type, Euro standards, size of vehicle, speed, capacity levels and other 
energy related choices. According to these options it is then generated the energy consumption and 
emissions, like CO2, CO, NOx, SO2, HC and particles, for the different modes available for the total trip, per 
kilometer traveled, for all persons, per person and per person kilometer. 
The energy consumption is calculated for three scenarios; current performance, performance according 
to desirable goals and best technology, and performance as it is estimated for 2002. In order to estimate

5.4.1 Reliability of public modes

many of those departures were on-time. Reliability and punctuality are measured either from the starting

5.4.2 Frequency of public modes

Frequency levels as perceived by users reflecting the level of service, may be contradicting with bus 
operator benefits. Higher frequency is not always beneficial for operators although higher level of service 
can attract more passengers. For a more balanced evaluation among users and operators, frequency 
assessment can be compared with the passenger demand and capacity of public modes as well.

5.4.3 Travel time 
Travel time is defined as the time required to reach a destination from a certain origin. In order to monitor 
progress across time towards achieving goals, travel time should be measured for specific routes. Then, it 
is possible to evaluate reduction or increase in travel times for these Origin-Destination pairs across 
different modes of transport. In accordance with the ITS Action Plan 2015-2016 for Copenhagen, there 
have been set goals for reduction in travel times for selected routes  specifically for bike, bus and car. In 
the report “Københavns Kommunes administrationsgrundlag for trafikledelse 2014-2018” travel times are 
calculated for selected routes based on which travel time reductions are aimed (Københavns Kommune, 
Rambøll, 2014). 
 
In accordance with the Bicycle Strategy 2011 – 2025, the target for cyclists is to improve journey time by 
10% until 2018 for seven selected routes (The City of Copenhagen, 2011). For bus service, the priority is 
given on A and S bus lines as the main lines of the city's network regarding passenger demand and goals 
are set for both morning and afternoon rush hours. For cars, goals have been set for eight different routes 
which represent the main routes for car traffic in the city. Since it is expected an increase in car traffic in 
the coming years, it is a challenge to ensure that the travel time does not increase on the major roads 
(Københavns Kommune, Rambøll, 2014). Service goals refer to travel time reductions up to 2 minutes for 
selected cycle routes, up to 3 minutes for the selected bus routes and for cars to remain at the same levels 
with some improvements in selected routes corresponding to 1-2 minutes. Detailed information on travel 
times for these routes can be found in Appendix III.  
 
These measurements and targets provide a good basis for future monitoring. As travel times are only 
available for one year, this indicator is not included in the index calculation. However, it is possible to be 
considered when the next counting will be available which will enable to set the minimum threshold value.

5.5 Domain 4: Effects 
This domain examines the impacts of transport services with respect to air pollution, safety and noise. Air 
pollution is further divided to pollutants harming human health and those contributing to climate change. 
For this purpose CO2 emissions are examined as total emissions coming from the traffic sector without 
the intention to compare modes. The function and efficiency of transport modes is described in passenger 
transport volume and energy consumption per mode in the “Transport service and modes” domain. 
5.5.1 Air pollution 
Air pollution affects human health, contributes to climate change and damages the ecosystems (European 
Environment Agency, 2014). Figure 52 shows the different air pollutants for each impact sector as they 
were explored by the EEA. The focus here is on the impacts on human health and climate change since 
ecosystems are rarely found in urban areas and a focus should be given especially when they are located 
in close proximity to transport infrastructure. 
The main pollutants harmful for the human health related with the transport sector are some of them 
directly emitted into the atmosphere and others formed from complex chemical reactions following 
emissions of precursor gases. These pollutants derive from fuel combustion as well as from vehicle and 
road wear. Specifically within cities significant local sources include among others vehicle exhausts and 
road dust resuspension.

The air quality limit values for the city of Copenhagen are currently based on EU directives. The long-term 
goal of the current EU air pollution policy is to attain “levels of air quality that do not give rise to significant 
negative impacts on, and risks to human health and the environment” (European Environment Agency, 
2014). For this purpose, there are set some values above which air pollutants should not be emitted. These 
are presented for the specific air pollutants measured for Copenhagen municipality presented below.  
5.5.1.1 Local pollution 
Local air pollution is measured as the exhaust pollution from urban traffic which is harmful for human 
health. In respect to this, transport sector contributes significantly to emissions of nitrogen oxides (NOx), 
non-methane volatile organic compounds (NMVOC), methane (CH4), carbon monoxide (CO) and other 
particulate matters (PM). Copenhagen municipality currently presents NO2, PM2.5 and PM10 pollutants at 
a regular basis. In Green Accounts 2009, it is stated that lead (Pb), carbon monoxide (CO) and sulphur 
dioxide is far below the current limit values showing that these air pollutant levels are also being 
monitored (The City of Copenhagen, 2010). 
The air pollutant values are from the station of Copenhagen municipality which is located in H.C. 
Andersens Boulevard.  At this station are observed the highest values among the 3 stations of the 
municipality in total (Department of Environmental Science, Aarhus University, 2013), due to the higher 
levels of traffic. 
Data is generated from the online database of the Danish Centre for Environment and Energy at Aarhus 
University in connection with the annual reports of the same institute (Ellermann, et al., 2015). The values 
coincide with the data presented in Copenhagen’s Green Accounts for the latest years. The annual limit 
values are those introduced by EU in 2005, 2010 and 2015 for PM10, NO2 and PM2.5 respectively, 
considering yearly average concentration of pollutants (Institut for Miljøvidenskab, 2015). Concentration 
of PM2.5 emissions, for as long as data is available, hasn’t exceeded the annual limit value set by EU 
whereas PM10 concentrations remained under the limit only after 2007 (Figure 53). Concentrations of NO2 
emissions as an annual average, are constantly above the reference value of 40μg/m3 set by EU.  
The World Health Organization (WHO) has also developed Air Quality Guidelines and set reference levels 
as limit values for air pollutants. These limit values are mostly different from EU values and more stringent 
as well. Concentration levels of PM2.5 as a yearly average are exceeded when taking into account the limit 
value of 10 μg/m3 set by WHO.

5.5.1.2 Carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions 
Several air pollutants are also contributing to climate change, having a potential impact on the planet's 
climate and global warming. Tropospheric ozone O3 and black carbon (BC), a constituent of PM, are 
examples of air pollutants that are short-lived climate forcers and contribute directly to global warming. 
Other PM components such as organic carbon, ammonium, sulphate, and nitrate may have a cooling 
effect (European Environment Agency, 2014). 
With respect to climate change, Copenhagen municipality is currently measuring CO2 emissions and has 
set the ambitious goal to become CO2 neutral by 2025 including all sectors. The plan is that the direct 
emissions of CO2 will be reduced at such a level that renewable energy generation will compensate for 
those. Specifically for road traffic emissions the goal for 2025 is to have 150.000 tons of CO2 emissions 
less compared to 2005 levels (The City of Copenhagen, 2009).

5.5.3 Noise pollution

5.2 Index development 
The index aims to monitor progress across time towards achieving specified goals. For this purpose 
threshold values are set for each indicator with the maximum represented by desirable goals and the 
minimum by the reference value of the specified goal or the lowest observation indicating the worst 
performance observed. When a goal is fulfilled for a certain indicator then the indicator takes the 
maximum possible score. When the next measurement takes place, for example after a year, indicators 
are updated with the new values and goals as well in the case new are established.  
Multidimensional issues such as sustainability can be measured successfully using composite indices 
because of their ability to integrate various measures into a single comparable value. Despite the lack of 
a common structure the development of a composite index generally involves five steps (Zheng et al., 
2013): 
1. identifying the issues to be measured 
2. selecting appropriate indicators and variables 
3. normalizing the data to render them dimensionless 
4. weighting the components and  
5. aggregating the weighted components into a single composite index score 
At this point the first 2 steps are already carried out whereas the next steps are discussed further on.

6. Discussion 
The assessment of sustainability and specifically of sustainable urban mobility is proven to be a complex 
task.  It is a fact that there is not a common definition among the research community which could work 
as a reference point for the analysis and assessment of sustainable mobility. It is often useful that a 
consensus on the field under research exists so that progress can be achieved. The theory on sustainable 
urban mobility had as a starting point related solid concepts such as sustainability and sustainable 
transport. Other related terms such as accessibility and passability were distinguished from mobility with 
the former to represent the result of good mobility and the latter to be an essential part of it. Key aspects 
of mobility include among others the easiness of movement or the potentiality which refers to the 
possibility of being able to move around.  
Sustainable mobility was explored based on a broad literature review. This research led to the definition 
of sustainable urban mobility and to the basic domains that best describe it; urban space, transport service 
and modes, passability and effects. Urban space includes characteristics of urban design which may 
promote sustainable mobility; higher densities and mix of land uses can bring activities and households 
closer so that people will need to move less and for smaller distances which can be covered easier by non-
motorized modes. Accessibility and proximity to public modes is also an element of urban space which 
can refer to density of transit stops or coverage area of public transport.  
Urban space acts as the first layer on top of which transport services and modes are performing. This 
second domain is about the function of transport modes regarding efficiency, share of trips across modes 
and utilization of the transport system. Passability is the third domain which refers to the level of service 
experienced by users of the transport system. This domain includes aspects such as reliability and 
frequency of public modes, quality of services, available choices and travel time of trips across transport 
modes. The fourth domain is the last layer of sustainable urban mobility named effects. Effects of mobility 
refer to air and noise pollution, traffic safety, land take and degradation of the environment. These four 
domains summarize in a holistic way the concept of sustainable urban mobility. 
The concept on sustainable urban mobility worked as a basis for the investigation of different indicators 
that are able to represent it. Indicators were searched among the literature on sustainable transport and 
when possible only addressed to urban mobility. The indicators were selected in order to match with the 
concept previously defined and indicators out of this scope were excluded. Definition of criteria on 
==PAGE:116==
 
103 
 
selecting indicators was also the guidance for this procedure taking into account representation, 
operation and application criteria that indicators should comply with. These criteria were applied later on 
as well in the application of the framework in a real context. This research led to a long list of indicators 
from selected scientific references categorized in the four domains previously defined. The indicators 
corresponded and covered each element of sustainable urban mobility as conceptualized previously. 
Prioritization of indicators 
In order to prioritize this long list of indicators a questionnaire was developed targeted to relevant 
stakeholders able to assess the importance of different indicators in evaluating sustainable urban 
mobility. This first list of indicators describing sustainable urban mobility was the main input for the 
questionnaire together with some questions for the understanding of stakeholders’ status. Stakeholders 
were selected from those experienced with the transport sector with gained knowledge in this field. 
The final list of indicators was the outcome of the prioritization according to responses on the 
questionnaire. The prioritization method was done by assigning weights to each answer ranging from 0 to 
4 for Unimportant to Very important answers accordingly. The weighting score assumed that all answers 
have the same distance from each other which is not always the case in reality considering that there is 
often the dilemma between two proximate answer options. This issue could be softened if the answer 
choices, meaning the scale, would be broader. Such a scale would be however more consistent with 
further responses.  
Furthermore, the opinion of the stakeholder groups accounted equally although the number of 
respondents in each of the groups was not the same. In particular, the majority of the participants 
represented research community and few answers were gathered from the rest of the relevant 
stakeholder groups namely NGO’s, Transport service providers and Transport authorities. It was 
considered that the limited answers have a great value as well and in order to capture all the different 
opinions it was given an equal weight to all groups for the selection of the most important indicators. 
An alternative way to prioritize the indicators could be the Analytic Hierarchy Process which would 
examine the importance stakeholders give to the different indicators by means of pairwise comparisons. 
With the 36 selected elements as presented in the questionnaire this would create 630 possible pairwise 
combinations which would be very hard to deal with. This method could be applied if the list of indicators 
was shorter. 
==PAGE:117==
 
104 
 
Approach to the framework 
The top-down approach adopted for the development of the framework is considered comprehensive 
and rationally developed. This approach often involves the risk that the indicators as finally selected could 
not be fully utilized in practice. The approach was based on selecting the most appropriate indicators that 
fit with the concept of sustainable urban mobility as it was defined according to the literature review. The 
final list of indicators was created based on the perspective of relevant stakeholders who may have 
contradicting interests. In the end, the final list was not certain that will fit with the perspective and goals 
of a city, and thus not be useful in practice. On the other hand, a bottom-up approach means that 
indicators are selected according to the specific needs of a city. This could have as a result an incomplete 
list of indicators which would represent sustainable urban mobility only partially. The advantage of a 
bottom-up approach would be that goals are already established and indicators are developed based on 
the particular requirements. 
The motivation behind this framework was the lack of a solid index describing sustainable urban mobility 
for Copenhagen municipality. The framework was developed in accordance with such standards, meaning 
to be applicable for urban areas with a relatively advanced transport system but not specifically for 
Copenhagen. The municipality was selected only after the final list of indicators was created disregarding 
until that point specific requests or needs that a city could have. This approach is considered more 
unbiased compared to focusing on specific needs of a city which enables the framework to be adaptable 
for other cities as well. 
Analysis and results of indicators for Copenhagen municipality 
The assessment of Copenhagen’s performance on the selected indicators involved several challenges. 
Availability and dispersion of data was one of them. Data was searched among several different sources 
and organizations including Copenhagen municipality, Movia, the Danish Road Directorate, Technical 
University of Denmark, the Danish Centre for Environment and Energy and other databases available 
online. Some of the selected indicators were not readily available or were not measured as they were 
defined. This had as a result for some indicators to be measured and developed from scratch, like the 
energy consumption per mode, while others were created based on gathered information like the 
frequency of public modes or specific air pollutants. Moreover, data for several indicators was not 
consistent across time which created heterogeneity in the presentation of indicators. The timing of 
collecting data was also a limitation as a lot of the data experts were not available at that time. 
==PAGE:118==
 
105 
 
The analysis of the selected indicators for Copenhagen municipality had as a result 8 out of 18 indicators 
to have fulfilled the goal defined. The indicators that have achieved the goal are PM2.5, PM10 and BaP air 
pollutant concentrations, reliability of S-train and Metro, frequency of 5A bus line and proximity to bus 
stops. This suggests that other indicators could be of crucial importance or relevance to monitor as well. 
For example buses of 5A line run frequently enough but it is not certain the level of service regarding 
occupancy rates. What is also of importance with this respect is reliability and other measures could be 
examined like travel times or connectivity and synchronization between different bus lines or other modes 
like train and metro. 
Index process 
In order to communicate all aspects describing sustainable urban mobility, a single index was developed. 
For this purpose threshold values were defined in order to locate each indicator within a range that could 
show the current level of its performance. The upper limit value was searched among the several goals 
that Copenhagen municipality has set for the different areas of improvement. Most of the goals are very 
ambitious; this is a fair logic when willing to achieve progress. Certainly, setting goals is more than 
necessary towards success. Whenever goals didn’t exist for certain indicators, it was formulated a 
desirable scenario to be achieved based on relevant assumptions. Most of the goals are set for 2025 and 
the defined scenarios are based on this year as well. 
Lower limit values were based on past performance.  The period of time of the assessment varied among 
the indicators according to the available data. The minimum threshold value was set at the reference 
year’s value of a certain goal when this was defined. When a reference year of a goal did not exist, then 
the minimum threshold value was set at the poorest performance observed at the period of time 
examined. This had as a result that minimum threshold values were not established for a fixed year across 
indicators. Even so, monitoring progress towards achieving goals is possible with the method described in 
the Chapter “Index Development”. 
In order to overcome the mix of positive and negative impacts across indicators, the minimum threshold 
value for each indicator represented the poorest performance and not the lowest arithmetical value. 
Similarly, the goal was defined as the desirable performance regardless the numerical definition of 
indicators where higher numbers would mean further negative impacts. This was the case for all indicators 
under “Effects” domain. It was therefore possible to trace evolution of indicators regardless of the 
different impacts of an increase or decrease in numerical values. 
==PAGE:119==
 
106 
 
In order to aggregate the different indicators into a single index, they were rescaled to the same range 
from 0 to 100. Normalization serves the purpose of putting together variables with different units of 
measurement. The result of normalizing the indicators depends on the threshold values established as 
they work like benchmarks for the new scale. The range of benchmarks for different indicators plays also 
a role. When referring to time series of data and threshold values representing values of a certain year it 
may have as a result that new data at a next point in time will exceed the selected range. In that case the 
index should be recalculated in order to maintain comparability (European Commission, 2014). As an 
alternative method, standardization which takes into account the mean and variance for observations 
during a period of time, was not possible as series of data were available for a limited number of 
indicators. 
Alternative methods of defining minimum threshold values 
The index was tested to examine if changes in particular indicators also affect the index. It was observed 
that rising any of the indicators’ value increases the value of the index whereas when decreasing values 
of the indicators the value of the index drops as well. Moreover, the framework is adaptable in the sense 
that threshold values can be modified which will then change the result accordingly. This is applicable in 
the long term as well when new goals will be established. 
Comparison with the average of the examined period – Method 2 
An alternative way of setting minimum threshold value is tested which defines this value as the average 
of the reference period examined. The indicators that have fulfilled the specified goal do not change value 
when changing method, as the goal is in any case fulfilled. Indicators for which data was not complete or 
observations exist only for very few years were not included in this comparison. These indicators are 
proximity to train stations, passenger car and bus energy consumption, and noise. Energy consumption 
calculations are based on two scenarios which were created in order to locate the current value, so 
changing between the two methods do not make any difference due to the lack of time series data. Data 
for noise pollution is only available for 2009 and 2013, so again the average method couldn’t be applied. 
The comparison between the 2 Methods is conducted for those indicators that these methods may affect 
as presented in the following Figure 73.

Setting the average as a minimum threshold value has the advantage that the current value is compared 
with a mean performance of the latest years and not compared to a specific year which could be an outlier 
in a series set of data. However, the progress across time is a bit underestimated as the average variations 
are slighter. If this average period is consistent across all indicators, which is not the case for reliability 
and CO2 emissions, then the difference between the 2 methods is the distance of each indicator from the 
central point. For traffic safety the case is that initially the performance was compared to 2009-2011

comparison between the respective progresses of the selected indicators is now possible according to the 
red line illustration. This method is more accurate in representing progress among several indicators. With 
both methods, yearly monitoring is feasible individually for each indicator.

method (Figure 75). The preferable method is Method 3, as the common basis as a minimum threshold 
value is more accurate when comparing indicators and presents better consistency among the 
representation of data. With this method is required that common kind of data exists for all indicators.

Suggestion and further research 
Data can be a very powerful tool while awareness and handling of it is yet another issue. Lack of data or 
data not properly organized can be an obstacle to the right use of it and gain less benefits from the 
prescribed possibilities. It is also crucial that the methods of calculation are well established and known 
as well as possible changes of those across time. Regular monitoring and creation of desired databases 
can minimize inconsistency and lead to better results and quality of information. It is also a matter of fact 
that confidentiality of data may prevent research and therefore progress. 
Further research on the index may include causal relationships among indicators meaning testing the 
influence each indicator has to the index but also to the rest of indicators. Is bike density growth over time 
related to an increase in number of bike trips? Which indicator relates more with higher share on greener 
modes? This research can point out not only which indicators to improve, but also which other to explore 
that may interrelate with them. Such knowledge can lead to the establishment of different weights 
according to the resulted importance. Causality tests require however time series for all variables which 
are often not available (European Commission, 2014). 
Future work involves among others the research on other possible indicators relevant to measure for 
Copenhagen municipality including the establishment of goals for all areas that progress is desirable. For 
this purpose, systematic collection of the relevant data necessary for the evaluation of selected indicators 
is suggested as well as setting consistent threshold values across indicators. The current research has 
focused on urban passenger mobility. Other sides of urban transport are also worth assessing including 
maritime and aviation sector or freight transport which can also affect mobility and account for negative 
impacts as well.

7. Conclusion 
Sustainable urban mobility represents a variety of elements which can be summarized under four basic 
domains; design and utilization of urban space, performance of transport services and modes, passability 
as the experienced level of service and effects on the environment and society. A solid definition of the 
concept under research enables the identification of the appropriate indicators that best describe it. The 
selection of the indicators representing sustainable urban mobility should match with the concept and 
comply with selected criteria. As sustainable urban mobility is a complex issue, indicators describing this 
concept are expected to represent a variety of measurements. It is therefore required to prioritize 
indicators in order to emerge the most important and relevant ones. Involving stakeholders in the 
prioritization process can highlight the indicators that are most relevant to measure and assess 
representing the performance of sustainable urban mobility. 
It order to communicate this multidimensional concept of mobility, a single index was developed 
according to the selected indicators analyzed for Copenhagen municipality. For this purpose, threshold 
values were defined for each indicator based on which the current performance of each indicator is 
shown. Setting threshold values was complicated especially due to the variance of data and lack of 
established goals across indicators. The sustainable urban mobility index was calculated as 64.5% for 
Copenhagen municipality indicating the overall progress different indicators have made until now towards 
achieving 2025 goals.  
Two alternative methods of defining minimum threshold values were explored and compared to the initial 
method developed. The two methods have both drawbacks and benefits compared to the initial method 
based on the assessment carried out for 5 indicators. However, with the current available data as those 
presented in the analysis of the indicators, none of these methods can be applied in a consistent manner 
across all indicators in order to calculate the overall index. Selecting the most appropriate method of 
setting threshold values depends on the need and the purpose of the index. 
It is a challenge to accomplish sustainable mobility and indicators are essential in order to monitor 
progress towards achieving related goals. A definition of the appropriate indicators together with a 
systematic collection of data and monitoring is crucial. A vital step towards achieving sustainable mobility 
is to define the concept which can work as a keystone for future improvements.
