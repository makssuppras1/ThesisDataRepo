Binaural speech segregation in noisy and reverberant
environments using deep neural networks
Philippe Gonzalez
Hearing Systems Section, Department of Health Technology
Technical University of Denmark
Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark
s181758@student.dtu.dk
Abstract—The present study describes a feedforward neural
network-based binaural speech processing system that can en-
hance speech in both noisy and reverberant conditions. The
neural network utilizes both binaural and spectral features,
such that the model can discriminate between the spectro-
temporal patterns of speech and noise as well as suppress room
reverberation. The effectiveness of each type of feature and their
combination is systemically investigated. Attention is paid to the
generalization of the neural network to unseen material and
conditions by maximizing the variability in the training while
minimizing the assumptions about the acoustic scene. A multi-
conditional training approach is used to imitate the effect of room
reverberation by including diffuse noise during the training phase
of the model, which aims to reduce the sensitivity to a speciﬁc
room. The selected speech material is spoken by a large set of
different talkers, while the noise types reﬂect a wide variety of
everyday-life environments. Finally, the spatial location of the
target talker is randomized and not provided to the system.
Results show that although spectral features are substantially
more effective than binaural features in improving predicted
speech quality, the combination of both types of features achieves
the best performance. In all the considered acoustic conditions,
the present system shows a substantially higher performance in
terms of predicted speech quality compared to a statistical and
learning-free speech enhancement algorithm.
Index Terms—Binaural speech separation, denoising, derever-
beration, neural networks, ideal ratio mask.
I. INTRODUCTION
A major challenge of hearing-impaired listeners is to focus
on a speciﬁc target source in reverberant and noisy environ-
ments. In the case of a target talker, the presence of noise
sources in the acoustic scene decreases the signal-to-noise-
ratio (SNR), which naturally degrades speech intelligibility
due to auditory masking both in time and frequency [1]–
[3]. Late reﬂections from reverberation also deteriorate speech
intelligibility due to temporal smearing [4]–[7], and reduces
the performance of widely used technical applications such
as speaker identiﬁcation [8] and speech recognition systems
[9]. It was also shown that the combined effect of noise
and reverberation reduces speech intelligibility to a greater
degree than individually [10], [11]. This source separation
task is often referred to as the “cocktail party problem”, and
developing solutions that can enhance a target signal in the
presence of noise and room reverberation can show useful in
many ﬁelds such as communication systems or hearing aid
design.
A general approach to attenuate noisy and reverberant
components is to apply a gain function to the time-frequency
(T-F) representation of the signal. Ideally, the gain function
should attenuate noise and late reﬂections in individual T-F
units, while maintaining the direct-speech components. For
single-channel signals, this can be done by ﬁrst estimating the
statistical properties of the signals. Indeed, speech and noise
can be modelled as stochastic processes, e.g. autoregressive
moving-average (ARMA) models, and the denoising task
can be reduced to estimating parameters via the expecta-
tion–maximization (EM) algorithm [12]–[14]. Ephraim and
Malah [15] proposed the decision-directed approach which
combines two different estimates of the SNR in individual
T-F units (the a priori and the a posteriori SNR). This
improved SNR estimation was subsequently used to derive a
gain function and substantially reduced the amount of musical
noise artifacts that are typical for single channel speech
enhancement methods [15], [16]. D¨orbecker and Ernst [17]
extended the approach proposed by Ephraim and Malah [15]
to binaural signals; the decision-directed approach was used to
estimate the speech components in the left and right ear signals
separately. The auto-power spectral densities of estimated
speech components in each channel are then compared to the
auto-power spectral densities of the unprocessed noisy speech
mixture. This ratio yields a time-varying gain function, termed
beamformer-independent post-ﬁlter [18], which can be applied
to the left and right ear signals. This was reported to provide a
signiﬁcant suppression of noise as well as a reduction of room
reverberation. One downside of these probabilistic techniques
is that they require a strong prior about the statistics of the
signals in order to be effective, and work best if the noise is
stationary.
Another common estimator for the gain function is the
interaural coherence (IC), which is a binaural feature measur-
ing the correlation between the two ear signals. Indeed, late
reverberation can be approximated as a diffuse sound ﬁeld, for
which the IC is low compared to direct-sound components. A
mapping can thus be made between the IC and the gain values
as proposed in [19] and [20]. However, if other directional
noise sources are present in the acoustic scene, they would
induce coherent left and right ear signals for which the IC
would be high as well. Other multi-channel methods usually
involve microphone array processing algorithms such as delay-
and-sum (DAS) beamformers, which compensate for the delay

between the microphone signals such that the target source
components add up constructively. While this is a robust
technique, one limitation is that the direction information
towards which the beamformer should be steered needs to be
previously estimated.
Recent years have seen the soar of machine learning tech-
niques and deep neural networks (DNNs), and thanks to su-
pervised learning approaches, most of the drawbacks from the
previously mentioned techniques are resolved. Mappings can
be automatically established between carefully chosen features
and measures of speech activity, instead of relying on strong
priors about the statistics of the signals, or heuristic mappings
like in [19] or [20]. The time delay estimation required to
steer an eventual beamformer can also be performed by a
neural network, using features allowing the localization of
the sound source. This obviously comes with a cost: the
DNN must be trained on great amounts of data, usually the
more the better. In speech processing, it is easy to generate
great amounts of data from a database of speech and noise
recordings and room impulse responses (RIRs). However, the
model is trained using a particular dataset of features, and
any deviation from the distribution of features seen during
training is likely to decrease performance when applying the
model to unseen conditions. Thus, the DNN must be trained
on data that either covers a wide range of conditions, or isn’t
too speciﬁc such that the model can generalize. Generalization
of neural networks in speech enhancement systems is a major
challenge due to the high number of parameters that affect
the conditions: room acoustics, SNR, spectral properties of
the sources as well as their position all greatly contribute to
the variety of possible audio mixtures. The architecture and
the training of DNNs are also subject to a high number of
parameters, such as the number of layers, the type activation
functions, the learning rate or the regularization technique.
May [21] showed that substantial improvements in predicted
intelligibility and quality of reverberant speech can be achieved
by training a feedforward neural network with a set of binaural
cues. Speech components dominated by direct-sound typically
show reliable interaural time differences (ITDs) and interaural
level differences (ILDs) that are associated with the direction
of the speech source. This reliability of interaural cues is
reﬂected by high values of IC [22]. While the resulting system
was shown to be effective in suppressing late reverberation,
most everyday listening situations also include directional
noise components that cannot be discriminated using binaural
cues only, since an interfering directional source would induce
reliable ILDs and ITDs as well. The attenuation of directional
noise would thus not be possible without additional informa-
tion about the location of the target source, or the usage of
additional features allowing to discriminate the target from the
directional noise components.
Few studies have used DNNs to enhance both reverberant
and noisy mixtures. Zhang and Wang [23] obtained promising
results when simulating a true “cocktail party problem” by
placing multitalker babble noise sources equally spaced around
the receiver. However, since a total of 37 individual noise
sources were superimposed, the resulting noise was diffuse and
more similar to a stationary noise. This makes the segregation
task easier compared to attenuating an individual interfering
noise source, which would induce reliable binaural features.
Moreover, the target was always placed at an azimuth angle
of 0° in front of the receiver, which allows the neural network
to rely on binaural cues to separate the target from interfering
sources emerging from other directions. The algorithm was
also trained and tested on speech material spoken by a single
female talker, which results in a highly specialized system
that is unlikely to work for unseen talkers. Zhao et al. [24]
also used a DNN to deal with speech in both reverberant and
noisy conditions, but only in the case of monaural mixtures.
Furthermore, reverberation was applied to the target speech
signal only, which in addition of being unrealistic simpliﬁes
the separation task since the noise signal does not present
temporal smearing.
The following project aims at developing a binaural speech
processing algorithm that can enhance speech distorted by
room reverberation as well as directional and diffuse noise
components. Realistic binaural audio mixtures are simulated
by adding reverberation to both the target speech signal
and the noise components. A limited number of directional
noise sources are placed non-uniformly around the receiver,
which more closely resembles realistic acoustic scenes. A
feedforward neural network is trained with a set of binaural
features complemented by a set of spectral features, such that
the network can discriminate between the spectro-temporal
patterns of speech and noise. Generalization of the neural net-
work is attempted to be maximized through different means:
ﬁrst, a multi-conditional training (MCT) approach is employed
according to [25], which is using spatially diffuse noise to
simulate the effect of room reverberation. Second, speech
material from a wide variety of different male and female
speakers is used, as well as real noise recordings from 10
different everyday-life environments. Finally, the target and
noise sources are randomly placed in the frontal plane of the
receiver. Unlike in [21], no beamformer is used to align the
left and right channels of the mixtures. Four experiments are
conducted to systematically evaluate the proposed algorithm.
The ﬁrst experiment investigates through a series of sub-
experiments the inﬂuence of some neural network architec-
ture parameters and regularization techniques. The second
experiment analyzes the effectiveness of models trained on
individual features, and assesses the complementary behavior
of spectral and binaural features for the task of binaural speech
enhancement in both noisy and reverberant environments.
The third experiment evaluates the effect of ﬁxing the target
location during the network training phase by comparing
its performance against a network trained on mixtures with
random target locations. Finally, the last experiment compares
the performance of the present neural network-based system
with the statistical speech enhancement algorithm proposed by
D¨orbecker and Ernst [17].

A. Time-frequency (T-F) analysis
The ﬁrst step in the proposed algorithm consist of a T-
F analysis preprocessing stage. The audio mixtures are ﬁrst
decomposed on K = 64 frequency bands using a bank of
inﬁnite impulse response (IIR) ﬁlters. A ﬁlterbank of 4-th
order Butterworth band-pass ﬁlters is used. The ﬁlters are
equally spaced on a mel scale in order to simulate the human
auditory perception of pitch. The lower cutoff frequency of
the lowest ﬁlter is 63 Hz, while the higher cutoff frequency of
the highest ﬁlter is 7.5 kHz. Butterworth ﬁlters were preferred
over gammatone ﬁlters for their more accurate reconstruction
and ﬂatter total frequency response. The ﬁltered signal is then
segmented into 32 ms-long frames with a 16 ms-long frame
shift. This means that, at frequency band k and time frame
l, N = 512 samples are available for feature extraction. The
samples obtained from a binaural mixture x(t) after ﬁltering
and framing are denoted xkl,L(n) for the left ear signal and
xkl,R(n) for the right ear signal, where n ∈[[1, N]] is the
sample index in the current frame.

E. Neural network architecture
The chosen network architecture is a feedforward neural
network with one input layer, one hidden layer with rectiﬁed
linear activation (ReLU), and one output layer with sigmoid
activation. It was implemented and trained using the PyTorch
package [29]. A sigmoid activation is used in the output layer
since the IRM takes values between 0 and 1. The Adam
optimizer for back-propagation [30] is used, together with
the usual mean squared error (MSE) criterion. In order to
avoid over-ﬁtting, regularization is applied by using either
dropout [31], batch normalization [32] or both. Batch nor-
malization is applied in the hidden layer, before the non-
linearity. Speciﬁcally, each mini-batch is normalized using
running estimates of the mean and variance. These estimates
are updated with a momentum of 0.1 during training, and ﬁxed
during testing. Dropout is performed after the non-linearity
with a rate of 0.2, meaning 20% of the nodes in the hidden
layer are randomly discarded for each training iteration. The
effect of regularization is discussed in section IV. The mini-
batch size is set to 32 as suggested in [33]. Early stopping
with a patience of 20 epochs is used: if the validation error
has not seen any improvement over 20 epochs, the training
is interrupted. Temporal context is incorporated by stacking
features from a predeﬁned number of frames. The set of
stacked frames deliberately does not include future frames in
order to ensure the suitability of the algorithm for real-time
applications. The beneﬁt of using temporal context is discussed
in section IV. Finally, the hidden layer contains as many nodes
as the input layer, which is deﬁned by the selected feature set
and the amount of context stacking.
III. EVALUATION
A. Databases
Reverberant and noisy mixtures are simulated by convolving
clean speech recordings and real noise recordings with binau-
ral room impulse responses (BRIRs) for reverberant conditions
or head related impulse responses (HRIRs) for anechoic con-
ditions. This results in binaural signals that are perceived to
come from a speciﬁc spatial location, thus the term directional.
The BRIRs and HRIRs used in this project were measured
by the University of Surrey [34]. The BRIRs were measured
with a Cortex (MK.2) Head and Torso Simulator (HATS) in
4 different rooms (A, B, C and D) at 37 azimuth angles
between -90° and 90° with steps of 5°. The characteristics
of each room are depicted in Table I in terms of direct-to-
reverberant ratio (DRR) and reverberation time T60. Room A
presents the least reverberation, while room D shows the most
severe condition. The anechoic HRIRs were measured in a
similar way in a very large room and truncated before the

ﬁrst reﬂection. The speech material is taken from the TIMIT
Acoustic-Phonetic Continuous Speech Corpus [35], and the
real noise recordings from the Tampere University (TAU)
Urban Acoustic Scenes 2019 Development dataset [36]. The
TIMIT database contains a total of 6300 sentences spoken
by 630 different speakers from 8 major dialect regions of the
United States. The TAU database consists of 1440 10-seconds-
long audio segments recorded in scenes from 10 different
categories: airport, shopping mall, metro station, pedestrian
street, public square, road, tram, bus, underground metro and
park.
B. Model training
To train the network, a multi-conditional training (MCT)
approach is employed according to [25]. Speciﬁcally, the
training mixtures are generated using anechoic HRIRs and
added with diffuse noise in order to simulate the effect of room
reverberation. This was shown to produce remarkably strong
generalization results as the neural network is not trained on
a limited set of reverberant rooms [25]. The target talker is
placed at a random azimuth angle between -90° and 90° with
steps of 5° in the frontal plane of the receiver by randomly
selecting one of the 37 HRIRs. Up to 3 directional noise
sources are placed at random locations between -90° and 90°
using 3 other HRIRs randomly chosen without replacement.
No more than 3 noise sources are used in order to make
the acoustic scene realistic — placing noise sources every
5° around the receiver would make the total noise sound
diffuse and stationary, and the speech separation task would
be easier. Finally, diffuse noise is simulated by placing 37
independent noise sources between azimuth angles -90° and
90° every 5° in the frontal plane of the receiver. Each noise
track consists of white Gaussian noise, and the total noise is
equalized such that its long-team average spectrum (LTAS)
matches the one of the TIMIT speech database. The SNR
between the directional noise and the diffuse noise is randomly
selected for each mixture and ranges from 0 to 30 dB. Namely
it is the sum of directional noises whose level is adjusted, and
all the directional noises are set to have the same level. The
target speech signal SNR is also randomly selected and ranges
between 0 and 15 dB, with respect to the sum of diffuse noise
and directional noises. Finally, the overall level of the mixture
is randomized as well by scaling the signal such that the root-
mean-square (RMS) value is between -20 and 0 dB.
Since a large number of models are trained, the default
number of mixtures constituting the training and validation
datasets are set to 1000 and 200 respectively. A decimation
factor of 2 is used, since the time frames in the T-F analysis

C. Model evaluation
The models are tested on mixtures simulated with BRIRs
of the rooms described in Table I, which were not seen
during training since anechoic HRIRs were used instead. No
diffuse noise is used anymore, as the mixtures present true
reverberation. The range of azimuth angles for the target
and noise sources is the same as for the training mixtures.
Similarly, the maximum number of noise sources remained set
to 3, and the RMS value ranged from -20 to 0 dB. The model is
tested in different conditions with varying target speech SNR
and room. Speciﬁcally, the four different rooms are tested
at SNRs between 0 and 15 dB with steps of 3 dB. This
gives a grid of 24 different conditions for which 50 noisy and
reverberant mixtures are simulated each. The TIMIT sentences
and TAU noise segments used for evaluation are taken from the
15% left aside during the training phase, meaning the network
has not seen any material used for evaluation during training.
Figure 3 shows a schematic of an example simulated acoustic
scene.
Performance is assessed using the PESQ (Perceptual Eval-
uation of Speech Quality) metric [37] as provided by [38],
which predicts the speech quality of the mixture and is
widely used to evaluated speech enhancement algorithms. The
predicted speech quality of the enhanced signal is compared
to the output of a system using a unity mask, reﬂecting the
unprocessed sound mixture. This is because the total frequency
response of the ﬁlterbank is not perfectly ﬂat. The ﬁlterbank
also has a cutoff frequency at 63 Hz, which by itself improves
speech intelligibility by removing low frequency noise — this
improvement is not due to the neural network processing and
should thus not be captured by the performance assessment.
This comparison with the input signal yields a ∆PESQ value
measuring how much the speech quality of the input signal
has improved. The ∆PESQ scores are then averaged across
the 50 testing mixtures for each of the 24 conditions. Finally,

IV. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
In the following, four experiments are conducted. Exper-
iment A investigates the effect of regularization techniques
(namely dropout, batch normalization or both) as well as some
neural network architecture parameters (amount of context
stacking and number of layers). Experiment B methodically
compares the effectiveness of individual features, and assesses
the complementary behavior of spectral and binaural features
for the task of binaural speech enhancement in the presence
of both reverberation and noise. Experiment C attempts to
validate the critic made to Zhang and Wang [23] on the
location of the target talker by comparing models trained with
two different paradigms: the ﬁrst half of models is trained
on mixtures with ﬁxed target locations, while the second half
is trained on mixtures with random target locations. Finally,
experiment D compares the performance of the present neural
network combining binaural and spectral features with the sta-
tistical speech enhancement algorithm proposed by D¨orbecker
and Ernst [17].
A. Experiment A: Inﬂuence of neural network architecture
parameters and regularization techniques
1) Regularization: Figure 4 shows the ∆PESQ scores of
models trained with different regularization settings, as a
function of SNR and room. The models are trained with
MFCCs only, and it is assumed results from this comparison
can be extrapolated to other features. Globally, the models
show higher results the easier the acoustic conditions are, i.e.
at high SNRs and low reverberation. This increase is larger at
low SNRs and stagnates at higher SNRs; the performance at
15 dB SNR is actually lower than at 12 dB. One explanation
can be that the higher the SNR, the smaller the room for
improvement, since the less noise to be removed there is in the
mixture. This phenomenon and the difﬁculty for the network to
enhance the mixture at low SNRs seem to balance each other
at 9 dB. It can also be argued that the 15 dB SNR condition
represents the upper limit of the range of conditions the neural
network was trained on. The neural work is not expected to
perform well at SNRs higher than 15 dB, and is likely to have
adjusted his weights for more reasonable values.
Even though room C presents a substantially longer rever-
beration time than room B, the performances with these two
rooms are similar. The reason is the difference in DRRs shown
in Table I. Here room C presents a substantially higher DRR
than the rest of the rooms, meaning the direct-sound in room
C has more energy relative to late reﬂections than in the other
rooms. In other words, the simulated late reﬂections present
low energy even though the reverberation time is high. The
DRR contributes to the easiness of the acoustic conditions
and explains why room C presents a similar performance to
room B.
Dropout regularization gives the best results in terms of
∆PESQ in every condition. The difference is higher at low
SNRs and light reverberation (room D). This suggests dropout
regularization is effective at boosting the removal of com-
ponents presenting spectro-temporal characteristics of noise;
at high SNRs, the background signal energy consists of a
higher proportion of energy from late reﬂections. These late
reﬂections present the same spectro-temporal characteristics as
the speech direct-sound, and their removal do not seem to get
improved by the regularization.
Surprisingly, batch normalization presents the weakest re-
sults in terms of ∆PESQ in almost every condition. The per-
formance is even lower than when applying no regularization
at all. Further investigation shows that the models regularized
with batch normalization actually show the strongest results
in terms of MSE, i.e. accuracy in the prediction of the
IRM. Nonetheless, the focus is on the ∆PESQ score since
it provides a perceptual measure of the actual speech quality
improvement, and in the following dropout regularization is
retained.

2) Context stacking: Figure 5 shows the ∆PESQ scores of
models trained with different amounts of temporal context, as a
function of SNR and room. The models are again trained with
MFCCs only, and it is assumed results from this comparison
can again be extrapolated to other features. The number of
stacked feature frames is converted to milliseconds as follows:
when no context stacking is applied, the features are calculated
from samples spanning on a single time frame, which is 32 ms
long. Then, since the frame shift is 16-ms-long, the amount of
temporal context increases by 16 ms for each stacked feature
frame. Up to 4 extra features frames are stacked, giving values
of temporal context up to 96 ms. Higher values of temporal
context are not considered for computational reasons (using all
the features with 96 ms of context requires 1475 input nodes
for the neural network).
In all conditions, the higher the amount of temporal context,
the better the performance. The difference in performance is
greatest when increasing the amount of context from 32 ms
(no stacking) to 48 ms (one extra frame), and from 80 ms
(3 extra frames) to 96 ms (4 extra frames). The difference
in performance between models is consistent across all SNR
values. This consistency is not observable as a function of
room reverberation; the performance difference between mod-
els is lower for severe acoustic conditions (room D) as well
as for easy acoustic conditions (room A). It can be argued
that at high reverberation times (room D), feature variation
across time frames deviates signiﬁcantly from the one seen
during the training phase, as diffuse noise was used instead of
true temporal smearing. In room A, since the segregation task
is easier, it can be argued the neural network doesn’t require
information from past time frames to make a good prediction
of the IRM. This can explain why the difference is greater for
rooms B and C. In the following, the models are trained with
4 extra frames of context (96 ms).

are trained with dropout regularization and 4 extra frames of
context (96 ms). Three different values for the number of
hidden layers are tested: 1, 2 and 3. Dropout was applied
after the non-linearity in each hidden layer for all the models.

B. Experiment B: Feature comparison
In order to systematically investigate the complementary
behavior of spectral and binaural features for the task of
speech segregation in both noisy and reverberant mixtures,
the effectiveness of each individual feature described in II-C
and II-B is ﬁrst evaluated. This is done by training models
on each individual feature, i.e. ILDs, ITDs, ICs, PDFs or
MFCCs, and comparing the performance of the obtained
models. Figures 7 and 8 show the results in terms of MSE and
∆PESQ respectively. Overall, the binaural features perform
much poorer than the spectral features in all conditions, with
a difference of up to 0.6 in ∆PESQ score between the model
trained on ILDs and the model trained on MFCCs when tested
with room A. This difference in performance is also observable
in terms of MSE. This can be explained by the energy of
the background signal consisting mostly of energy from the
directional noise sources, more than from late reﬂections.
Better ∆PESQ and MSE scores are thus achieved by removing
directional noise components instead of late reﬂections. The
spectral features (PDFs and MFCCs) are the ones capable
of suppressing directional components since they encode the
differences in spectro-temporal characteristics between speech
and noise. The binaural features (ILDs, ITDs, ICs) are not
effective in removing directional noise without providing the
system with the target direction information or a beamforming
preprocessing step, as the directional noise sources also induce
consistent ILDs and ITDs and high ICs. The binaural features
can still help removing reverberation, since late reﬂections
resemble a diffuse sound ﬁeld with inconsistent ILDs and
ITDs and low ICs (while the spectral features encode similar
information for the late reﬂections since they present the same
==PAGE:10==
10
spectro-temporal characteristics as the direct-sound). This is
why the performance for binaural features is not zero.
In addition to the individual features, the all-binaural and
all-spectral feature sets were tested, namely a model trained
with ILDs, ITDs and ICs, and a model trained with PDFs and
MFCCs. The scores of the all-binaural model are lower than
the IC-only model, but higher than the ILD-only and ITD-only
models in almost all conditions. It seems the ILDs and ITDs,
which present poor results, drag down the performance of the
ICs, which are by far the best binaural feature. This can be
explained by the inability of the model to make a mapping
between ILD and ITD values and speech activity, since the
target source position is random. The ICs show values close
to 1 independently of the source location, and even though
directional noise sources also induce high ICs, speech quality
is still improved by removing late reﬂections for which the
IC is close to 0. Interestingly, the all-binaural model shows
the highest MSE, even though it shows substantially higher
∆PESQ scores than the two worst models. The all-spectral
model shows slight improvement compared to the MFCC-
only and PDF-only models in some conditions, but on average
the performance is the same as the MFCC-only model which
shows the highest ∆PESQ score out of all the single feature
models.
Finally, a combined binaural and spectral model was cho-
sen based on the previous results. MFCCs were retained
considering they provide the highest performance out of the
single feature models. The ICs were then added to make the
ﬁnal model as they showed to be the most effective binaural
features. The combination of binaural and spectral features
was expected to provide a signiﬁcant improvement, as it was
expected that the different nature of the features would enable
the network to suppress both directional noise and room rever-
beration. However, only a moderate performance improvement
in terms of ∆PESQ is observed between the MFCC-only
model and the combined model. One reason can be that the
MFCCs individually achieve results that are already high, as
it seems most of the speech quality improvement is performed
by removing directional noise components. Moreover, the
combined model shows higher MSE values compared to the
MFCC-only model in all conditions. Nonetheless, since the
model shows the highest ∆PESQ score, it is the one retained as
the ﬁnal model for this project. The model is trained again with
a training dataset of 10000 mixtures and a validation dataset
of 2000 mixtures. The ﬁnal ∆PESQ scores for this model are
shown in Figure 9, which are higher in all conditions after
retraining on the bigger dataset. The average ∆PESQ score
over all conditions is 0.54, the highest score being 0.88 in
room A at 12 dB SNR, and the lowest one being 0.29 in room
D at 0 dB SNR. From the score distribution it is clear SNR
and reverberation both play an important role in the difﬁculty
of the segregation task: at low SNRs the scores are low in all
rooms, and in room D the scores are low for all SNR values.
Conversely, at high SNRs the scores are varying substantially
as a function of the room, and in room A the scores are varying
substantially as a function of SNR.

C. Experiment C: Inﬂuence of the position of the target during
the training paradigm
Zhang and Wang [23] proposed a binaural speech enhance-
ment system based on a neural network trained and tested on
mixtures with a ﬁxed target source at an azimuth angle of
0° in front of the receiver. By ﬁxing the target location, the
system can rely on binaural features to suppress any other
signal coming from other directions, in addition of removing
late reﬂections from reverberation. To try and prove this,
different models previously considered are trained again on
mixtures with ﬁxed target location. These newly obtained
models are tested, together with the ones trained on mixtures
with random target location, on mixtures with ﬁxed target
location. The results are shown in Figure 10. Four different
feature combinations are considered, namely IC-only, ILD and
ITD and IC (all-binaural model), MFCC and PDF (all-spectral
model), and MFCC and IC (combined model). Each model
was trained on mixtures with either random of ﬁxed target
location, yielding 8 different models in total. As expected, the
all-binaural model is the one showing the largest improvement
from the random to the ﬁxed conﬁguration in all conditions,
since the ITDs and ILDs now show consistent zero values
corresponding to the location of the target at 0° azimuth angle.
It should also be noted an even bigger performance difference
is expected if using 37 superimposed directional noise sources
as in [23], as the total noise would be diffuse.
The IC-only model shows a lower performance difference,
which was expected since the IC takes high values indepen-
dently of the direction of the signals. There is still a slight
improvement in all conditions which can be explained by two
reasons. First, the CCF from which the IC is calculated takes
only 33 values, meaning the left and right ear signals are
perfectly aligned for only a discrete range of lags or directions.
When the target is located at 0° azimuth angle, the peak of the
CCF is located at lag 0, which is covered by the discrete range
of lags. This yields more consistent ICs in the ﬁxed training
paradigm than in the random one. A second explanation can
be that the measured BRIRs induce more coherent left and
right ear signals in the 0° azimuth angle case than in off-
centered directions due to the scattering effect from the HATS

D. Experiment D: Comparison with a baseline speech en-
hancement system
In order to demonstrate the beneﬁt of using neural networks
over more conventional methods for speech enhancement, the
model previously obtained by combining MFCCs and ICs is
compared to a baseline algorithm. The baseline algorithm is
the statistical binaural speech processing system proposed by
D¨orbecker and Ernst [17], which was reported to provide
a signiﬁcant suppression of noise in realistic situations as
well as a reduction of room reverberation. It is a learning-
free algorithm, meaning it is expected to be less specialized
but more generally applicable to a wide range of acoustic
conditions. Figure 13 shows the ∆PESQ scores of both system
as a function of SNR and room. The short-time Fourier
transform (STFT) parameters for the D¨orbecker and Ernst
system were set such that they coincide with the ﬁlterbank-
based T-F analysis (frame length and shift size). It can be
seen the present system shows signiﬁcantly higher results than
the baseline system in all conditions. The present system
shows an average ∆PESQ of 0.54 across all conditions,
while the baseline algorithm shows an average score of 0.24.
The absolute score difference is quite consistent across SNR
values, but at low SNRs the relative performance difference
is much higher. At 0 dB SNR, the neural network-based
system shows a score that is more than 3 times higher than
the baseline model (0.39 vs. 0.12). From these results it is
clear that the proposed system constitutes a powerful tool
for the task of speech enhancement in noisy and reverberant
conditions. Figure 12 shows the output of the neural network
together with the gain function from the D¨orbecker and Ernst
system for an example noisy and reverberant mixture. The
values of the D¨orbecker and Ernst gain function were grouped
into mel bands such that the output from each system can be
better compared. It can be seen the neural network captures
speech activity patches in the T-F domain more accurately. The
baseline algorithm attenuates speech components too much at
all frequencies, as if it was only detecting speech components
above a certain level. In contrast, the neural network manages
to enhance even the harmonics and high frequency transients
presenting low energy.

V. CONCLUSION
This project presented a neural network-based binaural
speech processing system for mixtures corrupted by both
directional noise and room reverberation. The presence of both
noise and room reverberation was tackled by presenting the
neural network with both spectral and binaural features. The
motivation was that spectral features allow to differentiate the
spectro-temporal patterns of speech and noise, while binau-
ral features allow to identify room reverberation since late
reﬂections induce inconsistent ITDs and ILDs and low ICs.
Each feature was ﬁrst evaluated individually. Spectral features
showed to provide substantially higher ∆PESQ scores than the
binaural features in all conditions. Indeed, even though ILDs
and ITDs show values that are consistent in the presence of
speech activity, no speciﬁc value can be mapped to speech
activity as the target position is random. ICs however show
values close to 1 independently of the target location and
are by far the most effective binaural features. But this is
also the case in the presence of directional noise sources,
which ultimately explains why the spectral features are more
effective. Combining the MFCCs with ICs provided small
improvements compared to the MFCC-only model, which
presented already high scores. One explanation is that most of
the speech enhancement is achieved by removing noise from
the directional sources, more than performing dereverberation.
The combined spectral and binaural model was compared
to a baseline statistical learning-free speech enhancement
algorithm. The present system showed signiﬁcantly higher
results in all conditions, with ∆PESQ scores more than 3 times
higher at low SNRs.
One way to increase performance can be to train the model
on true reverberation. This can be done by extending the
MCT approach by using artiﬁcial and generic BRIRs, e.g.
exponentially decaying white noise. In this way, the effect
of temporal smearing can be included in the training, while
preventing the model from being optimized for a speciﬁc
room. Another solution to strengthen the ILDs and ITDs can
be to use their discrete time difference. Indeed, the derivative
should be 0 in the presence of speech activity independently
of the talker location. Other technical upgrades include the
usage of the entire CCF as a feature, instead of just its peak
location and value. To reduce the dimensionality of the CCF,
principal component analysis (PCA) can be performed as in
[23]. The CCF can also be calculated at ﬁner lag values using
fast Fourier transform (FFT) interpolation. Finally, more so-
phisticated neural networks that can explicitly model temporal
context, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) [39], could
be used.
Great attention was paid to the generalization of the neural
network through the use of an MCT approach, as well as
randomizing a large number of acoustic parameters when
generating the training mixtures. This means that the proposed
system constitutes a powerful tool that can enhance binaural
speech in a wide range of conditions, independently of the
room acoustics, the talker positions, the type of noise sources,
the SNR or the overall level.
