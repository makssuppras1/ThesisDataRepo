Master thesis –  
Spirulina as a low impact protein source 
By Christian Emil Gottlieb 
Abstract 
The aquacultural industry is growing every year, and production is heavily reliant on marine 
fishmeal and oil. But stagnant catch numbers mean that alternative sources of essential amino 
acids and omega-3 are needed. Spirulina, dried Arthrospira species, are a promising source of 
these nutrients. This study conducts a life cycle assessment to judge the sustainability of using 
Spirulina as protein source in fishmeal for two important fish species in the European 
aquacultural sector: Gilthead sea bream and rainbow trout.  
To reduce costs and impacts of the Spirulina cultivation, low electricity-cost solutions are 
combined with the use of aquacultural wastewater as a source of nutrients for the Spirulina. 
Effluents from rearing of sea bream and trout are the main focus, in order to establish circularity. 
The system boundary of the study covers both the algae farm and the fish farms from receival of 
wastewater till farm gate, including processing of fish into fillets for human consumption. 
Spirulina used for direct human consumption as a protein source and omega-3 supplement are 
also covered and compared to consumption of filleted fish. 
Results highlight that Spirulina, if the impacts of fisheries and marine eutrophication are included 
in the assessment, can reach impacts at the level of conventional fishmeal. However, this 
performance is dependent on a source of concentrated aquacultural wastewater. Flow-through 
fish farms, even intensive ones, benefit far less from integration of Spirulina as wastewater 
treatment and nutrient source. The choice of low electricity cost drying methods is of high 
importance for sustainability, and the competitiveness of the algae farm, in terms of cost and 
environmental impacts, is sensitive to the algal productivity and the cultures density at harvest. 
Future studies could adapt similar prospective modelling to larger scale algae farms or tailor a 
farm design around an existing RAS farm willing to provide primary data.  
Given Spirulina meal’s potentially low environmental impact and comparable benefits to 
fishmeal, comparative life cycle assessment could be performed between low cost Spirulina 
meal and the plant-based alternatives currently being used to replace fishmeal.

Background 
In 2022, aquacultural production of animal products surpassed that of capture fisheries for the 
first time, supplying 51% of the world’s 185 million tonne production (FAO, 2024). Fish farms have 
long dominated inland production (i.e. freshwater species), and stagnant catch numbers 
combined with ever increasing demand for aquatic animal foods has led to aquaculture’s 
extreme growth (Yarnold et al, 2019; Sanchez-Matos et al, 2023; FAO, 2024). However, fisheries 
and fish farms are linked in more than just customer base: Marine products in the form of 
fishmeal (FM) and fish oil (FO) remain the most important ingredients in the feed for many farmed 
fish species even after years of proportional reduction of FM and FO in feed mixtures (Einarsson 
et al, 2019; Boissy et al, 2011). Substitution of FM with plant sourced protein meal often requires 
the addition of biosynthesised lysine and methionine because plant meals do not have the right 
balance of amino acids for fish or other animals. Plant oils can supply crude lipids but lack some 
essential fatty acids (FA), especially omega-3 fatty acids (Einarsson et al, 2019; Bimbo, 1990). 
Plant sourced ingredients also generally have lower digestibility, requiring more feed and resulting 
in higher COD and nutrient levels in the farms’ wastewater (ww). These factors can lead to 
alternative feed mixtures producing lower farm yields or inferior carcass quality (Nandeesha et al, 
1998) and might increase reliance on antibiotics (Einarsson et al, 2019). The burden of fertilisers, 
pesticides, and extensive land use associated with agricultural products also finds many of them 
more environmentally impactful in most categories than the marine products they need to 
replace (Boissy et al, 2011). It is likely that economic factors will continue increase the role of 
aquaculture and non-marine ingredients in the global industry, so there is a need for better suited 
alternatives to the limited natural supply of FM and FO. This challenge has caused an increased 
interest in industrial-scale production of microalgae.  
Microalgae, along with other phytoplankton, lie at the lowest trophic level and serve as the basis 
for the majority of life in the oceans in the role of primary producers. Microalgae contain large 
amounts of polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA) including omega-3 FAs (specifically EPA and DHA). 
Fish are only rich in omega-3 due to consuming algae, as they cannot synthesise EPA nor DHA 
themselves. Cultivation of microalgae is an emerging industry and is growing rapidly alongside 
other types of aquacultures. Globally, it makes up less than 0.5% of the yearly 38 mil. tonnes (wet 
weight) algal biomass production, as it is dwarfed by macroalgae (seaweed) farms. Among these, 
the genus Arthrospira, most commonly the species A. platensis and A. maxima, contributes the 
highest share (Alagawany et al, 2021; FAO, 2024). In Europe alone, over 142 tonnes, dry weight 
(DW), is produced yearly of these species, compared to the roughly 182 tonnes DW/year total for 
all other microalgae species (Araujo et al, 2021).

The composition, cultivation and potential of Arthrospira species (Spirulina) 
While Arthrospira, being cyanobacteria, cannot be scientifically classified as algae, they are 
treated as microalgae within the industry and referred to as such formally within academic 
papers and product catalogues (FAO, 2024; Zhang et al, 2020). When sold, Arthrospira species 
are usually referred to as ‘Spirulina’. While some Spirulina is sold as frozen fresh biomass, most 
is sold in the form of dried powder or compressed pellets (see Figure 1). Spirulina contains health-
beneficial pigments and antioxidants, has a balanced amino profile and a notably higher protein 
concentration than other microalgae (Yarnold et al, 2019) (see Figure 2). Many studies have been 
carried out showing Spirulina’s potential in fish feed as an additive (Mustafa et al, 1994; Saéz et 
al, 2022; Saéz et al, 2024) or as a replacement for protein feed (Galafat et al, 2022; Nandeesha et 
al, 1998; Napolitano et al, 2022). Results and optimal levels of inclusion varied between fish 
species. Carp and tilapia found positive results with replacement values as high as 75% (Yarnold 
et al, 2019; Nandeesha et al, 1998), while gilthead sea bream (Sparus aurata) showed positive 
effects at the fry stage from inclusions of 10% (Galafat et al, 2022). Given the number of factors 
at play, beneficial effects from even higher levels of inclusion might be possible.

Spirulina is a relatively large microalgae and forms long filaments. This makes it viable to harvest 
Spirulina through macro-filtration or sieving. Many other algae would require microfiltration with 
far lower throughput and higher costs, which forces farms to rely on centrifugation for dewatering, 
leading to higher energy costs and lower harvesting efficiencies (Benemann & Oswald, 1996; 
Milledge & Heaven, 2013; Molina Grima et al, 2003). The algal density in ponds at the time of 
harvest is only around 0.05% dry weight (Milledge & Heaven, 2013; Fasaei et al, 2018) so very large 
volumes of water must be harvested and processed to obtain even one kilogram of algae solids. 
Dependent on the processes used, dewatering usually results in a 50 to 200-fold concentration 
of algal biomass (Molina Grim et al, 2003). Without further preservation, the concentrated algal 
biomass will begin rapid biochemical deterioration. Dehydration is by far the most widespread 
method of preservation, with algae reaching a shelf stable moisture level of around 10% (Silva et 
al, 2021). Removing so much water requires large amounts of energy, and stress from the process 
can cause reduction of protein, fatty acids, or other nutritional qualities.  
Freeze-drying is the most conservative drying form in terms of preserving protein and lipid content 
(Aljabri et al, 2023; Desmorieux & Hernandez, 2004; Molina Grima et al, 2003) but its high cost 
limits its use to research or more expensive products. Spray drying is the current industry 
standard due to its high efficiency, speed, and because the atomisation process leads to a 
powder with minimal need for grinding. However, spray drying severely impacts the nutritional 
quality and appearance of the Spirulina (Desmorieux & Hernandez, 2004) along with still 
presenting significant energy costs (Show et al, 2015). Oven drying presents a potential lower cost 
option, but the process must be adjusted carefully to properly conserve nutritional elements. At 
temperatures of 40 °C or higher, loss of pigments and proteins will begin, with higher 
temperatures seeing proportionally higher losses. But long exposure at low drying temperatures 
likewise leads to significant nutritional loss (Aljabri et al, 2023; Desmorieux & Hernandez, 2004; 
Neves et al, 2019). Innovations in hot air drying of algae is the subject of a large number of 
scientific studies (Costa et al, 2016; Desmorieux & Decaen, 2005; Schmid et al, 2022; Silva et al, 
2021) though adoption in large scale industry has been slow. One especially attractive technology 
would be the use of indirect solar drying: air is heated in solar collector and then sucked through 
a dark drying chamber where it sucks up moisture before being expelled outside through a vent 
(Silva et al, 2021). Commercial designs pioneered by BBKW Drying Solutions incorporate electric 
heaters and dehumidifiers for higher consistency and better performance in temperate climates. 
The electricity costs of the commercial design remain far lower than spray drying (BBKW, n.d.) 
and a study by Schmid et al (2022) tested hybrid solar dryer design with two species of microalgae 
and found “No significant differences were found between the applied drying technologies for 
total proteins, carbohydrates, lipids, and fatty acid profiles” when compared with freeze drying.   
Together, dewatering and drying constitute the majority of electricity consumption for algae farms 
(Benemann & Oswald, 1996). As a result, round 75% of economic costs are for drying alone (Show 
et al, 2015; Aljabri et al, 2023). While microalgae are quite hardy, they require specific nutrient 
media and controlled conditions to form monocultures with algal densities high enough to be 
worth the expensive harvesting process. These media constitute the third major source of 
expenses and potential environmental impacts. Along with ensuring a suitable pH and salinity for 
the algae, the medium needs to supply a source of inorganic carbon along with other nutrients, 
most importantly nitrogen and phosphorus. These are usually added in the form of fertilisers like 
urea or chemical salts like sodium nitrate. The mass of nutrient salts used can easily outweigh

the mass of produced algae powder tenfold (Chalermthai et al, 2023), leading to high costs and 
impacts from sourcing and transport. A promising solution would be the use of wastewater as a 
cheap source of nutrients. Microalgae grow naturally in effluents rich in organic matter and their 
use in wastewater treatment has long been documented (Benemann & Oswald, 1996). Various 
effluents have been tested as potential microalgae media but for fish feed production the use of 
aquacultural wastewater is logistically attractive  (Cardoso et al, 2021; Noguiera et al, 2018;).  
Purpose 
The purpose of this study is to assess the environmental impact and potential of substituting 
fishmeal with dried Spirulina powder within a contemporary European context. The study focuses 
on the algae species A. platensis and the fish species rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) and 
gilthead sea bream (Sparus aurata). These species are the most important algae and fish species 
within the European aquacultural sector and innovations to their production could have large 
economic and environmental benefits (Breuer & Twisk, 2024; FAO, 2024). The effect of growing 
Spirulina on fish farm effluent is studied, with scenarios using the effluent from raising of tilapia 
(Oreochomis niloticus), which shows the greatest potential according to research (Cardoso et al, 
2020a; Cardoso et al, 2021) and effluent cycled from the modelled trout and sea bream farms 
(lower nutrient levels but better representativity). These scenarios are compared alongside 
scenarios growing Spirulina purely on an industry standard nutrient salt medium (Rajasekaran et 
al, 2016) against fishmeal (anchovy derived) for a total of six scenarios (see Error! Reference 
source not found.).

Methodology 
This study follows guidelines for attributional life cycle assessment according to ISO14040/14044 
methodology (ISO, 2006a; ISO, 2006b). Analyses were conducted with the SimaPro 9 LCA 
software (PRé, 2012) and the IMPACT World+ impact assessment method (Algez et al, 2024). With 
the version 2.1 update of IMPACT World+ the indicators ‘Fisheries impact’ and ‘Marine ecotoxicity’ 
were added. These damage indicators cover impacts that were previously hard to sum with 
standard methods and should make it easier to assess fisheries and coastal fish farms. While 
categories assessing marine health and impacts of agriculture and fish farms., the final products 
are for human consumption, so the Area of Protection (AoP) of Human Health should be 
considered as well. The human health benefits of Spirulina and fresh fish will also be discussed.

System boundaries encompass both the Spirulina farm and the fish farms that make use of the 
Spirulina protein meal (see the area within the dashed blue border in Figure 3). All parts of 
microalgae cultivation from receival of nutrient media components up to and including 
dehydration of the Spirulina are considered foreground processes (see the area within the solid 
green line in Figure 3). The modelling of aquacultural wastewater and the elements of the 
convective solar dryer are also considered within the foreground. Processing of live fish into fish 
fillets is considered with the system, but the by-products are subject to system cut-off. The 
boundaries were inspired by Bohnes & Laurent (2021) and Kallitsis et al (2020). 
Background processes cover production of all ingredients and materials. Capital goods are 
included for the algae farm in detail. Larger infrastructure (i.e., buildings) are excluded, but 
==PAGE:8==
 
Page 8 of 32 
 
enclosed raceways and drying sheds are included.  Internal pumping and circulation for the 
farms is included but water networks between fish and algae farms are not modelled. Land 
occupation is modelled with all aquaculture taking place on non-arable, coastal land. For algae 
farms, occupied area is assumed equal to the area of the ponds plus 10% to account for 
processing facilities (Chalermthai et al, 2023). Land needed for acceptable performance for the 
hybrid solar dryers is assumed equal to three times their area physically covered.  
Recycling is included as an empty process to cover disposal of steel equipment. Where this is 
not possible or realistic, regionally specific disposal processes are used. Wastewater is 
assumed emitted to nearby coastal waters, with concentrations and treatment dependent on 
the scenarios. It has to be considered that while nitrogen is typically the limiting nutrient for 
eutrophication in marine waters, the Mediterranean Sea (incl. the Tuscan coast) is largely 
phosphorus limited (Lazzari et al, 2015). The use of antibiotics and veterinary drugs at the fish 
farms were not directly modelled for any of the scenarios, due to lack of data and since total 
impacts associated with their production and transport tends to be very low (Sanchez-Matos et 
al, 2023). However, the effect the medicinal waste can have on the effluent will be discussed 
later. 
Studied system 
The studied system is based on a small (~0.4 ha) algae farm in Orbetello in Tuscany (Italy) that 
specialises in growing Spirulina sp. (150-200 kg/yr of dry Spirulina products) to sell locally. Algae 
are grown in four open 15×5 m raceway ponds (total volume 60,000 L) made of wood that is 
covered with plastic tarps. The ponds are kept in transparent plastic tents to achieve 
temperatures closer to Spirulina’s optimum of 30-35 °C (Soni et al, 2019) but as no artificial 
heating is used temperatures will be lower for much of the year. Paddlewheels ensure 
circulation and resuspension, and pumps are only used during harvesting. Harvested algal 
liquid is concentrated using a pressure sieve and belt filter to achieve a concentration of around 
20% dry mass before being extruded onto trays with a piston filler for even drying (Milledge & 
Heaven, 2013; Alfa Laval, n.d.; Faudi, n.d.). During dewatering, the biomass is washed with well-
water to remove excess salts. The concentrated biomass is transferred to trays in preparation 
for drying while the discharged water is recycled. Minimal water is lost during harvesting 
(<0.5%), so well water is mainly consumed for washing, occasional tank cleaning, and volume 
adjustment during the height of summer. The water is mechanically mixed with nutrients salts 
equivalent to the industry standard ZM (Direct communication with Spirulina Farm, 2024).

The spirulina farm produces high quality Spirulina intended for human consumption. Despite 
not using freeze drying, their processing manages to preserve most protein and a large amount 
of pigments. Along with further tailoring the production for consistent high protein 
concentration, the scale must be increased considerable to provide adequate Spirulina meal 
supply for the fish farms. Since the scale affects some efficiencies and therefore impacts, the 
scale is set to 72.9 times scale for all scenarios. With a more compact design, this will require 
around 2.4 HA.  is assumed that the local area can support this, given the low water use and the 
ability to rely on brackish water if necessary. The small-scale farm has production year-round, 
but to decrease costs and impacts, activities are halted during Winter (incl. November) when 
conditions would result in very low algal densities. Autumn and Spring are both assumed to 
have slower algae growth than the summer months, resulting in harvest being conducted 13 
days apart so that algal densities might remain constant. Inversely, at the height of summer 
(assumed July), harvests are conducted every 7 days. The standard harvest rate of 9 days is 
assumed because it is the typical peak of the exponential phase (Soni et al, 2019) and the other 
frequencies are adapted from the farm’s current, seasonally dependent harvest practices. 
The scaling up also involved the switch from continuous cultivation to batch cultivation: With 
continuous cultivation, only a fraction of the pond’s volume is harvested at a time (less than 
half). However, no inoculum is needed for the culture to reestablish itself, and harvest are 
frequent, usually conducted every 4-7 days depending on the season. Batch cultivation 
becomes more attractive at larger scale as pond area and equipment is used more efficiently. 
While the farm is active, inoculum for a batch is sourced from harvest-ready ponds, with each 
pond requiring ~3 m3 algae liquid (concentration 0.5 g/L) to be mixed with growth medium 
(Chalermthai et al, 2023). A small, closed tank should provide enough inoculum to restart 
production after winter (Chalermthai et al, 2023; Benemann & Oswald, 1996), with the 
resources required to run it considered negligible.  
In total, ponds are harvested 23 times a year, with ponds being 90% emptied by the pumps. 
Harvesting efficiency was assumed moderately high at 85%, due to the use of two-step filtration

Drying 
The algae farm used as reference uses low temperature oven drying to preserve valuable fatty 
acids and protein. In this paper, drying is performed using convective solar drying (CSD) based on 
Schmid et al (2022) and direct communication with BBKW Drying Solutions. CSD is already 
seeing industry use with Spirulina (Phytobloom, n.d.) and has been shown to preserve fatty acid 
profiles, total protein, carbohydrates and lipids as well as freeze drying methods (Schmid et al, 
2022). CSD is assumed to perform as well as the current custom system. Drying will take 
moisture from 80% to 10.4% w/w, with an electricity consumption of 13 kWh/ton wet biomass. 
This electricity consumption is based on a weighted average for Spirulina drying, accounting for 
higher productivity during summer where costs are lowest (Direct communication with BBKW 
Drying Solutions, 2024).   
Nutrient medium 
For standard cultivation, the growth medium is Zarrouk salts dissolved to a dilution typical for 
Spirulina cultivation. The amount of salt needed was assumed similar to Napolitano et al (2022) 
and scaled according to yearly biomass production (dry weight): ~5 kg salts per 1 m3 algae liquid 
when including liquid used as inoculum. The Zarrouk medium should ensure the high pH (around 
10) needed for a healthy Spirulina monoculture and salinity was also assumed to be close to 
optimum (Soni et al, 2019). For the scenarios NT-SB and NT-RT, the wastewater composition is 
based on Cardoso et al (2020a), in which raw wastewater from a tilapia (Oreochomis niloticus) 
farm was supplemented with 25% Zarrouk medium1. For these scenarios, the amount of Zarrouk 
salts used was 25% of the standard scenarios, and mixing energy costs were likewise reduced. 
For the circular scenarios, the composition was derived from typical wastewater from large scale 
sea bream and trout farms respectively (Rizzo, N., 2024; Bohnes & Laurent, 2021; Maiolo et al, 
2021). For diluted effluents, the degree of Zarrouk supplementation was adjusted so that 
phosphorus concentrations matched those in Cardoso et al (2020a). Additional sodium nitrate, 
a major component of Zarrouk medium, was then added till the nitrogen concentration matched 
as well. For effluents with very high concentration, the level of dilution necessary to reach the 
target concentrations was calculated and then any addition of phosphate salts or nitrate salts 
were avoided. The other components of the Zarrouk medium were kept at 25%, as this ensures 
high alkalinity, salinity, and most importantly an inorganic carbon source. Any differences 
between the other nutrient concentrations in the wastewater from those of Cardoso et al (2020a)

Recycling and treatment of water 
When utilising aquaculture wastewater, the algae farm takes on the role of a treatment plant. 
Utilising larger volumes of water is now encouraged as long as removal rates remain high (see 
Table 3), while excessive recycling can lead to harmful accumulation of metals that algae are, for 
better or for worse, unable to take up (Cardoso et al, 2021). For these scenarios, the stream from 
dewatering is therefore emitted to the ocean rather than reused. This disposal mirrors the fate of 
typical fate of aquacultural effluents, but pollutant (nutrient) concentrations will be significantly 
lower (see Table 6 in the Results section). Clean water is still consumed for rinsing, but 
wastewater can be used for both feed mix and tank volume control.  
Blending, storage, and transport 
Costs and impacts of blending are assumed negligible. Packaging was assumed reusable since 
the product would be delivered directly to the fish farm that the algae farm is integrated into. 
Spirulina powder was assumed shelf stable enough that quality would not deteriorate before it 
was used. Since algae production would have to be located near the fish farms for the circular 
scenarios, transport of Spirulina powder was considered negligible for all scenarios.

Discussion 
Sensitivity analysis 
A number of major and minor assumptions had to be made while modelling the Spirulina farm. 
This was due both to lack of primary data and due to the necessity of scaling up production. 
Given the low impact of electricity consumption for Spirulina production, most changes are 
unlikely to make change in ranking of scenarios. Along with the use of low energy-cost 
dewatering machinery (see Appendix 4), the use of the a hybrid convective solar dryer can be 
credited with the low electricity consumption. More standard practice is to use a spray dryer. 
The effect of selecting either method will be shown with a sensitivity analysis. For assessment 
of the spray drying method, we must also account for an estimated loss of around 10% protein 
(Desmorieux & Hernandez, 2004), but a higher solids concentration due to lower moisture level 
(7% compared to 10.3% for the hybrid solar dryer) (Charlermthai et al, 2023). As a result 153 g of 
spray-dried Spirulina are needed per FU. 
For dewatering, higher energy costs and maintenance costs might occur if the scale of the farm 
does not fit the most efficient equipment or the two-step process requires a change in 
processing. This is drop in efficiency is modelled as a 50% increase in the use of electricity and 
dewatering machinery. 
Yield of the algae farm, i.e. frequency of harvest and mean algal density at harvest, was based on 
statements given by the primary source about their current yearly yield and harvesting practices. 
The mean algal density of 0.502 g/L was calculated as the average of a 150-200 kg dry 
powder/year production assuming that only 1/4 of the tank was harvested at a time with a 
harvesting efficiency of 70%. 0.05 g/L seems plausible as it lies in the middle of ranges for Spirulia 
in open ponds (Milledge & Heaven, 2013; Fasaei et al, 2018).  While concentration as low as 0.02 
g/L have been reported (Milledge and Heaven, 2013; Noguiera et al, 2018), scientific articles tend 
to have much higher concentrations at harvest, usually above 1 g/L (Charlemthai et al, 2023; 
Cardoso et al, 2020a; Cardoso et al, 2021). A decrease and increase in algal density at harvest of 
50% is tested, which corresponds to values plausible for open ponds. Multiple effects on process 
flows would follow, including changes in consumption of Zarrouk and share of algae liquid 
dedicated to inoculation. 
The results of all these tests give a good picture of which factors influence the farm’s performance 
the most (see figure below).

With this sensitivity analysis of the farm’s production, we see that dewatering is not a very 
significant factor, at least not with the way it is modelled. Change of drying process, meanwhile, 
would highly affect impacts, more than doubling some impacts, mainly due to higher electricity 
consumption. The change of algal density is interesting, as it is shown to not be a linear effect. A 
drop in algal density at harvest is more detrimental than the benefits of an identical increase in 
density. This is partly due to more of the algae liquid having to be used internally to achieve a 
starting concentration of 0.1 g/L. Increases in algal density also mean that more Zarrouk 
medium must be consumed and also more electricity for mixing. But also, the same amount of 
drying and dewatering yields more product. More nutrients can also be treated by a more 
productive algal pond.  
For the second comparison, rapeseed oil and meal where among the most impactful process for 
multiple categories, especially with ‘Land transformation’ and ‘Land occupation’ (see Figure 9). 
Whether this contribution is disproportionate when compared to their percentage of the feed 
composition is harder to say. The process chosen for rapeseed oil in this study was from Agri-
footprint, and represented rapeseed oil at farm gate in the Italian market, with extraction being 
pressing rather than solvent based. This process was compared with its solvent extraction 
equivalent, Agri-footprint processes for sunflower oil, and two rapeseed oil processes from the 
Ecoinvent database. The FU for the comparison was “1 kg vegetable oil at farm gate”.

No significant differences results (less than one magnitude difference) were found between the 
Agri-footprint processes, with rapeseed oil possibly having consistently lower impact than 
sunflower oil. However, the Ecoinvent processes scored significantly differently in multiple 
categories. Within the ‘Land transformation’ category, the Rest of World (RoW) process for 
rapeseed oil has an impact several magnitudes higher than the other processes. In other EQ 
categories, it and the Europe region specific process score consistently lower. Likewise, the RoW 
process for rapeseed meal in Ecoinvent was found to be two magnitudes higher than the 
alternatives. As this is the process used in the study’s model, the switch to a more regionally 
specific process could significantly change the impact. 
For the third comparison, Spirulina grown on aquacultural wastewater had the lowest impacts 
overall. It is possible that some consumers might be reluctant to consume this product due to 
its growth medium. However, it should be noted that farmers already use aquacultural waste in 
the production of agricultural goods (Bohnes & Laurent, 2021). 
Within the third comparison, inclusion of additional nutritional benefits with one FU was 
mentioned. Including the health benefits of PUFA and omega-3, along with minerals like 
calcium, could be done by incorporating a HENI-score (Stylianou et al, 2018). A higher level of 
detail for the Spirulina and fish would benefit the model if this score is to be incorporated, as 
several macronutrient concentrations have a great uncertainty. Furthermore, for completeness, 
it would require the products to be at the same level of preparation. The fish in this studied are 
not frequently consumed raw, and even in those cases the fish must be completely frozen first 
to kill any parasites. Other types of preparation might alter nutrient contents or digestibility. 
Lastly, the shelf-life and storage requirements of the products remain very different, which 
along with the weight differences would make leaving out transport a glaring omission.  
More significantly, 80% of the pond volume would be harvested each time. This move a batch 
process would be adopted greatly increasing the harvested volume to better utilise area and treat 
more wastewater by increasing throughput.  
 
==PAGE:24==
 
Page 24 of 32 
 
“Marine ecotoxicity” is usually considered an important category when assessing aquacultural 
production. For this study, only minor impacts were found within this category and thus it was left 
out. The low impact of this method could partly be due to not modelling the use of antibiotics and 
other medicine within agriculture. It is common to leave this out, but some studies (Sanchez-
Matos et al, 2023), indicate it could obscure impacts to this category. Medicinal waste in the 
effluent could also have an impact on the quality of Spirulina and its suitability for consumption, 
but whether this is the case would require further study.  
Conclusion 
Despite the Spirulina protein meal performing worse than fishmeal across most categories, the 
pressure from Fisheries impact means that Spirulina might still have potential in fish feed. 
However, this requires making use of concentrated aquacultural wastewater to reduce the 
reliance on costly nutrient salts. Most flow-through aquacultural systems are unlikely to benefit 
from trying to incorporate Spirulina production. Within a European context, Recirculating 
Aquacultural Systems seem to be the most promising option. However, these also carry higher 
impacts per produced ton than intensive flow-through, mainly due to higher electricity costs.  
Electricity costs are also a limitation of the Spirulina farm, as equipment not tailored for 
sustainable production can make the product potentially much less sustainable than fishmeal. 
Productivity of the algae ponds must be kept high to ensure low costs, large wastewater 
treatment through-put, and a more sustainable Spirulina meal.   
Future LCA at this level should perhaps focus on comparing Spirulina with plant-based protein 
alternatives, whether for fish feed or for human consumption. In such cases, more regionally 
precise modelling of ingredients is necessary as the results of this study proved sensitive to the 
modelling of background ingredients. 
For future modelling building on this study, it would likely be better to adapt a large-scale algae 
farm. This would provide more specific and broadly relevant primary data and product chains for 
transport modelling. Alternatively, working with existing RAS farms could give detailed data on 
wastewater, dietary preferences, and carcass nutrition. A Spirulina farm could then be modelled 
to support this RAS, tailored in both scale and product.

Appendix 4 
Additional details about the modelling for dewatering and drying at the Spirulina farm. 
Seasonality means limiting work to 245 days a year, but a seven-day work week is assumed as 
cultivation should be overseen.  
The first sieve is modelled based on a 25 µm basket filter from Faudi (n.d.) which has a flow of 
28 m³/h. Pumps are assumed to be able to run over night, but even at the modelled scale one is 
enough to cover twice the water that needs to be sieved during the active season. The mesh 
fineness is considered suitable for Spirulina and should allow for easy harvest (Benemann & 
Oswald, 1996). The basket filter has an electricity cost of 0.2 kWh/m3 and should result in a 
concentration of 5%. Next, a three-belt pressure filter (cost, 0.25 kWh/m3) ensures that the 
algae reach a concentration of 20%, based on a two-step process. Post-sieve volume is much 
smaller so one belt-filter will able to handle full-scale production with low uptime and 
infrequent maintenance requirements (Milledge & Heaven, 2013; Molina Grima et al, 2003; 
Faudi, n.d.). Lastly, a semi-automatic piston filler (0.167 kWh/m3) helps staff distribute the 
dewatered paste onto trays for easier drying in the Black Boxes (made-in-china.com, n.d.).
