Abstract
Species of the genus Pseudomonas are environmental bacteria and they are involved in many
important subjects such as pathogenesis, biological control, and bioremediation. In each sce-
nario, it is crucial to understand the ecological role and mechanism of Pseudomonas. However,
the poor species-level resolution of 16S rRNA gene-based methodologies hampers the reliabil-
ity of population studies. The purpose of this thesis was to develop a new, high throughput
methodology to study Pseudomonas species. To achieve this, a workﬂow based on in silico and
in vitro PCR was used to examine potential primer candidates to develop an amplicon-based
methodology around. With in silico PCR, thirteen primer pairs were tested. Here, ﬁve were
selected due to their ability to amplify and diﬀerentiate Pseudomonas species. The ﬁve primer
pairs were veriﬁed using in vitro PCR methods. Here, one primer pair, known as PsEG30F
/ PsEG790R, targeting the sigma factor 70 gene (rpoD) gene was selected. The primer pair
ampliﬁed eleven positive control strains, while not amplifying any of ﬁve non-Pseudomonas
species. In addition, it ampliﬁed DNA from soil samples in suﬃcient amounts. Therefore, a
rpoD-amplicon-based methodology was developed. The new methodology diﬀerentiated Pseu-
domonas species in a deﬁned DNA mixture and in an environmental sample with high resolu-
tion. This was coherent with cultivation-based studies of the samples. In comparison, the 16S
rRNA gene-amplicon-based method could not achieve the same resolution.

Project Aim
Background: Many of the species belonging to the genus Pseudomonas are of public interest.
Species such as P. aeruginosa, P. putida, and P. ﬂuorescens are correlated to topics such as
pathogenesis, bioremediation, and biological control. To improve our knowledge and further
advancements, it is crucial to understand their ecological role, including how the population
exploit ecological niches, and how they aﬀect other microorganisms.
Problem: Currently, no high throughput method has been developed to follow the changes
in a population of Pseudomonas, while maintaining a high resolution for identiﬁcation. The
16S rRNA gene is the standard in high throughput methods. However, studies have shown
the resolution at the species-level of the 16S rRNA gene is too low to diﬀerentiate between the
species of Pseudomonas correctly.
Hypothesis: Based on Yamamoto and Harayama (1998) and Mulet et al. (2010), we hypoth-
esise alternative genes could be integrated as the target for a high throughput methodology
while maintaining a suﬃcient resolution to diﬀerentiate between Pseudomonas species in the
population.
Aim: In this thesis, the main objective was to develop a new methodology based around ampli-
con sequencing for identiﬁcation and diﬀerentiation of Pseudomonas in any given environment.
For this, new/alternative target genes, which contained high resolution for identiﬁcation, were
investigated. A combination of in silico and in vitro PCR was used for selection of the ideal
primer pair. The selected pair was implemented into an amplicon-based method, to evaluate
the resolution of the approach.

2
Introduction
2.1
A sustainable future
In 2015, the United Nations (UN) released the 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs; ﬁg-
ure 1), giving a shared vision for a sustainable future. As described by the UN, the goals are
interconnected with one another and all should be accomplished by future advancements. The
overall goal is to achieve a prosperous development with sustainable technologies and innova-
tions. According to the Microbiology Society, microbiology should establish itself as a key area
to understand and advance current systems and knowledge in completing the SDGs. Topics,
such as diseases, food production, and bioremediation, are vital to improve for sustainability
purposes.
Figure 1: The 17 Sustainable Development Goals.
In such topics, it is important to understand that they are biological systems. Therefore, it is
no longer ideal to focus on one single element since it often reveals little about the complex-
ity. For example, in agriculture, plants and their rhizo- and phyllosphere interact with one
another continuously. These interactions can both be positive, in terms of growth promoting
microorganisms, and negative, in terms of disease-causing phytopathogenic organisms (plant
pathogens) (Fravel, 2005; Pal and Gardener, 2006; Choudhary et al., 2009; Junaid et al., 2013).
As the human population is predicted to reach 9.8 billion people by 2050 (United Nations,
Department of Economic and Social Aﬀairs, 2017), it is important to ensure suﬃcient food
production compared to today. However, expanding agricultural areas is not a viable solution
since the World has limited optimal, farming regions. Also, biodiversity is heavily aﬀected
7
==PAGE:9==
by the decreasing landscape and pesticide usage, further endangering species. In conventional
agriculture, it has become a tradition to use pesticides, to improve the yield of the harvest by
reducing plant pests (Ecobichon, 2001; Lid´en, 2006; Polyrakis, 2009; Mahmood et al., 2016).
However, pesticides cause many undesirable eﬀects (World Health Organization and United
Nations Environment Programme, 1990; Ecobichon, 2001; Lid´en, 2006; Polyrakis, 2009; Mah-
mood et al., 2016). Pesticides aﬀect human health by causing chemical burns, cancer, or acute
toxicity (Lid´en, 2006; Polyrakis, 2009; Mahmood et al., 2016). In addition, pesticides are of-
ten volatile, soluble, and persistent chemicals, which gets distributed throughout target and
oﬀ-target environments (Polyrakis, 2009). Here, pesticides kill non-targeted species, aﬀecting
Nature and biodiversity (Pilling and Jepson, 1993; Polyrakis, 2009; Mahmood et al., 2016).
Such a problem is the true testament to the need for the development of sustainable technolo-
gies that keep eﬃciency while limiting the negative environmental impact. One solution, which
has been studied for more than 40 years, is the use of biological control (biocontrol). Biocon-
trol agents are microorganisms that reduce the growth of plant pathogens and/or reduce plant
disease (Fravel, 2005; Pal and Gardener, 2006; Junaid et al., 2013). Especially the two genera,
Bacillus and Pseudomonas, have been well described in biocontrol (Tari and Anderson, 1988;
Burr et al., 1995; Milus and Rothrock, 1997; Wilson, 1997; Wulﬀet al., 2002; Kloepper et al.,
2004; Matthijs et al., 2007; Tendulkar et al., 2007; Choudhary et al., 2009; Lee et al., 2009;
Lanteigne et al., 2012; Issazadeh et al., 2012; Ji et al., 2013; Oteino et al., 2015). Research
has shown certain biocontrol agents to be equally as good as pesticides (Bargabus et al., 2002;
Fravel, 2005; Pal and Gardener, 2006), and some products have already been commercialised
by companies such as Novozymes and Chr. Hansen Holding A/S. However, some agents fail to
perform when applied. For this, further understanding is needed before the implementation of
an agent, including the mechanisms and persistence of it (Fravel, 2005; Junaid et al., 2013).
To understand and improve the technologies for biocontrol and similar scenarios, further devel-
opment and understanding is needed. Biological niches and environments are complex commu-
nities, containing diﬀerent families, genera, species, and strains (Tringe et al., 2005; Fierer and
Jackson, 2006; Janssen, 2006; Raynaud and Nunan, 2014; Bahram et al., 2018). It is important
to recognise microbial communities, their diversities, and their dependence on environmental
factors and changes.

2.2
Environments and diversity
2.2.1
Environments
All around the World, a variety of environments can be encountered, from the depth of the
ocean to the terrestrial landscape, from Movile Cave into animal bodies. According to Whit-
man et al. (1998), the total number of bacterial cells on Earth has been estimated to 4−6∗1030
cells. To give more comprehensible numbers; in topsoil 1010 cells/g of prokaryotes, in the ocean
5 ∗105 cells/ml, and in the human body 3.8 ∗1013 cells (Whitman et al., 1998; Raynaud and
Nunan, 2014; Sender et al., 2016).
The human, terrestrial, and oceanic body diﬀer from each other in terms of the environmental
compositions of chemistry and microorganisms. Living organisms require energy and nutrients
to maintain the cell and its activities (Madigan et al., 2017; Jansson and Hofmockel, 2018). If
an organism cannot produce or receive suﬃcient resources, its growth is hampered and colonis-
ing the environment becomes hard. This causes certain species to be present in larger number
than others, in a constant competition and interaction as resources changes in already nutrient-
limited environments (Jansson and Hofmockel, 2018).
However, a strong bacterial coloniser in one environment might be weak in another. Many biotic
and abiotic factors, such as temperature, pH, spatial availability, nutrients, and commensal
microorganisms in the environment, determine composition of the microbial community (Fierer
and Jackson, 2006; Janssen, 2006; Fierer et al., 2007a,b; Delmont et al., 2011; Raynaud and
Nunan, 2014; Forster, 2017; Madigan et al., 2017; Bahram et al., 2018). Bahram et al. (2018)
describes the bacterial taxonomic diversity, composition, richness, and biomass in topsoil to
dependent on pH, nutrient concentration, and to a lesser extent climatic variables.
Figure
2 and 3 show taxonomic and functional diversity as functions of abiotic factors. It is worth
noting, the environmental factors, such as pH and water activity, ﬂuctuates over time, changing
the microbial community accordingly (Daniel, 2005; Fierer and Jackson, 2006; Jansson and
Hofmockel, 2018). Human activity also inﬂuence the microbial composition, as Ogino et al.
(2001) showed with an artiﬁcially induced oil spill.
2.2.2
What is diversity?
Microenvironments can be composed of many microorganisms and the distribution among them
can vary (Tringe et al., 2005; Fierer and Jackson, 2006; Fierer et al., 2007a,b; Delmont et al.,
2011; Forster, 2017). To study the environment, two key numbers are often used. The species
richness and diversity describe the microbial composition. Species richness is the measure for

Even though environments and microbial communities are diverse, species within share genes
across taxonomic groups (Woese et al., 1990; Santos and Ochman, 2004; Madigan et al., 2017).
The genome of a bacterium is known as its genotype and this can be used for identiﬁcation. The
genes encode features, such as DNA- and RNA-polymerase, sigma factors, ribosome structures
and protein involved with primary metabolism, are often well preserved due to their importance
in survival and reproduction. All the genes (with a high homogeneity) that are shared across a
selected population is referred to as the core-genome (Woese et al., 1990; Madigan et al., 2017).
These populations can be on the species-, genus-, or even domain-level (Woese et al., 1990).
In contrary, the pan-genome is the genes the organisms across the selected population do not
share (Madigan et al., 2017). To identify and diﬀerentiate microorganisms, the pan- and core-
genome are important to establish a phylogenetic relationship between them. It is useful since
two microorganisms, whom are evolutionary close, (often) share a larger core-genome than two

Beside the genotype, the expression of genes in an organism can be studied (Barrow and

2.3
Pseudomonas and its role in society
2.3.1
Characteristics of Pseudomonas species
The genus Pseudomonas contains a wide variety of species. According to Parte (2018), the
genus contains 254 species and 19 subspecies (including synonyms). Not only is the genus
Pseudomonas rich in number of species, but they are also found in a variety of environments
such as soil, animals, fresh water, and marine (Spiers et al., 2000; Lloyd-Jones et al., 2005;
Mulet et al., 2009; Brenner et al., 2010; Silby et al., 2011; Selezska et al., 2012; Monteil et al.,
2014).
Many of the Pseudomonas species are of public interest. Some of the species have been found to
be opportunistic pathogens such as Pseudomonas syringae, which is a notorious plant pathogens
(Mansﬁeld et al., 2012), or Pseudomonas aeruginosa, which can cause infections (Brenner
et al., 2010; Abdelraouf and Tam, 2010). On the other side, Pseudomonas species have also
shown to produce natural products, some of which are desired for their antimicrobial traits
(Gross and Loper, 2009). The group of Pseudomonas ﬂuorescens are often investigated for
natural products, such as pyoverdines and 2,4-Diacetylphloroglucinol (DAPG), some of which
are prominent in biocontrol (Tari and Anderson, 1988; Keel et al., 1992; Milus and Rothrock,
1997; Wilson, 1997; Matthijs et al., 2007; Choudhary et al., 2009; Gross and Loper, 2009;
Lanteigne et al., 2012; Oteino et al., 2015). In addition, some Pseudomonas species can promote
plant growth and suppress plant diseases (Tari and Anderson, 1988; Shanahan et al., 1992;
Dow et al., 2000; Wang et al., 2004; Matthijs et al., 2007; Choudhary et al., 2009). Some
strains belonging to P. putida bioremediate environments by degrading pesticides and other
contaminants to less toxic compounds (Kilbane et al., 1983; Niu et al., 2009; Pradeep and
Subbaiah, 2015; Kahlon, 2016; Khalid et al., 2016).
2.3.2
The negative impact of Pseudomonas species
One of the notorious opportunistic human pathogens is Pseudomonas aeruginosa (Boukadida
et al., 1993; Brenner et al., 2010; Abdelraouf and Tam, 2010). Along with Staphylococcus aureus
and Haemophilus inﬂuenzae, P. aeruginosa is associated with chronic pulmonary infection of
cystic ﬁbrosis patients (Boukadida et al., 1993; Brenner et al., 2010; Abdelraouf and Tam,
2010). To combat infections, antibiotics is an option, however, Pseudomonas species often
show tendencies to resist and tolerate certain antibiotics (Brenner et al., 2010; Abdelraouf and
Tam, 2010). Boukadida et al. (1993) studied P. aeruginosa in cystic ﬁbrosis. Interestingly,
Boukadida et al. (1993) showed the population of P. aeruginosa varied over a period of 15-
18 months, with strains emerging and reemerging. Here, it is critical to understand how and
12
==PAGE:14==
why such a pattern can be observed and correlated to prevent future inﬂammations. However,
it is critical to diﬀerentiate on a species level, since other Pseudomonas species could also
be present. Another species isolated from cystic ﬁbrosis patients is P. cepacia (Stableforth
and Smith, 1994). Advancements in technologies and knowledge within this ﬁeld would help
improving the SDG number 3 (Good health).
2.3.3
The positive impact of Pseudomonas species
Over the last century, more areas Worldwide have been contaminated with pesticides, oil, plas-
tic, industrial chemicals, metals, etc (reviewed by Kahlon, 2016). Pollutants, such as pesticides,
not only aﬀect human health but also biodiversity (Pilling and Jepson, 1993; Ecobichon, 2001;
Polyrakis, 2009; Staley et al., 2015; Mahmood et al., 2016). Any advancements addressing pol-
lution would help achieve the SDGs number 3, 6 (clean water and sanitation), 12 (responsible
consumption), 13 (climate action), 14 (life below water), and 15 (life on land). Looking into
the Microbial World, some bacteria, such as P. putida can degrade such pollutants (reviewed
by Kahlon, 2016). P. putida degrade pollutants by biotransformation, biodegradation, and/or
mineralisation (Kilbane et al., 1983; Niu et al., 2009; Sangwan et al., 2012; Pradeep and Sub-
baiah, 2015; Staley et al., 2015; Kahlon, 2016; Khalid et al., 2016).
However, not all biological systems contain species capable of these types of degradation. A pol-
luted system can be bioaugmented with a natural bioremediation strain (reviewed by Kahlon,
2016). For this, it is important to understand the introduced strain and its mechanism. Tools
have to accurate to study, understand, and predict potential beneﬁcial and detrimental con-
sequences. It is especially important for the method to diﬀerentiate on a species-level since
non-remediation strains of the same genus might be present. Niu et al. (2009) showed the
strain P. putida ZWL73, could decrease the initial concentration of 4-chloronitrobenzene with
98% over a 6-day period in a native-soil microcosm, while the native soil community only de-
creased the concentration with 20% over a 30-day period. The population of P. putida ZWL73
increased while 4-chloronitrobenzene was presence and decreased as the concentration depleted
(Niu et al., 2009). However, Niu et al. (2009) did not give a detailed distribution of Pseudomo-
nas strains in the samples, potentially questioning how the population changed and if ZWL73
solely increased.
Another way to improve the state of the environment is to minimise the pollutants released.
Pesticides are used to control plant pathogens. Some species belonging to the genus of Pseu-
domonas have demonstrated to control these, while others have demonstrated capability to

promote plant growth (Tari and Anderson, 1988; Keel et al., 1992; Milus and Rothrock, 1997;
Wilson, 1997; Dow et al., 2000; Wang et al., 2004; Matthijs et al., 2007; Choudhary et al.,
2009; Lanteigne et al., 2012; Oteino et al., 2015). Introducing such biocontrol agents instead
of pesticides would potentially reduce plant diseases, while not polluting the environment and
maintining the yield. For this, as with the bioaumentation with bioremediation strains, it is
important to the role of the biocontrol agent. Such advancements would help to achieve the
SDGs number 3, 6, 12, 13, 14, and 15 similar to bioremediation.
2.4
Measuring and identifying Pseudomonas species
The diversity of the sample can be determined by diﬀerent procedures, all of which give diﬀerent
answers (reviewed by Daniel, 2005). Some of the procedures are; cultivation-based, clone library,
qPCR, and amplicon sequencing (reviewed in Daniel, 2005).
2.4.1
Cultivation and phenotypical analysis of Pseudomonas
The classic approach is cultivation-based phenotypical identiﬁcation. After a sample is gath-
ered, it is treated to select for certain species and afterwards the sample is plated on agar plates
(Lauritsen, 2017a,b). These types of methods oﬀer great selectivity since many microorganisms
are sensitive to diﬀerent factors. For example, Pseudomonas species are isolated by cultivating
with antibiotics, while Bacillus species are isolated by heat treating the sample (Lauritsen,
2017a,b). If the sample is not selectively treated, all microorganism can in principle grow.
After the microorganisms have been cultivated, they can be further assessed for their pheno-
types, metabolic activities, sequenced for genomic information, and examined for production
of natural products (Barrow and Feltham, 1993; Mulet et al., 2012).
To select and cultivate Pseudomonas some key characteristics should be considered. One way to
select Pseudomonas species is by introducing antibiotics in the agar plates (Lauritsen, 2017b).
Pseudomonas species are naturally tolerant and resistant towards an array of antimicrobial
compounds. P. aeruginosa utilises lytic enzymes, eﬄux pumps, and mutations to achieve the
resistances (Abdelraouf and Tam, 2010; Brenner et al., 2010). As reviewed in Abdelraouf and
Tam (2010), Pseudomonas aeruginosa shows resistance towards β-lactams, aminoglycosides,
Quinolones, Fluoroquinolones, and Polymyxins. It is therefore natural, to use one or more of
these antimicrobials for the isolation.
To identify the isolated species, key phenotypical characteristics can be consulted. As described
by Brenner et al. (2010), the genus Pseudomonas consist of Gram-negative, rod-shaped bacteria
14
==PAGE:16==
belonging to the phylum of Gammaproteobacteria and can generically grow on both deﬁned,
minimal media, and complex media. Some of the species; are motile (using ﬂagella), have
growth optimum between 4oC and 45oC, can grow on a variety of organic compounds, produce
pigmented siderophores (pyoverdines), and can grow on pH 4.5 and lower, just to mention a few
Brenner et al. (2010). For speciﬁc details, Barrow and Feltham (1993) or Brenner et al. (2010)
can be consulted. For phylogenetic analysis, experiments such as those proposed by Barrow
and Feltham (1993) or Brenner et al. (2010) can be used to map and identify the isolates.
However, this does not necessarily give the full diversity, and the methods can be inconclusive
or lead to wrong identiﬁcation due to deﬁciencies.
2.4.2
Sequence-based identiﬁcation of Pseudomonas species
An alternative to the phenotypical analysis is the sequencing of the 16S rRNA (Lauritsen,
2017b). The 16S rRNA gene is distributed across prokaryotes, qualifying it as an analytical
marker to determine the phylogenetic relationship between all species, including Pseudomonas
(Woese et al., 1990). Using 16S rRNA gene-speciﬁc primers allow for ampliﬁcation of the gene
(Lauritsen, 2017a,b). After amplifying the gene, PCR products can be sequenced and further
blasted against know database such as NCBI, identifying the genus and species of the isolate
as the closest match (Lauritsen, 2017a,b).
One problem to this is the low species-level resolution of the 16S rRNA gene for Pseudomonas
(Yamamoto and Harayama, 1998; Case et al., 2007; Mulet et al., 2009; Bodilis et al., 2012;
Kahlon, 2016). Yamamoto and Harayama (1998) showed the 16S rRNA gene with variable
regions, did not provide same phylogenetic relationship among twenty Pseudomonas strains, as
the sequencing of a non-variable region of 16S rRNA, gyrB (gyrase), and rpoD (sigma factor
70). It was suggested that multiple mutations were introduced in the variable regions by single
mutation events (Yamamoto and Harayama, 1998). gyrB, and rpoD showed similar clustering
of the Pseudomonas species and showed cohesion with phenotypic analysis of outlier groups
(Yamamoto and Harayama, 1998). Yamamoto et al. (2000) further proved this by showcasing
gyrB and rpoD diﬀerentiated Pseudomonas species in a similar pattern in a larger dataset.
Acinas et al. (2004) showed a similarity of 99% (of 16S rRNA genes) can exclude operational
taxonomic units (OTUs).
Changing from unique ribotypes to clustering of 99% similarity
caused the number of OTUs to decrease from 1633 to 520 (Acinas et al., 2004). This indicates
the 16S rRNA does not contain suﬃcient variation to diﬀerentiate certain species.
Mulet et al. (2010) and Mulet et al. (2012) support this theory. In Mulet et al. (2010), the
15
==PAGE:17==
PCR products of gyrB, rpoB, rpoD, and 16S rRNA were compared to identify the number of
polymorphic sites. The rpoD product exhibited 70.39%, while gyrB and rpoB exhibited 51.82%
and 44.41%, respectively (the 16S rRNA was not mentioned) (Mulet et al., 2010). Further-
more, in the supplementary material of Mulet et al. (2012), a table with pairwise comparison
of closely related species of Pseudomonas was shown. The tables of the 16S rRNA, gyrB, and
rpoD genes have been included here (see table 2). As seen, the dissimilarity between species
can be less than 1% when comparing 16S rRNA genes, which is lower than common rule of
thumb at 2-3%. The gyrB shows 10-20% dissimilarity and rpoD shows 10-30% dissimilarity
(Mulet et al., 2012). The higher variations could indicate that the genes rpoD, gyrB, and rpoB
would have higher resolutions compared to 16S rRNA.
In addition to the poor resolution of the 16S rRNA gene, it has been reported that certain
Pseudomonas species have multiple alleles (Case et al., 2007; Bodilis et al., 2012). Bodilis
et al. (2012) examined nineteen Pseudomonas strains and reported them to possess four to
seven copies of the 16S rRNA gene. In addition, Bodilis et al. (2012) found regions of the
16S rRNA to be similar between species. It was reported the motif of hypervariable region
1 was shared between evolutionary distant Pseudomonas strains (Bodilis et al., 2012). This
observation was, therefore, hypothesised to be either partial horizontal transfer or evolutionary
convergence (Bodilis et al., 2012). Once again, other single-copy housekeeping genes, such as
rpoB, rpoD, or gyrB, should be considered for identiﬁcation to avoid such problems (Yamamoto
and Harayama, 1998; Yamamoto et al., 2000; Case et al., 2007; Mulet et al., 2009).
2.4.3
Identiﬁcation of Pseudomonas by Multiple Locus Sequence Typing
Another common technique, and alternative to single-sequencing, is Multiple Locus Sequencing
Typing (MLST). MLST increases the resolution by including more than one genetic marker,
thereby increasing the total number of nucleotides aligned.
Sarkar and Guttman (2004) determined the relationship between sixty species of Pseudomonas,
using an MLST approach for the genes rpoD, gyrB, acnB (aconitate hydratase B), cts (citrate
synthase), gap (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase), pgi (phosphoglucoisomerase), and
pfk (phosphofructokinase). The evolution of the species was considered stable and they were
clustered into four clades. Interestingly, the relationship between a P. syringae and a host
seemed to be independent of evolutionary history, suggesting outside factors play a vital role
and thereby contradicting some phenotypical identiﬁcation methods (Sarkar and Guttman,
2004).

2.4.4
Evaluation of cultivation-based methods
Unfortunately, cultivation-based methods are biased. In reality, approximately 1% of soil bacte-
ria can be cultivated (with current cultivation methods) and many microorganisms with public
interest are thereby excluded (reviewed in Daniel, 2005). None of the aforementioned methods
can avoid cultivation bias and it is impossible to adjust the methods to function directly on
environmental samples. Li et al. (2013) investigated how cultivation-dependent methods aﬀect
the isolation of Pseudomonas species from the soil. Pseudomonas species were isolated from
environmental samples and identiﬁed by sequencing the 16S rRNA gene. As a comparison,
quantitative PCR (qPCR; see section 2.4.5) targeting Pseudomonas was used to estimates the
total amount of Pseudomonas species present (Li et al., 2013). Li et al. (2013) reported less
than 4% of the total Pseudomonas population could be cultivated using Pseudomonas-speciﬁc
medium. This clearly indicates that cultivation-based studies miss important species and do
not give an adequate representation of the composition of Pseudomonas present. Therefore,
cultivation-independent methods, such as qPCR, clone libraries or amplicon sequencing, should
be considered.
2.4.5
Quantitative PCR and ﬂuorescence probing
qPCR and ﬂuorescence probing are types of targeted techniques (Pernthaler et al., 2008; Ar-
boleya et al., 2012; Singh et al., 2014). qPCR targets and ampliﬁes one unique gene of the
targeted species. Each cycle of the PCR reaction causes ﬂuorescens probes to be activated and
can be monitored. Each point corresponds to a cycle number, and the release of ﬂuorescence
follows an exponential function. From the function, the initial gene pool can be calculated
which in turn provides the abundance of the target species (Arboleya et al., 2012; Li et al.,
2013; Singh et al., 2014). However, the primers have to be speciﬁc for the group under investi-
gation, meaning a deep metagenome has to use a library of primers to cover all possible species,
18
==PAGE:20==
or the primers are unspeciﬁc and target higher taxonomic units such as order, class or phylum,
which provide less insight in the targeted system and its diversity (Arboleya et al., 2012; Singh
et al., 2014). Probing-based methods use ﬂuorescence dyes and microscopy to identify and
diﬀerentiate between phylogenetic units (Pernthaler et al., 2008). However, as with qPCR, a
colour is needed for each probe, meaning an entire library of probes and colours has to be used,
or higher taxonomic units should be targeted which still provide a sub-optimal interpretation
of the system (Pernthaler et al., 2008). These methods are in theory viable, but both are rarely
used due to their practical limitations. Interestingly, Arboleya et al. (2012) showed similar
results when comparing amplicon sequencing and qPCR, however, they relativised the data of
qPCR to the amplicon sequencing.
To understand the function of biocontrol strains, it is necessary to follow the changes in the
population. Wang et al. (2004) used luminescence mutants to trace P. ﬂuorescens CS85 in soil
assays. Using serially dilution and detection methods, the colony forming units (CFU) could
be determined (Wang et al., 2004). The results identiﬁed P. ﬂuorescens CS85 to colonise in
the upper part of the root system and persisted over a longer period of time (Wang et al.,
2004).
This setup is good for a model system, where spatial position can be determined
with confocal microscopy (not done in Wang et al. 2004) and still allow for accounting of
bacterial distribution. However, this method falls short, since it relies on ﬂuorescence at a
given wavelength which other microorganisms might share.
At the same time, neither the
entire population, the sub-population, or closely related species can be studying, disallowing
further understanding of how the implementation of a biocontrol agent aﬀects the community.
2.4.6
Clone library
Another cultivation-independent method is clone libraries. Clone libraries ca be constructed
in many diﬀerent ways depending on the application of the study. Dependent on the vector
chosen for the study, DNA fragments of up till 40 kbp (cosmids and fosmids) or in some cases
more (bacterial artiﬁcial chromosomes; BACs) can be inserted (reviewed in Daniel, 2005). The
fragment can be obtained in several ways. Restriction enzymes and fragmentation methods
can be used on extracted DNA, allowing for fragmented genes (genomes) of a (meta-)genome
to be inserted into vectors (Daniel, 2005; Fierer et al., 2007b; Rajendhran and Gunasekaran,
2008; Monta˜na et al., 2012). The vectors are transformed into host organisms (clones). This
allows for a close approximation of the metagenome. Alternatively, primers targeting speciﬁc
sequences can be used to amplify desired genes (Daniel, 2005; Fierer et al., 2007b; Monta˜na
et al., 2012). The targeted genes could be phylogenetic markers, such as the 16S rRNA gene,
19
==PAGE:21==
to determine the diversity in a sample, or it could be a functional gene of interest (Ogino et al.,
2001; Daniel, 2005; Case et al., 2007; Fierer et al., 2007b; Rajendhran and Gunasekaran, 2008;
Deman`eche et al., 2009; Monta˜na et al., 2012). One beneﬁt of creating a library is the ability to
make functional screenings (Daniel, 2005; Fierer et al., 2007b; Rajendhran and Gunasekaran,
2008; Monta˜na et al., 2012). Clones with inserts can display desired functions, such as degra-
dation of polymers or antimicrobial production. A clone, showcasing a desired trait, can be
further investigated to identify what gene and mechanism are behind the function. Dependent
on the depth and aim of the study, hundreds or thousands of clones can be generated.
Rogers et al. (2003) used a clone library-based method to detect the diversity of cystic ﬁbrosis
patients in a single sampling. Rogers et al. (2003) clustered units of more than 98% similarity
together. It was found that 61 clones out of 103 were identiﬁed as P. aeruginosa. However,
following the observation of Acinas et al. (2004) and Mulet et al. (2010) two strains with
99% similarity can be two diﬀerent species. This means, Rogers et al. (2003) might not have
identiﬁed every strains correctly. Comparing this observation to Boukadida et al. (1993), this
approach is clearly faulty. As mentioned earlier, Boukadida et al. (1993) isolated and identiﬁed
diﬀerent strains of P. aeruginosa from cystic ﬁbrosis patients. This means the method proposed
by Rogers et al. (2003) would not have enough resolution to reach similar results as Boukadida
et al. (1993) which is problematic since the clone library is supposed to be an improvement.
To avoid this an alternative gene could be targeted.
Mulet et al. (2009) did a clone library study targeting the rpoD gene for soil samples. Mulet
et al. (2009) investigated the diversity of Pseudomonas in environmental samples using clone
libraries. For this, the primer pairs Ps-for / Ps-rev (ITS1), 70F / 70R (rpoD), and PsEG30F /
PsEG790R (rpoD; preliminary testing) were included. The Ps-for/Ps-rev and 70F/70R identi-
ﬁed Pseudomonas species, while PsEG30F/PsEG790R did not amplify any DNA (Mulet et al.,
2009). However, the depth in Mulet et al. (2009) was low, since twenty-eight and eight clones
with Pseudomonas as their closest matches, for Ps-for/Ps-rev and 70F/70R, respectively. Mulet
et al. (2009) suggests the poor result stems from a weak database of rpoD genes. However,
with a database as Hesse et al. (2018), such methods can now be improved and developed.
However, clone libraries also have problems including labour time, and extraction-, PCR-, and
database-biases (Kauﬀmann et al., 2004; Daniel, 2005; Case et al., 2007; Fierer et al., 2007b;
Rajendhran and Gunasekaran, 2008; Mulet et al., 2009; Monta˜na et al., 2012). Rogers et al.
(2003) only did a single sample, leaving out the potential to follow the changes in population
over time. Considering this, it would be hard to generate a clone library for each time point in
20
==PAGE:22==
a real-time study. In contrast, an amplicon-based strategy could be utilised. Amplicon-based
sequencing is regarded as a good method for identiﬁcation of diversity in a given sample, as
long as the primers are speciﬁc (Lundberg et al., 2013; Charlop-Powers et al., 2016; Sch¨oler
et al., 2017; Wen et al., 2017). In addition, amplicon sequencing avoids some of the steps that
are included in clone libraries, reducing potential biases.
2.4.7
Amplicon-based
Amplicon sequencing is another cultivation-independent method (Knief, 2014; Sch¨oler et al.,
2017). By extracting DNA from an environmental sample and performing a PCR reaction,
amplicons for the targeted gene is generated. The amplicons are then sent for sequencing and
can afterwards be identiﬁed and clustered to a database of genes (Lundberg et al., 2013; Knief,
2014; Sch¨oler et al., 2017; Wen et al., 2017). The advantage is barcoded primers can be used to
generated barcoded amplicons (Knief, 2014). Diﬀerent barcoded amplicons can be sequenced
together using NanoPore or MiSeq sequencing techniques since each PCR reaction has a unique
barcode and therefore can be separated after sequencing. Amplicon sequencing can target any
gene including genetic markers and functional genes. This allows the user to study speciﬁc ele-
ments of an environmental sample (Lundberg et al., 2013; Charlop-Powers et al., 2016; Sch¨oler
et al., 2017; Wen et al., 2017). This method is considered a high-throughput method and allows
for massive data generation and a better way to do real-time studies (Daniel, 2005; Fierer et al.,
2007b; Rajendhran and Gunasekaran, 2008; Knief, 2014). The high sequence power required for
amplicon sequencing has been satisﬁed as next-generation sequence methods, such as NanoPore
and MiSeq, have been developed and matured. One advantage is the fact diﬀerent amplicon-
based methods can be performed in parallel. Genes, both biomarkers and/or functional, can
be targeted in the same sample, allowing information of samples to be combined in one-to-one
relation.
An example of targeting functional genes is shown in Charlop-Powers et al. (2016). Charlop-
Powers et al. (2016) developed primers targeting polyketide synthases (PKS’s) and non-riboso-
mal peptide synthetases (NRPSs). Targeting both PKS’s and NRPS’s at the same time, allowed
Charlop-Powers et al. (2016) to identify how gene clusters for secondary metabolites were dis-
tributed across the USA. On the other hand, targeting housekeeping genes, such as the 16S
rRNA gene (prokaryotes) and the 18S rRNA gene (eukaryotes), can be used to determine the
richness and diversity of the environment (Case et al., 2007; Lundberg et al., 2013; Sch¨oler
et al., 2017; Wen et al., 2017). Combining Charlop-Powers et al. (2016) (or any other gene
encoding a function) with a genetic marker could allow for correlation of certain functionalities
21
==PAGE:23==
to species. However, the genetic marker needs high resolution to correlate speciﬁc species and
strains to functional genes.
Most metagenomic studies utilising amplicon sequencing, target the 16S rRNA gene. This gene
brings a good indication of the composition of the entire microbial community (at the genus-
level) since all bacteria are considered to have a version of it (Woese et al., 1990; Lundberg
et al., 2013; Hao et al., 2016; Wen et al., 2017). As mentioned, the 16S rRNA gene provides
a low species-level resolution of Pseudomonas (Yamamoto and Harayama, 1998; Mulet et al.,
2009, 2010, 2012; Bodilis et al., 2012; Kahlon, 2016; Forster, 2017).
However, other genes
could be the genetic marker instead of the 16S rRNA gene. These candidates have already
been mentioned multiple times already; gyrB, rpoB, and rpoD, as suggested by Yamamoto
and Harayama (1998); Yamamoto et al. (2000); Mulet et al. (2009, 2010). Due to their higher
variation, these genes could give a higher resolution compared to the 16S rRNA gene. However,
to our knowledge, no such high-resolution, amplicon-based methodology for Pseudomonas has
been purposed or reported in the literature.
2.5
Aim of the project
The aim of this project was to establish a new, high-throughput methodology to detect and
diﬀerentiate between species belonging to the genus Pseudomonas in any given environmental
sample. Primer pairs from the literature and this study were tested by a combination of in
silico and in vitro PCR methods. From here, one primer pair was selected to give the highest
resolution and preferable solely targeting the genus Pseudomonas. The primer pair was imple-
mented into an amplicon-based methodology and was tested against a deﬁned Pseudomonas
DNA mixture and against DNA extracted from soil. The methodology was compared to a 16S
rRNA-based-amplicon sequencing.

3.2
In silico PCR
465 complete genome sequences of Pseudomonas species were downloaded from NCBI (All avail-
able as of 18-02-2019). Eleven sets of primers from prior studies were selected as candidates
(table 3). Using an online-available ”in silico PCR” algorithm (URL: https://github.com/
egonozer/in_silico_pcr, downloaded 19-02-2019), PCR products were produced for each
primer pair. The settings allowed for up to one mismatch and/or one deletion/ insertion, and
the output sequences were orientated in the same direction as the forward primer, otherwise,
the settings were the defaults. The PCR products of a target gene were aligned using MUltiple
Sequence Comparison by Log-Expectation (MUSCLE v3.8.1551; default settings; Edgar (2004))
to generate a multiple sequence alignment (MSA). FastTree (Version 2.1.10 Double precision,
No SSE3; Price et al. (2010)) calculated the phylogenetic distances (ML Model: Jukes-Cantor,
CAT approximation with 20 rate categories) to generate phylogenetic trees, which were illus-
trated with R (Version: 3.5.2, packages: ”ggtrees”,”ggplot2”). The script for in silico PCR can
be seen in supplementary listing S1. In addition, in silico PCR reactions were also conducted
against a small library of twenty-four non-Pseudomonas genomes (supplementary table S1).
The primer pairs chosen for further in vitro validation, were determined by a) qualitatively
examined for its capability to group the strains in phylogenetic relationship according to Hesse
et al. (2018), b) the number of amplicons generated in the in silico PCR, c) average length of
the amplicons, and d) limited number of hits against the non-Pseudomonas library.
3.3
In silico-Primer optimisation
After initial experimentation, rpoD was found to have the highest resolution. The MSA of the
initial rpoD result was examined for alternative primer sites. A small script in R (Version: 3.5.2,
packages: ”ape”) was constructed, which examined each nucleotide in the MSA, by looking if
the frequency of a single base was at least 95% or if the frequency of two bases combined was at
least 98% in the alignment. Conserved regions were deﬁned as 16-mers (or longer) of nucleotides
following the aforementioned criteria. Primers were designed based on the conserved regions
and investigated for ampliﬁcation using in silico PCR as described above (section 3.2).

4.3
Development of the rpoD-amplicon-based methodology
To investigate the performance of the rpoD-amplicon-based methodology, it was tested in two
setups; 1) DNA ampliﬁcation of a deﬁned DNA mixture, and 2) DNA ampliﬁcation of DNA
from the soil. The rpoD-amplicon-based methodology was compared to a V3V4-based method.
For the deﬁned DNA mixture, the two methods showed a diﬀerence (ﬁgure 6). The rpoD-
based method identiﬁed eight out of the initial eleven strains (table 6), where P. lurida had
the highest abundance. Compared to the expected values, two extra species, P. monteilii and
P. brenneri, were found, while P. libanesis, P. cedrina and P. putida were barely represented
(ﬁgure 6). However, with further analysis, the disappearance of the three strains P. libanesis,
P. cedrina and P. putida and the addition of P. monteilii could be explained (see section 4.4).
Comparably, the V3V4-based method could only identify P. aeruginosa and P. ﬂuorescens,
where the later should not be represented (Nine of the strains belong to the P. ﬂuorescens
group, but are identiﬁed as diﬀerent species).
For the soil ampliﬁcation, the two methods were compared using ﬁve biological replicates of
DNA extracted from soil (P5). Here, the rpoD-based method showed the capability to diﬀeren-
tiate between species (ﬁgure 7). Ten diﬀerent species were identiﬁed in the soil sample with P.
jessenii and P. lini had the highest abundances. When compared to cultivation-based methods
(supplementary ﬁgure S6; unpublished data; PhD-student Morten Lindqvist Hansen), a similar
distribution was seen (Not all sampling data have been recorded). The V3V4-based method
indicated that less than 2% of the V3V4 abundance belonged to Pseudomonas and identiﬁed
everything as P. aeruginosa.

some of the appearances and disappearances can be explained by further analysis. P. libanesis

had to be changed from C to Y to achieve annealing (theoretically). Assuming a PCR product
from P. libanesis is poorly ampliﬁed, it can be expected the ﬁnal mixture of PCR products
contain a small fraction of P. libanesis which cannot detect.

5
Discussion
5.1
In silico-based investigation of primers
Thirteen primer pairs from the literature and this study were investigated for their potential
to identify and cluster Pseudomonas species. The primers included from the literature have
been used to establish phylogenetic relationships either alone or by multiple locus sequencing
typing (MLST) (Yamamoto et al., 2000; Hilario et al., 2004; Sarkar and Guttman, 2004; Ait
Tayeb et al., 2005; Mulet et al., 2009; Dai, 2013; Lu et al., 2017). The primer pairs were tested
against a library of Pseudomonas strains as positive controls, along with a library of a variety of
non-Pseudomonas organisms as negative controls. For the positive database, 465 Pseudomonas
genomes were downloaded from NCBI, and the negative database was selected strain outside
of the genus Pseudomonas. After looking into the established database, two discoveries were
found. The ﬁrst discovery was that the database was heavily biased towards P. aeruginosa,
which was not surprising since the species is involved in infections and has been subject in
many studies (Brenner et al., 2010; Abdelraouf and Tam, 2010). The second discovery was
the annotation quality. After preliminary results, some of the strains in the library showed
bizarre clustering patterns compared to Hesse et al. (2018). Here, it was found some of the
annotations were incorrect (for example a P. ﬂuorescens grouped in the P. aeruginosa-group in
supplementary ﬁgure S3). These were discounted in the qualitative analysis of the phylogenetic
relationships.
The primer pairs, 16sF-LYP-3 / 16sR-LYP-3, and 16S-rRNA-F / 16S-rRNA-R showed poor
ampliﬁcations in the in silico PCR. It should be emphasised, the in silico PCR only gives a
conservative estimation on the potential products generated. This is due to the fact that com-
putational power lies in recognising precise patterns. To adjust for this, settings allowing for up
to one mismatch and/or one insertion/deletion were used. These settings were the maximum
relaxation in the function. However, if a more relaxed function was used, the analysis time
would be expected to increase. The beneﬁt of conservative estimation of the in silico PCR is
that an in silico PCR product will (most likely) give an in vitro PCR product. In the results, it
was bizarre that the primers 16sF-LYP-3 / 16sR-LYP-3 did not yield any product. According
to Lu et al. (2017), the primer pair is supposed to be universal for identiﬁcation. The primers
are not degenerated, which would only allow for strict matches to be recognised and is suspected
to be the reason behind. This exempliﬁes the disadvantage of a conservative in silico PCR. At
the other end of the scale, 16S-rRNA-F / 16S-rRNA-R generated 2266 amplicons, which is an
average of 4.87 16S rRNA genes per genome included (assumed every genome had amplicons).
40
==PAGE:42==
This follows the principle showed by Bodilis et al. (2012), where nineteen Pseudomonas strains
had 4-7 alleles of the gene. The variation in the number of alleles of the genes would cause a
skewed representation of the Pseudomonas species present.
Furthermore, six primer pairs, atpD-F / atpD-F, carA-F / carA-R, glt-F / glt-R, gyrBBAUP2
/ APrU, UP-1E / APrU, and recA-F / recA-R, showed poor ampliﬁcation, generated prod-
ucts for the negative control strains, showed strange ampliﬁcation with high maximum length,
and/or showed poor phylogenetic trees compared to Hesse et al. (2018). When looking into
the literature of the primers, most of the studies use a limited selection of strains (Yamamoto
et al., 2000; Hilario et al., 2004; Sarkar and Guttman, 2004; Ait Tayeb et al., 2005; Mulet et al.,
2009; Dai, 2013; Lu et al., 2017). Some of the primers might be good in speciﬁc subspecies
identiﬁcation of cultivated strains, but the results indicate these targets are poor for establish-
ing relationships across the entire genus. From here, ﬁve of the six primer pairs were removed
for further analysis with the only exception of carA-F / carA-R, since the pair established a
reasonable phylogenetic relationship (see supplementary ﬁgure S1).
On the contrary, the primer pairs gapA-Fps / gapA-Rps, PsEG30F / PsEG790R, PsEG30F
/ PsJL490R, and PsEG30F / PsJL628R all showed high ampliﬁcation numbers, appropriate
product lengths, good phylogenetic relationships (see table 7 and ﬁgures S2, S3, S4, and S5).
These four pairs along carA-F / carA-R were chosen for further investigation. It was noted
that the primer pairs gapA-Fps / gapA-Rps and carA-F / carA-R have rarely been used in
the literature (Hilario et al., 2004; Sarkar and Guttman, 2004).
To improve this part of the framework some adjustments can be made. First, it would be
advisable to select a deﬁned training set and a deﬁned test set of genomes from the targeted
genus. For Pseudomonas, it is suggested to use the database Hesse et al. (2018) has provided
as a training set, due to the total number of diﬀerent species. As the test set, the genomes
from the NCBI database could be used. This would allow for less bias to be introduced in the
primer creation since no species would be represented more than once. Clarke et al. (2014)
used a similar approach with the inclusion of a training set and test set, in addition to using
an algorithm to identify conserved and variable regions in search of universal primers targeting
mitochondria. Using an iterative process, Clarke et al. (2014) generated primers and analysed
them. This approach generates strong candidates for metagenomic ampliﬁcation and could
reduce potential biases before in vitro testing (Clarke et al., 2014) and should be implemented
into the framework for future purposes.

5.2
In vitro PCR veriﬁcation of the in silico selected primers
Due to the conservative nature of the in silico PCR, the chosen primer pairs were experimen-
tally veriﬁed. DNA was extracted from eleven Pseudomonas strains, mostly belonging to the
ﬂuorescens-group, as the positive control. DNA from ﬁve non-Pseudomonas bacteria was in-
cluded as negative controls. Only a small sub-selection of strains was chosen since a soil-based
PCR would be used later.
The results can be seen in ﬁgure 5. The primer set PsEG30F / PsJL490R showed poor ampliﬁ-
cation, while PsEG30F / PsJL628R showed a strong ampliﬁcation of the Pseudomonas strains,
but also generated unspeciﬁc products for some of the negative control strain. These results
were not surprising since the in silico PCR algorithm used, strictly identiﬁes speciﬁc patterns.
The algorithm does not account for PCR-eﬃciency, nor does it recognise slightly unspeciﬁc
matches. The primer set carA-F / carA-R could amplify many unspeciﬁc products both for
positive and negative controls, and the pair was rendered futile. This result was very surprising
since Hilario et al. (2004) used the primer set in an MLST setup. MLST uses multiple genes
to include a higher number of nucleotides to determine the phylogenetic relationship (Maiden,
2006). However, if the primers generate unspeciﬁc products, the entire identiﬁcation is compro-
mised and renders the method proposed in Hilario et al. (2004) ineﬀective. carA-F / carA-R,
PsEG30F / PsJL490R and PsEG30F / PsJL628R were all discontinued due to these results.
However, gapA-Fps / gapA-Rps and PsEG30F / PsEG790R showed promising results against
the positive controls, negative controls, and DNA extracted from soil. PsEG30F / PsEG790R
showed strong ampliﬁcation for ten of the eleven strains, with the remaining weakly ampliﬁed
and no ampliﬁcation for any of the negative controls. This was interpreted as a good candidate
for further investigation. Yamamoto and Harayama (1998); Yamamoto et al. (2000); Sarkar and
Guttman (2004); Mulet et al. (2009, 2010, 2012); Lu et al. (2017) have all included rpoD-based
primers in their identiﬁcations and have shown good identiﬁcation, which established a good
believe in the primer pair.
The primer pair gapA-Fps / gapA-Rps could also amplify all the Pseudomonas and only one
unspeciﬁc band showed for the Achromobacter xylosoxidans. At this point the PCR setup had
not been optimised, so the weak bands were not considered too problematic. However, due
to the unspeciﬁc band for Achromobacter xylosoxidans, it was decided to experimentally verify
the ampliﬁcation aﬃnity by creating a small clone library. As seen in table 8, only three out
of twelve clones had Pseudomonas species as their closest matches. This result indicated that
42
==PAGE:44==
gapA-Fps / gapA-Rps would generate too many unspeciﬁc reads, rendering the pair impractical
to use in an amplicon-based methodology. However, the result was not unexpected. First,
many of the biomarkers are chosen, since they are universally shared genes and/or conserved
(Madigan et al., 2017; Santos and Ochman, 2004).
gapA is the gene for glyceraldehyde-3-
phosphate dehydrogenase (Sarkar and Guttman, 2004), an essential protein in glycolysis, and
it would be expected such a protein could be shared among species (Santos and Ochman, 2004).
It would, therefore, be expected at least some (if not all) the conserved genes would be shared
across taxonomic units (Santos and Ochman, 2004). Secondly, the library only used twelve
clones, which gives an inadequately statistical representation (Daniel, 2005; Rajendhran and
Gunasekaran, 2008). All in all, the primer pair gapA-Fps / gapA-Rps was discontinued, leaving
the primer pair PsEG30F / PsEG790R as the primary candidate for the methodology.
5.3
Experimental veriﬁcation of the rpoD-based methodology on a
deﬁned Pseudomonas DNA mixture
The methodology presented was intended to be amplicon sequencing-based. Therefore, the pri-
mary candidate PsEG30F / PsEG790R had to be implemented and experimentally veriﬁed to
measure the quality of the newly founded methodology. This was done by 1) amplifying DNA
from a deﬁned DNA mixture from Pseudomonas species, and 2) amplifying DNA extracted of
soil. Both setups were compared to primers targeting the V3V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene.
For the deﬁned Pseudomonas mixture, the DNA from eleven strains were mixed, ampliﬁed using
PCR and sent for MiSeq sequencing. Here, the methodology identiﬁed eight out of the eleven
strains. However, after looking into the results the missing three strains could be explained.
One of them, P. libanensis, was ampliﬁed poorly in the in vitro PCR which most likely meant
the relative fraction of PCR product for this strain was too low to be detected. As mentioned,
a single change in the reverse primer at position 11 from C to Y would theoretically allow for
P. libanensis Nina5.10 to be ampliﬁed in a suﬃcient amount. The second one, P. putida, shows
the database might be less than adequate. After aligning the three strains (the two from the
database and P. putida KT2440), it was shown P. putida KT2440 was identiﬁed as P. monteilii
DSM14164. Since the collection of Hesse et al. (2018) and P. putida KT2440 are type strains,
it is highly unlikely any of the strains is wrongly annotated. Alternatively, the two species P.
monteilii and P. putida might be too closely related to be separated, especially since the phylo-
genetic diﬀerences recorded were small (ﬁgure 8). Hesse et al. (2018) used a one-hundred gene
MLST to determine the relationship between type cultures. However, to determine how distinct
the two species are multiple type strains P. putida and P. monteilii strains can be examined
43
==PAGE:45==
with an MSA. As such, the phylogenetic relationship between them can be distinguished. For
the third one, P. cedrina May11.4, it was seen, that the partial PCR product change the iden-
tiﬁcation from P. cedrina to P. lurida when compared to the database. As further analysis
showed (ﬁgure 9), the excluded gap of 150 nt carries a high variance region. To avoid this, either
one of the two primers could be moved inward, so a smaller region was targeted. However, the
new region should still contain enough resolution, otherwise, the methodology loses its purpose.
With the corrections in mind, a comparison of the rpoD and S16 based approaches can be made.
Here, the PsEG30F / PsEG790R showed a good capability to identify the strains included in
the study and each abundance was close to its expected value. Here, it should be mentioned the
dilution relationship used for the mixture was based on weights (mass/volume) and not genomes
(genomes/volume). This can skew the distribution, since a smaller genome weighs less, meaning
a higher number of genomes are needed to achieve the same weight (increasing the pool of the
targeted gene). This was not accounted for. In addition, the abundances were similar across
the replicates. This shows the power in the primer set, Mulet et al. (2009) originally proposed
for future identiﬁcation. The quality of the methodology is especially recognisable when com-
pared to the 16S based approach. Here, only the P. aeruginosa was recognised, meanwhile, the
remaining ten strains were identiﬁed as P. ﬂuorescens. This includes P. putida, which belongs
to another group in the Pseudomonas genus according to Hesse et al. (2018). This falls in line
with multiple studies mentioning the low resolution of 16S (Yamamoto and Harayama, 1998;
Case et al., 2007; Mulet et al., 2009, 2010, 2012; Kahlon, 2016).
As described in section 3.9, all the reads generated with PsEG30F / PsEG790R were aligned
to a database of rpoD of the type strains in Hesse et al. (2018). It is important to emphasise
that this method does not allow for diﬀerentiation on the strains-/subspecies-level). All reads
from strains of the same species will be identiﬁed as that species. To improve the methodology,
the algorithm has to be rewritten to allow for diﬀerentiation on the subspecies-level before a
conclusion of the resolution at that level can be made.
5.4
Experimental veriﬁcation of the rpoD-based methodology on an
environmental sample
The rpoD-amplicon-based methodology was also examined for its potential to detect and dis-
tinguish Pseudomonas species in an environmental sample. DNA from ﬁve replicates of one
sample site (diﬀerent extractions, same sampling site) was ampliﬁed with PCR and sent for
MiSeq sequencing.
Here, the method identiﬁed ten diﬀerent Pseudomonas species and the
44
==PAGE:46==
abundances were similar across the replicates. All the identiﬁed species were all associated
with soil (Brown, 1915; Andersen et al., 2000; Kwon et al., 2003; Sikorski et al., 2001; Tvrzov´a
et al., 2006; C´amara et al., 2007; Delorme et al., 2002). P. jessenii had the highest abun-
dance and the highest variation. When discussing the results from the amplicon sequencing
with PhD student Morten Lindqvist Hansen, whom was responsible for cultivation assays for
the site, it was told they corresponded with his isolation results. He isolated and identiﬁed
30 Pseudomonas colonies (22 P. jessenii; 3 P. ﬂuorescens; 1 P. corrugata; 1 P. putida; 3 P.
koreensis). This representation matches the distribution. However, following Li et al. (2013) it
can be questioned if the cultivation method gives a good representation of the diversity. Li et al.
(2013) could only cultivate less than 4% of the Pseudomonas species present in environmental
samples. This newly-developed methodology can work around the cultivation step in a high
throughput manner, avoiding isolation biases, while maintaining high resolution.
The rpoD-based method also showed a higher resolution compared to the 16S-based approach.
All reads belonging in the 16S-based approach were assigned as P. aeruginosa. This result
follows the deﬁned mixture, and the literature, showing the low resolution of 16S and its un-
reliability (Yamamoto and Harayama, 1998; Case et al., 2007; Mulet et al., 2009, 2010, 2012;
Bodilis et al., 2012; Kahlon, 2016). However, the 16S-based approach did identify the composi-
tion of species in the sample. In addition, the 16S-based approach determined that less than 2%
of the total reads originated from the genus Pseudomonas. Inherently, the rpoD-based method
does not possess this competence and can only identify the distribution of Pseudomonas species
within the sub-population.
The closest study of this methodology was performed by Mulet et al. (2009), where the PsEG30F
/ PsEG790R were created and used. In the study, PCR products were ampliﬁed using diﬀer-
ent the primer pairs Ps-for / Ps-rev (Pseudomonas-speciﬁc 16S primers) and 70F / 70R (rpoD
primers for the entire gene). The template DNA originated from environmental samples (Mulet
et al., 2009). The PCR products were cloned into plasmids and further transformed into E.
coli cells. After cultivation, the plasmids were extracted and sent for sequencing (Mulet et al.,
2009). In one setup, the initial PCR reaction (before transformation) used the primers were
70F / 70R and later the PsEG30F / PsEG790R were used to amplify products for sequencing
(Mulet et al., 2009). The Pseudomonas-speciﬁc 16S primers (Ps-for / Ps-rev) setup created
ﬁfty-eight clones. Twenty-eight of these were identiﬁed as Pseudomonas, where twenty-ﬁve of
those were identiﬁed as P. frederiksbergensis (Mulet et al., 2009). Unfortunately, this result
was not further analysed, since they considered the skew towards P. frederiksbergensis unrea-
sonable (Mulet et al., 2009), even though it might have reﬂected the true population. Using
45
==PAGE:47==
the 70F / 70R alone, 207 and 150 clones from diﬀerent sites were obtained, yielding only 8
and 12 clones identiﬁed as Pseudomonas (Mulet et al., 2009). Due to the limited amount of
Pseudomonas, these results do not tell anything their population and their potential role in the
environment. The ﬁnal experiment with 70F / 70R for the ﬁrst round and later the PsEG30F
/ PsEG790R, yielded 111 clones, 46 of which was identiﬁed as Pseudomonas, most in the P.
aeruginosa-complex (Mulet et al., 2009). This alone suggests PsEG30F / PsEG790R is better
at targeting Pseudomonas species compared to Pseudomonas-species 16S primers. A similar
result was seen in this study, although the V3V4 primers are not speciﬁc for Pseudomonas.
Unfortunately, Mulet et al. (2009), did not have a setup only using PsEG30F / PsEG790R.
However, the rpoD-amplicon-based methodology presented in this work have shown a greater
in depth characterisation since here the entire metagenome of Pseudomonas has been covered.
Initially, the setup was examined against soil samples from three diﬀerent sites (referred to as
P5, P8, and P9). Unfortunately, two of the sites did not provide suﬃcient ampliﬁcation. P8 re-
peatedly did not show bands in gel electrophoresis, while P9 showed inconsistency bands across
the ﬁve biological replicates. It was hypothesised that the two sites contained too few pseu-
domonads to detect. After discussing with PhD student Pernille Kjersgaard Bech, whom did a
parallelised study using V3V4 on the same soil samples it was reported, the two sites P8 and
P9 showed the genus Pseudomonas to have an abundance of less than 0.1%. The results were
also discussed with PhD student Morten Lindqvist Hansen, whom also did cultivation-studies
of the samples. He reported Pseudomonas could be isolated from these samples, however, to a
lesser extent with eight from P8 and sixteen from P9 isolates compared to thirty from P5. This
conﬁrms that the populations of Pseudomonas in P8 and P9 are low, but presence. Further
investigations have to be conducted to identify the lower limit of the new rpoD-amplicon-based
methodology. However, after the PCR reaction was optimised (see ﬁgure 10), P9 began showing
stronger bands, indicating higher eﬃciency and could potentially be included in future studies.
In addition to investigating the rpoD-amplicon-based method, methods for AD- and KS-domain
ampliﬁcation were done in parallel. Unfortunately, this did not yield any consistent results
and was therefore excluded from the study. However, parallelising the rpoD-amplicon-based
with AD-and-KS-amplicon-based methodologies could allow for correlation of Pseudomonas to
secondary metabolites as a potential driver or natural inhabitant, improving the methodology
presented in Charlop-Powers et al. (2016). However, it should be emphasised if a correlation
exists, it does not give the causality but would allow for new hypotheses for further investigation
of natural products and their role in ecological niches. But, the primary goal is to develop and
improve methods, so such observations can be recorded in the ﬁrst place.

Future perspectives
A few additional optimisations of the framework have been suggested throughout the discus-
sion. 1) Use well-deﬁned training and test sets of genomes for the selection of primers. This
potentially includes an automated process similar to Clarke et al. (2014). This would allow
for a bioinformatic approach to selecting key genetic markers. 2) Optimise the PCR reaction.
Eﬃciency is important, especially with a small pool of target genes. This would ensure proper
ampliﬁcation, potentially reducing the number of PCR cycles or increase the ampliﬁcation
limit. 3) Improve on the database from Hesse et al. (2018). The database already contains a
large selection of type strains, however, in the case of P. putida/P. monteilii (and similar) the
classiﬁcation criteria have to be well established. 4) Rewrite the rpoD-amplicon script to allow
for diﬀerentiation on the strain-level.
As the methodology stands, it should be further tested against environmental samples. In the
present study, three soil samples were included. However, two of the three samples did not yield
consistent products. Therefore, it is advisable to investigate the lower limits of the methodology
by covering a higher array of samples. This includes other types of samples, such as marine,
blood, and plants, to investigate the full potential of the rpoD-amplicon-based methodology.
The detection limit can be investigated by spiking samples with Pseudomonas DNA in diﬀerent
concentration which generates a standard curve.
The ultimate next step is the implementation of the methodology into population-related stud-
ies. The resolution of the rpoD-amplicon-based methodology allows for studying of the com-
position of Pseudomonas species in the environment and model systems.
Due to the high
throughput nature of amplicon sequencing, the methodology can also be used in real-time
studies. This is especially critical for bioaugmentation since the consequences of the introduced
strain should be well understood. Introduction biological control agents into the environment
can be a good idea, however, understanding how persistent the species is, and how abiotic
and biotic factors aﬀect it, are important to map the underlying mechanisms and ecological
function.

Conclusion
In this thesis, a new rpoD-amplicon-based methodology has been developed. Using in silico
PCR, ﬁve primers pair amplifying species from Pseudomonas with good resolution. The ﬁve
primer pairs were further tested using in vitro PCR methods. Here, only one primer pair solely
ampliﬁed Pseudomonas in suﬃcient amounts. The pair was PsEG30F and PsEG790R which
targets the sigma 70 factor. The pair was implemented in an amplicon-based methodology. The
rpoD-amplicon-based method showed good resolution for identiﬁcation and diﬀerentiation of
Pseudomonas species in both a deﬁned DNA mixture and in an environmental sample achiev-
ing similar results as cultivation-based methods. Comparatively, the 16S-approach could not
achieve the same resolution.
