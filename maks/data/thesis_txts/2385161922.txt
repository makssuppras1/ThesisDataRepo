Abstract 
Fish is one of the important source for human to intake enough unsaturated fatty acid 
for daily life. Food fortified with fish oil gradually become a better and convenient 
option for people with limited access to fish in their daily diet.  
However, fish oil is vulnerable to oxidation due to its multi double carbon bond 
structure. To protect fish oil in the food, electrospraying becomes an emerging method 
because it can protect fish oil by a physical barrier (capsule shell) without heat 
treatment. Also, antioxidant can be added into the capsules to further improve the 
oxidative stability of fish oil. 
The aim of the project was set to analyze the oxidative stability of capsules produced 
by emulsion containing fish oil, emulsifiers and biopolymers. Also, antioxidant 
extracted from seaweed Fucus vesiculosus was added into one capsule group to test 
the efficacy. 
The result indicated that emulsion containing 0.5% Citrem, 0.5% WPC, 1% pullulan, 
15% dextran and 20% fish oil (% of solid content) was the optimum recipe. Then the 
emulsion was sent to pilot plant in Spain to produce capsules in large scale. For the 
first batch, microfluidizer was used to incorporate fish oil into emulsion in DTU and 
the capsules were called Control-Microfluidizer. The second batch used ultraturrax to 
incorporate fish oil into emulsion in pilot plant and capsules were called 
Control-Ultraturrax and Seaweed-Ultraturrax (added antioxidant).  
The result showed that homogenization approaches affected the physicochemical 
properties of the capsules and ultraturrax performed better than microfluidizer. For the 
size of capsules, Control-Microfluidizer group had an average diameter of 3.022 ±

Regarding to encapsulation efficiency, Control-Microfluidizer capsules (86.86% ± 
1.45%) was significantly lower than Control-Ultraturrax (91.69% ± 0.92%) and 
Seaweed-Ultraturrax (90.42% ± 0.11%). For peroxide value, Control-Microfluidizer 
(43.81 ± 7.31 meq/kg oil) was significantly larger than Control-Ultraturrax (24.87 ± 
0.37 meq/kg oil) and Seaweed-Ultraturrax (27.37 ± 1.18 meq/kg oil) at the end of 21 
days storage experiment. Also, the formation of heptanal, nonanal, 2-pentyl-furan,

Seaweed antioxidant only significantly inhibit the formation of heptanal and nonanal 
compared to control group during 21-day storage experiment. Also, it showed the 
ability to protect fish oil in early storage days (Day 0 to Day 8) based on the result of 
1-penten-3-one, 1-penten-3-ol, hexanal, 2-hexenal and (E,E)-2,4-heptadienal. Except 
for these volatiles result, seaweed antioxidant did not show clear inhibition effect for 
the oxidation of capsules and the physicochemical properties had no significant 
difference compared to control group.  
However, the oxidative stability of all the capsules were significantly lower than fish 
oil. The problem might be the oxidation of fish oil was already being initiated during 
the production. Therefore, in further studies, the oxidation during production needs to 
be avoid.

1. Introduction and theory 
Food is important in the daily life of human to provide energy and nutrition. 
Nowadays with the development of economy, more and more people are pursuing 
healthy eating styles and intake enough nutrition as suggested.  
One of the important food in human diet is fish. Fish contains high amount of ω-3 
unsaturated fatty acids which have been investigated in plenty of studies for their 
beneficial for human health (Calder & Yaqoob, 2009). One study indicated that EPA 
and DHA could partly inhibit several aspects of inflammation in human cell (Calder, 
2014). Also, EPA and DHA were components in brain and eye cells, which were 
crucial during the growth of infant (Shahidi, 2011). Moreover, the risk of 
cardiovascular disease could be reduced by intake of ω-3 fatty acids (Albert et al., 
2002). Some studies suggested that ω-3 fatty acids might reduce the risk of depression 
for human (Shahidi, 2011).  
The demand for ω-3 fatty acids increase dramatically in recent years with the trend for 
healthy food. The market for ω-3 fatty acids is expected to be $ 6,955 million in 2022 
with a Compound Annual Growth Rate of 14.9% from 2016-2022 (Allied Market 
Research, 2016). 
Consequently, functional food fortified with fish oil could be a promising way to 
improve the intake of the essential fatty acids for human with limited access to fish in 
their daily diet.  
Encapsulate fish oil into capsules is a good way to protect the oil from oxidation, 
methods such as spray-drying and electrospraying are used to produce fish oil-loaded 
capsules. But the protection effect varied between different wall materials and 
methods (García-Moreno et al., 2016; Hogan, O’Riordan, & O’Sullivan, 2003; 
Torres-Giner, Martinez-Abad, Ocio, & Lagaron, 2010). Therefore, this project was 
carried out to analyze the oxidative stability of fish oil encapsulated in dextran 
capsule. 
1.1. Hypothesis 
 Encapsulation of fish oil into dextran capsules by electrospraying can provide 
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
physical protection for fish oil against lipid oxidation. 
 The approach used to emulsify fish oil can affect the physicochemical properties 
and oxidative stability of the electrosprayed dextran capsules. 
 Seaweed antioxidant can improve oxidative stability of fish oil encapsulated in 
dextran capsules by inhibiting or retarding lipid oxidation. 
1.2. Objectives 
 Optimize the composition of emulsion to produce fish-oil-loaded dextran 
capsules by electrospraying. 
 To investigate the effect of the oil-incorporation approach (ultraturrax only or 
ultraturrax + microfluidizer) on the stability of emulsions, physicochemical 
properties (morphology, oil distribution and encapsulation efficiency) and 
oxidative stability of dextran capsules. 
 To evaluate the effect of seaweed antioxidants on the oxidative stability of 
fish-oil-loaded capsules.

2. Theoretical background 
2.1. Fish oil and polyunsaturated fatty acid 
Eicosapentaenoic acid (C20:5n-3, EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (C22:6n-3, DHA) 
are two important long chain omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids. They both have 
carboxyl group and methyl group at the beginning and the end of chain respectively. 
More than one double bonds appear in the carbon chain of omega-3 polyunsaturated 
fatty acids and the first one is at ω carbon next to the methyl end (Figure 1). EPA and 
DHA can be converted from α-Linolenic acid (C18:6n-3, ALA), which is also one of 
the polyunsaturated fatty acid and is commonly existed in plant oils such as linseeds 
and rapeseed oil (Calder & Yaqoob, 2009). 
 
 
  
Figure 1 Chemical structure of ALA, EPA and DHA 
However, the conversion rate of ALA to EPA and DHA is limited in human (Burdge 
& Calder, 2006). Therefore, it is essential to intake fatty acids from food enriched 
with EPA and DHA or from nutritional supplements. Fish or fish oil is the main 
source of ω-3 fatty acids. It is reported that 1 g fish oil capsule contains around 0.3 g 
of EPA and DHA, and the amount of individual fatty acid depends on the type of fish 
oil (Calder & Yaqoob, 2009).

2.2. The oxidation of fish oil 
However, the ω-3 fatty acids are vulnerable to oxidation during all stages from raw 
materials to the food consumed by human such as production, storage, etc. In general, 
there are three types of oxidation. 
2.2.1. Autoxidation 
Autoxidation is the main oxidation process happens in dark and in the absence of 
enzyme. There are three steps in the lipid autoxidation process. 
 Initiation 
Metal ions and heat are initiators of the lipid autoxidation. When unsaturated fatty 
acid (LH) is exposed to the initiators, hydrogen radical (H·) will be removed from the 
fatty acid and free lipid radical (L·) will form (Equation 1) (Jacobsen, Sørensen, & 
Nielsen, 2013). 
LH → L ∙ + H ∙                    (1) 
 Propagation 
When oxygen exist, it can react with highly reactive free lipid radical L· and form 
peroxyl radical (LOO·). Peroxyl radical can react with another unsaturated fatty acid 
to form hydroperoxide (LOOH) and a new free lipid radical. The new free lipid 
radical can react with oxygen circularly (Equation 2, 3) (Jacobsen et al., 2013).  
L · + 𝑂2 → LOO ∙                    (2) 
LOO · + LH → LOOH + L ·                    (3) 
For linolenic acid, it has two bis-allylic methylene groups, which are very active and 
can easily loss a hydrogen atom. When hydrogen is abstracted from the linolenic acid, 
hybrid pentadienyl radical will form. Since the two bis-allylic methylene groups acts 
independently, two types of hybrid pentadienyl radical may exists and react with 
oxygen at the end carbon position. Therefore, 4 types of hydroperoxides can be 
produced as shown in Figure 2 (Frankel, 2005). 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
 
Figure 2 Autoxidation of linolenic acid (Frankel, 2005). 
Hydroperoxide is regarded as primary oxidation product, which is an important 
indicator of the degree of oxidation. Hydroperoxide can decomposed to alkoxyl lipid 
radical with presence of initiators (Equation 4) and further degraded (Figure 3) to 
secondary oxidation product volatile such as aldehydes, alcohol and furanes (Gueraud 
et al., 2010). The rancid off-flavors of oxidized lipid are from the formation of volatile, 
while hydroperoxide is tasteless. 
To inhibit lipid oxidation, antioxidant can be added. Primary antioxidant can react 
with peroxyl radical and form less reactive antioxidant radicals. Secondary 
antioxidant can chelate iron to reduce the decomposition of hydroperoxide. While 
antioxidant such as phenolic antioxidant can have pro-oxidative effect because the 
phenoxyl radicals are unstable and can take hydrogen atom from lipid (Jacobsen et al., 
2013). 
LOOH +  LH → LO ∙ + 𝑂𝐻−                   (4)

 Termination 
There are several reactions can happen at the termination stage when radicals are 
accumulated to high level (Equation 5, 6, 7) (Frankel, 2005). 
LOO · + LOO · → LOOOOL → non radical product + 𝑂2                 (5) 
LO · + L ∙ → LOL                    (6) 
L · + L ∙ → LL                    (7) 
The oxidation of fish oil not only reduced the nutritional value of product, also it 
causes deterioration and forming unpleasant smell. Therefore, the shelf-life and 
customer acceptance for the food are reduced.  
2.2.2. Photooxidation 
The photooxidation can happen when unsaturated fatty acids are exposed to light. 
Photosensitizers such as pigments can absorb energy from light and change its excited 
state. Then the oxidation pathways depend on the type of sensitizers (Equation 8, 9).

Since storage experiment of the project was carried in dark, photooxidation had 
limited effect on the result of oxidation. 
2.2.3. Enzyme oxidation 
Enzyme oxidation occurs in raw materials by lipoxygenases (Lox). The enzymes can 
be activated by hydroperoxides containing cis,cis-1,4-pentadiene moiety and the iron 
atom in the active center of enzyme will be oxidized (Frankel, 2005). Then the 
enzyme will bound with unsaturated fatty acid and react with oxygen to form 
hydroperoxide (Equation 10). 
Lox˗Fe2+ +  LOOH → Lox˗Fe3+ +  LH → Lox˗Fe2+˗L∙+ 𝑂2  
→ Lox˗Fe2+˗LOO∙+ 𝐻+  → LOOH                (10) 
Lipoxygenases exists in fish tissues and it should not present in the refined 
commercial cod liver oil used in this project. Therefore, enzyme oxidation would not 
happen in the experiment. 
2.3. The methods to prevent oxidation 
To solve the oxidation problem and protect ω-3 fatty acids, fish oil can be 
encapsulated into biopolymer capsules. The biopolymers act as physical barrier to 
protect fish oil from oxygen, light and prooxidants. Then the capsules are 
incorporated into food. Spray drying is the most common method used in the food 
industry for encapsulation (Bruschi, Cardoso, Lucchesi, & Gremião, 2003; 
López-Rubio & Lagaron, 2012). Electrospraying is a promising alternative to 
spray-drying (Anu Bhushani & Anandharamakrishnan, 2014; Lim, 2015).  
To produce electrosprayed/spray-dried capsule loaded with fish oil, oil-in-water 
emulsion should be prepared. The emulsion contains oil, emulsifiers and polymers, 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
also surfactant can be added to reduce surface tension when needed. Proteins and/or 
polysaccharides are added to the emulsion to form the shell of capsule in the 
electrospraying process (Ghorani & Tucker, 2015). After adding all the materials, the 
oil will be emulsified in the biopolymer solution by using rotor-stator devices such as 
ultraturrax and homogenizers such as two-valve and microfluidizer. This allows to 
break down oil droplets and make them stable in the emulsion (avoiding flocculation, 
coalescence and creaming). During electrospraying/spray-drying, the emulsion will be 
dried and oil can be wrapped by polymers and form sphere capsules. 
 Spray drying 
In spray drying process, a pump transports an oil-in-water emulsion to a spray nozzle 
at the top of the tank. The heated drying gas at 140 – 160 °C in the tank can dry the 
sprayed emulsion and dried powder can be obtained through cyclone (Bruschi et al., 
2003) (Figure 4). The advantage for spray drying is that it has high production yield. 
However, when the emulsion is exposed to high temperature during spray drying, it 
might deteriorate the fish oil and oxidation might be initialized.  
 
Figure 4 Diagram of spray drying equipment 
 Electrospraying 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
An emerging method to encapsulate bioactive ingredients such as omega-3 fatty acids 
is electrospraying. The advantage of the method is that it can produce dried 
nano-capsules at room temperature, therefore the heat-liable bioactive compounds 
will not be affected (Anu Bhushani & Anandharamakrishnan, 2014).  
In electrospraying, the emulsion after homogenization is then added to a syringe on 
the automatic pump. High voltage provider (1 - 30 kV) is connected on the needle of 
syringe to charge the tip. A conductive collector is placed at certain distance away 
from the needle tip and connected to earth. When voltage is applied on the tip, a static 
electric field is formed and it can charge the tip to generate a force against surface 
tension. When voltage increases to a certain level, the force will overcome surface 
tension and a charged jet will be ejected from the Taylor-cone formed at the tip of the 
needle. When low viscoelasticity emulsion is used, the jet is unstable and will break 
down into droplets. The solvent is evaporated during the way of the droplets flying to 
the collector (Figure 5).  
 
Figure 5 Diagram of electrospraying 
The morphology of capsules can be controlled by modifying the property of emulsion 
or process conditions. High concentration of polymers in the emulsion (or polymers 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
with high molecular weight) will increase the viscoelasticity of the emulsion, 
therefore increase the stability of the jet during elongation. As a result, more fibers 
will form. On the contrary, the jet is unstable and can break down to small droplets 
when the polymer concentration is low (or polymers with low molecular weight) (Anu 
Bhushani & Anandharamakrishnan, 2014). Process conditions including voltage, flow 
rate and distance between collector and needle can also affect the morphology of the 
final capsules. Studies on electrospinning shows that process condition can affect the 
diameter of fiber and the formation of bead defects within the fiber (Weiss, 
Kanjanapongkul, Wongsasulak, & Yoovidhya, 2012). The voltage affects the amount 
of polymer emulsion ejected from the Taylor cone, it was reported that the relation 
between diameter and voltage was like a “U” shape and there could be an optimum 
voltage to get lowest product diameter (Ghorani & Tucker, 2015). Meanwhile flow 
rate and distance are related to the solvent evaporation. Flow rate and distance must 
be set to suitable value according to voltage to obtain dried capsules. 
The electrospraying technology was chosen for the project because it could produce 
smaller capsules than spray drying (Drusch, 2007; Torres-Giner et al., 2010). Smaller 
capsules are preferred because they are more easily to incorporate into food matrices 
without affecting the texture of food.  
The encapsulation efficiency of electrospraying capsules is 80.4% by Chakraborty, 
Liao, Adler, & Leong (2009) and almost 90% by López-Rubio & Lagaron (2012). 
While the encapsulation efficiency for spray drying capsules is from 39% to 41% by 
Bruschi et al. (2003) and from 62.3% to 95.7% by Carneiro, Tonon, Grosso, & 
Hubinger (2013). High encapsulation efficiency is preferred to protect the 
encapsulated compound.  
Electrosprayed capsules have high specific surface area, which can better release 
bioactive during digestion. While the high specific surface area can also increase the 
risk of oxidation and cause agglomeration between capsules (Anu Bhushani & 
Anandharamakrishnan, 2014).

show that

2.4. Dextran 
Dextran is a polysaccharide glucan produced by the fermentation of Leuconostoc 
mesenteroides and widely used in industries such as pharmaceutical and agricultural 
(Pharmacosmos, 2017). Dextran is a long chain polysaccharide mainly linked by 
α-1,6-glycosidic linkages with some branches linked by α-1,3-glycosidic linkage (see 
Figure 6) (Fathi, Martín, & McClements, 2014). 
 
Figure 6 Structure of dextran (Pharmacosmos, 2017) 
Dextran is used in several studies to produce electrospinning/electrospraying products 
as wall material because of its properties such as water soluble and biocompatible 
(García-Moreno et al., 2017; Pérez-Masiá, Lagaron, & Lopez-Rubio, 2014). Although 
the dextran emulsion showed better antioxidant property than other biopolymers 
(pullulan and whey protein concentrate), the capsules produced by dextran emulsion 
had low oxidative stability because of the poor interaction between dextran and fish 
oil (García-Moreno et al., 2017). Therefore, this project combined dextran with whey 
protein concentrate and pullulan to analyse the oxidative stability of the capsules 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
made from them. 
2.5. Antioxidants 
Antioxidant can be added into encapsulated fish oil to further improve the oxidative 
stability. Serfert, Drusch, & Schwarz (2009) analysed the effect of antioxidant in fish 
oil loaded capsules and found out that a combination of tocopherol, lecithin and 
ascorbyl palmitate or using rosemary extract could significantly retard the 
autoxidation in capsules during storage. Similar result was found that the oxidative 
stability of zein fibre could be significantly improved by ferulic acid (Yang et al., 
2017). 
Seaweeds have been regarded as source of food for long period. They can be added 
into food to give additional nutrition value or been cooked as food itself. Seaweed are 
rich in sulphated polysaccharides, pigments and phlorotannins (polyphenolic 
secondary metabolite), therefore they are also important sources of natural antioxidant 
(Sabeena Farvin & Jacobsen, 2013). Natural antioxidants are preferred to replace 
synthetic antioxidant because of the toxicity effect of synthetic antioxidant on human  
(Lindenschmidt, Tryka, Goad, & Witschi, 1986).  
Fucus vesiculosus is a widely distributed brown alga and contains high amount of 
phlorotannins (Wang et al., 2012). Phlorotannins are divided into three types for its 
inter-phloroglucinol linkages (Figure 7). Phlorotannins can act as primary antioxidant 
because of its radical scavenging capacity. Phlorotannins contains several hydroxy 
group (-OH), which can react with free lipid radicals in propagation step and donate 
hydrogen atom. Therefore, antioxidant radicals are formed, which are less reactive 
than free lipid radicals and can also react with free lipid radicals directly (Jacobsen et 
al., 2013). The iron chelating ability of ethanol extract of Fucus vesiculosus indicates 
its function as secondary antioxidant by chelating and inactivate metals using two 
neighboring hydroxy group, thus avoided the formation of volatile by decomposition 
of hydroperoxide (Jacobsen et al., 2013). Therefore, Fucus vesiculosus ethanol extract 
is a multi-functional antioxidant. 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
 
Figure 7 Three types of phlorotannins (Isaza Martínez & Torres Castañeda, 2013) 
The type of solvent can affect the amount of total phenol content in extract. Ethanol 
extract of F. vesiculosus had higher total phenolic content than water because most 
proteins could precipitate in ethanol and some phenolic compound with reversible 
bounds were left in the solution (Sabeena Farvin & Jacobsen, 2013). Study shown that 
the antioxidant activity of ethanol extracts (80% ethanol v/v) derived from F. 
vesiculosus possessed high radical scavenging activity which can be correlated to a 
high phlorotannin content (Hermund, Jacobsen, & Nielsen, 2016). 
Previous studies have determined the antioxidant efficacy of different Fucus 
vesiculosus extract in food matrices added fish oil. For high moisture food system,  
Honold, Jacobsen, Jónsdóttir, Kristinsson, & Hermund (2016) added different 
concentrations of extracts to fish-oil enriched mayonnaise and studied the oxidative 
stability. The results showed that high concentration of seaweed extract improved the 
oxidative stability of the mayonnaise, while low concentration of water extract of 
young leaves appeared to have pro-oxidative effect. Since the presence of metals is 
the main part of oxidation in oil-in-water emulsion systems, the metal chelating 
property plays the most important role of the antioxidant ability in the extract (Honold 
et al., 2016).  
In another study, acetone and ethanol extract of Fucus vesiculosus were added to fish 
oil-enriched granola bars, which was low-moisture system (Karadağ et al., 2017). 
Results showed that by adding the seaweed extracts the oxidative stability were 
improved and lipophilic antioxidant such as tocopherols might have high antioxidant 
efficacy.  
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
These studies demonstrated that ethanolic extracts have the ability to work as an 
antioxidant in different food systems.

3. Materials and methods 
3.1. Materials 
Commercial cod liver oil was provided by Maritex A/S, which contained 9.2% EPA, 
11.4% DHA and the peroxide value (PV) for the fish oil was 0.38 ± 0.04 meq/kg oil 
(García-Moreno et al., 2016). Whey protein concentrate (WPC, Lacprodan® DI-8090) 
was provided by ARLA Food Ingredients. Tween 20 was provided by Sigma-Aldrich. 
Citrem (Citric acid ester without antioxidants) was provided by Danisco. Pullulan 
(Molecular weight = 200,000 Da) was provided by Hayashibara Co., Ltd. Dextran 
(Molecular weight = 70,000 Da) was provided by Pharmacosmos A/S.  
 Seaweed antioxidant 
Seaweed F. vesiculosus was harvest and washed with tap water. Then it was 
freeze-dried and pulverized to powder. During extraction, 80% ethanol v/v was added 
to disperse seaweed powders and incubated on platform shaker. The mixture was 
centrifuged and filtered after 24 hours incubation (Hermund et al., 2016). 
Antioxidant properties of seaweed antioxidant (ethanol extract from F. vesiculosus): 
Total phenolic content, 20.4 ± 2.4 g GAE/100g dw; DPPH radical scavenging 
capacity (1/EC50 ± SD), 75.3 ± 15.4 mg dw/mL; Iron chelating ability (1/EC30 ± SD), 
2.1 ± 0.3 mg dw/mL; Reducing power (1/EC0.5 ± SD), 7.9±0.0 mg dw/mL was 
obtained in our lab as described in Hermund et al. (2016). The antioxidant activities in 
the extract were from both phlorotannins and other compounds in the extract such as 
tocopherols and pigments (Hermund et al., 2016).  
3.2. Production of emulsion for electrospraying and process 
optimization 
 Lab trial approaches 
Different combinations of WPC and polymers were tested to obtain best morphology 
of capsule. To prepare the emulsion for electrospraying, WPC, pullulan and dextran 
were mixed in distilled water, stirring overnight using magnetic stirrer at 1000 rpm.

Fish oil (20% of solid content) was incorporated into the mixture using ultraturrax 
(Ystral GmbH, Ballrechten-Dottingen, Germany) at 16000 rpm for 1 minute and 
continue stirring for 2 minutes. Then, the oil-polymer emulsion was homogenized 
through microfluidizer (M-110L Microfluidizer processors, Newton, MA, USA) at 
9000 psi for 3 passes. The emulsion production diagram is shown in Figure 8.

The production method above use ultraturrax to produce a coarse emulsion by 
breaking down fish oil into droplets. Then the homogenizer microfluidizer applies 
high pressure to the emulsion and pass it through a chamber (CIXC, F20Y, internal 
dimension 75 µm), in which the internal structure can force collision in the emulsion 
and further reduce the size of droplets (Horn, Nielsen, Jensen, Horsewell, & Jacobsen, 
2012).  
After the homogenization, the fish oil droplets could be stably distributed in the 
emulsion for 1 week, and nano-capsules with few fibers around could be produced 
using the optimum recipe in the lab-scale electrospraying equipment (results shown in 
Appendix 1). Nonetheless, some oil films and droplets appeared on top of the 
emulsion after passing through microfluidizer, which might affect the encapsulation 
efficiency of oil and reduce the oxidative stability. 
 1st oil-incorporation approach 
Therefore, to solve the issue, Citrem was added to the recipe to improve the effect of 
emulsification. Furthermore, the emulsion preparation was modified as: WPC and 
polymers (pullulan and dextran) were dissolved separately in distilled water and 
stirred overnight using magnetic stirrer at 500 rpm. Citrem and fish oil were added 
into the WPC mixture when ultraturrax the mixture at 16,000 rpm for 1 minute and 
continue stirring for 2 minutes under nitrogen flow, then 3 passes through 
microfluidizer (9000 psi). Mix the emulsion stabilized with citrem and WPC and

3.4.2. Distribution of oil 
 Oil distribution 
Laser diffraction method was conducted to measure the oil droplet distributed in

3.5. Oxidative stability of capsule 
Storage experiment was carried out after receiving the capsules from pilot plant. The 
capsules and fish oil were stored in 10 mL vial at room temperature in dark with 
similar headspace. The experiment lasted for 21 days and at day 0, 3, 8, 14 and 21, the 
samples for peroxide and secondary oxidation products measurement were flushed 
with nitrogen and stored in -40 °C freezer for later analysis. 
3.5.1. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) 
FTIR technology can also been used to analyze the oxidative stability of oil. 
Functional groups vibration can be shown on FTIR spectrum at similar range, 
therefore can be identified qualitatively and quantitatively (Tenyang et al., 2013).  
A storage experiment was carried out to measure the change of absorbance for 28 
days. Three groups of capsules produced in pilot plant were measured. Fish oil as well 
as blank sample (produced by emulsion without oil and blend oil (20% of solid 
content) into the blank capsules to simulate 0% encapsulation efficiency) were also 
measured for comparison. 
10 mg of capsule was placed on the crystal of the Spectrum 100 spectrometer (Perkin 
Elmer, Waltham, MA, USA). The range of measurement was from 4000 cm-1 to 650 
cm-1. A unique peak was found for only fish oil and capsule at 3012 cm-1 (Figure 13), 
which was belonged to cis-double bond (=CH) C-H stretching vibration (Giménez, 
Gómez-Guillén, Pérez-Mateos, Montero, & Márquez-Ruiz, 2011). Previous studies 
have shown the correlation between the height of 3012 cm-1 peak and the degree of 
oxidation (Tenyang et al., 2013). The measurement was done in triplicate per sample.

3.5.2. Peroxide value (PV) 
Bligh and Dyer method was used to extract oil from capsules with modification and 
measure the actual oil content, the experiment was done for duplicate (Bligh & Dyer, 
1959). 
0.5 g of capsules were dissolved in 10 mL distilled water in the bottle. Then 15 mL 
chloroform and 30 mL methanol were added into the bottle. The mixture was 
homogenized by ultraturrax for 30 s at 14000 rpm. After adding 15 mL chloroform, 
another 30 s ultraturrax homogenization was done at the same condition. 15 mL of 
distilled water was added to the bottle and bottles were balanced in pair by adding 
distilled water before centrifugation. Then the mixture was centrifuged at 2800 rpm 
for 10 min at 18 °C. After centrifugation, the upper phase was sucked away and the 
remaining liquid was filtered by phase separation filter paper. 
Then 7 – 9 g of extract was measured into test tubes and evaporate by nitrogen flow in 
fume hood. The remaining liquid was weighted and poured into beaker (dried and 
weighted) for evaporation. 10 mL chloroform/methanol (7:3 v/v) was added to tubes 
followed by 50 µL thiocyanate solution and 50 µL Fe(2+)chloride solution. Standard 
group with Fe(3+)chloride solution and blank group were also prepared. After 5 min 
incubation in darkness at room temperature, the absorbance of liquid in the tubes was 
measured in spectrophotometer at 500 nm using photometric method (Shantha &

Decker, 1994) to calculate PV. 
3.5.3. Secondary oxidation products 
Secondary oxidation products (volatile) were formed after the decomposing of 
peroxide. To investigate the type and amount of volatiles in capsules and fish oil, 
Dynamic headspace GC-MS (Gas Chromatograph-Mass Spectrometry) method was 
used. 
Capsule 
0.4 g of capsules, 10 mg internal standard (4-methyl-1-pentanol), 1 mL 
antifoam (Synperonic 800 mL/L water) and 5 mL distilled water were added into 100 
mL purge bottle. After gently shaking to dissolve the capsule, the bottle was 
connected to a purge head and bubble with nitrogen at 250 mL/min in 45 °C water 
bath for 30 mins. A Tenax-GR tube was connected to the purge head to collect and 
concentrate the released volatiles. After 30 mins collection, the tubes were dried by 50 
mL/min nitrogen flow for 20 mins. 
Fish oil  
4 g of fish oil and 30 mg internal standard (4-methyl-1-pentanol) were 
added into 100 mL purge bottle. The bottle was connected to a purge head and bubble 
with nitrogen at 150 mL/min in 75 °C water bath for 30 mins. A Tenax-GR tube was 
connected to the purge head to collect and concentrate the released volatiles.  
The dynamic headspace method for both capsule and fish oil were measured in 
triplicate. After volatile collection, the tubes were put on Automatic Thermal 
Desorption system (ATD-400, Perkin Elmer) to analyze by GC-MS. Capsules and fish 
oil samples were running under different methods. External standards were selected as 
2-ethyl-furan, 1-penten-3-one, pentanal, 1-penten-3-ol, (E)-2-pentenal, hexanal, 
2-hexenal, heptanal, 2-pentyl-furan, (E,E)-2,4-heptadienal and nonanal. The external 
standards were prepared based on the volatile compound found in the capsule/fish oil 
and were diluted to 2.5, 5, 10, 50, 100, 500 µg/g in ethanol. To make calibration curve, 
1 µl of each standard solution were added to Tenax-tube by syringe, then the tubes 
were flushed by nitrogen at 50 mL/min for 5 mins. Triplicate tubes were prepared for 
each concentration and two sets of calibration were made to analyze capsules and fish 
oil respectively.

4. Result and discussion 
4.1. Optimization of biopolymer concentration 
The morphology of capsules depends on both the concentration of polymers in the 
emulsion as well as their molecular weight and the process conditions used such as 
voltage, distance and flow rate. 
To obtain optimized recipe of emulsion, different combinations of raw materials (1% 
WPC, 1-2% pullulan, 15-20% dextran and 20% fish oil (% of solid content)) were 
tested and electrosprayed to check the droplet size of oil and morphology of capsules. 
The result showed that by decreasing the amount of dextran and pullulan in emulsion, 
the viscoelasticity of emulsion was also decreased. The polymer chain entanglement 
in the jet thus became unstable and break down to droplets. As shown in SEM images 
(Figure 14), more capsules were produced instead of fibers and the fiber in between of 
capsules were further decreased with the decreasing of polymer concentration. Similar 
result was found by García-Moreno et al. (2017) showing that the morphology of 
products changed from fibers to capsules with the decrease of biopolymer (pullulan, 
dextran and WPC) concentration. 
The emulsion with 1% WPC + 1% pullulan + 15% dextran + 20% fish oil (% of solid 
content) had lowest D[3,2] and d(0.9) value among all the emulsions, which could 
result in smaller capsule size after electrospraying. When electrospraying this recipe, 
the flow rate was relatively high and the production rate was around 0.1 g capsules/h. 
Also, capsules without fibers in between was preferred because they could be 
incorporated into food matrices easier. Therefore, based on the result of lab trials, 
emulsion with 1% WPC + 1% pullulan + 15% dextran + 20% fish oil (% of solid 
content) was chosen as the optimal recipe for the electrospraying.

4.3.3. Encapsulation efficiency (EE) 
The objective of electrospraying is to provide polymer shell to oil loaded in the 
capsule. Polymer shell act as physical barrier and reduced the contact between oil and 
oxygen. High encapsulation efficiency is preferred to improve the oxidative stability 
of oil, because the oil on the surface of capsules can react with oxygen or other 
initiators.  
Based on the result of experiment, the Control-Ultraturrax and Seaweed-Ultraturrax 
==PAGE:43==
43 
 
have EE of 91.69% ± 0.92% and 90.42% ± 0.11% respectively with no significant 
difference (p > 0.05) in between, which means most oil was encapsulated and 
protected in the polymer shell for these two groups. While for Control-Microfluidizer, 
86.86% ± 1.45% EE was obtained and it is significantly (p < 0.05) lower than the 
other two groups (Figure 22).  
Similar encapsulation efficiency was found in WPC capsules at around 90% 
(López-Rubio & Lagaron, 2012) and PVA (Poly vinyl alcohol)-fish oil fiber at 92.4% 
± 2.3% (García-Moreno et al., 2016). While for spray-drying, the encapsulation 
efficiency is relatively lower and varies by different wall materials from WPC 
capsules at 62.3% to waxy maize starch capsules at 95.7% (Carneiro et al., 2013). 
This result was supported by the experiment by Pérez-Masiá et al. (2014), it showed 
that spray drying had lowest encapsulation efficiency than emulsion electrospraying 
and coaxial electrospraying in polymer-lycopene capsules.

4.4.2. Peroxide value (PV) 
The theoretical oil content of the capsules was 20% based on the recipe of emulsion. 
According to the oil extraction results, the oil content in Control-Ultraturrax was 
11.31 ± 0.29 %, which was significantly lower (p < 0.05) than other two groups. For 
Control-Microfluidizer and Seaweed-Ultraturrax, the oil content was 16.24 ± 0.59 %

The differences with the theoretical content might be explained for the fact that 
extraction with Bligh and Dyer method might not extract all the fish oil from dextran 
capsules. In any case, the Control-Ultraturrax capsules presented a considerable lower 
oil content, which might be due to some error during the incorporation of fish oil in 
pilot plant.

While for Control-Microfluidizer (7.40 ± 0.65 meq/kg oil) and Control-Ultraturrax

electrospraying at inert atmosphere, improving encapsulation efficiency or testing 
different recipe of emulsion.  
Although the capsule with seaweed antioxidant has lowest PV among all capsules in 
the beginning of storage experiment, the difference is not significant during storage 
when the control capsules were produced in same method. The PV result show that 
the seaweed antioxidant did not have clear antioxidant effect to protect fish oil from 
primary oxidation during 21-day storage experiment. Meanwhile, from Day 8 to Day 
21, the PV of Seaweed-Ultraturrax was higher than control group (not significantly, p > 
0.05), which indicated that the seaweed antioxidant might have pro-oxidation effect. 
This might because F. vesiculosus ethanol extract contained iron of 14.5 ± 0.1 µg/g 
dry extract, which acted an initiator of lipid oxidation (Honold et al., 2016). 
Chlorophyll in the seaweed extract could act as photosensitizers to initiate 
photooxidation during the production and electrospraying processes. Also, when 
polyphenols in antioxidant reacted with peroxyl radical, phenoxyl radicals were 
formed. But compared to other antioxidant radicals phenoxyl radicals are unstable and 
can take hydrogen atom from lipid to initiate oxidation, so the primary oxidation 
might not be reduced or could even be promoted by the antioxidant.

1-penten-3-ol, hexanal, 2-hexenal and (E,E)-2,4-heptadienal (Appendix 2). While all 
of three capsules groups had much higher concentration of 11 types of volatiles 
compared to fish oil. Similar result was obtained in PVA fibers containing fish oil by 
García-Moreno et al., 2016, volatiles such as 1-penten-3-one, pentanal and 
1-penten-3-ol were much higher in the electrospun fibers than unprotected fish oil. 
The reason might be the fibers were already oxidized after production with a PV of 
54.6 ± 9.3 meq O2/kg oil (García-Moreno et al., 2016).  
The low antioxidant effect of F. vesiculosus extract in dextran capsules might because 
the ultraturrax was not efficient enough to incorporate extract into the emulsion. And 
most of the phenolic compound were hydrophilic. So, the antioxidant might not 
appear in the interface between oil and water to avoid oxidation but in water phase 
instead.  
While Serfert et al. (2009) obtained a clear antioxidant effect in spray-dried capsules, 
combination of lipophilic antioxidant, metal chelator and synergistic compound were 
added into emulsions containing fish oil, syrup and starch and the emulsion was 
spray-dried. This indicated that lipophilic antioxidant might be more efficient in low 
moisture system like capsules produced from oil-in-water emulsion.

5. Conclusion 
Different combination of fish oil-biopolymers emulsions was tested to produce 
electrosprayed capsules. Emulsion containing 0.5% Citrem, 0.5% WPC, 1% pullulan, 
15% dextran and 20% fish oil (% of solid content) was chosen as optimum recipe and 
used to produce dextran capsules by electrospraying equipment in pilot plant in Spain 
for two batches. 
The homogenization approaches affected the physicochemical properties of the 
capsules. Fine capsules with no obvious fiber were produced in pilot plant at 10 kV, 
1.5 mL/min for Control-Microfluidizer group with average diameter of 3.022 ± 1.912 
µm. Meanwhile the Control-Ultraturrax and Seaweed-Ultraturrax groups were 
produced at 15 kV, 1.8 mL/min, 24ºC, 40 %RH in pilot plant and the mean diameter 
were 1.719 ± 1.183 µm and 1.358 ± 0.748 µm respectively, both were significantly 
lower than Control-Microfluidizer group. 
The high encapsulation efficiency (91.69% ± 0.92%, Control-Ultraturrax; 90.42% ± 
0.11%, Seaweed-Ultraturrax) of capsules homogenized by ultraturrax indicated that 
most fish oil was encapsulated into the capsules, although there was still a 
considerable amount of non-encapsulated oil which was not physically protected by 
the wall materials against lipid oxidation. Significantly more fish oil was unprotected 
for Control-Microfluidizer capsules (86.86% ± 1.45%). 
Also, capsules homogenized by ultraturrax had peroxide value at 24.87 ± 0.37 meq/kg 
oil (Control-Ultraturrax) and 27.37 ± 1.18 meq/kg oil (Seaweed-Ultraturrax) at Day 
21 when stored in dark in room temperature, which were significantly lower than 
capsules homogenized by microfluidizer (Control-Microfluidizer) at 43.81 ± 7.31 
meq/kg oil. Optimization of production process improved oxidation stability of 
capsules according to the formation of heptanal, nonanal, 2-pentyl-furan, 
(E)-2-pentenal and 2-hexenal during 21-day storage experiment in 11 volatiles 
measured. 
Therefore, ultraturrax homogenization approaches significantly improved the 
oxidative stability of the dextran capsules compared with microfluidizer in most of 
physicochemical properties. 
==PAGE:53==
53 
 
Seaweed antioxidant Fucus vesiculosus ethanol extract was added into the solution 
before incorporation of fish oil. The result indicated that no clear inhibition effect was 
found for the antioxidant since it did not affect most of the physicochemical 
properties of the capsules significantly such as capsule size, encapsulation efficiency 
and peroxide value. While for volatile formation, seaweed antioxidant could only 
significantly inhibit the formation of heptanal and nonanal compared to control group. 
For remaining 9 volatiles, seaweed antioxidant showed the ability to protect fish oil in 
early storage days (Day 0 to Day 8) based on the result of 1-penten-3-one, 
1-penten-3-ol, hexanal, 2-hexenal and (E,E)-2,4-heptadienal. These volatile data 
indicated that F. vesiculosus ethanol extract could partly inhibit the formation of 
volatiles due to its iron chelating function. 
However, the oxidative stability of all the capsules were significantly lower than fish 
oil. Fish oil had a significantly lower peroxide value at 0.56 ± 0.04 meq/kg oil to 3.73 
± 0.63 meq/kg oil at Day 21 than all the capsules. The same situation happened in the 
concentration of volatiles. The problem might be the oxidation of fish oil was already 
being initiated during the production. Therefore, in further studies, the oxidation 
during production needs to be avoid. 
 
 
==PAGE:54==
54 
 
6. Further application and prospects 
Further study on reducing the oxidation of fish oil during production of emulsion and 
capsules is needed to investigate the oxidation of encapsulated fish oil comparing to 
original one from similar level of oxidation at the beginning of storage experiment. 
Meanwhile, concentration of polymers and electrospraying process condition might 
be further optimized to reduce the size of capsules. Then seaweed antioxidant with 
different concentration can be tested to verify the antioxidant effect. 
Since current results were measured only on capsule itself, further research should 
incorporate the capsules into food matrices to investigate the protection effect of 
capsules in real situation.
