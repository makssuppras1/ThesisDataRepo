Summary 
HVAC systems in buildings, especially in health care facilities, consume substantial amount of 
energy to maintain a standard required indoor air quality and thermal comfort. During the 
COVID-19 epidemic, the World Health Organization (WHO) recommended to reduce the risk 
of cross-infection by high indoor ventilation rate at minimum 6 ACH or 60 L/s·person in 
hospital rooms (WHO Roadmap to improve and ensure good indoor ventilation in the context 
of COVID-19, 2021). ASHRAE Standard  recommends for infection isolation rooms, the 
ventilation rate to be at minimum 12 ACH in total and minimum 2 ACH of outdoor air 
(ASHRAE Standard 170, 2021).  
However, current total volume ventilation is energy inefficient because it ventilates the whole 
room including non-occupied zones (Melikov, 2020). Localized ventilation, i.e., advanced 
ventilation based on source control was used in the present study to reduce AHU system 
ventilation rate while improving the indoor air quality by removing human-emitted pollutants 
locally. The study focused on assessing the energy saving potential of two types of advanced 
ventilations - ventilated mattress with local heating (VM) and local bed ventilation (LBV), 
individually and in combination. Three hospital rooms were simulated in IDA ICE. The hospital 
rooms included a ward (none of two patients was infected) to evaluate the energy saving of 
the VM, a patient room (one of two patients was infected) and an infection isolation room 
(both patients were infected) to evaluate the energy savings of the LBV only and the 
combination of the LBV and the VM.  
It was found that the energy saving provided by the VM was 22.5%. The LBV at 80% exhaled 
air removal efficiency (EARE) provided the highest energy saving of 83.6% at reduced 
background ventilation rate in the infection isolation room compared with the reference case 
with only background ventilation system at 12 ACH. When the two advanced ventilation 
systems were used together, the energy saving slightly decreased.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Indoor air quality 
Indoor air quality has a direct impact on the well-being, health, comfort, learning outcomes 
and productivity of occupants, especially for medical staff and patients in hospitals who spend 
long period of time staying indoors. A major issue related to occupants’ health is the 
concentration of pollutants. According to ASHRAE, indoor contaminants are consisted by 
particulate matter, organic and inorganic gases (ASHRAE Position Document on Indoor Air 
Quality, 2020). In indoor space, there are several sources of pollutant contained, including 
building material, furnishing, equipment and occupants. The emission rates of these sources 
except occupants can be reduced by developing low-emission materials.  
Human being is considered as the largest source of indoor pollutants. Human related 
pollutants, namely bio-effluents, are emitted by breathing (exhaled bio-effluents) and 
through the skin (dermally emitted bio-effluents) (Bekö1 et.al., 2020). Diet, health condition 
and activities have influence on the generation rate of bio-effluents. Dermally emitted bio-
effluents caused sensory nuisance (Tsushim, Wargock and Tanabe, 2018), mainly generates 
volatile organic compounds (VOCs) and odors from mouth, armpits, groin, head and feet 
(Dormont et al., 2013). Exhaled air by an infected person may contain also infectious particles 
(Douwes et al., 2003), which spread in air by ventilation airflow and human activities. Thus, 
there is a risk of cross-infection between occupants in such scenario. 
Indoor air pollutants may cause health problems like eye irritation, allergies, coughing, 
sneezing or headaches. In hospital, occupants are also under the risk of airborne cross-
infection. 
1.2 Airborne infection 
Airborne transmission is a disease transmission occurring by inhaling droplets of different size 
containing infectious virus. The virus laden droplets are generated during speaking, coughing, 
sneezing or other respiratory activities (Rezaei and Netz, 2021).  
1.2.1 Transmission route 
Airborne transmission depends on the size of fluid droplets, which are expelled by respiratory 
containing pathogens.  
The transmission of large droplets falling down to nearby room surface and objects is defined 
as short-range transmission. The initial diameter of the droplets range from less than 0.1 μm 
up to 100 μm. Under typical indoor environments a short-range transmission (up to 1.5 – 2 
m) due to relatively large droplets and small aerosols occurs. At longer distance transmission 
may occur due to aerosols, known as long-range transmission. Another transmission mode 
==PAGE:15==
2 
 
is contact with surfaces and fomites with settled viruses (REHVA, 2020). 
According to REHVA COVID-19 guidance document (August 3, 2020), diameter of droplet 
lower than 50μm reduces to approximately half during a short desiccation process. The 
desiccated droplet nuclei can be suspended in air for a long time and travel long distance 
beyond 1.5 m by airflow, this transmission is defined as long-range transmission. Droplets 
with diameter lower than 5-10μm, will be transported further than 2 meters by the room air 
distribution and thermal plume (Tang et.al., 2021). The risk of infected by short-range 
transmitted particles is higher than that by long-range transmitted particles (Melikov, 2020) 
Surface contact transmission only occurs when the exposed person touches their face after 
touching droplets landing surface. It can be avoided by hand hygiene and surface cleaning. 
1.2.2 Sars-Cov-2 
Coronavirus disease is one of the most widely recognized airborne disease. It is caused by 
Sars-Cov-2 virus, also known as coronavirus. Size of the coronavirus is around 80-160 
nanometer. The virus has ability to remain active for several hours after expelled by infected 
person. Coronavirus transmission routes are same as many respiratory viruses with an 
additional fecal-oral route. Flushing toilet with lid closing and making sure sanitary devices 
sealed with water appropriately could be used as precautions.  
Coronavirus viability is affected by relative humidity and temperature of air. However, it is 
reduced effectively only in a high RH (above 80%) and temperature (above 30℃) environment, 
i.e. beyond the acceptable thermal comfort range for people. Without specified cleaning, the 
virus keep active up to 3 hours in indoor air and 2-3 days on room surface in common 
building environment (REHVA, August 3, 2020). 
1.2.3 Recommendation of airborne transmission control by building service 
system 
Generally, the risk of short-range exposure can be reduced by use of face masks or keeping 
distance from an infected person. For reducing long-range transmission risk, ventilation is 
also considered as a necessary measure to control indoor pathogen concentration. 
Sars-COV-2 transmission occurs indoors frequently, especially in crowded, confined rooms 
without or with poor ventilation. Even the indoor spaces are well ventilated, supply airflow 
enhances spread of small droplets. According to the US Center for Disease Control the 
hierarchy of the control methods of COVID-19 transmission risk is as shown in Figure 1. 
Common recommended ventilation solutions mitigate the risk of airborne transmission by 
increasing indoor air change rate. Thus, the concentration of virus laden particles in the air is 
reduced. Local exhaust of the infected exhaled air can be efficient elimination control methods.

Figure 1: Traditional infection control pyramid adapted from US Centers for Diseases Control 
The European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control (ECDC) guideline recommends 
increasing ventilation rate and extend operation hours of HVAC systems to ensure healthy 
indoor environment (ECDC, 2020). In order to remove droplets safety, recirculation and the 
use of energy-saving settings in ventilation system should be avoided.  
1.3 Ventilation of hospital patient rooms  
High air quality environment is required in medical facilities for the health of patients and 
medical staff. More pollution sources exist in hospitals than in common residential buildings. 
The concentration of total volatile organic compounds (TVOCs) increases due to medical 
equipment operation, medical treatments and cleaning (Jung et.al. 2015). In addition, particles 
might be generated from patients. 
Ventilation system provides outdoor air to indoor spaces to control the air quality by dilution 
and removal of pollutants. The ventilation principles are categorized according to the driven 
forces used to generate air movement:  natural ventilation, mechanical ventilation and 
hybrid ventilation. 
Airflow through the building which is driven by wind-induced effect or thermal buoyancy 
caused by vertical pressure difference is called natural ventilation. Indoor air quality of natural 
ventilated room is hard to control because it depends on outdoor air quality. That is the 
reason why hospitals seldom apply natural ventilation as the main ventilation strategy. 
Mechanical ventilation, also called forced ventilation, supplies and conditions outdoor air with 
mechanical system. It includes total volume ventilation and localized ventilation. The following 
Chapter 1.3.1 introduces total volume ventilation, and Chapter 1.5 presents two localized 
ventilation systems as advanced ventilation strategy. 
Hybrid ventilation is the combination of natural and mechanical ventilation. 
1.3.1 Air distribution methods 
Mechanical ventilation system always consists of fans and heating, cooling or air conditioning 
units, which supplies conditioned outdoor air to create comfortable indoor environment. In 
==PAGE:17==
4 
 
practice, two main air distribution methods, mixing air distribution and displacement air 
distribution are used. 
Mixing air distribution, known as mixing ventilation, is an air distribution method which 
provides outdoor air to dilute indoor polluted air. The air is supplied to the rooms via diffusers, 
nozzles or other units most often installed on the ceiling. The clean and polluted air are mixed 
in the entire room. Thus, it aims to keep the indoor temperature uniform within the occupied 
zone and in an acceptable range. 
Displacement ventilation (displacement air distribution) supplies clean and cool air at a low 
level close to the floor. The supplied air rises to the upper zone of the room helped by 
buoyancy flows generated by heated objects. The polluted air is displaced upward during this 
process and exhausted through the exhaust diffusers placed on ceiling or walls. 
1.3.2 Energy consumption 
In the building sector, hospitals are the buildings with the highest energy consumption per 
unit area (Balaras et. al.,2007). The large heating, cooling and ventilation load requires use of 
large amount of energy. Generally, the ventilation and the whole HVAC system as well as 
several medical equipment operate continuously for 24 h. 
In hospital patient rooms, mixing ventilation is mostly used. However, the ventilation system 
is energy inefficient. In the room with mixing ventilation strategy, the clean air is mixed with 
the room air. Thus, the inhaled air is already polluted. The system requires higher air change 
rate to maintain the indoor air quality. The entire volume of the room is ventilated including 
non-occupied zone.  
Instead of total volume ventilation, source control can be applied to improve the indoor air 
quality. With this method, contaminants are removed/reduced by filtration and air cleaning 
close to the source, e.g., the occupant. Since the pollutants are removed locally, the supplied 
by the system ventilation rate can be reduced and energy can be saved. Examples of this 
approach are introduced in Section 1.5. 
1.4 Hospital patient room related ventilation requirements 
Hospitals and health care facilities are considered as complex facilities because of the high 
indoor environment requirements. Depending on the type of room, patients, climate and 
country differences, standards and guidelines give recommendations for design of the indoor 
environment in order to ensure occupant's satisfaction. 
1.4.1 Indoor climate standard (EN-16798-1) 
EN-16798-1 (2019) recommends the design environmental input parameters of different 
type of buildings. 
Design of ventilation aims to remove pollutions generated from occupants’ body, building 
==PAGE:18==
5 
 
materials and system. Interiors are designed by very low pollution emitting materials. The 
building is defined as very low-polluting building which requires 0.5 L/(s·m2) to remove 
emission of pollution from building materials (DS/EN16798-1). The standard categorizes the 
indoor environment in four levels based on the indoor environment expectation. Category I 
buildings apply for special requirement occupants who are very sensitive and fragile and 
expect high quality in-door environments, like patients (DS/EN15251, 2007). The Category I 
requires 10 L/s airflow for each occupant for dilution of generated bio-effluents dilution. The 
upper limit for the CO2 concentration above outdoor air is 550 ppm in living rooms, 380 ppm 
in bedrooms.  
The design temperature of non-residential building maintains 21-23 ℃ in heating season 
and 23.5-25.5 ℃ in cooling season. Relative humidity is within the range of 30% and 50%. 
1.4.2 Hospital focused standards 
Focusing on health care facilities, ASHRAE standard provides design requirements for 
ventilation systems (ASHRAE Standard 170, Ventilation of Health Care Facilities,2021). 
According to the standard, ward indoor environment keeps between 21-24℃ and RH 
maintains under 60%. The outdoor air change less than 2h
-1 is not allowed, the minimum total 
air change is 6 ACH in patient room and 12 ACH in infection Isolation room.  
1.4.1.1 Health care memorandum 
The memorandum is served as a reference of system design and installation. It mainly assures 
equipment and system components operation in the hospital and avoid microbial growth in 
duct system (Heating and ventilation systems Health Technical Memorandum 03-01, 2007).  
The memorandum recommends indoor temperature range from 18 to 28 ℃, total indoor air 
change 6 and 10 times per hour in patient rooms and infection Isolation rooms respectively. 
For thermal comfort reasons, such a large indoor temperature difference is unacceptable to 
the occupants, especially sensitive patients. 
1.4.1.2 WHO roadmap  
Due to the pandemic of COVID-19, WHO published the ventilation criteria regarding to 
airborne virus elimination (Roadmap to improve and ensure good indoor ventilation in the 
context of COVID-19, 2021). WHO Roadmap only ensures and improves indoor air quality; it 
makes no provision for other indoor environmental control parameters. The minimum 
ventilation airflow is 60 L/s for each room occupant or 6 ACH for the whole ward. 
1.5 Advanced energy efficient ventilation of hospital patient rooms 
based on source control 
In order to maintain a high-level air quality and protect occupants against airborne infection 
risk, large ventilation rate is recommended in hospital compared with common public building, 
==PAGE:19==
6 
 
during Sars-Cov-2 pandemic. As stated in Chapter 1.2, use of recirculation for energy saving 
should be avoid for decreasing the transmission of virus. It means that large amount of energy 
is needed for HVAC system operation.  
Advanced ventilation methods are designed to create local bed micro-environment for the 
user to ensure personal thermal comfort and inhaled air quality. Two advanced ventilations 
are introduced in the following chapters. They can be applied in health care facilities, 
especially hospital patient rooms. They have potential to improve the air quality and reduce 
energy consumption. 
1.5.1 Ventilated mattress 
An advanced method of source control studied by Bivolarova et al. (2016) is a ventilated 
mattress (VM) placed on the top of the bed mattress (Figure 2). The VM is made of three-
dimensional spring fiber. The mattress is in a coverlet. Fan is installed on the side of the 
mattress where person’s head is located. The fan draws the air from the bed micro-
environment into the mattress from the feet side where two openings are made. Thus, the air 
in the bed micro-environment polluted by body effluents is sucked through the mattress. The 
flow of air cools the body of the person lying on the mattress. The spring fiber is covered by 
activated carbon fiber fabric (ACF) treated with acid so that the air drawn through the mattress 
is cleaned. The exhaust air is cleaned locally by ACF fabric and discharged back to the room 
for recirculation. 1.5 - 5 L/s air could be moved by the fan integrated in the ventilated mattress. 
98% of the pollutants generated from the user’s body can be captured and removed by the 
VM. The use of ventilated mattress improves the indoor air quality. 
 
Figure 2: Bed integrated ventilation method (VM) (Adapted from Bivolarova et al., 2014b) 
In addition, the ventilated mattress has the potential of improving thermal comfort. Local 
cooling is provided by the airflow through the mattress, which increases conductive heat loss 
from the body parts in contact with the mattress, especially the back side and back of the user 
(Bivolarova et al., 2014b). The local cooling is beneficial for the person in bed when the room 
temperature is relatively high, i.e., summer season. Thus, energy may be saved by maintaining 
the room air temperature above the recommended in the standard because of the provided 
by the mattress pleasant local cooling. During the heating season local heating is integrated 
on the top of the VM providing local heating of the feet.

1.5.2 Local bed ventilation 
Another advanced air distribution method is local bed ventilation, presented by Melikov et al. 
(2011). It is a named Hospital Bed Integrated Ventilation Cleansing Unit (HBIVCU). HBIVCU is 
an advanced air distribution system that evacuates the exhaled air close to the breathing zone 
of the patient in the bed and thus reduces the spread of contaminated air in the room. The 
operation principle of the local ventilation unit is presented in Figure 3.  
The unit is installed on the hospital bed support frame. The HBIVCU is mobile and able to 
follow the bed adjustments. Two air terminal devices (1 and 2) are mounted alongside close 
to the patient’s head. They are connected to air conditioning and distribution box (main unit), 
installed at the back of the bed on the head-side (not shown in the figure). Room air is sucked 
by one of the units (unit 1), disinfect in the unit (HEPA filter and UVGI) and then is supplied 
horizontally and gently over the person in the bed (3). The supplied flow guides gently the air 
exhaled by the person (4) in the bed (can be infected) toward the box (2) on the opposite side 
of the bed where it is disinfected and released back to the room or to the room ventilation 
exhaust. Clean air is also supplied upwards in a vertical/inclined direction (5) and in downward 
direction (6) at the two sides of the lying person. The purpose of the two upward vertical air 
curtains (5) is to prevent the patient from being exposed to contaminated air coming from a 
sick person (doctor, nurse or other occupants) inside the room and also to constrain the 
exhaled air between the two curtains guiding it towards the ceiling where it is exhausted. The 
two downward vertical curtains (6) provide fresh air close to the patient’s breathing zone and 
also local cooling to the head region. Control of the temperature and the flow rate of the 
horizontal jet of clean air and control over the flow rate of the vertical air curtains are provided 
to the person in the bed. Figure 4 shows another dimension of the local bed ventilation.  
The local bed ventilation includes three functions: 1) pollution source control by removing the 
air exhaled by the person in the bed (air can be infected); 2) providing clean air to the 
breathing zone of the person; 3) local cooling of person’s head at warm room environment. 
The removal of the exhaled air reduces the pollution generated in the room and thus the 
needed ventilation rate. At present the HBIVCU is efficient to remove exhaled particles.

1.6 Justification of the study 
Air quality and thermal comfort in health care facilities are major concerns of indoor 
environmental design (Izydorczyk, 2018). Uncomfortable and polluted environment causes an 
adverse effect on the recovery of patients.  
The advanced ventilation can be more efficient than the currently used total volume 
ventilation in creating a comfortable indoor environment. By removing pollutants locally at 
==PAGE:22==
9 
 
the source (person in bed), indoor air quality is improved, and energy consumption decreased.  
This strategy is opposite to the presently used/recommended strategy of improving indoor 
air quality and reducing airborne transmission by increasing ventilation rate to dilute the 
pollutants; strategy that leads to increase of energy consumption and investment costs for 
huge HVAC units.  
The ventilated mattress has high cooling capability on the body segments In contact with its 
surface (Bivolarova, 2014). Therefore, local heating integrated in the ventilated mattress is 
important for thermal comfort of the patient. This however will lead to increase of the energy 
consumption. The energy saving potential of the ventilated mattress combined with local 
heating has not been studied. However, the performance of the ventilated mattress with 
regard of energy consumption without the local heating has been studied (Manuel, 2016). 
The results reveal substantial energy saving.  
The ventilated mattress removes only the bio-effluents emitted from the human body. 
Pollutants like infectious particles generated from air exhaled from patients should be 
controlled by additional units. Therefore, local bed ventilation is needed to improve indoor 
air quality and protect other patients from cross-infection. However, the energy saving when 
the local bed ventilation is used has not been studied in detail.  
This study investigates the reduction of energy consumption in hospital rooms by using only 
ventilated mattresses, only local bed ventilation, and a combination of two advanced 
ventilation methods with total volume ventilation.

3.2.2 Strategy 2: Focus of Cross-infection design  
As discussed in the Introduction chapter (section 1.5.2, Fig. 3, 4) local ventilation enables to 
accomplish the most effective infection control method. Fan is used to suck the room air into 
the box located on one side of patient’s head. Filters integrated in the box effectively remove 
the particles exhaled by the patient in the bed. UVGI light may be placed in the unit to disinfect 
air from viruses (not applied during the present simulations). The filtered air is supplied gently 
over the patient’s head to guide the air exhaled by the patient toward the second box 
attached to the opposite side of the bed. In this box the air is filtered by the particles exhaled 
by the patient and is recirculated in the room. Because the “virus laden particles” generated 
by the patient are removed locally the room ventilation rate can be removed. The background

room ventilation rate is reduced as much as to obtain the same risk of airborne transmission 
as recommended by the guidelines in case of rooms with total volume mixing ventilation. 
Therefore, energy saving can be achieved by reducing the room background airflow rate 
designed for airborne transmission control.

Wells-Riley equation (Riley et al., 1978). It is presented in detail in Appendix 1. However, as

the potential adverse effect and to evaluate indoor air quality. Thus, IF was used to evaluate 
the risk of airborne cross-infection. It was defined as “the proportion of air mass exhaled 
from the infected person that is then inhaled by the exposed person.” (Bennett et al., 2002).

3.3 Simulation 
3.3.1 Building location & weather data 
In order to analyze the energy need in wards equipped with advanced ventilation, the model 
was built referencing the layout mentioned in Chapter 3.1, located in Denmark.  
The design outdoor temperature was taken as -12℃ in winter and 26℃ in summer when 
the relative humidity of outdoor air is 60%. Location file named Kastrup_061800 (ASHRAE 
Fundamentals 2013 data) for Copenhagen was used as the weather input in the simulation. 
==PAGE:40==
27 
 
Environmental parameters like temperature, relative humidity, wind profile and solar radiation 
were set in the climate data. The default ambient CO2 concentration was set at 400 ppm. 
Periods when the outdoor temperature was below 10°C are considered as heating season 
and periods above 15 ° C are considered as cooling season. The heating season in 
Copenhagen runs from October to April, while the cooling season is between June and 
August.  
3.3.2 The Model 
The model was built based on the ward layout in Chapter 3.1. It had one external façade 
oriented to south with a window. External blind was used as the integrated window shadow 
device to avoid glazing. Considering the daylight need, the area of the window was chosen 
as 2.72 m
2 (1.2 m×2,2 7m, W×L). The window was located 1 m above the floor. The door of 
the room was placed on the wall opposite to the external wall. If the sunlight exceeded 100 
W the blind would automatically draw. Figure 11 shows the geometry of the room model. 
 
 
Figure 11: Simulation model built in IDA ICE

Thermal transfer performance difference between envelopes causes thermal bridge, a source 
of heat loss. It was assumed that typical thermal bridge was created in standard hospital 
building, the default corresponding coefficient values in IDA ICE are shown in Figure 12.

3.3.4 Infiltration 
Infiltration was defined as the uncontrolled air supply through the leakage of building 
structure. Depending on the difference between indoor and outdoor environment, infiltration 
leaded to unexpected heat and moisture gain. Typical infiltration was determined based on 
the wind driven outdoor airflow, which was chosen as 0.5 ACH at 50Pa in IDA ICE. The internal 
algorithm automatically corrected these values taking wind profile of default urban into 
consideration. The calculated infiltration was 0.38887 L/(s·m2) of heated floor area. 
==PAGE:42==
29 
 
 
Figure 13: The infiltration inputs in IDA ICE model 
3.3.5 Plant 
The default provided plant system was composed by chiller, boiler and controllers.  
The chiller converted the electrical power to chill water to 5℃, and pumped the cold water to 
AHU cooling coil. It had unlimited cooling capacity to meet the needs under any conditions, 
the coefficient of performance (COP) was set to 3.8, which is a typical value used for cooling. 
An ideal pressure-controlled pump was used to operate the cold-water circulation.  
The boiler used purchased energy to warm water to 35℃ for the heat exchanger and 
domestic hot water for the building. It also had unlimited heating capacity while the efficiency 
was set to 90%. Both boiler and chiller were always operated. 
3.3.6 Internal heat gain and schedule 
The cooling load of a system was defined as the sum of heat gains, which includes: 
- 
Solar radiation through windows 
- 
Solar heat transmission through constructions 
- 
Heat accumulation in constructions 
- 
Internal heat gains 
The values of first three items were calculated automatically by IDA ICE at the basis of building 
envelop. Internal heat gains were considered as total heat generation from occupants, lights, 
and equipment, etc. Cooling load comprised by occupants varied with their number, activity 
level, occupancy schedule and clothes insulation. It was determined as 73.6W for each person 
mentioned in Chapter 3.1. 
According to EN-15251 (2007), required illuminance maintains above 100lx in general wards. 
Nine light-emitting diode lamps were installed in the wards. The power of each lamp was 5

lm, equivalent to 102.6 lx.

4. Result 
Building operation and design, and the requirement of indoor environment, including heating, 
cooling, ventilation and lighting, affect the energy consumption of building (DS/EN 16798-1, 
2019). In this study, the energy savings potential of the advanced ventilation was evaluated 
by simulating the total annual energy need. Three air quality controlling strategies were 
investigated. The achieved percentage of energy savings compared with the reference cases 
without advanced ventilation in each scenario was determined.  
The results of the energy simulation are presented by figures. The volume flow rate and 
advanced ventilation usage are indicated in the figures.  
In all cases, indoor relative humidity was controlled by the AHU system. The simulation results 
for the relative humidity remained around 50%, which was within the range recommended in 
standards. Therefore, these values are not presented in the figures with the results. 
4.1 Potential energy saving with the ventilated mattress 
Figure 17 presents the energy needs of the two cases simulating the scenario focusing on 
improving indoor air quality. The following cases are compared: only background ventilation 
(RF-39.74) and combination of the ventilated mattresses and local heating (providing thermal 
comfort to the patients) together with reduced background ventilation (VM-39.74-29.94). In 
scenario 1, the ventilated mattress was used to remove bio-effluents emitted by the body of 
the two patients, while the pollution due to the exhaled air and the building generated 
pollution remained and were removed by the background ventilation in the room. As it was 
already defined body emitted pollution was considered 50% of the total pollution generated 
by each patient and 50% pollution was due to exhaled air, i.e., of the total 10 L/s, 5 L/s was 
needed for removal of body bio-effluents and 5 L/s for removal of exhaled air. As defined in 
the previous chapter, previous research revealed that 98% of the body emitted bio-effluents 
were removed by the mattress. Thus, the ventilation flow rate was reduced from 39.74 L/s in 
the reference case without VM to 29.94 L/s in the case when the mattress was used 
(mentioned in calculations in the Method section, Eq.2 and Eq. 4).Electricity, which maintains 
the operation of appliances such as air handling units, plants, lights and medical facilities, is a 
major part of the energy need in both cases, with and without use of the VM. The ventilated 
mattress provided local heating when indoor operating temperature was below 23 ℃. The 
energy need for the local heating had been calculated separately and labelled by its value in 
Figure 17. According to the whole-year simulation, air temperature was kept in a range 
between 22.23 and 23 ℃ for 70.8 hours in the case when the VM was used and the 
background ventilation rate was reduced from 39.74 L/s to 29.94 L/s. The case is abbreviated 
in the table as VM-39.74-29.94. The local heating consumed about 1 kWh electricity, less 
than 1% of total electrical energy consumption. 
Energy need for the AHU heating reduced (about 51.8%) in the case VM-39.74-29.94. Since 
ventilation rate decreased to 75.3% (29.94L/s) of the ventilation rate (39.76 L/s) in the reference 
==PAGE:49==
36 
 
case without use of the VM, indoor RH requirement could be fulfilled without dehumidifying 
supply air in summer. The energy need for the whole AHU system operation was also reduced 
by 14.8%. 
In case VM-39.74-29.94, the internal heat gain in the room increased due to the ventilated 
mattress. However, the cooling energy need was reduced to 56.3% of that in the reference 
case, because the VM provided a thermal comfort micro-environment for the patients. Local 
cooling was provided by increasing the exhaust airflow rate of the ventilated mattresses about 
6890 hours when the indoor temperature was higher than 24℃. Since fan power of 20 W was 
sufficient to provide airflow rate in the range of 1.5 - 5 L/s, no additional energy was needed 
when increasing its exhaust airflow rate. The overall energy consumption of the patient room 
was reduced with 22.5% by using a bed equipped with the ventilated mattress and local 
heating compared to the case with only CAV background ventilation.

5. Discussion 
Standards and guidelines (reviewed in Chapter 1.4) recommend ventilation at high airflow 
rate for acceptable indoor air quality and reducing the risk of airborne cross-infection. At the 
same time, it is an important challenge to create a healthy and high air quality indoor 
environment for occupants while saving energy. This can be achieved by ventilation based on 
pollution source control and advanced air distribution (Melikov 2016). 
This study determined the energy saving potential of two methods of advanced ventilation 
combined with reduced background mixing ventilation in wards in Denmark. The methods 
were ventilated mattress able to remove and clean up to 98% of human body generated bio-
effluents and local bed ventilation capable to remove and clean up to 80% of the air exhaled 
by the patient in the bed. Numerous simulation cases were designed to study the energy 
saving under three ventilation strategies, including: CAV system with/without the ventilated 
mattress, CAV system with/without local bed ventilation and CAV system with/without VM 
coupled with LBV. Several assumptions were made to simplify the simulation. 
In this study, it is assumed that the patients were reclining in beds whole day during a year, 
and the influence of medical staff on indoor environment was ignored. The energy use of the 
VM and the LBV, as well as the medical equipment was kept constant. Since the intake fraction 
of exposed person was calculated with constant inflow, outflow and air pressure, the window 
and the door of the wards were assumed to be always closed. 100 W was assumed as the 
maximum heat load due to solar radiation through the window; the shading was activated 
when this value was exceeded. However, in reality, patients’ activities are unpredictable, the 
equipment and ventilation system and units operate under different patterns determined by 
different factors (occupation time, time during the day, illness of the patients, etc.), large 
differences exist between people with regard to the generated amount of virus laden particle 
in the exhaled air, etc. The frequency of window and door opening and shading drawing are 
depended on occupants. 
5.1 Ventilated mattress 
The use of the ventilated mattress removed most of the body generated bio-effluents. 
Therefore, the same indoor air quality level as in the reference case with mixing ventilation 
only was achieved at reduced background ventilation rate. The results of the simulations 
revealed that the use of the ventilated mattress led to energy saving of 22.5% compared with 
the reference case without mattress because of reduction of the ventilation rate. Compared 
with 39.76 L/s outdoor airflow in the reference case, less of water content was introduced in 
the room at the reduced ventilation rate（29.94 L/s）when the VM was used and the relative 
humidity in the room did not increase above the recommended level without 
dehumidification. Thus, the additional heating energy for air dehumidification was saved, the 
AHU did not need to cool down for condensation and reheat the transported outdoor air to 
==PAGE:58==
45 
 
acceptable supply temperature. 
The reduction of the supplied ventilation air led to increase of the room temperature in 
summer. However, thermal comfort of the person in bed could be maintained due to the local 
body cooling. By increasing the exhaust flow rate through the mattress, the body parts in 
contact with mattress’s surface become cooler. Thus, when the room temperature increases 
above the recommended upper limit, it can still provide thermal comfort to the patient in the 
bed. Thus, the energy need of AHU cooling in summer was reduced because thermal comfort 
could be achieved at high level of operative temperature and it was not necessary to cool the 
room to 24℃.  
In winter, conditioned supply warm air heated the room air and maintained the indoor 
temperature in an acceptable range. Compared with the reference case, less heating would 
be provided by the AHU system because of less supplied air, thus the room temperature 
decreased in the case with VM at the reduced background ventilation rate (29.94 L/s). 
Additionally, ventilated mattress provided unwanted cooling to patient’s body. Therefore, 
local heating had to be used for thermal comfort environment. Under this condition, the 
energy need of local heating was 1 kWh during the whole year because the period of room 
temperature below 23℃ was only 70.8 h. Internal heat gain including heat generated by 
occupants, equipment and lights have positive influence on maintaining comfortable indoor 
temperature in winter, maximum at 420.2 W.  
Figure 25 shows the energy need per unit of the case with and without ventilated mattress in 
previous research (Manuel, 2016). The energy saving of VM was 31%, higher than 22.5% in the 
present study. The justification of this energy saving difference would be the reduction of 
ventilation rate. In Manuel’s research, the ventilation rate in reference case without VM was 4 
ACH. It was also assumed that the half of the ventilation rate supplied in spaces for diluting 
the dermally bio-effluent, which could be 100% removed by ventilated mattress. Therefore, 
the ventilation rate of the case with VM was 3 ACH, half of the recommended ventilation rate 
of a patient room (6 ACH). Local heating (providing thermal comfort to the patient) was not 
integrated in the mattress in this previous research, which would increase the energy need of 
ventilated mattress.  
In present study, the patient room was designed as common published building, the reference 
ventilation rate was 1.30 ACH (39.74 L/s), much lower than the reference case at 4 ACH in 
Manuel’s research. The local heating had little impact on the energy saving, because it only 
consumed 1 kWh during the whole year, less than 0.1% of the total energy need. Therefore, 
ventilated mattress is more energy efficient in large background ventilation room.

5.2 Local bed ventilation 
Local bed ventilation was used to control the concentration of airborne droplets in the room. 
Depending on the local conditions, such as posture of the person in bed, design of the unit, 
etc. the exhaled air removal efficiency of the local bed ventilation may be in the range 40 – 
80%. 
Three reference cases with only CAV system were used in this project: the patient room at 
3.91 ACH (corresponding to 60 L/s·person), the patient room at 6 ACH (corresponding to 
184.24 L/s) and the infection isolation room at 12 ACH (corresponding to 368.48 L/s), the 
ventilation rate were recommend by standards and guidelines. The energy need of cases are 
shown in Figure 26. Due to the large amount of supply airflow, the reference case of the 
infection isolation room had the highest energy need, about 2 times higher than the reference 
case of patient room at 6 ACH (184.24 L/s), and three times higher than the reference case of 
patient room at 3.91 ACH (60 L/s·person).  
==PAGE:60==

Figure 26: Annual energy need of hospital rooms with CAV system only (reference cases) and local 
bed ventilation (LBV) in conjunction with reduced background ventilation. 
It is seen that the local bed ventilation with 80% exhaled air removal efficiency showed the 
best energy saving performance as 83.6% in infection isolation room, which both occupancy 
patients were infected. This great energy saving was due to a large ventilation rate reduction 
from the recommended 12 ACH (368.48 L/s) to 2 ACH (61.39 L/s).  
The acceptable airborne concentration in the patient room is higher than the concentration 
in the infection isolation room. This is because the recommended ventilation rate for patient 
room for removing particles was lower than the recommend ventilation rate for infection 
isolation room. According to the ASHRAE standard 170 (2021), the minimum ventilation rate 
should be 2 ACH in hospital room. Therefore, when the ventilation rate of the patient room 
was 2 ACH (61.39 L/s), less than 80% exhaled air removed was sufficient to reduce the airborne 
particle concentration to the acceptable concentration level. When the ventilation rate of the 
reference case was 6 ACH, both LBV with 80% and 60% exhaled air removal efficiency achieved 
maximum energy saving about 66.3%. LBV with exhaled air removal efficiency ranging from 
40% to 80% obtained 49.8% energy saving when the ventilation rate of the reference case was 
3.91 ACH (120 L/s).

The justification of this energy saving efficiency difference would be the reduction rate of 
background ventilation airflow. In infection isolation room, the acceptable airborne 
concentration was lower than patient room, which means higher requirement of removal 
efficiency on air exhaled by infected patient. The LBV at 40% exhaled air removal efficiency  
was not sufficient to reach same IF level as reference case (at 12 ACH) with large background

However, the maximum LBV removal efficiency of 80% might not be always achieved in reality. 
The posture of the patient and other factors, which affect the removal efficiency, is 
unpredictable. It is difficult to maintain the removal efficiency in a constant level. The LBV 
might remove 60% air exhaled by patient on average. In other words, the maximum energy 
saving potential of LBV may be lower than 83.6% in reality. 
The energy need of the local bed ventilation depends on the amount of the room air moved 
to the HBIVCU to guide the exhaled air. If the LBV would be designed efficiently, large amount 
of exhaled air might be removed by low airflow. According to the affinity law, the fan power 
is considered to be proportional to cube of the airflow (Basic Pump Parameters and the 
Affinity Laws, 2012). Therefore, the power of fan integrated in an improved LBV could be 
assumed as 20 W instead of current fan power at 28.5 W. The energy need of improved LBV 
would be reduce from 300 W to 210.5 W, as proportional to fan power. Even though the 
internal heat gain reduction would lead to an increase of cooling demand and a reduction of 
heating demand, the improved LBV might save more energy need of electricity. In infection 
isolation room, the improved LBV at 60% exhaled air removal efficiency will save 10 kWh than 
the current LBV at same removal efficiency annually. The simulation results are listed in Table

5.3 Combination of VM and LBV 
Ventilated mattress was applied to improve the indoor air quality. It has no benefit on cross-
infection control, which was the main concern of design of environment in rooms occupied 
by infected person. By using local bed ventilation equipped with ventilation mattress, airborne 
particles and dermal bio-effluent were partly removed from the air locally, indoor 
environment was maintained in a high air quality level and exposed person were protected 
from the cross-infection.  
Ventilation mattress ensured a thermally comfortable micro-environment around the patient 
bed by providing local heating and cooling. Thus, the range of acceptable indoor temperature 
was extended from 21-24 ℃ to 18-28 ℃. Larger acceptable temperature range means less 
heating or cooling of the required supply background air needed to maintain the environment 
temperature, thereby the energy need of AHU heating, cooling, and operation were reduced. 
However, addition energy of electricity was consumed for operating the ventilated mattress 
and the local heating. With same reference case and exhaled air removal efficiency, the energy 
saving of the combination of LBV and VM were slightly less than the LBV. Figures 28, 29 and 
30 present the energy need of cases with LBV only and cases with the combination of LBV 
and VM.

5.5 Limitation of the study  
IDA ICE is a software for entire indoor environment simulation, focusing on units which have 
ability to control the whole room environment. Total volume ventilation is the only mechanical 
ventilation method in the software. In the simulation model, VM and LBV were set as an 
equipment but not local ventilation units, and the simulation took into account only heat 
generation and electricity use into consideration. The cooling effect of the VM and the LBV 
due to air movement were not calculated in the simulation.   
In addition, local heating is unable to be incorporated as a heating unit in IDA ICE. There are 
several heating/cooling units built in the software already, however, local heating/cooling is 
not one of those default heating/ cooling methods. In this study, the energy need of local 
heating is calculated by the hours when indoor temperature was below 23 ℃ in the 
simulated model. A comfortable thermal micro-environment in bed can be achieved by the 
cooling effect of the VM and the local heating. The local heating will heat the air, then increase 
the temperature in the micro-environment while the indoor temperature still keeps in a same 
value. Therefore, the hours of indoor temperature below 23 ℃ in the simulation results 
might longer than the operating hours of local heating in reality. 
==PAGE:68==
55 
 
Overall, the simulations of this study had several limitations, and the results only demonstrate 
the energy saving potential of advanced ventilation units, not their real energy saving 
capability.  
5.6 Improvement 
The simulated model was designed for the conditions in Copenhagen, Denmark. The design 
temperature in winter is -12℃ and 26℃ in summer with air relative humidity at 60%, which 
means low cooling indoor demand and high heating demand. However, the energy need of 
the hospital room might be changed when the advanced ventilation units are used in other 
cities, for example buildings in hot climate which requires higher cooling. Due to the limitation 
of temperature difference between supply air and indoor environment, air handling unit might 
be insufficient to remove heat load of the room in this situation, addition cooling method like 
radiant cooling panel has to be used to maintain the space temperature. Thus, the energy 
saving of advanced ventilation varies because of the energy need of cooling increase. This 
study can be improved by investigating the energy saving potential of advanced ventilation 
under different climates. 
In this project, it was assumed that occupants were always present in room, lying in bed 
without any activities for simplification, as stated in page 44. For more realistic results, the 
variation of occupants’ schedule, movement and thermal insulation including clothes and 
covering should take into account in further works. Additionally, the power of objects 
integrated in advanced ventilation, like HBIVCU fans and filters, should be considered. These 
parameters might have either positive or negative influence on the energy need of the room.

6. Conclusions and Recommendations 
In the context of ensuring indoor air quality and avoiding airborne cross-infection, the energy 
performance of advanced ventilation was studied in hospital wards. Energy simulations were 
performed to determine the energy saving potential of local bed ventilation and ventilated 
mattress by reducing the background ventilation rate. The conclusions of the study are the 
following: 
 
22.5% of energy need of the patient room was reduced by using a hospital bed equipped 
with a ventilated mattress with local heating, even though local heating used addition 
energy for generating acceptable thermal micro-environment around the patient in the 
bed; 
 
The risk of cross-infection in patient room with CAV system at 3.91 ACH (120 L/s) is 
higher than that with CAV system at 6 ACH (184.24 L/s) recommended by WHO guideline 
(2021), the exposed person intake fraction of airborne particles is 0.022% and 0.016% 
respectively. 
 
The energy need of patient room with CAV system at 3.91 ACH (120 L/s) is lower than 
that with CAV system at 6 ACH (184.24 L/s) due to lower ventilation rate. 
 
When the reference case was the patient room at 3.91 ACH (120 L/s), the energy saving 
of LBV with exhaled air removal efficiency ranging from 40% to 80% was 49.8%. 
 
When the reference case was the patient room at 6 ACH (184.24 L/s), the maximum 
energy saving was 67.2%, obtained by LBV at exhaled air removal efficiency 60% or 80%. 
 
Due to the high ventilation rate at 12 ACH, the visitors are under low cross infection risk 
in infection isolation room which both patients are infected, the calculated intake fraction 
is 0.009%. 
 
LBV at 80% exhaled air removal efficiency preformed greatest energy saving at 83.6% in 
infection isolation room, because the use of this LBV led to the greatest reduction of 
background supply ventilation rate to 2 ACH when same intake fraction as reference case 
at 12 ACH was reached. 
 
Under same background ventilation rate, hospital room with the combination of LBV and 
VM with local heating need slightly more energy than the hospital room with only LBV. 
Because of the aim of energy saving, it is better to use LBV only in hospital rooms. 
 
The use of advanced ventilation systems  not only can provide energy saving, but also 
have potential on saving initial cost and space of ventilation system installation. The size 
of ducts and air handling units might be decreased because of the reduction of supply 
ventilation rate. 
Recommendations: 
 
The simulation should be improved to take the cooling effect of advanced ventilation 
==PAGE:70==
57 
 
into account. The local heating should be modelled in the simulation model. 
 
The energy saving potential of the advanced ventilations might be impacted by outdoor 
climate. This needs to be studied; 
 
The impact of variation of occupants’ schedule, movement and thermal insulation 
including clothes and covering on the energy need should be considered in further works;

The Wells-Riley model is an applicable model for simple and rapid assessment of infection 
risk for many diseases. Infectivity of pathogen and infection source are taking into 
consideration implicitly.

The model also has its limitation and uncertainty. It assumes that the risk of infection is the 
same for the room occupants. However, the exposure level has spatial variation, pathogen 
concentration decreases with the distance from the source occupant generating pathogens. 
The effect of room airflow turbulence and size of virus laden airborne particle on their 
dispersion and deposition are also ignored in the model. 
Because of these limitations of the classical Wells-Riley transmission model, the concept of 
intake fraction is used in this study to determine the Sars-COV-2 infection risk. 
The intake fraction concept. 
The exposed occupants are under the risk of inhaling virus laden airborne particles exhaled 
by an infected person and mixed with the room air. As a simple measure of the relationship 
between pollutant emitting and individual intake, intake fraction (IF) is applied to determine 
potential adverse effect and to evaluate indoor air quality.  
Though several pathways including ingestion, dermal uptake and inhalation should be 
considered during intake fraction calculation, the main intake route of airborne virus is
