Problem formulation 
Identification of antioxidants in polymers by HPLC 
All polymer materials contain a large number of additives such as stabilizers, antioxidants and pigments. The 
composition of the material has important effects on the performance of the material and is often unknown or 
not reported by a supplier. As an example, a change in antioxidant composition or type may decrease the 
lifetime of a polymer material significantly. In addition to this, handling of products such as sterilization is 
known to degrade/change the antioxidant and the shelf-life of a product. 
The purpose of the project is to develop a HPLC procedure for determination of antioxidant levels and types 
in polymer materials used in Coloplast A/S. During the project all phases of the analytical challenge should 
be handled. Initially, a method that for extraction of additives from the products should be established. The 
extracts should be analyzed by HPLC and compared to known standard antioxidants to determine the content 
and type of antioxidants used. For this purpose a selection of standards should be prepared and their 
respective characteristics determined for use in later analysis (small library for analysis). After establishing 
an effective method for the pristine raw materials, the method should also be evaluated for use on sterilized 
samples in a second phase of the project. 
In parallel to the investigation at Coloplast, identification of the various antioxidants or additives should be 
investigated at DTU-FOOD using HPLC MS/MS as well as GCMS (GC-QTOF-MS). This will involve use 
of the prepared standards for establishment of a library of the known antioxidants for the additional 
analytical techniques.  
To the extent possible, with respect to time, unknown components in the analyzed samples should be 
identified using a combination of the available analytical equipment at all three locations.

Abstract 
 
An HPLC/UV method was optimized for the identification and quantification of the antioxidants BHA, 
BHT, Tinuvin 328, Irganox 1010, Irganox 1330, Irganox 1076 and Irgafos 168 in the extracts of Coloplast 
polyurethane catheters. The separation was achieved in a C18 column, using acetonitrile and water as the 
mobile phase in a gradient elution mode. The throughput and overall resolution of the method was improved. 
The optimized method was validated as for accuracy and precision. Good linearity (R2 ≥0.999) was observed 
for all antioxidants in question. Moreover, the calibration curves showed a linear relationship between 
concentration and peak area over a range of [1 – 100 µg/ml] for the antioxidants Irganox 1010, BHT, BHA 
and Irganox 1330. The range of linearity was smaller [2,5-100 µg/ml] for the antioxidants Tinuvin 328, 
Irganox 1076 and Irgafos 168. 
The optimized HPLC method showed high accuracy (Relative standard deviation < 2%) for all antioxidants 
in question. However, the optimized HPLC method cannot provide any satisfactory quantitative and 
qualitative information on antioxidants radiation degradation products. 
Coloplast method of antioxidants extraction from polyurethane catheters was validated for accuracy. The 
analytical recoveries were less than 60 % for the antioxidants BHA, BHT, Tinuvin 328, Irganox 1010, 
Irganox 1330, Irganox 1076 and Irgafos 168.

1. Introduction 
 
Coloplast is a Danish company that specializes in advanced healthcare products and services that deal with 
healthcare issues comprising: wound care, skin care, ostomy continence care, as well as surgical urology. 
Among the above mentioned products, urinary catheters manufactured by Coloplast are of particular interest 
for this study. These medical devices are made from polyurethanes and are used by patients suffering from 
urine retention. Thus, they play a vital role for patients' health.    
Due to its characteristics, polyurethane plays an important role in medical devices manufacturing. However, 
as a polymer, it contains antioxidants whose role is to inhibit chemical changes within the polymer. 
Although, each component used in the formation of a polymer plays a vital role in the chemical makeup of 
the final product. Therefore, it is critical to control the quantities of additive and chemicals used since they 
would affect different strengths and variables of the final product.  
The purpose of this study is thus, to optimize an HPLC method developed for determination of antioxidants 
in polyurethane catheters. For this purpose, chemical analytical procedures will be developed and tested. The 
final optimized method will be used to quantify antioxidants in a number of Coloplast sterilized as well as 
unsterilized catheters. Thereby, the effect of catheter sterilization on its antioxidants content would be 
estimated. In addition to the HPLC/UV technique, gas chromatography and mass detection are to be used for 
accurate determination of antioxidants and their radiation degradation products, at trace levels in Coloplast 
urinary catheters. 
2. Technical background 
2.1. 
The Catheter: Polyurethane 
Coloplast catheters are manufactured primarily from thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU). Polyurethanes are 
very versatile polymers that have been widely used for construction of a wide range of biomedical devices, 
such as vascular prostheses, heart valves and artificial organs (Erkesh.et. al., 1990).  Moreover, thermoplastic 
polyurethane is a block copolymers of the (AB) n type consisting of alternating sections of hard segments and 
soft segments, see figure 1. Hard segments are composed of  aromatic or aliphatic diisocyanate, and a diol or 
chain extender, whereas soft segments are composed of polytetramethylene ether glycols polyols 
(POLYMEG )(Cauich-rodríguez, et. al., 2012).The (POLYMEG) polyols are linear diols with a backbone of 
repeating tetra methylene units connected by ether linkages, and capped with primary hydroxyl units.  
Polyurethane can be prepared by reacting POLYMEG polyols with the diisocyanate to form a prepolymer 
that is reacted with the extender (Diol) to form the final polyurethane (POLYMEG® POLYOLs 
==PAGE:10==
9 | P a g e  
 
Application). In addition, the hard segments have urethane functional groups that hydrogen bond to each 
other in the form of extended chains, with N-H “donor” groups hydrogen bonded to C=O “acceptor” groups. 
 
Figure1: Formation of polyurethane from polyol, Diisocyanate and a diol extender(Edited). 
The properties of the formed polyurethane depend on its composition; Hard and stiff or soft and rubbery 
materials can be produced from polyurethane by varying its raw material composition. The primary variables 
available to the formulator are the molecular weight of POLYMEG polyols, the type of diisocyanate and the 
type of extender. Hence, as the weight fraction of hard segment (diisocyanate plus extender) is increased, the 
hardness of the elastomer increases. In addition, more subtle change in the elastomer can be made by varying 
the isocyanate/hydroxyl ratio and changing the polymerization process used to produce the elastomer. 
Methylene diphenyl diisocyanate (MDI) is a common biostable type of diisocyanate used for preparation of 
polyurethane(Cauich-rodríguez et al., 2012), whereas  ethylene glycol (EG) and diethylenglycol (Cauich-
rodríguez et al., 2012) are common diols used as chain extenders for preparation of biostable and 
biodegradable polyurethanes.  
2.2. 
Polyurethane oxidative degradation  
Polyurethanes, just like other polymers, are susceptible to oxidation(Hawkins, Murray, 1964). Oxidation is 
simply degradation that occurs due to the reaction with oxygen. The oxidative degradation of a polymer will 
occur at any given stage of its lifetime including processing, storage, or application (Enrique Saldivar-
Guerra, 2013).  Basically, oxidation reactions maybe heat initiated or light initiated. Oxidative degradation 
cannot be initiated by tensile stresses, as they are not sufficient to break bonds and thereby increase radical 
concentration(Wright, 2001). Other causes of oxidation can be metal ions sources in polymer products; e.g. 
==PAGE:11==
10 | P a g e  
 
pigments and catalyst residues from the polymerization stage and contamination during processing sources.  
Oxidative degradation of a polymer has severe effects on the polymer quality and performance. For instance, 
oxidative degradation of a polymer can reduce its mechanical properties and thereby causing cracking and 
altered visual appearance (Constantine D. Papaspyrides, 2014). To protect the polymer from oxidation and 
other degradative factors (e.g. storage aging and fatigue), additives like antioxidants are added to the 
polymer, to the raw polymeric material and under processing or fabrication of the final product. Antioxidants 
are organic compounds that extend and maintain the life time of polymers by inhibiting or retarding the 
polymer oxidative degradation(Kutz, 2011).  
Antioxidants are categorized into two classes: Primary and Secondary (Wright, 2001); Primary antioxidants 
(e.g. Amines & Phenolic) are hydrogen donors; they are phenolic compounds and react with peroxy radicals 
to form hydroperoxides, thereby preventing the abstraction of hydrogen from the polymer backbone. Figure 
2 shows the structures of some common primary antioxidants. 
    
    
    
      
 
Figure 2: Primary antioxidants BHA, BHT, Irganox 1076, Irganox 1330 and Irganox 1010, respectively. 
BHA is an example of primary small molecule antioxidant that act as free radical scavengers, it is mostly 
used in the field of preservation of human food.  BHT is an effective thermooxidative stabilizer, however, it 
is sensitive toward light and heat and can turn yellow, in addition to its high volatility problem. Higher 
molecular weight hindered phenols are usually used in polyurethanes (POLYMEG® POLYOLs 
Application).Examples of more hindered phenols are Irganox 1330, Irganox 1010 and Irganox 1076. These 
are high molecular weight sterically hindered primary antioxidants.  Another type of polymer additives is the 
UV absorber Tinuvin 328, see figure 3. Tinuvin 328  absorbs ultraviolet light from the chromophore of the 
polymer and convert it to into harmless thermal energy and thereby protecting the polymer during its useful 
lifetime (Drobny, 2006). Irgafos 168 is an example of secondary antioxidants, shown in figure 3.

respectively.

Primary antioxidants, after donating a hydrogen, are themselves turned to phenoxy radicals; they can react 
with another peroxy radical forming a stable product. However, as a radical, phenoxy radicals formed can 
also initiate a new oxidation cycle by abstracting a hydrogen from the polymer chain. Therefore, the majority 
of the phenolic antioxidants contain two tertiary butyl groups on the 2 and 6 positions; their role is to form a 
steric barrier and hindrance as to shield the formed phenoxy radical (sterical hindrance) and prevent initiation 
of a new oxidation cycle. However, the steric hindrance works both ways; that is, it also causes a decrease in 
radical scavenging rate. The effectivity of the antioxidant is determined by the ratio between the increased 
radical scavenging rate and the initiation rate (Kutz, 2011).  
Secondary antioxidants (e.g. Phosphites & Thioesters), are responsible for stability during processing, where 
they e.g. break down the unsatbile peroxides formed during the formation of polymers. Secondary 
antioxidants for instance, phospites, reduce hydroperoxides to the corresponding alcohol and are transformed 
into phospates, see figure 3.  
Problems associated with the use of antioxidants in medical devices include AOs leaching from the polymer 
to its surface where it can interact with the surrounding tissue. For instance, Landmark midline catheter is an 
example of coated polyurethane catheters where the antioxidant- containing coating material was suspected 
to cause hypersensitivity like reactions for patients when catheterized with this type of catheters(Silverstein, 
Witkin, Frankos, & Terr, 1997). Landmark midline catheters are constructed of an inner layer of 
polyurethane and a coating of the biomaterial Aquavene, which is a blend of polyurethane and polyethylene 
oxide to which butylated hydroxyanisole (BHA), butylated hydroxytoulene (BHT), and triallyl-s-
triazinetrione (TTT) are added. Some patients experience some hyper-sensitivity like reactions when 
catheterized with this type of catheters, the condition that prompt manufacturer to reexamine the 
biocompatibility of the coating material. Among different tests, the coating was tested for potential leachable 
antioxidants that can be the reason of these hypersensitivity reactions.  
Another problem associated with the use of antioxidants is their degradation; prior to consumer usage, 
polyurethane catheters are sterilized by radiation. Moreover, radiation energy causes alterations to the 
additives content in the polymer; some additives react with the applied energy and are no more active and 
thereby degraded into different compounds. Therefore, additives are added to the polymer in concentrations 
that assure the proper function of the polymer even after their partially loss by sterilization. In addition to 
degradation by sterilization, additives can also be degraded under the polymer processing, since they also 
protect the polymer against processing harsh conditions such as high temperature. One common route of 
antioxidants Irganox 1010, Irganox 1330, Irgaonx 1076 and Irgafos 168  degradation is by splitting off a tert-
butyl group and/or splitting off a di-tert-hydroxy-toluene group(Bell, 2014), shown in figure 4.

As the case with antioxidants leaching, degradation products can also leach to the surface causing chronic

degradation products detected in the polymer should be also qualified and quantified.  
3. Literature search 
 
Numerous publications and research papers outline analysis of additives in polymers by prior extraction step 
with subsequent separation of the extract using high performance liquid chromatography combined with UV/ 
Vis absorbance detection. Most antioxidants contain functionalized aromatic groups and thereby offer 
distinctive ultraviolet/visible spectral opportunities(Woodman, 2003) making UV/VIS detection common for 
their analysis, however, many other detection methods were also tried in some particular cases: ELSD and 
FTIR (Dopico-García et al., 2012). However, due to the low concentration of additives in the polymer matrix 
(0.1- l.0 %)(Wheeler, 1968) more sensitive detectors as mass spectrometers have been mostly employed, 
making MS detection after liquid chromatographic separation the state-of-the art in modern instrumental 
analysis(Stiftinger, 2011). LC-MS Interfaces such as ESI and API have been widely employed, however, 
trends toward direct ambient additives analysis have led to development of new ionization techniques, such 
as atmospheric solid analysis probe, that are based on the direct desorption of ions from the polymer 
surface(Trimpin, Wijerathne, & McEwen, 2009).These ionization techniques make it possible to obtain mass 
spectra of intact polymer molecules. 
Common methods and techniques used for analysis of antioxidants in polymers are briefly discussed in this 
chapter, however, literature is overwhelmed with the various methods of additives analysis in polymers and 
is much more than this thesis can encounter.  
3.1. 
Sample Preparation Prior to Chromatographic Analysis 
 
Different methods are available for the extraction of antioxidants and other additives from 
polymers(Albertssond, A. C., 20028). These methods can be divided into ‘conventional’ and ‘new’(H. 
Vandenburg, Clifford, & Bartle, 1997). Conventional methods compromise liquid solid extractions and 
polymer dissolution/precipitation techniques; these methods are often laborious, very time consuming and 
can use large amounts of solvents, whereas the new extraction methods are more automated and result in 
reduced extraction time and solvent usage. Supercritical fluid extraction (SFE), microwave assisted 
==PAGE:14==
13 | P a g e  
 
extraction (MAE) and pressurized fluid extraction (PFE) are examples of the new extraction methods.(H. J. 
Vandenburg, Clifford, Bartle, Newton, & Ts, 1999). The new techniques have proven useful for the analysis 
of polymer additives, however, many laboratories do not have the instrumentation available or funding to 
obtain specialized equipment.  
Concerning extraction time, the quickest methodologies have been achieved using either MAE (3-40 
minutes) or ASE (10-40 minutes), and 30 minutes is the average extraction time reported. In most cases, 
extraction temperatures ranged between 25 °C and 150ºC. Several extraction techniques, such as Soxhlet, 
solution/precipitation, MAE, heating and ASE have been compared in an inter-laboratory study, remarking 
MAE and solution/precipitation techniques as providing the best results(Dopico-García et al., 2012). 
3.1.1. Soxhlet extraction 
The liquid solid extraction method has been based on the use of hydrocarbon solvents to extract the additives 
from a polymeric sample without dissolving it. This procedure can be carried out through a Soxhlet 
extraction; Soxhlet extraction is commonly used for extraction of low molecular weight polymer additives 
such as antioxidants (Albertssond, A. C., 20028). The extraction here is carried out at high temperature in a 
Soxhlet apparatus with a special cellulose container, the thimble, which allows the reflux of the solvent 
through the polymer sample, see figure 5. The solvent used should swell the polymer but not dissolve it 
while still be able to dissolve and thereby extract the additives from the polymer matrix.  
                            
Figure 5: Soxhlet apparatus (Edited) 
A simple procedure of Soxhlet antioxidants extraction from polypropylene involves cutting the polymer 
sample (5 g) in small pieces and placing it in a 125 x 35 mm cellulose thimble for extraction in a Soxhlet 
apparatus with 250 ml of methylene chloride (6 h). The extracting solvent was evaporated to dryness and the 
residue dissolved in 2 ml THF. The THF solution was then filtered through a PTFE filter of 0,2 µm pore size 
in order to remove undissolved material. The filtered solution was ready to be analyzed by HPLC(El 
Mansouri, Yagoubi, & Ferrier, 1998). 
==PAGE:15==
14 | P a g e  
 
According to the author(John Scheirs, 2000), for quantification, 6-8h chloroform Soxhlet extraction time is 
adequate for most polymer additives, in terms of reproducibility and exhaustive extraction. For simple 
identification purposes, however, a 3h extraction is sufficient. 
It is noteworthy to mention that the diffusion coefficient of additives in polymers at 40 °C is around 10–10 
cm2 s, which means that for an extraction of approximately 20 min a sample thickness of 0.3mm is suitable. 
Extraction from a thin film or ground particles is therefore necessary for more efficient extraction. It is 
recommended that the grinding is done with the frozen polymer to diminish loss of volatile antioxidant.  
Extract obtained by Soxhlet exytraction are liable to be contaminated with low molecular weight polymer 
which in the majority of cases does not interfere in the subsequent analysis(Jenkins, 1972). Besides, during 
Soxhlet extraction, thermal decomposition of certain volatile additive(s) may result from heating organic 
solvent to temperatures as high as 110 °C. 
3.1.2. Dissolution /precipitation method 
One remark on the conventional methods (for example, Soxhlet extraction) is that sometimes the additive 
recovery is low. This can be due to several reasons, for example, the antioxidant to be extracted is of too high 
molecular weight or the polymer matrix could have relatively high degree of crystallinity which prohibits the 
extraction solvent from effectively permeating the polymer and solvating the antioxidant. To ensure a better 
recovery of the desired antioxidants, a dissolution/precipitation method can be applied. This method 
compromises a complete dissolution of the polymer sample and there by liberating the whole additives 
content of the  host polymer(Pritchard, 1998).  A common approach of this method is the dissolution of the 
polymer in a strong solvent that totally dissolves the polymer(Stiftinger, 2011). Removal of the dissolved 
high molecular weight polymeric material can be achieved by precipitating it  by for ex. ethanol(H. 
Vandenburg et al., 1997).The extracts obtained then contain the additives and the co-extracted soluble 
polymer fraction(the oligomeric wax) (Smith & Taylor, 2002), that can be removed by filtration. Removal of 
the solvent can be achieved by evaporating it, for ex. under reduced pressure(Egsgaard, H.; Larsen, E.; 
Pedersen, Walther Batsberg; Carlsen, 1992). Evaporation leaves the extract dried, the desired 
chromatographic solvent is then added to re-concentrate the analyte. Depending on the polymer type, 
different solvents and non-solvents can be used. 
In one variation of the Dissolution/precipitation technique, additives may be extracted from a polyolefin 
polymer sample (~0,5 grams) by dissolving them in toluene (~50 ml) by refluxing. This dissolution is 
followed by re-precipitation of the polymer by adding ~25-50mL methanol. After filtration, an aliquot of the 
filtrate is evaporated to dryness and reconstituted in 0,5 ml of appropriate solvent for chromatographic 
analysis(Stiftinger, 2011).  
==PAGE:16==
15 | P a g e  
 
In another variation, antioxidants extraction was carried out on 5 g polymer material with 50 ml THF at room 
temperature for 16 hours. The solvent was decanted and evaporated under reduced pressure to approximately 
5 ml. The removal of the dissolved polymer was achieved by precipitating it by adding 50 ml methanol. The 
supernant was evaporated and the precipitation procedure was repeated(Egsgaard, H.; Larsen, E.; Pedersen, 
Walther Batsberg; Carlsen, 1992).The dried extract was then reconstituted in an appropriate solvent for 
chromatographic analysis. 
In some instances, when applying the dissolution precipitation method, the polymer does not need to be 
precipitated out of the solution. For example, polyurethane samples can be readily dissolved using hot 
dimethylformamide (DMF) and the solution can then by directly analyzed for antioxidant content by high 
pressure gel chromatography, employing an in-line guard column to filter out the polymer(Pritchard, 1998). 
It should be noted that some additives, such as phosphite stabilizers, can undergo conversion to the 
phosphate, due to the presence of oxygen during the extraction. Another factor to bear in mind is that certain 
additives exhibit high volatility and maybe lost from the system (for example BHT)(John Scheirs, 2000) 
3.1.3. Ultrasonic extraction 
Ultrasonic extraction uses ultrasonic vibrations to extract samples in polar solvents in an ultrasonic bath, see 
figure 6. The polymer sample and the appropriate polar solvent are placed in a tube that is partially immersed 
in an ultrasonic bath filled with water. The method principally works by agitating the solution of the polymer 
and solvent, and producing cavitation in the liquid. This would be expected to enhance the rate of transfer 
across the polymer/liquid boundary layer(H. Vandenburg et al., 1997) and thereby increasing the extraction 
efficiency. Extraction by ultrasonic vibration can recover additives at high rates in approximately 30 min, 
compared to 8h and 12h for the reflux and Soxhlet methods, respectively.  
  
 
Figure 6: Ultrasonic bath  
A simple procedure of Ultrasonic extraction of additives from polypropylene involves weighing 0.2 g freeze-
ground polypropylene and add it along with 10 ml chloroform into 21 ml glass vials. The vials were 
immersed above the level of solvent in water in a preheated ultrasonic bath. The temperature of the water 
was maintained at 40–44 °C by intermittent use of the internal bath heater. Samples were removed at 
intervals and analyzed for the antioxidant Irganox 1010(H. J. Vandenburg et al., 1999). 
==PAGE:17==
16 | P a g e  
 
Despite the abundance of ultrasonic baths in modern laboratories, this method has not been widely exploited 
to date(Scheirs, 2000). One limitation could be that it must be performed under conditions which protect the 
hearing of laboratory personnel. 
3.1.4. Microwave assisted extraction  
The method relies on using microwave energy to heat solvents in contact with a sample in order to partition 
analytes from the sample matrix into the solvent(Coleman & Crumpler, 2004). To perform the microwave 
assisted extraction solvents that absorb microwave energy are necessary. To extract the additive, the solvent 
and the sample are added into a sealed vessel and placed in the microwave oven, see figure 7. Many samples 
can be extracted at the same time. Sealing the vessels is important since the microwave energy can heat the 
solvents- depending on the analyst selection-to 2-3 times above their boiling point (Freitag & John, 
1990).This results in an increase in extraction efficiency, which allows the extraction solvent to be chosen 
based on its chemical properties, not its boiling point, as it is with Soxhlet extraction (Richter & Shah, 2000).  
 
Figure 7:  Microwave used for assisted extraction. 
Microwave assisted extraction has been widely used for the extraction of additives from polymers (Dopico 
Garcia et al., 2004). When combined with HPLC-UV, Irgafos 168 and Irganox 1076 were successfully 
extracted from low density polyethylene with dichloromethane as the extraction solvent(Jonas Alin, 2010). A 
simple procedure of this method is that samples (polypropylene) of 2.5 g of polymer were put into the 
microwave extraction vessel, mixed with 25 ml of ethyl acetate–n-hexane (75:25, v/v) and finally, heated for 
15 min in a microwave oven with 1000 W microwave power supplied. The extraction temperature was 125 
°C.  After the extraction, the vessels were allowed to cool to ambient temperature before opening. Before 
injection on the HPLC, each extract was filtered using a 0.45-mm porosity PTFE membrane syringe 
filter(Freitag & John, 1990).  
Another example of Microwave assisted extraction of additives from polypropylene involves weighing 0,3 g 
sieved freeze-ground polypropylene and transferring it into the extraction cells of the MAE. A small amount 
of glass wool was placed at the bottom of the cells before adding the polymer to help prevent the particles 
agglomerating. Solvent (30 ml) was added and heated in the microwave oven for the required time. After 
cooling,(IS was added and) the extract was analyzed by HPLC(H. J. Vandenburg et al., 1999).

3.1.5. Supercritical fluid extraction  
Gases such as CO2 and N2O become fluid when temperature and pressure reach a critical point called the 
supercritical phase. In supercritical fluid extraction (SFE), this fluid is used to extract target substances in 
solid samples. Figure 8shows the equipment used for supercritical extraction. The sample is placed in an 
extraction chamber through which the supercritical fluid is forced, the target substances are extracted from 
the samples and trapped in vials by small amounts of methanol or ODS resin. Instruments connecting the 
SFE apparatus and on line GC and LC have been developed in which the series of steps from extraction to 
analysis have been automated.  
 
Figure 8: Supercritical fluid extractor.  
The fluids used for SFE include CO2, N2O, SF6, methanol, and water. The fluid used is chosen to meet the 
analytical purpose. The most commonly used is CO2 as it can easily reach supercritical conditions and has 
clear advantages (e.g., low toxicity, inflammability and cost, and high purity) over other fluids. However, 
supercritical CO2 is very non-polar, so modifiers are added to improve extraction efficiency for polar 
compounds. .Modifiers for CO2 include methanol, dichloromethane, acetonitrile, water etc.(Zougagh, 
Valcárcel, & Rı́os, 2004). The role of a modifier in the extraction of polymer additive(s) is generally 
considered to either swell the polymer and/or to improve the solvent strength of the CO2. 
Extraction of polymer additives using the supercritical fluid extraction combined with supercritical fluid 
chromatography and mass spectrometry was shown to provide complete qualitative analysis in single run 
that took approximately one hour, the extraction also included additives that could not be analyzed by GC-
MS.  
Samples of biocompatible thermoplastic polyurethane can by analyzed first by cutting the polymer into small 
pieces (1*1*5mm). Next, one piece was placed in the 0,5 ml extraction cell and extracted using pure carbon 
dioxide for 10 minutes at 300 atm. and 60 °C. (MacKay, 1994).

3.2. Antioxidants Analysis 
The characterization of antioxidants has been successfully carried out by GC / FTIR and HPLC/UV .The 
chromatographic specificity of LC is often lower than that of GC, however, the high molecular mass and 
polarity of many AOs preclude the use of gas chromatography. On the other hand, high-performance liquid 
chromatography has been widely used to effectively separate polymer additives. The utility of these 
chromatographic techniques is greatly enhanced by mass spectrometric detection since it provides the 
sensitivity and detectability required to compound identification capability. A brief review on these 
techniques is shown here. 
3.2.1. Liquid Chromatography 
High-performance liquid chromatography coupled to UV has been widely used to effectively separate and 
detect polymer additives. Most antioxidants have chromophores and thereby can be determined by UV 
absorbance. However, the low sensitivity of UV detection disables their detection at very low concentrations. 
Mass spectrometry (MS) is the most versatile technique for the analysis of organic constituents at the trace 
level. In addition, different MS parameters are available as to estimate optimum conditions for the 
determination of each specific analyte.  Mass spectrometry combined with chromatography enables accurate 
determination of wide range of polymer additives, however, MS requires the sample to be in gas phase. As to 
overcome the barrier of volatility, different interfaces have been developed for combing HPLC with MS, for 
example Electrospray Ionization (ESI) and Atmospheric Pressure Chemical Ionization (APCI). The function 
of these interfaces is to transform the liquid sample to a gas sample suitable for mass spectrometric detection. 
Mass spectrometry with atmospheric pressure ionization has become state of the art for identification of 
components in polymeric materials after separation by liquid chromatography (Wolfgang), for instance, mass 
spectra of Irganox 1330, BHT Irganox 1010, Irganox 1076, Irgafos 168 and Tinuvin 328 were acquired by 
atmospheric pressure chemical ionization in positive mode, enabling determination of the mentioned 
antioxidants. Identification of antioxidants is based on comparison of the peaks' characteristics (Mass, 
retention time) with those of known standards.  
Reversed phase HPLC is preferred over normal phase HPLC procedures (Dopico-García et al., 2012) since 
most antioxidants are phenols that in nature are hydrophobic and soluble in alcohols. C8, phenyl and 
Octadecyl-silica (ODS) are common reverse phase stationary phase with ODS being the most employed for 
antioxidants reverse phase separation. (López, Martínez, Del Valle, Orte, & Miró, 2001), (Vargo & Olson, 
1985), (Reingruber, Himmelsbach, Sauer, & Buchberger, 2010).

organic modifier, eventually accompanied with water or methanol. Other mobile phase systems have also

3.2.2. Gas Chromatography 
 
Gas chromatography (GC) has a high resolution and sensibility, and is one of today’s major analysis 
technologies(Choong & Lin, 2001). However, GC is only applicable to the volatile and low molecular 
weight AOs, for example, volatile antioxidants as BHA and BHT can be determined by GC with high 
recovery, as shown in table 2. On the other hand, most polymer antioxidants are of low volatility, for 
instance Irganox 1076 and Irganox 1010, and therefore are not suitable for gas chromatographic (GC) 
analysis. In such a case, the GC determination is preceded by sample pretreatments aimed at chemically 
transforming the low volatile antioxidant into a volatile form, suitable for GC. Such transformations include, 
for example, Chemolysis, which is to free a small molecule from the macromolecule. The released molecule 
has a lower boiling point than the parent compound, and can thereby be analyze by GC(Lee, 2012). One 
common pattern of Chemolysis is by breaking down ester bonds. 
 Determination of the antioxidants Irganox 1010, Irganox 1076 and Irgafos 168  by GC Chemolysis enabled

6. Results and Discussion 
 
Different experiments were carried out as to fulfill the aims of this study. HPLC method optimization, being 
the main one, was achieved by varying the conditions and the factors that govern the separation process. The 
optimized method was then applied for determination of AOs and their degradation products in extracts from 
unsterilized catheters as well as their corresponding sterilized samples. Moreover, a validation study was 
carried out, as to demonstrate the viability of both the extraction method and the optimized HPLC method. 
All the experiment procedures were performed in total precautions and care including extraction, separation, 
and analysis. The experiment details including results, procedures, and technical errors have been discussed 
and reported. 
6.1. 
Method optimization 
HPLC is one of the most commonly used analytical techniques for antioxidants determination.  Depending 
on the overall separation requirements and nature of antioxidants in question, different methods can be 
developed and subsequently optimized. 
A reversed phase high liquid chromatographic (RP-HLC) method for antioxidants determination has been 
developed by Coloplast. The method enables separation and identification of the 5 common polymer 
antioxidants: BHT, Irganox 1010, Irganox 1330, Irganox 1076 and Irgafos 168.   
The method development process was carried out using an Aeris peptide C18 column (3,6 µm, 50 *4,6 mm) 
at temp. 63 °C. Moreover, the separation was performed in a gradient mode using water as mobile phase-B 
and acetonitrile as mobile phase-C (No mobile phase A was used). These conditions allowed a satisfactory 
AOs separation (Rs >1) within 13 minutes and at a flow rate of 0,5 ml/min. 
The aim of this analysis is to optimize the current method, as optimization process is the next phase of 
method development.  Basically, method optimization process is directed by the following goals: To obtain 
higher throughput, to achieve higher resolution and to eliminate any excess run time. Thus, efforts will be 
directed toward attaining these goals.  
A typical elution profile for the AOs obtained by this method is shown in figure 9. The separated sample 
contains the antioxidants: BHT, Tinuvin 328, Irganox 1010, Irganox 1330, Irganox 1076 and Irgafos 168.  
Initially, Tinuvin 328 was not used for the method development process but later during the analysis as in 
attempt to improve the method throughput. Nevertheless, Tinuvin 328 identification by rt.(5,2 min.) has been 
achieved in advance, which was the same case for the other AOs.

6.2. 
Identification of AOs radiation degradation products by HPLC/UV 
 
Coloplast polymeric catheters are sterilized by irradiation. The radiation dosage varies depends on the level 
of sterilization and on the polymer's ability to react against the ionizing process, for example, some catheters 
are irradiated at 2*60 KGy while others have a maximum limit of 2*40 KGy. The common irradiation 
dosages being used are 1*25 KGy, 2*25 KGy, 2*40 KGy and 2*60 KGy.  
It is well known that irradiation induces some undesirable chemical changes in the catheter polymeric 
material, including the additives being added to the polymer. For instance, antioxidants degradation in the 
polymer is one of the common processes that takes place under catheter irradiation. Moreover, determination 
of products of such reactions is critical for the assessment of the catheter biocompatibility. 
The aim of this analysis is thus to determine antioxidants radiation degradation products in Coloplast 
catheters. However, this is not straightforward and requires a preliminary information on AOs response to 
radiation. Such information can be obtained by inducing radiation degradation in the AOs. For this purpose, 
four portions from each of the reference standard antioxidants (BHT, Irganox 1010, Irganox 1330, Irganox 
1076 and Irgafos 168) were subjected each to one of the following four radiation dosages (1*25 KGy, 2*25 
KGy, 2*40 KGy and 2*60 KGy). HPLC samples were then prepared from each irradiated portion and were 
analyzed by the optimized HPLC/UV method. 
Basically, the HPLC/ UV analysis is supposed to provide a qualitative information on AOs degradation 
products, however, this is quite challenging because such products are often of low concentration, and the 
applied UV detection method is of relatively low sensitivity. As to increase the concentration of possible 
degradation products, HPLC/UV analysis was carried out on high concentration samples (100 µg/ml).

6.3. 
Identification of antioxidants degradation products by GC/MS 
 
Since HPLC/UV analysis of irradiated AOs couldn’t provide any acceptable results, GC/MS was applied as 
to obtain more reasonable information on AOs radiation degradation. For this purpose, samples of irradiated 
Irganox 1010 were prepared and analyzed by GC/MS. However, the mass spectra obtained showed neither 
molecular ion peaks nor fragments peaks corresponding to Irganox 1010.  
It is well known that Irganox 1010 is a large sterically hindered molecule and it is too involatile to be 
measured by GC, but, the more volatile degradation products are more liable to detection by such analysis. 
Probably, the reason that no degradation products were determined could be due to their very low conc. or to 
the low sensitivity of the applied quadrupole mass spectrometer.  
In addition to irridated Irganox 1010, four irradiated catheters were GC-MS analyzed; the chromatograms 
obtained show the same peak profile; Most peaks in these chromatograms were corresponding to the polymer 
raw materials, for example, amides and alcohols. These compounds were able to escape the precipitation and 
filtration steps during sample preparation. An example of a catheter GC-MS spectrum and identified peaks1

6.4. 
Quantitative analysis  
6.4.1. Antioxidants Extraction 
The quantitative and qualitative analysis of antioxidants in Coloplast catheters requires their prior separation 
out of the polymer.  Different techniques have been developed for this purpose, for example: Soxhlet 
extraction, ultrasonic extraction, and the dissolution precipitation method, which is the method used for 
antioxidants extraction from Coloplast catheters.  
28 catheters were handed in at Coloplast, and were extracted as follows: 500 mg catheter was added to 10 ml 
THF that is supposed to fully dissolve it. Dissolution ensures that the catheter liberates whole its antioxidants 
content. Moreover, the percent of catheter (mass) to solvent volume was 50:1. The dissolution process had 
occurred at room temperature, and mechanical shaking was applied for four hours in order to enhance the 
dissolution efficiency and rate.  
After the catheter had been totally dissolved, the solution was filtered using a 0,45 µm syringe filter (see 
figure 20). Basically, the purpose of a 0,45 µm syringe filtration is to remove any polymeric wax remnant 
which could interfere with the analytes, and interrupt the final results. In addition, it can also remove any 
sticky, insoluble particles that would clog the HPLC system. However, this step can be considered as 
additional option since no significant changes regarding the final results have been reported (See Appendix 
E).   
 
 
Figure 20: Procedure of Coloplast method of antioxidants extraction from polyurethane catheter. 
Next, a 150 µl of filtered catheter solution was added into 1,5 ml isopropanol (ratio of catheter solution 
volume to that of non-solvent is 1:10). The solution was released directly as one portion to isopropanol. A 
polymer clump was immediately formed in isopropanol before it had precipitated at the bottom of the 
centrifuge tube. 
The centrifuge tube was then vortexed and centrifuged at 13400 r/min. The supernant was poured into the 
barrel of a 0,45 µm filter syringe. Meanwhile, 0,5 ml isopropanol was added to the precipitate and then 
poured into the initial supernant in the 0,45 µm filter syringe´s barrel.  
==PAGE:47==
46 | P a g e  
 
The solution was filtered into a centrifuge tube and the tube was placed on an electric evaporizer for 
overnight at 55 °C. The purpose of such step was to completely remove isopropanol. Finally, the dried 
extracts remained in the tube were reconstituted in 400 µl THF, and the solution formed was vortexed and 
was used for HPLC sample preparation. 
It is worth mentioning here that the filters used in the current analysis were not THF resistant (not 
Polytetrafluoroethylene PTFE), and a blind HPLC/UV analysis (on antioxidant- free THF) using these filters 
showed that the applied syringe filter has many extractable compounds (see Appendix G). In addition, a 
blind test on PTFE syringe filters showed also some extractable compounds (see appendix G). These peaks 
appear in the chromatograms of all extracted catheters, interfering with the actual results. 
6.4.2. Quantification of antioxidants in catheter extracts  
 
The external calibration method was applied for the quantitative determination of AOs in Coloplast catheters 
(An internal standard method was tested but with no realistic results). For this purpose, nine standards 
solutions, spanning the concentration range [1-100 µg/ml], were prepared from reference standards 
antioxidants:  BHA, BHT, Tinuvin 328, Irganox 1010, Irganox 1330, Irganox 1076 and Irgafos 168. 
Standard solutions samples were analyzed by the optimized HPLC/UV method. 
 A calibration curve, figures 21-27, relating concentration (µg/ml) to absorbance peak area was obtained by 
HPLC for each of the antioxidants standards. The calibration curve showed a linear relationship between 
concentration and peak area over a range of 1 – 100 µg/ml for the antioxidants Irganox 1010, BHT, BHA, 
and Irganox 1330. The range of linearity was smaller (2,5-100 µg/ml) for the antioxidants Tinuvin 328, 
Irganox 1076 and Irgafos 168. An acceptable correlation factor of 0,99 was obtained for all calibration 
curves. Relative standard deviation of calibration curve of each antioxidant is shown in table 20.  It is worth 
mentioning here that Irgafos 168 is an unstable compound and it gradually oxidizes to its oxidation product 
efter solution preparation. A way to to quantify Irgafos 168 is to use the sum of the area of Irgafos 168 and 
Irgafos 168 oxidation product, however, this is unrealistic since Irgafos 168 oxidation product has an 
additional chromophore (oxygen) and thereby it differently absorbs UV light.

8. Conclusion 
 
Coloplast HPLC/UV method for antioxidants determination in polyurethane catheters was optimized. 
Optimization was achieved by adjusting the flowrate, the temperature and the mobile phase strength.  The 
optimized method was tested for determination of antioxidants and their radiation degradation products, in 
extracts from Colplast polyurethane catheters. However, the results obtained were insufficient and probably 
unrepresentative. Moreover, the obtained results indicate that radiation does not induce significant 
degradation inAOs. 
The extraction method was validated for accuracy and the result showed a very low recovery for the 
antioxidants BHA, BHT, Tinuvin 328, Irganox 1010, Irganox 1330, Irganox 1076 and Irgafos 168. 
Moreover, the HPLC/UV method was validated for accuracy and the relative standard deviation was less 
than 2% for all antioxidants in question.
