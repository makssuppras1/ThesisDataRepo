Multi-Probe System
Abstract
Parts of a Multi-Probe System for preforming spherical measurements of antennas in
the Bluetooth band have been investigated in this Thesis. The system is comprised
of a number of probes placed on a sphere around an antenna to measure its total
radiated power and radiation pattern. The probes for the system was design as dual-
linearly polarized Vivaldi antennas, with a return loss better than 10 dB and isolation
of 30 dB. Due to edge currents disrupting the radiation pattern during the measure-
ments of the probe, the E-plane had a beam squint of 20°. A solution to this problem is
suggested in this thesis by placing quarter-wavelength slots on the side of the probes.
The size of the system was determined to be one meter with 64 probes placed in a Fi-
bonacci grid. Using Friis Equation the errors in the system are estimated. A program
is made to calculate the power and radiation pattern of a antenna. Using data from a
simulation of all 64 probes the power was calculated with an error of less than 4 %.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Technology has in recent years become more reliant on wireless communication. De-
vices like computer equipment, cameras, speakers and headphones now use Blue-
tooth to connect to other devices. In the hearing aid industry, hearing aids have also
become more dependent on wireless communication and especially Bluetooth. This
increase in wireless products means more devices need to incorporate an antenna
to receive and transmit signals in the Bluetooth band. As hearing aids get smaller so
does the antenna and with more customisation options available the antennas have
to be able to function in many different conditions.
Hearing aids have several standards they need to fulfil before they can be sold. It is
therefore crucial that the devices can be measured and tested to confirm that they
live up to the standards, without the measurements taking too much time. One of
those standards is the level of total power an antenna can radiate in the Bluetooth
band. When manufacturing a hearing aid, it is therefore important to measure the
total radiated power (TRP) of the antenna. To properly get the TRP of the antenna
the measurements are often performed in an anechoic chamber. The problem with
anechoic chambers is that they are usually large, expensive and measurements can
take a long time. The reason why measurements take a long time is because a single
probe has to measure the radiation of the antenna in all directions, moving mechan-
ically from one position to another. The measurement time can be reduced by using
more probes to measure the antenna as in the MVG SG Evo (stargate) facility [1] and
in the Multi-Probe Array Rotation (MPAR) Measurement System [2], where multiple
probes are placed on an arc around the antenna. This reduces the measurement time,
but some mechanical movement is still needed, limiting the time saved.
In this thesis an alternative type of antenna measurement system is proposed and
investigated. The system is dedicated to hearing aid antennas and its design was pre-
formed in collaboration with the hearing aid company GN. The system consists of
1
==PAGE:12==
Multi-Probe System
multiple identical probes placed on a sphere around the antenna. The probes will
measure the radiated power of the antenna in all directions without the need for any
mechanical movement. The system would therefore be able to measure the TRP in a
very short time. This would drastically reduce the time needed to preform antenna
measurements and improve the efficiency of the tests.
Due to time constraints, it was not possible to completely design or build the entire
system but several aspects of the system were analysed and studied. In this thesis
a prototype for the antenna used as the probe of the system is designed, simulated,
manufactured and tested. Based on the tests further improvements to the design of
the antenna are proposed.
The measurement system itself is also investigated; the size of the system and the
number and placement of the probes is determined. Potential errors in the system are
identified and estimated. A full simulation of the system with all the probes is made to
test the performance of the system. Finally, a data processing program is proposed for
the system, capable of calculating the TRP and radiation pattern of a antenna under
test (AUT). The program is tested with the data from the full simulation.
The thesis is organized as follows: First, a short overview of antenna measurement
theory is provided. Next, the design and simulation of the probe antenna are pre-
sented. Afterwards, the measurement results of the probe antenna are showcased
and discussed. Based on the measurements an improved probe design is presented.
Thereafter, the design of the measurement system is analyzed. The thesis finishes
with a summary and a conclusion.

Chapter 2
Theory of Antenna Measurement
2.1
Introduction
Antenna measurements are done by placing one or more probes at a certain dis-
tance around the AUT. The probe/probes are antennas specifically designed to re-
ceive power in the same frequency as the AUT. When measuring antennas it is im-
portant to ensure the data transmitted from the AUT to the probe are not disturbed
by other elements or scattering from surfaces around the AUT. Precise Antenna mea-
surements are therefore often done in anechoic chambers where the antennas are
shielded from interference from the outside, and scattering inside the chamber is re-
duced by absorbers.
Antenna measurements can vary greatly depending on several different factors. For
example, the distance between the antenna and measurement probes, the geometric
structure of the measurement facility and the parameters desired to be measured.
These factors were taken into consideration in the design of the multi-probe system
described in this thesis and the theory behind them are outlined in this chapter.
2.2
Near-Field and Far-Field
One of the most deciding factors in antenna measurements depends on whether the
AUT is measured in the near-field or in the far-field.

2.2.1
Far-field Measurements

distance as [3]:

An electrically small antenna is an antenna much smaller than the wavelength often
defined as ka < 1, where k is the wave number and a is the radius of the minimum
sphere around the antenna. If the distance to the antenna is less than the far-field
distance the antenna is in the near-field.
Measuring antennas in the far-field is preferred but can be difficult given the distance
needed between the AUT and measurement probe. If the needed distance is too large
it is not feasible to measure in an anechoic chamber, as the chamber would have to
be too big and expensive to build. These antennas can instead be measured in the
near-field in anechoic chambers.
2.2.2
Near-field Measurements
Near-field antenna measurements are more complicated than far-field measurements
as the measured data must be transform from the near-field to the far-field with a
near-field-to-far-field (NF/FF) transformation. The transformation is necessary as
antenna parameters are usually defined in the far-field. For the transformation to
succeed, the AUT has to be measured over a surface with a minimum sampling rate.

2.4.2
Radiation Pattern
The radiation pattern of an antenna shows how much the antenna radiates in dif-
ferent directions. The pattern has to be measured on the surface of a sphere for a
number of (θ,φ) coordinates. This gives a 3D pattern with a resolution depending on
the number of measurements. As 3D patterns can be difficult to read, the radiation
pattern are often presented as two dimensional pattern cuts. These cuts are made by
keeping either θ or φ constant while varying the other. Typical pattern cuts are for
φ = 0° and φ = 90°.

Chapter 3
Probe Antenna Design
3.1
Introduction
In this chapter the design and simulation of the antenna used as the probe for the
multi-probe system is described. The probe has to fulfill several criteria for the system
to work as required. These specifications were given by GN and have been outlined
below:
1. The probes needed a -10 dB bandwidth covering the Bluetooth band (2.4 GHz
to 2.48 GHz [14]). This is the band the hearing aid antennas will be using.
2. The probe had to be dually linearly polarized. This would mean that when used
for measurements, the radiated power could be measured in the vertical- and
horizontal plane without any need to rotate or move any of the probes.
3. The isolation between the two linear polarizations had to be above 20 dB so the
vertical- and horizontal-plane of the AUT could be measured separately with-
out interference.
4. Because a relatively high number of probes would have to be placed on a limited
area they would have to be as small as possible while still radiating properly.
5. The radiation pattern of the probes would have to be strongest in the forward
direction without too much power radiating to the sides. This would keep the
coupling between the probes to a minimum.
6. The probes should be easy and cheap to manufacturer in order to keep the costs
low.

3.2. Probe Considerations
3.2
Probe Considerations
Many different types of antennas can be used as probes in measurements. Open-
ended waveguides and horn antennas are both very common as probes. However,
open-ended waveguides are usually only polarized in one direction and horn anten-
nas would be too difficult to construct, so neither fit the requirements of the probe.
Various types of small dual polarized antennas were investigated as potential probes
for the project. Three types of antennas were considered: Dipoles, patch and slot
antennas. These types were investigated because they are easy to manufacture, and
are relatively small in size.
Dipole antennas are both small and simple to make, and by placing two orthogonal
to each other a dual-polarized antenna is obtained. In [5] and [6] two novel dual-
polarized dipoles were designed. The problem with these antennas is their low gain
and narrow bandwidth. In [6] a ground plane is added to the antenna improving the
gain and bandwidth, but this makes the antenna much larger. The large beam width
of these antennas could also lead to coupling between the probes. It was therefore
decided not to use dipole antennas.
Patch and slot antennas both have the advantages of a planar structure. Therefore,
they can be easily and cheaply manufactured with microstrip technology as a printed
circuit board (PCB). This gives a lot of freedom in the design, meaning they can vary
greatly in gain, bandwidth and size.
Novel patch antennas like [7] and [8] were investigated as potential probes but it was
decided to use the more well-known tapered slot antenna (Vivaldi antenna) made on
a PCB. The Vivaldi antenna was chosen because they have the potential for a large
bandwidth with high gain [3], and have previously been used as probes for antenna
measurements [9] and even used in multi-probe antenna measurement systems [10]
[11].
The Vivaldi antenna consists of two main components, an exponential tapered slot,
acting as the radiation component and a microstrip-to-slot transition as the feeding
network. The tapered slot is cut into a copper layer on top of a substrate with the
microstrip line as the feeding network, in copper, on the back. The 2D structure of
the Vivaldi antenna design for this project can be seen in Figure 3.2.
The classic Vivaldi antenna is only single linearly polarized, but it can be made dual-
polarized by combining two antennas. In order to combine the antennas a slit is cut
down the middle. One starting at the top and going down to the end of the taper and
one starting at the end of the taper and going down to the bottom. The antennas can
then be slid together in a cross shape making it dual-polarized, see Figure 3.1 .

3.3.2
Feeding Network
The exponential tapered slot needs to be connected to a feeding network for it to
receive and transmit any power. The feeding network has two functions it should
fulfill. It should match the ending part of the exponential taper to a microstrip line
on the back of the substrate, and match this line to a 50 Ωs load in form of a SMA
connector/coaxial cable. Since the coaxial cable is unbalanced and the antenna is a
balanced structure, it should also feed the antenna symmetrically. To achieve this, a
slot-line-to-microstrip transition was used as the feeding network of the antenna.
In [14] the transition is described as a slot-line and a microstrip line located on the
bottom side of a substrate. The microstrip line is placed orthogonal to the slot-line.
For the transition to work the two lines must be matched. To ensure a good match
over the entire bandwidth the slot and microstrip are extended with quarter wave-
length stubs. The bandwidth can be further improved by making the stubs round.
An example of these types of stubs can be seen in Figure 3.3. The stub for the slot-
line works as a shorted stub and for the microstrip the stub is open.
With power being delivered, from the feedline to the middle of the antenna the feed-
ing becomes balanced. The feedline therefore works as a balun.
The transition with the round stubs is used in many Vivaldi antenna designs with
some alterations. Instead of using a full circle for the open microstrip stub a circle
section with a certain angle is used [16] [17]. The same approach was used in this
thesis where the angle was chosen to be 105° as seen in Figure 3.2b. The radius of
both stubs was 8.5 mm as it was found to improve the matching if the radius were
kept the same [18].
As mentioned previously, the slot-line and microstrip line need to have the same
impedance to be matched. At the same time the microstrip line also has to be matched
to 50 Ωs for the SMA connector and coaxial cable. The microstrip line accommodate
both impedances by adjusting the width of the line.

The slot-line has an impedance of 119 Ωat 2.44 GHz (calculated using TxLine). For
it to be matched to the microstrip line it should be 0.425 mm wide. However, thanks
to the two matching stubs, the width does not need to be this narrow and is chosen
through simulations, to be 0.83 mm. This yields an impedance of 94 Ω. At 50 Ωthe
width of the microstrip line should be around 3 mm and from simulation the width
was chosen to be 2.74 mm.
In some designs [18] and [19] the width changes gradually getting wider the closer
to the load it is. In this thesis however, it was decided to use a different method from
[20] and [13]. The feedline was made up of smaller rectangular sections that got wider
the closer they were to the load. The reason this section approach was chosen for the
antenna was that it provided more freedom in the design and was simpler to optimise
in the simulations.

3.5. Dual Antenna with Connected Ground Plane
The slit cut in the vertical antenna meant that the top copper layer was cut in two.
This copper layer acts as the ground plane with only one side being connected to the
feedline/SMA connector. The two halves couples to each other when cut, but the
antenna becomes very unstable from the unconnected potential of half the ground
plane. The antenna will however still function but with the instability greatly influ-
encing the performance of the antenna during measurements.
The problem with the instability could be solved relatively easily by using a small
piece of conducting material and connecting the two halves together after the dual
antenna had been assembled. This stabilized it but also changed the characteristics
of the antenna. A new set of simulations was therefore made of this new antenna with
a connected ground plane.

The S-parameters for the new antenna can be seen in Figure 3.13. The change has

When the measurement of the antenna was first carried out the cable interfered in

To prevent currents from running down the cable, a Bazooka Balun was added to a

a piece of copper tape a quarter of a wavelength long (3 cm) rolled around the cable.
The end closest to the antenna were open, leaving a bit of space between the copper
and cable while the other end was soldered to the cable, short circuiting the balun. A
picture of the Balun can be seen in Figure 4.3.
The Bazooka Balun works as a short-circuited quarter-wave transmission line. The
unwanted current running down the cable from the antenna, will see a very high (ide-

cable choking it [3].

New Antenna Design
5.1
Introduction
The measurements of the probe antenna in Chapter 4 showed how the peak of the
main beam of the co-polarization in the E-plane got flattened and shifted toward the
negative θ-values. This effect is called beam squint and is the result of currents run-
ning down the edges and cable of the Vivaldi antenna, interfering with the radiation
in the E-plane.
In this chapter, a new design for the dual Vivaldi antenna is made. This design pre-
vents current from flowing down the side, by adding quarter-wavelength-slots to the
antenna, improving the radiation. Unfortunately, due to time constrains, the new
Vivaldi antenna could not be manufactured or measured but the simulations of the
design is presented below.
5.2
Antenna Design
The new antenna design was mostly based on the first dual-polarized Vivaldi antenna
with only a few changes, including the quarter-wavelength slots, made to the design.
The biggest change made in the design, was that the two halves of the antenna no
longer was difference sizes. The initial reason for making the two halves different sizes
was to align the center frequencies of the two. But with the ground plane connected
for both halves, the center frequencies could be aligned without changing the size of
one half. The dimensions for the vertical part of the first dual Vivaldi antenna was
therefore used for both the vertical and horizontal parts of the new dual-polarized
Vivaldi antenna.

Chapter 6
Measurement Chamber
6.1
Introduction
The initial outline of the multi-probe system was, it had to consist of a number of
probes placed on a sphere around an AUT. These probes would measure the power
radiated from the AUT in the far-field getting the TRP of the antenna. The measure-
ments would be done in the far-field to avoid a near-field-to-far-field transformation.
The size of the chamber the probes would be placed in, would have to be small and
movable so it could be stored out of the way when not in use. But large enough to
measure the AUT in the far-field.
In this chapter a preliminary investigation and design of the multi-probe measure-
ment chamber is made. Due to time constrains it was not possible to physically build
the chamber, but a setup of how the chamber would function with all the probes was
designed. In the design the overall structure of the system was outlined, the size of
the chamber were considered, the placement and number of probes were investi-
gated, and the biggest sources of errors in the chamber where identified and esti-
mated as well as the influence the probes would have on each other. All these factors
are presented and discussed below. Finally, in this chapter a full simulation with all
the probes, was made and used as test data for a data-processing program for the
Multi-Probe System. The probes used in all the simulations was the one designed in
Chapter 3 with the connected ground plane.
In the design of the chamber no absorbers or outside shielding was included, neither
was the construction holding the probes and AUT in place included. This was outside
the scope of this thesis.

6.2
Structure of the Switching System
The multi-probe system would, when completed, consist of a number of probes mea-
suring the vertical and horizontal polarization of the AUT. Each polarization would be
measured separately for one probe at the time, quickly switching from probe to probe
making a full measurement in under a minute. To achieve this, the probes will be
connected to a switching system that will control which probe will be measuring and
which polarization is to be measured. The switching system was not realized during
this thesis due to time constrains, but an overview of a possible system is described in
this section to give a better understanding of the measurement chamber as a whole.
An outline of a possible switching system can be seen in Figure 6.1.
Each probe would be connected to a single-pole-double-throw (SPDT) switch that
would control the polarization. The switch will terminate the antenna port in a 50 Ω
load when not active. Eight of the SPDT switches are combined in a single pole eight
throw (SP8T) switch, that in turn is combined with eight other switches in a new SP8T
switch and so on, until all probes are connected as seen in Figure 6.1. By connecting
a VNA or spectrum analyser (SA) with the final switch in the chain, a measurement
of the AUT from each probe could be made by controlling the switches. The switches
would be controlled electrically, by a microcontroller (e.g. Raspberry Pi), which will
guarantee a fast switching and therefore acquisition of the measurements. Ideally the
switching/measurement of the AUT could be done in less than one minute.

6.4
Probe Placement
The measurements of the chamber are made in the far-field, meaning the probes
placed upon the surface of a sphere do not need to fulfill the sampling criteria men-
tioned in Equation 2.3 and 2.4. This gives more freedom in the placement of the
probes. But the surface area of the sphere, where the probes will be placed, has a
limited area, and the position of the probes cannot be changed once the system has
been built. The positions of the probes will therefore have to be chosen carefully.
In classical spherical antenna measurements, a probe measures the AUT at points of
a regular (θ,φ)-grid with a constant angle between the points. This approach does
not give a uniform sampling across the sphere, but yields a denser sampling around
the poles than at the "equator".

This non-uniformity presents a problem for the probes closest to the poles. For in-
stance, if the sampling rate where set to 20° the distance between the probes would
have to be approximately 6 cm. This would leave no room to the probes themselves,
which, have a diameter of 7 cm.
A solution could be to remove every second or third probe close to the poles so each
remaining probe would have enough space. However, when measuring the TRP of an
antenna it is important to measure the power all around the AUT. There is no need
to have a dense sampling at the poles. Therefore, a more uniform distribution of the
probes was investigated to see if the TRP could be measured more effectively.

Summary
The calculations of the errors from misalignment in this section is only an estimation,
as using Friis Equation is a simplification of the system. The errors calculated will also
change if a different test antenna is used. Nevertheless, the calculation gives a good
indication of where the largest errors comes from in the measurement chamber. The
rotation of the AUT resulted in the largest error for the individual probes but would
not affect the TRP but only the radiation pattern. The second largest source of errors
in the chamber was the horizontal distance between the antennas. If the error in the
distance were to come from a misplaced probe the error could most likely be com-
pensated for by calibrating the chamber. Misalignment of the AUT could lead to an
error in the measurement but this error would not affect the measurement too much.
In the calculation the error was -13.8 dB for a misalignment of 1 cm.

(θ,φ) grid making the radiation patterns easy to produce. However, with the probes
being placed in a Fibonacci grid the patterns cannot be directly computed. To get the
(θ,φ) cuts the data from the probes has to be interpolated over the whole sphere.
The interpolation was done by first taking the points on the sphere and placing them
in a rectangular coordinate system with (φ,θ) as (x, y ). The directivity of a point
would then be in the z-direction. This introduced a problem for the points placed
on the edges of the coordinate system: φ = 0°, φ = 360°, θ = 0° and θ = 180°. On
the sphere, each point has the same number of neighbors but in the (x, y ) plane, the
points at the edges has fewer making the interpolation difficult at these points. To
solve this, the data was "wrapped" around in the φ direction so the points at the end
of φ appeared at the beginning and vice versa. A visualization of this can be seen
in Figure 6.18, where the Fibonacci points are plotted in blue in a rectangular (φ,θ)
coordinate system. The red circles are the points being wrapped around the φ-axis.
The green points in the figure represent the points at the poles of the sphere. At these
points, θ = 0° and θ = 180°, the directivity changes depending on the angle of φ. At
φ = 0° the directivity will be the value measured for one polarization, but at φ = 90°
it will be the value measured at the other polarization. To account for this five points
at θ = 0° and θ = 180° for φ = 0°,90°,180°,270° and 360° are added to the data as seen
in Figure 6.18. These points have the values of alternately the vertical and horizontal
polarization of that point. If the data for the vertical polarization is being used, the

Chapter 7
Conclusion
A preliminary investigation of a multi-probe system for preforming spherical mea-
surements of antennas in the Bluetooth band has been performed in this thesis. The
system consists of a measurement chamber, with probes placed on a sphere around
the AUT that could measure the TRP and radiation pattern of the antenna.
A dual-linearly polarized Vivaldi antenna was designed as the probe for the system.
The probe was composed of two, slightly different, single Vivaldi antennas, combined
in a cross shape to achieve the dual polarization with each half having their own port.
Simulations of the probe were made with HFSS and the S-parameters and radiation
pattern were measured. The measurements of the S-parameters showed a good agree-
ment between simulations and measurements. The probe had a return loss better
than 10 dB in the Bluetooth band and an isolation between the two ports of more
than 30 dB. For the radiation pattern of the probe some deviations between the mea-
sured and simulated results could be seen. The co-polarization in the H-plane was
around 1 dB lower in the measurements and the E-plane was flattened and shifted
more than 20° to the side. The reason for the difference was found to come from edge
currents running down the side of the probe and cable interfering with the radiation
of the antenna. A new design for the probe was therefore proposed, which could re-
duce the current by adding rectangular quarter-wavelength slots to the side of the
probe. The new probe was composed of two identical Vivaldi antennas and should,
when manufactured, reduce the shift in the E-plane, improving the directivity and
return loss of the probe.
In addition to the probe, the overall setup of the measurement chamber was inves-
tigated. The size of the sphere the probe will be placed on, was determined to be
one meter in diameter with 64 probes. The probes were placed in a Fibonacci grid
rather than a regular grid as it allowed a more uniform distribution of the probes and

a smaller error when numerically integrating over the sphere to obtain the TRP. The
errors in the measured TRP in the chamber from misalignments of either the probes
or the AUT was estimated with Friis Equation. The largest error was found to come
from a change in the distance between the probe and antenna, where a misalignment
of 1 cm could result in a error of -13.8 dB in the power between the two.
Finally, a simulation of the measurement chamber with all 64 probes was preformed.
The data from the simulation was used to calculate the TRP and the radiation pattern
of the AUT using a data processing program written for the thesis. The radiation pat-
tern of the AUT was recreated with great accuracy and the error in TRP was below 4
% compared with the TRP from HFSS.
With the full chamber simulation, the system could measure the TRP of an AUT within
an error of 4 % and obtain the correct radiation pattern of the AUT. From this it can be
concluded, that the concept of the multi-probe measurement system works, and that
a dual-linearly polarized Vivaldi antenna can be used as the probe for the system.
In this thesis, several aspects of the multi-probe system have been explored, but some
still remain for future work. The proposed improved design for the probe antenna
should be manufactured and measured so it can be compared with the simulations.
The structure holding the probes and AUT in place should be designed and fabri-
cated as well. The switching system used to collect the data from each probe as well
as the code controlling the switches, also need to be designed. Absorbers to avoid
reflections from the chamber and shielding of the chamber to prevent interference
from outside should still be included. Last but not least, a method to calibrate the
measurement chamber, should be developed to minimize the errors in the chamber.
