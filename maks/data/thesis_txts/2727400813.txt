Thesis abstract 
 
4D Printing of soft responsive polymer microstructures. 
Keywords: additive manufacturing, 3D design, microrobotics, two-photon polymerization, 
smart materials, pH sensing. 
 
4D printing creates material systems capable of changing their shape, structure  
or functions directly off the printing bed. Combined with recent advances in micro 3D 
printing technologies, such as two-photon polymerization (2PP), it can overcome  
the inability to create complex 3D structures. This way different smart materials,  
such as hydrogels, whose traditional fabrication methods could not meet design expectations, 
can be employed in smart microdevices. The thesis below attempts to validate  
the custom-made, anionic-type hydrogel photoresist as a useful material in 4D printing  
and its swelling response to different pH levels. 
In order to do so, a set of different microstructures was modelled using CAD software 
and printed using Nanoscribe Photonic Professional GT+, employing the 2PP technique. 
Throughout several parameter sweep iterations, each structure was optimized for achieving 
the most stable structures and characterized using SEM. Obtained microstructures were 
exposed to different pH-levelled aqueous environments to examine their behaviour  
and characterised live under optical microscopy. The key dimensions of microstructures 
were measured when dry and wet and compared to determine their swelling ratio.  
It was found that used hydrogel formulation expended considerably in a pH~13 
environment. In pH~2 and pH~7 microstructures swelled on a similar level, which  
was connected to rehydration after fabrication. Depending on examined object size  
and geometries, different swelling ratios were determined. In addition, the higher dose used 
for microstructure fabrication corresponded directly to an increase in swelling ratio across 
all pH levels. 
Obtained results prove that used hydrogel photoresist is valid for 2PP fabrication  
and responsive towards high pH environments by visible swelling. Complex geometry 
hydrogel structures are possible to obtain with high precision, allowing for freedom of design 
crucial for application in a variety of different fields. In further perspective, this material can 
be used for sensing microdevices or custom-modelled carriers.

1. Aim of the project. 
 
It is highly desired across the biomedical sector to achieve precisely manufactured 
devices of complex geometries, that express dynamic behaviour required by further 
application. Hydrogel materials meet the requirements of medical science due to their 
excellent biocompatibility, water absorption capacity, stimuli-responsive behaviour  
and good mechanical properties [1]. However, their traditional fabrication methods often 
lack the capability to create complex 3D structures. This can be overcome with 4D printing, 
a new generation of additive manufacturing that creates material systems capable  
of changing their shape, structure or functions directly off the printing bed. With recent 
breakthroughs in micro 3D printing technologies, such as enabling faster fabrication using 
two-photon polymerization (2PP) technique, the creation of microstructures has advanced 
significantly [2]. Combining 4D printing and the 2PP, new possibilities emerge not only in 
what we can print but also in what we can make those prints do. This project aims at 
scrutinizing custom-made hydrogel photoresist as valid material for the purpose of 4D 
printing, employing 2PP technology, and characterizing its behaviour when exposed to 
different pH levels. The results will allow us to precisely design innovative, smart material 
micro-tools for various applications.The following work intertwines to a great degree with 
current projects realised in the DTU Nanolab POLYMIC group. Examined material can be 
used to fabricate microrobotic sensing devices, which could be applied for precise 
microscale sensing. They will allow us to qualitatively identify regions with different pH 
levels by simple microscopic observations of their shape change. Another implementation 
lies in fabricating artificial cell scaffolding for drug delivery studies. Artificially created cells 
are promising alternatives to culture cells, which take a lot of resources, while being  
time-consuming and lacking reproducibility, making them challenging to work with. 
Hydrogel-based scaffolds can be printed fast and efficiently employing the 2PP technique, 
overcoming previously mentioned constraints while mimicking the original cell types to  
a great degree due to the material’s nature. Their surface can potentially be functionalised 
creating membranes, which will help in understanding interactions vital for the development 
and testing of new drug molecules [3]. With freedom of design provided by 4D printing,  
a wide range of new possibilities appears, where the only limiting factor lies withing our 
creativity.

2. Theory background. 
2.1 4D printing – embracing the freedom of structure and design individuality. 
 
In 2012 during the TED talk conference held at MIT, Skylar Tibbits presented static 
objects, fabricated using a conventional three-dimensional (3D) printing method. They 
transformed themselves into more complicated structures over course of the time, when 
exposed to water [4]. Soon after, in 2013, the first research paper, concerning the fabrication 
of thermodynamically programmed soft composites expressing complex 3D configurations, 
was published and marked the beginning of a new era for material manufacturing [5].  
Four-dimensional (4D) printing is defined as ‘a targeted evolution of the 3D printed 
structure, in terms of shape, property, and functionality. It is capable of achieving  
self-assembly, multi-functionality, and self-repair. It is time-dependent, printer-independent, 
and predictable’ [6]. The definition introduces a primary advantage over traditional  
3D printing, which is being possible to ‘have a functioning device that you just pick up from 
the printer’ [7], thanks to the dynamic objects mechanics, reducing further assembly 
requirements. Size change capabilities introduce another benefit in terms of fabrication 
limitations, lifting the problematic large one-part structure production. Through 
computational folding, objects larger than printers can be successfully printed due to material 
structural shrinkage and folding, allowing for compression to secondary form, which  
is released into the original after creation [8]. The rest of the advantages and possible 
disadvantages are similar to the ones brought up by 3D printing, including reduced usage  
of energy, material, cost and time optimisation accounting for more sustainable growth  
in the manufacturing sector. The major disadvantage brought up to public attention lies  
in access to material systems suitable for 4D printing [9]. At the same time, it promotes  
a need for research in smart material formulation for manufacturing purposes, pushing the 
current state of the technology further [10].  
The main fundamentals of 4D printing technology are presented in Figure 1. Diagram 
showcasing five fundamentals of 4D printing technology, adapted from [6]., which  
are the 3D printing facility, present stimulus, smart material system, interaction mechanism  
and mathematical modelling [6]. The process relies on those segments and they must  
be taken into account while designing a successful workflow. 4D printed structures may  
or may not be composed of one or more materials, where additional factors such as swelling 
ratio and thermal expansion coefficient play an important role in obtaining desired  
shape-shifting behaviour [11]. Herein, the 3D printing facility should be accustomed  
to chosen materials with respect to the chosen additive manufacturing (AM) process of 
interest. The second consideration is the printing material, which must be sensitive to stimuli, 
despite being sandwiched layer by layer. These materials are frequently referred  
to as programmable or smart materials, discussed further in Chapter 2.2. The third aspect  
is stimuli, which is a direct cause of structure actuation and shape change. Stimuli can fall 
into the category of being physical or chemical and will be further discussed in Chapter 2.2. 
Interaction mechanisms are intricate pathways that required material to actuate. Sometimes, 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
 
a simple exposition of stimuli is not enough, and a certain sequence of action under  
an appropriate amount of time might be necessary to achieve desired results [6]. Interaction 
mechanisms can be mechanical loadings and other physical manipulations, as in the example 
of the constrained-thermo-mechanic mechanism [12]. Figure 2 showcases known  
shape-shifting mechanisms applied in 4D printing [6]. Lastly, mathematical modelling  
is an advantageous utility tool for designing material construction and distribution across 
fabricated devices. It allows for establishing the necessary connections between core system 
elements, binding material structure, desired final shape, material properties and stimulus 
properties together [6].

Out of necessity for predicting the time-dependent behaviour of any 4D printed 
structure, a detailed study on modelling was conducted in an attempt to systemize  
the shape-morphing phenomenon across different materials and geometries. As such, three 
laws of 4D printing were proposed, which would provide a better understanding 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
 
of the physical occurrence associated with the shape-changing process of fabricated 
structures:  
1. The first law states that nearly all shape change behaviours originate from  
the fundamental phenomenon, which is relative expansion between active and passive 
materials [13].  
 
2. The second law states that nearly all multi-material 4D-printed objects exhibit  
shape-morphing behaviours due to four physical factors: mass diffusion, thermal 
expansion, molecular transformation, and organic growth [13]. The factors above 
directly lead to relative expansion between active and passive material described by the 
first law.  
 
3. The third law states that the time-dependent shape morphing behaviour of nearly all 
multi-material 4D printed structures is governed by two types of time constants [13]. 
Depending on the stimuli and material used for 4D printing, these constants can be 
equal, big, or disappear with regard to one another [12]. 
According to Hype Cycle for Emerging Technologies report published in August 2018 
by Gartner, 4D printing has been named one of the technologies enabling Transparently 
Immersive Experiences, which is at the centre of extending smarter living, public spaces  
and working [14]. Following the hype cycle pictured in Figure 3 [15], 4D printing should 
enter a peak of inflated expectation right about now, becoming a mainstream study topic  
and expecting rapid progress over the course of the next five years.

2.1.1 Two-photon polymerization. 
 
Two-photon polymerization (2PP) is a leading direct laser writing technology  
in ultraprecise microfabrication techniques [19]. It is widely applied in a range  
of highly-specialised fields including photonic crystals, mechanical structures with 
moveable or interlocking parts, microchannel and microfluidic devices, and biocompatible 
templates [20]. It has a number of benefits over other traditional microfabrication processes, 
like the superior resolution of the fabricated structure due to the nonlinear response  
of photoresist, creating complex structures directly from Computer-Aided Design (CAD) 
projects and the capability of producing tall microstructures [19].  2PP is a nonlinear optical 
process based on the two-photon absorption (2PA) phenomenon. The principle of 2PA is the 
ability of the molecule to simultaneously absorb the energy of two photons, due to the 
‘virtual energy state’ [21], in order to reach the excited state from the ground state [22],  
as shown in Figure 4 [19].  
 
 
Figure 4. Schematic comparing a) one-photon absorption and b) two-photon absorption. Inspired by [19]. 
 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
 
2PA begins inside the focal volume of the beam, where there is sufficient photon density 
provided by the high-intensity laser. The sufficient dose will exceed the polymerization 
threshold and lead to photoresist polymerization in the target region while limiting 
polymerization in the out-of-focus regions [23], which are transparent to the wavelength  
of the photons. Schematic of the 2PP process is shown in Figure 5 [23]. 
 
 
Figure 5. Schematic of 2PP fabrication mechanism based on a) chemical reaction mechanism and b) visible outcome of 
fabricated structure, inspired by [23]. 
 
A typical 2PP setup has the following components: a laser source and light-direction 
system, a beam-focusing and motion-stage system, a computer-aided control system,  
and a charge-coupled device-based monitoring system. High-precision piezoelectric stages 
are typically paired with Galvano-mirror scanners and linear stages for large-range 
manufacturing [22]. Piezo stages have a positioning precision of 1 nm, but their speed  
is limited to a maximum of 100 µm/s, and their operating field is normally less than 500 µm. 
A mirror scanning system using galvanometric scanners is ideal for producing 
microstructures with precise geometry quickly since it has essentially no mechanical inertia 
[24]. An example of a 2PP setup is shown in Figure 6. 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
 
 
Figure 6. Schematic setup for 2PP fabrication. Figure redrew based on [2]. 
 
The locally polymerized region is referred to as a voxel, which stands for 3D volume 
element, represented by two-dimensional pixels and the thickness of the object’s slice [25]. 
The fabricated structures are created as layers of voxels organised in XY plane due to galvo 
scanning or piezo scanning method, making it a precise voxel-by-voxel process. After layer 
completion, the beam is shifted on the Z-axis by the printing stage to repeat the process until  
the model is successfully fabricated [26]. 
 
2.1.2 Model translation and process parameters in the Nanoscribe system. 
 
The success of fabricating desired structure depends on a number of variables 
influencing model creation as well as the polymerization process and its damage thresholds. 
They can be divided into structural and dose parameters. Each 3D CAD model has to be 
translated from Standard Tessellation Language (STL) file into a language understandable 
by printer software. Translation deconstructs vectorized model into the array of voxels  
in space, by slicing it into multiple layers, each printed individually. Slicing distance (SD) 
is a parameter determining the distance between adjacent layers on the Z-axis and as such 
the thickness of the voxel building unit. The smaller the SD, the finer the resolution and the 
more voxels have to be polymerized. As such, more precise structures with intricate details 
can be fabricated with lower slicing distance values, but at the same time, they promote 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
 
higher printing times. Therefore, depending on the situation, it might be important to find 
the right balance between fabrication time and structure resolution. SD can be set into the 
fixed or adaptive mode, in which the SD value is varied depending on preprogrammed 
conditions in regard to the proposed geometry. Hatching distance (HD) refers to the interval 
between parallel lines on the X–axis, constructing each voxel or layer of the structure. It is 
used to create internal infill by laser hatching alongside determined trajectories, which 
provides support and stability to printed objects. The smaller the HD, the denser the infill, 
which influences the mechanical properties of the fabricated structure [27]. Figure 7 
presents a visual representation of slicing distance and hatching distance corresponding to 
structure translation [27]. 
 
Figure 7. Graphical representation of HD and SD definition, creating building blocks of 2PP fabricated structures, inspired 
by [27]. 
  
The dose parameters are Laser Power (LP) and Scan Speed (SS). LP refers to the 
intensity of the photon beam used for transferring the energy into photoresist material, that 
is polymerizing in the focal point after exceeding the threshold. SS is the speed at which the 
laser beam moves across the photoresist during the printing process. It is the measure of how 
quickly the laser traces the preprogrammed pattern. The LP and SS can be adjusted to deliver 
a certain dose, which is defined as intensity multiplied by time, Equation 1 [28].

Furthermore, the degree of reaction conversion is directly dependent on the dose 
parameters. The higher the exposition, the more completed, and depended on the photoresist, 
crosslinked the structure is. That directly determines the mechanical properties of the printed 
==PAGE:18==

structure and is the preferred way to make the structure more stable [27]. Too much dose can 
lead to the resin being overexposed locally, causing photo-ionization and leading to rapid 
local temperature increase. This will result in permanent damage to the structure due to 
thermal damage and can be noticed by the appearance of micro-bubbles [29]. Figure 8 
pictures the influence of dose on the degree of polymerization on panel  
a) and the over-exposed structure on panel b), which was captured live during sample 
printing session. 
 
Figure 8. a) Plot introducing the dose influence on the degree of conversion [28]. b) Optical microscopy image of damaged,

The last thing that has to be addressed is the proximity effect of polymerization.  
It is described as the occurrence of artefacts caused by voxel writing in close spatial 
proximity to each other [30]. It reduces the dose threshold for over-exposing and damaging 
the structure with the laser beam, during writing with overlapping structures in close vicinity. 
As such, it can be defined as a structure-dependent phenomenon varying with laser writing 
parameters [31]. One of the ways to limit the proximity effect to happen is to decrease the 
writing speed and adjust the beam intensity accordingly [31]. The proximity effect  
is a limiting factor in obtaining structures in high resolutions [30]. It leads the user to 
carefully consider the aforementioned structural and dose parameter values for the desired 
model to be fabricated since they are strongly interconnected with each other. A number of 
experimental sessions may deem necessary to achieve wanted results.

2.2 Smart materials – stimuli-responsive systems. 
 
The definition of a smart material was formulated at the end of the 20th century in the 
publications of Takagi [32] and Rogers [33]. Intelligent materials are materials capable of 
reacting to external stimuli, causing significant changes in their properties. Whether the 
materials themselves are ‘smart’ is considered a topic of debate. It is argued that there are 
no smart materials by themselves, but instead, materials with specific intrinsic properties 
that may be used in products, systems, or buildings that display ‘smart’ behaviour [34]. 
Stimuli triggering material response can be divided into physical, chemical, and biological 
categories. Physical interactions include heat, light, mechanical stress, and magnetic  
and electric field actuators. Chemical interactions include changes in pH, ionic strength  
and solvent interactions. The last biological category includes reacting to the exposition  
of various microorganisms [35].  Smart materials can be classified based on the stimulus 
driving the change in their properties. This is a difficult task, that might cause inaccuracies 
since some of these material classes have various names while being triggered by the same 
sort of input. Inverse piezoelectric and electrostrictive materials, for example, are both driven 
by an electric field but have distinct crystallographic properties [36]. Moreover, there are 
instances where a material can respond to a combination of two or more stimuli at the same 
time. The complex interaction between multiple mechanisms expands the possibilities for 
customizing the material's application [36].  Smart materials express different actions 
categorized in Table 1. Those categories are not exclusive and may overlap with each other 
in the region of the particular material system [34]. 
 
Table 1. Actions performed by smart materials upon actuation. 
Expressed action 
Description  
Shape changing
Material changes shape in response to an external stimulus.
Self-actuating
Automated actuation in response to an external stimulus.
Self-sensing 
Allows automated detection and sometimes quantification 
of external stimuli.
Self-diagnostic 
Allows automated detection of defects. Closely aligned 
with self-sensing.
Self-healing
Automatic self-repair of damage.

2.2.1 Hydrogel materials. 
 
The emergence of hydrogels dates back to 1960. when Wichterle and Lim first proposed 
the use of hydrophilic networks of 2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate for contact lenses [37]. 
Since then, interest in hydrogels and their use in biomedical and pharmaceutical applications 
has increased. Hydrogels are defined as crosslinked polymer network structures that can 
absorb and hold a substantial amount of water while maintaining a unique shape. They can 
be categorized based on polymer source (natural or synthetic), type of crosslinking (physical 
or chemical), electric charge (ionic or non-ionic) and stimuli–responsiveness [38]. Smart 
hydrogels fall into the category of Shape Changing Polymers (SCP). When exposed 
to a stimulus they shift their shape by swelling and deswelling when stimuli are no longer 
present. This is a non-programable shape-changing behaviour, which cannot be externally 
manipulated after the fabrication of the material [39]. Other SCP can perform programmable 
behaviour, which is referred to as shape memory polymers, but hydrogels do not fall into 
this category [39].  
Hydrogel's ability to respond to pH changes is directly connected to the ionic nature  
of their network, caused by the presence of different unbound ionic pendant groups [40]. 
They react by sudden changes to their volume under pH variation in an aqueous environment 
[41]. Based on those elements, they are further categorised as anionic, cationic  
and ampholytic. Anionic hydrogels include acidic groups in their network, which expands 
when the pH level is higher than the acidic dissociation constant (Ka) of corresponding acidic 
groups. Cationic hydrogels include basic groups in their network, which expand at pH levels 
lower than their basic dissociation constant (Kb). Ampholytic hydrogels contain both acidic 
and basic groups throughout the hydrogel network, which causes swelling to occur between 
pH levels constrained between Ka and Kb constants of included groups [40]. Figure 9 
represents the schematic swelling response of ionic hydrogels in various pH-levelled 
environments [38]. 
 
Figure 9. Schematic of swelling behaviour of anionic, cationic and ampholytic pH-sensitive hydrogels across different pH 
levels, reprinted from [38]. 
 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
 
Immersing a pH-sensitive hydrogel into a solution causes the diffusion of a solution into 
the polymetric network with the aforementioned pendant groups. Based on diffusion 
constants, different pH levels of the aqueous environment and influx of corresponding 
H+/OH-, pendant groups get ionised, generating changes in the polymer network and causing 
an electrostatic field to occur and ionic imbalance [37]. The difference in the balance of ionic 
species concentration inside and outside of the hydrogel leads to the occurrence of osmotic 
pressure causing swelling and deswelling of hydrogel material [38]. Osmotic pressure  
is balanced against the elastic/viscoelastic force arising from the polymer network. 
Therefore, the swelling equilibrium of a pH-sensitive hydrogel is achieved when the 
elastic/viscoelastic force balances the osmotic pressure, solvent pressure and electrostatic 
forces [38]. The effect of mixing force between the pH-sensitive hydrogel and aqueous 
solution can be determined not to play a significant role in the swelling mechanism of ionic 
hydrogels. Figure 10 showcases a sample pH-responsive hydrogel system, while exposed to 
the solvent. 
 
Figure 10. Schematic of the reference and current configurations of a pH-sensitive hydrogel. Arbitrary shape undergoes 
deformation due to exposure to external stimuli. Reprinted from [38]. 
In attempts to model the deformation response of ionic hydrogel, the most important 
factor is to determine whether the system is mono- or multi- phase. Monophasic models 
consider the hydrogel as a single-phase matter combining the polymer network, solvent and 
ions into one. They are based on poroelasticity theory [42] and are suitable for hydrogel 
materials expressing nano- and micro-porosity [38]. Multiphasic models treat polymer 
networks, solvent and ions as separate phases capable of exchanging momentum, which  
is more accurate for macro-porous hydrogel structures, hence finding application  
in biomechanics in relation to studies of biological tissues. While multiphasic models 
provide a more accurate analysis of hydrogels’ behaviour, monophasic models are more 
commonly used due to their simplicity in trying to characterize pH-responsive systems [38].

3.2 Instrumentation and workflow. 
 
The design of microstructures was performed using Autodesk Inventor 2023 CAD 
software. Importing Standard Tessellation Language (STL) files of 3D designs  
and translating them into corresponding printing job files was conducted using DeScribe 
software, version 2.7. Fabrication of microstructures was carried out using Nanoscribe 
Photonic Professional GT+ (Nanoscribe GmbH, Germany) designed for micro 3D printing 
using the 2PP method, operated using NanoWrite software, version 1.10.5. The system 
makes use of a femtosecond fibre laser source with a wavelength of 780 nm, LP varying

5. Topological characterization using SEM imaging. Structures were examined on their

reprinting experiments that were conducted.  
 
6. Material actuation experiments were run once optimal printing parameters were

3.3 Photoresist preparation. 
 
Hydrogel photoresist was prepared in separate batches beforehand, according to the 
predefined composition showcased in Table 2. The recipe was prepared by the Florea Lab 
team, at Trinity College Dublin and as of today, the manuscript is yet to be published. 
It is a synthetic hydrogel which will become chemically crosslinked as a result of 2PP 
fabrication. The photoinitiator of choice was weighed on the analytical scale before 
transporting it to UV-resistant laminated glass vials. Then, in the following order, SR9035 
and acrylic acid were transferred and weighed by volume inside the vial and enclosed  
to avoid loss of volatile acrylic acid from the composition. Lastly, the vial was heated up in 
a water bath to propagate the dissolution of the photoinitiator inside the mixture.  
The prepared photoresist is an anionic type hydrogel material and is expected to visibly swell 
in high pH environments, in agreement with Figure 10 in Chapter 2.2.1.

3.5 Microstructure designs. 
 
As discussed in Chapter 2.1.1, the 2PP technique provides great geometry versatility 
for microstructure fabrication. While the main topic of the project is to characterize  
custom-made hydrogel material in terms of swelling behaviour, it has to be performed on a 
number of microstructures. Hydrogels are suitable for application in versatile fields. During 
the project, the main pursuit was focused on microrobotics fabrication, capable of chemical 
sensing, and the creation of cell-mimicking scaffolds for further functionalisation and

3.5.2 Arced armed microrobot scaffold. 
 
Figure 13 shows a simple triangular microrobot scaffold. The presented design was 
conceptualized with the microrobot’s ability to be controlled using optical trapping.  
This is achieved by using different geometry and dimension choices. Three spheres included 
==PAGE:26==

in the model allow for manipulating the structure in space using the optical trapping 
phenomenon and maintaining three degrees of freedom. This permits the user to move the 
microrobot in the XY plane, Z axis and rotate the structure. Spheres are connected with thin 
arced arms, creating a scaffold for potential working features of microrobots, that could be 
added with further designs. The arms are not modelled through simple cylinders. A soft arc 
modelled beneath the actual arm was added, to ensure better stability of the structure during 
the layer-by-layer build-up. It makes more contact points with spheres, which limits the 
amount of free-hanging voxels and harsh, sharp structure transition during fabrication. For 
this particular application, it was important to take the weight-limiting approach, which  
is expressed by thin arms. Microrobot has three contact points with substrate surfaces 
through created spheres, which should make it easier to detach from the substrate by external 
forces, or increased tension in the structure caused by a response to hydrogel actuation. The 
final model put to the printing was designed with 10 µm diameter spheres, 30 µm arm length  
and 3 µm diameter at the thinnest point. Those design choices ensure the light weight of the 
structure and correspond to the efficient entrapment of spheres in the laser beams. A precise 
sketch of this particular microrobot model can be found in Appx. A Figure 42.

3.5.4 Microstructures biomimicking human cells. 
 
The next designs are meant to mimick the cells found in nature. At the very beginning, 
it is important to highlight, that designed models are ideally symmetric, which does not 
correspond to irregular shapes commonly found in real-life cells [45], [46]. This makes it 
impossible to replicate them in every possible aspect. When designing the models, the 
approximate real-life dimensions were not always taken into account for the ease of the 
modelling but were scaled down accordingly during the fabrication step, to achieve  
a structure as close as possible to their real-life counterparts.  
Red blood cells (RBC) are an essential component of the mammalian circulatory system. 
They play a crucial role in oxygen transport from the lungs to body tissues across the 
cardiovascular system, which makes red blood cells indispensable for sustaining the body's 
physiological functions. Their unique biconcave shape increases their surface area, 
facilitating a more efficient exchange of gases across membranes. The lack of organelles 
influences RBC flexibility and deformability, making them capable of navigating through 
thin capillaries across the circulatory system. These shape features make them fascinating 
on their own and interesting for application in various fields ranging from drug delivery 
systems to biosensors [47]. Figure 15 showcases the RBC followed by the proposed designs 
made for the sake of the project. The key features identified as necessary for the designs 
were the disc shape of a cell, that is not exposed to external stress, and the slipperlike shape 
present in small blood vessels, which ensures more efficient blood flow in the system [45]. 
The aspect ratio of those was further downscaled to fit an approximate range of 7,5 – 8,7 μm 
in diameter and 1,7 – 2,2 µm thickness in order to match the real-life counterparts, using 
DeScribe software during the model translation step [48]. Precise sketches of designed RBC 
models can be found at Appx. A Figure 43 and Figure 44.

Smooth muscle cells (SMC) are an integral part of the muscular system, responsible for 
various involuntary contractions within organs and tissues. They possess a spindle-like shape

organs like the digestive tract, blood vessels, and the respiratory system. Smooth muscle 
cells have remarkable contractile abilities and can sustain contractions for extended periods 
without becoming fatigued. They are controlled by the autonomic nervous system  
and hormones, allowing them to perform vital functions such as blood flow regulation,

3.6 Fabrication. 
 
Successful fabrication of microstructures involves several steps. They can be divided 
into STL file translation, printer operations and sample development. Translating designed 
models into code understandable by printing control software is done using Nanoscribe 
DeScribe. Loading the STL file into DeScribe causes an import wizard to appear, guiding 
the user through the process of parametrization for the desired model. Here, the user can 
freely transform the object's position in space, and dimensions and choose desired printing 
parameters, which were described in Chapter 2.1.2. Afterwards, the software compiles  
a single job file ready to execute. Alternatively, the user can use the advanced STL 
processing menu, which proposes two additional batch processing and parameter sweep 
workflows. Batch processing allows for processing multiple STL files using predefined 
recipes without employing a regular import wizard, while parameter sweep offers simple 
optimization of existing recipes by changing desired process parameters. Parameter sweep 
is a useful option for conducting experiments on several different printing parameters, which 
influence the topology of the designed microstructure. The next step is to prepare the printer 
for microstructure fabrication, by mounting a clean objective into the corresponding carousel 
placeholder. Afterwards, NanoWrite software is initialised and calibrated for printing.  
To prepare a sample, the user chooses an appropriate sample holder, places a borosilicate 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
 
coverslip inside and fixes it with tape. On the bottom side of the coverslip, a drop of 
Immersol oil is put. On the top side of the coverslip, a photoresist is applied. However, the 
preparation of printing cells for hydrogel samples requires a custom adjustment to regular 
workflow. Due to the high vapour pressure of acrylic acid present in the prepared photoresist, 
a solution for limiting evaporation has to be proposed. For that reason, the top side of the 
coverslip is taped with two parallel stripes, on top of which a small microscope panel is 
placed, creating a small gap between two separate surfaces. After printing cell preparation 
is finished, the photoresist is channelled through the gap on the side using the pipette. Due 
to the capillary effect, the resin will spread inside, creating a uniform layer and pushing air 
out. This way, the user ensures ease of finding the interface between resin and coverslip. A 
schematic of a custom printing cell is presented in Figure 18. With the sample prepared and 
mounted into the printing stage, the sample has to be approached through NanoWrite. The 
software will move the objective into the sample, dipping it into the oil and finding the 
interface automatically. After loading the printing job file, the user can start the job and the 
printer will execute the procedure. 
 
Figure 18. Schematic of constructed printing cell for fabrication using custom photoresist. Panel a) presents an isometric 
view and panel b) presents cut-through layer by layer. 
 
With the finished printing operation, the user should exchange the holder and move the 
stage back to the lowest Z-drive limit, to safely unmount the sample holder. The printing cell 
is disassembled to receive the coverslip. The last step is to develop fabricated 
microstructures by getting rid of residual photoresists. The unmounted coverslip is 
submerged vertically into a PGMEA bath for 20 minutes, and then submerged again into 
IPA bath for additional 15 minutes, to ensure proper cleaning of the substrate. Otherwise, 
during the imaging of structures, there can be visible traces of resin, which make it difficult 
to properly characterize topology as shown in Figure 19.

4. Results and discussion. 
4.1 Shape and topography characterization. 
 
One of the main goals of the project was to fabricate stable microstructures serving  
as microrobots and cell scaffolds. It was achieved by several iterations of different parameter 
sweeps, examining the influence of structural voxel formation and dose on the stability  
of designed structures. Parameter sweep is a great printing parameter optimisation tool, 
allowing to quickly mass produce a matrix of microstructures and compare the result  
side-by-side with other structures in close vicinity. In an ideal world, when printing out the 
matrix of choice, the best-optimised result should gravitate toward the middle of the area, 
leaving the deformed structures at the limits of processing parameter values. Following 
chapters focus on finding the best processing parameter values for different microstructures 
as well as determining the possible printing limitations. Optimisation focused on finding the 
best Hatching Distance (HD), Laser Power (LP) and Scanning Speed (SS) values.  
 
4.1.1 Smallest size feature limit testing. 
 
As a starting point, a model described in Chapter 3.4.1 had been printed out using  
a simple parameter sweep between the object's voxel structure presented by HD versus 
exposure LP. Its purpose was to determine printing resolution and the limit on how small  
of a feature can be achieved using the 2PP technique for the used custom hydrogel resin. 
First iteration printing sweep job parameters included constant SD = 0.3 µm, HD ranging 
from 0.2 – 0.3 µm with 0.05 µm step, constant SS=10000 µm/s and LP ranging from  
60-100% with 5% step (from now on referred to as SP1). The resulting matrix is pictured in 
Figure 22. Obtained structures are stable most of the time, with well-established cylinder 
features on top of the cuboid and cut features inside the block across the sweep matrix. It is 
good and valuable information meaning that material can be successfully polymerised even 
with a small dose on the low-end spectrum. On the contrary, it may not be enough for 
creating fine features 
such 
as 
barely structured 
cylinders and holes with  
1 and 2 µm diameters. It is mainly visible at the cut-off of 75% LP level when structures 
below that are notably less sharp (red-frame). HD does not show a visible sign for this type 
of structure, probably due to the simple geometry and bulky nature of the structure.  
To improve the chances of creating well-established small features, another parameter sweep 
was programmed. This time exposure parameters were swiped by narrowing LP down, 
ranging from 75 – 100% with 5% step and SS ranging from 10000 – 7000 µm/s with  
SS = 0.3 µm and HD = 0.2 µm kept at constants (from now on referred to as SP2). LP was 
narrowed because structures below 75% LP did not provide sufficient results for printing 
small features.

4.1.5 Process parametrization summary. 
 
Previous chapters explored a variety of printing parameters and their influence on 
structural stability. Table 3 summarizes the optimal values for obtaining well-established 
objects depending on the type of source model. It is clearly visible, that each print job should 
be optimised for a given object since no universal set of parameters could work with the 
same satisfactory results across different structures. The only exception is cell scaffolds, 
which were printable with satisfactory results across wide parameter sweeps. One could 
argue that due to their simpler geometry, and lack of hanging structures, it was easier to

4.2 Shape change behaviour. 
 
Once optimal processing parameters were established and allowed for the constant 
reproduction of well-structured objects, the project entered into the vital stage of performing 
material actuation experiments. After producing several batches of microstructures, they 
would give sufficient information about their behaviour during the exposition. The material 
used throughout the project is anionic-type hydrogel based on acrylic acid, as described in 
Chapter 2.2.1. Therefore it is expected, that scrutinised samples will swell the most when 
exposed to high pH solutions, and shrink again when exposed to low pH values. For the 
purpose of the project, the swelling ratio will be defined as the ratio between the length of 
the solid hydrogel in the solvent over the length of the dry solid hydrogel. It was expected 
that due to the strain between the material and the coverslip interface caused by excessive 
volume expansion, microstructures would detach from the surface. Due to the liquid flow 
caused by sucking up the water using laboratory tissue, microstructures would move across 
the coverslip and flow away according to the flow direction. This phenomenon is shown in 
Figure 29. Optical imaging of swollen microstructures detaching submerged in pH~13 
solution. Structures visibly move along the flow of the liquid from a) to c). Based on that it 
was decided to refer to the size average calculated by taking independent measurements from 
each object as a base for quantifying the swelling ratio. 
 
Figure 29. Optical imaging of swollen microstructures detaching submerged in pH~13 solution. Structures visibly move 
along the flow of the liquid from a) to c).

degree of swelling is vital. From the point of device design, it is important if the material 
behaves in a similar manner across different object sizes. The geometry of the structure can 
be a limiting factor for successful application. The first set of pH actuation experiments 
focuses on the examination of swelling across different-sized objects. A set of 3 x 3 arrays 
composed of cylinders with radius 5, 12.5, 25, 37.5 and 50 μm and cuboids with sides 10 25, 
50, 75 and 100 μm were printed out and prepared for the actuation test. Processing

function. An acidic environment can become a good switch for the material to deactivate it, 
but in the perspective of this particular sensing microrobots, that feature might not prove 
useful. Microrobots created with this material will be applicable for detecting high pH 
substances, but will not be of service for detecting neutral and lower pH levels, as clear 
differentiation between those two conditions cannot be found.

4.2.6 Material characterization challenges. 
 
The way characterization experiments were conducted brought up a number  
of challenges, that could not simply be resolved. One of the major ones was hydrogel objects 
detaching from the substrate, which was briefly mentioned at the beginning of Chapter 4.2. 
After a full cycle of liquid exposition, the remaining dry coverslip usually looked like the 
features presented in Figure 39. chaotic and disorganised. 
 
Figure 39. Optical microscopy image showcasing example of disorganised microstructures after full actuation cycle. 
It was not rare for the structures to start bending or sticking to each other throughout the 
process, as shown in Figure 40 panels a) and b). An additional hindrance was trying to focus 
sharply through the liquid drop. Oftentimes it led to slightly distorted imaging on very high 
magnifications, which made it harder to precisely determine the change in microstructure 
key dimensions, which could previously be observed in Figure (4.2) and Figure 39 panel c).   
==PAGE:51==
51 
 
 
 
Figure 40. Optical microscope imaging of a) structures bent upon actuation and b) structures that stuck together after 
detaching from the surface. 
 
The whole actuation process also was not ideal, since objects reverted back to their 
original size when aqueous solutions were no longer present. The main idea behind swelling 
experiments was to see, whether a material will exhibit switch-like behaviour when going 
back and forth between low pH~2 and high pH~13, which could not be examined 
in the set-up that was used. The plan to improve the characterization would be to apply 
microstructures directly into a closed aqueous environment with elements capable of titrating 
buffer solutions inside and a pH sensor to validate the change of H+ ion concentration. This 
way, one could achieve more precise results in an environment closer to the end-goal 
application, without compromising with rehydration from a dry state. In addition, if the 
microscope was already submerged into the liquid cell, the problems with liquid drop 
causing loss of focus would disappear. Available microfluidic systems might prove useful 
for further study of this hydrogel material.

5. Conclusion and future outline. 
 
The research conducted for the purpose of this project aimed at the validation  
of homemade hydrogel photoresist as a material suitable for 2PP 4D printing. In order to 
achieve it, a customised printing cell was employed, to counteract monomer volatilization 
limitation caused by the addition of acrylic acid. The formulation was deemed successful for 
fabricating complex microstructures, designed beforehand for the purpose of microrobotic 
sensing devices and biomimicking cell scaffolds for drug delivery studies.  
The topology of obtained microstructures was examined using SEM. On this basis,  
an attempt to further optimization of structures was taken. After performing several 
parameter sweep experiments, optimal parameters yielding the most stable structures were 
identified and used for the mass production of testing samples for the later part of the project. 
It was noted, that there is no universal set of parameters capable of flawless fabrication  
of several different models, and as such, an individual approach towards each design should 
be taken.  
Obtained hydrogel microstructures were exposed to different aqueous solutions with 
pH~2, pH~7 and pH~13 levels accordingly. Formulated material responded positively  
to external stimuli by swelling across the object’s dimensions. Since examined hydrogel was 
an anionic type, it was expected to swell the most in a very basic pH environment, which 
was confirmed across several independent experiments. In acidic and neutral pH 
environments, materially expressed similar behaviour. Most likely, dried-out hydrogel 
simply rehydrates in those conditions, without any significant electrostatic interactions 
taking place. However, an acidic stock solution might prove as a useful switch,  
for contracting already swollen structures from basic environments.  
Different object geometries reacted in a different manner upon actuation. It was 
observed that for simple shapes the smaller the object, the more it swells. Across that study, 
a linear model could be proposed, but when faced with even smaller, more geometrically 
complicated features, the swelling behaviour reaches extreme values outside of the model. 
It might be interesting to further pursue characterising swelling behaviour dependent on size 
and more intricate shape choices. This study might prove extremely useful for the creation 
of innovative micro-tools based on this particular formulation.  
Exposure dose on the material during fabrication was scrutinised to determine whether 
it has an influence on structure swelling behaviour. After examination of several independent 
objects, it was concluded that the higher the dose, the higher the swelling ratio of structures. 
It is important information at the design stage of microstructure devices, as it enables a more 
precise approach towards tool application during modelling. 
Due to hydrogel being a building material, one-part hydrogel microrobots are easy  
to detach from the surface upon actuation. This phenomenon makes them easy to harvest 
using simple methods, like sucking them up using the syringe, and directly placing them into 
the target environment area. It holds a practical benefit at the application stage. 
==PAGE:53==
53 
 
 
If larger device stability is necessary, a microrobot can be made using two separate 
materials. A harder backbone composed of different polymers and sensing elements based 
on the hydrogel can be successfully fabricated. This way microrobot is more resilient  
to damage while maintaining its functionality. Because the backbone structure does not 
swell, and hence does not detach from the substrate upon actuation, it is necessary to propose  
a reliable harvesting procedure for collecting the microdevices. On the other hand, it might 
prove useful at the step of optical trapping, as the laser beams manipulating the spheres will 
not have to be adjusted based on the swelling factor.  
Hydrogel biomimicking cell scaffolds are well replicated based on provided models. 
Similarly to other structures, they express swelling behaviour and can be successfully used 
in further biomedical research. 
Performed work remains open-ended with a lot of additional areas to explore. Hydrogels 
are isometric materials, but for the purpose of this project and experienced limitations, they 
were only examined in two dimensions. It would be very interesting to investigate the 
behaviour across objects' Z-axis as well for a full picture of expressed shape change 
behaviour. Two additional parameters that should be characterised in regard to the used 
hydrogel formulation are shaped fixity and shape recovery ratios. It could be achieved by 
simply increasing the number of repetitions across independent exposure cycle experiments. 
The original idea to do so was abandoned due to difficulties in regards to capturing objects 
moving inside the drop of liquid across the coverslip. Hydrogels are known to be responsive 
to the temperature as well. This project did not explore this area, but it might be noteworthy 
for future perspective. Lastly, no mechanical properties were measured and creating 
experimental setup capable of determining simple parameters. 
2PP technique of microfabrication proved to be effective in fabricating complex 3D 
geometries, which was often a struggle for creating intricate hydrogel-based devices. 2PP 
and examined smart material systems combined, can significantly advance the field of 4D 
printing, opening new possibilities in not only what can we create, but also how can we use 
it for highly specialised applications. It is an exciting field with a lot of opportunities to 
change our lives for the better, that are yet to be discovered.

D. Project plan 
 
4D printing of soft responsive polymer structures 
Master thesis project plan, Marcin Piotr Piekarczyk (s213037) 
 
Planned activities 
• 
Literature Studies 
A thorough literature study concerning the subject will be carried out. It will assume 
understanding and application of concepts regarding microrobotic design and structure, 
additive manufacturing – precisely direct laser writing method, using two-photon-
polymerization (2PP) phenomenon, as well as background knowledge of stimuli-
responsive smart materials, to perform shape programming discussed below. Based on 
the performed literature study, a range of suitable materials will be selected to carry out 
the project work. 
A thorough literature study was prepared and presented in Chapter 2.  
 
• 
Model design 
A range of 3D models will be designed using conventional CAD software in order to 
fabricate desired microstructures suitable for 4D printing. In order to achieve a 
satisfactory geometry of the models and later on optimize the structures into working 
microrobots, several crash test samples are expected to be fabricated in cycles. 
A variety of 3D models were prepared and presented in Chapter 3.5. A sample model 
for testing minimal printable features was introduced in Chapter 3.5.1 and used as a 
crash test sample. 
 
• 
Model fabrication  
Designed models exported to .stl file format will be later converted using DeScribe 
software to a code understandable by the printer. Fabrication will be carried out using 
the Nanoscribe Photonic Professional GT+ operated with NanoWrite software. The 
process will be optimized for the best results as necessary. 
Prepared 3D models were successfully fabricated using Nanoscribe Photonic 
Professional GT+. Microstructure fabrication was optimised in terms of structural and 
dose parameters by carrying out a number of parameter sweep experiments. Results were 
discussed in Chapter 4.1 and consolidated in Chapter 4.1.5. 
 
==PAGE:84==
84 
 
 
• 
Model characterization 
Fabricated microstructures are to be examined using optical microscopy for preliminary 
assessment, and later on for details on obtained topography. 
Fabricated models were successfully characterised using optical microscopy and SEM 
microscopy to obtain a better view of microstructure topology. Results were discussed 
in Chapter 4.1 
 
• 
Shape programming and characterisation 
Obtained structures will be exposed to aqueous solutions with varied pH levels to 
determine their response toward stimuli. Exposed structures will be characterized using 
optical microscopy to determine the change in their dimensions, which will provide 
insight into their behaviour as well as possible applications for microrobotics. The goal 
of the master project would be to successfully characterize the shape-changing properties 
of fabricated structures depending on different pH levels. This could ultimately lead to 
the successful fabrication of a microrobot to perform a simple task that goes beyond the 
scope of this project. 
A set of manufactured microstructures was exposed to different pH-levelled aqueous 
environments and examined life using optical microscopy. Results provided insight into 
material behaviour and can be applied to later stages of smart micro robotic device 
design. Discussion on the topic was held in Chapter 4.2. 
 
• 
Imaging treatment 
Microscopic images involving fabricated structures will be scrutinized in terms of model 
shape and structure. Based on the imaging, precise measurements of the structure's 
dimensions before and after shape programming will be taken. The findings will be 
presented in an appropriate manner, highlighting the process behind reaching the set 
goal. 
Hydrogel microstructures were measured in terms of their key dimensions. Material 
behaviour was captured on figures presented in Chapter 4.2. 
 
• 
Stimuli-responsive data treatment 
Obtained measurements will be used to calculate the degree of mechanical deformation 
observed on the structures. 
Obtained measurements were processed to determine the swelling behaviour of the 
hydrogel microstructure's key dimensions. Results were organised into plots and tables 
presented in Chapter 4.2 and Appx. D. 
==PAGE:85==
85 
 
 
• 
Thesis writing 
The thesis will be written in parallel to project work, after accumulating enough research 
study as well as obtaining data. In an ideal world, theory background will be done in a 
month's time, followed by another month's worth of work to describe the experiment and 
discuss the results. Last month is reserved for minor corrections of the contents.  
The original writing plan was modified. A decision to fully commit to experimental work 
was made and the thesis was written in bulk after acquiring all of the necessary data. 
 
• 
Sideshow preparation 
Based on carried out tasks a defence presentation will be composed where obtained 
results will be shared and discussed over. 
The defence presentation was made on time. 
 
Milestones 
1. Gathering enough literature background and sorting it in an organised manner.  
Milestone has been met on time. Necessary literature was gathered and used as a 
foundation of the theory chapter.  
 
2. Determining ideal geometry for following microstructures, based on performed crash 
test samples. 
 
Milestone has been met on time in a slightly modified version. Due to the freedom of 
design, it was decided that the smartest approach would be to determine the limit on how 
small of a featured user can obtain in regard to different process parameters. Obtained 
information laid a solid foundation for further designs with the custom-made hydrogel 
photoresist. 
 
3. The theoretical background chapter of the thesis is finished. 
Milestone has not been met on time. During that period intensified efforts in regard to 
laboratory work were necessary and required full focus on the performed operations, to 
produce a sufficient amount of data. The theoretical background was written in bulk with 
the rest of the thesis. 
 
 
==PAGE:86==
86 
 
 
4. Experiments in the laboratory are finished. 
Milestone has been met two weeks before the planned date. Postponing thesis writing 
allowed for intensified effort in executing planned experiments, which gave a necessary 
peace of mind.  
5. The bulk of the thesis is written – only corrections remain. 
Milestone has been met on time. The thesis was almost complete at this point and entered 
the correction phase. 
6. Handing of the final thesis. The defence presentation is ready. 
Milestone has been met on time. The thesis was uploaded to the internal university system 
and results were presented and explained in a public defence.
