Abstract
The ever increasing number of interconnected devices proves that the Internet of Things
(IoT) era is here and expanding. More and more technologies are implemented in order to
ensure communication between devices. For example, the Low-Power Wide-Area Network
(LPWAN) technologies are used to cover long distances for transmitting data. Long Range
Wide Area Networks (LoRaWAN) and Sigfox, designed to support long ranges, transmit
low bit rates and require low power between devices in the network. The aim of this thesis
is to show how these technologies can be used as a method of reliability, in case of failures
such as disconnection from a base station. The next chapters will describe the reasons why
LPWAN technology should be taken into consideration when designing a network, what are
their capabilities and their limitations.
This thesis provides a theoretical evaluation of LPWAN technologies, LoRa and Sigfox in
particular. It also describes the encryption mechanisms and the importance of using security
algorithms to protect data that is being enhanced.
After choosing the appropriate technology, extensive experiments can be performed to eval-
uate the performances and show if it can be applied to real situations.
Security, power
consumption, data transmission and costs are factors that must be taken into account when
defining a network that could be used by the average consumer.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Wireless technologies have become a part of our lives, by giving us the means to commu-
nicate with people regardless of their location. The rapid growth of wireless networks and
increased affordability in the past two decades has changed the way people interact with
each another. Every day, more and more users connect their devices to the Internet. Some
well known examples of wireless technologies used to connect and collect data are WiFi, 5G
and Bluetooth.
The concept of connecting devices to the Internet and perpetually transmitting data is known
as IoT. The constant reduction in components size and increased capabilities represents one of
the key enablers for the expansion of IoT [1]. Another factor that led to this expansion is the
sustained innovation in wireless technologies, resulting in a variety of ways to connect devices
to the Internet. Each of these technologies can enable devices to communicate wireless,
given the hardware capabilities installed. However, each wireless method has advantages
and disadvantages that must taken into consideration, according to the situation. Cost,
power consumption, maintenance, bit rate and range are essential when deploying a wireless
communication method.
1.2
LPWAN Technologies
LPWAN technologies are best suited for long range communications. These wireless com-
munication methods are used between devices that can transmit low bit rates of information
over large areas. They are ideal for public use because they are operating on unlicensed fre-
quency bands. The requirements for these protocols are low power consumption, resulting in
long time usage on battery power and long ranges to transmit to installations put in remote
areas. The most popular LPWAN Technologies are LoRaWAN and SigFox.
1.2.1
LoRaWAN
Long Range (LoRa) technology consists of two parts: LoRa and LoRaWAN modulation.
One represents the physical layer in the OSI model, and the other is a network architecture
[2].
The LoRa Alliance, maintains THE LoRaWAN infrastructure, offering certification
procedures in order to ensure compatibility [3]. Devices can communicate through gateways
directly to the Internet or to other devices. The user can provide a LoRa node and a gateway,
provided that these are registered on an official website, thus expanding the coverage provided
by LoRa devices.
1.2.2
SigFox
Created in 2009, SigFox operates in the unlicensed sub-GHz bans of 868 MHz, 902 MHz
and 433 MHz in Europe, North America and Asia. Employing UNB modulation, SigFox is
capable of sending 12 bytes payload every 6 seconds, but due to regulations, it allows users

==PAGE:11==
only 140 messages in one day [3]. Messages are transmitted to a gateway connected to a
SigFox server.
1.2.3
D2D Connectivity
These technologies help establish a device-to-device (D2D) connectivity, making it possible
to send data from one UE to another without being in close proximity. The D2D technique
enables the realization of ad-hoc mesh networks. It was first introduced in the 3rd Genera-
tion Partnership Project (3GPP) community as a competitive technology for public safety
networks (used especially during cellular coverage issues) [4]. The main advantages of using
D2D are the enhancing network performance and enabling new proximity services in the
area. It can help improve spectrum efficiency, reduce delays, and extend the network cov-
erage [4]. The emergence of the fifth-generation technology standard for broadband cellular
networks (5G) has pushed for revisiting D2D communications, with new research trends in
Vehicle-to-Vehicle (V2V) communications, UE-to-Network relaying for IoT and millimetre
wave technology [4].
1.3
Motivation
The spectrum is getting increasingly saturated with the rising number of IoT devices. This
leads to interferences and queues that would disturb transmitting data under normal circum-
stances. Reliability is a crucial factor when designing a network architecture, thus it must
be taken into consideration when creating one. In case of a failure, a 1+1 method is ideal in
order to keep the infrastructure working. For 1:1 method, each span would have a backup
capable of carrying the data. In case of a communication failure between the two devices,
caused by damage to one base station, the system could simply switch to the backup and
continue the traffic while the main path is be restored.

2
LPWAN
The expansion of interconnected devices has led to an increased need of alternative technolo-
gies in telecommunication. The existing protocols become less and less sustainable in regard
to coverage, power consumption and cost. Due to the increase of devices being deployed,
in terms of transmitting data in passive nodes, battery life is becoming more important in
multiple applications scenarios [6]. If carefully designed, the power usage can be extended
in battery lifetime. Considering all the above-mentioned characteristics, a transmission of
information at the cost of low power usage can be obtained by using a LPWAN technology.
LPWANs have been designed to offer low power and low data rate communication over wide
range covered areas. Communication, management, and deployment costs are essential is-
sues that must be taken into consideration when designing a LPWAN network. Nodes need
to be cheap, have a lifetime of up to ten years when using a battery and a distance between
the nodes exceeding ten kilometres [7]. Providing wide area ccoverage should not come at
the expense of signal transmission, even in harsh environments. Using sub-GHz band for
end devices, a reliable communication can be established while maintaining a low budget
consumption. Low frequency signals have the advantage of being more robust to multi-way
fading, while penetrating obstacles. In addition, because the more popular wireless tech-
nologies like Wi-Fi, Bluetooth and ZigBee use 2.4 GHz, these frequencies are less crowded
[3]. Figure 2 shows how various technologies are categorized according to the range coverage
on which they operate.
Figure 2: Classification of wireless technologies based on their
operating range-coverage [8].

Wireless technologies like ZigBee and Bluetooth are classified as Wireless Personal Area
Network (WPAN), WiFi as Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN) and cellular and LPWAN
as Wireless Wide Area Network (WWAN) [8].
One of the main factors taken into consideration when using a LPWAN technology is cost
effectiveness, allowing it to compete with cellular networks. As seen in figure 3, LPWAN
technologies are designed for features that complement and in some scenarios, supersede
cellular and short range wireless communication methods.

terms of their bandwidth, range, energy consumption and

in unlicensed frequency bands.

depending on its capacity, communications networks such as LPWAN are implemented by
reducing the bandwidth of the channel, which varies inversely as the radius of communication.
LPWAN protocols adopt a robust modulation and one-way communication protocols that
ensure low power consumption. In short, the basic concept of LPWANs is based on capitaliz-
ing on the low bit rate of IoT application requirements to increase communication range. In
other words, it involves reducing the modulation rate, which implicitly increases the receiver
sensitivity by putting more energy into each symbol transmitted. This allows a receiver to
decode strongly attenuated signals.
Two of the more popular technologies are SigFox and LoRa.
2.1
SigFox
SigFox (created in 2010) is a LPWAN technology that focuses on connecting devices which
use low amounts of power, such as sensors, smartwatches and health monitors. It uses Ultra
Narrow Band modulation (UNB). UNB can send 12 bytes of payload in 6 seconds using a
band of 100 Hz [10].
In Sigfox, communication is established by using Differential Binary Phase-Shift Keying (D-
BPSK) modulation scheme in an Ultra Narrow Band (UNB) spectrum. D-BPSK increases
the uplink range and the power received offers good protection against interferences by
concentrating into a very narrow bandwidth [11]. As showed in figure 4, the phase of a
carrier signal is varied to a logical one or zero, representing instantaneous changes in phase
and thus, the capability to filter signals with a narrow bandwidth and an increase in receiver
sensitivity.
Figure 4: D-BPSK Modulation
SigFox allows the users to send up to 140 messages a day, due to regulations of the unlicensed
spectrum band frequency. The sent messages are received by a gateway connected to the
SigFox server, providing access to other SigFox nodes that require to see the information.

transmission frequencies. For each packet, three copies are sent at three different frequencies

SigFox allows the users to send 4 messages per day from the network to a device.
Figure 5 represents the downlink and uplink frames, where message exchanges are unidirec-
tional or bidirectional over unlicensed spectrum. In Europe, the bands are between 868.00 -
868.60 MHz and 869.40 - 869.65 MHz and in the USA, the 902 MHz band is used [11].

Because the devices are not bound to a base station in particular, signal association is not
needed. The BSs are connected through the public Internet with a single cloud-based core

SigFox has become one of the main LPWAN technologies, growing into a rival for LoRa
technology.
2.2
LoRa
When describing LPWAN technologies, one technology has gained traction in the recent
years. LoRa refers to the physical layer of the LPWAN technology and stands for Long
Range. Developed by Semtech, LoRa modulation technologies represent a method through
which devices communicate over long ranges. Compared to WPAN technologies, they use
protocols which enable the creation of large coverage area IoT networks that can be used
anywhere [13]. As seen in figure 7, the architecture of LoRa is composed of a user equipment
being a node and a gateway through which LoRa nodes communicates to the network. The
network server decodes the received data and sends it to the application layer where the
packet is visualized.
Page 8 of 68
==PAGE:18==
Figure 7: LoRa Architecture [14].
Providing low power and data speed communication over a wide coverage area, the physical
layer uses Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS) to allow links between devices at long distances.
Resistant to interference, CSS is suited for harsh and dense environments. The technique
of spreading a narrow band signal provides a two-way communication between devices. The
chirp signals are generated by transmitters with different frequencies over time without
changing the phase between the symbols.
The CSS technique ensures phase continuity
between different chirp symbols in the Open Systems Interconnection (OSI) physical layer,
allowing for the payload of each transmission to range between 2 to 255 octets [15]. The
analyse in paper "Chirp Spread Spectrum Toward the Nyquist Signaling RateâOrthogonality
Condition and Applications" illustrates how the gains of CSS can be set independently
with the sum of orthogonal chirps and show how orthogonal CSS are flexible for various
applications.
Spread spectrum methods are techniques in which a signal is deliberately
spread in frequency domain [13].
Figure 8: CSS Amplitude and Frequency Modulation [14]
A more comprehensive work on chirp spread spectrum can be found in Matt Knight’s work
"Reversing LoRa" [16].

2.2.1
Long Range Capabilities
Both LoRa and Sigfox have wide coverage capabilities. These features lie in the modulation
techniques of each technology as well as operating frequencies implemented at the physical
layers. The goal of these two technologies is to achieve a Link Budget while transmitting data
at long distances. In communication systems, a Link Budget is a numerical representation
of all the gains and losses in a communication channel from the transmitter to the receiver.
This Link Budget can be expressed as:

(2)

𝐺𝑆𝑌𝑆𝑇𝐸𝑀- system gains such as those associated with directional antennas
𝐿𝑆𝑌𝑆𝑇𝐸𝑀- system losses

The Link Budget results from the radiated power minus the sensitivity of the receiver and
all the losses [17].
The Link Budget decreases with an increase in channel losses. For a successful transmission,

Due to their transmission frequencies, LoRa and Sigfox are categorised as LPWAN technolo-

and reliable communication channel at low power and low costs, compared to a cellular
infrastructure. The sub-1GHz bands used are unlicensed frequency bands, implying the sig-

China = 470-510 MHz
Korea = 920- 925 MHz
Japan = 920-925 MHz

Sub-1GHz bands are subject to regulations on radio emission, thus radio transceivers must
adopt a Listen-Before-Talk Policy or Duty Cycled transmission. This is used to optimize

A LoRa architecture can consist of multiple nodes installed by users and gateways connected
to a LoRa server, where information can be stored and transmitted. Illustrated by TCP/IP
communication with gateways and end nodes, network servers are application servers that
operate across the transport layer. As mentioned before, LoRa stands for the physical layer
working on the first layer (L1), with the primary function of sending Application layer data
to the medium [21]. Using CSS modulation, LoRa encodes symbols into multiple signals
of up-chirp and down-chirp frequencies with a constant chirp rate [22]. A LoRa frame is
initialized by a long constant-chirp preamble, which is used by the receiver to lock the LoRa
signal [22]. The preamble is then followed by two reverse-chirps used for synchronization,
thus indicating the end of the preamble.
Because there is no discrepancy between two
preamble signals from two different LoRa nodes, the receiver may listen to an undesired
signal. Therefore, the detection of a preamble is crucial in the reception of a LoRa frame.
LoRa transmissions can be characterized by key parameters [22]:
BW - Bandwidth is responsible for defining the width of the radio frequency that is being
used in the transmission band. An increase in the BW results in higher effective
data rate, reduced time-on-air cost and reduced receiver sensitivity due to noise
accumulation. A decrease in BW requires more accurate quartz crystal oscillators in
order to achieve higher sensitivity, thus increasing costs [22].
CF - Carrier Frequency defines the frequency of the medium used in LoRa transmissions.
Several LoRa chipsets support frequency bands from 137 MHz to 1020 Hz [23].
CR - Coding Rate characterizes the FEC rate used by the LoRa node to shield against
interference and can be configured to 4/5, 4/6, 4/7, 4/8. The higher the CR is set,
the better the protection offered is at the cost of time-on-air.

3.2
LoRaWAN
If LoRa is the physical layer that uses CSS, then LoRaWan radio modulation technology is
the MAC protocol using unlicensed frequencies to transmit messages. The LoRa Alliance is
designing the MAC protocol for LoRa. Their aim is to provide interoperability among LoRa
installations and establish a standardization of LPWAN technologies [13].It is intended to
offer expediency over battery life, capacity in the network and freedom for users to develop
IoT applications [32]. Introduced back in 2015, the LoRaWAN MAC protocol is an open
source protocol managed by LoRa Alliance [13], running on the LoRa physical layer. The
LoRaWAN protocol provides the mechanisms that enable communication between devices
and gateways in the LoRa network.
As specified earlier, the architecture consists of an
end device, a gateway and a network.
The data is transmitted from the user device to
the network through a gateway, which acts as a communication link between the two ends.
A single end device can be connected to multiple gateways, where the network makes the
data available to the device. Communication between end devices and gateways is made
over the LoRa Protocol and the communication between gateways and network is made
possible over TCP/IP, where the gateway has to be connected to the Internet through wired
or wireless methods (for example Ethernet or WiFi). LoRaWAN does not provide inter-
device communication, meaning data can only be send from one end device to another via
a gateway. All LoRaWAN devices implement at least a Class A functionality. In addition,
Class B and Class C type devices can be implemented [32], based on the need of usage.

LoRa protocols utilize PHY (Physical Layer ) headers for transmission and reception of data.
Two PHY formats exist, one for uplink and one for downlink. The difference between the
two is in the cyclic redundancy check. The uplink format is structured as follows: Preamble,
PHDR, PHDR-CRC, PHYPayload, CRC. The PHYPayload variable length is between 0 to
255 bytes. LoRaWAN’s MAC data messages are held within the PHYPayload of the LoRa
protocol. The structure of the PHY payload is as follows: MHDR, MACPayload, Message
Integrity Check (MIC). The MAC header and the MIC are fixed to a length of 1 octet and
4 octets [25]. The MACPayload however, is dynamic in size due to the data rate which is
in use. For each message that is transmitted in a LoRaWAN network in Europe, we have a
MHDR of 1 byte and a MIC of 4 bytes.

<25 percentage - Low utilization resulting in close to 0 packet loss.

> 50 percentage - This percentage can be caused by too many sent messages or noise
interference resulting in an increased number of packets being dropped.

4.5
Results
Testing the connectivity of the "Meshtastic" devices as well as their power consumption
resulted in a communication layer based on LPWAN Technology. By testing the average
energy consumption, it has resulted the nodes could work on average 3 days before in need
of recharge of the power supplier. Of course, a power bank can be replaced for example with
a solar panel and eliminate this problem all together by having the two sources connected.
The testing of the distance of course could be stretched over more kilometers. Because this
is LoRa Technology, from the theoretical material provided by Semtech, the distance can
be in the hundreds of kilometers as long as the nodes have a clear line of sight. The idea
of this project was to test a method of communication between devices where a cellular
network coverage would not be present. Of course, testing it during a hike on a mountain
or in a forest would make it ideal since there is little coverage from cellular network. For
example, in the forests and mountains of Romania, people realize when they go for a hike,
there is little to no coverage. In order to test the resiliency of the LoRa nodes, the tests were
done in the city where interferences can occur either from a technological factor or a human
factor. During the tests, some messages were not received. Once a period of wait in between
messages was established, there was no packet loss. The window of wait was used in order
to ensure a channel utilization below 25 percentage in order to prioritize messages and to
avoid any packet loss. The TTGO V1.0 reached a 40 percent of channel utilization due to
broadcasting its location on the GPS. The duty cycle is represented on the application by
AirUtilTX (TX Air Time Utilization). For all the experiments, this percentage has been
kept below 10, thus keeping the duty cycle withing the limitations imposed by IEEE.

5
Conclusion
The focus of this thesis has been to show the performance of LoRa technology and if it can
act as a reliability method for cellular network in an urban or rural environment.
The intent of this research was to define a decryption of LPWAN technologies and their
characteristics, as well as what makes them so appealing for market use. Comparison between
LPWAN technologies, specifically LoRa and Sigfox. Transmission parameters between the
two technologies are almost identical and choice goes down to the price of deployment. Of
course, because LoRa is easily available, this would be the technology used for the project.
A more detailed description of how LoRa and LoRaWAN networks work has been done. For
the project chosen, the technology used is LoRa due to its availability and flexible use, as well
as the cost of deployment and maintenance. The example chosen being the "Meshtastic"
application where a LoRa mesh network was created to transmit messages between two
parties. This would represent a good method of reliability for users in case of failure of
the cellular network or failure of power grid supplying for example WiFi routers in homes.
Of course, the aim of this project was to analyse a reliability method based on LPWAN
technology, proving that LoRa and "Meshtastic" can play a significant role in device-to-device
communication. There are other methods of ensuring reliability for cellular networks out
there. That being said, another method that can be used in order to ensure communication
between people which want to exchange messages. Of course, LPWAN technologies in general
should not be the defining "go to" technologies when designing a backup infrastructure for
IoT, but due to its cost in deployment, power and maintenance it is definitely a worthy
consideration as a reliable method.
For future works, the community of "Meshtastic" desires to implement a solution for the
nodes to function as gateways and access the LoRaWAN layer for a better way of commu-
nicating. Personally, I’m happy to assist with this project in any way I can since I can see
the potential in this technology.
