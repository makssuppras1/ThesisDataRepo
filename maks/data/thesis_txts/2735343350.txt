Abstract
The Islamic presence in the Iberian Peninsula, known as Al­Andalus, had a significant
impact on glaze manufacturing practices. This study focuses on the analysis of the glaze
of 18 ceramic fragments unearthed during archaeological excavations, applying archaeo­
metric methods to understand their material properties and shed light on the manufac­
turing techniques employed by the society of that time. These samples were retrieved
from galleries 4 and 5 of Palmela Castle and can be dated to the 8th to 11th centuries,
corresponding to the period of Islamic occupation in Palmela before the Reconquista.
To analyze these fragments, techniques such as Optical Microscopy (OM), Colourime­
try, X­ray fluorescence (XRF), and Scanning Electron Microscopy with Energy Dispersive
Spectrometry (SEM­EDS) were employed, all conducted without the need for sampling.

Introduction
1.1
Research Motivation
Al­Andalus refers to the territories in the Iberian Peninsula that were under Muslim rule
during the period of 711 AD and 1492 AD. It was marked by a blending of Muslim, Chris­
tian, and Jewish cultures, leading to advancements in various fields, including art and
ceramics. This presents a unique opportunity for scholarly exploration, particularly in the
realm of Islamic ceramics. This period, characterized by significant political and cultural
shifts, witnessed the evolution of ceramic art and technology, reflecting the dynamic in­
terplay of Eastern traditions and local craftsmanship [1][2].
Central to this research is the investigation of ceramic glazes from the Castle of Palmela.
The Castle of Palmela (Fig.1.1), strategically located between the estuaries of the Tagus
River and the Sado River, is a significant landmark that has witnessed the succession of
different civilizations, including the period of Al­Andalus [3][4]. This thesis focuses on an­
alyzing the composition, manufacturing techniques, and degradation processes of these
ceramics, utilizing modern analytical methods like XRF and SEM. This study aims to con­
tribute to the broader understanding of Islamic craftsmanship, its influence on subsequent
artistic expressions, and the technological skills of the period.
Figure 1.1: Castle of Palmela [5]
The compositional nuances and manufacturing techniques of these ceramic glazes can
provide insights into the material science and technical methods of the Al­Andalus era.
By adopting a multi­analytical approach, this research aims to analyze the details of these
artefacts and determine their composition and possible firing processes.

1.2
Aim and Objectives
Through the years there were several archaeological interventions on the different
premises of the castle. Throughout these works, it was possible to verify the long oc­
cupational history of the castle, with architectural evidence from different centuries, with
a large number of ceramic artefacts being recovered [6].
To gain insight into the ceramics’ origins, production methods, and cultural significance
from the Castle of Palmela, an array of advanced analytical techniques was employed,
including X­ray fluorescence (XRF), optical microscopy, colourimetry and scanning elec­
tron microscopy (SEM). Through the application of these complementary analytical tech­
niques, this master’s thesis aims to provide a comprehensive understanding of the ce­
ramics from the Castle of Palmela. This endeavour will significantly contribute to our ap­
preciation and knowledge of these artefacts’ cultural heritage and historical significance.
The main questions that this project aims to answer are:
• What are the primary elemental and mineralogical components of the glazes found
on the ceramics at the Castle of Palmela?
• What can the glazes of the ceramics indicate about their manufacturing processes?
• How can the data from the different analyses shed light on the firing temperatures
and techniques used in the production of these ceramics?
• Are there any discernible patterns or consistencies in the manufacturing techniques
used for the ceramics?
• How do the ceramics from the Castle of Palmela compare to other known ceram­
ics from the Al­Andalus period in terms of composition, style, and manufacturing
techniques?
• What similarities or differences can be discerned when comparing the ceramics from
the Castle of Palmela with those from other sites of the Al­Andalus period?

General Background
2.1
Historical Context
The Islamic period in the region historically known as Al­Andalus spans from 711 AD,
with the Umayyad conquest, to 1249 AD, after the end of the Portuguese Reconquista
(Fig.2.1). It is possible to distinguish separate moments during this long occupation period
[1] [2].
Figure 2.1: Portuguese ceramics under Muslim domain timeline [7]
Initial Conquest and Establishment (711–756 AD) Al­Andalus first fell under Islamic
control after the defeat of the Visigothic king Roderic at the Battle of Guadalete in 711 AD.
The initial phase of the conquest progressed rapidly, except in the northern mountainous
areas. This swift advancement highlighted the efficiency and organization of the Muslim
military forces. The early administrative structure aligned with the Umayyad Caliphate,
with Córdoba established as the capital, introduced new governance and with it, a rich
influx of cultural and artistic traditions from across the Muslim world [1] [2].
Umayyad Emirate (756–912 AD) The Umayyad Emirate, founded in 756 AD by Abd
al­Rahman I, established an independent state after the Abbasid revolution in the East.
This period witnessed the continuation of traditions established during the initial conquest,
with an emphasis on maintaining the legacy of the Umayyads [1][2].

==PAGE:12==
Caliphate of Córdoba (912–1031 AD) The proclamation of the Caliphate of Córdoba
by Abd al­Rahman III signals a golden age for Al­Andalus. This era was distinguished
by significant economic, cultural, and architectural development. Notably, it marked sub­
stantial innovation in ceramic glazing techniques, influenced by both Eastern traditions
and local craftsmanship. The Caliphate period also saw the flourishing of education, with
universities in Al­Andalus attracting Muslims and non­Muslims to come study [1] [2].
Taifas, Almoravids, and Almohads (1031–1238 AD) The disintegration of the Caliphate
led to Al­Andalus’s division into Taifas, small independent Muslim­ruled kingdoms. Each
Taifa was ruled by its leader and had its distinct cultural and political identity. Political
instability and conflicts made these kingdoms vulnerable to the North African Muslim dy­
nasties, first the Almoravids and later the Almohads, who both tried to unify the territory [1]
[2]. The ceramics of these periods continued to evolve, showcasing styles that mirrored
the changing political and cultural diversity under these two differing dynasties.
Ceramics were integral to daily life and artistic expression in Al­Andalus. The Islamic
influence persisted post­Muslim rule, evidenced by Hispano­Moresque ware (Fig. 2.2),
which fused Islamic motifs with European styles.

2.1.1
Castle of Palmela during the Islamic period
The Castle of Palmela, located in Setúbal (Fig.2.3), is historically significant due to its
strategic positioning that favoured both political and military purposes [4]. Constructed
during the Umayyad period in the 8th­9thcenturies, the castle witnessed the multifaceted
lives of its rulers and the communities that inhabited it. Archaeological evidence reveals
a multitude of structures and objects from living spaces, functional areas, and garrison
facilities, such as silos, channels, and cisterns. A vast assortment of ceramic artefacts

were recovered, including everyday items like pots, bowls, dishes, jugs, and mugs, as

the main fortifications. The fortified enclosure itself, built from scratch during the Umayyad

The castle’s chronology is further illuminated by historical accounts: after its construc­
tion by the Muslims, it was occupied by the troops of D. Afonso Henriques in 1147, only
to experience a series of conquests and reconquests. By 1205, after a period of destruc­
tion by the Moors, the castle underwent reconstruction, marking the onset of Christian
occupation. The rich archaeological findings underscore the castle’s significance during
its Islamic occupation from the 8th to 12th centuries [4].
2.2
Geological Context
The Castle of Palmela is located on the Arrábida Peninsula, which is situated along
the western coast of Portugal, in a midpoint position between the estuaries of the Tagus
and Sado rivers, as seen in the map in Fig. 2.4. The region exhibits a notable geomor­
phological diversity [10]. The northern and eastern plains belong to the Tertiary Basins
of the Tagus and Sado rivers, whereas the Arrábida mountain range, situated within the
Arrábida Natural Park, forms part of the Mesozoic Margins’ geomorphological zone [11].

More specifically, the castle is on the Palmela hill which is a distinctive feature within
the pre­Arrábida mountainous region. Standing around 237 meters high, the hill provides
superior defensive advantages due to its commanding view of the adjacent plains and
its challenging access routes from all directions except the north [12][13]. This strategic
positioning likely influenced earlier cultures, notably the Islamic communities of the time,
to select this site as an ideal location for fortification [4].
In the context of this study, which examines the glazes on Islamic period ceramics,
understanding the local soil types is critical to determine whether the materials for the
glazes were obtained locally or imported from elsewhere. The Arrábida Peninsula fea­
tures primarily two distinct soil compositions: the plains are covered by siliceous­clayey,
detrital soils, while the mountainous core is characterized by limestone soils [14]. The
predominant soil type across the peninsula is the former. Other minerals that might also
be relevant such as iron (Fe), copper (Cu), lead (Pb), zinc (Zn), arsenic (As), and anti­
mony (Sb) are known to be mined in the area around Montemor­o­Novo [15]. This implies
that the artisans of that era would have access to all the essential materials to create their
glazes in the areas surrounding Palmela.
2.3
History of Ceramics and Glazes ­ Glaze Process
The history of ceramics is as old as human civilization itself, with the earliest known
pottery dating back to the Neolithic period, around 7,000–6,000 BC [16]. Ceramics have
played an important role in the development of cultures around the world, serving not just
utilitarian purposes but also as objects of artistic expression.
As a material, ceramics are inorganic, non­metallic materials that are typically produced
by heating and subsequent cooling of materials with crystalline or partly crystalline struc­
tures. Ceramics are known for their high hardness, which is a result of their strong ionic
and/or covalent bonds. They have excellent compressive strengths but are often brittle,
making them susceptible to tensile stress and leading to brittle fracture. Ceramics have
8
Ceramics from the Castle of Palmela.
==PAGE:15==
high melting points and are stable at elevated temperatures, which makes them ideal for
high­temperature applications such as furnace linings, kiln furniture, and thermal barriers.
They generally have low thermal conductivity, contributing to their insulative properties.
Thermal expansion in ceramics must be carefully considered, as different expansion rates
can lead to thermal shock and cracking upon rapid temperature changes. The inertness
of ceramics makes them resistant to chemical attack and corrosion. This chemical stabil­
ity is due to the strong ionic and covalent bonds within the ceramic materials. As such,
ceramics are often used in applications involving harsh chemical environments [17]. The
combination of these properties is what makes ceramics so resistant to degradation and
one of the primary reasons ceramics are frequently retrieved in good condition from ar­
chaeological sites.
The first ceramics were made from clay and were fired on bonfires or pit fires. These
early objects were typically coarse and porous, used for storage, cooking, or as simple
drinking vessels. Traditional ceramic materials include clay minerals, silica (SiO2), and
feldspars (KAlSi3O8 – NaAlSi3O8 – CaAl2Si2O8). Clays are primarily composed of silicate
minerals mixed with varying amounts of water and organic impurities. Upon firing, the clay
particles are fused through a process known as sintering, which enhances the strength
and durability of the ceramic [16].
Later, the ceramics started to be coated with glazes with the earliest evidence of glazed
ceramic ware dating back to the 4th millennium BCE in Egypt and the Near East [18].
Glazes are a vitreous substance applied to the surface of a ceramic object. They serve
several functions: to provide a decorative finish, to create a non­porous layer, and to
enhance mechanical and chemical durability [19]. They are indicators of the technological
capabilities of a civilization, as well as the availability and exchange of raw materials.
Moreover, glaze compositions can reflect the trends and influences in art and culture,
providing insights into cross­cultural interactions and economic conditions [20].
Nowadays, a typical glaze is composed of a glass former, flux, and stabilizer. Silicon
dioxide is a common glass former, while fluxes, such as sodium carbonate (Na2CO3) or
potassium carbonate (K2CO3), lower the melting point. Stabilizers like alumina (Al2O3)
are added to prevent the glaze from crystallizing and to increase its viscosity [16].
In the era of Islamic ceramics, materials like sand or finely milled quartz served as the
primary silica contributors for glaze creation [21]. The glaze constituents are then finely
ground to ensure uniformity and then mixed with water, forming a suspension. This sus­
pension is uniformly applied to the surface of bisque­fired ceramics, either by immersion,
brushing, or pouring. Upon drying, the water component of the slip evaporates, leaving a
layer of the glaze material on the ceramic substrate. After application, the ceramics un­
dergo a second firing in a kiln, where temperatures are managed to facilitate the melting
of the glaze ingredients. This stage is critical, as the glaze needs to melt into a glass­like
coating without over­melting or under­firing. The kiln’s atmosphere — whether oxidizing
or reducing — is another variable that influences the final appearance of the glaze. Upon
completion of the firing cycle, a slow and controlled cooling phase ensues to prevent
the formation of cracks due to thermal stress, guaranteeing the structural integrity and
longevity of the glaze [22]. The mastery of these techniques is evident in the enduring
brilliance and diversity of glazes from the Islamic period, which remains a testament to
the period’s advanced ceramic technology.

Over time, the technology spread and diversified, with notable advancements such
as the development of tin­opacified glazing (the forerunner to the opaque white glaze)
in Islamic ceramics of the 8th century and the subsequent emergence of lusterware, a
technique that added metallic sheen to ceramics, becoming prominent in the 9th century,
which were unprecedented in the European context before this period [20]. Because
of this, glaze composition is also critical for determining the chronological placement of
ceramic pieces. Since glaze technologies evolved, the identification of specific glaze
types can often be correlated with particular periods.
The introduction of new glazing techniques, particularly tin­glazing, was a revolutionary
development attributed to Islamic potters [20]. This technique involved the application
of a lead­based glaze mixed with tin oxide to create a white, opaque surface upon which
colourful motifs could be painted [20]. The lusterware technique involved a complex multi­
firing process that resulted in a shimmering, metallic finish, demonstrating the potters’ so­
phisticated understanding of chemical transformations at high temperatures [16]. Islamic
ceramics in the Iberian Peninsula were often adorned with intricate geometric patterns,
arabesques, and calligraphy [23].
The colour of ceramic glazes is primarily determined by transition metal oxides. These
oxides can act as colourants, producing a wide range of hues [24]:
Oxides and Ceramic Colors
• Iron oxide (FeO) can give a yellow, red, brown, or green colour, depending on its
oxidation state and the firing atmosphere.
• Copper oxide (CuO) produces greens in an oxygen­rich environment and reds in a
reducing atmosphere.
• Cobalt oxide (CoO) is used for blue colours, being one of the most stable colourants.
• Chromium oxide (Cr2O3) provides a stable green colour.
• Manganese dioxide (MnO2) can produce purples and browns.
• Titanium dioxide (TiO2) is used to alter the colour effects of other oxides and can
produce a range of colours depending on particle size and concentration.
From an engineering perspective, the formation of ceramics is crucial to their final prop­
erties.
During the Al­Andalus period, certain forming processes were prevalent such
as[25]:
• Hand­building: An artisanal technique where the ceramic body is shaped by hand.
• Wheel­throwing: The use of a rotating wheel to form symmetric ceramic pieces
was another technique likely employed during this period [26].
With technological advancements, new forming processes emerged, expanding the
possibilities of ceramic design and functionality. These can include [25]:
• Slip Casting: A method where a liquid clay body slip is poured into moulds, allowing
for more intricate designs and consistent reproductions.
• Extrusion: The forcing of clay through a mould to create long shapes with uniform
cross­sections.
• Pressing: Using a press to form ceramic shapes from powders or granules.

• Injection Moulding: A method for forming complex shapes by injecting a viscous
clay body into moulds.
Each of these processes, both historical and modern, is chosen based on the desired
characteristics of the final product, including shape, size, and physical properties. The
evolution from handcrafted techniques to more industrialized methods illustrates the dy­
namic nature of ceramic production, reflecting changes in technology, artistic trends, and
cultural influences over time.
In modern times, ceramics exhibit remarkable potential due to their versatile properties,
which are now being used in cutting­edge applications. Advanced ceramics are essential
in the aerospace industry for their thermal resistance, serving as heat shields on space
shuttles [27]. In the field of electronics, ceramics form substrates and insulating bodies
in circuitry due to their excellent electrical characteristics [28]. Bioceramics are revolu­
tionizing medical implants as they bond readily with bone, leading to advancements in
orthopaedics [29]. These examples can illustrate the growing potential of ceramics as a
base of technological innovation across various sectors.
2.4
Archaeometric Studies of Ancient Ceramics
2.4.1
Introduction to Archaeometry
Interdisciplinary approaches, especially the use of archaeology, materials science, and
cultural history, have become invaluable in the study of historical artefacts. By integrat­
ing these disciplines, researchers gain a richer and better understanding of an artefact’s
journey. The integration of these fields allows for a multi­dimensional perspective. For
example, while archaeology might shed light on where and how an object was utilized,
materials science can delve into the specifics of its composition, helping reveal its ori­
gin and the techniques used in its creation. At the same time, cultural history provides
a broader context, detailing the object’s significance within a society, and its symbolic
meaning [30].
However, this approach is not without its challenges. Merging disciplines means com­
bining methodologies, terminologies, and paradigms that were developed independently
of each other. The formation of the CIGA – Cerâmica Islâmica do Garb Al­Andalus (Is­
lamic Ceramics form the Gharb Al­Andalus) research group represent a significant ini­
tiative in addressing the stagnation in the study of Islamic ceramics from the Al­Andalus
period in Portugal. This collective, comprised of researchers specializing in Islamic ce­
ramics, was established in response to the observed need for revitalized research in this
area. Their efforts are crucial in standardizing ceramic terminologies and methodologies,
thereby ensuring consistency and uniformity in the examination of ceramics from this his­
torical era. Despite this group’s primary focus being within the archaeological research
field, this standardization is particularly beneficial because it provides a common language
that facilitates clearer communication, enhances collaborative research, and allows for the
cumulative building of knowledge within the different scientific communities [31].
Archaeometry, often referred to as archaeological science, is the application of scientific
techniques to the analysis of archaeological materials. It is a scientific field that combines
archaeology, chemistry, physics, and biology, aimed at answering questions about the
past through the study of material remains [32]. This discipline uses a multitude of an­
alytical methods to characterize the composition, structure, and properties of artefacts,

==PAGE:18==
contributing to the clarification of ancient manufacturing processes, trade networks, and
technological advancements [33].
The evolution of archaeometry can be traced back to the early 20th century, with a
notable rise in scientific interest in archaeological questions [32]. The initial phase was
characterized by the application of simple chemical and petrographic techniques [34].
However, the real transformation in the field began with the development of radiocarbon
dating by Willard Libby in the late 1940s, which revolutionized the ability to place artefacts
and sites in an absolute chronological framework [35]. The establishment of dedicated re­
search centres and journals such as Archaeometry in 1958 marked the formal recognition
of archaeometry as a distinct field of study [36].
As the field has evolved, so have its techniques, expanding from basic chemical analy­
sis to include methods that interact with the fundamental particles of matter. The introduc­
tion of more sophisticated techniques, like neutron activation analysis (NAA) in the 1950s
and its wide adoption in the following decades, expanded the field’s capabilities. The lat­
ter part of the 20th century saw the proliferation of advanced methods such as XRF and
SEM­EDS, which are now commonplace in archaeometric studies [32]. These methods
have significantly enhanced the non­destructive analysis of artefacts, preserving them for
future study and public display [37].
Today, archaeometry continues to evolve with the improvement of new technologies
and methodologies, such as isotopic analysis and digital imaging techniques [32]. These
advancements continually refine our understanding of the past, granting us unprece­
dented insights into the material culture of ancient societies. In ceramic analysis, ar­
chaeometry can reveal not only the composition and source of the clay and glazes used
but also the techniques and temperatures at which the ceramics were fired [30]. By exam­
ining the physical and chemical properties of artefacts like ceramic glazes, we can glean
information about aesthetic choices, technological capabilities, and resource access of
earlier civilizations [20]. Such studies also play a pivotal role in cultural heritage conser­
vation, guiding the preservation and restoration of artefacts through an understanding of
their original construction methods [38]. This is critical for ensuring appropriate care and
environmental conditions. Additionally, the provenance of ceramics revealed through the
composition of clay bodies and glazes, uncovers historical production centres and trade
networks [39].
2.4.2
Analytical Techniques for the Study of Ceramic Glazes
Archaeological research needs to navigate the challenge of extracting data from arte­
facts without compromising their integrity. Methods such as X­ray Fluorescence (XRF)
have become essential in reconciling the need for material preservation with the necessity
of retrieving the needed information. The study of 17th­18th­century tiles from a mediae­
val hermitage in Valencia illustrates this dual advantage [40]. By employing XRF and
XRD, the research team could delve deep into the composition and manufacturing tech­
niques of the tiles without compromising their structural integrity [40]. This ensures that
the artefacts remain intact for future generations, all while providing current researchers
with invaluable data about their historical context [37].
While non­destructive techniques have brought significant advancements to archae­
ological research, they are not devoid of limitations. The resolution, accuracy, and the
specific type of information that can be extracted vary with each method. For instance,

while XRF offers insights into the elemental composition of ceramic artefacts, it might
not provide in­depth details about the crystallographic structure, a domain where XRD

In terms of technological insights, the analysis of glaze can reveal the firing temper­
atures achieved by ancient kilns [41]. It also uncovers the raw materials used in glaze
production, which can be traced back to their geological origins, aiding in provenance
studies. The application of colourimetry and optical microscopy (OM) is instrumental in
this aspect of archaeometric analysis. Colourimetry, through the precise measurement of
the hue, can infer the impact of different colourants on the colour changes between the
different samples.
Optical microscopy complements colourimetric data by allowing for a closer examina­
tion of the glaze’s texture and the interaction between the glaze and the body of the ce­
ramic when observed in cross­sections. Variations in crystal size and the degree of vit­
rification observed under the microscope can suggest different temperature regimes and
cooling rates.
Understanding the nuances of glaze composition also assists in conservation efforts.
Recognizing the vulnerabilities of different glaze types to environmental factors is crucial
for preserving these artefacts in conditions that minimize deterioration [38].

3.2
Optical Microscopy
Optical microscopy, using a light microscope, is utilized in this study to examine the
glazes on the ceramic artefacts from Palmela’s Castle at a microscopic level. This tech­
nique is instrumental in observing the intricate details of the glazes, including texture,
layering, and any features that might not be visible to the naked eye.
The majority of the samples were suited for examination under the optical microscope.
However, the unique geometry of C Pal 92 297, lacking any flat surfaces, rendered it
unsuitable for this method of investigation.
The visual data garnered from optical microscopy is crucial for a comprehensive un­
derstanding of the glazes. When integrated with findings from other analytical methods,
such as Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and X­ray Fluorescence (XRF), it provides
a more comprehensive understanding of the glaze’s composition, texture, and any crack
patterns or impurities.
3.3
Colourimetry
In the analysis of ceramic glazes, precise colour measurement can be important for both
qualitative and quantitative assessments. To this end, it was employed a portable hand­
held colourimeter. This device facilitated the acquisition of objective colourimetric data,
specifically the L*a*b* values and the corresponding hexadecimal (HEX) colour codes for
each of the samples.
The colourimetric L*a*b* system, developed by the International Commission on Illumi­
nation (CIE) [42], is a globally recognized standard for colour measurement. This system
is designed to be perceptually uniform, with each colour space dimension correlating to
human vision. The L*a*b* values consist of three parameters:
1. L* (Lightness): Ranging from 0 to 100, the L* value represents the lightness of
the colour, where 0 represents black and 100 represents white. This axis provides
a gradation of the colour’s brightness, which can be essential for assessing the
intensity and depth of glazes.
2. a*: This value indicates the position between red/magenta and green, where posi­
tive values represent red/magenta and negative values denote green. The magni­
tude of the a* value depicts the intensity of these colours, providing insight into the
hue’s dominance.
16
Ceramics from the Castle of Palmela.
==PAGE:23==
3. b*: Representing the position between yellow and blue, positive b* values corre­
spond to yellow, while negative values indicate blue. Similar to a*, the scale of b*
illustrates the saturation level of these colours in the glaze.
Additionally, the HEX code, a hexadecimal format for colour representation, was recorded.
HEX codes are widely used in digital colour applications, offering a convenient and stan­
dard method for describing colours in a format compatible with various digital and graphic
design tools.
In this analysis, a focused approach was taken to examine how variations in the Lab*
values influenced the colours of the ceramic glaze samples. This method involved a de­
tailed observation of changes in each parameter of the Lab* colour space – Lightness
(L*), chromaticity coordinates a* (red to green) and b* (yellow to blue) – to assess the
impact of different elements on the glaze’s colour. By recording the shifts in these val­
ues, an understanding of how specific components within the glazes affect their visual
appearance was achieved. For instance, an increase in the lightness value (L*) could be
associated with certain oxides, while shifts in a* and b* values might indicate the influence
of different metal ions. Through this comparative analysis, it is feasible to deduce which
elements contribute to the hue, saturation, and lightness of the glazes, thereby providing
a more comprehensive understanding of the material’s colourimetric properties about its
elemental composition. This methodology is essential in discerning the subtle variations
in hue, saturation, and lightness that are often imperceptible to the naked eye.
3.4
X­Ray Fluorescence
By exposing the ceramic artefacts from Palmela’s Castle to X­rays, the elements present
will emit characteristic fluorescence. Analysis of this fluorescence can determine their
elemental composition, shedding light on the materials used. Given the historical and
archaeological significance of the ceramic artefacts from the Castle of Palmela, it was
imperative to ensure their preservation. Thus, unlike many other analytical processes
that require sample preparation, the ceramics in this study were analyzed in their extant
state. This non­invasive approach ensures the integrity of these ancient artefacts, making
XRF particularly suitable for this research. To minimize the error, each measurement was
repeated 3 times.
In XRF analysis, characteristic X­ray lines are used to identify and quantify elements
in the sample. For most elements in this study, the Kα lines were employed due to their
prominence and reliability in XRF spectra. However, for lead (Pb), the M line was utilized.
This choice was driven by the distinct visibility of the M line for lead in the spectra, ensuring
accurate detection and quantification amidst potential overlaps or interferences.
3.5
Scanning Electron Microscopy
A powerful microscopy method, SEM employs a focused electron beam to produce
detailed images of a sample’s surface. Combined with energy­dispersive X­ray spec­
troscopy (EDS), SEM can also deduce the sample’s elemental composition. For this
study, SEM was pivotal in observing the surface features of the ceramic artefacts from
the castle, enhancing the understanding of their production methods and origins [43].
The artefacts were analyzed as they were, ensuring that the historical integrity of these
pieces remained uncompromised. Consequently, due to the non­invasive requirement

analysis. Out of the 18 ceramic samples, only 7 were deemed suitable for SEM examina­

The elemental data procured from SEM­EDS provides insights into the sample’s el­
emental composition. However, in the realm of ceramics, particularly when discussing
glazes, the convention is to refer to compositions in terms of their oxide forms. To transi­
tion from elemental data to oxide compositions, stoichiometric conversions are employed.
For example, data representing calcium (Ca) is translated to its oxide counterpart, calcium
oxide (CaO), by taking into account atomic weights and oxide stoichiometry. In a similar
vein, data for silicon (Si) would be converted to silicon dioxide (SiO2). This conversion
ensures consistency when comparing SEM­EDS results with data obtained from XRF and
other scientific literature, facilitating a standardized approach to data interpretation.

The detailed examination of the glaze compositions revealed intriguing variances in the
formulation across samples. The results from the XRF analysis can be found in Tables

colours or sides of the same sample. For instance, the differences between ”CPal 92 530

A consistent observation across many samples is the prominent presence of PbO, with
concentrations reaching as high as 84.24 wt% in samples like ”CPal 92 297”. This ele­
vated PbO content is indicative of the ceramic traditions of the Islamic period in Portugal,
where lead­based glazes were prevalent [44]. These lead glazes were known for their
low melting point, since the elevated polarity of Pb2+ ions extends the distance between
electrons and oxygen within the silicate structure, thereby lowering the temperature re­
quired to break the Si­O bonds. The low melting point made these glazes suitable for the
low­temperature firing processes, typically at 900­1050ºC [45].
Notably, there were no lead deposits near Palmela [12], yet the ceramic glazes from
the Castle of Palmela are lead­based. This indicates that lead had to be procured from
another location, or the ceramics were produced elsewhere. Evidence suggests that the
nearest lead deposits were at Montemor­o­Novo, seen in the map in Fig.2.4, implying that
this could have been the source of lead for the lead glazes[15].

Besides being one of the main points on the communication routes in the Gharb Al­
Andalus, Palmela was also connected to Montemor­o­Novo (Fig.4.4). This is particularly
noteworthy in ceramic glazes since, as seen in the geological context, Montemor­o­Novo
is known for its lead mining and likely provided the lead used in the lead glazes of the
ceramics found at Palmela. This geographical and geological link between Palmela and
Montemor highlights the intricate network of resource exchange during that time [1].
Typically, lead would be encountered in its natural state as galena, a mineral composed
of lead sulfide (PbS). Craftsmen would heat this mineral in a furnace, allowing the air to
turn the lead part of galena into a usable form, known as lead oxide [21]. This process,
known as roasting, also releases a gas called sulfur dioxide, as illustrated by the equation:
2PbS + 3O2 →2PbO + 2SO2
With this roasting, it was possible to refine the lead by removing sulfur, which is unde­
sirable in the creation of glazes. Additionally, this process alters the lead into an oxide
form that blends more seamlessly with additional components of the glaze. In the past,
this process was carried out with little oversight concerning the release of toxic gases
such as sulfur dioxide, which posed threats to environmental and human health [21]. The
resulting lead oxide could then be mixed with other ingredients to create the glaze for the
ceramics.

4.2.1
Yellow Glazes (Fig.3.2)
Iron can take on a range of colours from yellow to brown and red, depending on its con­
centration and the firing conditions. In the context of ceramic glazing, the colour imparted
by iron ions is influenced by their oxidation states and light absorption properties. This
absorption mechanism results in the reflection of unabsorbed light, which determines the
glaze’s visible colour. Iron in the Fe2+ state tends to produce a bluish hue due to its high
absorption in the infrared spectrum, whereas Fe3+ exhibits lower absorption bands in the
ultraviolet and blue regions translating into the yellow colours observed in the samples.
The Fe2+/Fe3+ ratio in glazes typically increases with firing temperature. Consequently,
the yellow glazes from the Castle of Palmela likely indicate a lower firing temperature.
Additionally, the firing atmosphere plays a crucial role in the final glaze colour. The intro­
duction of elements like manganese, often detected in some fragments, can trigger redox
reactions, leading to the oxidation of iron and resulting in yellow or brown tones [46].
The orange colouration of CPal 93 66 corroborates the role of iron oxide as a key influ­
encer in the chromatic properties of the glaze. Furthermore, the sample CPal 93 66, with
the next highest iron content, exhibits a distinctly orange hue. This correlation between
iron content and colouration is consistent with the known effects of iron oxides in ceramic
glazes.
Samples like CPal 92 297 demonstrate an exceptionally high lead oxide (PbO) concen­
tration, characterizing the dominant role of lead in the formulation of these glazes. The
significant PbO content, ranging from 55.20 wt% to 84.24 wt%, indicates the traditional
methods employed in creating these glazes.

SEM Observations
Despite some differences in absolute value, the SEM­EDS and XRF analyses exhibit
a consistent elemental proportionality. This consistency underscores the reliability of the
comparative approach. Notably, the green glazes exhibit higher copper content, align­
ing with expectations and the yellow glazes are distinguished by their higher iron oxide
(FeO) levels. Lighter­coloured samples, specifically C Pal 92 48 and C Pal 93 96, contain
lower iron concentrations yet are enriched with calcium. Historically, calcium has been
employed to lighten glazes [48], particularly when seeking to offset a darker ceramic body.

The study of glazes from the Islamic era reveals that, despite the inherent chemical
stability and durability of ceramics, the passage of eight to nine centuries can lead to
significant alterations in their composition. Lead glazes, in particular, are susceptible to
weathering processes such as lead leaching and the dissolution and re­precipitation of
silica. The resulting weathered surface layer often exhibits a depletion of alkali metals
(Na2O and K2O) and lead, leading to a compositional transformation from its original state
[46].
Typically, the analysis of such weathered glazes would involve invasive methods, such
as cutting or polishing, to reach unaltered layers. However, due to the imperative of pre­
serving the integrity of these historical samples, only non­destructive analytical techniques
were employed.
Consequently, the presence of corrosion products was an expected
feature within the analytical results, reflecting the glazes’ long­term interaction with envi­
ronmental factors. These findings provide insights into the degradation mechanisms of
ancient glazes and also underscore the need for careful consideration of conservation
and analysis methods in the context of heritage preservation.
Utilizing SEM allowed for a useful examination of individual samples, focusing on de­
tailed mapping and analysis of corrosion patterns.

5
Conclusions
The examination of glaze components from the Castle of Palmela has shown a promi­
nent presence of lead oxide (PbO) characteristic of Islamic ceramic traditions in Portugal.
These lead­based glazes, suitable for the low­temperature firing techniques of the time,
typically at 900­1050ºC, reveal a deep understanding of materials by Islamic artisans.
The near­linear Pb/Si ratio across samples indicates a standardized approach to glaze
formulation, aiming for specific properties such as melting point and opacity.
Oxides such as iron Fe2O3 and copper (CuO) were found in the samples. Variations
in CuO content were found to correspond with colourimetric data, influencing the glaze
hues, with higher CuO concentrations linked to darker, greenish colours. The relationship
between the copper oxide concentration in the glaze and the colour metric ’a*’ values is
evident. A higher copper oxide content correlates with negative ’a*’ values, indicative of
a greenish hue in the glaze. Conversely, lower copper oxide levels are associated with
positive ’a*’ values, which correspond to a reddish hue. This pattern underscores the
significant role of copper oxide in determining the colouration of the glazes. Similarly to
copper oxide, variations in iron oxide (Fe2O3) content also exhibited a notable correlation
with the colourimetric ’b*’ values, which reflect the degree of yellow­blue tint in the glaze.
These findings not only align with known Islamic period ceramics but also provide valu­
able insights into the long­term degradation processes, underlining the significance of
these artefacts in cultural heritage and the necessity of informed conservation efforts.

6
Future Research
This thesis on Islamic ceramics sets the stage for further research in several key areas.
A significant area of interest lies in studying the corrosion of these ceramics to better
understand and mitigate their degradation over time. Additionally, attempting to recreate
these ancient ceramics using modern techniques could confirm historical practices, and
studying local raw materials can also help in determining the provenance questions that
arose.
Expanding on the corrosion aspect, it’s crucial to explore how environmental factors
over centuries have impacted the glazed ceramics from the Islamic period. Detailed stud­
ies on the mechanisms of corrosion could offer insights into the specific chemical pro­
cesses that have altered these artefacts. Understanding these processes is not only vital
for preserving the remaining artefacts but also for reconstructing the original appearance
and composition of the glazes. Such research would deepen our understanding of the
long­term interactions between ceramic materials and their environments.
