Abstract
The cost of energy coming from wind as a source, has been declining over the past decades
due to lower capital costs, higher availability and production, as well as a more eﬃcient
operation. Nonetheless, operation and maintenance cost tend to remain constant or some-
times increase due to anticipated operations in the wind turbine or in the wind farm in
general. Therefore, degradation analysis and early indication of failure has been drawing
more attention.
As seen in this study OPEX contributes to the 7.1% of the total expenditure of the onshore
wind turbine, where the fatigue costs are involved. The paper presents an analysis of the
aerolastic loads on a wind turbine in multiple scenarios in order to determine the fatigue
loading. The scenarios are based on nominal conditions and some derating strategies,
formed by normal operation, constant derated power and constant derated rotor speed.
The characteristic of the loads causing the highest fatigue damage are identiﬁed, so to pro-
vide a vast database for further analysis and a posible optimization using model predictive
control. Fatigue analysis is performed using rain-ﬂow counting and Palmgren-Miner linear
damage assumption, the contribution to lifetime fatigue damage from deterministic load
variations is quantiﬁed, as well as the contributions from operation at diﬀerent mean wind
speeds and turbulence intensities.
The use of down-regulation or curtailment control strategies for wind turbines oﬀers means
of supporting the stability of the power grid and also improving the eﬃciency of a wind
farm. Thus, in this work is investigated the performance of derating strategies with diﬀer-
ent rotor speed set- point, and in particular, their eﬀect on the turbine structural fatigue
throughout the main components are evaluated.
The numerical results obtained from the high-ﬁdelity turbine simulations showed that
compared to the typical derating strategy, the derated turbines might perform better with
lower rotor speed set-point but it is crucial to ensure such a set-point does not drive the
turbine into stalled operations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Nowadays, wind power is undergoing a rapid development globally and in a number of
countries covers an increasing part of the power supply. At the same time an ongoing
liberalization of power markets is taking place and owners of wind power plants are re-
sponsible for trading the power at the spot market and ﬁnancially handling the balancing.
After some observations performed in Jutland, Dk where wind-generated power from time
to time covers almost the 100 % of total power consumption, some examples have been
retrieved analyzing the power spot market and how large amounts of wind power in the
short-term are handled at.
According with Morthorst, there is a tendency towards more wind power in the system in
the short run leading towards relatively lower spot prices, whereas less wind power leads to
higher spot prices. Furthermore, a stronger relationship is found at the regulating market,
where there is a fairly clear tendency that the more wind power produced, the higher is
the need for down-regulation, and, correspondingly, the less wind power produced, the
higher is the need for up-regulation [1], however, the transactions in buy-sell of energy
are outside of the scope of the study, even though is a great subject of interest due to,
production cost per kWh has been reduced signiﬁcantly within the last 20 years and this
trend is expected to continue, resulting in a fully competitive technology in the mid future.
Since aging is a fact of life and is inevitable for all kinds of machinery, a consideration
on the reliability of individual components in a Wind Turbine has to be taken, as well as
the eﬀect impacted on availability, but above all things in a personal perception, it has
to be considered any impact on the energy production of turbines. If the capacity fac-
tors decrease signiﬁcantly with age, the production will show a lower cumulative lifetime
output, increasing the levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) from any plant. If the rate of
degradation were to be too great, it could become worthwhile to prematurely replace the
turbines with new models, implying that the economic life of the turbine is shorter than
the technical life, further increasing its cost[2].
The objective pretended in this report is to primarily on minimizing its structural degra-
dation and possibly maximize the total output power of the WT depending on energy
prices. If a project is to be done, before installing a WT on a particular site, it must be
ensured that the structure is suﬃciently robust to withstand the environmentally-induced
loads during its entire lifetime among many other factors.
In [3] is stated that modern WTs are fatigue-critical machines, i.e., the design of many of
their components is dictated by fatigue considerations. Therefore, the research focused in

this thesis involves the mechanical/aerodynamic engineering part of a WT, which involve
the power production and fatigue of the DTU-10MW RWT turbine model.
Controllers on WTs are generally designed to achieve a reasonable trade-oﬀbetween en-
ergy production and component loading during typical turbulent conditions while limiting
the loads experienced in extreme situations[4]. At the end, this is an economic trade-oﬀ
depending on the energy price and the eﬀect of loads on capital costs, maintenance costs
and plant lifetime. The key purpose of modelling and control, even for just one WT is to
use control variables to ensure a speciﬁc level of performance, as capturing the nonlinear
stochastic behaviour in a mathematical model and then use the model to design a con-
troller able to guarantee its performance.
This thesis seeks to generate inputs that could provide and ﬁnd an optimal solution for the
said trade-oﬀsince both energy prices and environmental conditions change with time and
it is appropriate to include a realistic and coherent framework where the controller can
be able to modify its behaviour under diﬀerent conditions. For example, when turbulence
is high and energy prices low, it may be actually more economical to produce less power
and limit fatigue load accumulation, while in an opposite situation it may be possible
to produce more than the rated power level for limited short time of periods, gaining
additional revenue while not exacerbating loads unduly.
1.1
Motivation
Aging is a fact of life and just as conventional forms, power generated by a WT gradually
decreases over its lifetime, fact which translates into lesser proﬁt out of it. The lifetime
of a WT is often assumed to be 20 years, therefore optimizing production considering
ambience factors in order to proﬁt the most out of a WT and perhaps enlarge its lifetime
certainly makes a desirable subject of study. By the aid of HAWC2, damage equivalent
load values can be determined and the lifetime of a turbine can be assessed. Accurate
predictions can be made on forces, moments and vibrations of a turbine structure for given
wind conditions. Knowing those factors, one also can be more aware of tempting times
of rutine and non-rutine mantenaince, likewise, reducing maintenance costs represents an
engaging challenge that could be endured by reducing dynamic loading. I believe that
with a dynamic controller(outside of the scope of the study) the goal could be achieved.
1.2
Aim
In the present work, the beneﬁts and challenges of producing power in a single WT to its
maximum capacity considering its adaptation to diﬀerent turbulence characteristics are
analyzed. The study aims at fulﬁlling the following speciﬁc goals:
• Implement an algorithm for modifying the WT set-points in response to indicators
of turbulence derived on key turbine components.
• Tune algorithm to make it ready for experiments on turbulence-based operation
adaptation by the generation of diﬀerent inputs.
• Generation of a conﬁdent database of fatigue outputs of key turbine components.

2.1
Wind conditions
Wind Turbines are subjected to environmental and electrical conditions that may aﬀect
their loading, durability and operation. The environmental conditions are further divided
into wind conditions which this study focuses and other environmental conditions, this
conditions are subdivided into normal and extreme categories. The normal conditions
generally concern recurrent structural loading conditions, while the extreme conditions
represent rare external design conditions. Wind conditions are the primary external con-
ditions aﬀecting structural integrity.
The wind conditions include a constant mean ﬂow combined, in many cases, with either
a varying deterministic gust proﬁle or with turbulence. The ﬂow inclination angle is to
be invariant with height.
Turbulence or turbulent ﬂow is a ﬂow regime characterized
by chaotic, stochastic property changes[5]. This includes low momentum diﬀusion, high
momentum convection, and rapid variation of pressure and velocity in space and time. It
denotes random variations in the wind velocity from 10 min averages[6]. The turbulence
model includes the eﬀect of varying wind speed, shears and direction and allow rotational
sampling through varying shears. The three vector components of turbulent wind velocity
are deﬁned:
• Longitudinal - along the direction of the mean wind velocity.
• Lateral - horizontal and normal to the longitudinal direction.
• Upward - normal to both the longitudinal and lateral directions, i.e. tilted from
the vertical by the mean ﬂow inclination angle.
The stochastic wind or turbulence can be determined and simulated, two models are
given as reference in Annex C of the IEC61400-1 for design load calculations. The Mann
uniform shear model and Kaimal spectral and exponential coherence model. The one used
in HAWC2 is the Mann uniform shear turbulence model and ﬁts fundamentals is based
in isotropic turbulence in neutral atmospheric conditions. The energy spectrum is given
based on the Von Karman spectrum, it will not be discussed but for more information can
be referred to [6]. In isotropic turbulence, the properties of turbulence like variance and
turbulent length scale is identical for all three direction corresponding to vortex structures
being circular.[7]

2.2
Aeroelastic simulation
Design of wind turbines and analysis of the fatigue load exposure of their structural el-
ements are based on aero-elastic simulations, where the relevant external loading (i.e.
gravity and a stochastically varying wind ﬁeld) are applied to a dynamic system consti-
tuted by the elastic wind turbine[8]. By ”aero-elastic” it indicates a feed-back mechanism
between the aerodynamic loading and the elastic response of the structure, which is indeed
the case. The result of an aero-elastic simulation is structural load histories, serving as an
input to a subsequent fatigue analysis, which will be discussed further on.
When considering the aero-elastic context of a wind turbine, it is purpose of study the
mutual eﬀects of structural ﬂexibility and aerodynamic forces. When the aerodynamic
forces change, the WT’s deformations change. The deformations alter the relative orien-
tation of any component with respect to the wind ﬂow, and thus the aerodynamic forces
change again[9]. This corresponds to a coupled problem since both interactions inﬂuence
each other, which are common on modern WTs and can be seen on control strategies,
wind, waves and wakes of other turbines, among others.
All these diﬀerent aspects have mutual inﬂuences and the complete set of interactions
becomes very complex. HAWC2 is capable of and is used to study the dynamic behaviour
of a wind turbine exposed to all these diﬀerent actors. It has been validated and exten-
sively used in researches and industries all over the world. The software does not have
a well deﬁned user interface, the user is expected to know the coding structure of the
program and can make suﬃcient changes in the .htc ﬁles which is the interface to the
solver. This makes the software extremely ﬂexible which enables the user in simulation
modelling and creating user deﬁned equations which can be then solved using the software.
The aerodynamic part of the code contains extension of the BEM theory with three dy-
namic stall models. These include, Stig Oye model, Active trailing edge ﬂap model and
also a model of modiﬁed Beddoes Leishmann model. The software is capable of taking into
account the skew inﬂow, the tip loss correction method or Prandtl’s correction method,
dynamic inﬂow and the wind shear characteristics that usually occur in the real environ-
ments[7].
The structural formulation of HAWC2 is coupled to an aerodynamic model capable of
calculating the aerodynamic forces on the blades based on rotor aerodynamic principles.
It is important to note that the aerodynamic models used in HAWC2 should be classiﬁed
as engineering methods.
2.3
Loads
A WT structure is designed to withstand steady loads several times larger than nominal
loads, and so it is necessary to study fatigue loading with respect to the lifetime of a wind
turbine. In [10], is stated that modern WT are fatigue-critical machines. i.e., the design
of their components is dictated by fatigue considerations, also mostly dynamic loads are
responsible for fatigue and reduced lifetime of WTs.
The three most important sources for the loadig of an upwind horizontal-axis WT are[3]:

• Gravitational loading - Is caused by the gravitational ﬁeld of the Earth and
rotation of the blades. A blade rotating downward experiences diﬀerent forces than
a blade rotating upward. It causes a sinusoidl loading on the blades with a frequency
corresponding to the rotor rotation of one per revolution (1P).
• Inertial loading - Occurs when the WT changes the rotation speed. Certain parts
on the blades experience diﬀerent changes that results in inertial loading, another
source is the centrifugal force acting on the blades.
• Aerodynamical loading - Is caused by the ﬂow passing the wind turbine and
varies in space in time, for example, a wind ﬁeld contains a velocity proﬁle with a
bigger magnitude that is relatively high from the ground because of shear eﬀects,
whereas the turbulent eﬀects introduce time-varying bahaviour in a wind ﬁeld. Also,
according to [3], the yaw and tilt angle of a WT causes additional aerodynamical
periodical forces on a wind turbine. Loading at the end, can lead to fatigue damage
and breakdowns.
2.4
Fatigue Loading
Fatigue is a phenomenom that occurs in a microscopic scale, manifesting itself as deteriora-
tion or damage. Consequently, it has been studied extensively with diﬀerent perspectives.
In general, fatigue is understood as the weakening or break down of a material subjected
to stress, specially a repeated series of stresses.
From a materials perspective, it can be also thought of as elastoplastic deformations caus-
ing damage of a certain material or structure, compromising its integrity. Since is necessary
to ensure a certain life span under normal operating conditions of a WT in a turbulent
environment, fatigue is regarded a critical factor[11].
Wind turbines are subjected to complex systems of variable loads, such as waves and
uneven loads, particularly they are susceptible to fatigue damage. The cycling loading of
a WT’s structure could cause failure if some critical level of damage is exceeded, once the
damage is initiated, it grows with the load cycling until the misadventure of failure occurs.
This, failure process occurs because of some main reasons as:
• The net section stress, accounting for the loss of section caused by the damage,
exceeds the ultimate strength of the material[12].
• A critical crack forms by the accumulation of damage.
Perhaps the most recognized and used measure for fatigue damage estimation is the rain-
ﬂow counting (RFC) method, which is used in combination with the Palmgren-Miner
rule. In the WT context, the impact on fatigue from a load can be described by a damage
equivalent load (DEL); basically, a single, constant-rate fatigue load that will produce
equivalent damage[11]. The calculations and methods used are described afterwards in
section 2.4.1.

2.5
Wind Turbine Control
As stated earlier, in general, the goal of WT control is to minimize the cost of wind energy.
This can be translated into a number of technical objectives, namely maximizing power
production, minimizing structural degradation, and active power control. In this study
only power production and load performance indicators are discussed.
In 2.4.1 loading or fatigue reduction is addressed, with a controller the active loading
takes use. e.g., by changing the pitching angle or generator torque, while passive entails
the design of the structure. A large amount of the current control methods rely on the
calculation of the damage either by DEL, which can be only used as port-processing tools;
other methods are based on minimization of some norm of the stresses on diﬀerent com-
ponents of the WT, hoped to reduce fatigue, but they are not a reliable characterization
of the damage [15].
Typically, in nominal operations, the control inputs (pitch angles and generator torque)
and rotor speed set-point are pre-deﬁned, that leaves limited rooms to manoeuvre. In
contrast, in derated operations, WT operate at a lower eﬃciency compared to nominal
operations, allowing the controller to interpret the power set-point, with more freedoms,
into diﬀerent sets of control inputs and rotor speed set-point[16].
In the nominal operation of a WT, normally the rated rotational speed is reached before
the rated power and therefore there is a region where the rotational speed is constant and
we can maximize the generated power[17]. Consequently, this motivates the development
of derating control strategies that not only can track the power set-point, but also take
into account other factors such as the fatigue of key turbine components. As deﬁnitions:
• Derated power(Pd) - Is the power output of a turbine demanded to generate in
the derating operations, which is typically lower than the rated value Pr and above
the minimum power output.
• Derated rotor speed (ωd) - Is the rotor speed in derating operations when the
turbine reaches the demanded power.

HAWC2
Risø-DTU is the leading research institute in the ﬁeld of developing aeroelastic codes in
Europe. The software HAWC2, acronym of ”Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation
Code 2nd generation” is an aerolastic code with the purpose of calculating the WT re-
sponse in time domain. It is one of the most advanced softwares for analyzing both oﬀshore
and onshore wind turbines, it uses Blade Element Momentum (BEM) method for the aero-
dynamic model, Multibody dynamics is used in the Structural dynamic model and wind
simulation is done with the help of Wasp engineering. In this work the software is used to
perform the experiments and calculate the WT response in time domain, calculating the
the DEL of the components on the WT.
For convenience, there is a distinction between properties related to the mean wind speed
and turbulence properties. The mean wind properties originates from the low frequent
wind variations, this involves mean wind speed at hub height. Wind shear, which is wind
velocity proﬁle as function of height. Wind direction, where positive direction is with
the wind from the right, seen from the wind turbine in the default wind direction. The
slope of the mean wind speed, which is used if the mean wind speed comes from below.
This is most used in complex terrain where ﬂow could be forced up when passing a hill
or mountain. Positive direction in HAWC2 is with the wind from below. In principle it
is also possible to model the wind veer, which is the wind direction proﬁle as function of
height. This is however still only possible in HAWC2 when using a special user deﬁned
shear method reading special input text ﬁles.
Turbulence is characterized by a turbulence vector ﬁeld, which has a mean value of zero
in all directions. This turbulence ﬁeld, is rotated to follow the direction of the mean wind
with respect to wind direction and wind slope. Turbulence intensity is normally given
for the u direction, but a relative intensity in the v and z direction is also present. The
relation between intensity in u and v, and between u and z is typically kept constant and
not really an input parameter to the used turbulence models[20].

5.1
Conclusions
The cost of energy coming from wind as a source, has been declining over the past decades
due to lower capital costs, higher availability and production, as well as a more eﬃcient
operation. Nonetheless, operation and maintenance cost tend to remain constant or some-
times increase due to anticipated operations in the wind turbine or in the wind farm in
general. Therefore, degradation analysis and early indication of failure has been drawing
more attention to the people involved. As seen in this study OPEX contributes to the
7.1% of the total expenditure of the onshore wind turbine, where the fatigue costs are
involved.
The previous sections of this paper described the procedure for estimating speciﬁc life-
time damage equivalent loads for each component of the WT and in every direction. The
estimations are trustworthy only within the bounds of the variable space used, and ex-
trapolation is either not possible, or may lead to unpredictable results. It is therefore
important to ensure that the distributions used for load assessment are not outside the
bounds of validity of the load estimation model.
The variable bounds presented on this paper are based on a certain degree of considera-
tion of atmospheric physics employed in the relationships between wind speed, turbulence,
wind shear, wind veer and turbulence length scale. However, in some situations it may
be more practical to choose other bound deﬁnitions for consistency with the IEC 61400-1
deﬁnitions of the normal and extreme turbulence models. The currently suggested variable
bounds include the normal turbulence (NTM) class A.
The eﬀects on the lifetime fatigue loads by practical bounds of variation in environmental
variables was studied, leading towards saying that the mean wind speed and turbulence
are factors having the highest inﬂuence on fatigue loads, whereas the Mann turbulence
length scale was found to also have an inﬂuence but in minor scale as 15 diﬀerent seeds
were evaluated.
39
==PAGE:56==
Chapter 5. Conclusions and further work
Also, the studies of the derating strategies with diﬀerent rotor speed set-point were pre-
sented, ﬁnding that the derated turbine operating at a lower rotor speed resulted in a
higher tower structure and foundation fatigue damage, but a lower drive-train and blade
loading and. This work also showed that a low rotor speed set-point without care could
lead the turbine to the stalled operations, where the fatigue damage on the tower would
increase signiﬁcantly.
5.2
Further work
With the values predicted for fatigue DEL and the determination of the total cost for
the DTU 10MW RWT in this model, the following step would be to design a dynamic
controller in model predictive control with Wind turbine control objectives well deﬁned,
one base would be according to [27] and explore more control strategies for down regulation
like constant tip speed ratio or other set points for the rotor speed and rated power. Also,
design a predictive model able to forecast the energy prices hourly and not use a ﬁxed
price as in this model, making a time varying controller able to tune towards the optimal
model-based methods, as mentioned.
