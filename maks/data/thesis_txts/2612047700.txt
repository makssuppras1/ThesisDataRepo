ABSTRACT
The purpose of this thesis is to analyse the small-signal stability of large-scale offshore wind farms
connected to weak grids, focusing on mitigation methods such as the synchronous condenser.
The study is regarding converter-instability and electromagnetic interactions between passive
transmission system elements and the active elements of the wind farm in the near-synchronous
frequency range. The multiple-timescale control systems of the grid-side converters causes cross-
couplings with the electromagnetic dynamics of the weak-grid power system causing oscillations
in a wide range of frequencies. Grid-following converters may not be able to maintain synchronism
during weak-grid conditions, as the wind turbines have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the offshore
voltage while the synchronising loop requires the voltage for maintaining synchronism, thus a
positive feedback dynamic loop can emerge.
The thesis thoroughly treats the complex state-space modelling and linearisation of a generic
offshore wind farm design. Subsequently, a eigenvalue-based sensitivity approach is utilised for
investigating the small-signal dynamics and pole locations as a function of various parameters.
A moving average model based on synchronous reference frame controllers has been utilised for
the converter-based wind turbines enabling eigenvalue-based investigations. Through detailed
aggregation-methods, the collector system of the wind farm can be represented by equivalent wind
turbines. Eventually, the system stability and the complex interactions of the system components
are analysed, and the participation analysis is utilised for estimating the root causes and potential
mitigation measures of the unstable modes.
The inner loop and phase-locked loop of the wind turbines participate in numerous interacting
modes with the grid components. The equivalent interconnecting impedance from the WTs to the
reference bus affects the specter of stable operating points and controller settings, as a weak grid
connection causes a larger sensitivity to the mode-locations. Speciﬁc interacting modes between
the WTs become unstable for particularly weak-grid conditions. The nonlinear PLL has a large
impact on wind farm stability and can even cause instability if the PLL-bandwidth is selected
too high. By installing a synchronous condenser at the onshore substation, the critical modes
are pushed towards stability for very-weak-grids, due to the stabilising effect of the synchronous
condenser. The damping of modes related to the PLL and current controllers is increased due to
the change of the equivalent impedance seen from the wind turbines. The damping effect of the
synchronous machine is largest for the weakest grid conditions, while the overall impact on wind
farm stability is near negligible for strong grid connections. Furthermore, the impact from electrical
design speciﬁcations of the synchronous condenser is investigated for the impact on the overall
system interactions and potential of increasing the mode damping of critical modes.
Based on these studies the STATCOM has a marginal impact on the near-synchronous modes.
Through proper selection of the terminal inductance, and controller gains the damping of
supersynchronous modes can be improved. However, for high-frequency analysis, the converter-
based components are better analysed through a full converter model.

INTRODUCTION
The offshore wind industry has experienced rapid development in recent years as the focus
on renewable energy is increasing. Legislators worldwide are disclosing visions of becoming
carbon neutral, with large-scale renewable energy generation such as offshore wind farms as the
foundation. However, as offshore wind farm projects become larger the complexity increases from
an engineering perspective, new challenges emerge, and accurate modelling becomes essential for
validating and improving the design.
Offshore wind farms are pushed further from shore increasing the cable distance, which is a
challenge with HVAC-based transmission. The longer distance combined with the high capacitance
of high voltage cables pushes the natural resonances of the system to lower frequencies. The
presence of low-order resonance increases the risk of interactions between said resonances, active
components, and the onshore grid. Active power electronic devices such as converter-based (Type 4)
wind turbines [1] introduce ﬂexibility for complying with grid code requirements and maintaining
efﬁcient control. However, the converter-based generation introduces inherent challenges of stability
and non-passivity.
This thesis is conducted in a collaboration with Ørsted and aims to analyse large offshore
wind farms and assess the potential of improving the small-signal stability by installing an
onshore synchronous condenser. The relevant theory for eigenvalue-based analysis and state-
space modelling are described for the passive elements of the power system and the voltage source
converters representing the wind turbines. The stabilising effects of synchronous condensers are
analysed through eigenvalue-based sensitivity analysis. Furthermore, the relevant parameters
to consider when designing a synchronous condenser for enhancing small-signal stability are
investigated.
1.1
Background
The increasing complexity of large-scale offshore wind farms is highly related to the converter-
based wind turbine conﬁgurations and the correlated weak-grid conditions of the far-shore systems.
Converter-based wind turbines are well-known for the decoupling between the generator and the
grid hence the turbines do not provide any natural inertial- or fault-response as the conventional
generation. Consequently, the response to grid-perturbations is entirely based on the control strategy
utilised for the grid-side converter [2]. The decoupling between the generator- and grid-side systems
of the Type 4 wind turbines, generally introduces several advantages due to its high-speed ﬂexibility
and controllability such as enabling variable speed operation of the generator with higher efﬁciency
and compatibility to a large range of wind speeds [3]. The reactive power capabilities and grid
supporting services are signiﬁcantly improved compared to the other wind turbine types. However,
the converter interface to the grid also introduces some drawbacks such as power quality problems
and stability challenges.

1. Introduction
Typically, large offshore wind farms have a STATCOM installed at the onshore substation to
provide dynamic reactive power support to comply with the local grid code requirements [4].
Alternatively, the synchronous condenser can also provide reactive power control, increase the short
circuit level of the system, and consequently raise the short circuit ratio of the system i.e., improve
the conditions for the synchronising loops of the wind turbines. Synchronous condensers are a
well-known technology for reactive power- and inertia support in conventional power systems [5]
and will be applied to a generic offshore wind farm design in this thesis to investigate its stabilising
effects. Furthermore, the general impact of the onshore STATCOM is investigated, but no power
oscillation dampers are considered for the generality of this study.
The multiple-timescale control systems of the grid-side converters can result in cross-couplings
with both electromechanical dynamics of electrical machines and the electromagnetic dynamics
of the power system causing oscillations in a wide range of frequencies [6]. Furthermore, the
transmission system of offshore wind farms are characterised as weak due to the low fault-levels.
These weak-grid conditions can introduce additional challenges of the grid-following converters
not being able to maintain synchronism i.e., the small-signal stability can be compromised. For
a weak grid connection, the WTs have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the offshore voltage, while the
synchronising loop requires the voltage for maintaining synchronism. Consequently, a positive
feedback loop can emerge from this dynamic causing controller instability. Thus, it is critical for
system developers to locate and mitigate undesired interactions between the dynamic controllers of
the wind turbine, the passive transmission system, and the onshore components such as dynamic
reactive compensators or grid resonances [2].
Small-signal stability of a power system is the ability of a power system to maintain synchronism
and stable operation when subjected to small perturbations [7]. When small perturbations e.g.,
a small voltage change occurs in the system the active components will react to fulﬁll their
operating point under the new conditions. This responsiveness of the system can cause oscillations
depending on the controller settings and overall system damping. The small-signal stability
of a converter-based power system is typically investigated with impedance-based analysis or
eigenvalue-based analysis. The impedance-based analysis is based on the ratio between internal
and external impedances at a speciﬁc location. Thus, the system is assessed from the point of
impedance aggregation and must be repeated for all nodes. Hence the impedance-based analysis
is cumbersome for large-scale systems despite the relative simplicity of the modelling when only
considering linear controllers [2].
The eigenvalue-based stability analysis is conducted by evaluating the eigenvalues of the system
matrix, as recommended in [8]. This method provides a holistic view of system stability based on a
given operating point and enables the user to establish the origin of potential complications through
the participation factors [6]. The real part of the eigenvalues testiﬁes the damping, while a possible
imaginary part shows the oscillatory frequency. If a positive real part is present for any mode for a
certain operating point, means that a perturbation can cause oscillations and divergence eventually
forcing the system to saturate or become unstable [9]. Utilising the state-space model-based
methodology is cumbersome for large systems as the system order increases. Furthermore, with
multi-vendor components and the competitive environment, advanced vector ﬁtting algorithms can
become necessary for making numerical data state-space compatible [2].

1. Introduction
1.2
Formulation of the problem and research objectives
This thesis will include an in-depth theoretical investigation of the small-signal stability challenges
in weak-grid large offshore wind farms. Extensive state-space modelling will be utilised for the
modelling of a generic offshore wind farm to enable investigations of near-synchronous component-
interactions by means of the eigenvalue-based analysis. The performance of active mitigation
measures will be investigated through eigenvalue- and participation factor-analysis. A time-domain
model will be developed in PSCAD for validation of the linearised state-space model. The following
questions will be scientiﬁcally investigated and discussed in this thesis:
• What component speciﬁcations are critical for development of state-space models for
eigenvalue-based small-signal stability investigations in the near-synchronous frequency
range?
• How does the system conditions and characteristics impact the small-signal stability and
multi-component interactions in offshore wind farms?
• How can onshore components such as the synchronous condensers improve the stability?
And what design speciﬁcations are of signiﬁcant relevance?
To resolve the above problems the following research- and learning objectives have been deﬁned:
• Develop state-space models for power systems with converter-based renewable energy.
• Undertake eigenvalue-based small-signal stability assessments and analyse the results.
• Undertake detailed analyses interfacing control theory and power system analysis,
considering the relevant internal controllers of grid-following converters.
• Analyse the impact of implementing mitigation methods e.g., synchronous condensers in
offshore wind farms.
• Evaluate the validity of models within the near-synchronous frequency range.
• Evaluate the interactions between active and passive components in a generic offshore wind
farm topology by means of the eigenvalue-based analysis.
1.3
Methodology, scope, and limitations
This section settles the overall methodical structure and scope of this thesis, while the limitations
are listed to ensure a transparent basis of the analytic results and conclusions.
1.3.1
Methodology
This thesis will initially evaluate the necessary theory for developing a state-space model for
eigenvalue-based stability analysis. The theoretical framework will be described in terms of
modelling considerations and analysis based on relevant literature. Once the basic knowledge is
acquired, the linearised state-space model will be validated by comparing the results to a nonlinear
time-domain simulation in PSCAD. The overall model will be based on a generic wind farm
topology with component speciﬁcations provided by Ørsted.
The wind turbine-, synchronous condenser-, and STATCOM-models are developed as part of
the project, based on the relevant theory to ensure genericity and complete transparency of the
internal controllers. This is done to ensure no vendor-speciﬁc challenges are highlighted and
to maintain a scientiﬁc and non-commercial approach. In case a standardised WT-model such
as IEC61400-27 is implemented and used for eigenvalue-based stability there may be certain

1.3.2
Scope
The studies of low-frequency power oscillations in conventional systems, caused by interactions
between synchronous generators are well-deﬁned [7]. Based on the power angle characteristics
of the synchronous generator, small perturbations in a system can cause power oscillations in the
system. The system’s capability to recover after a small perturbation is the small-signal stability
hence if an oscillatory behaviour is not damped the system is said to be small-signal unstable
i.e., the amplitude of the oscillations increase. As converter-based generation plants take over in
modern power systems, a similar damping effect is desired elsewhere. The multiple-timescale
control systems of the grid-side converters can result in cross-couplings in various frequency
ranges, supersynchronous harmonic interaction, and subsynchronous interactions. Thus, small-
signal stability is no longer preserved to the classical electromechanical oscillations but rather
a phenomenon of multi-frequency nature due to the electromagnetic [8]. The electromagnetic
interactions are per deﬁnition represented by differential equations [10]. Consequently, an extension
of the classical small-signal stability analysis techniques is needed [8][11][2].
The scope of this thesis is to investigate the small-signal stability of a generic large offshore wind
farm design and to investigate the impact of installing a synchronous condenser or STATCOM
in the transmission system of these wind farms with a focus on stability. The state-space and
time-domain model of a generic wind farm topology will be subjected to various study cases of
various operating conditions, to obtain a deeper understanding of the small-signal relevant dynamics
in converter-based systems. The WTs will be modelled with a main focus on the grid-side converter
and correlated control loops to analyse the potential interactions with grid elements during weak
grid conditions [12]. For weak grid conditions, the WT output inﬂuences the offshore voltage
signiﬁcantly, while the synchronising loop regulates based on the terminal voltage which can cause
positive feedback dynamics and deteriorate the controller performance. The nonlinear outer loop
and phase-locked loop (PLL) of the grid-side converter, can cause negative damping at certain
frequencies during weak-grid conditions [11]. Consequently, the effects of installing a synchronous
condenser at the onshore substation to improve the grid strength will be investigated in this thesis.
A moving average dq-frame model which neglects the switching modulator, is implemented
allowing for analysis of system and grid dynamics up to approximately fsw
2 [6][13]. Due to the
wide timescale dynamics of converters, and the electromagnetic states of the network the system
matrix order increases signiﬁcantly, in opposition to the classical eigenvalue analysis for rotor angle
stability [7], where oscillations are predominantly electromechanic and the network equations are
algebraic. The dq-frame model has been selected, due to its relatively straight-forward linearisation
process, and it enables time-invariant modelling for balanced systems [14]. The computational
burden of the state-space modelling is relieved by the Component Connection Method [15].

1.3.3
Limitations
The analysis presented will be based on the following overall limitations and assumptions:
• The frequency range of interest is limited to the near-synchronous area i.e. the dynamics
of the modulation circuit and high-frequency harmonics are neglected. Furthermore, this
assumes the internal ﬁlters of the wind turbines are ideal and ﬁltering all harmonics from
the switching circuit. Consequently, the converters can be modelled as a controlled voltage
source behind the coupling reactance, where the magnitude is set by the WT controllers.
This simpliﬁcation is explained as the moving average model in [6].
• The system is assumed to be in balanced conditions i.e. no zero- nor negative sequence is
present. The delta-star conﬁgured transformers of offshore wind turbines entail that the WT
controllers would not observe the zero-sequence.
• The onshore grid connection point is represented by a simple Thévenin equivalent, as no
information is available regarding the onshore system. The strength of the equivalent is
varied throughout the study.
• Only one radial export circuit is considered in the offshore transmission system.
• The park controller is neglected for this study, but it could be interesting to investigate in
future work as it introduces an additional level of controls.
• The array cable layout is aggregated on a string basis to facilitate the modelling. This is done
in accordance with [16] with analyses of the potential risks of doing so. The aggregation is
necessary, as the PSCAD model providing the initial conditions of linearisation is limited to
200 nodes [17].
• Non-linearities of the system such as saturation curves of transformer cores have been omitted
for the state-space modelling.
• Breaker and switchgear speciﬁcations will be neglected. Furthermore, harmonic ﬁlters are
neglected to represent the worst case. By inclusion of the normally installed harmonic
ﬁlters, the small-signal stability is improved for super-synchronous modes, as the harmonic
resonances of the system are shifted to permissible areas [18].

THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK
"Power system stability is the ability of an electric power system, for a given initial
operating condition, to regain a state of operating equilibrium after being subjected
to a physical disturbance, with most system variables bounded so that practically the
entire system remains intact."
— IEEE/CIGRE Joint Task Force on Stability Terms and Deﬁnitions [23]
Power system stability is a highly complicated topic, which requires insights in complex math and
understanding of the nonlinear power system [24]. The eigenvalue-based small-signal stability
assessment relies on state-space modelling, which will be described in the following. Furthermore,
as power systems are inherently nonlinear, powerful linearisation processes are necessary to enable
stability investigations of a speciﬁc equilibrium. This section introduces the concept of small-signal
stability, followed by an in-depth explanation of the eigenvalue-based analysis. The Component
Connection Method (CCM) which enables a modular state-space modelling approach is described.
Finally, the transformation from phase quantities to dq-components in a synchronous reference
frame is presented as it parts signiﬁcantly in simplifying the state-space representation.
2.1
Small-Signal Stability
Small-signal stability is the ability of a system to maintain synchronism when subjected to a
small perturbation. The response of any dynamic power system is highly dependent on the initial
conditions, grid strength, and controller settings of the system. Due to the high variability of modern
power systems, the steady-state conditions are never truly reached i.e. small perturbations are
occurring continuously [25], and small-signal stability must be secured with adequate margin. In
classical power systems, the large synchronous machines govern the voltages and angles throughout
the system i.e. the electromechanical dynamics of such systems is typically of interest for small-
signal studies [12]. The characteristics affecting the low-frequency power oscillations the damping
is well-deﬁned and related to the directly coupled inertia of masses in the synchronous generators
[7, p.699-714][26]. In case a system has insufﬁciently damping the duration of potential oscillations
is signiﬁcantly extended, and causes premature wear and deterioration of the components and
generators [10].
However, for this thesis analysis, the interactions between the active and passive elements are of
interest in converter-dominated grids i.e. the control schemes of these converters will be the main
factor to determine the dynamics. The operational characteristic of converter-based renewables
is signiﬁcantly different from conventional power plants [2]. Furthermore, large-scale integration
of renewables introduces issues of weakened grid strengths, small-signal stability issues, voltage-
rise problems in distribution systems, and the potential reverse power ﬂow affecting protection
systems [27]. Thus, the system response to disturbances for weak-grid conditions is dictated
by the converters, having a substantial effect on the system voltage and frequency. Combined

with the grid-following technology which requires the voltage waveform for the synchronising
loop, undesired small-signal sensitivities can emerge. Consequently, small-signal stability is an
important category in the broad ranges of stability studies, which is typically investigated through
eigenvalue-based and impedance-based analysis [11][12][2].
The impedance-based assessment is a powerful and relatively simple method, where the system
is lumped into equivalent impedances at the node of interest. The stability assessment is relying
on local properties, based on the impedance-ratio hence dynamic behaviours can be concealed
[2]. The impedance-based model characterises the relation between inputs and outputs hence the
classical stability indices such as phase- and gain margins are applicable. The impedance sweep is
common for preliminary investigations of systems [19], but the nonlinearities of the PLL and the
outer loop controllers in the low-frequency range makes the impedance-based method challenging
[2].Hence, for this study of investigating potential mitigation measures, the insights of internal
states through participation analysis is essential to obtain a deeper understanding of the underlying
problems.
2.1.1
Frequency Range
In small-signal stability analysis of conventional power systems, two types of instability are
typically treated 1) lack of synchronising torque causing a steady increase of rotor angle, and
2) lack of damping torque causing rotor oscillations [7, p.20-27].Both categories occur in the
subsynchronous and quasi-DC frequency area, typically at frequencies below < 2 Hz and can
cause either local- or interarea- power oscillations. The analysis of these low-frequency power
oscillations is related to the electromechanical dynamics of the synchronous generators. Thus,
the studies can be done neglecting the network dynamics i.e. as algebraic equations using phasor
representation of the system.
In the opposition, the performance of grid-connected converters relies on properly tuned controllers,
which can interact with the passive transmission system, or even with the electrical machines of the
system within a wide range of frequencies [6]. The control systems operate at multiple timescales
for regulating the currents and powers exchanged with the power system [6]. Hence, an increasing
amount of converter-based generation broadens the frequency range at which critical small-signal
interactions can occur [2]. Consequently, the interactions of interest are often electromagnetic,
where the frequency range is not limited to quasi-DC frequencies. By deriving the differential
equations of the system and merging them into the state-space system, these electromagnetic
dynamics of the passive transmission system are represented. This increases the system order
signiﬁcantly, but through the participation analysis, the modes representing interactions between
multiple active components can be highlighted and analysed explicitly.
The numerous control structures, time constants and internal states are making a complete model
of the wind turbine an extensive task. The internal control loops are all linked to speciﬁc frequency
ranges, hence by settling the frequency range of interest, some dynamics and controllers can be
omitted without compromising the modelling accuracy [6]. The frequency range of the grid-side
converter controllers is shown in Figure 2.1. The modulator circuit and terminal LCL-ﬁlter are
typically related to harmonic emission studies i.e. high-frequency studies where the impedance-
based method is commonly used [28]. To limit the scope of this thesis, these high-frequency
interactions and harmonic stability aspects are not considered.

an analytic aspect, these circuit elements are also directly linked to the time-domain differential

2.2.1
Validity of Linearisation
The assumption that the linearised system yields valid results for a stability assessment is based on
Lyapunov’s indirect method stating that, if a linearised model is unstable around its initial operating
point the original nonlinear system will also be unstable [2]. The opposition theorem by Poincaré,
claims that information on the stability of the nonlinear system based on a linearised model will
be only exact at the linearised equilibrium [31]. Practically, this requires the perturbation to be
small, as the linear approximation reduces the validity of the model to a small area around the
initial operating point.
Furthermore, when assessing the dynamic performance of a system the deﬁnition of small is not
generic, as it is highly dependent on the system conditions. Consequently, the application and type
of study must be kept in mind [31]. A classical example of how the conditions affect the conﬁdence
area of a linearisation is founded in the classical (P, δ)-characteristics (2.7) which express the
transmittable power through a series reactance, based on the sending end VS and receiving end

2.3.2
Mode Characteristics
The composition or location of the eigenvalues λ in the complex plane can explain the behaviour of
the system, similarly to the poles of a closed-loop transfer function [32, p.304-305]. Lyapunov’s
ﬁrst method describes the correlation of eigenvalues of the system matrix A and system stability.
The overall relation between the eigenvalues of the linear system and the small-signal properties of
the system is summarised by [7, p. 699-714]:
1. The original system is asymptotically stable when the eigenvalues have a negative real part.
2. The original system is unstable when at least one of the eigenvalues has a positive real part.
3. If the eigenvalues have real parts equal to zero, the system is not presentable by the ﬁrst-order
Taylor series expansion.

2.4.1
Improper Transfer Functions
An improper transfer function is characterised by the number of zeros being larger than the number
of poles or simply, the numerator polynomial having a larger order than the denominator [2]. Such
a transfer function can typically occur in meshed power system where the current of some inductive
branches, or voltage of capacitive loops, are no longer independent. An example of such a system
is shown in Figure 2.3, where it is obvious by means of Kirchoffs Current Law, that only two of the
three branches can be expressed independently, as the current through the inductors will sum to
zero. For normal state-space modelling, these constructions are resolved by amending an additional
coefﬁcient matrix E to (2.6) i.e.:
˙x = A x + B u
y = C x + D u + E ˙u
(2.30)
The CCM is not capable of treating the additional derivative of inputs, hence either the system
must be modelled differently, or algebraic workarounds can be utilised [2]. The improper transfer
functions are avoided by modelling the transformer cores as a parallel resistance and inductance,
Rc and Lm respectively [19, p.93-127].

3.1
Wind Farm Overview
Large offshore wind farms are placed increasingly further from shore for energy yield optimisation
[37] and to reduce the risk of delays due to local complaints [38] etc. The transmission system
connecting the wind turbines to the onshore transmission system is also optimised for maximum
active power transfer. A generic high voltage alternating current (HVAC) offshore transmission
system is developed in state-space and time domain for this thesis. The single line diagram (SLD)
is shown in Figure 3.1 while component speciﬁcations for the analysis in Chapter 4 are listed in
Appendix B.
Figure 3.1. A typical transmission system design for HVAC connected offshore wind farms.
The WTs of a wind farm a typically interconnected in daisy-chain conﬁguration by array cables
connecting them to the OSS. Each connection to the OSS or string, will be merged based on the
apparent power losses [16]. For this study, three strings are connected to each OSS transformer,
namely a short, medium and long string in terms of combined cable lengths resulting in 6 WTs in
total.
A 220 kV export cable connection is assumed in this thesis and close proximity of the conductors
in HV cables is well-known to incite a large capacitance, causing a large reactive power unbalance.
The shunt reactors performing the static compensation of the cable capacitance are sized for
obtaining a 100% compensation for the entirety of this thesis. Max. 40% of the compensation
can be energised simultaneous with the cable, due to the risks of zero-missing phenomenon and
complicated energisation [39]. It is assumed that the onshore reactor is energised before the cable
and the offshore reactor is energised simultaneous to the cable hence the 100% compensation is

split 60% onshore, and 40% offshore. Typically, a park controller provides the reactive power

3.1.1
Dynamic Reactive Power Compensators
Any power generating plant connected to the transmission system operator (TSO) e.g., a large wind
farm connected at a 400 kV substation, must comply to the local grid codes at the Point of Common
Coupling (PCC)[40]. Developers of large offshore wind farms can be forced to install dynamic
reactive power compensator such as a STATCOM at the onshore substation to ensure the grid code
compliance at the PCC and to mitigate bottleneck situations [4].
STATCOM
The static synchronous compensator (STATCOM) is a converter-based reactive power sink/source
in the FACTS family. The STATCOM consists of a DC/AC converter behind a coupling inductance
with a DC-side capacitor. The STATCOM reacts by controlling the terminal voltage in relation to
the bus of interest. Namely, if the voltage at its terminals is decreased it generates reactive power
which will raise the voltage [41]. The modulation circuit will not be analysed or considered in
this thesis. The current output of the STATCOM is independent of the system voltage i.e. the
reactive power changes linearly with the system voltage. However, the overloading capability of
the STATCOM is limited by the thermal limitations of the solid-state switches.
In relation to large offshore wind farms, the issues of deteriorated damping are attributed the
reduced inertia, and the strictly limited fault response of converters yielding a low fault level [27].
Furthermore, the overall grid strength at the OSS in radial offshore wind farms is deﬁned by the
system impedance. This is also an indicator of stability, as a large grid impedance introduces issues
of the WT-converters losing synchronism. Neither of these issues are directly mitigated by the
insertion of a STATCOM. Nevertheless due to the common onshore installation, the small-signal
related effects of the onshore voltage control will be investigated in Chapter 4. Furthermore, the
coexistence of multiple controllers, at a wide range of frequencies can cause interactions that can
deteriorate system damping and even cause instability [42].
Synchronous Condenser
The high controllability of the wounded rotor synchronous generator is well-deﬁned in literature
[7][19][31]. By means of a closed-loop excitation loop known as an Automatic Voltage Regulator
(AVR) the reactive power output of machine can be regulated rapidly as the machine gets under-
or overexcited. Hence the voltage at an adjacent bus can be controlled. During transient states
however, an excitation control entirely deﬁned by the AVR can reduce the damping torque of
the synchronous machine during certain system conditions [43]. For low reactance systems and
low power setpoints an AVR is supposed to introduce additional damping torque at the cost of a
negative synchronising torque component. However, when the loading of the synchronous machine
is high and the external reactance is high the opposite effect may cause oscillatory instability [7,
p. 762-766].

The inherent grid strengthening effects of the machine can suppress instability [42] and thereby
enhance the conditions for the PLL of grid-following converter as it will be shown in Chapter
4. A well-tuned power system stabiliser (PSS) can improve the oscillatory performance of the
synchronous machine further, but has not been analysed in this thesis. The low inertia of the future
power system is discussed intensively in literature as it challenges frequency stability [44]. The
synchronous condenser introduces a natural inertial response, which can be of great value in a
modern grid. The inertial impact on near-synchronous interactions between passive and active
components is analysed in Chapter 4.
The synchronous condenser offers the same ﬂexibility as the STATCOM, with an additional
overloading capability enabling full reactive power output for several seconds after the voltage
drops [44]. Furthermore, the synchronous condensers and STATCOMS has similar low-voltage
characteristics being signiﬁcantly superior to the thyristor-based Static Var Compensator (SVC).

3.3
Wind Turbine Model
For this thesis, only the Type 4 wind turbine is investigated as it is common for offshore use. The
alternative DFIG has a lower reliability compared to the permanent magnet synchronous machine
in Type 4 i.e., it is not implemented offshore as maintenance costs are signiﬁcant [50]. From the
grid perspective, the dynamics of a Type 4 WT dynamics relies on the grid-side converter and
proper modelling of controllers is necessary. The grid-side converter is typically controlling the
terminal current hence reactive and active power through various sub-controllers and modulation
strategies. Numerous control methods are available for converter-control e.g., the sequential
proportional-integral (PI) controllers [20].
The model implemented for these studies is PI-controller based with the grid voltage feedforward,
which is commonly used for current-controlled grid-following converters [20, p. 313-320]. However,
the PI-controller is unable to track a sinusoidal reference without steady-state error and the integrator
is sensitive to periodic disturbances compared to alternative controllers [21]. By introducing a
synchronous frame (dq-frame) the variables are presented as dc-values and the performance of the
PI-controllers is enhanced [51]. The overall converter-model is illustrated in Figure 3.6 consists
of sequential PI-controllers with individual applications. Furthermore, as the entire transmission
system is developed in different synchronous reference frames the interfacing signals are converted
between systems using the linearised correction matrix ∆R(θPLL) as explained in section 2.6.
Each controller will be investigated and the modelling procedure presented. The WT will eventually
be a separate subsystem as shown in Figure 3.6 consisting of the internal controllers where the
HV-side of the unit transformer will represent the interface to the grid model.
3.3.1
Limitations
By narrowing the frequency range of the model as explained in section 2.1.1, several inherent
limitations emerge. Neglecting the mechanical dynamics and generator-side converter of the
WT discards the possibility of investigating the classical electromechanical rotor angle stability.
However, based on the analysis of [22], Type 4 WTs has a minimal impact on the rotor angle

3.3.2
Phase-Locked Loop
The VSC has no mechanical coupling to the synchronously rotating power system dictating its
synchronism, hence a control loop locks the reference of the converter to the angular rotation of
the terminal-voltage. A Phase-Locked Loop (PLL) can be developed in many different shapes and
complexities [20, p.169-205]. A typical and simple PLL-structure is the synchronous reference
frame phase-locked loop (SRF-PLL) operating based on the q-axis voltage [20, p.182].
The SRF-PLL operates based on a three-phase signal which is translated to dq-quantities through
a Park Transformation (2.32), where the transformation angle θPLL is fed back from the PLL
itself [53]. The SRF-PLL is typically preferred due to its relative simplicity compared to other
PLL-structures, frequency insensitivity and inherent detection of the grid frequency and voltage
[54].

3.4
STATCOM
As mentioned in section 3.1 the STATCOM is a VSC with a DC-capacitance and high-performing
reactive power capability for a wide range of grid voltages [44]. According to IEEE the STATCOM
is a synchronous static generator that is operated as a static compensator of Vars in parallel
connection with either capacitive or inductive output current which is controlled independently of
the system voltage [60].
The STATCOM-model in Figure 3.10 is based on the same foundation as the WT-model
developed in section 3.3, considering the moving average model [6] and neglected DC-controller.
Consequently, the limitation of the STATCOM corresponds to section 3.3.1. The possibility of
including active ﬁlter functions and power oscillation dampers in the STATCOM has not been
considered for this study, as correct tuning of these parameters is beyond the scope of this thesis,
but it is noted as future work perspectives. Generally, the STATCOM is a grid-side converter with
no power-train connected i.e. its active power transfer is limited to the losses. Furthermore, the
optimal gains of the WT and STATCOM are different due to their different applications which must
be kept in mind when tuning for large-scale studies. The outer loop controller is different, as the
STATCOM performs voltage-control at a node in the system or reactive power control at a given
branch.

3.7
Time-Domain Model
To validate and support the analyses employing the linear state-space models, a time-domain model
is developed in EMTDC PSCAD v.4.6.3 with an Educational License i.e. it is limited to 200
nodes [17]. The time-domain models are all developed speciﬁcally for this thesis to ensure full
transparency of the internal controllers etc. The time-domain models are all developed utilising
standard components in PSCAD. The time-domain models are still limited to the controllers
included in the linear model. Thus, a similar structure to the state-space model is implemented as
the active components are modelled as separate subsystems, with the interconnecting electrical
network as the main system.
For a numerical tool such as PSCAD to be sufﬁciently accurate and to minimise the computational
burden, a proper time-step must be selected for the studies. The time-step is selected to 25 µs, as it
allows for a comprehensive plotting resolution while being computationally feasible.
The synchronous condenser is modelled using the internal wounded rotor synchronous machine and
the SCRX19 exciter model in PSCAD. The exciter module in PSCAD is modelled with feedback
of Ifd to enable a negative current logic which acts as an overexcitation current limitations and
mitigation of the risk of negative ﬁeld currents. Magnetic saturation is still neglected in the time
domain model, as it is not relevant for the analysed topic. Similarly, the winding distribution of the
rotor, mechanical friction etc. are also neglected. The synchronous condenser model is presented
in Appendix B.

4.1.2
Stability Criterion
Small-signal stability has been described in section 2.1 and a positive real part will cause the
mode-response to grow exponentially and potentially cause oscillatory behaviour or instability.
The suitable stability criterion is highly dependent on the system and modelling method of interest.
From classical control theory, the phase- and gain margins are well-deﬁned and easy to implement
for linear time-independent, single-input single-output (SISO) systems.
However, a complicated multi-converter high-order MIMO-system is difﬁcult to evaluate through
Bode plots and Nyquist’s Criterion [2] i.e. the properties of the eigenvalues can be utilised for
detailed and holistic investigations. As described in section 2.3 numerous explanatory variables
are related to the modes, namely the real- and imaginary-component and the natural frequency
and damping coefﬁcient. By observing the location of the modes, their margin for instability is
assessed based on their damping coefﬁcient [11]. A pole in the right-hand plane (RHP) is unstable.
Conservatively, the least damped active mode of the system corresponds to the overall damping of
the system i.e. the minimum damping of the system is a measure of stability [28].
59
==PAGE:70==
4. Eigenvalue-Based Small-Signal Stability Analysis
According to [65], the critical modes are the oscillatory modes located close to the imaginary
axis. But as some passive modes have inherent low damping with no participation from active
elements, these are not considered critical. Some of the passive modes founded in the main path of
active power may have low damping, due to the generally low resistance to minimise the power
losses. By categorisation of the modes based on participation, the relevant modes can be localised.
Considering the mode-speciﬁc participation composition, a generic ﬁgure for least damping does
not justify the comprehensive and complex system, thus the modes are categorised accordingly:
• Network Modes: Predominant participation from the electrical network with insigniﬁcant
impact from the active elements of the system. These modes are not critical for the operation
as they are inherently stable or stationary due to the passivity. Can be poorly damped due to
the low system resistance, but not unstable.
• Controller Modes: Dominant participation from the active components with minimal or no
participation from the network dynamics. The damping of these modes is typically correlated
to the tuning of the controllers. Poor damping can typically be mitigated by proper tuning.
• Interacting or Critical Modes: Combined participation from the active controllers and the
network states. These modes are interesting to analyse as they represent interactions between
different elements, and affect system dynamics signiﬁcantly. Even small participation from
active elements can affect the damping of the given mode.
For the investigation of how active components can affect the system the interacting modes are
primarily relevant. The complex participation factors for any nonzero-mode will sum to one i.e.
the participation can be seen as a percentage as explained in section 2.3. For modes with numerous
participants, the average participation per state will decrease, hence a qualitative evaluation is
necessary.

4.2.3
PLL-Bandwidth
The converter-instability caused by the nonlinear Phase-Locked Loop is deﬁned in literature
[2][55][51]. The loop-bandwidth determines the frequency and phase-lock time. A low bandwidth
reduces the level of phase noise and increases the accuracy of output at a cost of longer phase-lock
time. Contrarily a high bandwidth reduces the accuracy but the lock time is shortened [66]. Hence
a wide bandwidth under weak-grid conditions can entail the PLL to misperceive the voltage as
the voltage waveform is easily distorted. Furthermore, the load angle δ is typically larger in
weak grids resulting in larger δ ﬂuctuations when active power ﬂuctuates according to the Pδ-
relations. Frankly, reducing the bandwidth while maintaining critical damping of the controller is a
compromise between PLL settling time and system stability [67].

Summary
The purpose of this study case is to investigate the small-signal stability issues related to a wind
farms connected to a weak grid. Several critical modes show participation between passive elements
of the transmission system and the wind turbines in the subsynchronous frequency range. All
wind turbines participate in the unstable and critical modes with numerous internal controllers
contributing. The passive system is represented by inherently passive modes, but participation from
active controllers can push the modes to the RHP.
The base case is unstable for PLL-bandwidths above 48 Hz, as highlighted with the purple mode
during no-load operation in section 4.2.3. Hence, the fast-responding wide-bandwidth utilised for
strong grid conditions is not convenient for weak grid connections i.e., site-speciﬁc tuning of the
PLL can improve the system stability. Reducing the PLL-bandwidth is a possible mitigation to
this instability. As the active power transfer increases, it has a damping effect on the PLL-dictated
critical mode marked with purple.
Another critical mode marked with blue highlights an increased cross-axis participation during
full-load conditions. The q-axis inner-loop dominated mode with additional participation from the
PLL and passive system gets interfered by an increasing d-axis participation during full-load. The
mode to become unstable at cable lengths above 148 km. The damping of the blue mode can be
improved by reducing the PLL-bandwidth, or potentially by enlarging the coupling inductance.
• Blue Mode: Constitutes the q-axis inner loop controller, grid and onshore transformer. The
cross-axis interactions increase at high power setpoints deteriorating the damping. Unstable
as cable length (impedance) becomes too large.
• Green Mode: Lower-frequency exponent for the ﬁrst impedance resonance peak. The
frequency decreases with cable length due to additional capacitance. Gets damped by
increasing PLL-bandwidth, as their mode-frequencies align.
• Purple Mode: PLL and numerous passive elements. Becomes unstable for the base case
with PLL-bandwidths above 48 Hz at no-load. This mode gets damped as active power
increases.

Rating
A synchronous machine generally can be simpliﬁed to a voltage source behind an impedance [7,
p.184-188] - where the rating is effectively determining the impedance. A high rating entails a low
impedance and thereby a better reinforcement of the grid at the connected and adjacent busses.
The angular rotation of the bus voltage becomes reluctant to momentary perturbations. The modes
related to the angular rotation and dq-reference systems are expected to be affected positively, and
the interactions between the active and passive elements damped. The compensation equipment
at the onshore substation is installed for compliance purposes, thus the SynCon rating affects the
mode-damping and small-signal stability in general but also a compliance aspect. The grid code
compliance is not considered in this thesis.

rating of the unit transformer. As the machine rating is incremented between 50 MVA and 500 MVA
the mode trajectories are presented in Figure 4.14, where the full-load case without the SynCon

the base case marked with red is assigned to differences in linearisation point as the SynCon has no

changes in rotational speed and thereby represents the ability to oppose changes in system frequency
[68]. Consequently, inertia has a large impact on the frequency- and transient stability as high
inertia is stabilising the system. The inertia of a machine or system can be quantiﬁed by the inertia
constant H deﬁned by (4.2).

The inertia constant is utilised in the dynamic modelling based on Newtons 2. Law as explained in

From classical small-signal stability investigations of conventional power systems the modes are
categorised as low-frequency global and local power oscillations between generators [7]. For such
electromechanical dynamics, the inertia has a large impact on the overall dynamic response. Based
on [22] the WT- and WF-participation in inter-area modes is typically orders of magnitudes smaller
than the impact from conventional synchronous generators as explained in section 2.1.1. The

the SynCon and onshore power plants as it is outside the scope of this thesis.

Grid strength is a term related to the voltage-sensitivity following a fault or disturbance in the
power system [69]. A weak grid has a relatively low short circuit level, and is susceptible to voltage
changes and typically affected by load ﬂuctuations [12]. The system impedance generally deﬁnes
the short circuit contribution or strength of a grid equivalent. The Short Circuit Ratio (SCR) (4.3)
is used as an indicator of grid strength at any system node[69][12].

• Increased harmonic distortion.
• Instability of plant voltage control systems.
For this thesis, the SCR is calculated based on the minimum short circuit with no contribution

Overall Summary
The overall effects of the different parameters analysed in this chapter are summarised in Table
4.9. Most of the studies are conducted at full-power setpoints to induce the worst-conditions for
the PLL- and q-axis dynamic interacting mode marked with blue. However, no-load conditions
introduced the worst conditions for the PLL and d-axis dynamics based on section 4.2.1. Eventually,
these results are not directly applicable for tuning-studies due to to the large interconnection of
parameters and extensive studies should be made for each component to the optimise the settings.
Based on section 4.4 the presence of a SynCon is not unambiguously positive. However, in section
4.5.2, the SynCon generally had a stabilising effect on a weak system. Thus, the SynCon is not
necessarily improving the system damping signiﬁcantly for the comparably strong grid of section
4.4.1, but it is reducing the system sensitivity to grid-perturbations, and making the modes reluctant
to move. It strengthens the grid and minimises the dynamics of angular velocity caused by grid
perturbations, as it mitigates the risk of WT-controllers interacting with the circuit elements in the
near-synchronous frequency range. The impact is highly dependent on the MVA-rating, and the
grid properties at which it is inserted. A higher machine rating yields a smaller impedance and a
coherent improvement of the operating conditions for the converters. Based on the grid strength
investigation in section 4.5 the SynCon will have signiﬁcant effects for very-weak grids. The AVR
parameters can contribute to stabilising SynCon-related internal and interacting modes by selecting
a low proportional gain. Contrarily, a large proportional gain can destabilise the system. The
PLL-related stability issues are described in section 4.2. Stability is enhanced by decreasing the
PLL-bandwidth. The onshore SynCon reduces the equivalent Thévenin impedance seen from the
WTs and improves their capability of voltage-angle tracking, mitigating the risk of WTs losing
synchronism. As the voltage becomes less sensitive to perturbations, the closed-loop action of the
PLL is better stabilised.
The STATCOM has a minimal impact on the system modes in the low-frequency range. Partly, due
to the large impedance partially separating the voltage control offshore and onshore. Furthermore,
as the grid strength is unaffected, the operating conditions of the WTs are not improved i.e., an
oscillatory mechanism can still occur as the WTs deﬁnes the grid state. Through the analysis in
section 4.3 it impacts the higher frequencies, enabling damping-improvement by tuning of the
coupling inductance and controller settings.
4.6.1
Uncertainties
To investigate speciﬁc cases and detailed design studies, the sensitivity testing approach of this
thesis is not optimal. However, for mapping the overall dynamics and system dependencies it is
useful. It must be emphasised that the developed WT-, STATCOM and SynCon-model utilised,
does not enable direct and generic application of these results, but rather introduces a conceptual
investigation. However, due to the transparency of these models, it is possible to adapt the platform
with relative ease. The simpliﬁcations to the converter-models in section 3.3.1, and the overall
assumptions of the frequency range in section 2.1.1 affects the overall applicability for wide-range
studies. Furthermore, the absence of the DC-controller may hide the severity of the issues, as the
nonlinear DC-controller can cause damping deterioration, in a similar manner as the PLL [11].
The cross-component interactions depend on the speciﬁc point of linearisation, hence the observed
dynamics may differentiate for other typologies, setpoints or controller-tunings. Furthermore, the
outcome of these studies in Table 4.9 are all subject to the limitations of section 1.3.3.

5
CONCLUSION
This chapter concludes the thesis and summarises the ﬁndings. The purpose of this thesis is to
analyse the small-signal stability of large-scale offshore wind farms connected to weak grids,
focusing on mitigation measures such as the synchronous condenser. The study is regarding
converter-instability and interactions between passive transmission system elements and the active
elements of the wind farm in the near-synchronous frequency range.
5.1
Results and Summary
The component speciﬁcations needed for state-space modelling for small-signal stability
investigations are severely dependent on the frequency range of interest. For the study of component-
interactions in the near-synchronous frequency range, a moving average-based model has been
utilised for the converter-based wind turbines. This approach limits the number of relevant sub-
components and eases the modelling burden with minimal loss of accuracy in the scoped frequency
range. Through detailed aggregation-methods, the collector system of the wind farm is represented
by equivalent wind turbines. The passive transmission system is represented by differential
equations to consider the electromagnetic dynamics in the interactions between passive and active
components. Each subsystem and iteration of the state-space model is validated by comparison
to a developed time-domain model. The eigenvalue-based methodology is utilised for analysing
system stability and the complex interactions of the system components. The participation analysis
is utilised for determining the root causes and potential mitigation measures of the unstable modes.
The inner loop and phase-locked loop of the wind turbines participate in numerous multi-component
interacting-modes, where the damping is largely affected by the system characteristics and
conditions. The overall sensitivity of the system has been analysed and discussed in Chapter
4 and summarised in Table 4.9. The interconnecting impedance from the WTs to the PCC affects
the spectre of stable operating points and controller settings. A weak grid connection introduces a
larger sensitivity to the mode-locations thus larger risk of instability. Consequently, a weak grid
calls for detailed controller tuning for optimal operation. The PLL-bandwidth has a large impact
on the sensitivity of the wind farm modes and can cause unstable modes if selected too high.
A speciﬁc interacting-mode between the WTs and multiple passive components becomes unstable
for particularly weak-grid conditions, causing the system to diverge. By installing a synchronous
condenser at the onshore substation, this mode was pushed to a stable location, due to the stabilising
participation from the synchronous condenser. The damping effect of the synchronous machine is
largest for the weakest grid conditions, as it improves the damping by up to 16% in these studies.
The impact on overall wind farm stability is near negligible or even deteriorating for a strong grid
connection dependent on the AVR settings and system setpoint. The damping of modes related
to the angular references and power transfer is increased, due to the reduction of the equivalent
impedance seen from the wind turbines. Other critical modes become less sensitive to changes in
grid parameters by the presence of a synchronous condenser.
88
==PAGE:99==
5. Conclusion
The synchronous condenser rating has a signiﬁcant impact on the stabilising effect in regards to
critical modes. To some extent, a large machine improves the damping, but it is highly dependent
on the impedance-proﬁle of the machine. The damping of the electrical modes with participation
from the synchronous condenser is increased by a larger armature resistance. The damper winding
resistance can affect the damping of the synchronous condenser dominated modes as it affects the
dynamics during changes in angular velocity. The machine inertia has a marginal impact on the
small-signal stability of the system. Finally, the AVR settings can contribute to the damping by
larger time constants and overdamped controller settings.
A STATCOM is typically installed onshore at large offshore wind farms to ensure grid code
compliance. Based on these studies, the STATCOM has a marginal impact on the near-synchronous
modes. Through proper selection of the terminal inductance, the damping of supersynchronous
modes can be affected positively. However, for high-frequency modes, the converter-based
components are better analysed through a complete converter model.
5.2
Perspectives
The presented analysis sheds light on an expanded small-signal stability methodology currently
being matured in the industry and research communities. For an improved understanding of the
underlying dynamics, alternative methods could be an interesting addition, as the physics can get
effaced in the eigenvalue-based analysis.
The results provide a holistic overview of near-synchronous characteristics and interactions between
large-scale converter-based generation and passive grid elements. The presented methodology can
be utilised for investigating boundary conditions in terms of transient stability time-domain studies
etc., by adopting case-speciﬁc component and system-parameters. Furthermore, the eigenvalue-
based study allows for investigating mitigation measures through the participation analysis. For
investigations at broader frequencies e.g. for classical system rotor-angle stability or harmonic
stability assessments the developed model must be expanded for proper veracity. However, due to
the segmented structure, additional controllers or components can be added at relative ease.
5.3
Future work
The following questions arise based on the present study, due to the broad complexity of power
system stability, the low-frequency interactions of wind farms, and the limitations of this thesis:
• Analyse the extended small-signal stability by means of other methods, to obtain a better
understanding of the underlying dynamics of cross-component interactions.
• Analyse the potential beneﬁts of active damping loops such as the Power System Stabilisers
at the synchronous condenser, and Power Oscillation Dampers and active ﬁltering capabilities
of the STATCOM. Principally, PODs can be applied to the WTs as an additional outer loop.
• Other frequency ranges of the issues. Specify the models for mechanical rotor angle power
oscillations, or for high-frequency harmonics to ensure the harmonic stability beyond the
frequency-limit for the applied model of this thesis.
• Make a partially or fully automated tuning algorithm based on the sensitivity approach
utilised in this thesis to maximise system damping.
• Analyse the additional aspects of system stability such as frequency- and transient stability to
highlight the full potential beneﬁts and risks of installing a synchronous condenser onshore.

D VECTOR FITTING
In certain subsystems neither the transfer function or internal state space equations are accessible
e.g. for commercial wind turbine models or cable models considering frequency dependency.
For these types of subsystems the so-called vector ﬁtting [45] [46] [47] can be used to indirectly
reconstruct or approximate the state space behaviour of such a system. In the example of the WT,
the internal state space equations rely on the control structure which consequently must be well-
known and deﬁned. For this thesis a generic grid side converter control loop has been developed
hence VF algorithms will not be necessary for the WT. However, the wide range of frequencies
involved in converter systems may require a valid frequency dependent representation of black box
subsystems[28]. Vector ﬁtting enables implementation of rational approximations of a numerical
terminal characteristic of a black box model. By utilisation of a rational approximation through a
robust and iterative linear least square process a stable pole-set representing the numerical input
data is obtained [45]-[47]. The algorithm outputs a rational function expressed as a sum of partial
fractions given by (D.1).
f(s) ≈

Where f(s) is the approximation as a function of the Laplace operator s, N is the order of the
approximation, rm and pm are the residues and poles respectively. Furthermore, D is the feed-
through gain matrix and E is only present if the transfer function [45] - [47]. Practically, the
procedure is fed numerical values or measurements from a stable system i.e. the output poles and
approximation in general will be stable [28]. To realise the poles and residues into the state space
model of the adjacent system some equivalents can be made to electrical components according to
[2, p.143-145].
Order
Similar to any approximations of deﬁnite fractions, the chosen order is of high importance for the
accuracy of the VF. Choosing a too low order can yield inaccuracies as the number of poles will
not match the resolution required, while too high orders can cause unstable poles to occur due to
overﬁtting. Consequently, the order should be selected to the minimum order that can minimise the
ﬁtting error to an acceptable level [28]. To illustrate the impact of the selected order an impedance
has been ﬁtted with numerous orders in Figure D.1 within a frequency range of up to 2 kHz. The
low-order approximation of N = 4 is only capable of approximating the initial 250 Hz, and is not
adapting for the resonance peaks. However, by increasing the order for N = 8, the approximation
is performing signiﬁcantly better, as the error is reduced in Figure D.1(b). Finally, when increasing
the order excessively, where N = 60 overﬁtting is clearly observed as the model is not capable of
approximating the ﬁnal resonance of the trained frequency area.

Passivity
In the example above the vector ﬁtting procedure is utilised for approximating the frequency
characteristics of a series impedance up to 2 kHz. But the behaviour of the model at frequencies
beyond the trained area is not obvious from Figure D.1. Passivity is characterised by a component
or system having positive real part of its equivalent admittance for all frequencies i.e. contribute to
system damping for any frequency [74]. Basically, passivity is a condition of stability as any system
only consisting of passive elements is always stable [75]. However, passivity of all components
can be impossible, as electrical machines and converters have frequency-regions of negative real
part due to the nonlinearities. Consequently, it is critical to ensure that said regions of impassivity
does not appear in critical frequency ranges [75].
