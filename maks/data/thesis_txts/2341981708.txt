II
RESUMÉ
Fra et tidligere speciale vides det, at der er et stort potentiale for at bruge hustagene 
og restbæreevnen i store dele af de eksisterende bygninger i København, opført 
imellem 1850 og 1930. Med denne viden som grundlag vil der blive udarbejdet et 
forslag til et nyt koncept for opførelse af fleretagers overbygninger på toppen af den 
eksisterende bygningsmasse, stræbende efter tilføjelse af minimum tre ekstra etager. 
Specialet starter med en introduktion af de to projekter, der gav idéen til at bygge 
fleretagers tilbygninger på toppen af eksisterende bygninger. Begge projekter er 
placeret i Hamburg, Tyskland, og er blevet brugt som inspiration i udviklingen af 
konceptet for overbygning på københavnske bytage.
Der er blevet gennemført en række baggrundsstudier til undersøgelse af de aspekter, 
der er relevante for projektet. Befolkningsvæksten i København undersøges med 
henblik på den byfortætning, der lige nu foregår. Studierne er gennemført med et 
blik mod fremtiden, hvor det voksende behov for boliger i København forventes at 
fortsætte det næste årti.
I planlægningen af en tilbygning er bevaringsværdien en vigtig faktor. Opgaven 
beskriver derfor kortfattet det system, der i Danmark benyttes til at identificere fredede 
bygninger samt til fastlægning af bevaringsværdien for bygninger bygget før 1940.
Hovedfokus i baggrundstudierne er de grundlæggende principper, der gælder for det 
murede byggeri i København, der er bygget i perioden fra 1850 til 1930. Dette omfatter 
de regler, der var gældende i den betragtede periode samt den byggeteknologi, der 
blev anvendt, hvor også de forskellige bygningsdele, der er vigtige for projektet, 
bliver beskrevet. Studierne danner grundlag for et case studie, der bliver udført af en 
udvalgt karré i København.
Det tidligere speciale, som er nævnt ovenfor, er revurderet for at granske de 
beregninger, der er blevet udarbejdet, for at finde ud af om den samme restbæreevne 
ligeledes er til stede i andre etageboligbyggerier i København. Som allerede nævnt, 
udvælges en karré i det centrale København til et case studie. Den betragtede 
bygning er bygget af massivt murværk og bliver brugt som fundament for den nye 
konstruktion. Bygningen er nøje registreret, hvor det omkringliggende område, 
bygningens geometri, arkitektoniske udtryk, bygningsdele og konstruktive system er 
beskrevet som forberedelse til udviklingen af konceptet.
Case studiet gennemføres ved både en konstruktionsanalyse samt en lys- og 
skyggeanalyse af de eksisterende forhold. Til disse analyser er en mindre del af 
bygningen udvalgt til dybdegående undersøgelser. Denne del af bygningen er den, 
der bedst repræsenterer det typiske murede byggeri i København.
Den statiske undersøgelse af de eksisterende forhold viser, at konstruktionen 
har restbæreevne. Derfor er det muligt at udvikle et koncept for tilføjelse af et 
antal ekstra etager oven på den eksisterende konstruktion. I udviklingen af 
konstruktionsudformningen bliver det undersøgt, om den eksisterende konstruktion 
kan bære den tilføjede ekstra vægt, hvilket afhænger af, hvordan lasten bliver overført 
til de eksisterende nedenstående vægge.
Et koncept for at bygge fleretagers tilbygninger på toppen af eksisterende 
bygningskonstruktioner er udviklet ved brug af forskellige metoder med hensyn til valg 
af konstruktionsmateriale, lys- og skyggeforhold, arkitektonisk udtryk, bærende system 
og bygningsreglementet. Den del af bygningen, der bliver undersøgt i case studiet, 
benyttes ligeledes i konceptudviklingen. Den arkitektoniske og konstruktionsmæssige 
udformning udføres for denne del af bygningen, der består af seks lejligheder i alt, der 
er fordelt på tre etager.
Som en del af konceptudviklingen, indeholder rapporten en materiale-sammenligning

af tre forskellige materialer til det bærende system – stål, armeret beton og 
krydslamineret træ (CLT). Sammenligningen vedrører forskellige aspekter såsom 
vægt, CO2-udledning, bæredygtighed, brand og mekaniske egenskaber. Formålet 
med undersøgelsen er at understrege, at CLT er det mest optimale materiale til brug 
i konceptet. Derfor bruges CLT elementer til opbygningen af det bærende system.
Løsninger, til samlinger i overgangen mellem de tilføjede etager og den eksisterende 
bygning, er undersøgt i detaljer, for at sikre at de vandrette og lodrette kræfter 
overføres til det eksisterende bærende system.
Undersøgelserne i specialet fokuserer på opførelsen af tre ekstra etager på toppen 
af den eksisterende bygning. Dette er gjort med henblik på fremtiden. Idealistiske 
omstændigheder er visualiseret – helt uden begrænsninger fra det danske 
bygningsreglement, der inkluderer regler om højdegrænseplan, brandforhold og 
andre bestemmelser. Dog beskrives og diskuteres de regler, der er relevante for 
projektet i forbindelse med de udfordringer, der opstår, når der bygges i højden i 
Danmark. Løsninger, til hvordan det er muligt at håndtere den nuværende lovgivning, 
er fremført.
Som en afslutning på specialet vil et forslag til et endeligt koncept blive udviklet ved 
hjælp af den opnåede viden. Specialet påpeger at det er muligt at tilføje tre ekstra 
etager på toppen af den eksisterende bygningsmasse i København.

ABSTRACT
From a previous master thesis, it is known that there is a big potential for using the 
rooftops and the remaining load bearing capacity in many of the existing buildings 
in Copenhagen, built in the years between 1850 and 1930. With this knowledge, a 
proposal for a new concept is developed for building multi-storey additions on top of 
the existing building stock, aiming for minimum three extra levels.
The thesis starts by introducing the two projects that gave the idea of building multi-
storey additions on top of existing building structures. Both projects are placed in 
Hamburg, Germany, and has been used as inspiration in the development of the 
rooftop addition concept.
Background studies are performed in order to investigate essential aspects that are 
of relevance for the project. The populations growth in Copenhagen is investigated in 
correlation with the urban densification that takes place just now. The studies are done 
with a view to the future, where the increased demand for housing in Copenhagen are 
expected to continue over the next decade. 
When planning an addition to an existing building, the preservation value of the 
building is a factor of concern, and the thesis is shortly putting forward the system that 
in Denmark is used to identify listed buildings and to state the preservation value for 
building built before 1940. 
The main focus in the background studies are the basic principles of the masonry 
buildings in Copenhagen, built in the period 1850-1930. This includes the regulations 
that were valid in this period as well as the building technology used, where the 
different building elements, which are important for the project, are described. The 
investigations form the basis of a case study that is performed of a selected building 
block in Copenhagen.
The previous master thesis, mentioned above, is reviewed in order to scrutinise the 
calculations made, and to find out if the same residual bearing capacity will be found for 
other multi-storey masonry buildings in Copenhagen. As already mentioned, a housing 
block in the central Copenhagen is selected for a case study. The considered building 
is built of solid masonry and is used as foundation for the new structure. The building 
is registered in detail and the surrounding area, building geometry, architectural 
style, building elements and structural system are described in preparation for the 
development of the concept for a rooftop addition. 
The case study is completed by both a structural, and a light- and shadow analysis 
of the current circumstances. One smaller part of the block is chosen for thoroughly 
investigations, which is the part of the building block that best represents a typical 
multi-storey masonry building in Copenhagen.
The structural analysis of the existing conditions shows that the structure has residual 
load bearing capacity. It is therefore possible to develop a concept for adding extra 
floors to the existing building structure, that will be referred to as “the rooftop addition” 
throughout the report. The development of the structural design of the rooftop addition 
examines if the existing structure can carry the extra weight added, which depends on 
how the loads get transferred to the existing walls below. 
A concept for the rooftop addition is developed by using different methods, concerning 
choice of material, light- and shadow conditions, architectural expression, structural 
design and building regulations. The part of the building, that is investigated in the 
case study, is used in the development of the concept as well. The structural and 
architectural design is performed for this part of the building, which consist of six 
apartments in total that are divided between three floors. 
As a part of the concept development, the report includes a comparison of using three 
different building materials for the structural system – steel, reinforced concrete, and 
==PAGE:7==
V
cross laminated timber (CLT). The comparison concerns various aspects such as 
weight, CO2 emission, sustainability, fire conditions and mechanical properties. The 
objective for these investigations is to emphasize that CLT is the most optimal material 
to use for the construction of the rooftop-housing concept. This is why CLT elements 
are used for the structural system. 
Solutions for the connection between the multi-storey addition and the existing building 
are examined in detail, in order to make sure that the horizontal and vertical loads are 
transferred to the existing structural system.
In this project the investigations focus on constructing three extra levels on top of 
the existing structure. This is done with a view to the future. Idealistic circumstances 
are pictured – without the constraints of Danish rules and laws regarding building 
heights, fire conditions, as well as requirements from other building regulations and 
district plans. However, the regulations of relevance for the project are described and 
discussed in regard to the challenges that arise, when building in height in Denmark. 
Solutions on how to deal with the present legislations are outlined.
With this knowledge, a proposal for a final concept is developed in the end of the 
thesis for building multi-storey additions on top of the existing building stock. The 
thesis underlines that it is achievable to add extra floors on top of the existing building 
stock in Copenhagen.

1.1  BACKGROUND
Copenhagen is growing – the Danish inhabitants seek towards bigger cities. Since 
1995 the Copenhagen population has risen by 23% and the growth of the population 
is anticipated to continue towards 2025. [Kbh Nøgletal, 2015] This tendency will put 
pressure on the real estate market in the future. Thus innovative solutions for design 
and construction of housing will be necessary.
The city of New York is a good example of a city where the population is continuously 
growing. It is therefore needed to utilize the space of the city to its full potential in order 
to create residential spaces for new inhabitants. One way this need is accommodated 
is by building homes on top of the existing neighbourhoods around the city. In 
Hamburg the vertical space is used to its advantage as well. The Elbphilharmonie 
concert hall, which is under construction, is built on top of an existing warehouse.
In the municipality of Copenhagen, a rapid activity in construction work takes place 
at present. Several new areas are included in the densification of the city, where 
mainly office buildings and multi-storey buildings for housing are built. An examples is 
Ørestad, which is an area that accommodates around 20.000 residents. Additionally, 
Nordhavn in the Northern Copenhagen is under a large development that will result in 
7.000 workplaces and 2.500 new residents. [Clausen & Ingdam, 2012] It shows that 
the expansion of the city takes place in areas near the city centre, that already are 
relatively dense. This tendency may in the long term result in a lack of space, where 
every last square meter of land within the boundaries of Copenhagen is going to be 
used.
The need for more space and more homes is expected to increase, and at some point 
the only possibility of accommodating this need is to make use of the vertical space. 
A great part of the rooftops in Copenhagen are not used. It is estimated that around 
200.000 m2 flat roofs are at disposal, equalling 40 soccer fields, which have a great 
potential of adding extra floors on top of them. These rooftops can therefore be a part 
of a solution for the further densification of Copenhagen. [Clausen & Ingdam, 2012] 
A previous master thesis, about utilisation of Copenhagen rooftops [Clausen & 
Ingdam, 2012], shows that the existing masonry buildings built between 1850-1950 
have residual load bearing capacity. Based on these results, it is expected that the 
existing building stock can be extruded several floors, which can be utilized to house 
newcomers to Copenhagen. 
The thesis is a vision for the future. The requirements for green building and the impact 
on the environment will highly probable be stricter for new construction in the future. 
Green building and sustainability are key aspects of concern in the present and future 
construction industry, due to the fact that the building sector accounts for almost 40% 
of the CO2-emission in Denmark, and up to 75% of the climate burden derives from 
construction materials. [COWI] [Ingeniøren]
This background forms the basis for further investigation in this master thesis.

1.2  PROBLEM STATEMENT
The problem statement is based on the abovementioned aspects, and is formulated 
by stating two overall questions followed by a further description of the issues that will 
be addressed, as follows:
What if we in Copenhagen could have the same approach and attitude towards 
transformation of the existing building stock and utilization of the vertical space as 
seen in other big cities around the world? 
How will a proposal for a concept of a rooftop addition look, based on the residual 
load bearing capacity of the existing masonry buildings in Copenhagen?
This master thesis aims to cover and investigate the qualities and issues that are 
present in the implementation of building additions on top of existing masonry buildings 
in Copenhagen. It is desired to study whether it is possible to make use of the existing 
building stock as foundation for an addition of more floors. In that connection, it is 
essential to examine what initiatives that are necessary for the project to be realised. 
This will be done by selecting one building stock that represents a typical masonry 
building built in the period 1850-1950. The thesis will scrutinise the building block on 
the basis of the calculations known from former investigations of the residual load 
bearing capacity of existing masonry buildings. It will be vital to determine which 
elements in the existing structure that can be further loaded, and how the loads from 
a rooftop addition can be transferred to these elements. Therefore, it is desired to 
make a proposal for a system that will transfer the loads between the existing building 
structure and the new rooftop addition. 
When building in the height, there are particular aspects in the Danish Building 
Regulations that have to be taken into account. There will therefore be a need for 
considering these aspects in relation to the project. It is significant to critically relate 
to the relatively strict requirements regarding for instance height-limiting plane, fire 
performance, and accessibility. Another problem that arises, which needs to be 
addressed when building in the height, is the shadows that will be casted on the 
surrounding buildings. 
By investigating the problems stated, a final concept for the construction of a rooftop 
addition can be made.

4.2  LISTED AND WORTH PRESERVING 	
	
BUILDINGS
When planning an addition to a building a major factor of concern is the preservation 
value of the building. This value is based on an assessment of the architectural, 
culture-historical and environmental value of the individual buildings. The originality 
and conditions of the buildings comes into play as well. In Denmark a system has been 
developed to chart the architectural heritage, identify listed buildings and state the 
preservation value for buildings. The system is called SAVE, Survey of Architectural 
Values in the Environment, and is containing classification for buildings built before 
1940. The purpose of the SAVE method is to have a tool that guides the citizens when 
rebuilding and in that way take care of the Danish building culture. It is also used by 
politicians to get an overview of the culture-historical and architectural qualities in the 
different municipalities and to secure that the development in the cities is done without 
breaking the outlined guidelines. [Bevaringsværdige Bygninger]
The method is based on an evaluation of the five following building circumstances: 
[Kulturstyrelsen]
	
	
•    Architectural value
	
	
•    Culture-historical value
	
	
•    Environmental value
	
	
•    Originality
	
	
•    Condition
Each of these circumstances are assigned a value on a scale from 1 to 9, which in the 
end are summed up to get the overall preservation value. The final value is found by an 
overall impression of the quality and condition of the building, but it is the architectural 
value and the culture-historical value that are of greatest importance. [Kulturstyrelsen]
Values between 4 and 6 are considered as medium values, which implies that the 
building is in good condition, but renovations that does not fit the original character and 
style of the building have been made. Values between 1 and 3 are considered as high 
values. A preservation value on 3 means that colours, materials and the architectural 
appearance have to be as the building originally was built with. The preservation value 
1 means that the building is listed, and very high demands, both internal and external, 
are valid for the maintenance of the condition and originality of the building. When a 
building has a preservation value of 1 or 2 it is almost impossible to go through with 
even small modifications. With a preservation value of 3 it is possible to do changes 
that do not affect the architectural expression towards the street. [Clausen & Ingdam, 
2012] See examples on buildings with the nine different preservation values on next 
page.

4.3  MASONRY BUILDINGS 1850-1930
In the period considered, running from 1850 to 1930, the valid building legislations 
were crucial to the design and construction of the buildings built. The procedure was 
in this way very standardised, which resulted in fundamentally identical masonry 
buildings. This is the reason why it is important to study and investigate both the 
regulations that were valid back then and the building technology that was used. The 
chapter will also contain a section with descriptions of the different building elements 
that are considered important for the project. 
The references used in the following sections are [SBi 142], [Danske Bygningsmodeller], 
[Dansk Byggeskik] and [Clausen & Ingdam, 2012].
4.3.1  REGULATIONS
Copenhagen’s first complete and modern construction legislation became effective 
May 1st 1856. It was ground-breaking by its several clearly formulated requirements 
concerning construction- and material conditions in the common buildings within 
housing. These requirements had a content and were drawn up so that they could 
be carried on almost unchanged in the later legislations in Copenhagen. The reason 
why these laws were remaining steady for such a long period was that no significant 
changes were made within the common practice of building technology. There was 
only exceptionally a need for engineering assistance, because the requirements of 
the law were so accurate written. [SBi 142]
With the experience from two big fires in the 18th century the legislation stated that 
brick-built masonry had to be used as main construction material. Exterior walls 
were constructed of masonry and inner walls were made by either masonry, timber 
frames or double board partitions. Horizontal divisions and roof trusses were wooden 
constructions and the roof was covered by tile, slate or metal. [SBi 142]
Requirements regarding the layout and geometry of the building were drawn up as 
well. The room height had to be a minimum of 2,5 m, basements used for housing 
could maximum be placed 1,25 m below street level and access to two staircases 
from all apartments was required. In the legislation of 1856 the maximal height of a 
building was set to 15,70 m. The building height was measured from the ground up 
to the top of the cornice, which meant that the height of the roof construction had to 
be added, see Figure 4.3 below The typical depth of a building was 10 m and the 
maximal slope of the roof was 45°, which resulted in a height of the roof construction 
of maximum 5 m. The total height from the ground to the ridge had to be maximum 
20,70 m. [Clausen & Ingdam, 2012]
The legislation of June 30th 1871 encompassed only few modifications. The main idea 
with the publication was to outline rules to be valid throughout the city of Copenhagen. 
[SBi 142]
Figure 4.3: Left – Maximum allowed total building height in 1956 (33 alen = 20,7 m). Right – 
Maximum allowed total building height in 1989 (37 alen = 23,2 m). [SBi 142]

The legislation of 1889 was basically a continuation of the two earlier laws, but some 
new regulations were added and the passed on requirements were in most cases 
tighten up. The most essential change was the regulations regarding inner walls. For 
the load bearing inner walls it became required to use at least 1-brick thick solid 
masonry. This new regulation caused that inner walls made as timber frames became 
unusable in practice. Some other vital additions were the demand for daylight access 
to the living spaces and specifications for the minimum sizes of these rooms. [SBi 142]
From 1728 throughout the 19th century the measurement system consisted of the 
following units (written in Danish):
1 favn = 3 alen = 6 fod = 72 tommer = 864 linjer
That changed in 1907 where the metric system was implemented in Denmark. The 
exact conversion from the former units to the new metric system was as follows:
1 alen = 0,6277 m and 1 tomme = 2,615 cm
In this way the actual content in the legislation was not changed, but it made it

4.3.2  BUILDING TECHNOLOGY
After 1850 the change in and the development of the building technology was big 
compared to earlier periods. Many innovations within construction and products 
emerged in the end of the 19th century, as well as in the beginning of the 20th 
century. However, the development within housing, such as blocks of flats, was not 
considerable. [Dansk Byggeskik]
By “block of flats” is meant buildings constructed with several storeys, where 
residences are arranged at each floor. In the period, the same layout was generally 
seen in all apartments. The basis for the layout was that stairs and kitchens were 
placed across from each other. From here a varying number of rooms emerged, which 
were connected to a hallway. An example of a typical floor plan can be seen in Figure

The strict regulations introduced in the middle of the 19th century represented a 
landmark for the construction of buildings in general. A certain standard was required 
for buildings, which had a decisive impact on the quality level of the buildings that were 
constructed in the following years. Even if these new and tighten building regulations 
became effective, the buildings, especially within housing, were constructed with 
almost the same technology as seen before. As earlier, the materials used were wood 
and masonry and the load bearing structure consisted of longitudinal inner and outer 
walls with floor joists spanning crosswise. Besides working as partitions, the function 
of the transverse walls was to support the building in the horizontal direction. [SBi 142]
In accordance with the building technology described, five different trades were 
represented at the construction site; the bricklayer, the carpenter, the joiner, the

The architectural style for the period did not have much impact on the building 
technology used at that time. The opposite was more likely the case – the building 
technology formed the basis of the architectural appearance, because of the strict 
legislations that resulted in an established approach for the construction of buildings. 
[Engelmark, 2013]

4.3.3  BRICKWORK
The following section is mainly based on [SBI 248].
The residual bearing capacity of the masonry depends on the strength properties 
of the brickwork. The purpose of the section is therefore to give a brief overview of 
which strength properties that have been chosen for use in the calculations of the 
brickwork, and on what basis they have been chosen. Additionally, the dimensions of 
the brickwork will be decisive for how much the brickwork can carry. That is why the 
dimensions of the components in the brickwork are introduced as well.
The strength properties of the brickwork are dependent on the strength properties of 
the stones and mortar respectively. An insight into the type and composition of the 
two materials is therefore essential, although not much valuable information exist. The 
majority of all the multi-storey masonry buildings, built between 1850-1930, were built 
as massive walls and the mortar used was lime mortar. The strength of the lime mortar 
is not known, besides the knowledge of its low strength. The brickwork was built to 
meet the regulations at the time. The regulations made demands on the thickness of 
the masonry, but did not take a stand to the strength of it. [SBI 248] 
There was an estimation of 700 brickwork factories producing approximately 500 
million bricks for the buildings at the time. The bricks used in the multi-storey masonry 
buildings were therefore manufactured at several various factories that used their own 
individual methods and materials to produce the bricks. This is, together with the lack 
of strength regulations, the major reasons for why the strength of the brickwork from 
the time period studied is unknown. [SBI 248]  
A requisite, when building on top of the existing building structure, is to have sufficient 
knowledge about the strength properties of the masonry. Based on what is known 
from the manufacturing and regulations at the time, no prior knowledge is obtained 
about the strength of the specific bricks. The Eurocode 6 recommends that the 
characteristic compression strength of a brickwork should be determined from testing 
a specimen. [DS/EN 1996-1-1] This is especially relevant when determining the 
==PAGE:34==

strength of existing brickwork, due to the fact that the brickwork has been standing for 
50-100 years. During these years, the brickwork has been exposed to different loads, 
and movements have simultaneously occurred, caused by moisture and temperature 
variations. However, if the strength of the masonry is decreasing over the years, it 
will be noticeable in the surface of the brickwork. [SBI 248] A sample of the actual 
brickwork can help determine the real conditions of the brickwork. The testing can

As a base for [SBI 248] tests have been made in report [SBI 2015:02], which investigates

strength and stiffness of a given brickwork. The equation for the modulus of elasticity

FOUNDATIONS
Underneath all load bearing inner and exterior walls foundations were laid. The 1856 
regulations concerning foundations were basically based on their function and use. 
The buildings were to be constructed on a foundation that corresponded to the size 
and weight of the building. The traditional masonry buildings contained many load 
bearing walls and were therefore naturally on the heavy side of the weight scale. 
Besides the building conditions, the soil conditions had to be taken into account 
when constructing the foundations. These function based rules were results of the 
extremely diverse soil conditions in Copenhagen. Furthermore, it would naturally be in 
the owners own interest to construct a solid foundation.
The soils bearing capacity were deciding for the geometry of the foundations. On a 
solid ground strip footings were constructed in a wider version than the wall above. The 
knowledge of the soil conditions was based on experience. Empirical determination of 
the foundation design was therefore often made.
Some sort of a vapor barrier was required to be placed between the foundation and the 
exterior walls in the basement to prevent rising moisture from the ground. Furthermore, 
the foundations were to be protected against frost. Therefore, the underside of the 
foundations should be placed in frost free depth. This would always be the case for 
buildings containing a basement floor.
Not much changed with the regulations in 1889. The foundations were still to be 
executed in accordance with their function. A new initiative required that a vapour 
insulating layer had to be placed between the foundations and all basement walls. 
Furthermore, a vapour barrier was required to be placed vertically between the 
basement outer wall and the soil.
In the very end of the 19th century the foundation started being constructed of concrete 
instead of masonry, see Figure 4.6. Regarding moisture, the concrete was far less 
sensitive to it moisture leading to fewer problems related to soil moisture. Problems 
primarily occurred when the concrete used or the casting were of poor quality.

INNER WALLS
Regulations concerning inner walls were not included in the Danish building regulations 
until 1856. The laws regarding inner walls, included in these new regulations, were 
weak compared to the regulations about the other building elements. Until the end of 
the 1800s, inner walls were in housing constructions mostly build as timber framing. 
Buildings with brick-built inner walls were a rarity in this period. 
The main partition was constructed of load bearing timber framing with brick infill that 
had a thickness of ½ a brick. A timber frame wall consists of studs and cross-braces 
clamped to the bottom and the top plate. The studs transfer the vertical load, and the 
cross-braces give the wall stability in the longitudinal direction. That is the reason why 
two contrary sloping cross-braces always were a part of a timber frame wall. A typical 
construction of a main partition with filled out timber framing is showed in Figure 4.14

There were no requirements for the dimensions and distances in the timber frame

for all levels. For the sake of the cross-braces the stud spacing was normally about 
1,25 m for the outermost sections in the timber frame walls. The timber frame wall 
could be constructed with an individual or an interconnected sill and top plate, which 
implies that the walls could be considered as individual diaphragms or as one large 
through-going diaphragm. The beams in the horizontal divisions were used as sill and 
top plate for the transverse timber framing walls. The timber frame walls that went 
through more levels had to be supported by a massive masonry wall in the basement 
with a thickness of 1 brick.
The rules regarding inner walls were changed in the building regulations of 1889. It 
was now required that inner walls that carried floor joists had to be built by massive 
masonry walls. Now, inner walls were constructed in consideration of their function as 
bearing or non-bearing and the use of timber frame inner walls stopped.
The Copenhagen building regulations distinguished, as already mentioned, between 
two types of inner walls: Bearing and non-bearing. It was typically only the main 
partition and the walls around staircases that were load bearing. The flat divisions 
and the partitions between individual rooms were considered as non-bearing walls. 
Furthermore, a distinction was made between “heavy” and “light” inner walls. Walls 
made of concrete and masonry falls into the category “heavy” and board partitions 
are categorised as “light” walls. The stabilising system always features heavy inner 
walls – bearing or not.
When looking at load bearing walls, the load is transferred directly to the foundation 
without affecting any other building elements. Light inner walls are always non-bearing 
and their load is transferred through horizontal divisions to the vertical load bearing 
elements.
Inner walls that were used for partitions could be made of planks or double board 
walls with exterior reeds and plaster. These walls typically had a thickness of 200 mm.

The joists were typically timber with a quadratic cross section. They ran in the 
transversal direction and spanned between the longitudinal load bearing facade walls 
and the main partition. The size of the joists was depending on the span. A rule of 
the thumb was that a quadratic cross section of 155 x 155 mm was used if the joist 
span were equal to or less than 3 meters. A span longer than 3 meters required an 
increasement of the cross section’s height and width. The dimensions of the joists 
were approximately 175 x 175 mm since the house depth normally was 9-10 m from 
facade to facade. Towards the gables and the transversal massive masonry walls the 
joists width could be reduced by half.  
Rectangular cross sections were also used in the floor construction. In case of a 
rectangular beam, minimum half of the reduced width were to be added to its height. 
However, the norm was to use quadratic cross sections.
The centre distance between the joists were decided regardless of their cross section 
size. The maximum centre distance between the joists were 940 mm. The joists were 
working as supports for the facade walls. Therefore, every third joist were, according 
to the requirements, to be through-going and anchored to the facades in both ends. 
At the load bearing walls, both the longitudinal facades and the main partition, the 
joists rest on a 105 x 105 mm sill beam. This connection can be seen in Figure 4.17.

STAIRCASE SHAFT
Two escape routes had to be accessible from every apartment according the 
regulations. Therefore, there were both a main staircase and a back staircase 
connected to all apartments. The walls around the staircases were constructed of 
timber frames with a thickness of ½ a brick. The staircase itself were made out of 
wood.  
The flight of stairs and the landings were carried by exchanges in the floor joists, 
which were anchored in the walls around the staircase. 
The main staircase was a straight half landing stair. The back staircase was made as 
a half turn stair with one landing or with no landing at all. Figure 4.21 illustrates the two 
types of staircases.
The staircase wall was connected to the outer wall by a beveled cut-off from the 
spandrel thickness under the windows towards the staircase wall. This was done in 
order to create space for the landings. The reduction of the outer walls was possible, 
because it was the staircase walls that carried the landings, not the outer walls. The 
connection is shown for different wall thicknesses in Figure 4.20.
As for the inner walls, the walls around the staircases changed after the regulations in 
1889. The walls were now made by massive masonry walls with a thickness of ½ - ¾ 
a brick. The staircase itself were still to be constructed in wood. However, concrete 
stairs replaced the wooden stairs in the end of the period, from about 1930. Hereafter, 
the back stair could be discarded, because a concrete stair was considered to be 
a fireproof escape route. Figure 4.22 illustrates how a typical main staircase were

4.4  SUB-CONCLUSION
The background studies have been investigated and presented in the sections above 
to obtain sufficient knowledge about the demands for dwellings in Copenhagen, the 
possibilities of utilizing rooftops of the existing masonry buildings constructed in the 
period 1850-1930, and, last but not least, the building technology for these masonry 
buildings.
It is predicted that the high demand for housing in Copenhagen will continue to 
increase, which underlines that it will most likely be necessary to relate differently to the 
existing buildings when creating new homes in the future. Additionally, investigations 
show that there is a potential of utilizing the rooftops in 57% of the existing masonry 
buildings built in the considered period. 
The studies of the building technology give an overall picture of the history, regulations 
and building technology of masonry buildings as well as how the different elements 
were originally constructed. This results in an understanding of the conditions of the 
masonry buildings that are relevant for the further project. In general, the construction 
of masonry buildings in the considered period is very alike. In that way, it is possible 
to draw conclusions on what a building will look like, without performing very thorough 
examinations. The background studies indicate that masonry buildings have 
untapped potentials due to their massive and solid construction, which can turn out to 
be beneficial in the development of a rooftop addition.
The subsequent chapters are composed on the basis of the background studies and 
will bear the mark of the information that has been stated so far.

DISCUSSION
From the calculations it can be seen that the eccentricity has a huge impact on the 
bearing capacity. An increased eccentricity gives a decreased bearing capacity. In 
some cases, this eccentricity is not realistic. The result for the facade walls is a good 
example. The facade walls currently have load bearing capacity equalling zero in the 
third floor, due to the big eccentricity in the wall. This eccentricity occurs because 
of the decreased load after removing the roof. It is assumed, that this will change, 
when the rooftop addition contributes with extra vertical load to the facade walls. A 
decreased eccentricity will result in an increased compressive bearing capacity. The 
facade walls are therefore assumed to be able to carry the extra load from the rooftop 
addition.
The main partition is calculated to be overloaded. In multi-storey masonry buildings in 
Copenhagen, it is often seen that the main partition walls settle and that cracks occur. 
This main partition is heavily loaded, compared to its slender cross section. It can be 
seen from the load collection that the facade walls and the main partition are affected 
by approximately the same load on the ground floor – where the facade walls are 1,5 
brick thicker than the walls in the main partition.
When building on top of the existing structure, it is important to transfer the load to 
the walls that have a sufficient bearing capacity, both to the walls that needs extra 
load due to the eccentricity, and to the stabilizing walls. Adding on extra floors does 
not only give extra vertical load, but the moment from the wind load will increase as 
well. As already mentioned, the stabilizing walls needs extra vertical load, when the 
moment from the wind load is increasing with the extra building height. An increased 
weight is helping with stabilizing the building. Therefore, it is important to ensure 
that the stabilising walls get loaded as well. However, too much load will impact the 
bearing capacity as well, and can cause failure of the walls.
Additionally, it is important to emphasize that the stability of the building block is more 
complex than presented. There are more stabilizing walls in each direction, than 
included in the calculations of the individual wings. The walls in the entire block will 
work together and help stabilizing the building in the different directions.
There is residual bearing capacity in the walls in the existing building block. After 
estimating an approximate load of the new rooftop addition, an investigation can be 
done to figure out how many extra floors that can be added on top of the building. This 
will depend on how the loads get transferred and which walls that carries which load. 
An investigation of this will be made in Section 6.4.

5.4  CHALLENGES & POTENTIALS
The case study shows that the considered building is giving rise to both challenges 
and potentials that are going to be present in the development of the concept.
The area, in which the building block is located, is a typical example of how the city of 
Copenhagen looks like. It represents the common structure of Copenhagen very well, 
which is mostly areas with high-density housing. However, this only goes for two sides 
of the building that faces other tall building volumes. The two other sides are facing an 
open and green area, which can be a challenge in regard to the increased wind load 
that occurs when adding extra floors. This can have an impact on the overall stability 
of the building, especially when adding several extra floors on top of the existing 
structure.
Another factor of concern is the plan geometry of the building block that can lead 
to some issues. The layout of the new apartments in the rooftop addition may be 
difficult to solve, because of the irregular plan geometry of the building block. The 
geometry contains some angles that must be taken into consideration in the solution 
for the transition between the existing part and the new addition on top. However, 
these angles can contribute positively in the stability of the whole building structure. 
The study of the architectural style of the existing building shows that the facades of 
the building block are picturing how the facades of most older masonry buildings in 
Copenhagen appear. The three different facade expressions will form a good basis 
for the concept that preferably must fit most buildings built in the period 1850-1930. 
The towers placed in the western facade may be technically challenging, because 
they are interrupting the space where the rooftop addition is going to be constructed. 
From an architectural point of view, these vertical elements can add value to the 
expression of the two united building bodies – the existing and the rooftop addition. 
The height and volume of the towers makes the roof look less tall and massive, when 
observed from the street. The existing building’s roof is about 5 meters, which almost 
corresponds to two floor heights. This is a quality for the project, because the roof will 
be replaced with an addition, where the aim is to build as tall as possible within the 
constrains that are present.
The structural analysis of the existing building shows that some walls have residual 
load bearing capacity. This allows for building a rooftop addition with the existing 
structure as foundation. However, not all existing walls can contribute in carrying 
the rooftop addition, because they are already entirely utilised. These walls can not 
be included in the system that will carry the added loads. This has to be taken into 
account in the development of the structural design, where it is important to transfer 
the load to the walls that have a sufficient bearing capacity. The result of the structural 
analysis indicates that some of the walls, for instance the facade walls, needs more 
load if they shall be able to carry the extra load from the rooftop addition.
From the light- and shadow analyses both qualities and issue have been discovered. 
The tall building body casts shadows in the backyard and on the building itself as well 
as on the surrounding areas. A big challenge is that the backyard already is relatively 
casted by shadows. The rooftop addition will most likely cast more shadows in the 
backyard, leading to a decrease of sun hours. 
Another matter of concern is the daylight conditions in the existing apartments that 
can be worsen due to the addition on top. However, the roof on the existing building 
is tall, but it is still crucial to examine the conditions after the rooftop addition has been 
constructed. Furthermore, the apartments that will be placed in the addition can be 
overheated and there is a risk for the occupants to be blinded. The high placement of 
the new apartments can, on the other hand, provide spacious views.
The challenges and potentials will be further described and integrated in the following 
chapter, where the concept is developed.

6.1  MATERIAL OPTIONS
In any project it must be decided what materials that the building is going to be 
constructed from. This is done on the basis of the factors that play a role in the 
particular project. Normally a building is constructed from a multitude of materials, but 
this chapter will focus on what building material is best suited for the structural system 
of the rooftop addition. There are a wide range of construction materials available on 
the market, which is why a proper selection of the material has to be done to meet 
project goals. 
In the preliminary phase of the project, wood in the form om cross laminated timber 
(CLT) was considered suitable for the structural system. This first presumption 
was based on the already identified conditions that would be essential for an 
actual implementation of the project, such as weight and CO2 emission. Due to the 
abovementioned reasons, an investigation of CLT’s properties compared to two other 
materials is conducted. This is done to emphasise and confirm that CLT is the most 
optimal material for the project.
This section will first of all present the factors that the project mainly depends on. 
There will be placed most emphasis on these factors in the final choice of construction 
material for the rooftop expansion. The choice of material is a complex process that 
has to be included as a part of the concept development to obtain both the functional 
and structural requirements for the project. That is why the final decision will be based 
on several factors and issues that are outlined below in the comparison of three 
different materials: Steel framing, reinforced concrete elements and CLT elements.
6.1.1  DECIDING FACTORS
As already mentioned, some factors are decisive in the choice of construction 
material. To be exact, two factors have been picked out as the main determinants for 
the project.
The first and most important factor to take into consideration is the weight of the material. 
The rooftop addition will be carried by the existing masonry construction underneath. 
The masonry has a certain compression strength, and if the magnitude of the vertical 
loads on the masonry gets too large, a failure may occur. Immediately, this means 
that the less weight from the new construction on the top, the higher extension and 
the more floors can be erected. This will lead to the maximum number of apartments, 
and in this way an efficient utilisation of liveable square meters is obtained. However, 
if the magnitude of the load gets too small, in relation to the increased building height, 
stability problems may appear.

Moisture 
None of the three materials are completely resistant to moisture, but they are affected in different ways, as stated 
in the table, e.g. corrosion of steel and wood’s dimensional changes. However, problems related to moisture can 
be avoided if the focus is pointed at it from the beginning of a project. The matters of concern are the design of 
proper connections as well as the building envelope construction. 
 
Thermal Performance 
From the thermal conductivity values it is seen that CLT is almost 400 times better as a thermal insulator than 
steel and about 6 times better than concrete. 
 
As a solid material, CLT can act as a thermal mass together with the internal building elements so that it stores 
heat at day, which is released at night. In this way CLT contributes to a reduction in the heating and cooling of a 
building and savings in the overall use of energy. [Karacabeyli & Douglas, 2013] 
 
Sustainability / CO2-emission 
The comparison of the CO2-emission and sustainable factors states that CLT is the only truly renewable and 
reusable material of the three compared materials. In addition, CLT has the lowest consumption of energy 
throughout its life-cycle. It stores a large amount of carbon that can lead to a negative carbon footprint – negative 
embodied energy. [KLH UK] In this way, CLT actually removes CO2 from the atmosphere, when looking at the 
total CO2 inputs in the form of both stored and released CO2, as visualized in Figure 6.4 below. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The fact that CLT is able to store carbon can be beneficial in the long-term and can turn out to compensate for 
the operational environmental impact of a building. This also goes for the production of the adhesives used to 
glue the boards together, that contributes to the overall environmental footprint of a CLT element as well. It is 
energy consuming and emits chemicals of a certain amount. The sustainability of CLT elements is further 
described in Section 6.1.4. 
 
Durability 
It is impossible to predict the exact lifetime of construction materials. The real lifetime of a building, from 
construction to demolition, depends on a number of parameters such as design, construction, technical quality, 
surroundings, maintenance etc. These are influencing the lifetime of all the included building components and 
materials.  
 
The construction materials will last longer than for example materials used for surfaces, meaning that some 
components and materials have to be replaced or repaired during the lifetime of a building. In this way, the 
lifetime of a building can change during the building’s actual life.

Weight 
The weight does not only have an influence on how many floors that can be added to the existing structure. It will 
be an important factor of concern regarding the transportation of the material from manufacturer to construction 
site as well. The trucks that carries the material have limits for how much weight they can drive with. In 
consequence of these limits only a specific volume of the material can be shipped at a time. The volume of steel 
and concrete that can be carried on one truck will be far less than the volume of CLT. 
 
Mechanical Properties 
The mechanical properties of the three materials are differing in different ways, as described shortly in the 
following: 
 
Steel has good tensile strength, and the great stiffness of the material can be beneficial for applications with long 
spans. Furthermore, very slender cross-sections can be obtained. Steel is a ductile material that significantly 
extends before failure actually occurs. The failure of steel constructions is warned due to the plastic behaviour of 
steel. 
 
Concrete is good in compression – the compression strength is at least ten times as great as the tensile strength. 
Reinforcement is essential to obtain necessary tensile strength. The strength of concrete is mainly depending on 
the cement paste’s strength, but several other factors such as cement type, cement amount, the aggregate type, 
degree of hydration. [Materialebogen, 2008] For reinforced concrete subjected to bending and tension, the 
reinforcement, that has a bigger tensile strength and ductility than concrete, absorbs the tensile forces. The 
ductile behaviour of steel will in these cases lead to a warned failure of the concrete.  
 
Wood has fine strength and stiffness properties compared to its weight. However, the mechanical properties vary 
in the different directions. The properties are dependent on the structure of the wood as well. CLT’s in-plane 
properties are good. CLT is applicable for shear walls due to its good dimensional in-plane stability. 
 
The mechanical properties of CLT can vary among the different manufacturers, which is why it in some projects 
can prove to be beneficial to use different manufacturers for the different elements – e.g. one manufacturer for 
slabs and one for wall. In this way, an optimal structure can be obtained.

The availability of CLT is limited in the Nordic countries. However, Sweden do have one manufacturer, called 
Martinsons that since 2003 have produced CLT elements. Martinson is located in Northern Sweden.

costs of a CLT project. 
 
Erection / Handling & Transportation  
The fact that CLT is relatively new on the construction market causes that there is limited documentation of 
the specific construction work and the erection process of CLT buildings. However, the techniques used in 
the construction of precast concrete buildings are in some way similar to the methods that are developed so 
far and has been used for the current construction with CLT elements. The concrete industry is far more 
experienced and most systems and techniques are thoroughly developed. In this way, the CLT construction 
team can easily adapt the transportation, lifting and handling techniques from the concrete element market. 
Additionally, most CLT manufacturers have developed a range of lifting systems to be used for the handling 
and installation of CLT elements on the construction site. However, several factors come into play in the 
choice of techniques and systems, and are of course depending on the individual project. For instance, the 
location of the building can be a deciding factor - whether the project is placed in the middle of a big city on 
a narrow construction site or at an open, accessible site on a field in the countryside has to be taken into 
account. The type and complexity of the structure and its individual components are crucial in the choice of 
erection systems as well. [Karacabeyli & Douglas, 2013]

Material Price / Construction Costs 
If the project is going to be implemented, these two factors would be some of the most important factors to 
consider. The total costs for the project is crucial to the owner, and will affect the future occupants of the rooftop 
as well. The occupants will obviously wish for as low rents as possible. In this project, the price has not been a 
focus point, and are not highly emphasised in the choice of material. 
 
Concrete is the cheapest alternative if the prices of the raw materials are compared. When looking at the prices 
for the delivered and mounted materials it is still concrete that stand out as the cheapest material.  
The price of CLT is today still high, because of the product’s comparatively recent emergence as well as the 
lack of knowledge concerning the use of CLT as a construction material. Until now, projects with CLT as 
constructional system indicate that CLT is cost-competitive, and over time it has the potential of being even 
more competitive. [Mahlum, Walsh & Coughlin] 
 
It has to be mentioned that it is a very complex task to estimate the exact material prices, because many 
variables come into play, such as manufacturing and transportation cost, salary for the workmen, equipment rent 
etc. Furthermore, when comparing different materials, the price of each material has to have a frame of 
reference to serve as foundation. This can for instance be the same type of elements, which are having the 
same function and have to carry the same load so that they can act as representative specimens. The material 
price per m3 is an estimation that can give an overall idea of what level the different prices are at, even though 
the three materials are in many aspects different 
 
To reach the total price of a building system many benefits have to be considered that may add value to the 
overall economical perspective of a project. The following are a few examples of factors that can have an 
influence on the total cost of a construction project: 
• 
Faster building completion means earlier occupancy by the future users. 
• 
Relative low weight of the structure can result in decreased foundation costs. 
• 
Prefabricated elements increase the efficiency on the construction site. 
• 
Recycling or conversion to energy in the end of the material’s life. 
• 
Global sustainability contribution. 
• 
Reduced installation cost. 
 
Therefore, the cost of a material depends on what priorities that are assigned by the owner of the specific 
project. The building market has gradually brought issues like sustainability and operational costs to focus even 
though the direct cost of a material often carries weight in the choice of construction material. [XLam]

Architectural Qualities / Quality of Living 
Architecture is a topic that can be debated and discussed in many ways. This is due to the fact that people have 
individual perceptions of what can be seen as an architectural quality.  
 
However, in the choice of construction material, it is important to consider human factors such as the architectural 
qualities of using CLT as construction material for the extension on top of existing masonry buildings in 
Copenhagen. Especially, when adding a new building body to an existing building it is crucial to consider the 
interconnection between the different construction materials used as well as the overall aesthetics. It is certain 
that it will have an influence on the quality of living, because both architecture and urban planning will affect the 
users of the building. The quality of living and the physical environment are essential in regard to the occupants’

The comparison of the three different materials illustrates that each material has its 
individual strengths and weaknesses. These are differing from each factor and has 
been used in the choice of structural material in the project. This project is a vision that 
will be a part of the future urban densification in Copenhagen, which is the reason why 
abovementioned and compared aspects are vital for the final result.
The choice has fallen on the use of CLT as the structural material for the rooftop 
addition. The choice is based on the “Importance Guideline” with emphasis on the two 
deciding factors. CLT has proved to be the best fit regarding the wishes and demands 
that has been outlined in the project. It is clear that CLT is the best option within both 
weight and overall CO2 emission.
Firstly, the light weight of CLT provides a potential for building on existing rooftops. In 
that way, the use of CLT can contribute to the densification of Copenhagen, because 
these new top floors can accommodate new housing opportunities. The light weight 
of CLT eases both the transportation and the on-site handling of the elements. 
Furthermore, the waste coming from the production and the construction work on-
site can be used for biofuel, which is climate neutral. However, the waste on the 
construction site can be kept to a minimum due to the prefabrication of CLT elements.
There is a desire to use a material that can contribute with some amount weight, but 
not too much, regarding the stability of the whole building, as seen in Section 5.2 
concerning the structural conditions of the existing structure.
Secondly, CLT elements can turn out having a beneficial impact on the environment, 
especially when compared to other material alternatives with identical functionality 
like steel and concrete. The life time of buildings is long compared to other products, 
meaning a building will influence and have consequences for the environmental footprint 
far out in the future. That is why the environmental quality of current construction and 
the creating of energy efficient and long-lasting solutions are significant. There must 
therefore be a focus on this in all the building’s life cycle stages. [Energistyrelsen, 
2015] The aim is to build an addition that contributes to the reduction of the overall 
CO2 footprint in the future world.
The comparison shows that CLT is beneficial within other aspects as well. This goes 
for the mechanical properties such as the good dimensional in-plane stability that CLT 
elements have. CLT is a simple construction system that makes it possible to achieve 
a solid and stabile structure with large spans and tall plate heights. [Karacabeyli & 
Douglas, 2013]
The comparison has underlined the preliminary thoughts concerning CLT as structural

project emphasises, and is therefore used in the further concept development.

6.1.4  CROSS LAMINATED TIMBER
This section is an introduction to cross laminated timber – also known as CLT. CLT 
elements are used for the structural system in the concept for the rooftop addition.
Cross laminated timber is a relatively new structural building material that was initially 
introduced and developed in Austria in the beginning of the 1990’s. The prevalence 
of finished buildings built with CLT is steady increasing, especially in Europe, where 
it has been used for both low- and mid-rise residential, commercial and industrial 
applications, among others. Currently, there is over one hundred projects in Europe 
that are built with CLT, and about 500 m3 CLT is produced in Europe every year. 
[Schickhofer, 2016] However, CLT has still not been included in any standard, but a 
group of specialists are currently formulating a section about CLT that will be included 
in Eurocode 5 (EN 1995-1-1). [proHolz]
The emergence of CLT construction was partially driven by the awareness of green 
building and the common concern for the environment as well as the attention on 
materials’ effect on the health and well-being of occupants living in wooden houses. 
[Bejder, A. K., 2012]
CLT is a product of global interest and has to a lesser extend been implemented in 
countries like United States, Canada, Japan among others. An acceptance of CLT at 
the construction market requires that the product meets the valid building legislations. 
[Karacabeyli & Douglas, 2013]
In the following, a description of the structure of the individual CLT elements is outlined. 
Furthermore, the application potentials that CLT offers in relation to structural design 
and performance are described as well as the building types that CLT can be suitable 
for. 
In the end, CLT’s characteristics concerning building enclosure design are explained, 
where some of the studied aspects are interconnected with the subsequent 
sustainability aspect of the product.
This introduction to CLT will put forward both the main advantages of CLT as well as 
the issues that needs attention when using CLT as structural construction material.
STRUCTURE OF CLT ELEMENTS
Cross laminated timber elements are massive timber elements that are a part of the 
timber products known as engineered wood. The CLT building system characterises 
both the traditional timber frame construction and a more innovative product within the 
use of wood in construction. [Karacabeyli & Douglas, 2013] 
CLT elements are made of several transversal and longitudinal layers of wood boards 
that are arranged crosswise to each other with an angle of 90 degrees, resulting in a 
symmetrical cross-section, as visualised in Figure 6.5 below. The boards are placed 
side-by-side in each layer and glued together on their narrow sides. The boards are 
having a width of 40 to 300 mm, are 6 mm to 45 mm thick, and they are connected in 
the longitudinal direction typically by finger joints.
A cross-section of a CLT element consist of at least three layers of boards and nine 
layers as a maximum – always an uneven number. The standard dimensions of the CLT 
elements are widths up to a maximum of 3 m and lengths up to 24 m. The thickness of 
the elements varies from 50 to 300 mm. [Schickhofer, 2016] [proHolz]
==PAGE:110==
100
CHAPTER 6
The elements are cut to the shape and size that is wished for at the manufacturer, and 
are ready for assembling when delivered on site. All elements have pre-cut holes for 
doors, windows, piping and installations. The surface of the individual panels can be 
produced with different finish as desired. [Lewis, 2010]
Several manufacturers of CLT have arisen in Europe since the emergence of the 
product. The three manufacturers that have been investigated in this project are 
Stora Enso, KLH Massivholz and Binderholz. These have been used to obtain specific 
information about the structural performance of the elements. In that connection it is 
important to mention that both the dimensions and material properties of the elements 
varies among the manufacturers. This is the reason why it has been chosen to make 
use of more than one manufacturer for the production of the elements, and in that way 
benefit from the best manufacturers of the different element types. Furthermore, the 
variation has been taken into account in the structural design of the rooftop addition, 
see Section 6.4.
APPLICATION POTENTIALS
A CLT element is a solid wood product that can be used in the construction of a load 
bearing structure. The thickness of the elements allows for individual structural use 
for instance as slab (floor and ceiling), wall or roof, because they have good strength 
and stiffness properties. Because of the large format and material structure only few 
plates are needed to obtain necessary structural stability. The size and large scale of 
the elements enables large openings without any interruptions from beams or lintels. 
In addition, the element’s comparatively small thickness makes it possible to create 
more floor area and spacious room layouts. [Bejder, A. K., 2012]
CLT elements are capable of carrying loads in- and out-of-plane due to the crosswise 
lamination, when being used as slab or shear wall elements respectively, as illustrated 
in Figure 6.6 below. One of the main advantages of the relatively simple structure of 
CLT is the high in-plane dimensional stability caused by the intersecting layering. 
[Karacabeyli & Douglas, 2013]
Figure 6.5: An exploded view of the orientation of the individual boards (left) that forms a 
typical CLT element (right). [Ramboll]
Figure 6.6: The application of a CLT wall (left) and a CLT slab (right) in the structure. [proHolz]
==PAGE:111==

The elements have two-way spanning capacity for example when used as floor system 
supported on all four sides. However, the main direction of the load bearing capacity 
is decided by the direction that has the highest stiffness, as indicated on Figure 6.7. 
The outer layers will always be the layers with the highest stiffness. [Schickhofer, 2016]
When vertical loads are applied to a CLT wall element, normal stresses are occurring 
in the board layers that are running parallel to the load. Additionally, the wall plate can 
carry big horizontal loads, due to the crosswise structure, which is enabling the use of 
CLT in multi-storey buildings.
The loads, that are working perpendicular to the plane of a slab, are resulting in 
bending stresses that are distributed through the layers placed parallel to the slab’s 
span direction. [Holzleimbau e.V.]
The orientation of the layers has an impact on the load bearing capacity, due to the 
fact that wood is an anisotropic material. The bending stiffness of a panel depends on 
the crosswise layers’ rolling shear modulus that rely on several factors. Rolling shear 
occurs when the loads acts in-plane of the element. This is due the layer structure of 
the CLT elements, see Figure 6.8.
Manufacturers are aware of the issue of rolling shear and are producing the elements 
with board widths so that rolling shear deformations should not affect the structural 
design. [Karacabeyli & Douglas, 2013]
The design of a CLT building can be as simple as with concrete and steel, but due 
to the unawareness of CLT and the comfort with using well known materials, CLT is 
often deselected in the choice of material. During the past two decades CLT has been 
discovered by several architects and they have taken advantage of the diversity and 
flexibility of the product. Each element can be formed and cut on request, which allows 
complex geometries and unique buildings without ending up with a very complicated 
construction. Furthermore, the cross-laminated structure of the elements entails that 
holes can be cut in the elements, as long as enough material is remaining to secure 
the stability. The elements can be individually designed by the architect, fabricated at 
the manufacturer and quickly constructed on the building site. In this way, the use of 
CLT elements gives the architect a great freedom in the design of the building. [Mayo, 
2015]

The structural design of the concept has been carried out on the basis of the former 
chapters of the concept development, and the guideline presented below. The 
chapter is divided into the three different parts that the structure consists of, and 
each section carefully describes the considerations around and the calculations of 
the structural design. Each chapter is presented as a solution on how the separate 
structural systems should be designed, whereas a summary of the final structural 
design is presented in Chapter 7, as a conclusion on what is presented here. 
All calculations are based on the regulations stated in the Eurocodes and the 
appurtenant Danish national annexes.

6.4.1  GUIDELINE
In advance of the design process, a guideline with a list of criteria for the design has 
been outlined. These criteria formed the basis of the structural design, by creating 
constraints that the design should comply with. The criteria are as follows: 
	
1. The new structural system should be designed in a way so that it is able to 
	
    carry and transfer the lateral and vertical loads acting on the structure. 
	
2. The loads from the new structure should get transferred to the existing 	
	
    facade walls and stabilising walls, described in Section 5.7.
	
3. The existing walls should be able to carry the extra load, without any 
	
    additional reinforcements.
	
4. The load bearing structure in the rooftop addition is built with wooden		
	
    elements.
	
5. The new system should be designed to allow a flexible layout of the new 	
	
    addition – not dependent on the layout of the existing structure. 
	
6. The flexibility goes for the local flexibility within an apartment as well. It is 
                 desired that the architectural layout should not be dependent on the 
	
    structural walls and their position. 
	
7. A wish for the height of the structural horisontal divisions to be as low as 	
	
    possible.
An overview has been created by defining this guideline for the design. The challenges, 
that the concept is facing, has been identified, and ideas on how they should be 
solved has been found.

6.4.3  ROOFTOP ADDITION
The first part of the structure is the separate system for the rooftop addition. The 
rooftop has mainly been designed with a view to creating a structure that can carry 
and transfer the vertical and lateral loads that act on the structure. The loads get 
transferred through the load bearing slabs, beams and walls to the load bearing 
transition system placed below the addition.
A smaller part of the roof top addition, consisting of two apartments, is chosen for a 
closer investigation. The apartments correspond to two of the more standard sized 
apartments, with a simple geometry. The design that is made for the chosen apartments 
will be valid for the entire structure, even though some alternative solutions have to be 
made, where the geometry is more complex. The chosen part is located in the west 
wing – referred to as wing B in Chapter 5. Figure 6.28 illustrates the floor plan for level 
4, where the apartments studied are highlighted. 
STRUCTURAL SYSTEM
The structural system in the rooftop addition consist mainly of load bearing slabs and 
walls. Additionally, beams are placed beneath the edge of the displaced volumes at 
the level above the considered level. Their purpose is to carry the facade walls in the 
transition between the terrace and the apartment. The span direction of the beams 
will be parallel to the facade walls and they will rest on the transversal walls in the 
building. The span direction of the slabs is, like for the beams, parallel to the facade 
walls. The slabs span between the transversal walls in the building, which transfers 
the vertical loads from the slabs to the load bearing transition system. By letting the 
beams and slabs span in the same direction, a lower total height of the floor structure 
can be obtained. The two horizontal elements can be placed in almost the same level, 
as seen in Section 7.9.1.
Being a part of the initial design, it was self-evident that the transversal flat division and 
staircase walls were utilized as load bearing walls for the slabs and beams. However, 
these walls are placed with a distance of 10 meters, which is a relatively long span for 
the slabs. A long span results in a demand for a relatively tall cross section, in order 
to obtain a sufficient stiffness of the slab. Therefore, an additional transversal wall 
is placed in both apartments, that reduces the span width of the slabs by half. The 
added transversal walls will additionally contribute to stabilizing the structure. In the 
bigger apartments, the transversal flat division walls are placed with an even larger 
distance. In these apartments, there will be a need for additional transversal walls, in 
order to carry the slabs and beams.

6.4.4  LOAD BEARING TRANSITION SYSTEM
The load bearing transition system has mainly been created based on criteria 5, stating 
that a flexible layout of the rooftop addition is wanted. A transition system between the 
rooftop addition and the existing structure will assure that the loads get transferred 
between the two systems and give the opportunity of creating a layout independent of 
the existing layout. By creating a solution, where the new layout is independent of the 
existing one, gives the opportunity of reusing the concept for other existing structures 
that might have a complete different layout than the case study building.
FORCE DISTRIBUTION
There are vertical and lateral loads that acts on the rooftop addition, which needs 
to be transferred to the existing load bearing and stabilizing walls. As concluded 
in Section 5.2, both the facade walls and the stabilizing fire and gable walls need 
extra vertical load from the new structure, in order to have a stable structure with 
a sufficient bearing capacity in the future conditions. The load bearing transversal 
walls in the new layout are displaced, compared to the existing transversal walls. The 
vertical loads from these new walls have to be transferred the existing walls, which 
the transition system will be in charge of doing. Additionally, the facade walls need 
extra load. The system should therefore be able to distribute the forces both to the 
existing transversal, stabilizing walls and the longitudinal facade walls. This goes for 
the vertical as well as the horizontal forces. 
The forces that needs to be transferred originates from the dead load of the elements 
in the rooftop addition, imposed loads, snow load and wind load. A grating system is a 
structural system that is able to distribute these forces to the load bearing walls in the 
existing structure. By making the correct arrangement of the beams in the grating and 
by making connection that are able to transfer the loads, the system will secure that the 
forces will be distributed as wanted to the existing walls. A more detailed description 
of the loads that are to be transferred and how the grating system structurally works 
are presented below.

STATIC BEHAVIOUR
A grating system consist of a certain number of beams placed in two directions, 
which crosses in an angle of typically 90 degrees. One of the static characteristics 
of the grating system is that an applied point load in a node will be distributed in the 
two directions of the beams in the grid. Because of the connections between the 
beams in the crossing the entire grating will deform from the applied load. The result 
of this is that the stiffness of the system can reach a considerable large magnitude. 
Additionally, an increased indeterminate inner static behaviour will occur, which 
results in an uncertainty around the force distribution throughout the grating, which 
is complicated to to determine. This behaviour of the grating additionally leads to a 
structure that is able to carry unpredictable loadings of a certain magnitude. A failure 
of a bay or a connection will therefore only lead to a different force distribution in the 
grating. [Søeberg & Sørensen, 1982]
The resulting stiffness of the system against deformations will depend on the beams 
cross section and the connection method in the crossing of the beams. 
As for most structures, there is a wish for optimizing the material consumption when 
designing the grating system. The shape of the cross section has a great impact on 
this matter. A slender cross section is often chosen for this purpose, which can cause 
problems for a single span structure in relation to lateral torsional buckling. In these 
kind of structures, there is often a need for bracings along the length of the structure. 
A grating system will “automatically” be fixed in all crossings, why there will rarely 
occur torsional buckling problems in these type of structures. Based on the forces 
in the beams, that the grating consists of, the material consumption for every single 
beam, can be optimized. This will however not be considered in the concept, but it is 
an aspect that could be studied closer in further investigations.

The design of the grating system that is transferring the vertical and horizontal forces 
from the new to the existing structure is designed in the following. 
The same area is chosen for the calculations of the grating system, as for de rooftop

6.4.5  EXISTING STRUCTURAL SYSTEM
The existing structure is checked for the load from the added structure. Both the 
vertical and lateral load have increased. The existing walls that will be analysed are 
placed below the two apartments that has been investigated in the previous sections. 
These walls are not necessary the most critical ones, but are, as already explained, 
placed in a part of the building that represents a standard geometry and load affection. 
The most critical load combinations, found in the structural analysis of the existing 
structure are chosen for further studies in the following chapter. These combinations 
are STR1 (imposed load dominating) and STR3 (wind load dominating, dead load 
unfavourable). The combinations are used for checking the bearing capacity in 
compression and for checking the stability respectively. The loads that are affecting 
the structure after adding the rooftop addition are presented in the following sections. 
VERTICAL LOADS FROM GRID
The reactions from the loads in the grating system corresponds to the loads that will 
be transferred to the walls in the existing structure. The loading will have the shape 
of a uniformly varying load, which is transformed into a uniformly distributed load for 
usage in the calculations of the bearing capacity. Firstly, this is done by determination 
of the total amount of load, equalling the area beneath the varying load distribution 
graph. Secondly, the magnitude of the uniformly distributed load, which will give the 
equivalent area is found, corresponding to the equivalent total amount of load. The 
transformation is illustrated in Figure 6.42 below.
The existing fire walls will additionally get loaded by the neighbouring gratings. In order 
to get an exact load from these walls, the grids need to be analysed. This will however 
not be done as a part of the present project. Instead, an estimation of the loading is 
performed, in order to get the approximated expected load. The heaviest loaded fire 
wall in wing B is illustrated in Section 5.2. This wall is the one that will be analysed in 
the following. The neighbouring grating, that this wall is carrying, has approximately 
the same size as the grating analysed – with a depth and width of approximately 10 
m. Therefore, it is expected that the fire wall gets loaded by a vertical load that is the

6.4.6  DISCUSSION
It can be seen from the results, that the existing structure has an insufficient bearing 
capacity, if using the approach presented in this thesis. The calculations that has 
been executed are based on simplified methods and the rough assumptions that 
are made along the way. A lot of the assumptions made have been done in order to 
simplify the rather complex structure. Additionally, it is important to emphasize that 
the assumptions are conservative, already starting by the assumptions made for the 
strength and stiffness of the masonry. As described in section 4.3.3., the material 
properties for the masonry needs to be determined by taking samples of the existing 
masonry, in order to get a more accurate picture of how the masonry will behave 
structurally. The samples could result in a decreased strength and stiffness as well. 
However, the examples referred to in Section 4.3.3, shows bigger values than the 
ones used in this project.
In the stability calculations, the stabilising flat division walls were neglected, based 
on calculations made, which showed that these walls were overloaded. This does 
not mean that these walls do not contribute to the overall stability of the building. 
The contribution from flat division walls will result in a smaller loading on the other 
stabilizing walls, the fire walls included. This involves that the moment in the walls 
would most likely be smaller than calculated above. The facade walls located at the 
end of each stabilizing wall could also be included in the stability calculations. This 
can be done by adding the load and the area of the walls to the fire walls. The walls 
will contribute to the moment capacity as the flanges contributes to the stiffness in an 
I-beam.
When checking the compressive bearing capacity, it appears that the facade walls 
facing the backyard are overloaded,. The masonry percentage in these facade walls 
is smaller than in the facade walls facing the street. Therefore, they have a smaller 
bearing capacity. Additionally, the walls are carrying an extra floor with facade walls 
from the rooftop addition. Because of this, the facade walls that faces the backyard 
are overloaded. Could the height be decreased towards the backyard? This could 
result in improved light- and shadow conditions in the backyard as well.
Lastly, some conservative choices have been made in the calculations of the 
facade walls. The most critical eccentricity in the facade walls, that are found for 
load combination STR3, are used in the calculations of the bearing capacity for all 
load combinations. This results in a case were the walls are checked for both the 
maximum amount of vertical load from STR1 (imposed load dominating) in addition to 
the most critical load combination for the maximum lateral load, STR3 (dead load is 
unfavourable), at the same time.
A conservative approach has been used from start to end, which has resulted in a 
robust model of the building. By fine-tuning and doing more detailed calculations, it 
is expected that the results will change and that a sufficient bearing capacity will be 
obtained.

CLT elements are produced with fire resistances of 30, 60 and 90 minutes, which in 
this case is not sufficient. The central precondition for the use of wood for the structural 
system is establishing a fire safety plan that allows the CLT elements to maintain a 
significant structural capacity when exposed to fire. This is in particular an important 
criterion to take care of, when the building is build for residential use.
CLT is a combustible material that contributes to fire, because it burns on its surface 
and releases energy, leading to propagation of fire as well as development of smoke. 
[Klippel, Schmid & Frangi] This means that the CLT elements will contribute to the fire 
as long as the surfaces of the CLT elements are exposed. However, CLT is not readily 
ignited, because of its relatively low area to volume ratio. This results in a relatively 
high combustibility compared to traditional timber framed construction. When the heat 
on the surface excess a sufficient temperature, the surface starts to char slowly at a 
predictable rate. [Mayo, 2015] The thickness of the charred layer grows during a fire, 
leading to a decreased cross-section of the CLT element’s cross-section. This char 
functions as a protecting layer for the underlying timber. [Klippel, Schmid & Frangi]
The required fire resistance of the load bearing structure constructed of CLT elements 
can be achieved in different ways, for example by oversizing the elements so that the 
expected charring depth in the wood is taken into account. In that case, it is possible 
to have uncovered wooden interior surfaces.
Secondly, the oversized elements can be combined with fire protective plates to slow 
down the charring. In that way, the size of the elements can be reduced, but the 
bearing capacity of the elements is still not fully utilised.
The last option is to completely protect the wood against fire in the time, where the 
fire resistance of the wooden building element is expected to be undamaged. This is 
done by encapsulation of the load bearing CLT elements with, for instance, gypsum 
boards. [Vestergaard & Jensen, 2015]
The chosen solution for this project is to make a combination of oversizing the 
elements contemporary with the use of fire protective gypsum boards. This has been 
incorporated in the structural design of the CLT elements, as seen in Section 6.4 .
Generally, placing a 15 mm layer of fire protective gypsum board on the exposed side 
of the CLT element will improve the fire resistance with additionally 30 minutes, and 
two layers will offer 60 minutes extra. Two layers of fire protective Knauf Danogips’ 
Secura Boards Type F-1 are used as fire protective system for the load bearing and 
stabilising elements in the rooftop addition, as seen in Figure 6.53. [Knauf Danogips]
It is limited how many tests that have been conducted in full-scale concerning the fire 
protection of CLT. However, one research conducted in Italy has indicated that the 
solid CLT elements wrapped up in gypsum are only slightly damaged, when subjected 
to 60 minutes of fire. [Mayo, 2015]

6.5.4  PLOT-RATIO
The plot-ratio is meant to indicate and ensure that there is not living too many people 
in dense conurbations. In addition, it is ensured that it is comfortable for the occupants 
to live in and around their homes. In Denmark, restrictions have been formulated 
regarding the established areas to comply with these demands. The plot-ratio is

6.5.5  DAYLIGHT CONDITIONS
The amount of light in a room used for stay and habitation must be satisfactory for 
the occupants, without adding unnecessary heat to the room. There is a wish for 
good daylight conditions in all the apartments, meaning as much light as possible 
contemporary with avoiding both overheating and glare.
The daylight quality depends on several factors. First of all, the window design, 
location, surroundings, orientation and light transmittance have an impact on the 
quality of light in a room. The distance to the neighbouring building bodies and their 
height also impacts the daylight in a building. This includes the individual wings in a 
building block as well – their height and how they are located relative to one another 
will impact the daylight conditions in the other wings.
In addition, the inflow of light depends on the placement of the windows in the building 
envelope as well as their shape and dimensions. The higher a window is placed, 
the deeper is the ingress of light, and the better distribution of light in the room. 
However, a high window placement may lead to glare, and in that case a need for 
solar screenings arises. It has to be ensured that the occupants have appropriate 
view from the apartments as well. [Josefsen & Homann, 2014]
The amount of daylight that gets into a building has influence on the overall energy 
consumption of the building. Good daylight conditions can lead to a smaller need for 
electrical lightning, and contribute to the well-being and comfort of the occupants.
==PAGE:159==
149
CONCEPT DEVELOPMENT
A room has sufficient daylight if the total area of the vertical windows corresponds to 
10% of the floor area contemporary with a light transmittance on 0,75 for the windows 
as a minimum. This applies to “standard” conditions of the placement and design of 
the room and the building respectively.
The daylight in a room used for habitation is considered to be sufficient if the daylight 
factor is 2% or more in half of the room. Generally, it is strived towards that the daylight 
should be the primary source of light in the daytime. [Bygningsreglementet]

6.5.6  ACOUSTIC INDOOR CLIMATE
The building must be designed and constructed so that satisfactory sound conditions 
are ensured for the occupants. Good sound conditions are essential for a good indoor 
climate. In a block of flats, good sound conditions imply that the occupants are not 
bothered by noise coming from installations, staircases, adjacent apartments, or traffic. 
Correct sizing of both interior and exterior building elements as well as installations 
is essential to obtain the desired soundproofing of the building. Furthermore, it is 
important to focus on connections as well as flanking building elements. [Josefsen & 
Homann, 2014]
The requirements valid for the rooftop addition is the requirements that corresponds 
to new build. The legislations regarding the sound conditions in a building are stated 
in the Danish Building Regulations 2015, Section 6.4. [Bygningsreglementet] In this 
project it is strived to meet the relevant legislations regarding sound and acoustics, 
but it is not one of the main aspects. However, the use of CLT as structural elements 
in the concept proposal has naturally given rise to an overall study of the sound 
performance of the elements.
An essential benefit by using CLT as construction material is that the elements can 
achieve adequate sound insulation. It is well-known that the mass of a material is 
an important factor concerning sound insulation in buildings, because the mass 
is affecting the performance of the insulation. This is why CLT elements with their 
massive structure have better sound insulation compared to lightweight wood 
construction, but worse compared to concrete. [Karacabeyli & Douglas, 2013] Raw 
CLT elements used for walls and floors are more than likely not able to meet minimum 
acoustical requirements without adding layers of sound-absorbing acoustic insulation, 
suspended ceiling, among others.
When using CLT as construction material it is generally advantageous to make the 
structural walls several stories tall to speed up the construction time, but on the other 
hand it is having an adverse effect on the flanking noise and sound transmission. 
[Mayo, 2015]
However, construction with CLT elements is relatively new to the building construction 
market, and many acoustical tests of CLT still needs to be done. [Karacabeyli & 
Douglas, 2013]

From the sections described above, it is seen that the project can not meet the 
present requirements stated in the Danish Building Regulations 2015 within all the 
highlighted aspects. This means that it would not be possible to realize the project as 
the regulations look today. This goes in particularly for the height and fire conditions

of the building with the rooftop addition. There are not many high-rise buildings to be 
found in Denmark, which is due to the regulations stating that all buildings taller than 
22 m needs a separate permission. Furthermore, it is not legal to build buildings of 
wood that are more than four floors. 
The requirements are based on old experiences, as for example the big fires in 
Copenhagen in 1728 and 1795. [SBi 142] A question that comes to mind in this relation 
is if the regulations have anything to do with reality today – are they too strict? Many 
conditions in the building technology are constantly changing, why it can be discussed 
if the regulations should follow this ongoing development to a greater extent.
One of the big challenges when building in height, in relation to the urban densification, 
is to violate the traditional requirements. This concerns the present fire rules that must 
be rephrased so that a generally acceptance is obtained of the fact that buildings 
taller than four floors can wholly or partially contain load bearing building elements 
that are incombustible.
In Norway, one of the tallest timber buildings in the world has been constructed. The 
building is 14 floors tall, and the fire design of the building performs according to 
Eurocode 5 [DS/EN 1995-1-1] by the use of the so-called “reduced cross-section 
method” that takes into account the effective cross-section after charring at the outside 
surface. The load bearing system is in the project required to resist 90 minutes of fire 
without failure, and fire protection such as sprinkling and fire painting in escape routes 
are incorporated as well. [Abrahamsen & Malo] These experiences from Norway 
shows that it is possible to build taller buildings of timber without reducing the overall 
fire safety. 
It is evident that changes have to be made in the Danish Building regulations if it should 
be possible to build rooftop additions on the existing building stock in Copenhagen.

7.10  ERECTION METHOD
The overall aim of the erection method for the rooftop addition is to avoid bothering 
the current occupants, living in the existing building, as little as possible. There are 
several factors that comes into play in the planning of the erection process. By being 
aware of these factors from the initial phase of the project, a smooth erection may be 
achievable. The following is a description of how the current occupants are taken into 
consideration throughout the construction of the rooftop addition. Furthermore, it is 
important to consider how the transportation, handling, lifting and assembling of the 
large CLT elements can be done in the construction of the new structure on the rooftop
The first step is to completely cover the existing building from the ground up to above 
the roof by the use of scaffolding. The roof of the existing building is going to be 
completely demolished, to make space for the new rooftop structure, as seen on 
Figure 7.14 below. The structure will be open throughout a long period of time, why 
the cover of the building is important. There has to be enough space for the rooftop 
addition beneath the covering.
The construction site in this project is a vulnerable place, where the risk of water 
damage is comparatively high. CLT is moisture sensitive and has to be protected 
against all kinds of external influences that can affect the performance of the individual 
elements, such as water infiltration. This has to be ensured during the manufacturing, 
transportation, handling and assembling of the CLT elements as well.
The existing roof is demolished from the outside in, and the roof at the uppermost floor 
is maintained. This is done to avoid rehousing of the occupants living on the 3rd floor 
of the existing building. Additionally, the floor construction above the third floor will not 
be used to carry the rooftop addition. In the demolishing of the roof, the workmen have 
to be aware that the uppermost part of the facade walls must be tore down so that the 
top of the facade is placed lower than the top of the fire and gable walls, but higher 
than the rest of the existing masonry walls. Firstly, this I done to make sure that the top 
of the facade walls and the fire and gable walls are in the correct level in relation to 
the two levels in the grating, that they are carrying. The gable and fire walls carry the 
upper layer of the grating, while the facade carries the lower one. Secondly, it is done 
in order to ensure that the walls, that do not have a sufficient bearing capacity, will not 
carry any load from the rooftop addition.
The erection of the rooftop addition is going to be conducted so that different tasks 
can be executed at the same time as parallel tasks. In this way the erection time is 
speeded up and the current occupants are not bothered longer than necessary. The 
erection procedure starts by constructing the structural system with CLT elements at 
the bottom floor of the addition, 4th floor, and work upwards. When the structural system 
on the 4th floor is finished, the same is done at the next floor, 5th floor, simultaneously 
with the exterior and interior layers at the 4th floor, and so on. It is essential to enclose 
the construction on each floor as soon as possible before the interior work can start. 
To success in using this type of construction method it is crucial to coordinate between 
the different disciplines, because they will work simultaneously at the same floor. It is 
mostly impossible to obtain an entirely smooth and trouble-free process, which is 
why lags have to be included to take potential delays into account, so that the start of 
another task will not be affected. 
Scheduling and coordination are both key elements in the work with prefabricated CLT 
elements. The erection of the building must be tracked in a way so that the correct 
sequence of the mounting of each element is secured. The tracking as well as all 
design issues have to be prepared thoroughly before the construction start. In this 
way, work efficiency may increase, the process may speed up and the construction 
time is kept to a minimum. [Mayo, 2015]

The discussion contains considerations abut the different aspect that the conducted 
project encompasses. This is done in order to give an overview of the main challenges 
and potentials of the project. The discussion is based on the following two questions:
	
-   What could have been done differently in the project?
	
-   What prospective potentials does the project have?
The project has demonstrated that more than one approach can be used to make a 
proposal for a rooftop addition, but it is difficult to know from the very beginning what 
approach is the most optimal. This is why one approach at a time must be tried out. 
If an approach turns out not to match the project, another approach must be tried out 
and so on. In a large and complex project like this, several failed attempts can be 
necessary to get to the most optimal approach. In this project it has not been possible 
to try out several approaches, due to the limited period of time. However, if the project 
should be repeated or further developed, it could be valuable to make use of another 
approach for e.g. trying out a different structural system. By doing so, other results 
could have been achieved.
It can be seen from the results, that the existing structure has an insufficient bearing 
capacity, when using the approach presented in this thesis. The calculations, that 
have been executed, are based on simplified methods and rough assumptions have 
been made along the way. A lot of the assumptions made have been done in order 
to simplify the rather complex structure. Additionally, it is important to emphasize that 
the assumptions have been conservative, already starting by the assumptions made 
for the strength and stiffness of the masonry. The material properties for the masonry 
could be specified by taking samples of the existing masonry, in order to get a more 
accurate picture of how the masonry will behave structurally. The samples could result 
in a decreased strength and stiffness as well. 
Additionally, it is important to underline that the stabilizing behaviour of the building 
block is more complex than presented. There are more stabilizing walls in each 
direction, than included in the calculations of the individual wings. The walls in 
the entire block will work together and help stabilizing the building in the different 
directions. In the stability calculations, the stabilising flat division walls are neglected, 
based on calculations made, which shows that these walls are overloaded. This does 
not mean that these walls do not contribute to the overall stability of the building. 
The contribution from the existing flat division walls will result in a decreased loading 
on the other stabilizing walls. This involves that the moment in the walls would most 
likely be smaller than calculated above. The facade walls located at the end of each 
stabilizing wall could also be included in the stability calculations. This can be done 
by adding the load and the area of the walls to the fire walls.
Hypothetically, it would be necessary to strengthen either the existing masonry 
structure, the foundations or both, in an attempt of adding more floors. The bearing 
capacity of foundations are not calculated, because it is beyond the limits of this 
project. However, it is important to emphasize that the bearing capacity of the 
foundations will be essential to investigate, because it will have an impact on the 
outcome of the project. If the foundations are not sufficient, there will be a need for 
strengthening of the existing foundations. This will have an influence on the extent of 
the construction work, that will impact both the cost and the time of the entire project. 
In that connection, it would be relevant to study how a strengthening of the foundations 
could affect the bearing capacity of the existing building, and how many extra floors 
it could lead to.
The investigations and calculations shows that building on top of the existing building 
is rather complex. It depends a lot on how the added structure is built and how the 
forces are transferred to the existing structure. It would be desirable to find a rule of 
==PAGE:183==

thumb or a general code of practice on how an existing structure will behave when a 
specific number of floors are added. This has however proved to be a difficult task, 
because of the large magnitude and complexity of the project, where many factors 
come into play. Although, this could possibly be obtained in further studies.
Some challenges are present in terms of acceptance of the building regulations. There 
are many rules and constraints, which causes that it is complicated to actually realise 
the project at present. This goes in particular for the height and the fire conditions 
of the building. However, these requirements are based on old experiences, and it 
can be questionable if the regulations have anything to do with reality today. Many 
conditions in the building technology are constantly changing, which is why it can 
be discussed if the regulations should follow this ongoing development to a greater 
extent.
One of the big challenges when building in height, in relation to the urban densification, 
is to violate the traditional requirements. This concerns the present fire rules that must 
be rephrased so that a generally acceptance is obtained of the fact that buildings 
taller than four floors can wholly or partially contain load bearing building elements 
that are incombustible. In this connection, it would be obvious to adapt or learn from 
the experiences that have been obtained in other countries.
Lastly, it has to mentioned that the building block considered falls within preservation 
value three (and four). This has not been taken into consideration in the development 
of the concept. The concept is challenging the strict regulations about preserving 
the architectural expression of the existing building mass in Copenhagen – is the 
concept for the rooftop addition ruining the original expression, or does it enhance 
and underline the existing expression with its appearance?

CONCLUSION
There has been developed a solution for a sustainable three storey high rooftop 
addition in the project. The investigations show that there is a possibility of building 
on top of the existing building structure, both in regard to the structural, architectural 
and practical aspects. However, it is seen that the project can not meet the present 
requirements stated in the Danish Building Regulations 2015 within all the aspects 
highlighted in the project. This means that it would not be possible to realize the project 
as the regulations are formulated today. This especially goes for the height and the 
fire conditions of the whole building. It is evident that changes have to be made in the 
Danish Building Regulations if it should be possible to build rooftop additions on the 
existing building stock in Copenhagen.
The structural analysis of the existing conditions shows that the structure has residual 
load bearing capacity. However, not all walls have a sufficient bearing capacity. When 
building on top of the existing structure, it is important to transfer the load to the 
walls that have sufficient bearing capacity, both to the walls that needs extra load 
due to the eccentricity, and to the stabilizing walls. Adding on extra floors does not 
only give extra vertical load, but the moment from the wind load will increase as 
well. The stabilizing walls needs extra vertical load, when the moment from the wind 
load is increasing with the extra building height. An increased weight is helping with 
stabilizing the building. Therefore, it is important to ensure that the stabilising walls 
get loaded as well. However, too much load will impact the bearing capacity, and can 
cause failure of the walls.
These demands for the structural system has been accommodated by the choice 
of using CLT elements for the structural system. The light weight of CLT provides a 
potential for building on existing rooftops, because it can contribute with a certain 
amount of weight, but not too much.
Furthermore, CLT elements can turn out having a beneficial impact on the environment, 
especially when compared to other material alternatives with identical functionality, 
like steel and concrete.
The structural design of the rooftop addition shows that the existing structure does 
not have sufficient bearing capacity to carry the three extra floors. A conservative 
approach has been used from start to end, which has resulted in a robust model of the 
building. By fine-tuning and doing more detailed calculations, it is expected that the 
results will change and that a sufficient bearing capacity will be obtained.
By making a bearing transition system between the old and new structure, the concept 
will be easier to reuse and should be able to be used at most existing structures. 
However, it still needs to be modified for each single case.
Finally, a proposal for a concept of the rooftop addition have been developed in the 
project, and it has been underlined that additions on top of the existing building stock 
can actually be achieved. The concept can be a part of the strategy regarding the 
densification of Copenhagen. The possibility to build on top of the old neighbourhoods 
will deal with the lack of ground space that is present in Copenhagen. In this way, 
homes for newcomers can be build within the constraints of Copenhagen, where it is 
mostly desired to live.
