Abstract 
 
Qeqqata Municipality, in Greenland, has shown initiative in desiring more sustainable 
developments, by examining the possibility for implementing wastewater treatment in 
Sisimiut. However, there is further planning and research required in reaching the full 
potential for the community.  
This study aimed to investigate different alternatives for handling sludge from the chemical-
mechanical treatment process planned in Sisimiut.  
 
By conduction field measurements on wastewater flow rates, chemical and physical 
characterisation of wastewater and sludge generated through chemical enhanced primary 
sedimentation; estimations on the quantities of sludge that could be expected and the 
characteristics of the generated sludge were performed.  
 
During April and May 2016, wastewater flow rates at two outlets where there are 
investigations on implementing wastewater treatment facilities, were determined through field 
measurements. Estimations on the yearly quantities of wastewater discharged at the outlets 
were based on records on water consumption by residents in Sisimiut. Wastewater samples 
from the two outlets were analysed for organic content, nutrient levels and solids 
characteristics.  
 
The quantity and characteristics of sludge produced from chemical enhanced primary 
sedimentation was determined based on results from the field measurements and laboratory 
analyses. Sludge was further analysed for the concentration of trace elements and nutrient 
levels, in order to assess the need for further processing in order to get a quality meeting 
Danish standards, in relation to regulations and laws on disposal and use.  
The concentrations of trace elements were within the regulations given by the Danish Order 
on the use of waste for agricultural purposes. 
 
An evaluation on the feasibility of the various options for sludge treatment in cold climate 
regions was performed as support in providing a foundation to further development of designs 
towards a treatment plant. With an estimated yearly sludge volume of 3187 m3 after 
thickening, and a dry mass of nearly 13 metric tons per year, it is clear that it is necessary to 
include sludge management as a significant part of the overall planning strategy for 
wastewater treatment. Processes and management strategies evaluated for implementation in 
Sisimiut is dewatering by filter-belt press or sludge-freezing beds, stabilisation through 
anaerobic digestion or composting, incineration and land application.

Acknowledgements 
 
This report evaluates alternatives for sludge and biosolids management in regards to 
implementing chemical-mechanical wastewater treatment in Sisimiut, Greenland, and is a 30 
ECTS masters project written in collaboration with ARTEK DTU and DTU Civil 
Engineering. This thesis concludes the author’s Master’s degree in Environmental 
Technology from University of Stavanger, with a major in Water Science.  
Fieldwork in Sisimiut was conducted in the period between April 24th and June 12th of 2016 
Laboratory analyses were performed at ARTEK in Sisimiut between June 07th and June 14th, 
and at DTU in Lyngby between June 29th and June 31st. The report was submitted September 
19th, and defended September 26th.

1 Introduction 
 
Wastewater treatment in the Arctic is difficult due to the climatic conditions (1,2,3), large 
differences in economical situation between communities, lack of personnel for operations 
and maintenance, and the limited accessibility in some remote regions. In general, treatment 
of municipal wastewater is poor or non-existing in many parts of the Arctic (4). However, there 
are examples where treatment is performed.  
 
Sludge is a product resulting from wastewater treatment, and raw sewage sludge contains 
large amounts of pollutants. If the sludge is processed sufficiently, some of these pollutants 
are reduced and the solids can be environmentally safe to apply on land and also be used for 
agricultural purpose (5). Solids that meet beneficial use criteria are then termed biosolids (6).  
There are many diverse processing methods for sludge and biosolids that have been proven 
successful in temperate regions (6,7,8). However, not many of these methods are used widely in 
the Arctic. If adapting the already established methods for treatment of sludge in temperate 
regions to cold climate regions, new windows of opportunity can be opened when it comes to 
exploiting a resource that is found wherever there are people, namely, their resulting 
wastewater. If the technology can be further developed, there are several benefits to aspects of 
society through economical enhancements, increased environmental sustainability and further 
advancement in public health and awareness.  
 
The characteristics of the sludge determine what kind of processing is suitable for further 
treatment or reuse, and will depend greatly on the source, the type of treatment process it has 
been through, and how long it has been allowed to age. The parameters that are analysed 
when determining a suitable way to handle the biosolids are the organic content, nutrients, 
metals, pathogens and toxic organics (6). 
This report aims to evaluate the alternatives available for sludge and biosolids management, 
in cold regions.

1.1 Sisimiut and the sustainability project 
 
Sisimiut, in Qeqqata Municipality, is the second largest town in Greenland, located on the 
west coast at 66°56’20’’N, 53°40’20’’W (9). At this latitude, there are no trees, and Sisimiut is 
in an area with discontinuous permafrost. Metrological data from 1961 to 1990 reports that 
yearly average day temperature is -3,9℃, with a precipitation average of 383 mm a year (10). 
The average day temperature is above zero degrees Celsius in the months of June to 
September, and the average day temperature does not reach above 10℃. 
 
There are approximately 5540 inhabitants and 2211 households in Sisimiut in 2016 (11,12)
.  
Wastewater from the town is discharged untreated to the ocean, and based on the online 
technical map provided by NunaGIS and information provided by the municipality, there are 
nine different outlets in total, including wastewater from the shrimp processing facility. The 
situation in the town today stands that only 80% of households have wastewater discharge 
connected to the municipal sewer system. In Sisimiut, around 198 and 232 households have 
bucket toilets or collection tanks, respectively, and the content is collected by trucks and then 
discharged directly to sea (13). As previously mentioned, the piped wastewater is also 
ultimately discharged untreated to sea. There has been a lot of research done on the discharge 
recipients in Sisimiut, and the environmental impacts of receiving raw wastewater. One area, 
Ulkebugten, contains one of the larger outlets and discharges in a sill fjord (14), which then 
limits the circulation of water. The sediments here show elevated levels of anthropogenic 
contamination, such as nutrients and heavy metals (15). Where the content of the bucket toilets 
and the collection tanks are released, on the northern side of town by the so-called Chocolate 
Factory, it is occasionally possible to see traces of discharge onshore as the tide rises (13). This 
is also where one of the larger outlet is located.  The raw wastewater is not only a source of 
chemical contamination, but the microbial components also pose a risk to the ecosystem and 
human health, such as the risk of direct contact with pathogens through human activity or an 
increase in antibiotic resistant microorganisms (16). 
 
In 2012, Qeqqata Municipality presented a plan called the “Sustainability Project 2020”, 
focusing on sustainable development in an Arctic community (17). One focus area is to 
minimize the environmental impacts of discharging raw wastewater into the marine 
environment. In relation to that, they have decided to investigate the feasibility for 
implementing a wastewater treatment plant in Sisimiut, being the first domestic treatment 
plant in Greenland. The treatment is a chemical-mechanical process, where a coagulant and a 
flocculent are added to the wastewater. A rotating belt removes the flocs formed and the 
produced sludge is scraped off and put into containers (18,19). A pilot project was used in 2013 
to evaluate the effectiveness of this proposed treatment plant (20,18), where analyses were 
performed on the effluent quality and the sludge characteristics (18). So far there are no 
Greenlandic requirement for the effluent quality from wastewater treatment or the use of 
biosolids on land, which means that when evaluations of the pilot performance were made, 
Danish standards were used. The report concludes that the sludge is suitable for agricultural 
purposes, according to “Danish Order on the use of waste for agricultural purposes” (21), 
with exception to the values of LAS, recommending that the sludge be further composted to 
reduce the high LAS concentrations. Apart from this conclusion however, the report does not 
suggest any further plans for sludge handling.

In Sisimiut, many of the households have direct outlets for grey water to the surrounding 
environment. There are also households that do not have pressurised in-home water services, 
leaving the residents dependant on collecting water from service houses located in different 
parts of town or provided from a water truck (22). Unfortunately, there is little mapping on how 
many households have grey water discharge into the surrounding environment. It is therefore 
not necessarily valid to base estimations on total wastewater discharge numbers on the town’s 
water consumption, due to the numerous unrecorded discharge outlets.  
 
During previous projections for numbers on the treatment plant for Sisimiut, little 
consideration was taken into account when it came to the amount of sludge that was projected 
to result from the treatment process. In order to plan for proper disposal or processing 
facilities for sludge, their quantities, together with the sources and characteristics of the solids, 
must be known. The amount of solids entering the wastewater treatment plant may vary, and 
for smaller plants, like the one planned in Sisimiut, large variations may be expected (6)
.  In 
Sisimiut, the general plan is to have two smaller wastewater treatment facilities, one located 
in Ulkebugten and one by the Chocolate Factory (18).  
 
The most commonly used method for disposal of sludge in cold regions is the use of sludge 
pits, as the pits can be quarried in various materials, including permafrost, snow, and rock (2). 
Although, studies on the treatment performance on these pits have shown that they do not 
deliver a sufficient treatment of human waste. Other methods used are sludge drying and 
freezing, and disposal to landfills. Unfortunately, none of these methods consider biosolids as 
a valuable resource. With other processing methods, the solids can be used for recovery of 
raw materials, heat and energy, or for agricultural purposes (7). The sludge management from 
the wastewater treatment in Sisimiut should therefore be fully considered and further 
incorporated into a larger plan on the municipal level. There is a need for further investigation 
into what processes would be feasible for the facility, with consideration to the biosolid 
characteristics and the potential for reuse and recovery, in aiding for overall sustainable 
developments in relation to wastewater treatment in Greenland.

2 Theoretical background  
 
There are several considerations to take before implementing a wastewater treatment facility. 
Planning in the Arctic can be challenging due to climatic constrains, higher costs as a result of 
the climatic conditions and the remoteness of some regions. There might also be a diversity of 
cultures with different lifestyles and values, which may cause differences when it comes to 
the objectives and necessity for wastewater treatment.  
 
Building a treatment facility in a cold climate region, like Sisimiut, can be somewhere from 
two to five times more expensive than in temperate regions (2)
. This is generally because of a 
larger insulation requirement due to lower temperatures, areas with permafrost, and higher 
transport costs. This requires a comprehension for how to work around the environmental 
constraints, and planning needs to be innovative, simplified and cost effective. 
 
The management of solids generated through wastewater treatment can be one of the most 
expensive and difficult sections of the overall treatment process (6). The product, biosolids, 
can be used beneficially, although in many cases in the Arctic it is not. 
 
The following chapter aims to give an introduction to some of the parameters that needs to be 
investigated with consideration to treatment performance, chemical and physical 
characteristics of wastewater and biosolids, and options for managing biosolids. 
 
2.1 Flow measurements  
 
Having estimations on the quantity of wastewater is an important part when planning a 
wastewater treatment plant. The amount of water that can be expected at a treatment facility 
can vary greatly in composition and flow in terms of hours, days, and seasons. These 
variations are of importance when it comes to design, operation and control of a treatment 
plant (8). Flow measurements serve to present data for calculations on pollutant mass loading, 
estimating treatment costs and plant capacity, and to provide usable information for 
enforcement purposes (23). Flow is usually reported as the hydraulic flow rate (mass or volume 
over time), and the parameter can either be measured or estimated theoretically based on 
sources information.  
 
In general, wastewater is categorized into four main sources in relation to flow (6): domestic 
wastewater, which includes wastewater from residences, institutional and commercial 
facilities, industrial wastewater, stormwater and infiltration or inflow. Infiltration happens 
when water enters the collection system through e.g. leaking joints or cracks, and inflow is 
when stormwater enters through e.g. access port covers (manholes).  
 
The flow and the components found in wastewater will be influenced by what kind of 
collection system is constructed. There are typically three different systems (24): sanitary 
collection, storm collection and combined collection systems. In Sisimiut there is a sanitary 
collection systems, with no collection system for stormwater. There is little industrial 
wastewater in the town, with the exception of a shrimp process facility. However, the facility 
has a separate collection system that discharge to sea. Thus, the expected wastewater flow in 
the sanitary collection system in Sisimiut has two major components, namely domestic 
wastewater and infiltration/inflow.

Wastewater flow rates from domestic wastewater are usually determined from existing 
records or by conducting field measurements (6)
. In Sisimiut, there are no existing records for 
wastewater flow. However, flow rates of domestic wastewater is a function of the water 
consumption (24), hence records on water consumption may be used for estimating wastewater 
flow rates. Metcalf & Eddy (2003) states that numbers on water consumption are suitable for 
areas where 90 percent or higher ends up as wastewater, and this method is therefore typically 
used in regions with little agriculture.  
 
Nukissiorfiit Qeqqa, the potable water supplier in Sisimiut, has records over water 
consumption for the town (22). These records could be separated into household use and 
industrial use, with numbers on daily consumption. Figure 2.1 shows the annual fluctuation in 
water consumption in Sisimiut, by household use. The graphs do not include water

base total wastewater discharge numbers on the town’s water consumption. For this reason, 
field measurements may be the better option to acquire data on wastewater flow rates.

way of obtaining flow rates data is by doing volumetric measurements. This method 
associates the measurement of volume to the time required to measure the volume. There are 
several techniques to perform such measurements, like the bucket method or vessel volume 
(25). The bucket method involves filling a bucket of known volume from a wastewater stream,

data by measuring a vessel volume and the frequency at which it empties.  
 
In general, wastewater flow tends to follow a diurnal pattern (6). At hours when water 
consumption is low, consequently the flow will be low as well, typically early in the morning. 
The flow rate during these hours will mostly consist of infiltration water and small quantities 
of sanitary water. The first peak flow usually takes place after the first peak water use late 
morning. Generally a second peak occurs late afternoon, and the variations between high and 
low peaks depend on the number of households connected to the outlet, and the length of the 
collection system. For smaller communities the amplitude of peak flow rates may be larger 
due to lower storage capacity in the collection system.  
 
It can be assumed that the contribution of infiltration and inflow water to the collection 
system in Arctic areas will vary greatly with seasons. During winter months when there are 
colder temperatures, hence less water in liquid form, the contribution will be low. It can be 
expected that during spring and early summer when there is snowmelt and rainfall, the 
contribution will be larger. In Sisimiut there are large, monthly differences in the quantity of 
precipitation (26), as seen from Figure 2.2. 
 
There are peak amounts of precipitation the past three years around August to October. From 
Figure 2.3, it is indicated that these are months with temperatures above freezing. The 
temperatures reaches above zero degrees around April - May, which indicates that these are 
the months where the majority of melting can be expected. The contribution of infiltration and 
inflow water to the total wastewater flow rate can thus be predicted to be largest in the months 
between April and October. The quantity of this contribution needs to be taken into 
consideration when dimensioning the capacity for a wastewater treatment facility.  
With measurements and data on the yearly quantity of wastewater expected at the treatment 
plant, the yearly load of biosolids can be estimated based on the characteristics of the 
wastewater and the efficiency of the treatment performance.  
 
It is also essential to include predictions on population growth for a region when 
dimensioning a treatment plant. Qeqqata Municipality have done a projection of the 
population in Sisimiut from 1999 to 2011, which shows that if the population growth 
continues at the current trend, the population in 2024 will be at 5874 inhabitants (27). There are 
some new residential areas planned in areas around Ulkebugten (28). All new commercial and 
residential areas are now being connected to the collection system (29), but depending on 
where these new households and residential areas are situated, the contribution to the 
treatment facility will vary.

2.2  Chemical-mechanical wastewater treatment  
 
The treatment method investigated for wastewater treatment in Sisimiut is a chemical-
mechanical process. The facility is described in chapter 2.2.1. Following is a summary from 
Rambøll’s report (18), on why this processing facility was investigated as an option for 
wastewater treatment in Sisimiut.  
 
Firstly, the process was considered favourable due to its robustness. Compared to other 
processes, such as different biological treatment methods, the process is less sensitive to 
changes in characteristics, such as fluctuation in temperatures. As the process does not require 
a heated environment, the operational cost will also be lower than compared to a biological 
process.  
 
Secondly, the facility consists of few components, which are not relatively costly and can be 
kept stored. It is evaluated that maintenance can be done locally, and that operation of the 
process requires personnel with practical experience, as opposed to an extended education. 
The report states that treatment performance can be assessed visually, and that there is no 
need for electrodes or laboratory facilities to optimize the daily operations.  
 
2.2.1 Treatment mechanism 
 
The process is composed of three mixing basins, and a final settling basin where a belt filter is 
included. The coagulant is added in the first mixing basin, followed by addition of NaOH for 
pH adjustment in the second basin. The third basin is where the flocculent is added. In the 
final basin, the flocs are allowed to settle, and removed by a belt filter.  
 
During the pilot project in 2013, two different coagulants were investigated, Kemira PAX-
XL60 and PIX 118. Superfloc C-2260 was used as a flocculent. The flocculent is a cationic 
polyacrylamide polymer, in the form of an emulsion.  
PIX 118 is a coagulant with ferric chloride sulphate as the precipitating agent, and PAX-
XL60 has polyaluminum hydroxidechloride silicate as the precipitating agent. There are some 
environmental concerns with the use of coagulants containing aluminium (30). Aluminium can 
have acute toxic effects to fish and other aquatic plants and organisms if concentrations 
exceed the species threshold level. At lower concentrations, it can have an impact on growth, 
reproduction and behaviour. Other damages may occur if aluminium accumulates on the gills 
of fish. However, these effects are mainly in fresh water environments, and it is debated if 
aluminium will have the same impact on the marine ecosystem (31). This is due to the 
relatively constant pH in the marine environment and a generally high concentration of 
particles, where aluminium will be rapidly removed through the formation of colloids.  
Aluminium in biosolids is usually in an insoluble form, but under acidic conditions it can 
become mobile and certain plants accumulate high concentrations of aluminium (32). However, 
it is not expected to biomagnify in the terrestrial food chain.  
 
In general, enhanced chemical precipitation increases the removal of suspended solids, COD, 
phosphorous and heavy metals (6). The efficiency of the process depends on the quantity of 
chemicals added, mixing regime, and to what extent the process is monitored and controlled. 
According to the DLVO theory, it is the particle-particle interactions that determine whether 
coagulation will occur (33). In wastewater, particles typically have a negative surface charge (6). 
By lowering the surface charge, through the addition of counter ions, coagulation is

As mentioned, particles have a negative surface charge, which causes the solids to repel. In 
addition, the particles are weakly attracted to water molecules, called hydration (34).  
This is either by weak chemical bonding or by capillary action. For sludge, this may interfere 
with dewatering and thickening. However, the addition of metal salts will reduce this effect. 
 
Flocculation involves the same principle as for coagulation, where differences between the 
particles surface charge and the polymer are neutralised (35). However, the addition of a 
polymer causes bridging between the particles, as it adsorbs to the particle. This may give 
stronger aggregates as the repulsive forces between the particles are reduced further.  
 
A disadvantage with chemical unit processes is the increased total dissolved solids (TDS) 
content in the effluent (6). When a chemical is added to the wastewater for enhanced 
sedimentation and phosphorous removal, the TDS concentration will always increased.  
 
Another disadvantage when it comes to chemical precipitation is the generation of a larger 
volume of sludge (34), which requires further handling, treatment and disposal. Information 
about the quantity of solids in the influent and effluent water of a treatment plant is an 
important tool when estimating biosolids production. Chemical precipitation leads to the 
formation of two sludge components:  Primary sludge, which consists of particles and colloids 
form the inlet, and chemical sludge, consisting of the chemical flocs formed. Typically the 
addition of iron as a precipitation agent doubles the quantity of sludge, compared to no 
chemical addition. The addition of aluminium can produce as much as three times the 
quantity of sludge. Accurate predictions on the quantity of produced sludge is difficult, hence 
also here bench-scale tests are performed in an attempt to estimate the sludge production.

2.3 Sludge handling 
 
There are diverse processes and operations to deal with the constituents removed in 
wastewater treatment. The main issue concerning sludge management is that the sludge is 
composed mostly of the matter that is subject to concern of untreated wastewater. In addition, 
only a small portion is solid matter, and the organic fraction of the solids will decompose and 
become offensive. The objectives for sludge management are therefore to reduce the water 
and organic content, and provide solids that are acceptable for reuse and final disposal.  
 
In the following chapter, an overview of the main processes for sludge operations and 
procedures will be presented. There is also a closer look on which of these have been tried in 
a cold climate region. The last part of the chapter presents the Danish laws and regulations on 
using sludge in agriculture and other land application. 
 
2.3.1 Available alternatives for processing and management  
 
Biosolids is the term used for sewage sludge that has a beneficial purpose (6). The transition 
from sludge to biosolids is through reduction of water content and reduction of pathogens in 
order to protect human health and the receiving environment. Hence, before biosolid can 
reach the final stage of disposal and be declared environmentally safe, further treatment 
processing is necessary (36).

Many of these technologies are not particularly pertinent for a region like Sisimiut, 
considering the operation conditions. It is not possible to go into detail about each process, 
but a closer description on some of the mechanisms will be discussed in Chapter 6 of this 
report. The relevant basic techniques will be further described in this chapter, with the 
objective to give an overview on the fundamental principles behind each process.  
 
Stabilisation is performed with the aim to reduce pathogens, remove repugnant odours and 
minimize the potential for putrefaction (6). All of the three objectives are related to microbial 
degradation of the organic fraction of the sludge. These scourge conditions are eliminated 
through reduction of the volatile content, and inhibiting microbial growth by creating 
unsuitable environments though the addition of chemicals. In addition, there can also be 
generation of profitable by-products, such as methane gas and nutrient rich solids (38). After 
the sludge has been stabilised to meet beneficial use criteria, it is considered to be biosolids.  
Before selecting a stabilisation process for sludge, there are various important factors to be 
considered. The quantity of sludge generated from the wastewater treatment process needs to 
be addressed, together with the supporting processes prior to stabilisation and the application 
of the final product (6). For instance, if the biosolids are intended for land application, 
pathogen reduction has to be addressed.  
 
Overall, thickening is considered a stage in the wastewater treatment process. For the facility 
in Sisimiut, this will be the separation of sludge from the liquid by the filter belt. As the water 
is allowed to drain off from the sludge, it is already at this stage dewatered to some extent. 
Depending on the water content after this process, it may be necessary to further dewater the 
sludge. There are several benefits through having a dewatered sludge (6). By increasing the 
solids content, the costs of transportation may be reduced considerably. Further, the potential 
for odour development and putrefaction may decrease.  In general, the sludge is also easier to 
handle. Dewatering is required in advance of incineration to increase the energy value by 
reducing moisture.  Likewise by dewatering the sludge prior to composting, the required 
quantity of bulking agents will be reduced.  
 
Incineration of biosolids and sludge will reduce the volume drastically, up to 80-90%. In 
addition, it kills pathogens and destroys most organic chemicals (36). Downsides by 
incineration are that trace elements are not removed and there is a release of carbon dioxide. 
Furthermore, there is no reclamation of the nutrient rich matter. There is also requirement of 
dealing with fly ash that is produced in the process. The residues after combustion can in 
some cases also be classified as hazardous waste (39), if they exceed pollutant regulations, 
especially if co-incinerated with municipal waste. In Sisimiut municipal there is incineration 
of waste, and it can be assumed that the procedure is already well established. 
 
Application to land can be done in several ways. It can be used beneficially for agricultural 
purposes, distributed to land or disposed to landfilling (6). Economically, landfill disposal is 
favourable compared to other options (36). 
Before using biosolids on land, there are several criteria and evaluations that need to be 
considered. There are regulations to the quality of biosolids, especially in relation to pathogen 
content and heavy metal concentrations. In addition, are also considerations when it comes to 
the disposal/application site. There has to be a screening process to evaluate the suitability of 
the area intended to receive the biosolids, typically in correlation to topography, soil 
characteristics, and accessibility and in relation to residential and commercial areas.

2.3.2 Sludge and biosolids management in cold climate regions 
 
In northern Canada, and Alaska, US, wastewater treatments with lagoons are frequently used 
(40,41), and it has been stated that municipal wastewater sludge in these situations has been 
overlooked (42). In Alaska, regions that do have sludge handling mostly have incineration or 
landfilling as the process of disposal (41). However, Fairbanks has a large composting facility, 
treating 20-30 tons of sludge per day (43). The town Iqualuit, in Nunavut Canada, has had a 
pilot project where composting of municipal sludge was researched, and freeze-thaw process 
was tried for dewatering (40,42). The project was proven to be successful, and as both the 
freeze-thaw and composting process was performed outdoors, using no additional facilities 
other than the natural climate in the region, it is a cost-effective way of managing sludge.  
In Tromsø, in the northern part of Norway, composting is also used for municipal sludge (44). 
This is performed in a windrow system, where the rows are turned and mixed periodically 
during composting. The finished material is used as landfill cover.  
 
The freeze-thaw process has also been evaluated as a way of stabilising sludge through the 
reduction of pathogenic content, and also the effects of long-term freezing (45). The report 
states that long-term freezing and repeated freeze-thaw cycles showed a noticeably reduction 
in microbial growth, however, it concludes that it is not sufficient for total elimination.  
 
Composting has been tried out in Sisimiut, using organic municipal waste (46). The initiative 
was a project performed in 2015 through the Technical University of Denmark. The aim was 
to reduce the amount of organic waste sent to incineration. The project proved to be a success, 
with good moisture content, nutrient balance and low heavy metal content, appropriate for 
composting. However, the result from analysis on the organic fraction of the compost showed 
low concentrations. Overall, the report suggests that further research on composting the 
municipal waste from the town should be carried out.  
 
Anaerobic digestion is in general not beneficial in cold climate regions, due to the required 
heating for mesophilic degradation to ensure biogas production and sufficient treatment of the 
wastewater (47). However, a study from University of Alaska showed that using cold-adapted 
microbes improved the efficiency of biogas digesters under cold climate regimes (47). By using 
psychrophilic methanogens from the Alaskan thermokarst lake mud and mud from 
ecosystems in other cold, climate regions, they achieved methane production at temperatures 
down to 1℃ through anaerobic digestion of farm manure. The psychrophiles were also more 
sustained to temperature and pH fluctuations. However, a cost analysis performed in the ame 
project showed that the process was not cost-effective for cold climate regions, but stated that 
large-scale biogas operations may have potential in cold climate regions in relation to using 
waste from fisheries in the biogas production.

2.4 Characterization 
 
Well-designed handling systems have in some cases operated marginally because of 
inaccurate or not sufficient estimates on wastewater loadings (6). Adequate characterisation of 
wastewater and biosolids is therefore crucial for selection, and proper dimensioning and 
operation of treatment processes. 
The parameters that are analysed for this project were the organic content, nutrients, and 
heavy metals listed in the Danish Order, with the exception of mercury. Pathogens and 
xenobiotic compounds were not investigated. The following chapter serves as a description of 
the different analytes that were investigated for the purpose of this project.  
 
2.4.1 Solids 
 
When characterizing wastewater, the most important physical characteristic is the total solids 
(TS) content. Total solids are composed of matter in solution, floating-, settleable- and 
colloidal matter (6). The parameter is used to estimate total mass of sludge, and is further 
fractionated into total volatile solids (TVS) and total fixed solids (TFS). In general, TVS are 
considered the organic portion of the total solids, and is used as an estimate on the 
decomposable matter in wastewater and biosolids (36). TVS are again fractionated into volatile 
settleable solids (VSS) and volatile dissolved solids (VDS). It is an important parameter when 
determining the potential odour development when managing biosolids and sludge. 
Typically, TFS represents the inorganic fraction in biosolids and wastewater, which is also 
fractionated into fixed suspended solids (FSS) and fixed dissolved solids (FDS).  
 
The settleable solids (SS) are identified as the solids that will settle out by gravitational 
forces. This can be used to estimate the volumetric sludge production for a treatment facility.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:24==
 
25
2.4.2 Organic content 
 
The major pollutant in wastewater is organic matter (8). An important parameter for 
determination of organic pollution in an aquatic environment is the oxygen demand. The use 
of oxygen demand as a parameter does not concern a specific compound, but rather the 
combination of substances and conditions (24)
. Two important parameters used are the 
chemical oxygen demand (COD) and biochemical oxygen demand (BOD).  
 
COD 
Metcalf & Eddy (2003) (6) defines COD as “the oxygen equivalent of the organic material in 
wastewater that can be oxidized chemically using dichromate in an acid solution”. The COD 
parameter represents the electron donation potential of wastewater, as the process of 
microbial oxidation for treatment use the organic components as primary electron donors 
during growth (49). COD measurements are used for mass balances in wastewater treatment, 
and by subdividing into suspended and soluble fractions, the parameter is useful in relation to 
the design and operation of a treatment process (8). COD can be further fractionated into 
biodegradable and unbiodegradable COD (6). As these fractions can only be determined by 
comparing analysis of raw and biologically treated wastewater, or settled wastewater, BOD 
serves as an estimation for the biodegradable organics fraction. In general, COD is higher 
than BOD because there are more organic substances that can be oxidized chemically, rather 
than biologically. Other influences are the oxidation of inorganic substances by dichromate 
that may increase the apparent organic content, and toxicity may inhibit microbial degradation 
in the BOD test.  
BOD 
The BOD parameter represents the amount of oxygen required for microbial degradation of 
organic matter in an aerobic environment at a specified temperature (6). The organic content is 
degraded into cell material for microbes, and approximately two thirds is oxidized to other 
products (50).  
In a BOD analysis, two stages of decomposition occur. The first stage, the carbonaceous 
stage, is the oxygen required to decompose organic carbon, to carbon dioxide (51). The second 
stage is the nitrogenous stage where organic nitrogen, ammonia and nitrite are converted to 
nitrate. The second stage represents the combined oxygen demand for carbonaceous and 
nitrogenous decomposition. In general the nitrogenous stage starts after six days. However, in 
wastewater, nitrification may occur in less than five days if nitrifying bacteria is present. 
Inhibitors can be added to the test to prevent nitrification if the intention is to measure 
carbonaceous BOD. A standard BOD test is analysed over a period of five days. Both COD, 
BOD can be fractionated into soluble and particulate BOD. During the five days analysis 
mainly the soluble organic matter is oxidized in such a short time (50).  
 
2.4.3 Macronutrients 
 
Wastewater contains elements that are nutrients for plants, animals and microorganisms. Such 
nutrients are different forms of nitrogen (N) and phosphorous (P). The addition of raw 
wastewater to a recipient may have a fertilizing effect due to the elevated levels of nutrients. 
This can lead eutrophication, hence result in excess algae growth in the water body (52). 
Together with stratification, it can result in saprobiation due to microbial degradation in the 
sediments. Oxygen depletion and production of toxic by-products may have negative effects 
on the ecology of the water column. 
==PAGE:25==
 
26
 
In general, the nutrients readily available for plant uptake in biosolids are close to the same 
for nitrogen and phosphorus. However, plants require one-fifth to one-half as much 
phosphorous as nitrogen (5). The potential of phosphorous contamination of surface waters 
from runoff and leaching, when biosolids are applied to land, is therefore greater.  
 
Nitrogen 
Total nitrogen (TN) is the sum of all forms of dissolved and particulate, organic and inorganic 
nitrogen in wastewater. Inorganic nitrogen is present in wastewater in several different 
oxidation states, either positive or negative. The main forms of importance is ammonia (NH3), 
ammonium (NH4
+), nitrogen gas (N2), nitrite ion (NO3
-) and nitrate ion (NO3
-) (6). The organic 
fraction found in wastewater consists of compounds such as proteins and amino acids (53). 
These are converted to ammonium through microbial degradation in an aquatic environment, 
and the relative amount of ammonia present can be an indication of the ageing that has taken 
place in wastewater (6). In aqueous solutions, ammonium nitrogen exists either as ammonium 
ion or ammonia gas, depending on the pH of the solution. As pH increases above 7, the main 
form is ammonia gas. Nitrite nitrogen is quite unstable, and is easily oxidized to nitrate form. 
Even though nitrite is found in small amounts, it is of concern because it is highly toxic to 
most aquatic species. Under aerobic conditions, ammonium nitrogen is biodegraded into 
nitrites and nitrates, and a dominance of nitrate nitrogen indicates that stabilization has 
occurred with respect to oxygen demand. 
Inorganic nitrogen in biosolids is in the form of nitrite and ammonium, which are bioavailable 
for plants, but are easily lost to denitrification and volatilisation (54). Organic nitrogen is more 
immobile, but must be mineralised into inorganic nitrogen by microbiological processes in 
order to be bioavailable for plant uptake. Organic nitrogen can therefore be considered to be a 
slow release contributing to plant nutrition (5). Microorganisms also decompose inorganic 
nitrogen, converting it to organic compounds. In general, the total nitrogen content in 
biosolids is composed of approximately 70% organic nitrogen and 30% inorganic nitrogen (54). 
Total Kjeldahl nitrogen (TKN) is a measurement for total organic and ammonia nitrogen, and 
can be used to determine the nutrient content of biosolids (6).  
 
Phosphorous 
Total phosphorous (TP) is the sum of all forms of dissolved and particulate phosphorous in 
wastewater, and includes inorganic orthophosphates and polyphosphates, and organic 
phosphates (6). Orthophosphate is the form that is readily available for uptake by algae and 
macrophytes without further breakdown (55). Polyphosphates are more complex, and is 
converted into orthophosphate by the action of hydrolysis (24). This is a slow process, but even 
takes place in the wastewater collection system. The organic form of phosphorous is bound to 
organic compounds in the wastewater, hence it is mainly found as particulate phosphorous.  
In biosolids, phosphorous is primarily found in the form of Al, Fe and Ca phosphates (54), and 
since the process of removal chosen in Sisimiut is by the addition of either Alum or Ferric 
coagulant, the same can be expected for the biosolids produced here. These compounds are 
not available for plant uptake, hence its contribution to plant nutrition is minimal (56). 
 
2.4.4 Trace elements 
 
Some trace elements found in wastewater and biosolids are essential nutrients for plant 
growth, however at higher concentrations, it may have toxic effects to humans, animals and 
plants (36). The trace elements are typically referred to as “heavy metals”, and they have the 
ability to accumulate in the soil and the potential to biomagnify in the food chain (34). There 
==PAGE:26==
 
27
will be different sources for heavy metals in wastewater. In general, they are present in 
potable water, faeces and urine, and detergents. There is also addition to the collection system 
typically through stormwater inflow and corrosion from pipe systems.  
 
Heavy metals will adsorb to organic and inorganic particles in wastewater, hence most trace 
elements will be removed with the sludge (6). Determination of the metal content is important 
to evaluate the suitability of sludge and biosolids for reuse and toxic effects in treatment. 
3 Objectives 
 
Qeqqata Municipality has shown initiative in desiring more sustainable developments, 
although there is further planning and research required in reaching the full potential for the 
community. This study aimed to investigate different alternatives for handling sludge from 
the chemical-mechanical treatment process planned in Sisimiut. 
 
Wastewater flow rates at the two outlets where it is planned to establish wastewater treatment 
facilities, by Ulkebugten and by the Chocolate Factory, were determined through field 
measurements. Assessing the total wastewater discharge and relating the results to records on 
water consumption provided estimation on the yearly quantities of wastewater discharged. 
Wastewater samples from the two outlets were analysed for organic content, nutrient levels 
and solids characteristics.  
 
The quantity and characteristics of sludge produced from chemical enhanced primary 
treatment was determined, using PIX 118 and Superfloc C-2260 for coagulation and 
flocculation, respectively. Sludge was analysed for heavy metal concentrations and nutrient 
levels to assess the suitable processes required to get a quality meeting the Danish standards, 
in relation to regulations and laws on disposal and use. An evaluation on the feasibility of the 
various options was performed as support in providing a foundation to further development of 
designs towards a treatment plant. The evaluation involved the identification of available 
sludge management technologies, and addressing which of these had been tried out in regions 
similar to Sisimiut. By reviewing sewage sludge alternatives proven successful in cold, 
climate regions, appropriate processes are discussed. The results from characterisation of 
sludge and investigations on the quantity produced will aid in the evaluation. Some of the 
focus areas during the evaluation will be on operation in cold, climate conditions, 
maintenance requirements, and the final product after treatment. No overall cost analysis is 
conducted. However, this report will distinguish between high-cost and low-cost processing 
alternatives.

4.3.1 Jar test 
 
A jar test was performed in an effort to determine the optimum dosage of coagulant and 
flocculent for treatment.  
 
For the coagulation test, the coagulant used was PIX 118, which is a solution containing iron 
chloride sulphate. The coagulant was used in a 1:10 solution by diluting 10 mL of PIX 118 
with 90 mL distilled water, and mixing the solution using a magnetic stirrer. 
For the flocculation test, Superfloc C-2260 was used in a 0.2% solution. This was prepared by 
mixing 2 mL Superfloc with 1 L distilled water, using a magnetic stirrer. The solution needed 
maturing for 20-30 minutes with continuous mixing. The mixing was done at low rpm (<30 
rpm) to avoid breaking the polymer chains.  
 
Coagulation test 
Three 1000 mL beakers were weighed before adding the sample. Approximately 800 mL was 
transferred into all three beakers, and the beakers were again weighed to find the accurate 
volume of the sample (assuming density of 1000 kg/m3). Before starting the test, initial pH 
and TSS was analysed to determine the effectiveness of the coagulant. Using a magnetic 
stirrer ensured homogenous sample. With the stirrer on approximately 150 - 200 rpm, 0.8, 1.4 
and 2.0 mL of PIX 118 (1:10) solution was added to the three jars respectively. The rapid 
mixing was left on for 30 seconds, before reducing it to approximately 30 rpm and mixing for 
10 minutes. The stirrer was then stopped, and the samples were left to settle for another 10 
minutes. After sedimentation, 100 mL sample was drawn off about 1 cm below the surface, 
by using a plastic syringe, for TSS determination. Final pH was again measured.  
The method description stated that turbidity and alkalinity was to be measured together with 
TSS and pH, but as this was not possible to conduct at the time, the determination of the 
optimum dose at the day of analysis was done by visually evaluating the turbidity of the three 
samples. Not until the following day, when the result from the TSS analysis was ready, was it 
possible to determine which dose had the actual highest removal efficiency.

Coagulation and flocculation test

which in this case was determined to be 2.0 mL PIX 118 (1:10) solution. 30 seconds after the

The sediments in the cuvette were brought in to suspension by inverting, before 2.0 mL

4.4 Analyses of sludge 
 
The solids were analysed for trace metals and total kjeldahl nitrogen (TKN). A composite 
sample was filtrated through filter with 12-15 µm nominal pore sizes. The samples contained 
high concentrations of solids; hence filtration was done in a matter of days, as filtration was 
by gravitation. The filters were dried overnight at 105°C, and the solids were weighed.  
Raw samples from both Ulkebugten and the Chocolate Factory were filtrated, along with 
sludge from precipitation with and without chemicals from the two outlets.  
 
Trace elements 
Trace metals were analysed by inductively coupled plasma (ICP). The sample was prepared 
by digestion, according to Danish Standard (DS 259) for determination of acid-soluble metals 
in sediments. The trace elements that were investigated was aluminium (Al), cadmium (Cd), 
chromium (Cr), copper (Cu), Iron (Fe), nickel (Ni), phosphorous (P), lead (Pb) and zink (Zn). 
 
According to the standard, 1.00 g of dried, powdered solids are to be weighed into 
autoclavable pyrex glass bottles with a screw cap, and added 20.00 mL of the 1:1 nitric acid 
solution using a pipette. As it was not possible to gain 1.00 g of solid sample, the added 
sample was less than according to the standard.  
The samples were autoclaved for 30 minutes. After reaching room temperature, samples were 
filtrated through a 0.45 𝜇𝑚 filter using a vacuum pump and the bottles were rinsed with 
distilled water and filtrated. The filtrate was transferred to 100.00 mL volumetric flasks and 
distilled water was added until the total volume was 100.00 mL. After mixing, 20 mL was 
transferred into plastic vials and refrigerated until evaluated by ICP. A laboratory technician 
performed the evaluation by ICP.  
 
Total Kjeldahl nitrogen 
The method determined the total organically bound nitrogen in sediments by converting it to 
ammonium through boiling in sulphuric acid, using Kjeltabs as a catalyst. The solution is 
made alkaline by the addition of NaOH, which converts ammonium into ammonia. This is 
then distilled into boric acid, which reacts with ammonia. The consumed amount of boric acid 
is a measurement of the ammonia present in the sample, and this is determined by titration of 
the distillate with sulphuric acid.  
 
The method required 1.00 g of solids for the analysis, but as it was not possible to attain 1.00 
g of sample, the mass of solids used in the analyses was less. The solids were transferred to 
destruction test tubes. Two Kjeltabs were added, along with 10 mL concentrated sulphuric 
acid. Two test tubes were analysed as blank samples. The test tubes were placed on a heat 
block, and heated to 420°C. As the samples turned green, they were left for another 30 
minutes of heating. The samples were left to cool before distillation. Distillation was 
performed using Kjeltec 2200 programme 1. After distillation, the samples were titrated using 
autotitration TIM 854, with sulphuric acid.

5.1.2 Limitations and future work 
 
According to the EPA, the bucket method is recognized as one of the standard methods for 
wastewater flow measurements. The method used in these measurements is an adapted 
version of this, using a plastic bag fitted into the wastewater pipe. Overall the method gives a 
good indication on the flow at the time of concern, although the EPA states that the 
measurements should be done for at least 10 seconds. Because of the volumetric capacity of 
the bag, the measurements were only performed in a matter of seconds (ranging between 1 - 4 
seconds). This makes the method precarious, especially during periods with high flow rates, 
yielding results with high standard deviations. 
 
Another issue is the fit of the bag in the pipe. One can assume that some water escapes the 
device, as the bag is not fitted perfectly to the pipe. This can give lower values for flow than 
the actual result. Also, as the water enters the bag, there is a “backflow” when the water meets 
the bottom of the bag. It was therefore crucial to end the measurement before the backflow 
==PAGE:44==
 
45
escaped. Due to the difficulty of precision for this matter, this may also have given lower 
results than the actual flow.  
 
As the method requires two people, one person to sample the water and one person to time the 
sampling, one can assume that there is a lag between the two people performing the 
measurements. Especially since several individuals performed these measurements, the error 
will not be consistent. With a measuring period of only seconds, this lag may have a larger 
influence on the overall result.  
 
There was a change in the method used by Ulkebugten and the Chocolate Factory. It was not 
possible to physically get down in the manhole by the Chocolate Factory, and the bag was 
therefore altered and attached to shafts, to be able to lower the bag into the pipe. This may 
have influenced the results, although it is assumed to be minor. However, it should be taken 
into consideration when comparing the results from the two locations. 
 
Even though there are several critics to the methodology, the results give a good indication on 
the daily fluctuations in wastewater flow at the two outlets. Continuous measurements should 
be performed in order to get accurate measurements for yearly fluctuations in wastewater 
flow rates. This will also aid in the work of dimensioning the wastewater treatment plant, as it 
will include contributions from infiltration/inflow water. Measurements can either be from a 
closed or an open channel, and the devices needed for such measurements come in different 
price ranges.

For the result of the analyses using coagulation and flocculation, there are larger variations in

It may seem that the flocculent may have had an impact on the particle surface charge for the 
analysis on samples from the Chocolate Factory, as the results show a decrease in percent 
removal from the addition of coagulant to the addition of flocculent. There are several reasons 
why this may be. Mixing regime is a crucial step during the addition of both coagulants and 
flocculants. In general, polymers used for flocculation have poor share stability and at rapid 
mixing the long chains are prone to destruction.  There is also the possibility of the polymer 
chain collapsing on itself if mixing is insufficient and there is inadequate contact between 
particles and the polymer. Another reason may be that the neutralisation by coagulation was 
to such an extent that the addition of the cationic polymer caused an overall charge reversal of 
the particles.  
 
As the highest dosage of both coagulant and flocculent was used in the sedimentation tests, it 
may seem that this is not the optimum dosage for the most effective removal of solids. It can 
be expected that this has influenced the results for the further analyses.

5.4 Limitations and future work 
 
Wastewater characteristics, along with sludge characteristics, will have daily, weekly and 
monthly variations. Thus, the result form this analysis represents the characteristics of the 
wastewater discharged at the day of sampling. Furthermore, samples were not gathered in 
volumes proportional to flow, hence the concentrations of the different parameters may be 
under or over estimated. In retrospect, it would have been more beneficial to gather samples 
in volumes proportional to flow, to get a more representable characterisation of wastewater 
and sludge.  
 
Optimally, all analyses would have been performed at ARTEK, in Sisimiut, using 
unpreserved wastewater. However, due to limited time, available equipment and unforeseen 
circumstances, the option chosen was considered a good alternative.  
 
In hindsight of the jar test, it would have been beneficial to use a larger range of dosages. It is 
clear that the optimum dosage was not used for the analyses on samples from the Chocolate 
Factory; hence, different dosages should be tried out. Using a jar tester would also have given 
better control over the mixing regime, and thereby reducing the possibility of inaccurate 
results due to insufficient mixing.

6 Sludge management in Sisimiut 
 
In the following chapter, there will be a closer description on some treatment processes for 
sludge and a discussion on the feasibility for operating such a process in a region like 
Sisimiut. By reviewing sewage sludge alternatives proven successful in cold, climate regions, 
appropriate processes are discussed. The results from characterisation of sludge and 
investigations on the quantity produced will aid in the evaluation. Some of the focus areas 
during the evaluation will be on operation in cold, climate conditions, maintenance 
requirements, and the final product after treatment. No overall cost analysis is conducted. 
However, this report will distinguish between high-cost and low-cost processing alternatives. 
 
6.1 Dewatering 
 
The amount of dewatering necessary for the sludge will depend on the water content after 
gravity-belt thickening, choice of further treatment processes and the intended final disposal. 
 
It can be assumed that thickening reduces the estimated sludge volumes from Table 5.21 and 
5.22 drastically. By increasing the solid content from 3% to 6%, the total volume is reduced 
by 50% (38). Since thickening with gravity belt are designed to gain 5-7% thickening, it can be 
assumed that this is the case for the planned treatment facility. 
 
In the following section, two alternatives for dewatering are discussed; the belt-filter press, 
and sludge-freezing bed. The belt filter press is considered as a feasible option due to the belt 
unit already planned for the wastewater treatment facility, and with some adjustments the 
dewatering could be even more effective. The sludge-freezing bed is considered as it exploits 
the natural climatic conditions to dewater the sludge.  
 
6.1.1 Filter-belt press 
 
A belt-filter press is a technique used for dewatering sludge by applying mechanical pressure 
to the sludge (6). The basic principle of the process is that a filter belt removes conditioned 
flocs from the wastewater, and water is drained in the initial stage. Another belt is applied on 
top of the flocs on the filter belt, running in the same direction, and pressure is applied on the 
two belts by pressure rollers (38). A simplified sketch of the process is shown in Figure 6.1. 
The sludge is squeezed between the cloth belts in a low-pressure section, followed by a high-
pressure section, and finally removed by scrapers (6). This type of process can generate a 
solids concentration up to 20% to 30% (38).

Some variables may affect the performance of the belt-filter press, such as the characteristics 
of the sludge, and the belt’s porosity, speed and width. A thorough investigation into design 
criteria should be conducted to evaluate the sizing and area required for the process. However, 
the belt-filters available have a width between 0.5 to 3.5 m (6), and there are several 
configurations of the pressure rollers (34), which can reduce the area required.  
 
Some benefits of this process are that there is a relatively low energy requirement, and the 
capital and operating costs are considered to be low (6) in comparison to other mechanical 
dewatering processes. In general, it is considered easy to maintain (59), however, there are 
some limitations for the belt-filter’s lifespan and sharp objects in the sludge might damage the 
belt. Following, as it is not advised to have automated operation, the process requires staffing 
(38). The filter also needs washing, which can require large amounts of water. It is crucial that 
both drainage and wash water is returned for treatment. 
 
Since the sludge is not stabilised, there is also the potential of the development of offensive 
odours, however, this can be controlled with good ventilation (59). Ventilation with indoors 
wastewater treatment is of even more importance in cold, climate regions. With exposed 
surfaces of warmer wastewater, there is the risk of condensation and ice formation on the 
surfaces of walls, doors and other structures (2). Control of the indoor humidity is therefore 
necessary. The exposure to aerosols containing airborne microorganisms is also of concern (6). 
Having an enclosed process will reduce both the ventilation requirements and the risk of 
exposure to pathogens. 
 
Storage of sludge that has not gone through stabilisation may also cause odours (59). Sludge 
from chemical precipitation can undergo degradation similar to primary sludge, which has a 
very offensive odour, but at a slower rate (6). The production of gas can be considerable if the 
storage time is long, hence storage time should be kept at a minimum. It is therefore 
beneficial to stabilise the sludge after dewatering, by e.g. composting, or incinerate the 
sludge. If the dewatering process yields sludge with solids concentration up to 30%, the 
sludge can be incinerated without further treatment or with the use of auxiliary fuels (38).

6.1.2 Sludge-freezing bed 
 
Sludge-freezing beds utilizes natural freeze-thaw cycles, and is a recognised method for 
sludge dewatering (6).  When sludge freezes the ice formation causes the sludge to concentrate, 
as the ice front pushes flocs together (38). When the sludge thaws and the water drains off, the 
sludge can have a solids concentration of 20%, and if it is allowed to dry for a short period, 
solids concentration from 50% to 70% can easily be achieved (60). The process is also 
considered to be very effective on sludge from chemical precipitation. However, using sludge 
that has not been stabilised may cause offensive odours, as mention previously.  
 
This method is suitable for regions that have 3 months per year, or more, with subfreezing 
temperatures, hence it is an applicable method for Sisimiut.  
The basic features of the system are a basin with an under-drain system, which is usually 
covered to protect from precipitation (38) and in some cases, windbreaks to protect against 
snowdrift (61). If the cover is transparent, it also allows for a more rapid thawing during 
warmer months. Previous examples from Arctic areas, as discussed in chapter 2.3.2, have 
used piles to dewater the sludge, rather than constructed beds. However, this makes control 
over the drained melt water more difficult. 
 
Sludge is applied in layers, which freezes, and another layer is applied on top. The thickness 
of these layers should be somewhere from 23 cm to 46 cm in cold climate regions (60), and the 
total depth of all the layers applied through a freezing period can be up to 180 cm (38). U.S. 
EPA (1978) has published guidelines on how to determine the design depth, using 
metrological data and other climatic information. In general, the system is designed for the 
worst-case conditions. For temperate regions, this will be the warmest winter. For very cold 
climates, however, the limiting design depth will be the depth that can be thawed during 
warmer months (60). As the sludge thaws, it is allowed to drain. It can then be left to dry until 
the desired solids content is achieved. The dry solids are then removed mechanically.  
 
Assuming that thickening reduced the sludge volume by 50%, the total sludge from both 
outlets will 3187 m3, using results for 2015, form Table 5.21 and 5.22. Using the maximum 
design depth of 1.8 m, the required area for dewatering would be 1771 m2.  
 
The sludge-freezing bed is considered the treatment process with the lowest capital cost and 
energy consumption (38). However, it may require large areas of land, as the depth in some 
cases is relatively shallow. The critical step in operation is to ensure that the previously 
applied sludge layer is frozen before applying a new layer. This can be done using a small 
pick or an axe; hence, the process requires minimal attention and skills to be successful (6). 
Even so, the removal of dried sludge is demanding work, as the volume of accumulated 
sludge will be large. 
 
In contrast with the belt-filter press, this process yields large quantities of dried, accumulated 
sludge, as opposed to a continuous feed throughout the year. The dry sludge needs further 
handling, which may be a greater procedure due to the larger quantities.  
 
During months where temperatures are above subfreezing temperatures, the system can be 
used as a drying bed or the sludge can be stored. However, both options may result in a high 
production of odours, which can become a problem for residents in the area.

6.2 Stabilisation  
 
As mentioned previously, stabilisation is performed to reduce pathogens, reduce the potential 
putrefaction and development of odours.  
 
Results from table 5.19 and 5.20, show that the COD/BOD ratio is low for all samples 
analysed, hence the organic content is easily degradable. This indicates that stabilisation 
through biological processes might be possible. However, these results do not include BOD 
analyses of sludge from enhanced primary sedimentation. 
 
Composting has already been investigated in Sisimiut, and other parts of the Arctic, hence this 
is evaluated as a suitable process for stabilisation of the sewage sludge. Another alternative 
evaluated is anaerobic digestion. Both techniques have the potential of producing beneficial 
products during or from the process: nutrient rich biosolids or biogas through anaerobic 
digestion. 
 
6.2.1 Composting 
 
Composting is the process where organic material is biodegraded to a stable product (6). The 
system produces pathogen-free compost through thermophilic temperatures, under proper 
operational conditions (34). Up to 30% if all volatile solids are converted into carbon dioxide 
and water during degradation, and simultaneously there is an increase in temperature that 
eventually can lead to the destruction of pathogenic organisms (6). The duration of which the 
compost is kept at a specific temperature, the holding time, is of importance when 
determining the use of the end product (38). Composting can be used for stabilisation or 
sanitation, depending on the temperature in the compost and holding time. The different 
criteria between sanitation and stabilisation are discussed later in this section.  
Composting can be achieved both under aerobic and anaerobic conditions (6). However, 
aerobic composting ensures an accelerated decomposition. This minimises the potential for 
offensive odours, and at the same time results in a higher increase in temperature, which is 
necessary for the destruction of pathogens.  
 
As discussed in chapter 2.3.3, on Danish laws on disposal and use, there are restrictions on 
applying composted biosolids, originating from sewage sludge, to land. These restrictions 
concern heavy metal content, concentration of certain xenobiotic compounds, and the 
presence of pathogens.  
 
Investigations into the presence of pathogens are an important part of ensuring that all 
requirements in the Danish Order are met. Only sanitised biosolids can be used in private 
gardening and to produce eatable crops. To reach sanitation there are specific requirements 
for temperature and holding time. If the compost reaches the minimum temperature of 52℃, 
the required holding time is 10 hours, according to the Danish Order. However, the retention 
time in the reactor is a minimum of 7 days. At a temperature of 55℃, the holding time is 
reduced to 6 hours. It is not recommended that the temperature increase beyond 65℃, as this 
could result in reduced biological activity (6). Hence, composting requires special attention to 
operating conditions, with continuous monitoring to ensure optimal conditions in the 
composting matter. 
If the biosolids are to be used on land for purposes other than producing eatable crops, such as 
landfill cover or as fertilizer for non-eatable plant growth in recreation areas, the Danish 
==PAGE:59==
 
60
Order does not have the same criteria as for sanitation. Both controlled and uncontrolled 
compost can be used for these purposes. 
 
If heavy metal concentrations are exceeding the requirements for land application, one option 
can be to co-compost with municipal organic waste. This may result in an overall reduction in 
the total metal content in the composted material (6). Another benefit with co-composting is 
that dewatering of the sludge may not be required.  
Co-composting in Sisimiut may be a feasible option, as composting of organic waste has 
already been investigated as a supplement to incineration due to lack of capacity at the 
incineration plant (62,46,63). Composting was performed in a container system with special 
attention on simplicity of operation and maintenance (62).  Floors and walls were installed to 
ensure ventilation, and the compost was pre-heated with warm air. In a matter of days, the 
compost reached hygienic temperatures. However, analyses of the compost on the chemical 
characteristics contend that the compost is not suitable for plant growth or as biocover due to 
the quality of the compost (63). However, recommendations suggest that the quality can be 
improved with minor adjustments. None of the previous investigations include analyses on 
the pathogenic content or the presence of xenobiotic compounds, hence, all reports suggests 
that further research is done. 
 
There should be an investigation on co-composting municipal organic waste and sludge 
generated through chemical-mechanical wastewater treatment, and whether this is a feasible 
alternative for Sisimiut. All parameters listed in the Danish Order have to be analysed in order 
to determine if the biosolids are suitable for land use.  
Previously, composting in Sisimiut has been performed in containers. However, as large 
quantities of municipal solid waste can be expected, in addition to sludge from wastewater 
treatment, the effectiveness of having composting outdoors should be investigated. This 
reduces the requirement of additional constructions, thus may also reduce the overall cost.  
 
6.2.2 Anaerobic digestion 
Anaerobic digestion is the process where organic, and some inorganic, material is converted 
into methane-containing gases, in the absence of molecular oxygen (6). During the treatment 
process, there is also destruction of pathogens and a reduction in sludge volume.  
Typically, dewatering is not required prior to anaerobic digestion (6). However, it might be 
required after the process, although, the sludge volume requiring dewatering will have been 
reduced.   
 
As with most biological processes, there are some environmental factors of importance for the 
optimal operation of anaerobic digestion. The presence of substances that may act like 
inhibitors, such as toxic materials, the bioavailability of nutrients and trace metals, and the 
operating temperature are some factors of that will affect the performance (34,6). It is 
considered that the operation of anaerobic digestion requires skilled personnel (6), as the 
process is vulnerable to changes and recovery from disturbances can be slow.  
 
The operating temperatures influence the rate of methane production (34). Most systems 
operate between 30 and 38℃, the mesophilic temperature range (6). However, as discussed in 
chapter 2.3.2, on sludge and biosolids management in cold climate regions, it is possible to 
produce methane at psychrophilic temperatures. Even so, psychrophile bacteria have a lower 
growth rate than mesophiles (8); hence the process will have a higher efficiency at higher 
temperatures. The bacteria responsible for methane production are, in general, sensitive to 
==PAGE:60==
 
61
temperature changes; thus, maintaining a stable operating temperature is critical to ensure 
optimal process performance. 
 
The process might require energy to avoid large fluctuations in temperature. The cost of 
heating will vary in terms of the required heat to increase the temperature of the incoming 
sludge or compensate for heat loss to the surrounding environment (6), although this can be 
regulated with a higher degree of insulation of the digester tank. However, with methane 
production, it is possible to utilize the digester gas for heating. The expected production of 
digester gas will depend on the content of volatile solids of the sludge, and the degree of 
biodegradation in the digester tank. Results reported in Table 5.17 and 5.18 show that the 
VSS/TSS ratio is low for the majority of samples; hence, the process might need 
supplemental matter in order to be successful.  
Co-digestion with municipal organic waste, or even organic waste from the shrimp process 
facility, is an alternative to increase the potential of methane gas (34). Further investigations 
into what operating conditions may yield a profitable methane production should be 
performed.  
 
After digestion of the sludge, the biosolids might be suitable for land application. Depending 
on the water content, dewatering might be required. Further, the same requirements as 
mentioned in the previous chapter, from the Danish Order on the use of waste for agricultural 
purposes, do apply for biosolids processed through anaerobic digestion as well. Hence, 
monitoring of biosolids quality has to me performed in order to dispose the solids in an 
environmental and sanitary manner.  
 
6.3 Land application 
 
If the quality of biosolids meet the requirements set by the Danish Order on the use of waste 
for agricultural purposes, land application is an option for further treatment and final 
disposal. Further treatment of biosolids by the application to land can be achieved through 
reduction of toxic organic substances and pathogens, along with the beneficial exploitation of 
the nutrient rich soil (6).   
 
The Danish Order also includes certain requirements for the application site, and the 
application itself in relation to performance, amount and time of year.  
There are several considerations in relation to the area where biosolids are to be used. To 
reduce the potential of runoff and erosion, the topography and soil characteristics of the site 
intended for application of biosolids needs investigations. The location, in relation to 
residential and commercial areas, and the accessibility of the area is something that also needs 
to be addressed in the overall planning of further use or disposal.  
 
The results on dry mass from sludge, from Table 5.23 and 5.24, show that the yearly 
generation of dry solids can be considerable. As mentioned previously, application to land can 
be done in several ways, such as beneficial use for agricultural purposes, distributed to forest 
areas or other to land areas, or disposed to landfilling (6). However, in Sisimiut there are no 
agriculture or forest areas, thus it can be questioned if the demand for this natural fertilizer is 
immense at this moment.  
The most favourable option, economically, are landfill disposal or cover material at the 
disposal site, which might be an option for Sisimiut.

6.4 Incineration 
 
Another method for handling sludge, used in the Arctic, is incineration. Incineration yields the 
highest reduction in volume for sludge, and toxic compounds and pathogens are destroyed in 
the process. There are several requirements when it comes to incinerating waste, such as air 
pollution control, handling of residues and fly ash, and control of operating temperature (38). 
The process requires skilled personnel for operation and maintenance, and the system has 
high operational and capital costs (6). Even so, as there already is incineration of municipal 
waste in Sisimiut, co-incineration of sludge with municipal waste is considered an option for 
handling. However, there are indications that the incineration plant in Sisimiut is experiencing 
capacity restrictions (13), and the addition of sludge may cause further problems. 
 
Sludge that is incinerated is usually dewatered, but not stabilised (6). The dewatering is a 
crucial step in the preparation for combusting the solids, to reduce energy costs and to gain a 
higher combustion efficiency (38). According to Wang et al. (2007) sludge with a solid content 
of 30% does not need further dewatering, and can be readily incinerated without the use of 
additional fuel material. Both dewatering with belt-filter press and freezing bed can achieve 
this degree of dewatering with proper operation. However, belt-filter press would yield a 
continuous dewatering of the sludge, which means that incineration could happen daily or 
weekly throughout the year without the need of long time storage.  
Sludge dewatered by freeze-thawing may have to be stored after it is removed from the bed 
due to the generation of large quantities in a rather short amount of time, and distributed to the 
incinerator throughout the year. This depends on the capacity of the incinerator. Again, with 
unstabilised sludge, there is the potential of odours. However, it is not viewed as beneficial to 
stabilise the sludge before incineration, as the process of combustion will sterilise the 
residues.

7 Conclusion 
 
There are several alternatives for handling sludge and biosolids that can be appropriate for 
Sisimiut. Dewatering using freezing-beds in combination with stabilisation through 
composting may seem cost favourable as it exploits the natural climatic conditions and has 
less requirements for constructions as both processes can be situated outdoors. In addition, in 
relation to operation and maintenance, it does not demand highly skilled personnel. However, 
it requires larger areas of land, and continuous monitoring is needed if the biosolids are to be 
applied to land. If the sludge is co-composted with municipal organic waste, dewatering of the 
sludge may not be required, thus, the required land area might be reduced.  
 
The filter-belt press is a feasible option for dewatering sludge. It is not readily affected by 
climatic constrains, and has the potential of increasing the solid content to such an extent that 
the sludge can be incinerated without further treatment. As the dewatering is a continuous 
process, the load of sludge at the incineration plant could be distributed throughout the year.   
 
Anaerobic digestion has the potential of producing highly profitable biogas. However, if this 
is possible to achieve under the climatic conditions in a region like Sisimiut needs further 
investigation. As the process needs close attention to detail, due to the vulnerability of the 
system, it is not considered the most feasible option for sludge treatment in Sisimiut at this 
time. 
 
If, or rather, when Qeqqata Municipality decides to implement wastewater treatment in 
Sisimiut, the sludge management should be fully considered and incorporated into overall 
planning in aiding for an overall sustainable development in relation to wastewater treatment 
in Greenland.
