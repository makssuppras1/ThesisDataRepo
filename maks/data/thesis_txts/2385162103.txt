Abstract
This study is an initial attempt to investigate the possibility of permanent magnet wind
turbine generators with superconductors as permanent magnets with FE software.
A
method was developed that can simulate the behavior of the magnetized bulk super-
conductor inside the magnetic ﬁeld of the generator. It comprises of an H-formulation
model that creates and inﬂuences the magnetization of the superconductor and a genera-
tor model that reacts to the superconductor with its surrounding ﬁeld and determines the
overall performance of the system. Two superconducting permanent magnets were tested,
one fully and one partially magnetized. The results show that the superconductors loose
more than 50% of its magnetization in the operating generator. The fully magnetized bulk
superconductor can keep enough magnetic ﬁeld strength to make the generator perform
better than with conventional permanent magnets.

1 Introduction
The share of the electricity generation through wind power is growing fast in many coun-
tries all over the world [1]. The wind turbine manufacturing market is growing rapidly
with several large established companies in Europe and North America like Siemens, Ves-
tas and General Electric and new players growing fast in Asia like Ming Yang [2]. New
markets are emerging as more and more countries are interested in wind power as part
of their transition to renewable energies, not at last to comply with the Paris Climate
Agreement of 2015. Wind power is also starting to become independent of political sup-
port since the ﬁrst park without government subsidies is being planned in Germany [3].
The areas where wind parks can be built are limited, because of population density, wind
speed distribution, and water depth for oﬀshore turbines. The erection of a park is a
major share in the cost of energy, especially for oﬀshore parks [1]. This makes it currently
more economical to use large turbines with high power outputs. The manufacturers in-
crease their power rating with every new model they bring to the market, but there are
technical challenges that come with that. The current technologies cannot be scaled up
indeﬁnitely [4]. The largest turbine today is the Vestas V164 with a maximum power
output of 9.5 MW [5]. Currently there are two design strategies competing in the largest
turbines on the market. There are turbines with synchronous generators and gearboxes
which have the gearbox wear and possible failure as a disadvantage and there are perma-
nent magnet direct drive generators that have a large radius and are dependent on the
price of rare-earths. [6]
Superconductor technology could play a vital part in increasing the turbine power ratings.
With superconductors it is possible to build generators that have a much higher power
density than with the materials used today. The generators would become smaller and
lighter [7]. The eﬃciency can be increased if the consumption of the cryogenic cooling
system will be lower than the copper losses of the conventional windings. Many studies
have been done with simulated designs of synchronous generators with electrical excita-
tion [8, 9, 10]. The superconducting wire manufacturer American Superconductors has

made several wind turbine designs using their products that are currently in the certiﬁ-

will ﬁnancially break even with the performance beneﬁts at around 10 MW [12]. Despite
all this research activity, their is no study investigating the use of permanent magnet
superconductors for the excitation of a synchronous generator, to the knowledge of the
author.
Permanent magnet superconductors are bulks of a superconducting material, that have
a magnetic ﬁeld trapped inside of them after they had been magnetized. They can keep
this magnetic ﬁeld as long as they are kept in the superconducting state. The research
of these bulk superconductors is motivated by the possible use in magnetic bearings and
electrical machines. [13] The remanent ﬂux density of superconducting permanent mag-
nets can be much higher than that of conventional permanent magnets.
The current
record is a trapped ﬁeld of 17.6 T [14]. H-formulation is an established method to simu-
late the magnetic behavior of superconductors with ﬁnite element software that has been
validated by many studies through experiments [15, 16, 17]. While most studies focus on
the magnetization of the bulks, few have investigated how the trapped ﬁelds react to the
inﬂuence of an external magnetic ﬁeld. Those that did, suggest that the trapped ﬁeld will
be weakened, but not eliminated [18].
This study ﬁnds a way to investigate direct drive permanent magnet superconductor wind
turbine generators. It simulates the placement of a superconducting permanent magnet
inside an operating wind turbine generator and compares it to a conventional permanent
magnet electrical machine.
The used magnets are a fully and a partially magnetized
GdBCO bulk at 77 K.
The questions to be answered are, how the superconductor’s
magnetic ﬁeld will be eﬀected, what will happen to the performance of the machine, and
is this a concept worth pursuing further analysis on. This report states the results of
the simulations, but the most work in this study was the development of the method to
measure the inﬂuence of the magnetic ﬁeld inside an electrical machine on the supercon-
ducting permanent magnet.
It was assumed that the strong trapped magnetic ﬁelds in the superconducting magnets
will enhance the performance of a permanent magnet generator signiﬁcantly. They would
initially be weakened during the machines operation, but hold a certain level of remanent
ﬂux density, depending on the external magnetic ﬁeld.
The used generator design is an up scaled version of the Polinder et al. DDPMG [19],
which will be kept constant during the simulations. It will serve as a reference with con-
ventional permanent magnets and it is the machine that the superconducting permanent

2 Theory
2.1 Superconductivity
The resistivity of most conductors is a continuous function of the temperature. A decrease
in temperature means a decrease in resistivity. A superconductor’s resistivity drops sud-
denly to zero, when it is cooled under a certain critical temperature Tc [20]. The eﬀect is
shown in ﬁgure 2.1. This phenomenon was ﬁrst discovered in 1911 by Heike Kamerlingh
Onnes, when he cooled mercury with liquid helium under its critical temperature of 4 K.
Multiple theories have been developed since then to explain superconducting behavior
such as BCS [21] and London [22]. The superconducting state is not only constricted by a
Figure 2.1: The sudden drop in resistivity when cooling a superconductor under its critical
temperature. Picture: [23]
maximum temperature. There is also a critical current density Jc and a critical magnetic
ﬁeld Bc. The material will leave the superconducting state if any of these three parame-
ters are exceeded internally. The quantities of the critical parameters are also dependent
on each other. The higher one value goes, the lower the other two values become. This
creates a three dimensional space of superconductivity, which is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.2.

Superconductors exclude external magnetic ﬁelds that they are placed in by creating
an opposite ﬁeld so that the internal ﬁeld strength is zero. The ﬁeld is created by a
current that runs under the superconductor’s surface in a circular fashion, perpendicular
and counter-clockwise to the external ﬁeld. A certain current is needed to create the
counter ﬁeld. Since the current density is limited by Jc, the current is distributed over
a penetration depth λ under the surface of the superconductor. The external magnetic
ﬁeld penetrates the superconductor as deep as λ, while it gets weekend on the way by the
counter ﬁeld. [25]
Superconducting materials are classiﬁed into type I and type II superconductors. Type
I superconductors have to exclude magnetic ﬁeld. Type II superconductors can allow
narrow tunnels through the material, where the outside ﬁeld bundles like in a vortex. A
circular current in the superconductor goes around the tunnel to eliminate the ﬁeld around

2.2 GdBCO
Gadolinium-barium-copper-oxide (GdBa2Cu3O7−x) or GdBCO is a type II superconduct-
ing material from the (RE)BCO family, where RE stands for rare earth. In comparison
to the other superconductors of that family, it is able to carry a lot of current in strong
magnetic ﬁelds and high temperature. [20] The material can be used as a good permanent
magnet in the form of a bulk. The highest ﬁeld that has been achieved in an experiment
is 17.6 T at 26 K. [14]
To get high trapped ﬁelds in the bulk, it is desirable to have a homogeneous critical cur-
rent density Jc. This is achieved by producing the magnets as large single grains with top
seeded melt growth. A seed crystal is placed in the middle of the surface of the material
and then gets heated up to a processing temperature under the melting temperature. [26]

2.3 Cryocooling
The cooling of superconductors to cryogenic temperatures is done by immersing it in a
cryogenic liquid that is kept cool by a cryocooler. The apparatus is insulated with vacuum.
If temperatures close to 0 K are desired, the cooling can be done in multiple stages. The
ﬁrst stage might cool down to 60 K. The second stage would be inside the ﬁrst stage and
cool down to temperatures as low as 2 K with a diﬀerent liquid as the ﬁrst stage. The
liquids boiling point is the temperature that the superconductor will usually be cooled
down to. An overview of commonly used cryogenic ﬂuids and their boiling temperatures
is given in table 2.2. [20]

2.4 H-Formulation
The magnetic ﬁeld in superconductors is complicated to calculate, because they have a
highly nonlinear current-voltage characteristic. Simple geometries can be solved analyti-
cally, more complex ones have to be solved numerically for example with FEM software.
H-formulation, as described by Hong et al. [24], is a method of expressing the electric
ﬁeld strength E and the current density J as variables in a partial diﬀerential equation
for the magnetic ﬁeld strength H.

sectional area as is needed to counter that ﬁeld change.

Q = |E| · |J|. The rise in temperature during a ﬁeld change will lower Jc and ⃗J becomes

2.5 Finite Element Machine Design in Comsol
The software for numerical FEM simulations used in this study is Comsol Multiphysics
5.2a. It allows to build models of free geometries. Rectangles and circles can be positioned,
combined and subtracted from each other to form domains that are surrounded by their
boundaries. Values can be measured in, or be assigned to one or multiple domains or
==PAGE:20==
2 Theory
20
boundaries. Values or functions can be implemented in the deﬁnitions. A commonly used
deﬁnitions feature in this study is interpolation in which data can be found as a function
of another parameter in an imported table via linear interpolation. The domains can be
programmed to move, as is necessary for example for the rotor of a generator.
Physics are sets of equations that determine the relations and physical rules between
parameters for the whole geometry or only parts of it. Many diﬀerent preset physics can
be applied to the model, like a physics for rotating electrical machines. It is also possible
to create one’s own set of physical rules in free physics.
The size and distribution of the ﬁnite elements can be controlled by a mesh that has to
cover the whole model and can be adjusted in certain areas as is needed. A ﬁner mesh
means more accuracy of the results, but also more degrees of freedom for the simulation
and longer computing time of the solution.
It is possible to built the models in 2 dimensions in the x-y plane. The model will in
this case either get a certain length in the z-axis deﬁned or will be seen as inﬁnitely long,
depending on the chosen physics.
There are a lot of possibilities to create error when programming the model. It is therefore
advisable to imitate a known model as a reference, before modifying it in the way that
suits the experiment.
2.6 Magnetic ﬁeld
The magnetic ﬁeld is a force ﬁeld like gravity or the electric ﬁeld. What makes it diﬀerent
from these two ﬁelds is that magnetic ﬂux never has a source like a mass in gravity, or
a charge the electric ﬁeld. The ﬂux lines are always continuing in circles. That means
that the amount of ﬂux that goes into a given volume has to come out of it as well.
Magnetism is measured in two diﬀerent ﬁelds, the magnetic ﬁeld strength H [A/m] which
is independent of its medium’s material and the magnetic ﬂux density B [T] which is
inﬂuenced by the material.
The relation between the two is deﬁned as B = µ0µrH,
where µ0 is the magnetic permeability of vacuum and µr is the relative permeability of
the material, or the ability of a material to carry magnetic ﬂux. Materials that have a
relative permeability µr >> 1 are called ferromagnetic materials. They will attract ﬂux
when they are placed inside a magnetic ﬁeld. [28]
If a material keeps ﬂux going, after it has been taken out of a magnetic ﬁeld, it has
remanent ﬂux density. These materials are called permanent magnets (PM). When a

2.7.2 The Superconductor during PFM
The pulsed magnetic ﬁeld induces a current inside of the superconductor as if it was a coil.
After Lenz’ law, the current’s direction is such that is opposes the ﬁeld change that caused
it. When the pulse is over the peak and the external ﬁeld is becoming weaker again, the
superconductor wants to counter the new ﬁeld change, ergo maintain the ﬁeld that had
penetrated it. The circular current inside reverses, starting from the edges and progress-
ing towards the core. When the pulse is over and the external ﬁeld gone, the current in
the superconductor keeps ﬂowing due to lack of resistance, leaving the superconductor
magnetized. The higher the pulse, the more current ﬂows and the stronger is the mag-
netic ﬂux in the bulk. The maximum magnetization is reached, when the whole cross
section of the superconductor carries the critical current density Jc. Diﬀerent shapes and
magnitudes of the current pulse can inﬂuence the maximum trapped ﬁeld Btrap, because
the superconductor can heat up diﬀerently and thereby lower Jc. The superconductor
will always react to a ﬁeld change with the maximum possible current density, which is
Jc in the smallest possible area. When the ﬂowing current is enough to counter the ﬁeld
change, the current density and the area it ﬂows in will remain constant.
Figure 2.6 shows the superconductor in the pulsed external ﬁeld for diﬀerent points in
time with a peak time of τ = 10 ms. The whole area of the magnet carries Jc at the peak
of the pulse and the ﬁeld is so strong that it does not get inﬂuenced by this. One millisec-
ond later, the external ﬁeld has already gotten weaker and the current has reversed on
the outer edges of the superconductor to maintain the inner ﬁeld. At 25 ms the current is
fully reversed and the remaining external ﬁeld is inﬂuenced by the superconductor’s ﬁeld.
After 120 ms the external ﬁeld has retreated completely and the ﬂux comes alone from
the current that is ﬂowing inside the bulk.
Other magnetization methods like zero ﬁeld cooling, where a superconducting material
is cooled down to superconducting state while being in a constant magnetic ﬁeld, can
achieve higher trapped ﬁelds than PFM [16]. However PFM is considered the most prac-
tical method, because it is faster, it only requires a simple coil and it allows for a lighter
and more compact design [29].

2.7.3 Degree of magnetization
A superconductor can be magnetized to diﬀerent strength depending on the applied pulsed
ﬁeld, the rise time and the superconductor’s temperature. Figure 2.7 shows a full magne-
tization on the left and a partial magnetization on the right side with the current density

strong enough pulsed ﬁeld will ﬁll the entire cross section of the bulk superconductor with
the maximum current density. This will lead to the strongest possible trapped ﬁeld in a
simulation with constant temperature. The distribution of magnetic ﬂux density in the
bulk will be rather uneven. It will have a strong peak in the center and weak ﬁelds on
the sides. When the pulsed ﬁeld is lower, the bulk does not get ﬁlled completely with
current during the peak. During relaxation, the current on the outer edges will reverse
to maintain the ﬁeld that had penetrated that area. The current further in will keep the
same direction to keep the ﬁeld in the center at zero. It makes no diﬀerence that the ﬁeld
that tries to enter the center of the bulk is an internal ﬁeld. The magnetic ﬁeld on the
magnet’s surface will be strong on the sides and drop to zero in the center of the bulk.

6 Discussion
This study investigates the possibility of using bulk superconductors as permanent mag-
nets inside permanent magnet direct drive wind turbine generators. The results show that
the superconductors will loose a substantial amount of their magnetization during the ma-
chine’s operation, but that it will drop quickly and not decay continuously. The generator
can still produce much more power output than the reference machine that uses conven-
tional permanent magnets, if the superconductor was magnetized with a strong enough
ﬁeld in the beginning.
The weakening process is converging as steps 2 and 3 get repeated in a loop. The box’
ﬁeld that gets measured in each iteration of the loop, is to a large extent determined by
the magnetization of the superconductor. The superconductor is then being inﬂuenced in
the H-formulation model by that measured box’ ﬁeld, the very ﬁeld it has a large inﬂuence
on. The codependency of the magnetization and the box’ ﬁeld is not represented in either
of the two models, as the transferred information is always static. The developed method
of repeating the box’ ﬁeld measurement in step 2 and the penetration of the supercon-
ductor with that ﬁeld in step 3, is a way to successfully create the missing codependency.
In a model generator model with integrated H-formulation for the magnet domains, this
codependency would be given automatically and there would be no need for the feedback
loop. The whole weakening process would happen smoothly in the ﬁrst few microseconds
of the simulation.
The lower torque of the partially magnetized superconductor can only partly be explained
with the lower magnetization M. Both magnets loose similar shares of their initial mag-
netization M0, but the fully magnetized superconductor manages to keep a lot more of
the initially measured torque T0 after the weakening process. In the little section were
the curves in ﬁgure 5.2 overlap, the torque of the fully magnetized superconductor is sig-
niﬁcantly higher at the same M. If the curves were to be continued, they would run in
parallel with a separating gap between them. This is an indicator that the magnetic ﬁeld
distribution of the fully magnetized superconductor is superior for the generator perfor-
mance.
==PAGE:60==
6 Discussion
60
When the superconductor reacts to a ﬁeld change, the current adjustments start on the
sides and then continue to come into the center as the ﬁeld change increases. The super-
conductor is acting as a unit to keep ﬁeld changes out of its domain as good as possible.
A foreign ﬁeld cannot be stopped, it can only be diverted. The superconductor tries to
carry the ﬂux lines of the foreign ﬁeld around the outsides by having the appropriate
currents ﬂow on the edges.
The ﬂux distribution through the superconductors is not uniform like in the conventional
permanent magnets. The resulting areas of saturation in the rotor core were expected.
The ﬂux that exits the magnets towards the rotor core splits roughly in the middle for the
conventional magnets and the fully magnetized superconductor. However, not expected
was that the ﬂux from the partially magnetized superconductor goes almost entirely to
one side. This can be explained with the distribution of the ﬁeld inside the bulk. Field
lines in a medium of equal permeability try to become parallel and equally spaced to one
another. The complete void of magnetic ﬁeld in the center makes the ﬂux lines from both
sides of the center want to come together as they enter the core. Shortly after, they have
to turn away again to go towards the next magnet on their respective sides. The double
turn goes against the ﬁeld lines desire to stay parallel and they are more in favour of
continuing together to one side than to split again. A tiny imbalance, like an unsymmet-
rical grid or the position of the teeth can start a cascading eﬀect in which more and more
ﬂux gets pulled to one side. It cannot be observed before the weakening process (bottom
graph in ﬁgure 5.8), because it is dependent on the interaction between the ﬁeld in the
superconductor domain and the ﬁeld in the generator. This codependent interaction ﬁrst
takes full eﬀect during the weakening process. The phenomenon is not happening for the
fully magnetized superconductor, because the ﬂux is more dense in the center of that
superconductor than on the sides. The ﬂux lines try to even out the diﬀerent spacing as
they enter the core and by that, already get directed apart from one another. The split
in the middle is the logical consequence.
The simulation of the superconductor in the H-formulation model is performed at a con-
stant temperature. The superconductor has heat losses of Q = E ·J, especially during the
pulsed ﬁeld magnetization, which are not taken into account in this study. The critical
current density Jc is a function of the magnetic ﬂux density B and the temperature T.
With a constant temperature, Jc becomes dependent on B only. The superconductor does
not heat itself up and can as a result carry more current. After the pulse, it will have a
stronger trapped ﬁeld Btrap. Since the heating in the superconductor diﬀers locally, the
distribution of current density would also be diﬀerent if thermal modelling was included

irrelevant when T is constant. This means that the results of the simulations in this study
are only qualitative indicators on the behavior of superconductors and generators. While
the same Btrap can be achieved in an experiment with a superconductor that has a lower
initial temperature than 77 K, the distribution of the ﬂux through the superconductor
and the reaction to the ﬁeld inside the generator would be diﬀerent.
Including the iron core in the H-formulation model is out of the scope of this study. In-
stead, the superconductor is only surrounded by air in that model. This means that the
core has to be at a distance to the magnet when measuring the box’ ﬁeld in the generator
model, so that the box is only measuring in air. The high permeability of the iron would
otherwise change the measured magnetic ﬁeld strength. This falsiﬁes the outcome of the
weakening process, because the ﬂux has to go through a small gap of air, which imposes
a higher magnetic resistance than the iron.
No losses are considered in the power calculation as it is just a means of comparison
between the diﬀerent machines. The superconductors need to be kept cool with cryocool-
ing equipment. There will be heat losses from inside the superconductors and insulation
losses of the cooling installation. The conventional machine that serves as a reference
does not have these cooling losses. For an equivalent comparison they would need to be
subtracted from the calculated power output of the two machines with superconductors as
permanent magnets. The estimation of these losses are outside of the scope of this study
and cannot play into the comparison of the three machines. However, this study does not
aim to give a precise estimate on the performance, it rather explores the possibilities of
FEM simulation.
Many studies before have simulated the magnetization of bulk superconductors of dif-
ferent materials, dimensions and temperatures [15, 16, 17]. This is the ﬁrst study that
implements the magnetized superconductors into a generator and investigates the inter-
action of the magnet’s and the generator’s magnetic ﬁelds. The qualitative results show
what phenomena have to be taken into consideration and what challenges are to be ex-
pected in this ﬁeld. A functioning method has been developed that can be applied for
diﬀerent generators or other applications for bulk superconductors. The method can eas-
ily be extended to address the limitations that are mentioned in the previous paragraphs.
The signiﬁcance of the ﬁeld distribution inside the superconductor is also shown by this
study.

7 Conclusion
The purpose of this study was to develop a method to investigate permanent magnet
superconductor generators. It opens up a new ﬁeld of research to challenge the existing
studies and designs of electrically exited superconducting wind turbine generators. The
wind turbine industry is likely to build the ﬁrst superconducting wind turbine with this
technology within the next ﬁve years. Permanent magnet generators are used in some
of the biggest wind turbines on the market today. It is inevitable that superconductor
permanent magnets will be tried out as the use of superconductors in wind turbines
matures, which is why this ﬁeld needs further investigation.
The most signiﬁcant advancement that can be made to this study is to add thermal
modelling to the simulation. It will make the critical current density not only a function
of the ﬂux density but also of the temperature. This would eﬀect the current strength
and distribution in the bulk, giving an accurate magnetization that could be validated
with experiments. Another important improvement would be to add the rotor core to the
H-formulation model. The box’ ﬁeld could then be measured without the gap between the
magnets and the core. These two measures would give accurate results for the weakening
process. To make a proper comparison to the conventional permanent magnet technology,
an optimized generator design with an accurate estimation of the cooling losses needs to
be made.
This study has shown that it is not only the value of the magnetization M, that counts
for good generator performance, but also the distribution of the magnetic ﬁeld in the
superconductor. The ﬁeld distribution can be inﬂuenced by a more restricted shape of
the magnetizing ﬁeld. The optimal ﬁeld distribution and how to achieve it, needs to be
investigated.
