ABSTRACT 
This study presents the findings of several analyses made at Borgerskolen in Høje Taastrup 
municipality. Together with DTU Byg and DTU Compute a measuring system has been 
established, measuring temperatures, CO2 concentrations, relative humidity and noise levels in 
all the class rooms. The objective was to investigate the indoor environment and develop a 
FDD tool to evaluate the building management system. A method to calculate the supply 
ventilation rate was developed in order to investigate the reasons for the poor air quality in the 
rooms. The calculated ventilation rates was used to estimate the occupancy in order to be able 
to better control the systems. 
The temperature was demonstrated to be very low compared to typical design temperatures and 
the CO2 concentrations were high compared to recommended values. The calculated supply air 
flow rates are dependent on the CO2 concentrations in the rooms, however the supplied air flow 
rates were not high enough in order to keep the CO2 concentrations at a sufficient level. Based 
on the developed FDD tool it was concluded that the building management system at 
Borgerskolen had several faults and room for improvement to both the control systems and the 
indoor environment. Overall the implementation of a FDD tool will be beneficial in order to 
verify the control of the buildings systems, reduce energy use while maintaining occupant 
satisfaction.

INTRODUCTION 
Today, management systems are used to automatically operate many of the major systems in 
buildings. Modern building management systems also allow the operation of multiple systems 
to be coordinated according to advanced building-level strategies. This automation of all the 
controls has lead to a situation where the performance of the building is highly dependent on 
the on the control system. An important part of commissioning should therefore be to ensure 
the systems are operating properly. 
Commissioning of HVAC systems is a proven process that reduces energy consumption and 
improves occupant comfort in buildings. Despite having many benefits, commissioning is often 
a one-time process done before handing over the building to the building owner over in order 
to secure a non-faulty operation. The evolving capabilities of building management systems 
can help making commissioning an integrated part of the operation and maintenance of the 
building systems by offering to automate some parts of the commissioning process. Developing 
a detailed system that detects and diagnose faults in the operation of the building have 
capabilities to verify and optimize the performance of building HVAC systems using the 
capabilities of BMS. 
This has great aspects with regards to indoor climate as well as more energy efficient systems 
management.

BUILDING MANAGEMENT SYSTEM 
The Building management system (BMS) is traditionally the only system that allows the 
building owner to monitor and log the buildings performance. This chapter will present how a 
traditional BMS is designed. 
The BMS consists of a system installed in the building. It controls and monitors building 
services responsible for heating, cooling, ventilation, air conditioning among many more. 
Because of this the BMS has a potential to reduce energy consumption and facilitate building 
operation, monitoring and maintenance while improving occupant’s satisfaction. The BMS can 
achieve this potential by providing a wide range of sensors (e.g. sensing CO2 concentration, 
temperature, humidity, zone airflow, occupancy level), which provide information that enables 
decision-making on how the building equipment should be controlled, aiming at reducing 
expenses and energy consumption while maintaining occupant comfort. 
The BMS consists of a hardware side with the following components: sensors, actuators, 
wiring, switches and control devices and a software side for the control algorithms, user 
interface and other miscellaneous functionality. The boundary for the hardware side is therefore 
the point of interface to the energy system and the controlled environment  (ECBCS, 2004). 
The overall generalized architecture of the BMS can be organized into three-levels as shown 
in Figure 2.1. The management level, where the building management is visualized, and data 
is available through logs and trends. The automation level, where the measurements are 
processed, and control loops are executed. The field level, where the sensors are located and 
the monitoring takes place. (Domingues, Carreira, Vieira, & Kastner, 2016) (Bhatt & Verma, 
2015)

2.1 
Management level 
The management level is the top level of the BMS. Here information about the entire system 
is available. An interface is presented to the operator to manual make changes to the system. 
Here a vertical access to the automation level values is provided, including the option to change 
parameters such as schedules and setpoints for the operator to control the buildings systems. 
Alerts are generated to inform the operator of exceptional situations like technical faults or 
critical conditions. Depending on the installed sensors, it is possible to monitor the indoor 
environment and energy use on different levels. The monitored data can be archived and 
afterwards extracted via a server connection.

systems in the building. The automation uses data prepared by the field level, and by

variable. The automation level controls e.g. fan speed in the air handling unit, valve positions 
and supply temperatures in the heating and cooling system. Time schedules, set points and 
heating as well as cooling curves are typically placed in the automation level allowing the units 
to start and stop at certain times and control the temperatures. It is possible to change these via

2.4 
Challenges regarding traditional BMS 
A poorly operated BMS has a major impact on the indoor environment and energy use. The 
purpose of the BMS is to ensure that the plants deliver the services as projected, with the least 
possible energy consumption and service and maintenance costs. The technical systems must 
be maintained to work properly, this also applies to the BMS that controls and regulates the 
plants. However, it is often that these facilities are not operated and maintained accordingly, 
which often result in poor comfort for the building’s users and a higher energy consumption 
(Videncenter for energibesparelser i bygninger, 2016). 
Rarely continuous control of the systems is performed – there is no ongoing commissioning, 
and the needed skills are often lacking among the operators. Often the building is used in a 
different way than intended in the design phase, and this is rarely corrected in connection with 
the operation (Strategy Living, 2012). 
(Taneja, 2014) states that “All too often a building’s energy performance does not meet design 
expectations, particularly a new building’s energy savings projection that overstates 
achievable performance. Across the high-performing building industry, these unrealistic 
energy performance goals have come from, among other things, inadequate modeling and 
benchmarking practices, unreliable monitoring and equipment controls systems, significant 
changes in space usage and processes during occupancy and tenant improvements, failure to 
include operations staff in goal setting or accurately communicating the design intent to the 
staff, and lack of adequate budgets for commissioning, evaluation and ongoing benchmarking 
Taneja et al 2008). An important link toward delivering sustainability and energy savings 
targets that has been too often missing is the role of building operations staff. In order to get 
newer green buildings operating at the efficiency level near where predicted in the design 
process, smart building toolkit and active engagement of Building Operators and Facility 
Management staff are critical” These are some of the same issues mentioned in (Videncenter 
for energibesparelser i bygninger, 2016) and (Strategy Living, 2012).

The problems regarding a high energy use and poor indoor environment quality are among 
other things caused by an inadequate control of the building systems and an inadequate use of 
the BMS.

COMMISSIONING 
Commissioning is a quality process that focuses upon verifying and documenting whether the 
performance of a building’s systems is in accordance with the design intent and the owner’s 
operational needs. Due to the complexity of building designs and the complexity of building 
systems constructed today, commissioning is necessary, but not automatically included as part 
of the typical design and contracting process (National Laboratory Sustainable Design Guide, 
2002).  
 Commissioning is often viewed as a task performed after the building is constructed and before 
it is handed over to the building owner to check operational performance. However, it is 
favored to have a broader view which starts in the pre-design phase goes through the 
construction process and continues during operation. This broader view aims at bridging the 
gaps between 4 different visions. The expectations of the building owner, the project of the 
designer, the assembled system of the contractor and the running system of the operator. 
Bridging these gaps will consists for example in: 
• 
Clarifying the expectation of the building owner to obtain the owner’s project 
requirements so that the owner and designer understand one another and are in 
agreement. 
• 
Applying functional performance testing procedures which will enable the contractor 
the building owner and the designer to verify that the system is clearly operating as 
expected. 
• 
Producing system manuals which will enable the operator to take best profit of the 
ideas of the designers and of the system realized by the contractor to fulfill owner 
requirements. 
• 
Producing at regular interval report which will enable the operator and the building 
owner to check that the operation continues to fulfill these requirements. (ECBCS, 
2004). 
These points can help address the issues mentioned in (Taneja, 2014). 
==PAGE:18==
8 
 
In practice one can differentiate 4 types of commissioning which are represented in Table 3.1: 
Initial Commissioning is a systematic process applied to production of a new building and/or 
an installation of a new system.  
Retro-Commissioning is the first-time commissioning is applied to an existing facility that 
was not previously commissioned.  
Re-Commissioning is a commissioning process implemented after initial commissioning or 
retro-commissioning in order to verify, improve and document the performance of the building 
systems. 
On-Going Commissioning is a continuation of the Commissioning Process into the occupancy 
and operations phase for the purpose of maintaining, improving and optimizing the 
performance of the building systems.

3.1 
Commissioning using building management system 
The capabilities of building management systems offer opportunities to automate some parts 
of the commissioning process and can generate benefits over the entire lifetime of the building. 
This section summarizes different approaches for carrying out commissioning activities using 
the building management system. 
“The evolving capabilities of Building Energy Management Systems (BEMS) can help to 
circumvent the barriers to commissioning by offering opportunities to automate some parts of 
the commissioning process. Automation or semiautomation of certain aspects of the 
commissioning process has the potential to reduce costs for commissioning, thereby leading to 
more widespread application of the process. Furthermore, automating this essentially manual 
process could allow its application on a regular basis, generating benefits over the entire life 
==PAGE:20==
10 
 
of a building. Developing a detailed systematic automated approach will improve the quality 
assurance process and could even integrate energy audit capabilities that improve the overall 
performance of buildings.” (Choinière & Corsi, 2003). 
3.1.1 Types of commissioning tests 
In annex 40 (ECBCS, 2004) two approaches to commissioning using the management system 
has been considered, passive and active test. Passive test involves using the management 
system to monitor and record sensor and actuator signals from energy systems operating under 
normal conditions. These tests are non-invasive in that way they do not introduce any artificial 
disturbances in the system. In passive testing it is important to select points to monitor and 
apply appropriate data analysis methods. Active testing involves making artificial changes to 
the system under controlled circumstances in order to investigate the behavior of the system in 
a faulty operating mode. 
3.1.2 Fault detection and diagnosis to commission the management system and 
energy system 
Fault detection and diagnosis (FDD) is an area of investigation concerned with automating the 
process of detecting faults with physical systems and diagnose their causes. The primary 
objective of a FFD system is to early detect occurring faults and diagnose their causes, making 
it possible to correct the faults before additional loss of service occurs. This is accomplished 
by continuously monitoring the operation of the system, detect uncharacteristic conditions, 
then evaluate the significance of the detected faults and decide how to respond (Katipamula & 
Brambley, 2005). Automated FDD shows promise in three basic areas of building engineering: 
(1) commissioning (2) operation and (3) maintenance. Commissioning involves in part 
ensuring that systems are installed correctly and operate properly. Problems found during 
commissioning, e.g. installation errors, incorrectly sized equipment and improperly 
implemented controls (e.g., incorrect schedules, setpoints and algorithms) are typically done 
manually. Automation of an FDD tool could automate much of the functional testing and 
interpretation of the results and have the ability to continuously or periodically repeat the test 
throughout the lifetime of the facility.  
==PAGE:21==
11 
 
During building operation, FDD tools could detect and diagnose performance faults, many of 
which go undetected for a long time in many commercial buildings. Often these faults are only 
discovered when it leads to an unsatisfactory indoor environment and the users of the building 
complain about these issues. Some building performance problems are compensated with 
automatic compensation by controllers, so occupants experience no discomfort (e.g. if the heat 
exchanger in the ventilation unit stops working, the heating coil will compensate to maintain 
the desired supply air temperature), but the penalty is often increased energy consumption. 
Fundamental FDD methods 
The methods for detecting faults and subsequently diagnosing their causes are the basic 
building blocks of FDD systems. Several different methods are used to detect and diagnose 
faults. The major difference between these approaches is the knowledge behind the formulating 
of the diagnostics. Diagnostics can be based on a priori knowledge (e.g. models based entirely 
on first principle) or driven completely empirically (e.g. by black-box models). Both methods 
use models and both use data, but the approach to formulating the diagnostics different.  
First-principle model-based approaches use a priori knowledge to specify a model that serves 
as the basis for identifying and evaluating differences between the actual operating states 
determined by measurements and the expected operating state determined by the model. Purely 
empirically approaches does not use a priori knowledge of the process, but instead derive 
behavioral models only form the measured data from the process itself. (Katipamula & 
Brambley, 2005) 
Example methods 
Model-based methods can be quantitative or qualitative. Quantitative models are sets of 
quantitative mathematical relationships based on the underlying physics. Qualitative models 
are models consisting of qualitative relationships based on knowledge of the underlying 
physics. The boundary between these two model-based methods can to some extent become 
blurred. In (Katipamula & Brambley, 2005) a scheme for categorizing the different FDD 
==PAGE:22==
12 
 
methods is used and shows the difference between model based methods and process history 
based methods. This scheme is shown in Figure 3.1. 
 
Figure 3.1: Classification scheme for FDD methods. 
Quantitative model-based methods include those based on detailed physical models as well as 
those based on simplified models of the physical processes. Qualitative model-based approach 
includes rule-based systems and models based on qualitative physics. Rule-based systems are 
further distinguished between those based on expert rules for which there may be no underlying 
first principles from physics, rules derived from first principle and simple limit checks. 
In contrast to the quantitative model-based and qualitative model-based approaches where a 
priori knowledge of the process is assumed, the last method is based solely on process history, 
to use this method a large amount of historical data of the process needs to be available. These 
models include black-box models, for which the models are derived purely from the data 
available on the process and gray-box models, that use first principles or engineering 
knowledge to specify the outline of the model, but for which some parameters are determined 
from process data.

Quantitative Model-Based Methods 
Model-based methods rely on analytical redundancy by using explicit mathematical models of 
the monitored process, plant or system, to detect and diagnose faults. In contrast to physical 
redundancy (wherein measurements from multiple sensors are compared to each other) with 
analytical redundancy, sensor measurements are compared to values computed analytically 
with other measured variables serving as model inputs. 
Quantitative model-based approaches can be further subdivided into detailed and simplified 
physical models. Physical models are based on a priori fundamental understanding of the 
physical principles governing the behavior of the system. In a physical model based FDD 
approach, the behavior of the system and values of outputs are predicted or estimated for a 
given set of measured inputs (e.g., temperature, pressure, flow rate) and model parameters (e.g., 
heat transfer coefficient) and compared to the measured output from the system. 
The detailed physical models are based on detailed physical relationships and characteristics 
of all components in a system. Detailed models can simulate both normal and faulty operational 
state of the system and can therefore be used both in passive and active commissioning tests. 
One of the weaknesses of the detailed physical model is that the level to which the detailed 
physical characteristics must be specified for the system is not always easy to obtain and apply. 
In contrast to the detailed physical model, simplified physical modeling generally employs a 
lumped parameter approach, which is computationally simpler. 
Strengths of FDD based on quantitative models include: 
• 
Models are based on physical or engineering principles. 
• 
They provide the most accurate estimators of output when they are well formulated. 
• 
Detailed models based on first principles can model both normal and “faulty” 
operation; therefore, “faulty” operation can be easily distinguished from normal 
operation.  
 
==PAGE:24==
14 
 
Weaknesses of FDD based on quantitative models include: 
• 
They can be complex and computationally intensive. 
• 
The effort required to develop a model is significant. 
• 
These models generally require many inputs to describe the system, some for which 
values may not be available. 
Qualitative Model-Based Methods 
Fault detection and diagnosis based on qualitative modeling represents another broad category 
that uses a priori knowledge of the system. Unlike quantitative modeling-based methods in 
which knowledge about the system is expressed in terms of mathematical relations, qualitative 
models use qualitative relations or knowledge bases to draw conclusions regarding the state of 
the system and its components (e.g. whether operations are faulty or normal).  Some qualitative 
models are made by deriving knowledge statements from process history data such as for expert 
systems where obtained knowledge within a process is used to derive rules governing normal 
and faulty operation. Qualitative model-based methods can be further subdivided into rule-
based and qualitative physics-based models, see Figure 3.1. 
Rule-Based Systems 
The rule-based modeling technique use a priori knowledge to derive a set of if-then-else rules 
(Katipamula & Brambley, 2005). Rule-based systems can be based solely on expert knowledge, 
which is inferred from experience or can be based on first principle. 
Strengths of FDD based on qualitative models are: 
• 
They are well suited for environments where large amount of data about the systems 
is available. 
• 
These methods are simple to develop and apply. 
• 
Their reasoning is transparent, and they provide the ability to reason even under 
uncertainty. 
==PAGE:25==
15 
 
• 
They possess the ability to provide explanations for the suggested diagnoses because 
the method relies on cause-effect relationships. 
Weaknesses of FDD based on qualitative models include: 
• 
The methods are specific to a system or a process. 
• 
Although these methods are easy to develop, it is difficult to ensure that all rules are 
always applicable and to find a complete set of rules, especially when the system is 
complex. 
• 
The simplicity is lost as new rules are added to extend the existing rules or 
accommodate special circumstances. 
• 
These models, to a large extent, depend on the expertise and knowledge of the 
developer. 
Selection of methods for FDD applications 
The selection of method for FDD plays a critical part in development of the FDD system. As 
discussed there is a wide range of methods available for FDD with multiple strengths and 
weaknesses. Some methods have few data requirements (e.g. qualitative models); while others 
require extensive data. There are several approaches to detecting and diagnosing faults in 
building systems. They differ widely on the type of system to which they are applied, the 
necessary degree of knowledge about the diagnosed system, the degree of automation, 
tolerance to false alarms and the input data required. Development of detailed physical models 
is expensive, and very situation specific and not practical in most instances. Therefore, either 
simplified models or knowledge bases are more useful. 
For some FDD tools, fault diagnosis may not be needed because the detection of faults 
sufficiently isolates the fault. On the other hand, fault diagnosis may not be possible because 
of lack of resolution in the data. The methods used for detection are often different from those 
used in diagnosing. During detection, actual measurements are compared to expected values to 
identify that a fault has occurred. Diagnosis is more complex and requires search methods to 
isolate the fault and its cause. 
==PAGE:26==
16 
 
The amount of measured data required plays a big part in the selection of a method for both 
detection and diagnosis. A limited set of measured data will lead to the selection of a more 
detailed physical model. However, physical models may require other inputs such as physical 
characteristics and design details of the system and therefore require a lot more information 
about the specific system. In general, most buildings HVAC systems have a limited set of 
sensors, that often are implemented for control purposes only. The cost of additional sensors 
should be considered when selecting methods that require data beyond those that are normally 
provided for controls. On the other hand, methods that rely on limited set of measured data 
may not be sensitive to detection and may have difficulty in isolation of the causes of 
faults.(Katipamula & Brambley, 2005).

4.2 
Borgerskolen’s building management system 
The BMS on Borgerskolen has a simple set up. The system is a CTS-system that controls the 
air handling unit, heating plant and domestic hot water. 
4.2.1 Field level 
The control of the school is divided into a number of zones each controlled on its own. The 
part of the school investigated in this study is controlled by one system and has its own AHU 
and heating central. 
The AHU unit is equipped with three temperature sensors and two pressure sensors, measuring 
the temperature upstream of the heat exchanger, the supply temperature, the return temperature 
and the supply and return pressure. The AHU is equipped with a heating coil that ensures a 
supply temperature as requested by the setpoint. 
The heating central is also equipped with three temperature sensors two on the building side 
and one on the district heating side. The two on the building side measure the supply 
temperature and return temperature to the heating central. The last temperature sensor is on the 
district heating side and measures the return temperature to the district heating plant. In all the 
classrooms the exhaust duct is equipped with a CO2 sensor that controls the damper opening 
and the supply flow rate. This control is a closed loop and cannot be changed from the CTS.

The third set of rules check if the system has the correct air supply temperature, more precisely 
that the heat exchanger and heating coil is on during the heating season, off during the free-
floating period, and that the cooling coil is on during the cooling period. 
In order to decide the mode of operation three checks were made as shown in Figure 8.3, 
namely if Troom < Tcrit, min, Troom = [Tcrit, min;Tcrit, max] or Troom > Tcrit,max.  
This may seem quite simple. However, it can be quite challenging to decide which measured 
temperature Troom should be.  
One way is if Troom is the average air temperature of all the buildings rooms. By doing so there 
may occur situations where the south facing rooms need cooling because of a high solar heat 
gain, while the north facing rooms need heating. By simply taking the average air temperature 
of all the rooms, some of the rooms will receive cooling, while actually needing heating or vice 
versa. 
==PAGE:52==
42 
 
Another way to control the system is to control according to the lowest room temperature. By 
doing so cooling is only applied when all the room temperatures are above Tcrit, max. Instead of 
lowering the supply air temperature by mechanical cooling, the supply dampers to the rooms 
should open more in order to increase the air change rate and ventilate more of heating load 
away until all the supply dampers in all the rooms are fully open, then the air temperature 
should be lowered. 
A weighted average of all the temperatures in the building that gives the least number of 
dissatisfied occupants is probably the best way Troom to use. This means that some rooms may 
be a little too warm or too cold in order for the rest of the buildings users to obtain a satisfactory 
indoor environment. 
The first rule checks if the heat exchanger and heating coil is on during the heating period. If 
the supply temperature as a minimum is 5 degrees higher than the supply temperature, the heat 
exchanger and the heating coil is on. For the supply temperature to be higher than the return 
temperature the air must be heated. This check only assures that the air is heated but it does not 
check if it is by the heat exchanger and the heating coil or by the heating coil alone. This means 
that the heat exchanger can be malfunctioning, and the heating coil compensates for this, which 
results in a higher energy use. 
The second rule checks whether the heating coils is on, the heat exchanger is off and if the 
cooling coil is off during the free-floating period.  If the supply temperature is higher than the 
return temperature the heating coil is on which is an error since it should be off. Secondly it is 
checked if the supply temperature is equal to or two degrees above the temperature upstream 
from the heat exchanger. If this is the case, the heat exchanger and heating coil is off, and the 
only heat supplied to the air is the heat generated by the supply fan. Last it is checked if the 
cooling coil is off. This is done by comparing the temperature upstream from the heat 
exchanger with the air supply temperature. If the temperature upstream from the heat exchanger 
is higher than the supply temperature the cooling coil is on and the air is cooled which is an 
error since it should be off. These set of rules are demonstrated below.

12 DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION 
During this study an in-depth analysis of the indoor environmental climate and control of the 
building systems of Borgerskolen, has been performed.   
The indoor environment analyses clearly show considerable issues. Often system faults are 
only discovered when it leads to an unsatisfactory indoor environment and the users of the 
building complain about these issues. 
From the indoor environmental analyses, it is clear that the thermal problems are primarily 
experienced in the heating season due to very low temperatures in the school. The rooms 
HTK_104 and HTK_105 have the poorest thermal environment with 50% of the occupied 
hours under 21 ℃. In order to solve this problem further analyses needs to be done in order to 
identify the specific causes. These include checking if the radiators are functioning and if the 
power is sufficient to cover the heat loss of the building. 
Another significant finding is that the CO2 concentration exceeds 1000 ppm which is 
considered as the maximum limit for the air quality to be satisfactory. This is a problem 20% 
of the occupied hours. When linking this information with analysis calculating the ACR in the 
rooms it is clear that the supplied ventilation rate is significantly lower than recommended in 
DS 3033. 
The systems are controlled by CTS which has been implemented, however the control 
algorithms and parameters are not verified. The staff on the school monitoring the system in 
order to react on failure alarms, but knowledge has not been sufficiently transferred in order 
for staff to maintain the settings. 
Automated FDD shows promise in operation and maintenance. An example of a FDD tool has 
been developed in order to verify the implemented parameters and ensuring that the systems 
are installed correctly and operate properly. When using this FDD tool on the measured data 
from the school a couple of operation errors have been identified. 
 
==PAGE:76==
66 
 
The ventilation system is in operation every hour every day of the week even when the noise 
measurements shows no sign of occupants present. This results in a way higher energy use than 
necessary and just by changing the schedule the school could reduce its energy use by a 
significant amount. 
These findings are support by checking the operation of the supply fan. 
Furthermore, the heating system is checked using the FFD tool. These findings support the 
hypothesis that the heating system cannot emit the necessary heat in the rooms. From the check 
of the heating system it is has been identified that the cooling in the system is very low, around 
10℃. This suggests a clear issue with the radiator system This could be under dimensioning or 
malfunctions somewhere. In order to verify the reasons, the system needs to be further 
investigated. 
Finally, the occupancy in the rooms has been calculated from the determined air change rates. 
These calculated occupancies vary a lot compared to the expected based on the size of the 
classes. However, a precise estimation of the occupancy over the cause of weeks would be a 
great tool in order to better control the building systems. If the heating, ventilation and cooling 
can be controlled by the occupancy and it is possible to precisely predict the number of people 
this can be a mean to manage the peaks and reduce the energy consumption.  
The investigations done in this thesis and the developed FDD tool gives a good foundation in 
order to detect and diagnose problems and optimize the operation and maintenance of the 
building systems in order to obtain a better indoor environment as well as a lower energy 
consumption.
