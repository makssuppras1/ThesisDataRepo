Abstract
Denmark has a power system with a large share of wind and low inertia, especially in DK2.
Heavy duty electric trucks carry large batteries and could help keep frequency stable, but
they must still meet tight duty cycles. This thesis models trucks one by one with realistic
schedules from driver rules, battery sizes by weight class, and charger and site limits. It
builds two physics baselines for depot load, As Soon As Possible and Just In Time, and
tests three allocators, a multi agent RL policy, Earliest Deadline First, and Equal Share.
Flexibility is then measured with simple formulas that use the feasible import and export
at each step and the energy that must be paid back after an activation. Results show
that Just In Time exposes strong down regulation, while As Soon As Possible exposes up
regulation for short windows. Vehicle to grid greatly increases up and symmetric capacity.
Aggregation helps but with non linear gains due to site limits and recovery budgets. Earli-
est Deadline First tracks setpoints exactly and is a safe choice for bids, while the learned
policy can realize activations when envelope slack exists. The study gives practical guid-
ance on what a DK2 depot can credibly offer and how baseline choice and vehicle mix
shape that offer.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
High shares of wind power make frequency control harder, especially in Eastern Denmark
(DK2) where system inertia is low. At the same time, heavy duty electric vehicles (HDEVs)
trucks carry large batteries and could help stabilize the grid. The open question is whether
these vehicles can reliably provide frequency services given their tight duty cycles.
Policy and market changes make this timely. Denmark plans to enable independent ag-
gregation, so third parties can pool dispersed resources like EV fleets for ancillary ser-
vices. Market products are also moving toward smaller bid sizes and shorter durations ,
making participation feasible for modest fleets. In transport, new road pricing that favors
zero emission trucks, higher tolls, purchase and infrastructure support, and a national
build-out of high power truck charging further accelerate adoption. Together, these trends
create an opportunity but also uncertainty about how much flexible capacity HDEVs can
offer without disrupting operations.
1.2
Objectives
The goal of this thesis is to evaluate, with realistic detail, how much and under what con-
ditions HDEVs can support frequency regulation in a wind-dominated, low-inertia system.
We focus on four objectives:
1. Flexibility vs. duty constraints: Quantify how much up/down regulation is actually
available once real routes, rest times, and charging needs are respected.
2. Per-vehicle quantification: Model flexibility at the vehicle level (not just station
level) by tracking drive or dwell, state of charge, and feasible charging power over
time.
3. Heterogeneity and infrastructure limits: Study how differences in battery sizes,
schedules, and charger or site constraints shape aggregate flexibility, and identify
subsets.
4. Activation and recovery: Measure the recovery energy after a frequency event,
the risk of missing departure targets, and battery throughput implications, to define
practical activation limits.
1.3
Literature Review
Early work on passenger EVs showed that controlled charging and V2G can support pri-
mary and secondary frequency control, both in simulation and field trials. Demonstrations
in Denmark proved that standard EVs can deliver frequency containment reserves reliably
and for long durations, with monitored impacts on battery health and normal usage.
Beyond feasibility, studies have explored how aggregators forecast, coordinate, and bid
EV flexibility into markets. These typically model fleets in aggregate and focus on light-
duty contexts (home or workplace charging), showing revenue potential but highlighting
sensitivities to tariffs and degradation costs.
Heavy duty fleets are less studied. Recent research begins to quantify flexibility for com-
mercial vehicles like buses and trucks, showing meaningful potential but strong variability

across vehicle types and schedules. EU projects on smart charging and V2G underline
barriers such as user acceptance, standards, and rebound effects, which also matter for
HDEVs.
A key gap is high resolution modeling of individual heavy vehicles. Many analyses treat a
depot as a single unit which hides differences across trucks. Policy outlooks in Denmark
anticipate more DER participation via independent aggregation, but they do not resolve
how flexibility breaks down per asset. Industry planning for truck charging overnight de-
pots plus highway fast charging hubs suggests growing access to opportunity charging,
which could increase usable flexibility if managed well.
This thesis addresses the gap by modeling HDEVs at the per-vehicle level with realistic
duty cycles. We quantify when and how each truck or bus can adjust charging for fre-
quency support while still meeting transport needs, and we aggregate these results to
assess what a fleet could credibly offer in DK2’s evolving market framework.

Background and Context
The aim of this chapter is to provide foundational data and insights upon which the flexi-
bility model is built. This chapter documents factual data with careful estimations majorly
from three reports, namely the KF24[1] ,the Danish road directorates report on HDEVs
2023 [2] and the Energinet (Danish TSO) ancillary services outlook 2024 [3].
3.1
Danish Power System and balancing outlook
The landscape of Danish energy mix is quite unique from other Nordic countries. In con-
trast to Norway where most of the electricity is generated from hydropower or Sweden,
which utilizes a combination of hydropower and nuclear, Denmark’s electricity generation
exhibits a major reliance on wind power. This is a direct consequence of Denmark’s ge-
ographical characteristics, which heavily favor wind resource exploitation. Wind power
represents the primary component of Denmark’s electricity generation portfolio. In 2023,
wind turbines generated 19,393 GWh, accounting for more than 50% of total electricity
generation [4] . This is a substantial increase from 7,809 GWh in 2010 and 14,133 GWh in
2015. This consistent growth of wind power in Denmark’s electricity portfolio is central to
Denmark’s target of achieving 100% renewable electricity generation by 2028 and fossil
fuel free energy and utilities sector by 2030 [5]
3.1.1
Bidding Zones
Denmark has two bidding zones belonging to two different synchronous areas illustrated
in Figure 3.1. DK1 and DK2 are linked internally by the Great Belt HVDC connection,
this link explains why zones can have different market outcomes yet remain connected
operationally. DK1 (Western Denmark) is part of Continental Europe, while DK2 (Eastern
Denmark) is part of the Nordic synchronous area and participates in Nordic balancing mar-
kets. Geographically, DK2 covers the region of Zealand and the eastern Danish islands
including Bornholm.

3.1.2
Need for Balancing
As variable generation rises, and conventional generation is being replaced by inverter
based variable renewable energy, this introduces a new technical challenge in power sys-
tem operation. Conventional synchronous generators typically used in fossil fuel based
plants due to their rotating mass inherently provide system inertia, which is a measure
of the systems resistance to changes in frequency which acts as a buffer for imbalances
between demand and consumption. However, wind and solar do not have provide this
inherent system inertia. This reduction in grid inertia leads to higher susceptibility against
frequency deviations caused by sudden changes in generation or load. In 2025 the grid
inertia, measured as kinetic energy, is estimated to be below the required volume of 120–
145 GWs 1–19% of the time. Whereas in the period 2010-2015 the estimated inertia was
below 140GWs 4% of the time or less [6] The lowest kinetic energy values observed were
during summer nights with high wind production. The projected increased risk of maintain-
ing system inertia in Denmark is therefore mainly due to the increase of wind penetration
into the grid [6].
By 2040 Energinet’s overall need for ancillary services is projected to rise by approxi-
mately 150% [3]. This need for balancing is met by the BSPs (Balancing Service Providers)
in the balancing market which offer various balancing products based on their response
times. As of 2024 40% of the market potential share for balancing in the DK2 zone was
met by fossil fuels. Historically they have been a reliable source of providing balance but
given the future de-carbonization goals and increased need for balancing services, these
sources need to be displaced and the balancing portfolio needs to be diversified.
3.1.3
Services and Future Outlook
Ancillary services are defined as flexible production and consumption of electricity pro-
cured to balance the power system. The supply and demand of electricity must equal at
all times of the day to ensure stable grid frequency at 50Hz [3].
The type of services are broadly categorized into up regulation and down regulation, and
symmetric products and asymmetric products. The table below summarizes these prod-
ucts for the DK2 zone.
Excess supply or low demand causes increase in frequency and a supply deficit or ex-
cess demand causes a decrease in grid frequency. In cases of power deficit ancillary
service providers increase production or decrease consumption to provide up regulation.
Whereas, in cases of excess power, ancillary service providers decrease production or
increase consumption to provide down regulation.
In symmetric market products, ancillary services must be deliverable as both up regulation
and down regulation. Whereas, Asymmetric products must be deliverable as either up
regulation or down regulation.

The need for FFR is directly linked to the level of system inertia. Consequently, the need
for FFR is projected to grow significantly. While the market is expected to be relatively
stable until 2030, a sharp increase is anticipated thereafter, with the average need rising

mFRR
The requirement for up-regulation is expected to decrease from 600 MW/ in 2025 to 480

double, reaching 200 MW/h. [3]

Operational Patterns
The driving patterns, rest periods of HDEVs may differ to those of HDVs. The general-
ized patterns for charging, rest and driving periods are not yet available. Therefore, this
study relies on existing regulations and meaningful assumptions to derive the possible
operational patterns.
3.3.1
Driving and Rest Regulations
According to European regulations [10], the daily driving period is limited to 9 hours, which
can be extended to 10 hours twice a week at most. After which an extended rest period
must be taken of at least 11 hours which can be reduced to 9 hours for a maximum of
three times a week. The extended rest can be split into 3 hours rest followed by 9 hour
rest to make a total of 12 hours. The total weekly driving duration is limited to a maximum
of 56 hours and the total fortnightly driving time to 90 hours.
Additionally drivers must take a 45 minute break (separable into 15 minutes followed by
30 minutes) after a continuous driving period of 4.5 hours.
A consecutive rest period of 45 hours is to be taken after six days of working which can
be reduced to 24 hours every other week.
The regulation also allows flexibility under exceptional circumstances of up to 2 addi-
tional driving hours to accommodate the driver reaching their place of residence or the
employer’s operational center in order to take the regular weekly rest period.

5.1
Baseline Charging Modeling
Determining a realistic baseline charging profile for heavy-duty EV (HDEV) fleets is chal-
lenging due to limited public data on typical charging behavior. Prior studies indicate that,
in the absence of smart charging incentives, EV users tend to front load charging, i.e.
plug in and charge at full power immediately upon arrival in order to maximize their avail-
able charge as soon as possible. This As Soon As Possible (ASAP) charging behavior
shows a users preference for immediate charging and ensures the vehicle reaches the
desired state of charge well before its departure. On the other hand, fleet operators or
smart charging systems can deliberately plan charging schedules for various reasons.
For example, a fleet manager might enforce slower charging to reduce battery stress and
degradation, or to create headroom for market participation.
Alternatively, charging can be timed to coincide with off peak electricity prices, saving on
energy costs by shifting charging to cheaper hours. Both of these strategies could serve
as potential baselines in a controlled smart charging plan.
In this work, rather than assume a specific user baseline, we quantify the technical flexibil-
ity potential by defining two extreme baseline charging strategies based purely on physical
limits. We define Just In Time (JIT) charging and As Soon As Possible (ASAP) charging
as two bounding cases. JIT charging uses the minimum power necessary at each time
step such that each EV exactly meets its required energy by its departure time (delaying
charging as long as possible while still finishing on time).JIT spreads the charging load
evenly over the available dwell time and avoids any unnecessary early charging.
Whereas, ASAP charging is the opposite extreme where each EV charges at the max-
imum allowable power (limited by either the vehicle’s charge rate or the site’s capacity)
immediately upon plugging in, and continues until its energy requirement is fulfilled. This
front loaded approach results in the battery reaching the target SOC well before depar-
ture and then idling. Both JIT and ASAP baselines guarantee the EV receives the needed
energy by departure, but they represent very different load shapes.

Earliest-Deadline-First (EDF) Heuristic
This greedy approach ensures that vehicles which are closest to their departure (and still
need energy) have charging priority, thus reducing the risk of missing any EV’s required
24
Heavy-Duty EV Aggregation for Frequency Stability in Wind Dominated Power Systems
==PAGE:33==
charge by its deadline. EDF based scheduling has been widely used as a heuristic in EV
charging studies and is known to be simple and myopic it uses only the deadline infor-
mation (and current need) to make decisions, without regard to factors like battery size
or how much time other vehicles have left. Nonetheless, it can be effective in scenarios
where meeting each EV’s departure SOC is the primary objective. [16]
The allocator will always fully utilize the station’s power setpoint if there are enough ac-
tive HDEVs with energy needs, distributing that power to the most time critical vehicles
first. This makes it a useful benchmark for charging performance. However, EDF does
not consider any notion of fairness or future consequences a vehicle with a later deadline
might get little to no charge until higher priority vehicles are satisfied. Furthermore, EDF
ignores battery cycling costs or grid signals. These limitations are highlighted in the liter-
ature [17] found that straightforward policies like EDF tend to perform worse in terms of
feasibility because they don’t jointly consider all relevant factors.

We also evaluate an Equal Share (ES) allocation strategy, which represents a fairness
oriented but naive baseline. Under the equal share policy, the station’s available power
at each time step is simply divided evenly among all currently plugged in EVs that still
require energy.
Each vehicle receives an equal portion of the power budget, capped by its own maximum
charge rate and remaining energy need. The controller continues this equal allocation
until either all available power is distributed or all EVs are fully satisfied.
However, ES fundamentally ignores differences in urgency, for example an EV departing
in 1 hour and another departing in 5 hours will still receive the same power at a given
moment, which could easily lead to the near term departure not reaching its required
charge in time. Thus, equal splitting is likely to be suboptimal in utilization of the available
time and capacity. Policies like ES fail to account for the joint considerations of deadlines,
rates, and needs, so they often cannot guarantee a feasible schedule for all EVs in heavier
load scenarios.[17] We still include ES as a baseline to illustrate the outcomes of a purely
equal and naive allocation, and to contrast its behavior against EDF and the learned MARL
policy.

5.3
Flexibility Quantification
To quantify the potential technical flexibility of the HDEV charging system, we first formal-
ize the feasible charging envelope within which the station load can vary. These limits
are determined by vehicle and infrastructure constraints.At any time t during an HDEVs’
dwell, the charging power is bounded between a minimum Pmin(t) and maximum Pmax(t)
feasible level. Pmax(t) is the total power draw if all present EVs charge at full rate (capped
by their charge rates and the site capacity), and Pmin(t) is the lowest aggregate power that
still ensures all EVs meet their energy requirements by departure (intuitively, the station
could even be idle at times if schedule slack allows, or export power if V2G capable). Any
acceptable charging profile P(t) must satisfy P min(t) ≤P(t) ≤P max(t) for all t.
The baseline charging plans defined earlier (ASAP and JIT) in fact trace the two extreme
envelopes of this feasible band. For example under an ASAP baseline u(t) = Pmax(t)
whenever an EV has remaining demand, whereas a JIT baseline b(t) operates at or near
Pmin(t) at each moment. These bounds form a flexibility envelope for the aggregator’s
load.
Therefore, the difference between the ASAP and JIT baselines at a given time represent
the instantaneous power headroom, which is how much the station could increase or
decrease its consumption relative to a given baseline at that time.
This headroom is fundamentally a function of the EVs’ battery states and charging capac-
ities. For example an EV with a low SOC and plenty of time remaining provides downward
flexibility by allowing its charge to be delayed, whereas an EV near full charge offers little
downward flexibility but could even discharge power if V2G capable.

Discussion
Key Takeaways
• The MARL policy successfully enacts activations that create the appropriate head-
room (upward for JIT and downward for ASAP) and performs the energy neutral
recovery automatically. This validates the use of our controller agnostic, post pro-
cessing flexibility computation as a reliable upper bound, with MARL providing a
practical way to deliver those services.
• Baseline choice dominates the shape of flexibility. JIT exposes large down capacity
with small up, while ASAP exposes large up with negligible down (without V2G).
• Aggregation helps but saturates. Adding trucks increases both up and down capac-
ity, but nonlinearly due to the instantaneous limits Pmin and Pmax and the rebound
budget that must recover deviations after the window.
• Allocator matters for operability. Heuristics like EDF perfectly track the site baseline
and maintain feasibility, the learned MARL policy shows small errors in small fleets
and degrades in the 5 truck JIT case. This supports using EDF for conservative,
reportable capacity numbers.
• Given the projected rise in down regulation needs (surplus wind) and growing aFRR
and FFR requirements, a JIT oriented depot operation can reliably supply down
reserves at scale without V2G, while V2G adoption unlocks large up and symmetric
capacities. For depots targeting symmetric products, either operating closer to JIT
or enabling controlled export and re shaping the baseline to avoid hugging Pmax
during availability windows.
• JIT operation yields stable down-regulation across product durations, making it well
suited to DK2 down products with 15–60 min activations (e.g. mFRR down).

Conclusion
This work set out to quantify when and how heavy duty electric trucks can support fre-
quency control without breaking transport needs. By modeling each vehicle and its day,
enforcing charger and site limits, and comparing two simple baselines for depot load, the
study mapped the feasible envelope of operation and the sustainable capacity that fits
inside it.
Three findings matter most. First, baseline choice dominates the shape of flexibility. Just
In Time sits near the lower envelope and therefore offers large down regulation with mod-
est up. As Soon As Possible sits near the upper envelope and offers the opposite, strong
up for short windows and little down unless export is allowed. Second, vehicle to grid
shifts the picture. It lifts up regulation under both baselines and is essential for symmetric
products when the depot would otherwise hug the upper envelope. Third, scale helps but
not in a straight line. Adding trucks increases headroom and recovery energy, but site
caps and the need to pay back energy limit gains.
On controllers, Earliest Deadline First is simple and tracks the depot setpoint exactly,
which makes it a reliable benchmark and a conservative choice for capacity declarations.
The learned policy shows that smart allocation can realize the envelope in practice and
recover energy automatically, but its tracking and robustness depend on operating point
and training scope.
Overall, DK2 depots can supply meaningful down reserves today with Just In Time oper-
ation even without export. With growing vehicle to grid capability, depots can unlock large
up and symmetric capacity. The methods and results provide a clear, reproducible way
to size offers that respect both grid needs and departure targets.
7.1
Application and future work
Applications
• Product selection: For symmetric products, enable export or adjust the baseline
so the depot is not at the upper limit during the window.
• Bidding practice: Derate for controller error, telemetry, and forecast error to make
credible hourly offers. Use Earliest Deadline First by default and use the learned
policy when there is slack.
• Planning: Use the per vehicle model to test battery size, charger count, and vehicle
to grid share. Place chargers at depots and hubs where headroom matches market
needs.
Future work
• Battery health: Add a wear model and report energy throughput and cycling costs
with revenue.
• Markets and prices: Co optimize energy cost and reserves. Add price based base-
lines and test weekend and holiday patterns.
• Network effects: Extend to many depots and highway hubs and respect feeder
limits.
• Hardware in the loop: Validate with a lab charger or a small pilot and test activation
tracking, metering, and recovery.

The consumption rate for HDEVs is complex to compute due to the fact that it highly
depends on the use case of these vehicles. The consumption rate for trucks equipped
with cooler, freezers, concrete mixers, refuse collection arms, etc are much higher than
trucks used just for delivery of goods. As an example the Mercedes eActros 400 with a
GCW of 18tonnes according to the manufacturer testing conditions was tested as a refuse
collector and a concrete mixer, in these cases the consumption rate was much higher at
1.8644 kWh/km.
The consumption rate also depends on the test conditions for which the range was de-
termined, it is not uncommon for these trucks to be tested at 60% loading. However, in
such cases where range was determined at a different load than GCW, the load at which
it was tested is used to calculate the consumption rate in table ??.

terrain, cabin consumption (heating or cooling) and any weather effects. However, for this
simulation all these factors besides weather are not considered. To account for change in
consumption rate due to temperature, a constant factor of 10% is used for winter months
[weatherconsumption]. Therefore it is also necessary to define winter months, in Den-
