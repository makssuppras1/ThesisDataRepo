Abstract
The recently launched Surface Water Ocean and Water Topography (SWOT) mission
provides Sea Surface Height (SSH) observations over a 120 km wide swath at a 2 km
resolution. This presents a unique opportunity to map the mesoscale and submesoscale
ocean dynamics that are not currently resolved by conventional altimetry. The aim of this
project is to produce high resolution maps of the SSH field that are consistent in time and
space along the East Greenland coast. From the SSH field, geostrophic currents and
relative vorticity can be derived and used to study the mesoscale (20km-200km) and sub-
mesoscale (200m-20km) dynamics developing in the East Greenland Current (EGC) and
along the Polar Front. The submesoscale is associated with vertical exchanges of nutri-
ents, heat, and carbon, and is thus particularly important for understanding the effects of
the warming and freshening the region is experiencing due to climate change. Because of
its location at high latitudes, the study area benefits from a small Rossby number and short
satellite revisit time. However, in the period between November and June, a large part
of the East Greenland shelf is covered by Arctic sea ice that has been transported south
by the EGC. To overcome the problem of limited altimetry observations in these months,
SWOT data in the marginal ice zone will be exploited by masking individual ice floes. The
linear Optimal Interpolation (OI) methods traditionally used on conventional altimetry is
not able to fully exploit the high resolution content of the SWOT observations because
the decorrelation time of the small scale dynamics are much smaller than the satellite re-
visit time. Thus, a Dynamic Optimal Interpolation (DOI) method based upon conservation
of potential vorticity in a quasi-geostrophic framework is applied to assimilate SWOT and
conventional altimetry data. The spatial resolution is significantly increased compared to
the DUACS products which are based on OI of conventional altimetry data. The coverage
is significantly increased in coastal and sea-ice covered areas. The capability of the DOI
method in capturing mesoscale and submesoscale features is validated with Sea Surface
Temperature (SST) data. This project illustrates how combining high resolution SWOT
data with a dynamic model can increase both the accuracy and the spatial and temporal
resolution of SSH mapping.

Introduction
Conventional nadir altimetry has substantially increased our understanding of basin-scale
ocean circulations and larger mesoscale dynamics over the past 30 years through mea-
surements of Sea Surface Heights (SSH) [1]. SSH is a valuable oceanographic param-
eter because it reflects the surface pressure field and thus carries information on depth-
integrated ocean dynamics, geostrophic surface currents, and relative vorticity [2].
Since 1992 a continuous constellation of 2-7 nadir radar altimeters have been measuring
1-D SSH with centimeter accuracy [3]. 2D mapping of these SSH observations using a
statistical space-time Optimal Interpolation (OI) based on measurement errors and spa-
tial and temporal correlation scales [4] produce maps that resolve ocean scales down to
150 km wavelength at mid-latitudes [1]. The resolution is limited by the low space-time
sampling of the constellation and by the noise level and spatial resolution of the nadir
altimeters (about 7 km) [3].
The ocean finescale encompasses small mesoscale (20 km to 100 km) and submesoscale
(200 m to 20 km) ocean features such as eddies, fronts, and filaments. At these scales the
flow is not in geostrophic balance and therefore the finescale features are often associ-
ated with significant vertical velocities, connecting the deep ocean with the ocean surface.
Thus they play an important part in the vertical transport of heat, carbon, and nutrients
that influence ocean physics and ecology up to climate-scales [1]. Furthermore, finescale
features link large scale balanced flow with small scale turbulence and are thus responsi-
ble for redistributing energy between scales [5]. With conventional altimetry for example
large mesoscale eddies (> 150 km) can be detected but their formation and dissipation to
smaller scales are not captured [1].
In 2008 development of a new type of altimetry mission capable of resolving finescale
ocean features began. Resolving finescale features required significant improvements
over nadir altimetry techniques in both temporal and spatial resolution, as well as improved
precision at small scales because the magnitude of the SSH signal decreases at smaller
scales [3]. In December 2023 such a mission, the Surface Water and Ocean Topography
(SWOT), was launched and it has since revolutionized the altimetry world by providing
2D SSH observations over a 120 km wide swath at unprecedented resolution. This has
been achieved by using a new interferometric technology that employs two antennas and
utilizes the relative phase difference between them [3].
The decorrelation time of oceanic features declines dramatically as the spatial scale de-
creases and the small signals observed by SWOT must therefore be sampled at high
temporal resolution. The sampling of SWOT is significantly better than any nadir altimetry
mission due to its large swath width providing global coverage in 21 days. This is much
better than even a constellation of 5 nadir altimeters [3]. However, the decorrelation time
at a spatial scale of 15 km is about 1 day and while the sampling of SWOT might be better
than 21 days in some locations, i.e. at high latitudes or at cross-over points, the mission
does not have the temporal sampling required to properly resolve the smallest scales seen
in the data. The statistical based 2D mapping techniques used on conventional altimetry
will thus not be able to fully utilize the small scale signals observed by SWOT [1],[6],[7].
A solution to this problem, that was proposed in 2014 [6], is to instead use a Dynamic
Optimal Interpolation (DOI) approach where the SSH observations are propagated over
the temporal gaps using model dynamics from a quasi-geostrophic (QG) 1.5-layer model

relying only on a single parameter: the Rossby radius of deformation. The method has
since been tested on both synthetic SWOT data [8] and real nadir data [7] and it has
proven to be very efficient at mapping SSH with improvements over OI methods in both
error and resolution.
In this project the DOI approach described above will be applied on SSH observations from
SWOT and conventional altimetry data to produce SSH maps that have high resolution
and are continuous in time and from which geostrophic currents and relative vorticity can
be derived. It will be conducted on a 1 million km2 area in the Greenland Sea to improve
the knowledge of finescale features developing on the East Greenland Shelf and in the
East Greenland Current (EGC). The outcome of this project may help explain the effects
of freshwater changes in the Arctic and how these are linked to for example phytoplankton
blooms [9]. The results will be validated by a correlation analysis between the computed
SSH maps and Sea Surface Temperature (SST) data showing if the small scale structures
seen in the SST are captured by the QG-model. Additionally, qualitative validations will
be carried out by comparing the maps with SST data, and Chlorophyll-a data.

Theory
This section describes relevant theory that will be used in the following chapters. This
includes background of the study area, relevant ocean physics, basic principle of satellite
altimetry, and theory behind data assimilation.
2.1
The Nordic Seas
[10]. The Nordic Seas is a region consisting of the Greenland Sea, the Norwegian Sea and
the Iceland Sea. It is connected to the Arctic sea through the Fram Strait, and borders the
North Atlantic along the Greenland-Scotland Ridge.Figure 2.1 shows a map of the region.
The bathymetry is characterized by three basins and wide continental shelves along the
east-coast of Greenland and north of Iceland. The general circulation consists of cyclonic
circulation within the basins (at depth) and boundary currents (at the surface). In figure
2.1 only the surface currents are shown. The Norwegian Atlantic Current is a northward
flowing current of warm (6-8oC) and saline (about 35 ppt) Atlantic Water. It follows the
coast of Norway, splits in two, and enters the Arctic on both sides of Svalbard. Side
branches feed warm water into the Greenland Basin. There are also smaller amounts
of warm Atlantic waters entering through the Denmark Strait flowing north of Iceland. In
the western part of the region the East Greenland Current (EGC) flows south along the
shelf of Greenland. It transports cold Polar Water characterized by low temperature (sub-
zero to 5 oC) and low salinity (less than 34.4 ppt) from the Arctic into the North Atlantic.
Deflections of the EGC such as the Jan Mayen Current, caused by the Jan Mayen Ridge,
feed cold water into the circulation in the Greenland Basin. There is also a side branch
called the East Icelandic Current which carries some of the water into the Iceland Sea
and the Norwegian Sea. The rest of the EGC exits through the Denmark Strait. The Polar
Front and the Arctic Front are regions of large horizontal gradients in temperature and
salinity located on either side of the Greenland Basin, separating Polar and Atlantic water
masses.
The western part of the region and the EGC are the focus of this project. Because the
EGC carries a lot of sea ice the Greenland coast is generally unnavigable in the winter
months and our knowledge of the current system is limited. The Greenland Sea Project
(1987-1994) deployed current meter moorings in the EGC at 75oN. From this project we
know that the long-term mean currents follow the depth contours and that the current core
is located about 40 km from the shelf break. They found that the core extended to a depth
of 2400 meters and had mean velocities of up to 0.3 m/s, while the mean velocities on
the shelf were about 0.1 m/s. Meanders and eddies caused variations in the current of
up to 0.3 m/s on timescales of a week to a month and the variations were most intense in
the upper layers where currents of 0.6 m/s were observed. Additionally they found strong
seasonal variations with stronger currents in winter.

Most of the sea ice in the EGC originates from the Arctic Ocean, although some ice for-
mation occurs within the EGC in sea ice leads and polynyas. There is also a contribution
from landfast ice which grows from the coast in winter, and then breaks up and drifts in
spring. The sea ice along the Greenland coast can be divided into 3 groups; landfast
ice that stays in place, pack ice in floes that are in almost free drift, and the marginal ice
zone which is the transition zone between pack ice and open ocean. The sea ice cover is
subject to large interannual fluctuations that can be as large as seasonal variations within
a year.

The effects are felt if the nondimensional parameter is much smaller than 1 and not felt
if the nondimensional parameter is much larger than 1. They can therefore be used to
categorize the dynamical processes of the oceans that occur at different scales as seen
in figure 2.2.
At the largest scale Ro and Fr are much smaller than 1 and the fluid motion is constrained
by both rotation and stratification, which puts it in a state close to both hydrostatic and
geostrophic balance. This is the case for the global general circulation system that in-
cludes ocean gyres and western boundary currents, but also the case for mesoscale
eddies and fronts with a horizontal scale between 20 km and 200 km. Contrarily, at the
smallest scales we see turbulence because viscous effects dominate over rotation and
stratification [11].
In between these are the submesoscale dynamics which have typical horizontal scales of
200 m to 20 km and includes submesoscale eddies, fronts, and filaments. At these scales
Ro ∼1, suggesting that rotational effects are important but that they do not constrain the
motion to the same extent as at the mesoscale. This allows the development of strong
vertical motion 10-100 times larger than those associated with mesoscale motion. As
with the mesoscale, Fr << 1 and stratification effects are important which makes them
sensitive to the depth of the mixed layer. Because of this there is a strong seasonal cycle
with smaller scale structures and strong vertical velocities in winter when the mixed layer
is deep, and larger mesoscale eddies with weaker vertical velocities in summer [1].

nondimensional parameters: Reynolds Number (Re), Froude number (Fr), and Rossby
number (Ro). Figure from [5].
Submesoscale features develop through dynamical instabilities in geostrophically bal-
anced flows such as horizontal shear instabilities or baroclinic instabilities along fronts.
In polar regions submesoscale eddies can develop when melt water in the marginal ice
zone generates horizontal density gradients. Submesoscale flows are important because
they connect the large scale balanced dynamics with small scale turbulence and are re-
sponsible for redistributing energy to smaller scales. Furthermore, their strong vertical
motion plays an important role in the transport of heat and carbon between the ocean
surface and the ocean interior which are key factors in climate change. They are also
responsible for transporting nutrients from the deep ocean to the surface where there is
light, triggering phytoplankton blooms. They are thought to be especially important for
ocean biology because the high vertical velocity can transport material across the mixed
layer on time scales of ∼1 day which is comparable to the response time of marine mi-
croorganisms [1].

2.3
Satellite Altimetry
Satellite altimeters measure heights by transmitting radar signals and measuring the echos
that are returned by surface scatter points. The echos contain information about the sur-
face that caused the reflection and for simple surfaces, such as the ocean surface, the
round-trip time can be derived with high accuracy. Round-trip times can be converted
to range measurements by scaling with the speed of light. The range measurements
are then referenced to a terrestrial reference surface by including independent measure-
ments of the satellite position from e.g. GPS or ground stations. The reference surface
used in satellite altimetry is a simple approximation of the Earths shape called the Refer-
ence Ellipsoid and heights referred to this surface are called Sea Surface Heights (SSH)
[13].
In order to obtain a precise elevation estimate several error effects must be taken into ac-
count. The radar signal is delayed when traveling through the atmosphere and the delay
is dependent on e.g. the water content or state of the ionosphere. Often, the satellites
carry additional instruments to compute corrections for these effects, for example a ra-
diometer that measures the water content in the troposphere and a dual frequency radar
to estimate the ionospheric delay. The heights must also be corrected for the state of the
sea surface and instrumental errors [13]. Depending on the application, corrections can
also be made for tides using a tide model.
Figure 2.3 shows the evolution of the altimeter constellation in the last 10 years. The
constellation currently consists of eight missions, out of which seven are nadir altimeters
which sample the SSH along 1-dimensional lines. The eight is the recently launched
SWOT satellite, which is a wide-swath altimeter providing SSH in two-dimensional swaths
by utilizing interferometry. SWOT spent the first three months (starting in March 2023) of
operation in a special Calibration and Validation (Cal/Val) orbit with limited coverage but
with a very short repeat cycle of just 1 day. In July 2023 the orbit was switched to the
nominal Science orbit with global coverage and a 21 day repeat cycle. The measurement
principles behind nadir and wide-swath altimetry are explained in the following sections.

2.3.1
Nadir Altimetry
Since it’s introduction in the 1990’s, nadir altimetry has been the only method to measure
SSH, and is therefore referred to as conventional altimetry. The basic principle behind this
method is to point the antenna directly in the nadir direction and measure the elevation
along points located on the satellite ground track as shown in figure 2.4a. In this way the
SSH is computed simply by subtracting the range r from the satellite height H:
SSH = H −r
(2.22)
This is illustrated in the cross-track - z plane in figure 2.4b.
The radar footprints are circular with diameters of about 15 km [13]. On recent altimetry
missions, such as CryoSat-2, Sentinel 3A/B, and Sentinel-6, Synthetic Aperture Radar
(SAR) techniques have been used to process the altimetry data and increase the along-
track resolution [14]. The SAR technique utilizes the Doppler effect caused by the fast
movement of the satellite. It induces a frequency shift in the back scattered signals in the
along track direction and this can be used to split the radar footprint into smaller Doppler
strips. The radar footprint of Sentinel 3 is about 14 km and it can be split into strips that
are only 300 meter wide in the along track direction. The SAR mode does not affect the
cross-track resolution. Figure 2.4a shows a Doppler strip inside each radar footprint.
The main limitation of a nadir altimeter is the lack of coverage. Because the SSH is only
measured in 1D lines, the distance between neighboring ground tracks are large (typically
more than 100 km at mid-latitudes), and even a constellation of 4-5 altimeters does not
provide sufficient coverage to sample small mesoscale signals [3].

Sea Surface Heights
The heights measured by altimeters are SSH referenced to the Reference Ellipsoid. The
largest part of this signal is the Geoid which is the surface of constant geopotential that
coincide with the ocean at rest disregarding disturbances from winds, currents, and tides.
The other and much smaller part of the signal is the dynamic part of the topography, also
called Absolute Dynamic Topography (ADT), and it is the parameter of main interest to
oceanographers. Unfortunately, the Geoid is not known with sufficient accuracy at small
scales and the ADT can thus not be formed simply by subtracting the Geoid from the SSH
[13]. Therefore an alternative reference surface called the Mean Sea Surface (MSS) is
often used and heights referred to this surface are called Sea Level Anomalies (SLA):
SLA = SSH −MSS
(2.29)
The MSS is a temporal average of the SSH computed over many years by measurements
from multiple altimeter missions. The reference period typically used is the 20-year period

2.4
Data Assimilation
Data assimilation is a technique whereby observational data is combined with a numerical
model to estimate the evolving state of a system. It is a way to combine a numerical model
that is consistent in space and time but often too simple to account for all the involved
physics with real observations that are noisy and not available everywhere at all times.
Data assimilation is typically done in a sequential time-stepping manner where the model
state at each time step is updated with inputs from new observations.
2.4.1
Back and Forth Nudging
The assimilation method employed in this project is Back and Forth Nudging (BFN). The
BFN algorithm works by propagating a model forward and backward in time over a fixed
time window (BFN window) while at each time step adding a feedback term that is based
on observations [8]. The process is repeated until the forward and backward trajectories
converge. Then the BFN window is shifted and the algorithm is run again. Each BFN
window is associated with an output window that is shorter than the BFN window and
centered on it such that only model estimates influenced by both past and future observa-
tions are kept. The BFN windows are placed with overlaps to avoid gaps between output
windows as shown in figure 2.7.
The model state is given by the prognostic equation:
∂X
∂t = M (X, t)

different nudging coefficients. Note that computation of the feedback term requires that
the observations are direct observations of the state variable. In the forward propagation

In practice some operations are performed on the observations before they are used in
the BFN algorithm. They are extended in space and in time with exponentially decreas-
ing weights to avoid numerical instabilities that can arise from abrupt transitions between
observations and no observations. The BFN window is divided into assimilation time win-
dows, each of which are associated with a feedback term. At a specific grid point x and
in a specific assimilation time window centered at t all observations which fall within a
radius of 2D and a time 2τ from the center of the window are combined in a weighted av-

K(x, t) = K0e−[(t−ta)/τ]2e−[d(x)/D]2

τ must be fixed for each type of observation. K0 defines how strong the nudging is, D
defines its spatial influence, and τ defines how long it is used to nudge.
Boundary conditions must be supplied along the boundaries of the model grid. To ensure
a smooth integration they are applied by Gaspari Cohn functions in a relaxation zone
between the model and the boundary. At the outer edge the boundary conditions are
given full weight and at the inner edge the model estimate is given full weight.

Previous Works
In the literature data assimilation of altimetry data are sometimes referred to as Dynamic

Ballarotta [7] applied the method developed by Ubelmann on real nadir altimetry data in

and coastal regions. Validation with an independent nadir mission showed an increase
in average performance of 10-15% over OI methods. In addition to this they performed a
qualitative assessment based on SST that showed improvements in mapping of intense
mesoscale jets and fronts.
Guillou [8] used the same DOI method as Ubelmann and Ballarotta in an observing sys-
tem simulation experiment in the Gulf Stream region. Realistic datasets of SWOT and
nadir altimetry data were produced from a high resolution ocean model. The DOI method
is compared with the DUACS OI method and shows improvements in both RMS error
and effective resolution. The performance is increased when SWOT data is used in com-
bination with conventional altimetry compared to scenarios where only conventional or
only SWOT data is used. The code used in this project to run the assimilation model was
developed by Guillou and is available here [18].

Finite Size Lyapunov Exponents
Finite Size Lyapunov Exponents (FSLE) is a Lagrangian-based method used to study hor-
izontal transport and mixing processes in the ocean. They quantify divergence or stretch-
ing of horizontal flow and because vertical velocities are orders of magnitudes smaller
than horizontal velocities, horizontal stretching is linked to mixing. FSLE can be used to
visualize transport processes and detect for example transport barriers or vortex bound-
aries [19].
Mathematically, FSLE is calculated as the inverse of the time it takes two tracers initially
separated by a fixed distance δ0 to reach a separation of a fixed distance δf. The tracers
are followed in time by integrating the velocity field forward in time. FSLE value, λ, at
location x and time t is given by:
λ(x, t, δ0, δf) = 1
τ log
(δf
δ0
)

where τ is the time from t to the time when the tracers reach separation δf. It is computed
four times with initial separation in four orthogonal directions and the direction that gives
maximum value is selected as λ. To obtain the spatial distribution of FSLE, λ is computed
at x with grid spacing of ∆x which can be smaller than the grid spacing of the original
velocity field. The FSLE analysis is restricted to structures and transport occurring at the
length scales defined by δ0 and δf only.

Methods
In this chapter we will assimilate SWOT and conventional altimetry data in a Quasi-
Geostrophic (QG) model using the methodology described in section 2.4. The chapter
is structured into four sections: section 4.1 presents the study area, section 4.2 describes
the preprocessing steps performed on the altimetry data before they are used in the QG-
model, section 4.3 describes the setup of the assimilation model, and section 4.4 explains
the post processing steps that are applied to the output of the QG-model. The flowchart
in figure 4.1 gives an overview of the process.
4.1
Study Area
The study area is shown in figure 4.2. It is a 1.08 million km2 area centered on the EGC.
To the west it is bounded by the Greenland coast including the outlet of the Scoresby-
sund Sound. The lower boundary is set at 64.5oN to include the Denmark Strait and the
upper boundary is set at 78oN because it is the northernmost latitude covered by SWOT.
To reduce computational complexity and maximize the useful area, the study area is di-
vided into three regions where three separate assimilation models can be run and merged
together in the end.
Figure 4.2 shows the study area and the three regions along with the bathymetry (a), the
MDT (b), and the first baroclinic Rossby radius of deformation (c). The bathymetry is com-
prised of a wide continental shelf along the Greenlandic and Icelandic coast, a shelf break,
and deep basins such as the Greenland basin in region 3. Other bathymetric features are
the shallow Denmark Strait in region 1 which is associated with undersea overflow and
the Jan Mayen ridge in region 2 where the EGC is deflected to the east. The MDT is
highly correlated with the bathymetry, and gives rise to a southward flowing geostrophic
current (the EGC) which follows the shelf break. The first baroclinic Rossby radius of
deformation is a parameter that will be used in the assimilation model. It describes the
length scale where vortex stretching becomes more important than relative vorticity in the
conservation of potential vorticity. In the study area it is about 1 km with slightly larger
values along the shelf break, meaning that relative vorticity is more important here than
in the rest of the area. It is largely dependent on latitude and it increases to about 240 km
in the near-equatorial band [25].

4.2
Data Preprocessing
The input data to the BFN assimilation model is swath altimetry from SWOT and nadir
altimetry from five different missions. In addition to this the DUACS L4 product is used as
boundary conditions. Some preprocessing steps are applied to the datasets before they
are used in the assimilation model. This includes applying geophysical corrections, ap-
plying a reference surface, and masking noise and sea ice. Table 4.2 lists the geophysical
corrections that are used on each of the missions.
The heights measured by the altimeters are SSH referenced to the ellipsoid, but the quasi-
geostrophic equations used in the assimilation model requires the heights to be referenced
to a surface of constant geopotential such as the geoid. As described in section 2.3.3 the
SSH referenced to the geoid is ADT and they are found by subtracting an MSS and adding
an MDT. As seen in table 4.2 different MSS and MDT are used for SWOT and the nadir
missions. However they are all referenced to the same 20-year period. The MDT used
on SWOT is the one shown in figure 4.2b. Four atmospheric corrections are used on all
missions: a ionospheric correction, a dry troposphere correction, a wet troposphere cor-
rection, and a Dynamic Atmospheric Correction (DAC). In the case of SWOT all of these
are from models, while for nadir some of the corrections are computed from instruments
on the satellites. The ocean tide correction used is either FES2014b or FES2022b and
tide corrections for internal tide, pole tide, solid Earth tide, and loading tide are also ap-
plied. Sea State Bias (SSB) corrections are applied on the nadir observations but not on
SWOT observations. This is because the SSB correction distributed in the L2 file is only
available in ice-free areas, meaning the use of it would cause discontinuous jumps along
the ice edge.
4.2.1
Conventional Altimetry
The nadir altimetry data used in this project is DUACS level 3 products where the geophys-
ical corrections described above have already been applied and no additional preprocess-
ing steps are applied here. However, some important corrections applied by DUACS [4]
should be noted. The raw altimeter data has been subject to a quality control based on L2
quality flags, parameter thresholds, and mono- and multi-mission crossover corrections.
This step removes all data in areas with sea ice. The data has also been homogenized
with respect to a reference mission in order to reduce geographically correlated errors
stemming from e.g. instrumental errors, processing errors, or orbit residual errors.
The DUACS level 4 product is a gridded product derived by OI of the level 3 data using
an interpolation scheme based on observation errors and spatial and temporal correlation
scales [4]. The level 3 data is filtered with a 65 km low-pass filter before being interpolated
to reduce noise and to remove small-scale signals that cannot be accurately resolved by
the OI due to limited spatial and temporal sampling. This is important because it means
that scales smaller than 65 km are not captured in the DUACS maps even though they
have a resolution of 0.25 degrees (i.e. about 28 km in latitude direction). No altimeter
data is used from sea-ice covered areas and the DUACS L4 product in these areas are
derived by extrapolation.

4.3.1
Grid
Apart from the parameters discussed above a model grid must also be defined. The
study area consists of three regions, in which independent assimilation models will be run
individually. This decision was partly made to reduce computational complexity because
the area is very large and using a single large rectangular region would include a lot of area
which we are not interested in, and partly because it allows us to use a simple latitude-
longitude grid whilst maintaining a longitude grid spacing of around 2 km everywhere. If
this was done on the whole area with a longitude spacing of for example 0.1 degrees the
grid spacing would be 2.3 km at 78oN but 4.8 km at 64.5oN. A regular latitude-longitude
grid has the advantage over for example a stereographic grid that the east-west and north-
south gradients of the SSH can easily be computed. The grid spacing within each section
is chosen so it is close to 2 km which is the resolution of the SWOT data. The three
sections were created with an overlap of 1 degree as seen in figure 4.8 such that their
output can be merged by means of a weighted average in the overlap zone. The red
lines in figure 4.8a mark where the output in the overlap region are given equal weight.

4.3.2
Boundary Conditions
A 40 km buffer zone is applied around each region to be used as a transition zone for the
boundary conditions that are applied in this zone with Gaspari-Cohn weights decreasing
from 1 at the outer edge to zero at the edge of the inner zone, ensuring a smooth inte-
gration. The inner zones, the buffer zones, and the weight of the boundary conditions
are shown in figure 4.8. Normally the coastline would be treated as the outer edge of the
boundary zone and the boundary conditions would be applied with full weight here. How-
ever this would mean that at distances up to 15 km from the coast the boundary conditions
would be given at least 50% weight and this would significantly hinder the data from con-
tributing in these areas. Therefore the outer edge of the boundary zone is moved 10 km
inland such that boundary conditions only dominate (more than 50% weight) within 5 km

of the coastline (full line in figure 4.9b). A consequence of this is that some land areas
such as the peninsula at (76.5oN,18oW) and the island at (71oN,8oW) are not treated as

on for example how the currents flow around these areas such that they will still be prop-

Boundary conditions are prescribed with ADT from the DUACS L4 gridded product. In
those areas where it is not available, for instance in the land areas within 10 km of the

a shorter transition zone here while keeping the 40 km transition zone at all borders that

nudged separately i.e. with separate feedback terms and weights. In principle each of

for simplicity and because LR is very small in the area which makes the vortex stretching

4.4
Post-processing
The output of the assimilation model is gridded maps of ADT in three regions on the
regular Cartesian grids specified in table 4.4. The model covers 19 months with an output
every three hours which results in three pairs of 4,612 ADT fields. Post-processing is now
applied in the following order:
1. Outputs in the buffer zones (gray areas in figure 4.8), land areas, and areas with ice
concentration > 75%, are replaced with nan values.
2. The zonal- and meridional geostrophic current components are computed from the
ADT fields using equation 2.15 and 2.16, and relative vorticity is computed from
these using equation 2.7. These computations are performed before re-gridding the
data, because the zonal- and meridional gradients are easily computed on regular
Cartesian grids.
3. A stereographic grid centered on longitude 24oE and latitude 72oN with a resolution
of 2 km by 2 km is set up, and the ADT, the geostrophic current components, and
the relative vorticity fields are re-projected onto this grid using bilinear interpolation.
4. The output of the three sections are now on the same grid and can be merged.
Within the overlap zones (see figure 4.8a) the merged products are computed as
weighted averages of the outputs in the two regions. They are given equal weight
along the red line in figure 4.8a. 55 km (corresponding to 0.5 degrees latitude) from
the red line, they are either given full weight (if located inside a region) or given zero
weight (if located on the edge of a region).

5.3
Correlation with SST
It is expected that mesoscale and submesoscale features such as eddies and fronts are
reflected in both the ADT field and the SST field, especially in the Greenland sea where
cold Polar Water and warm Atlantic Water meet. SST and ADT are not directly related, but
a relationship exists because both are related to density. Their correlation are strongest
in winter when the ocean is stratified into homogeneous layers, and in highly turbulent
regions [29].
Here we perform a correlation analysis between the QG-model ADT output and SST data
to validate if small-scale dynamical structures seen in the SST are resolved in the QG-
model. For comparison, correlations are also carried out between DUACS and SST to
quantify the improvement. The SST data is only available in cloud-free conditions and
very little data is available in the winter months. The correlation analysis is performed in
the period from August 2023 to the end of October 2024 such that only the QG-model
outputs based on SWOT science data is used. Pixels with sea ice concentration larger
than 5% are removed from the datasets and SST data with a quality level [27] less than 3
(out of 5) are also discarded. Prior to the analysis, the QG-model and DUACS fields are
re-projected to the SST grid which is a 5 km stereographic grid. The three datasets are
then filtered with a 75 km high-pass filter to isolate the small mesoscale and submesoscale
signals. The filtering is done with a Hanning filter. In the filtering process all data within
a 37.5 km band around the edges of the datasets are discarded which also means that
the QG-model is not validated near the coast or ice-edge, and that the amount of data
decreases significantly. Figure 5.4 shows an example of the data at one time step (28-08-
2024 00:00). Here a, b, and c are SST, QG-model ADT, and DUACS ADT respectively,
after they have been re-gridded. Only pixels with valid data in all three datasets are shown.
d, e, and f show the data after it has been filtered. In this example the front associated
with the EGC can be seen clearly in all three datasets north of latitude 71oN and from the
filtered versions it is evident that the shape and location is captured better in the QG-model
than in DUACS.

Finite Size Lyapunov Exponents
An FSLE analysis is performed on the QG-model using one month of data of the velocity
field from the month of August 2024. The FSLE distributions are computed by Michael
Hart-Davis from Deutsches Geodätisches Forschungsinstitut at the Techinal University of
Munich. The velocity fields are re-projected to a regular Cartesian grid with grid spacing
of 0.4 degrees in longitude and latitude direction and the FSLE components are computed
at this grid spacing. The tracers are simulated five days forward in time at each time step
(3 hours) and the FSLE is therefore only computed in the first 26 days of the month.
Figure 5.9 shows the velocity field from the QG-model (a) and a snapshot of the FSLE
distribution (b) from the 7th of August 15:00. High FSLE values ∼0.7 represent large hor-
izontal stretching and divergence of the flow. In the figure these are found at the edges
of eddies, such as the eddies at the outlet of Scoresbysund or the large 100 km eddy at
(15oW;72.5oN), and along high-velocity parts of the EGC, for example at (25oW;67oN).
Most other parts of the EGC are associated with medium FSLE values ∼0.5 indicating
more stable flow as would be expected of near-geostrophic motion. Even more stable
flow is observed in the North Atlantic water flowing north of Iceland which is seen as a
weak current with speed of ∼0.2 m/s in figure 5.9a but barely distinguishable in the FSLE
distribution (5.9b). The lowest FSLE values are found in the center of eddies because
the eddy boundaries contain the flow and makes it unable to reach the final separation
distance. The FSLE analysis enhances our ability to detect mesoscale and even subme-
soscale eddies that are difficult to pick up from the velocity field alone such as the 20 km
eddy at (10oE;74oN).

Discussion
In this project it has been shown how SWOT data can be interpolated in space and time by
including quasi-geostrophic model dynamics. The model is relatively simple; it considers
just 1.5 layers and relies only on the Rossby radius of deformation, boundary conditions,
and altimetry observations. Moreover, the assimilation technique employed (back and
forth nudging) is both conceptually and computationally simple [8]. Despite this, the results
presented in the previous chapter appear very promising. Here the results of the QG-
model in the form of ADT, geostrophic currents, and vorticity have been compared to the
gridded DUACS product, and there is a substantial improvement in spatial resolution i.e.
a factor of 13 in the latitude direction and a factor of about 4 in the longitude direction.
The temporal resolution is also increased, from 24 hours to 3 hours. Not only is the
resolution increased, but it has also been shown how the QG-model is able to accurately
resolve small mesoscale signals seen in SST and chlorophyll-a data such as eddies with
diameters of 20-50 km that are not possible to resolve with DUACS. It has also been
shown how the shape and location of ocean fronts seen in SST data are very precisely
captured in the QG-model. The increase in the level of detail means that relative vorticity
can now be derived and exploited. In figure 5.7h and 5.8h it is seen that relative vorticity
derived from DUACS is not a very meaningful parameter because the gradients in ADT
are very weak.
6.1
Calibration Phase
The assimilation model has been run on the period from April 2023 to October 2024 and
SWOT data from both the calibration phase and the science phase have been assimilated.
It could be argued that there is no real benefit to be gained from performing the assimi-
lation in the calibration phase, since the data already has adequate temporal resolution
to resolve small scale ocean dynamics [1]. Here it has been done to merge it with nadir
altimetry and obtain a map covering the whole area which represents the best available
information at each location. However, it is not an ideal map, and the large differences in
level of detail can be misleading. This is why the QG-model output from this period has
not been used in the validation and can be regarded as a spin-up period. Besides that,
a snapshot from this period was presented in section 5.2 to illustrate how DOI methods
can be used to utilize more of the information in the nadir altimetry data than what can be
achieved with the statistical OI used in DUACS.
6.2
SWOT Correlated Errors
Assimilating SWOT data into continuous maps requires careful preprocessing of the data
because of the correlated errors in SWOT, the presence of sea ice, and the presence of
noise. In figure 4.6a and 4.7a it was shown how the correlated errors cause an offset in
ADT between neighboring SWOT tracks of up to 0.1 m. If this effect were not corrected
for it would cause large gradients in the QG-model output along the boundaries of tracks
creating artifacts in the velocity field. In this project the problem was tackled by fixing
the long-wavelength signal in SWOT to DUACS which turned out to be effective and no
unwanted effects of this method have been noticed in the QG-model output. Nonethe-
less, in the future we may consider other methods for homogenizing the data to avoid
dependence on DUACS. An entirely different way to deal with SWOT correlated errors
in QG-model assimilation proposed by Guillou [8], is to limit nudging of potential vorticity

6.3
Sea Ice Removal
Another important step applied in the data preprocessing is the removal of sea ice. Sea
ice is a complication in the study area because it covers the Greenland shelf about 70%
of the year as described in section 4.1.2. A large part of the ice (about 30% in summer)
is marginal sea ice which is in free drift and has large leads where the ADT signals can
be picked up. It was therefore decided to utilize the SWOT data in these locations using
a very simple ice masking process. The examples in A.3 give an indication on the ef-
fectiveness of the sea ice removal in varying sea ice conditions. The sea ice appears to
have been removed successfully apart from one example (figure A.6) where the sea ice
edge has remained in the data. It is problematic if signals of sea ice find their way into
the assimilation model where they are interpreted as ADT: Small ice floes for instance,
have the same signature as anticyclonic eddies and the sea ice edge has the same signa-
ture as a large geostrophic current and could be misinterpreted as the EGC. We currently
have no way to validate the QG-model output in the marginal ice zone other than to look
at the input data. It is therefore possible that the output in these zones (for example the
snapshots shown in figure 5.1 and figure 5.3), although they may appear realistic, are con-
taminated with false eddies and currents. The assimilation model would benefit greatly
from more sophisticated ice masking techniques as well as a course of action to validate
the QG-model output in the marginal ice zone.
6.4
Assimilation Method Improvements
The assimilation method could be improved in various ways. It may benefit from an in-
crease in temporal resolution from 3 hours to for example 1 hour, and it would be desirable
to setup the grid in a different way such that a single model could cover the whole area.
This could be achieved by shifting from a Cartesian grid to a stereographic grid which could
achieve constant resolution in both directions. There is also a need to setup the boundary
conditions better around the coastline. The current setup appears to serve its purpose of
restricting the influence of boundary conditions near the coast as seen in figure 5.8 where
the QG-model produced an output that conformed well with SST and chlorophyll-a data
within 10 km from the coast. Some unwanted artifacts can however also be seen in this
example. In the future, the boundary condition setup should be altered such that no land
areas are treated as open ocean by the model. Additional experiments could be carried
out regarding the nudging parameters to fine tune their values. There are some signs that
SWOT is nudged too much in the output, such as false gradients at swath edges (best
seen in the videos), and it should also be investigated if nadir data should be given more
weight. Considering the small-scale features seen in the SST data and the correlation
between SST and ADT it could be an option to include the SST data in the assimilation
model. This is relatively straightforward to do, since SST can also be converted to poten-
tial vorticity in a quasi-geostrophic framework [1][31].

6.5
Model Validation
The QG-model output has been quantitatively validated with SST data via a correlation
analysis and qualitatively validated by visual comparison with SST data and chlorophyll-a
data in small regions. Overall, the validation has shown a significant improvement in the
QG-model’s ability to resolve fine-scale dynamics compared to DUACS. In the correlation
analysis, the data was high-pass filtered before computing correlations such that it could
be evaluated if the QG-model detects the same small scale signals as the SST. If this had
not been done the long-wavelength part of the signals would dominate the correlation and
the evaluation would have been useless since the long-wavelength signal in the QG-model
and DUACS is the same. From the correlation results it was seen that the QG-model
exhibited moderate negative correlation (-0.1 to -0.4) with SST in the areas along the shelf
break and moderate positive correlation (0.1 to 0.4) in the East Icelandic Current and just
south of Scoresbysund. In all other areas such as in the basins or in the area below latitude
66oN the correlation is very low (<0.1). There is a clear connection between the correlation
values and the activity of the flow (seen for example in the mean FSLE score, figure 5.10)
and it is suspected that the correlation is low in these less active areas because both the
ADT and SST signals are weak here. Correlation between SST and DUACS exhibits the
same spatial patterns but with weaker magnitudes. Mean absolute correlations of 0.129
was achieved for the QG-model and 0.096 for DUACS. Overall the absolute correlation
values are low i.e. rarely above 0.5. However, in the qualitative examples (figure 5.7 and
5.8), where excellent visual agreement was seen, the correlation values are also quite low
(-0.373 and 0.110) and goes to show that moderate correlation values can indicate good
alignment. In the future, other datasets than SST and chlorophyll-a should be considered
for validation of the QG-model, such as high resolution ocean models or in-situ drifter
data.

7
Conclusion
The SWOT satellite has unveiled details about finescale ocean features that have never
been observed by conventional altimetry. In this project we have used this new data to
map mesoscale and submesoscale dynamics taking place at or around the East Green-
land Shelf. SWOT observations have been used in combination with conventional al-
timetry data to compute gridded maps of ADT, surface geostrophic currents, and relative
vorticity at a 3 hour temporal resolution over a 19 month period.
Finescale structures in the ocean typically evolve and disappear within days, but at most
locations in the study area there are at least 10 days between subsequent SWOT ob-
servations, which means that they are very difficult to map using traditional space-time
interpolation methods. For that reason the altimetry data has been assimilated into a sim-
ple 1.5 layer quasi-geostrophic model based on conservation of potential vorticity which
is able to propagate the ADT field dynamically over temporal gaps.
The results of the QG-model look promising: many small-scale eddies and currents can
be seen in the velocity and vorticity fields, and known currents such as the EGC, the Jan
Mayen Current, and the East Icelandic Current are resolved with much greater detail than
in DUACS maps. Expected seasonal effects in eddy scale and vertical velocity are also
captured by the QG-model. Horizontal stretching and mixing processes in the area have
been analyzed using FSLE. The FSLE scores indicated high mixing activity on the shelf
to the west of the EGC and more stable flow to the East of it. Additionally, it highlighted
the location and shape of mesoscale and submesoscale eddies in the dataset.
A correlation analysis, correlating high-pass filtered versions of ADT field and SST data,
have been used to validate the QG-model results. The same analysis was performed on
DUACS maps to quantify the improvement in mapping of finescale features. Along the
shelf break and in other active areas the absolute correlation has increased from ∼0.1
to ∼0.3. Furthermore, the results have been validated qualitatively in two small regions
known to be associated with eddy formation. The QG-model results display excellent
agreement with SST and chlorophyll-a data. The QG-model can accurately map small 20
km eddies that are not seen in DUACS and it can resolve the shape of temperature fronts
with great detail.
In this project we have shown how SWOT observations can be combined with quasi-
geostrophic model dynamics to produce high-resolution maps of the SSH field which are
able to resolve finescale structures seen in SST and chlorophyll-a data.
