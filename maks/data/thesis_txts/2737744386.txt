Foreword
This master thesis is conceived as a preliminary study focused exclusively on the
exploration of minimal structures within the realm of civil engineering. The aim of this
investigation is to analyze the potential of minimal structures, without yet addressing
the numerous of other crucial aspects typically involved in the comprehensive
engineering of bridge design.
By intentionally narrowing the scope of this research, this thesis aims to pull in one
direction, examining the efficiency, sustainability, and innovative possibilities inherent in
such designs. The approach taken here is deliberate: I hope that this concentrated will
spark interest and provoke further inquiry, by others or myself, to continue investigating
the yet unexplored aspects of minimal structures, ultimately contributing to the
advancement of sustainable and innovative engineering solutions.

Abstract
This master thesis investigates the challenges and potential of incorporating minimal
structural designs in bridge construction to enhance sustainability. By focusing on min-
imal structures, the study explores the efficiency and innovative possibilities of such
designs in civil engineering, specifically in the context of medium-span bridges (50 to 250
meters). The thesis provides a historical background on material economy, tracing its
roots from Renaissance principles to modern advancements in topology optimization and
Michell structures. It assesses the practicalities of applying minimal structures, including
design complexity, construction feasibility, and sustainability considerations.
The research is divided into several sections: theory and definitions of minimal structures,
technicalities of bridge loads, case studies, and practical implementations.
The case
study involves the Mumbai Trans Harbour Link Bridge, analyzing it as a benchmark
for comparison.
The study applies three specific load cases based on European and
Indian standards to evaluate the structural integrity and material efficiency of minimal
structure-inspired designs. Key findings suggest that while minimal structures can offer
significant material savings and sustainability benefits, practical challenges such as spatial
constraints, robustness, and construction logistics must be addressed. Furthermore, the
thesis emphasizes that the case study only demonstrates a few examples and that in
reality each practical case is different, thus giving rise to numerous possible ways of
implementing minimal structures.
The thesis concludes by emphasizing the need for further research to optimize minimal
structures for real-world applications, promoting sustainable and innovative engineering
solutions in bridge construction.

Introduction: Historical Background & Relevance
of Material Economy
Structural engineering has long been influenced by the principle of material economy.
This concept emphasizes the use of the least amount of material necessary to create
structures that resist loads effectively. This influence is based on several different fac-
tors, perhaps the dominating one being material cost. Attempting to build with as little
material as allowable, thereby maximizing the profits (or minimizing the costs) of a con-
struction, generates an incentive for investors to contribute to the frequent development
of our societies. The second largest aspect that influences the material economy is re-
source production and allocation. Today, in an era where the environmental impact of
human activities is scrutinized more than ever, the construction industry, a significant
contributor to global resource consumption and waste production, is under increasing
pressure to adopt more sustainable practices. By prioritizing material economy, the sec-
tor has the potential to significantly reduce its ecological footprint, thereby contributing
to the conservation of natural resources and the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions
associated with the production, transportation, and processing of building materials.
Historical examinations show that ideas of material economy date back to the Renais-
sance, specifically to Galileo Galilei’s (1564-1642) studies on beam design. Galilei’s work
suggested that the cross-sectional height of a beam should be greatest where bending mo-
ments are highest, in his case of a three-point flexural test, at mid-span, and less where
stresses are lower, near the supports [18].
Figure 1: Galileo Galilei [10]
Many other researchers have delved upon
the question of topology optimization and
on trying to find geometrical solutions that
require the least amount of material.
The most significant advancement in the
field came with the work of Albert George
Moulton Michell in 1904. Michell, an Aus-
tralian engineer, extended Maxwell’s [12]
ideas into what is now known as the the-
ory of Michell structures or trusses. In his
landmark paper, ”The Limits of Economy
of Material in Frame-Structures,” Michell provided a mathematical formulation for de-
signing structures that use the least amount of material possible while still fulfilling
load-bearing requirements. W.S Hemp, Erik Reitzel and W. Johnson, refined these ideas

when they showed how fracture lines caused by overloading of materials have a relation-
ship to the path of forces running through the structural system. Through a specific
analogy, they established how information about optimal force paths can be obtained,
since these paths directly indicate the shapes of the corresponding structures with the
lowest material consumption [20]. These findings is what Reitzel, in both his articles and
his book, refers to as ”minimal structures” or ”minimal systems”.
This thesis is basing its itself on applying these long-standing principles within the context
of modern bridge construction. This blend of past and present will hopefully contribute
to the development of structures that are both efficient and sustainable. The ongoing task
for engineers is to respect historical knowledge while leveraging modern advancements,
allowing the field of structural design to progress responsibly.
Figure 2: Circular construction process [21]
The principle of material economy in struc-
tural engineering is more than an exercise
in budget-consciousness; it now becoming
a critical component in the sustainable de-
sign of our built environment. At its core,
it advocates for the minimal use of building
materials to achieve structural sufficiency,
which directly contributes to sustainabil-
ity by conserving resources and reducing
waste.
With the construction sector re-
sponsible for a significant portion of global
resource consumption and waste produc-
tion, optimizing structures to use no more than the necessary amount of material, re-
duces the depletion of natural resources and lowers the carbon footprint associated with
material processing and transportation. According to DTU’s sustainability departments
website, ”Construction and production of building materials are estimated to account for
5-12% of the world’s carbon emissions. In the EU, construction waste makes up 35% of
the total quantity of waste” [21].
To add to that, Nik.D. Oikonomou in the 2005 article ”Recycled concrete aggregates”
published in ”Cement and Concrete Composites”, states that 50% of the raw materials
from nature are used in the construction section, and that the amount of construction
rubble and demolition waste produced annually correspond to 500kg for each citizen living
in the European Union [15]. This means that any significant reduction in material use
will spare several thousand, if not million tons, of waste material that also needs to be
managed, perhaps through the use of other non-renewable resources.

Moreover, designing structures to use materials more efficiently can lead to the creation
of buildings and infrastructure that are not only less resource-intensive but also poten-
tially more elegant and functionally versatile. By challenging architects and engineers to
consider the optimal use of materials, the industry can push the boundaries of design,
creating spaces that are both innovative and in harmony with their natural surroundings.
Ian Firth, a former director for the engineering group COWI and President of the Insti-
tution of Structural Engineers talked in his TED talk, published 27.06.2018, about the
importance of bridges being functional, safe, serviceable, but also beautiful. He goes on
to say;
”If we go on building mediocre, ugly environments, it’s something like a large
scale vandalism, which is completely unacceptable” [7].
Since numerous of the common features of minimal structures are spotted elsewhere in
nature, it can be argued that the shapes created also provide an aesthetic that could
serve as more pleasing than some designs, primarily focused on practicality.

Aim, scope and research questions
The aim of this thesis is to explore the viability and impact of incorporating minimal
structural designs in bridge construction. It seeks to critically analyze how minimalist
design principles can be applied within the field of bridge engineering to not only reduce
material usage but also to maintain, or potentially enhance, structural integrity and sus-
tainability. The investigation focuses on the practicalities of such design approaches, in-
cluding the challenges and opportunities they present within current engineering practices
and standards. Through this exploration, the thesis aims to contribute to the broader
discourse on sustainable construction practices by providing insights into how minimal-
ism can be integrated into bridge design and construction, thus promoting efficiency and
sustainability.
The scope of the thesis is focused on exploring the feasibility and practicality of im-
plementing minimal structural designs specifically for what are known as medium-span
bridge structures, which essentially means spans of 50 to 250 meters. To create a basis
of comparison with the Mumbai Trans Harbour Link Bridge, the spans created in the
case study will be limited to 50 meters but are in theory applicable for longer spans. The
focus lies on the theoretical and practical application of minimal structural designs, eval-
uating their effectiveness in achieving structural efficiency and sustainability. However,
the scope explicitly excludes the mathematical optimization of these structures, mean-
ing that while conceptual design and practical implementation considerations are within
the scope, detailed mathematical algorithms or optimization processes for determining
the optimal design parameters fall outside the realm of investigation. It should in addi-
tion be noted that calculations of structural members, verifications, as well as non-traffic
loads from Danish road directory’s ”VEJLEDNING TIL BELASTNINGS- OG BEREG-
NINGSGRUNDLAG” and consequently ”EN 1991-2, Eurocode 1: Actions on structures
– Part 2: Traffic loads on bridges”, will be neglected to create a realistic scope and a
more specific discussion.

This thesis aspires to solve its aim through answering three research questions.
Research Question 1:
How can minimal structural design principles be adapted for the engineering of
bridges with spans of 50 meters to reduce material volume, considering real-world
constraints?
Research Question 2:
What are the potential benefits and limitations of using minimal structural designs
for bridges?
Research Question 3:
In what ways can minimal structures enhance structural efficiency and sustainability,
and how can the construction industry adapt its practices to incorporate minimal

2
Theory: What Signifies a Minimal Structure?
2.1
Minimal Structures
As a general approach, minimal structures are characterized by their efficiency in material
usage, adhering to principles of plastic optimum design. The definition and exploration
of the different geometries involve understanding the relationship between the tension
and compression stress limits and do not depend on other material properties. It also
involves treating the structure as a whole, contrasting traditional design, where only some
dimensions or components are taken into account [11]. This foundational concept enables
the exploration of various structural configurations that achieve optimal performance with
minimal material use.
A minimal structure is categorized by a few specific properties. To gain a deeper under-
standing of the principles discovered by previous researches, an illustration presenting six
different beam-like structures is presented in Figure 3. All of these six frameworks are
comprised of different profiles providing an equilibrated structure, supporting mid-span
load (in orange) using one support at each end of the structure.
The theoretical structures in Figure 3, are taken from ”Fra brud til form” [19], and
is a representation of how a structure serving the same function, can show substantial
variation in its weight, based on its structural form. The example is calculated using
normal steel (weight= 7850kg/m3) with a mid-span load of P = 100kN and supports
that are loaded with P/2. The spans are 5 meters long and it is assumed that there is
no buckling in the compression members. Table 1, shows that from a solid beam cross
section (a) to the lattice construction (f) there is approximately 95% reduction in weight,
exclusively as a result of the change in structural form.
Thought experiments like this one paved the way for comprehending which features cre-
ate structures with less material and which features are crucial to avoid if the principal
goal of the structure is material economy. Through a variety of studies, using differ-
ent mathematical methods, researchers demonstrated that for any given set of forces in
equilibrium, there exists an optimal layout of members (beams and columns) that can
support these forces with the minimum volume of material [11].

The lattice girder that uses the theoretically least amount of material for the above load
and constraint configuration is illustrated in Figure 4. The layout is characterized by

the rods in a force-transmitting lattice are to form angles with each other, which are
specifically different from 90 degrees. In addition to the members being perpendicular
while the girder is unloaded, the form of a minimal structure should be such that all
compression and tension rods remain perpendicular while the load is applied [19].
2.1.1
Michell Structures
This seemingly surprising result that tension and compression rods in minimal construc-
tions are perpendicular to each other, was already indicated in a fundamental work carried
out by A.G.M. Michell as early as in 1904 [13]. The issue originally framed for trusses was
reformulated into an optimization problem for continuum bodies. This transition to a
8
==PAGE:15==
Figure 4: Simple Minimal structure, weight = 43kg [19]
continuum framework emerges from the phenomenon in which the total volume decreases
as the number of potential truss members increases. This ongoing process does not seem
to have an end, thereby leading to optimal structures manifesting as continuum bodies
with a fibrous micro-structure or a field of infinitely small members. In simpler terms, as
the number of potential members in a truss increases, it enables the formation of truss
members to be positioned in a more efficient layout, which contributes to a reduction in
the overall volume [11]. This discovery led these specific structural arrangements to be
named ”Michell structures”.
The majority of structures depicted in this thesis are based upon arrangements that
have practical applicability and therefore consist of a finite number of members. In this
section, these structures are drawn to show the configuration of such structures using
an arbitrary, yet practically realistic, number of members.
This is only to say that
mentions of ”minimal structures” refer to practical structures as drawn in this theory,
while ”Michell structures” technically are the idealized structures with significantly more,
or an infinite number of members.

2.1.5
Challenges of Sustainability
The definition of sustainability holds numerous aspects and is a concept that is too broad
for consideration in its entirety. Instead, the discussions in this thesis will be based on the
factors of sustainability that are in direct relation to the construction forms, construction
method, and material consumption.
Since the scope of this thesis is narrowly defined, the focus from a sustainability point of
view is limited to the qualities implementation of minimal structures has on the construc-
tion practice. This implies that the topics of discussion will include the manufacturing
of material, on-site processes, operational life, resource and material consumption, and
other environmental impacts that directly relate to the structure.
The essence of sustainability in construction lies in minimizing environmental impact
while enhancing efficiency and longevity in building projects.
Adopting material economy in structural design also encourages the use of innovative
materials and construction techniques. Engineers are increasingly turning to recycled ma-
terials, renewable resources, and advanced composites that provide the required strength
and durability with less material volume compared to traditional options.
From a civil engineering perspective, the only part of the total CO2-equivalent gas emis-
sions of the materials that can be controlled is in the choice of product, the choice of
transport and the choice of erection method. Fortunately, a standard practice for pro-
ducers is to declare an environmental product declaration that contains information on
greenhouse gas emissions for the various stages in the production of the product from the
production of raw materials, transportation of raw materials, production of the product,
and transportation of the finished product. This makes the choice of product easier and
helps in choosing environmentally friendly products.

According to the Eurocode, bridges are classified into four bridge groups. These four
bridge groups consist of bridges for private use, pedestrian bridges, light-trafficked bridges,
and normal public road bridges. All bridges in this thesis will fall into bridge group 1
entailing the following:
Group I: This group includes all bridges that are expected to be able to transfer heavy
dense traffic within the framework of the traffic laws throughout their lifetime, including
the transfer of heavy special transports following specific guidelines [3].
This is to make the design relevant to the most commonly designed bridges and to fall
within the category of medium-span bridges.
Now, in compliance with the European standard, the traffic loads relevant to review
are presented as 4 load models (LM). Each LM is designed to be applied to a notional
lane on the bridge.
A notional lane is a 3 meter wide independent path that runs
along the longitudinal length of the bridge for which these load models are to be placed
in.
The notional lanes are independent of the width of the driving lanes, and only
work as a domain for the load models to be placed in. Should there be a remaining
area of the carriageway after determining the number of notional lanes, an additional

Now that the three load cases have been defined and an understanding of the load of

economic structures. This study will revolve around investigating how minimal structure
design principles can be adapted for the engineering of bridges with spans of around 50
meters, considering real world constraints, inherently answering to research question 1.
To provide a clearer and more relevant picture on how minimal structure-inspired bridge
structures could look, it is appropriate to do a comparison between how bridges of decided
span length are built today and how they could be built if solely material economy were
prioritized.
The case study will consist of a three base structure examining;
a) Mumbai Trans Harbour Link Bridge (MTHL) (box girder), designed by COWI
b) Idealized minimal bridge systems only considering material volume
c) Hybrid version between the two, where an optimized shape is created, taking build-
ability, and practicality into account.

of sustainability, as well as the most important overlooked parameters and other factors
that need to be considered before being able to provide a conclusion.

4.1.1
Optimization
Although the task of this study is to examine the material optimization of minimal
inspired structures, looking at some of the optimizations made for beam bridge construc-
tions provides an idea of where the emphasis on development has been in the previous
years.
The biggest material optimization factor for the beam bridge is the box girder profile,
which is specifically designed to distribute the loads down into the web. This reduces the
thickness of the slab and the self-weight of the bridge. This layout also allows the bridge
to have longer spans, reducing the number of necessary columns.
Despite the fact that the box girder has not gone through dramatic changes in the last
70 years, it is worth noting another specific detail about the MTHL. As seen in Figure
21, the profile has an inner lining on the inside of the webs and the bottom slab. This is
an attachment point for the prestressing rods. The configuration of the rods, in contrast
to solid concrete girders, moves from top to bottom and vice versa in the continuous
beam depending on where along the box girder the bending stresses are positive and
negative. Figure 22 shows a sketch based on COWI’s detail drawings. The figure makes
it clear how the post-tensioning rods are placed to hold up for the maximum negative
bending moment that occurs at the supports. In a way, the reinforcement follows the
bending moment diagram, utilizing one of the principles in the fracture line analogy of,
strengthening the structure using material where the greatest forces arise. More detailed
drawings from COWI can be found in Appendix B.

4.2
Minimal Structure-Inspired Bridge
To see what an idealized minimal bridge construction should be based on, we need draw
inspiration from the different designs introduced in the theory. However, because all de-
signs are set up based on one specific load or load combination, we need to find the mini-
mal structure and combination of structures that together proves to be able to withstand
the defined loads. In essence, which structural combinations would have the capacity to
endure the specified loads and how could this ideal structure be envisioned?
It is essential to understand that for a structure to be perfectly minimal, the construc-
tion should incorporate an infinite number of infinitesimally small members. Since that
obviously cannot be the case for a real structure and the objective of this section is to
explore potential designs for minimal structures if they were realistically proposed for
bridge construction, a simplification of the otherwise extremely complex structure takes
place ahead of this study. According to the interactive Web application ”LayOpt”, the
removal of members from Michell structures may have structural volumes only a few per-
cent above the true optimum solution [5]. So, for a bridge of our size, it seems reasonable
to create a structure where there are seven nodes along the bridge deck, including the
two supports. This will serve as the basis for the development of the structures in this
study.
4.2.1
Verification
To verify that the topology of a truss is optimized, the previously mentioned Web appli-
cation called ”LayOpt”, is used. LayOpt allows users to rapidly identify the minimum
volume layout (or ’topology’) of a 2D truss, based on loads, supports, a domain, and
a tension/compression stress ratio. For more information on LayOpt, see Appendix B,
or visit the website at https://www.layopt.com/truss/. To give a brief introduction,
”LayOpt” describes itself, per the website’s information page, with the following:
”LayOpt makes use of mathematical optimization methods to obtain a mini-
mum volume truss structure for a given problem. This section provides a brief
introduction of the theory of absolute minimum material design and more in-
formation about the methods used in the web app.
The theory behind absolute minimum volume structures has received academic
interest for over a century. A seminal paper in the field was published by
Michell (1904), leading to the emergence of so-called Michell structures. The
rules governing such structures are seemingly simple, yet the resulting struc-

4.3.1
Adding spatial constraints
Even though all combined idealized minimal structures would create strange, yet aston-
ishing structures, they require a large degree of freedom when it comes to leveraging
space and yet a small degree of error when it comes to manufacturing and assembling
of joints. So, which part of the previous section can be taken into consideration when
attempting to create a minimal structure-inspired bridge that will stand when removing
some of these liberties?
Initially, the most important aspect to review is the utilization of the space below the
bridge deck. Most of the area below the bridge, which should remain unoccupied for the
possibility of other forms of transport, should serve as a spatial boundary. In this case,
the entire area will be removed for use. The image below, Figure 33, shows a minimal
structure for a UDL and its gradual transformation as more and more of the space below
the deck is removed, starting from the bottom and moving up. Notice how Figure 33 (d),
which does not utilize the area below the bridge deck, stands taller than sub-figure (a).
In pure theoretical terms, by not allowing the space below the bridge to be utilized, the
arches rise from two parabolas to one semicircle. However, continuing to maintain the
notion of a 7-nodal point setup on the bridge deck, the construction turns out as seen
in Figure 33. It is worth noting that changing the roller support into a pinned support
returns the arch back into a parabola shape. This is due to the static horizontally directed
forces that can be obtained by the pinned support versus the roller, which can only react
to vertical loads.
Now that we have looked at the direct implementation of minimal structures to bridge
constructions, it is important to take a step back and review which parts of these struc-
tures can realistically be applied. Since we are basically attempting to create a structure
based on a UDL and point loads that in theory will have a variable placement on the
bridge, taking directly after the minimal structures defeats the purpose of implementing
a minimal layout, as a minimal structure is specifically designed for one load case. Com-
bining these structures as in the section above, provides no assurance that the interaction
between these different structures will be able to uphold the variable nature of vertical
point loads. We are consequently forced to strengthen the structure in another way.
One approach is to strengthen the initial members by applying additional tension and
compression members, emulating a traditional truss system, not unlike in Figure 3 (f).

Shape Optimization Tool (SOT)
Let us look at how we can take this a step further using the shape optimization tool.
Shape optimization (SOT) in Fusion 360 focuses on enhancing the stiffness and strength
of a design while minimizing the use of material. By defining constraints and loading
conditions, shape optimization algorithms remove unnecessary material, resulting in an
efficient structure.
For bridge-truss optimization, this means that the software helps
create a truss design that can support the required loads with the least amount of material,
reducing weight and cost without compromising strength.
However, the results of a shape optimization study do not give any indication of the
stresses caused by the loads. Therefore, it is crucial to ensure that the new design can,
in fact, withstand operational loads by conducting a static stress study or another stress-
related analysis [1]. That being said, the SOT still provides an idea of which members
are redundant or ”more redundant” than others.
The result of the shape optimization tool is shown in Figure 39. The SOT considers
the arch especially the member adjacent to the supports, as the most important not to
remove. The four most centrally place cross-members (stiffening members) show prac-

cross-members show more importance. Although the arch and the girder are naturally
the most important, the SOT provides an overview over the grading of importance of
each part of the structure. It is important to be aware of the fact that a real bridge will

called ”Generative Design” (GD). GD works by leveraging cloud computing and AI. It
involves setting up the design problem by specifying constraints, loads, and materials.

This approach is particularly advantageous for bridge-truss optimization as it can produce
innovative truss configurations that might not be intuitive to human designers. GD can be
optimized for various objectives, such as weight reduction, material efficiency, or specific
performance criteria.
What is significantly better about GD, compared to designing only using the SOT, is
that the designer can provide an input structure that the generative design is based on.
This way, the concepts of minimal structures can be preserved in the design approach.
Generative design, can also create truss configurations that are optimized for load-bearing
performance, material use, and manufacturability.

5
Discussion: Material Economy & Topology Opti-
mization in Holistic Bridge Design
This chapter discusses material economy and topology optimization within the context of
holistic bridge design, moving from theoretical applications to practical implementations.
Since there are numerous aspects of bridge design that in principle need to be considered
in the context of construction practicality and sustainability, this section of the thesis
will strictly be associated with buildability and material consumption.
This discussion will be two-parted, reflecting on the practical and sustainable approaches
as well as potential benefits and limitations of using minimal structural designs in terms
of efficiency and sustainability. The first part of the discussion will deal with design
complexities and practical challenges, answering to research question 2, and the second
part will reflect on the environmental impacts focusing on material efficiency and longevity
of minimal structural design, answering to research question 3.
5.1
Practical Challenges of Implementing Minimal Structure
Bridge Constructions
5.1.1
Design complexity
As discussed in the theory, for a Michell structure to be perfectly minimal, an infinite
amount of supporting members (non-arch members) need to be designed in such a way
that they are simultaneously stressed to the full utilization of the material. For sim-
plicity of design, the case study in the previous section was based on 7 nodes chosen
as connection points for the vertical members. This was determined on account of the
Pedersen structure and the semi-idealized structure from Reitzel, which are comprised of
the same 7-nodal configuration. In practise, there is nothing that prevents there being a
greater number of members, in theory, creating a bridge that is more closely related to a
Michell structure. There are several examples of cable-stayed bridges that deploy a simi-
lar type of construction technique, only using a pylon or a tower as a tensioning-support
for the cables. This suggests that using minimal structures as inspiration for the design
of medium-span bridges is definitely achievable. Building on this, let us look at another
example of how to use the concept of minimal structure in pylon-based bridge design.
In the 2018 article published by Fairclough et al. on theoretically optimal bridge forms
under gravity loading, a five-part figure demonstrating a 30-point node configuration of

Although the design itself of such structures does not pose a threat to its possible rise,
a couple of other challenges make it more difficult to keep the material volume to a
minimum. The first is the height of the bridge. Figure 45 shows a graph over span-to-
volume ratio of different cable-based bridges of different heights. Moving from a-h, the
figure shows a considerable drop in volume. Here, (a) represents a suspension bridge
with span-to-dip ratio of 9, and (c) a suspension bridge with optimal pylon height with
a span-to-dip ratio of 4.0. From lines (a) and (c) it can be seen how much the volume
decreases with increasing height of the bridges. For spans relevant to the case study
sizes, the difference is much less, yet still enough to provide a consequential difference
in volume. The flip side of building a higher bridge is that it is not only more difficult
to construct, it might require more material than initially foreseen as wind loads pose a
significantly larger threat to taller constructions. Furthermore, height can influence the
overall stability of the structure, requiring more robust foundations and support systems,
which can substantially increase the complexity, material consumption, and cost of the
project. Very tall bridges may also pose a threat to transport of pre-fabricated parts.

or height-to-length ratios are quite small, meaning a 50 meter span bridge based upon a
minimal structure will be quite high-reaching. Looking at a triple-split pylon structure,

5.2
Sustainability Considerations
What this thesis ultimately is attempting to achieve is to inspire a way of designing that
reduces the necessary volume of material needed to construct a bridge.
The reduction of volume and consequently the weight of the main body-structure has a
compounding effect. A less heavy weight of the construction calls for fewer heavy lifts on
the construction site, weaker, and thereby less extensive foundations, which again saves
transport resources and the resources needed for creating the means of transport, etc. It
is the sum of the cuts in all the different posts of the construction process that ideally
generates the visible impact on the industry’s pollution problems.
To get an idea of which measures the industry is attempting to fully implement to mit-
igate the environmental impact of construction, from a consulting perspective, Monica
Lucas, the section manager for sustainable buildings at Rambøll, Structural Eng, Norway,
has provided some insight into which parts are in focus when attempting to design as
sustainably as possible. This will serve as a foundation for how minimal structures can
play a role in future construction. From what she has shared, there are predominantly
three aspects that need to be considered when designing a construction.
The three aspects in question are:
• Optimization of geometry
• Awareness of production method and procurement
• Design and facilitation for reuse of material (design for disassembly).
Note that the proceeding information was collected through talks, and some documents
that, due to corporate classification, will all fall under the reference ”Rambøll NO” [14].
5.2.1
Optimization of Geometry: Material Efficiency
The most sustainable materials are those that were never produced in the first place.
Optimizing constructions to reduce material use is therefore a key factor in increasing
57
==PAGE:64==
the sustainability of projects. From a designers perspective, the challenge with this aspect
is that the number of hours spent on engineering might increase due to the complexities
mentioned in the previous section.
Rambøll advises their designers to use FEM-designs and other optimizations solvers that
are developed internally, to calculate which profile results in the least amount of material
for each members, yet still performing structurally within the defined requirements.
In addition, the balance between the design of robust structures and optimized structures
should be considered. As mentioned in this report, a high utilization rate is correlated
with a low material redundancy and thereby an effective structure. This answers directly
to what is discussed in this thesis and gives material-economic structures and optimization
further relevance in today’s design practices.
5.2.2
Production and Procurement: Environmental Impact
When it comes to production methods and material procurement, there are especially two
main methods are used to produce steel today – blast furnace and electric arc furnace.
Production with a blast furnace and oxygen converter uses ore, lime, and coal, as well as
some scrap (0-25%). Worldwide, about 70% of all steel is produced using this method
today, even though it has by far the highest emissions. In Europe, about 50% is produced
using this method. Production with an electric arc furnace is based on scrap (90-100%).
This method has about 70% lower emissions than ore-based production. Additionally, it
avoids the extraction of new raw materials and the disposal of scrap.
Beyond the two dominant production methods to date, production with Direct Reduced
Iron (DRI) is on the rise. If the hydrogen is produced with green energy, this produc-
tion method will be nearly ”emission-free.” By 2026, commercially available steel from
hydrogen-reduced iron ore will be available, but it may take additional years before large
quantities are available.
A big issue with producing members directly based on a shape optimization is that
members have to be independently made due to the specificity of its structural role. We
know that a large part of steel production is standardized, and custom-made profiles
require more time, more energy, and are much more costly. If no consideration was to
be placed on production cost or time management, the absolute best way to construct
would be to use only custom-made members for all parts in the construction.
One way in which time consumption, cost, and energy use are exclusively related to the
size of the profile is additive manufacturing or 3D printing. Even though this is no where
58
==PAGE:65==
near a common practise in construction, it is seen more often in machine engineering,
creating smaller movable parts or chassis’ for machinery. What is especially satisfactory
about 3D-printing is that in addition to creating members that can transform cross sec-
tions in the same member dependent on the receiving stress, it can also create internal
structures that support the external structure, making ”simpler” profiles that could with-
stand a greater amount of load (see Figure 61 in Appendix C for image example). This
could lead to massive development in design, giving more freedom to pursue aesthetics
and placing less boundaries on engineering approaches. In addition, it provides the op-
portunity to create a structure where the factor of safety along with the utilization factor
is equal in all parts of the construction.
5.2.3
Reuse Potential: Longevity and Durability
There would be a significant climate and environmental benefit if we can facilitate more
reuse. The production rate of new members decelerates as the expansion of lifetime of
construction members increases. In the design phase, reuse can be facilitated by choosing
bolt solutions instead of welding and choosing stock lengths like 6m, 9m, 12m, 18m etc.
for members. This reduces waste and simplifies reuse.
However, not only does this require the restructuring of several industry sectors, but it
goes directly against the previous argument of designing exclusively custom-made mem-
bers. Taking design for disassembly (DfD) and reuse into account, there has to be a
profitable exchange of lifetime design of the bridge and lifetime design and reuse po-
tential of the members.
This assessment must be compared with the environmental
profitability of custom-made members. What speaks against DfD is that standardized
solutions challenge architectural design and limit the space to create new and interesting
constructions.
Perhaps a refined approach would be to custom-make members that are project-specific
and use standardize options where possible. Still, for DfD to work as intended, several
different industries have to change their direction of thought.
Prefabricated elements are also much better suited for reuse, where structural parts can
be reused in their entirety. Reuse can be facilitated by not adding toppings and joint
casts or sealing lift anchors. This may require additional measures for the structures
global stability and affect acoustic properties, etc. Another consideration is the storage
of elements between dismantling and new installation. For example, if slabs are designed
for exposure class X0, they cannot be stored outside exposed to moisture and frost.
Over time, DfD and reuse of members may be a sustainable approach for designers,
59
==PAGE:66==
however as of today it is deemed non-profitable because of practical challenges:
Among many other factors some of the main practicall challenges with reuse are:
• Availability of products
• Time aspect of demolition
• Costs of testing and verification
As mentioned in the introduction, cost is usually the primary driver in construction
logistics and while some estiamte the additional costs of to vary from 50-100%, awareness
of reuse is increasing, and several initiatives are underway to simplify the process.
5.3
Summary
This thesis investigates the challenges and possibilities of implementing minimal struc-
tural designs in bridge construction, with an initial focus on enhancing sustainability.
Research shows that while incorporating minimal structures into bridge designs is feasi-
ble, it requires more comprehensive and meticulous planning compared to conventional
methods. A primary finding is that minimal structures can indeed be applied to bridge
engineering, although with significant challenges such as the buckling of members and
the additional reinforcement ”optimally” tall bridge designs.
The study delves into the intricacies of minimal structures, emphasizing their potential
to optimize material usage and reduce the environmental footprint. The methodology
adopted includes an extensive literature review to understand the historical and theoreti-
cal background of minimal structures. Additionally, self-learning of the Autodesk Fusion
360, with all its simulation programs, played a pivotal role in analyzing and optimiz-
ing these designs, enabling detailed simulations of various load conditions and structural
behaviors.
Several cases were studied to illustrate how the practical applications and benefits of
minimal structures in bridge construction could be carried out. These cases highlighted
the theoretically extremely low material usage when minimal structural designs were
employed, demonstrating a potential for significant environmental benefits. Other Michell
based structures like split-pylons could be realistically built, acting as one example of how
structures using the tension/compression orthogonality can inspire minimal and beautiful
structures. However, the research also identified key challenges, particularly related to the
60
==PAGE:67==
stability and uncertainty of the interplay between the minimal structures. The buckling
of slender members under load, the lack of robustness that needs to be accounted for,
and the extended design time were found to most likely be significant hurdles that need
to be addressed.
The thesis underscores the importance of adopting a holistic approach to bridge design,
considering both structural and environmental impacts. By focusing on material efficiency
and resource utilization, the research aligns with the broader goals of sustainability in the
construction industry. It suggests that despite the increased design complexity, minimal
structures can potentially have a place in bridge construction, making construction more
resource-efficient and environmentally friendly.
In conclusion, the findings of this research contribute to the growing body of knowledge
on sustainable construction practices. They provide insights into the feasibility and ben-
efits of minimal structural designs, highlighting both the potential and the challenges of
implementing such innovative approaches in bridge engineering. This work serves as a
foundation for future research and development in the field, encouraging further explo-
ration and refinement of minimal structural concepts to achieve greater sustainability in
construction.
5.4
Future Research
To be sure of the real environmental footprint of implementing minimal structures, a
broader study would have to take place, where the cases are inflicted with all other loads
and limit states, to see, for a real-world application, if there is a significant material
reduction. This then has to be weighed up against the presumably additional emissions
of the rise of such complex structure. Only then can one get a clear idea if the minimal
structure-inspired bridges serve the planets well being.
Future research should also focus on advancements in manufacturing technologies to
produce specialized members more cost-effectively and with a reduced environmental
footprint. Innovations in material science could lead to the development of new materials
that offer greater strength-to-weight ratios and enhanced durability, further optimizing
the efficiency of minimal structures.
Additionally, more sophisticated simulation tools and methods are needed to better pre-
dict unforeseeable loads, and design based on worst-case scenarios. These tools should
incorporate advanced algorithms and computational techniques to provide more accurate
and reliable assessments of structural performance under various load conditions. This
61
==PAGE:68==
could be thought of as a digital twin, where the application of realistic loads can all be
tested and analyzed to attempt to design as close to the permissible safety factors as
possible.
Collaboration with industry stakeholders, including engineers, architects, and construc-
tion companies, is essential to refine these designs and develop practical guidelines for
their application. Now that artificial intelligence is on the rise, such collaboration can
more easily facilitate the translation of theoretical concepts into real-world solutions,
ensuring that minimal-volume layout can be effectively implemented in actual bridge
projects.
Furthermore, ongoing research should investigate the lifecycle impacts of minimal struc-
tures, from production and construction through to maintenance and eventual decom-
missioning. This holistic approach will provide a comprehensive understanding of their
sustainability benefits and inform strategies to maximize their environmental and eco-
nomic advantages.
In summary, future work should aim to overcome the current challenges of minimal
structural designs through technological innovation, interdisciplinary collaboration, and
continuous refinement of design and simulation techniques. By addressing these areas, the
construction industry can move closer to realizing the full potential of minimal-volume
design and achieve sustainable and efficient bridge designs.

LayOpt
Background LayOpt makes use of mathematical optimization methods to obtain a minimum
volume truss structure for a given problem. This section provides a brief introduction to the
theory of absolute minimum material design and more information about the methods used in
the Web App.
Theory The theory behind absolute minimum volume structures has received academic interest
for over a century. A seminal paper in the field was published by Michell (1904), leading to the
emergence of so-called Michell structures. The rules governing such structures are seemingly
simple, yet the resulting structures can be very complex. For example, members in tension and
compression should always intersect at right angles, so that they are not working against each
other. A very simple example is shown below; the central solution allows the minimum volume
of material to be used. When the members form an angle less than 90 degrees, the force in each
member increases, leading to a higher volume. Conversely, when the angle is greater than 90
degrees, the force in the members is reduced, but their length is increased.
Figure 50: [5]
For most problems, ensuring that all members meet at right angles requires families of curved
members.
Generally, there will be infinitely many, infinitesimally thin such members.
For
example, a vertical load applied midway between two supports will be supported by a half-
wheel form, with a semicircular outer member and infinitely many ’spokes’ connecting to the
central loaded point. Clearly, a structure with infinitely many members will not be practical
to construct; however, structures of a similar layout but consisting of fewer members may have
structural volumes only a few percent above the true optimum solution. The figure below shows
approximations to the bicycle wheel type structure; the true minimum volume solution for this
problem with unit limiting stress in tension and compression is pL / 2, where in this case the
span L is 16, leading to a solution of 25.133.

LayOpt stress ratios
The stress ratio in LayOpt seems to create a lesser volume when the arbitrarily relation between
the tension and compression members is greater.
However, through dialog with one of the
creators of the application, Dr.
Helen Fairclough of the University of Sheffield, it becomes
apparent that it is merely due to how the normalization of the volume is implemented. The stress
ratio control consistently elevates one type of stress (tension or compression) while maintaining
the other at a base level. Within an arbitrary unit system, the ratios displayed represent the
true measures of tensile and compressive stresses. Consequently, adjusting the slider in any
direction to allow for higher stress limits leads to a decrease in the resultant volume.
Autdoesk Fusion 360
All knowledge about Autodesk Fusion 360 was acquired through trial and error, in addition to
self-learning using YouTube and other sources on the Internet. This part of the appendix will
go through the steps required to run the different type of simulations in Fusion 360, and more
of the results that can be obtained from these simulations.
Initially, the program presents the user with an interface similar to the one in Figure 52, perhaps
with other color schemes. Every 3D model in Fusion is designed through a 2-dimensional sketch
that then is extruded, making one body of the one sketch (this can be adjusted so that there
is one body for several sketches, but not vice versa). When sketching, one is forced to choose
a planar surface to create a 2-D sketch on, in which it is important to choose a plane that
gives you the opportunity to later adjust the view to make the structure ”stand” and not of
”lie”. This is intuitively solved if you choose the wrong planar surface the first time. After the
extrusion has taken place, other adjustments, such as holes or fillets, can be taken into account
(see Figures 53 & 54). For the Pedersen model, the structure in itself consisted of one sketch
and one body, while the underlying supports and boxes used for positioning loads were applied
to small boxes that later in the simulation stage were joined to the main body. One hole was
made in the support on one side of the bridge, for a pinned connection to take place in the
simulation stage.
