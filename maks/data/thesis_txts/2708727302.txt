Abstract
This project emphasis on the importance of sustainable construction since global warming
is on the rise since the end of the previous century as it is the need of the hour. This
report explains extensively the procedure and results on Stamped earth and the influence
of binders on it.
This project aims on the reuse of waste construction soil at Vinge and Hedehusene. The
soil obtained from these respective construction sites were preliminary tested before the
start of the manufacturing of the clay samples. After some tests it was found that the soil
from Vinge was the favorable one to proceed with finding the influence of binders.
The binders tested in this report are rapid cement, calcium oxide and metakaoline. The
samples were manufactured with variable water contents of 10%, 12% and 14% depend­
ing upon the binder in use. During the initial manufacturing stage, the samples were
manufactured with 3 rams per layer and each layer weighing about 225 g and a total of
3 layers. The speed of the rammer head was 400 mm/s and a force of 2 KN. Once de­
molded the samples would be wrapped in a plastic foil so that there would not be any loss
of water content. These samples would then be cured for 3 and 14 days.
After the initial testing a new ideal manufacture condition was devised, where each layer
would be compacted 5 times with a force of 2 KN and the rammer head speed of 800
mm/s. These samples were then demolded and wrapped in plastic foil and cured for 3
and 14 days. Eventually a new curing method was also tried which provided positive
results where the samples would be kept wrapped in plastic foil for 11 days and then
unwrapped and cured for the next 3 days before testing it for compressive strength.

1
Introduction
Sustainable construction is defined as a way to make use of renewable and recyclable
materials for construction; so as to which would cut down the construction byproducts
such as energy, construction waste and CO2 levels. This type of construction reduces
the dependency on fossil fuels from the procurement of raw materials to the final phase
of construction; since about 36% of the CO2% [1] is accounted for by the construction
industry. The primary aim is to bring down the CO2% which is done with the help of one
of the naturally available materials; soil. Out of the total waste produced annually all over
Europe, waste soil is the major contributor to it. The construction industry in Denmark
accounts for about one third of the total waste produced in an year. In the year 2015
the total waste produced by the industry accounts to about 9 million tonnes. In order
to curb the exploitation of arbitrary usage of raw materials and reduce global warming
a sustainable built environment is very much the need of the hour. This is where the
Rammed earth technique comes into picture [2].
Rammed earth technique of construction has been around for centuries and stood the
test of time but it is not one of the extensively used method of construction in the modern
times. In this technique of construction the soil is rammed multiple times to produce either
building block bricks or directly the wall. The soil is rammed with the help of a rammer/head
to compact the soil into well compacted layers. In this project the protagonist being the
clay, which is natural and readily available will be primarily used. The soil samples were
obtained from two different construction sites in Copenhagen; namely located at Vinge
and Hedehusene. The clay obtained for this project was deemed as surplus by the project
managers at the respective construction sites which will be put to use.
1.1
Different types of sustainable construction methods
The different types of sustainable earth constructions are namely:
1.1.1
Rammed earth
Rammed earth is a method of making bricks from locally obtained soil/clay where the
clay is poured into the mold and and then rammed either manually with the help of a
hand held rammer or mechanically with the help of a compaction machine. Rammed
earth bricks need a sophisticated mold/form­work designed as per the requirement for
the manufacture of bricks. For the manufacture process of this type of bricks specially
skilled and experienced workers are necessary. These bricks once manufactured need
to be cured in the oven depending upon the requirement.

1.1.2
Adobe bricks
Adobe bricks are basically sun dried bricks made out of mud and straw. In this case the
straw acts as a fiber that hold the composition of the bricks. This type of bricks are usually
hand molded and sun baked. But special care needs to be taken during the production
phase as these bricks are highly susceptible to failure during this time phase. The raw
materials required for the manufacture of these bricks are locally sourced and all the
materials required are abundantly available in the nature. The earth used for manufacture
needs to provide good cohesion which is possible with soil that has good amount of clay
presence in it. Since these bricks are cured naturally there is zero to none CO2 footprint.

1.1.3
Compressed earth blocks
Compressed earth blocks are defined as mechanically compressed damp earth which is
usually stabilized with a binder cement in a prefixed ratio.They are usually in the shape of
blocks. These blocks are manufactured by compressing the earth in a mold at a particular
force. When it comes to construction of walls with this methods the bricks are layed on
top of each each other like any other masonry construction and then the bricks are stuck
to each other with the help of a slurry made out of water, clay and soil. If the aim is to
achieve a higher strength/bond between each layer or bricks, even cement motar is made
use of.

1.2
Development phase
During the initial phase of preparation of soil for production of samples it was decided that
the clay would be oven dried for 96 hours since, the clay would be put to heat on a Friday
and then taken out on Monday so that there would be enough soil throughout the week
for sample preparation.
Also it was predetermined that the soil that would be used for production would have to
pass through 8mm sieve. This was pre determined considering the mold sizes being 60
mm * 120 mm.
It was also predetermined that the molds in which the samples would be produced would
be cylindrical and not cubical/rectangular since, the cubical/rectangular samples would
chip off during production and demolding. At the start the samples manufactured were
of bigger dimensions and each sample would weigh about 3 Kgs, since a lot of sample
would have to be produced in the future to complete the test matrix it was unrealistic and
that is why the size of the mould was sized down to 60mm*120mm and these samples
would weigh about 675g.
During the pilot manufacturing phase, the first ever set of sample was manufactured man­
ually by pouring multiple layers of soil and then hand ramming each layer 25 times with a
rammer to manufacture it. During the demolding of the first ever sample, it was observed
that the sample in the middle and bottom was well compacted where as the top layer did
not posses the same adhesiveness that the lower layers did.

Methodology
This section will elaborate the step by step procedure for the manufacturing of the soil
samples with different soil.
3.1
Sample manufacturing procedure
3.1.1
Samples with no binders
This type of samples only comprise of soil with a prefixed water content and a standard
manufacturing process.
• The soil procured from both the construction sites; Vinge and Hedehusene, is firstly
poured in a huge metal tray and placed in the oven which is preset to 50◦C for 96
hours i.e 4 days,to completely remove the water content from it.
(a) Clay drying oven in a tray to dry for 96 hours
before usage
(b) Wet soil placed in a tray to dry for 96 hours
before usage
Figure 3.1: Manufacturing of samples with no binders. Picture courtesy of Rasmus.
• After 96 hours the the dried soil is then smashed to fine particles manually with the
help of a mallet/hammer.
• The smashed soil is then passed through a sieve of 8 mm, the soil passing through
the sieve is further used and the rest retained on the sieve is discarded.
• The predetermined water content is added to the dried sieved soil with respect to its
weight and mixed thoroughly.
• This mix is then let to rest for 24 hours before the start of production.
• For the pilot production, the force used for each ram was 2KN and a speed of 400
mm/s.
• The samples where produced in a stainless steel mould of 60mm*120mm.
• After a few trial and error tests, it was determined that each sample would comprise
of 3 layers and each layer would comprise of about 225g of sample.

• The mould then would be placed in the center and 225g of soil is then added to it
and rammed 3 times at the speed of 400mm/s and a force of 2KN.
• The same procedure is repeated 2 more times to manufacture the sample.
3.1.2
Samples with cement as a binder
For this type of sample the soil would be mixed with a particular amount of cement and a
prefixed water content.
• The soil is preheated at 50◦C for 96 hours and then smashed manually and passed
through 8mm sieve.
• Then a fixed amount of cement by weight with respect to the dried sieved soil.
• This mix is then mixed evenly in a mixer for 3­4 minutes.
• Once the mix is homogeneous, the water is added to it by weight ( weight = weight
of cement + weight of dried soil ) for about 30 seconds when the mixer is still running
with the help of a funnel.
• Once the water is added, the mixer is let to run for about 3­4 minutes more.
• Once the mix is ready, the workability is about 60 minutes.
• So the samples need to be produced within 60 minutes.
• The production method for the cement samples would be the same as that above
that is; 225g in each of the three layers, rammer speed of 400mm/s and a force of
2KN.
3.1.3
Samples with alkaki activated binders
In this case the binders selected were CaO, Metakaoline and Ca(OH)2.
• The soil is preheated at 50◦C for 96 hours and then smashed manually and passed
through 8mm sieve.
• The prefixed binders are then added by weight to the soil in the mixer and mixed till
a homogeneous mixture is obtained.
• Then a prefixed amount of water is added to the mix by weight and then evenly
mixed.
• This mix is then made use of for the production of the samples with binders in it.
• The production method for the binder samples would be the same as that of the
cement and regular samples that is; 225 g in each of the three layers, rammer speed
of 400 mm/s and a force of 2 kN.

Production
This chapter will mainly focus on the test series
4.1
Samples with cement as a binder
Stabilization of the clay obtained from the construction site at Vinge was picked for further
cement stabilization. It was the purpose of this project to define the right balance between
the percentage of cement to be added with the right amount of water content to obtain the
maximum strength. The variables evaluated comprises of different percentage of water
content.
4.1.1
Laboratory procedure
The following steps were carried out during the production phase:
Pulverization
The procured soil would firstly be oven dried for 96 hours at 50◦C. Once the soil would
completely loose its natural water content, it would be smashed with the help of a hammer
to break it down completely and the bigger stones would manually be separated. This
smashed soil would then be sieved and the soil passing through 8mm sieve would be
made use of for the sample production.
Treatment
The samples would usually be produced in a batch of 10. To produce such a batch, the
oven dried and sieved soil of about 7 kg was poured in the Varimixer and then 5% of rapid
cement that is 350 g would be added to the soil and then this mixture is dry mixed for about
3­4 minutes. Since the water content was the variable, different batches of samples with
water content of 12% and 14% were considered.

If the chemical reaction between the clay, cement and water was solely based on hy­
dration of cement then this chemical reaction would be termed chemically inert. During
the curing phase the cement particles would bind closely to the clay particles and for a
strong skeleton around the clay particles forming a very strong outer shell . Also since the
cement particles are finer in size than the clay particles, it would fill in all the air pockets
which would intern help in reducing permeability and swelling.
In addition to hardening, different other chemical reactions take place between the hydrat­
ing cement and clay particles, which would also result in stringer bonds between the two.
But at the same time due to this chemical reaction the clay participating in this reaction
would also loose some of its chemical properties; which would make it less plastic and
expansive when it comes in contact with water.
Compaction
During the initial sample production phase, the samples would be compacted with the help
of the instron. The samples would be produced in stainless steel mould with a diameter
of 60mm. With some trial and error it was determined that to obtain samples of standard
dimensions, we would manufacture the samples in three layers. Each layer would weight
about 225 g of the mix. Before the start of the manufacture process, the instron would be
preset with fixed data. The force of each layer would be 2KN and the speed at which the
rammer would compact each layer was 400mm/s. The mould would then be centered with
respect to the rammer and fixed tightly. The cement and clay mix would then be poured
manually in the steel mould and this would be compacted 3 times with a force of 2KN.
after the first layer has had 3 rams, for the next layer 225g of mix would then be added
and rammed 3 more times. This procedure would be carried out 3 time for the production
of one sample. The only variable here being the water content.
After some testing regarding the search for ideal test methodology it was found out that
the samples would give a maximum strength if the number of rams was increased to 5
and the speed at which the rammer would come in contact with the soil in the mould was
increased to 800mm/s.
Curing
The samples once demolded were quickly wrapped in a plastic foil and stored in a cli­
mate chamber that had a room temperature of 22◦C and a humidity of 65%. These sam­
ples would then be tested after 3 and 14 days since manufacture. Whereas for the ideal
method, the samples would be kept wrapped in the plastic foil for 11 days and then ex­
posed to atmosphere for 3 days that is 72 hours before testing.
Testing
Once the samples are cured for 3 or 14 days, they are then tested to determine the com­
pression strength. The unconfined compression test is carried out following the standard
ISO 17892­7:2018 with the help of the instron machine.

or seashells that have calcium carbonate in them. It is obtained by a process named
calcination; where the materials are burned at about 825◦C which liberates CO2 and in
turn producing quicklime.

The chemical reaction between the clay particles and lime binder can be distinguished
into two types; rapid reaction (ion exchange and flocculation) and slow reaction (carbon­
ation and pozzolonic reaction). Although the former reaction maybe completed in a few
days whereas the latter slower reactions may take up to years. In the case of rapid re­
action when lime comes in contact with clay and water it flocculates due to the increased
amount of electrolyte which results in an ion exchange between the lime and clay parti­
cles. Whereas in the case of slower reaction, carbonation takes place. Carbonation is
limited to the surface of the sample that is exposed to the atmosphere dude to which lime
reacts with CO2 to form Calcium carbonate; which plays an important role in enhancing
and cementing the soil particles together and increase the strength.

Problems faced
Since the start of this project there have been multiple challenges that had to be overcome
for the smooth and efficient production of samples. One of the first major problem was
breaking down of the soil that was left in the oven for 96 hours to completely lose the
natural water content. Then the soil would become really hard and difficult to prepare for
the production. The soil had to manually broken down completely so that it would pass
through 8mm sieve.
During the pilot production of samples, the samples were produced with the help of a
hydraulic demolding machine. Since there was no firm base/foundation for the mould to
rest on during the compaction, multiple steel discs had to be placed on top of one other to
make the base firm for the production. Also during the demolding of the samples with the
same machine, since the head of the demolding machine was shorter than the height of
the steel mould, the samples would not be completely demolded but they would partially
stuck in the mould and then eventually fail during the demolding phase. To curb this issue
eventually we had to move to start using the instron for the manufacture of the samples.
Another major issue that arose after moving to the instron was once again the demolding
part. Since demolding the samples with the help of the hydraulic machine was out of
the equation, improvisation was required, that is when a transparent PVC pipe of twice
the length of the steel mould came into the picture. So to demould, the mould would be
inserted upside down in the PVC pipe and then with trial and error method, the rammer
would need to pass through the mould to get the samples out.
Figure 5.1: Demolding failure
With this improvisation of demolding procedure, there arose a new problem that was that
the rammer head would sometimes hit the walls of the steel moulds while demolding and
then damage the head and the mould at the same it. To curb this issue the demolding
had to be done with high precision since the room for error was very low. This issue was
encountered once during demolding where a couple of moulds were completely damaged
and the rammer head was out of shape and was of no further use. Eventually the rammer
head to be filed so that it would fit in the mould and we could continue manufacturing the
samples.

Conclusion
This project was developed to reuse the waste soil from two different construction sites:
Vinge and Hedehusene. This report emphasis on the influence of different binders on the
soil procured from the site at Vinge.
For the first test series 5%rapid cement by weight of the soil was used as a binder and it
was concluded that the samples with 12% water content attained the higher compressive
strength than the 14% samples after a span of 3 days and the same trend was observed
by the samples which were cured for 14 days. Also when the same batch of samples was
manufactured with the ideal conditions the compressive strength for 12% samples was
found to be same as that of initial manufacture procedure. It was also noted that under
ideal manufacture process the 14% water content samples failed during the demolding
phase.
In the second test series 5% Calcium Oxide (CaO) by weight was used as a binder. When
comparing the compressive strength attained by the samples with 10%, 12% and 14%
water content after 3 days it was found that the strength displayed by 12% samples was
the highest whereas for the samples with 14 days of curing the highest strength was
attained by 14% samples. This test series was eventually discontinued and was not tested
for ideal manufacturing method as the strength displayed was very less when compared
to that of the cement binder.
During the final test series 2.5% Calcium oxide (CaO) and 2.5% Metakaoline was used
as a binder and mixed with the soil by weight. It was found that after 3 days of curing
the samples with 14% water content displayed the highest strength when compared to
the other water content ratios. Whereas after 14 days of curing the same trend followed
and 14% displayed the highest strength. When the samples with the same ratio and mix
was manufactured with ideal conditions, after 14 days it was found that the samples with
12% water content attained the higher compressive strength than 10% and 14%. Also
when the same batch of samples was manufactured with ideal conditions but during the
curing phase after 11 days the samples were freed of the plastic wrap and let to cure for
the next 3 days with atmospheric temperature set in the climate chamber; it was observed
that the strength of the 12% and 14% samples had increased. The highest strength was
displayed by 14% samples and it was also concluded that for 14% samples the strength
had increased 3 fold by just removing the plastic foil 3 days prior to the compressive
strength test.
When comparing the influence of all the three binders it was concluded that the highest
strength was attained by the cement binder samples after 14 days, though the strength on
the samples with ideal manufacturing process was slightly higher than that of the samples
produced with initial manufacturing process.

Future scope
This project provides an overview of the influence of binders on the clay obtained from
the construction site at Vinge. The results obtained from this report portray the possibility
of performance deviation under different conditions.
From the results obtained for the samples with cement binder, a set of samples have
been manufactured with 12% water content under ideal manufacture conditions, have
been left to cure for 11 days and then 3 days before the compression test; the samples
will be unwrapped of the plastic foil and then tested. Eventually the obtained results will
be noted down and compared to the results obtained by curing the samples in plastic foil
for 14 days and other binders.
The results obtained from the samples with CaO and Metakaoline binder combination,
display that just by unwrapping the samples 3 days prior to the compression test the
strength increases almost three fold for samples with 14% water content. Thus a few more
batches of the same sample with the same manufacturing ratio under ideal conditions
should be manufactured and freed of the plastic foil 7 days and 11 days prior to testing to
check the difference in the strength.
Water immersion tests still have to be carried for all the samples infused with binders and
manufactured with the ideal manufacturing process and depending upon the obtained
results, a new conclusion can be defined.
