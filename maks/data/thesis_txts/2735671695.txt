Abstract
This thesis attempts to develop a simple model for predicting various coastal processes, such as
wave dynamics and sediment transport. These complex phenomena are described using established
formulations and theories while also incorporating new formulations in select cases. The objective
is to approximate these processes in a time-efficient manner by creating a model that strikes a
balance between complexity, simplicity, and accuracy.
The results generated by the model are tested against three case studies: experiments from Baldock
(1998), Boers (2005), and LIP11D Delta flume are used, where the beach profiles investigated are
linear sloping beach profiles and bared beach profiles.
The model achieves a good approximation of the different processes compared to experiments;
however, several expected inaccuracies due to model simplicity are observed. The inaccuracies in a
single sub-model affect the overall accuracy of the final processes as the outputs of the sub-models
are used in subsequent sub-models, as seen in the case of wave heights impacting the concentration
profiles. Even though the sub-models imprecision impacts the final outputs, some of the inaccura-
cies do stem from a less-than-perfect choice of formulation and exclusion of coastal processes, as
seen in some positions for the undertow.
In summary, the model demonstrates good approximations of the diverse coastal processes dis-
cussed in this thesis while highlighting further refinement and improvement areas.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
In the coastal regions, many complicated processes take place as waves interact with the environ-
ment, creating sediment transport that alters the morphology of a given profile. These processes
have been explored for the past decades, and with rising sea levels, understanding these processes
becomes more important as more land develops into coastal zones.
Many attempts have been made to make model formulations for the individual processes within
sediment transport, and full models have been made that can predict the behavior of coastal zones
being impacted by different wave scenarios. Some of these models are very time-consuming and
complex, like Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) models, which can run for several weeks with
no guarantee of a complete model.
This thesis aims to create a time-efficient model based on a simple formulation consisting of existing
formulations and theory while implementing new formulations for some processes. As the model
is based on simple formulations, a lot of the complexity is taken out of the system, giving rise
to inaccuracies. Therefore, the success criteria are based on getting good approximations of the
different processes being calculated while also addressing and mitigating any inherent inaccuracies
that may arise due to the model’s simplicity.
Different test cases have been selected to test the model’s validity against experimental data to
determine any inaccuracies caused by the model’s simplicity. These test cases are Baldock (1998)
experiments, which has a linear sloping beach profile, and the LIP11D Delta Flume Experiments,
where two cases were selected, one with a linear sloping beach profile and one with a more complex
profile consisting of two breaker bars and Boers (2005) experiments which is a scaled-down version
of the LIP experiments consisting of a breaker bar profile.
These experiments contain different amounts of data, which means not every case has data to be
tested against every process calculated in the thesis.

2
Model description
This chapter describes the numerical model built in Matlab, its theoretical background, and which
model inputs are used in each sub-model.
As new formulations have been used throughout the model, calibrations have been made based on
experimental data from various sources, which is discussed and presented in section 3.
The model is based on a main script that holds the inputs, calculates wave parameters, and
collects the results from the function scripts used to formulate the parameters.
2.1
General Inputs
The model sets the Cartesian coordinates so the horizontal axis x = 0 corresponds to the first
offshore point, and x increases towards the shore. The vertical axis y = 0 corresponds to the sea
water level (SWL), and z is the distance from the local water depth to the sea water level.
The physical parameters used in the model have been assumed as gravitational acceleration
g = 9.81 m/s2, water density ρs = 1000 kg/m3, kinematic water viscosity ν = 10−6 m2/s, the
sediment relative density s = 2.65, the bed porosity n = 0.4 and the critical Shields parameter
θc = 0.045.

DISCUSSION
4
Discussion
As described throughout section 3, the model has been compared to experimental data from three
case studies, namely Baldock (1998), Boers (2005), and the LIP11D Delta flume experiments.
Through these experiments, the model underwent testing on both a stable inclined beach profile
and an exposed beach profile.
The stable inclined beach profile seen in the Baldock cases validates their formulation of the
proportion of waves breaking Qb, which is the basis for the formulation of Q used in this model.
When the model was tested using the Baldock formulation for Q, the model ended up performing
poorly when comparing the results to the Boers cases, which had a bared beach profile, and the
formulation was changed to a second-order differential equation to retain the momentum in the
wave breaking which proved to increase the performance of the predictions for Q in both Baldock
and Boers cases with the parameters α and γ chosen to get the best average result for all cases.
With the new formulation for Q, the model performed better in the Baldock cases and after the
first breaker bar in the Boers cases.
The second breaker bar is where the model proves itself very inaccurate. The experiment Q rapidly
increases while the model predicts a near-constant value until very near the shoreline. Since Q is
used in the other sub-models, the inaccuracies influence the other modeling results.
As the wave heights are a central part of the entire model, the inaccuracies discovered in the
proportion of waves breaking affect the wave heights, which tend to be slightly over-predicted due
to a possible less optimal choice of α and γ to compensate for the inaccuracies provided by the Q
formulation.
The formulation for Q should be revised to improve the model as it relies on the Baldock for-
mulation, which was proven ineffective at predicting the Q for bared beach profiles. Some of the
inaccuracies may originate from using local water depth parameters in the Battjes and Janssen
(1978) formulation in Equation 13, which could be too sensitive to depth alterations. To determine
the causalities, more cases with bared beach profiles or alternating water depth have to be tested,
as only one case study of bared profile with the proportion of waves breaking data available has
been tested.
However, even though the model over-predicts the wave heights in some cases highlighted in the
previous section and has some impact in the following sub-models, it does not explain all the in-
accuracies observed in the comparisons between the model and experimental data. The model is
considered simple, and many coastal processes have been excluded to retain its simplicity while
having a good approximation of results

CONCLUSION
5
Conclusion
Firstly, the dissipation model, particularly the proportion of waves breaking Q, was compared with
experimental data across different bathymetric configurations. The model showed reasonably good
agreement with the Baldock experiments. However, challenges arose in accurately predicting wave
breaking in more complex bathymetric scenarios, such as those observed in the Boers experiments
with breaker bars.
The dissipation model’s ability to predict the wave heights Hrms was assessed across the vari-
ous experimental setups. Overall, the model performed well in predicting wave height evolution
towards the shoreline, though with slight over-predictions in most cases.
To obtain a better result for the dissipation model, a better formulation for the proportion of
waves breaking Q is needed as it has been proven very inaccurate for bared beach profiles, as seen
in the inner surf zone of the Boers cases. With a better formulation for Q, further optimization
of dissipation constants and maximum wave height coefficients is needed to adjust the interaction
between the wave heights and Q to obtain the correct wave heights.
The undertow model’s validation demonstrated good agreement with experimental data, partic-
ularly in regions with accurate wave-breaking predictions. However, discrepancies were noted in
areas where the dissipation model’s predictions were inaccurate, which influenced the velocity pro-
files of the undertow to some degree. The inaccurate wave heights do not explain the inaccurate
undertow profiles, as their shape in some positions does not match either, which means a more
complex formulation is needed to capture every position.
The suspended sediment model validation showed promising results as the model captured the con-
centration profiles reasonably well, especially in case 1B. The concentration profiles were slightly
off in case 1A due to inaccurate wave heights, which was later determined when the case was tested
with the wave heights from the experiment, and a significant increase in accuracy was observed.
Through the tests with the experiment wave heights as inputs, the interconnectivity of each sub-
model was clearly represented as the undertow model improved slightly while the concentration
profiles improved substantially, which also would improve the sediment transport accuracy would
improve as well.
In summary, while the model showed good agreement with experimental data in several aspects,
further efforts in parameter tuning, model refinement, and data validation are essential for enhanc-
ing the capabilities of the model across diverse morphologies and hydrodynamic conditions.
However, it should be noted that the model is simple and excludes many coastal processes, which
would add complexity. As the model strives for a good time-efficient approximation, the time-
consuming computations are left for models like a CFD model, and this model is considered a
success overall.
