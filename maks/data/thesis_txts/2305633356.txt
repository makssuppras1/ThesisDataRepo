Abstract 
Methanol steam reforming to hydrogen was performed with a compact methanol reformer (CMR) and the 
reformate gas was tested for content of hydrogen, carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide. The CMR consisted 
of an evaporator with sufficient heating elements, catalytic reformer with catalyst Cu/Zn/Al and a condenser. 
Special emphasis was placed on obtaining high methanol conversion and high percentage of H2 and 
relatively lower percentages of CO and CO2 in the overall reformate gas. The contents of H2, CO and CO2 at 
atmospheric pressure were examined at 240  , 260   and 280  . Additionally 0.5 bar and 1 bar 
overpressure were used for further test. The gas composition was analyzed on a GC.  
The obtained H2 contents at lower loads (specific hydrogen yield  0.40 m3/h∙Icat) was detected being 75% at 
280   with all three pressure settings. A higher H2 and lower CO profile was observed with 0.5 bar at 260   
however higher methanol conversion was detected at 280  . With 1 atm at 280   a methanol conversion of 
95% was observed at 1.37 m3/h∙Icat and 1.95 m3/h∙Icat and with 0.5 bar at 0.89 m3/h∙Icat and 1.39 m3/h∙Icat. For 
the CO2 and CO contents with the same flow (load) it was 23-24 % and 1-1.7 % respectively. 
The test with the CMR generally demonstrated enhanced combustion of methanol fuel, no deactivation of the 
catalytic activity, a relatively high yield of H2 and lower content of CO.

1. Introduction 
Natural gas, oil and coal are the three major forms of fossil fuels and are our main sources of energy. They 
are also raw materials in a wide range of products such as gasoline and various petrochemical and chemical 
products (pharmaceuticals, plastics, synthetic materials etc.) [1]. 
 
Fossil fuels are a combination of hydrocarbons made of different ratios of carbon and hydrogen. By 
combustion of fossil fuels, the carbon is converted irreversibly into carbon dioxide (CO2) and the hydrogen 
into water (H2O) [1]. Aside from petroleum, oil, natural gas and coal there are also found a lot of other heavy 
hydrocarbons in the world, as heavy-oil deposits found in Venezuela, oil shales in US Rocky Mountains and 
massive tar sand deposits i.e. in Canada. The stable hydrates of methane are found in the Siberian tundras 
and along the oceans which holds significant options for natural gas deposits [2]. However, the current 
approximates of our oil funds shows that they would only last for some 40 years at the present rate of 
consumption [2].  
To prevent diminishing of natural resources there is a need to build up reasonable, new and safe ways for 
energy storage and supply in addition to efficient production of hydrocarbons. However, combustion of 
hydrocarbons or pollutants generated in the internal combustion engine, for instance nitrogen oxides (NOx), 
sulphur oxides (SOx) and carbon dioxide (CO2) are also a threat to the environment as it results in land 
damage, smog, acid rain and changes in the composition of the atmosphere [3,4]. 
In the nineteenth century, a lot of efforts were made to develop new fuels and engines for automobiles. The 
real breakthrough for automobiles came in 1876, with the invention of the four-stroke engine also known as 
"the Otto engine". The modern combustion engine was developed by Gottlieb Daimler in 1885. It was 
working on gasoline introduced throughout a carburetor, which was build up on the first four wheeled 
automobile. The first T-Ford automobile was produced by Henry Ford in 1908 [3]. 
Today the environmental impact using an automobile is associated with the toxic substances that are released 
from the internal combustion. These pollutants have different impact on the environment and can be 
categorized in two main groups [3]:   
1. Substances that are produced as unburned fuel and which passes through the engine 
2. Substances that are produced as a by-product of the combustion 
Regulations have been constructed with the aim of reaching zero emissions by modifying the internal 
combustion engine, but this has proven to be not realistic [3].  
Fuel cells for automotive applications have been exploited in the transportation industry. Especially 
hydrogen is the fuel of interest in use of motor vehicles [5]. Investigations have been made for methanol as 
fuel for onboard reforming of methanol to produce hydrogen. The studied reformers have been compact 
==PAGE:13==
Page 13 of 95 
 
units, including microchannel reformers, plates or hydrogen membranes. Due to copper being sensitive to air 
through the shut-down and start-up processes, other catalysts for the reforming of methanol are utilized for 
the reactions [6]. 
 
As methanol is by itself a brilliant fuel it can be produced from biomass and blended with gasoline as fuel 
[1]. Methanol as a hydrogen carrier is a liquid that can easily be stored, transported and used [7]. 
 
2. Background 
2.1 Hydrogen energy 
Hydrogen is not a natural energy source on the Earth and needs much energy to be generated [1], but is still a 
subject of interest as the combustion of it is clean giving mainly water and releasing energy. Hydrogen is 
only found in its oxidized state (H2O) and does not have a fuel value [8]. However it can be produced from 
various renewable and non-renewable sources [8]. Additionally it is favorable as it is non-toxic and has 
abundance in the universe.  
Dihydrogen is a colorless and odorless gas that has a melting point at 13.7 K and a boiling point at 21 K. It 
has a low density of 0.009 g/dm3 which is almost insoluble in water. At 25    it is unreactive as it has a high 
H-H bond dissociation enthalpy (436 KJ/mol). Many reactions of H2 require high temperature, pressure or a 
catalyst. [9] 
The main source for hydrogen have been natural gas, but it is also possible to generate it by water 
electrolysis, photo electrolysis or steam reforming [7,8]. The main feature of hydrogen production is to 
separate hydrogen from feed-stocks of hydrogen at the level of purity that is desired for the given application 
[9,10].  
The process of hydrogen production mainly depends on the starting point. For example, the production from 
methane is only desired if the energy is either involved in methane or can easily be transformed to methane 
[13]. In the case of fossil fuels the natural gas transformation is rather easy whereas oil is more complicated 
[13]. Hydrogen is conceivably emission-free fuel and has a specific energy content of 140.4 MJ/kg (for 
instance gasoline only has 48.6 MJ/kg). The most common method to produce hydrogen is steam methane 
reforming. It is a process that requires two steps: 1) methane-steam reaction at 700-1100   to form synthetic 
gasses CO and H2 and 2) further reaction of CO in order to produce more hydrogen [8].  
By production of hydrogen through non-fossil sources, (e.g. as carbon capture and storage, CCS), it is 
possible to obtain near zero green house gas emissions [12]. Approaches in order to reach zero-emissions 
include hydrogen vehicle. Hydrogen vehicle is a motor vehicle that uses H2 as onboard fuel for motive power 
[13, 14]. The chemical energy of H2 is converted to mechanical energy in motor assemblies in vehicles. This 
==PAGE:14==
Page 14 of 95 
 
process is done by simply burning of H2 in an internal combustion or reacting H2 and O2 in a fuel cell to run 
electric motors [14]. Future drive systems for vehicles are expected to have an energy carrier in the form of 
methanol to enable onboard conversion of methanol into hydrogen syngas by the reaction with water. The 
syngas is converted into electricity with fuel cell application and the electricity supplied to the electric 
system of the vehicle [16]. Toyota and Daimler-Benz have productively validated onboard steam reforming 
of methanol with oxidation in their prototype vehicles and demonstrated high hydrogen concentration [17].  
 
2.3 Renewable energy and methanol fuel  
Methanol is a colorless, volatile, flammable and relatively light liquid also known as methyl alcohol 
(CH3OH). It has a very distinctive odor similar to the one of ethanol. It is found in antifreeze solutions and in 
solvents. Ingestion of a very little amount of pure methanol, such as 10 mL, can cause permanent blindness 
and with increasing volume such as 30 mL it is likely to be fatal. The toxicity of methanol is low, but it 
makes toxic metabolites. As a result, formic acid is formed and accumulated. Formic acid causes acidosis 
which further increases the toxicity and makes a better diffusion of formic acid into cells [18].  
Commercially methanol is one of the greatest products manufactured in the world, as it can be produced 
from different kind of resources, such as natural gas, biomass, wood and coal [10]. It is also possibly to yield 
methanol through thermodynamically methane oxidation  or simply by bioprocessing [10].  
In the later years, fuels as methanol formed from steam reforming products, has become significantly 
important in various areas. Methanol is not only utilized in many different chemical syntheses but also could 
be used for hydrogen storage and transportation. Under reasonable circumstances and conditions it is a 
liquid, which makes it more attractive for transportation and is even more preferred than natural gas [19].  
There are lots of advantages of using steam reforming over the other methanol conversion methods, as it 
corresponds to lower CO production [20].  Heavier fuels, such as those with C-C bonds, involve a different 
conversion mechanism, which does not hold the same advantage for steam reforming over partial

3. Methanol reforming  
Reforming is a process that takes several steps to accomplish. The hydrocarbon feedstock (liquid fuel as 
methanol or natural gas) is processed thermochemical at high temperature with a catalyst in order to generate 
H2-rich gas. Different reformers can be utilized for this purpose, with all giving different results. The 
reaction of the liquid fuel counts on the reformer and usually reacts with O2 or steam, which at elevated 
temperatures gives a synthetic gas of H2, CO, CO2, CH4 and H2O. The synthetic gas is additionally 
proceeded to obtain increased amount of hydrogen, which includes steps such as conversion of the CO in the 
synthetic gas (water shift gas reaction in reaction (3)). The hydrogen as the last step is acquired through this 
liquid fuel. However the purity of the hydrogen can vary. In e.g. fuel cell utilizations the acquired purity can 
be up to 99.999% [27].  
 
3.1 Catalyst 
The active component on the methanol reforming catalyst (CuO/ZnO/Al2O3) is copper. It is also the active 
component for methanol synthesis and decomposition. Higher copper content in the catalyst contribute with 
higher conversion and activity. The Zinc oxide is used to keep the textural support in segregating copper. 
Alumina is used to obtain higher surface area support in order to increase Cu dispersion. A drawback of the 
Cu-catalyst is that copper tends to sinter at temperature above 300   [10]. 
 
3.2 Evaporator 
For the drive system of the methanol reformer, the evaporator serves as the unit to provide endothermic 
methanol reforming with heat. The heat is necessary for the reforming reaction and for converting all 
possible gases from the system that still holds burnable portions into CO2 and H2O [25]. Usually an 
evaporator comprises of a fibrous support contained with wire nets where the catalyst is placed. The fibrous 
support is used to obtain better heat maintenance between the hot and cold reaction zone [28]. To achieve an 
optimal heat distribution several heat sources is to be used. The combustion process is controlled by 
measuring the outer side (the catalyst side) and the inner side with thermocouples [25].

4. Gas detection 
4.1 The Gas Chromatography (GC) principle 
Gas chromatography (GC) is an analytical separation technique that is used to analyze volatile gases in the 
gas phase. The gaseous analyte is transferred through a column by a gaseous mobile phase, also known as 
the carrier gas. Volatile gas is here injected through a septum (a rubber disk) and further into a heated port, 
where the sample is repeatedly evaporated. The vapor is carried away through the column with the help of 
the carrier gasses He, N2 or H2. The separated analytes run through a detector and the obtained responses are 
presented on a computer (Figure 1). The requirement for the column is that it must be hot enough to supply 
enough vapor pressure for analytes to be eluted in a realistic time. To have the analytes in the gaseous form, 
the detector is held at a higher temperature compared to the column [29]. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
4.2 Sample injection 
Sample injection for GC systems can be divided into three overall categories: Split injection, splitless 
injection and on-column injection. The general principle for a split/splitless injection is that sample is 
injected with a gas tight syringe for gases (for analytical chromatography a volume between 0.1-0.2 µL is 
used) from an injection port consisting of a silanized glass liner. The carrier gas then carries the vaporized 
sample into the hot chromatography column [29]. On-column injection is used for quantitative analysis 
where sample decomposes above boiling point. Therefore the solution is injected directly into the column 
without it passing a hot injector.  
Decomposed samples and septum tend to collect in the gas liner and should frequently be replaced. The 
lifetime of the rubber septum is approximately 20 manual injections or approximately 100 autosampler 
injections [29].

4.2.1 Split injection 
Split injection is preferred for analytes that make up more than 0.1 % (>0.1%) of the sample. A small amount 
of 0.1 µL is used to obtain high resolution. 0.2-2% of a sample is delivered to the column since the materials 
in the sample are too much for a column of 0.32 mm in diameter. The injection temperature is held high (e.g. 
350  ) in order to help fast evaporation and sample is injected fast ( 1 sec.) through the septum. A flow of 
the carrier gas carries the sample into the mixing - and vaporizing chamber where the vaporized sample is 
split in two parts: one part (a small portion) is moved into the column by the flow for the carrier gas and the 
other part (being the major part of the sample) is escaped through the split exit. The sample entering the 
column makes up only 0.3 - 20% of the initial introduced sample [30]. The sample portion that does not 
reach the column is called split ratio and varies typically from 50:1 to 600:1. Other types of split injection 
include: Conventional Vaporizing Injection that is a sample injection into a permanently hot chamber, a 
programmed temperature vaporizing (PTV) split injection and on-column split injection [30]. 
 
4.2.2 Splitless injection and direct injection 
Splitless injection is an injection with no split of the flow (see 4.2.1 split injection) in order to keep the large 
part of the sample material from going out in the split outlet. The splitless injection is performed with an 
injector that also can be used for split injection (e.g. split/splitless inlet). The split exit is closed (split outlet 
in Figure 2 is closed) before sample injection. Upon injection the carrier gas almost transfers the entire 
sample vapor (low boiling solvent) from the vapor chamber into the column. The glass liner for splitless 
mode is a straight empty tube with no mixing chamber. Injector temperature is approximately 220   (which 
is lower than of split injection) since the sample can decompose at higher temperatures due to sample 
material spending longer time in the port (   sec.) compared to split mode. 80% of the sample is utilized to 
the column [29]. The split exit is re-opened to purge the remaining sample from the injector.  This method is 
suited for trace analysis as splitting of the sample material is avoided to obtain higher sensitivity. Solute 
concentration of 0.1-500 ppm (ng/pL) is used, were 0.1 ppm is the FID detector limit [22,20].

4.2.2.1 Direct injection  
Direct injection uses an injector with no split outlet, so that split injection or flushing of the vaporizing 
chamber can take place after sample transfer. The main feature of this injection is that the sample passes 
through the vaporizing chamber, but then is directly transferred to the column. The sample is vaporized when 
introduced to a hot chamber and moved with the flow directly to the column as a vapor [31]. The entire 
sample with direct injection and a splitless inlet system is transferred into the column without any splitting. 
Special liners are made for this method, such as the "uniliner", that has a tapered part underneath, which 
allows a press-tight connection. Uniliners also has a side-hole that makes injection possible with electronic 
pressure control (EPC) which is used to pulse the pressure in the injection port [32]. The chromatographic 
separation starts instantly when sample reaches the column. The advantage of this injection method is that a 
complete transfer of the vaporized solute material into the column is obtained compared to the splitless 
injection where only   80% of sample solute material is delivered to the column [31]. 
 
4.3 Temperature and pressure programming and Carrier Gas 
 In most gas chromatographs the sample injection is controlled via temperature programming or pressure 
programming. With every column come a lower isothermal limit and an upper programmed temperature 
limit. The lower limit is the limit that the column can be kept for a long time and the upper programmed 
temperature limit is the temperature limit the column should be exposed for only a small number of minutes 
at the end of the programmed run. However a lot of gas chromatographs has inbuilt electronic pressure 
control of the carrier gas. It is therefore possible to increase flow of the mobile phase by increasing the inlet 
pressure which gives a decreased retention time. The pressure programming compared to the temperature 
programming minimizes the retention time of the late-eluting compounds. When the sample run has ended 
the pressure is reduced to its original value and ready for the next run, which saves a lot of time as there is no 
need to wait for the hot column to cool down before the next injection. Programmed pressure is useful for 
analysis, but it does not stand excessive temperatures [29].

4.4 Column 
The columns mainly used for the analysis are capillary columns, also known as open tubular columns. They 
consist of fused silica (SiO2) and are coated with polyimide to obtain support and protection from 
atmospheric moisture. Polyimide is a polymer that is able to 
endure temperatures up to 350  . With open tubular columns it 
is possible to obtain higher resolution, a decreased analysis time 
and a better sensitivity than packed columns. The only drawback 
is that it has less sample capacity than the packed columns.  
There are three types of open tubular columns (as illustrated in

coated open tubular column (SCOT) and porous layer open

stationary liquid phase in the inner wall. PLOT column in the other hand has solid particles that are acting as 
the active stationary phase. SCOT columns compared to WCOT columns can handle better amount of 
sample due to the high surface area [29].

4.5.1 TCD 
Thermal conductivity detectors are frequently used in gas chromatography due to its robustness, 
independence of auxiliary gases and its universality as they act in response to all analytes [38]. Usually it is 
useful for packed columns as it can be less sensitive than other detectors with tubular columns. TCD is based 
on the principle of measuring and reacting to changes in the thermal conductivity of the pure carrier gas and 
the target substance detected (it determines the ability of a substance to transfer heat from a hot zone to a 
cold zone) [37,39]. The sensitivity of the response to different solutes is defined by the solutes thermal 
conductivities (TC) in relation to the carrier gas. Here solutes with TC close to carrier gas will have a small 
response whereas solutes with greater difference between TC and carrier gas will produce better sensitivity 
[37]. The most commonly used carrier gas for TCD is helium. Helium has one of the highest thermal

from the outlet ("L" in Figure 9). Reformate gas sample was 
collected in Tedlar gas sampling bags in order to avoid 
contamination of the reformate gas with air. The bag was filled 
with gas sample through the gas sample outlet (see Figure 10) 
and the push and pull valve closed. Before each sample cycle 
the Tedlar bag was cleaned to prevent contamination of the 
sample gas. The fluoroplastic Tedlar bags was flushed with 
reformate gas; by filling it up with the gas and removing it 
completely. This procedure was repeated twice (or more) 
before the final sampling. Leak test was executed in order to 
determine if the sample gas inside the bag was leaking out. The sample filled bag was in a fume hood 
slightly pressed on and the bag checked for gas leakage. When the sample cycle was completed the bags 
were alternatively filled with clean air and cleaned as described earlier. Maximum 90% of the fluoroplastic 
Tedlar bags could be filled with gas as more caused bursting of the bag due to overpressure. The septum 
became toiled after maximum 30 manually injections and was replaced frequently. The experiment with all 
the different flow rates was repeated with reformer temperatures 260   and 240  . 
An extractor arm fume hood was installed near the reformate output in order to avoid CO contact. The CO 
content in the air around the CMR was monitored with MSA Altair® single gas detector for CO.

rate in seconds and the volume in mL were as previously kept for later use. 
The experimental procedure for the pressurized system was similar to the measurements at atmospheric 
pressure. However changes in the system layout was carried out to optimize the system, prevent leakage of 
gases (mainly CO) from the system as a result of high pressure and to obtain improved gas product quality.

7. Discussion 
For the compact methanol reformer setup an aluminum heating block was utilized for more efficient heat 
distribution and combustion. No complication during CMR sampling was encountered, however 
experimentations with overpressure showed instability in reformer and evaporator temperatures. The 
temperature was fluctuating relatively to a higher and lower temperature from the set-point. This was 
probably due to the system not being optimized for higher pressure. Alternatively an advanced compressor 
and flow/pressure controller could be utilized for the system.  
The principle for the peristaltic pump is established on the idea of alternating compression and relaxation of 
the tube by a rotating shoe. The rotating shoe enables suction of fluid in one end and discharge of it in the 
other end of the tubing through the rotating movements. However due to the rotating movements the tubing 
is exposed to wear and should be regularly changed. This problem was encountered during third round of 
hydrogen measurements (H2 measurements at 1 bar) and discovered due to delay of reformate gas output at 
low and high flow rates. All H2 experiments at 1 bar were repeated.  
One of the greater issues with the CMR during the experimentation was CO leakage from the system at 
higher flow rates (1 atm: flow 140, 0.5 bar: flow 110-120, 1 bar: flow 90). CO is an odorless and poisonous 
gas which upon exposure at higher concentrations causes death. Carbon monoxide was present in the 
reformate gas as result of the WGS reaction, however in varying concentrations depending on the hydrogen 
yield/flow setting and applied pressure. Altair CO detector was used to monitor the CO concentration in the 
air near the CMR setup. The CO long-term exposure value (8 hours) is 25 ppm and short-term exposure limit 
(15 min) 100 ppm (Control of Exposure to Biological or Chemical Agents O. Reg. 833 [41]). For further 
experimentation it could be studied whether the CMR setup should be built in a fume hood in order to avoid 
CO exposure.   
The reformate gas was collected in Tedlar bags in order to manually inject the sample into the GC. However 
it is not known how superior these bags are to seal the gas inside the bag. For instance for every sampling 
from the bag the syringe needs to be inserted into the septa of the bag which most thinkable causes some 
leakage of reformate gas during the process. In order to minimize leakage of sample during sample injection 
an auto sampler could be utilized. 
For the detection of hydrogen a MoleSieve (Molecular Sieve) column was used and the same settings for the 
GC software were utilized throughout the experiments. However huge deviations were in the single peak 
areas frequently observed. The peak area of the pure hydrogen gas sample varied from 1500 to 4000+ in 
intensity, the baseline was shifted and noise was observed. This variation was also obtained in the reformer 
sample results. The calculations were performed on triple reproducible measurements however practically 
more than triple measurements were performed to obtain reliable results. 
==PAGE:73==
Page 73 of 95 
 
This variation in GC response was a high indication of column degradation. High temperatures decompose 
the stationary phase which causes bleeding [29]. Therefore if higher baseline is obtained at lower 
temperatures, this will highly indicate column degradation. Columns that contain fused silica and are coated 
with polyimide tend to break when there is a fragile point on the coating (since it is the coating that protects 
the fused silica tubing). When the tubing is exposed to frequently and rigorously heating and cooling it puts 
stress on the tubing. The larger the columns usually are the higher probability is there for it breaks. 
Another possibility is that the column could have been contaminated during other experiments. Non-volatile 
residues do not elute but collect in the column, which further hinder the partitioning of solutes out of 
stationary phase. Additionally they may also interact with the separation of solutes and cause peak tailings, 
loss of peak size and baseline shift. 
The injection technique used for all the experiments was direct injection. Even though this technique gives 
great resolution and increased peak area it also causes residue accumulation due to large amount of sample 
being injected directly into column. Other possible contaminants are 
materials in gas lines, ferrule and septa particles (due to septum becoming 
damaged as a result of many injections). The first thing that was performed 
to minimize the disturbance in the system was to bake out the column, which 
includes having the column at high temperatures for a long time. The column 
temperature was set to the isothermal temperature limit of the column 
(300  ) and kept at this temperature for 30 min. A consequence of this 
method is that it can change the contaminating residues to materials (most 
likely insoluble) that cannot be removed by simply solvent rinsing. 
Additionally to get rid of these errors the gas liner were checked for 
contaminations. Decomposed samples and septa can accumulate in the glass 
liner. As seen in Figure 44 our glass liner (left) is contaminated with green 
and red septa as well as other decomposed samples (indicated with the 
change of the color in the bottom). Glass wool was used near the bottom to 
seal the glass liner outlet in order to entrap particles and prevent them from 
going into the column. To prevent the rubber septa from going into the GC 
liner or column, it was renewed for approximately every 20-30 injections. 
Likewise, the inert press fit glass column connector (right in Figure 44) was 
changed due to contaminations from the column as well. A piece of the column in the press fit indicates that 
a part of the column may have been broken inside the press fit connector.   
A frequently experienced problem through the single runs with the GC was the front inlet pressure and the 
gas detector reporting a “shut down” message. However most probably this was caused due to the damaged

septa which resulted in a system leak or it could be that some part of the detector (a module that

The prior method that was utilized to determine the methanol content in the reformate condensate was GC 
headspace method. The condensate with methanol/water was transferred into certified vials and heated on a 
water bath at 65   (bp. MeOH) in 5 minutes. Due to evaporation the methanol vapor was collected at the top 
of the sealed (septa seal) certified vial. The methanol vapor was retrieved from the vial by inserting the tip of 
the syringe through the septa from the top. The vapor was collected and injected further into the GC. 
However no manageable results were obtained. 
The obtained results from the experiments generally show a hydrogen content of 75% at 0.41 m3/h∙Icat 
(corresponding to a flow setting 30) at 280   and 260   for all three pressure settings. At 240   the content 
of hydrogen is only 73 %. At atmospheric pressure we have that the H2 content is more efficient as from flow 
setting 30–140 (0.41 m3/h∙Icat – 3.80 m3/h∙Icat) the H2 only decreases with 5% for 280   and 260  . However 
H2 content at 240   decreases to 65% at same conditions. The highest methanol conversion is at 1 atm 
obtained with 280   at 1.37 m3/h∙Icat (flow 60) and 1.95 m3/h∙Icat (flow 80) indicating 95-96% methanol 
conversion. Overall hydrogen yield 1.05–2.56 m3/h∙Icat) gives > 90% methanol conversion. At 260   only 
1.66 m3/h∙Icat (flow 70) and 2.26 m3/h∙Icat (flow 90) gives   90% conversion. 240   does not meet any of 
these requirements as the conversion is 88% and lower for all the settings. Additionally, the highest CO2 
content is observed at 240  , giving 29% CO2 at 4.14 m3/h∙Icat. The highest obtained CO2 at 280   is 25.5% 
whereas 27% CO2 is acquired at 260  . 
Temperature 240   is not examined further with 0.5 bar and 1 bar due to insufficient methanol conversion 
and hydrogen content in the reformate gas.   
The reformate gas with 0.5 bar gives 75.02% and 75.4% H2 at 0.32 m3/h∙Icat for 280   and 260   
respectively. Overall better results for hydrogen is obtained at 260   with 0.5 bar. However looking at the 
methanol conversion we have that 280   gives higher conversion especially from 0.35-1.39 m3/h∙Icat (flow: 
30 40, 50, 60). The conversion is 100% at 0.35-0.90 m3/h∙Icat whereas it is 95% at 1.39 m3/h∙Icat. At 260   
the CO and CO2 content are overall lower by 1.5% and 2 % compared to 280  . 
The H2 and CO contents acquired at 1 bar show higher H2 and CO contents at 280   compared to 260  . At 
2.41 m3/h∙Icat (flow 80) we have 72.4% H2 and 0.7% CO with 280   whereas 70.8% H2 and 0.4% CO is 
obtained with 260  . No measurements at 1 bar were performed to determine methanol conversion and CO2 
content in the reformate gas due to limited time.   
The CO contents are noticed to be at a higher level with rising temperature. Comparing the results from 
Emonts et al. 1997 it is clear that lower CO contents are obtained with our reformer (at hydrogen yields 
==PAGE:75==
Page 75 of 95 
 
between 2 m3/h∙Icat–4 m3/h∙Icat). Furthermore it is stated in Emonts et al. 1997 that operation conditions below 
95% methanol conversion are not of interest for operating a CR. In this case the only operating settings of 
our reformer that fits the requirements at atmospheric pressure are the loads 60 (1.37 m3/h∙Icat) and 80 (1.95 
m3/h∙Icat) at 280  . At 0.5 bar it is flows: 30, 40, 50 and 60 at 280   and flow 30 and 40 at 260  . Based on 
these requirements a possible operating point for the reformer may be 1.39 m3/h∙Icat at 0.5 bar with 280   
and 1.95 m3/h∙Icat at 1 atm with 280   for a conversion rate of 95%.  
A possible future work would be to exploit gas cleaning unit and fuel cell (PEMFC) to the CMR setup in 
order to produce electricity.

8. Conclusion 
Due to the environmental impact of using automobiles, companies are seeking for potential application of 
hydrogen energy in vehicles. By the use of non-fossil fuels in preparation of hydrogen the vehicle propulsion 
is not expected to have any contribution to CO2 emissions. Steam reforming of methanol was utilized on a 
compact reformer to obtain high yield H2 production. Methanol is preferred as reformer fuel due the non-
presence of C-C bonds that needs to be broken (in contrast to methane) and for its low reforming temperature 
(approximately 160-300  ). 
Earlier studies and test of compact methanol reformer have shown 95% conversion of methanol at 280  . 
Our CMR was expected to be more efficient due to the use of the aluminum block with more heating 
elements compared to earlier setup designs. The CMR was tested at three different temperature settings: 240 
 , 260   and 280   for a pressure of 1 atm, 0.5 bar and 1 bar. The reformate gas were analyzed on a GC. 
The H2 content was detected being 75% H2 at 280   and 260   for all the pressure settings with the lowest 
load (flow 30   0.40 m3/h∙Icat). Higher CO contents in the reformate gas was detected at approximately 0.40 
m3/h∙Icat giving for a temperature of 280   2.9%, 2.7% and 2.4% at 1 bar, 0.5 bar and 1 atm respectively. 
The highest %CO2 and lowest %H2, %CO and conversion was observed at 240   indicating insufficient 
reforming. The highest methanol conversion was obtained at 280  . Looking at an operating point of 95% 
conversion the optimal flow was 60 and 80 (1.37 m3/h∙Icat and 1.95 m3/h∙Icat) at 1 atm and 50 (0.89 m3/h∙Icat) 
and 60 (1.39 m3/h∙Icat) at 0.5 bar. For these two pressure settings and the loads at 280   a CO2 content of 23-
24 % and CO of 1-1.7% were obtained. During the gas detection analysis on a GC varying peak areas were 
obtained for the pure reference compounds as well as the reformate compounds. The variation in the peak 
areas made it difficult to reproduce results. A high uncertainty could be due to manual injection of the 
sample gas in the GC port. Alternatively an auto sampler can be utilized for further test of CMR gas 
contents. CO content at higher flows was realized causing leakage from the system. Eventually higher flows 
(above 120 at 1 atm and 0.5 bar or higher than 90 at 1 bar) could be excluded from the test. It was possible 
with this study to demonstrate high H2 production and lower CO content in the reformate gas compared to 
earlier studies.
