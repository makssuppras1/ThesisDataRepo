Abstract 
This report refers to the implementation of BIM regarding the use of the Prefabrication 
method in construction projects. This study also demonstrates the impact the Construction 
Logistics has on the general design and construction process of a project, always 
considering Prefabrication. The lack of a proper logistical planning and at the same time 
the existence of the impression that is costly, can influence the construction activity of a 
project. Specifically, Prefabrication, as it is directly related with Construction Logistics it 
seems it has affected the project managers and clients’ decisions to adapt offsite fabrication 
into their projects. Despite the benefits that this method can provide, project participants, 
in most cases do not prefer to adapt prefabrication. The main focus of this study was how 
BIM can influence the Offsite Manufacturing Process (OMP) and Construction Logistics 
and how this triangle can be successfully implemented in large scale and complex projects 
even if those demanding and challenging projects are located in crowed and busy city 
centers.  
 
This report concludes that the prospect of a further evolution of BIM and its functions on 
the Offsite Fabrication method can reduce the overall construction time and cost of large-
scale projects. Also, the elimination of the errors through the level of development of 
design process, logistical planning accuracy, the use of ICT, and the recording of the 
detailed monitoring operation, have proved that large construction projects are capable to 
fully adapt prefabrication.  
 
The report actualizes that a new generation regarding the construction approach of a project 
is meant to change the way project stakeholders implement BIM. This shift, from the 
regular construction operation and the limited use of BIM and Prefabrication to projects 
where Offsite Manufacturing Process will be entirely adapted, is possible.

1.1 Background 
To begin with, it is important to mention that AEC industry consists of three different 
domains and the strong connection between them. Specifically, Architecture, Engineering 
and Construction create what we call today AEC industry. This industry is widely project 
based and every project is considered as a unique operation even if it might have the same 
characteristics from some of the previous ones. Thus, the location, the owners, the users 
and the stakeholders even the society, are considered as those specific parameters which 
make each construction project unique. In addition, differences can be found in the design 
and construction process between projects due to the aforementioned elements which can 
result in the limitation of the efficiency and productivity. 
These two meanings should be reconsidered in order to increase the value of the AEC 
industry. Building Information Modeling (BIM) is that kind of tool which can boost the 
entire process, techniques and practices in a project. According to the BIM Handbook 
(Chuck Eastman, 2011), BIM has been characterized as the most promising development 
in AEC industry. With BIM technology more than one accurate virtual models can be 
constructed digitally, something that provides a better analysis and control during the 
design process. The advantage is seen during the reuse of these digitally formed models, 
which contain the precise geometry and data, for the support of other activities such as the 
construction, procurement – supply chain and offsite fabrication. This way, the transition 
from the digital form to the reality of the project can become feasible. 
BIM is defined by The National Building Information Model Standards (NBIMS) 
(National Institute of Building Sciences, 2015) as: “BIM is the digital representation of 
physical and functional characteristics of a facility. As such it serves as a shared 
knowledge resource for information about a facility forming a reliable basis for decisions 
during its lifecycle inception onward”  
Beyond the digital capabilities, BIM provides managerial benefits regarding collaboration, 
communication and problem solving situations among the project team of the project 
(Chuck Eastman, 2011). 
Furthermore, the offsite fabrication and the supply of the manufactured components to the 
construction site require considerable planning and accuracy during the design phase. 
Together with the reduction of the labor costs, risks is one of the aspects that contractors 
want to apply in a project. The idea behind the offsite fabrication method is the breaking 
down process of the pieces of the project, in order to be assembled easily in the construction 
site. For that reason BIM provides contractors with the capability to input BIM component 
details directly, including 3D geometry, material specifications, finishing requirements, 
delivery sequence and timing, before and during the fabrication process (Chuck Eastman, 
2011, pg. 295). 
There are different links in this process, from the prefabricated components design and the 
manufacture of them to the delivery of these parts to the construction site and their 
assembly. These links are described as CSCM (Construction Supply Chain Management). 
==PAGE:11==
 
3 
 
It is a sector that can potentially be developed further along with the continuous 
development of BIM. 
According to the Improving Construction Logistics report (Strategic Forum for 
Construction Logistics, 2005) the construction industry’s development has been slower in 
contrast to other industries such as the manufacturing industry. However, there is a lot of 
potential for a significant change in AEC industry as long as its stakeholders realize the 
benefits that the proper design of good logistics can provide.   
         
1.2 Problem Definition 
BIM is a key factor for the transformation of the construction industry. As it has been 
recently established in the AEC industry, the majority of projects’ participants can see the 
benefits in its outcome regarding time planning and cost estimation. Also, benefits have 
observed during the design phase and the construction progress, the level of accuracy of 
the models and eventually, the collaboration and coordination of the project participants.  
It has also influenced the offsite fabrication process. The manufacturing of the 
prefabricated components has been transformed to one of the most developed fields in AEC 
industry with a lot of potential for a further development in the future. A typical example 
is the construction of a high rise residential block in China1, where the construction process 
was completed within 19 days (The Guardian, 2015). The entire building was made from 
modules where all the necessary installations have been implemented in the factory’s 
controlled and safe environment.  
Therefore, this example could be considered as the beginning of a new era in the AEC 
industry because of the use of BIM and Prefabrication. However, regarding prefabrication, 
as it was mentioned in the SmartMarket Report (McGraw Hill, 2011), it has been observed 
that the transportation-logistics of the components from the storage location to the 
construction site is something that the owners and in general the participants of the projects 
should pay attention to. The reason is the high cost of this operation which can lead in 
negative results similar to the previous methods. In addition, Construction Supply Chain 
often does not have a standard and well-structured configuration (Jack C.P. Cheng, 2010). 
Furthermore, it is important to clarify, that the BIM use in a project, especially in the 
Prefabrication Method and supply chain, might be depended on the level of project 
complexity and the needs of each organization. In that case, constructing, for example, a 
single house using BIM, would not bring the desired financial result. On the contrary, the 
outcome would be considered as a non-profitable investment for the company because of 
the expensive license cost of BIM tools.

1.3 Aim of the project 
The purpose of this master Thesis is the investigation of BIM’s impact on prefabrication 
and construction logistics in construction projects. Specifically, the goal is to demonstrate 
how the prefabrication method can be entirely applied in large scale and complex projects 
using BIM.  
Also, the aim of this study is to focus on the issues of the supply of prefabricated 
components and what the role of BIM is in this field. How the whole operation could 
become more effective and more integrated for the success of the project completion will 
be discussed. Eliminating the errors during the design phase a reduction of the total cost 
would be achieved. Also, improving the efficiency and specifying the role of each 
participant, a further reduction of the strict timescales would be reached. 
Moreover, the current literature, theories, perspectives and interview would contribute to 
the development of a more coordinated strategy which would clarify the participants’ role 
in the supply stages of the project.  
 
1.4 Research question 
The research question of this report is: 
 How far can Prefabrication go based on BIM’s functions? 
Sub-questions formed in the early stages of this study, will be used as a guideline in order 
to avoid any deviations from the target of the investigation. The questions are: 
 What is the connection between BIM, Prefabrication and Construction Logistics? 
 What is the impact of BIM on construction logistics during construction process? 
 How much prefabrication is used in large-scale and complex projects? 
 Is there any further development regarding the design and construction approach 
of a project?

3.1 BIM 
 
3.1.1 BIM definition 
It has been obvious that the AEC industry needs a change. The opportunity for this change 
appeared during the economic downturn in 2008 when the AEC industry was affected the 
most. The change that the industry experienced over time, was negligible. For example in 
U.S the construction industry relative to all nonfarm industries over a period of 45 years, 
from 1964 until 2009, has not seen any significant change (Chuck Eastman, 2011). 
The most recent significant improvement in the AEC industry is the implementation of 
Building Information Modeling (BIM). BIM is considered as the combination of the 
evolution of technology with the newest information management systems. For a better 
understanding of what BIM is, it can be described as an integrated process built on 
coordinated, reliable information about a project from design through construction and into 
operations (Autodesk, 2008). By adopting BIM, architects, engineers, contractors and 
owners can easily create coordinated, digital design information and documentation; use 
that information to accurately visualize, simulate, and analyze performance, appearance 
and cost; and reliably deliver the project faster, more economically and with reduced 
environmental impact (Autodesk, 2008).   
The inefficient approaches of traditional design and construction practices have been 
replaced by a 3D model which supplies all project’s participants, Architects, Engineers and 
Constructors, with a more integrated perception upon the project. It also offers upgraded 
tools for a more efficient approach regarding planning, design, construction and 
management of buildings. 
 
3.1.2 What is NOT BIM? 
In addition, together with the definition of what is BIM, it should be equally mentioned 
what is not BIM. 
Thus, to avoid any confusion of the utilities BIM can offer, below four definitions that BIM 
users should have in mind are described, according to BIM Handbook ed. 2 (Chuck 
Eastman, 2011): 
 Models that contain 3D data only and no (or few) project attributes. Specifically, 
it refers to models that are just fine regarding the visualization purpose of it but 
cannot provide any support for data integration and design analysis. One example 
is Google’s Sketch Up application which is considered as an excellent tool for 
schematic design but is not suitable for any other type of analysis apart from the 
geometry and appearance of the structure. In addition, Rolf Büchmann-Slorup, 
Head of Department at MT Højgaard’s VDC project office, during the interview 
agreed that Google’s application is that kind of tool that cannot offer you more 
than a good visualization result (see Appendix).  
==PAGE:20==
 
12 
 
 Models with no support of behavior. It is that category of models which cannot 
utilize parametric intelligence since the results do not provide protection against 
the creation of inconsistent or inaccurate views of model. 
 Models that are composed of multiple 2D CAD reference files that must be 
combined to define the building, and 
 Models that allow changes to dimensions in one view but are not automatically 
reflected in other views.

3.1.4 What are the benefits regarding construction and fabrication? 
In order for the fabrication part of the BIM cycle to be enabled the method of breaking 
down the structure into pieces should be adopted. Therefore, the detailed designed pieces 
are already defined in 3D and the stage of their automated fabrication, by the use of 
technology based on the numerical control machinery, is followed.  This technology 
enables its users to develop in greater detail, to process the designed pieces as well as to 
retain links that demonstrate the design. This can reduce the cost and the construction time 
(Chuck Eastman, 2011, pg. 23). In addition, BIM is beneficial since the detailing/accuracy 
that it provides in the design components, allows parts of the building structure to be 
manufactured offsite, from the smallest to the larger ones. This would not have happened 
in the traditional way of design method, with 2D drawings, due to the 
limitations/restrictions in the way components are designed (Chuck Eastman, 2011, pg. 
23). This tremendous contrast is obvious when BIM allows the design of even the smaller 
installation parts of a project such as screws. 
 
Moreover, in Chuck Eastman’s BIM Handbook, edition 2 page 24, a quicker response to 
any possible changes in the design phase could be succeeded with the use of BIM system. 
Through the clash detection process, the design responsible may proceed to the checking 
operation of the structure by combining the models together. Any updates can easily appear 
visually by instantly indicating all the reflected consequences of the project in an accuracy. 
Also, the BIM system can easily share, estimate and resolve all the aforementioned 
consequences by discarding the time-consuming traditional “paperwork”.  
Furthermore, BIM’s Handbook, (Chuck Eastman, 2011, pg. 24) explains that the virtual 
3D models can eliminate the appearance of the errors through the clash detection process. 
All the conflicts and constructability problems of a structure can be detected and, therefore, 
can easily be resolved. The reduction of the errors in this phase is characterized as one of 
==PAGE:22==
 
14 
 
the most crucial parts with a direct reflection to the overall cost and the delivery time of 
the project. This provides a leaner process to the team. 
 
A significant benefit the BIM system can add to the design and construction process of a 
project is the synchronization of the planning of these two processes. According to BIM 
Handbook, (Chuck Eastman, 2011 pg. 24) by using the 4th dimension of the BIM cycle 
(Figure 2) linking the 3D model of a design with the construction process could be easily 
achieved. At that point, BIM’s 4th dimension can provide to the project’s participants 
information regarding the day-by-day construction process by revealing problems. This 
offers potential opportunities for further improvements regarding site organization, crews 
and equipment. It also provides information on the construction objects which play either 
a major role to the process or have a limited reflection to it. Cranes and scaffolding are 
examples of highly important objects. The main point is that every single construction 
activity either big or limited can be linked to the design and construction process in order 
to provide a more integrated schedule planning.  
 
Another important positive aspect that the BIM system provides to construction and 
fabrication is the synchronization of the procurement with the design and construction. The 
BIM Handbook, (Chuck Eastman, 2011, pg. 25) indicates the accurate quantities of all the 
materials and objects that are included in the design phase. It is important for the 
subcontractor and the vendors to know the exact number of materials that are needed during 
the construction phase, together with the specifications and properties of them. 
 
It is mentioned in BIM Handbook, (Chuck Eastman, 2011, pg. 24 – 25) that a better 
coordination between the general contractor and the subcontractors is needed in order to 
ensure the work in the construction site. The resources provided come from the 
implementation of Lean Construction Techniques. As mentioned before, BIM can provide 
high level of accuracy on the design of the materials resources that are required for the 
construction progress. Thus, the planning and scheduling of the subcontractors’ work can 
be improved as well as enhancing the Just-In-Time arrival of people, equipment and 
material on site. As a result, this model achieves the integration of collaboration among the 
construction site teams. A more analytical description of Lean Construction Techniques 
appears below.      
    
3.1.6 Lean construction 
It is crucial for the reader of this study to understand what Lean Construction is. First of 
all, the method Lean Construction is directly connected to the meaning of Lean production 
which first appeared, tested and developed by the Japanese car manufacturing company 
TOYOTA led by Engineer Ohno (Howell, 1999). Specifically, the article The Machine 
That Changed The World: The Story Of Lean Production (Womack, 1991) determined the 
term Lean, which was coined by the research team working on international auto 
production, to reflect both the waste reduction nature of the Toyota production system and 
to contrast it with craft and mass forms of production.  
 
Lean manufacturing has a great influence in the construction industry, and although the 
aforementioned meaning of Lean was adopted 20 years ago, in reality, it has not been 
==PAGE:23==
 
15 
 
established in the construction industry as it was considered a more unique domain than 
the manufacturing one (Intergraph White Paper, 2012) 
 
According to BIM Handbook, (Cuck Eastman, 2011, pg. 325) the term Lean Construction 
is related to the process improvement in order for building and facilities to meet the 
requirements of the client by restricting the consumption of the resources. It also mentions 
that lean construction focuses on the elimination of any obstacles and the removal of any 
bottlenecks that appear, thus it aims to the control of the workflow through stability.  
A typical example of the extended duration of subcontractors’ tasks is the lack of 
communication among them and the lack of a proper planning before and/or during their 
working process where things tend to shift into unstable and unpredictable situations. As a 
consequence the subcontractors justify this situation by blaming other subcontractors that 
they have failed to implement their planning during the construction progress. Specifically, 
reasons such as the inability to have the task completed on time, the delay in the delivery 
of the necessary materials, are often used by subcontractors (Chuck Eastman, 2011, pg. 
325).  
In order for unpleasant situations, like the aforementioned, to be avoided, Chuck Eastman 
with his BIM Handbook (2011), mentions that 
“One of the primary ways to expose waste and improve flow is to adopt pull flow control, 
in which work is only performed when the demand for it is made apparent downstream in 
the process, with the ultimate pull signal provided by the end of each process by the client.” 
He also underlines that 
“Waste is not only material waste but process waste: time spent waiting for inputs, rework 
and the like” 
BIM system has the ability to enable the Lean Construction Method which can influence 
the participants in a prefab project. According to the BIM Handbook, (Chuck Eastman, 
2011, pg. 326) these contributions could be: 
 
 The greater use of prefabrication – preassembly and in general the offsite 
manufacturing process as the errors in the design phase are reduced due to the 
virtual construction. In addition, as the accuracy is increased, regarding the 
components/parts of the structure, this leads to shortened product cycle times from 
the fabricator’s side. Also, the creation of these components/parts of the structure 
in the factory’s controlled environment, enhances the site safety as the workers 
work under safer conditions. 
 The enhancement of the workflow stability, as the careful planning of all the stages 
of the project activities are checked every week. The Lean Construction’s tool that 
is commonly used, for this checking process, is the “Last Planner System2” in which

 The improvement of teamwork and coordination among the participants of a project

 The impact on the time that is needed for the fabrication and the delivery of the

3.1.7 Integration project delivery 
Based on the Integrated Project Delivery: A Guide (AIA, 2007) the IPD is defined as: 
“…a project delivery approach that integrates people, systems, business structures and 
practices into a process that collaboratively harnesses the talents and insights of all 
participants to optimize project results, increase value to the owner, reduce waste, and 
maximize efficiency through all phases of design, fabrication, and construction.” 
 
Historically, the IPD as well as the Lean Construction Method mentioned in the previous 
section, were used mostly in the manufacturing industry. Companies such as TOYOTA 
and Boeing follow a strict approach in all stages of the manufacturing process on their 
products in order to be approved and be brought in the market. In general the final product 
development process is relied upon information-rich digital models that are used for the 
design, manufacture, field support etc. (Autodesk Whitepaper, 2008). 
 
In the AEC field, IPD operates the same as in the manufacturing industry. Specifically, 
according to the Integrated Project Delivery and Building Information Modeling (Autodesk 
Whitepaper, 2008) the strategy that is used facilitates: 
 
“Cross-functional project teams collaborating on a building’s design, construction, and 
lifecycle management for optimized owner outcomes—using collaborative, model-based 
technology as a platform.” 
 
In the BIM Handbook, (Chuck Eastman, 2011, pg. 9) this kind of collaboration starts from 
the early design stages of a project until the delivery of it to the client. The key is the 
collaboration of the teams by working together and using the most appropriate tools for the 
integration of the project in order for the client’s requirements to be met within time and 
cost. Then, the owner of the project becomes a part of the project in two ways: either by 
being a part of the team for the decisions taken or by hiring a representative consultant or 
the combination of them. BIM is that kind of tool which can be a “catalyst” for any decision 
taken regarding the design of the project on matters such as cost, energy, functionality,

3.2 Prefabrication  
 
3.2.1 What is prefabrication/offsite fabrication? 
Taking into account the previous section, offsite fabrication is a method that has rapidly 
grown internationally and has started to become widely accepted from project participants.  
The latest statistical data for the offsite fabrication show that this sector together with the 
evolution of technology, during the last years, has gained a share of 3 – 4% of the annual 
global building construction activity (£ 100 billion) which can rise up to 80% of the 
construction cost for some projects (Nigel Fraser et al, 2015). 
  
According to the An Offsite Guide for the Building and Engineering Services Sector (Nigel

created temporary production facilities close to the construction site (or field factories)”

assembly operation. Based on An Offsite Guide for the Building and Engineering Services

3.2.4.2 Drawbacks 
Beyond the many advantages the offsite fabrication process provides, there are some 
challenges for the full implementation of it. According to the SmartMarket report 
Prefabrication and Modularization: Increasing Productivity in the Construction Industry 
(McGraw Hill, 2011) these challenges are directly connected to the early commitment 
regarding engineering and design work, the increase of transportation logistics 
requirements and the limitation of the available providers. Specifically 
 Early Commitment 
 
The participants of the project consider this method as an inflexible operation where 
the design work has to be completed before the manufacture and construction 
assembly process. As it requires a precise-defined planning, the participants 
observe constrains regarding their delivery strategy. 
 
 Transportation logistics requirements (Construction Logistics) 
 
One of the key stage for the successful implementation of the offsite fabrication 
method is the consistency of the supply chain focusing on the transportation of the 
prefabricated components. Barriers such as size and weight limitations, route 
restrictions, permitting requirements and the need for lifting equipment need to be 
taken into consideration in the very early planning stage. Also, the outcome from a 
not functional planning would lead to an increase of the overall cost of the project. 
The next section analyses the impact of construction logistics on the offsite 
fabrication. Also, the role of BIM will be further described. 
 
 Providers 
 
Due to constraints on its logistical aspect offsite fabrication faces some limitations. 
The number of the available providers is limited, automatically adding an extra 
“headache” for the participants of the project during the preconstruction stage as 
well as producing the prefabricated components.

3.3 Construction Logistics 
There is no doubt that construction is characterized from its assembly process (Alan 
Mossman, 2007). In addition, construction projects are balanced in a thin line during their 
delivery progress where uncertainties such as weather, which can influence project 
schedule and quality, have to be taken into consideration (K-C. Shih & S-S. Liu, 2010). 
For that reason - also linked to the previous section - offsite fabrication is a construction 
method that can eliminate the uncertainties due to the manufacturing operation which takes 
place in a factory’s controlled environment (K-C. Shih & S-S. Liu, 2010).  
 
Construction logistics play a significant role to the progress of the project. However, it is 
not considered as a common topic in the AEC industry despite the benefits that a proper 
planning can provide during the construction process. These benefits are: an increase in the 
construction productivity, safety and delivery improvements as well as a reduction in 
carbon footprint (Alan Mossman, 2007) 
 
In general for the implementation of the prefab method at least four participants should 
collaborate with each other and they are: client, architect, contractor and prefab 
manufacturer. Thus, the connection among BIM, Prefabrication and Construction Logistics 
can contribute to the improvement of the construction operation from the manufacture of 
prefab components till the onsite assembly of them. Two of the major factors that can be 
influenced during this operation are cost and schedule planning which should be included 
in the aforementioned connection in the early design phase of the project.        
 
3.3.1 What Construction Logistics is and how it works 
Construction Logistics is a sensitive subject regarding the lean operation of the construction 
process. Construction logistics according to Serra and Oliveira (2003) is: 
 
“A multidisciplinary process applied to a given construction to ensure the supply, storage, 
processing and availability of material resources on the construction site, to dimension the 
production teams and to manage the physical flows of production”. 
 
Based on K-C. Shih & S-S. Liu’s (2010) article, An Optimization Model for Precast Project 
Planning Using Group Concepts, a prefab project is consisted from hundreds – thousands 
components depending on the size of the project. They also mention that in real time 
projects, these prefab components are being standardized into groups in order for three 
advantages to be achieved: components can be unified simplifying the workflow, high 
production can be achieved where resources can be utilized repeatedly, and components 
can have the same quality even if they work as substitutes. Linked to these advantages is a 
high degree of standardization that is necessary to be established otherwise the offsite 
fabrication method could be proved inefficient. 
 
In addition, K-C. Shih & S-S. Liu (2010) described the stages that are followed for the lean 
progress of the construction phase of a prefab project. Specifically, it is important that 
before the planning of the project, information regarding the components that will be used 
in the project, is sufficient, for the proper preparation of the manufacturing process. 
Moreover, the project’s components are grouped according to the standardized shape, 
==PAGE:40==

material and strength. The architect’s components design contributes to this categorization. 
Then, the production line is ready to begin taking into account the specific installation 
information. This kind of information indicates what the quantity of the components is and 
when they should be delivered to the construction site. Thus, the factory would be in 
position to arrange the exact quantity of the resources which are required for the beginning 
of the manufacturing process.

the storage stages. Specifically 
 
 Molds:  
As mentioned before, for the production stage, component information is 
needed. Molds can produce components which are aligned with the same 
component type. Furthermore, due to the high cost of the steel, molds’ 
utility is a crucial part for the factory’s production line. It could be turned 
into a key issue. 
 
In addition, components which are identical in the matter of shape, 
material and strength can be produced with the same resources. Also, the 
same type of molds can be used for the production of specific type of 
components.  
 
In general the whole production line should be aligned with the molds’ 
utilization, therefore planners should organize the production of the 
project’s components according to the molds’ type. Furthermore, it is clear 
that the resources of the factory are completely harmonized with the molds.  
However, some barriers have to be taken into consideration, such as: the 
type and quantity of the molds that are needed for a successful prefab 
components production, the level of the efficiency and the cost of such a 
mold utilization, the avoidance of any possible change in the type and use 
of the molds and other resource issues. 
 
 Components and zones: 
Concerning the component’s storing process, 2 or 3-dimensional problems 
with loading considerations should be taken into account. As components 
are considered difficult to be stored after their production stage, the ways 
that should be followed for the storage of them depends on the experience 
of the planner. The cluster-analysis6 method is useful in the construction 
components storing phase.

Based on the article Value of Adopting Building Information Modeling (BIM) to the Supply

coordination during the supply chain operation. Also, these values are described: 

 Provides collaborative design and construction procedures as a common single 
source to support all the parties  
 Improves communication means between client, design professionals, contractors, 
suppliers and manufacturers  
 Improves individual satisfaction  
 Identifies possible risks and mitigates them  
 Information sharing between all the parties of the supply chain  
 Early cost certainty  
 Predictable planning  
 Lowers the cost of the supply chain (reduces logistical, delivery, operational 
costs)  
 Proper information exchange among supply chain parties  
 Provides an action plan for uncertain “In case” incidents.  
 Real time designing, constructing and estimation for architects, contractors and 
quantity surveyors   
 Develops sustainability all through the supply chain  
 Develops an expert visualization for all the parties to plan their roles and duties  
 Productivity is increased so that the result benefits all the parties  
 Before the actual building process, everything is built on a virtual media so that 
most of the possible risks, errors can be identified and get corrected to build up an 
accurate output. Reviews are possible before the actual application.  
 High level of Maintenance, operations and facilities management  
 BIM model obviously speeds up the whole process with a greater collaboration of 
work  
 Loss of data in between the supply chain is kept to a minimum  
==PAGE:46==
 
38 
 
 Many of the problems cannot be identified until the project is under construction. 
But BIM can identify them before the construction. Thus, it reduces errors. So 
that all the parties are confident about the design when the project is executed and 
ready to build.  
 Decision making for the supply chain has been easy because of the scheduling 
(4D) and costing (5D) based on multiple, accurate real-time information as these 
provide the best information that can be acquired at that moment.  
 BIM reduces reworking cost, time and labor

 Opens more job opportunities on BIM related jobs

during the operation of Construction Logistics. These constrains are: 
 
 Model can be changed without the input of all the stakeholders  
 Design and construction fees do not support a more efficient BIM model. Therefore

 BIM does not support the Legal and contractual Insurance aspects  
 Ownership of the model cannot be determined during the process stage and, thus,

 A committed proper learning and training is needed for the development of the

 2D drawings are generated at the first stage for the approvals but 3D drawings are 
distributed among sub-contractors. Shop drawings are distributed to all the supply 
chain parties and sometimes unnecessary parties also have access to the model, 
which gives an unsecured status. (Example: toilet fitting suppliers, etc.)  
 Constraints for the live face to face interaction of supply chain involvers. 
Minimizes the live contacts and makes most of the parties isolated with the 
automated model  
 Traditional roles of some professions have been minimized as the automated 
system originated them.  
 Arguments have arisen on the value of the positions of quantity surveyor and 
architects because of the BIM automation.  
 Specialist contractors (sub- contractors- small firms) struggle with high costs of 
BIM.

Moreover, the general contractor, in order to accomplish this prefabrication challenge, 
followed an approach which is already in use in the manufacture industry. Analytically, the 
method that was followed is called Design for Manufacture and Assembly (DfMA). This 
method aims to the production of a huge number of high-quality products in a more 
efficient way. Specifically, in this project, the design team managed to produce high-
quality construction components such as floor-slab elements, structural columns and 
==PAGE:60==

modular platforms by simplifying the whole supply chain from the modeling operation. In 
other words, it is considered as an update of the classic prefabrication approach, aligned 
with the evolution of automated technology, high speed production line and management 
approaches.  
 
In addition, this method had an impact on the general outcome of this project. The general 
contractor by implementing DfMA in the construction process, managed to achieve the 
goal of 70:60:30:0. In details, more than 70% of the project was achieved using offsite 
manufacture products which led to 60% reduction of the onsite workforce. For example, 
during the assembly process, only 6 people were needed to complete the fitting of precast 
concrete planks in an area of four tennis courts in a single day. Otherwise it would have 
taken twice the time and twice the man power. This was due to the ability of the 
prefabricated products, which took only ten minutes in order to be fitted on the structure. 
Also, 30% reduction in the general design programme of the project was/ achieved. The 
outcome from these percentages, from the safety and sustainability perspective, reached 
0% of onsite accidents and carbon emissions during the construction process.  
 
Moreover, in Laing O’Rourke’s paper Offsite Construction, this achievement become 
feasible due to Laing O’Rourke’s £100 million investment factory. The factory’s 
production line specialized on the production of concrete components using approaches 
similar to the ones that are used in automobile and aerospace sectors. The fact is that the 
production line of the factory uses machines which are controlled by 3D software. This 
kind of software is used, also, from the design team of the project. The general goal of this 
venture is the production of building components in offsite controlled condition with the 
most efficient way by leaving only the construction site preparation and assembly operation 
to be done in situ.  
    
4.1.3 Construction Logistics 
The logistical operation for the completion of the structure had played a key role. Taking 
into consideration the location of the building, characterized by narrow and crowded streets 
with restrictions in the movement of huge vehicles, any possible errors during the 
construction operation could have brought negative results. In addition, the restrictions 
existed also in the site and its boundaries as the available working space was limited 
especially for construction work areas such as the basement, cores and building services.   
 
Related to the construction method another challenge for the supply chain process was the 
transportation of huge prefabricated components. Specifically, for the construction of the 
megaframe of the building, the 24 m diagonal beams – almost the length of a standard 
swimming pool – had to be transported to the site and be assembled at the right time. 
However, in order to avoid the huge traffic in the city center and any possible delays in the 
time table, the whole operation would have to proceed during night hours between 1 am 
and 5 am when the police escort would be mandatory. In this case, using BIM, the 
participants of the project achieved to complete an efficient strategy for the just-in-time 
delivery and assembly.  
 
==PAGE:61==
 
53 
 
Moreover, in the delivery phase the project team decided to implement the Radio 
Frequency Identification (RFID) for the piloting of the project. With this application, the 
team was able to track and monitor the building prefabricated components from the 
manufacture, supply and installation process through the attached data tags on these 
components. The outcome was to control the workflow of the process and to prevent any 
delays in the general operation. 
 
Also, the connection of BIM and RFID could give rich data of the project, in real time. 
Therefore, the control is enhanced through these performance indicators. This 
technological aspect gave a little more integration in the construction of the building. 
 
Furthermore, part of the supply chain is the lifting process of these prefabricated 
components – “tables” and structural parts – where was proved to be an additional 
challenge in the overall construction operation. Analytically, the equipment for this 
process, cranes, had to be able to lift components up to 45 tones but the heaviest-duty crane 
available could “only” lift 32 tones. For that special occasion, the general contractor 
managed to make a special contract in order for two new more powerful cranes to be placed 
in the construction site. 
 
The extra-ordinary thing regarding construction logistics, was that the manufacturing and 
transportation operation of the prefabricated components and especially the “tables”, had 
to be delivered to the project location within 24 hours. This meant that the combination of 
BIM, Prefabrication, Construction Logistics upgraded the project to another level 
regarding time, where a group of 24 people managed to assemble 41 floors of these offsite 
manufactured “tables” in one year.

prefabricated project give some results and predictions regarding the future use of BIM in

6.1 Conclusion 
The purpose of this study was the investigation of how the design process of huge scale 
prefabricated projects could become more efficient through the proper use of BIM and how 
it can influence the logistical operation in the construction process. Observations have been 
made regarding the approaches that were followed in the case studies. The results showed 
that the level usage of BIM has a direct influence in all project phases. Starting from the 
decision regarding the use of Prefabrication method up to the delivery of the project in 
budget and on time.  
 
This study showed the impact BIM has on AEC industry and its connection with offsite 
fabrication. The strong bond between BIM and Prefabrication led to the general evolution 
of the manufacturing process and its supply chain. It is obvious that construction logistics 
is part of the design of a project something that is underestimated from the majority of the 
construction firms. As a result, bottle necks occurred during the construction process which 
influenced the general schedule and budget of the operation by simultaneously creating a 
chain reaction in all the tasks of the project. 
 
The 122 Leadenhall Building project showed that BIM, Prefabrication and Construction 
Logistics enhanced the project delivery. It also showed that proper planning of the project 
in the pre-construction phase could eliminate possible barriers that appear in different 
situations during the assembly process. The participants of the project agreed that new 
approaches regarding the design phase will lead to more successful results in the future. 
Approaches such as Digital Engineering with the use of BIM, DfMA combined with the 
right logistical planning and RFID and CNC technologies leverage the just-in-time 
delivery. 
 
Regarding Prefabrication, the shift from the onsite regular function to the offsite 
manufacture of the components was considered as a big challenge with a high level of risk.  
The case of an error in the design and manufacture operation of a single component could 
be catastrophic for the whole operation, maximizing the risk. However, 3D visualization, 
and the high level of accuracy provided through ICT, boost the monitoring of the supply 
chain of the offsite products leading to improvements in time, cost and information flow. 
 
However, the moment when a decision is taken for the implementation of Prefabrication in 
a project is important in order for it to be effective. Specifically, the figure below illustrates 
two conditions where the decision for using Prefabrication can influence the continuation 
of the overall assembly operation of the project. It is crucial the decision is taken at the 
early stages of the design process as it will contribute to the quality development of the 
project. Otherwise, obstacles such as delays and cost expansion would possibly appear if 
this crucial decision is taken before the beginning of the construction operation. This 
indicates that the communication between customers and responsible managers of the 
project should be solid in order for possible bottle necks in the supply chain operation of 
the project to be avoided.

Thus, the successful delivery of the project created the question “Why has it taken 
construction so long to start embracing techniques which have been embedded in 
manufacturing for decades?” Although BIM has given to Prefabrication method a new 
potential to be used in future projects, the biggest barrier is the implementation of all of 
BIM dimensions in the overall approach of a project. 
 
In addition, taking into consideration the event which took place in New York, World 
Architecture Day, in 2013, architects discussed the potential that Prefabrication method has 
on the construction of complex skyscrapers. The main subject, though, was if 
prefabrication can overcome this challenge or not. Vishaan Chakrabarti, partner at SHoP

construction is on track to achieving this. He, also, stated that modular construction can 
reduce the general cost from 20% to 25% while technology in materials and machinery as 
well as the improvement in the design progress increase quality.

Denmark’s construction industry has a great experience as it is considered one of the 
pioneers’ countries regarding the use of Prefabrication in construction projects. Also, it is 
worth pointing out that the Prefabrication activity in Denmark started in the 1960’s as the 
need for accommodation was increased due to World War II. Thus, taking data from 
Denmark’s construction activity contribute to a better analysis regarding the further 
improvement of Prefabrication in construction projects.

The creation of manufacturing centers in strategic locations, based on the demand of 
prefabrication projects, could be an interesting study topic. The investigation of those

strong parameter that companies should take into account in order such a concept to be

Fabrication Method and the impact that Construction Logistics had on the construction 
process on large-scale and complex projects such as the case studies in Chapter 4. However, 
the whole analysis of this investigation was based only on web search, interviews and 
bibliography. Thus, the outcome was considered more theoretical from the initial preset 
goal of this report. Therefore, a future research based on numerical results and the 
participation of the author in real time projects is recommended. The analysis and the 
recording of the BIM usage in the design phase, the decision and the crucial planning of 
the offsite manufacturing process and the coordination – monitoring of the supply chain 
during the construction process would bring a better optimization in the overall approach 
of a future study. 
 
Another recommendation is the participation of the author in the manufacturing process of 
a project’s components in a factory. By observing all the stages and recording possible 
barriers would enable the analysis of the evolution of this process and propose strategies 
for the integration of it. In addition, monitoring the flexibility the factory provides 
regarding each prefab order would be an addition to the aforementioned proposals. 
 
Beyond these, the most important recommendation is the further analysis of BIM functions 
and its adjustments in the Prefabrication method and Construction Logistics. How the 
development of parametric software and their numerical algorithms in the design process 
of the project components can influence the implementation of the offsite manufacturing 
operation in a project’s sequence. Therefore, the creation and the analysis of these 
components digitally from the architectural, structural, energy and clash detection 
perspective as well as their production line process, transportation and assembly of them 
on construction site, would lead to the emerge of significant data. Also, a further 
investigation of the requirements of BIM Level 3 could give additional information on 
BIM’s future development. Most importantly, the further investigation of how Digital 
Engineering can minimize factors such as risk and waste from the project increasing quality 
through the development of parametric software, such as Dynamo, within the 
Prefabrication section.

Moreover, the impact that International Standards Organizations have on the AEC industry 
evolution and the creation of a global BIM community under the same roof should be 
evaluated. The pros and cons of this operation could provide important data for the purpose 
of these International Standards Organizations.
