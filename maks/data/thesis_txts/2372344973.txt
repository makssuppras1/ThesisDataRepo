Abstract 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is now a common tool, used for different purposes and in different 
sectors (industry, policy making, research, etc.). One of the most discussed points in the LCA 
methodology is the modelling of multi-functional processes. Indeed, the choices made for the modelling 
of multifunctionality have a considerable influence on the results of an LCA study. A particular case of 
multifunctionality, but which appears in many studies, is the modelling of recycling. 
Recycling is always considered in a life cycle where materials are used and it has two functions: 
treating the waste and producing a new raw material. Besides, a material can be recycled several times and 
used in very different products, so many life-cycles can be considered. Therefore, the impacts related to 
the recycling activity need to be allocated between these two functions and between different life cycles: 
the life-cycle that produces the recycled material and the life-cycle that uses the material. The issue of 
recycling is also gaining importance in general in waste treatment but also in product design, especially in 
Europe where it is encouraged by the European institutions. 
Recycling can be modelled by several methods that stem from different approaches of LCA. There 
are currently many standards and guidelines available for companies that would like to perform LCA to 
assess the environmental performances of their products. However, even though all these standards 
generally claim to comply with the ISO 14040 and ISO 14044 requirements, the lack of clarity in the ISO 
standards and the debates among the LCA community, have led to different interpretations and therefore 
to different methods usable by companies. The recent years have also seen the development of new 
methods for handling recycling in LCA, especially with the pilot phase of the Product Environmental 
Footprint (PEF) that aims to be the reference standard in Europe for LCA of products. 
But the modelling choices for recycling can lead to very different results and may have an impact 
on the final decision-making, and there is therefore a need for a clear overview of what these modelling 
choices impart for the results. The main goal of this master thesis is therefore to investigate this issue of 
recycling in LCA from a company perspective. The objectives will be to understand how companies deal 
with this issue of recycling in LCA, what are their problems and what the consequences are whether they 
choose a method or another one.   
First, a literature study enables the full understanding of the theory behind the modelling of 
recycling in LCA. Especially, the issue of attributional and consequential modelling is developed, as well 
as the use of substitution or partitioning to solve multifunctionality in recycling. An overview of standards 
and guidelines for LCA of products shows that end-of-life formulas can be classified in general methods: 
the recycled content method, the end-of-life recycling method, the waste mining method and the methods 
based on the number of recycling loops. It also shows an opposition between methods favouring the use of 
recycled material and the ones favouring the recyclability of products. The evolution of end-of-life 
formulas, the PEF being the last one, tends toward more complex formulas that are actually mixes of 
different methods. The new PEF formula is even a mix of fundamentally opposed approaches. 
The modelling of a case study with six different methods illustrates the fact that the choice of the 
recycling method in LCA of products is rarely questioned but it has a strong influence on the results and 
as a consequence on the recommendations that can be given from these results. However, the PEF formula 
offers an opportunity to assess the influence of the choice of the end-of-life method easily. The interviews 
of LCA consultants and the modelling of a case study have enabled the identification of other practical 
problems for modelling recycling like the difficulty to find data despite the increasing number of 
parameters in end-of-life formulas and the necessity to have a dedicated database to use an end-of-life 
formula.  
Generally, this master thesis highlights the necessity of having a good understanding of the methods 
for modelling recycling in LCA of products. Understanding their underlying principles, their biases and 
their structure enables a better modelling of recycling in practice and the good interpretation of LCA 
results. It is all the more important in the case of the PEF end-of-life formula because of its higher 
complexity.

Introduction 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is an environmental assessment tool that can be apply to products. Its 
use is growing. Indeed, this tool can have many applications for a company: improving the design of its 
products, supporting a communication about their environmental performance or just complying with a 
specific regulation. The principles of LCA are defined in the ISO standards 14040/44 but yet there are a 
lot of debates regarding some major methodological points. One of the most discussed issues 
 is 
the 
way to solve multifunctionality. These debates resulted in the development of different LCA 
methodologies that differ from each other.  
The case of recycling is a particular case of multifunctionality that can be found in most of the 
LCAs of products. Indeed, recycling has two functions: treating waste and producing secondary material. 
Therefore, recycling is involved both in the first stage of a life cycle with the material production and at 
the end-of-life stage with its recycling. In each case, impacts related to the recycling activity need to be 
allocated between different life cycles: the life-cycle that produces the recycled material and the life-cycle 
that uses the material. However, there are different methodologies regarding the way this allocation should 
be done and the way the end-of-life is modelled in general. Modelling recycling in LCA has been 
discussed for years but it still remains a big issue because the number of LCAs where recycling is an 
important part of the model is increasing. It is due to the fact that LCA is used more and more by 
companies for environmental assessment and that the introduction of secondary material and the 
recyclability of products are design options that are gaining importance. 
 Companies will generally follow a methodology that is given in a standard when they perform 
LCA. Today, many standards can be used. The EN15804 for construction work, the BP-X-30-323 in 
France, the EPD international system or the carbon footprint are example of such standards. All the 
standards generally present different LCA methodologies, especially for modelling recycling. The 
consequence is that today many approaches co-exist. Some methods will favour the use of recycled 
material while other methods will favour the recycling at the end-of-life. Some methods are attributional, 
others are consequential. There are even methods that consider the number of times a material is recycled. 
This proliferation of methods and end-of-life formulas is not a minor issue because the modelling choices 
for recycling can lead to different results and may have an influence on the final decision-making. Hence, 
there is a need for a clear overview of what these modelling choices impart for the results of an LCA. 
 
 The main goal of this master thesis is therefore to investigate this issue of recycling in LCA, from a 
company perspective. The objectives will be to understand how companies deal with this issue of 
recycling in LCA, what are their problems and what the consequences are whether they choose a method 
or another one. 
The master thesis aims first to explain in detail the different types of recycling methods that a 
company could use, in order to understand what the underlying principles of each method are, and what 
they imply for the LCA and its results. The thesis will also pay special attention to the PEF methodology 
which is a new LCA methodology developed by the European Commission (European Commission, 
2013) and that will probably be one of the most used methods in the future. This PEF methodology 
proposes a new end-of-life formula that introduces some innovations. Analysing it thanks to the 
conclusions drawn from the current methods for modelling recycling is interesting in the scope of this 
thesis. The purpose is also to understand, from the point of view of a company performing LCA, the 
consequences of having many methods, in order to see how the choice of a method can influence the 
results of an LCA and the recommendations given from the interpretation of these results. The deep 
theoretical analysis of the end-of-life formulas will be accompanied by a more practical analysis. 
Understanding how these end-of-life formulas are perceived and applied is essential because the intended 
application of a formula may differ from the reality. What problems companies performing LCA of 
products typically face when they have to model recycling with an end-of-life formula; what it implies and 
what limitation of the new PEF end-of-life formula can be predicted from that, are questions that are also

1. General presentation of LCA 
Life cycle assessment (LCA) can be defined as a method to assess the environmental impacts which 
can be attributed to the life cycle of a product (Rebitzer et al., 2004). This environmental assessment 
method is characterized by the use of multiple environmental impact indicators (e.g. climate change, 
acidification, eutrophication, ecotoxicity, human toxicity, resource depletion, etc.) occurring through the 
multiple stages of a product life cycle (from material extraction to end-of-life disposal). 
Environmental assessment of products in general provides information regarding the potential 
consequences of a product on the environmental. It can be used to support decision or improve the 
knowledge about a product. For example, results of an environmental assessment can help a decision 
maker to make an informed decision considering also the environmental consequences of its decision. It 
can also help a product designer to consider the environment in its design choices to reduce the impacts of 
its product. One of the advantages of LCA, as an environmental assessment tool, is that it will measure 
and therefore help to avoid the burden-shifting by considering the entire life cycle and assessing different 
environmental impacts. Burden-shifting usually occurs when an environmental impact is displaced 
between different stages of a life cycle or when the reduction of one kind of pollution increases another 
type of pollution.  
LCA is still a young scientific discipline since the first international standards where defined at the 
end of the 1990’s (Rebitzer et al., 2004). Today its principles and its framework are defined in the ISO 
14040 (ISO, 2006a) and the requirements to conduct a LCA in the ISO 14044 (ISO, 2006b). 
==PAGE:9==
 
 
3/109 
An LCA study usually comprises four phases, as defined in the ISO standard (ISO, 2006a) and 
detailed in other guides like the ILCD handbook (EC-JRC, 2010): 
 
Goal and scope definition: definition of the parameters of the study 
 
Life cycle inventory (LCI): inventory of the inputs and outputs of all the processes belonging to 
the life cycle of the product system studied 
 
Life cycle impact assessment (LCIA): interpretation of the emissions from the inventory as 
potential impacts 
 
Interpretation: analysis of the results from the impact assessment in relation to the goal and scope 
of the study, it often includes sensitivity and uncertainty analyses 
The detail of the four phases of an LCA study will not be explained further here but such 
information can be found in many books, articles or guidelines (EC-JRC, 2010; Hauschild and Barlaz, 
2011; Rebitzer et al., 2004). It is important to note that LCA is not a linear process but an iterative one 
where the results and the experience from one phase will lead to modifications of the earliest phases 
(Figure 1). 
 
Figure 1: The LCA phases, from (ISO, 2006a) 
LCA is primarily performed for products, but it can also be applied to any good, service or system. 
It is also used for many different purposes like product development, decision making, policy evaluation, 
marketing, etc. 
Given the complexity of LCA and its various applications, it usually requires the use of LCI 
databases (ecoinvent 3, ELCD, base carbone ADEME, etc.) and software (SimaPro, Gabi, Umberto, 
OpenLCA, EASETECH, etc.). 
Since this master thesis was conducted in collaboration with a consultant company specialized in 
eco-design, this document will focus on product LCAs in a product development context. SimaPro was the 
LCA software used in combination with the ecoinvent 3 database.

2. General presentation of recycling and the related issues 
2.1. Introduction to recycling 
Recycling is a waste management option which has been growing in the last decades. In OECD 
countries, the recycling rates increased from 9.9% in 1995 to 23.5% in 2008 (Swart and Groot, 2015). In 
the European Union, the recycling rate of municipal solid waste has increased from 22.7% in 2007 to 
28.8% in 2015 (Eurostat, 2017). Recycling more waste is encouraged by many governments which often 
set quantitative targets for the recycling rates (JORF, 2015; Miljøstyrelsen, 2013). However, the definition 
of the activities included in the term recycling varies between countries or stakeholders so the different 
terms about recycling will be defined below. 
This increasing interest for recycling from authorities, companies or people in general comes from a 
more general interest of the society to tend towards a more sustainable development. One way to achieve a 
more sustainable development is to increase our resource consumption efficiency. Many concepts related 
to this goal appeared recently like dematerialization, decoupling of the economy or the circular economy 
(Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2012; Van Ewijk and Stegemann, 2016) and they often have in common to 
give a high importance to recycling. 
Even though recycling is now strongly motivated by environmental concerns, recycling has always 
existed for economic reasons. For example, metals or glass have been recycled for a long time because 
their recycling is relatively easy and provides fairly good secondary material at affordable prices 
compared to the primary material. 
2.2. Definition of recycling 
 
In the European Union, waste is regulated by the “Waste Directive” 98/2008 (European 
Commission, 2008). The waste directive is the basis for the specific waste regulations within the European 
Union and it establishes many definitions related to waste and waste management. Especially, it clarifies 
the difference between disposal and recovery and gives the definition of recycling (Pagh and Wejdling, 
2011). 
The definition of recycling according to the waste directive is given below: 
“Recycling means any recovery operation by which waste materials are reprocessed into products, 
materials or substances whether for the original or other purposes. It includes the reprocessing of organic 
material but does not include energy recovery and the reprocessing into materials that are to be used as 
fuels or for backfilling operations;” (European Commission, 2008) 
 
 
 
This definition clearly states the difference between material recycling and energy recovery and it 
considers that a material can be recycled either in a closed or an open loop. This definition however, does 
not make any difference between the different qualities of recycling (cf. downcycling), which is 
commonly criticized since it does not stimulate the production of high quality recycled material. 
 
Recycling is sometimes separated between mechanical and chemical (also called feedstock) 
recycling, but this differentiation is not relevant for this report and will not be developed. 
2.3. Recycling in the waste hierarchy 
Recycling is often seen as the most sustainable way to handle the end-of-life of a product, but it is 
not necessarily true and even if this master thesis deals mainly with recycling, the other alternatives should 
not be forgotten.  
==PAGE:11==
 
 
5/109 
The waste hierarchy is one very famous tool which gives a priority order to the different waste 
management options. It originates from the 1980’s and has been formally defined in Europe in 2008 by 
the waste directive (Van Ewijk and Stegemann, 2016). The hierarchy is also commonly named “3R” 
(reduce, re-use, recycle) worldwide. 
The priority order defined by the waste directive is the following (European Commission, 2008) : 
a. 
Prevention 
b. 
Preparing for re-use 
c. 
Recycling 
d. 
Other recovery, e.g. energy recovery 
e. 
Disposal 
This order is considered as the priority order of what “constitutes the best overall environment 
option in waste legislation and policy” (European Commission, 2008) and it clearly states that prevention 
of waste generation and re-use should be preferred to recycling when possible. 
The waste hierarchy is also criticized for not reaching its goal to increase dematerialization (Van 
Ewijk and Stegemann, 2016) because of the lack of guidance or the non-distinction between the different 
kind of recycling for example. 
Another limit of the waste hierarchy is that for some types of waste or in some specific conditions, 
the priority order of the best environmental option can be different from the waste hierarchy. Deviation 
from the waste hierarchy can however be justified from an environmental point of view by a “life-cycle 
thinking” (European Commission, 2008). Indeed, LCA is more and more used in waste management 
(Laurent et al., 2014a), partly to go beyond the waste hierarchy. Therefore, proper modelling of recycling 
or re-use in LCA becomes essential given the key role of these waste management options. 
2.4. The environmental benefit of recycling 
Recycling is usually described as a waste management option more sustainable than incineration or 
disposal for example, as formulated in the waste hierarchy. But the origin of this assumed environmental 
benefit needs to be explained in detail. The recycling processes themselves are not necessarily impacting 
less the environment. For example, recycling processes often require energy and the specific collection 
schemes need an additional transportation compared to a “normal” disposal. 
The benefits of recycling stem from the second life given to the material: 1/ the material is not lost in 
the nature as would be waste in a landfill or fumes and ashes from an incinerator and 2/ the recycled 
material (also called secondary material) will be used in another product. The use of secondary material 
will potentially avoid the extraction of virgin raw material which would have been necessary if this 
secondary material was disposed of. 
Another point of view is that a material will need in any case to be disposed of when it reaches its 
end-of-life and therefore as soon as a material is extracted from the nature and introduced in the 
technosphere, it will need an end-of-life treatment; but recycling (or re-use) will postponed this treatment 
and impact (theoretically it could be infinitely). 
What is important to understand is that the benefits of recycling are often not directly quantifiable in 
the life-cycle of a product or material and are mainly due to avoidance of impacts in other life cycles. This 
makes this issue of recycling difficult to model as described in this report. 
Furthermore, it could be said that recycling is not a proper “end-of-life” or “waste management” 
activity but actually also a “material extraction or production” activity and this distinction is also reflected 
in the recycling methods. It also explained why modelling recycling properly in LCA is not only important 
for waste management assessment but also for companies performing LCA of products. Unfortunately, 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
6/109 
this is often “disregarded or oversimplified” in the last case (Hauschild and Barlaz, 2011) and it is the 
subject of this report. 
2.5. Some key concepts and definition about recycling 
Numerous concepts and terms are related to recycling. Some of them are defined in this part in 
order to clarify them for a better understanding of the rest of the report. 
a. 
Recycling 
In LCA papers, the term “recycling” often includes energy recovery or re-use because they are 
usually modelled similarly in LCA; see the ILCD handbook for example (EC-JRC, 2010). However, in 
this report the term recycling will be used exclusively for material recycling, as defined in the waste 
directive (see part 2.2). 
b. 
Recycled content 
The recycled content is the proportion of an input material that has been recycled in a previous life-
cycle. It will be noted R1 in this report, in accordance with the PEF guide (European Commission, 2013). 
c. 
End-of-life recycling rate 
Many definitions are available for this term. It generally represents the proportion of a material that 
will be recycled in another life cycle. But where this recycling rate should be measured is unsettled. It is 
sometimes defined as “the amount of recycled material that is collected at the end of life divided by the 
available material after the use phase”, which is a collection rate (Schrijvers et al., 2016a). It can also take 
into account the losses in the collection and in the recycling process as defined in the PEF (European 
Commission, 2013). The second definition will be used in this report and it will be noted R2 in 
accordance with the PEF guide (European Commission, 2013). 
d. 
Closed-loop and open-loop recycling 
The notion of closed and open loop is related to both recycling and its modelling in LCA which 
often creates confusion about the definition. 
We talk about closed-loop recycling when the secondary material from a product system is recycled 
into the same product system, according to the ISO 14044 standard (ISO, 2006b). The open-loop recycling 
takes place when the secondary material is recycled (partly or fully) in a different product system. This 
definition does not depend on the quality of the recycling but only on the system in which the recycling 
takes place. The ILCD handbook or the PEF guide (EC-JRC, 2010; European Commission, 2013) use this 
last definition. 
The confusion between closed and open-loop recycling comes from the specific case of open-loop 
recycling where the secondary materials is recycled into a different product system but keep the same 
“inherent property” (ISO, 2006b). The LCA modelling for this case is then similar to the closed-loop 
recycling case but it is still formally an open-loop recycling case. This is illustrated in Figure 2 where the 
“technical description” is the definition given above. 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
7/109 
 
Figure 2: Distinction between a technical description of a product system and allocation for recycling in the case of 
closed and open-loop recycling; from(ISO, 2006b). 
As expressed in the ILCD handbook the distinction between open and closed loop is not always 
easy (EC-JRC, 2010). This distinction is sometimes also criticized and considered not useful because 
closed-loop recycling is not necessarily better than open-loop recycling from an environmental point of 
view (Geyer et al., 2016). In an example with used oil, (Geyer et al., 2016) show that it is not the recycling 
in an open or closed loop which is relevant to answer the question of which type of recycling is better but 
the processes displaced in each case. From a modelling perspective, closed-loop recycling can be 
considered as a particular case of open-loop recycling. 
e. 
Downcycling 
Downcycling is often confused with open-loop recycling but it actually represents the case of 
recycling where there is a change in the “inherent property” of the material. There are different cases of 
downcycling (EC-JRC, 2010): 
 
The secondary material can only replace the primary material in some applications where 
the functional requirements are not too demanding 
 
A higher amount of secondary material is needed than for the primary material to provide 
the same functionality 
 
The secondary material needs to be mixed with primary material or higher quality 
secondary material to meet the technical or legal requirements 
 
The secondary material replaces a different material than the primary material 
Examples of change of inherent properties leading to downcycling can be the shortening of fibres in 
paper, the reduced mechanical performance of polymers or the accumulation of tramp elements in metals. 
 
3. Important LCA methodological points related to recycling 
3.1. Attributional vs. consequential modelling 
In LCA, there are two main modelling approaches: attributional and consequential modelling. The 
choice between attributional LCA and consequential LCA is determining since it influences how the data 
is chosen and the LCI performed. It is also involved in the way of handling cases of multifunctional 
processes (see part 3.2). This point is especially important for the recycling and it will be extensively 
explained here. 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
8/109 
The distinction between attributional and consequential modelling is not mentioned in the ISO 
14040 and ISO 14044 standards (ISO, 2006a, 2006b), which has resulted in different definitions and 
views about attributional and consequential modelling among the LCA community. Indeed this issue has 
been largely discussed recently (Ekvall et al., 2016). 
The distinction between the two modelling approaches originates from the fact that LCA modelling 
depends on the goal of the study. From the beginning of the 1990’s, two distinct goals were already 
distinguished: “describing a product system and its environmental exchanges” and “describing how the 
environmental exchanges of the system can be expected to change as a result of actions taken in the 
system” (Rebitzer et al., 2004). Many terms have been used to try to define these two approaches: 
accounting, book-keeping, retrospective or descriptive for attributional modelling and change-oriented, 
effect-oriented, market-based or prospective for consequential modelling (EC-JRC, 2010). 
Even today, it is difficult to give a clear and undisputed definition of the two approaches because, as 
illustrated by (Ekvall et al., 2016), many definitions exist with slight differences but which can be of great 
importance when interpreted. However the following definition, defined at a relatively recent workshop 
(UNEP/SETAC, 2011) and reused in recent studies (Pelletier et al., 2015; Schrijvers et al., 2016a), will be 
used for this report: 
 
Attributional LCA: “attempts to provide information on what portion of global burdens 
can be associated with a product (and its life cycle). In theory, if one were to conduct 
attributional LCAs of all final products, one would end up with the total observed 
environmental burdens worldwide. The systems analyzed ideally contain processes that are 
actually directly linked by (physical, energy, and service) flows to the unit process that 
supplies the functional unit or reference flow.” (UNEP/SETAC, 2011) 
 
Consequential LCA: attempts “to provide information on the environmental burdens that 
occur, directly or indirectly, as a consequence of a decision (usually represented by 
changes in demand for a product). In theory, the systems analyzed in these LCAs are made 
up only of processes that are actually affected by the decision, that is, that change their 
output due to a signal they receive from a cause-and-effect chain whose origin is a 
particular decision.” (UNEP/SETAC, 2011) 
 
The conceptual difference between attributional and consequential LCA can be illustrated with the 
Figure 3 taken from (Weidema, 2003). The circle represents all the environmental exchanges. In 
attributional LCA, we want to attribute a share of these exchanges (inside the dotted lines) to the specific 
activity investigated. In consequential LCA, we want to see the changes in these exchanges resulting from 
the addition or removal of a specific activity (Weidema, 2003). 
 
Figure 3: The conceptual difference between attributional and consequential LCA; from (Weidema, 2003)

Practically, attributional modelling is the most used and is usually applied to learn about the 
existing impacts of a product system, to perform hot-spot identification or to make environmental product 
declaration (Schrijvers et al., 2016a); whereas consequential modelling is generally employed when the 
LCA is conducted for decision making. 
This differentiation of decision-context situations in order to choose the appropriate modelling 
approach has been described in the ILCD handbook with four situations A, B, C1 and C2 (EC-JRC, 2010) 
and is illustrated in Figure 4. It has however been criticized by some researchers because of 
inconsistencies in the handbook regarding the approach to follow for situation A (Ekvall et al., 2016). 
Some people also argue that attributional LCA should not be used since the ultimate goal is always to 
improve the studied product system even in case of hot spot identification or product declaration 
(Weidema, 2003). 
 
The main difference in the modelling between both approaches lies in the choice of data. On one 
hand attributional modelling implies the use of actual processes used in the system studied, when 
available, or a mix of the current technologies. For example the current French electricity mix could be 
used for a process taking place in France. On the other hand, consequential modelling implies the use of 
marginal data. For example, if a new process requiring a lot of electricity is introduced in France, the 
additional electricity will surely come from natural gas, oil or coal power plant (the marginal processes 
here) and not from nuclear or hydraulic energy which constitutes the baseload of the French electricity 
mix. 
Nevertheless, the choice of the marginal data in consequential LCA can be extremely complicated 
and there are debates, among others, if short-term or long-term marginal data should be used (Zamagni et 
al., 2012). 
The other difference in the modelling that is highly influenced by the choice between attributional 
and consequential LCA is the case of multifunctional processes and it will be discussed in the next part 
(part 3.2). 
 
To conclude this section, there is not a preferred approach. Both are criticized, the attributional one 
because of its lack of accuracy and the consequential one because of the high uncertainty due to the choice 
of marginal data (Heijungs and Guinée, 2007). And even though they are based on different philosophies 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
10/109 
and should not be combined, it is actually often the case that LCI are a mix of the two modelling 
approaches (Laurent et al., 2014b).  
The practice in the company where this master thesis has been conducted is to use attributional 
modelling most of the time. This choice has been made because of the case-studies assessed, which are 
generally existing products, but also because the available databases are mainly providing attributional 
data and the consequential ones are not considered reliable. 
3.2. Multifunctionality and allocation 
Multifunctionality is very important in LCA modelling and as such solving it is one of the “most 
controversial issues in the development of LCA” (Rebitzer et al., 2004). It is particularly relevant for 
recycling because its modelling is mainly a multifunctional problem. However, this part will deal with 
multifunctionality in general to lay the foundation of the issue of recycling in LCA. 
The definition of multifunctionality, unlike its solutions, is pretty simple. We talk about 
multifunctionality in LCA when a multi-functional process is modelled, i.e. a “process or system that 
performs more than one function” (EC-JRC, 2010) or said in another way, a process which “delivers 
several goods and/or services” (European Commission, 2013). 
The problem is that usually, when performing LCA, we are interested in only one of the functions 
or products of such a process and we want to assess the environmental impact of this specific function or 
product. And as expressed by (Hauschild and Barlaz, 2011), when we conduct an LCA we want to set 
artificial boundaries around the system we are interested in, but in the real world environmental impacts 
are not associated with a product or a function but with the process that cause them. 
This multifunctionality problem needs to be solved and it is where allocation appears. The 
definition of allocation, according to the ISO 14040 is “partitioning the input or output flows of a process 
or a product system between the system under study and one or more other product systems” (ISO, 
2006a). This is the formal definition of allocation, which will also be called “partitioning” for clarity, 
because it is common that all the ways to solve the problem of multifunctionality are included in the term 
“allocation”. The other ways to deal with multifunctionality are in fact to avoid it by further refining the 
process to have different sub-processes for the different functions (called sub-division) or to expand the 
boundaries of the system under study, to include all the other functions (called system-expansion). 
The ISO 14044 (ISO, 2006b) gives a procedure with a hierarchy to follow to solve the 
multifunctionality problem. The full allocation procedure is given in Appendix 1 and can be summarized 
as follow: 
1) Avoid allocation by performing sub-division or system expansion 
2) Perform partitioning based on physical relationship (i.e. there is a causality relationship 
between inputs and outputs reflected by a physical parameter) 
3) Perform partitioning based on other relevant relationship (e.g. mass, economic-based 
allocation, etc.) 
Even though the ISO 14044 clearly gives a procedure to solve multifunctionality, this procedure is 
widely criticized (Pelletier et al., 2015; Reap et al., 2008; Weidema, 2014) for different reasons. Some of 
them will be explained later like the lack of definition of “key-terms”, the system expansion or the link 
between the allocation hierarchy and the attributional or consequential approaches seen before. These 
numerous critics are partly justified by the fact that many new guidelines do not follow the procedure in 
the ISO standards even if they often state to be compliant with it (Weidema, 2014). 
 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
11/109 
The main issue with system expansion is whether substitution (or subtraction) should be considered 
as system expansion. What is system expansion and substitution is illustrated in Figure 5 for the simplified 
case of an incinerator. When we want to compare two systems (here incineration of waste) and one of 
them performs an additional function, we need to change the functional unit and to expand the system to 
include the co-function (here producing electricity). It is done by adding an independent process which 
performs only the co-function, to the system in which it does not exist (here the right system). The 
alternative, the substitution approach, is to subtract the burdens and benefits of this process (here 
producing electricity) from the system with the two functions (here the left system). Expanding the system 
to include the new process or subtracting the new process from it can be seen as equivalent and it explains 
why substitution is generally considered as being a kind of system expansion. 
 
 
Figure 5: The principle of system expansion and subtraction to obtain functional equivalence between different 
systems in LCA of waste management; from (Hauschild and Barlaz, 2011) 
 The case of substitution was not mentioned in the ISO 14044 which led to different interpretation 
of the allocation procedure. For many people substitution is considered as being the same as system 
expansion  while for other the equivalency between system expansion and substitution does mean they are 
equal since the functional unit changes and therefore does not justify it (Heijungs, 2014). 
 For example, the ILCD handbook (EC-JRC, 2010) refines the ISO allocation procedure by clearly 
considering substitution as system expansion. On the other hand, the PEF guide (European Commission, 
2013) gives a new hierarchy where direct and indirect substitutions are defined and are added respectively 
as an option to the step 2 and 3 of the ISO hierarchy.  
 
 The other issue with the ISO allocation hierarchy, and somewhat related, is that this ranking “does 
not refer to the goal of the study” (Ekvall and Tillman, 1997) or said differently it does not take into 
account the modelling approach: attributional or consequential. This implies that different study goals 
would not require a different allocation procedure. However, in practice, solving the problem of 
multifunctionality is often considered differently according to the type of study. 
Indeed, substitution is generally seen as a consequential method to handle allocation and it is not 
always considered as correct for an attributional LCA. For example, if substitution is applied, the impact 
related to all the product in the world do not add up to give the worldwide impact (Schrijvers et al., 2016a) 
as attributional LCA was defined before (part 3.1). Similarly, partitioning is more seen as an attributional 
method. 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
12/109 
The fact that the choice of the allocation procedure should also be based on the goal of the study 
and the type of LCA modelling is even illustrated in the ILCD handbook (EC-JRC, 2010) with the 
recommendation of different ways to handle multifunctionality according to the context situation (see 
Figure 4). It is even more explicit in Annex C (EC-JRC, 2010), dealing with multifunctionality in the case 
of recycling were two different methods are proposed depending on the attributional or consequential 
nature of the study. So even in the ILCD handbook, which is supposed to be a European guide to perform 
LCA in compliance with the ISO standards, there is a contradiction between having an allocation 
procedure identical in all cases or dependent on the goal of the study. This issue was highlighted in many 
articles and many authors. For example, (Pelletier et al., 2015) recently proposed “that the ISO 14044 
multifunctionality hierarchy should explicitly differentiate between attributional and consequential data 
modelling application”. 
 There are other issues related to this ISO multifunctionality hierarchy regarding the interpretation 
that people make of some terms, “shall” or “should” for example, to interpret it liberally which in the end 
allows everyone to model multifunctionality as they prefer (Pelletier et al., 2015; Weidema, 2014). 
 
All these issues about the solutions to solve multifunctionality are of course also valid for the case 
of recycling or re-use. But there are even more divergent interpretations in the case of recycling because 
the ISO 14044 is ambiguous by stating that allocation for recycling should be handle the same way as any 
multi-functional process but by providing a specific hierarchy at the same time (Weidema, 2014). And 
actually most of the guidelines or standards provide formulas for modelling recycling which often 
contradict the ISO hierarchy. This lack of a clear guidance agreed by all on how to model 
multifunctionality explains partly why recycling is modelled in so many different ways in the different 
standards, as it will be presented later.  
4. The main underlying principles for modelling recycling 
4.1. Recycling: a multifunctional problem 
Modelling recycling in LCA is a multifunctional problem. Two main functions can be identified for 
a recycling process: treating the waste and producing a new raw material. Therefore, the impacts related to 
the recycling activity need to be allocated between these two functions and between different life cycles: 
the life-cycle that produces the recycled material and the life-cycle that uses the material. Hence, this 
problem usually appears at the production and at the end-of-life stage in a product life cycle. Furthermore, 
a material can potentially be recycled multiple times and then involved many life cycles between which 
the impacts need to be allocated. Actually, at least three system levels can be identified to deal with this 
multifunctional issue: the recycling process, the product life cycle investigated and the cascade or material 
life cycle (Ekvall and Tillman, 1997). 
This part will present the main conceptual types of solution to solve the multifunctional problem of 
recycling and their limitations. The details of specific methods will not be addressed. The focus is on the 
underlying concepts, which are related to the different approaches to solve multifunctionality in general 
and the distinction between attributional and consequential LCA, as presented before. The description of 
the different concepts will be illustrated with Figure 6 showing a simplified case involving only two life 
cycles: one produces a recycled material from a virgin material and the other one uses it before disposing 
it.

4.2. System expansion (without substitution) 
The first way to solve the problem of multifunctionality in the modelling of recycling is to avoid it 
by including all the functions in the system investigated. This is the classical system expansion concept. 
System expansion, where another system is added and the functional unit modified, is differentiated here 
from substitution, where another system is subtracted but the functional unit remains unchanged, as 
advised by (Heijungs, 2014). Substitution is presented independently in the next part (part 4.3). Figure 6a, 
illustrates this definition of system expansion where the system is expanded to include the two life cycles 
in the example. 
System expansion can be applied in consequential LCA and can theoretically always solve the 
issue. Its use in attributional LCA is however debatable since the purpose of attributional LCA is to 
estimate the impact of a specific product (Pelletier et al., 2015). Usually system expansion is not applied 
in the case of recycling, especially when open-loop recycling is at stake, because the focus of the study is 
to report the impact of a specific system and expanding it would include too many undesirable systems or 
life cycles. Its feasibility is often questioned and the added systems or processes can also involve new 
multifunctional problems (Heijungs and Guinée, 2007). 
4.3. Substitution 
The concept of substitution to solve multifunctionality problems is also known as “subtraction”, 
“avoided burden” or “crediting”. The principle is to substitute the “not required function with an 
alternative way of providing it” (EC-JRC, 2010). 
In case of recycling the not required function or the co-product will typically be the waste treatment 
at the beginning of a life cycle and the production of recycled material at its end. The second case is 
illustrated in Figure 6b where the focus is on the left life cycle. The production of recycled material is 
considered as replacing the conventional production of this material (Heijungs and Guinée, 2007). In such 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
14/109 
a case all the impacts due to the recycling activity are accounted for but the impacts of a conventional 
production of the material (the displaced or substituted activity) are subtracted. 
 
Substitution is clearly applicable in consequential modelling and is even the method recommended 
for such LCA modelling (EC-JRC, 2010). But while its use is also widespread in attributional modelling 
(Laurent et al., 2014b), this is questionable because the substitution takes place over time (see below) and 
the addition of all the impacts from all the final products would not sum up to give the worldwide impacts 
(Schrijvers et al., 2016a). (Pelletier et al., 2015) for example, recommend no to use substitution for 
attributional modelling. In the same time the ILCD handbook states that substitution is applicable for 
attributional LCA in context situation C1 for existing or past recycling operations substituting virgin 
material production. (Gala et al., 2015) also developed the substitution methods for attributional modelling 
by promoting the use of mix of recycled and virgin material for the substituted production, contrary to 
what is usually done by considering only virgin material. 
 
 
One of the issues with substitution in the case of recycling is the time perspective, which is 
illustrated in Figure 7. LCA for a product is usually performed when it is under design or already 
manufactured (see t=0a in Figure 7), thus the production stage is well known. But for products with a long 
lifetime, like construction material, the end-of-life will happened a long time after (t=80a in the figure) so 
what will be the recycling technology or the substituted process is very uncertain and it also means that 
the burden and the benefits that will occur in the future are accounted for today. (Frischknecht, 2010) 
classified the substitution approach as “risk-seeking” because the recycling will potentially take place in 
several decades and credits are granted for that recycling without being sure it will be recycled in the 
future.  
 
Figure 7: Environmental impacts in the course of time during production, use and end-of-life (recycling) of a long-
living metal product; from (Frischknecht, 2010) 
 
Generally, implementing substitution is more complex than it seems at first sight. This is mainly 
due to the choice of the substituted process. Some authors even recommend not using substitution, 
evoking a “philosophical” problem: “how can one decide which process is avoided given the fact that it is 
avoided, and hence not there?” (Heijungs and Guinée, 2007).  
Identification of an alternative conventional process is indeed difficult since the substituted 
process needs to have the same function as the not-required function of the system assessed. Making 
assumptions regarding the displacement that occurs is needed too. For example, if the recycled material is 
assumed to displace the production of virgin material, it is necessary to know to which extent. Often a 
one-to-one approach is chosen (e.g. 1 kg recycled material replaces 1 kg virgin material) but this is hardly 
ever the case and the “dynamic of demand and supply” should be considered (Geyer et al., 2016). Taking 
into account the market behaviour is however very complex and unpredictable but an economic study of 
this case shows that the displacement of production in case of recycling is not as simple as it is usually 
modelled (Zink et al., 2016). Market-based substitution will also face the same criticisms as economic 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
15/109 
allocation (see part 4.4). Other physical factors also need to be considered to correct this substitution and, 
for example, (Vadenbo et al., 2016) proposed a framework to apply substitution properly and in a 
consistent way. 
Anyway, the “arbitrary” nature of the choice of the substituted processes will always be criticized 
but sometimes it is not even possible to identify a substitution process or this substitution process can be 
multifunctional itself, making the problem almost infinite (Heijungs and Guinée, 2007; Pelletier et al., 
2015). Nevertheless some authors claim it is always possible to use substitution (Weidema, 2003). 
4.4. Partitioning 
Partitioning, also called allocation and defined previously in part 3.2, can be applied to the 
multifunctional issue with recycling. The inputs and outputs of the recycling process are partitioned 
between the treatment of the waste and the production of a secondary material, as illustrated in Figure 6c. 
This partitioning is done using partitioning criteria. In most of the cases, the same criterion is used for 
both inputs and outputs. Which one using is subject of debates within the LCA community (Hanes et al., 
2015). 
The ISO 14044 gives a specific order to follow for the choice of the partitioning criterion (ISO, 
2006b): 
1) Physical properties (e.g. mass, energy content, etc.) 
2) Economic value (e.g. market prices for scrap and virgin material, revenues, etc.) 
3) “The number of subsequent uses of the recycled material” (ISO, 2006b) 
Two different schools of thoughts can indeed be identified when dealing with allocation, between 
proponents of physical allocation and proponents of economic allocation (Pelletier et al., 2015). These 
approaches will be developed further. The third approach is a bit different and specific to the recycling 
case. A recycled material can be used and recycled in multiple life cycles. Hence, partitioning can also be 
seen as the distribution of the impacts occurring during the production of virgin material, the recycling 
process and the final end-of-life treatment, between all the different life-cycles that use this material 
(Ekvall and Tillman, 1997). This approach differs slightly from the “classical” partitioning and is reflected 
in some methods for modelling recycling (see part 5.5). The basis for partitioning is usually the number of 
recycling loops for a given mass of material in such cases. 
 
Partitioning is usually seen as an attributional method to deal with multifunctionality since it 
corresponds well with the goal of attributional LCA, as defined before (part 3.1), which is to attribute a 
share of the global impacts to a specific product or activity. By partitioning, a share of the impacts from 
the recycling process is artificially attributed to one of the functions, excluding the other ones of the study. 
Normally, partitioning is not used in consequential studies since system expansion or substitution will 
better reflect the consequences of a change. 
To perform partitioning criteria must be defined with physical or economic data (as detailed further) 
but which process need to be partitioned is also extremely important. In case of recycling the question can 
be where in the recycling chain allocation is performed (e.g. after collection, pre-treatment, sorting or 
reprocessing). This question is well explained in the ILCD handbook were it gives advice to identified the 
“true joint-process” (EC-JRC, 2010). 
 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
16/109 
a. 
Physical partitioning 
Proponents of physical allocation consider LCA (especially attributional LCA) as being “natural 
science-based” and as such the LCI (where it is necessary to make some allocations) should keep a 
physical realism. They consider that the results of an LCA should reflect the physical reality of the system 
modelled to be meaningful (Pelletier et al., 2015). Therefore, they defend the use of only physical 
parameters (mass, energy, etc.) to define the partitioning criteria so that the partitioning is really “based on 
an underlying physical relationship” as stated in the ISO14044 (ISO, 2006b). The choice of the physical 
parameter (e.g. mass, energy) for partitioning cannot be made arbitrarily but must be relevant and made on 
a case-by-case basis (Pelletier et al., 2015).   
 
Nevertheless, it is sometimes impossible to find a single physical partitioning criterion that 
represents the complexity of some products (Pelletier et al., 2015). The functions to separate by 
partitioning can also be based on qualitative properties (e.g. beauty, comfort, etc.) (Ardente and Cellura, 
2012) and consequently cannot be reduced to one physical parameter. This is one of the arguments of the 
proponents of economic allocation. The physical realism of physical partitioning is also questioned by 
some scientists since the mass and energy balances are changed when partitioning is applied (Weidema 
and Schmidt, 2010). In fact, (Weidema and Schmidt, 2010) demonstrated that only the balance for the 
physical parameter used as a partitioning criterion (e.g. mass) is preserved, the other balances between 
inputs and outputs will be changed (e.g. energy, CO2, etc.). 
 
In the particular case of recycling, physical allocation is actually not really used since there is 
generally only one product from a recycling process and no physical parameter would be relevant for 
partitioning. Partitioning can be made between the processes considered as treating the waste and the one 
producing the material, but the distinction between a waste and a product cannot be based on any physical 
parameters since waste is by nature socially-defined1. 
b. 
Economic partitioning 
 
Practically, economic allocation is widely applied, even though it is the least preferred option 
according to the ISO hierarchy for allocation (Ardente and Cellura, 2012; Pelletier et al., 2015). In the 
case of recycling, the use of economic allocation is even more important since it is usually how the 
distinction between product and waste is done: the output of a process is considered as waste if its 
economic value is negative and as a product or co-product if it is positive (Ardente and Cellura, 2012). For 
example, the ILCD handbook recommends that all the impacts from recycling/end-of-life processes where 
the economic value of the material output is below zero, must be attributed to the waste and as soon as the 
economic value is above zero, the impacts need to be allocated to the secondary material (EC-JRC, 2010). 
The price of scrap material is the main economic parameter used in the case of recycling. 
 
Proponents of economic partitioning have generally a vision of LCA that is not only physically-
based but also considers the socio-economic aspects. One of the main arguments in favour of the use of 
economic partitioning is the “economic causality” (Pelletier et al., 2015). It is considered that the value of 
a product is what motivates the existence of a process producing it (Ardente and Cellura, 2012). Therefore 
it makes sense to use economic parameters to partition the impacts of a process. It can also be seen as a 
matter of “fairness” in the distribution of impacts (Pelletier et al., 2015). Indeed, in most cases where the 
economic allocation is used, one of the co-product as a much higher value (e.g. mining activity) and is the 
reason the process takes place so it should take most of the environmental burden (Ardente and Cellura,

as “any substance or object which the holder discards or intends or is required to discard” (European Commission, 
2008). This definition of waste is subjective since it depends on the holder and not on the object/material itself. 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
17/109 
2012). In case of recycling, for example, the secondary material (the waste) could be seen as the co-
product of low quality and should bear less impact that the product generating the waste. Using economic 
allocation can also be a way to incentive a desired behaviour. In the case of recycling, the use of recycled 
material or the recyclability of a product can be favoured for example. Another major argument, already 
presented before, is that a price would represent the aggregation of many information reflecting the 
complexity of a product (e.g. quality, beauty, etc.) that a single physical parameter could not do (Pelletier 
et al., 2015).  
 
There are however also strong limitations to the use of economic allocation. Some are 
fundamental limitations due to the use of market information in general. For example, (Pelletier and 
Tyedmers, 2011) consider that since no ecosystem goods or services meet the criteria of the standard 
economy, which are yet the basis of all economic activities, a market information (e.g. price) does not  
“embody relevant information” and therefore the use of any economic information should be excluded 
from LCA. Concretely, fluctuation of market prices because of changes in supply or demand, or market 
distortions such as monopolies or subsidies, are major problems to define an economic partitioning 
criterion (Ardente and Cellura, 2012). Moreover, proponent of physical allocation will argue that prices 
can change independently of the physical flows and environmental burdens (Pelletier and Tyedmers, 
2011). In the case of recycling, the market prices of scraps can be subject to strong variations overtime for 
different reasons which are often independent of the recycling process. The use of economic allocation to 
incentive a desired behaviour can also be criticized since it means there would be a pre-defined desirable 
behaviour that would automatically be favoured in the environmental assessment (e.g. recycling) that will 
therefore confirm the superiority of this behaviour. 
 
Generally, whatever the type of allocation, partitioning is criticised because many cases showed that 
the result of a study can change according to the choice of the partitioning criteria (Hanes et al., 2015). 
Consequently, it could be possible to “game” the result of an LCA by choosing partitioning criteria 
according to the objectives of the study (Hanes et al., 2015). The arbitrary choice of the allocation factor is 
a recurrent critic of allocation. The fact that allocation does not respect the balances of inputs and outputs 
for all the physical parameters, as presented before, is another one (Weidema and Schmidt, 2010).  Some 
authors however defend partitioning because LCA is inevitably arbitrary as soon as multi-functional 
problems appear, and solving such problems is artificial. It should be accepted that partitioning “provides 
an artificial solution to an artificial question” according to (Heijungs and Guinée, 2007). 
 
As explained, there are divergent views in the LCA community about how to solve multi-
functionality in general and in the specific case of recycling. Some argue that substitution is better than 
allocation while others argue the opposite and even within these “main methods” there are debates about 
the theoretical principles or the practical application. This explains why there are so many ways to handle 
the modelling of recycling in LCA. The next chapter will now go into more practical details by presenting 
and explaining the main elements that are usually found in the end-of-life formulas that deal with 
recycling in LCA.

5. The main end-of-life methods 
This part will present the main methods to model the end-of-life in LCA. The purpose is to present 
general methods with representative formulas. These formulas are not extracted from specific standards or 
guides but are representative of each type of end-of-life formula. Even though every standard will give 
formulas with their own characteristics, it is generally possible to classify them into main categories. 
 
The parameters found in many end-of-life formulas will be first introduced. The notation 
presented in this part will be used in the rest of the report. Furthermore, when formulas coming from 
specific standards are presented, they are “translated” with this notation for a better understanding of the

5.1. Parameters usually found in the end-of-life formulas

5.2. The recycled content method 
a. 
Introduction to the method 
This method is also called the “cut-off” method, sometimes “100:0” or even “méthode des stocks” 
in French. It is probably the most used method for modelling recycling in the case of LCA of products. It 
is the method used in the database ecoinvent v2 (Weidema et al., 2013) as well as in one of the three 
system models proposed in ecoinvent v3, which is often named “Alloc Rec” (ecoinvent, 2017). It is also 
recommended by many standards or guides like EN 15804 (CEN, 2012), GHG Protocol for Products 
(WRI and WBCSD, 2011), PAS2050 (BSI, 2011), the PEP Ecopassport (Association P.E.P, 2015) or for 
EPDs (The International EPD System, 2015). Generally these standards are applicable for LCA of 
products and this method is never used in LCA of waste management systems for example. 
The recycled content approach attributes all the impacts from the extraction and production of the 
primary material to the first life cycle that uses it. The impacts from recycling and collection of the waste 
intended to be recycled are attributed to the life cycle that uses the secondary material. Inclusion of 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
20/109 
collection in the first or second life cycle can however vary according to the standards. Regarding the non-
recycled waste, all the impacts are attributed to the life cycle generating the waste, even in case of 
incineration with energy recovery. 
 
The recycled content approach can be considered as a specific case of economic allocation; an 
allocation 100:0, where all the impacts from the multifunctional process (the recycling process) are 
allocated to the user of secondary material. From this point of view, the Figure 8, extracted from the 
instructions of the International EPD System (The International EPD System, 2015), is interesting because 
it shows that the method sets the boundary between the life cycle that produce the secondary material and 
the life cycle that uses it, at the point where the market value of the material is the lowest, i.e. just before 
being recycled. 
 
Another way to view this method is to consider that there is a stock of material which is used for 
producing secondary material. No impact is attributed when material is taken from the stock since it is 
considered as a waste, and hence does not carry the impacts of its previous life cycle, and no impacts are 
attributed when material is sent to the stock (RECORD, 2011). 
 
Figure 8: Outline of where to set the system boundary between product system in the recycled content method; from 
(The International EPD System, 2015) 
 
(Frischknecht, 2010) analysed the recycled content approach from different perspectives and it 
concluded that this method is in line with the “strong sustainability” concept and it supports a “risk-
averse” attitude. The “risk-averse attitude comes from the fact that impacts occurring today are accounted 
today and not shifted to the next generation. The “strong sustainability” considers that “natural capital 
must not be substituted by man-made capital and thus environmental impacts are strictly linked to the 
product (man-made capital) that causes them, irrespective of any potential future reuse of it” 
(Frischknecht, 2010). In short, this means that the emissions occurring in a life cycle (decrease of the 
natural capital) cannot be compensated by the production of secondary material (increase of the man-made 
capital), even if this secondary material could avoid emissions in the future. Here it is the case because no 
credit is given for recycling with the recycled content method. It is also in line with the Polluter-Pays 
Principle (PPP) as explained in the International EPD system which even called the method “the PP 
allocation method” because the waste generator has to carry all the impacts of the material (especially its 
production) until it is sent to a waste treatment. 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
21/109 
In practice this method is widely used because it is very easy to understand and to apply. This is due 
to the fact that no data is needed about what happened to a material in other life cycles or about its quality 
(Ekvall and Tillman, 1997). It is also favoured by industrial companies because it gives incentives to the 
use of secondary material which is mainly what the industrial companies have an influence on 
(Cederstrand et al., 2014). It is however criticized, by the metal industry for example, because it does not 
reward the recyclability of the material at the end-of-life and it would create “environmental 
inefficiencies” because it can stimulate the secondary material market toward a use where it is not the 
most environmentally efficient (Atherton, 2007).

5.3. The end-of-life recycling method 
a. 
Introduction to the method 
The end-of-life recycling method is also known under many different names like “recycling 
potential”, “0:100”, “recyclability substitution”, “avoided burden”, “crediting” or even “substitution” or 
“system expansion. 
The underlying principles and the fundaments of this method, as well as its pros and cons have 
already been discussed in part 4.3 about substitution. 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
22/109 
This method is widely used in LCA of waste management systems (Laurent et al., 2014b) since it 
takes into account the recycling only at the end-of-life and not the use of recycled material. It is less 
common in LCA of products but it is nevertheless recommended in different standards like the ISO/TS 
14067 (ISO, 2013), ISO/TR14049 (ISO, 2012), the ILCD handbook (EC-JRC, 2010), the BPX30-323 
(AFNOR, 2011) for secondary material with a market where the demand is higher than the supply, or in 
the Module D of the EN15804 (CEN, 2012). It is also one of the three system methods available in 
ecoinvent v3, which is named “substitution, consequential, long-term” usually shortened in 
“consequential”. 
As partially explained in part 4.3, the end-of-life recycling method considers that when a material is 
recycled, the life cycle that has produced this material bears the burden of the collection and the recycling 
processes but it also gets the credits for the avoided production of virgin material. This is the principle of 
substitution: it is assumed that the generation of secondary material will displace the production of the 
equivalent primary material. Therefore, the conventional production of this primary material can be 
considered has being avoided and the life cycle that generates recyclable material should be credited for 
that. 
Regarding the material input, the use of secondary or primary material is modelled in the same way, 
i.e. production of virgin material is modelled no matter if the material effectively used is virgin or 
recycled. This is maybe counterintuitive but it can be explained by the fact that the burdens and benefits 
from the recycling of the material were already accounted for in the previous life cycle where it was 
produced. When the material was recycled in the previous life cycle, a credit was given for avoiding the 
production of primary material, anticipating that the secondary material would be used in a future life 
cycle. Therefore, the secondary material carries a credit that needs to be given back when it is effectively 
used. This is done by considering a process of primary material production for the use of secondary 
material. 
The understanding can be facilitated with an example where 1kg of virgin material is entirely 
recycled in a first life cycle and a second life cycle uses 1kg of this secondary material and then disposes 
of it. Figure 6b illustrated this case (in part 4.1).  In the first life cycle, primary material is used so the 
impact of its production is considered (+𝐸𝑣) but the material is entirely recycled at its end-of-life so 
credits for the avoided production of virgin material are given (−𝐸𝑣). In the second life cycle, the use of 
the secondary material is modelled by giving back the credit (+𝐸𝑣) that it carries. The result for the impact 
due to the primary material, when the two life cycles are added, is (𝐸𝑣−𝐸𝑣+ 𝐸𝑣) = 𝐸𝑣, which makes 
sense because 1kg of primary material is effectively used in the two life cycles. If the credits were not 
given back when the secondary material is used, then the result would have been that no impacts for the 
use of primary material would have been accounted for in the two life cycles. 
 
 Regarding the principles behind the method (see 4.3), this method is in line with the “weak 
sustainability” concept and it supports a “risk seeking” attitude  according to (Frischknecht, 2010), as 
opposed to the “strong sustainability” and the “risk averse” attitude explained for the recycled content 
method (part 5.2). Indeed, it grants credits now to a recycling that will occur in the future and it considers 
that gains in the man-made capital stock (production of secondary material) can compensate losses in the 
natural capital stock (depletion of primary material) (Frischknecht, 2010). 
The method is not widely used for LCA of products because companies have usually a limited 
influence on the end-of-life of their products. Nevertheless, the method is strongly promoted by industries 
where recycling is already common, like the metal industry (Atherton, 2007). This is already reflected in 
the BPX30-323 for example; where the end-of-life recycling method is recommended for material with a 
high demand for secondary material, like metals or glass, in order to incentive the recyclability of 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
23/109 
products. Even though this method is not used so much alone, it is a part of the recent methods detailed 
later (chapter 6). It is why having a clear understanding of the end-of-life recycling approach is essential.

5.4. The waste mining method 
a. 
Introduction to the method 
This method is sometimes called “100:0” too, or “Substitution – Credit for Use of Recovered 
Material”. It does not seem to appear by itself in any standard but it is cited or presented in some 
publications (NCASI, 2012; Schrijvers et al., 2016a) and was the subject of discussions in the LCA List 
(Rossi, 2014). It was also introduced with the “recyclability substitution” method in the ILCD handbook 
(EC-JRC, 2010). This method is however presented here because it was part of the end-of-life formula in 
the first version of the PEF (European Commission, 2013). 
 The method is similar to the end-of-life recycling method since it uses substitution but it is also 
close to the recycled content method because it incentives mostly the use of secondary material. The 
approach consists in rewarding the use of secondary material by giving credits for the avoided waste 
disposal to the life cycle that uses this material. This means that for the life cycle producing a recyclable 
material, the material will be considered as following the conventional waste treatment in the model, 
regardless of its real recycling. The credits for avoiding this disposal are then given to the life cycle using 
the material.  
This method can be considered similar to the recycled content method in the perspective of the kind 
of sustainability or the support of a risk perception (see part 5.2.a). Nevertheless, the underlying principles 
of this approach regarding the way to solve multifunctionality are the same as for a substitution method, as 
explained in part 4.3. The waste mining method could actually be considered as the opposite of the end-of-
life recycling method because it is a substitution (therefore consequential?) method that incentives the 
recycled content in products while the end-of-life recycling method incentives their recyclability. 
 
The same difficulties as for the end-of-life recycling method will arise for the choice of the 
conventional waste treatment that is avoided. Nonetheless, since there are no credits for avoided primary 
material production, no problems of correction or quality factors will appear.

5.5. The allocation methods based on the number of recycling loops 
Another approach to model recycling in LCA is to do an allocation based on the number of 
recycling loops. This approach usually considers that recycling is different from the usual cases of 
multifunctionality because the secondary material produced can itself be recycled multiple times and this 
should be taken into account (EC-JRC, 2010). There are however different ways to perform this kind of 
allocation. Some guidance documents give some formulas (EC-JRC, 2010; ISO, 2012) and some other 
methods are proposed in published articles (Mengarelli et al., 2016; Newell and Field, 1998; Schrijvers et 
al., 2016a). 
A common characteristic of these methods is that they are “material oriented” instead of “product 
oriented”. Or in other words, they look at the cascade or material level and not at the recycling process 
level or the product life cycle level, as defined in (Ekvall and Tillman, 1997). Practically, this means that 
they usually consider average recycling rates or average recycled contents for a type of material, 
independently of the product studied. This is a strong limitation when performing LCA of products since 
the recycled content in the product is usually known. For example, this approach will not differentiate a 
product using recycled material or being recyclable from a product which is made of 100% virgin material 
and is not recyclable. Moreover, it is impossible to model a complex recycling scheme with such method.

assumptions are made regarding the primary material production, the recycling processes and the disposal

recycled in a similar system and this is hardly the case in practice, except for some applications and 
materials. Actually, these methods are mainly promoted or used for metals or glass which are materials 
recycled to a great extent and with a limited loss of inherent property. 
These limitations explain why the methods based on the number of recycling loops are rarely used

5.6. Review of the standards and methods available 
The previous parts have shown that different modelling methods exist for recycling. As a 
consequence, the standards differ in the methods they recommend. The importance of standards is 
probably greater in the economic sphere than in the academic one for several reasons. For example, 
performing an LCA can be mandatory for a company or an industry and therefore it will need to comply 
with a given standard. Another frequent case is the environmental product declaration (EPD) that must 
fulfils some requirements. Also, when a consultant company needs to justify the use of a specific method 
to a client, it is easier if the method comes from a recognized standard at the national or European scale. 
Some recent publications reviewed different standards or guides, focusing mainly or partially on the 
end-of-life modelling (Allacker et al., 2014; Cederstrand et al., 2014; Manfredi et al., 2015; Schrijvers et 
al., 2016a). These reviews illustrate the multiplication of standards and guidance documents in the last

the procedure for modelling recycling.

5.7. Summary 
This chapter showed that there are multiple methods to handle recycling in LCA and they are often 
based on opposite philosophies. From a company perspective that wants to assess the environmental 
impact of a product, it is a problem. Recycling appears both in the material and end-of-life stages of a 
product. Therefore, a company has usually different design options to improve the environmental 
performance of its products: using more recycled material and/or improving the recyclability of its 
products. 
If the methods based on the number of recycling loops are set aside, the problem of the methods 
described before is that they have “extreme behaviours” regarding recycling from a product perspective. 
Indeed, some methods will give strong incentives to the recyclability of the product and neglect the use of 
recycled material (e.g. end-of-life recycling) while at the opposite some will reward the use of recycled 
material in a product, giving few incentives to its recyclability (e.g. waste mining or recycled content). 
Table 1 summarized the main methods presented in this chapter by giving representative end-of-life 
formulas, their pros and cons, their underlying principles and the design strategies they favour. 
 
The fact that different methods with almost opposite behaviours are available for companies is 
problematic for different reasons. When there is no requirements to use a specific method or standard, a 
company could choose the method which would favour the most the eco-design strategy it has conducted 
(for e.g. the recycled content method for a product using mostly secondary material). Another problem 
could be, for example, a company which need to comply with a given standard to make an EPD (where 
the recycled content method is used), but which has chosen to improve the recyclability of its product. 
Then, the supposedly environmental improvements in the design that it has made will almost not be 
visible in the result of the LCA. 
An additional concern that has emerged recently is the fact that recycling of different materials should 
probably be modelled differently to represent different market situations. For example, metals are easily 
recyclable and the demand for recycled metals is actually higher than their production so what needs to be 
favoured is the design of metal products that are recyclable. The case of plastic is different because a lot of 
plastic is collected for recycling on account of legal obligations (see chapter 2) but the demand for 
recycled plastic is limited. In this case it could be interesting to incentive the use of recycled plastic to 
create outlet for the secondary material. But in the same time the production of recycled plastic of higher 
quality needs to be promoted too. Bad design of plastic products, in regard to their recyclability, is one of 
the main reasons for the low quality of secondary plastic. So maybe an approach that favours both the 
recycled content and the recyclability of products could be appropriate for plastic. Of course, whether 
LCA should incentives certain behaviours is debatable and was discussed in part 4.4.b. The fact is that the 
development of methods giving incentives to both the use of recycled material and the recyclability of 
products seems to be the trend in the evolution of the methods for modelling recycling and it will be 
presented in the next chapter.

6.2. The PEF end-of-life formula from 2013 
In the last years, the European Commission has been developing a European method to calculate the 
environmental footprint of products (PEF) or organisations (OEF). The development of a new standard 
was considered necessary because of the concerns of industries and member states about the  proliferation 
of methods and approaches which are “similar-but-different” (Galatola and Pant, 2014). Indeed, this has 
already been illustrated in this report for the end-of-life, but similar variations between methods also exist 
for other important LCA modelling issues. The purpose of the European environmental footprint is to 
“provide a common basis for measuring and communicating environmental performance, which would be 
recognised by market actors across Europe”(Galatola and Pant, 2014). 
A first guide for the PEF was published as an Annex of the commission recommendation of 9 April 
2013 (European Commission, 2013). This was also the beginning of a pilot phase with several 
stakeholders that should lead to a final version of the common rules for performing PEF or OEF studies 
(PEFCR and OEFCR respectively) in the end of 2017. In this report, the focus and discussion will be only 
about the end-of-life part of the PEF. The PEF methodology is very important from a company 
perspective since it aims to be the reference standard at the European scale and it wishes to cover almost 
all the potential applications interesting for a company (eco-design, environmental accounting, 
communication, etc.). It was the method used by default in the company where this master thesis was 
conducted when no specific standard was required. For all these reasons the explanation of this method is 
more detailed in this report. 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
36/109 
The PEF introduced an end-of-life formula (equation 17) in Annex V of the first PEF guide (European 
Commission, 2013). Its main features are that it is applicable for both open-loop and closed-loop 
recycling; it takes into account energy recovery, downcycling and allocates burdens and benefits of 
recycling equally between the producer and user of recycled material. It is indeed a 50/50 formula similar 
to the one in the BPX30-323 but it is applicable for all materials and in all market situations. It is a “one-
formula-fits-all method” (Schrijvers et al., 2016b).

The reactions to this new formula have been contrasted. Some parts were considered interesting by 
some stakeholders, like the fact that it rewards both the recycling and the use of recycled material or that 
there was only one formula for the end-of-life, including all cases of recycling and energy recovery. Some 
of the authors of the PEF also published an article reviewing some of the standards presented in part 5.6 
assessing them with 8 criteria relevant for the modelling of end-of-life in a product policy context 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
37/109 
(Allacker et al., 2014). They concluded that the PEF modelling approach is “better suited for use in 
product policy-support contexts” than the other ones (PAS2050, BP X30-323 and ISO/TS 14067) 
(Allacker et al., 2014). 
However, the PEF method also faced many criticisms in general, some of which were very tough 
(Finkbeiner, 2014). Some criticisms are addressed directly to the new end-of-life formula and summarized 
here. 
 
 The fact that there is only one formula increases the reproducibility of studies but it does not 
improve the comparison of studies. Instead it creates bias because this formula can represent a 
“best case solution for one material and a worse case solution for another material” (Finkbeiner, 
2014). The reply to this point is that an approach with different formulas like in the BPX30-323 
was considered too “complex” (Galatola and Pant, 2014). 
 
Similarly, the choice of a 50/50 allocation factor is criticized for being arbitrary and it neglects the 
fact that there are only some materials for which both the use and the production of recycled 
material is environmentally beneficial (Schrijvers et al., 2016a). This will be partially corrected in 
the last version of the PEF (see part 6.3). 
 
It is possible to “cheat” with the quality factor that could be set to obtain the “desired” result 
(Finkbeiner, 2014). This criticism is similar to the one for the use of allocation factor in general 
(see part 4.4). The people in charge of the development of the PEF replied that some further 
guidance will be made for the definition of the quality factors in the PCR (Galatola and Pant, 
2014). 
 
The number of times a material is recycled is not considered, a material which will be recycled 
only once will have the same burden as a material recycled several times (Lehmann et al., 2016). 
This limitation is also true for many other methods. Since what happened in other life cycles is 
unknown most of the time, this is something hard to take into account, as explained in part 5.5. 
 
It is difficult to interpret the results (Lehmann et al., 2016). This is mainly due to the fact that 
everything (material production and end-of-life) is included in the same formula, so identification 
of the main processes responsible for the impact is not straightforward.  
 
The formula can give unrealistic results like the fact that the part sent to landfill in the model is 
never zero, even in the case of a material 100% recycled at the end-of-life (Finkbeiner, 2014). 
This limitation is clearly due to the part of the formula based on the waste mining method. 
 
The formula requires the collection of too many factors (“18 factors regarding the EoL product 
system”) and this can be difficult or impossible (Lehmann et al., 2015). However, it should be 
noted that many parameters come from the fact that energy recovery is included in the formula 
while it is usually considered separately. The most problematic parameters are probably 
𝑅1, 𝑅2, 𝑅3 and 𝑄𝑠𝑄𝑝
⁄
. 
 
The formula favours incineration instead of recycling because 50% of the benefits of recycling 
are allocated to the material recycled while 100% of the benefits from incineration are allocated to 
the material incinerated (Lehmann et al., 2016). This contradicts the objectives of the EU directive  
on waste (European Commission, 2008) and the waste hierarchy (see part 2.3). This is indeed one 
of the main criticisms of the PEF formula. Actually, it would be difficult to allocate only 50% of 
the impacts (or benefits) of incineration to the producer of the waste since it means that 50% of 
the impact (or benefits)  would need to be apply to the user of electricity from waste incineration 
and the origin of the electricity used is often unknown. This would need at least a change in the 
databases to be applicable

would be equal to the ratio of market prices for primary and secondary material (Schrijvers et al., 2016a), 
which would give an infinite number of possible values for A. 
For the Circular Footprint Formula, three values are possible for A: 
 
A=0.2: When the production of secondary material is low and the demand high. The 
formula then favours recycling at the end-of-life. 
 
A=0.5: When there is equilibrium between supply and demand for secondary material. The 
focus is both on the use and the production of secondary material. 
 
A=0.8: When the production of secondary material is high and the demand low. The 
formula then favours the use of recycled material. 
This means that no extreme values are proposed. For example, with A=0.8, even if the recycling is 
favoured at the end-of-life, the use of recycled material is not completely neglected. The choice of the A 
factor is not given to the LCA practitioner since Annex C of the guidance document (European 
Commission, 2017) gives a list of materials and applications with their respective A value (see Figure 17).

The second major change is the use of the recycled content method instead of the waste mining 
method. The CFF is actually a mix between the end-of-life recycling method (part 1 −𝐴) and the recycled 
content method (part 𝐴). The previous PEF formula was a mix between the end-of-life recycling method 
(50%) and the waste mining method (50%). This change simplifies a lot the formula (as illustrated with 
Figure 15 and Figure 16) because there is no need to account for the avoided waste disposal when using 
secondary material or to find the conventional disposal for the material normally sent to recycling. So less 
data is required, it is easier to apply and it solves some issues raised for the PEF like the unrealistic results 
for the material recycled.  
However, the mix of the recycled content approach with the end-of-life recycling, could be 
strongly criticized for mixing two opposite LCA modelling approaches. While the end-of-life recycling 
and the waste mining substitution approaches were both based on the substitution principle, the recycled 
content is not. Moreover, substitution methods are generally considered as being a kind of consequential 
modelling while most people usually classify the recycled content method as a purely attributional one. 
This issue was raised at the third workshop on the end-of-life formula by Ekvall (Ekvall, 2016). His 
argument is that since the two modelling approaches are supposed to answer different questions, how 
could they be mixed in one formula or method? This also reflects the debate within the LCA community 
about the definition of attributional and consequential modelling (see part 3.1) and whether the 
substitution approach is purely consequential (see part 4.3).

7.1. Introduction to the case-study 
During this master thesis, I modelled and assessed a case-study from a client of EVEA. This case 
study is very interesting in the scope of this master thesis since it deals mainly with recycling, from a 
product perspective with both the use of recycled material and the recycling at the end-of-life being at 
stake. It is an interesting case study to illustrate the behaviour of the different methods described and to 
investigate their influence on the results of an LCA and on the recommendation given to a client. A 
simplified version of the real case-study is presented here to facilitate the understanding. Nevertheless, the 
results of this simplified version are similar to the ones of the real case-study because the main materials 
have been kept with the same proportions.

a. 
Goal and scope 
The client is a multinational company working in the cosmetics industry. It supplies cosmetic 
display stands (called NGM) to the stores selling its products. NGMs are mainly made of metals and 
polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA). Almost every eight years, the client designs new NGMs which replace 
the old ones. It is therefore responsible for taking the old NGMs back and dispose of them properly. 
The case study focuses on the scope of France and the replacement of the NGM from 2008 by the 
2016 version is assessed. Previously, everywhere in France, the NGMs were taken back by a logistic 
company which dropped them at a local recycling station. There, the treatment of the NGMs could vary 
locally, but they were probably sent to incineration or to landfill because the NGM2008 was not originally 
designed to be recycled. In 2016, it was decided to set a recycling program where all the NGM2008 used 
in France would be collected and sent to one manual sorting facility. All the NGMs would be manually 
dismantled in this facility. This program has already started today and the recyclable parts are sent to 
recycling facilities for plastics or metals and the non-recyclable ones are sent to an incinerator with energy 
recovery. In the same time, the NGM2016 has been “eco-designed” to be easy to dismantle and the 
quantity of different materials stuck together was strongly reduced, which should enable it to be entirely 
recyclable at its end-of-life.

All the results, tables and figures, are available in Appendix 10. Generally, results follow the same

Theoretically, the results should be the same, no matter the method, in the case of an entirely closed 
loop (100-100) or in the case without recycling (0-0). Verifying that the same result is obtained with 
different formulas for such cases is actually a way to check if the end-of-life formulas do not double count 
or forget to count some impacts. Here it is not the case. It is especially visible for the 0-0 scenario (see 
Figure 19-e) and it can be explained easily. The reason is that the waste generated during the 
manufacturing stage is modelled independently of the waste generated at the end-of-life of the product. 
The recycling rate (𝑅2) defined for the NGM can be applied only to the material in the NGM and do not 
include the losses from the manufacturing process. For example, there are losses in the metal working 
processes and the metal scraps generated are always sent to recycling, independently of the end-of-life of 
the product itself. This means that when the value of 𝑅2 is set to 0 in an extreme scenario (see Table 3), 
there is still a bit of recycling in the model due to the recycling of the scrap from the manufacturing 
process. Therefore, the 0-0 scenario is not a “true” 0-0 scenario and it explains why there are still small 
differences between the methods in this scenario, they are due to the recycling of the scrap from the 
manufacturing process which is modelled differently with different methods. It is the same reason that 
explains the differences in the scenarios 50-50 or 100-100 that should normally give equal results with all 
the methods. 
Actually, if the losses in the production processes are removed from the model, the same result is 
obtained for all the methods in each of the scenarios 100-100, 50-50 and 0-0 (results not shown). 
However, this illustrates that the modelling of the waste generated during the manufacturing stage is a 
practical problem and it is developed later (part 8.2.b). 
 
The method that really gives different results is the APOS method from ecoinvent (AllocDef). This is 
due to the fact that the database used in that case is different from the one used in all the other methods 
(the ecoinvent recycled content database). The difference between the APOS and the recycled content 
database from ecoinvent will be discussed in part 8.2.a. Nonetheless, it can be noted that the AllocDef 
method gives very high results compared to the other ones, especially for the impact category “mineral 
resource depletion” (see Appendix 10).

The scenario 100-0, i.e. the use of 100% of secondary material but no recycling at the end-of-life 
illustrates well the behaviour of the different methods (see Figure 19-b). In such a case, the recycled 
content method (RecC) gives a low score because the secondary material does not bear any burden from 
its previous life cycle. The waste mining method (WastM) gives a score even lower since credits for 
avoiding the disposal of the material are attributed to the user of the secondary material. In contrast, the 
recycling at the end-of-life method (SubEoL) gives a score more than twice as high as the recycled content 
or waste mining methods. The explanation is that using secondary material is equal to using primary 
material with this method. The old PEF formula (PEF1) gives a score in between. This is logical since it 
was a 50-50 method mixing the waste mining and the recycling at the end-of-life methods. The CFF 
formula (PEF2) gives a score higher than the old PEF formula. It can be explained by the fact that the CFF 
uses an allocation factor A for each material. Here, a factor 0.2 is attributed to the metals that represent the 
largest amount of material (50kg over 70kg). The CFF is a mix between the recycled content and the 
recycling at the end-of-life methods and when A is lower than 0.5, as it is here, the CFF has a behaviour 
which tends toward the one of the recycling at the end-of-life method. It is what can be observed in the 
results.  
 
The scenario 0-100 (Figure 19-c) also illustrates the opposite behaviour since methods favouring 
recycling at the end-of-life will give the lowest scores. The ranking: SubEoL < PEF2 < PEF1 < RecC < 
WastM is therefore consistent. Regarding the APOS, it appears from the results on extreme scenarios that 
its behaviour is actually similar to the one of the recycled content method, i.e. favouring the use of 
recycled material over the recyclability. 
 
This part has shown that for some situations, applying different end-of-life methods will give 
results that can vary with a factor two. These situations were called “extreme scenarios” but they are not 
==PAGE:52==
 
 
46/109 
necessarily unrealistic and can be representative of real cases. For example, it is common that products 
containing 100% of recycled material are not recyclable because of the low material quality or their 
application. For example, benches made of a mix of recycled plastic which cannot be recycled or waste 
bags made of recycled plastic but that cannot be recycled because of the function of the product itself. If 
somebody wants to report the environmental performances of such products and communicate them, he 
will try to comply with a standard. It will surely be one standard using the recycled content method (BSI, 
2011; The International EPD System, 2015; WRI and WBCSD, 2011) that will give a low score. If 
however it was chosen to use the PEF, the score would then be at least 50% higher for climate change (see 
Figure 19-b). But both standards are supposed to be made for reporting environmental performances and 
they all claim to comply with the ISO14040/4 standards. Therefore it seems that the choice of the 
standard, and as a consequence the method for modelling the end-of-life, has a great influence on the 
results. Besides it would be possible to choose the standard that gives the “best” result for a product.

is in between, at 100 kgCO2eq. These differences are not negligible when they are multiplied by the 
number of NGM at stake (around 1000 in France for example). It can also influence the recommendation 
that the consultant will give to its client. If the consultant had used the end-of-life recycling method it 
could have concluded that the three scenarios were somewhat equivalent while using the recycled content 
method its recommendation would have been to improve the design of the NGM2016. Consultants need 
therefore to be aware of the biases that are related to the choice of an end-of-life method when they 
perform LCAs where recycling or materials are important contributors to the impacts.  
These results illustrate also another problem linked to the diversity of end-of-life methods. We 
could imagine that the company had done an LCA eight years ago for its NGM2008. Therefore it would 
have used the recycled content method which was the most common method for LCA of products at that 
time. Today, it performs an LCA of its NGM2016 and decides to do it with the BPX-30-323 (AFNOR, 
2011) which has been developed meanwhile and is widely used in France. For metals, which represent the 
major part of the NGM2016, the BPX30-323 recommends using a method equivalent to the recycling at 
the end-of-life method. If it then compares the score of the NGM2016 (scenario 3) with the end-of-life 
recycling method to the score of the NGM2008 (scenario 1 or 2) with the recycled content method it could 
have concluded that for climate change, its new NGM performs better than the old version (see Figure 21). 
It is of course not right to compare LCA results of different studies made with different methods. 
Nevertheless, it is not likely that a company will make a new LCA for a product if it already has one and 
an engineer which is not an LCA specialist will probably not pay attention to that. 
The proliferation of standards with different approaches for the end-of-life, as it has been 
illustrated in the previous parts of this thesis, is therefore a problem for comparison of LCA of products 
over time. The fact that the choice of the method for modelling end-of-life can have a bigger influence on 
the result than the design choices themselves is another big issue. This also means that it is possible to 
choose a specific method to obtain the desired results for example. 
 
From the point of view of the methods proliferation, the PEF initiative is interesting. Indeed, even 
though it adds a new method, its aim is to unify all the different methods used by companies for different 
purposes (eco-design, EPD, communication, etc.). The next part will therefore present some sensitivity 
analyses made on the results for this case study with the new PEF end-of-life formula (the CFF), in order 
to analyse its behaviour and its sensitive parameters.

d. 
Presentation of an end-of-life method sensitivity analysis  
As demonstrated earlier, the choice of the end-of-life method can have a high influence on the 
results of an LCA and can possibly change the ranking of the different scenarios assessed. However, this 
is usually never tested in the different sensitivity analyses carried out for an LCA study. Indeed, testing 
another end-of-life method will require to change the structure of the model or to introduce many 
parameters. The new CFF formula offers a new opportunity here. Its complexity and its many parameters 
make it possible to implement easily what could be called an “end-of-life method sensitivity analysis”. 
As explained before, the CFF can be seen as a mix between two methods: the end-of-life recycling 
and the recycled content methods. The importance of each method in the CFF depends on a coefficient 
(A) which is defined for each material. If the model using the CFF is well structured and parametrized, 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
52/109 
varying the value of A is equivalent to changing the end-of-life method applied. A can vary from 0, where 
the CFF is equivalent to the end-of-life recycling method, to 1 where it is equivalent to the recycled 
content method. These two methods gave the most extreme results (see 7.3.b), so varying A in all its range 
of possible values (0 to 1) is a way to see the influence of changing the end-of-life formula. 
This principle was applied on the case study to compare the ranking of the different scenarios 
according to the value of A. Even though each material has its own value for A, for most of the cases only 
two values are used: 0.5 (for plastic) and 0.8 (for metals). The steps to follow to perform the analysis are 
the following: 
1. Defining an interval for the A values (here 0.05) 
2. Creating a set of parameters with all the possible combinations of values for 
(𝐴𝑚𝑒𝑡𝑎𝑙; 𝐴𝑝𝑙𝑎𝑠𝑡𝑖𝑐) with the 𝐴 coefficients taking values between 0 and 1 with the interval 
0.05. Here, it gives 441 combinations: [0;0.05;0.1;…;0.95;1]x[0;0.05;0.1;…;0.95;1]. 
3. Computing the LCA model with all the combinations of parameters defined previously, for 
the three scenarios. This is normally easy to do with recent LCA software in which the use 
of parameters is possible. 
4. Creating a matrix that gives for each combination, the scenario with the lowest score for a 
given impact category (see Figure 25). The same can be done for the scenario with the 
highest score. The use of a different colour for each scenario enables the easy visualization 
of the results. 
 
Figure 25 shows that for most of the combinations of (𝐴𝑚𝑒𝑡𝑎𝑙; 𝐴𝑝𝑙𝑎𝑠𝑡𝑖𝑐), scenario 2 gives the lowest 
score for climate change. Nevertheless, there are some combinations for which scenario 1 gives the lowest 
score, this corresponds to what was already identified in Figure 21. The matrix demonstrates easily that 
the comparison of scenario 1 and 2 is not consistent with all the end-of-life formulas and that the 
difference between scenario 1 and 2 is therefore not so significant, contrary to what the other sensitivity 
analyses suggested. For all the other impact categories, scenario 2 gives the lowest score independently of 
the values of A and scenario 3 is always the scenario giving the highest score. 
A deeper analysis of the matrix shows that some combinations of values for (𝐴𝑚𝑒𝑡𝑎𝑙; 𝐴𝑝𝑙𝑎𝑠𝑡𝑖𝑐)

number of recycling loops but these methods are not the most used. Another limitation is due to the energy 
recovery because here all the benefits are attributed to the life cycle producing the material recovered 
energetically which is normally not the case in the original recycled content method (see part 5.2) so the 
representation of the recycled content using the CFF is not perfectly right. This was also listed as one of 
the limits of the PEF itself.

An important lesson from the interviews is about the choice of a specific method for modelling end-
of-life and more generally the choice of an LCA methodology. There are generally two main situations. In 
the first one, it is necessary to follow a given standard because it is a requirement for the use of the LCA 
results. The classical example is an EPD for a construction material which must be performed according to 
the EN15804 standard. In this case, the LCA practitioner has no liberty regarding the end-of-life 
modelling: the standard is very precise about it. In that case it is the recycled content method which is 
used. The second case is when LCA is used as a tool to assess the environmental performance of a 
product, but not with the purpose of using the LCA result for an official communication. LCA can be a 
tool to validate eco-design choices for example. Then, the LCA consultant is completely free to choose 
whichever method he likes. 
However, for the consultants interviewed, the practice is generally to use a method they can easily 
justify to their client. It is also important that the same method is used by default by all consultants in the 
company. The PEF method is therefore the method used because it comes from a European authority so it 
is considered to have a strong legitimacy. The method is also considered as more “fair” than the other ones 
because it considers both the use of recycled material and the recycling. This is why this method has been 
extensively described before (see 6.2 and 6.3). 
8.2. Practical problems faced when modelling recycling in LCA 
During the interviews and while I was modelling the case study, some issues appeared regarding the 
modelling of recycling in LCA, in addition to the problem of the choice of the method. This part will 
focus on two issues which have been identified as the most problematic ones. 
a. 
The multiplication of parameters for which data is very uncertain 
The first answer that came when consultants were asked what problems they faced when they had to 
model recycling in LCA is “the lack of data”. Finding data regarding waste management in general is 
difficult. As soon as a product is sent to a municipal waste management system at its end-of-life, there are 
a lot of uncertainties about its real end-of-life because waste management systems vary a lot locally and 
the waste treatment will depend on many factors that can be independent from the product’s design. Some 
average statistics are sometimes available at the scale of countries, but for some type of waste only. These 
statistics usually do not represent the heterogeneity of the real end-of-life. Even when a product is 
collected separately and sent to a specific waste treatment, the uncertainty about its end-of-life remains 
high, especially for recycling. Finding information about the end-of-life of a product can therefore be very 
time consuming and this information may not even exist. 
Nevertheless, the trend for the end-of-life formulas is to increase the number of parameters 
involved. Looking at the evolution of formulas show that many parameters have appeared progressively 
like the quality factors in the end-of-life recycling method, first for the material which is sent to recycling 
(𝑄𝑆𝑜𝑢𝑡𝑄𝑝
⁄
), and then for the virgin material which is used in the model but corresponds to the secondary 
material used in reality (𝑄𝑆𝑖𝑛𝑄𝑝
⁄
). Now, the PEF has introduced an allocation factor between the 
recycling at the end-of-life and the use of recycled content (𝐴). Furthermore, the modelling of energy 
recovery requires many parameters and an allocation factor (𝐵) has even been added in the CFF. This 
addition of new parameters comes from a greater interest for the modelling of the end-of-life that was 
probably neglected in the beginning of LCA, especially for LCA of products. These parameters enable the 
modelling of more complex end-of-life scenarios and more accurately. The problem is that on one hand 
the number of parameters in the end-of-life formulas has increased a lot, but on the other hand the lack of 
data and the uncertainty of the information available have not been solved.  
==PAGE:62==
 
 
56/109 
The example of PMMA recycling in the case study presented in chapter 7 gives a good illustration 
of the problem. In that case, there is a specific recycling program for the NGMs. They are all collected and 
treated at the same place. However, knowing what is the end-of-life of the PMMA from the NGM is not 
obvious. The PMMA which is not mixed with other materials is separated and collected by a waste 
company “to be recycled”. How it will be recycled is unknown and the waste company did not want to 
communicate this information. Indeed, the waste directive (European Commission, 2008) requires only 
that the waste company provides a certificate that the PMMA will be recycled but there is no requirement 
to say how. It is therefore necessary to make an assumption about the real recycling of the PMMA and 
several possibilities exist for this plastic. It could be grinded to be used as filler in acrylic paint, mixed 
with other plastics, sent in China or even depolymerized into MMA to produce new PMMA. Since it is 
already impossible to know what kind of recycling takes place, defining properly the recycling rate R2 is 
impossible. The collection rate is known but the loss in the sorting and recycling process can be 
significant for plastics. Defining parameters like 𝑄𝑆𝑜𝑢𝑡𝑄𝑝
⁄
 does not make more sense. 
The case of PMMA is already a good illustration of the problem and yet it is a case where the exact 
quantity of material “sent to recycling” is known. A more problematic example is the case of WEEE that 
was present in the not-simplified version of the NGM. WEEE is made of a mix of materials (e.g. LED, 
printed circuit board, plastics, metals, etc.). Once it is collected by a waste company, it is almost 
impossible to know what the end-of-life of each material will be. Usually, a large part is not even recycled 
but is probably recovered energetically like the plastics which contain flame retardants. 
The problem of data quality is therefore a major issue for modelling the end-of-life of a product. 
The new PEF end-of-life formula, which involves more parameters and is more complex, enables the 
model to be closer to the reality. However, this is true only for specific cases where data is available with 
a good quality. In most of the cases, the quality of data regarding the end-of-life is low and the additional 
parameters will only increase the uncertainty of the model. 
A positive point of having so many parameters is that it could raise people’s awareness about the 
importance of the end-of-life and its key parameters. The side effect is that it takes much more time to 
model the end-of-life and the risk of mistakes is increased.  
 
The creators of the PEF are surely aware that data has a key role in the PEF end-of-life model. 
Indeed, they wish to provide a set of default data for the main parameters that must be used if no specific 
data can be found or justified. Annex C of the new PEFCR guideline (European Commission, 2017) gives 
default values for different materials and applications. For now, only values for 𝐴, 𝐵, 𝑅1, 𝑅2 and 𝑅3 are 
available. Normally, data for the quality factors should be provided in the PCR too. Providing default data 
is a good thing for LCA professionals since finding the data is often the most time consuming task and it 
can help to model quickly the first iteration of an LCA or to perform an LCA screening. It will also limit 
the possibility of choosing arbitrarily default parameters that could give the desired results. However, 
using a default parameter will still be arbitrary and may not be representative of the real case study 
modelled. This does not necessarily solve the issue of the data quality and it will inevitably favour some 
products or materials. 
The interview and the observation of the practice regarding recycling suggest that these default 
values will be extensively used since it is a good time-saving trick. The time spent to create the LCA 
model is very important for LCA consultants, so if they can save time somewhere they will probably take 
advantage of it, especially if they have already “lost” time to model the complex PEF end-of-life formula. 
Therefore, it can be guessed that it is only for few situations that real data will be used instead of the 
default one: if data is easily available, if it has a considerable contribution to the results or if the results 
==PAGE:63==
 
 
57/109 
could be “improved” because the real value for the parameter would give better results than the default 
one. 
As default values are expected to be widely used, the process to define the default values in the 
PEFCR is very important and attention should be pay that it is really transparent. Given the importance 
that this default values will have in the future PEF studies, it is possible that some lobbying takes place to 
influence the choice of the default values since such a choice could favour some types of products or 
materials in future LCA studies. As a consequence, it is likely that many discussions and debates will arise 
in the future, regarding the default parameters for the PEF formula. 
b. 
The need for entire databases adapted to the end-of-life formulas 
As presented earlier, many standards and end-of-life formulas exist. However, there are not as many 
databases as standards and this is somewhat a problem. To model industrial products, it is essential to have 
access to databases for all the background processes (e.g. material extraction and refining, electricity, 
etc.). Today there are only few databases that contain this background data and the most famous one is 
ecoinvent which is widely used. 
It is important to make the distinction between an end-of-life modelling method which refers to a 
formula that states how the different processes (𝐸𝑣, 𝐸𝑅𝑒𝑐𝑐𝑦𝑙𝑒𝑑, 𝐸𝑑, 𝑒𝑡𝑐. ) should be allocated and linked 
between each other; and a database that contains all these processes. The problem is that it is necessary to 
model the end-of-life or to solve the problem of multifunctionality for the background processes 
themselves so a database is generally associated with a “system model”. It is therefore necessary that the 
system model of the database used is consistent with the recycling method and the end-of-life formula 
applied.  
For example, the ecoinvent database is available with three system models: consequential, cut-off 
and APOS (allocation at the point of substitution). From these three, only one is consistent with some of 
the standards presented: the cut-off system model which is equivalent to the recycled content method. The 
consequential system model is not relevant here since the recycling methods presented in this thesis were 
supposed to be attributional ones. The allocation method from ecoinvent (also called “allocation default”) 
is not recommended by any standard but was investigated in the case study (see chapter 7). This method 
gave strange results and scores were generally higher than with other methods. 
For example, when the case study for the NGM was assessed, it was noticed that the sum of the 
process for the recycling of aluminium (waste treatment) and the process for producing secondary 
aluminium (material process) gives very different impacts depending on the database used: the recycled 
content or the APOS (here noted AllocDef) one (see Figure 26). In theory, both databases use an 
allocation method and in that case the impact of recycling should be allocated between the producer of 
secondary material and the user of this secondary material. In the case of the recycled content approach, 
everything is attributed to the user of the material so the production of secondary material with the 
recycled content approach should be equal to the sum of the recycling and the production of secondary 
material with the allocation method from ecoinvent (APOS) since the source of the data is the same. This 
is not the case and it explains why the results with the allocation method were sometimes very different 
from the other methods in chapter 7.

Actually, the allocation strategy is different between the recycled-content and APOS system models 
and even though results are similar for most of the processes, they differ for recycled materials and waste 
(Steubing et al., 2016; Wernet et al., 2016). From the case of aluminium it seems that this difference can 
be highly significant (Figure 26). The problem with the aluminium process has also been highlighted 
recently in the LCA List because someone was surprised to find carrots in this inventory. It is actually 
normal because the APOS system model performs first an expansion of the system to include treatment 
processes for by-products (Wernet et al., 2016), which results in the presence of many material seemingly 
unrelated. 
 
Modelling the end-of-life with a formula like the PEF, for which there is not yet a dedicated 
database raises some concerns. The only suitable database from ecoinvent in such a case is the recycled 
content one because it is known what the interesting processes contain (𝐸𝑣, 𝐸𝑅𝑒𝑐𝑦𝑐𝑙𝑖𝑛𝑔, 𝐸𝑅𝑒𝑐𝑦𝑐𝑙𝑖𝑛𝑔𝐸𝑜𝐿, etc.) 
and there are not already some allocations between life cycles. Nevertheless, it is necessary to be very 
careful when performing the LCI. Indeed, a common mistake is to mix two different methods for 
modelling the end-of-life: the one we want to use (e.g. the CFF) and the one per default in the database 
(e.g. the recycled content one). This is especially important for the waste generated at the manufacturing 
stage of the life cycle. Figure 27 illustrates this issue. 
Figure 27a presents a case where the recycled content method is modelled with the Alloc Rec 
database from ecoinvent. The focus is on the manufacturing process. For example, in the cases-study the 
steel was transformed into a final piece of metal with the process “Metal working, average for steel 
product manufacturing {RER}| processing | Alloc Rec, S”. To model 1kg of transformed steel, it is 
necessary to call 1kg of steel and 1kg of this “metal working process” separately. But there are losses in 
the transformation of the steel so the process “metal working process” calls 0.227kg of additional material 
to compensate this loss (arrow 1 in Figure 27a). This process also includes the end-of-life treatment of the 
0.227kg of scrap steel resulting from the loss (arrow 2 in Figure 27a). Therefore, this process from the 
database already includes the material and end-of-life stage of the additional material and they are 
modelled with the method associated with the database, i.e. the recycling method here. 
 
==PAGE:65==

manufacturing processes from the database, additional material is called to compensate the loss (1) and the scrap resulting 
from this loss is sent to a specific end-of-life process (2). The additional material due to the loss in the manufacturing process 
(pink) is treated differently than the material ending up in the product (green). 
 
When the CFF is applied, the usual mistake is then to model properly the material and end-of-life 
stages for the material contained in the product but to forget to modify the manufacturing processes. In 
such a case, the material resulting from the loss in the manufacturing process (0.227kg here) is modelled 
with the recycled content method while the material in the product (1kg here) is modelled with the CFF as 
illustrated in Figure 27b. This creates a double problem: there is a mixture of two different methods in the 
model and the additional material that is called in the manufacturing process (0.227kg) is a pure virgin 
material while it should be a mix of virgin and recycled material like the steel used in the NGM. 
To avoid this it is necessary to modify the manufacturing process from the database to model the 
additional material with the CFF in the same way as the material in the product and to model the end-of-
life of this additional material according to the CFF. 
 
From the interviews, it seems that some consultants are aware of this problem. However, it is not 
obvious that everyone performing an LCA will think about looking inside all the processes of the database 
to see if the materials and waste are modelled properly. It is especially true when these processes are not 
appearing at the material or end-of-life stages but at the manufacturing or use stages, where less attention 
is paid to the modelling of the end-of-life. Theoretically, all the background processes should be modified 
to model the material use and the end-of-life according to the end-of-life method we want to use (e.g. the 
PEF). In practice, as long as there is not a PEF database, it is impossible to model the end-of-life properly 
according to the PEF methodology because the model will always mix two methods (here the recycled 
content one with the PEF one). 
==PAGE:66==
 
 
60/109 
If the part of the LCI modelled with the recycled content method in a PEF model was negligible it 
would not be a problem, but as illustrated here with the loss in the manufacturing process superior to 20%, 
it can be significant. The conclusion is that an end-of-life formula should be applied, only if the database 
used is consistent with this method. 
 
This example from the case study raised another issue, related to the structure of the databases or 
the LCA software and the structure of the end-of-life formula. The PEF gives one formula that is valid for 
one material. This formula includes everything for this material: use of primary and secondary material, 
recycling at the end-of-life, energy recovery and disposal. In theory it seems possible to include all these 
processes (including the end-of-life) in one material process that will be called in the model. If the 
recycling rate and the energy recovery rate at the end-of-life are known, it is theoretically not a problem to 
call the end-of-life processes at the material stage in the model. In practice it is not really possible to have 
one end-of-life formula for each material because one material can be used in different pieces of a product 
and have different end-of-life. For example, in the NGM some pieces of PMMA were made only of 
PMMA and were recyclable, while in other pieces, the PMMA was mixed with other materials and 
therefore not recyclable. Also between the material used in the product itself and the material that will be 
lost in the manufacturing stage, the end-of-life is completely different. As a consequence, except for very 
simple products, it is not possible to have one set of parameters (𝑅1, 𝑅2, 𝑅3, 𝑄𝑆, etc.) for each material. In 
the case study presented it was done like that, for example, one 𝑅2 value was calculated for each material 
but this required a lot of time and calculations to take everything into account. 
In practice LCA of products are modelled with a product assembly perspective because it is the way 
industrial companies design their products and therefore the information is usually available as a BOM 
(bill of material) where a product is seen as an assembly made of sub-assemblies which are themselves 
made of sub-assemblies or basic items. LCA software, like SimaPro, allows the modelling of products 
with assemblies. However, this requires the use of a waste scenario for the end-of-life where each sub-
assembly can undergo a different end-of-life treatment. 
Therefore, to use the PEF in LCA of products, it is necessary to clearly separate the material part 
from the end-of-life part of the formula. It is also important to understand how the formula is made 
because the flows that are modelled do not correspond to the physical flows. And the introduction of an 
allocation factor makes it even more unclear. For example, material sent to recycling is for one part sent to 
recycling in the model ((1 −𝐴) × 𝑅1), with a credit for avoiding virgin material production, and for 
another part (𝐴× 𝑅1) not modelled. It is the same for the use of secondary material which calls both 
secondary and primary material in the model. The diagram presented in Figure 16 helps to understand this 
for the CFF but it is also necessary to have a clear and modular structure for the PEF to model it with the 
current databases and software.

Conclusion 
 
This master thesis main objective was to investigate the issue of recycling in LCA, from a 
company perspective. The observation of the practice of LCA consultants and their interviews have shown 
that companies rely mainly on LCA standards to model the recycling occurring in the life cycle of  
products. However, it has also been highlighted that the number of standards or end-of-life formulas has 
strongly increased in the last decade. Sometimes there are only slight differences between end-of-life 
formulas, but these differences can also be highly significant. The following paragraphs give the different 
conclusions and the main learnings from the master thesis. 
 
The existence of different approaches to model recycling in LCA is related to different 
fundamental approaches of LCA and recycling. 
In LCA in general, different modelling approaches exist and the distinction between attributional 
and consequential modelling is fundamental. This is reflected in the different ways to solve the 
multifunctional problem of recycling. Usually substitution is opposed to partitioning. Both approaches 
have pros and cons and their proponents. What is important to understand is that they are based on 
different visions of LCA that are reflected by the numerous debates in the LCA community. Attributional 
modelling is widely used by companies to perform LCA of products but there are still many debates like, 
for example, whether substitution can be used in an attributional model. 
Another opposition that is found among the proponents of partitioning, but that is more general in 
the case of recycling, is whether socio-economic aspects should be considered in LCA. Indeed, economic 
information is commonly used in end-of-life formulas but it is also much criticised. 
Last but not least, there is a divergence between favouring the use of recycled material in the 
products or their recyclability. 
These different approaches have led to the development of different methods and end-of-life 
formulas to model recycling. Furthermore, even though LCA is supposed to be defined by the 
ISO14040/44 standard, the ambiguity of this key standard regarding multifunctionality and recycling has 
led to different interpretations and is partly responsible for the proliferation of standards with different 
approaches of recycling. 
 
The methods in the different standards can be classified into four main general methods for 
modelling recycling. 
Some general methods have been distinguished: the recycled content method, the end-of-life 
recycling method, the waste mining method and the methods based on the number of recycling loops. The 
last category includes many methods but they are hardly applicable to LCA of products because they are 
material-oriented instead of product-oriented. The recycled content method and the waste mining method 
both favour the use of recycled material, but the first one has a partitioning approach whereas the last one 
has a substitution approach. The end-of-life recycling method is a substitution method that favours the 
recyclability of products. The question of which process is displaced, and to what extent, is very important 
for methods involving substitution. 
 
The new standards propose more complex methods which are actually mixes of different 
methods; the new PEF formula is even a mix of methods with fundamentally different approaches. 
The new LCA standards (BPX30-323, PEF) follow a new trend that consists in mixing the 
different methods identified in this thesis. The approach is usually to mix two methods with opposite 
==PAGE:68==
 
 
62/109 
behaviours regarding the design option favoured: use of recycled content vs. recyclability. The purpose is 
to have a method that is fair with a less extreme behaviour regarding recycling. This kind of methods can 
also be adapted to each material according to the market situation of the secondary material. For example, 
in the case of a material for which the demand for secondary material is higher than the supply, the 
method can favour the recyclability of products containing this material. 
The new Product Environmental Footprint (PEF) promoted by the European authorities to 
harmonise the different standards available for companies is following this new approach. It is essential to 
understand this standard as it will probably be the most used in the future. The new PEF end-of-life 
formula, called the Circular Footprint Formula (CFF) mixes the recycled content method with the end-of-
life recycling method. It can be criticised for mixing two methods that stem from two different approach 
of LCA with a partitioning method and a substitution method. It is also criticised to favour incineration at 
the expense of recycling. Its complexity and the numerous parameters it involves also introduce new 
challenges regarding its applicability for a company. 
 
The choice of the recycling method in LCA of products is rarely questioned but it has a 
strong influence on the results of an LCA and as a consequence on the recommendation that can be 
given from these results. 
The application of six different end-of-life formulas to a case study showed that the choice of the 
method has a strong influence on the results. In comparative studies, when the difference between 
scenarios is low or when the case study involves a high quantity of recycled material or a high recycling 
rate, the choice of the method can even change the ranking of the different scenarios. As only one end-of-
life modelling method is generally tested in an LCA study, this bias cannot be measured by a sensitivity or 
uncertainty analysis. 
The observation of the practice from a LCA consultant company showed that they do not cheat to 
use the method that would give the desired results, however it is still a risk to consider. The practice is 
generally to always use the same standard for consistency between the projects, for example the EN15804 
for construction material and the PEF for any other product. In such a case, if a method is used 
systematically without knowing its limits and biases, this can lead to give recommendations that would 
have been different with another method. It is therefore essential to be aware of what are the underlying 
principles of an end-of-life method, its biases and the design strategy it favours. 
 
Nevertheless, the new PEF EoL formula gives an opportunity to assess the sensitivity of an 
LCA model to the choice of the recycling method. 
The Circular Footprint Formula increases the complexity of an LCA model but it is not just a 
drawback and it can be used to improve the sensitivity analysis of the results. Since the new PEF end-of-
life formula is a mix of methods and this mix is defined with an allocation factor, this factor can be used to 
change the end-of-life formula applied. This master thesis proposed a simple procedure to investigate the 
influence of the choice of the end-of-life method and visualize it. 
 
Finding data of good quality to model the end-of-life is problematic for LCA practitioners. 
The PEF provides default values to fix that but it could raise new concerns. 
Finding data for the end-of-life is very time consuming in practice and sometimes impossible. 
However, the new formulas include more and more parameters. This increases the accuracy of the model 
but the uncertainty of some parameters can be very high. For example, the case study showed that the 
results were very sensitive to the quality ratios which are parameters that are usually very uncertain. To 
help LCA practitioners finding data and to limit the possibility to manipulate the results, the PEF provides 
==PAGE:69==
 
 
63/109 
default values for most of the parameters. The analysis of the practice from LCA consultants suggests that 
these default values will probably be widely used in future LCA studies. Thus, the default values in the 
PEF are likely to be subject of many discussions in the future and attention must be paid that the process 
to define them is transparent. 
 
The PEF end-of-life formula should not be used as long as it has not a dedicated database. 
Databases are closely linked to the end-of-life modelling methods. Indeed, the multifunctional 
problem of recycling has already been solved with a certain method for the processes in a database. 
Therefore, a database is associated with one end-of-life approach. This is sometimes forgotten and it 
results in mixing different end-of-life modelling methods in one LCA. It is especially important for the 
material loss from manufacturing process where less attention is paid to the end-of-life but that can have a 
significant influence on the results. Furthermore, it seems that the database ecoinvent “allocation at the 
point of substitution” should be avoided when recycling is important in an LCA model. Finally, it is 
important to understand how the PEF end-of-life formula is structured to model it correctly in LCA 
software, especially when a product is modelled with an assembly approach.

Appendix 1 
The ISO 14044 hierarchy for allocation 
 
This is the allocation procedure to follow according to the ISO 14044 
“4.3.4.2 Allocation procedure 
a) Step1: Whenever possible, allocation should be avoided by: 
1) dividing the unit process to be allocated into two or more sub-processes and collecting 
the input and output data related to these sub-processes, or 
2) expanding the product system to include the additional functions related to the co-
products, taking into account the requirements of 4.4.3.3. 
b) Step 2: Where allocation cannot be avoided, the inputs and outputs of the system should be 
partitioned between its different products or functions in a way that reflects the underlying 
physical relationships between them. For example, input and outputs are changed by quantitative 
changes in the products or functions delivered by the system. 
c) 
Step 3: Where physical relationship alone cannot be established or used as the basis for 
allocation, the inputs should be allocated between the products and functions in a way that 
reflects other relationships between them. For example, input and output data might be allocated 
between co-products in proportion to the economic value of the products” (ISO, 2006a)

Appendix 2 
Overall presentation of four methods based on the number of recycling loops 
This appendix gives an overall presentation of four methods to model recycling based on the 
number of recycling loops. The details of the methods and the calculations are available in separated 
appendixes further down. 
 
1. Method from the ILCD handbook and (Newell and Field, 1998) 
The formula which is given in the ILCD handbook (EC-JRC, 2010) and recommended for 
attributional modelling takes into account all the impacts (production, recycling, disposal) from the 
different recycling loops and distributes them on a per-kg basis. This method had already been introduced 
ten years before with the name “Product Stream Life Cycle Inventory” (PS-LCI)(Newell and Field, 1998). 
It considers that a secondary material can have different inventories because a part of it may have 
been recycled only once before, while another part may have been recycled multiple times. The number of 
cycles a specific secondary material has already made is not practically measurable. Therefore, the 
approach consists in taking an average number of recycling loops. 
The average inventory (of primary material production, recycling and end-of-life) per unit of 
material (𝑒) is defined and calculated. The method considers first the total amount of material which will 
be used over its multiple life cycles (𝐼), also called “total mass” (see Figure 28a, up). Then it calculates 
the total inventory (primary material production, recycling and end-of-life) related to this material in the 
multiple life cycles (𝑈), also called “total load” (see Figure 28a, down).

The ISO14049 (ISO, 2012) gives examples on how to solve the multifunctional problem of 
recycling. Especially, it presents a way to perform allocation based on the number of recycling loops for 
the case of open-loop recycling. The approach is different from the other multi-recycling approaches 
which consider a pool of material where all the impacts (Ev, Ed, Er) from all the life cycles are shared 
between all the life cycles. Here, only the impacts of the first life cycle are shared between the first life 
cycle and the next ones. The impacts of the end of life taking place in next life cycle are not attributed to 
the first one for example. The method is a “true” partitioning method since it defines an allocation factor 
to use for the processes (production, recycling and disposal) of the life cycle studied. The details of the 
calculations and the method are presented in Appendix 6. 
The method is presented in the ISO14049 on one example but is hardly generalizable to all the 
cases of recycling, because it requires information on the fate of the secondary material produced as well 
as on the origin of the secondary material used.

give an end-of-life formula which is derived from a classical partitioning method between two life cycles
