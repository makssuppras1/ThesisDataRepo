Abstract
Energy poverty is a term used to indicate the general difficulty or inability of a household
to meet its basic energy needs. It is closely related to the concepts of energy justice and
sustainable energy transition. However, even if an institution such as the EU adopts such
a definition and prefers to track this phenomenon under a consensual framework, most
countries do not reach consensus on adopting a definition for energy poverty or on using
a single indicator.
Given the importance of mitigating energy poverty in the context of a realistic and sus-
tainable energy future, it is important to understand its main determinants and drivers to
support policy making.
This study analyzes these determinants for 23 European countries and differentiates be-
tween five main areas (the Nordics, Baltics, Central, Southern, and Eastern Europe). By
using a panel ARDL model for 2012–2021, the results suggest that economic develop-
ment (per capita GDP) and increased CO2 emissions per capita drive the spread of energy
poverty at a European level. On the other hand, per capita primary energy consumption
mitigates energy poverty. Although, results are not necessarily true at a regional level,
where heterogeneity is found.
Furthermore, the study investigates the potential effects of dispatchable, variable low-
carbon sources, and fossil fuel primary production on energy poverty at a European and
regional level. Results show that VRE primary production at an aggregate level mitigates
energy poverty; however, this does not hold true in the Nordic countries. Heterogeneity is
also observed across the results of the primary production from hydroelectric and nuclear
sources on energy poverty at a regional level. Fossil fuel primary production, on the other
hand, is found to be a driver of energy poverty in both the Nordics and Central European
countries.
Keywords: Energy Poverty, Fuel Poverty, Energy Systems.

Introduction
The motivation for this thesis is the importance of understanding and mitigating energy
poverty in the context of a sustainable energy future. Energy poverty is closely related to
the concepts of energy justice and sustainable energy transition. However, most countries
do not have a consensus on a definition for energy poverty or on using a single indicator
to track it.
Given the significance of addressing energy poverty in the pursuit of a realistic and sus-
tainable energy future, it is crucial to understand its main determinants and drivers. This
knowledge can inform policy making. The purpose of this thesis is to contribute to this
understanding by analyzing the determinants and drivers of energy poverty in European
countries, and by examining the potential effects of different sources of primary energy
production on energy poverty at both the European and regional level.
Overall, the contribution of this thesis is to provide insights into the complex issue of
energy poverty in Europe, and to offer a nuanced understanding of the potential effects
of macroeconomic indicators as well as different sources of primary energy production on
it at both the European and regional level. These findings can inform policy making and
support the transition to a more sustainable energy future.
This paper is structured as follows: the introduction provides a literature review and de-
scriptive statistics, as well as an overview of the countries included in the study and their
current policies. The second section presents the scientific question and motivation for
the study. The third section describes the method used, including details on the data,
panel unit root tests, lag length, panel cointegration test, panel ARDL, and panel causal-
ity test. The fourth and fifth sections present the results and discussion for Model 1 and
Model 2, respectively, including analysis for Europe as a whole and for the five subgroups
(Nordics, Baltics, Central, Southern, and Eastern Europe). The sixth section discusses
the limitations of the study and presents the conclusion.
Energy poverty is a term used to indicate the difficulty or inability of a household to meet its
energy needs, moreover, it is closely related to the concepts of energy justice and energy
transition.
However, even if an institution such as the EU adopts such a definition and prefers to
track this phenomenon under a consensual framework, most of the countries does not
reach consensus on using a single indicator.
In literature [1], [2], [3], [4] it is possible to find five categories of indicators:
• Consensual-based indicators (EU-SILC)
• Expenditure-based indicators (10%, MIS, LIHC, 2M, HEP or 2/M)
• Direct Measurement indicators (e.g., prices, income, technology, leakages)
• Multidimensional indicators
• Outcome-Based Indicators
Consensual-based
Consensual-based indicators, also known as subjective indicators, are based on self-
reported statements about people’s perceptions of thermal comfort and their ability to

3
==PAGE:16==
fulfill their energy needs. These indicators rely on people’s own assessments of their
situation, rather than objective measurements or data.
Expenditure-based
Expenditure-based, or objective, indicators are constructed using data on households’
income and energy expenditure. When a household’s expenditure on energy is above
or below a certain threshold, it is considered to be energy-poor. This approach allows
for the identification of energy poverty based on objective data, rather than subjective
self-reported statements.
Direct Measurement
Direct measurement indicators compare a household’s actual energy consumption with a
fixed set point for temperature and humidity. This set point is based on recommendations
from the World Health Organization (WHO) or national standards. These indicators can
be used to estimate the gap between the recommended temperature and humidity levels
and the actual levels in a household.
Multidimensional
Multidimensional indicators use a variety of metrics to identify energy-poor households
and track the severity of their conditions. These indicators may take into account fac-
tors such as energy prices, income, cooking and lighting solutions, pollution, and safety-
related issues.
Outcome-based
Outcome-based indicators focus on the effects of energy poverty, such as arrears on utility
bills and disconnections, or health outcomes, such as excess mortality in the winter. This
approach allows for the tracking of the consequences of energy poverty, rather than just
its prevalence or severity.

The definitions used to measure energy poverty can lead to different results in terms of the
incidence of energy poverty across countries. Comparing consensual and expenditure-
based indicators is expected to yield a negative linear correlation, which suggests that
these dimensions may be addressing different dimensions of the same phenomenon or

seem to track the economic dimension of the issue, as visible in Figure 1.2c.
The European Commission Energy Poverty Advisory Hub suggests three sub-definitions
of energy poverty:
• Perceived EPOV: Indicates the perceived energy scarcity affecting the household
respondents to questionnaire-based assessments. It tracks psychological discom-
fort and perceived social distance between the respondent and the surrounding con-
text’s ability to fulfill subjective energy needs. Consensual-based indicators fall un-
der this category.
• Objective EPOV: indicates the inability to fulfill the households’ energy needs due
to economic factors. It is measured with expenditure-based indicators and indicates
those households whose economic capacity is consistently constrained from their
energy expenditure up to a certain threshold. Expenditure-based, outcome-based,
direct measurement, and multidimensional indicators fall under this category.
• Hidden EPOV: Indicates those households whose energy expenditure lies in the
lower tail of the country distribution, excluding energy-efficient ones. It keeps track

division implies that to mitigate energy poverty, a multi-dimensional approach is needed

Making an analogy between poverty and energy poverty, the trickle-up theory states that
economic growth tends to trickle up to the middle classes but not the poorer classes,
increasing poverty. The trickle-down theory states that economic growth plays a crucial
role in poverty reduction, provided that the distribution of income remains constant. [35]
The trickle effect might also be transferred to the concept of energy poverty. In particular,
drawing on an indirect social influence perspective, studies clarify the definition of trickle
effects as a process whereby perceptions, feelings, attitudes, or behaviors of a source
affect those of a transmitter, which then affect those of a recipient. [36]
These considerations suggest that expenditure-based indicators might not adequately
track all the households falling under an energy poverty condition.

• be comparable across countries
it is chosen in this work as an energy poverty indicator to ultimately analyze a country’s
performance in this study.

1.2
Countries Overview and Current Policies
There are various approaches to identifying energy poverty, although many of these don’t
take into consideration a single definition of the issue, ultimately leading to tracking differ-
ent things. Even at a European level, these divergences are acknowledged.
Not having a shared definition of energy poverty inevitably affects the policies adopted to
address it. It is then crucial to reach an agreement and investigate the country-specific
reasons that generate the diffusion of energy poverty and its predictors.
For example, Denmark addresses energy poverty only under the social lens of vulnerable
households, therefore, policies targeting those individuals as thermal efficiencies enhanc-
ing programs are prioritized. A substantial part of energy-poor individuals benefits from
these schemes, in fact, the number of households unable to keep their homes adequately
warm is less than half of the EU average, but still the highest in the Nordics. In addition,
looking at the expenditure-based indicator, Denmark is one of the countries with highest
household electricity prices when including taxes and levies. This is closely connected to
the number of households with a high share of energy expenditure in their income, which
is 1.4 points above the EU average, and part of these households might not benefit from
the mentioned social schemes.
Another example comes from Bulgaria: thanks to a portfolio of measures destined to re-
duce the number of households unable to keep their homes adequately warm, a negative
trend can be observed during 2005-2018, going from 70% to almost 34% (EU average
2018 7.3%). However, this backfires in the number of households which end up with
having arrears in their utility bills, increasing from 20% in 2005 to 30% in 2018. The poli-
cies adopted by the country are on the same line as the Danish ones, meaning financial
assistance, with a great focus on vulnerable households and buildings renovations. The
same effect on consensual-based indicators is found: a substantial decrease in the In-
ability to keep house adequately warm, and an increase in arrears on utility bills. Bulgaria
has instead expenditure-based indicators which point out an overall good performance,
being even lower than the EU average in 2018. This case points out how policies aimed
at mitigating energy poverty might cause a burden-shifting effect.
Lithuania shows another peculiar condition: in the same period, the general increase
in wages and living standards contributed to mitigating the households with arrears in
utility bills from 21% in 2005 to 9.2% in 2018 [33]. Similarly, the number of households
not being able to keep their homes adequately warm decreased from 35% to 28%, with
some fluctuation, mainly driven by energy prices and electricity markets integration with
Finland, Sweden, and Poland [33]. Expenditure-based indicators are instead in line with
the EU average. No specific measure to directly mitigate energy poverty is in place other
than social policies, such as heating compensation or government-sponsored efficiency
programs.
Portugal also shows a consistent reduction in the number of households not being able to
keep their homes adequately warm, from 40% in 2005 to 19.4% in 2018. The reason might
be found in a social tariff aimed at providing financial support to vulnerable households
[33]. Around 14% of total households have benefited from the social tariff, slightly reducing
income inequality. Again, the policies address energy poverty indirectly through social
tariffs, providing financial aid for households’ energy expenditure, and energy efficiency
programs. The effects are in line with the ones in other countries on consensual-based
indicators.

1.2.1
Nordics
Overall, the Nordic countries keep energy poverty under low or very low levels of diffusion
despite the cold climate environment that households face during the winter months. The
policies in place in these countries are focused on social support for vulnerable house-
holds and low-income families. In the Nordic context, although, expenditure-based en-
ergy poverty exceeds the European average, which might be explained by a substantially
higher per capita energy consumption. This also underlines how cultural factors play their
role, with Scandinavian countries tending to prioritize a comfortable living space over their
expenditure.
Moreover, the number of households with low absolute energy expenditure is also higher
than the European average, which might indicate an high energy efficiency of buildings, or
that eventually a share of energy poverty is constituted by hidden energy poverty. House-
holds that are more exposed to fall under objective energy poverty in Sweden and Finland
are constituted by middle-income families [33]. This might be caused by the absence of
any policy in support of their energy expenditures, together with the willingness to keep
an acceptable temperature inside their houses during the cold months.
Analyzing the number of households with low energy expenditure into those that fall below
the poverty threshold, those that limit energy expenditure for cultural reasons, and those
that live in well-insulated buildings (not considered to be energy poor) can help target
more effective policies. Despite the significant decrease in the cumulative annual energy
consumption of households in the Nordics over the period 2010-2020, with a reduction
of 872 kWh per capita, the number of households with high energy expenditure remains
well above average EU levels. While this reduction is the highest among the European
countries studied, the colder climate in these countries means that efficiency and savings
policies may be crucial in reducing hidden energy poverty.

Denmark
In Denmark, the share of households unable to appropriately maintain home warmth
dropped steadily from 9% in 2005 to 2.8% in 2021. A mix of social and economic policies
and initiatives targeted to enhance the thermal efficiency of the buildings might be respon-
sible for the noticeable drop in 2008 [33]. Households with electricity bill arrears follow a
different trend instead, going from 3% to 5% between 2007 and 2018, to decrease again
to 2.9% in 2021. it is possible that rising energy costs might have caused indicators to
perform worse over time starting in 2008 onward.
Given the high values of the consensual-based energy poverty manifestation in Denmark
in 2008, but the high values of the expenditure-based energy poverty, the three pillars
of energy justice become relevant to discuss. Due to the application of social and eco-
nomic reforms combined with targeted thermal efficiency measures in buildings, a rapid
decrease in energy poverty was seen in one year. On the other hand, the increase in
household energy bills might have played a central role in the number of households with
arrears on utility bills, which have fluctuated over time. This might be a burden-shifting
effect caused by excluding policies on retail energy prices in the Danish social policy to
mitigate energy poverty.
Therefore, even though Denmark shows one of the best performances in Europe related
to energy poverty diffusion, the role of further research to mitigate burden-shifting effects in
those areas that are not addressed by social policies might play a central role in avoiding
eventual future rebound effects. In fact, adopting energy strategies at a national level
influences the performance of a country on energy poverty manifestation.
Sweden
In Sweden, the number of households unable to maintain a sufficient level of heating
has been consistently low over the past few years and has never gone above the 3%
threshold.
Indicators based on expenditures show worse performance in Sweden than in the rest of
the EU (28.7% ref. 2015). Although the expenditure-based indicator used to describe the
state of energy poverty is not accurate, in fact, in Sweden, rent typically includes energy
charges.
Also, the Swedish example points out the efficacy of social-based reforms and supports
available to those vulnerable households that might suffer from perceiving a certain dis-
comfort in fulfilling their energy needs. The strategy adopted to deal with the spread of
energy poverty in Sweden lies in various forms of social policies that contribute signifi-
cantly to keeping indicators at a low level. A strong social support system called social
assistance measures helps low-income families meet living expenses such as housing
and electricity. At the same time, the housing allowance provides financial support for
housing expenses such as heating costs, depending on the temperature zone, and the
size of the house.
Nevertheless, as expenditure-based indicators point out, in 2015, 28.7% of the house-
holds in Sweden had a high share of their income spent on energy, well above the Eu-
ropean average (16.2%), although, this might be due to the higher per capita energy
consumption in Sweden (64 MWh in 2015) compared to the European average (38 MWh
in 2015).
In addition, 26.1% of Swedish households have a considerably low energy expenditure,
nevertheless, given the extremely low consensual-based energy poverty level, instead of
indicating households that restrain their consumption, this might indicate good insulation

Finland
In Finland, the number of households that state they are unable to adequately heat their
homes remains fairly constant at a very low level between 1.1% and 2.6%. The number
of people living in households that have arrears on electricity has also remained fairly
constant between 6.6% and 8.4%, although, only 1.3% of households stated that they
perceive a lack of adequate ability to heat their homes in 2021. The gap between the
inability to pay the bills and being able to heat homes might be explained by the inability
to interrupt the supply to those who have arrears due to consumer protection policies.
Also in Finland, a high proportion of households spend a high share of their income on
energy consumption (22.3%) compared to the EU average (16.2%). This might indicate,
similarly to Sweden, high energy expenses due to the poor energy efficiency of certain
buildings and/or a high energy consumption per capita (60MWh in 2015). At the same
time, the proportion of households with extremely low energy expenditure is 29.9%, indi-
cating a large number of well-insulated households instead of a spreading phenomenon
of hidden energy poverty not evident in consensual-based indicators.
Finland addresses the issue of energy poverty with a comprehensive social bolster frame-
work in Finland. The basic income support and the housing allowance provide financial
assistance to families to cover their basic costs, such as nourishment, clothing, heating,
and power costs. In addition, the disconnection protection measure prevents users from
being disconnected from the supply.
1.2.2
Baltics
It is evident that the Baltic countries have a higher sensitivity to economic trends in the
macro-European area given the dips observed on the consensual-based indicators. This
might be due to the development levels of the countries, which are among the worst per-
formers in the west European area in terms of GDP per capita, which also seems to have
been negatively affected in the years following the financial crisis of 2008. Therefore,
even though indicators have some variability across the Baltics, the policies targeted at
energy poverty mitigation in these countries might be unsuccessful if they discard eco-
nomic growth. In fact, even though social policies can have a certain degree of effec-
tiveness, to reach low levels of energy poverty, a general increase in salaries and living
standards is needed, especially due to the cold climate in the area itself.
Apart from the generalized need to foster economic growth, the low salary levels, and
the attention to be put on cultural factors causing households to underspend instead of
fulfilling their energy needs, a country-specific approach is required in order to address
the different indicators’ performances. In fact, while Estonia requires policies that are
more similar to the ones needed in the Nordic area, Lithuania might need a more struc-
tural approach, especially given the probability of the households restraining their energy
consumption, which might imply high levels of the ”heat or eat” effect added to a diffused
perceived discomfort.
Estonia
In 2021, Estonia saw 4.1% households having overdue debts on utility bills, performing
better than the European average. Households perceiving themselves as incapable of
keeping their domestic environment adequately warm were at 2% in 2021, less than a
third of the EU average. Similarly to Finland, this gap results from the disconnection
protection enforced in the country. Estonia’s performance within the expenditure-based
indicator is slightly more regrettable than the EU normal: 18.7%. Approximately 18.9% of
Estonian households have an exceptionally low energy expenditure (EU at 14.6%).

In Estonia, the number of households with arrears in utility bills increased between 2007

Lithuania
Lithuania has a poor performance in regard to the number of households under perceived
energy poverty (25% in 2020) compared to the EU average of 7.3%. The number of in-
dividuals that had arrears on their utility bills was 9.2%, which was higher than the EU
average of 6.6%. The gap between the number of individuals incapable of keeping their
households satisfactorily warm and the number of households with arrears on utility bills
might be caused by constrained expenditure due to low salary levels. Lithuania’s per-
formance within the expenditure-based indicator is slightly higher than the EU normal:
13.9% of households spend a big share of their annual income on their energy expendi-
ture, while those who have an exceptionally low energy expenditure are in line with the
European average.
Lithuania lacks a definition and a strategy to mitigate energy poverty in the country, with
the government addressing the problem under general social policies. The country pro-
file of Lithuania diverges from the Nordics and Estonia; in fact, while expenditure-based
indicators are at average levels, perceived energy poverty touches three times more peo-
ple than the European average. The opposite performance of Lithuania in relation to
the Nordics might suggest the presence of a singular cultural context which induces the
households to adopt a conservative behavior in regard to their energy expenses. Hence,
they are saving on their income instead of sufficiently spending on energy commodities.
Nevertheless, even though the GDP per capita is higher compared to the other Baltics,
the performance on energy poverty is much worse than that of the other Baltic countries
on perceived energy poverty.

Germany

declined from 5.0% in 2010 to 2.7% in 2018, to then spike up over 6% in 2020. However,

the proportion of residents in households with unpaid utility bills dropped to 3.0% in 2018.

Scientific Question and Motivation
This work focuses on the perceived issue of energy poverty as the declared perception
of not being able to fulfill the subjective energy needs and measured with the indicator
tracking the inability to keep the houses adequately warm.
In fact, being the only indicator able to keep track of a present social discomfort originating
from energy scarcity, it allows us to identify the entity of people affected by such discom-
fort. The causes of the perception of scarcity can differ across regional areas and eco-
nomic status, and it can be investigated through multidimensional approaches based on
macroeconomic indicators, more traditional ones based on quantitative measurements,
or by tracking the presence of a perceived issue.
Literature shows that a set of secondary indicators is used to identify the drivers of energy
poverty. Gross Domestic Product per capita (GDPpc) [37], energy efficiency (in consump-
tion per capita: Conspc) [38], and carbon emissions per capita (CO2pc) [39], [40] have
been identified in the literature as predictors of households’ ability to keep home ade-
quately warm in EU and regional studies for different time-periods.
Given the existing evidence regarding the macroeconomic drivers of energy poverty, this
work investigates a sample of 23 countries divided into 5 subgroups (Nordics, Baltics,
Central, Southern, and Eastern Europe), for 2012-2021, with a panel ARDL approach,
which among GDP per capita, energy consumption per capita, and carbon emissions per
capita has a long-run and short-run statistically significant relationship with the inability to
keep houses adequately warm.
In addition, due to recent evidence in the literature on the mitigating effects of renewable
energy production on energy poverty [41] in the EU [42] even with regional heterogeneity,
studies start to approach energy poverty and its drivers under the lens of energy sources.
[40] Moreover, evidence from China suggest that reducing energy poverty requires a tran-
sition from fossil fuels to hydroelectric, nuclear, wind, and solar power [43]. For these rea-
sons, a second empirical econometric model is implemented, adopting energy production
as independent variables.
The second model investigates the relationships between primary energy production from
dispatchable low-carbon technologies (hydro and nuclear), fossil fuels, and variable re-
newable energies (solar and wind) and the inability to keep houses adequately warm for
the given sample of countries and time periods. The choice of separating variable renew-
able energy from nuclear and hydroelectric production finds its origin in literature [40].
There are several reasons for the motivation behind this study on the drivers of energy
poverty.
Firstly, energy poverty is a significant issue that affects a large number of people, also
in developed countries. Being able to identify the causes of energy poverty and poten-
tial solutions can help alleviate the negative effects of energy scarcity on individuals and
communities.
Secondly, such a study can help policymakers and organizations develop effective strate-
gies to reduce energy poverty and promote right energy access.
Finally, studying the relationship between macroeconomic indicators and energy poverty
can provide valuable insights into the broader relationship between economic develop-

Overall, conducting a study on the drivers of energy poverty can have significant social
and economic benefits.

Method
The methodology used and the steps of the analysis are based on methods present in
recent literature ([44], [45], [46], [47]).
Figure 3.1: Illustration of the panel estimation method used in the analysis. (author’s own
elaboration)
Figure 3.1 mentions the steps that have been implemented in this work. After defining the
key variables, the country groups, and the period analyzed in Section 2, different panel
unit root tests are performed. The cointegration level of the variable is chosen (I(0) or I(1))
based on the number of statistically significant tests, then the Pedroni cointegration test
is performed on variables integrated of order I(1). If the Pedroni test shows evidence of
cointegration, the error correction term (ECT) of the panel ARDL estimation is expected
to show statistically significant coefficients; alternatively, the panel ARDL is dropped and
is expected to be inconclusive.

3.5
Panel ARDL
A panel autoregressive distributed lag (ARDL) model is a type of econometric model that
can be used to analyze the relationship between a dependent variable and one or more
independent variables over time. The panel ARDL is beneficial because:
• it simultaneously estimates short- and long-run dynamics, when traditional methods
of cointegration only assess the long-term correlation in the equations,
• it can be used with different orders of integration, I(0), I(1), or a mixture of I(0) and
I(1) variables as long as none of them are I(2). [56]
Furthermore, it accommodates a different number of lags on each variable [57], [54].
Study shows using the data of 190 countries grouped under low-income, middle-income,
and high-income from 1991 to 2019 and a panel ARDL approach that heterogeneity is
present and that the stage of economic development is a key factor in determining the
energy poverty level of a country. [58]
Moreover, a second study uses the panel ARDL estimates to show that energy poverty
has a negative influence on economic development in both the long run and the short run.
[56]
In addition, panel studies have started investigating the relationship between energy poverty
and the use of renewable energy sources: one considers 25 provinces from the China
Family Panel Studies (CFPS) as the research objects from 2012 to 2016. The study
found that renewable energy technology innovation alleviates household energy poverty,
and the alleviation effect increases when marketization reaches a certain level. Although,
significant regional heterogeneity exists. [59]
The model purposed here investigates how different variables affect the share of house-
holds perceiving to be unable to keep their houses adequately warm in the panel coun-
tries. The relationship between lagged values of the share, as well as lagged values of
the independent variables, can be investigated.
The Pooled Mean Group (PMG) approach is used in the estimation.

might happen both in lower income groups, perceiving higher income groups as capable
of higher or more efficient energy consumption, or by higher income groups comparing
themselves with high-income groups belonging to other cultural contexts.
The effects of consumption per capita and CO2 emissions per capita seem easier to ex-
plain and might suggest that energy poverty at a European level is mitigated by increased
consumption at the cost of CO2 production.

Limitations and Conclusion
6.1
Limitations
Data limitations have an impact on this study. The indicator of the inability to adequately
heat homes is only available for the period 2012-2021 and is measured annually, which
requires a panel analysis. Additionally, some countries have missing data and were there-
fore excluded from the analysis, resulting in a total of 23 countries being included. The
availability and relevance of external data sources for the same time period of 2012-2021
also influence the goals of the analysis. The limited dataset also affects the analysis of
country groups such as the Nordics and the Baltics, as there are only a small number
of observations per group (30). These data limitations may contribute to the numerous
coefficients that do not reach statistical significance and reduce the findings of the study.
The data used in 3.4 are countrywide indicators and provide a macroeconomic analysis
of energy poverty. However, including household-specific or regional data would have
allowed for a more detailed analysis. While data on individual households has been col-
lected by the EU-SILC project, it is not publicly available for analysis, so this study is
unable to examine differences between local regions, household types, or social status.
Model 2 is based on data at an aggregated primary level, which includes losses, electric-
ity, transport, and heat production, and may therefore contain a significant level of noise
in the estimation. This is particularly relevant for low-carbon sources, which are primarily
electricity production technologies and only a portion of which is used for electric heating
or district heating applications, which also only cover a portion of total household heat de-
mand. A more detailed analysis of Model 2 would have provided a deeper understanding
of the relationship between energy sources and the spread of energy poverty.

6.2
Conclusion
This study aims to understand the main determinants and drivers of energy poverty in
European countries in order to inform policy making and contribute to the transition to
a sustainable energy future. To do so, the study analyzes the potential effects of differ-
ent sources of primary energy production on energy poverty at both the European and
regional level.
The unit root tests conducted indicate that the majority of the variables in the two models
are stationary at the first difference.
The results show that, at the European level, GDP per capita and CO2 emissions per
capita have a positive long-term impact on the percentage of households unable to ade-
quately heat their homes. However, GDP per capita has a negative short-term effect on
this issue.
There is variation in the relationship between GDP per capita and energy poverty among
different country groups. In the Baltic countries and eastern Europe, an increase in GDP
per capita leads to a reduction in energy poverty, which is the opposite of the trend ob-
served at the European level. This suggests that economic growth can help mitigate
energy poverty in countries with lower GDP per capita. Additionally, short-term analysis
shows that GDP per capita also helps reduce energy poverty in the short term in Nordic
countries and central Europe.
At the European level, an increase in energy consumption per person leads to a decrease
in energy poverty. However, in the Baltic countries, this trend is reversed and an increase
in energy consumption per person actually worsens energy poverty. This may be due to
the fact that the increased energy consumption does not adequately address the energy
poverty caused by the colder winters in this region.
An increase in solar and wind energy production at the European level leads to a de-
crease in energy poverty. However, there is some variation in this trend among different
country groups. For instance, in the Nordics, energy poverty is reduced by an increase in
hydroelectric and nuclear energy production, but it is worsened by an increase in renew-
able and fossil fuel energy production. In central European countries, fossil fuel energy
production also leads to higher levels of energy poverty.
These findings provide a nuanced understanding of the complex issue of energy poverty
in Europe and can inform policy making to support the transition to a more sustainable
energy future.
